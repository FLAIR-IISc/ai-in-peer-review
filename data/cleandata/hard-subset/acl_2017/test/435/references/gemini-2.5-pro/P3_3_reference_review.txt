### Summary and Contributions

This paper addresses the task of "causal meaning disambiguation," which involves determining whether a lexical marker connecting two events signals a causal relation. The authors argue that previous methods relying on handcrafted syntactic and lexical features are limited by the ambiguity of language and the coverage of resources. They propose that a neural network can learn to disambiguate causality by encoding the context of the two related events. The main contribution is an empirical demonstration that a straightforward dual-input LSTM architecture, using only pre-trained word embeddings, outperforms a strong feature-engineered SVM baseline (Hidey and McKeown, 2016) on the AltLex corpus. A secondary contribution is a useful analysis of the model's performance, including a qualitative look at specific examples where the context-aware neural model succeeds over the baseline.

### Strengths

1.  **Strong Empirical Results:** The paper presents convincing results that demonstrate the effectiveness of its proposed approach. The "Pair LSTM" model achieves a notable improvement over the state-of-the-art system on the AltLex dataset (e.g., a 2.13% F1 increase on the "non-bootstrapping" set). More importantly, the proposed model often achieves a better balance between precision and recall than the baseline, suggesting it is learning a more robust representation of causality rather than simply memorizing features. This is a solid and valuable empirical finding.

2.  **Clear Hypothesis and Sound Motivation:** The paper is well-motivated. It grounds its argument in linguistic theory (e.g., Neeleman and van de Koot, 2012) to argue that causality is not tied to fixed syntactic structures or a closed class of causative verbs. This provides a strong justification for moving away from feature-based systems toward models that can capture more nuanced contextual meaning. The central hypothesis—that neural networks can encode this context effectively—is clearly stated and rigorously tested through the experiments.

3.  **Methodological Simplicity:** The proposed neural architecture is simple and composed of standard, well-understood components (LSTMs, dense layers). This is a strength, not a weakness. It demonstrates that significant gains can be achieved without overly complex or task-specific architectural engineering, reinforcing the power of sequential context encoding for this task. The model is described with sufficient detail (hyperparameters, optimizers) to facilitate reproducibility.

4.  **Insightful Analysis:** The analysis in Section 4.3 is a high point of the paper. By providing concrete examples of instances misclassified by the baseline but correctly classified by their model (Table 8), the authors give concrete evidence for their claims. The discussion of ambiguous markers like "which then" and context-dependent verbs like "break" effectively illustrates the model's ability to leverage context, which is the core argument of the paper. This qualitative analysis significantly strengthens the quantitative results.

### Weaknesses

1.  **Limited Architectural Novelty and Exploration:** While the proposed model is effective, the "Pair LSTM" architecture is a fairly standard Siamese-like network for relation classification. The paper frames its contribution as proposing a "neural network architecture," which slightly overstates the methodological novelty. The primary contribution is more accurately described as the successful application and validation of this type of architecture for this specific task. Furthermore, the comparison is limited to the "Stated Pair" variant. A stronger paper would have compared this dual-input model against a simpler, strong neural baseline, such as a single BiLSTM that encodes the entire sequence `e1 + marker + e2`, to more robustly justify the architectural choice.

2.  **Positioning within Contemporary NLP:** The related work section focuses almost exclusively on pre-neural and feature-based systems. While this effectively sets up the comparison with the Hidey and McKeown (2016) baseline, it overlooks the broader context of neural models for discourse relation classification (e.g., on the PDTB) that were emerging at the time. Acknowledging and differentiating from other neural approaches to similar relation classification tasks would have provided a more complete picture of the landscape and better situated the paper's contribution.

3.  **Evaluation Limited to a Single Corpus:** The experiments are conducted solely on the AltLex corpus. While this is the most suitable dataset for the task as framed, the paper's broader claims about the superiority of neural context encoding would be more compelling if supported by results on other relevant datasets, even if smaller. For instance, evaluating on the explicit causal relations in the PDTB or Causal-TimeBank could demonstrate the model's generalizability and robustness across different domains and annotation schemes. The current evaluation, while strong for AltLex, makes it difficult to assess how dependent the model's success is on the specific properties of this (bootstrapped) corpus.

4.  **Minor Lack of Clarity in Model Description:** The description of the model's second input is slightly ambiguous. Section 3.3 states it is "the concatenation of the lexical marker and the second event." However, it is unclear how these two components are concatenated or if there is a special token to delineate them. This detail is important for understanding how the LSTM processes the marker versus the event text. Figure 1 seems to imply two distinct inputs ("Lexical Marker" and "Event 2") feeding into the second LSTM block, which contradicts the text's description of a single concatenated input. This ambiguity should be resolved for clarity and reproducibility.

### Questions to Authors

1.  Could you clarify the precise structure of the second input to the LSTM? Is it simply the token embeddings of the marker followed by the token embeddings of the second event in one sequence? Have you experimented with a simpler single-encoder baseline (e.g., a BiLSTM over the full `e1 + marker + e2` text), and if so, how did it compare to your dual-input model?

2.  Your finding that the independent "Pair LSTM" outperforms the "Stated Pair LSTM" is interesting. It suggests that initializing the second event's encoding with the final state of the first event is detrimental. Do you have further intuitions as to why this might be? Could it be that the causal relationship is not a simple sequential continuation but a more abstract relation between two independently understood propositions, which is better captured by the higher-level dense layers?

3.  Given the imbalanced nature of the dataset, have you considered using a weighted loss function during training as an alternative to downsampling the majority class? This might allow the model to learn from all available data while still accounting for the class imbalance.
