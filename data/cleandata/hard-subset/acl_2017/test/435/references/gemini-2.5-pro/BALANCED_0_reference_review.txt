**Summary of the Paper and Main Contributions**

This paper addresses the task of causal meaning disambiguation, which involves determining whether a lexical marker connecting two events signals a causal relationship. The authors argue that previous methods relying on handcrafted features, syntactic patterns, or lexical resources are limited in coverage and flexibility. They propose a neural approach using Long Short-Term Memory (LSTM) networks to encode the textual context of the two events and the connecting marker, hypothesizing that this is sufficient to disambiguate the causal meaning. The proposed models are evaluated on the AltLex corpus, where they are shown to outperform a strong feature-based SVM baseline, which was the previous state-of-the-art on this dataset.

The main contributions of this work, as I see them, are:

1.  An empirical demonstration that a standard neural sequence encoding architecture (a dual-encoder LSTM) can outperform a complex, feature-engineered system for causal lexical marker disambiguation. This result validates the hypothesis that deep context encoding, without reliance on external lexical resources or syntactic features, is an effective approach for this task.
2.  A systematic evaluation of different neural model configurations for this task, including two architectural variants ("Pair LSTM" and "Stated Pair LSTM"), as well as an analysis of different padding strategies and optimizers, providing insights for future work on this dataset.

**Strengths**

1.  **Strong Empirical Results:** The paper presents a clear and positive main result. The proposed LSTM-based models consistently outperform both a majority-class baseline and the previous state-of-the-art system on two versions of the AltLex corpus. The improvement over the state-of-the-art (e.g., 2.13% F1 and 7.72% Accuracy on the non-bootstrapping corpus) is significant and demonstrates the effectiveness of the proposed approach.
2.  **Well-Motivated Problem and Approach:** The introduction and related work sections provide a compelling motivation for the task. The authors clearly articulate the limitations of prior work that depends heavily on syntactic assumptions or feature engineering, making a strong case for a more flexible, context-driven neural method. The problem is well-scoped and the approach is a logical next step in this research area.
3.  **Thorough and Careful Experimentation:** The experimental design is sound. The authors compare against appropriate baselines, including the direct state-of-the-art on the same dataset. They responsibly test on two versions of the corpus ("non-bootstrapping" and "bootstrapping") and also conduct experiments on a class-balanced subset to ensure the results are not merely an artifact of the original data distribution. This level of diligence strengthens the credibility of the findings.
4.  **Good Analysis and Clarity:** The paper is well-written and easy to follow. The analysis in Section 4.3, particularly Table 8, provides valuable qualitative evidence of the model's strengths. Showing specific examples where the model correctly disambiguates ambiguous markers (like "break" or "which then") that the previous SOTA model misclassifies effectively illustrates the practical benefits of the context-encoding approach.

**Weaknesses**

1.  **Limited Technical Novelty:** The primary weakness of the paper is the limited novelty of the proposed method. The "Pair LSTM" model is a standard dual-encoder architecture that has been widely used for various sentence-pair classification tasks (e.g., NLI, paraphrase identification). While its application to this specific task is new and the results are valuable, the paper does not introduce a new neural technique or architecture. The contribution is therefore more empirical than methodological.
2.  **Omission of Stronger Contemporary Baselines:** The paper compares against a 2016 SVM-based system. In the context of current NLP research, a major omission is the lack of comparison to or discussion of Transformer-based models (e.g., BERT). A dual-encoder using BERT would be a much stronger and more contemporary baseline. Without this comparison, it is difficult to assess whether the proposed LSTM model is a truly effective solution or simply a better model than an outdated feature-based one.
3.  **Ambiguity in "Context" Encoding and Missing Details:** The paper claims to encode the "context," but the method description suggests the inputs are only the text spans of the two events (`e1` and `e2`) and the lexical marker (`l`). This approach seems to discard the surrounding sentential context (text before `e1`, between the spans, and after `e2`), which could be crucial for disambiguation. This design choice is not clearly justified. Furthermore, the paper lacks important details for reproducibility, such as the LSTM hidden size, whether the LSTMs are bidirectional, and the architecture of the dense layers following the encoders.
4.  **Weak Justification for Architectural Choices:** The argument for using LSTMs over CNNs in Section 3.3 is somewhat superficial. Stating that CNNs are not suitable because causality lacks a fixed syntactic structure and requires a predefined kernel size overlooks the ability of CNNs with multiple kernel sizes to capture local patterns of varying lengths. A more convincing argument would have been an empirical comparison. Similarly, the choice to concatenate the lexical marker `l` with `e2` rather than `e1` or encoding it separately is not justified.

**Questions to Authors**

1.  Could you please clarify what exactly constitutes the input to your LSTMs? Does it include only the annotated event and marker spans, or also the surrounding text within the sentence? If the surrounding text is excluded, could you explain the rationale for this decision, as it seems to contradict the goal of encoding the broader context?
2.  The "Pair LSTM" model is a well-established architecture. Could you please position your contribution more explicitly? Do you see the primary contribution as the empirical result of applying this architecture to the causal disambiguation task, or is there a more subtle technical novelty I have missed?
3.  For reproducibility, could you provide the key hyperparameters of your best-performing models? Specifically, the LSTM hidden dimension, whether the LSTMs were unidirectional or bidirectional, and the sizes of the dense layers used for classification.
4.  While you demonstrate strong performance against the 2016 baseline, how would you expect your model to perform against a more modern baseline, such as a fine-tuned BERT-based classifier, which has become the standard for many NLU tasks?
