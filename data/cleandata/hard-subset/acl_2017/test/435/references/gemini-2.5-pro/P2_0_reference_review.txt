**Summary of the Paper and Contributions**

This paper addresses the task of causal meaning disambiguation, which it defines as a binary classification problem: determining if a relation between two events, signaled by a lexical marker, is Causal or Non-Causal. The authors argue that previous methods relying on syntactic patterns or fixed lexical resources are too restrictive. They propose that a neural network can better capture the contextual nuances that signal causality. Their proposed model uses a dual-input LSTM architecture to encode the event preceding the marker and the marker plus the subsequent event. They evaluate their model on the AltLex corpus, demonstrating superior performance over the previous state-of-the-art, a feature-rich SVM-based system.

From my perspective, the main contributions are:

1.  **An effective application of a standard neural architecture to causal relation classification.** The paper successfully demonstrates that a relatively simple LSTM-based model, which relies solely on word embeddings to encode context, can outperform a complex, heavily feature-engineered system on this task. This validates the central hypothesis that deep contextual encoding is more effective than relying on predefined linguistic features for causality detection.
2.  **A thorough experimental evaluation and analysis.** The authors conduct a rigorous set of experiments on two versions of the AltLex corpus. They compare multiple configurations of their own model, test against a strong baseline, and perform an additional experiment on a class-balanced dataset to validate their findings. The qualitative analysis provides convincing examples that support their claims about the model's ability to handle ambiguity.

**Strengths**

1.  **Clear Motivation and Problem Formulation:** The paper provides a solid background on the linguistic and computational approaches to causation. It effectively argues against the limitations of syntax-based and lexical-resource-dependent methods, building a strong case for a context-aware neural approach. The task is framed clearly as a binary classification problem, which is well-suited for the proposed method.
2.  **Strong Empirical Results:** The proposed model achieves significant improvements over the previous state-of-the-art on the "non-bootstrapping" version of the AltLex corpus (+2.13% F1, +7.72% Accuracy) and also outperforms it on the more challenging "bootstrapping" version. These results are not marginal and convincingly show the superiority of their approach over the feature-based baseline.
3.  **Robust Experimental Setup:** The evaluation is comprehensive. The authors compare against both a simple majority-class baseline (B1) and the original, strong baseline for the dataset (B2). They explore different architectural variants (Pair LSTM vs. Stated Pair LSTM), optimizers, and padding strategies, providing a good sense of the model's design space. The inclusion of experiments on a balanced version of the dataset further strengthens the reliability of their conclusions.
4.  **Insightful Qualitative Analysis:** The error analysis in Section 4.3 is a key strength. The examples chosen (e.g., the non-causal use of the causative verb "break," and the ambiguous marker "which then") compellingly illustrate the model's ability to leverage context to make correct predictions where the baseline fails. This analysis directly supports the paper's central claim.

**Weaknesses**

1.  **Limited Architectural Novelty:** The "Pair LSTM" architecture is a standard and well-established model for sentence-pair classification tasks. While its application here is successful and appropriate, the model itself does not represent a novel technical contribution to neural network architectures. The paper's contribution is therefore more empirical and applicational than methodological.
2.  **Lack of More Modern Baselines:** The primary comparison is against a feature-based SVM system from 2016. In the intervening years, neural methods have become standard. The paper would be significantly stronger if it compared its LSTM model against other common neural baselines for this type of task, such as a Siamese CNN, an attention-based model, or, most importantly, a fine-tuned transformer-based model (e.g., BERT). Without such comparisons, it is difficult to ascertain whether the performance gain is due to this specific LSTM setup or simply the general superiority of neural representation learning over the 2016-era feature-based approach.
3.  **Unjustified Input Segmentation:** The model's inputs are defined as `e1` and `l + e2` (lexical marker concatenated with the second event). This is a critical design choice, but the paper offers no justification for it over other possibilities, such as feeding the entire sentence as a single input with span markers, or treating `e1` and `e2` as inputs and handling `l` separately. This specific segmentation could influence what the model learns, and the lack of rationale or ablations on this choice is a missed opportunity.
4.  **Minor Clarity Issues:** The description of the padding strategies is confusing. The paper states they evaluated padding based on the "maximum, the mean and mode of the length." Padding is a procedure to make sequences a uniform length, typically by adding tokens up to a maximum length. It is unclear how "mean" or "mode" length is used as a "strategy." This needs clarification. Additionally, the reference list contains formatting errors.

**Questions to Authors**

1.  Could you please clarify your padding strategy? Specifically, what does it mean to use the "mean" or "mode" of the input lengths as a padding strategy? Are you padding all sequences to a fixed length that corresponds to the mean/mode length of sequences in the training set and truncating longer ones?
2.  Your model splits the input into `(e1)` and `(l + e2)`. What was the reasoning behind this specific segmentation? Did you experiment with alternative ways of representing the events and the lexical marker, for instance, by feeding the entire text to the LSTM and using the hidden states corresponding to the event/marker spans?
3.  Given the widespread success of pre-trained transformer models, how do you see your LSTM-based approach positioned relative to a baseline like a fine-tuned BERT model? A brief discussion on this would help contextualize your results within the current landscape of NLP.
