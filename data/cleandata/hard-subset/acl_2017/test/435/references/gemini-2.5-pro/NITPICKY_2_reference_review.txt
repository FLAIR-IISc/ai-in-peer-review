**Summary and Contributions**
This paper addresses the task of causal meaning disambiguation, which it frames as a binary classification problem: determining if a lexical marker between two events signals a causal or non-causal relationship. The authors propose a dual-input LSTM-based neural network that encodes the two events and the marker to make this classification. The model is evaluated on the AltLex corpus, where it is shown to outperform the previous state-of-the-art feature-based SVM system.

As I see them, the main contributions of this work are:
1.  An empirical demonstration that a standard dual-encoder LSTM architecture, trained only on word embeddings without explicit linguistic features, can achieve state-of-the-art performance on the AltLex causal disambiguation task.
2.  A useful qualitative analysis of the model's performance on ambiguous connectives and causative verbs used in non-causal contexts, which effectively highlights the benefits of contextual encoding over feature-based methods.

**Strengths**
1.  The paper tackles a relevant and non-trivial problem in discourse analysis. The ambiguity of causal markers is a well-known challenge, and the paper's focus on data-driven contextual disambiguation is a sound direction.
2.  The experimental results are solid. The proposed model demonstrates clear improvements over both a strong majority-class baseline and the prior state-of-the-art system from Hidey and McKeown (2016). The gains in F1-score and Accuracy on both versions of the AltLex corpus are convincing.
3.  The analysis in Section 4.3 is a high point of the paper. By providing concrete examples in Table 8 where the proposed model succeeds and the baseline fails (e.g., with the highly ambiguous "which then" or the non-causal use of "break off"), the authors provide compelling evidence for their central claim regarding the power of neural context encoding.
4.  The authors perform several experiments to understand their model's behavior, including comparing different optimizers, evaluating connected vs. independent LSTMs, and testing on a class-balanced version of the dataset. This thoroughness adds value to the experimental section.

**Weaknesses**
Despite its strengths, the paper suffers from a substantial number of flaws in its presentation, methodological clarity, and experimental design, which collectively undermine its quality.

1.  **Poor Presentation and Formatting:** The paper's presentation is unacceptably careless for a conference submission.
    *   The entire References section is an unformatted, raw dump of bibliographic data, complete with BibTeX-like syntax (`\.,`, `\Q2017\E`) and stray question marks. It is illegible and demonstrates a severe lack of attention to detail.
    *   There are missing elements referenced directly in the text. Most critically, "Table 6," which should describe the downsampled dataset, is completely absent.
    *   Annotations in examples are inconsistent and erroneous. For instance, in Section 3.1, the `e1` segment is not marked in the example for `[so]l`. In Table 8, the annotation for `e2` in the first example is misplaced. These errors impede clarity.

2.  **Unclear and Questionable Methodological Choices:** Several core aspects of the methodology are either not justified or deviate from standard practice without explanation.
    *   **Input Representation:** The model takes `e1` as one input and the concatenation of the lexical marker and the second event (`l + e2`) as the other. There is no motivation for this specific configuration. It is unclear why the marker `l` is grouped with `e2` rather than being handled separately or grouped with `e1`. This arbitrary design choice requires justification.
    *   **Padding Strategy:** The authors state they evaluated padding to the "maximum, the mean and mode of the length of the two inputs." Padding to a mean or mode length is a highly unorthodox procedure that implies truncating longer, potentially informative sequences. The paper offers no rationale for this strange choice over the standard practice of padding to the maximum length in a batch. This raises concerns about the technical implementation.

3.  **Lack of Novelty in the Proposed Model:** The proposed "Pair LSTM" architecture is a standard dual-encoder or Siamese-like network. While its application to this specific problem is new, the architecture itself lacks novelty, which slightly weakens the paper's contribution. The framing of this standard architecture as a key contribution is an overstatement.

4.  **Incomplete Experimentation:** In the experiment with the balanced (downsampled) dataset, the authors only compare their model to the weak B1 baseline (majority class). The far more important comparison against the B2 baseline (Hidey and McKeown, 2016) is omitted. Without this result, it is impossible to know if the model's advantage holds in a setting with a different class distribution, making this part of the analysis incomplete.

**Questions to Authors**
1.  Could you please clarify and justify your padding strategy? Why did you experiment with padding/truncating sequences to the mean and mode length, and how does this affect sequences longer than that threshold?
2.  What is the rationale for structuring the model's input as `(e1)` and `(l + e2)`? Have you experimented with alternative configurations, such as a three-part input `(e1, l, e2)` or treating `l` as part of the first event?
3.  For the experiments on the balanced dataset (Table 7), could you provide the performance of the B2 baseline (Hidey and McKeown, 2016)? This is a critical comparison that is currently missing.
