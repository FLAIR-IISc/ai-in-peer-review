### Review

**Summary of the Paper and Main Contributions**

This paper addresses the task of causal meaning disambiguation: determining if a lexical marker connecting two events signals a causal relationship. The authors argue that previous feature-engineered systems are limited by their reliance on specific syntactic patterns and lexical resources. To overcome this, they propose a neural architecture based on dual Long Short-Term Memory (LSTM) networks that encode the context of the two events involved in the potential causal relation. The model is evaluated on the AltLex corpus, where it is shown to outperform previous state-of-the-art methods.

As I see them, the main contributions of this work are:

1.  **An effective application of a neural architecture for causal relation classification.** The paper demonstrates that a straightforward dual-LSTM model, which relies solely on pre-trained word embeddings without complex feature engineering, can outperform a strong, feature-rich SVM-based system on this task. This highlights the power of learned contextual representations for capturing the nuances of causal language.
2.  **A thorough empirical evaluation and analysis on the AltLex dataset.** The authors conduct a comprehensive set of experiments on two versions of the AltLex corpus. The comparison against both a majority-class baseline and the previous state-of-the-art, coupled with a qualitative error analysis, provides convincing evidence for the model's ability to handle lexical and contextual ambiguity more effectively than prior work.

**Strengths**

1.  **Clear Motivation and Problem Formulation:** The paper is well-written and well-motivated. It provides a clear overview of the linguistic and computational background of causality detection, effectively arguing for the need to move beyond methods reliant on pre-defined patterns or lexical resources. The problem is framed as a binary classification task, which is a well-scoped and appropriate formulation.
2.  **Sound Experimental Design:** The experimental setup is methodologically sound. The choice of the AltLex corpus and the direct comparison to the system introduced in the same paper (Hidey and McKeown, 2016) allows for a clear measure of progress. The inclusion of the majority-class baseline (B1) is crucial, as it correctly identifies the strong lexical priors in the dataset and demonstrates that the proposed model learns more than just the dominant sense of each connective. The evaluation of different architectural variants (e.g., "Stated Pair LSTM" vs. "Pair LSTM") and optimizers is thorough and provides useful insights.
3.  **Strong Empirical Results and Analysis:** The proposed model achieves state-of-the-art performance on a standard benchmark for this task. While the F1-score gains are modest, the improvements in accuracy are more substantial, and the qualitative analysis in Section 4.3 is particularly compelling. The examples in Table 8, which show the model correctly classifying ambiguous cases (e.g., a non-causal use of the verb "break" or the ambiguous connective "which then"), strongly support the central claim that encoding the broader context is key to robust causal disambiguation.

**Weaknesses**

1.  **Limited Architectural Novelty:** The proposed architecture, consisting of two parallel LSTMs to encode a pair of text sequences for a classification task, is a standard and well-established pattern in NLP. While its application here is effective, the model itself does not introduce a novel neural network design. The contribution lies more in demonstrating the effectiveness of this standard approach on this specific task, rather than in methodological innovation. The paper could be strengthened by a brief discussion of why this architecture is well-suited for the task and whether more complex alternatives (e.g., using attention mechanisms) were considered and why they might or might not be beneficial.
2.  **Modest F1 Performance Gains:** The improvement in F1 score over the B2 baseline is relatively small: 1.64 percentage points on the "non-bootstrapping" set and between 0.66 and 1.06 points on the "bootstrapping" set. While the paper highlights a better Precision-Recall balance, the marginal nature of the F1 improvement raises questions about the practical significance of the new model. The claims of outperforming the state-of-the-art would be much stronger if supported by statistical significance testing.
3.  **Lack of Reproducibility Details:** The paper omits several key details required for reproducibility. Important hyperparameters such as the LSTM hidden state size, learning rates, batch size, and the number of training epochs are not reported. While ranges for dropout and regularization are given, the final values used in the best-performing models are not specified. This makes it difficult for other researchers to verify the results or build upon this work.
4.  **Unjustified Input Representation:** The model concatenates the lexical marker (l) with the second event (e2) to form a single input sequence. This design choice is not explicitly justified or analyzed against alternatives. For example, the marker could have been treated as a separate input, or its embedding could have been concatenated with the LSTM outputs. It is unclear how this design choice affects the model's learning and whether it is optimal for capturing the interaction between the events and the marker.

**Questions to Authors**

1.  Given that the F1 score improvements over the B2 baseline are modest, have you performed statistical significance tests (e.g., a bootstrap test or McNemar's test) to validate that your model's superior performance is statistically significant?
2.  To ensure the work is reproducible, could you please provide the full set of hyperparameters used for your best-performing models reported in Table 5? This would include the LSTM hidden dimension size, the exact dropout and regularization values, the learning rates for Adadelta and Adam, and the batch size.
3.  The model architecture combines the lexical marker (l) and the second event (e2) into a single input. What was the motivation for this choice over alternative designs, such as a three-input model (e1, l, e2) or concatenating the marker's embedding at a later stage? Did you experiment with other ways of incorporating the marker's information?
