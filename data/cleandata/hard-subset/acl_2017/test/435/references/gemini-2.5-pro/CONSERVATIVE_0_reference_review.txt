**Summary of the paper and main contributions**

This paper addresses the task of causal meaning disambiguation, which involves classifying whether a relationship between two text spans (events), signaled by a lexical marker, is causal. The authors argue that existing methods relying on hand-crafted linguistic features are limited. They propose a neural network model based on a dual-LSTM architecture to encode the context of the two events and classify the relation. The model is evaluated on the AltLex corpus, where it is shown to achieve a modest improvement over the previous state-of-the-art, a feature-based SVM system.

As I see them, the primary contributions of this work are:
1.  An empirical study applying a standard dual-LSTM architecture to the task of causal relation classification on the AltLex dataset.
2.  A demonstration that this neural approach can achieve a marginal performance improvement over a strong, feature-engineered baseline without relying on external lexical resources like WordNet or VerbNet.

**Strengths**

1.  **Clear Problem Formulation:** The paper targets a well-defined and significant problem in discourse analysis and natural language understanding. The motivation for moving beyond feature-based systems that rely on potentially incomplete lexical resources is sound.
2.  **Reproducibility and Sound Baselines:** The experiments are conducted on a publicly available dataset, and the authors compare their method against appropriate baselines, including a majority-class classifier and the state-of-the-art system that originally introduced the dataset. This provides a clear context for interpreting the results.
3.  **Systematic Ablation:** The authors systematically evaluate different architectural choices, such as padding strategies, optimizers, and the connection between the LSTMs. This provides useful insights into what works best for this particular task setup.
4.  **Clarity of Writing:** The paper is well-structured and clearly written, making the authors' approach and experimental setup easy to understand.

**Weaknesses**

1.  **Limited Methodological Novelty:** The proposed "Pair LSTM" architecture is a very standard design for sentence-pair classification tasks. Architectures that separately encode two text sequences with LSTMs/RNNs and then combine their representations for a final classification are well-established in the literature for tasks like natural language inference and paraphrase identification. The paper presents this as a primary contribution, but it is more accurately described as an application of an existing, off-the-shelf neural architecture to a new task. The lack of significant methodological innovation is a major weakness.
2.  **Marginal Empirical Gains:** The performance improvement over the Hidey and McKeown (2016) baseline is quite small. On the main "bootstrapping" dataset, the best-performing model ("Pair LSTM Mean Adam") improves the F1-score from 80.90% to 81.56% and accuracy from 79.58% to 80.68%. While this is an improvement, such a marginal gain does not provide compelling evidence that the proposed neural approach is fundamentally superior. For a new method to be convincing, it should ideally offer a more substantial performance leap or other benefits (e.g., efficiency, interpretability), which are not demonstrated here.
3.  **Lack of Simpler Neural Baselines:** The paper compares a feature-based SVM directly with an LSTM model. A more thorough evaluation would have included simpler but strong neural baselines. For instance, a model that simply averages the word embeddings for each event span and feeds the resulting vectors into a multi-layer perceptron would be an important reference point. Without this comparison, it is difficult to ascertain whether the performance gain comes specifically from the sequential modeling of the LSTM or merely from the use of pre-trained word embeddings in a neural framework. This is a critical missing experiment for validating the architectural choices.
4.  **Superficial Analysis:** The analysis in Section 4.3 relies on a handful of anecdotal examples. While these examples are illustrative, they do not constitute a rigorous analysis of the model's behavior. The central claim is that encoding "context" is key, but the paper fails to provide a deep investigation into *what specific contextual cues* the LSTM model is learning that the feature-based SVM misses. A more systematic error analysis, comparing the types of errors made by both models, or the use of model probing techniques, would be necessary to substantiate the claims about the model's learning capabilities.

**Questions to Authors**

1.  Could you please clarify the novelty of the "Pair LSTM" architecture? Given that similar dual-encoder models are standard for many sentence-pair tasks, what specific aspects of your architecture are tailored to or novel for causal disambiguation?
2.  The empirical gains over the feature-engineered baseline are modest. What is the primary argument for adopting this neural model? Does it offer advantages in terms of computational efficiency, robustness to domain shift, or scalability that would justify its use over the established SVM method, despite the small performance difference?
3.  To better understand the contribution of the LSTM's sequential processing, would it be possible to provide results for a simpler neural baseline, such as a feed-forward network that operates on averaged GloVe embeddings of the input event spans? This would help isolate the benefits of your chosen architecture.
