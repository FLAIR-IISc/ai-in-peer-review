### **Summary and Contributions**

This paper addresses the task of causal meaning disambiguation, which it defines as a binary classification problem: given two events (e1, e2) and a potential lexical marker (l) connecting them, determine if the relationship is Causal or Non-Causal. The authors argue that previous methods relying on syntactic patterns or handcrafted lexical features are too restrictive. They hypothesize that a neural network can better encode the surrounding context to make this distinction.

In my view, the main contributions of this work are:
1.  **State-of-the-Art Performance on the AltLex Corpus:** The paper successfully applies a standard dual-encoder LSTM architecture to the task and demonstrates significant improvements over the previous state-of-the-art, feature-engineered SVM model from Hidey and McKeown (2016). This provides strong evidence that end-to-end neural models, which learn representations directly from text, are better suited for this nuanced disambiguation task than systems relying on predefined linguistic features.
2.  **Thorough Experimental Analysis:** The authors conduct a solid empirical evaluation. They test multiple model variations (e.g., "Pair LSTM" vs. "Stated Pair LSTM"), explore different optimizers and hyperparameters, and correctly identify and address the issue of class imbalance in the dataset by running a second set of experiments on a balanced subset. The qualitative error analysis in Section 4.3 provides valuable insight into the model's strengths.

### **Strengths**

1.  **Clear Problem Formulation and Motivation:** The paper is well-written, with a clear introduction that grounds the problem in linguistic theory before transitioning to the computational task. The authors effectively critique prior work's reliance on fixed syntactic structures and limited lexical resources, providing a strong motivation for a context-aware neural approach. The explicit decision to focus on "causal meaning classification" as a distinct sub-problem is a sensible scoping of the work.

2.  **Strong and Well-Analyzed Empirical Results:** The proposed model achieves a notable improvement over the state-of-the-art on the "non-bootstrapping" AltLex dataset (75.40% vs 73.76% F1) and a more balanced performance on the "bootstrapping" version, with a significant gain in precision. The authors go beyond simply reporting metrics; they analyze the precision-recall trade-offs of different optimizers and provide a compelling qualitative analysis (Table 8) showing concrete examples where their model correctly handles ambiguity (e.g., the causative verb "break" in a non-causal context, or the ambiguous marker "which then") that the baseline misclassifies.

3.  **Methodological Soundness and Rigor:** The experimental setup is robust. The authors use appropriate baselines: a simple-but-strong majority class baseline (B1) and the original paper's SOTA system (B2). The investigation into the effects of class imbalance and the subsequent experiment on a reduced dataset (Table 7) demonstrates a careful and thoughtful approach to evaluation, strengthening the credibility of their claims.

### **Weaknesses**

1.  **Limited Architectural Novelty:** The primary weakness of the paper is the lack of methodological novelty. The proposed "Pair LSTM" architecture is a standard dual-encoder model widely used for sentence-pair classification tasks (e.g., NLI, paraphrase identification). While its application to this specific problem is successful and valuable, the paper presents it as a novel proposed architecture without sufficiently acknowledging its standard nature. The main contribution is therefore an application of an existing method rather than the development of a new one.

2.  **Unclear Comparison Conditions for the SOTA Baseline:** The paper compares its results to "B2," the system from Hidey and McKeown (2016). However, it is not specified whether the B2 results were taken directly from the original paper or if the authors re-ran the baseline system on their exact data splits. This is a critical detail. If the numbers are simply copied, any differences in preprocessing, data splitting, or evaluation scripts could make the comparison less reliable. A direct, controlled comparison is necessary to make definitive claims of superiority.

3.  **Evaluation on a Single Dataset:** The paper's claims are based entirely on results from the AltLex corpus. While this is the most suitable large-scale corpus for the task, the generality of the approach is not tested. Evaluating the model, even in a zero-shot or fine-tuning setting, on other causality-annotated corpora (like Causal-TimeBank or relevant subsets of PDTB) would have significantly strengthened the paper's claim that context encoding via LSTMs is a broadly effective strategy for causal disambiguation.

4.  **Lack of Justification for a Key Design Choice:** The model processes `e1` as one input and the concatenation of the lexical marker and the second event (`l + e2`) as the other. There is no justification provided for this specific grouping. Why not encode all three components separately? Or concatenate `l` with `e1`? While the chosen configuration works well, the paper would be stronger if it discussed or empirically justified this architectural decision over other plausible alternatives.

### **Questions to Authors**

1.  Regarding the comparison with the B2 baseline (Hidey and McKeown, 2016), could you please clarify if you re-ran their system on your exact train/dev/test splits to ensure a fair and direct comparison, or if the results were taken from the original publication?

2.  The "Pair LSTM" model is a well-established architecture for sentence-pair tasks. Could you please position your contribution more clearly in this context? Is the main contribution the demonstration that this standard architecture is highly effective for causal marker disambiguation, or are there subtle but important novelties in your model that I have missed?

3.  Could you provide the reasoning behind your input representation choice, where the lexical marker `l` is prepended to the second event `e2`? Did you experiment with other configurations, such as encoding `l` separately or prepending it to `e1`, and if so, how did they perform?
