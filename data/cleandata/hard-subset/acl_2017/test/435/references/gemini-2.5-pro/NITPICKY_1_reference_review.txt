**Summary and Contributions:**

The paper presents a neural network approach for classifying causal relations between two text spans linked by a lexical marker. The proposed model is a dual-input LSTM architecture that encodes the two events (and the marker) and classifies the relationship as Causal or Non-Causal. The model is evaluated on the AltLex corpus, where it is shown to achieve a modest performance improvement over the previous state-of-the-art, a feature-based SVM system. The main contributions of this work are an empirical demonstration that a standard neural architecture can outperform a complex feature-engineered system on this task, and a qualitative analysis that highlights the model's ability to handle ambiguous causal markers by leveraging contextual information.

**Strengths:**

1.  **Direct Comparison to State-of-the-Art:** The authors rightly compare their system against the work that introduced the AltLex corpus (Hidey and McKeown, 2016), which serves as the primary state-of-the-art baseline (B2). This provides a clear and relevant benchmark for assessing the proposed model's performance.

2.  **Effective Qualitative Analysis:** Section 4.3 provides a valuable analysis of specific examples where the proposed neural model succeeds and the baseline fails. The discussion of the ambiguous causative verb "break" and the connective "which then" effectively supports the paper's central hypothesis that neural context encoding is beneficial for this task, moving beyond simple aggregate metrics to offer concrete evidence.

3.  **Systematic Ablation of Model Choices:** The paper evaluates several design choices, including two LSTM architectures ("Stated Pair" vs. "Pair"), different optimizers (Adadelta vs. Adam), and unconventional padding strategies. This systematic approach provides insight into which components contribute most to the model's performance on this dataset.

**Weaknesses:**

1.  **Lack of Methodological Novelty:** The proposed neural architecture—a dual-encoder LSTM followed by dense layers—is a very standard model in NLP. While its application to this problem is valid, the paper does not introduce any significant architectural or methodological innovation. The contribution is therefore limited to being an application paper, which, while useful, is less impactful. The justification for using LSTMs over CNNs is also cursory and not entirely convincing.

2.  **Significant Gaps in Reproducibility and Clarity:** The paper omits several crucial details required for another researcher to reproduce the work.
    *   **Missing Hyperparameters:** Key architectural details such as the LSTM hidden state size and the specific dimensions of the dense layers are not provided. Regularization values for the best-performing models are also not specified, only the range of values explored.
    *   **Ambiguous Input Representation:** The second input is described as a "concatenation of the lexical marker and the second event," but the exact method of concatenation is not explained. This is a critical detail for the model's input layer.
    *   **Unconventional Padding:** The use of "mean" and "mode" of sequence lengths as padding targets is highly non-standard and poorly explained. Standard practice involves padding to a fixed maximum length or the maximum length within a batch. This choice would seemingly lead to data loss via truncation for a large portion of the dataset and requires strong justification, which is currently absent.

3.  **Flawed Experimental Design in Balanced Setting:** The experiment with a downsampled, more balanced dataset is inadequately designed and reported. The choice to downsample the majority class "by a factor of ten" is arbitrary. More critically, the results in Table 7 compare the model's performance only to the weak majority-class baseline (B1). The absence of a comparison to the far more important B2 baseline on this modified dataset renders the experiment inconclusive and its results difficult to interpret.

4.  **Poor Presentation and Formatting:** The paper suffers from a distracting number of presentation flaws that suggest a lack of careful preparation.
    *   **Bibliography:** The reference section is improperly formatted, appearing as a raw BibTeX output with visible artifacts (e.g., `et al\.,`, `?`, `\Q2017\E`). This is a major formatting error.
    *   **Missing Figures/Tables:** Table 3 and Table 6 are referenced in the text but are not included in the paper.
    *   **Inconsistencies and Errors:** There are numerous grammatical errors, typos, and stylistic inconsistencies. For example, citations in the text ("Neeleman and van de Koot") do not match the reference list ("Neeleman and Koot"), and commas are used as decimal separators in Table 7 ("69,84%"). Figure 2 is cluttered and difficult to read. These issues cumulatively detract from the paper's credibility.

**Questions to Authors:**

1.  Could you please specify the exact hyperparameters of your best-performing models? This includes the LSTM hidden state size, the precise architecture of the dense layers (number of units per layer), and the dropout/regularization values used.
2.  Can you clarify the padding methodology? How exactly is the padding length determined using the "mean" and "mode" of sequence lengths, and why was this approach chosen over standard max-length padding, which avoids truncating longer sequences?
3.  In your experiment on the balanced dataset (Table 7), why was the comparison with the B2 baseline omitted? This comparison seems essential for understanding the model's behavior when class imbalance is reduced.
