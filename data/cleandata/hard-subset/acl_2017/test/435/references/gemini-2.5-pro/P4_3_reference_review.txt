### Summary

This paper addresses the task of causal meaning disambiguation, which the authors define as a binary classification problem: determining if a relation between two pre-identified event spans, connected by a lexical marker, is Causal or Non-Causal. Arguing that previous feature-based methods are limited by their reliance on specific syntactic patterns and lexical resources, the authors propose a neural network architecture based on Long Short-Term Memory (LSTM) units. The proposed model, named "Pair LSTM," uses two separate LSTMs to encode the two event spans and their connecting marker, using only pre-trained word embeddings as input. The model is evaluated on the AltLex corpus, where it is shown to outperform a feature-engineered SVM baseline and a majority-class baseline, demonstrating the effectiveness of contextual encoding for this task.

### Main Contributions

1.  **An effective application of a standard neural architecture to causal meaning disambiguation.** The paper successfully applies a dual-encoder LSTM model to the task. While the architecture itself is not novel, its application demonstrates that this standard approach, relying solely on contextual encoding from word embeddings, can outperform a complex, feature-engineered system on the AltLex benchmark dataset.
2.  **Empirical analysis demonstrating the limitations of connective-based heuristics.** The experiments show that a simple LSTM-based model significantly outperforms a strong baseline that classifies relations based on the majority sense of the connecting lexical marker. The qualitative analysis further provides concrete examples of how the model leverages broader context to correctly classify ambiguous connectives and causative verbs used in non-causal contexts.

### Strengths

1.  **Strong Empirical Performance.** The proposed model achieves state-of-the-art results on the AltLex dataset. On the "non-bootstrapping" version, the "Pair LSTM Max Adadelta" model improves the F1 score by 2.13% and accuracy by 7.72% over the previous system of Hidey and McKeown (2016). On the more challenging "bootstrapping" version, the proposed models also demonstrate superior performance, particularly in achieving a better balance between precision and recall. These results provide solid quantitative evidence for the method's effectiveness.
2.  **Clear Problem Motivation and Model Presentation.** The paper effectively motivates the need for a context-aware model that moves beyond reliance on fixed lexical markers or syntactic patterns. The authors provide a clear rationale for choosing LSTMs over CNNs for this task. The proposed "Pair LSTM" architecture is straightforward, well-described, and its superiority over a connected variant ("Stated Pair LSTM") is empirically demonstrated, lending support to the design choice of encoding the two event arguments independently.
3.  **Insightful Qualitative Analysis.** The analysis in Section 4.3 and Table 8 is a significant strength. By presenting specific examples where the proposed model succeeds and the baseline fails, the authors provide qualitative evidence for their central claim. The examples, such as the non-causal use of the verb "break" and the correct disambiguation of the ambiguous marker "which then," compellingly illustrate the model's ability to capture nuanced contextual meaning that simpler models or feature-based systems might miss.

### Weaknesses

1.  **Limited Architectural Novelty.** The core contribution is framed as a new neural architecture ("we contribute a neural network architecture"). However, the "Pair LSTM" model is a standard dual-encoder architecture, widely used for various sentence-pair classification tasks like Natural Language Inference and paraphrase detection. The novelty lies in the successful application of this architecture to causality detection, not in the architecture itself. This overstatement of architectural novelty diminishes the paper's contribution.
2.  **Technically Unsound Experimental Section.** A significant part of the experimental evaluation (the final part of Section 4.2, Table 7) is based on a flawed premise. The authors claim the AltLex corpus is "not balanced" and proceed to create a "more balanced dataset" by reducing the Non-Causal class by a factor of ten. However, their own data (Table 6, which appears to show the full training set statistics) indicates the original training sets are already quite balanced (e.g., 7606 Causal vs. 7929 Non-Causal). The described downsampling procedure would, in fact, create a severely *imbalanced* dataset heavily skewed towards the Causal class. This undermines the validity and motivation for this entire set of experiments and calls into question the technical soundness of the experimental design.
3.  **Artificial Task Formulation.** The task is defined on inputs where the causal arguments (e1, e2) and the lexical marker (l) are already identified. This is a significant simplification of the real-world problem of causality extraction from raw text. The paper does not discuss the practical implications of this setup or how the model would be integrated into a realistic end-to-end pipeline. This limits the scope of the contribution to a sub-problem with pre-processed inputs.
4.  **Lack of Clarity in Reporting and Writing.** The paper contains several instances of unclear or awkward phrasing and grammatical errors that impede readability. Furthermore, the reporting of results in Table 5 is incomplete, as it omits configurations that performed worse than the baselines. A full ablation study showing the impact of different padding strategies and optimizers would have provided a more transparent and comprehensive picture of the model's behavior. The justification for selecting the "best" model configuration in the "bootstrapping" setting is also not sufficiently detailed.

### Questions to Authors

1.  The core of your proposed model is a dual-encoder LSTM, a well-established architecture for sentence-pair tasks. Could you please clarify the specific novelty of your architecture beyond its successful application to the task of causal disambiguation?
2.  In Section 4.2, you state that the AltLex corpus is "not balanced" and use this as a rationale to create a reduced dataset for the experiments in Table 7. However, the class distribution for the training set shown in Table 6 appears to be very close to balanced. Could you please clarify this discrepancy? What was the rationale for reducing the Non-Causal class by a factor of ten, a step that would create a heavily imbalanced dataset?
3.  The presented system assumes that the event spans (e1, e2) and the lexical marker (l) are provided as input. How do you envision this model being used in a practical, end-to-end setting where these elements must first be identified from raw text? Does this task pre-supposition limit the real-world applicability of the findings?
