### Summary and Contributions

This paper addresses the task of causal meaning disambiguation, which involves determining whether a lexical marker connecting two events signals a causal relation. The authors propose a dual-input LSTM-based neural network architecture to encode the two events and their connecting marker, classifying the relationship as Causal or Non-Causal. The system is evaluated on the AltLex corpus, where it is shown to outperform a strong feature-based SVM baseline from prior work.

From my perspective, the main contributions of this work are:

1.  An empirical demonstration that a standard recurrent neural network architecture, using only pre-trained word embeddings, can achieve state-of-the-art performance on the AltLex causality classification benchmark, marginally outperforming a heavily feature-engineered SVM system.
2.  An analysis of different configurations of a dual-LSTM model for this task, including exploring the effect of sharing state between the LSTMs, different padding strategies, and optimizers.

### Strengths

1.  **Solid Empirical Results:** The proposed model achieves state-of-the-art results on a recognized public benchmark (AltLex). While the improvements are modest, the paper successfully demonstrates that a neural approach can outperform the previous best system, which relied on extensive feature engineering and external lexical resources. This provides a valuable data point for the field, reaffirming the trend of neural models obviating the need for manual feature design in many NLP tasks.

2.  **Systematic Model Exploration:** The authors responsibly evaluate several variations of their architecture. They compare a model where the LSTMs for each event are independent ("Pair LSTM") versus one where the state is passed from the first to the second ("Stated Pair LSTM"). They also experiment with different padding strategies and optimizers. This thoroughness adds credibility to their final model selection and provides useful insights, such as the finding that independent encoding of the two event arguments is more effective.

3.  **Clear Problem Formulation and Presentation:** The paper is well-written and easy to follow. The task is clearly defined, the related work is appropriately contextualized, and the proposed method is described with sufficient detail. The experiments are straightforward and the results are presented clearly in tables.

### Weaknesses

1.  **Limited Methodological Novelty:** The primary weakness of this paper is the lack of methodological innovation. The proposed "Pair LSTM" architecture is a very standard and straightforward application of LSTMs to a text pair classification task. It involves encoding two separate text sequences with LSTMs, concatenating their representations, and passing them through a feed-forward classifier. This architectural pattern has been widely used for numerous NLP tasks (e.g., natural language inference, paraphrase identification) for several years. The paper presents this as a novel contribution ("we contribute a neural network architecture"), but the architecture itself does not contain any new components or techniques that would be of broad interest to the community.

2.  **Marginal Performance Gains:** The reported improvements over the state-of-the-art baseline (B2) are quite small. On the main "bootstrapping" dataset, the best proposed model achieves an F1-score of 81.96%, compared to the baseline's 80.90%—an improvement of just over 1 percentage point. Given the lack of novelty in the method, such a marginal gain is not a particularly strong argument for acceptance. The paper would be more compelling if the improvement were substantial, or if the method itself were more innovative.

3.  **Insufficient Analysis:** The analysis of the results is superficial and does not provide deep insights into *why* the neural model works better. The paper claims the model is better because it "encodes the meaning of those events," but this is a generic statement. The analysis in Section 4.3 is based on a few anecdotal examples from Table 8. A more rigorous error analysis is needed. For instance:
    *   What types of causal relations does the LSTM model correctly identify that the SVM-based system misses?
    *   Conversely, are there specific cases where the feature-based system is superior?
    *   The B1 baseline (most frequent class for a marker) is remarkably strong, achieving an F1 of 80.05% on the bootstrapping data. This suggests that a large portion of the dataset consists of relatively unambiguous markers. The paper does not adequately analyze how much of its performance comes from simply learning these frequent, unambiguous cases versus genuinely disambiguating the difficult, context-dependent ones.

4.  **Limited Scope of Comparison:** The comparison is limited to a single feature-based system from 2016. While this is the established SOTA on this dataset, the field of neural NLP has advanced significantly. It would have been more convincing to compare against other, potentially more powerful but still "standard," neural baselines. For example, a model using pre-trained transformers like BERT would provide a much stronger point of comparison for what a modern neural approach can achieve on this task. While I acknowledge that BERT may have been concurrent or subsequent to this work's development, the reliance on a basic LSTM architecture feels somewhat dated.

### Questions to Authors

1.  Given that the proposed "Pair LSTM" is a standard architecture for sentence-pair tasks, could you clarify what you see as the primary novel contribution of your method? Is the contribution the architecture itself, or is it the empirical finding that this standard architecture works well for this specific task?

2.  The performance gain over the strong SVM baseline (B2) is marginal (~1% F1). Could you provide a more compelling argument for the superiority of your approach beyond this small metric increase? For instance, does your model show better performance on a specific subset of challenging, ambiguous connectives? A more detailed breakdown of performance by connective type would strengthen your claims.

3.  Your error analysis is limited to a few positive examples. Could you provide a more systematic error analysis? What are the primary categories of errors your best model makes? How do these compare to the errors made by the B2 baseline? This would help the reader understand the actual advantages and limitations of your approach compared to feature-based methods.
