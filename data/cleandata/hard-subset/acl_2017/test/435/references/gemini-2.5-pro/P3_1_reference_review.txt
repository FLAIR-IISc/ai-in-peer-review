### **Summary and Contributions**

This paper addresses the task of causal meaning disambiguation: determining whether a lexical marker between two events signals a causal relationship. The authors frame this as a binary classification problem (Causal vs. Non-Causal). They propose a neural architecture based on two parallel LSTMs ("Pair LSTM") that separately encode the first event (e1) and the combination of the lexical marker and the second event (l + e2). The resulting representations are concatenated and passed through dense layers for classification. The model is evaluated on the AltLex corpus, where it is shown to outperform previous feature-based state-of-the-art methods.

From my perspective, the main contributions are:

1.  **A strong empirical demonstration** that a standard neural architecture, using only pre-trained word embeddings without any handcrafted linguistic features, can outperform a complex feature-engineered SVM system for causal marker disambiguation. This validates the hypothesis that contextual encoding is sufficient and effective for this task.
2.  **A useful analysis of the model's performance**, particularly in the qualitative examples (Table 8), which highlights its ability to handle lexical and contextual ambiguity (e.g., with the verb "break" or the ambiguous connective "which then") where the previous SOTA fails.

### **Strengths**

1.  **Clear Motivation and Problem Formulation:** The paper does an excellent job of situating the problem within linguistics and computational linguistics. The introduction clearly articulates the shortcomings of previous syntax- and lexicon-driven approaches to causality, providing a strong motivation for a data-driven, context-sensitive neural model. The decision to separate "causal meaning classification" from "causal argument identification" is sensible and focuses the work effectively.

2.  **Solid and Systematic Evaluation:** The experiments are well-designed. The authors compare their model against two reasonable baselines, including the previous state-of-the-art system on the same dataset. They responsibly test multiple configurations of their own model (e.g., padding strategies, optimizers, "Stated" vs. "Pair" LSTMs) and report results on both the original and bootstrapped versions of the AltLex corpus. The additional experiment on a class-balanced subset is a good sanity check that strengthens the findings.

3.  **Effective Analysis and Insight:** The qualitative analysis in Section 4.3 is a key strength. The examples in Table 8 provide compelling, concrete evidence for the paper's central claim. Showing that the model can correctly classify instances where a typically "causative" verb is used non-causally, or where a highly ambiguous marker is used causally, demonstrates that the model is learning nuanced contextual patterns beyond simple lexical triggers.

### **Weaknesses**

1.  **Limited Architectural Novelty and Comparison to Modern Baselines:** The primary weakness is the novelty of the proposed method. The "Pair LSTM" architecture is a very standard approach for sentence-pair classification tasks and is not, in itself, a novel contribution. The paper frames it as "contribut[ing] a neural network architecture," which overstates its novelty. More importantly, the evaluation is missing comparisons to stronger and more contemporary neural baselines. By the time of this work's potential publication, architectures incorporating attention mechanisms or, critically, large pre-trained transformers (e.g., BERT), have become the de facto standard for text classification. A simple BERT-based classifier would serve as a much stronger baseline and is necessary to truly situate the performance of this LSTM-based model. Without it, the claims of outperforming the "state-of-the-art" are limited to an older paradigm of feature-based models.

2.  **Ambiguity in Input Representation:** A critical methodological detail is underspecified. The paper states that the two inputs are the first event (e1) and the concatenation of the marker and the second event (l + e2). It is unclear if these inputs are *only* the text spans of the events/markers, or if the full sentence context is somehow included. The example `(He fell in love...)e1 ([so]l he could help her)e2` suggests the inputs are just the substrings within the parentheses. If this is the case, the model is discarding potentially crucial context from the rest of the sentence, which seems counterintuitive to the paper's hypothesis about encoding context. If the full sentence is used, the paper needs to explain how the different components (e1, l, e2) are demarcated for the two separate LSTMs. This lack of clarity makes the method difficult to reproduce and interpret.

3.  **Evaluation Limited to a Single Corpus:** The experiments are conducted exclusively on the AltLex corpus. While this is appropriate for a direct comparison with Hidey and McKeown (2016), it limits the claims about the model's generalizability. The AltLex corpus itself is constructed via a bootstrapping process from Simple Wikipedia alignments, which may introduce specific biases or artifacts. Showing how the model performs on other, differently-constructed causality datasets (e.g., PDTB, Causal-TimeBank), even in a zero-shot or fine-tuning setting, would have significantly strengthened the paper's conclusions about the robustness of the neural approach.

### **Questions to Authors**

1.  Could you please clarify the exact format of the input fed to the two LSTMs? Are the inputs just the extracted text spans for e1 and (l + e2), or is the entire sentence provided to both encoders with some form of masking or segmentation? How do you ensure the model has access to the full relevant context?

2.  Given that the "Pair LSTM" is a fairly standard architecture, would you agree that the primary contribution is more the successful application and analysis of this model for causality, rather than the architecture itself?

3.  Could you comment on why more recent and powerful neural architectures, such as BiLSTMs with attention or transformer-based models like BERT, were not considered as baselines? These models are often very strong on sentence/clause-pair classification tasks and would provide a more contemporary point of comparison for your results.
