### **Summary and Contributions**

This paper addresses the task of causal lexical marker disambiguation: determining whether a given linguistic expression connecting two events signals a causal relation. The authors argue that previous methods, which rely heavily on feature engineering and external lexical resources, are limited by the coverage of those resources and struggle with ambiguity. They propose a neural architecture based on a two-input LSTM network that encodes the context of the two events to make its classification. The model is evaluated on the AltLex corpus, where it is shown to outperform the previous state-of-the-art system of Hidey and McKeown (2016).

In my view, the main contributions of this work are:

1.  **An effective application of a standard neural architecture to causal marker disambiguation.** The paper demonstrates convincingly that a relatively simple LSTM-based model, relying only on pre-trained word embeddings, can outperform a complex, feature-engineered SVM system on this task. This provides strong evidence that neural context encoding is a superior approach for this problem.
2.  **A thorough empirical evaluation and analysis.** The authors systematically explore several configurations of their model (architectural variants, padding strategies, optimizers) and compare them against strong baselines on two versions of the AltLex corpus. The qualitative analysis in Section 4.3, which highlights specific examples of ambiguity that their model handles correctly, is particularly insightful.

### **Strengths**

1.  **Strong Empirical Results:** The primary strength of this paper is its performance. The proposed model achieves state-of-the-art results on the AltLex corpus, outperforming the original system from Hidey and McKeown (2016) by a notable margin in both F1-score (e.g., 75.40% vs. 73.76% on the non-bootstrapping data) and accuracy (77.90% vs. 71.86%). The gains are consistent across different versions of the dataset. This is a clear and significant result.

2.  **Well-Motivated Methodology:** The motivation for moving away from feature-engineered systems towards a neural approach is sound and well-argued. The authors correctly point out the limitations of relying on fixed syntactic patterns or the semantic coverage of resources like WordNet for a phenomenon as nuanced as causality. The choice of an LSTM to encode the sequential context of the events is a standard but appropriate and effective way to test their hypothesis.

3.  **Good Analysis of Model Behavior:** The analysis in Section 4.3 provides compelling qualitative evidence for the model's strengths. By showing specific, challenging examples (e.g., the non-causal use of the causative verb "break," or the highly ambiguous marker "which then"), the authors demonstrate that their model is not simply memorizing markers but is genuinely sensitive to context. This analysis strengthens their central claim that context encoding is key. The discussion of the Precision/Recall trade-off for different optimizers is also a valuable detail.

### **Weaknesses**

1.  **Limited Technical Novelty:** While the application is effective, the neural architecture itself—a two-input ("Siamese-like") LSTM followed by dense layers—is a standard and widely used pattern for sentence-pair classification tasks in NLP. The paper does not propose a novel neural component or training technique. The contribution lies entirely in the successful application of this existing methodology to the specific problem of causality disambiguation.

2.  **Unclear Justification for Input Representation:** The model takes `e1` as one input and the concatenation of the lexical marker and the second event (`l + e2`) as the other. This design choice feels somewhat arbitrary and is not justified. Why is the marker prepended only to the second event? Causal markers can link events where the cause appears textually after the effect (e.g., "The window broke *because* John threw a rock"). Concatenating `l` with `e2` might bias the model or be a suboptimal representation. A more natural formulation might treat `e1`, `l`, and `e2` as three distinct inputs or process the full sentence context.

3.  **Problem Framing and Positioning:** The paper frames the task as "causal meaning classification." However, given that the input is always a triplet of `(e1, l, e2)`, the task is more precisely **discourse connective disambiguation**, specialized for causality. The related work section focuses on causality extraction in general but misses a clear connection to the extensive literature on discourse connective disambiguation within frameworks like PDTB. Positioning the work more explicitly within this subfield would clarify its relationship to prior art and highlight what is specific to causal connectives versus other types (e.g., temporal, contrastive).

4.  **Scope of Evaluation:** The experiments are confined to a single corpus, AltLex. While this is the most suitable large-scale corpus for the task, the claims of the paper would be stronger if the model's robustness was tested on other relevant datasets. For example, evaluating on the explicit causal relations in the Penn Discourse TreeBank (PDTB) would show how the model performs on a different domain and annotation style. Even if smaller corpora like Causal-TimeBank are not suitable for training, they could potentially be used as additional test sets to assess generalization.

### **Questions to Authors**

1.  Could you please elaborate on the reasoning behind the specific input representation where the lexical marker `l` is concatenated with the second event `e2`? Have you explored alternative representations, such as treating `l` as a separate input or providing the entire sentence as context to a single encoder (e.g., BERT-style)?

2.  The finding that the "Stated Pair LSTM" (with a shared state) performs worse than the independent "Pair LSTM" is interesting. Your conclusion is that the assumption about a direct sequential relationship "does not hold." Could an alternative explanation be that this simple state-passing mechanism is too weak to capture the complex semantic relationship, and a more sophisticated interaction mechanism (like attention) between the two LSTM outputs might be more effective?

3.  How do you see this work on explicit marker disambiguation contributing to the broader and arguably more difficult challenge of identifying *implicit* causal relations, where no lexical marker is present? Does your context-encoding approach offer a path forward for that task?
