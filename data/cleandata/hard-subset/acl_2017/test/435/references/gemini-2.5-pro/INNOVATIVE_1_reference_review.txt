### Summary and Main Contributions

This paper tackles the task of disambiguating whether a lexical marker signals a causal relation between two events. The authors argue that previous work has been overly dependent on brittle, hand-crafted features, predefined syntactic patterns, and lexical resources, which limits generalizability. The paper's core thesis is a conceptual one: that causality is not defined by a fixed set of linguistic constructions but is an emergent property of the surrounding context, which can be effectively captured by neural sequence models.

From my perspective, the main contributions of this work are:

1.  **A conceptual shift towards purely context-based causality detection.** The paper makes a bold and compelling case for moving away from feature-engineered systems towards an end-to-end approach that learns to identify causality from contextual semantics alone, as encoded by LSTMs. This represents a significant and refreshing new direction for the field.
2.  **An effective, simple neural architecture as a proof-of-concept.** The authors propose a dual-input LSTM model that, despite its simplicity, successfully operationalizes their central thesis. By showing that this model outperforms a strong, feature-rich baseline on the AltLex corpus, they provide convincing evidence for their claim.

### Strengths

1.  **High Novelty and Conceptual Boldness:** The paper's primary strength lies in its conceptual contribution. It challenges the long-held assumption that causality detection requires deep linguistic feature engineering and instead posits that a sufficiently powerful sequence model can learn the relevant semantic cues directly from text. This is a forward-looking perspective that has the potential to redefine how we approach not just causality, but other complex discourse relations as well. I highly value this kind of ambitious, paradigm-shifting work.
2.  **Elegant and Generalizable Methodology:** The proposed model is laudable for its simplicity. By eschewing complex, task-specific architectures and ancillary linguistic resources (like WordNet or VerbNet), the authors present a clean and generalizable framework. This minimalism is a feature, not a bug; it demonstrates that the power of the approach comes from the contextual word representations and the LSTM's ability to compose them, rather than from arcane feature engineering. This makes the method more robust and easily adaptable to new domains or languages.
3.  **Strong and Well-Analyzed Empirical Results:** The paper demonstrates clear empirical superiority over the state-of-the-art system on the AltLex dataset. The gains are not merely incremental; the proposed model achieves a much better balance between precision and recall, suggesting it is learning a more reliable signal for the Causal class. The qualitative analysis in Section 4.3 is particularly persuasive, offering concrete examples where the model correctly handles ambiguous causative verbs (e.g., "break off") and markers ("which then") that fool the feature-based system. This provides strong support for the central hypothesis that context is king.

### Weaknesses

While I am very enthusiastic about this paper, I see a few areas for improvement, which I view as acceptable limitations for a work that introduces a new direction rather than fatal flaws.

1.  **Standard Neural Architecture:** The conceptual framework is highly novel, but the "Pair LSTM" architecture is a fairly standard application of recurrent networks for a text-pair classification task. The paper could have been strengthened by exploring architectures more explicitly tailored for modeling relations between text spans, such as models incorporating cross-attention. This feels like a missed opportunity to push the envelope on the modeling front as well as the conceptual one. However, as a first demonstration of the context-only hypothesis, the current model serves its purpose as a powerful proof of concept.
2.  **Constrained Task Definition:** The work focuses on disambiguating an already identified lexical marker (`l`) between two given events (`e1` and `e2`). This formulation bypasses the often more challenging upstream problems of identifying the event spans and potential causal markers in the first place, not to mention the problem of purely implicit causal relations that lack any marker. The authors acknowledge this by framing their work as the first of two subtasks, but the contribution should be understood as addressing a specific, constrained version of the overall causality extraction problem.
3.  **Superficial Error Analysis:** The analysis in Section 4.3 provides excellent anecdotal evidence of the model's strengths, but it remains brief. A more systematic error analysis would have been beneficial. For instance, what kinds of causal or non-causal contexts does the model still struggle with? Is it sensitive to sentence length, syntactic complexity, or the presence of negations or modals? A deeper dive would provide a clearer picture of the model's capabilities and limitations.

### Questions to Authors

1.  The "Pair LSTM" architecture, which encodes the two events independently, outperformed the "Stated Pair LSTM," where the representation of `e1` initializes the LSTM for `e2`. This is somewhat counter-intuitive, as one might expect the context of the first event to directly inform the processing of the second. Do you have a hypothesis for why this architectural separation proved more effective?
2.  Your model concatenates the lexical marker `l` with the second event `e2` as a single input. What was the reasoning behind this choice, as opposed to treating `l` as a distinct third input or incorporating its representation in a different manner (e.g., via a gating mechanism)?
3.  The paper argues against CNNs by stating one would "need to know beforehand the length of the context." However, multi-kernel CNNs are standard for capturing n-gram patterns of various lengths. Could you elaborate on why LSTMs are fundamentally a better choice for this task, or was this decision based on preliminary experiments?
