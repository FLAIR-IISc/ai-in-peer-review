Summary and claimed contributions
This paper tackles causal meaning disambiguation: given two annotated events e1, e2 and a potentially causal lexical marker l, decide whether the relation is Causal or Non-Causal. The authors argue for treating causality classification as two subtasks (causal meaning disambiguation and argument identification) and focus on the former. They propose two LSTM-based architectures that encode the two inputs (e1, and l concatenated with e2), experimenting with whether to pass the LSTM state from the first to the second (“Stated Pair LSTM”) or to encode both independently (“Pair LSTM”). Using GloVe embeddings and MLP layers on top, they report improvements over a strong lexical-resource SVM baseline (Hidey & McKeown, 2016) on the AltLex corpus in both its non-bootstrapping and bootstrapping versions, with analysis on ambiguous markers.

As I see it, the main contributions are:
1) An empirical demonstration that a simple two-input LSTM model trained only on word embeddings can outperform feature-rich SVMs for causal marker disambiguation on AltLex, with consistent gains in F1 and accuracy.
2) A clear problem framing that separates causal meaning disambiguation from causal argument identification, with accompanying analysis of ambiguous lexical markers and dataset distribution effects.
3) A small architecture/optimization exploration (independent vs. stated LSTMs; padding/optimizers) with insights that independent encodings perform better and Adam tends to give more balanced precision/recall.

Strengths
- Empirical gains over a strong baseline: On AltLex (non-bootstrapping), the best LSTM variant improves Causal F1 from 73.76% (SVM) to 75.40% (+1.64) and accuracy from 71.86% to 77.90% (+6.04). On bootstrapping, best F1 increases from 80.90% to 81.96% (+1.06) and accuracy from 79.58% to 80.52% (+0.94). These are consistent improvements across two dataset variants and also hold (with statistical testing) on a reduced, more balanced setting.
- Clean end-to-end setup: The method relies only on pretrained GloVe embeddings and task supervision, avoiding hand-crafted lexical resources. This directly addresses prior limitations around coverage and feature engineering and is likely to generalize better to unseen expressions (as suggested by the “which then” example).
- Sensible architectural finding: The observation that encoding e1 and (l+e2) independently works better than initializing the second LSTM from the first is plausible and useful guidance for future pair-encoding approaches in discourse relation classification.
- Useful analysis: The paper discusses class distribution issues in AltLex, shows that majority-per-connector is a very strong baseline (useful context), and provides qualitative examples where the neural model handles ambiguous markers/causative verbs better than the SVM baseline.
- Task framing: Separating causal meaning disambiguation from argument identification is a coherent formulation that could simplify dataset construction and system design.

Weaknesses
- Limited novelty: Using LSTMs to encode spans and an MLP classifier is a standard recipe. The two-input setup and “state passing” variant are not conceptually novel. The contribution is primarily empirical on a single dataset rather than methodological.
- Experimental rigor and clarity: Important details are missing or unclear: whether LSTMs are uni/bidirectional, hidden sizes, number of layers, pooling of LSTM outputs (last state vs. pooling), batch size, epochs, early stopping, whether embeddings are fine-tuned, and how padding by “mean” or “mode” length is implemented (rounding, truncation policy). The padding equation is confusing (mean/mode of sequence length across the dataset vs. over x itself). Without these, reproducibility is limited.
- Potential tuning on test set: Many configurations are compared but no development set or tuning protocol is described. Selecting the best configuration on the test set risks overfitting and inflates reported gains. No variance across random seeds or confidence intervals are provided.
- Baseline coverage: The comparison omits simple strong neural/non-neural baselines (e.g., averaged embeddings + logistic/MLP; bag-of-ngrams + logistic; biLSTM with attention; or modern pretrained encoders). Given the straightforward nature of the task, it is important to show that LSTMs are necessary beyond a pooled embedding approach. Also, a marker-only baseline would quantify how much context beyond l is used.
- Dataset limitations and generalization: All evaluations are on AltLex. No results on other corpora with explicit connectives (e.g., PDTB causal relations or Causal-TimeBank) are provided, limiting claims about generalization. The paper acknowledges limited resources but does not attempt even a small cross-dataset test.
- Writing and presentation issues: Numerous typos, broken table cross-references (e.g., Table 3 not shown), inconsistent statistics (e.g., accuracy gain reported as 7.72% but appears to be ~6.0%), and incomplete/broken references hurt clarity. The method section intermixes math with unclear dimensions and contradicting descriptions (e.g., LSTM encoding vs. vec operator).

Questions to authors
- Architecture details: Are the LSTMs uni- or bi-directional? What are the hidden sizes, number of layers, and dropout placements? How do you aggregate LSTM outputs (last state, mean/max pooling, attention)?
- Padding/truncation: How exactly are “mean” and “mode” padding lengths computed and applied? How do you handle sequences longer than the chosen padding length (truncate from left/right)? How are lengths rounded?
- Training protocol: How were hyperparameters selected (optimizer, dropout, L2, padding strategy)? Was a development set used? If not, how do you avoid implicit test-set tuning? How many training epochs? Any early stopping criteria?
- Inputs and context: Do you only feed e1 and (l+e2), or any tokens from the broader sentence context? If only the event spans are used, how do you reconcile the claim that the model encodes the “context” of the relation?
- Baselines: How does a pooled embedding baseline (e.g., average of e1 and l+e2 followed by MLP) perform? What about a “marker-only” baseline? Can you report results with a bag-of-words/logistic baseline?
- Generalization: Have you tested on PDTB explicit causal relations or Causal-TimeBank to assess transfer? Any cross-domain evaluation?
- Reproducibility: Will you release code, hyperparameters, and preprocessing scripts?

Overall assessment
The paper addresses a worthwhile problem with a clear empirical finding: simple neural encoders can outperform feature-rich models for causal marker disambiguation on AltLex. However, the methodological novelty is limited, experimental details are insufficient for reproducibility, and evaluation breadth is narrow. With clearer training/tuning protocols, stronger and fairer baselines, variance estimates, and at least one additional dataset, the work would be substantially strengthened. As-is, I see this as an incremental but useful empirical contribution that needs tightening in experimental rigor and presentation.
