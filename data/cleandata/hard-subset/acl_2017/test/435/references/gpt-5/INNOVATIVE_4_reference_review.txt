Summary and Contributions
This paper addresses causal meaning disambiguation: given two events and a potentially causal lexical marker, determine whether the relation is causal. The authors argue that causality should be encoded via context rather than fixed lexical/syntactic patterns. They propose a simple two-input LSTM architecture (Pair LSTM and a variant that initializes the second LSTM with the first’s final state), using GloVe embeddings and dense layers, and evaluate on the AltLex corpus (non-bootstrapping and bootstrapping versions), outperforming a feature-based SVM baseline.

Contributions (as I see them):
1) Empirical finding: a straightforward LSTM-based contextual encoder improves over a strong feature-engineered SVM baseline on AltLex for causal disambiguation, with modest but consistent gains.
2) Task framing: clear separation of causal meaning classification from causal argument identification, with a focus on the former and a context-centric view that deemphasizes reliance on fixed connectives.
3) Analysis of ambiguous connectives: small but informative examples showing cases (e.g., “which then”) where contextual encoding helps beyond lexical priors.

Strengths
1) Conceptually grounded motivation for context-based disambiguation. The paper articulates why causality cannot be exhaustively captured by lexical lists or specific syntactic constructions, aligning the modeling choice (contextual LSTMs) with the linguistic argument. This is a sensible direction and fits broader trends in discourse modeling.

2) Empirical improvements over prior feature-based work. On AltLex, the proposed models outperform both the majority-per-connective baseline and the Hidey & McKeown SVM, notably on the more realistic bootstrapped split and in overall accuracy. The non-bootstrapping gains are larger (e.g., +2–8% depending on metric), reinforcing that the neural encoder captures signal beyond connective frequencies.

3) Focused architecture and ablations that test intuitions. The paper compares two ways of coupling event representations (connected vs independent LSTMs), explores different pooling/“padding” strategies, and contrasts optimizers. The finding that independent encoders with late fusion work better than state-initialization is useful to the community.

4) Some qualitative analysis on difficult, ambiguous markers. The examples illustrate that the model can override misleading lexical priors, which supports the central claim (context matters).

Weaknesses
1) Limited novelty in modeling. The core method—two LSTMs over event spans with GloVe—is standard and expected for this task as of several years ago. The “stated pair LSTM” variant is a minor architectural tweak that underperforms. The argument against CNNs is unconvincing (modern CNNs use multi-kernel/dilations and handle variable context lengths), and there is no comparison to stronger contemporary baselines (e.g., BiLSTM with attention, ELMo/BERT-style pretrained encoders, or simple sentence-level transformers), which substantially limits the contribution.

2) Narrow and somewhat fragile evaluation. All results are on a single corpus (AltLex). No validation on PDTB causal senses or Causal-TimeBank (even if small, could be used for zero-shot or fine-tune/transfer). Class imbalance and the dependence on known lexical markers make it unclear how well the approach generalizes to settings without an explicit marker or to out-of-domain text.

3) Clarity and technical detail gaps. The description of “zero-padding strategies” as max/mean/mode is confusing; those are pooling or aggregation functions, not padding. Key implementation choices (tokenization, event span extraction, LSTM hidden sizes, sequence truncation lengths, training regimes, early stopping, seeds, run variance) are missing or under-specified. Reporting single numbers without variance leaves uncertainty about robustness.

4) Limited analysis and ablation depth. Beyond optimizer/pooling, there’s no ablation of inputs (e1 vs marker vs e2), no attention analysis, no length/context sensitivity checks, and minimal error taxonomy. The paper claims context is key, but does not quantify which parts of the context contribute most.

5) Presentation and referencing issues. Several typos, formatting problems (equations, tables), and incomplete/malformed references detract from readability. The related work omits stronger modern baselines and overstates the need for LSTMs over alternative architectures without evidence.

Questions to Authors
1) How are e1 and e2 spans obtained and fed to the model? Are they gold spans from AltLex, and how would your method perform without gold event boundaries (more realistic setting)?
2) Please clarify the “zero-padding strategies” (max/mean/mode). Are these temporal pooling operations over token embeddings? If so, how are sequences made comparable before LSTMs, and where exactly is pooling applied?
3) How sensitive are results to the inclusion of the lexical marker in the second input? What happens if the marker is omitted or unseen at test time?
4) Do you aggregate results over multiple random seeds? Please report mean and standard deviation for the main configurations.
5) Have you tried a single encoder over the concatenated [e1, marker, e2] with segment embeddings or attention across the two events? Or a simple pretrained transformer baseline (e.g., fine-tuned BERT) for a fair contemporary comparison?
6) Can you report performance on additional datasets (e.g., PDTB causal senses) in a transfer or zero-shot setting to demonstrate generalization?

Overall, while the motivation is sound and the empirical gains over a feature-based baseline are welcome, the modeling novelty is modest and the evaluation scope is limited. I encourage strengthening with clearer methodology, modern baselines, and broader validation.
