Summary
This paper addresses causal meaning disambiguation: given two events and a lexical marker potentially indicating causality, determine whether the relation is causal or not. The authors argue that causality should not be constrained to fixed lexical/syntactic patterns and propose LSTM-based architectures that encode the two event spans (and the marker) to classify causal vs. non-causal. Experiments on the AltLex corpus (both non-bootstrapped and bootstrapped versions) show improvements over a majority-class-per-marker baseline and a feature-rich SVM baseline (Hidey & McKeown, 2016). The best model attains modest gains in F1 and accuracy, with analysis suggesting it can handle some ambiguous markers better than the SVM.

Contributions (as I see them)
1) A pairwise LSTM architecture (with and without state initialization between the two encoders) for disambiguating the causal meaning of potentially ambiguous lexical markers based on context.
2) An empirical study on the AltLex corpus demonstrating small but consistent improvements over prior feature-based methods, and a brief qualitative analysis of ambiguous cases.

Strengths
- Clear problem motivation and task setup: The paper persuasively argues why causality should be modeled from contextual semantics rather than fixed syntactic patterns, aligning with prior evidence that syntactic cues alone are insufficient.
- Empirical improvements over strong baselines: On the AltLex corpus, the best LSTM variants outperform both the majority-per-marker baseline and the feature-rich SVM of Hidey & McKeown, with larger gains on the non-bootstrapped split and competitive performance on the bootstrapped split.
- Examination of multiple architectural choices: The comparison of “Pair LSTM” vs. “Stated Pair LSTM,” optimizers (Adam vs. Adadelta), and padding strategies provides some insight, e.g., that independent encoders (Pair LSTM) tend to work better than initializing the second from the first.
- Some qualitative analysis: The discussed examples show the model correctly classifying instances where lexical priors (e.g., “break”) or ambiguous expressions (“which then”) would mislead a more lexicalized baseline.

Weaknesses
- Limited novelty and under-specified architecture: Using LSTMs to encode sentential context for relation classification is a well-trodden path. The architectural description and equations are confusing/inconsistent (e.g., the “padding strategy” defined via max/mean/mode over matrices, the dimensionalities in Eq. 2, what exactly the LSTM outputs that are then “vectorized”). Important hyperparameters (hidden sizes, bidirectionality, attention, pooling, epochs, batch size, early stopping, seeds) are not reported, harming reproducibility.
- Evaluation limited to a single resource and setup: All experiments are on AltLex with gold event spans and the gold lexical marker provided as input. This risks overestimating real-world utility: systems typically must detect spans/markers. There is no evaluation on other datasets (e.g., PDTB ambiguous connectives) or any cross-domain test to assess generalization. The downsampled “balanced” evaluation changes the test distribution, further complicating interpretation.
- Baselines are dated given the task: The strongest comparison is to an SVM with lexical resources. Missing are standard neural baselines such as BiLSTM with attention/pooling over the whole sentence, and especially pretrained contextual encoders (ELMo/BERT/RoBERTa) fine-tuned for pair classification, which are known to substantially improve discourse relation and connective disambiguation. Without these, it’s hard to assess the true contribution.
- Metrics and statistical rigor: The focus on Causal-class F1 and overall accuracy obscures performance on the Non-Causal class; macro-F1 would be more informative under imbalance. Significance testing is only reported for the downsampled experiment; no significance is reported for the main comparisons on the official splits. Multiple runs with different seeds are not reported, so variance is unknown.
- Clarity and presentation issues: Several tables appear garbled (sizes in Tables 3/6/7 are unclear), references are incomplete or inconsistently formatted, and figures are referenced but not shown. Some claims (e.g., about padding strategies) are hard to verify due to missing detail.

Questions to Authors
- Input representation: At test time, do you require gold event spans and the gold lexical marker? Have you tried excluding the marker from the second input to test whether the model truly leverages context rather than specific markers?
- Padding strategy: Please clarify what “max/mean/mode” padding means operationally. Are you setting sequence lengths by max/mean/mode of length distributions, or applying a pooling operation? The equations are unclear.
- Model details: Are the LSTMs bidirectional? What are the hidden sizes, pooling or last-state selection, dropout locations, training epochs, batch size, and early stopping criteria? How many runs and seeds?
- Baselines: How would your model compare to a BiLSTM/attention over the full sentence, and to a pretrained Transformer (e.g., BERT) fine-tuned on the same task?
- Generalization: Have you evaluated on PDTB ambiguous connectives (e.g., since/as/while) or on a subset where the marker distribution differs from training (beyond the bootstrapped set), to verify robustness?
- Significance: Are gains over B2 on the main (non-downsampled) splits statistically significant?

Decision: Weak reject
The paper tackles an important problem and shows encouraging gains over feature-based baselines, with a reasonable modeling intuition. However, the contribution is incremental; the model description and experimental rigor need strengthening; and the evaluation lacks modern baselines and broader tests that would be expected for ACL. I encourage the authors to (i) clarify and solidify the architecture/reproducibility, (ii) add stronger neural baselines (especially pretrained LMs) and macro-F1/variance reporting with significance, (iii) evaluate beyond AltLex and without gold spans/markers, and (iv) expand the error analysis toward ambiguous markers and cross-split generalization. These steps would substantially improve the paper’s impact and clarity.
