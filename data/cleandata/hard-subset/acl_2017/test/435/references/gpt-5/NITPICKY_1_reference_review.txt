Summary and main contributions
- Proposes a simple two-input LSTM-based classifier for causal meaning disambiguation: one encoder for e1 and another for the concatenation of the lexical marker with e2; compares a “stated pair” LSTM (second initialized from first) versus independent encoders plus dense layers. Claims LSTMs better capture context than CNNs for this task.
- Empirical evaluation on the AltLex corpus (Hidey & McKeown, 2016), both “non-bootstrapping” and “bootstrapping” splits, showing modest improvements over (i) a strong per-connective majority baseline and (ii) the SVM feature-based system of Hidey & McKeown.
- Positions causality classification as two subtasks (meaning disambiguation; argument identification) and focuses on the former.

Strengths
- Empirical gains on AltLex: On the non-bootstrapping split, the best LSTM variant improves F1 on the Causal class by ~1.6–1.7 points and accuracy by ~6–7 points over the feature-based baseline; on the bootstrapping split, gains are smaller but present. This shows a neural approach can outperform feature-rich SVMs without external lexical resources.
- Sensible architectural insight: The “Pair LSTM” (independent encoders) consistently outperforms the “Stated Pair LSTM,” suggesting that encoding arguments independently and modeling their interaction downstream is beneficial.
- Some analysis beyond headline numbers: notes on class imbalance, distribution shift between train/test in the bootstrapped set, and qualitative examples where the neural approach succeeds (including a particularly ambiguous marker).
- Uses only pre-trained word embeddings (GloVe) and minimal task-specific engineering, providing a lean alternative to resource-heavy feature engineering.

Weaknesses
- Limited novelty and missing comparisons: The architecture is standard; similar pair-encoding LSTM/BiLSTM approaches exist in implicit discourse relation classification. No comparisons to strong neural baselines (e.g., BiLSTM encoders over the full arguments, multi-kernel CNNs, attention models) or modern pretrained encoders (at minimum a basic BERT fine-tune). The argument that CNNs are ill-suited because of kernel size is unconvincing; multi-width CNNs are common.
- Methodological clarity and reproducibility issues:
  - The “zero-padding strategies” (max/mean/mode) are unclear/incoherent. Padding should not involve mean/mode of lengths; truncation policy is unspecified.
  - Equations contain dimensional inconsistencies (e.g., vec mapping, W ∈ R^{100×nd} applied to x without explicit flattening/aggregation; LSTM outputs vs dense layers unclear).
  - Critical hyperparameters are missing: LSTM directionality, hidden size, number of layers, whether embeddings are fine-tuned, dropout per layer, training epochs/early stopping, batch size, optimizer settings. No code release.
  - No statement about using a development set; many configurations reported suggest tuning on test.
- Evaluation design does not substantiate the main claim (contextual disambiguation of ambiguous markers):
  - The dataset is dominated by unambiguous markers; improvements may largely come from memorizing these. There is no focused evaluation on the ambiguous subset (e.g., since, after, AltLex expressions with both labels). Per-marker and per-subset (ambiguous-only) results are essential.
  - Only Causal-class precision/recall/F1 and overall accuracy are reported; no macro/micro-F1, per-class F1, or confusion matrices. Given class imbalance, accuracy is not informative.
  - Gains over the SVM baseline are small; no statistical significance reported for these comparisons or variance across multiple random seeds.
- Data handling and reporting issues:
  - Tables are incomplete/broken (e.g., Table 3/6 numbers garbled; “,606 7,929 15,534”), figure/table numbering inconsistent (e.g., “Table 5.1”), results formatting inconsistent. It is difficult to verify sizes/splits.
  - It is not clearly stated how e1 and e2 spans are derived from AltLex (PDTB Arg1/Arg2? Are AltLex phrases always included in the “l + e2” input? Is broader sentence context included or truncated?).
- Writing and references: The paper contains numerous typos, grammar issues, and broken citation metadata (e.g., “Kruengkrai et al.,? \Q2017\E”), which hinders readability. Some theoretical claims in the intro are overextended relative to the empirical contribution.

Questions to authors
- Input construction: Precisely define how you extract e1, l, and e2 from AltLex. Do you use PDTB Arg1/Arg2 spans? Is any surrounding context included? How do you tokenize/normalize? Are embeddings fixed or fine-tuned? Are LSTMs uni- or bidirectional? Hidden sizes and number of layers?
- Padding/truncation: What exactly are the “max/mean/mode” strategies? To what sequence length(s) do you pad? If you use “mean/mode,” how do you handle sequences longer than this length? What is the truncation policy and its impact on performance?
- Hyperparameter selection: What development set did you use? How many runs per configuration and with which random seeds? Report mean±std for key metrics. Provide significance tests against the SVM baseline.
- Baselines: Please compare to (i) a BiLSTM over each full argument (without the explicit marker), (ii) a multi-kernel CNN baseline, and (iii) a simple fine-tuned BERT-base model over the same inputs. Also include a BoW/logistic regression baseline.
- Ambiguity-focused evaluation: Report per-marker performance and results on the ambiguous subset only (markers with both labels in train). Also test a variant that removes the marker tokens from the input to quantify how much the model relies on the marker vs context.
- Metrics: Provide macro-F1 and per-class F1, and confusion matrices for both splits. Given imbalance, accuracy alone is not informative.
- Reproducibility: Will you release code, configuration files, and preprocessing scripts? Clarify training epochs, early stopping, batch size, learning rates, dropout, and regularization settings for the best models.
