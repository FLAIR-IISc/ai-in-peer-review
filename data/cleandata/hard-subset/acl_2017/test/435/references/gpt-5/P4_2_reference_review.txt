Summary
The paper addresses causal meaning disambiguation: given two events and a potentially causal lexical marker, determine whether the relation is causal or not. The authors argue that prior approaches overly rely on predefined syntactic patterns or lexical resources with limited coverage. They propose an LSTM-based neural architecture that encodes two inputs—(i) the first event and (ii) the lexical marker concatenated with the second event—followed by dense layers to predict causality. Using GloVe embeddings and various configurations (two LSTM variants, three padding strategies, two optimizers), they evaluate on the AltLex corpus (non-bootstrapping and bootstrapping versions), comparing against a frequency baseline and the feature-rich SVM of Hidey and McKeown (2016). The proposed models generally outperform both baselines, particularly on the non-bootstrapping split; on the bootstrapping split, improvements are smaller but often positive. A small analysis of ambiguous connectives and a downsampling experiment with statistical testing are provided.

Main contributions (as assessed)
1) A simple two-input LSTM architecture for causal lexical marker disambiguation that encodes event context without handcrafted features or external lexical resources.
2) Empirical evaluation on both versions of AltLex showing consistent improvements over a frequency baseline and a prior SVM-based method, with analysis of optimizer/padding choices and a downsampling study with McNemar’s test.
3) Framing and motivating causal relation identification as two subtasks (causal meaning disambiguation and causal argument identification), focusing on the first.

Strengths
- Empirical gains over strong baselines on an appropriate benchmark. On AltLex (non-bootstrapping), the best LSTM configuration improves F1 for the causal class by ~2.1 points and accuracy by ~7.7 points over the prior SVM, indicating effective learning beyond connective frequency. On the bootstrapping split, several configurations modestly improve or match the prior SVM while yielding better precision-recall balance in some cases.
- Clear, resource-lean modeling approach. The method uses only pre-trained word embeddings and sequence modeling, avoiding dependence on external lexical resources. This supports the claim that context encoding can mitigate limited coverage of handcrafted features.
- Sensible experimental variations and partial analysis. The paper compares two LSTM interaction schemes (with/without state passing), explores padding strategies and optimizers, and reports that independent encoding (Pair LSTM) generally works better. The downsampling experiment addresses class imbalance and includes McNemar’s test, and the paper provides illustrative error cases where the neural model disambiguates ambiguous expressions.
- Task framing and motivation. The decomposition into causal meaning disambiguation and causal argument identification is clearly articulated, and the focus on context rather than fixed syntactic patterns is well-motivated by related work and examples.

Weaknesses
- Limited novelty and missing modern baselines. The architecture (LSTM over GloVe with dense layers) is standard, and the paper claims CNNs are unsuitable without empirical comparison. It omits stronger contemporary baselines such as transformer-based models or contextualized embeddings, which are commonly used for discourse relation classification and could substantially change the performance landscape.
- Incomplete methodological clarity and potential tuning leakage. The paper does not describe the development/tuning protocol (e.g., presence of a dev set, early stopping, epoch counts, batch size). Hyperparameters of the LSTM (hidden size, layers) are unspecified beyond dense vector size. It is unclear whether model selection was done on the test set, which would compromise validity.
- Evaluation metrics and statistical rigor. With heavy class imbalance, reporting only causal-class F1 and overall accuracy may be misleading; macro-F1 or AUROC would better reflect performance. Statistical significance is not reported for the main test results; only the downsampling experiment includes McNemar’s test. Gains on the bootstrapping split are small (<1 point), making significance assessment important.
- Dataset scope and generalization. All experiments are on AltLex; no results on other causal datasets (e.g., Causal-TimeBank) or out-of-domain splits are provided, limiting claims about generality. The paper argues broad coverage but does not test beyond the single corpus.
- Reproducibility and presentation issues. Several tables contain numeric inconsistencies/typos (e.g., Table 6 training sizes), some references are incomplete/misformatted, and model details (tokenization, span extraction for e1/e2, pre-processing) are under-specified. The decision to concatenate the marker only with e2 is not justified by ablation.

Questions to authors
- Data splits and tuning: Was a separate development set used for hyperparameter selection and early stopping? If not, how was model selection performed without peeking at the test set?
- Architecture details: What are the LSTM hidden sizes, number of layers, dropout placement, batch size, training epochs, and stopping criteria? Please specify the exact best configuration(s).
- Input formulation: Why is the lexical marker concatenated only with e2? Did you try (a) separate encoders for e1/e2/marker, (b) sharing the marker with both, or (c) excluding the marker to test the contribution?
- Baselines: Have you evaluated stronger neural baselines (e.g., BiLSTM with attention, ELMo/BERT/transformers fine-tuned on AltLex)? How do your models compare?
- Spans and preprocessing: How are e1 and e2 spans defined/extracted in AltLex and tokenized? Are there cases with missing arguments or multi-sentence contexts, and how are they handled?
- Metrics and significance: Please report macro-F1 (and/or per-class F1 for both classes) and statistical significance for the primary comparisons on the standard splits.
- Reproducibility: Will you release code, configurations, and scripts to reproduce the reported numbers?
- Typos/inconsistencies: Please correct numerical inconsistencies in Tables 3/6/7 and complete/standardize the references.
