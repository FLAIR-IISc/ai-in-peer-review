Summary and contributions
- Proposes an LSTM-based neural architecture for disambiguating whether a relation between two annotated events (e1, e2), along with a candidate lexical marker l, is causal or not. Two variants are explored: “Pair LSTM” (independent encoders for each input) and “Stated Pair LSTM” (initializing the second LSTM with the first’s final state).
- Empirical evaluation on the AltLex dataset (non-bootstrapping and bootstrapping splits) shows modest improvements over a feature-engineered SVM baseline (Hidey & McKeown, 2016) and over a connective-majority baseline. Some analysis of ambiguous markers is provided.

Strengths
- Empirical gains on AltLex: The proposed models outperform the connective-majority baseline across settings and report small but consistent improvements over the SVM baseline in F1 for the Causal class (+1–2 points) and accuracy (~+1 point on bootstrapping, ~+6 on non-bootstrapping). This suggests the neural approach learns beyond per-connective priors.
- Simple neural formulation without external lexical resources: The approach relies on pre-trained word embeddings rather than WordNet/VerbNet/PropBank features, potentially improving generalization to unseen markers (as illustrated by examples such as “which then”).
- Clear separation of subtasks: The paper usefully separates “causal meaning classification” from “argument identification,” clarifying scope and avoiding confounding factors in evaluation.
- Some architectural exploration: The comparison between “Pair” vs “Stated Pair” LSTMs, optimizer choice (Adam vs Adadelta), and padding strategy hints at useful practical observations (e.g., encoding e1 and [l + e2] independently performs better).
- Preliminary significance on a rebalanced setting: McNemar tests on the reduced dataset indicate the proposed model significantly outperforms the majority-per-connective baseline, albeit under altered class priors.

Weaknesses
- Serious methodological clarity issues: The “zero-padding strategies” are defined as max/mean/mode with equations that are mathematically inconsistent for padding (pad(x) = {max, mean, mode}), and the description conflates sequence-length padding with pooling/aggregation. It is unclear how sequences are truncated, padded, or batched. The mapping vec(x): R^{n×d}→R^{nd} and use of W∈R^{100×nd} together with LSTM encoders is also unclear/inconsistent (are LSTM outputs flattened? pooled? which time step is used?). These ambiguities make the architecture irreproducible.
- Missing crucial experimental details: No LSTM hidden size, number of layers, bidirectionality, dropout placement, regularization type (L2? activity?), batch size, number of epochs, early stopping criteria, tokenization choices, or OOV handling are provided. No random seeds, variances, or confidence intervals are reported; results may not be robust.
- Baseline and comparison shortcomings: The SVM baseline (B2) reimplementation is not fully documented (feature set parity, hyperparameters, code availability), and no stronger neural baselines are included (e.g., BiLSTM over full sentence with marker position indicators; attention models; sentence-level CNNs; or transformer-based encoders). The claim that LSTM is preferable to CNN is asserted without a controlled comparison.
- Evaluation limited to a single dataset with issues: Only AltLex is used. The dataset contains many unambiguous connectives, making the majority-per-connective baseline strong and potentially inflating gains. The paper’s own Table references are inconsistent/missing (Table 3 is referenced but absent; Table 6 has malformed numbers; figures are referenced but not accessible), and there is no analysis on out-of-domain or more diverse corpora (e.g., PDTB implicit relations, Causal-TimeBank for explicit CLINKs). The rebalanced “factor-of-ten” experiment alters the test distribution and complicates interpretation.
- Risk of label leakage and incomplete modeling of context: Concatenating l with e2 but not modeling positions/scope within the full sentence may inadvertently exploit surface marker presence rather than context. There is no ablation for removing l, encoding l separately, or using the whole sentence with span indicators. The approach presumes gold spans (e1, l, e2); the paper does not discuss how these are identified in real scenarios nor performance sensitivity to span errors.
- Claims vs evidence: The “context encoding” claim is only modestly supported (small gains with no error bars). The rationale against CNNs is not empirically validated. The discussion of temporal/counterfactual constraints is interesting but not operationalized in the model.
- Writing, references, and formatting quality: Numerous typos and formatting errors (“bootsrapping,” “Stated LSTM,” inconsistent math), incomplete or malformed references (question marks, odd escape sequences, missing venues/pages/years), misnumbered/missing tables/figures, and contradictions (e.g., GloVe “cased” 840B vectors are commonly uncased) detract from scientific rigor.

Questions to authors
- Architecture and padding details: Precisely define the input pipeline. What are the maximum sequence lengths for e1 and [l + e2]? Do you truncate longer sequences? How exactly are padding lengths chosen for “max/mean/mode,” and what does Equation (1) operationally do? What is the final vector fed to dense layers (last hidden state, pooled LSTM outputs, or flattened sequences)? Please provide exact LSTM configurations (hidden sizes, layers, bidirectionality), dropout placement, regularization type and coefficients.
- Baseline B2: Did you reimplement Hidey & McKeown (2016) or use their code? Are the features and splits identical? Please provide hyperparameters and verification of comparability. Any tuning on the test set?
- Data splits and statistics: Provide the complete statistics for both AltLex variants (train/dev/test sizes, class balance per split, counts of ambiguous markers). Clarify the anomaly in Table 4 (“Causal in train, Non Causal in test,” etc.) and fix malformed tables (e.g., Table 6).
- Reproducibility: Will you release code, pre-processing scripts, and trained models? Without this, the work is not reproducible given the current level of detail.
- Ablations and stronger baselines: Can you report (i) results without l; (ii) l encoded as a separate input; (iii) full-sentence BiLSTM/transformer with span indicators; (iv) CNN baseline; and (v) variance over multiple seeds? Also, show significance tests on the main (unaltered) test sets.

Overall recommendation
While the idea of using neural context encoding for causal marker disambiguation is sensible and the reported improvements over a feature-based baseline are encouraging, the paper suffers from methodological ambiguities, incomplete experimental detail, limited evaluation, and numerous presentation issues that undermine confidence and reproducibility. I recommend rejection in its current form; substantial clarification, stronger baselines, thorough ablations, and improved writing/formatting would be needed for reconsideration.
