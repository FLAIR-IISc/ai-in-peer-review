Summary
This paper tackles causal meaning disambiguation of lexical markers by encoding the two events and the connective with LSTM-based architectures. Using the AltLex corpus (Hidey & McKeown, 2016), the authors compare two variants: (i) Pair LSTM (independent LSTMs for the two inputs, concatenated), and (ii) Stated Pair LSTM (the second LSTM initialized with the first’s final state). They report improvements over two baselines: a per-connective majority baseline (B1) and the SVM feature-based system of Hidey & McKeown (B2). They also explore “padding” strategies (max/mean/mode), two optimizers (Adadelta/Adam), and provide some qualitative analysis.

Contributions (as I see them)
1) A simple neural baseline for causal meaning disambiguation that encodes event contexts without handcrafted features, showing modest improvements over a feature-rich SVM on AltLex.
2) An empirical analysis on AltLex highlighting class imbalance, connective ambiguity, and the impact of optimizer/“padding” choices; limited examples suggest contextual cues beyond the connective can help.
3) A framing that separates causal meaning classification from causal argument identification.

Strengths
- Addresses a relevant task with practical interest: disambiguating causal usage of connectives is important for discourse parsing and information extraction.
- Simpler modeling than prior feature-engineered methods: the LSTM setup with pretrained embeddings is straightforward and potentially more generalizable than curated lexical resources (Section 4).
- Empirical gains over B2 on AltLex: On the non-bootstrapping split, Pair LSTM Max Adadelta improves F1 for the Causal class (75.40% vs. 73.76%) and accuracy (77.90% vs. 71.86%) per Table 5; on the bootstrapping split, several configurations slightly exceed B2 (e.g., F1 81.56% vs. 80.90%).
- Class-imbalance awareness: the paper examines a reduced (more balanced) version of AltLex and shows the neural model outperforms B1 there as well (Table 7), with McNemar’s test vs. B1.
- Some qualitative evidence that context matters: examples where the model succeeds on ambiguous expressions (e.g., “which then”) and non-causal senses of “break” (Table 8) suggest the model is not purely a connective lookup.

Weaknesses
- Limited novelty and insufficient baselines: The architecture is a standard LSTM classifier; there is no comparison to stronger, modern, and established baselines such as BiLSTM with attention over the entire span, contextualized encoders (e.g., ELMo/BERT/RoBERTa), or even simple bag-of-words/averaged embeddings with an MLP. Without these, it is hard to credit the contribution beyond a modest neural baseline.
- Methodological clarity issues: The description of “zero-padding strategies” using max/mean/mode (Eq. 1) appears to conflate padding with pooling/aggregation. “Mode” over R^{n×d} is ill-defined; if this is temporal pooling, it should be stated as such and justified. The flow from token sequences to fixed-size representations (Eq. 2) is unclear.
- Evaluation rigor is inadequate:
  - No multiple runs/seed variance, confidence intervals, or significance tests vs. B2. Reported gains over B2 on the bootstrapping split are small (≤0.7 F1), and could be within variance.
  - Metrics: reporting only F1 for the Causal class and overall accuracy on an imbalanced dataset is suboptimal. Macro-F1, per-class F1, and PR-AUC would be more appropriate. Accuracy is particularly misleading in skewed settings.
  - Hyperparameter tuning and early stopping criteria are not described; train/dev/test splits, epoch counts, and selection protocol are missing.
- Potential connective overfitting and weak generalization evidence:
  - The model ingests the connective along with event2; without experiments on unseen connectives and/or masking the connective, it is unclear how much the model relies on the token vs. the context. The qualitative examples are anecdotal; a systematic per-connective breakdown (unseen, rare, ambiguous) is needed.
  - Generalization to other corpora (PDTB explicit/implicit causal relations, Causal-TimeBank) is not attempted, even as a small-scale out-of-domain evaluation.
- Incomplete analysis and ablation:
  - No ablation of input components (events only vs. connective only vs. both), no analysis of the “stated” initialization effect beyond raw scores, and no study of input length/truncation sensitivity.
  - The reduced (balanced) experiment omits comparison to B2 and other simple classifiers (e.g., MLP on averaged embeddings), so conclusions about robustness are limited.
- Presentation quality: Several tables/values are inconsistent or incomplete (e.g., Table 3 not shown; odd counts), equations are imprecise, and references contain typos/incomplete bibliographic details. This hinders reproducibility.

Questions to Authors
- Clarify the “zero-padding” strategies: Are these actually temporal pooling operations (max/mean/mode over the time dimension)? How is “mode” defined for continuous embeddings?
- What are the exact train/dev/test splits for both versions of AltLex? Were sentences/connectives deduplicated across splits? How were hyperparameters chosen, and was early stopping used on dev?
- How many runs per configuration? Please report mean ± std over multiple seeds and significance vs. B2.
- How much does the model rely on the connective token? Please report: (i) performance when masking or removing l, (ii) performance on unseen/rare connectives, and (iii) per-connective breakdowns of precision/recall.
- Can you compare against a simple averaged-embeddings MLP, a BiLSTM over the whole sentence, and a transformer baseline (e.g., fine-tuned BERT) to position your gains against established methods?
- Do you have any cross-corpus results (e.g., training on AltLex, testing on PDTB explicit/implicit causal relations, or on Causal-TimeBank) to demonstrate generalization?

Recommendation
Given the limited novelty, methodological ambiguities, and insufficient evaluation against established neural baselines with statistical rigor, I cannot recommend acceptance at this time. The problem is important, and a clarified, thoroughly validated study with stronger baselines and clearer analysis would be much more compelling.
