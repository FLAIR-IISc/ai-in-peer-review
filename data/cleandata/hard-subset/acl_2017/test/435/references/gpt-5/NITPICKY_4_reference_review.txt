Summary
This paper tackles causal meaning disambiguation: given two event spans (e1, e2) and a lexical marker l, decide whether the relation is Causal or Non-Causal. The authors argue that causality should be determined by contextual semantics rather than predefined syntactic patterns or resource-constrained features. They propose a two-input LSTM-based architecture that encodes (i) e1 and (ii) l concatenated with e2, followed by dense layers, and evaluate on the AltLex corpus (Hidey & McKeown, 2016), reporting improvements over a majority-by-connective baseline (B1) and the feature-based SVM from Hidey & McKeown (B2). They also report experiments on two AltLex variants (“non-bootstrapping” and “bootstrapping”) and an additional downsampled setting.

Main contributions (as I see them)
1) Empirical finding: an LSTM-based contextual encoder can outperform a feature-based SVM on AltLex causal disambiguation, particularly in precision and overall accuracy.
2) Experimental evidence across two AltLex variants and hyperparameter settings (optimizers, pooling/padding choices), plus a small qualitative analysis illustrating some ambiguous cases.
3) Framing of the task into two subtasks (causal meaning classification and argument identification), and focusing on the first using minimal external resources (GloVe only).

Strengths
- Problem relevance: Causal disambiguation of connectives/AltLexes is practically useful and under-resourced. Using a contextual neural model is a reasonable direction that avoids reliance on brittle patterns and resource coverage.
- Competitive results: On AltLex “non-bootstrapping,” your Pair LSTM surpasses B2 in F1 and accuracy; on “bootstrapping,” several LSTM configs match or exceed B2. The improvement in precision (and better precision–recall balance with Adam) is noteworthy given B2’s tendency for false positives.
- Dataset coverage: Evaluating on both AltLex variants is useful, especially as the “bootstrapping” variant contains train–test connective distribution shifts.
- Some qualitative insight: The examples in Table 8 (e.g., “which then,” “break off”) suggest the model can use context beyond connective priors, at least in specific cases.
- Simplicity and low resource dependence: The architecture is relatively simple, uses only pre-trained embeddings, and may be more generalizable than feature engineering tied to lexical resources.

Weaknesses
- Limited novelty and positioning: Neural models for discourse relation/connective disambiguation are well-studied (e.g., work on PDTB implicit/explicit relation classification, connective sense disambiguation). The paper does not engage with this broader literature or compare to standard neural baselines (BiLSTM/attention, simple average-embeddings logistic regression, or modern encoders), and only contrasts with a single SVM baseline from Hidey & McKeown. The contribution reads incremental.
- Method description is confusing/inconsistent: 
  • “Zero-padding strategies” defined as max/mean/mode are not zero-padding; they resemble pooling or an aggregation choice. The math in Eq. (1)–(2) is imprecise: x∈R^{n×d}, W∈R^{100×nd}, tanh(W·x+b) implies flattening variable-length sequences, contradicting the LSTM encoder and “padding” discussion. 
  • “Stated Pair LSTM” likely means “stateful/initialized pair,” but the description is unclear (e.g., hidden/cell state sizes, uni/bidirectionality, whether initial_state is set for both h and c).
  • Important implementation details are missing: LSTM hidden sizes, number of layers, whether embeddings are fine-tuned, OOV handling, tokenization, maximum sequence lengths, batch size, epochs, early stopping/selection criteria, regularization type (L2?).
- Evaluation design risks and insufficient analyses:
  • No development set or model selection protocol is described. Multiple configurations are compared directly on the test set, suggesting potential test overfitting.
  • The class/connective imbalance makes B1 very strong; to substantiate the “context” claim, you need per-connective evaluation, results on ambiguous-only markers, and leave-one-connective-out (or remove marker tokens) to demonstrate generalization beyond marker identity.
  • No ablations: effect of removing the marker from the input; replacing LSTM with average-of-embeddings; comparing Pair LSTM vs Stated Pair LSTM with controlled capacity; effect of e1/e2 only.
  • No statistical significance tests except McNemar vs B1 on downsampled data; none vs B2 or on full data.
- Data and preprocessing clarity gaps:
  • It is unclear how e1/e2 spans are obtained in AltLex (are Arg1/Arg2 provided and how mapped to “events”?). How do you handle sentences with multiple candidate events or multi-sentential spans?
  • Table 3/6 numbers appear corrupted or inconsistent (e.g., “,606 7,929 15,534”), making it hard to verify class distributions. Table/figure references are sometimes mismatched or incomplete.
- Writing quality and formatting issues:
  • Numerous typos/grammar (“bootsrapping,” “later/latter,” “the later approaches”), inconsistent notation and punctuation, and many bibliographic inconsistencies/placeholders (e.g., “Kruengkrai et al\.,? \Q2017\E”). Equations and symbols misuse (IR for R, etc.). These significantly hinder clarity and reproducibility.

Questions to Authors
1) Data and spans: How exactly are e1 and e2 spans obtained from AltLex? Are they sentence-level arguments (Arg1/Arg2), event heads, or manually provided spans? How do you tokenize and truncate? What is the average length of e1/e2? 
2) Model selection: What was your dev set? How did you choose hyperparameters (optimizer, pooling/padding, dropout, regularization, LSTM size)? How many runs/seeds per config, and do you report mean±std?
3) Padding/pooling: Please clarify the “zero-padding strategies.” Are you pooling (max/mean/mode) over time, or choosing a padding length using max/mean/mode, or filling pad tokens with max/mean/mode values? How does this interact with the LSTM encoder?
4) Architecture details: LSTM hidden size(s), number of layers, bidirectional or not, whether embeddings were fine-tuned, dropout placement, regularization type, batch size, epochs, early stopping criteria.
5) Controlling for marker identity: What is performance on the subset of ambiguous connectives? What happens if you remove the marker tokens from the input? Can you report leave-one-connective-out results?
6) Baselines and ablations: Can you compare against a simple average-of-embeddings + logistic regression baseline, a single BiLSTM with attention over e1+l+e2, and a CNN baseline to substantiate the LSTM choice?
7) Statistical testing: Can you provide significance tests vs B2 on both corpora?

Recommendation
Given the methodological ambiguities, missing details, limited positioning/novelty, and numerous writing/formatting issues, I recommend rejection. The empirical direction is promising, but the paper needs clearer modeling, stronger, controlled evaluations, and a thorough rewrite for clarity and reproducibility.
