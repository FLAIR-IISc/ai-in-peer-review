Summary and claimed contributions
This paper studies causal meaning disambiguation: given two event spans (e1, e2) and a lexical marker (l), determine whether the relation is causal. The authors argue that causality should be modeled from context rather than fixed lexical/syntactic patterns. They propose an LSTM-based “pair” architecture that encodes e1 and the concatenation of (l, e2), followed by dense layers for classification. Two variants are tested: (i) independent LSTMs for the two inputs (Pair LSTM) and (ii) state-initialized LSTMs where the second LSTM is initialized from the first (Stated Pair LSTM). They evaluate on the AltLex corpus (Hidey & McKeown, 2016), comparing to (a) a strong per-marker majority baseline and (b) Hidey & McKeown’s SVM with lexical-resource features. They report modest improvements in F1 and accuracy, and present qualitative analyses for ambiguous markers.

Contributions (as assessed)
1) A context-encoding neural baseline for causal lexical marker disambiguation on AltLex that outperforms a strong per-marker baseline and slightly improves over the prior SVM-based system.
2) Empirical comparison of pair-encoding variants (with/without state sharing), optimizers, and sequence-length padding strategies; and analysis indicating that independent encoders plus context interaction via dense layers work best.
3) A task framing that separates causal meaning classification from causal argument identification, with examples illustrating challenges of ambiguous markers beyond fixed connectives.

Strengths
- Sound motivation for context-based modeling: The paper clearly articulates why relying on fixed lexical/syntactic patterns or causative verb lists is insufficient, and positions LSTMs as a means to encode broader contextual cues. This aligns with the observed ambiguity of markers like “since/after,” and is supported by examples from AltLex.
- Competitive empirical results on a public benchmark: On the AltLex non-bootstrapping split, the best model improves F1 for the causal class over the SVM baseline (73.76 to 75.40) and substantially improves overall accuracy (71.86 to 77.90). On the bootstrapping split, several configurations slightly outperform the SVM in F1 (e.g., up to 81.96 vs 80.90) and accuracy (80.68 vs 79.58). The gains are modest but consistent across configurations, indicating robustness.
- Strong baselines and analyses: The per-marker majority baseline is appropriate and strong given class/connective skew. The paper explores two architectural choices (state-sharing vs independent encoders), two optimizers (Adam/Adadelta), and alternative sequence-length settings, and observes consistent trends (independent encoders and Adam generally yield better precision/precision-recall balance).
- Qualitative evidence on ambiguous cases: The paper includes examples where the proposed model handles ambiguous or rare expressions (e.g., “which then”) better than the SVM, suggesting it leverages contextual semantics beyond lexical identity.
- Use of standard resources and metrics: The study uses widely available GloVe embeddings and reports precision, recall, F1 for the causal class and overall accuracy, allowing straightforward comparison.

Weaknesses
- Limited novelty: The core methodology—two LSTM encoders over text spans with concatenation and dense layers—is standard for sentence-pair classification. The “Stated Pair LSTM” (second LSTM initialized from the first) is a known design; the paper’s novelty is mainly in applying such architectures to AltLex causality disambiguation rather than introducing new modeling ideas.
- Incomplete and sometimes unclear methodological detail: Key implementation details are under-specified, impeding reproducibility. Examples include LSTM hidden sizes, exact sequence length policy (the “padding strategies” are described with an equation that is mathematically unclear: pad(x) = {max, mean, mode} over what?), truncation policy, training epochs, batch sizes, whether GloVe is fine-tuned, OOV handling, and event span extraction from AltLex. The formulae in Section 3.3 are imprecise (e.g., dimensions for vec(x), relation to the LSTM output).
- Evaluation scope and statistical rigor: Results are limited to a single corpus (AltLex). No significance tests are reported against the SVM baseline, and improvements are small (≈1–2 F1). The paper reports significance for comparisons to the per-marker baseline on a reduced dataset, but not for the main comparison to the SVM. It would be important to control for connective identity (e.g., via ablations removing the marker token) to confirm contextual gains.
- Practicality and task setup: The study assumes gold event spans (e1, e2) and an identified marker l. In real settings, argument extraction and marker detection are non-trivial; the paper does not quantify how errors in upstream steps would affect performance. The claimed task decomposition remains theoretical here; only the first subtask is addressed.
- Minor inconsistencies and presentation issues: Some reported improvements do not match table differences (e.g., stated +2.13% F1 and +7.72% accuracy vs numbers shown). Reference formatting is inconsistent. Several typos and formatting issues reduce clarity.

Questions to authors
- Event span definition and extraction: How exactly are e1 and e2 defined and extracted from AltLex? Are they the text spans before/after the marker, or annotated event triggers and arguments? How do you handle multiple clauses or nested structures?
- Sequence length policy: Please clarify the “padding strategies.” Are you setting a fixed sequence length equal to the max/mean/mode of observed lengths, and then padding/truncating accordingly? What are the numeric values used? How sensitive are results to this choice?
- Model details: What are the LSTM hidden sizes, number of layers, dropout placements/values, batch size, number of epochs, early stopping criteria, and whether word embeddings are fine-tuned? How are OOV and casing handled?
- Role of the lexical marker: Did you run an ablation excluding the marker token from the input to quantify the contribution of context vs connective identity? Conversely, what happens if only the marker is used (to compare against the per-marker baseline in a learned fashion)?
- Statistical significance vs SVM: Are the improvements over B2 statistically significant (per-split and aggregated), and if so, under which test?
- Data splits and preprocessing: Do you follow exactly the train/dev/test splits from Hidey & McKeown? If different, please specify. How do you handle duplicate or near-duplicate sentences from the bootstrapping process?

Overall, the paper presents a reasonable context-based neural baseline with modest improvements over prior work on a public dataset. Clarifying the methodology, adding ablations and significance testing against the SVM baseline, and broadening evaluation would strengthen the technical soundness and impact.
