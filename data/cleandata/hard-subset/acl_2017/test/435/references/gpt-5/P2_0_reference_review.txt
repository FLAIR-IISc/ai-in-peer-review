Summary
This paper tackles causal meaning disambiguation for lexical markers (e.g., since, after, due to) by modeling the context around two events and a putative marker. The authors propose two neural architectures that encode the two event spans (and the marker concatenated with the second event) with LSTMs and classify the relation as Causal vs. Non-Causal. Experiments on the AltLex corpus (both non-bootstrapping and bootstrapping splits) compare against (i) a per-connective majority baseline and (ii) the feature-based SVM from Hidey & McKeown (2016). The proposed models yield modest improvements in F1 and accuracy and show higher precision than the feature-based baseline. Some qualitative analysis and a small imbalance experiment are provided.

Contributions (as I see them)
- Empirical result: A simple two-input LSTM encoder that conditions on the local context of (e1, l, e2) achieves small but consistent gains over a strong feature-based baseline on AltLex, especially in precision, supporting the claim that contextual encoding helps disambiguate ambiguous causal markers.
- Architectural/experimental insight: Pair-wise independent encoding of the two arguments (Pair LSTM) outperforms initializing the second with the first’s state (Stated Pair LSTM), and Adam tends to improve precision/recall balance; a brief analysis of class imbalance is included.
- Framing: A clear statement of the two-step view of causal relation processing (causal meaning classification vs. argument identification), with this work focusing on the first step.

Strengths
- Relevance and focus: The paper addresses an important, under-explored subproblem—disambiguating the causal sense of markers that can be temporal or causal. The AltLex corpus is an appropriate benchmark for this focus.
- Competitive performance with a simple model: Across both AltLex versions, the proposed models generally outperform the per-connective majority baseline and the feature-based SVM, with the best models yielding +1–2 F1 points (Causal class) and +1–8 accuracy points, and improved precision. This supports the core hypothesis that contextual neural encoders can reduce overreliance on lexical priors.
- Some analysis beyond headline numbers: The paper contrasts two architectural variants (Pair vs. Stated Pair LSTM), reports optimizer effects, and includes a small imbalance study with a statistical test (McNemar) showing the neural model’s improvement over the strong majority baseline.
- Qualitative examples: The examples where the neural model succeeds and the SVM fails (e.g., with which then, non-causative uses of break) illustrate the intended benefits of context encoding and help ground the claims.

Weaknesses
- Clarity and technical precision: The model description is inconsistent and underspecified. The paper claims LSTM encoders, yet the equations describe flattening padded sequences and applying a dense layer; the “zero-padding strategies” (max/mean/mode) are ill-defined (the notation pad(x) = {max, mean, mode} is not meaningful as written), and it is unclear whether sequences are truncated/padded to corpus-level statistics. Critical implementation details are missing: uni/bidirectional LSTMs, hidden sizes, pooling over time, how the LSTM outputs are combined, sequence length limits, training regimen, early stopping, random seeds, and variance over runs.
- Limited novelty: The architectures are standard multi-input LSTM encoders (akin to Siamese/twin encoders) with minor conditioning. There is no substantive modeling innovation or new learning objective; improvements likely stem from replacing hand-engineered features with distributed representations.
- Evaluation scope and strength: Results are reported on a single dataset (AltLex) and split (two variants from the same source), with small gains (often ~1 F1 point) and no significance testing on the main results. There is no cross-dataset evaluation (e.g., PDTB ambiguous connectives) or domain transfer, limiting claims of generality. The quantitative analysis of error types is minimal.
- Task setup and realism: The model assumes gold identification of e1, e2, and the lexical marker; the paper does not explain how these spans are obtained from AltLex or how robust the method is when spans are noisy or when the marker is implicit. This reduces the practical impact and makes it hard to compare to end-to-end discourse parsing settings.
- Baselines and ablations: While the per-connective majority and Hidey & McKeown SVM baselines are reasonable, the paper lacks modern or strong neural baselines (e.g., sentence-level BiLSTM/attention without explicit event segmentation; bag-of-embeddings MLP; a model using only the marker identity; or an ablation removing the marker to quantify context vs. lexical reliance). Without ablations, it is hard to isolate what parts of the architecture matter.
- Writing and references: The paper has numerous typos, incomplete or malformed references, partially missing tables/figures, and unclear notation. This hinders reproducibility and raises concerns about rigor.

Questions to Authors
- Data and spans: How are e1, l, and e2 spans obtained from AltLex? Are the event spans annotated, or are they heuristically extracted? If heuristics are used, please detail them and their accuracy.
- Model specifics: Are the encoders uni- or bi-LSTMs? What hidden sizes, number of layers, and pooling strategy over time are used? How are the LSTM outputs mapped to the 100-d vectors (last state, max-pool, attention)?
- Padding/truncation: Please clarify the “max/mean/mode” padding strategies precisely. To what fixed length are sequences padded/truncated in each strategy? Why would “mean” or “mode” be preferable to “max”, given potential truncation?
- Training protocol: How are hyperparameters selected (dev set, search strategy)? How many runs per configuration, and what is the variance? Any regularization beyond dropout/L2? Do you use early stopping?
- Significance and error types: Are the main improvements significant (e.g., bootstrap test)? Can you provide a breakdown by connective, especially for highly ambiguous ones (since, after), and an analysis of typical false positives/negatives?
- Baselines/ablations: Can you add comparisons to (i) a sentence-level BiLSTM/MLP that does not rely on gold event spans, (ii) a bag-of-embeddings MLP, and (iii) ablations that remove the marker, the first event, or the second event to quantify their contributions?

Overall, the problem is important and the results are encouraging, but the paper needs substantially clearer methodology, stronger baselines/ablations, and broader evaluation to meet ACL standards.
