Summary and contributions
- The paper tackles disambiguation of causal meaning for lexical markers in context, framing causality classification as two subtasks and focusing on the first: deciding whether a relation between two annotated events (e1, e2) with a suspected marker l is causal or not.
- It proposes two LSTM-based architectures: (i) Pair LSTM that encodes e1 and [l + e2] independently and classifies the concatenated representations; (ii) “Stated Pair LSTM” where the LSTM for the second input is initialized with the final state of the first, to model interdependence.
- It evaluates on the AltLex corpus (non-bootstrapping and bootstrapping versions), comparing against (B1) majority-per-marker and (B2) the SVM system of Hidey & McKeown (2016), reporting modest gains in F1 on the causal class and accuracy. There is a small qualitative analysis.

Strengths
- Problem relevance and scope: Disambiguating causal meaning for ambiguous markers like since/after is a well-motivated discourse task; using a corpus with explicit marker annotations is appropriate.
- Empirical comparison on a public dataset against the published method that introduced the corpus. In several settings, the proposed models outperform the SVM baseline (e.g., on non-bootstrapping, +2.1 F1C and +7.7 Acc), indicating that learned context representations can help beyond feature engineering tied to lexicons.
- Clear experimental observation that encoding e1 and [l+e2] independently (Pair LSTM) is generally better than state-passing between LSTMs, which is informative for future modeling of relational inputs.
- Some attempt to probe class imbalance via downsampling and to discuss the effect of connectives’ distribution shift between train/test in the bootstrapping version; a small qualitative error analysis highlights cases where context trumps verb-level heuristics.

Weaknesses
- Limited novelty and outdated modeling: Using LSTMs over GloVe for sentence-level disambiguation is not novel for this problem space, and the arguments against CNNs are unconvincing. Crucially, no comparison to stronger, established baselines (simple bag-of-embeddings/logistic regression; BiLSTM with attention; contextualized encoders such as ELMo/BERT/RoBERTa fine-tuning) is provided. Given the task and data size, a marker- and context-aware transformer baseline is expected and likely strong.
- Method description lacks clarity and rigor: The “zero-padding strategies” section is confusing and appears mathematically incorrect (pad(x) defined via max/mean/mode over x in R^{n×d}); it is unclear whether this refers to sequence length selection, pooling over time, or padding value. The LSTM output used (last state vs pooled sequence) is unspecified. Critical training details are missing (epochs, batch size, early stopping, seed, number of runs), hindering reproducibility. The figure referenced is not included; terminology oscillates between “Stated LSTM” and “Stated Pair LSTM.”
- Evaluation design is insufficient:
  - Only a single corpus is used; no cross-corpus validation on Causal-TimeBank/PDTB causal relations or out-of-domain testing. The claims of generalization are therefore weak.
  - The reported improvements are small in the stronger bootstrapping setting (≈+1 F1C, ≈+1 Acc), with no statistical significance reported on the main results and no variance across multiple seeds.
  - Metrics focus on F1 for the causal class and overall accuracy; macro-F1 and per-marker breakdowns (especially on ambiguous markers) are not given. A marker-only baseline (predicting by lexical item identity, not just majority class per marker) and an overall majority-class baseline are missing.
  - The “reduced” (downsampled) dataset experiment changes the class distribution and yields significance only versus B1; it is not informative for comparing to B2 or to stronger baselines.
- Task assumptions limit applicability: The model assumes gold event spans and an explicit lexical marker l; the paper claims applicability to implicit relations but does not evaluate without l (or with l empty). This constrains the practical utility compared to end-to-end discourse relation identification.
- Potential confounds and leakage: Given that marker identity is a very strong signal, the model might rely heavily on l rather than context. No ablation removing l, or controlling lexical overlap between train/test, is presented. Without such controls, gains may reflect learning marker priors rather than true contextual disambiguation.
- Writing and presentation issues: Numerous typos and formatting problems; incomplete and inconsistently formatted references; missing figure(s); tables are hard to follow. These issues complicate assessment and reproducibility.

Questions to authors
- Architecture and pooling:
  - What exactly are the “max/mean/mode” strategies? Are these pooling operations over the time dimension, choices of sequence length for padding, or something else? Please provide a precise mathematical description, including the LSTM output used (last hidden state vs pooled sequence).
  - What are the sizes of the LSTM(s), dropout placements, number of parameters?
- Training and evaluation protocol:
  - What are batch size, number of epochs, early stopping criteria, learning rates, and weight decay? How were hyperparameters selected? How many random seeds were run? Please report mean±std over multiple runs and statistical significance for the main tables.
  - Are you using the exact same train/dev/test splits as Hidey & McKeown (2016)? If not, please align or justify differences.
- Baselines and ablations:
  - Please include: (i) a simple logistic regression over averaged GloVe vectors of e1, l, e2; (ii) a BiLSTM with attention over the concatenated sequence; (iii) contextualized encoder baselines (e.g., fine-tuned BERT/RoBERTa) with the same inputs; (iv) a marker-only baseline and an overall majority-class baseline; (v) ablations removing l and/or e1/e2 to quantify reliance on each component.
  - Provide per-marker and especially ambiguous-marker performance to demonstrate true disambiguation.
- Task scope:
  - How would the method handle truly implicit relations without explicit l? Have you tried training with l removed or empty to test robustness?
  - Do you rely on gold event spans at test time? If so, how sensitive is the model to noise in event extraction?
- Generalization:
  - Can you evaluate zero-shot or fine-tuned transfer to Causal-TimeBank or PDTB causal relations to support claims of generality?
- Resources:
  - Will you release code, processed data, and scripts to ensure reproducibility?

Overall recommendation: I lean toward rejection. The modeling novelty is limited, the method description is unclear, and the evaluation lacks strong, established baselines, ablations, and significance analyses. The empirical gains are modest and confined to a single dataset under assumptions (gold events and markers) that restrict applicability. Strengthening the baselines (including contextual models), clarifying and tightening the method, adding ablations and cross-corpus tests, and improving presentation would substantially improve the submission.
