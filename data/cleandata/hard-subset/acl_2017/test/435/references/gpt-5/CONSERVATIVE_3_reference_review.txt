Summary
This paper tackles causal meaning disambiguation: given two event spans and a candidate lexical marker, decide whether the relation is causal or not. The authors propose two simple LSTM-based architectures that encode (i) the first event and (ii) the concatenation of the marker with the second event, followed by dense layers. They evaluate on the AltLex corpus (non-bootstrapping and bootstrapping versions), comparing to (a) a majority-per-connective baseline and (b) Hidey & McKeown (2016)’s feature-based SVM. The proposed models achieve modest improvements in F1 and accuracy, especially on the non-bootstrapping split, and they provide a brief qualitative analysis of a few ambiguous cases.

Contributions (as I see them)
1) An empirical study showing that a simple LSTM-based encoder over event spans can match or slightly outperform a feature-engineered SVM on the AltLex dataset for causal disambiguation.
2) A task framing that separates causal meaning classification from argument identification, and an input formulation that conditions on event spans and the marker.
3) A small qualitative analysis highlighting specific ambiguous cases where context helps beyond connective priors.

Strengths
- Empirical improvements over a strong, established feature-based baseline: On the non-bootstrapping split, the best LSTM achieves higher F1 and notably higher accuracy than Hidey & McKeown (2016), suggesting that contextual sequence models can reduce reliance on lexical resource coverage.
- Focus on a relevant, under-resourced subtask: Causal relation disambiguation for ambiguous/AltLex markers is important and not fully captured by traditional explicit-connective resources.
- Simplicity and resource-light approach: Using only pre-trained GloVe embeddings and avoiding external lexical resources is attractive for portability and potentially better coverage of diverse lexicalizations.
- Some robustness checks: Results are reported on two versions of AltLex and a more balanced downsampled variant; a McNemar test is provided (albeit only for B1 comparisons).

Weaknesses
- Limited novelty and justification: The architectures are straightforward (two LSTMs plus MLP), and the “Stated Pair LSTM” is a minor variant of passing the final state. The main methodological choice (LSTM vs CNN) is asserted rather than demonstrated; no comparison is provided to stronger, established sequence encoders (e.g., BiLSTM over full sentence, attention mechanisms) or to modern pretrained encoders (e.g., transformer-based models). In 2025, the absence of transformer baselines substantially weakens the methodological contribution.
- Insufficient evaluation scope and controls: Evaluation is confined to AltLex; no cross-corpus transfer (e.g., PDTB causal, Causal-TimeBank) or cross-domain testing to assess generalization. Crucially, there is no analysis restricted to ambiguous markers, where the task is most meaningful; unambiguous markers likely inflate scores and can be captured by the majority-per-connective baseline. An ablation that removes the lexical marker from the input (or evaluates only ambiguous markers) is needed to demonstrate learning beyond connective identity.
- Methodological clarity issues and potential confounds: The description of “padding strategies” (max/mean/mode) appears to be pooling over time rather than padding; this is unconventional and unclear. Key hyperparameters are missing (LSTM hidden sizes, number of layers, sequence length limits, batch size, epochs, early stopping criteria). The train/dev/test protocol is not described; it is unclear whether a dev set was used and how hyperparameters were tuned, raising concerns about potential tuning on test. It’s also unclear whether B2 was reimplemented under identical splits or numbers were borrowed.
- Baseline coverage is incomplete: Besides B1 and B2, there should be strong, simple baselines: (i) logistic regression/MLP over averaged embeddings; (ii) BiLSTM/attention over the entire sentence; (iii) Siamese/interaction models over the two spans; (iv) fine-tuned BERT/roBERTa over concatenated spans with marker and optionally sentence context. Without these, it is hard to assess whether LSTMs provide a true advantage beyond connective priors.
- Statistical significance and error analysis are limited: Improvements over B2 on the main test sets are modest (especially on the bootstrapping split), and no significance tests vs B2 are reported. The error analysis is minimal and anecdotal; per-marker performance, especially for ambiguous markers, and confusion patterns would strengthen claims.
- Presentation and reproducibility: Numerous typos and formatting issues (tables, references) impede clarity. Code and trained models are not released, and the description is not sufficient to reproduce results.

Questions to Authors
1) Padding/Pooling: Please clarify the “pad(x) = {max, mean, mode}” operation. Is this actually temporal pooling over the sequence? How does it interact with the LSTM outputs? Why call it padding?
2) Experimental protocol: What were the train/dev/test splits? Was a dev set used for hyperparameter selection and early stopping? How many epochs? Batch size? LSTM hidden sizes? Number of layers? Dropout and L2 values actually selected?
3) Baseline comparability: Was B2 reimplemented and trained on your exact splits for both AltLex versions? If not, how are apples-to-apples comparisons ensured?
4) Ambiguous-only subset: What is the performance when restricting evaluation to ambiguous markers (e.g., since/after/which then etc.)? Similarly, what happens if you remove the lexical marker from the input—does the model still outperform B2?
5) Context scope: Why restrict inputs to e1 and l+e2 rather than including the entire sentence (or both spans with a learnable interaction/attention)? Did you try sentence-level encoders?
6) Pretrained encoders: Did you try modern pretrained transformers (e.g., fine-tuning BERT/RoBERTa) or even contextualized embeddings (ELMo)? If not, why, and how do you expect your method to compare?
7) Significance: Are the main improvements over B2 statistically significant on the full test sets?
8) Generalization: Any results on PDTB causal relations or Causal-TimeBank, or cross-corpus evaluation to demonstrate robustness?
9) Reproducibility: Will you release code, exact preprocessing, and model configs to enable replication?

Overall, while the task is worthwhile and the reported gains over a feature-based baseline are encouraging, the methodological novelty is limited and the evaluation lacks the rigor and breadth expected for endorsing a new approach. Strengthening baselines, clarifying methodology, and providing focused analyses on ambiguous cases would substantially improve the paper.
