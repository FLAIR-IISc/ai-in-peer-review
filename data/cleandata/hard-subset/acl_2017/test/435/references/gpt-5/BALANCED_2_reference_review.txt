Summary and claimed contributions
This paper tackles causal meaning disambiguation: given two event spans (e1, e2) and a putative lexical marker l, decide whether the relation is causal or not. The authors argue that causality is not tied to fixed syntactic patterns and propose to learn contextualized representations of the events with LSTMs. They evaluate on the AltLex corpus (Hidey & McKeown, 2016), comparing against (i) a majority-per-connector baseline and (ii) the SVM system from Hidey & McKeown. They explore two architectures (independent encoders vs. initializing the second LSTM with the first’s final state), several pooling/padding strategies, and optimizers. They report consistent improvements over the per-connector baseline and modest gains over the SVM on both “non-bootstrapping” and “bootstrapping” AltLex splits, plus qualitative analysis of ambiguous markers.

As I see them, the main contributions are:
1) An LSTM-based, resource-agnostic model for causal disambiguation of event pairs that achieves small but consistent improvements over a strong feature-based SVM baseline on the AltLex corpus.
2) An empirical study across two corpus variants (including a more challenging distributional setting), with observations about ambiguity, the importance of context encoding, and qualitative examples illustrating generalization to ambiguous markers.
3) A modeling finding that encoding e1 and (l+e2) independently (“Pair LSTM”) outperforms initializing the second from the first (“Stated Pair LSTM”), informing future design choices.

Strengths
- Empirical improvements over a reasonable state-of-the-art baseline on a relevant benchmark. On AltLex “non-bootstrapping,” Pair LSTM (Max, Adadelta) improves F1 for the Causal class (75.40 vs. 73.76) and accuracy (77.90 vs. 71.86) over the SVM. On “bootstrapping,” the best neural variant is slightly better in F1 (81.56 vs. 80.90) and accuracy (80.68 vs. 79.58). These are modest but consistent gains across settings.
- Resource-agnostic method likely to generalize beyond hand-engineered features. Using only pre-trained GloVe embeddings and simple sequence encoders, the approach avoids dependence on lexical resources (WordNet/VerbNet/PropBank), which can limit recall and coverage. The qualitative examples include success on highly ambiguous forms like “which then,” supporting the claim that context matters.
- Experimental breadth and some statistical testing. The paper explores two architectural variants, pooling/padding choices (max/mean/mode), and optimizers (Adam/Adadelta). It also examines a rebalanced subset to probe class imbalance and reports McNemar’s test showing significant gains over the per-connector baseline in that setting.
- Clear task framing. The paper cleanly separates causal meaning disambiguation from argument identification, a useful conceptual decomposition for the community.

Weaknesses
- Limited novelty and incomplete baseline coverage. The core model (two LSTMs + dense layers over pre-trained word vectors) is a straightforward application of standard sequence modeling. The paper does not compare against strong sequence-encoding baselines (e.g., CNN, BiLSTM with attention, average-of-embeddings + logistic regression), nor more modern contextual encoders (e.g., ELMo/BERT-style models). Given the modest gains over the SVM, stronger baselines are needed to establish significance.
- Methodological clarity and reproducibility issues. Key details are unclear or inconsistent:
  - The “zero-padding strategies” (max/mean/mode) are unusual and not well defined; the equations suggest pooling rather than padding, with dimensional inconsistencies (e.g., W ∈ R100×nd multiplied by x ∈ Rn×d). This needs precise specification.
  - Important hyperparameters (LSTM hidden size, number of layers, dropout application points, batch size, epochs, early stopping criteria), embedding fine-tuning vs. frozen, tokenization and OOV handling are not fully described.
  - Development set, model selection, and tuning protocol are not reported.
  - Figures/Tables/Counts have errors or omissions (e.g., Table numbering, garbled counts like “,606,” missing Table 3 in the text), and references contain formatting errors, which hinders replication.
- Evaluation design leaves open questions about what the model learns. The model is fed the lexical marker concatenated with e2, which could allow it to rely on marker identity. There is no experiment masking the marker or testing generalization to unseen markers. Per-marker performance (especially for ambiguous ones like since/after) and an “unseen connective” split would strengthen claims that contextual encoding, rather than marker memorization, drives gains.
- Limited analysis and statistical rigor relative to the SVM baseline. Statistical significance is shown only vs. the per-connector baseline in the balanced subset; there is no significance testing vs. the SVM. Metrics emphasize Causal-class F1 and overall accuracy; macro/micro-F1 across both classes and confidence intervals would be more robust in imbalanced settings. Error analysis is brief; a typology of false positives/negatives by marker, event type, and syntactic realization would add insight.
- Scope and dataset limitations. The model processes only the e1 and (l+e2) spans, not broader sentence/context, yet claims to “encode context.” Only AltLex is used; cross-corpus evaluation (e.g., on Causal-TimeBank) or zero-shot transfer would better demonstrate generalization. The rebalanced experiment omits the SVM baseline, complicating interpretation.

Questions to authors
- How are e1 and e2 spans obtained from AltLex? Are they annotated, or do you heuristically extract them? Please detail preprocessing and span selection.
- What exactly are the “max/mean/mode” padding strategies? Are you pooling over time before the dense layers? Please clarify the equations and provide a schematic.
- Do you include the lexical marker tokens in the input? If so, can you report results with markers masked to test reliance on marker identity?
- What are the full hyperparameters (LSTM hidden size, layers, optimizer settings, batch size, epochs), and did you fine-tune embeddings? How did you select them (dev set protocol)?
- Can you report per-marker performance, especially for ambiguous connectives (since, after), and an “unseen connective” evaluation (train without a marker, test on it)?
- Are the gains over the SVM statistically significant (e.g., McNemar or bootstrap tests)?
- Will you release code and preprocessing scripts?

Suggestions for improvement
- Add stronger baselines: average-of-embeddings + logistic regression, CNN, BiLSTM with attention, and a transformer encoder fine-tuned on AltLex. Include an ablation with and without marker tokens and with whole-sentence vs. event-span inputs.
- Report macro- and per-class F1, confidence intervals, and significance vs. SVM. Provide per-marker breakdowns and an unseen-marker evaluation.
- Clarify the model description and training protocol; fix equations, tables, and references; include a model diagram. Release code.
- Expand error analysis and discuss typical failure modes (e.g., temporal vs. causal ambiguity, metaphorical causation, nominal vs. verbal events).
- Consider cross-corpus tests (e.g., train on AltLex, test on Causal-TimeBank) or adversarial splits to measure robustness and true context understanding.

Overall recommendation
Borderline reject. The paper addresses a worthwhile problem and shows encouraging results with a simple, resource-agnostic model. However, the methodological clarity, baseline coverage, and evaluation rigor are insufficient for ACL at this stage. Strengthening baselines, clarifying the method, and deepening the analysis would significantly improve the submission.
