Summary
This paper tackles causal meaning disambiguation: given two spans (e1, e2) and a putative causal marker l in context, decide whether the relation is causal or not. The authors argue against relying on fixed syntactic patterns or lexical resources and propose LSTM-based models that encode the two arguments (with and without transferring LSTM state from e1 to e2). Inputs are pre-trained GloVe embeddings; the two encodings are concatenated and passed through MLP layers. Experiments are conducted on the AltLex corpus (both non-bootstrapping and bootstrapping versions), with comparisons to (i) a per-connective majority baseline and (ii) the SVM feature-based system of Hidey and McKeown (2016). The neural models outperform both baselines, notably with +2.13 F1 and +7.72 accuracy on the non-bootstrapping set, and modest F1 gains (≈0.6–1.1) on the bootstrapping set. A small qualitative analysis illustrates cases where the model handles ambiguous markers or non-causative uses of causative verbs.

Main contributions (as I see them)
1) Empirical finding: A simple neural pair-encoding (LSTM + MLP) using only word embeddings can outperform a strong feature-based SVM on causal relation disambiguation in AltLex, including for ambiguous/shifted markers.
2) Task framing and dataset use: A clear decomposition of causality classification into (i) causal meaning disambiguation and (ii) argument identification, and a focused evaluation protocol on the AltLex corpus in both construction regimes, including some analysis of class imbalance and label shift.
3) Design exploration: A (limited) comparison of pair architectures (with and without state transfer), optimizers, and aggregation/padding strategies, with observations that independent encodings with Adam tend to balance precision/recall better.

Strengths
- Relevance and problem setup: Causal disambiguation is important for discourse understanding and downstream tasks. The paper makes a reasonable case that rigid syntactic or lexicon-based approaches have coverage limits and that contextual encoding is beneficial.
- Consistent improvements over baselines: The proposed models outperform both the connective-majority baseline and the prior SVM system. The gains on the non-bootstrapping set are sizable in accuracy and F1; on bootstrapping they are smaller but consistent across several configurations.
- Focus on ambiguous markers: The analysis includes cases where the connective distribution shifts from train to test (bootstrapping) and where markers like “which then” are highly ambiguous. The model’s success on such cases supports the contextual learning claim.
- Simplicity and resource independence: Using only pre-trained embeddings (no external lexical resources) improves generalization beyond curated lexicons, as argued by the authors and reflected in the results.
- Task framing: Separating causal meaning disambiguation from argument identification is a useful perspective and clarifies scope.

Weaknesses
- Limited novelty and insufficient methodological clarity: The modeling choices (two LSTMs + MLP) are fairly standard; the paper does not convincingly motivate architectural specifics, and some descriptions/equations are confusing or incorrect. For example, the “zero-padding strategies” section conflates padding with aggregation (max/mean/mode) and equations do not align dimensionally (e.g., vec/tanh with W∈R100×nd applied to x∈Rn×d).
- Incomplete experimental rigor: There is no mention of a development set, early stopping, or variance across random seeds. It is unclear if best systems are chosen on test. Only one dataset is used (AltLex), and no cross-corpus evaluation (e.g., Causal-TimeBank) is provided. Statistical significance is reported only for the reduced dataset experiment, not the main results.
- Baselines could be stronger: Beyond per-connective majority and the SVM from Hidey & McKeown, no simple neural/text baselines are included (e.g., average-of-embeddings + MLP; BiLSTM over the concatenated token sequence [e1; l; e2]; or a CNN baseline like Kruengkrai et al.). An ablation with the lexical marker alone (l-only) or removing l would reveal how much the model relies on the marker versus contextual spans.
- Data and preprocessing unclear: The paper assumes (e1, e2, l) are given; how event spans are extracted/segmented from AltLex is not fully specified. Some corpus statistics (Table formatting) are garbled, and the reported counts are hard to reconcile. Reproducibility details (hidden sizes, bi- vs uni-directional LSTM, dropout placement, regularization type, learning rate schedules) are missing. Code/data splits are not provided.
- Evaluation choices with imbalance: Reporting only class-specific P/R/F1 for Causal and overall accuracy, given heavy imbalance, can be misleading. Macro-F1 or AUROC would be appropriate. The downsampling experiment changes the test distribution and is of limited external validity; the main claims should be supported on the original distribution with proper metrics and significance testing.

Questions to authors
- What was your development protocol? How did you select hyperparameters and architectures without using the test set? How many runs per configuration, and what variance did you observe?
- Please clarify the “padding strategies”: Do you mean temporal pooling (max/mean/mode) over LSTM outputs, or something else? The equations in §3.3 seem dimensionally inconsistent; can you restate them precisely?
- Are the LSTMs uni- or bi-directional? What are their hidden sizes? Where is dropout applied? What regularization (e.g., L2) did you use?
- How exactly are e1 and e2 defined/extracted in AltLex? Do you include tokens outside those spans (e.g., sentence context)? How do you tokenize multi-word markers?
- Did you try ablations removing the marker l, or using l-only? This would help quantify the contribution of contextual spans versus the marker itself.
- Do you plan to release code and preprocessed splits to ensure reproducibility?

Additional comments
- Consider stronger and more diverse baselines (e.g., average-of-embeddings MLP, BiLSTM over full sequence, CNN, and a modern contextual encoder if allowed), thorough ablations, macro-averaged metrics, and significance tests on the main results.
- Clarify/correct equations and tables; ensure references are complete and formatted. Provide error analyses by marker type and by directionality (e1 before/after e2).

Overall, this is a timely application with promising empirical gains, but methodological clarity, experimental rigor, and baseline breadth need strengthening to meet ACL standards.
