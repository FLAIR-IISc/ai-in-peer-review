Summary and contributions
This paper tackles causal meaning disambiguation: given two spans annotated as events (e1, e2) and a lexical item susceptible of signaling causality (l), decide whether the relation is Causal or Non-Causal. The authors argue that causality is not tied to a fixed set of lexical/syntactic constructions and propose to encode the context of the two events with neural models rather than feature-engineering tied to lexical resources.

Contributions as I see them:
1) A simple two-input LSTM-based architecture (“Pair LSTM”) that encodes e1 and l+e2 and classifies causal meaning, with ablations showing that independently encoding the two arguments outperforms initializing the second LSTM with the first (“Stated Pair LSTM”).
2) An empirical evaluation on both the original and the bootstrapped versions of the AltLex corpus, showing consistent gains over (i) a per-connective majority baseline and (ii) the SVM feature-based system of Hidey and McKeown (2016), alongside limited qualitative analysis.
3) A small analysis of pooling/optimizer choices and a reduced, more balanced data setting indicating robustness to class imbalance, with a McNemar test for significance in that setting.

Strengths
- Focus on an important, under-resourced task: Disambiguating causal meaning beyond fixed connective lists is central to discourse parsing and IE. The paper motivates why reliance on specific causative verbs or syntactic templates is limiting and positions the task usefully as a first step in a broader pipeline (causal meaning classification followed by argument identification).

- Competitive empirical results on a relevant benchmark: On AltLex (both non-bootstrapped and bootstrapped versions), the proposed models outperform a strong per-connective majority baseline and the prior SVM feature-based system in overall accuracy and in most cases F1 for the Causal class (e.g., F1C 75.40 vs 73.76 and Acc 77.90 vs 71.86 on non-bootstrapped; F1C 81.96 vs 80.90 and Acc 80.68 vs 79.58 on bootstrapped). The bootstrapped set is arguably more challenging due to train/test connective distribution shifts; the method remains strong.

- Minimal reliance on lexical resources: Using only pretrained word embeddings (GloVe) avoids the coverage restrictions of WordNet/VerbNet/PropBank features used by prior work, and the qualitative examples suggest the model can resolve some genuinely ambiguous markers (e.g., “which then”) via context.

- Insightful architectural observation: The finding that encoding e1 and l+e2 independently (“Pair LSTM”) beats state-passing (“Stated Pair LSTM”) is plausible and informative for future designs, indicating the relationship may be better captured by later interaction layers than by initializing sequence states.

- Some robustness analysis: The downsampling experiment to reduce imbalance and the McNemar test provide early evidence that the improvements are not an artifact of skewed class distributions.

Weaknesses
- Limited novelty and narrow modeling exploration: Using LSTMs to encode context for sentence-level relation classification is standard. The architectural variants are modest (state-passing vs independent encoders; simple pooling choices). Missing are stronger baselines such as (i) a single BiLSTM over the full sentence, (ii) attention over the two spans, (iii) a simple averaged-embeddings + classifier baseline, or (iv) contextual encoders (even pre-BERT options like context2vec). Without these, it is hard to attribute the gains to the specific pairwise design rather than to moving from feature-based SVMs to any reasonable neural model.

- Clarity and correctness issues in the model description: The “zero-padding strategies” are described via equations that instead look like temporal pooling (max/mean/mode over time), not padding. Dimensions and operations are confusing: vec(x) is defined as IRn×d→IRnd, yet the next line applies tanh(W·x+b) with W∈IR100×nd, which implies flattening, while earlier text says outputs of encoding layers are vectorized then passed through a dense 100-dim layer. It is unclear whether LSTM outputs are last hidden states or sequences, where pooling is applied, and how exactly padding interacts with pooling. These ambiguities hinder reproducibility and assessment of what design choices matter.

- Dataset and task setup constraints: The model assumes gold segmentation of (e1, l, e2). The paper does not explain how these spans are obtained from AltLex (are they provided, or extracted heuristically?). Since real applications require detecting both arguments and the signal, evaluating only on pre-segmented spans risks overstating practical impact. Moreover, evaluation is restricted to AltLex; Causal-TimeBank, albeit small, could at least be used for limited cross-dataset testing.

- Insufficient ablations and analysis: Key ablations are missing: (i) l+e2 only vs e1 only vs full sentence; (ii) removing l to test over-reliance on the connective token; (iii) effect of replacing GloVe with random or freezing vs fine-tuning; (iv) per-connective performance, particularly on ambiguous markers. The claim that context drives gains would be much stronger with such evidence.

- Experimental reporting gaps: Important training details are missing (epochs, batch size, learning rate schedules, early stopping, dev split, variance across seeds). Only one configuration is tested in the reduced dataset, and significance is not reported for the main tables. Some references are incomplete/garbled, and figures/tables are difficult to follow, reducing clarity.

Questions to authors
1) Architecture/pooling: Please clarify the exact sequence of operations. Are LSTM outputs last hidden states or full sequences? Where are max/mean/mode applied? What do you mean by “zero-padding strategies” if you pool? What are the actual tensor shapes through the network?

2) Span identification: How are e1, l, and e2 obtained from AltLex? Are boundaries provided in the corpus? If heuristics are used, please detail them and report span extraction accuracy.

3) Baselines/ablations: Could you add (a) a BiLSTM classifier over the full sentence; (b) a simple averaged-embedding + logistic regression baseline; (c) e1-only, l+e2-only, and sentence-only variants; and (d) a version without the lexical marker tokens to quantify their influence?

4) Training details: Please report hyperparameters (hidden sizes, dropout placement, batch size, epochs, early stopping, embedding fine-tuning vs frozen), data splits, and average/variance over multiple seeds. Did you use a development set to select pooling and optimizer?

5) Generalization: Can you provide even a small-scale test on Causal-TimeBank or another dataset to gauge cross-corpus robustness? Alternatively, report per-connective results focusing on ambiguous markers and on those with train–test label shifts in the bootstrapped set.

6) Rationale for concatenating l with e2 only: Why not symmetric modeling (e.g., include l with both events, or encode the full sequence with marked spans)? Did you try attention across the two encodings?

Overall, this paper addresses a relevant problem and shows solid empirical gains with a simple neural approach, but the novelty is limited and several clarity and evaluation gaps need to be addressed for a strong ACL paper.
