Summary and main contributions
- The paper proposes a neural approach to disambiguate the causal meaning of lexical markers from context, framing “causality classification” as two subtasks and focusing on the first: causal meaning classification. The model encodes two inputs—(e1) and (l + e2)—with LSTMs (either independent “Pair LSTM” or “Stated Pair LSTM” where the second LSTM is initialized with the first’s state), followed by dense layers. It uses GloVe embeddings, explores padding strategies (max/mean/mode), and compares optimizers (Adam/Adadelta).
- Empirical results on the AltLex corpus show consistent improvements over two baselines: a majority-per-connective baseline and Hidey & McKeown (2016)’s SVM with resource-based features. The best model improves F1 on the causal class and overall accuracy in both the non-bootstrapping and bootstrapping splits, with larger gains in the former (e.g., +2.13 F1 and +7.72 accuracy).
- Analysis suggests that independently encoding the two arguments outperforms the “stated” variant, and that the model can correctly handle some ambiguous markers (e.g., “which then”), supporting the claim that context encoding is beneficial.

Strengths
- Conceptual direction: resource-agnostic, context-driven causal disambiguation. The paper argues (and shows) that relying on distributional context alone can outperform resource-heavy feature engineering. This is a clean, general direction that scales to diverse lexicalizations and aligns with the AltLex motivation.
- Empirical improvements on a difficult dataset. The model consistently beats both baselines across corpus variants. In the non-bootstrapping split it improves F1 for the causal class and accuracy notably; in the harder bootstrapping split, it still edges out the SVM. The additional experiment on a rebalanced dataset and McNemar’s test provide some statistical backing.
- Useful architectural finding. The empirical result that separate encoders (“Pair LSTM”) beat the “Stated Pair LSTM” is a practical insight for relation encoding between two textual spans; likewise, optimizer/padding choices are documented with measurable effects.
- Focused, task-centric framing. The decomposition into causal meaning classification vs. argument identification is sensible and clarifies scope; the paper stays disciplined about that scope.

Weaknesses
- Limited novelty. Using LSTMs over span pairs with dense layers is a standard recipe; the “stated” initialization variant is minor, and the padding/optimizer sweeps are routine. There is no new model ingredient, training objective, or learning signal beyond standard supervised classification; as such, the contribution feels incremental rather than conceptually bold.
- Evaluation scope and baselines are dated/limited. The only dataset is AltLex; no cross-corpus or out-of-domain evaluation is provided. Stronger modern baselines (e.g., contextual encoders over the same inputs, sentence-level BiLSTMs with attention, or pretrained transformers fine-tuned on spans) are absent. Given the task’s nature, a transformer baseline could be decisive and would better assess the paper’s central claim about context encoding.
- Reproducibility and detail gaps. Important training details are missing or scattered: sequence truncation/maximum lengths, tokenization specifics, batch size, epochs/early stopping, random seeds, exact network sizes beyond the 100-dense projection, optimizer settings, and how class imbalance is handled during training (beyond the ad-hoc downsampling case). Code availability is not mentioned.
- Potential confounds not controlled. It is unclear how much the model relies on the lexical marker itself versus broader context. A marker-only baseline (and an events-only baseline) would help isolate the contribution of context encoding. Similarly, per-connective performance and robustness on truly ambiguous markers are not systematically reported; error analysis is light.
- Clarity and presentation issues. Several tables/figures are referenced but not cleanly presented; there are typos, inconsistent notation, and bibliographic formatting problems. Some claims (e.g., “context2vec supports LSTM for context”) are not tied to ablations here. The discussion around temporal/counterfactual constraints reads more theoretical than necessary for the experimental core.

Questions to authors
- Inputs and spans: Do you encode only the annotated event spans (e1 and e2) plus the explicit marker l, or the full sentences? How are event spans tokenized and ordered if they are discontinuous? Are encoders unidirectional or bidirectional?
- Training details: What are the maximum sequence lengths, batch size, number of epochs, early stopping criteria, learning rates/betas (for Adam), gradient clipping, and random seed strategy? Any class weighting used beyond the downsampled setup?
- Baselines: How would a simple BiLSTM or transformer (e.g., fine-tuned BERT/roberta) over “[CLS] e1 [SEP] l + e2” compare? Can you provide marker-only and events-only ablations to quantify the contribution of context?
- Generalization: Did you attempt cross-split evaluations (train on non-bootstrapping, test on bootstrapping and vice versa), or any transfer to Causal-TimeBank subsets? Any per-connective breakdowns, especially for ambiguous markers (since/after/which then)?
- Significance: Beyond McNemar on the downsampled setting, did you run significance tests (e.g., bootstrap resampling) on the main results?

Overall recommendation
While the empirical improvements are consistent and the resource-agnostic stance is appealing, the modeling is standard and the evaluation is narrow. Given ACL’s bar and the need for a more novel or ambitious contribution (e.g., stronger analysis, broader validation, or a new modeling idea), I lean toward rejection. I would be more positive with modern strong baselines/ablations, clearer reproducibility, and broader evaluation to substantiate the central claim about context-driven causal disambiguation.
