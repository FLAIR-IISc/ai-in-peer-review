Summary and contributions
This paper addresses causal meaning disambiguation for lexical markers by training an LSTM-based classifier that encodes two inputs: the first event (e1) and the concatenation of the marker with the second event (l + e2). The model is evaluated on the AltLex corpus (both non-bootstrapping and bootstrapping splits) against (i) a majority-per-connective baseline and (ii) the feature-based SVM of Hidey and McKeown (2016). Reported gains are modest but consistent in several settings.

As I see them, the main contributions are:
1) An empirical study showing that a simple pairwise LSTM encoder, fed only with pre-trained word embeddings, can match or slightly outperform a strong feature-based baseline on AltLex causal disambiguation.
2) A set of comparisons across two corpus variants, two model variants (paired vs “stated” LSTMs), padding strategies, and optimizers, with limited qualitative analysis of ambiguous markers.
3) A small experiment on a downsampled version of AltLex to probe class imbalance effects.

Strengths
- Problem focus and dataset choice: The paper tackles an important subtask (causal meaning disambiguation) and uses the most relevant available resource (AltLex), including both the original and bootstrapped splits. The task framing (separating causal meaning vs. argument identification) is sensible.
- Lightweight model that does not depend on handcrafted resources: The approach avoids reliance on WordNet/VerbNet/PropBank features, showing that distributional representations alone can be competitive.
- Empirical gains over a strong prior system: On non-bootstrapping AltLex, the best LSTM configuration beats Hidey and McKeown (2016) in F1 (Causal) and accuracy; on bootstrapping AltLex, several configurations are on par or slightly better. Precision is notably improved in multiple settings.
- Some analysis of ambiguity: The paper presents a few informative examples where the neural model correctly classifies instances that fool the feature-based baseline, including cases with ambiguous markers and causative verbs used non-causally.
- Attention to class imbalance: The downsampling experiment and the McNemar test (albeit limited) show awareness of evaluation pitfalls when classes are skewed.

Weaknesses
- Limited novelty and unclear modeling choices: The architectures are standard LSTM encoders with minor variants; the “stated” LSTM does not help empirically, undercutting the claimed modeling intuition. The “padding” strategy (max/mean/mode over sequence lengths) is nonstandard and underspecified; typical RNN practice uses masking with padding to the max length, not truncating to mean/mode. The paper needs to clarify this and justify why truncation would not harm.
- Missing strong, modern baselines: No comparison with simple yet competitive baselines (bag-of-words/logistic regression; averaged embeddings + MLP), nor with more established neural baselines (BiLSTM + attention), nor with contextual encoders (e.g., ELMo/BERT/RoBERTa) that are now standard for sentence/discourse classification. Given the task and dataset size, a fine-tuned transformer is a necessary baseline; without it, the empirical contribution is weak.
- Evaluation limited to one corpus and one task variant: Results are only on AltLex. There is no cross-corpus evaluation (e.g., PDTB causal explicit/implicit subsets, Causal-TimeBank) to demonstrate generalization beyond the construction used to build AltLex. The paper repeatedly argues for context-driven causality, but tests only within one distribution.
- Possible confounding from connective identity: Since the input includes the lexical marker and B1 performs strongly, the model may largely learn connective identity and local lexical patterns. No control experiments remove or anonymize the marker, stratify by unseen markers, or evaluate per-connective performance. The bootstrapping split partially addresses this but is insufficient to conclude that the model learns “context” rather than connective priors.
- Methodological and reporting gaps: 
  - No clear description of development set usage, early stopping, number of epochs, batch size, hidden sizes, dropout placement, whether embeddings are fine-tuned, seed variability, or variance over runs. 
  - Inconsistent and limited metrics: reporting only Causal-class P/R/F1 plus accuracy, without macro/micro F1 or per-connective F1. Statistical significance is only reported for one reduced setting; small gains elsewhere may be within noise.
  - The textual presentation has formatting issues (tables incomplete or malformed; typos; inconsistent decimals), and several references are incomplete.
- Reproducibility: No code or detailed configuration is provided. Given the small differences vs. the SVM baseline, reproducibility is important to substantiate the claims.

Questions to authors
- Data splits and tuning: What was your development set? How were hyperparameters selected? How many runs per configuration, and what is variance across seeds? Did any tuning use the test set?
- Padding/masking: Please precisely define the “max/mean/mode” padding strategy. Are sequences truncated to the mean/mode length? Are true sequence lengths masked in the LSTM? If not, how do you mitigate information loss from truncation?
- Embeddings: Did you fine-tune GloVe during training? How are OOV tokens handled?
- Control for connective identity: Can you report results when the marker is removed or anonymized, and when testing on markers unseen in training? Please add per-connective performance and a split that prevents connective leakage.
- Stronger baselines: Can you include (i) bag-of-ngrams + logreg/SVM, (ii) average-of-embeddings + MLP, (iii) BiLSTM+attention, and crucially (iv) a fine-tuned transformer (e.g., BERT-base) on the same inputs? These are necessary to contextualize the claimed gains.
- Generalization: Can you evaluate on additional datasets (e.g., PDTB causal relations, Causal-TimeBank) and/or cross-corpus transfer?
- Metrics and significance: Please provide macro-F1, per-class F1, and significance tests (e.g., bootstrap or McNemar) for the main reported improvements on the original (not reduced) datasets.
- Implementation details and release: Please provide full training details (epochs, batch sizes, hidden sizes, dropout, regularization coefficients, optimizer settings) and release code to enable replication.

Overall, while the task is worthwhile and the reported numbers are competitive with a prior feature-based system, the modeling novelty is limited and the evaluation is not sufficiently rigorous against established baselines or across datasets. Strengthening the baselines, controls, and reporting would be necessary for a convincing contribution.
