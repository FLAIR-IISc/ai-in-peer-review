Summary and contributions
- This paper tackles disambiguation of causal meaning for potentially ambiguous lexical markers by modeling the relation between two events and the marker with LSTM-based neural architectures. The authors evaluate on the AltLex corpus and compare to a connective-majority baseline and the SVM feature-based system of Hidey and McKeown (2016).
- Main contributions as seen here:
  1) A simple two-input LSTM architecture (“Pair LSTM” and a “Stated Pair LSTM” variant) for causal relation disambiguation that operates over event spans and a candidate marker, with modest improvements over a strong feature-based baseline on AltLex.
  2) An empirical study contrasting padding/truncation strategies and optimizers, and limited qualitative analysis of ambiguous markers, suggesting that contextual modeling beyond lexical resources helps.
  3) A critical reframing of the task as “causal meaning classification” separate from argument direction detection, aligning with PDTB-style practice.

Strengths
- Focused, relevant task: The paper addresses an important subproblem—disambiguating causal vs non-causal uses of markers like since/after—where coverage gaps and ambiguity hurt downstream discourse parsing.
- Simple, effective model: The two-input sequence-pair formulation is sensible for this problem class, and the Pair LSTM yields consistent improvements over the connective-majority baseline and, in several settings, over the Hidey & McKeown SVM. The best non-bootstrapping configuration improves causal F1 and overall accuracy meaningfully.
- Contextual argument: The qualitative examples where the model gets cases wrong for a causative verb (break) and right for “which then” provide some evidence that modeling context, not only lexical priors, improves disambiguation.
- Ablations on training recipes: The comparison of Adadelta vs Adam and exploration of padding length choices, while limited, show some care about optimization behavior and precision/recall trade-offs.
- Task framing: Separating causal meaning disambiguation from argument direction identification is a pragmatic decomposition that could support broader coverage and future modular systems.

Weaknesses
- Limited novelty and missing baselines: The architecture is a standard sentence-pair LSTM without attention or interaction, and the “Stated Pair LSTM” is a minor variant of existing sequence-pair encoders used in NLI and relation classification. Crucially, the paper lacks comparisons to strong modern baselines (e.g., a BiLSTM with attention, CNN, or—most importantly—transformer-based encoders like BERT/roberta fine-tuned on Arg1 [SEP] connective [SEP] Arg2). The claim that LSTMs are better than CNNs for this task is argued but not empirically validated.
- Dataset reliance and generalization: All results are confined to AltLex. There is no evaluation on PDTB explicit causal relations, Causal-TimeBank, or cross-dataset transfer to test robustness. The bootstrapping vs non-bootstrapping discussion is interesting, but broader generalization remains unclear.
- Experimental design and reporting gaps:
  - No development set details, early stopping criteria, or variance across random seeds; best configurations appear selected ex post from multiple runs/choices, risking overfitting.
  - Hyperparameters (hidden sizes, number of layers, weight sharing across the two LSTMs, bidirectionality, whether embeddings are fine-tuned) are not fully specified.
  - The “zero-padding strategies” (max/mean/mode of sequence lengths) suggest truncation to the mean/mode, but the paper does not quantify truncation rates or loss of information; this is an unusual and potentially harmful design.
  - Only a single McNemar test is reported on a downsampled setting; other improvements lack significance testing.
- Potential leakage and insufficient analysis of ambiguity:
  - The input concatenates the marker with e2, which may incentivize the model to rely heavily on marker identity. The paper does not quantify performance on truly ambiguous markers separately from unambiguous ones; Figure 2 is suggestive but not a rigorous breakdown.
  - No ablation removing the marker token(s) or replacing it with a placeholder to test reliance on arguments’ context.
  - No per-connector or per-ambiguity-bin evaluation; no calibration or error typology beyond a handful of examples.
- Presentation and reproducibility: Several tables have inconsistencies, missing numbers, and formatting errors; references are incomplete. Code and preprocessing details are not provided, limiting reproducibility. The description of how e1/e2 spans are obtained from AltLex is underspecified.

Questions to authors
- Architecture details:
  - Are the two LSTMs weight-shared or independent? Are they bidirectional? What is the hidden size? Is there any attention or cross-interaction beyond concatenation? Are embeddings fine-tuned?
  - In the “Stated Pair LSTM,” how exactly is the state passed (final hidden only, cell state too), and does it improve with biLSTM?
- Input representation:
  - How are e1 and e2 spans derived from AltLex? Are they contiguous text spans or heads plus arguments? Are there cases where events are not explicitly marked or are multi-sentence?
  - Why is the marker concatenated to e2 and not modeled symmetrically or as a separate input? Have you tried masking/replacing the marker to test reliance on argument context?
- Padding/truncation:
  - Please clarify the “max/mean/mode” padding strategy. Do mean/mode imply truncation of longer sequences? What proportion of tokens are truncated per configuration?
- Evaluation and analysis:
  - Provide a per-marker breakdown (especially for ambiguous markers) and report performance grouped by ambiguity. How does the model perform when the marker’s majority label in training conflicts with the test label?
  - Did you use a development set for model selection? Report mean and standard deviation over multiple random seeds and significance tests for main improvements.
  - Can you evaluate on PDTB explicit causal relations and/or Causal-TimeBank to test cross-corpus generalization? Alternatively, train on one AltLex version and test on the other without overlap.
- Baselines:
  - Please include a transformer baseline (e.g., BERT-base/roberta-base) fine-tuned on Arg1 [SEP] marker [SEP] Arg2, and a simple BoW/logistic regression baseline. Also consider a BiLSTM with attention over concatenated Arg1 [SEP] marker [SEP] Arg2.
- Class imbalance:
  - Instead of downsampling, did you try class-weighted loss or focal loss? How sensitive are results to the class distribution?

Overall, the paper targets a worthwhile problem and shows that simple sequence-pair modeling helps. However, the methodological novelty is limited and the experimental support is insufficient relative to contemporary baselines and analysis expectations. Strengthening baselines, clarifying methodology, adding per-connector analyses and cross-corpus tests, and improving reporting would substantially improve the submission.
