Summary and claimed contributions
- The paper tackles causal meaning disambiguation: given two event spans (e1, e2) and a candidate lexical marker l, predict whether the relation is causal or not. The task is framed as the first step of a two-step pipeline (disambiguation, then argument identification).
- The authors propose a simple LSTM-based architecture with two inputs: one encodes e1; the other encodes the concatenation of l and e2. They compare two variants: (i) Pair LSTM (independent encodings concatenated), and (ii) Stated Pair LSTM (the second LSTM initialized from the first).
- They evaluate on the AltLex corpus (Hidey & McKeown, 2016), in both “non-bootstrapping” and “bootstrapping” versions, and compare to two baselines: a connective-majority baseline and the SVM feature-based system from Hidey & McKeown (2016). They report gains in F1 (Causal class) and accuracy in several settings and offer some qualitative analysis and an experiment with reduced class imbalance.

My view of the contributions (in order of strength)
1) An empirical result: a straightforward LSTM-based context encoding can outperform a feature-rich SVM baseline on AltLex for causal meaning disambiguation, with modest but consistent gains in several settings.
2) An engineering comparison of two pair-LSTM variants, simple sequence-length choices for padding, and optimizer choices (Adam vs Adadelta), with an analysis of the bootstrapping vs non-bootstrapping AltLex splits.
3) A small qualitative analysis indicating the approach can handle some ambiguous markers and non-causative uses of causative verbs.

Strengths
- Empirical improvements over a reasonable prior baseline: On the non-bootstrapping AltLex split, the best Pair LSTM configuration improves Causal F1 and overall accuracy over Hidey & McKeown (2016), and on the bootstrapping split it achieves competitive or slightly better F1 with better precision. This supports the claim that context encoding with pre-trained embeddings helps disambiguate causal meaning.
- Sensible problem decomposition and focus: The paper isolates the disambiguation subtask, avoiding conflation with argument identification. Using event spans and a candidate marker mirrors how this subtask might be used downstream.
- Attention to dataset characteristics: The paper examines class imbalance, marker ambiguity, and the differences between bootstrapping and non-bootstrapping splits, and provides a reduced-imbalance experiment plus some statistical testing (McNemar vs B1).
- Qualitative examples: The case studies where the model correctly classifies instances that the SVM misclassifies (e.g., ambiguous “which then”, non-causal “break”) help readers understand where context encoding adds value.

Weaknesses
- Limited novelty in modeling: The core method is a standard LSTM encoder over spans with concatenation and MLP classification. There is no attention mechanism, no explicit cross-span interaction beyond concatenation, no contrastive or multi-task training, and no use of more recent contextual encoders. The “Stated Pair LSTM” variant (state initialization) is a minor tweak and underperforms. As a result, the conceptual contribution is modest.
- Model description clarity and reproducibility: Key details are missing or unclear. Examples: whether LSTMs are bi/unidirectional; hidden sizes; whether the two LSTMs share parameters; how sequence representations are derived (last hidden state vs pooling); padding “strategies” are described using max/mean/mode in a way that is mathematically unclear; training regimen (epochs, batch size, early stopping) is not specified. Without these, reproducibility is hard.
- Evaluation scope is narrow and may overstate generality: All experiments are on AltLex; no transfer to PDTB explicit/implicit causals or Causal-TimeBank (even as out-of-domain test). The model assumes gold event spans and a candidate lexical marker, which is a strong input assumption that limits practical applicability. No ablations are presented to test reliance on l vs e1/e2 vs full sentence context.
- Baselines are insufficient: In addition to the SVM, natural baselines such as (i) a connective-only classifier, (ii) simple bag-of-embeddings or averaged GloVe over the provided spans, (iii) a sentence-level BiLSTM/attention over full context, or (iv) a CNN baseline should be reported. Given that gains over the SVM are modest on the bootstrapping split (~+1 F1), stronger neural baselines would clarify the real advantage of the proposed design.
- Statistical testing and model selection: Statistical significance is only reported vs the majority baseline, and only on the reduced dataset; no significance testing vs the SVM on the main splits. Moreover, it is not clear how hyperparameters and the “best configuration” were chosen (is there a dev set, or was selection done on the test set?). This raises concerns about overfitting through configuration selection.
- Writing and presentation issues: Several passages are hard to parse, with grammatical issues and notation mistakes (e.g., padding formula), misformatted tables (some counts missing or garbled), and incomplete references. This affects clarity and confidence in the methodological details.

Questions to authors
- Data splits and model selection: What exact train/dev/test splits were used for both AltLex versions? How were configurations selected (dev performance or test)? Please report dev-set performance and specify if any test-set tuning occurred.
- Architecture specifics: Are the LSTMs bidirectional? Do the two LSTMs share weights? What is the hidden size and dropout inside the LSTMs/MLP? How are sequences reduced to fixed-size vectors (last state, max/mean pooling, attention)?
- Padding “strategies”: Please clarify precisely what “max/mean/mode” means in your padding procedure. Is this choosing the padded length per batch, or pooling the sequence, or something else?
- Baselines and ablations: Can you add (i) a connective-only and a bag-of-embeddings baseline, (ii) a simple BiLSTM over the full sentence, and (iii) ablations removing l or using l only? Also report per-marker results, especially on ambiguous markers, to substantiate the “context over marker” claim.
- Generalization beyond AltLex: Can you test on PDTB causal connectives (explicit/implicit) or Causal-TimeBank as an out-of-domain evaluation? Even a small-scale test would help assess robustness.
- Practicality: Since your model assumes gold event spans and marker, how do you envision integrating argument identification? Do you expect similar gains when the inputs come from an automatic argument extractor?

Overall recommendation
- Borderline reject. The empirical results are promising and the problem is important, but the modeling novelty is limited, the experimental scope and baselines are insufficient, and several methodological details are unclear. With stronger baselines and ablations, clearer descriptions, additional out-of-domain tests, and solid significance analysis, this work could be much more compelling.
