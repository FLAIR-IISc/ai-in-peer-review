Summary
This paper tackles causal meaning disambiguation: given two “events” (e1, e2) and a lexical marker l suspected to signal a relation, predict whether the relation is causal or not. The authors argue that causality is not tightly tied to fixed syntactic constructions and propose LSTM-based encoders to represent the two arguments and disambiguate the relation. They evaluate on the AltLex corpus (Hidey & McKeown, 2016), comparing against a strong “most frequent class per marker” baseline and the feature-rich SVM system of Hidey & McKeown. Their “Pair LSTM” (independent encoders for e1 and l+e2, followed by dense layers) achieves modest but consistent improvements over prior work on both the original and bootstrapped AltLex splits, with some analysis on ambiguous markers and a reduced-class-imbalance setting.

Main contributions (as I see them)
1) Empirical finding: A simple two-encoder LSTM architecture that encodes e1 and l+e2 with pre-trained word embeddings yields modest but consistent gains over the feature-based SVM baseline on AltLex, particularly in precision, with a better precision–recall balance.
2) Problem framing: An explicit argument that causal relation detection should be decomposed into (i) causal vs. non-causal meaning classification and (ii) causal argument identification, and that meaning disambiguation benefits from encoding both argument spans rather than relying on fixed syntactic patterns or lexical resources.
3) Analysis: Initial qualitative examples suggesting the model can correctly disambiguate notoriously ambiguous expressions (e.g., which then) and non-causal uses of classically causative verbs (break).

Strengths
- Relevance and focus: The paper addresses an important and persistent issue in discourse parsing—coverage gaps and ambiguity of causal markers—and highlights limitations of relying on fixed lists of connectives or causative verbs. The proposed framing is sensible and potentially useful for downstream discourse parsing pipelines.
- Competitive results with a simple model: On AltLex, the “Pair LSTM” yields small but consistent improvements over Hidey & McKeown’s SVM baseline (e.g., +2–3 F1 on the non-bootstrapping split; similar gains on bootstrapping) and clearly surpasses the strong per-marker majority baseline, indicating the model learns beyond marker priors.
- Reduced reliance on lexical resources: The model uses only pre-trained embeddings, avoiding brittle external resources. This is valuable given the long tail of AltLex expressions and for generalization to unseen markers.
- Some interpretive analysis: The paper includes example-level analysis highlighting cases where the LSTM succeeds despite misleading lexical priors (e.g., which then, non-causal break), supporting the claim that contextual encoding helps.
- Discussion of data issues: The paper acknowledges class imbalance and distribution shift between AltLex splits and reports a reduced-imbalance experiment, with significance via McNemar’s test on that subset.

Weaknesses
- Limited novelty and unclear modeling choices: The architectures are standard BiLSTM encoders with dense layers; the “Stated Pair LSTM” vs. “Pair LSTM” distinction is minor. More importantly, the padding/length strategy is confusing and potentially flawed: using mean/mode/max “padding length” as defined in Eq. (1) is not well-defined, suggests truncation of many sequences, and lacks justification. Key hyperparameters (hidden sizes, number of layers, dropout placement, batch size, epochs, early stopping) are missing.
- Narrow experimental scope and weak baselines: Evaluation is confined to AltLex. Causal-TimeBank is dismissed as “too small,” but even a small-scale or transfer experiment would strengthen generality claims. No ablations (e.g., remove l, remove e1, average-embeddings baseline), no CNN baseline despite motivating discussion, and no stronger sequence models (BiLSTM+attention) are compared. Given current standards, contextual encoders (ELMo/BERT) or at least a robust sentence encoder baseline should be considered.
- Insufficient control analyses: To substantiate the “context over marker” claim, results should be broken down by connective ambiguity (e.g., performance on ambiguous markers only, or on test cases whose marker label distribution differs from training). The paper narratively mentions some cases but lacks a systematic breakdown or significance tests on the main splits.
- Metrics and class imbalance: Reporting focuses on F1 for the Causal class and accuracy. Macro-F1 or AUROC would be more robust under imbalance; cost-sensitive training or calibrated thresholds are not explored. The significance test is reported only on the reduced subset, not on the main settings where improvements are modest.
- Clarity, presentation, and references: There are numerous typos, grammatical errors, and inconsistencies. Some tables/figures and counts are garbled (e.g., Table 3/6 numbers), equations are imprecise, and references are incomplete or inconsistently formatted. The data preprocessing pipeline (how e1/e2/l are extracted, tokenization, handling of unknowns) is under-specified, hurting reproducibility. No code is provided.

Questions to authors
- Padding/length policy: Please precisely define how sequence lengths are chosen and how padding/truncation is applied for e1 and l+e2. What fraction of instances are truncated under “mean” and “mode” lengths?
- Model details: What are the LSTM hidden sizes, number of layers (uni- or BiLSTM?), dropout locations/values, optimizer hyperparameters, batch sizes, training epochs, and early stopping criteria? How was hyperparameter tuning performed (dev set?) and are results averaged over multiple runs with different seeds?
- Inputs: Why concatenate l with e2 rather than include l as a separate input or encode the full sentence? Did you experiment with excluding l to test whether the model truly captures contextual causality?
- Control experiments: Can you report performance restricted to ambiguous markers and to test instances where the marker’s majority class in training disagrees with the gold label (you mention some in Section 4.3)? Also, provide macro-F1 and statistical significance on the main test sets.
- Baselines: Can you add a strong neural baseline such as (i) averaged GloVe + logistic regression, (ii) BiLSTM with attention over the concatenated full span, and (iii) a simple pretrained transformer encoder (fine-tuned) to contextualize your improvements?

Overall, this paper addresses a meaningful problem and shows that encoding both arguments with a simple LSTM yields small but consistent gains on AltLex. However, methodological novelty is limited, clarity is lacking, and the experimental evaluation is too narrow with insufficient ablations and controls. Strengthening baselines, clarifying the model, and adding focused analyses on ambiguity would substantially improve the paper.
