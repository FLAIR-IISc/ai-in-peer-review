Summary
This paper tackles causal meaning disambiguation: given two event spans e1 and e2 and a lexical item l that may signal causality, decide whether the relation is causal. The authors argue for separating causal meaning classification from causal argument identification, and propose LSTM-based architectures that encode the two inputs (e1 and l+e2) using pretrained GloVe embeddings and dense layers. They evaluate on the AltLex corpus (Hidey & McKeown, 2016), in both “non-bootstrapping” and “bootstrapping” splits, and compare against (i) a per-connective majority baseline and (ii) the feature-rich SVM of Hidey & McKeown. The proposed models generally improve F1 for the Causal class and overall accuracy, with the best reported gains of +2.1 F1 and +7.7 accuracy in one setting; they also present a small qualitative analysis and a downsampling experiment to examine class imbalance.

Contributions (as I see them)
1) Empirical finding: A simple two-branch LSTM that encodes event contexts using only pretrained word embeddings can outperform a strong, feature-engineered SVM on AltLex causal disambiguation, across two corpus versions.
2) Task framing: A clean articulation of causal meaning disambiguation as distinct from argument identification, with an input format that uses event spans and a (possibly ambiguous) marker.
3) Small empirical insights: comparative analysis of two LSTM variants (with/without state transfer), optimizer choices (Adam vs Adadelta), and simple sequence aggregation strategies, with observations about precision–recall trade-offs.

Strengths
- Clear, focused task definition and motivation: The paper persuasively argues that causality should not be tied to fixed lexical/syntactic patterns and motivates context-driven modeling. The separation of causal meaning vs argument identification is reasonable and useful.
- Competitive empirical results on a non-trivial corpus: On AltLex, the models generally surpass both a strong per-connective baseline and the SVM system. The improvements are consistent across corpus variants, with sizable accuracy gains in the “non-bootstrapping” setting and modest but consistent F1 improvements in the “bootstrapping” setting.
- Sensible architectural choices and ablations: Comparing a “stated” pair LSTM vs independent encodings is informative; the finding that independent encodings fare better is useful for the community. The optimizer study (Adam vs Adadelta) and the precision–recall trade-off discussion show careful attention to model behavior.
- Qualitative analysis: The examples where the neural model succeeds and the SVM fails (e.g., ambiguous connectives such as “which then,” and non-causal uses of causative verbs like “break”) illustrate the benefit of contextual encoding over hand-crafted lexical resources.

Weaknesses
- Limited novelty and unclear technical description: Using LSTMs for sentence-/span-pair classification is standard. The “zero-padding strategies” described as max/mean/mode are in fact aggregation/pooling operations, and the notation and equations are inconsistent (e.g., vec(x) mapping to R^{nd} then immediately using a 100-d dense layer). The paper needs to clarify what is padded, what is pooled, and where LSTM outputs (last state vs pooled states) are used. As written, the architecture is underspecified and partially self-contradictory.
- Incomplete baselines and missing ablations: There is no comparison to simpler neural baselines (e.g., average-of-embeddings + MLP, single BiLSTM over concatenated input, or attention between e1 and e2), nor to stronger encoders (e.g., contextual transformers), even as a discussion. Ablations removing the lexical marker, or testing e1-only / e2-only inputs, would directly measure where the gains come from and whether the model over-relies on l. The reduced-data experiment drops B2 entirely, limiting interpretability.
- Data and reporting inconsistencies: Several tables contain errors/typos and inconsistent counts (e.g., Table 3/4 totals, “,606” instead of a number). The class distributions and split sizes are unclear, and it is not stated precisely how train/dev/test are defined. The paper also mixes explicit/implicit examples without clearly stating which subsets of AltLex are used and how event spans and l are extracted for each case.
- Reproducibility and significance: No code or data processing details are released; many hyperparameters (dropout, regularization) are listed but not tied to specific runs. Statistical significance is only reported for the downsampling experiment and only vs the weakest baseline; the main results lack significance testing vs B2. Training details (epochs, early stopping, selection criteria) are missing.
- Writing quality and presentation: There are many grammatical errors, formatting issues, and figure/table artifacts that make it hard to follow (e.g., the frequency figure text, equations, and references). This detracts from clarity and reproducibility.

Questions to Authors
1) Architecture clarity: Please specify precisely the sequence representation pipeline. Do you pad sequences to a fixed length, then feed to an LSTM, then pool the hidden states (max/mean/mode), or do you pool embeddings before the LSTM? What exactly are “pad(x)=max/mean/mode” operations applied to? What is the final vector used per sequence (last hidden state vs pooled states)?
2) Inputs and span extraction: How are e1, l, and e2 spans obtained from AltLex? Are event spans always explicitly annotated in AltLex, and how do you handle implicit relations? How often is l empty, and what is passed in that case?
3) Splits and counts: Please provide consistent train/dev/test sizes for both corpus versions, with causal/non-causal counts per split. Which split protocol from Hidey & McKeown did you follow?
4) Baselines and ablations: Can you report (a) an average-embeddings MLP baseline, (b) a single BiLSTM over [e1 SEP l+e2], and (c) ablations removing l, or using only e1 or only e2? These would solidify where your gains come from.
5) Significance and selection: How many runs per configuration did you perform, what variance did you observe, and how were hyperparameters selected? Please report statistical significance vs B2 on the main test sets.
6) Error analysis and generalization: Beyond the few examples, do you see systematic improvements on specific marker families or event types? How does the model perform on instances whose connective distribution differs between train/test (as noted for the bootstrapped corpus)?

Overall, this work presents a reasonable and useful empirical study with modest gains, but it lacks methodological clarity, stronger baselines, and clean reporting. Strengthening these aspects would substantially improve its impact.
