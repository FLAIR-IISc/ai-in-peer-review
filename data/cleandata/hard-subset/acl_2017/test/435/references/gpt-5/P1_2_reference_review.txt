Summary
This paper addresses causal meaning disambiguation: given two event spans (e1, e2) and a potentially causal lexical marker l, decide whether the relation is causal. The authors argue that causality is not tied to fixed lexical/syntactic constructions and propose an LSTM-based architecture that encodes e1 and (l + e2) and then classifies the relation via dense layers. They compare two variants: (i) Stated Pair LSTM (initialize LSTM2 with LSTM1’s final state) and (ii) Pair LSTM (independent encoders). They evaluate on the AltLex corpus (non-bootstrapping and bootstrapping versions) against two baselines: a majority-per-connective baseline and the feature-rich SVM of Hidey & McKeown (2016). The proposed models improve F1 for the Causal class and accuracy, and the authors provide a brief qualitative analysis.

Contributions (as I see them)
1) Empirical result: A simple LSTM-based encoder of event contexts outperforms a feature-based SVM on the AltLex corpus (notably larger gains on the non-bootstrapping set; small but consistent gains on the bootstrapping set).
2) Architectural comparison and analysis: Evidence that independently encoding the two arguments (Pair LSTM) is preferable to initializing the second encoder with the first (Stated Pair LSTM), plus optimizer and “padding” strategy comparisons, and an analysis of precision–recall trade-offs under class imbalance.
3) Qualitative evidence that contextual encoding helps with ambiguous markers (e.g., which then; break), beyond per-connective priors.

Strengths
- Clear empirical gains over a strong feature-based baseline: On AltLex (non-bootstrapping), the best configuration improves F1 for the Causal class from 73.76% to 75.40% and accuracy from 71.86% to 77.90%—a sizable accuracy gain. On bootstrapping, several configurations yield modest but consistent improvements over the SVM (e.g., F1 from 80.90% to ~81.9%), suggesting the approach generalizes to noisier instances.
- Resource-light and general approach: The method relies only on pre-trained word embeddings and avoids handcrafted features and external lexical resources, addressing the coverage limitations noted for feature-based systems. This is a practical advantage for scaling to broader causal expressions and domains.
- Sensible modeling choices validated by results: The finding that independent encoders (Pair LSTM) outperform state-sharing (Stated Pair LSTM) supports the hypothesis that tying the encodings can hurt when the relation is not tightly determined by compositional coupling. The optimizer comparison (Adam vs Adadelta) and class-imbalance experiments add useful practical guidance.
- Attention to class imbalance and error profile: Reporting precision/recall (not just accuracy) and running additional experiments on a downsampled set (with McNemar tests) help assess robustness, especially given the skew in AltLex.
- Qualitative analysis suggests genuine contextual disambiguation: The examples where the neural model succeeds and the SVM fails (e.g., which then, and non-causal senses of break) support the claim that contextual cues, not just marker priors, drive the improvements.

Weaknesses
- Limited novelty and missing neural baselines: Using LSTMs to encode spans and classify relations is a standard recipe, and similar ideas have been explored in discourse relation classification and event relation tasks. The paper under-cites and does not compare to simple, strong neural baselines (e.g., BiLSTM over the concatenated sequence with separators; average-of-embeddings + MLP; CNN/RCNN; attention-based pooling; Transformer encoders). The incremental novelty over prior neural approaches (including Kruengkrai et al.) is unclear.
- Methodological clarity issues and notational inconsistencies: The “zero-padding strategy” is described via pad(x) = {max, mean, mode}, which is not a conventional padding method and is mathematically unclear. Dimensions in Eq. (2) are inconsistent (W ∈ R100×nd but output is also described as length 100). The LSTM sizes, number of layers, hidden dimensions, dropout placements, regularization choices, batch size, epochs, early stopping, and selection criteria (dev set) are largely unspecified. These issues impede reproducibility and weaken the methodological soundness.
- Narrow evaluation: The approach is evaluated only on AltLex. While AltLex is relevant, it is automatically induced and noisy; the paper acknowledges data scarcity but does not attempt even limited cross-corpus validation (e.g., PDTB causal relations, Causal-TimeBank) or domain transfer to support broader claims. No analysis is provided by marker ambiguity level or by AltLex bootstrapped vs non-bootstrapped subsets beyond aggregate metrics.
- Weak ablations on modeling choices: The paper motivates concatenating l with e2 and claims the two-step view (meaning disambiguation vs argument identification), but provides no ablations to test the contribution of including l, e1, or their order. A baseline that encodes the full span (e1, l, e2) with a single encoder and a separator token might be competitive; similarly, removing l would test how much the model relies on the marker vs context.
- Reporting and presentation quality: Multiple tables are hard to follow or appear corrupted (e.g., Table 3/6 numbers misaligned), references are incomplete/inconsistent, and there are grammatical errors throughout. Statistical significance is only reported on the reduced dataset; no significance test is provided for the main results. These presentation issues reduce confidence in the work.

Questions to Authors
- Please clarify the “zero-padding strategies” (max/mean/mode). What exactly is being computed and applied? How are variable-length sequences handled in the LSTMs?
- What are the full architectural and training hyperparameters (LSTM hidden size, number of layers, dropout placements, L2 coefficients, batch size, epochs, optimizer settings, learning rate schedules)? How was model selection done (dev set, early stopping criteria)?
- Did you try a single encoder over the concatenated sequence [e1] [SEP] [l] [e2]? If so, how does it compare? If not, can you provide this baseline?
- What is the contribution of including the lexical marker explicitly? Ablate l from the second input and report the change; also try including l with e1 or as a separate input.
- Can you report per-marker results, grouped by ambiguity (e.g., markers that are balanced in train vs markers that are highly skewed), and performance on instances where train/test distributions differ (noted for the bootstrapping set)?
- Have you attempted evaluation on PDTB causal relations or Causal-TimeBank to assess cross-corpus generalization, even if sizes are small?

Overall, this paper presents a reasonable, resource-light neural baseline that improves over a feature-based system on AltLex, with useful practical insights. However, the methodological clarity, novelty, and evaluation breadth need strengthening. I encourage the authors to tighten the technical description, add stronger neural baselines and ablations, and broaden evaluation to better substantiate the claims.
