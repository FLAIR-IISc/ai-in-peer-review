Summary and claimed contributions
This paper addresses causal meaning disambiguation: given two event spans (e1, e2) and a potentially causal lexical marker (l), predict whether the relation is causal. The authors propose simple two-input LSTM architectures (“Pair LSTM” and “Stated Pair LSTM”) that encode e1 and l+e2 with pre-trained GloVe embeddings, followed by MLP layers. They evaluate on the AltLex corpus (Hidey & McKeown, 2016), both the original and bootstrapped versions, and compare against (i) a per-connective majority baseline and (ii) the Hidey & McKeown SVM feature-based system. They report consistent gains in F1 and accuracy, and provide a small error analysis.

Contributions (as I see them):
1) Empirical result: a simple span-level LSTM (with off-the-shelf embeddings and no external lexicons) outperforms a strong feature-based baseline for causal relation disambiguation on AltLex across two corpus variants.
2) Analysis indicating that encoding the event context helps beyond connective memorization, with examples involving ambiguous markers and a reduced-class-imbalance experiment with significance testing.
3) A task framing that separates causal meaning disambiguation from argument identification, with a practical architecture that encodes argument spans.

Strengths
- Consistent improvements over prior baseline on two datasets: The proposed models outperform both the per-connective majority baseline and the SVM system on the non-bootstrapped and bootstrapped AltLex settings. On non-bootstrapped data, the best model improves causal F1 by ~1.6 pts (75.4 vs 73.8) and accuracy by ~6 pts (77.9 vs 71.9). On bootstrapped data, gains are smaller but consistent (~+1.0 F1, +1.1 acc). This shows value over feature-heavy approaches without relying on lexical resources.
- Reasonable precision–recall balance: The analysis highlights trade-offs between Adam and Adadelta optimizers and across architectures, with the best configurations improving precision while maintaining recall, especially in the bootstrapped setting. This is important given the class imbalance and presence of ambiguous markers.
- Simple, resource-light approach: Using only pre-trained word embeddings and span encoders is attractive for portability and generalization beyond the coverage of hand-built lexicons (a known limitation in prior work).
- Some qualitative insight: Examples where the model succeeds and the SVM fails (e.g., “which then”, non-causative uses of “break”) support the claim that contextual encoding can overcome connective-level heuristics. The reduced imbalance experiment plus McNemar testing (albeit limited) is a nice touch.

Weaknesses
- Limited novelty and missing ablations: Using LSTMs over spans with an MLP classifier is standard. The paper lacks ablations to justify architectural choices (two-input vs single-input sentence encoder; effect of including the marker; LSTM vs average-of-embeddings; CNN baseline as in Kruengkrai et al., 2017). Without these, the central claim that context encoding (as instantiated here) is key remains under-supported beyond modest gains.
- Clarity and technical description issues: The “zero-padding strategies” (max/mean/mode) are unclear, and Eq. (1) appears incorrect for padding. Shapes in Eq. (2) and the “vectorization” step are confusing. Crucial details are missing: uni/bidirectional LSTMs, hidden sizes, layers, optimizer settings, batch size, epochs, early stopping, random seed control, validation regime, and how hyperparameters were selected. Reproducibility is weak.
- Task and data framing: It is unclear how e1/e2 spans are obtained in AltLex and at test time. Are spans gold from annotation or heuristically extracted? For implicit relations (AltLex), how is “l” defined and concatenated? The practicality of the setting (needing event spans and a marker) is not discussed, nor how this integrates into a full pipeline. The evaluation is confined to a single corpus family (Wikipedia/Simple), limiting external validity.
- Evaluation gaps: Only Causal-class P/R/F1 and accuracy are reported; no macro/micro F1 across both classes, no per-marker breakdowns for truly ambiguous connectives, no significance tests on the main results, and no cross-validation or multiple random seeds. The reduced-imbalance experiment compares only against B1, not against the SVM or other neural baselines, making conclusions about generalization incomplete.
- Presentation and referencing: The writing has numerous grammatical errors; several tables/figures are referenced but not shown or are inconsistent (e.g., Table 3 missing; sizes/labels hard to follow). References are incomplete or malformed. This hampers clarity and confidence in the experimental setup.

Questions to authors
- How are e1 and e2 spans obtained from AltLex? Are they gold-annotated spans? If not, what extraction rules are used? How would your system operate in a realistic pipeline where event spans and markers are not given?
- Please clarify the “padding strategy” and fix Eq. (1). Do “max/mean/mode” refer to sequence length truncation/padding thresholds or something else?
- Are the LSTMs bidirectional? What are the hidden sizes, number of layers, dropout locations, batch size, epochs, and early stopping criteria? How many runs with different seeds did you average? How were hyperparameters chosen?
- Can you report ablations: (a) only connective tokens, (b) only e1+e2 without l, (c) single sentence-level BiLSTM over the whole input, (d) average-of-embeddings MLP, (e) a CNN baseline? These would substantiate the claim about context encoding and the choice of LSTM over CNN.
- For the bootstrapped set, can you provide per-marker results, especially for markers with shifted distributions between train/test and for genuinely ambiguous markers?
- Will you release code and preprocessing to enable replication?

Overall, the empirical gains are promising, and the idea of span-based contextual encoding is sensible. However, the paper’s novelty is modest, several technical and clarity issues remain, and the evaluation lacks ablations and broader baselines. Substantial revisions would be needed to meet ACL standards.
