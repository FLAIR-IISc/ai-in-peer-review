Summary and claimed contributions
- A task framing: The paper formalizes causal relation identification as two subtasks—(i) causal meaning disambiguation of a relation between two events and (ii) causal argument identification—and focuses on the first, arguing it should be driven by contextual semantics rather than syntactic templates or lexicon-bound “causative verbs.”
- A simple neural approach: A two-encoder LSTM architecture that encodes (e1) and (l + e2) and classifies whether the relation is causal, trained on the AltLex corpus. A “Stated Pair LSTM” variant initializes the second encoder with the first’s final state; a “Pair LSTM” encodes independently.
- Empirical results: On AltLex (bootstrapped and non-bootstrapped versions), the method outperforms a majority-per-connector baseline and an SVM-based system that uses lexical resources, with analysis on ambiguous markers.

Strengths
- Focus on context-driven disambiguation of AltLex/ambiguous connectives: The paper tackles a real pain point in PDTB-style discourse parsing—lexically ambiguous markers like since/after and AltLex variants—and frames it as a context inference task instead of relying on syntactic templates. This is an important perspective, especially for coverage.
- Simple neural model beats feature-heavy baseline: Despite its simplicity, the LSTM-based model consistently improves over the SVM/feature system of Hidey & McKeown on multiple splits, and over a strong “most frequent class per connective” baseline. This supports the claim that contextual semantics can help beyond lexicon-anchored features.
- Resource frugality and generalization potential: The model uses only pre-trained word embeddings without external lexical resources, which can broaden applicability to unseen markers and languages/domains with fewer lexicons.
- Some qualitative insights: The analysis on “break,” “which then,” and other ambiguous triggers usefully illustrates when context matters, and shows the model can override majority-class biases in certain cases.
- Evaluation across corpus variants and class balance: Reporting on both the original and a more balanced setup (down-sampled non-causal class) is informative; the added McNemar test for one setting is a plus.

Weaknesses
- Limited novelty in modeling: The architecture is a standard sentence-pair BiLSTM-style classifier (two encoders + MLP). There is no attention, cross-encoder interaction, or contrastive/metric learning; the “Stated Pair” initialization is a minor variation and empirically not helpful. As a methodological contribution, this is incremental.
- Missing modern baselines and ablations: No comparisons to stronger neural baselines (e.g., ESIM/bi-attention, InferSent/Siamese pooling), and especially no transformer-based fine-tuning (e.g., BERT/roberta/mDeBERTa) which are now standard for relation/discourse classification. Ablations to isolate the role of the lexical marker (e.g., masking l), pooling choices, or e1 vs e2 contributions are absent.
- Unclear/odd design details and reporting: The “zero-padding strategy” description (max/mean/mode of lengths) is unconventional and under-explained; Figure 1 is referenced but missing; several tables are hard to parse, with inconsistencies between text and numbers. Important hyperparameters (LSTM size, number of layers, bidirectionality, training epochs, batch size) and exact preprocessing choices are not clearly specified. Statistical significance is scarcely reported.
- Narrow evaluation and potential data artifacts: All results are on AltLex, a derived corpus with bootstrapped instances. No tests on PDTB 2.0 explicit relations, Causal-TimeBank, or cross-domain settings. The per-connector majority baseline being strong suggests label leakage via marker identity; the paper does not explicitly control for this (e.g., by evaluating unseen connectives or masking connectives at test time).
- Modest and uneven gains with limited error analysis: Improvements over the feature baseline are not consistently large across settings, and the P/R tradeoffs vary widely. Error analyses are anecdotal and do not quantify model behavior on hard cases (e.g., fully implicit causality, long-distance triggers, OOV AltLex).

Questions to authors
- Can you provide results with stronger baselines (e.g., ESIM/bi-attention, InferSent, and importantly transformer fine-tuning on AltLex)? Even a compact model like DistilBERT or RoBERTa-base would be informative.
- How exactly is padding determined when you say “max/mean/mode of lengths”? Is this sequence truncation or padding length selection? Please specify LSTM hidden sizes, directionality, pooling, dropout placement, epochs, batch size, and early stopping.
- Why concatenate (l + e2) rather than model l as a separate input and allow explicit interactions (e.g., attention between e1 and e2 conditioned on l)? Have you tried masking l to measure reliance on context vs marker identity?
- How does the model perform on unseen or rare connectives at test time (e.g., leave-one-connector-out), and on explicit PDTB causal markers vs AltLex?
- Will you release code, trained models, and exact data splits for reproducibility?

Recommendation
While the problem focus is worthwhile and the empirical results are positive against a feature-based baseline, the modeling is standard and lacks novelty, the comparisons miss widely-adopted modern baselines, and the experimental reporting has clarity gaps. Given ACL’s bar and the field’s current state, I find the contribution incremental. I recommend rejection, and encourage a revised submission with stronger baselines (including transformers), clearer methodology, ablations controlling for marker identity, cross-dataset evaluation, and deeper analysis.
