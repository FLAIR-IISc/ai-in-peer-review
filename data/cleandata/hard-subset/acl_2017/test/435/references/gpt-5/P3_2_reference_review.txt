Summary and main contributions
- This paper tackles causal meaning disambiguation for lexical markers (e.g., since, after) by modeling the context of two events, proposing LSTM-based architectures that take as input (e1) and (l + e2). The task is framed as the first stage of a two-step pipeline (causal meaning classification, then argument identification).
- The primary contribution is an LSTM-based, two-input classifier (“Pair LSTM”) that generally outperforms a strong per-connector majority baseline and the feature-based SVM of Hidey and McKeown (2016) on the AltLex corpus, across both “non-bootstrapping” and “bootstrapping” splits.
- A secondary contribution is an empirical comparison of modeling choices (linked “Stated Pair LSTM” vs. independent encoders; simple temporal pooling variants; optimizers) and a small qualitative analysis of ambiguous markers and examples.

Strengths
- Clear focus on a practically useful subproblem. The paper cleanly separates causal meaning disambiguation from argument role identification, and evaluates the former under realistic ambiguity introduced by AltLex. This focus is appropriate and underexplored relative to verb-centric causation.
- Empirical gains over a reasonable baseline. On AltLex, the proposed models consistently outperform (i) a strong per-marker majority baseline and (ii) the feature-based SVM from prior work, with best gains in accuracy and F1 for the causal class. These gains hold on both the original and the more challenging bootstrapped split, suggesting some generalization beyond memorizing connectives.
- Lightweight input and generality. Using only pretrained GloVe embeddings without external lexical resources suggests portability to new domains/languages (modulo embeddings). The qualitative examples include correctly disambiguated cases for ambiguous markers and causative-looking verbs used non-causally, which strengthens the claim that context matters.
- Sensible negative finding about encoder coupling. The “Stated Pair LSTM” that initializes the second LSTM from the first underperforms the independent “Pair LSTM,” indicating that naive state passing across arguments may be suboptimal; this is a useful practical observation for future modeling.
- Some attempt at statistical rigor. The reduced-class-imbalance experiment includes a McNemar test showing significant improvements over the majority baseline.

Weaknesses
- Limited novelty and missing strong baselines. By current standards, the architectures are simple; pairwise LSTMs without explicit interaction (e.g., cross-attention/bilinear matching) are unlikely to be state of the art. Critically, the paper does not compare against transformer-based sentence-pair models (e.g., BERT/RoBERTa fine-tuned on the task), nor against prior neural discourse relation models. This undermines the significance of the reported gains over older feature-based systems.
- Methodological clarity issues. The description of “zero-padding strategies” as max/mean/mode is confusing and likely refers to temporal pooling; “mode” on continuous embeddings is ill-defined. Core hyperparameters are missing: LSTM depth/hidden size, sequence length/truncation, batch size, number of epochs, learning rate, early stopping criterion, gradient clipping, and regularization actually used. It is unclear whether a dev set was used for model selection, raising concerns about tuning on the test set.
- Evaluation scope and analysis are insufficient. The study is restricted to a single dataset constructed via Wikipedia–Simple Wikipedia alignment, with notable class imbalance and potential stylistic biases. There is no evidence the approach generalizes to other resources (e.g., PDTB explicit/AltLex subsets, Causal-TimeBank) or domains. The paper reports only Causal-class metrics and accuracy; no per-marker breakdown (especially for ambiguous markers), no macro averages, and no confidence intervals for the main results. Ablations are missing: performance without the marker l; with only l; with e1/e2 swapped; or with masked lexical content to probe lexical memorization. Given the strength of the majority baseline, such controls are essential.
- Data handling concerns. The “balanced” evaluation is produced by downsampling the Non-Causal class by a factor of 10, which changes the test distribution and makes comparisons to prior work less meaningful. A more standard approach would use class-weighted losses, resampling during training only, and report metrics on the original test set or report macro-averaged scores.
- Related work gaps and some questionable claims. The discussion largely ignores a substantial literature on neural discourse relation classification and causal relation detection beyond the cited works (e.g., attention-based and transformer approaches). The argument against CNNs is overstated; modern CNNs with dilations/attention or transformers directly address variable-length contextualization.

Questions to authors
- Splits and tuning: What were the exact train/dev/test splits? Was a development set used for hyperparameter selection and early stopping? How many runs and seeds per configuration? Please report mean ± std.
- Architecture details: Number of LSTM layers/hidden units; dropout rates actually used; regularization strength chosen; sequence length limits and truncation policy; tokenization; OOV handling; gradient clipping and optimizer hyperparameters.
- Pooling vs padding: Please clarify the “max/mean/mode” operation. Are you temporally pooling LSTM outputs or pooling over embeddings pre-LSTM? How is “mode” defined on continuous vectors?
- Baseline fairness: Are the B2 numbers reproduced under your preprocessing and splits, or copied from Hidey and McKeown (2016)? If reimplemented, how was feature extraction aligned to their paper?
- Controlling lexical memorization: What is performance when (i) removing the marker l from the input, (ii) using only l, (iii) masking content words in e1/e2, or (iv) evaluating on a marker-disjoint split?
- Per-marker analysis: Please provide per-marker precision/recall, especially for ambiguous items and for the 27/8 cases where the majority label flips between train and test in the bootstrapped set.

Additional comments and suggestions
- Include stronger baselines: fine-tuned BERT/RoBERTa/DeBERTa with pair inputs (e1) [SEP] (l + e2), and simple cross-attention models. These are likely to be competitive and would contextualize your gains.
- Improve interaction modeling: replace independent encoders with token-level cross-attention or interaction pooling (e.g., ESIM-style) to capture fine-grained alignment between e1 and e2 contexts.
- Broaden evaluation: test on PDTB explicit/AltLex subsets and Causal-TimeBank (even if smaller), and report macro-F1; include significance tests for main results.
- Release code and detailed hyperparameters to support reproducibility.
- The writing could be improved; several tables/figures are incomplete or mislabeled in the current draft, which hinders clarity.
