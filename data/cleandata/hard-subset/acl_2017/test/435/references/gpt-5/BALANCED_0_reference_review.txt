Summary and claimed contributions
This paper tackles causal meaning disambiguation: given two events (e1, e2) and a lexical marker l potentially signaling a relation, decide whether the relation is causal or not. The authors argue that causality should be modeled via contextual meaning rather than via fixed lexical/syntactic patterns. They propose LSTM-based architectures that encode e1 and [l + e2], compare a “stated pair LSTM” (initializing the second LSTM with the first’s final state) against an unconnected “pair LSTM,” explore simple padding strategies, and evaluate on the AltLex corpus (Hidey & McKeown, 2016). They compare to two baselines: (B1) a connective-majority baseline and (B2) Hidey & McKeown’s SVM with resource-driven features. They report modest improvements in F1/accuracy over B2, especially on the non-bootstrapped split, and provide a few qualitative examples.

Contributions (as I see them), in order of strength
1) An empirical study showing that simple neural encoders (pair LSTMs with GloVe embeddings) can match or modestly outperform a strong feature-based SVM on AltLex causal disambiguation, without relying on hand-engineered lexical resources.  
2) An analysis of connective ambiguity and dataset bias in AltLex, including per-optimizer/padding sensitivity and a few illustrative cases where contextual encoding helps.  
3) A task framing that separates causal meaning disambiguation from argument identification, with inputs structured as e1 and [l + e2].

Strengths
- Clear task focus and reasonable motivation: The paper articulates why causality should not be reduced to fixed lexical/syntactic templates and positions causal disambiguation as a context-sensitive problem. The case studies (e.g., “which then”) support this.
- Strong, simple baseline comparisons: Including both a per-connective majority baseline and the Hidey & McKeown SVM makes the results interpretable. On the non-bootstrapped split, the best LSTM configuration improves F1 by ~1.6–2 points and accuracy by ~6 points; on the bootstrapped split, improvements are smaller but generally positive.
- Resource-light modeling: Avoiding hand-crafted lexical resources while achieving competitive results is valuable, especially for generalization beyond the coverage of those resources.
- Some attempt at robustness and significance: The authors explore two optimizers, two architectures, and padding variants, and report a McNemar test (albeit only against B1 on a downsampled setting).

Weaknesses
- Limited novelty relative to current standards: The architectures (two LSTMs + MLP, optional state passing) are standard, and the conditional/LSTM-pair idea is well-known (e.g., Siamese/conditional encoders). The argument that CNNs are inadequate is not convincing and does not engage with more recent sequence models (biLSTMs with attention, transformers, pretrained LMs such as BERT/RoBERTa).
- Missing modern baselines and broader related work: The paper does not compare against contextualized encoders (BERT/roberta fine-tuned on the task) or even a simple average-embedding logistic regression baseline. Literature on implicit discourse relation classification and neural event causality extraction is under-cited and under-compared; this undermines the claim of advancing the state of the art.
- Ambiguities and clarity issues in the method: The “padding” description is confusing (pad(x) = {max, mean, mode}); it seems to refer to sequence length selection rather than padding values. Architectural details are sparse (uni- vs biLSTM, hidden sizes, layer widths, dropout placement, early stopping, epochs, batch sizes). It’s unclear whether the “state passing” transfers both hidden and cell states. The rationale for concatenating l to e2 rather than treating l as a separate input is not ablated.
- Evaluation design and reproducibility gaps: Results are reported on a single corpus/split (AltLex); no cross-domain evaluation (e.g., PDTB causal relations, Causal-TimeBank) is attempted. There is no multi-run variance, seed sensitivity, or significance testing vs B2. The downsampling experiment is insufficiently specified (training vs test downsampling?) and tests significance only vs B1. Code or model artifacts are not provided.
- Writing and presentation: There are numerous typos, formatting glitches, and incomplete references. Figures/tables are sometimes referenced unclearly. Some theoretical discussion (e.g., generative semantics) feels lengthy relative to space devoted to concrete model/evaluation details.

Questions to authors
1) Data and spans: How are e1 and e2 spans obtained from AltLex? Are they gold spans provided by the corpus or derived heuristically? Any preprocessing/normalization steps?  
2) Architecture specifics: Are LSTMs bidirectional? What are the hidden sizes and MLP layer widths? Do you pass both hidden and cell states in the “stated” variant? Where exactly is dropout applied?  
3) Padding: Please clarify the “max/mean/mode” padding strategy. Are you choosing a target length per batch by taking the statistic over sequence lengths and padding with zeros?  
4) Training protocol: Number of epochs, early stopping criteria, batch size, learning rate schedules, and the chosen hyperparameters for the best runs? How many runs per setting; what is the variance?  
5) Baselines: Why not include (i) averaged GloVe + logistic regression/SVM, and (ii) fine-tuned BERT/roberta over the concatenated input [e1] [SEP] [l] [SEP] [e2]?  
6) Generalization: Did you try evaluating models trained on AltLex on PDTB causal relations or Causal-TimeBank to test cross-corpus transfer?  
7) Design choice: Why concatenate l with e2 instead of encoding e1, l, e2 as three inputs with cross-attention or a joint encoder? Any ablation?

Recommendation
Reject. While the task is important and the empirical results are modestly positive, the methodological novelty is limited and the experimental evaluation lacks modern baselines, broader datasets, and clarity needed for ACL standards. Strengthening the baselines (esp. pretrained encoders), clarifying and tightening the methodology, adding cross-corpus evaluations, and providing a deeper error analysis would substantially improve the paper.

Suggestions for improvement
- Include strong neural baselines (biLSTM+attention, BERT/roberta cross-encoder or prompt-style fine-tuning), plus a simple averaged-embedding classifier.  
- Add ablations: encode l separately; try attention between e1 and e2; compare uni- vs biLSTM; analyze state-passing impact.  
- Evaluate on additional corpora (PDTB causal relations, Causal-TimeBank) and/or perform cross-domain transfer.  
- Report multi-run averages with significance vs B2; release code.  
- Expand related work to implicit discourse relation classification and neural causality extraction; correct references.  
- Clarify padding, training details, and span extraction; provide per-connective performance and a richer error analysis.
