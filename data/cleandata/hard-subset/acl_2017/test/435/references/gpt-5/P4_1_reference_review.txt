Summary
This paper addresses causal meaning disambiguation: given two annotated argument spans (e1, e2) and a lexical marker l, decide whether the relation is Causal or Non-Causal. The authors propose LSTM-based architectures that encode the two inputs (first event; lexical marker concatenated with second event) and classify the relation. Two variants are tested: “Pair LSTM” (independent encoders) and “Stated Pair LSTM” (second LSTM initialized with the first’s final state). Experiments are conducted on the AltLex corpus (Hidey and McKeown, 2016), in both “non-bootstrapping” and “bootstrapping” versions, with comparisons to (i) a per-connective majority-class baseline and (ii) the SVM feature-based system from Hidey and McKeown (2016). The proposed models generally improve F1 (Causal) and Accuracy over baselines, with additional analysis on padding strategies and optimizers, and a small error analysis.

Main contributions (as assessed)
1) Empirical finding: a straightforward LSTM-based encoder of the two arguments (and marker) improves over the feature-based state-of-the-art on the AltLex benchmark, especially in precision, and achieves competitive/better balance of precision–recall on both corpus variants.

2) Architectural exploration: comparison of independent vs state-initialized “paired” LSTMs, along with optimizer and padding-length choices, showing that independent encoders performed better.

Strengths
- Empirical gains on benchmark: The best configurations outperform the per-connective majority baseline (B1) and the SVM feature-based system (B2) on AltLex. For the non-bootstrapping set, “Pair LSTM Max Adadelta” improves Causal F1 by 2.13 points and Accuracy by 7.72 points over B2; in the bootstrapping set, several LSTM configurations have comparable or better F1/Accuracy than B2, with better precision. These gains support the claim that context encoding with neural models helps beyond per-connective priors and hand-engineered features.

- Clear task scoping and motivation: The paper separates causal meaning classification from argument identification and motivates neural context encoding in light of the limited coverage and ambiguity of explicit markers and AltLexes. The focus on disambiguating ambiguous markers (e.g., since, after, which then) is appropriate and grounded in prior observations.

- Analysis beyond overall scores: The paper discusses precision–recall trade-offs, class imbalance effects, and includes qualitative examples where the neural model succeeds on ambiguous or deceptive cases (e.g., “which then” or non-causal uses of causative verbs like break). A small significance test (McNemar) is reported for the downsampled setting.

- Minimal reliance on external lexical resources: The approach uses only pre-trained embeddings, avoiding the coverage limitations of hand-crafted lexical resources highlighted in prior work.

Weaknesses
- Limited novelty: Using LSTMs to encode sentence arguments for relation classification is standard by now; pair/siamese LSTM architectures and simple dense classifiers have been widely applied to discourse relation, textual entailment, and semantic relation tasks. The paper positions itself mainly against a feature-based baseline, with only one neural prior (a Japanese CNN); it omits comparisons to contemporary neural discourse relation models (e.g., neural PDTB implicit relation classifiers), which reduces the novelty and contextualization.

- Clarity and technical specification issues: Key modeling details are underspecified or unclear.
  • Padding “strategies” are described as max/mean/mode, but Eq. (1) is not well-defined (pad(x) = {max(x), mean(x), mode(x)} for x ∈ R^{n×d} is mathematically incorrect). It is unclear whether sequences are truncated for mean/mode lengths and how truncation affects performance.
  • The LSTM outputs and subsequent “vectorization” (Eq. (2)) are ambiguously described. It is not clear whether the last hidden state, a pooled representation, or a flattened sequence is fed to a dense layer; layer sizes beyond the 100-d vector are not fully specified.
  • Training details (batch size, epochs, early stopping, dev set usage, embedding fine-tuning, OOV handling) are missing, hindering reproducibility.

- Evaluation protocol and baselines: 
  • The paper does not clearly describe development vs test splits or how hyperparameters were selected, raising risk of test-set tuning. 
  • Comparisons center on B1 and one feature-based system (B2). Stronger neural baselines (e.g., average-of-embeddings + logistic regression; single-sequence BiLSTM over concatenated Arg1–marker–Arg2; attention-based encoders) are not included.
  • Only one dataset family (AltLex) is used; while other corpora are small, even reporting results or transfer experiments on Causal-TimeBank would strengthen claims of generalization.

- Dataset handling and reporting issues: 
  • Several tables/figures contain formatting issues and inconsistencies (e.g., malformed counts in Table 6; typos). 
  • The class imbalance discussion is sensible, but the downsampling experiment reduces Non-Causal by an arbitrary factor of ten without investigating more standard rebalancing strategies (class weighting, focal loss). 
  • The paper notes distribution shifts between train/test in the bootstrapping set but does not analyze per-marker generalization (e.g., performance on markers unseen or label-flipped between splits).

- Limited ablations and error analysis: The design choice to concatenate the marker with the second argument is not ablated (e.g., removing the marker, encoding the marker separately, or symmetric encodings). Error typologies (e.g., temporal vs causal ambiguity, AltLex vs canonical markers) are not quantified.

Questions to Authors
1) What was the exact evaluation protocol? Please specify train/dev/test splits for both corpus versions and whether any hyperparameter/model selection used the test set. If a dev set was not available, how did you prevent overfitting in model selection?

2) How exactly are sequences padded/truncated under the “mean” and “mode” length choices? Are long arguments truncated, and if so, how many samples are affected? Please clarify Eq. (1) with correct definitions.

3) What is the precise encoder/decoder configuration? 
   - LSTM directionality (bi/unidirectional), hidden sizes, number of layers.
   - Whether embeddings are fixed or fine-tuned; OOV handling; tokenization details.
   - Training regimen: batch size, epochs, early stopping criteria, regularization specifics.

4) Did you evaluate simpler neural baselines (e.g., average of embeddings with logistic regression; single-sequence BiLSTM over [Arg1; marker; Arg2])? If not, can you add these to contextualize the gains due to the two-input LSTM design?

5) How does the model perform on subsets with ambiguous markers vs unambiguous markers, and on markers with label flips between train and test in the bootstrapping set? A per-marker analysis (precision/recall) would substantiate claims about context-driven disambiguation.

6) Can you report statistical significance for the main results (not only the downsampled setting) and provide variance across multiple runs/seeds?

7) Is code and preprocessing available to ensure reproducibility, including the exact mapping from PDTB/AltLex annotations to e1/l/e2 inputs?

Overall, the paper presents a clear, reasonable neural approach with measurable gains on a relevant dataset, but novelty is modest and important details of modeling and evaluation are under-specified. Addressing the clarity and evaluation concerns would strengthen the technical soundness and reproducibility.
