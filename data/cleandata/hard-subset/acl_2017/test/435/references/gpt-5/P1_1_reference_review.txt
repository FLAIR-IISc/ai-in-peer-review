Summary and claimed contributions
- This paper studies causal meaning disambiguation: given two events (e1, e2) and a potential causal marker l in context, decide whether the relation is causal or not. The authors argue causality should be learned from context rather than from fixed syntactic/lexical patterns.
- They propose LSTM-based architectures with two inputs (e1 and l+e2), compare a “Pair LSTM” and a “Stated Pair LSTM” (second LSTM initialized from the first), and evaluate on the AltLex corpus (non-bootstrapping and bootstrapping splits).
- They report improvements over a majority-per-connective baseline (B1) and, on some settings, over the feature-rich SVM baseline from Hidey and McKeown (2016) (B2). They also present limited analysis on ambiguous markers.

My view of the contributions (in strength order)
1) Empirical evaluation on the AltLex dataset of simple neural models for causal disambiguation, with competitive performance to B2 and consistent gains over B1 across settings.
2) Problem framing that separates causal meaning classification from argument identification, advocating learning contextual semantics for ambiguous markers.
3) Some qualitative analysis of cases where context helps disambiguate highly ambiguous markers.

Strengths
- Relevance and timeliness: Causal relation disambiguation for ambiguous markers (e.g., since, after, which then) is important and not fully solved by rule-based or resource-heavy methods. The paper focuses on a realistic setting (AltLex) that better reflects the variety of causal expressions.
- Empirical gains over strong baselines: Across both AltLex splits, most neural configurations outperform the majority-per-connective baseline and are competitive with or slightly exceed the SVM+lexicon system. In the non-bootstrapping split, the best model improves F1 on the Causal class (+2.13%) and accuracy (+7.72%) over B2; in bootstrapping, the best neural models match or slightly exceed B2.
- Sensible architectural finding: The “Pair LSTM” (independent encoders for e1 and l+e2 followed by MLP) consistently outperforms the state-initialized LSTM, suggesting that imposing a directional dependency between LSTM states is unnecessary or even harmful, and that late interaction via dense layers is sufficient. This is a useful practical insight.
- Preliminary analysis of ambiguity: The paper highlights examples where context overrides marker priors (e.g., which then, break), supporting the claim that contextual encoding helps beyond connector frequencies. The downsampling experiment shows the neural model maintains a better precision-recall balance than B1 under reduced class imbalance.
- Reduced reliance on lexical resources: Compared to B2, the approach uses only pre-trained embeddings and supervised data, which can generalize beyond the coverage of WordNet/VerbNet/PropBank.

Weaknesses
- Clarity and methodological ambiguities: Key modeling details are unclear or internally inconsistent. The description oscillates between using LSTMs to encode sequences and applying a dense layer to a flattened padded matrix (Eq. 2 suggests vec in R^{nd}), and the “zero-padding strategies” (padding to max/mean/mode length) are unconventional and not well-defined (mean/mode padding is non-standard). It’s unclear whether final LSTM states or flattened embeddings are fed to the MLP, whether the LSTMs are bidirectional, whether embeddings are fine-tuned, and how OOVs are handled. This significantly hinders reproducibility.
- Dataset usage and experimental rigor: There is no clear development split or hyperparameter tuning protocol. The paper reports many configurations and appears to select the best on the test set, risking overfitting to the test. Statistical significance is reported only for B1 vs the proposed model on the downsampled setting, not vs B2, and not on the main (full) datasets.
- Limited baselines and ablations: The most informative ablations are missing. In particular: (i) a “marker-only” baseline (e.g., using only l, or a bag-of-words/MLP over l without context), (ii) an “events-only” baseline without l, (iii) a simple bag-of-words logistic regression or linear SVM, and (iv) end-to-end BiLSTM over the full sequence (e1, l, e2) as a single input. Without these, it’s hard to quantify how much the gains come from contextual encoding vs. lexical identity of the marker.
- Generalization to unseen markers: The paper claims better handling of ambiguous markers but does not rigorously test generalization to unseen or distribution-shifted markers (e.g., leave-one-connector-out evaluation). The bootstrapping split contains some distribution shifts, but analysis is anecdotal and not controlled.
- Writing, presentation, and references: The paper contains numerous typos, incomplete references, and malformed tables (e.g., Table 3/6 values appear corrupted; several references have “?” markers). This reduces readability and raises concerns about carefulness. Some definitions (e.g., temporal vs positional ordering) are discussed at length but are not used in the experiments.

Questions to authors
- Model specifics: Are the sequence encoders unidirectional or bidirectional LSTMs? Do you use the final hidden state, a pooled representation, or a flattened matrix through a dense layer? Please clarify Eq. 1–2 and the actual computation graph.
- Padding strategies: How exactly are “mean” and “mode” padding lengths computed and applied in mini-batches? Do you truncate longer sequences? Why is this preferable to standard per-batch max padding or masking?
- Training/tuning: What development split was used for model selection and hyperparameter tuning? How many epochs, batch size, early stopping criteria?
- Inputs: How are e1 and e2 spans provided/extracted in AltLex? Are markers always included concatenated with e2? Did you try models without the marker or without events?
- Baselines: Can you report results for (i) marker-only features (one-hot or embedding), (ii) bag-of-words logistic regression over e1, l, e2, and (iii) single-input BiLSTM over the concatenated text?
- Generalization: Can you add leave-one-connector-out evaluation to test generalization to unseen markers?
- Significance: Can you provide statistical significance vs B2 on the full datasets?

Additional comments
- Include per-connector breakdowns and confusion matrices to illustrate performance on ambiguous vs unambiguous markers.
- Compare Adadelta vs Adam with a fixed architecture and report mean/std over multiple runs to assess stability.
- Clean up tables and references; ensure corpus sizes and splits are clearly reported and consistent; release code and preprocessing scripts.

Overall, the problem is important and the results are promising, but the paper needs substantial clarification of the methodology, stronger baselines/ablations, and more rigorous evaluation protocols to support its claims.
