- Paper addresses causal meaning disambiguation of lexical markers (e.g., since, after, due to) by modeling context around two events and the marker.
- Proposes two neural architectures using LSTM encoders over event spans and marker+second-event, classifying Causal vs. Non-Causal.
- Experiments run on AltLex corpus (non-bootstrapping and bootstrapping splits) against per-connective majority and Hidey & McKeown (2016) SVM baselines.
- Models yield modest but consistent gains in F1 and accuracy and notably higher precision than the feature-based baseline.
- Empirical contribution: two-input LSTM encoder conditioning on (e1, l, e2) shows small consistent gains, supporting contextual encoding for disambiguation.
- Architectural insight: Pair-wise independent encoding (Pair LSTM) outperforms state-initialized variant (Stated Pair LSTM); Adam optimizer helps precision/recall balance.
- Framing contribution: clear two-step view separating causal meaning classification from argument identification, with this work on the first step.
- Strength: tackles an important, under-explored subproblem with an appropriate benchmark (AltLex).
- Strength: simple model is competitive, improving causal-class F1 by ~1–2 points and accuracy by ~1–8 points across AltLex variants.
- Strength: includes analysis of architectural variants, optimizer effects, and a small class-imbalance study with McNemar test vs. majority baseline.
- Strength: provides qualitative examples where the neural model succeeds and the SVM fails, illustrating context benefits.
- Weakness: model description is inconsistent and underspecified—LSTM usage, padding strategies, and many implementation details are unclear or missing.
- Weakness: limited novelty—architectures are standard multi-input LSTMs with no substantive modeling innovation or new objective.
- Weakness: evaluation limited to a single dataset/splits with small gains and no significance testing on main results; lacks cross-dataset or domain-transfer evaluation.
- Weakness: unrealistic task setup assuming gold e1/e2/marker spans without explaining span acquisition or robustness to noisy/implicit markers.
- Weakness: missing stronger or modern baselines and essential ablations (e.g., sentence-level BiLSTM, marker-only baseline, removing components).
- Weakness: paper suffers from writing issues—typos, malformed references, missing tables/figures, and unclear notation hindering reproducibility.
- Question: how are e1, l, e2 spans obtained from AltLex—annotated or heuristically extracted, and if heuristics, what accuracy?
- Question: are encoders uni- or bi-directional LSTMs; what hidden sizes, layers, and pooling strategies map LSTM outputs to 100-d vectors?
- Question: clarify padding/truncation and the meaning of “max/mean/mode” padding strategies and chosen sequence lengths.
- Question: describe training protocol—hyperparameter selection, number of runs, variance, regularization, early stopping.
- Question: are main improvements statistically significant and can you provide per-connective breakdowns and error-type analysis?
- Question: can you add baselines/ablations: (i) sentence-level BiLSTM/attention without gold spans, (ii) bag-of-embeddings MLP, and (iii) ablations removing marker/e1/e2?
