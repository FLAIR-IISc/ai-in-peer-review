Summary
This paper tackles causal meaning disambiguation: given two events and a potentially causal lexical marker, decide whether the relation is causal or not. The authors argue that causality should be captured via contextual semantics rather than fixed syntactic patterns. They propose two LSTM-based architectures (“Pair LSTM” and “Stated Pair LSTM”) that encode (e1) and (l + e2) and make a binary decision. Using GloVe embeddings and the AltLex corpus (both “non-bootstrapping” and “bootstrapping” versions), they report improvements over (i) a majority-by-marker baseline and (ii) the SVM feature-based system of Hidey and McKeown (2016). They further present a small analysis on ambiguous markers and a reduced, more balanced subset.

Contributions (as I see them)
1) Empirical finding: A simple two-branch LSTM that encodes the context around candidate causal markers outperforms a strong SVM feature baseline on AltLex across two corpus variants, with better precision-recall balance on the “bootstrapping” set.
2) Problem framing and analysis: Clear articulation of the limits of syntactic/marker-only approaches and targeted analyses showing behavior on ambiguous markers and distribution shift introduced by bootstrapping.
3) Minor methodological variants: Comparison of two related architectures (with vs. without state initialization) and simple padding-length choices.

Strengths
1) Addresses a real gap: The paper targets disambiguation for ambiguous causal markers (e.g., since, after), a setting where rule-based and resource-heavy systems struggle. The focus on context encoding is appropriate and well-motivated.
2) Empirical improvements with reasonable baselines: The proposed models consistently beat majority-by-marker and the Hidey & McKeown (2016) SVM on both corpus variants. On the non-bootstrapping set, the best model improves F1 in the causal class by about 2.1 points and accuracy by 7.7 points; on the bootstrapping set, several configurations match or exceed the SVM with better precision-recall balance (e.g., Pair LSTM Mean Adam reaches 80.30 P / 82.85 R / 81.56 F1 vs. 77.29 / 84.85 / 80.90 for SVM).
3) Sensible modeling insight: The “Pair LSTM” (independent encoders with fusion in dense layers) tends to outperform the “Stated LSTM” (initializing the second LSTM with the first’s final state), suggesting that direct state passing is not necessary; this is a useful negative result about how to encode event pairs.
4) Analysis of ambiguity and distribution shift: The discussion of ambiguous connectives (e.g., “which then”) and of the bootstrapped split’s marker distribution mismatch shows that the method learns beyond majority-by-marker heuristics and is somewhat robust to shift.
5) Resource-light approach: Using only pre-trained word embeddings (without hand-engineered lexical features) is attractive for coverage and portability and aligns with the paper’s motivation.

Weaknesses
1) Limited technical novelty and unclear modeling details: Two LSTM branches on (e1) and (l+e2) with dense fusion is a standard design; the novelty is incremental. Critical details are underspecified or confusing: it is unclear whether the LSTMs are uni/bidirectional, hidden sizes, whether embeddings are fine-tuned, which sequence representation is fed to the dense layer (last state? pooled outputs?), and whether the two branches share parameters. Equation (1) conflates “padding” with choosing a target length via max/mean/mode and is mathematically imprecise; the zero-padding description is unclear.
2) Evaluation rigor and reproducibility: The paper explores many configurations (optimizers, padding, dropout, regularization) but does not specify a development set or hyperparameter selection protocol, increasing the risk of over-tuning to the test set. Only one statistical test is reported (McNemar on the reduced set vs B1). There is no mention of multiple random seeds/variance. Some tables contain apparent typos or missing numbers (e.g., Table 6 training set counts), undermining reproducibility.
3) Baseline coverage and fairness: The paper argues against CNNs but does not empirically compare to a text-CNN or to a simple BiLSTM/attention over the full sentence, nor to stronger modern baselines (e.g., a classifier over averaged embeddings, or a transformer encoder). The claim that CNNs require knowing “kernel size = context length” is not accurate; multi-kernel CNNs are standard for variable n-gram capture. Without these baselines, the gains over a 2016 SVM feature model are less compelling.
4) Task scope and data dependence: The task assumes that (e1), (l), and (e2) are given and focuses only on binary disambiguation. This excludes (i) identifying arguments/direction and (ii) cases without explicit markers. The sole evaluation on AltLex (automatically aligned Wikipedia/Simple Wikipedia with bootstrapped expansions) raises questions about domain generalization; no results are reported on PDTB explicit relations or Causal-TimeBank (even for a subset), nor on cross-domain transfer.
5) Clarity and presentation quality: There are numerous grammatical and formatting issues, incomplete references, and inconsistent numeric reporting. Figures/tables are referenced but not fully interpretable from the text alone. These issues make it hard to precisely understand the setup and would hinder others from reproducing the work.

Questions to Authors
1) Data and splits: What exact train/dev/test splits were used for both AltLex versions? Was a development set used to select optimizers, dropout, regularization, padding length, and architecture? How many epochs and what early stopping criterion?
2) Model specifics: Are the LSTMs bidirectional? What are hidden sizes and number of layers? Are embeddings fine-tuned? Do the two LSTMs share weights? What pooling/representation (last state vs pooling) is fed to the dense layer?
3) Input construction: How are e1, l, and e2 extracted from AltLex instances? Do you include surrounding sentence context beyond e1 and e2 spans? How are long spans truncated with the different padding choices?
4) Control for connective identity: Did you run experiments where the connective token(s) are masked to verify that the model uses context rather than memorizing marker identities? Relatedly, how does the model perform on markers unseen in training?
5) Baselines: Can you report results for (a) a single-sequence BiLSTM/attention over the full sentence; (b) a multi-kernel CNN; (c) a simple averaged-embeddings logistic regression? These would help isolate the value of the proposed two-branch design and the LSTM choice.
6) Generalization: Have you tested on PDTB explicit causal relations or Causal-TimeBank (even if small), or via cross-validation to assess robustness across domains?

Overall, the paper targets an important problem, shows modest but consistent gains over a reasonable prior baseline, and offers some useful insights. However, the limited novelty, underspecified methodology, insufficient baseline coverage, and presentation/reproducibility issues currently weaken the case for publication. Clarifying the setup and adding stronger, fair comparisons would substantially strengthen the submission.
