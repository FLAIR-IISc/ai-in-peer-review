Summary and claimed contributions
This paper tackles causal meaning disambiguation: given two identified events and a potentially causal lexical marker, decide whether the relation is causal. The authors argue that causality is not tied to specific syntactic constructions and propose LSTM-based architectures that encode the two arguments (and the marker) from word embeddings, followed by dense layers for classification. Experiments on the AltLex corpus (Hidey & McKeown, 2016) compare against a majority-per-marker baseline and a feature-rich SVM baseline.

As I see them, the main contributions are:
1) An empirical study showing that simple neural context encoders (pair of LSTMs over events plus marker) can match or modestly surpass a strong feature-based SVM on AltLex, particularly on the non-bootstrapped split, without relying on external lexical resources.
2) An analysis of lexical-marker ambiguity and distribution shift across AltLex splits, with qualitative examples suggesting the model sometimes leverages context beyond the marker identity.
3) A task framing that separates causal meaning classification from argument identification, and a dataset usage protocol focusing on ambiguous markers.

Strengths
- Resource-light modeling that generalizes beyond lexical resources: The proposed models use pre-trained GloVe embeddings and avoid engineered lexical/semantic features (WordNet/VerbNet), yet reach F1 competitive with or better than the SVM baseline. This is valuable for domains or languages with sparse resources.
- Sensible task decomposition and focus on ambiguity: The paper justifies isolating causal meaning disambiguation (vs. argument direction identification), and targets markers like since/after that are genuinely ambiguous. This keeps the evaluation aligned with the stated research question.
- Empirical gains on a strong baseline in one setting: On the “non bootstrapping” split, the Pair LSTM surpasses the SVM by +2.13 F1 (causal) and +7.72 accuracy, indicating that modeling local context of events plus marker can help over marker-majority or resource-driven features.
- Qualitative analysis beyond marker frequencies: The examples where the model correctly classifies against training-set majority (e.g., “which then”, non-causative uses of “break”) provide evidence the model can use context rather than memorize connectives only.
- Attention to label imbalance and distribution shift: The paper examines class imbalance, tries a downsampled variant, and notes that bootstrapped split contains marker-meaning shifts absent in training, a realistic challenge for discourse relation disambiguation.

Weaknesses
- Limited novelty and underpowered modeling relative to current standards: Using LSTMs to encode arguments and classify relations is well-trodden; there is no attention, contextualized encoders, or contrastive/context-aware training. Stronger and simpler baselines (e.g., BiLSTM/Transformer over the full sentence; averaged embeddings + logistic regression) are missing. Given current practice, a Transformer baseline (fine-tuned) is essential.
- Method description is unclear/inconsistent: The “zero-padding strategies” (max/mean/mode) are described as padding but correspond to pooling; equations have dimension mismatches (vec(x) mapping, tanh(W·x+b) over matrices); the “Stated Pair LSTM” initialization is not rigorously specified. This hinders reproducibility and makes it hard to assess what was actually implemented.
- Evaluation confined to a single dataset with small, inconsistent gains: Improvements over the SVM on the bootstrapped split are minor (≈ +0.6–1.1 F1), sometimes within expected variance, and there is no significance test reported against B2. No cross-dataset validation (e.g., Causal-TimeBank) or cross-domain robustness. Claims such as “encoding of context is required” are not causally supported by ablations (e.g., removing the marker, events, or shuffling contexts).
- Potential leakage via lexical marker identity and missing ablations: Because the marker is concatenated with e2, the model can partially rely on connective identity. While it beats the per-marker majority baseline, there is no controlled test showing necessity/sufficiency of the marker vs. context (marker-only, events-only, full-sentence-only ablations).
- Reporting and presentation issues: Results lack standard deviations or multiple runs; hyperparameters (epochs, batch size, early stopping, seed) and development protocol are underspecified; some tables have formatting/numbering issues; several references are incomplete. This falls below ACL reproducibility expectations.

Questions to authors
- Clarify the architecture: What exactly are the “padding” strategies (max/mean/mode)? Are these pooling operations over time? If so, where do they occur relative to the LSTM output? Please fix dimensionalities in Eq. (2) and specify the LSTM outputs used (final hidden vs. pooled hidden states).
- How is the “Stated Pair LSTM” initialized? Are both h and c states of the second LSTM initialized from the first? Are parameters shared between the two LSTMs?
- What is the precise input text span for e1, marker, e2? Do you use only the event phrases or the full sentence context around them? If only events, how are events extracted/normalized?
- Provide ablations: (a) marker-only; (b) events-only; (c) full sentence without explicit marker segmentation; (d) removing the marker from the input to test reliance on context.
- Training details: number of runs/seeds, batch size, epochs, early stopping, dev split and model selection criterion; report mean±std. Were results statistically significant vs. B2 on both splits?
- Did you try contextual encoders (e.g., BiLSTM over concatenated sentence; or a transformer like BERT) as stronger baselines? If not, why?
- Can you evaluate on another corpus (e.g., Causal-TimeBank) or perform cross-split generalization experiments to test robustness to marker distribution shifts?
- How do you handle class imbalance beyond downsampling? Did you try class weighting, focal loss, or threshold tuning?

Decision
Reject. The paper addresses an important problem and shows promising results with a resource-light neural approach, but the technical novelty is limited, the method description has ambiguities that impede reproducibility, stronger contemporary baselines and ablations are missing, and evaluation is confined to a single dataset with modest gains. I encourage the authors to (i) clarify and tighten the methodology, (ii) add rigorous ablations and stronger baselines (including transformer models and sentence-level encoders), (iii) report multi-run statistics and significance vs. B2, and (iv) validate cross-dataset robustness. These steps would substantially strengthen the contribution.
