Summary and contributions
This paper addresses causal meaning disambiguation: given two events (e1, e2) and a candidate lexical marker l, decide whether the relation is Causal or Non-Causal. The authors argue that causality should be modeled via contextual semantics rather than rigid syntactic patterns, and propose two LSTM-based architectures that encode (1) e1 and (2) l concatenated with e2. They evaluate on the AltLex corpus (Hidey and McKeown, 2016), comparing to (i) a majority-per-marker baseline and (ii) the original feature-based SVM system of Hidey and McKeown.

As I see them, the main contributions are:
1) Empirical result: a simple two-input LSTM classifier trained on GloVe embeddings outperforms a strong feature-based SVM baseline on AltLex, with consistent gains in F1/accuracy across both non-bootstrapping and bootstrapping settings.
2) Architectural observation: encoding e1 and (l+e2) independently (“Pair LSTM”) performs better than initializing the second LSTM with the first (“Stated Pair LSTM”), suggesting that late interaction is preferable for this task.
3) Task framing and qualitative analysis: the paper distinguishes causal meaning disambiguation from argument identification and offers small-scale examples where neural context encoding corrects errors made by feature-based methods.

Strengths
- Clear empirical target and dataset choice. Focusing on AltLex is sensible given the motivating claim that many causal markers are AltLex-like and context-dependent. The paper uses both the original and bootstrapped versions and discusses the distributional differences, which helps contextualize results.

- Simple, reproducible modeling idea with non-trivial improvements. Despite its simplicity, the “Pair LSTM” consistently matches or exceeds the prior SVM baseline, achieving higher precision and competitive recall, and improving overall accuracy. The result is practically useful for causal marker disambiguation pipelines.

- Insightful architectural takeaway. The finding that state-passing between the two LSTMs underperforms independent encodings suggests that modeling the relation via late fusion/dense layers suffices and may be more robust than assuming dependence between event encodings. This is a useful negative result for researchers designing pairwise encoders.

- Some analysis beyond aggregate metrics. The paper examines connectives with divergent train/test distributions, ambiguous markers (e.g., “which then”), and cases where causative verbs like “break” are non-causal, supporting the claim that context matters.

- Discussion situates the problem beyond surface syntax. The introduction and related work articulate why rigid syntactic or verb-class approaches are insufficient for causality, aligning the task with broader discourse relation classification.

Weaknesses
- Limited novelty relative to existing neural discourse relation work. The paper does not engage with or compare to substantial prior neural work on implicit/explicit discourse relation classification (e.g., Ji & Eisenstein; Rutherford & Xue; Qin et al.; Liu et al.; Zhang et al.) and event causality detection using neural models. The proposed architecture (two independent LSTMs + dense layers) is standard, and the paper’s claims about LSTMs vs CNNs are speculative without a direct comparison. In 2025, omitting transformer baselines (e.g., BERT/RoBERTa sentence-pair models) is a serious gap.

- Methodological clarity issues and missing details. Several aspects are underspecified or confusing:
  - “Zero-padding strategies” defined via max/mean/mode are unclear. Padding length should be a fixed integer; it is not clear what taking the mean/mode means in practice or how it is computed (per batch, per split, global?).
  - Key hyperparameters are missing: LSTM hidden size(s), number of layers, whether LSTMs are bi-directional, learning rate, batch size, epochs, early stopping, tokenization, OOV handling, and the exact train/dev/test splits.
  - No development set usage is described; it is unclear how hyperparameters were selected without test-set peek.
  - Regularization and dropout settings are listed but not tied to specific runs, and no variance across random seeds is reported.

- Evaluation design risks overestimating performance. The task includes the lexical marker as part of the input, and the dataset has many near-unambiguous markers; improvements may largely come from learning marker identity rather than context. The strong majority-per-marker baseline (B1) underscores this. The paper does not control for this effect (e.g., by evaluating only on ambiguous markers, masking/replacing the marker, or reporting per-marker breakdowns). Reported gains in the non-bootstrapping setting could be driven by marker memorization.

- Weak comparative baselines. Beyond B1 and the SVM from Hidey & McKeown (2016), there are no modern baselines. Even simple biLSTM/attention models over the full sentence, or off-the-shelf transformer sentence-pair classifiers, would be appropriate. The claim that LSTMs encode context better than CNNs is not validated experimentally on this dataset.

- Reporting and presentation issues. Several tables and equations are malformed or ambiguous (e.g., dataset sizes in Table 3/6, Equation (1) for padding, the connective frequency figure). Significance testing is only reported vs B1, not vs B2; and single-run results without confidence intervals hinder assessing robustness. The reduced dataset experiment (downsampling Non-Causal by 10x) alters the task distribution; its motivation and implications are not fully discussed.

Questions to authors
- Please clarify the “padding by mean/mode” procedure: what exact sequence length is used, how is it computed, and is it fixed across training/inference? How does it differ from standard max-length padding or bucketing?

- What are the full architectural and training hyperparameters (LSTM size, bidirectionality, layers; optimizer learning rates; batch size; epochs; early stopping; seed)? How were they selected (dev set?), and were results averaged across seeds?

- How are train/dev/test splits defined for both AltLex versions? Are they the same as in Hidey & McKeown (2016)? Was any model selection done on the test set?

- How much of the performance depends on the marker identity? Could you report: (a) results restricted to ambiguous markers (e.g., since/after and AltLexes with both causal and non-causal instances), (b) results when the marker is masked or replaced by a placeholder, and (c) per-marker performance breakdown?

- Can you compare against at least one transformer baseline (e.g., BERT/RoBERTa fine-tuned on (e1, l+e2)) and a simple biLSTM-attention sentence-pair model?

Overall assessment
The problem is important and the empirical gains over a feature-based baseline are promising. However, the methodological clarity, missing modern baselines, and lack of controls for marker memorization limit the strength and generality of the claims. I recommend rejection in the current form, with a clear path to strengthening: add rigorous baselines (including transformers), clarify and solidify the methodology, report multi-seed results, and provide analyses that isolate contextual understanding from connective identity.
