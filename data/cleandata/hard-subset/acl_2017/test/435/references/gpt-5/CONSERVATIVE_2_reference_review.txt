Summary and claimed contributions
This paper targets causality disambiguation for lexical markers (e.g., since/after) by encoding the two events and the marker with LSTM-based sequence encoders. Using the AltLex corpus, the proposed “Pair LSTM” and “Stated Pair LSTM” architectures are compared against (i) a majority-per-marker baseline and (ii) the feature-based SVM of Hidey & McKeown (2016). Reported gains are modest but consistent on both non-bootstrapped and bootstrapped AltLex splits; some qualitative examples are presented.

As I see it, the main contributions are:
1) An empirical demonstration that a simple LSTM-based sequence-pair encoder can outperform a feature/SVM baseline on AltLex causal disambiguation.
2) A focused study on ambiguous causal markers with limited qualitative analysis.

Strengths
- Focus on a real ambiguity: The paper addresses the well-known ambiguity of certain causal markers (e.g., since/after), an important subproblem with practical relevance in discourse parsing and event relation extraction.
- Simple approach with some gains: A straightforward LSTM pair model trained on GloVe improves over a strong lexical baseline and the prior feature/SVM model on AltLex, with clearer precision/recall trade-offs in several settings.
- Some attention to robustness: The authors test two corpus versions (bootstrapped/non-bootstrapped) and briefly explore class imbalance via downsampling, including a McNemar test on the reduced setting.
- Qualitative examples: Case studies where the neural model succeeds while the feature baseline fails (e.g., “which then”, non-causative senses of “break”) support the intuition that context helps beyond connective identity.

Weaknesses
- Limited novelty and weak methodological contribution: The architectures (“Pair LSTM”, initializing the second LSTM with the first’s state) are standard sequence-pair variants; there is no substantive architectural or learning innovation. Given the maturity of the area, this requires stronger empirical validation to be impactful.
- Incomplete and potentially confounded evaluation: 
  - Only the AltLex corpus is used; there is no test on PDTB, Causal-TimeBank, or other datasets, even as a sanity check (acknowledging potential size limits). This makes it hard to assess generalization.
  - The model sees the lexical marker as input. Since per-marker majority baseline is already strong, it is essential to control for lexical identity (e.g., remove/neutralize the marker token, train on a subset of markers and test on unseen markers, or evaluate per-marker performance). Without this, improvements may largely be due to learning marker-specific priors rather than context-driven disambiguation.
  - The reliance on gold event spans in AltLex sidesteps the harder end-to-end problem; there is no evidence the method is robust when event spans must be detected automatically.
- Insufficient baselines and ablations:
  - No comparison to strong neural baselines such as a sentence-level BiLSTM or transformer encoders (even a small BERT/RoBERTa fine-tune focusing on the spans and marker), or simpler classifiers (bag-of-words/logistic regression, averaged embeddings). Given the simplicity of the proposed model, stronger baselines are necessary to substantiate claims.
  - No ablation separating the effect of the marker token, the event encoders, or the “stated” vs “pair” connection beyond a few configurations. The “zero-padding strategy” (max/mean/mode) appears to conflate sequence-length selection/truncation choices with padding semantics and is insufficiently justified.
- Clarity and rigor issues:
  - Several tables and numbers are inconsistent or unclear (e.g., corpus sizes, formatting of counts). The notation in Section 3.3 is confusing: pad(x) = {max, mean, mode} is not standard; vec(x) mapping IR^{n×d}→IR^{nd} with a dense layer needs clarification; it’s unclear how padding/truncation are actually implemented.
  - Training protocol lacks detail (no dev set description, early stopping, seeds, variance across runs). Statistical testing is reported only for the reduced dataset and only for one comparison.
  - Metrics: Reporting F1 on the causal class is useful, but accuracy is not informative under class imbalance; macro-F1 or per-marker macro-averages would be more appropriate. No calibration or error-type breakdown beyond a few examples.
- Modest and uneven gains: Improvements over Hidey & McKeown are small (often ~0.5–2 F1) and not shown to be statistically significant on the original splits. On bootstrapped data, gains are marginal and mixed across precision/recall.

Questions to authors
1) Experimental protocol:
   - How were train/dev/test splits constructed for hyperparameter selection? Are results averaged over multiple seeds/runs? Please report mean±std.
   - Which hyperparameters were tuned on which split? Was any test data used in tuning?

2) Padding/truncation:
   - Please precisely define the “max/mean/mode” padding strategies. Are you truncating sequences to the mean/mode length? If so, how much input is dropped on average, and how do results change without truncation (e.g., padding to the per-batch max)?

3) Role of the marker:
   - How much of the gain remains if the marker token(s) are masked? Can you report per-marker performance, and a leave-one-marker-out evaluation to test generalization to unseen markers?

4) Baselines:
   - Why no comparison to a sentence-level BiLSTM or a lightweight transformer (fine-tuned on AltLex) that encodes the full sentence with span indicators? These are standard for relation classification and may set a stronger bar than the SVM.

5) Generalization and datasets:
   - Can you evaluate on PDTB causal vs temporal for ambiguous markers, or on Causal-TimeBank, even if small (few-shot or zero-shot), to demonstrate cross-corpus behavior?
   - How are event spans obtained in your experiments (gold vs predicted)? If gold, what is the anticipated performance drop in a realistic end-to-end setting?

6) Reproducibility:
   - Please release code, preprocessing scripts (event span extraction), and exact data splits to ensure replicability.

Overall, while the problem is worthwhile and the empirical results on AltLex are promising, the contribution is incremental, and the evaluation leaves open key questions about novelty, robustness, and generalization. I encourage substantial revision with stronger baselines, ablations controlling for marker identity, clearer methodology, and broader evaluation.
