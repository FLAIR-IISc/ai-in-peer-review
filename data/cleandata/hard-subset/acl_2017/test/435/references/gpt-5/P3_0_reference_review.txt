Summary and contributions
This paper tackles causal meaning disambiguation: given two annotated events (e1, e2) and a lexical marker l suspected to signal causality, decide whether the relation is Causal or Non-Causal. The authors argue that causality lacks a fixed syntactic signature; thus, context encoding is crucial. They propose two LSTM-based architectures that encode (1) the first event and (2) the concatenation of the marker and second event, followed by an MLP classifier. They evaluate on the AltLex corpus (Hidey & McKeown, 2016) and report small but consistent gains over a majority-per-marker baseline and a feature-rich SVM baseline.

As I see it, the main contributions are:
- A simple neural architecture for causal lexical marker disambiguation that slightly improves over a strong feature-based SVM baseline on AltLex, across both original and bootstrapped versions.
- An empirical analysis highlighting precision/recall trade-offs and behavior on ambiguous markers, with some qualitative examples.
- A task framing separating causal meaning classification from argument identification, with a focus on the former.

Strengths
- Focus on context rather than fixed patterns: The paper rightly emphasizes that causality often cannot be captured by a small set of patterns or resources. Using sequence encoders to represent context is a sound direction and is supported by qualitative examples (e.g., disambiguating “which then” and non-causal uses of causative verbs such as “break”).
- Competitive results on a challenging dataset: On the AltLex “non-bootstrapping” set, the best model improves F1 on the Causal class by ~1.6–1.7 points and accuracy by ~6 points over the SVM baseline; on the bootstrapped set, it edges out the SVM by ~1 point F1 and ~0.6–1 point accuracy. Given the strength of the per-marker majority baseline, these are non-trivial gains.
- Sensible architecture comparison and optimizer analysis: The comparison between “Stated Pair LSTM” (state-passing) and “Pair LSTM” (independent encoders) is informative; the latter performs better, suggesting that cross-sentence interaction may be better modeled via later layers than via state initialization. The optimizer analysis (Adam vs Adadelta) and its impact on precision/recall balance is useful.
- Attention to class imbalance: The authors attempt a reduced negative class experiment and report significance vs the majority baseline (McNemar), helping validate robustness under imbalance changes.
- Clarity on task scope: By explicitly isolating causal meaning disambiguation (with gold e1, l, e2) from argument identification, the paper avoids conflating subproblems and clarifies what is and isn’t addressed.

Weaknesses
- Limited novelty and unclear modeling details: The core method (two LSTM encoders + MLP) is standard for pair classification. More importantly, critical architectural details are unclear or internally inconsistent. The paper calls “zero-padding strategies” the use of max/mean/mode operations (which are pooling, not padding), and Equations (1–2) do not align with an LSTM over sequences followed by pooling; dimensions are unclear. A reproducible, precise description (sequence lengths, pooling over LSTM outputs vs embeddings, hidden sizes, layers, dropout placement) is missing.
- Narrow and potentially unrealistic evaluation setup: The model assumes gold identification of e1, e2, and l at test time (and concatenates l with e2). This limits applicability in end-to-end discourse parsing or IE pipelines where arguments and triggers must be detected. Moreover, the evaluation is confined to a single corpus (AltLex), which has known construction biases. No cross-corpus validation (e.g., PDTB explicit/AltLex held-out, Causal-TimeBank) is provided, so generalization is unclear.
- Incomplete baselines and ablations: Beyond the SVM and per-marker majority, there is no comparison to simple neural baselines (e.g., BiLSTM or CNN with bag-of-words/averaged embeddings, logistic regression over n-grams) nor to pairwise interaction models (decomposable attention/ESIM) that are well-suited to two-sequence classification. Ablations isolating the contribution of e1, l, and e2 (e.g., only l, l+e2, e1+e2, full) are missing, making it hard to assess whether the model truly leverages broader context vs memorizes marker priors.
- Modest gains and missing statistical testing vs SVM: Improvements over the SVM are small (≈1 F1 point on the larger “bootstrapping” set) and may be within variance. There is no significance testing against B2 (only against the majority baseline in the reduced set). Without multiple runs or confidence intervals, the robustness of gains is uncertain.
- Related work coverage and motivation gaps: The argument that CNNs are unsuitable because they require a fixed “context length” is weak; modern CNNs (stacked/dilated) model long contexts. The paper does not engage with recent neural discourse relation classification literature or contextual encoders. Even if one aims for simplicity, discussing why more expressive pairwise interaction or attention-based encoders were not used is important.

Questions to authors
- Architecture clarity: Please provide a precise, reproducible description. Are you running an LSTM over token sequences and then applying a pooling function (max/mean/mode) over time, or are you pooling the embeddings before the LSTM? What are the LSTM hidden sizes, number of layers, dropout rates (where applied), batch size, training epochs, early stopping criteria, and random seed handling?
- Input representation: Why is the second input “l + e2” instead of the full sentence or “e1 + l + e2” with markers around argument spans? Did you try including both flanking context around l, or models without the explicit l token to test reliance on overall context?
- Ablations: What is performance when using only l, only e2, only e1, or e1+e2 without l? This would substantiate the claim that context, rather than marker priors, drives gains.
- Generalization: Can you report results on PDTB explicit causal relations and/or Causal-TimeBank to test cross-corpus robustness? Conversely, train on PDTB and test on AltLex (and vice versa).
- Statistical testing and multiple runs: Are improvements over the SVM significant across multiple random seeds? Please report mean±std and significance tests vs B2 on both corpus versions.
- Handling class imbalance: Beyond downsampling negatives, did you try class-weighted losses or focal loss? How sensitive are results to the class distribution?
- Code and data splits: Will you release code and exact preprocessing? Please specify tokenization, OOV handling with GloVe 840B, maximum sequence lengths, and the exact train/dev/test splits used for model selection.

Overall, the paper addresses a relevant problem with a sensible, simple neural approach and offers modest improvements. However, to meet ACL standards, the modeling description must be clarified, baselines and ablations strengthened, and generalization and statistical rigor improved.
