Summary
The paper tackles causal meaning disambiguation for lexical markers (e.g., since, after) by framing it as a binary classification task over two events and a candidate marker. It proposes a two-input LSTM architecture (Pair LSTM vs. “Stated Pair” where the second LSTM is initialized from the first) using GloVe embeddings, and evaluates on the AltLex corpus (bootstrapped and non-bootstrapped splits), reporting improvements over a per-marker majority baseline and a feature-based SVM (Hidey & McKeown, 2016). Some small ablations (padding strategy, optimizer) and a brief qualitative analysis are presented.

Main contributions (as I see them)
- Empirical finding: a simple, context-encoding neural model (Pair LSTM) outperforms a strong per-marker baseline and a feature-based SVM on AltLex, including in a more challenging bootstrapped setting (e.g., F1 up to 81.56 and accuracy 80.68 with Pair LSTM Mean Adam).
- Task framing and analysis: explicit decomposition of “causality classification” into (i) causal meaning disambiguation and (ii) causal argument identification, and analysis showing the importance of context to handle ambiguous connectives and AltLexes.
- Ablations and significance: comparisons of two connection strategies (Pair vs. Stated Pair), optimizers (Adam/Adadelta), padding heuristics, and a McNemar test demonstrating significance over the per-marker baseline in a rebalanced setting.

Strengths
- Addresses an under-explored ambiguity: The focus on context-driven disambiguation of causal markers (beyond fixed connective lists) is important and practically relevant. The examples show the system can flip decisions against marker priors when context dictates.
- Empirical improvements on AltLex: The model improves over both the per-marker majority baseline and the feature-based SVM. On non-bootstrapping, Pair LSTM Max Adadelta improves Causal F1 by 2.13 points and accuracy by 7.72 over the SVM; on bootstrapping, several LSTM configs match or slightly exceed SVM F1 with better precision-recall balance in the best case (Pair LSTM Mean Adam).
- Simple, general approach: Using only pre-trained word vectors and LSTMs without hand-engineered lexical resources demonstrates generalization potential and reduces dependence on resource coverage.
- Some interpretability/analysis: The paper inspects difficult markers (e.g., “which then”) and cases where causative verbs do not imply causation, aligning with the claim that context encoding matters. The significance test under class rebalancing is a plus.

Weaknesses
- Limited novelty in method: Two-input LSTM encoders for relation classification are well established; the architectural variations (state initialization, padding heuristics) and optimizer choices are incremental. For ACL, the methodological contribution is weak, especially given the prevalence of transformer-based contextual encoders.
- Missing contemporary baselines: No comparisons to pretrained transformer models (e.g., BERT/RoBERTa) or even BiLSTM+attention/CRF variants. Given the task is sentence-level relation classification, a simple fine-tuned transformer is a strong and standard baseline likely to outperform LSTM+GloVe, weakening the paper’s claims.
- Evaluation scope and dataset limitations: All results are confined to AltLex. No tests on PDTB causal relations, Causal-TimeBank, or transfer settings. The model assumes gold segmentation of e1, l, e2 and does not test robustness to automatic extraction, limiting real-world applicability.
- Baseline clarity and fairness: It is unclear how faithfully the Hidey & McKeown SVM was reproduced (feature sets, parameterization, data processing). Given the sensitivity of feature-based baselines, more detail (or code) is needed to ensure fair comparison.
- Methodological clarity issues: The padding discussion/equations are confusing; it reads as choosing pad length by max/mean/mode rather than standard zero-padding up to max length. Details about tokenization, sequence truncation, LSTM directionality (uni/bi), hidden sizes, training splits (train/dev/test) and early stopping are not fully specified. Reporting accuracy on imbalanced data without macro metrics is suboptimal.

Questions to authors
- Can you clarify the exact padding/truncation procedure? What does “mean/mode” padding length mean in practice, and how were sequences longer than that handled?
- Are the LSTMs bidirectional? If not, did you try BiLSTM or attention over the encoded events?
- How exactly was the SVM baseline from Hidey & McKeown implemented (feature list, data preprocessing, hyperparameters)? Can you release code or a detailed reproduction protocol?
- Why not evaluate transformer baselines (e.g., fine-tuned BERT/RoBERTa) that model context end-to-end? If tried, how do they compare?
- How are e1, l, e2 obtained at test time? Is the model contingent on gold spans? What happens if spans are noisy or automatically extracted?
- Did you use a development set for model selection and early stopping? If so, please detail the split and selection criterion.
- Beyond AltLex, can you report results on PDTB causal relations and/or Causal-TimeBank to test generalization?
- Have you tried removing the marker token from the model input to quantify how much the model relies on context versus the marker itself?

Overall recommendation: Reject. The paper’s central idea—contextual neural encoding for causal marker disambiguation—is sensible and the empirical gains on AltLex are encouraging, but the methodological novelty is limited and the evaluation lacks comparisons to contemporary pretrained LMs and broader datasets. Strengthening the work with transformer baselines, cross-corpus evaluations, clearer baselines, and more rigorous methodological detail could make it much more compelling.
