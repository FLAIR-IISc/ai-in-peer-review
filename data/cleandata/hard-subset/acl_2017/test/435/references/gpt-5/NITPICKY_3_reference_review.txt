Summary and claimed contributions
This paper tackles causal meaning disambiguation between two annotated events in a sentence, given a potentially causal lexical marker. The authors propose a two-input LSTM-based architecture that encodes (i) e1 and (ii) [marker + e2], followed by dense layers for classification. They evaluate on the AltLex corpus (Hidey & McKeown, 2016), compare to a per-marker majority baseline and an SVM feature-based system, and report improvements in F1/accuracy in some settings.

As I see it, the main contributions are:
1) A simple LSTM-based pair-input classifier for causal meaning disambiguation, avoiding reliance on external lexical resources.
2) An empirical evaluation on the AltLex corpus with several implementation choices (two architectural variants, different optimizers, sequence-length settings), showing modest gains over a strong feature baseline on one split and roughly parity on another.
3) A small qualitative analysis of ambiguous markers (e.g., “which then”) suggesting contextual encoding can help beyond connective identity.

Strengths
- Addresses a relevant task: Focusing on disambiguating causal meaning rather than extracting arguments is useful, and AltLex is a suitable resource to probe ambiguity in causal markers. This scope is crisp and practically important.
- Simple, resource-lean approach: Using only pre-trained GloVe embeddings (without lexical knowledge bases) is attractive in terms of portability and potential domain robustness.
- Strong baselines included: The per-connective majority baseline (B1) is non-trivial given class skew, and the SVM feature system (B2) is a reasonable prior state of the art on AltLex.
- Some gains on the non-bootstrapped split: On the easier split, the best LSTM variant surpasses the feature baseline by 2–8 points (F1/accuracy), indicating that even a relatively simple neural encoder can learn beyond connective identity.

Weaknesses
- Methodological clarity and rigor are insufficient.
  - Model details are underspecified: hidden sizes, number of LSTM layers, uni vs bi-directional, whether embeddings are fine-tuned, pooling over time (last state vs pooling), batch size, epochs, early stopping, learning rates, regularization application points, random seed(s), and number of runs. No variance or confidence intervals; results appear to be single runs.
  - No development set or tuning protocol is described. Multiple configurations are compared directly on test, risking test-set overfitting and selection bias.
  - The “zero-padding strategies” are unclear/inconsistent. Writing pad(x) = {max, mean, mode} over IR^{n×d} is not meaningful mathematically; it likely refers to choosing the maximum/mean/mode sequence length for truncation/padding, but the paper’s notation (including W ∈ R^{100×nd}, tanh(W·x+b)) is dimensionally inconsistent.
- Evaluation design does not isolate contextual learning from connective identity.
  - The model’s second input includes the lexical marker; with heavy class skew per marker, the model may primarily learn connective identity rather than context. The strong performance of B1 underscores this risk.
  - There is no ablation excluding the marker (or masking it) to demonstrate that the model truly uses context. Nor is there a cross-connective generalization test (train without a marker, test on that marker) to support claims about generalization to unseen causal expressions.
- Limited and uneven improvements; metrics not robust to imbalance.
  - On the “bootstrapping” split (arguably harder and closer to the authors’ stated motivation), improvements over B2 are small and inconsistent; sometimes B2 has higher recall. Reporting only accuracy and F1 for the causal class (not macro-F1 or AUROC/PR-AUC) is problematic given strong imbalance and differing operating points. No statistical significance is shown versus B2; only a McNemar test against B1 on a modified dataset.
- Data and results reporting issues.
  - Multiple tables/figures are missing or malformed (e.g., Table 3 not shown; Table 4/6 contain garbled numbers; Figure 1/2 not provided). The test/train sizes and class distributions are unclear and sometimes contradictory.
  - The “reduced dataset” experiment is ambiguous: it is not clear whether undersampling applies only to training or also to test; yet test scores are reported. This undermines interpretability and comparability.
- Related work coverage and novelty positioning are weak.
  - Prior neural approaches to causal/contingency relations are cited (e.g., Kruengkrai et al., 2017), but there is no direct comparison or stronger baselines (e.g., BiLSTM over full sentence; average-of-embeddings classifier; attention models). The contribution is incremental without a compelling novelty argument or broader analysis (e.g., performance per marker type, length, syntactic configuration).

Questions to authors
1) What is the exact LSTM configuration (uni/bi-directional, hidden size(s), number of layers)? What pooling over time is used (last hidden state, mean/max pooling, attention)? Are embeddings fine-tuned?
2) What is the tuning protocol? Is there a held-out development set? How were dropout/regularization/optimizer and sequence-length choices selected? How many runs/seeds per configuration, and what are mean±std results?
3) How exactly are “max/mean/mode” padding strategies implemented? What are the resulting sequence lengths for e1 and [l+e2]? How do you truncate long sequences, and how many tokens are truncated on average?
4) Please clarify the dataset statistics: training/dev/test sizes, class distributions (per split), and the exact content of Tables 3, 4, and 6. In the “reduced dataset” experiment, which split(s) were undersampled—training only or also test?
5) To what extent does the model rely on the lexical marker identity? Can you report:
   - Results when the marker is masked or excluded?
   - Cross-marker generalization (train excluding a marker, test on that marker)?
   - Performance on instances where train-time majority for the marker contradicts test label (you partially discuss this; please quantify)?
6) Can you compare against stronger neural baselines (e.g., BiLSTM over the entire sentence; average-of-embeddings MLP; attention pooling), and report macro-F1 and PR-AUC to handle class imbalance?
7) Please provide statistical significance vs B2 (not only vs B1), including contingency tables or bootstrap tests.

Overall recommendation
While the task is meaningful and the approach is straightforward, the paper suffers from substantial methodological ambiguities, missing details, unclear or malformed tables/figures, and limited evidence that the model truly leverages context beyond connective identity. Given the incremental novelty and evaluation/design issues, I cannot recommend acceptance in its current form.
