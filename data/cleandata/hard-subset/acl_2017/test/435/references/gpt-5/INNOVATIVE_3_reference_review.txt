Summary and contributions
- Proposes a simple two-branch LSTM architecture (“Pair LSTM” and a variant with state transfer, “Stated Pair LSTM”) to disambiguate whether the relation between two annotated events is causal, given the context and a (possibly ambiguous) lexical marker.
- Empirically evaluates on the AltLex corpus, reporting consistent improvements over (i) a majority-per-marker baseline and (ii) the feature-engineered SVM from Hidey & McKeown (2016), with some analysis of ambiguous connectives and class imbalance.
- Argues for framing causality classification as two subtasks (meaning disambiguation and argument identification) and for context-based neural encoding over hand-crafted lexical/syntactic features.

Strengths
- Problem focus on ambiguous causal markers (e.g., since, after, AltLex forms) is valuable. The paper demonstrates that encoding event contexts improves over a strong per-marker majority baseline and over a feature-based SVM, particularly in precision and overall accuracy on the bootstrapped split. The qualitative examples (e.g., which then) are persuasive.
- Minimal feature engineering. Using only pre-trained GloVe embeddings and a simple LSTM yields competitive performance without reliance on WordNet/VerbNet/PropBank coverage, supporting the claim that distributional context helps capture causal meaning beyond curated resources.
- Systematic, if lightweight, exploration: compares two architectural variants (with/without state transfer), two optimizers (Adadelta/Adam), and several pooling/padding strategies; includes an experiment on a more balanced subset and reports a McNemar test there. The negative result that state transfer did not help is also informative.
- Clear practical takeaway: encoding each argument independently and combining them downstream worked better than forcing an explicit state-level dependency, suggesting that causal disambiguation benefits from flexible composition rather than hard coupling.

Weaknesses
- Limited novelty. LSTM-based pair encoding for relation/semantic disambiguation is well-trodden. The modeling choices (two input LSTMs + dense layers) are standard, and the “Stated Pair” variant is a minor tweak. The argument against CNNs is not empirically supported. In 2025, lack of comparison to contextualized transformer baselines (e.g., BERT/RoBERTa fine-tuning on the full sentence or spans) substantially weakens the contribution.
- Narrow and fragile evaluation. All results are on a single dataset (AltLex), with gains modest relative to a very strong per-marker majority baseline and the SVM. There is no cross-corpus validation (e.g., PDTB explicit/implicit causals, Causal-TimeBank) and no robustness checks across domains. Several improvements are small and significance is only reported for a subset.
- Methodological clarity gaps. The “zero-padding strategy” (max/mean/mode) is unclear/mis-specified in equations; it reads more like pooling than padding. It’s not specified whether the two LSTMs share weights, whether the representation uses last hidden state vs. pooling over time, whether GloVe is fine-tuned, and how OOVs are handled. Hyperparameter selection, early stopping, and number of runs/variance are not reported; results may be sensitive to random seeds and class imbalance.
- Missing stronger baselines and ablations. No comparison to simple but strong neural baselines (e.g., average-of-embeddings + MLP; BiLSTM with attention over the full sentence; transformer fine-tuning). No ablations that isolate the contribution of the lexical marker vs. event contexts (only marker, only events, removing l), or swapping e1/e2 to test temporal direction sensitivity.
- Presentation/rigor issues. Several typographical and referencing problems, incomplete citations, and some theoretical discussion that is not tied to experiments. Metrics are reported per causal class and accuracy, but macro/micro F1 over both classes and performance specifically on truly ambiguous connectives are not systematically analyzed. Code/model release is not mentioned.

Questions to authors
- Clarify the sequence handling: What exactly do “max/mean/mode” padding strategies do? Are these pooling operations over time? What is the final sequence representation fed to the dense layers (last hidden state, mean/max pooling, concatenation)?
- Do the two LSTMs share parameters? If not, why? Did you try shared weights?
- Are GloVe embeddings fine-tuned during training? How are OOV tokens handled?
- What are e1/e2 spans concretely in AltLex—contiguous spans provided by the corpus? How often are they full clauses vs. NPs? Any preprocessing heuristics?
- How many random seeds were run? Please report mean ± std for the main tables and significance for improvements over B2 on both splits.
- Stronger baselines: Can you compare against (i) average-of-embeddings + logistic/MLP; (ii) BiLSTM with attention over entire sentence; (iii) a transformer model (e.g., RoBERTa-base) fine-tuned on the task using sentence text with span markers for e1, l, e2?
- Ablations: What happens if you remove the marker l; use only l; or swap e1/e2? How sensitive is the model to event boundary errors?
- Generalization: Can you evaluate on PDTB (explicit/implicit causal) or Causal-TimeBank to test cross-corpus transfer, possibly via zero-shot or fine-tuning?

Overall assessment
While the paper addresses a relevant problem and shows that simple neural context encoding improves over feature-heavy baselines on AltLex, the modeling and empirical novelty are limited for ACL, and the evaluation lacks breadth and modern baselines. Without stronger comparisons (especially to transformers) and clearer methodology/analysis, the contribution feels incremental. I recommend rejection in its current form, but encourage a revision that adds stronger baselines/ablations, clarifies methodology, and broadens evaluation.
