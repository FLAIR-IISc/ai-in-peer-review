- Paper tackles causal meaning disambiguation: given e1, a marker l, and e2, decide if the relation is causal; proposes Pair LSTM and Stated Pair LSTM and evaluates on AltLex with GloVe.  
- Empirical contribution: a two-branch LSTM encoding (e1) and (l+e2) outperforms a majority-by-marker baseline and a 2016 SVM on AltLex (both bootstrapped and non-bootstrapped).  
- Framing/analysis contribution: clearly argues limits of marker-only/syntactic approaches and analyzes ambiguous markers and bootstrapping-induced distribution shift.  
- Methodological contribution: compares two related architectures (with vs. without state initialization) and simple padding-length choices.  
- Strength: addresses a real gap—disambiguation for ambiguous causal markers—motivating context-based modeling over rule/resource-heavy systems.  
- Strength: empirical improvements are consistent and show better precision-recall balance in some bootstrapped configurations.  
- Strength: modeling insight that independent encoders (Pair LSTM) outperform state-passing (Stated LSTM), a useful negative result.  
- Strength: analysis indicates the model learns beyond majority-by-marker heuristics and shows some robustness to distribution shift.  
- Strength: resource-light approach using only pre-trained embeddings (no hand-engineered lexical features) improves portability.  
- Weakness: limited technical novelty—two-branch LSTMs and dense fusion are standard—and several modeling details are underspecified (uni/bidirectional, sizes, pooling, weight sharing, embedding tuning).  
- Weakness: evaluation and reproducibility concerns—many hyperparameter choices with no development set protocol, no multiple seeds/variance reporting, and some apparent table typos/missing numbers.  
- Weakness: baseline coverage is insufficient—no comparison to text-CNN, single-sequence BiLSTM/attention, averaged-embedding classifiers, or transformer baselines; some claims about CNNs are inaccurate.  
- Weakness: narrow task scope and data dependence—the setup assumes given e1/l/e2 and only tests AltLex, lacking evaluation on PDTB, Causal-TimeBank, or cross-domain transfer.  
- Weakness: presentation issues—grammatical/formatting errors, incomplete references, and unclear figures/tables hinder understanding and reproducibility.  
- Question: what exact train/dev/test splits were used and was a development set used for hyperparameter/model selection and early stopping?  
- Question: model specifics—are LSTMs bidirectional, what are hidden sizes/layers, are embeddings fine-tuned, do branches share weights, and what pooling feeds the dense layer?  
- Question: input construction—how are e1, l, e2 extracted and is additional sentence context included; how are long spans truncated under different padding choices?  
- Question: did you run experiments masking connective tokens to test whether the model relies on context rather than memorizing marker identities, and how does it perform on unseen markers?  
- Question: can you report stronger baselines: (a) single-sequence BiLSTM/attention over full sentence; (b) multi-kernel CNN; (c) averaged-embeddings logistic regression?  
- Question: have you tested generalization on PDTB explicit causal relations, Causal-TimeBank, or via cross-domain validation to assess robustness?  
- Overall: modest but consistent gains on AltLex and useful analyses, but limited novelty, underspecified methodology, weak baseline coverage, and presentation/reproducibility issues weaken the submission.
