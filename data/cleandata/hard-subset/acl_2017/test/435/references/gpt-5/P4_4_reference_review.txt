Summary and main contributions
The paper addresses causal meaning disambiguation: given two annotated events (e1, e2) and a potentially causal lexical marker l, determine whether the relation is Causal or Non Causal. The authors argue that causality is not tied to fixed syntactic patterns and propose neural context encoding as a more general solution. They evaluate on the AltLex corpus (Hidey and McKeown, 2016), including both “non bootstrapping” and “bootstrapping” splits, and compare against a majority-per-connective baseline and the SVM feature-based system from Hidey and McKeown (2016).

As seen by this review, the main contributions are:
1) An LSTM-based two-input architecture that encodes e1 separately from the concatenation of l and e2, with systematic comparisons of two coupling strategies (state transfer vs independent LSTMs), several “padding/pooling” strategies (max/mean/mode), and optimizers (Adadelta/Adam), showing consistent improvements over strong baselines on AltLex.
2) An empirical analysis on AltLex highlighting class imbalance and connective ambiguity, including evaluation on the bootstrapped split with distribution shift and a downsampled version to study balance effects, plus examples indicating improved handling of ambiguous markers.

Strengths
- Clear motivation and task formulation. The paper articulates why relying on fixed syntactic patterns or lexicon-driven causatives is limiting, and reframes the problem as context-based disambiguation. The decomposition into two subtasks (meaning disambiguation; argument identification) is reasonable and aligns with prior observations.
- Competitive empirical results on a relevant corpus. On the AltLex “non bootstrapping” split, the best model improves over Hidey and McKeown (2016) by +1.6–2.1 F1 on the Causal class and +7.7 accuracy; on the “bootstrapping” split, it matches or slightly exceeds the prior system (e.g., F1 ~+1.1, accuracy ~+0.6). These gains hold across multiple architectural/optimizer variants, indicating robustness.
- Context encoding without external lexicons. The approach uses pre-trained GloVe embeddings and LSTMs, avoiding dependence on WordNet/VerbNet/PropBank. This supports better generalization to AltLex expressions not covered by curated resources and is consistent with the observed higher precision.
- Systematic ablations and practical insights. The comparison between “Stated Pair LSTM” (state transfer) and “Pair LSTM” (independent encoders) shows the latter generally works better, suggesting that independent encoding followed by dense layers to model interactions is sufficient. Optimizer choice trade-offs (Adadelta increasing recall vs Adam balancing precision/recall) are informative for reproducibility. The ambiguity analysis (e.g., “which then”) and non-causative uses of causative verbs (“break”) are useful qualitative evidence that context matters.
- Attention to dataset properties. The paper examines connective distributions and class imbalance, evaluates under distribution shift (bootstrapping split), and runs an additional experiment with reduced Non Causal instances, reporting significance improvements over the majority baseline via McNemar’s test.

Weaknesses
- Limited technical novelty. The architecture (two LSTMs, concatenation, MLP classifier, pre-trained embeddings) is standard. The main novelty lies in applying it to AltLex causal disambiguation. Without broader evaluations or methodological advances (e.g., attention over context, modeling full-sentence discourse structure), the contribution is incremental.
- Ambiguity and clarity of the “padding” vs pooling operation. The paper describes “zero-padding strategies” using max/mean/mode with a formula, which is atypical (padding is usually a fixed token; max/mean/mode are pooling/aggregation operations). It is unclear whether these are time-pooling over token sequences, padding values, or something else. This confound reduces reproducibility and technical clarity.
- Incomplete experimental details and limited baselines. Key hyperparameters are missing (LSTM hidden sizes, sequence truncation lengths, number of layers, early stopping, batch size, training epochs, dev set usage, seed). There is no baseline that uses only the connective l (or l+local window) to quantify the added value of encoding e1/e2. Significance testing is only reported vs the majority baseline, not vs the prior SVM system. Reporting focuses on the Causal class; macro-averaged metrics and ROC/PR curves are absent, which matters given imbalance.
- Narrow evaluation scope. All results are on a single, automatically constructed corpus (AltLex) derived from Wikipedia/Simple Wikipedia alignment. No cross-corpus validation (e.g., PDTB explicit/AltLex-like subsets, Causal-TimeBank) is provided, and no robustness checks to noisy event spans are reported. Since the method assumes gold event spans and the marker l, it evaluates a subtask that may be easier than end-to-end causal relation detection.
- Potential confounds and modeling choices. The second input is “marker + e2”; often context across the full sentence and inter-event material influences disambiguation. Restricting to e1 and l+e2 may omit discriminative cues. The discussion around class distribution (e.g., many unambiguous connectives) suggests that gains could partly reflect connective identity; an analysis controlling for connective identity (e.g., per-connective F1 or leave-one-connective-out) would strengthen the causal claim that context encoding drives improvements.

Questions to authors
- Please clarify the “zero-padding strategies” defined as max/mean/mode. Are these time-wise pooling operations over token embeddings? If not, how are they applied, and where in the pipeline? A precise mathematical description and schematic would help.
- What are the exact LSTM and training hyperparameters (hidden sizes, number of layers, dropout locations, batch size, learning rate schedules, epochs, early stopping criteria, random seeds)? Was a development set used for model selection?
- How are event spans (e1, e2) obtained and tokenized? Are they gold spans from AltLex, and are any surrounding words included? How are OOV tokens handled?
- How much does the connective alone explain performance? Please report baselines using only l (and l+small window) vs full inputs, and a per-connective breakdown or a leave-one-connective-out evaluation to assess reliance on connective identity.
- Can you report macro-averaged F1 across both classes, and significance tests vs B2? Additionally, provide confusion matrices and error analysis stratified by ambiguous vs unambiguous markers.
- Did you try encoding the full sentence (or both arguments plus intervening text) and/or attention mechanisms across tokens? If so, how did they compare?
- Will code, preprocessing scripts, and exact train/dev/test splits be released to enable replication?
