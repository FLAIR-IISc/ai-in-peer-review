Summary
The paper addresses causal meaning disambiguation: given two events and a lexical marker potentially indicating causality, determine whether the relation is causal or non-causal. The authors argue that rigid syntactic patterns or lexicon-driven features are insufficient, and propose LSTM-based architectures that encode the two event spans (and the lexical marker) to classify the relation. Experiments are conducted on the AltLex corpus (Hidey & McKeown, 2016), comparing against a per-connective majority baseline and the SVM-based method of Hidey & McKeown. The authors report small improvements in F1 and accuracy on both the “non-bootstrapping” and “bootstrapping” versions of the corpus, and present a small qualitative analysis.

Contributions (as I see them)
1) Empirical finding: A simple two-input LSTM-based classifier can match or modestly outperform a strong feature-rich SVM baseline on AltLex causal disambiguation, particularly improving precision.
2) Experimental observation: Tying the hidden state between the two event encoders (“Stated Pair LSTM”) underperforms independent encoders combined via dense layers, suggesting the relation is better modeled post-encoding.
3) Minor analysis: A few illustrative examples where the neural model correctly disambiguates contextually ambiguous markers that the baseline misclassifies.

Strengths
- Problem focus on contextually ambiguous causal markers: The paper targets a real gap in discourse analysis, namely that many causal markers (e.g., since, after, which then) are ambiguous and require context-sensitive modeling beyond rule-based or lexicon-limited approaches.
- Simple neural architecture with reasonable baselines: The comparison to both a connective-majority baseline and the feature-rich SVM baseline from the dataset paper is appropriate. The neural model achieves competitive or slightly better F1/accuracy, especially higher precision, which is valuable in downstream settings where false positives are costly.
- Attention to data imbalance: The authors explicitly discuss the skewed class distribution and provide an additional experiment with a reduced non-causal class, including a McNemar test indicating significance in that specific setting.
- Some qualitative analysis: The examples in Table 8 illustrate that context encoding can help disambiguate cases where lexical priors are misleading (e.g., break used non-causally; which then).

Weaknesses
- Serious methodological clarity issues and inconsistencies:
  - The “zero-padding strategies” are ill-defined. Equation (1) uses max/mean/mode as if they were pooling operations over input matrices, not sequence-length choices for padding/truncation. It is unclear how sequence lengths were chosen, how truncation was handled, and whether pooling was used at all. The term “zero-padding” is used incorrectly.
  - The model description is internally inconsistent. The text alternately suggests (i) LSTM encodings are produced, then (ii) the outputs are “vectorized” by a dense layer that takes flattened IR^{n×d} inputs with W in IR^{100×nd} (Equation (2)), which contradicts the standard use of the LSTM’s final hidden state. The exact computational graph (including whether LSTM outputs are used or ignored) is unclear. Figure 1 is referenced but not provided.
  - The “Stated Pair LSTM” is not precisely specified (how are initial states transferred? are LSTMs bidirectional? number/size of layers?).
  - Optimizer, dropout, regularization are only vaguely specified; no batch size, epochs, early stopping, dev split, or seed control are reported.
- Data description and results contain contradictions and omissions:
  - Corpus sizes are inconsistent across tables (e.g., Table 4 totals vs later training sizes; malformed numbers in Table 6, missing Table 3; inconsistent totals for bootstrapping vs non-bootstrapping). Train/dev/test splits are not described. It is unclear whether author numbers for B2 are reimplemented or copied from prior work, and whether experimental splits match H&M.
  - The evaluation focuses on F1 for the causal class and accuracy; macro-averaged F1 or per-class metrics would be more appropriate under imbalance. Only one significance test is reported, and only on an artificially rebalanced setting; no uncertainty estimates are given for the main results. Some gains are very small (≈0.5–2 F1), making significance doubtful.
- Limited novelty relative to discourse relation classification literature:
  - Applying LSTMs to relation disambiguation is not new; similar neural models have been used for implicit discourse relation classification in PDTB-style settings. The paper does not discuss or compare to that body of work (e.g., Rutherford & Xue 2016; Ji & Eisenstein 2015; Chen et al. 2016; Liu & Li 2016, among others). The architectural choices are standard and sparsely justified.
- Weak justification of input design:
  - The second input concatenates the lexical marker with the second event, but the first input omits the marker. There is no ablation to test whether including/excluding the marker, or using a single encoder over the full span, affects performance. No ablations on using BiLSTM vs LSTM, fine-tuning embeddings, or attention.
- Presentation and formatting quality:
  - Numerous typos/grammar issues throughout, malformed references (missing years, placeholders like “? \Q2017\E”), missing or mislabeled figures/tables, and incorrect naming of resources (e.g., “GloVe 840B cased” is typically uncased). These issues impede comprehension and reproducibility.

Questions to Authors
- Data splits: What are the exact train/dev/test splits for both corpus versions? Do they match those used in Hidey & McKeown (2016)? Are B2 numbers reproduced under your splits or copied? Please reconcile the contradictory corpus size numbers across tables.
- Model specifics: Are the LSTMs bidirectional? Hidden sizes? Number of layers? Do you use the final hidden state or the whole sequence output? Does Equation (2) actually flatten the padded input, or is it applied to the LSTM output? Please provide a precise, unambiguous description.
- Padding/truncation: What exactly do “max/mean/mode” strategies do? Do you pad to a fixed length equal to the max/mean/mode of observed lengths, and truncate longer sequences? How do results change under no truncation with dynamic RNNs?
- Embeddings: Are GloVe vectors fine-tuned during training? Which exact GloVe set is used (Common Crawl 840B, typically uncased)? How are OOV tokens handled?
- Evaluation: Did you tune hyperparameters on a held-out dev set? How many runs with different seeds, and what variance do you observe? Are the main test-set improvements significant?
- Fairness/ablations: Can you report ablations for (i) including/excluding the lexical marker; (ii) single concatenated input vs two-input model; (iii) state-tied vs independent encoders; (iv) BiLSTM; (v) removing pretraining?
- Reproducibility: Will you release code, scripts for data preprocessing (event extraction, tokenization, input construction), and exact configs to ensure replicability?

Overall recommendation: While the task is worthwhile and preliminary results are promising, the methodological ambiguities, data inconsistencies, limited novelty, and presentation issues are significant. I cannot recommend acceptance in its current form.
