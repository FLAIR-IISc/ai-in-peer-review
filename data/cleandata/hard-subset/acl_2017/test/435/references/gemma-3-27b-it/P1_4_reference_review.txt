## Peer Review for ACL Submission: "Neural Disambiguation of Causal Lexical Markers based on Context"

**Summary:**

This paper tackles the challenging problem of identifying causal relations between events, moving beyond reliance on explicit causal connectives. The authors argue that causality isn’t strictly tied to specific syntactic structures or lexical resources and propose a neural network architecture (specifically LSTMs) to encode contextual information for causal disambiguation. They evaluate their system on the AltLex corpus, demonstrating improvements over a strong baseline (Hidey & McKeown, 2016).  The paper correctly identifies limitations in previous approaches focused on syntactic patterns or limited lexical coverage.

**Main Contributions:**

1. **Empirical Validation of Contextual Encoding for Causal Disambiguation:** The paper provides empirical evidence, using a neural network, that encoding the context of events is effective for identifying causal relations, even when signalled by ambiguous lexical markers. This supports the authors' claim that causality isn’t solely defined by specific constructions.
2. **Evaluation on the AltLex Corpus:** Utilizing the AltLex corpus is valuable. This dataset is specifically designed to address the limitations of previous resources by focusing on implicit causal relations and ambiguous connectives, offering a more realistic benchmark for the task.
3. **Systematic Comparison & Strong Baseline:** The thorough comparison to a strong baseline (Hidey & McKeown, 2016) provides a credible demonstration of the proposed system's effectiveness.

**Strengths:**

1. **Well-Motivated Problem:** The paper clearly articulates the challenges with existing approaches to causality detection, highlighting the limitations of relying on syntactic structures or exhaustive lexical lists. The authors effectively argue for the need for methods that can leverage contextual information.
2. **Sound Methodology:** The use of LSTMs is a reasonable choice given the sequential nature of language and the need to capture long-range dependencies. The experimentation with different padding strategies and optimizers demonstrates a systematic approach to model configuration.
3. **Clear Results & Analysis:** The results are clearly presented and analyzed. The authors not only report overall performance but also delve into specific cases where their model outperforms the baseline, offering insights into its strengths and weaknesses. The analysis of performance on ambiguous connectives (like "since" and "after") is particularly valuable.
4. **Acknowledged Limitations & Future Work:** The authors openly acknowledge the limitations of their work, particularly the size and balance of the dataset, and propose future directions for research (creating a larger, more comprehensive corpus).

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Novelty of the Architecture:** While the application to causal disambiguation is valuable, the LSTM architecture itself isn't particularly novel. The core idea of using LSTMs for sequence encoding is well-established. The paper would benefit from a more in-depth discussion of *why* LSTMs are particularly well-suited for this specific task compared to other sequence modeling techniques.
2. **Lack of Ablation Studies:** The paper evaluates different configurations (Stated Pair LSTM vs. Pair LSTM) but lacks more granular ablation studies. For example, the impact of different embedding sizes, LSTM hidden layer sizes, or dropout rates isn't thoroughly investigated.  This makes it difficult to pinpoint the key components driving the performance gains.
3. **Insufficient Error Analysis:** While some examples are provided in Section 4.3, a more comprehensive error analysis would be beneficial.  Identifying systematic errors (e.g., failure to handle specific types of ambiguity, difficulty with long-distance dependencies) could inform future improvements to the model.
4. **Limited Scope of Evaluation:** The evaluation is solely focused on the AltLex corpus. While this corpus is valuable, assessing the generalizability of the approach to other datasets or domains would strengthen the findings.

**Questions to Authors:**

1. **Regarding architectural novelty:** Could you elaborate on the specific design choices within your LSTM architecture that are most critical for capturing causal relationships?  How does your approach differ from simply applying a standard LSTM to this task?
2. **Concerning error analysis:** Can you provide a more detailed analysis of the types of errors your system makes? What are the most common failure cases, and what linguistic phenomena seem to pose the greatest challenges?
3. **Regarding dataset limitations:** Given the class imbalance in the AltLex corpus, did you experiment with techniques like oversampling or cost-sensitive learning to mitigate its impact on performance?
4. **Future directions:** You mention creating a new corpus. What are the key characteristics of this corpus (size, annotation scheme, types of causal relations) that you envision would address the limitations of existing resources?



**Additional Comments:**

*  The reference list is comprehensive.
* The paper is generally well-written and easy to follow.
* A figure illustrating the "Pair LSTM" architecture would be beneficial.
* Consider including a discussion of related work on commonsense reasoning and its potential relevance to causality detection.
