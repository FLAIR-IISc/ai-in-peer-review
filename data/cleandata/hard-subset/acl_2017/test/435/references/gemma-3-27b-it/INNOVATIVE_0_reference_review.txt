This paper tackles the problem of causal relation disambiguation, moving beyond reliance on explicitly causal connectives. The authors argue, convincingly, that identifying causality requires understanding context rather than being strictly bound by syntactic patterns or pre-defined lexical resources. The core contribution is a neural network architecture (LSTM-based) applied to this problem, demonstrating improved performance over a strong baseline on the AltLex corpus. A secondary, but valuable contribution, is a thorough review of prior work highlighting the limitations of existing approaches.

**Strengths:**

1. **Novelty of Approach:** The central idea – that neural networks can learn to disambiguate causal meaning from context *without* strict adherence to predefined linguistic rules – is a strong one. This is a significant departure from many previous works which heavily rely on handcrafted features and lexical resources. This approach is particularly appealing given the inherent flexibility and nuance of natural language expression of causality.
2. **Strong Empirical Results:** The paper demonstrates a clear performance improvement over the existing state-of-the-art (Hidey & McKeown, 2016) on the AltLex corpus. While the improvements aren’t massive, they are statistically significant and support the authors’ hypothesis. The ablation studies exploring different LSTM configurations and optimizers are well-executed.
3. **Clear Motivation and Problem Definition:** The introduction does a great job of framing the problem and outlining the limitations of earlier approaches. The authors effectively motivate their work by pointing out the shortcomings of syntax-based and lexicon-based methods for capturing the complexities of causal reasoning in language. The clarification regarding the counterfactual constraint is also appreciated.
4. **Detailed Related Work:** The literature review is comprehensive and provides a solid foundation for understanding the challenges and existing attempts to address them. The authors demonstrate a deep understanding of the relevant research in causal reasoning and discourse analysis.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Scope of Evaluation:** The evaluation is solely based on the AltLex corpus. While this corpus is a valuable resource, it’s still relatively small and may not fully represent the diversity of causal expressions in natural language. Performance on other datasets or a more comprehensive evaluation would strengthen the claims.
2. **Modest Performance Gains:** While statistically significant, the performance gains over the baseline are not dramatic. This raises questions about the extent to which the proposed method truly unlocks a new level of understanding of causality, or simply refines existing techniques.
3. **Lack of Error Analysis:** The paper would benefit from a more detailed error analysis. Examining specific examples where the system fails would provide valuable insights into the limitations of the current approach and suggest avenues for future improvement. Are there specific types of causal expressions that are particularly challenging?
4. **Architecture Relatively Simple:** The LSTM architecture, while effective, is relatively standard. Exploring more sophisticated neural architectures (e.g., transformers) could potentially yield further improvements. However, given the focus on the *idea* of leveraging neural networks for contextual understanding, this is a less critical weakness.

**Questions to Authors:**

1.  Could you elaborate on the choice of GloVe embeddings? Did you experiment with other pre-trained embeddings (e.g., Word2Vec, fastText) or contextualized embeddings (e.g., BERT, RoBERTa)? If so, what were the results?
2.  The paper mentions the difficulty of handling ambiguous connectives like "since" and "after." Can you provide specific examples of sentences where these connectives caused misclassifications, and discuss the challenges in disambiguating their causal meaning?
3.  You correctly point out the limited size of the AltLex corpus. Do you have plans to create or utilize a larger corpus for future work? If so, can you describe your strategy for corpus construction and annotation?
4. What specific linguistic insights, if any, did you gain from analyzing the performance of your model on the AltLex corpus? Are there specific patterns or features that the model seems to be particularly sensitive to?



**Additional Comments:**

The paper is well-written and clearly organized. The formatting and presentation are excellent. The focus on a novel approach to a challenging problem, coupled with solid empirical results, makes this a promising contribution. I am inclined to accept this paper despite some minor weaknesses, as the core idea and initial results are compelling and represent a valuable step forward in the field.
