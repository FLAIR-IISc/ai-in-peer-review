## Review for ACL Submission: "Neural Disambiguation of Causal Lexical Markers based on Context"

**Summary:** This paper tackles the task of causal relation classification, specifically differentiating between causal and non-causal relationships signaled by potentially ambiguous lexical markers. The authors argue that existing approaches relying on syntactic structures or predefined lexical resources are limited and propose a neural network architecture based on LSTMs to encode contextual information for improved disambiguation. They evaluate their approach on the AltLex corpus, demonstrating improvements over a baseline system.

**Main Contributions (as I see them):**

1. **Empirical Evaluation on AltLex:** The paper provides a solid empirical evaluation of neural network approaches on the AltLex corpus, which is a valuable resource for this task due to its focus on ambiguous causal markers. This provides a benchmark for future work.
2. **Demonstration of Contextual Encoding:** The results show that neural networks, specifically LSTMs, *can* effectively leverage contextual information to improve causal relation classification beyond simple lexical lookup or rule-based systems.
3. **Identification of Limitations in Existing Approaches:** The paper effectively highlights the shortcomings of prior methods relying heavily on lexical resources or specific syntactic constructions, motivating the need for more flexible approaches.

**Strengths:**

1. **Well-Motivated Problem:** The paper clearly articulates the challenges of causal reasoning and the limitations of existing linguistic approaches.  The motivation for moving beyond predefined rules and lexicons is strong.
2. **Sound Methodology:** The LSTM-based architecture is a reasonable and well-established approach for sequence modeling and contextual encoding.  The experimentation with different padding strategies and optimizers demonstrates a thoughtful approach to model tuning.
3. **Strong Results:** The proposed system consistently outperforms the baseline, and the improvements, while not massive, are statistically significant.  The analysis showing better performance on ambiguous connectives (like 'since' and 'after') is particularly compelling.
4. **Clear Presentation:** The paper is generally well-written and organized. The explanations of the related work and the proposed method are clear and concise.
5. **Comprehensive Related Work:** The paper provides a thorough overview of relevant work in causal relation classification and discourse parsing.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Novelty of the Method:** While the application of LSTMs to this specific task and dataset is valuable, the proposed architecture itself is relatively standard.  The LSTM is a well-established technique, and the paper doesn't introduce any novel architectural components or training procedures.  The core contribution lies more in the application and empirical validation than in a fundamentally new technique.
2. **Insufficient Error Analysis:** While the paper highlights success on ambiguous connectives, a more detailed error analysis would be beneficial.  What types of errors does the system still make?  Are there specific linguistic phenomena or types of causal relationships that remain challenging?  Identifying these areas would provide valuable insights for future research.  The discussion of examples in Section 4.3 is a good start, but could be expanded.
3. **Lack of Ablation Study:** The paper compares different configurations (Stated Pair LSTM vs. Pair LSTM, different optimizers).  However, an ablation study systematically removing components (e.g., removing the dense layers, using different embedding sizes) could provide a better understanding of the contribution of each element to the overall performance.
4. **Data Set Limitations:** The AltLex corpus, while valuable, is relatively small.  The performance gains might not generalize to larger, more diverse datasets.  The authors acknowledge this but it's a significant limitation.
5. **Missing Discussion of Counterfactual Reasoning:**  The paper briefly mentions and dismisses the counterfactual constraint. However, a more nuanced discussion of the role (or lack thereof) of counterfactual reasoning in human causal perception would strengthen the argument.



**Questions to Authors:**

1.  Could you provide a more detailed error analysis, including examples of instances where the system fails and the reasons for those failures?
2.  Did you experiment with other neural network architectures, such as transformers? If so, how did they compare to the LSTM-based approach?
3.  How sensitive is the performance of your system to the choice of word embeddings (e.g., different dimensions, different training corpora)?
4.  Given the relatively small size of the AltLex corpus, what steps have you taken to ensure the generalizability of your results?
5.  Could you elaborate on why you believe the counterfactual constraint is not universally applicable to human causal perception, providing specific examples?



**Additional Comments:**

*   The formatting is generally good, but some equations could be improved for readability.
*   The references are generally well-cited and relevant.




**Overall Assessment:**

This paper presents a solid and well-executed study of neural network approaches to causal relation classification. While the proposed method lacks significant novelty in terms of the architecture itself, the empirical evaluation on the AltLex corpus and the demonstration of the benefits of contextual encoding are valuable contributions. The paper is well-written and clearly motivated, and addresses an important problem in natural language understanding. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions, contingent on addressing the weaknesses outlined above, particularly the need for a more detailed error analysis and a discussion of data set limitations.
