## Review for "Neural Disambiguation of Causal Lexical Markers based on Context"

**Summary:**

This paper investigates the task of identifying causal relationships between events, focusing on disambiguating the causal meaning of lexical markers (like "because," "since," "after") where their causal interpretation is not always clear. The authors argue that a neural network approach, specifically an LSTM, can effectively encode contextual information to address this ambiguity, outperforming existing methods that rely on predefined linguistic features or lexical resources. They evaluate their system on the AltLex corpus, demonstrating improved performance compared to a strong baseline.

**Main Contributions (as I see them):**

1. **Empirical Validation of Contextual Encoding:** The paper provides evidence, through experiments on the AltLex corpus, that neural networks *can* effectively learn to disambiguate causal meaning from context alone, without heavy reliance on hand-engineered features or external knowledge bases. This reinforces the power of end-to-end learning for this task.
2. **Evaluation on AltLex Corpus:** The use of the AltLex corpus is a valuable contribution. This corpus, containing nuanced causal expressions, provides a more challenging and realistic testbed than previous datasets, specifically targeting the ambiguity the authors aim to address.
3. **Demonstration of a Competitive Neural Architecture:** The proposed LSTM-based architecture achieves state-of-the-art results on the AltLex corpus, offering a strong baseline for future work in this area.



**Strengths:**

1. **Strong Experimental Results:** The results consistently outperform the baseline (Hidey & McKeown, 2016), demonstrating the effectiveness of the proposed approach. The ablation study regarding different padding strategies and optimizers further strengthens the results.
2. **Well-Motivated Problem:** The paper clearly articulates the limitations of existing approaches based on fixed linguistic rules or lexical resources, highlighting the need for a more flexible, context-aware method. The introduction effectively establishes the difficulty of defining causality linguistically.
3. **Thorough Related Work:** The paper provides a comprehensive overview of related work, positioning the current contribution within the broader research landscape of causal relation extraction and discourse analysis.  The critique of earlier work is insightful.
4. **Clear and Logical Presentation:**  The paper is well-written and logically structured, making it easy to follow the authors’ reasoning and methodology. The breakdown into sections (definition, data, system) is effective.
5. **Insightful Analysis:** The analysis of results, particularly the discussion of performance on ambiguous connectives like “which then” (towards the end of Section 4.3), is insightful and provides valuable interpretations beyond simply reporting numbers.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Scope of Evaluation:** While the AltLex corpus is a good choice, evaluating on a single dataset limits the generalizability of the findings.  Results on other corpora, even if with some modification of the task definition, would strengthen the claims.  
2. **Architectural Simplicity:** While effective, the proposed LSTM architecture is relatively simple.  Exploring more sophisticated architectures (e.g., Transformers, attention mechanisms) or incorporating pre-trained language models (e.g., BERT) might lead to further improvements and demonstrate a deeper understanding of the task. The paper acknowledges this implicitly by focusing on a core LSTM implementation.
3. **Lack of Error Analysis:** A more detailed error analysis, beyond the observation about “which then,” would be beneficial. Identifying the types of causal expressions that the system *still* struggles with could inform future research directions.  What are the common error patterns? Are there specific contexts where the network consistently fails?
4. **Missing Discussion of Counterfactual Reasoning:** The authors briefly dismiss the counterfactual constraint on causality, citing Neeleman & van de Koot (2012). However, the impact of *not* incorporating this aspect, and how it might influence the system’s ability to model human causal reasoning, isn’t fully discussed.

**Questions to Authors:**

1.  Could you elaborate on the computational cost of your approach compared to the baseline system (Hidey & McKeown, 2016)?  Is the gain in performance worth the increased computational resources?
2.  You mention the challenges of the "bootstrapping" version of the AltLex corpus due to class imbalance.  Did you experiment with techniques to address this imbalance (e.g., oversampling, class weighting) and, if so, what were the results?
3.  What types of errors does the system make on sentences with ambiguous connectives like “since” or “after”? Could you provide specific examples and your analysis of why the system misclassifies them?
4.  Given the limitations of the LSTM architecture and the success of Transformers in many NLP tasks, do you have plans to explore Transformer-based models for this problem?



**Additional Comments:**

*   The reference list is extensive and well-formatted.
*   The formatting of the tables could be slightly improved for readability (e.g., using bold text for the best results).




**Overall Assessment:**

This is a solid and well-executed paper that makes a valuable contribution to the field of causal relation extraction. The experimental results are convincing, the problem is well-motivated, and the writing is clear and concise. While there are some areas for improvement, the paper represents a significant step forward in applying neural networks to the task of causal disambiguation.  I recommend **acceptance** with minor revisions addressing the weaknesses outlined above.
