## Review for ACL Submission: Neural Disambiguation of Causal Lexical Markers based on Context

**Summary:**

This paper addresses the problem of causal relation disambiguation, specifically identifying whether a relationship between two events is causal, given a potentially ambiguous lexical marker. The authors argue that existing approaches relying on fixed lexical constructions or syntactic patterns are limited and propose a neural network architecture (LSTM) to encode contextual information for improved disambiguation. They evaluate their model on the AltLex corpus, demonstrating performance competitive with, and in some cases surpassing, a strong baseline.

**Main Contributions (as I see them):**

1. **Empirical Evaluation on AltLex:** The primary contribution is a solid empirical evaluation of neural network architectures on the AltLex corpus. This corpus is a valuable resource for studying nuanced causal language, and the results provide a useful benchmark.
2. **Demonstration of Contextual Encoding:** The paper effectively demonstrates that encoding the context of the potential causal relation via LSTMs can improve causal disambiguation performance over a strong feature-engineered baseline. This supports their hypothesis about the limitations of purely lexical or syntactic approaches.
3. **Analysis of Ambiguous Connectives:** The work provides insight into the challenges posed by ambiguous connectives (like "since" and "after") and highlights the importance of considering the full context for accurate disambiguation.



**Strengths:**

1. **Strong Empirical Results:** The results consistently show the proposed LSTM-based models outperforming the baseline (Hidey & McKeown 2016) on the AltLex corpus, particularly when using the Adam optimizer. This provides compelling evidence for the efficacy of the approach.
2. **Clear Motivation and Problem Definition:** The paper clearly articulates the limitations of prior work and provides a well-defined problem statement. The discussion of the tension between syntactic/lexical rules and human understanding of causality is insightful.
3. **Thoughtful Experimental Setup:** The authors meticulously describe their experimental setup, including different padding strategies, optimizers, and regularization techniques.  The use of both the "non-bootstrapping" and "bootstrapping" versions of the AltLex corpus allows for a more robust evaluation.
4. **Insightful Error Analysis:** The authors go beyond simply reporting numbers and delve into an analysis of cases where their system succeeds and fails, particularly focusing on the challenges of ambiguous connectives.  This demonstrates a deeper understanding of the problem.
5. **Focus on a Relevant & Underexplored Problem:**  Causal reasoning and its linguistic expression are critical for many NLP tasks. The focus on *disambiguation* within this domain, and the use of the AltLex corpus, addresses a genuinely important and under-explored area.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Novelty of the Architecture:** The core LSTM architecture itself is not novel. While the application to this specific problem and the comparison to the baseline are valuable, the architectural contribution is incremental. The paper would benefit from a stronger justification of *why* LSTMs are particularly well-suited for this task beyond simply stating that they encode sequential information.
2. **Lack of Ablation Study:** While different configurations of the LSTM are presented, a more systematic ablation study would be helpful. For example, what is the impact of removing the concatenation of the lexical marker? How sensitive is performance to the choice of word embeddings? This would offer greater insight into the relative importance of different components of the model.
3. **Corpus Limitations:** While the AltLex corpus is a valuable resource, it's still relatively small. The potential for overfitting and the limited generalizability of the results are concerns. The authors acknowledge this, but it remains a significant limitation.
4. **Limited Comparison to State-of-the-Art:** While the authors compare to Hidey & McKeown (2016), the field of relation extraction and causal reasoning has seen significant advancements in recent years with the advent of transformers. A comparison to a more modern baseline (e.g., a fine-tuned BERT or RoBERTa model) would provide a stronger assessment of the proposed method's competitiveness.



**Questions to Authors:**

1. **Ablation Study:** Could you provide an ablation study to demonstrate the impact of different components of your LSTM architecture, such as removing the lexical marker concatenation or varying the LSTM layer size?
2. **Transformer Baseline:** Have you considered comparing your model to a fine-tuned transformer-based model (e.g., BERT, RoBERTa) on the AltLex corpus? If so, what were the results? If not, could you provide a rationale for why?
3. **Error Analysis Details:** Could you provide more specific examples of the types of errors your system makes, particularly in the cases where it differs from the baseline? A qualitative analysis of these errors would be helpful.
4. **Future Corpus Development:** Could you elaborate on your plans for creating a new corpus for causality classification? What features will this corpus include, and how will it address the limitations of existing datasets?



**Additional Comments:**

* The paper is generally well-written and clearly organized.
* The references are comprehensive.
* The discussion of related work is thorough.



**Overall Assessment:**

This is a solid and well-executed paper that makes a valuable contribution to the field of causal reasoning. While the architectural novelty is limited, the empirical evaluation on the AltLex corpus, the demonstration of the benefits of contextual encoding, and the insightful analysis of ambiguous connectives are all significant strengths. I recommend **acceptance** with minor revisions to address the weaknesses outlined above. The authors’ response to the questions will also be crucial in determining the final assessment.
