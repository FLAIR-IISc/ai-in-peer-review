## Review for ACL Submission: Neural Disambiguation of Causal Lexical Markers based on Context

**Summary:**

This paper tackles the task of causal relation disambiguation, specifically determining whether a relation between two events is causal given a connecting lexical marker. The authors argue against approaches that rely on fixed syntactic patterns or exhaustive lexical resources for identifying causality, proposing instead a neural network architecture (LSTM) to encode the context of the relation and learn causal meaning. They evaluate their approach on the AltLex corpus, demonstrating improvements over a baseline SVM method.

**Main Contributions (as I see them):**

1. **Empirical Evaluation on AltLex:** The paper provides a valuable empirical evaluation of neural network models on the AltLex corpus, a relatively new and challenging dataset focused on non-canonical causal expressions. This evaluation helps establish a baseline for future work in this area.
2. **Justification for Contextual Approach:** The authors provide a reasonable argument, supported by relevant linguistic literature, for why a contextual approach to causality disambiguation is beneficial, moving beyond purely lexical or syntactic cues.
3. **Demonstrated Performance:** The LSTM-based approach achieves competitive results on the AltLex corpus, outperforming the established baseline.

**Strengths:**

1. **Strong Motivation & Literature Review:** The paper effectively motivates the problem by outlining the limitations of previous approaches to causality detection and situates the work within a strong foundation of relevant linguistic theories (Neeleman & van de Koot, Dowty, etc.). The related work section is comprehensive and well-organized.
2. **Sound Experimental Setup:** The use of the AltLex corpus is a key strength. It is a challenging dataset that necessitates a more nuanced approach than those relying solely on explicit causal connectives. The comparison to the Hidey & McKeown (2016) baseline provides a meaningful point of reference.
3. **Clear and Well-Written:** The paper is generally well-written and easy to follow. The architecture is clearly described and the experimental results are presented in a concise and understandable manner.
4. **Ablation Studies & Analysis:** The comparison of "Stated Pair LSTM" vs. "Pair LSTM" provides some insight into the importance of the connection between the LSTM layers, and the authors provide reasonable analysis of the results, pointing out the limitations of the baseline and the challenges posed by certain lexical markers.
5. **Relevance to the ACL Community:**  Causality and discourse relation identification are core NLP tasks. Improving performance on these tasks has broad implications for applications like question answering, summarization, and text understanding.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Architectural Exploration:** While the LSTM-based approach performs well, the architectural exploration feels somewhat limited. The paper focuses heavily on comparing the stated vs. paired LSTM configurations, but doesn't explore other potentially beneficial architectures (e.g., attention mechanisms, transformers) which are now common in sequence modeling.  Simply stating CNNs aren't suitable due to lack of fixed patterns is insufficient justification without trying alternatives.
2. **Hyperparameter Tuning Details:** The paper mentions experimenting with dropout and regularization, but provides limited details regarding the specific values tested or the search strategy employed.  More transparency in this regard would strengthen the reproducibility of the results.
3. **Limited Error Analysis:** While the authors discuss some specific challenging examples, a more comprehensive error analysis would be valuable. Identifying patterns in the misclassified examples could provide insights into areas for future improvement (e.g., specific types of causal relations the model struggles with).  Why are some ambiguous connectives harder to classify than others?
4. **Dataset Bias & Generalizability:** The AltLex corpus, while valuable, is still relatively small. There's a risk that the model is overfitting to the specific characteristics of this dataset.  Discussing the potential limitations regarding generalizability to other corpora would be beneficial.



**Questions to Authors:**

1.  Could you elaborate on the hyperparameter tuning process? Specifically, what ranges of values were explored for dropout and regularization, and what strategy was used to select the optimal values?
2.  Beyond the "stated" vs. "paired" LSTM comparison, did you experiment with other neural network architectures (e.g., transformers, attention mechanisms)? If so, what were the results? If not, can you provide a stronger justification for focusing solely on LSTMs?
3.  Can you provide a more detailed error analysis? What types of causal relations or lexical markers does the model consistently struggle with? Are there specific linguistic features that seem to contribute to misclassification?
4.  How do you believe the performance of your model would generalize to other datasets with different characteristics (e.g., different domains, different annotation schemes)?



**Additional Comments:**

*   The paper would benefit from a more visually appealing illustration of the proposed architecture.
*   Consider adding a discussion of computational complexity and efficiency.

**Overall Assessment:**

This is a solid paper that presents a reasonable approach to a challenging problem. The experimental results are promising, and the authors provide a strong motivation for their work. While the architectural exploration could be more extensive and the error analysis more detailed, the paper makes a valuable contribution to the field of causality detection. **I recommend acceptance.**
