This paper tackles the challenging problem of causal disambiguation, specifically identifying causal relations between events, and proposes a neural network approach to address limitations of previous rule-based and lexical-resource-dependent methods. The authors correctly identify the shortcomings of prior work, particularly the limited coverage of causal expressions and the difficulty of relying solely on syntactic structures or predefined lexical resources. The core contribution is the application of LSTMs to encode the context surrounding potential causal relationships, demonstrating improved performance on the AltLex corpus compared to existing baselines. A secondary contribution is the careful analysis of the AltLex corpus itself, highlighting class imbalances and potential sources of difficulty.

**Strengths:**

1. **Relevant and Important Problem:** Causal reasoning is fundamental to natural language understanding, and accurately identifying causal relationships is crucial for many NLP tasks. The paper addresses a clear and significant problem in the field.
2. **Sound Motivation & Thorough Related Work:** The introduction effectively motivates the research by outlining the inadequacies of existing approaches to modeling causality in language. The related work section provides a comprehensive overview of previous attempts, clearly positioning the current work within the broader research landscape. The critique of relying on pre-defined lexical resources and syntactic structures is well-articulated.
3. **Empirically Validated Improvement:** The authors demonstrate a clear performance improvement over existing baselines on the AltLex corpus. The experimental results are well-presented and analyzed, and the ablation studies (different LSTM architectures, optimizers, padding strategies) provide valuable insights into the factors contributing to the model’s success. The use of statistical significance testing (McNemar’s test) strengthens the claims.
4. **Insightful Analysis of the Dataset:** The observation about the class imbalance and the presence of ambiguous lexical markers (those with differing meanings in training and test sets) in the AltLex corpus is crucial. Recognizing these challenges informs the interpretation of the results and suggests directions for future work.
5. **Clear and Well-Written Paper:** The paper is generally well-written and easy to follow. The authors clearly define their task, explain their approach, and present their findings in a concise and organized manner.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Generalizability:** The evaluation is restricted to a single dataset (AltLex). While the authors acknowledge this limitation, it significantly weakens the claims of generalizability. The performance on other datasets with different characteristics remains unknown. The AltLex corpus, while valuable, may not fully represent the diversity of causal expressions found in natural language.
2. **Lack of Theoretical Justification for Architecture Choices:** The choice of LSTMs over other neural network architectures (e.g., Transformers) is justified primarily through reference to prior work showing LSTM's ability to encode sequential information. However, a more compelling argument could be made regarding *why* LSTMs are particularly well-suited for capturing the nuanced contextual information required for causal disambiguation, *compared* to alternatives.
3. **Superficial Error Analysis:** While the authors point to some examples of correctly classified ambiguous cases, a more detailed error analysis would be valuable. Identifying specific types of errors (e.g., errors related to temporal reasoning, counterfactual reasoning, or specific lexical items) would provide insights into the model’s limitations and guide future improvements.
4. **Missing Discussion of Counterfactual Reasoning:** Although the authors explicitly state they do not incorporate a counterfactual constraint into their definition of causality (following Neeleman and van de Koot), the paper lacks a discussion of how incorporating such reasoning might improve performance or address specific challenges.
5. **Limited Ablation Study Scope:** While the ablation study covers key architectural parameters, it does not explore other potentially relevant factors, such as different word embedding techniques or more sophisticated attention mechanisms.

**Questions to Authors:**

1.  How would the performance of your model change if evaluated on other datasets for causality detection (e.g., Causal-TimeBank, or a newly created dataset)? Do you have plans to evaluate on more diverse datasets?
2.  Could you elaborate on the rationale for choosing LSTMs over more recent architectures like Transformers, given their strengths in capturing long-range dependencies and contextual information?
3.  Can you provide a more detailed error analysis, identifying the types of errors your model makes and suggesting potential strategies to address them? Specifically, what types of causal relationships are most difficult for the model to disambiguate?
4.  Have you considered incorporating any form of counterfactual reasoning into your model, and if so, what were the challenges? How might such reasoning improve performance?
5.  Beyond the parameters explored in your ablation study (LSTM architecture, optimizer, padding), are there other architectural components or training strategies you considered but did not implement?

**Additional Comments:**

*   Table 8 is very helpful for illustrating the model’s ability to handle ambiguous cases.
*   The reference to “non bootstrapping” and “bootstrapping” versions of the corpus could be clarified in the text. A sentence explaining what bootstrapping refers to would be beneficial.
*   Consider adding a brief discussion of the limitations of the AltLex corpus in the conclusion.



Overall, this is a solid and well-executed paper that makes a valuable contribution to the field of causal reasoning. While the limited generalizability and lack of in-depth error analysis are weaknesses, the demonstrated performance improvement and insightful analysis of the dataset justify acceptance. Addressing the questions above in the author response would further strengthen the paper.
