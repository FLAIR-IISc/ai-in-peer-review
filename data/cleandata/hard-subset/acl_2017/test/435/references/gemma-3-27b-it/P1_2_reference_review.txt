## Peer Review for ACL Submission: "Neural Disambiguation of Causal Lexical Markers based on Context"

**Summary:**

This paper tackles the problem of causal relation disambiguation, focusing on identifying whether a causal relationship exists between two events, even when not explicitly signaled by common causal connectives. The authors argue that relying on predefined lexical or syntactic structures for causality is limiting and propose a neural network architecture (LSTM-based) to encode the context surrounding the potential causal link. They evaluate their approach on the AltLex corpus and demonstrate improved performance compared to a baseline system.

**Main Contributions (as perceived):**

1. **Empirical Evaluation on AltLex:** The application of neural network methods to the AltLex corpus is a valuable contribution. The AltLex corpus, with its focus on less explicit causal markers, is a challenging and important resource for this task, and this work provides a strong baseline for future research on this dataset.
2. **Demonstration of Contextual Encoding:** The paper successfully demonstrates that a neural network, specifically an LSTM, can effectively encode contextual information to improve causal relation identification, outperforming a feature-engineered SVM baseline.
3. **Critique of Traditional Approaches:** The paper provides a concise and well-articulated critique of traditional, rule-based or feature-engineered approaches to causality detection, highlighting their limitations in capturing the nuances of natural language.

**Strengths:**

1. **Strong Experimental Results:** The proposed LSTM-based model consistently outperforms the baseline on the AltLex corpus, demonstrating the effectiveness of the contextual encoding approach. The ablation study, comparing different LSTM configurations and optimizers, is well-conducted and provides valuable insights.
2. **Clear Motivation and Problem Definition:** The introduction effectively establishes the limitations of existing approaches to causality detection and motivates the need for a more flexible, context-aware method. The distinction between explicit and implicit causal relations, and the focus on AltLex expressions, is well-defined.
3. **Thorough Related Work:** The paper provides a comprehensive overview of relevant prior work in computational linguistics, covering both rule-based and machine learning approaches to causality. This demonstrates a strong understanding of the field and positions the current work appropriately.
4. **Analysis of Results:** The authors provide a useful analysis of the results, pointing out the strengths of their system in handling ambiguous connectives and the importance of balance between Precision and Recall. The example in Table 8 nicely illustrates this point.
5. **Good Writing Quality:** The paper is generally well-written, clearly organized, and easy to follow.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Novelty of the Method:** While the application to the AltLex corpus is valuable, the core method – using LSTMs for sequence encoding – is not particularly novel. The architecture is relatively standard. The primary novelty lies in *applying* a well-established technique to a specific and challenging dataset.
2. **Lack of Deeper Analysis of Error Cases:** While the paper acknowledges some error cases, a more in-depth analysis of *why* the model fails on certain instances could provide valuable insights for future improvements. What specific types of implicit causal relations are still challenging? 
3. **Limited Scope:** The paper focuses solely on the *classification* of causal meaning. The authors acknowledge the subsequent task of identifying causal arguments (cause and effect), but do not address it. A more comprehensive approach that integrates both steps could be more impactful.
4. **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** The paper mentions experimenting with dropout and regularization, but doesn't provide a detailed discussion of hyperparameter tuning. Understanding which hyperparameters are most critical and how they affect performance would be beneficial.



**Questions to Authors:**

1.  Can you elaborate on the types of errors your model still makes? Are there specific linguistic patterns or contexts that consistently lead to misclassifications? Providing a more detailed error analysis would be helpful.
2.  Given that LSTMs are well-established, what specific aspects of your architecture or training procedure were most crucial for achieving the reported performance gains on the AltLex corpus?
3.  You mention the two-step task of causality classification and argument identification. Do you have preliminary ideas on how to effectively integrate these two steps into a single model or pipeline?
4. Could you provide more information on the computational resources required to train and evaluate your model? This would help assess the practicality of replicating and extending the work.

**Additional Comments:**

*   The reference list is extensive and well-formatted.
*   The formatting of tables and figures is clear and consistent.
*   Consider mentioning the limitations of the AltLex corpus itself, such as potential biases or annotation inconsistencies, in the discussion.



**Overall Assessment:**

This is a solid and well-executed paper that makes a valuable contribution to the field of computational linguistics, specifically in the area of causal relation disambiguation. While the core method is not groundbreaking, the application to the challenging AltLex corpus, the strong experimental results, and the clear presentation of the work make it worthy of acceptance. Addressing the weaknesses outlined above, particularly through a more in-depth error analysis, would further strengthen the paper. I recommend **Accept with Minor Revisions**.
