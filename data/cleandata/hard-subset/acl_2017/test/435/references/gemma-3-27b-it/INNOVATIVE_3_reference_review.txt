## Review

This paper tackles the challenging problem of causal disambiguation, moving beyond reliance on explicit causal connectives and exploring neural approaches to infer causality from context. The core contribution lies in framing causality classification as a binary problem – causal *or* non-causal – and demonstrating that neural networks can effectively learn this distinction, even when traditional linguistic features fall short. While the methodology isn’t groundbreaking in its use of LSTMs, the *application* to this specific problem, and the clear articulation of why previous approaches (rule-based, feature engineering) are limited, is valuable. A secondary, but notable, contribution is the thorough engagement with prior work and a clear positioning of this research within the ongoing debate about the linguistic encoding of causality. Finally, the experiments on the AltLex corpus, a relatively new and useful resource, provide a solid empirical foundation.

**Strengths:**

1. **Novel Framing of the Problem:** The decision to treat causal disambiguation as a binary classification problem, focused purely on *whether* a causal relation exists, sidesteps the complexities of argument identification and counterfactual reasoning that plague many approaches. This simplification allows for a focused application of neural networks.
2. **Strong Motivation & Critique of Existing Work:** The paper provides a compelling argument for why traditional, linguistically-grounded approaches struggle with the inherent ambiguity and flexibility of causal expressions.  The authors convincingly demonstrate the limitations of relying on predefined causal verbs or syntactic structures.
3. **Empirical Results and Corpus Choice:**  The results on the AltLex corpus are promising, showing performance that surpasses the established baseline.  Selecting AltLex – a corpus explicitly designed to address the limitations of PDTB in capturing implicit causality – is a smart move, and makes the evaluation more relevant.
4. **Clear and Concise Writing:** The paper is well-written and easy to follow, even for readers unfamiliar with the nuances of causal reasoning. The logical flow from introduction to methodology to results is commendable.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Methodological Conservatism:** The chosen LSTM architecture, while reasonable, lacks significant novelty. While the authors justify this choice, it doesn't push the boundaries of the field.  Exploring more sophisticated architectures (e.g., transformers, attention mechanisms) might yield further improvements. This isn’t a fatal flaw, but limits the paper’s overall impact.
2. **Limited Ablation Study:**  While the paper experiments with different padding strategies and optimizers, a more detailed ablation study investigating the impact of different LSTM configurations (e.g., number of layers, hidden unit size) would strengthen the findings.  Understanding *why* certain configurations perform better is crucial.
3. **Corpus Limitations:** The AltLex corpus, while valuable, is still relatively small. This raises concerns about the generalizability of the results. The identified issues with the “bootstrapping” version (class imbalance) further exacerbate this concern.
4. **Superficial Error Analysis:** The example-based error analysis (Table 8) is insightful, but limited. A more systematic analysis of misclassified examples, categorized by error type (e.g., semantic ambiguity, syntactic complexity), would provide more actionable insights.



**Questions to Authors:**

1.  Could you elaborate on why you chose not to explore transformer-based models?  Given their success in capturing long-range dependencies, would they be a promising avenue for future work?
2.  The "Pair LSTM Mean Adam" configuration appears to perform best.  Could you provide a more detailed analysis of the learned representations in this model?  Are there specific patterns or features that seem to be driving its success?
3.  Regarding the imbalance in the “bootstrapping” version of AltLex, did you attempt any data augmentation or re-sampling techniques to mitigate this issue? If so, what were the results?
4.  The paper mentions focusing on *causal meaning classification*. Do you envision a future system that integrates this module with the *causal argument identification* task you described? If so, how would you approach this integration?



**Additional Comments:**

The reference list is extensive and appropriate. The discussion of related work is comprehensive. Minor stylistic improvements (e.g., tightening sentence structure in some places) could further enhance the paper's readability.



**Overall Assessment:**

Despite its methodological conservatism, this paper presents a valuable contribution to the field of computational linguistics. The novel framing of the problem, the strong motivation, and the promising empirical results justify acceptance. I recommend **acceptance** with minor revisions to address the weaknesses identified above. The potential impact lies not in a revolutionary methodology, but in a shift in perspective and a demonstration that neural networks can effectively tackle a long-standing challenge in natural language understanding.
