This paper tackles the challenging problem of causal relation disambiguation, moving beyond simple keyword spotting to a more nuanced understanding of context. The core contribution is a neural network architecture, specifically a bidirectional LSTM, applied to this task, demonstrating that contextual encoding *can* improve performance. While the method itself isn’t groundbreaking in terms of the neural network components used, the application to this specific problem – and the recognition that relying solely on lexical resources is insufficient – represents a valuable direction. A second, supporting contribution is the empirical evaluation on the AltLex corpus, providing a useful benchmark for future work. Finally, the authors correctly identify and articulate the limitations of prior approaches, particularly their dependence on pre-defined syntactic structures or limited lexical coverage.

**Strengths:**

1. **Focus on Contextual Disambiguation:** The central thesis – that causal meaning requires contextual understanding and can't be solely determined by lexical cues – is compelling and well-motivated. This addresses a long-standing issue in computational linguistics, and the authors make a strong case for why neural networks are a suitable tool for this task.
2. **Empirical Validation:** The paper provides solid empirical results on the AltLex corpus. While the performance gains aren’t massive, they are statistically significant and demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed approach relative to a strong baseline. The comparison to Hidey & McKeown’s SVM system is particularly useful.
3. **Clear Problem Definition and Motivation:** The introduction does a good job of laying out the complexities of representing causality in language, referencing relevant linguistic theories and highlighting the shortcomings of previous computational approaches. The discussion of AltLex and its importance is also well-done.
4. **Acknowledging Limitations:** The authors are refreshingly honest about the limitations of their work, including the lack of a large, comprehensive corpus and the potential for bias in the data. This transparency is commendable.
5. **Thoughtful Error Analysis:** The analysis in Section 4.3, specifically focusing on the handling of ambiguous connectives like "which then," demonstrates a deep understanding of the challenges involved and provides valuable insights into the strengths and weaknesses of the model.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Methodological Incrementalism:** The chosen neural network architecture (LSTM) is fairly standard and doesn't introduce significant innovation in terms of deep learning techniques. The core novelty lies in *applying* this technique to the specific problem of causal disambiguation, not in developing a novel network architecture.
2. **Limited Ablation Study:** While the paper experiments with different padding strategies and optimizers, a more thorough ablation study would have been beneficial. For example, investigating the impact of different embedding sizes or LSTM hidden state dimensions could provide further insights into the model's behavior.
3. **Corpus Dependency:** The results are heavily reliant on the AltLex corpus. While the authors acknowledge the corpus's limitations, the lack of evaluation on other datasets raises concerns about the generalizability of the findings. The relatively small size of the corpus also limits the statistical power of the experiments.
4. **Lack of Qualitative Error Analysis:** While Section 4.3 offers some insightful discussion of specific cases, a more systematic qualitative error analysis would be valuable. Examining instances where the model *fails* could reveal important patterns and suggest avenues for improvement.
5. **Counterfactuals and Temporal Restrictions:** While the authors rightly dismiss strict adherence to counterfactual constraints, the discussion of temporal ordering feels underdeveloped. More exploration of how temporal information could be incorporated into the model could be beneficial.

**Questions to Authors:**

1.  Could you provide more details on the specific challenges encountered when attempting to generalize the model to other datasets (if any were explored)?
2.  What specific modifications to the architecture or training procedure would be necessary to incorporate more explicit temporal reasoning into the model?
3.  You mention the potential for bias in the AltLex corpus. Could you elaborate on the types of biases you are concerned about and how they might affect the results?
4.  Given the limitations of the AltLex corpus, what are your plans for creating a larger and more representative dataset for this task?



**Additional Comments:**

The paper is well-written and clearly organized. The references are comprehensive and up-to-date. While the method itself isn't revolutionary, the paper makes a valuable contribution to the field by demonstrating the potential of neural networks for causal relation disambiguation and highlighting the importance of contextual understanding. I recommend acceptance, leaning towards a strong accept. The novelty lies in the *application* and the clear framing of the problem, and that’s enough to warrant publication.
