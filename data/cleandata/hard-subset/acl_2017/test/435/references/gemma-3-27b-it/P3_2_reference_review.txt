## Review for ACL Submission: Neural Disambiguation of Causal Lexical Markers based on Context

**Summary:**

This paper tackles the challenging problem of causal relation disambiguation, specifically identifying whether a relationship between two events is causal, even when signaled by non-canonical lexical markers. The authors argue that existing approaches relying on syntactic structure or predefined lexical resources are limited, and propose a neural network architecture (LSTM) to encode contextual information for improved disambiguation. They evaluate their approach on the AltLex corpus, demonstrating performance competitive with, and in some cases exceeding, the current state-of-the-art.

**Main Contributions (as I see them):**

1. **Empirical Validation of Contextual Encoding:** The paper provides strong empirical evidence that neural networks, specifically LSTMs, *can* effectively learn to disambiguate causal relations based on contextual information, offering a promising alternative to rule-based or feature-engineered systems.  The improvement over the existing baseline on a challenging dataset supports this claim.
2. **Focus on a Difficult Problem:** The work directly addresses the issue of non-canonical causal markers (AltLex), which are crucial for real-world understanding, but often neglected. Focusing on this aspect significantly advances the field beyond systems that primarily handle explicit causal signals like "because".
3. **AltLex Corpus Utilization:**  The paper effectively utilizes the AltLex corpus, providing a solid evaluation platform and contributing to the increased visibility and use of this valuable resource.

**Strengths:**

1. **Strong Experimental Results:**  The results consistently demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed LSTM architecture, achieving competitive or superior performance compared to the existing baseline (Hidey & McKeown, 2016). The ablation study with different padding strategies and optimizers is also well done, providing insights into the impact of different hyperparameters.
2. **Clear Motivation & Problem Definition:** The paper clearly articulates the limitations of previous approaches and provides a compelling justification for the proposed neural network approach. The explanation of why syntax-grounded theories fall short and the need for contextual understanding is well-reasoned.
3. **Thorough Error Analysis & Discussion:** The authors acknowledge and discuss the limitations of the model, offering insights into cases where it struggles (e.g., ambiguous connectives).  The example analysis (Table 8) highlights the model’s ability to correctly handle instances where the baseline fails, strengthening the claim of contextual understanding.
4. **Well-Structured and Readable:** The paper is well-written, logically organized, and easy to follow. The related work section provides a comprehensive overview of existing approaches.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Architectural Exploration:** While the LSTM architecture is a reasonable choice, the paper lacks exploration of more recent and potentially more powerful architectures like Transformers.  A comparison with a Transformer-based model would significantly strengthen the findings and demonstrate the robustness of the proposed approach.  Simply stating LSTMs are better at encoding sequential information isn't sufficient justification without a direct comparison.
2. **Hyperparameter Sensitivity & Reporting:**  While different optimizers and padding strategies are tested, a more detailed analysis of hyperparameter sensitivity (e.g., LSTM hidden layer size, dropout rate) would be beneficial.  A clear rationale for the final hyperparameter choices is lacking.  Reporting the standard deviation of results across multiple runs would also strengthen the reliability of the findings.
3. **Dataset Bias & Generalization:** The AltLex corpus, while valuable, may contain biases that influence the model's performance.  The authors acknowledge differences between the training and test sets in the bootstrapping version, but further investigation into potential biases (e.g., domain-specific language) and their impact on generalization would be valuable. Testing on another, independent dataset would be ideal (although acknowledged as difficult due to the scarcity of resources).
4. **Missing Ablation for Word Embeddings:** The choice of using pre-trained GloVe embeddings is reasonable, but an ablation study comparing performance with randomly initialized embeddings or other pre-trained embeddings (e.g., Word2Vec, FastText) could provide insight into the contribution of the pre-trained embeddings to the overall performance.



**Questions to Authors:**

1. **Transformer Comparison:**  Could you comment on the computational cost of training a Transformer-based model for this task and whether you considered it as an alternative architecture?  If not, why?
2. **Hyperparameter Tuning:**  What methods were used to select the final hyperparameters for the LSTM model (e.g., grid search, random search)? Can you provide a more detailed description of the hyperparameter search process?
3. **Dataset Bias:** Could you elaborate on potential biases within the AltLex corpus and how these biases might influence the model’s performance? Were any steps taken to mitigate these biases during training?
4. **Error Analysis Deep Dive:** Could you provide a more detailed qualitative error analysis, focusing on the types of errors the model makes and potential reasons for these errors? Providing specific examples (beyond Table 8) would be helpful.



**Additional Comments:**

*  The notation in Equation 2 is slightly unclear.  The dimensions of the vectors and matrices should be explicitly stated.
*  The introduction could benefit from a more concise overview of existing approaches to causal reasoning, focusing on those most relevant to the paper’s contribution.



**Overall Assessment:**

This is a solid and well-executed paper that makes a valuable contribution to the field of causal relation disambiguation. The empirical results are convincing, the motivation is clear, and the analysis is thoughtful. While there are some areas for improvement (particularly regarding architectural exploration and hyperparameter tuning), the paper is of high quality and deserves acceptance.  I recommend acceptance with minor revisions addressing the weaknesses and responding to the questions posed above.
