- Tackles causal relation disambiguation beyond explicit connectives using an LSTM to encode context.  
- Argues causality is not strictly tied to syntactic constructions or fixed lexical resources.  
- Evaluates approach on the AltLex corpus and shows improvements over a strong SVM baseline.  
- Empirical evaluation on a relevant dataset (AltLex) is thorough and valuable for the community.  
- Demonstrates that contextual encoding via neural networks can outperform feature-engineered baselines.  
- Provides a thoughtful discussion of linguistic theories of causation and limits of syntax/lexicon-based methods.  
- Uses Hidey & McKeown (2016) as a strong, fair baseline for comparison.  
- Clearly motivates the problem and articulates shortcomings of prior approaches.  
- Conducts thorough experimentation with multiple LSTM configurations and ablation studies.  
- Presents error-case analysis showing where the proposed model outperforms the baseline.  
- Focuses on a challenging and nuanced problem in causal relation disambiguation.  
- Limited exploration of neural architectures—no Transformers/attention models evaluated.  
- Inadequate hyperparameter details (dropout, regularization, tuning process) hindering reproducibility.  
- Error analysis lacks systematic categorization of mistake types for deeper insight.  
- Class-imbalance mitigation (reducing majority class) is simplistic; more advanced techniques could help.  
- Evaluation restricted to AltLex—limited discussion of generalizability to other datasets or domains.  
- Requests clarification on hyperparameter search values and tuning methodology.  
- Asks whether other architectures (Transformers/attention) were tried and rationale for choosing LSTM.  
- Requests more detailed, categorized error analysis comparing system and baseline failures.  
- Seeks information on computational cost (training time, memory) relative to the baseline.  
- Inquires about plans or preliminary results for testing the method on other datasets or domains.  
- Paper is well-written, well-organized, and references are comprehensive and relevant.  
- Minor typos noted (e.g., “rd” instead of “3rd”).  
- Overall assessment: solid contribution with promising results; recommend acceptance with minor revisions addressing architectural exploration, hyperparameter reporting, and deeper error analysis.
