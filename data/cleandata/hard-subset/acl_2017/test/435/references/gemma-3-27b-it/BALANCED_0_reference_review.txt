This paper investigates neural disambiguation of causal lexical markers, a challenging problem in natural language understanding. The authors argue that existing approaches relying on syntactic constructions or predefined lexical resources are limited and propose a neural network architecture based on LSTMs to encode contextual information for improved causal relation classification. The work is evaluated on the AltLex corpus.

**Contributions:**

1. **Empirical Evaluation of Neural Networks for Causal Disambiguation:** The most significant contribution is the demonstration that LSTMs can effectively encode contextual information for causal relation classification, achieving competitive results on the AltLex corpus, surpassing a strong baseline (Hidey & McKeown, 2016). This provides evidence supporting the hypothesis that neural networks can learn causal relationships from data without relying on explicit linguistic rules or resources.
2. **Focus on Contextual Disambiguation:** The authors rightly point out the limitations of rule-based and resource-dependent approaches to causality, and frame the problem as a contextual disambiguation task. This shift in perspective is valuable, recognizing that causal meaning is often determined by the surrounding text rather than inherent properties of individual words or phrases.
3. **Thorough Experimental Setup and Analysis:**  The authors conduct a reasonably thorough set of experiments, comparing different LSTM architectures, optimizers, and zero-padding strategies. They also analyze the results, noting the strengths and weaknesses of their approach and providing insights into the challenges of the task.  The exploration of a reduced dataset to mitigate class imbalance is a valuable addition.

**Strengths:**

1. **Strong Baseline Comparison:** Comparing against Hidey & McKeown (2016) is a strong point, as it's the established state-of-the-art on the AltLex corpus.  Outperforming this baseline provides a solid empirical basis for the proposed method.
2. **Clear Problem Definition and Motivation:** The paper clearly articulates the problem of causal disambiguation and motivates the need for a more flexible and data-driven approach than previous methods. The discussion of limitations in existing theories of causation is helpful.
3. **Detailed Architecture Description:** The description of the LSTM-based architecture is clear and well-explained, enabling reproducibility.
4. **Ablation Studies & Parameter Tuning:** The investigation of different LSTM configurations (stated vs. pair), optimizers (Adadelta, Adam) and padding methods demonstrate a good understanding of the factors influencing performance.
5. **Insightful Error Analysis:** The discussion of specific examples where the model succeeds or fails, particularly concerning ambiguous connectives like “which then”, offers valuable insights into the challenges of the task and potential directions for future work.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Novelty of the Architecture:** While the application of LSTMs to this specific task is valuable, the architecture itself is relatively standard.  The core contribution is more in the application and empirical evaluation than in a novel architectural design.
2. **Dataset Limitations:** The AltLex corpus, while useful, is still relatively small, potentially limiting the generalizability of the results. The class imbalance within the corpus (acknowledged by the authors) adds to this concern. The performance gains may be less pronounced on larger and more diverse datasets.
3. **Lack of Comparison to Stronger Neural Baselines:**  The comparison to B1 (majority class) and Hidey & McKeown (2016) is good, but a comparison to more recent and powerful pre-trained language models (e.g., BERT, RoBERTa) fine-tuned for this task would be very beneficial. These models have demonstrated state-of-the-art performance on many NLP tasks and would provide a more challenging benchmark.
4. **Insufficient Discussion of Counterfactual Reasoning:**  The paper briefly touches upon the counterfactual constraint on causality but ultimately dismisses it. While acknowledging its limitations, a more in-depth discussion of why and how it might be incorporated into a neural model could be valuable.
5. **Limited Analysis of Attention Mechanisms:** While LSTMs are used, exploring attention mechanisms could potentially highlight the parts of the input sentence most relevant for determining causality. This could provide further insights into the model's decision-making process.



**Questions to Authors:**

1. Have you experimented with more recent pre-trained language models (e.g., BERT, RoBERTa) fine-tuned for causal relation classification on the AltLex corpus? If so, how does your approach compare in terms of performance? If not, what are the challenges of applying these models to this task?
2. Can you elaborate on the potential benefits and drawbacks of incorporating a counterfactual reasoning component into the neural network architecture? What specific strategies could be used to model counterfactual relationships?
3. The paper mentions ambiguities with certain lexical markers. Could you provide a more detailed analysis of which markers are particularly challenging and why?
4. While you mentioned exploring different padding strategies, did you investigate other techniques for handling variable length sequences, such as masking?

**Additional Comments:**

* The formatting is generally good, but some references appear to be incomplete (e.g., “Kruengkrai et al\.,? \Q2017\E”).
* Consider adding a table summarizing the hyperparameters used in the best-performing configurations.



Overall, this is a solid paper that presents a reasonable approach to a challenging problem. While the architectural novelty is limited, the empirical evaluation and insightful analysis contribute to a better understanding of how neural networks can be used for causal disambiguation. Addressing the weaknesses outlined above, particularly by comparing to stronger baselines and exploring more advanced techniques, would significantly strengthen the paper. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions.
