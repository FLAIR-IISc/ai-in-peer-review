## Review for "Neural Disambiguation of Causal Lexical Markers based on Context"

**Summary:**

This paper tackles the challenging task of causal relation disambiguation, moving beyond reliance on explicit causal connectives. The authors argue that causality isn’t rigidly tied to specific syntactic constructions or lexical resources and propose a neural network approach (LSTM) to encode contextual information for more robust disambiguation. They evaluate their method on the AltLex corpus, demonstrating performance improvements over a strong baseline.

**Main Contributions (as I see them):**

1. **Empirical Evaluation on a Relevant Dataset:** The paper provides a thorough empirical evaluation of neural network models for causal disambiguation on the AltLex corpus. This corpus, specifically designed to address the limitations of previous resources, is a valuable asset for the community.
2. **Demonstration of Contextual Encoding:** The results demonstrate that a neural network architecture capable of encoding contextual information can outperform a feature-engineered SVM baseline, suggesting the utility of this approach for capturing nuanced causal relationships.
3. **Insightful Discussion of Causality in Language:** The paper offers a good overview of the ongoing debate around linguistic theories of causation and correctly identifies the limitations of syntax- and lexicon-based approaches.



**Strengths:**

1. **Strong Baseline & Comparison:** Choosing Hidey & McKeown's (2016) system as a baseline provides a solid benchmark and demonstrates a clear improvement with the proposed neural approach. The comparison is fair and highlights the benefits of the contextual encoding.
2. **Clear Motivation & Problem Statement:** The paper clearly articulates the shortcomings of existing approaches to causality and motivates the need for a more flexible, context-aware method. The arguments against relying on pre-defined lexical resources or syntactic structures are well-presented.
3. **Thorough Experimentation:** The authors explore several configurations of the LSTM network (stated vs. paired, different padding strategies, optimizers) showing a good level of experimental rigor.  The ablation studies help to understand the impact of different design choices.
4. **Analysis of Error Cases:** The discussion of examples where the system succeeds and the baseline fails, particularly concerning verbs like 'break' and ambiguous connectives, provides valuable insight into the strengths and weaknesses of the proposed approach.
5. **Focus on a challenging problem:** Causal relation disambiguation is inherently difficult and the authors appropriately acknowledge the nuances and complexities involved.



**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Architectural Exploration:** While the LSTM is a reasonable choice, the paper lacks a broader exploration of neural architectures.  Consideration of Transformers or other attention-based mechanisms, which are now standard for contextual encoding, would strengthen the work. The justification for choosing LSTM over CNN (pattern learning vs. sequential information) feels somewhat superficial.
2. **Hyperparameter Tuning Details:** The paper mentions dropout and regularization but lacks detail on the specific values used and the tuning process. This makes it difficult to reproduce the results and assess the robustness of the findings.
3. **Lack of Error Analysis Depth:** While the authors present some examples of error cases, a more systematic error analysis would be beneficial. For instance, categorizing the types of errors (e.g., misinterpreting temporal relationships, failing to capture subtle semantic cues) would provide valuable insights for future work.
4. **Data Imbalance & Mitigation:** The authors acknowledge the class imbalance in the AltLex corpus but the mitigation strategy (reducing the majority class) seems somewhat simplistic. More sophisticated techniques like weighted loss functions or data augmentation could potentially improve performance.
5. **Limited Generalizability Discussion:** The evaluation is solely on the AltLex corpus. While a good starting point, the paper doesn't discuss the potential generalizability of the approach to other domains or types of text.  




**Questions to Authors:**

1.  Could you provide more details on the hyperparameter tuning process, including the specific values explored for dropout, regularization, and learning rate?
2.  Did you experiment with any other neural network architectures besides LSTM (e.g., Transformers, attention mechanisms)? If not, can you elaborate on the rationale for focusing solely on LSTM?
3.  Could you provide a more detailed error analysis, categorizing the types of errors made by the system and the baseline? This would help to understand the specific challenges in causal disambiguation.
4.  What is the computational cost associated with your proposed method compared to the baseline, particularly regarding training time and memory usage?
5.  Do you have any preliminary results or plans for evaluating your method on other datasets or domains?




**Additional Comments:**

*   The paper is well-written and clearly organized.
*   The references are comprehensive and relevant.
*   The discussion of related work is insightful.
*   Minor: There are a few minor typos throughout the paper (e.g., “rd” instead of “3rd”).



**Overall Assessment:**

This paper presents a solid contribution to the field of causal relation disambiguation. The use of a neural network to encode contextual information is a promising approach, and the empirical evaluation on the AltLex corpus demonstrates its effectiveness. While there are some areas for improvement, particularly regarding architectural exploration and error analysis, the paper is well-written, clearly motivated, and provides valuable insights. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions addressing the weaknesses outlined above.
