This paper tackles the problem of causal discourse relation disambiguation, specifically addressing the limitations of existing approaches reliant on fixed lexical markers or syntactic constructions. The authors propose a neural network architecture leveraging LSTMs to encode context and classify causal relations, evaluating it on the AltLex corpus.

The primary contribution is a compelling argument, backed by empirical results, for a context-driven, neural approach to causal relation identification. The authors correctly identify the shortcomings of traditional linguistic rule-based and feature-engineered methods, and their LSTM-based approach demonstrably outperforms a strong baseline (Hidey & McKeown, 2016) on a challenging dataset. The explicit acknowledgement of the limitations of purely syntax-grounded theories of causation and the focus on capturing nuanced meaning through neural networks is a valuable direction. A second, though less prominent contribution is the thorough review and contextualization of existing work in causal reasoning and its computational linguistic implementations, providing a clear landscape of the challenges and prior attempts.

**Strengths:**

1. **Novelty of Approach:** The paper's central thesis – that neural networks can effectively disambiguate causal meaning without being rigidly tied to predefined linguistic patterns – is a significant departure from traditional methods. This is a bold move, and the results presented support the claim. The authors aren’t just tweaking existing techniques; they are proposing a fundamentally different way to approach the problem.
2. **Strong Empirical Results:** The consistent outperformance of the proposed LSTM model over the baseline on the AltLex corpus, particularly in scenarios involving ambiguous connectives, is a strong indicator of its effectiveness. The improvement on a challenging dataset known for its subtlety is noteworthy.
3. **Clear Problem Definition & Motivation:** The paper effectively articulates the limitations of previous work and motivates the need for a more flexible and robust approach to causal reasoning. The discussion surrounding AltLex and the challenges of non-explicit causal markers is particularly well-done.
4. **Careful Consideration of Linguistic Nuances:** The authors demonstrate a good understanding of the complexities of causal reasoning, acknowledging the difficulty of applying strict counterfactual or temporal constraints and advocating for a more human-centric approach aligned with cognitive understanding of causation (Neeleman & van de Koot, 2012).
5. **Thorough Experimental Setup:** The ablation study with different padding strategies and optimizers, while not deeply analyzed, indicates a commitment to rigorous evaluation.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Analysis of Error Cases:** While the paper presents overall performance metrics, a deeper dive into *where* the model fails would be beneficial. Identifying specific types of causal constructions or linguistic phenomena that still pose challenges could guide future research. Table 8 shows some examples, but a more systematic error analysis is missing.
2. **Lack of Comparison with Stronger Neural Baselines:** While the baseline from Hidey & McKeown (2016) is appropriate, comparing against more recent, state-of-the-art transformer-based models (e.g., BERT, RoBERTa) fine-tuned for text classification would strengthen the argument. The current comparison doesn’t fully demonstrate the superiority of the LSTM architecture over potentially more powerful alternatives.
3. **Corpus Dependency:** The reliance on a single corpus (AltLex) is a limitation. While AltLex is valuable, the generalizability of the findings to other domains or types of text is uncertain. Further evaluation on diverse datasets would be desirable.
4. **Simplification of Causal Argument Identification:** The authors acknowledge the two-step nature of causality classification (meaning disambiguation and argument identification) but focus solely on the former. A brief discussion of how the proposed approach might be extended to address argument identification would be helpful.

**Questions to Authors:**

1. Can you provide a more detailed error analysis, highlighting specific examples where the model struggles and explaining the potential reasons for these failures?
2. Have you experimented with transformer-based models (e.g., BERT) for this task? If so, how do their performance compare to the LSTM-based approach? If not, what were the reasons for not including this comparison?
3. What are the computational costs of your model compared to the baseline?
4. How might the proposed architecture be adapted to address the causal argument identification task?



**Additional Comments:**

The paper is generally well-written and clearly presented. The references are comprehensive and relevant. Despite the weaknesses, the novelty and promising results make this a valuable contribution to the field. I would lean towards acceptance, as the core idea is strong and the empirical validation is convincing, even if further experimentation and analysis would be beneficial.
