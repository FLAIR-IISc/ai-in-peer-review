This paper investigates neural disambiguation of causal lexical markers, arguing that neural networks can encode contextual meaning to identify causal relationships even when signaled by non-canonical markers. The authors propose a LSTM-based architecture and evaluate it on the AltLex corpus. The primary contribution, as I see it, is the application of a relatively simple neural architecture to the challenging problem of causal relation extraction, demonstrating competitive results against a baseline. A secondary contribution lies in the thorough contextualization of the problem, outlining limitations of previous approaches based on syntactic structure or lexical resources.

**Strengths:**

1. **Relevant Problem:** Causal relation identification is crucial for many NLP tasks, and the paper correctly points out the limitations of relying solely on explicit markers or predefined lexical resources. The focus on *disambiguation*—identifying causality when not explicitly signaled—is particularly valuable.
2. **Clear Motivation and Background:** The introduction provides a good overview of the challenges in representing causality linguistically and computationally, appropriately referencing key work in the field. The authors effectively establish the need for a more flexible approach than traditional rule-based or resource-dependent methods.
3. **Sound Experimental Setup:** The use of the AltLex corpus is a strong point. This corpus, specifically designed to address the issue of non-canonical causal markers, provides a suitable testbed for the proposed approach. The comparison to a baseline (Hidey and McKeown, 2016) is appropriate.
4. **Competitive Performance:** The results presented show that the proposed system outperforms the baseline in several metrics, demonstrating the effectiveness of the neural network approach. The analysis of performance with the reduced corpus is also insightful.
5. **Well-written and Organized:** The paper is generally well-written and easy to follow, with a logical structure and clear explanations of the methods and results.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Architectural Simplicity:** While the LSTM architecture is a reasonable starting point, it lacks novelty. The paper does not explore more advanced neural network architectures (e.g., Transformers) that have shown superior performance in similar sequence modeling tasks.  The justification for choosing LSTM over CNNs is somewhat weak. While the authors mention CNNs' reliance on kernel size, a more detailed discussion of the trade-offs would be beneficial.
2. **Limited Ablation Study:** The paper presents several configurations (different optimizers, padding strategies), but a more thorough ablation study is needed to understand the contribution of each component. For example, what is the impact of using pre-trained word embeddings versus training embeddings from scratch?
3. **Hyperparameter Tuning Details:** The paper mentions using different values for dropout and regularization, but does not provide specifics on how these hyperparameters were selected. A more detailed description of the hyperparameter tuning process is necessary for reproducibility.
4. **Lack of Error Analysis:** While the authors point to some correctly classified examples and misclassifications, a more systematic error analysis would provide valuable insights into the limitations of the model.  What types of causal relationships are particularly difficult to identify? What are the common sources of error?  The example with "which then" is good, but more are needed.
5. **Formatting and Typographical Errors:** Numerous minor formatting inconsistencies and typographical errors detract from the overall professionalism of the paper. For example, inconsistent use of italics and boldface, missing punctuation, and awkward phrasing are present throughout. (e.g., "rd" should be "th" in "rd International Conference", "causal connectives" is often not hyphenated when it should be.)

**Questions to Authors:**

1.  Why was a Transformer architecture not considered, given its strong performance on sequence modeling tasks?
2.  Can you provide a more detailed description of the hyperparameter tuning process, including the search space and the evaluation metric used?
3.  Please provide a more in-depth error analysis, identifying the types of causal relations that are most challenging for the model and the common sources of error.
4.  Could you discuss the computational complexity of your approach compared to the baseline method?
5.  The phrase “causal argument classification” is introduced but not explored. Is this intended for future work, or could it be briefly discussed in the context of the current limitations?

**Additional Comments:**

*   Please carefully proofread the paper to address the numerous formatting and typographical errors.
*   The references section is well-populated, but could benefit from more consistent formatting.
*   Consider adding a discussion of potential biases in the AltLex corpus and how these biases might affect the results.



Overall, while the paper addresses a relevant problem and presents a reasonable approach, the lack of architectural novelty, limited ablation study, and numerous minor flaws prevent me from recommending acceptance at this time. A major revision addressing these weaknesses is necessary.
