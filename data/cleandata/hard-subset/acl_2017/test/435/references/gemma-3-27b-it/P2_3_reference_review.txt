## Review for "Neural Disambiguation of Causal Lexical Markers based on Context"

**Summary:**

This paper addresses the task of causal relation classification, specifically focusing on disambiguating whether a relationship between two events is causal, even when signaled by ambiguous lexical markers. The authors argue against syntax-grounded approaches to causality and propose a neural network architecture (LSTM-based) to encode context and learn causal relations. They evaluate their approach on the AltLex corpus, demonstrating improvements over a baseline system and highlighting the importance of contextual encoding.

**Main Contributions (as I see them):**

1. **Empirical Validation of Contextual Encoding:** The paper provides evidence, through experimental results on the AltLex corpus, that neural networks *can* effectively learn to disambiguate causal relations based on context, outperforming a strong baseline that relies on lexical resources.
2. **Application of Neural Networks to a Specific Discourse Relation:** While neural networks are prevalent in NLP, applying them specifically to the nuanced task of causal relation disambiguation—particularly with a focus on non-explicit markers—is a valuable contribution.
3. **Thorough Analysis and Discussion of Limitations:** The authors acknowledge the limitations of their approach and the challenges inherent in the task, offering insightful discussions regarding the counterfactual constraint and the temporal ordering of events.



**Strengths:**

1. **Strong Experimental Results:** The proposed LSTM-based model consistently outperforms the baseline (Hidey & McKeown, 2016) on the AltLex corpus, both in the full dataset and the reduced dataset, demonstrating the effectiveness of contextual encoding. The use of ablation studies (comparing "Stated Pair LSTM" vs. "Pair LSTM") also provides helpful insights.
2. **Clear Motivation and Problem Definition:** The paper clearly articulates the limitations of existing approaches to causality and convincingly argues for the need for a context-aware, data-driven approach. The discussion regarding the limitations of relying on pre-defined syntactic structures or lexical resources is well-supported with references.
3. **Detailed Error Analysis and Discussion:** The analysis of specific examples (Table 8) where the proposed system succeeds while the baseline fails is particularly insightful. It shows the model's ability to handle ambiguous cases and demonstrates a nuanced understanding of causal inference.
4. **Comprehensive Related Work:** The paper provides a thorough review of relevant work in causal relation classification, covering both rule-based and machine learning approaches.  This establishes the context for the authors' contribution and highlights the novelty of their approach.
5. **Rigorous Experimentation:** The authors explore multiple configurations of their proposed model (different optimizers, padding strategies) and include a strong baseline for comparison. This demonstrates a commitment to thorough evaluation.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Dataset:** The AltLex corpus, while valuable, is relatively small.  This limits the generalizability of the findings and raises concerns about potential overfitting. While the authors acknowledge this, it remains a significant limitation.  The small size also makes it harder to draw strong conclusions about the relative importance of different architectural choices.
2. **Lack of Comparison to Stronger Neural Baselines:** While the baseline from Hidey & McKeown (2016) is a reasonable comparison, it’s not a state-of-the-art neural network model.  Comparing to more recent and powerful models like Transformers (e.g., BERT, RoBERTa) fine-tuned for this task would strengthen the paper’s claims.  The authors briefly allude to the limitations of relying on lexical resources, but a direct comparison with a powerful transformer-based model would be more convincing.
3. **Architectural Simplicity:** The proposed LSTM architecture is relatively simple. While it performs well, exploring more sophisticated architectures (e.g., attention mechanisms, pre-trained language models) could potentially yield further improvements. The justification for using LSTMs over CNNs is reasonable, but a brief exploration of CNNs, even as a quick ablation study, could be informative.
4. **Limited Discussion of Negatives:** The paper focuses primarily on successes. While the error analysis is good, a more in-depth discussion of *why* the model fails in certain cases (e.g., specific linguistic phenomena or types of ambiguity) would be helpful.



**Questions to Authors:**

1.  Have you experimented with pre-trained language models (e.g., BERT, RoBERTa) for this task? If not, could you briefly discuss why and what the expected benefits/drawbacks would be?
2.  The paper mentions limitations of the counterfactual constraint. Could you elaborate on specific cases in the AltLex corpus where applying the counterfactual constraint would lead to incorrect classifications?
3.  Could you provide more detail on the types of errors the model still makes? Are there specific linguistic constructions or types of ambiguity that consistently cause misclassifications?
4.  How sensitive is the performance of your model to the choice of word embeddings? Did you experiment with different embedding methods?



**Additional Comments:**

*   The paper is well-written and clearly organized.
*   The references are comprehensive and relevant.
*   The inclusion of the dataset details (size, class distribution) is helpful.




**Overall Assessment:**

This is a solid paper that makes a valuable contribution to the field of discourse relation classification. The authors present a convincing argument for the importance of contextual encoding and demonstrate the effectiveness of their proposed LSTM-based model on a challenging task. While the limitations regarding dataset size and lack of comparison to stronger neural baselines should be addressed, the paper is well-executed and offers insightful analysis. I recommend **acceptance** with minor revisions addressing the questions above.
