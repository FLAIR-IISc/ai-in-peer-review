## Review

This paper investigates neural disambiguation of causal lexical markers. The authors aim to address the limitations of existing approaches to causality detection, which often rely on predefined syntactic structures or lexical resources, by proposing a neural network architecture capable of encoding contextual information for improved causal relation classification. The primary contribution, as I see it, is an attempt to apply a relatively straightforward neural network model (LSTM) to a challenging problem – identifying causal relationships signaled by potentially ambiguous linguistic cues – and demonstrating performance gains over a baseline system on a specific corpus (AltLex). A secondary contribution is the thorough discussion of prior work in computational linguistics related to causality, offering a well-structured overview of the field.

**Strengths:**

1. **Clear Problem Definition:** The paper clearly articulates the challenges inherent in identifying causal relationships in natural language, particularly the limitations of rule-based and resource-dependent approaches. This provides a strong motivation for the proposed neural approach.
2. **Comprehensive Literature Review:** The related work section is remarkably thorough, covering a broad range of approaches to causality detection, from early rule-based systems to more recent machine learning methods. It effectively establishes the context for the present work and highlights the gap the authors are attempting to fill.
3. **Empirical Evaluation:** The authors evaluate their system on the AltLex corpus, providing a quantitative assessment of its performance. The comparison to a baseline system (Hidey & McKeown, 2016) offers a benchmark for evaluating the effectiveness of the proposed approach. The inclusion of results for different optimization algorithms (Adadelta, Adam) and padding strategies adds to the robustness of the evaluation.
4. **Detailed Analysis of Results:**  The inclusion of a qualitative analysis in Section 4.3, illustrating examples where the proposed system succeeds and the baseline fails, is a strong point. This demonstrates a nuanced understanding of the model's behavior and identifies areas for potential improvement.
5. **Acknowledged Limitations:** The authors openly acknowledge the limitations of the corpus and the need for a more comprehensive dataset, showing a responsible approach to research.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Architectural Simplicity:** While the LSTM architecture is a reasonable starting point, it's remarkably simplistic given the complexity of the problem. The paper lacks a justification for *why* this particular architecture was chosen over more sophisticated models (e.g., Transformers, attention mechanisms) commonly used in NLP tasks. The ablation studies regarding the “Stated Pair LSTM” are insufficient to justify its rejection, but do highlight the limited impact of the connection between the LSTMs.
2. **Limited Novelty:** The core idea of using LSTMs to encode context isn't inherently novel. The application to this specific problem of causal disambiguation is the main contribution, but the model itself doesn’t introduce significant architectural innovations.  The paper would benefit from a more explicit discussion of how this work builds upon and extends existing neural NLP techniques.
3. **Over-Reliance on a Single Corpus:** The evaluation is solely based on the AltLex corpus. While this corpus is valuable, the generalizability of the results to other datasets or domains is unclear. A more robust evaluation would involve testing on multiple corpora, even if it requires adapting the system to different annotation schemes.
4. **Insufficient Error Analysis:**  While Section 4.3 provides *some* error analysis, it is not extensive enough. A more detailed investigation of the types of errors the system makes (e.g., misclassifying specific types of lexical markers, struggling with particular sentence structures) would provide valuable insights for future work.
5. **Formatting and Presentation:** The writing style, while generally clear, is sometimes verbose and repetitive. Certain sentences are awkwardly phrased, and the paper contains numerous minor grammatical errors and typos (e.g., “rd” instead of “th” in “International Conference for Learning Representations”).  The table and figure captions could also be more descriptive. Several citations are missing year information.

**Questions to Authors:**

1.  Why was an LSTM architecture chosen over more recent and potentially more powerful architectures, such as Transformers? Did you experiment with any alternative architectures?
2.  Can you provide a more detailed error analysis, categorizing the types of errors the system makes and identifying the main sources of confusion?
3.  What are the limitations of the AltLex corpus, and how might these limitations affect the generalizability of your results?
4.  Could you elaborate on the rationale behind the different zero-padding strategies, and why “Max” padding yielded the best results?
5.  Are the performance differences between Adam and Adadelta statistically significant?

**Additional Comments:**

*   Please carefully proofread the paper to correct any grammatical errors, typos, and formatting inconsistencies.
*   The references section is incomplete, several citations are missing year information.
*   Consider adding a discussion of the computational cost of the proposed system.



**Overall Assessment:**

Despite a relatively straightforward approach and some minor flaws in presentation, this paper presents a reasonable contribution to the field of causal reasoning. The thorough literature review and empirical evaluation are commendable. However, the limited novelty of the proposed architecture and the reliance on a single corpus weaken the overall impact of the work. Given the numerous minor weaknesses, I recommend **rejection** with encouragement to revise and resubmit after addressing the points raised in this review. A more novel architecture, evaluation on multiple datasets, and a more in-depth error analysis would significantly strengthen the paper.
