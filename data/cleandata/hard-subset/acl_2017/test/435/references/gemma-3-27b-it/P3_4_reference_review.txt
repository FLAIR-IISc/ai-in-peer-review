## Review for ACL Submission: Neural Disambiguation of Causal Lexical Markers based on Context

**Summary:**

This paper tackles the problem of identifying causal relations between events, focusing on the challenge that causality is often signaled by non-canonical lexical markers. The authors argue against syntax-based approaches to causality and propose a neural network architecture (specifically LSTMs) to encode contextual information for improved causal relation disambiguation. They evaluate their approach on the AltLex corpus, demonstrating performance improvements over a strong baseline.

**Main Contributions (as I see them):**

1. **Empirical Validation of Contextual Encoding:** The paper provides evidence, through empirical results on the AltLex corpus, that neural networks *can* effectively encode contextual information to improve causal relation disambiguation, particularly where lexical markers are ambiguous. This lends support to the claim that moving beyond purely lexicon-based or syntax-based approaches is beneficial.
2. **Application to the AltLex Corpus:** The work applies deep learning methods to the relatively new and valuable AltLex corpus, establishing a strong baseline for future research on this dataset.  This corpus, with its focus on non-canonical causal markers, is particularly important for advancing the field.
3. **Thorough Comparison and Analysis:** The paper conducts a detailed comparison against a strong baseline (Hidey & McKeown 2016) and thoughtfully analyzes the results, including discussion of the impact of different LSTM configurations and optimization strategies.

**Strengths:**

1. **Strong Motivation & Problem Definition:** The paper clearly articulates the limitations of existing approaches to causality detection, particularly their reliance on limited sets of lexical markers and syntactic structures. The problem of disambiguating non-canonical causal markers is well-defined and relevant.
2. **Sound Methodology:** The choice of LSTMs is well-justified, given the sequential nature of language and the need to capture long-range dependencies. The experimentation is reasonably thorough, exploring different configurations and hyperparameters.
3. **Positive Empirical Results:** The results consistently outperform the baseline, demonstrating the effectiveness of the proposed approach. The ablation studies (different LSTM configurations, padding strategies, optimizers) provide valuable insights into the model's behavior.
4. **Detailed Analysis of Errors:** The authors delve into specific examples where the system succeeds and fails, providing a nuanced understanding of the challenges involved in causality disambiguation. The discussion of the ambiguities inherent in certain connectives is particularly insightful.
5. **Clear Writing and Organization:** The paper is generally well-written and logically organized, making it easy to follow the authors' line of reasoning.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Novelty of the Architecture:** While the application to this specific problem is valuable, the LSTM architecture itself isn’t particularly novel. The paper doesn't introduce any new LSTM variants or training techniques. The primary contribution lies in the *application* of a well-established technique, rather than a breakthrough in neural network design.
2. **Lack of External Validation:**  Evaluation is limited to the AltLex corpus.  While this is a good starting point, it would be beneficial to see the system tested on a different dataset, even with some adaptation, to assess its generalizability. The discussion of potential issues with the corpus’s class distribution (bootstrapping version) highlights this limitation.
3. **Limited Exploration of Feature Engineering:** The focus is solely on word embeddings.  While the paper explicitly argues against relying on handcrafted features, it might be worth briefly exploring the potential benefits of incorporating some simple linguistic features (e.g., part-of-speech tags, dependency relations) alongside the embeddings.  This could potentially enhance performance, especially for ambiguous cases.
4. **Missing Error Analysis Detail:** The error analysis could be more comprehensive. While the authors mention specific examples, a quantitative breakdown of error types (e.g., errors due to lexical ambiguity, syntactic complexity, world knowledge) would be helpful.

**Questions to Authors:**

1.  You mention that “causation is concerned with the human understanding of the world and not with the world itself.”  How does this philosophical stance influence your approach to modeling causality?  Specifically, how do you address the inherent subjectivity of causal judgments in your data and model?
2.  Given the limitations of relying solely on word embeddings, have you considered exploring pre-trained contextualized word embeddings (e.g., BERT, RoBERTa) as an alternative or complement to Glove? Would this potentially mitigate some of the ambiguity issues?
3.  You mention plans to create a new corpus. Could you elaborate on the characteristics of this planned corpus? What types of causal relations and lexical markers will be included?  How will it address the limitations of existing resources?
4.  The results show the “Pair LSTM Mean Adam” consistently performs well.  Can you provide any intuition as to why the ‘Mean’ padding strategy works better than ‘Max’ or other strategies?



**Additional Comments:**

*   The discussion of related work is comprehensive and effectively positions the paper within the broader literature.
*   The formatting and clarity of the tables and figures could be improved.  More descriptive captions would be helpful.
*   Consider briefly discussing the potential ethical implications of causality detection systems, such as the risk of reinforcing biases or misinterpreting complex social phenomena.



**Overall Assessment:**

This is a solid and well-executed paper that makes a valuable contribution to the field of computational linguistics. While the proposed architecture isn't groundbreaking, the application to the challenging problem of causal relation disambiguation with non-canonical markers is significant, and the empirical results are compelling. The authors have clearly identified a relevant problem, adopted a sound methodology, and presented a thorough analysis of their findings. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions addressing the weaknesses outlined above.
