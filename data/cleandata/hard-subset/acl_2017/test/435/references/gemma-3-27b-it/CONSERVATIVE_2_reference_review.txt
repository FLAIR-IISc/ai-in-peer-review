This paper explores the task of causal relation disambiguation, focusing on identifying whether a causal relationship exists between two events, even when not explicitly signaled by common causal connectives. The authors propose a neural network architecture based on LSTMs, trained on the AltLex corpus. The primary claimed contribution is a neural approach to causality disambiguation that outperforms existing methods by encoding contextual information without relying heavily on pre-defined lexical resources or syntactic constraints. A secondary contribution is the application of neural networks to the AltLex corpus, providing a new baseline for future research. A tertiary contribution is the analysis highlighting the limitations of relying solely on syntactic or lexical features for understanding causality.

**Strengths:**

1. **Addresses a relevant and challenging problem:** Causal reasoning is crucial for natural language understanding, and disambiguating implicit causal relations is a difficult task. The authors correctly identify the limitations of existing approaches reliant on explicit markers or hand-crafted features.
2. **Empirically sound evaluation:** The authors evaluate their model on the AltLex corpus, which is a valuable resource for this task, and compare against a reasonable baseline (Hidey & McKeown, 2016). The results demonstrate a performance improvement over the baseline, particularly with the “Pair LSTM Mean Adam” configuration.
3. **Clear Motivation & Problem Definition:** The paper provides a comprehensive overview of existing work in causal relation identification, outlining the shortcomings of previous approaches. The clear definition of the task as a two-step process (causal meaning classification and argument identification) is also commendable.
4. **Well-structured and readable:** The paper is generally well-written and clearly organized, making it easy to follow the authors’ line of reasoning and understand their methodology.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Lack of Novelty in the Method:** While the application to this specific task is valuable, the core methodology – using LSTMs for sequence encoding – is well-established and lacks significant novelty. The architecture is a relatively standard LSTM network; there's no innovative component within the neural network itself. The performance gains, while statistically significant, aren't substantial enough to justify a fundamentally new approach.
2. **Limited Baseline Comparison:** The comparison is limited to a single baseline, the Hidey & McKeown (2016) system. Including comparisons to simpler baselines, such as a majority class classifier or a bag-of-words model with logistic regression, would provide a more comprehensive understanding of the proposed method's effectiveness. More importantly, the Hidey & McKeown system is itself somewhat dated and may not represent the current state-of-the-art in feature-based approaches.
3. **Corpus Limitations & Data Bias:** The AltLex corpus, while useful, is acknowledged to have limitations in its class distribution and potential biases introduced during the bootstrapping process. The authors address this somewhat by experimenting with a reduced, balanced dataset, but the potential impact of these biases on the results isn’t fully explored. The authors also mention variations between the training and test sets in the bootstrapped corpus, which requires more in-depth discussion.
4. **Insufficient Error Analysis:** While the authors point to specific examples of successful disambiguation, a more thorough error analysis would be beneficial. Identifying the types of cases where the model struggles (e.g., specific lexical markers, complex sentence structures) could provide valuable insights for future research.

**Questions to Authors:**

1.  Could you provide a more detailed analysis of the types of errors made by your system? Specifically, are there particular lexical markers or sentence structures that consistently lead to misclassifications?
2.  How sensitive are the results to the choice of word embeddings? Have you experimented with different pre-trained embeddings, or considered fine-tuning the embeddings during training?
3.  The paper mentions the potential for a two-step task (causal meaning classification and causal argument identification). Do you have preliminary results or plans for addressing the causal argument identification step?
4.  You mention the limitations of the AltLex corpus. Could you elaborate on the potential impact of the corpus biases on the reported results, and how you might mitigate these biases in future work?



**Additional Comments:**

The paper is generally well-written and presents a reasonable approach to a challenging problem. However, the lack of novelty in the method and the limited baseline comparison prevent a strong endorsement. The contribution primarily lies in the application of a standard neural network architecture to a specific dataset and demonstrating its effectiveness in this context, rather than presenting a fundamentally new technical advancement.
