## Review for ACL Submission: Neural Disambiguation of Causal Lexical Markers based on Context

**Summary:**

This paper addresses the task of causal relation classification, specifically focusing on disambiguating whether a relationship between two events is causal, even when signaled by non-canonical (AltLex) markers. The authors argue against purely syntax- or lexicon-based approaches to causality, proposing a neural network (LSTM) architecture to encode contextual information for improved disambiguation. They evaluate their approach on the AltLex corpus, demonstrating performance improvements over a strong baseline.

**Main Contributions (as I see them):**

1. **Empirical Evaluation on AltLex:** The paper provides a thorough empirical evaluation of neural network models on the AltLex corpus, a valuable resource for studying non-canonical causal expressions. This evaluation provides concrete results and a baseline for future work in this area.
2. **Demonstration of Contextual Encoding:** The results show that utilizing LSTM networks to encode contextual information *does* offer an improvement over a strong baseline relying on lexical features. This supports the authors' central hypothesis.
3. **Nuanced Discussion of Causality:** The paper offers a good, concise overview of the linguistic challenges in defining and identifying causality, grounding the work in relevant theoretical frameworks.

**Strengths:**

1. **Solid Experimental Setup:** The use of the AltLex corpus is a significant strength. This corpus specifically targets the difficult problem of non-explicit causal markers, making the evaluation more relevant than using corpora like PDTB alone. The comparison to the Hidey & McKeown (2016) baseline is also appropriate.
2. **Clear Motivation & Problem Definition:** The paper clearly articulates the limitations of existing approaches to causality detection and effectively motivates the need for a context-aware neural network solution. The breakdown of the problem into causal meaning classification and argument identification is insightful.
3. **Well-Written and Structured:** The paper is well-written, logically structured, and easy to follow. The relevant related work is effectively summarized, and the methodology is clearly explained.
4. **Ablation Study and Parameter Tuning:**  The exploration of different LSTM architectures (“Stated Pair LSTM” vs. “Pair LSTM”), padding strategies, and optimizers demonstrates a careful approach to model development. The inclusion of detailed results for these configurations strengthens the conclusions.
5. **Error Analysis & Discussion:** The analysis of examples where the proposed system outperforms the baseline, particularly with ambiguous connectives like "which then," offers valuable insights into the model's strengths and limitations.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Novelty of Method:** While the application to the AltLex corpus is valuable, the LSTM architecture itself is not particularly novel.  The core contribution lies more in the application and empirical validation than in a fundamentally new neural network design.
2. **Lack of Deep Error Analysis:** While some error analysis is presented, a more comprehensive exploration of *where* the model fails (e.g., specific types of ambiguity, syntactic structures) would be beneficial.  Identifying systematic error patterns could inform future improvements.  For example, examining errors specifically linked to the counterfactual constraint, even if the authors discount it, could be informative.
3. **Data Imbalance:** The acknowledged class imbalance in the AltLex corpus (more non-causal examples) is a potential concern. The authors address this by reducing the number of non-causal examples. However, the impact of this data reduction on the robustness and generalizability of the model should be discussed. Was a different sampling technique considered (e.g., oversampling the minority class)?
4. **Missing Comparison to Stronger Baselines:**  Recent advancements in pre-trained language models (e.g., BERT, RoBERTa) have shown impressive results on various NLP tasks, including relation extraction. A comparison to a fine-tuned pre-trained model would provide a stronger benchmark for evaluating the proposed approach.
5. **Limited Scope of Evaluation:** The evaluation focuses exclusively on the AltLex corpus. Assessing the model's performance on other datasets (even with some adaptation) would increase confidence in its generalizability.



**Questions to Authors:**

1.  You mention the counterfactual constraint is not always applicable. However, could you elaborate on how incorporating aspects of counterfactual reasoning, even if not as a strict constraint, might improve the model's performance?
2.  The data reduction to address the class imbalance might introduce bias. Could you provide some analysis of how this reduction affected the performance on different subsets of the data?  Did you consider other techniques for mitigating the imbalance?
3.  What are the computational resources required to train and run your model? This information is important for reproducibility and assessing the practicality of the approach.
4.  You note that the “Stated Pair LSTM” performs worse than the “Pair LSTM”. What is your hypothesis for why connecting the LSTMs negatively affects performance?
5.  Would you discuss the limitations of only classifying causal *meaning* and not identifying the causal arguments (e1 and e2)? Are there downstream tasks where argument identification would be crucial?




**Additional Comments:**

*   Consider adding a visualization of the attention mechanism (if applicable) to provide further insight into what the model is focusing on when making its predictions.
*   The reference list is quite extensive.




**Overall Assessment:**

This is a solid and well-executed paper that addresses an important problem in computational linguistics. While the proposed method isn't groundbreaking in terms of architectural innovation, the empirical evaluation on a challenging dataset and the demonstration of the benefits of contextual encoding are valuable contributions. Addressing the weaknesses outlined above would further strengthen the paper and increase its impact. I recommend **acceptance** with minor revisions.
