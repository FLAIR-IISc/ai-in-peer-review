## Review

This paper tackles the challenging problem of causal relation disambiguation, moving beyond reliance on explicitly causal connectives. The authors correctly identify the limitations of existing approaches grounded in syntactic structure or pre-defined lexical resources, and propose a neural network-based solution to encode contextual information for improved disambiguation. 

**Main Contributions:**

1. **Framing Causality Disambiguation as a Contextual Problem:** The paper makes a compelling argument that causality isn't solely determined by lexical cues or syntactic structures, but requires understanding the broader context. This reframing is the most significant contribution, justifying the exploration of neural network approaches.
2. **Empirical Evaluation on AltLex:**  Applying a neural approach to the AltLex corpus, a valuable resource for studying implicit causality, provides a concrete evaluation of the proposed method. The authors demonstrate improvements over a strong baseline (Hidey & McKeown, 2016).
3. **Insightful Discussion of Limitations:** The authors acknowledge and discuss the limitations of their approach and the challenges of the task, including the class imbalance in the corpus and the subtleties of human causal reasoning.

**Strengths:**

1. **Novelty of Approach:** The core idea – leveraging neural networks to learn contextual representations for causality – is novel and promising. While LSTMs aren’t groundbreaking in themselves, their application to this *specific* problem of nuanced causal understanding, moving *away* from feature engineering and lexical constraints, represents a genuine advance.
2. **Sound Motivation and Background:** The introduction thoroughly and effectively establishes the limitations of prior work, providing a strong motivation for the proposed approach.  The discussion of different theories of causation provides useful context.
3. **Clear Experimental Setup:** The experimental setup is well-defined, with a clear comparison to a relevant baseline and a thorough description of the data and network architecture. The ablation studies (different padding strategies, optimizers) demonstrate a responsible investigation of design choices.
4. **Honest Analysis of Results:** The authors don't overstate their findings. They acknowledge the limitations of the corpus and the areas where their approach could be improved, and they provide insightful analysis of the error cases.
5. **Strong Performance:**  The results, particularly the improvement over Hidey & McKeown’s system, are encouraging and suggest the proposed approach has merit. The analysis demonstrating success with ambiguous connectives like "which then" is particularly strong.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Scope of Evaluation:** The evaluation is solely based on the AltLex corpus. While this is a valuable resource, it's not sufficient to fully demonstrate the generalizability of the approach. Evaluation on other corpora with different characteristics (e.g., different domains, types of causal relations) would strengthen the findings.
2. **Lack of Detailed Error Analysis:** While the authors acknowledge limitations, a more detailed error analysis, including specific examples of misclassified instances and a discussion of the reasons for the errors, would be beneficial. Understanding *why* the model fails is crucial for future improvement.
3. **Architectural Simplicity:** The proposed neural network architecture, while effective, is relatively simple. Exploring more sophisticated architectures (e.g., attention mechanisms, transformer networks) could potentially lead to further improvements.
4. **Missing Discussion of Counterfactual Reasoning:** The paper briefly touches on counterfactual reasoning but doesn’t integrate it into the model or analysis. Although the authors rightfully dismiss a strict counterfactual requirement, exploring ways to incorporate aspects of counterfactual thinking could enhance the model's ability to capture nuanced causal relationships.



**Questions to Authors:**

1.  Could you provide more detailed examples of cases where your system correctly disambiguates causality but the Hidey & McKeown (2016) system fails, highlighting the specific contextual cues your model utilizes?
2.  What were the primary reasons for choosing LSTMs over other recurrent neural network architectures (e.g., GRUs)? 
3.  Do you have any preliminary ideas for how to address the class imbalance in the AltLex corpus, beyond the reduction you performed for the additional experiments?  Would data augmentation techniques be applicable?
4.  Given the limitations of the AltLex corpus, what are your plans for evaluating the generalizability of your approach to other datasets and domains?

**Additional Comments:**

The paper is well-written and clearly organized. The related work section is comprehensive and provides a good overview of the field. The references are current and relevant.



**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper with a novel and promising approach to a challenging problem. While there are some weaknesses, the novelty of the approach, the clear presentation, and the encouraging experimental results outweigh these concerns. I believe this work makes a valuable contribution to the field of computational linguistics and has the potential to significantly advance our understanding of how to automatically detect and disambiguate causal relations. I recommend **acceptance** with minor revisions to address the questions and weaknesses outlined above. I particularly value the boldness of taking a neural approach to a problem traditionally tackled with hand-crafted features and linguistic rules.
