This paper addresses the challenging task of neural disambiguation of causal lexical markers. The authors argue that current approaches relying on syntactic constructions or pre-defined lexical resources are limited, and propose a neural network architecture to encode contextual information for causality classification. The core idea – leveraging neural networks to learn causal relationships directly from data – is promising and aligns with recent trends in NLP. 

**Main Contributions:**

1. **Empirical Demonstration of Neural Network Effectiveness:** The primary contribution is the demonstration that a neural network architecture, specifically LSTMs, can effectively classify causal relations within the AltLex corpus, outperforming a strong baseline (Hidey & McKeown, 2016). This provides empirical support for the hypothesis that neural networks can learn to encode causal meaning from context.
2. **Focus on Contextual Disambiguation:** The work correctly identifies the crucial role of context in resolving ambiguity in causal markers (e.g., ‘since’, ‘after’).  The focus on learning causal meaning *despite* the absence of explicit markers or inherent lexical cues is a valuable contribution.
3. **Comparative Analysis of Network Architectures & Optimizers:** The systematic experimentation with different LSTM configurations (stated vs. paired) and optimizers (Adadelta vs. Adam) provides useful insights into the performance of these components, offering a valuable resource for future research.

**Strengths:**

1. **Clear Problem Definition and Motivation:** The paper clearly articulates the limitations of existing approaches to causality detection and provides a strong rationale for the proposed neural solution. The discussion of how human perception of causality differs from syntactic approaches is well-presented.
2. **Appropriate Dataset Selection:** The choice of the AltLex corpus is suitable for the task. The corpus’s inclusion of “Alternative Lexicalizations” (AltLex) and implicit causal relations addresses a key weakness in previous datasets.
3. **Thorough Experimental Evaluation:** The authors conduct a comprehensive set of experiments, comparing their proposed methods with a strong baseline and systematically varying key architectural parameters. The ablation studies involving different LSTM configurations and optimizers are commendable.
4. **Detailed Analysis of Results:** The analysis section provides valuable insights into the strengths and weaknesses of the proposed system. The discussion of the performance on ambiguous connectives and the analysis of misclassified examples are particularly insightful.
5. **Well-Written and Organized:** The paper is well-written and logically organized, making it easy to follow the authors' line of reasoning.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Novelty in Architecture:** While the application to causal disambiguation is valuable, the proposed LSTM-based architecture itself isn’t particularly novel. It’s a relatively standard application of LSTMs to a sequence classification task. The core innovation lies more in the application and empirical validation, rather than a fundamentally new architectural component.
2. **Corpus Limitations:** The AltLex corpus, while better than previous options, remains relatively small. This limits the generalizability of the findings and makes it difficult to assess the robustness of the proposed system. The observed improvements over the baseline could be partly attributable to the model’s capacity exceeding the complexity of the dataset.
3. **Lack of Error Analysis Beyond Examples:** While the paper presents a few illustrative examples of correctly and incorrectly classified sentences, a more systematic error analysis would be beneficial. Identifying common error patterns and their causes would provide valuable insights for future improvement. For example, are errors concentrated on specific types of causal relationships (e.g., temporal vs. intentional)?
4. **Limited Comparison to More Recent Approaches:**  The baseline comparison is to a 2016 method.  More recent advances in contextualized word embeddings (e.g., BERT, RoBERTa) and transformer-based architectures could potentially outperform both the baseline and the proposed system.  A comparison to these state-of-the-art methods would strengthen the paper's claims.
5. **Superficial Discussion of Counterfactual Reasoning:** The paper briefly dismisses the counterfactual constraint but doesn't delve into the potential benefits or drawbacks of incorporating it. A more nuanced discussion of counterfactual reasoning in the context of causal inference would be valuable.



**Questions to Authors:**

1.  Have you considered using pre-trained contextualized word embeddings (e.g., BERT, RoBERTa) instead of GloVe? How do you anticipate this would impact the performance of your model?
2.  Could you provide a more detailed error analysis, identifying common error patterns and their potential causes?
3.  The paper mentions that certain lexical markers have different meanings in the training and test sets. How did you address this issue during training and evaluation? Did you explore any techniques to mitigate the impact of this mismatch?
4.  Could you elaborate on why the “Stated Pair LSTM” architecture performed worse than the “Pair LSTM” architecture? What specific characteristics of the causal disambiguation task might explain this result?

**Additional Comments:**

*   The notation in equation (2) could be clarified.
*   Consider adding a table summarizing the hyperparameter settings used in the experiments.
*   The discussion of the temporal restriction could be further elaborated, particularly in relation to the examples provided.



Overall, this paper presents a solid and well-executed study on neural disambiguation of causal lexical markers. While the architectural novelty is limited, the empirical results and insightful analysis make a valuable contribution to the field. Addressing the weaknesses outlined above would further strengthen the paper and enhance its impact. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions.
