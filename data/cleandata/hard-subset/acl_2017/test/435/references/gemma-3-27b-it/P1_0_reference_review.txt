## Peer Review for ACL Submission: Neural Disambiguation of Causal Lexical Markers based on Context

**Summary:**

This paper tackles the problem of identifying causal relations between events, focusing on the challenge that causality isn't always signaled by explicit markers. The authors argue against syntax-grounded approaches and lexical resource dependency, proposing a neural network architecture (LSTM) to encode contextual information for causal disambiguation. They evaluate their approach on the AltLex corpus, demonstrating improved performance compared to a baseline SVM system and a simple majority-class baseline.

**Main Contributions (as I see them):**

1. **Empirical Evaluation on AltLex:** The primary contribution is a thorough empirical evaluation of neural network models on the AltLex corpus, a valuable resource for studying implicit causality. This provides a concrete benchmark and insights into the effectiveness of neural approaches for this task.
2. **Demonstration of Contextual Encoding:** The paper successfully demonstrates that LSTM networks can effectively encode contextual information to improve causal relation disambiguation, outperforming a strong baseline reliant on lexical resources.
3. **Justification for a Neural Approach:** The authors present a reasonable argument against relying solely on syntactic structures or predefined lexical resources for identifying causal relationships, advocating for the potential of neural models to learn nuanced patterns from data.

**Strengths:**

1. **Relevant Problem & Motivation:** The task of causal relation identification is important for many NLP applications (question answering, summarization, etc.). The authors clearly articulate the limitations of existing approaches and motivate the need for a context-aware solution.
2. **Solid Experimental Setup:** The use of the AltLex corpus is a strength, as it specifically targets the challenging case of implicit causality.  The comparison against a well-established baseline (Hidey & McKeown, 2016) provides a solid point of reference. The exploration of different LSTM configurations and optimization algorithms demonstrates a reasonable experimental methodology.
3. **Clear Presentation:** The paper is well-written and logically structured. The background information on causality theories and related work is comprehensive and helps to contextualize the proposed approach.
4. **Analysis of Results:** The authors offer some insightful analysis of their results, particularly regarding the performance on ambiguous connectives and the benefits of their LSTM approach.  The discussion of the limitations of relying on lexical resources is also well-articulated.
5. **Good Performance:** The proposed approach consistently outperforms the baselines, demonstrating a tangible improvement in causal disambiguation accuracy.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Novelty of the Method:** While the application of LSTMs to this task is sound, the architecture itself is relatively standard. The core idea of using LSTMs for contextual encoding isn't groundbreaking; the novelty lies primarily in applying it specifically to this problem and demonstrating its effectiveness on a relevant dataset.
2. **Lack of Ablation Study:**  The paper would benefit from a more detailed ablation study. For example, exploring the impact of different embedding sizes, LSTM hidden unit dimensions, or the number of dense layers could provide more granular insights into the model’s behavior.
3. **Insufficient Error Analysis:** While the authors mention some challenging cases, a more systematic error analysis would be valuable. Identifying common error patterns could inform future improvements to the model or the dataset.  Specifically, providing examples of *incorrectly* classified instances would be helpful.
4. **Data Imbalance:** The paper acknowledges the class imbalance in the AltLex corpus but doesn't fully address its potential impact on the results.  More sophisticated techniques for handling class imbalance (e.g., weighted loss functions, data augmentation) could be explored.
5. **Limited Scope:** The paper focuses solely on the causal meaning classification subtask.  While a logical starting point, a future direction could involve integrating this with the causal argument identification task to provide a more complete solution.



**Questions to Authors:**

1.  **Error Analysis:** Could you provide a more detailed error analysis, including examples of sentences that your model misclassifies and the reasons for these errors?
2.  **Ablation Study:**  Could you perform an ablation study to investigate the impact of different LSTM configurations (e.g., hidden layer size, number of layers) on performance?
3.  **Class Imbalance:** Did you consider any techniques to address the class imbalance in the AltLex corpus (e.g., weighted loss functions, data augmentation)? If so, what were the results? If not, why not?
4. **Argument Identification:**  Do you have any preliminary thoughts or plans for integrating the causal meaning classification task with the causal argument identification task?



**Additional Comments:**

*   The references are well-organized and comprehensive.
*   The formatting of the tables could be improved for readability.  For example, using bold font for the best performing results.
*   It would be helpful to provide a link to the code and the trained models to facilitate reproducibility.



**Overall Assessment:**

This is a solid and well-executed paper that makes a valuable contribution to the field of causal relation identification. While the proposed method isn’t groundbreaking, the thorough evaluation on the AltLex corpus and the demonstration of the effectiveness of contextual encoding using LSTMs are significant achievements. I recommend accepting this paper, pending satisfactory responses to the questions raised above.
