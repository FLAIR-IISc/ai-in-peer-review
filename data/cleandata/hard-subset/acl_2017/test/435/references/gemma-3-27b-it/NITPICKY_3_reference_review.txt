This paper investigates neural disambiguation of causal lexical markers, arguing that a neural network can learn to identify causal relationships without relying on strict syntactic or semantic definitions. The authors propose a LSTM-based architecture and evaluate it on the AltLex corpus. 

The primary contribution, as I see it, is the application of a relatively simple neural network architecture to a challenging task – identifying causality signaled by often ambiguous lexical cues – and demonstrating competitive performance against a baseline. A secondary contribution is the thorough review of existing work in computational approaches to causality, providing a useful overview of the field's evolution and limitations.

**Strengths:**

1. **Relevant and well-defined task:** Causal relation identification is crucial for many NLP applications, and the focus on *disambiguation* – differentiating causal from non-causal uses of the same markers – is a valuable contribution. The AltLex corpus provides a suitable, albeit limited, testbed for this task.
2. **Clear motivation and positioning:** The paper effectively argues against overly restrictive, rule-based or knowledge-reliant approaches to causality, highlighting the limitations of syntax-grounded theories and the coverage issues of lexical resources. The neural approach is presented as a promising alternative.
3. **Empirically sound evaluation:**  The authors compare their approach to a reasonable baseline and present results with clear metrics. The ablation studies with different LSTM configurations and optimizers are helpful in understanding the model's behavior. The analysis of results with the reduced corpus is also a good addition. 
4. **Detailed Related Work:** The paper provides a comprehensive overview of prior work in causality detection, demonstrating a strong understanding of the field and highlighting the gaps their work addresses.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Lack of Novelty in Architecture:** While the LSTM architecture is reasonable, it is not particularly novel. The core idea of using LSTMs to encode context is standard practice in NLP, and the specific configuration lacks significant innovation. The “Stated Pair LSTM” architecture seems under-motivated and, as the results indicate, not particularly effective.
2. **Limited Dataset Size:** The AltLex corpus, while valuable, remains relatively small. This limits the generalizability of the findings and raises concerns about overfitting. The authors acknowledge this limitation, but it remains a significant weakness. The reduced corpus evaluation is a step in the right direction but doesn’t fully address the concern.
3. **Insufficient Error Analysis:** While the authors point to some specific examples of correct/incorrect classifications (Table 8), a more systematic error analysis is needed. What types of causal expressions does the model struggle with? What errors does the baseline make that the model avoids, and vice-versa? A deeper understanding of the model’s shortcomings would be valuable.  The connectionives `which then` mentioned is a good start, but much more is needed.
4. **Formatting inconsistencies and minor errors:** The paper contains several minor formatting issues (e.g., inconsistent spacing, incomplete citations - "Do et al\.," instead of "Do et al., 2011") and grammatical errors (e.g., "rd International Conference" should be "4th International Conference”). While not major, these detract from the professionalism of the submission. The writing style is sometimes convoluted and could benefit from clearer expression.
5. **Unclear Justification for Design Choices:** The choice of 300-dimensional GloVe embeddings is stated without much justification.  Similarly, the dropout values and regularization strengths are presented as hyperparameters tested, but the search process isn't described. 

**Questions to Authors:**

1.  Could you provide a more detailed error analysis, specifically categorizing the types of errors the model makes and comparing them to the baseline?
2.  What was the rationale behind exploring the "Stated Pair LSTM" architecture? Given its inferior performance, could you explain why it was included in the experiments?
3.  Have you explored any alternative neural architectures beyond LSTMs, such as transformers, which might be better suited for capturing long-range dependencies in text?
4. Could you detail the hyperparameter search strategy, including the range of values explored for dropout and regularization?



**Additional Comments:**

* The reference list appears to be missing some years for some entries.
* Please ensure consistent formatting throughout the paper.
* Consider adding a table summarizing the key statistics of the AltLex corpus (e.g., number of sentences, average sentence length, class distribution).



Overall, this paper presents a reasonable, though not groundbreaking, approach to causal relation disambiguation. The strengths lie in the clear motivation, well-defined task, and sound empirical evaluation. However, the lack of architectural novelty, limited dataset size, and insufficient error analysis prevent me from recommending acceptance. The numerous minor flaws further detract from the overall impression. I recommend **rejection** at this time, with encouragement for the authors to address the weaknesses outlined above and resubmit in the future.
