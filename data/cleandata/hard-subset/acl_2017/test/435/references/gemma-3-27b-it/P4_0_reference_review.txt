This paper addresses the task of neural disambiguation of causal lexical markers, arguing that neural networks can encode the meaning of events and discover underlying causal relations, moving beyond reliance on specific syntactic constructions or lexical resources. The authors present a neural network architecture and evaluate it on the AltLex corpus.

The primary contribution is the demonstration that a relatively simple LSTM-based neural network can effectively classify causal relations between events, achieving competitive performance compared to a previously published baseline (Hidey & McKeown, 2016). While the architecture itself isn’t groundbreaking, its application to this specific task, particularly given the challenges posed by the AltLex corpus (ambiguous connectives, implicit relations), is a valuable empirical contribution. A secondary contribution lies in the thorough experimental setup, including ablation studies (different optimizers, zero-padding strategies) and analysis of the results, providing insights into the strengths and weaknesses of the approach. The authors rightly identify the limitations of prior work that relies heavily on manually crafted features and lexical resources. Finally, the careful consideration of the theoretical underpinnings of causality, referencing relevant linguistic work (Neeleman & van de Koot, 2012), provides valuable context for the work.

**Strengths:**

1. **Empirical Performance:** The proposed model achieves strong results on the AltLex corpus, surpassing the previously reported baseline in most configurations. This demonstrates the effectiveness of the neural approach for this task. The clear presentation of results (Tables 5 & 7) facilitates comparison.
2. **Thorough Experimental Evaluation:** The authors conduct a well-designed set of experiments, systematically varying key parameters (optimizers, padding strategies) to understand their impact on performance. The inclusion of a reduced corpus version and the McNemar’s test further strengthen the empirical analysis.
3. **Well-Motivated Approach:** The authors convincingly argue for the limitations of rule-based and feature-engineered approaches to causality detection. Their focus on contextual encoding via neural networks addresses a key shortcoming of previous methods. The grounding in linguistic theory strengthens the justification for this approach.
4. **Detailed Analysis:** The authors provide a detailed analysis of the results, including specific examples of correctly and incorrectly classified sentences. This qualitative analysis offers valuable insights into the model's behavior and potential areas for improvement. The discussion of the "which then" example is particularly insightful.
5. **Clear Presentation & Relevant Literature Review:** The paper is well-written and clearly structured. The related work section is comprehensive and effectively highlights the contributions of this work relative to prior research.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Architectural Simplicity:** The proposed LSTM architecture is relatively standard and lacks novel architectural components. While simplicity is not necessarily a drawback, it limits the potential impact of the work in terms of architectural innovation. The authors don’t sufficiently justify *why* this specific LSTM configuration was chosen over more complex alternatives.
2. **Limited Dataset:** The AltLex corpus, while valuable, is still relatively small. This limits the generalizability of the results and the ability to train larger, more complex models. The authors acknowledge this limitation, but it remains a concern.
3. **Lack of Ablation on Embedding Layer:** The study evaluates different optimizers and padding strategies but does not investigate the impact of different word embedding methods (e.g., Word2Vec, FastText, BERT). Exploring alternative embeddings could potentially improve performance further.
4. **Insufficient Error Analysis:** While some example error cases are discussed, a more systematic error analysis would be beneficial. Identifying common patterns in misclassifications could provide valuable clues for improving the model.
5. **Limited Discussion of Counterfactual Reasoning:** While the authors correctly dismiss the strict requirement of counterfactual reasoning, a more nuanced discussion of its role (or lack thereof) in the model's decision-making process would be helpful.

**Questions to Authors:**

1.  Why was the LSTM architecture chosen over other sequence modeling techniques, such as Transformers? Did you experiment with any Transformer-based models?
2.  Could you provide a more detailed breakdown of the types of errors the model makes? Are there specific types of causal relations or lexical markers that the model struggles with?
3.  Did you consider using attention mechanisms within the LSTM architecture to better capture the relationships between the two events?
4.  The paper mentions the imbalance in the training data. Have you explored techniques like oversampling or class weighting to address this issue?

**Additional Comments:**

*   The reference list is comprehensive.
*   The writing quality is high, making the paper easy to understand.
*   A minor point: the figure captions could be more descriptive.
* Consider adding a discussion of computational complexity/efficiency of the proposed method.
