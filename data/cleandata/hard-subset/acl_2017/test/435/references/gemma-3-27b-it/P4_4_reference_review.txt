This paper investigates neural disambiguation of causal lexical markers. The authors argue that existing approaches relying on fixed linguistic constructions or semantic resources are limited, and propose a neural network architecture leveraging LSTM to encode context and disambiguate causal meaning. They evaluate their method on the AltLex corpus, demonstrating improvements over a baseline based on lexical marker frequencies and the state-of-the-art system from Hidey and McKeown (2016).

The primary contribution of this work is the demonstration of a neural network approach for causal relation classification that outperforms existing methods on the AltLex corpus. While the task itself is well-defined, the reliance on purely neural methods, eschewing hand-crafted features, is a worthwhile investigation. The empirical results presented show consistent improvements, particularly when using the “Pair LSTM Mean Adam” configuration, offering evidence that contextual encoding via LSTMs is effective for this task. A secondary, though less substantial, contribution lies in the analysis of the AltLex corpus and the observation of challenges related to ambiguous lexical markers and imbalanced class distributions. This analysis justifies further resource development in this area.

**Strengths:**

1. **Empirical Performance:** The proposed system consistently outperforms the baseline and the state-of-the-art (Hidey & McKeown, 2016) on the AltLex corpus. The reported improvements in F1-score and Accuracy (up to 7.72% improvement over SOTA) are significant and provide strong evidence for the effectiveness of the proposed approach. The ablation study, varying padding strategies and optimizers, provides insights into the optimal configuration.
2. **Justification for Neural Approach:** The paper convincingly argues against the limitations of rule-based and feature-engineered approaches to causality detection, highlighting the inherent ambiguity and lack of fixed linguistic constructions.  The success of the neural model supports the hypothesis that learning contextual representations is crucial for disambiguation.
3. **Thorough Experimentation:** The authors evaluate multiple configurations, including different LSTM architectures (Stated Pair vs. Pair), padding strategies (Max, Mean, Mode), and optimizers (Adadelta, Adam), demonstrating a systematic approach to hyperparameter tuning and model selection.
4. **Analysis of Corpus:** The authors acknowledge the challenges posed by the AltLex corpus, including class imbalance and ambiguous lexical markers, which provides valuable insights for future work and corpus development. The discussion of specific examples misclassified by the baseline but correctly classified by their model (Table 8) is particularly insightful.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Generalizability:** The evaluation is limited to the AltLex corpus.  While the authors acknowledge this, the generalizability of the findings to other datasets and domains remains unclear. The AltLex corpus itself, while larger than previous resources, might not fully capture the diversity of causal expressions in natural language.
2. **Lack of Comparative Analysis with Stronger Baselines:** While the comparison with Hidey & McKeown (2016) is relevant, it would be beneficial to compare the proposed system with more recent and potentially stronger neural baselines, such as Transformer-based models (e.g., BERT, RoBERTa) fine-tuned for relation classification. These models have shown state-of-the-art performance on various NLP tasks and could provide a more challenging benchmark.
3. **Shallow Error Analysis:** The error analysis is limited to a few examples (Table 8). A more systematic analysis of misclassifications, potentially categorizing errors based on linguistic phenomena (e.g., ambiguous connectives, complex sentence structures), would provide deeper insights into the limitations of the model and directions for improvement.
4. **Justification of Design Choices:** The rationale behind some design choices, such as the specific LSTM architecture and the use of tanh activation functions, is not fully elaborated. A more detailed justification, potentially referencing relevant literature, would strengthen the technical soundness of the paper.

**Questions to Authors:**

1.  Have you experimented with Transformer-based models (e.g., BERT, RoBERTa) as a baseline or alternative architecture? If not, can you discuss the potential benefits and challenges of using such models for this task?
2.  Can you provide a more detailed error analysis, categorizing the types of misclassifications and identifying common patterns?
3.  Why were tanh activation functions chosen over other commonly used activation functions like ReLU?
4. The corpus was reduced to balance classes. How would the results change if class balancing techniques (e.g. weighting) were applied instead of reducing the majority class?

**Additional Comments:**

*   The paper is generally well-written and clearly structured.
*   The references are comprehensive and relevant.
*   The notation and terminology are consistent and easy to understand.
