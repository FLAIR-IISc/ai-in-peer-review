This paper investigates neural disambiguation of causal lexical markers. The authors aim to address the limitations of existing approaches to identifying causal relations, which often rely on fixed lexical lists or syntactic structures. They propose a neural network architecture, specifically an LSTM, trained on the AltLex corpus to classify the causal relationship between two events. The primary claimed contribution is a neural network approach to causal relation disambiguation that avoids reliance on predefined linguistic features, demonstrating improved performance over a baseline SVM system. A secondary, less substantial contribution is the application of neural networks to the AltLex corpus, a relatively recent resource for causal relation annotation.

**Strengths:**

1. **Addresses a relevant problem:** Causal relation identification is crucial for numerous NLP tasks, and the limitations of traditional rule-based or feature-engineered approaches are well-acknowledged. The paper correctly points out the need for methods that can generalize beyond predefined patterns.
2. **Utilizes a valuable resource:** The AltLex corpus is a significant contribution to the field, providing a more comprehensive and challenging dataset for evaluating causal relation classifiers than previously available. Leveraging this corpus is a strength of the work.
3. **Clear presentation of the problem and related work:** The introduction provides a solid overview of the challenges in representing causality linguistically and summarizes relevant research in a coherent manner. The detailed overview of related work helps to contextualize the authors’ contribution.
4. **Demonstrates competitive performance:** The proposed LSTM model achieves improved performance compared to the baseline SVM system on the AltLex corpus, as evidenced by the presented results. While the improvements are not massive, they are statistically significant and support the authors’ claim.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Lack of significant novelty in the proposed method:** The use of LSTMs for sequence encoding is a standard technique in NLP and lacks substantial innovation. The architecture is relatively simple and doesn't incorporate any novel components or mechanisms tailored specifically to the causal relation disambiguation task. The authors’ justification for choosing LSTM over CNN is weak; simply stating that CNN requires defining a kernel size is insufficient.
2. **Insufficient ablation studies:** While the paper compares different padding strategies and optimizers, a more comprehensive ablation study would be beneficial. For example, the effect of different embedding sizes, hidden layer dimensions, and regularization techniques should be investigated to provide a deeper understanding of the model's behavior.
3. **Overstated Claims and Limited Generalizability:** The claim of avoiding reliance on predefined linguistic features is misleading. The use of pre-trained word embeddings (GloVe) inherently encodes linguistic knowledge. While the model doesn't explicitly use hand-crafted features, it benefits from the information captured in the embeddings, which were trained on large text corpora exhibiting inherent linguistic structures. The results are only presented on one corpus (AltLex), limiting the assessment of the model’s generalizability to other domains or datasets.
4. **Minor methodological inconsistencies:** The description of the training process lacks important details. For instance, the specific hyperparameters used (learning rate, batch size, number of epochs) are not fully specified, hindering reproducibility. The choice of evaluation metrics is appropriate, but a discussion of confidence intervals or statistical significance tests beyond the McNemar’s test would strengthen the results.
5. **Formatting and Typos:** There are several grammatical errors and typos throughout the paper, which detract from its overall professionalism. The citation style is inconsistent in places. For example, some citations lack a year. (e.g., “Neeleman and Koot, 2012” is used without a full citation in the references).

**Questions to Authors:**

1.  Could you provide a more detailed explanation of the hyperparameters used during training, including the learning rate, batch size, and number of epochs?
2.  How sensitive is the model's performance to the choice of pre-trained word embeddings? Have you experimented with different embedding models (e.g., Word2Vec, FastText)?
3.  While the authors state they do not add a counterfactual constraint to their definition of causality, how does the model handle examples that violate this constraint? Is there any analysis of such cases?
4.  Why wasn’t the stated LSTM architecture (“Stated Pair LSTM”) superior to the simpler “Pair LSTM”? Given the stated rationale of capturing the relationship between the two events, why did connecting the LSTMs not improve performance?

**Additional Comments:**

*   The discussion of the bootstrapping process in the AltLex corpus is confusing. Clarify the differences between the two versions and the impact on the results.
*   Consider including an error analysis to identify common types of misclassifications and provide insights into potential areas for improvement.
*   Please carefully proofread the manuscript to correct grammatical errors and inconsistencies in the citation style.



Despite the paper's strengths in addressing a relevant problem and utilizing a valuable resource, the lack of substantial novelty in the proposed method, insufficient ablation studies, and overstated claims lead me to recommend **rejection**. The paper's contributions are incremental and do not warrant acceptance at a highly selective conference like ACL. The numerous minor flaws further detract from the overall quality of the submission.
