This paper investigates neural disambiguation of causal lexical markers. The authors aim to address the limitations of existing approaches to identifying causal relationships in text, which often rely on predefined syntactic structures or lexical resources. They propose a neural network architecture, specifically an LSTM, trained on the AltLex corpus to classify the causal relation between two events. The primary contribution is the application of a neural network to this task, demonstrating the potential of encoding contextual information for improved performance. A secondary contribution is the empirical evaluation on the AltLex corpus, which, while existing, has not been extensively utilized.

**Strengths:**

1. **Addressing a Core NLP Problem:** Causal relation identification is a fundamental task in NLP with applications in text understanding, question answering, and information extraction. The paper tackles a challenging aspect of this problem – disambiguating causal meaning when explicit markers are absent or ambiguous.
2. **Empirical Evaluation:** The use of the AltLex corpus is a strength, as it contains examples of more subtle causal expressions beyond those typically covered by standard discourse connectives.  The authors provide a baseline comparison, demonstrating improvement over a simple majority class baseline and a previous system (Hidey & McKeown, 2016).
3. **Focus on Contextual Encoding:** The hypothesis that neural networks can effectively encode the context surrounding causal relations is reasonable and supported by the results.  The choice of LSTM is justified as a method capable of capturing sequential information.
4. **Detailed Analysis:** The inclusion of Table 8, showcasing examples where the proposed system outperforms the baseline, provides valuable insight into the model's strengths and weaknesses. The discussion of ambiguous cases (e.g., "break") is insightful.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Novelty of the Architecture:** The proposed LSTM architecture is relatively standard. While effective, it lacks architectural innovations that would significantly advance the field. The comparison to Hidey & McKeown (2016) feels somewhat dated, and a comparison to more recent, transformer-based approaches would be beneficial.
2. **Insufficient Baseline Comparison:** While a majority class baseline is included, it’s quite weak. A stronger baseline leveraging pre-trained language models (e.g., BERT, RoBERTa) fine-tuned for binary classification would provide a more rigorous evaluation of the proposed method.  The claims of outperforming Hidey & McKeown (2016) need stronger justification, considering the advancements in NLP since that work.
3. **Data Imbalance Issues:** The authors acknowledge the class imbalance in the AltLex corpus, but the mitigation strategy (reducing the number of instances of the non-causal class) is not clearly explained or justified. This raises concerns about the generalizability of the results.  A more robust approach, such as weighted loss functions or data augmentation techniques, should be considered.
4. **Lack of Ablation Studies:** The paper presents several configurations (different padding strategies, optimizers), but lacks a systematic ablation study to determine the contribution of each component.  For example, it's unclear whether the improved performance with “Pair LSTM Mean Adam” is due to the LSTM architecture itself, the padding strategy, or the Adam optimizer.
5. **Formatting and Style Issues:** The paper contains numerous instances of awkward phrasing, grammatical errors, and inconsistent formatting (e.g., inconsistent use of italics, abbreviations). While minor, these issues detract from the overall quality of the presentation. For example, the repeated use of "\Q..."\E" around years is unnecessary and distracting.

**Questions to Authors:**

1.  Why was a standard LSTM architecture chosen over more recent, transformer-based models?  Could you provide a brief comparison of performance using a pre-trained transformer model fine-tuned for this task?
2.  Please provide a more detailed justification for the data reduction strategy used to address the class imbalance. How does this strategy affect the generalizability of your findings?  Have you experimented with alternative techniques for addressing class imbalance?
3.  Can you provide a more detailed ablation study to isolate the contribution of each component of your proposed architecture (LSTM, padding strategy, optimizer)?
4.  Could you elaborate on the potential limitations of your approach, particularly in handling long-range dependencies or complex causal reasoning scenarios?



**Additional Comments:**

*   The reference list is poorly formatted.
*   The paper would benefit from a more concise and focused introduction.
*   Consider proofreading the paper carefully to address the numerous grammatical and formatting errors.



Overall, while the paper addresses an important problem and presents promising results, the lack of architectural novelty, insufficient baseline comparison, and minor technical issues prevent a strong recommendation for acceptance. I recommend **rejection** pending significant revisions addressing the weaknesses outlined above.
