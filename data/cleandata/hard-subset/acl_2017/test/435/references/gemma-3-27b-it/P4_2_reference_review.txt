This paper addresses the task of causal discourse relation classification, focusing on neural disambiguation of causal lexical markers. The authors argue that existing approaches relying on syntactic constructions or pre-defined lexical resources are limited, and propose a neural network architecture to encode contextual information for improved causal meaning classification. The experiments are conducted on the AltLex corpus.

The primary contribution of this work is the application of LSTM-based neural networks to the task of causal discourse relation classification, demonstrating performance competitive with, and in some cases superior to, a strong baseline (Hidey & McKeown, 2016).  A secondary contribution lies in the analysis of the AltLex corpus, highlighting the challenges posed by ambiguous causal markers and showcasing the system's ability to handle such cases.  A further, albeit minor, contribution is the empirical comparison of different padding strategies (max, mean, mode) and optimizers (Adadelta, Adam) within the proposed LSTM framework, providing insights into their impact on performance.

**Strengths:**

1. **Relevance & Motivation:** The problem addressed – causal relation disambiguation – is important for natural language understanding.  The authors correctly identify the limitations of rule-based and lexicon-dependent approaches, providing a strong motivation for exploring neural network-based solutions.
2. **Solid Experimental Setup:** The experiments are conducted on the publicly available AltLex corpus, allowing for reproducibility and comparison with existing work. The inclusion of a strong baseline (Hidey & McKeown, 2016) provides a meaningful benchmark.
3. **Thorough Evaluation:** The authors evaluate multiple configurations of their proposed LSTM architecture (with and without stated pairing, different padding strategies, and optimizers), demonstrating a careful exploration of the parameter space.  The inclusion of results on both versions of the AltLex corpus (“non bootstrapping” and “bootstrapping”) offers a more robust assessment of the system’s performance.
4. **Insightful Analysis:** The error analysis, specifically regarding the handling of ambiguous lexical markers (e.g., “which then”), provides valuable insights into the strengths and weaknesses of the proposed approach. The discussion of the difficulties related to the “bootstrapping” version of the corpus is also well-articulated.
5. **Clear Presentation:** The paper is generally well-written and logically structured.  The related work section provides a good overview of previous approaches to causality detection.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Novelty of the Core Method:**  While the application of LSTMs to this specific task is reasonable, the core method itself lacks significant novelty. LSTMs are a standard tool in NLP, and the proposed architecture is relatively simple. The paper doesn’t introduce any innovative LSTM configurations or training techniques.
2. **Lack of Ablation Study:** While different configurations are evaluated, a more systematic ablation study would be beneficial. For example, removing the dense layers after the LSTM and comparing performance would help to understand the contribution of each component.
3. **Limited Scope of Evaluation:** The evaluation is entirely focused on the AltLex corpus.  Evaluating the system on other datasets (e.g., PDTB, Causal-TimeBank) would demonstrate its generalizability.
4. **Superficial Error Analysis:** While the authors identify some challenging cases (e.g., “which then”), the error analysis is relatively superficial. A more in-depth analysis of the types of errors made by the system, along with potential reasons for those errors, would be valuable.
5. **Insufficient Discussion of Counterfactuals:** The paper briefly touches upon the counterfactual constraint in causality but doesn’t fully explore its implications for the task or potential methods for incorporating it into the model.

**Questions to Authors:**

1.  Could you provide a more detailed breakdown of the types of errors made by your system, categorized by the specific linguistic phenomena involved (e.g., ambiguous lexical markers, implicit causality, complex sentence structures)?
2.  How sensitive is your system's performance to the size of the training data? Would you expect significant improvements with a larger corpus?
3.  Have you considered using attention mechanisms within the LSTM architecture to focus on the most relevant parts of the input sequence for causality disambiguation?
4.  The "stated pair LSTM" configuration does not consistently outperform the "pair LSTM" configuration. Can you elaborate on potential reasons for this?
