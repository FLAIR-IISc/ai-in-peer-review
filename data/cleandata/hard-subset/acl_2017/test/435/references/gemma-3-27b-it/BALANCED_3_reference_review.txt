This paper addresses the challenging problem of causal disambiguation, specifically identifying whether a causal relation exists between two events expressed in text. The authors argue that existing approaches relying on syntactic structures or predefined semantic resources are limited and propose a neural network architecture (LSTM) to encode contextual information for this task. They evaluate their method on the AltLex corpus.

**Main Contributions:**

1. **Empirical Evaluation on AltLex:** The primary contribution is a thorough empirical evaluation of neural network models (specifically LSTMs) on the AltLex corpus for causal disambiguation. This corpus, as highlighted by the authors, is valuable because it contains a greater diversity of causal expressions than previous resources. This evaluation itself is a significant contribution, providing a benchmark for future work in this area.
2. **Demonstration of Neural Network Effectiveness:** The authors convincingly demonstrate that LSTMs can effectively encode contextual information to disambiguate causal meaning, outperforming a strong baseline (Hidey & McKeown, 2016). This supports their hypothesis and suggests a promising direction for future research.
3. **Analysis of Ambiguity & Connectives:** The paper offers valuable insights into the challenges of causal disambiguation, particularly concerning ambiguous connectives like 'since' and 'after'. Their analysis of correctly and incorrectly classified examples sheds light on the types of linguistic constructions that pose difficulties for current models.

**Strengths:**

1. **Strong Empirical Results:** The proposed LSTM-based models consistently outperform the baseline method on the AltLex corpus, demonstrating their effectiveness. The results are presented clearly and comprehensively.
2. **Appropriate Methodology:** The choice of LSTM is well-justified, given the sequential nature of language and the need to capture contextual information. The experimental setup, including the use of pre-trained word embeddings (GloVe) and consideration of different optimization algorithms (Adadelta, Adam), is rigorous.
3. **Clear Problem Definition and Motivation:** The authors provide a clear and concise introduction to the problem of causal disambiguation and effectively motivate their approach by highlighting the limitations of existing methods.
4. **Thorough Related Work:** The paper provides a comprehensive overview of relevant related work, demonstrating a strong understanding of the field.
5. **Insightful Error Analysis:** The detailed analysis of misclassified examples provides valuable insights into the challenges of causal disambiguation and potential areas for future improvement. Specifically, Table 8 is helpful in understanding where the model succeeds and fails.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Architectural Exploration:** While the authors evaluate different LSTM configurations (Stated Pair vs. Pair), the architectural exploration is relatively limited. Exploring more sophisticated LSTM variants (e.g., bidirectional LSTMs, attention mechanisms) or other neural network architectures (e.g., Transformers) could potentially lead to further performance improvements.
2. **Data Imbalance:** The AltLex corpus suffers from a class imbalance (more non-causal examples). While the authors address this issue with a reduced dataset in a later experiment, it's not clear whether this reduction fully mitigates the impact of the imbalance during initial training. Further investigation of techniques to address class imbalance (e.g., weighted loss functions, oversampling) could be beneficial.
3. **Lack of Ablation Study:** An ablation study to systematically evaluate the contribution of different components of the proposed architecture (e.g., the concatenation of the lexical marker and the second event) would provide a more nuanced understanding of the model's behavior.
4. **Limited Generalizability Discussion:** The paper focuses exclusively on the AltLex corpus. While this is a reasonable starting point, the authors do not sufficiently discuss the potential generalizability of their findings to other datasets or domains.
5. **Superficial Argument Identification Discussion:** The paper mentions the task of causal argument identification, but largely dismisses it. A brief discussion of how their approach might be extended to address this subtask, or the challenges in doing so, would enhance the work.



**Questions to Authors:**

1. Have you considered using bidirectional LSTMs to capture information from both past and future contexts?
2. Could you provide more details on the specific hyperparameters used for each LSTM configuration (e.g., hidden layer size, dropout rate, learning rate)?
3. Did you experiment with different word embedding initialization methods beyond GloVe?
4. How sensitive are the results to the zero-padding strategy used?  Could you provide a more detailed comparison of the performance of the different strategies?
5. Given the class imbalance in the AltLex corpus, did you explore any techniques for addressing this issue (e.g., weighted loss functions, oversampling)?

**Additional Comments:**

*   The use of tables and figures is effective in presenting the results and analysis.
*   The writing is generally clear and concise.
*   The references are appropriately cited.
*   It would be helpful to have a brief discussion of the computational cost of the proposed approach.



Overall, this is a well-written and well-executed paper that makes a valuable contribution to the field of causal disambiguation. The authors have successfully demonstrated the effectiveness of neural networks for this task and provided insightful analysis of the challenges involved. While there are some areas for improvement, the strengths of the paper outweigh its weaknesses. I recommend acceptance.
