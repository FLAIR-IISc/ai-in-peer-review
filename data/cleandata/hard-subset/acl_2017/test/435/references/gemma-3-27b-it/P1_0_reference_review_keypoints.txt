- Task: Identify causal relations between events, focusing on cases without explicit markers.
- Approach: Proposes LSTM-based neural architecture to encode context for causal disambiguation, arguing against syntax- or lexicon-only methods.
- Dataset: Evaluation performed on the AltLex corpus, targeting implicit causality.
- Results: LSTM approach outperforms a baseline SVM and a majority-class baseline.
- Contribution 1: Thorough empirical evaluation of neural models on the AltLex corpus as a benchmark.
- Contribution 2: Demonstrates that contextual encoding with LSTMs improves causal disambiguation.
- Contribution 3: Argues for neural models over reliance on syntactic structures or predefined lexical resources.
- Strength: Problem is well-motivated and relevant to downstream NLP tasks.
- Strength: Solid experimental setup including comparisons to a known baseline and exploration of LSTM variants.
- Strength: Clear, well-structured paper with comprehensive related work and background.
- Strength: Offers insightful analysis on ambiguous connectives and limitations of lexical resources.
- Strength: Consistent performance gains over baselines.
- Weakness: Methodological novelty is limited—the LSTM architecture is fairly standard.
- Weakness: Missing detailed ablation study on hyperparameters and architecture choices.
- Weakness: Insufficient systematic error analysis and lack of misclassification examples.
- Weakness: Class imbalance in AltLex is acknowledged but not adequately addressed with techniques like weighted loss or augmentation.
- Weakness: Scope limited to causal meaning classification, not integrated with causal argument identification.
- Questions for authors: Provide detailed error analysis with examples, run ablation studies on LSTM configurations, explain handling of class imbalance, and discuss plans to integrate argument identification.
- Additional comments: References are comprehensive; table formatting could be improved; include code and trained models for reproducibility.
- Overall: Solid, well-executed paper with valuable empirical contribution—recommended for acceptance pending satisfactory responses to raised questions.
