This paper investigates neural disambiguation of causal lexical markers. The primary claimed contribution is a neural network architecture for identifying causal relations between events, demonstrating that neural networks can encode contextual meaning to determine causality, achieving improved performance on the AltLex corpus compared to existing methods. A secondary, less compelling contribution is the re-assertion of the idea that causation isn't strictly tied to specific syntactic constructions or lexical resources, a point already well-established in the literature.

**Strengths:**

1. **Empirical Results:** The paper demonstrates consistent improvements over a baseline (Hidey & McKeown, 2016) on the AltLex corpus. While the gains aren't dramatic, they are statistically significant and indicate a viable approach. The ablation studies concerning the optimizer and padding strategies are well-conducted and insightful.
2. **Clear Problem Definition:** The authors clearly define the task of causal meaning classification and appropriately separate it from the more complex problem of identifying causal arguments. Focusing on disambiguation is a reasonable scope.
3. **Relevant Background & Motivation:** The introduction provides a good overview of the challenges in computational approaches to causality, referencing key works and highlighting the limitations of previous methods based on syntactic rules or lexical resources. The motivation for exploring neural approaches is thus well-established.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Lack of Novelty in Method:** The proposed neural network architecture – LSTMs with dense layers – is exceedingly standard.  There is nothing particularly innovative about the network *structure* itself. The value lies in its application to this specific task and the empirical validation, but the method itself doesn't represent a significant methodological advance. The rationale for choosing LSTMs over CNNs, while reasonable, is not deeply investigated.
2. **Over-Reliance on a Single Dataset:** The entire evaluation hinges on the AltLex corpus. While this corpus is acknowledged as relatively new, relying solely on it limits the generalizability of the findings. A more robust evaluation would involve testing on other datasets, or at least a careful analysis of how performance might vary across different domains or text types.  The paper acknowledges the corpus's class imbalance and attempts to address it, but the concern remains.
3. **Limited Error Analysis:** While the authors point to some interesting examples of correctly classified instances where the baseline fails, a more thorough error analysis is needed. Understanding *where* the model struggles—specific types of causal expressions, ambiguous cases—would provide valuable insights for future improvements. The discussion of the “which then” example is a good start, but more systematic analysis is needed.
4. **Missing Comparison to Stronger Baselines:** Comparing against Hidey & McKeown (2016) is appropriate as a direct comparison to the corpus's original evaluation. However, including more recent, and potentially stronger, neural baselines (e.g., pre-trained language models like BERT or RoBERTa fine-tuned for this task) would provide a more compelling demonstration of the proposed approach’s superiority.

**Questions to Authors:**

1.  Could you provide a more detailed error analysis, categorizing the types of errors the system makes (e.g., misclassifying temporal relations as causal, failing to capture subtle contextual cues)?
2.  Have you experimented with pre-trained language models (e.g., BERT, RoBERTa) as a baseline, or integrated them into your architecture? If not, why?
3.  The paper mentions a two-step task (causal meaning classification and argument identification).  Do you envision a joint model addressing both tasks simultaneously, and if so, what are the potential benefits?
4.  The 'bootstrapping' version of the AltLex corpus has instances that deviate from the training set’s class distribution. How might this affect the generalization ability of the model, and what steps could be taken to mitigate this issue?



**Additional Comments:**

The writing is generally clear and concise. Table and figure captions are informative. The reference list is comprehensive. The discussion of related work is adequate, but could benefit from a more critical assessment of the limitations of existing approaches. The claim about dismissing the counterfactual constraint feels somewhat hand-waved. A more nuanced discussion of its role in human causal reasoning would be valuable.
