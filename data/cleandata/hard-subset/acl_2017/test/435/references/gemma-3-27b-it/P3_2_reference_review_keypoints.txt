- Tackles disambiguating causal relations signaled by non-canonical lexical markers using context-aware neural models.  
- Proposes an LSTM-based encoder to learn contextual signals for causal relation disambiguation and evaluates on the AltLex corpus with competitive results.

- Empirically validates that LSTMs can learn contextual patterns to disambiguate causal relations, improving over a prior baseline.  
- Focuses on the challenging AltLex problem of non-canonical causal markers, addressing an under-studied but important case.  
- Uses the AltLex corpus effectively as an evaluation platform, increasing visibility of this resource.

- Strong experimental results showing competitive or superior performance to Hidey & McKeown (2016), with useful ablation studies.  
- Clear motivation and problem definition explaining limits of syntax-based methods and need for context.  
- Thorough error analysis with example cases highlighting model strengths and failure modes.  
- Well-structured, readable paper with comprehensive related work.

- Limited exploration of architectures—no comparison with Transformer-based models to test robustness.  
- Insufficient hyperparameter sensitivity analysis and lack of result variance reporting across runs.  
- Concerns about dataset bias and generalization beyond the AltLex corpus; no external dataset evaluation.  
- Missing ablation on word embedding choices (random init vs. other pre-trained embeddings).

- Were Transformer-based models considered and what is their computational cost for this task?  
- How were final LSTM hyperparameters selected (grid search, random search, etc.) and what was the search procedure?  
- What potential biases exist in the AltLex corpus and were any mitigation steps applied during training?  
- Can you provide a deeper qualitative error analysis with more specific examples beyond Table 8?

- Clarify dimensions and notation in Equation 2.  
- Condense the introduction’s overview of causal reasoning approaches to focus on the most relevant work.

- Overall: strong, well-executed paper with convincing empirical results; recommend acceptance with minor revisions.
