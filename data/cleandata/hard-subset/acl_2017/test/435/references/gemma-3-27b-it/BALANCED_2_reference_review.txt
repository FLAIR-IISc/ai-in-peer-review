This paper addresses the task of causal discourse relation classification, focusing on neural disambiguation of causal lexical markers. The authors argue that existing approaches relying on syntactic constructions or predefined lexical resources are limited, and propose a neural network architecture (LSTM) to encode the context of the relation for improved disambiguation. They evaluate their approach on the AltLex corpus, demonstrating performance improvements over a baseline SVM system.

**Main Contributions:**

1. **Empirical Evaluation on AltLex:** The primary contribution is a thorough empirical evaluation of neural network models (specifically LSTMs) on the AltLex corpus for causal relation classification. While the AltLex corpus itself is not new, applying and evaluating neural methods on it is a valuable step.
2. **LSTM-based Architecture for Contextual Encoding:** The paper proposes and investigates an LSTM-based architecture to encode the contextual information surrounding potential causal relations. This is a reasonable approach given the limitations of relying solely on lexical markers.
3. **Analysis of Ambiguous Connectives:** The paper highlights the challenge posed by ambiguous causal connectives (like ‘since’ and ‘after’) and demonstrates that their system can, in some cases, outperform a traditional approach on such instances.

**Strengths:**

1. **Clear Problem Definition and Motivation:** The paper clearly articulates the limitations of existing approaches to causal relation identification, especially concerning the coverage of non-canonical expressions. The motivation for moving beyond rule-based and lexical resource-dependent methods is well-presented.
2. **Solid Experimental Setup:** The experimental setup is reasonably well-defined, including a comparison to a strong baseline (Hidey & McKeown, 2016) and exploration of different LSTM configurations and optimizers. The ablation studies (e.g., “Stated Pair LSTM” vs. “Pair LSTM”) provide useful insights.
3. **Insightful Analysis:** The analysis of results, particularly regarding the performance on ambiguous connectives, is insightful. The authors correctly point out that the system's ability to handle such cases is a positive indicator. The discussion regarding the frequency of causal vs. non-causal examples and its impact is also valuable.
4. **Well-Written and Organized:** The paper is generally well-written and clearly organized. The related work section provides a good overview of existing approaches. The descriptions of the method and experiments are easy to follow.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Novelty of the Method:** While the application of LSTMs to this specific task is a reasonable approach, the proposed architecture itself isn’t particularly novel. Using LSTMs for sequence encoding is a standard technique in NLP.  The contribution lies more in the application and empirical validation rather than a novel architectural innovation.
2. **Corpus Limitations & Data Imbalance:** The AltLex corpus, while valuable, is relatively small. The class imbalance (highlighted in the paper itself) could be impacting the results and needs further attention. The authors mitigated this with a data reduction, but this wasn't fully reflected in their final results table.  Reporting results on the reduced dataset *alongside* the original would have been useful.
3. **Lack of Error Analysis:**  While the authors identify instances where their system outperforms the baseline, a more detailed error analysis would be beneficial. Understanding *why* the system makes mistakes (beyond simply stating that ambiguous connectives are difficult) would provide valuable insights for future improvements. What types of errors are most common? Are there specific linguistic patterns that consistently cause the system to fail?
4. **Limited Discussion of Counterfactuals and Temporal Ordering:** The paper briefly mentions the counterfactual and temporal constraints on causality but doesn't integrate them into the model. While the authors justify this decision, exploring the impact of explicitly incorporating these constraints (perhaps as additional features or through a more sophisticated model architecture) could be a promising avenue for future work.



**Questions to Authors:**

1.  Could you provide a more detailed error analysis, categorizing the types of errors the system makes?  Specifically, what linguistic features are associated with misclassifications?
2.  You mention reducing the dataset to address class imbalance.  Did you experiment with other techniques to address this issue, such as data augmentation or weighted loss functions? If so, what were the results?
3.  How sensitive are the results to the choice of word embeddings?  Did you experiment with different pre-trained embeddings (e.g., FastText, BERT)?
4.  Regarding the architecture, was any consideration given to attention mechanisms to better focus on the relevant parts of the input sequence?

**Additional Comments:**

*   Table 5 is difficult to read due to its width. Consider breaking it into multiple tables or using a more compact format.
*   The citation style is inconsistent (e.g., “Hidey and McKeown, 2016” vs. “Kruengkrai et al., 2017”). Please ensure consistency throughout the paper.
*   In the introduction, you state that you assess the method *in* the Hidey and McKeown (2016) corpus, but later clarify it’s the AltLex corpus. This needs clarification.




Overall, this is a solid and well-executed paper. The contribution is valuable, particularly the empirical evaluation of LSTMs on the AltLex corpus. While the method itself isn't groundbreaking, the careful experimentation, insightful analysis, and clear writing make it a worthwhile contribution to the field. Addressing the weaknesses identified above – particularly performing a more thorough error analysis – would significantly strengthen the paper. I recommend acceptance.
