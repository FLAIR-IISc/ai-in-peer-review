This paper addresses the task of causal discourse relation classification, focusing on neural disambiguation of causal lexical markers. The authors argue that existing approaches relying on syntactic constructions or lexical resources are limited and propose a neural network architecture to encode contextual information for improved classification. They evaluate their approach on the AltLex corpus, demonstrating performance improvements over a baseline system.

The primary contribution of this work is the application of a neural network (specifically, LSTMs) to the task of causal discourse relation classification *without* heavy reliance on pre-defined features or lexical resources. While neural approaches are increasingly common in NLP, applying them specifically to disentangle causal meaning signaled by potentially ambiguous lexical markers is a worthwhile endeavor. The exploration of different LSTM configurations (Stated Pair vs. Pair) and optimization strategies (Adadelta vs. Adam) provides a solid empirical investigation. A secondary contribution lies in the evaluation on the AltLex corpus, a valuable resource for this task, which has seen limited prior use. The detailed ablation studies and error analysis offer insights into the strengths and weaknesses of the proposed method.

**Strengths:**

1. **Relevant and Important Problem:** Causal reasoning is fundamental to natural language understanding, and accurately identifying causal relationships is crucial for various NLP tasks. The challenge of handling ambiguous causal markers is well-motivated.
2. **Solid Empirical Evaluation:** The paper presents a thorough evaluation on the AltLex corpus, comparing the proposed method against a strong baseline. The ablation studies exploring different architectural choices (LSTM configurations, optimizers) are well-executed and provide valuable insights. The use of McNemar's test to demonstrate statistical significance is appropriate.
3. **Clear and Detailed Methodology:** The paper provides a clear description of the neural network architecture, training procedures, and experimental setup. The inclusion of details regarding padding strategies and hyperparameter tuning increases the reproducibility of the results.
4. **Insightful Error Analysis:** The discussion of specific examples where the proposed system outperforms the baseline and the analysis of challenging cases (e.g., the verb "break", the connective "which then") are particularly helpful in understanding the model's capabilities and limitations.
5. **Well-structured and Readable:** The paper is generally well-organized, clearly written, and easy to follow. The related work section provides a comprehensive overview of previous approaches to causality detection.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Novelty in Architecture:** The use of LSTMs, while effective, is not particularly novel in the context of sequence modeling. The core innovation lies in applying this technique specifically to the causal disambiguation problem with a focus on context encoding, rather than proposing a fundamentally new architectural component.
2. **Data Limitations:** The AltLex corpus, while valuable, is relatively small. This limits the generalizability of the findings and the potential for training more complex models. The authors acknowledge this limitation but further investigation of data augmentation techniques or transfer learning from related tasks might strengthen the work.
3. **Lack of Comparison to More Recent Neural Models:** The comparison is primarily to the Hidey and McKeown (2016) baseline.  More recent and sophisticated neural models for discourse relation classification (e.g., those based on Transformers) should be considered for benchmarking, even if they require significant computational resources.
4. **Binary Classification Focus:** The task is framed as a binary classification problem (Causal/Non-Causal). A more nuanced approach, such as identifying the *type* of causal relation or the strength of the causal connection, could provide richer insights.
5. **Limited Discussion of Counterfactual Reasoning:** While the paper acknowledges and dismisses the counterfactual constraint, a brief discussion of its relevance to human causal understanding and potential avenues for incorporating it into the model could be beneficial.

**Questions to Authors:**

1. Could you elaborate on the potential benefits of exploring Transformer-based models (e.g., BERT, RoBERTa) for this task, considering their ability to capture long-range dependencies and contextual information? What are the computational challenges associated with applying these models to the AltLex corpus?
2. The authors mention the unbalanced class distribution in the AltLex corpus and employ a reduction strategy. Have you experimented with other techniques to address class imbalance, such as weighted loss functions or oversampling? If so, how did these techniques affect the performance of the system?
3.  The analysis focuses on distinguishing causal from non-causal relations. How well does the system perform on identifying the specific *type* of causal relation when explicit cues are present (e.g., cause-effect, reason-result)?
4.  You mention the desire to create a new corpus. What specific characteristics would this new corpus have that would address the limitations of the AltLex corpus (e.g., size, diversity of causal expressions, annotation scheme)?
