## Review for "Neural Disambiguation of Causal Lexical Markers based on Context"

**Summary:**

This paper addresses the task of causal relation classification, specifically determining if a causal relationship exists between two events given a connecting expression. The authors argue that existing approaches relying on syntactic structures or lexical resources are limited and propose a neural network architecture (LSTM) to encode contextual information for improved disambiguation. They evaluate their approach on the AltLex corpus, demonstrating performance exceeding a strong baseline and achieving competitive results.

**Main Contributions (as I see them):**

1. **Empirical Evaluation on AltLex:** The primary contribution is the thorough empirical evaluation of neural network models (specifically LSTMs) on the AltLex corpus. This corpus, with its focus on non-explicit causal markers, is a valuable resource for this task, and the results provide a benchmark for future work.
2. **Demonstration of Contextual Encoding:** The paper successfully demonstrates that leveraging contextual information via LSTMs improves performance over a baseline that relies on lexical marker properties. This supports the authors' core argument that causality isn't solely determined by explicit connectives.
3. **Focused Task Definition:** The authors clearly define the task as *causal meaning classification* rather than attempting full causal argument identification, acknowledging the complexity and narrowing the scope to a manageable problem.

**Strengths:**

1. **Strong Empirical Results:** The proposed LSTM models consistently outperform the strong baseline (Hidey & McKeown, 2016) on the AltLex corpus, showcasing a statistically significant improvement in F1 score (particularly in the “non bootstrapping” version). This provides strong evidence for the effectiveness of their approach.
2. **Relevant Background & Motivation:** The paper provides a good overview of existing approaches to causality in linguistics and computational linguistics, effectively motivating the need for a more flexible, context-aware approach. The critique of syntax-grounded and lexical resource-dependent methods is well-articulated.
3. **Clear Task Definition & Experimental Setup:** The authors clearly define the task and the evaluation metrics. The experimental setup is well-described, including details about the data preprocessing, model architecture, and hyperparameters. The ablation studies with different padding strategies and optimizers are valuable.
4. **Analysis of Results:** The authors present a thoughtful analysis of the results, highlighting the performance differences between different model configurations and discussing the challenges posed by ambiguous connectives. The example-based analysis (Table 8) is particularly insightful.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Novelty of the Method:** While the application to this specific task and dataset is valuable, the core LSTM architecture itself isn’t particularly novel. The contribution lies more in the application and validation than in a groundbreaking new neural network design. This isn't necessarily a fatal flaw, but it diminishes the overall impact.
2. **Corpus Dependency:** The results are entirely dependent on the AltLex corpus. While this is a good dataset, the generalizability of the findings to other domains or types of text isn’t clear. More evaluation on other corpora (even smaller ones) would strengthen the claims.
3. **Lack of Error Analysis:** While the authors provide some example-based analysis, a more systematic error analysis (e.g., categorizing the types of errors the model makes) would provide further insights into the model's limitations and guide future improvements.  What *types* of causal relationships are still hard to identify?
4. **Missing Discussion of Counterfactual Reasoning:** While the authors dismiss the counterfactual constraint, a brief discussion of its potential role in understanding human causal reasoning (even if ultimately not implemented in the model) would strengthen the connection to the linguistic foundations discussed in the introduction.



**Questions to Authors:**

1.  **Generalizability:** How do you believe the performance would generalize to other corpora with different characteristics (e.g., a domain-specific corpus like biomedical text)? Do you have plans to evaluate on other datasets?
2.  **Error Types:** Could you provide a more detailed error analysis, categorizing the types of errors the model makes (e.g., misclassifying temporal relations as causal, failing to capture subtle causal nuances)?
3.  **Alternative Architectures:** Did you explore other neural network architectures, such as Transformers, and if so, why did you choose LSTMs?
4. **Impact of Lexical Marker Embeddings:** Did you experiment with using pre-trained embeddings specifically for the causal connectives, rather than relying solely on general-purpose word embeddings like GloVe?



**Additional Comments:**

* The paper is well-written and generally easy to follow.
*  Minor: There are a few instances of awkward phrasing or typos throughout the text.
*  Consider adding a visualization of the model architecture to Figure 1.



**Overall Assessment:**

I believe this paper is a solid contribution to the field of causal relation classification. The empirical evaluation on the AltLex corpus is valuable, and the results demonstrate the effectiveness of using LSTMs to encode contextual information. While the method itself isn’t groundbreaking, the application to this specific task and dataset is significant.  I recommend **acceptance** with minor revisions addressing the weaknesses outlined above.
