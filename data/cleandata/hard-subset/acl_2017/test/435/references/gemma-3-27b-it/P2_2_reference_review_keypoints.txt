- Summary: LSTM-based approach disambiguates causal lexical markers from context and outperforms a strong baseline on the AltLex corpus.
- Contribution: Empirical validation that contextual neural encodings can disambiguate causality without heavy hand-engineering.
- Contribution: Evaluation on the challenging AltLex corpus targeting nuanced causal expressions.
- Contribution: Demonstration of a competitive LSTM architecture that sets a strong baseline for future work.
- Strength: Strong experimental results and ablation studies showing consistent improvement over Hidey & McKeown (2016).
- Strength: Well-motivated problem framing highlighting limits of rule/lexicon-based methods.
- Strength: Thorough and well-situated related work discussion.
- Strength: Clear, logical presentation and effective paper organization.
- Strength: Insightful analysis of performance on ambiguous connectives (e.g., “which then”).
- Weakness: Evaluation limited to a single dataset, reducing generalizability.
- Weakness: Relatively simple LSTM architecture; lacks exploration of Transformers or pre-trained LMs like BERT.
- Weakness: Insufficient detailed error analysis to identify persistent failure modes.
- Weakness: Inadequate discussion of counterfactual reasoning and its potential impact on modeling causality.
- Question: What is the computational cost of your approach versus the baseline, and is the performance gain justified?
- Question: Did you try techniques (oversampling, class weighting) to address AltLex bootstrapping class imbalance, and with what results?
- Question: What specific error types or examples occur for ambiguous connectives like “since” or “after,” and why are they misclassified?
- Question: Do you plan to explore Transformer-based models for this task?
- Additional: Reference list is extensive and well-formatted.
- Additional: Table formatting/readability could be improved (e.g., bold best results).
- Overall assessment: Solid, well-executed paper that merits acceptance with minor revisions.
