## Peer Review: Neural Disambiguation of Causal Lexical Markers based on Context

**Summary:**

This paper tackles the task of causal relation classification, specifically identifying whether a causal relationship exists between two events signaled by a lexical marker. The authors argue that existing approaches relying on syntactic constructions or lexical resources are limited and propose a neural network architecture (LSTM) to encode context and disambiguate causal meaning. They evaluate their approach on the AltLex corpus, demonstrating performance improvements over a baseline SVM system. The core claim is that neural encoding of context, rather than relying on predefined rules or lexical knowledge, is effective for this task.

**Main Contributions (as I see them):**

1. **Empirical Evaluation on AltLex:** The primary contribution is the application and thorough evaluation of neural network models on the AltLex corpus. This corpus, focusing on less-explicit causal markers, provides a valuable testbed for this problem.
2. **Demonstration of LSTM effectiveness:**  The paper shows that LSTM networks can achieve competitive performance in identifying causal relationships, suggesting the benefit of contextual encoding for this task.
3. **Highlighting limitations of rule-based/lexical approaches:** The paper correctly points out the limitations of traditional approaches that rely heavily on predefined linguistic rules or lexical resources, motivating the need for more flexible methods.

**Strengths:**

1. **Relevant and Well-Motivated Problem:** Causal reasoning is a crucial aspect of natural language understanding, and disambiguating causal expressions is a challenging and important task. The authors clearly articulate the shortcomings of existing approaches.
2. **Solid Experimental Setup:** The experimental setup is well-defined, including a clear description of the dataset, baseline, and evaluation metrics.  Comparing against the Hidey & McKeown (2016) baseline provides a valuable benchmark.
3. **Detailed Analysis and Ablation:** The authors experiment with different LSTM architectures (stated pair vs. pair) and optimization algorithms (Adadelta vs. Adam), providing insights into the influence of these choices on performance. The analysis of results on the bootstrapping version of the corpus is particularly insightful.
4. **Clear Writing and Organization:** The paper is generally well-written and logically organized, making it easy to follow the authors’ reasoning and understand the experimental results.
5. **Insightful Discussion of Limitations:** The paper acknowledges and discusses the limitations of its approach, such as the reliance on word embeddings and the lack of argument identification, fostering realistic expectations.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Novelty of Method:** While the application to the AltLex corpus is valuable, the core LSTM architecture itself is not particularly novel.  LSTM networks are a standard approach for sequence modeling, and the specific architecture used here is relatively straightforward. The primary innovation lies in the application to this specific problem.
2. **Lack of Comparison with Stronger Baselines:** While the comparison with Hidey & McKeown (2016) is a good starting point, comparing against more recent, state-of-the-art neural models (e.g., Transformers) would strengthen the paper. It is unclear how the proposed LSTM architecture performs relative to more powerful contemporary models.
3. **Data Imbalance:** The paper acknowledges the class imbalance in the AltLex corpus and attempts to address it, but the impact of the imbalance on the results remains unclear. More rigorous evaluation techniques, such as stratified cross-validation, could help mitigate this issue.
4. **Limited Error Analysis:**  While the authors provide some examples of correctly and incorrectly classified instances, a more comprehensive error analysis would provide further insights into the strengths and weaknesses of the proposed model. Understanding *why* the model makes certain errors could guide future improvements.
5. **Missing Discussion of Argument Identification:** The paper briefly mentions the subtask of causal argument identification but does not delve into its challenges or potential integration with the causal meaning classification task. Addressing this aspect would enrich the study.

**Questions to Authors:**

1.  **Transformer Comparison:** Have you experimented with Transformer-based models for this task? If so, how does their performance compare to the LSTM-based model? If not, why not?
2. **Error Analysis:** Could you provide a more detailed error analysis, categorizing the types of errors the model makes (e.g., misclassifying temporal relations as causal, struggling with specific lexical markers)?
3. **Data Augmentation:** Have you considered data augmentation techniques to address the class imbalance in the AltLex corpus?
4. **Argument Identification Integration:**  How would you envision integrating the causal argument identification subtask with the causal meaning classification task? Would a joint model improve performance?
5. **Hyperparameter Tuning:** What hyperparameters were explored during the training process, and how were they selected?  Could you provide more details about the hyperparameter tuning process?



**Additional Comments:**

*   The use of tables and figures is effective in presenting the experimental results.
*   The references are well-organized and relevant.
*   The paper is a solid contribution to the field of computational linguistics and provides a valuable evaluation of neural networks for causal relation classification.



Overall, this is a well-executed and insightful study. While the core methodology isn’t groundbreaking, the application to the AltLex corpus, the thorough experimental evaluation, and the insightful analysis make it a worthwhile contribution. Addressing the weaknesses outlined above, particularly by comparing against stronger baselines and providing a more in-depth error analysis, would further strengthen the paper. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions.
