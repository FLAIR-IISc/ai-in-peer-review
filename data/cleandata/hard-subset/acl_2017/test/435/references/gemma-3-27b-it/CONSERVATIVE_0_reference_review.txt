This paper investigates the task of causal discourse relation disambiguation, specifically focusing on instances where causal meaning isn’t signaled by standard connectives. The authors propose a neural network architecture – a pair of LSTMs – to encode the context of potential causal relations and classify them as causal or non-causal. The primary contribution, as I see it, is the application of a relatively straightforward neural approach to a challenging problem, evaluated on the AltLex corpus. A secondary, more modest contribution is the detailed examination of the AltLex corpus itself, highlighting its characteristics and challenges. Finally, the paper’s explicit acknowledgement of the complexities of defining and identifying causality, particularly regarding counterfactual reasoning, is a valuable discussion point.

**Strengths:**

1. **Focus on a difficult problem:** The disambiguation of implicit causal relations is a genuinely challenging task in NLP. Moving beyond explicitly signaled causality is crucial for achieving deeper language understanding. The authors correctly identify the limitations of approaches relying solely on predefined causal connectives.
2. **Empirical Evaluation:** The paper provides empirical results on the AltLex corpus, a resource specifically designed to address the limitations of earlier datasets. This evaluation, while not demonstrating groundbreaking performance gains, establishes a baseline for future work and showcases the applicability of neural methods to this specific corpus. The comparisons to the Hidey & McKeown (2016) baseline are helpful.
3. **Clear Problem Definition:** The authors provide a reasonable definition of the causality classification task, acknowledging the debate around counterfactual conditions and focusing on a more practical, observable definition.  The decomposition into causal meaning classification and argument identification is a sensible framing.
4. **Detailed Corpus Analysis:** The paper provides a useful analysis of the AltLex corpus, including the distribution of lexical markers and the presence of ambiguity. This analysis highlights the complexities of the dataset and informs the experimental setup.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Lack of Novelty in Method:** The proposed LSTM-based architecture is rather standard and lacks significant methodological innovation. While LSTMs are appropriate for sequence modeling, the architecture itself doesn’t offer a clear advantage over other established methods. The choice isn't particularly justified beyond a general preference for sequence modeling.  A more thorough exploration of alternative neural architectures or integration of attention mechanisms would have strengthened the contribution.
2. **Limited Performance Improvement:** The reported performance improvements over the baseline (Hidey & McKeown, 2016) are modest, particularly considering the complexity of the task and the application of a neural network.  This raises concerns about the effectiveness of the chosen architecture and the potential for overfitting, despite the regularization attempts. The gains are not substantial enough to warrant a strong endorsement.
3. **Over-Reliance on AltLex:** The exclusive evaluation on the AltLex corpus is a significant limitation. While AltLex addresses shortcomings of previous datasets, it is still a relatively small and potentially biased resource.  Generalizability to other domains and types of text remains unproven.  Evaluation on PDTB or a more diverse dataset would have been essential.
4. **Insufficient Error Analysis:** The paper lacks a detailed error analysis. Identifying the types of errors the system makes (e.g., misclassifying specific types of lexical markers, failing to capture long-range dependencies) would provide valuable insights for future research and model refinement.  The brief discussion of the “which then” example is a start, but more systematic analysis is needed.



**Questions to Authors:**

1.  Why was a standard LSTM architecture chosen over more recent and potentially more effective alternatives like Transformers or architectures incorporating attention mechanisms?
2.  Can you provide a more detailed error analysis, categorizing the types of errors made by the system and explaining the potential reasons for these errors?
3.  Have you considered evaluating your system on other datasets, such as PDTB, to assess its generalizability? If not, why?
4.  The paper mentions different padding strategies. Could you provide ablation study results comparing these strategies?



**Additional Comments:**

The paper is well-written and clearly presented. However, the lack of methodological innovation and the modest performance improvements limit its overall impact. The corpus analysis is valuable, but doesn't fully compensate for the shortcomings in the modeling approach. The discussion of related work is comprehensive, but somewhat descriptive rather than critically evaluative.
