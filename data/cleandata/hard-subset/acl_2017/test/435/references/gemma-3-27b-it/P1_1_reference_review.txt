## Peer Review for ACL Submission: "Neural Disambiguation of Causal Lexical Markers based on Context"

**Summary:**

This paper tackles the problem of identifying causal relationships between events, focusing on the challenge that causal meaning isn’t always signaled by explicit lexical markers. The authors argue against syntax-grounded or lexicon-dependent approaches, proposing a neural network architecture (LSTM) to encode contextual information for causal disambiguation. They evaluate their approach on the AltLex corpus, demonstrating improvements over a baseline SVM system and a simple majority-class baseline.

**Main Contributions (as I see them):**

1. **Empirical Evaluation on AltLex:** The paper provides a solid empirical evaluation on the relatively new AltLex corpus, offering valuable insights into the performance of neural models on a challenging dataset that specifically targets ambiguous causal expressions.
2. **Demonstration of Neural Network Effectiveness:** The work demonstrates that a relatively simple LSTM-based architecture can outperform existing methods (specifically, the Hidey & McKeown 2016 system) on this task, suggesting the value of contextual encoding.
3. **Focus on Implicit Causality:** The paper correctly identifies the importance of addressing implicit causal relationships, a crucial aspect of natural language understanding often overlooked by relying solely on explicit markers.

**Strengths:**

1. **Clear Motivation & Problem Definition:** The paper clearly articulates the challenges of causal relation identification, outlining the limitations of previous approaches rooted in syntactic structure or pre-defined lexical resources. The justification for a context-based neural approach is well-presented.
2. **Sound Experimental Setup:** The choice of the AltLex corpus is appropriate, given its focus on ambiguous causal expressions. The inclusion of strong baselines (Hidey & McKeown’s SVM and a majority-class classifier) provides a solid benchmark for comparison.
3. **Detailed Analysis & Discussion:** The authors present a detailed analysis of the results, discussing the impact of different LSTM configurations, optimizers, and padding strategies. They also offer insightful discussion regarding the limitations of the corpus and the potential for future work. The specific examples presented in section 4.3 are particularly helpful in understanding the model's strengths and weaknesses.
4. **Well-Written and Organized:** The paper is generally well-written and logically organized, making it easy to follow the authors’ line of reasoning.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Architectural Exploration:** While the LSTM architecture shows promise, the exploration of different neural network configurations feels somewhat limited.  The paper primarily focuses on comparing variations of the LSTM architecture (stated vs. paired) and hyperparameters, without considering other potentially relevant architectures like Transformers or attention mechanisms that are now commonplace in NLP.  
2. **Lack of Ablation Studies:**  The paper doesn’t present ablation studies to understand the contribution of different components of the proposed model. For instance, it would be beneficial to analyze the effect of using pre-trained word embeddings versus randomly initialized embeddings, or the impact of different embedding dimensions.
3. **Corpus Limitations Not Fully Addressed in Method:** While the authors acknowledge the class imbalance and potential for inconsistencies within the AltLex corpus (especially in the bootstrapped version), the proposed model doesn’t appear to incorporate any specific techniques to mitigate these issues (e.g., weighted loss functions, data augmentation). Addressing these issues within the model itself could improve performance.
4. **Limited Error Analysis:** The error analysis is primarily focused on specific examples. A more systematic error analysis, categorizing the types of errors the model makes (e.g., misclassifying temporal relations as causal, struggling with specific connectives), would provide valuable insights for future improvement.

**Questions to Authors:**

1. **Architectural Justification:** Could you elaborate on the rationale behind choosing LSTM over other contemporary architectures like Transformers, particularly given the sequence-to-sequence nature of the task?  Have you experimented with attention mechanisms?
2. **Corpus Imbalance Mitigation:**  Given the class imbalance and inconsistencies in the AltLex corpus, did you consider employing techniques like weighted loss functions or data augmentation to address these issues during training? If not, why?
3. **Ablation Study Details:**  Could you provide a more detailed justification for the chosen hyperparameters (e.g., dropout rate, regularization strength)?  Were ablation studies conducted to determine the impact of different hyperparameters on performance?
4. **Error Analysis Categorization:** Could you provide a more systematic categorization of the errors made by the model? What types of causal relations or lexical markers does the model consistently struggle with?

**Additional Comments:**

* The notation in Equation 2 could be clarified.  What exactly are W and b representing?
*  The reference to "rd International Conference for Learning Representations" in the Kruengkrai et al. (2017) citation seems incomplete.



Overall, this is a solid paper with a clear contribution to the field of causal relation understanding. While the architectural exploration could be expanded and the limitations of the corpus more actively addressed, the empirical results and insightful analysis make this a valuable contribution to the ACL community. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions addressing the points raised in the weaknesses and questions.
