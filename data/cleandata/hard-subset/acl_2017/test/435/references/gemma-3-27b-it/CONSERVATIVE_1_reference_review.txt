This paper investigates neural disambiguation of causal lexical markers. The authors identify a gap in existing approaches to causality detection – a reliance on fixed lexical constructions or limited syntactic patterns – and propose a neural network approach to encode contextual information for improved disambiguation. The primary contribution is an empirically validated neural network architecture (specifically, a Pair LSTM with Adam optimization) for classifying causal relations, achieving state-of-the-art results on the AltLex corpus. A secondary contribution is a clear articulation of the challenges in causality disambiguation, particularly the ambiguity of certain lexical markers and the limitations of existing datasets.

**Strengths:**

1. **Empirical Results:** The paper demonstrates a clear improvement over the existing baseline (Hidey & McKeown, 2016) on the AltLex corpus, a valuable resource for this task. The reported gains in F1-score and accuracy suggest that the proposed neural architecture effectively captures contextual cues for causality detection.
2. **Sound Motivation:** The authors provide a reasonable critique of existing approaches to causality, correctly pointing out their limitations in handling the nuances of natural language. The argument that causality isn’t strictly defined by syntactic patterns or specific lexical items is well-supported by references to Neeleman & van de Koot (2012) and other relevant work.
3. **Clear Task Definition:** The authors clearly define the task of causality *classification* (rather than full relation extraction) and justify this simplification, focusing on the core problem of identifying whether a causal relationship exists between two events. This focused approach allows for a more targeted evaluation.
4. **Thorough Experimentation:** The paper explores multiple configurations (LSTM architectures, optimizers, padding strategies) demonstrating a considered approach to model development. The analysis of results, including discussion of performance on ambiguous connectives, adds to the credibility of the findings.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Generalizability:** The evaluation is entirely confined to the AltLex corpus. While achieving SOTA on this dataset is positive, it provides limited evidence of the method's generalizability to other datasets or real-world applications. The corpus itself, while valuable, is relatively small and potentially biased.
2. **Lack of Novelty in Architecture:** The use of LSTMs, while effective, is not particularly novel. The core innovation lies in applying this architecture to the specific task of causal disambiguation *with the focus on contextual encoding*. The architectural choices themselves do not represent a significant advance in neural network design.
3. **Insufficient Comparison to Stronger Baselines:** While the comparison to Hidey & McKeown (2016) is fair, that work is already several years old. More recent, and potentially stronger, baselines utilizing pre-trained language models (e.g., BERT, RoBERTa) should have been considered to provide a more robust benchmark. A simple fine-tuned BERT model may achieve comparable or better performance with less architectural complexity.
4. **Limited Error Analysis:** The paper briefly touches upon the challenges posed by ambiguous connectives, but a more in-depth error analysis would be beneficial. Identifying common error cases and the reasons behind them could provide valuable insights for future improvements. Specifically, analyzing *why* the system fails on certain examples would be helpful.

**Questions to Authors:**

1. Have you experimented with pre-trained language models (e.g., BERT, RoBERTa) as a baseline or as part of your architecture? If not, why?
2. Can you provide a more detailed error analysis, categorizing the types of errors your system makes and the reasons for those errors?
3. What is the computational cost of your proposed system compared to the baseline method?
4. How sensitive is your system to the size and quality of the training data? Would performance significantly degrade with a smaller or noisier dataset?



**Additional Comments:**

*   The discussion of the counterfactual constraint is appropriate and well-reasoned.
*   The explanation of the AltLex corpus and its creation is clear and helpful.
*   The paper is generally well-written and organized.
