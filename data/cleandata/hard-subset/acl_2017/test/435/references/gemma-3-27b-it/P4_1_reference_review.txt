This paper investigates neural disambiguation of causal lexical markers, a challenging problem in natural language understanding. The authors aim to classify whether a relationship between two events is causal, leveraging a neural network architecture and a corpus of causal expressions (Hidey & McKeown, 2016). The primary contribution is the application of LSTMs to this task, arguing that they can effectively encode context for improved causal reasoning compared to prior approaches relying on linguistic rules or lexical resources. A secondary contribution lies in the empirical evaluation on the AltLex corpus, providing a benchmark for future work. A third, less prominent contribution, is the exploration of different padding strategies and optimizers within the LSTM framework.

**Strengths:**

1. **Relevant and Important Problem:** Causal reasoning is fundamental to human cognition and crucial for many NLP applications (e.g., question answering, text summarization). Addressing the ambiguity of causal markers is therefore a valuable undertaking.
2. **Sound Methodology:** The use of LSTMs is a reasonable approach given the sequential nature of language and the need to capture contextual information. The exploration of different LSTM architectures (Stated Pair vs. Pair) and optimization algorithms (Adadelta, Adam) demonstrates a thoughtful experimental design.
3. **Empirical Evaluation:**  The authors evaluate their approach on the AltLex corpus, a valuable resource for studying nuanced causal expressions. The comparison against a baseline (Hidey & McKeown, 2016) provides a clear indication of the proposed method’s performance.  The results show improvements over the baseline, demonstrating the efficacy of the LSTM-based approach.
4. **Detailed Analysis and Discussion:** The paper provides a detailed discussion of the experimental results, including an analysis of the impact of different hyperparameters and padding strategies.  The authors also acknowledge the limitations of the corpus and discuss potential avenues for future work. The analysis of misclassified examples in Table 8 offers insightful discussion of the challenges.
5. **Clear Presentation:** The paper is generally well-written and easy to follow. The related work section provides a good overview of the existing literature on causal reasoning in NLP.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Novelty:** While the application of LSTMs to this specific task is reasonable, the core idea of using neural networks for text classification isn't particularly novel. The contribution lies more in the specific application and empirical validation than a fundamentally new algorithmic approach.
2. **Corpus Dependence:** The performance is heavily reliant on the AltLex corpus. While it's a valuable resource, its limited size and potential biases (discussed in the paper) raise concerns about the generalizability of the results.  The ablation study with a reduced corpus (Table 7) partially addresses this but doesn’t fully resolve it.
3. **Lack of Ablation on Embedding Choices:** The paper uses pre-trained GloVe embeddings.  An ablation study exploring the impact of different embedding types (e.g., Word2Vec, fastText, BERT) would strengthen the results and provide insights into the robustness of the proposed method.
4. **Limited Error Analysis:** While the authors present some examples of misclassified sentences, a more systematic error analysis would be beneficial. Identifying common patterns in the errors could reveal weaknesses in the model and suggest directions for improvement.
5. **Missing Statistical Significance Testing**: While the authors mention a McNemar’s test, the results of the test are not presented with confidence intervals or p-values, making it difficult to assess the statistical significance of the improvements.

**Questions to Authors:**

1.  You mention the limited size of the AltLex corpus. Have you considered data augmentation techniques (e.g., back-translation, synonym replacement) to increase the size of the training set?
2.  The paper focuses on binary classification (Causal/Non-Causal). Could the model be extended to identify the *type* of causal relation (e.g., cause-effect, reason-result)?
3.  Could you elaborate on the choice of LSTM architecture? Why was a bi-directional LSTM not explored?
4.  In Table 8, you show examples where your method outperforms Hidey and McKeown (2016). Can you quantify how often this occurs across the entire test set?

**Additional Comments:**

*   The formatting is generally good, but the tables could be improved for readability (e.g., using bold font for significant results).
*   The references are comprehensive and well-formatted.
*   The motivation for not using the counterfactual constraint in the definition of causality is well-explained.
