This paper investigates neural disambiguation of causal lexical markers. The core contribution is a neural network architecture (specifically, LSTMs) applied to the task of identifying causal relationships between events, evaluated on the AltLex corpus. A secondary contribution is a reaffirmation of the limitations of purely syntax- or lexicon-based approaches to causality, advocating for contextualized understanding through neural networks. The authors also provide a thorough review of prior work in causal relation classification.

**Strengths:**

1. **Relevant Task:** Identifying causal relationships is a crucial task in natural language understanding, with applications in information extraction, question answering, and summarization. The focus on *disambiguation* – differentiating causal from non-causal uses of seemingly ambiguous markers – is a particularly valuable contribution.
2. **Comprehensive Related Work:** The paper provides a very thorough overview of previous approaches to causality detection, spanning rule-based systems, machine learning with hand-engineered features, and earlier attempts at leveraging distributional semantics. This demonstrates a strong understanding of the existing landscape.
3. **Empirical Evaluation:** The authors evaluate their system on the AltLex corpus, a valuable resource for this task, and compare against a reasonable baseline (Hidey & McKeown, 2016). The reported improvements in F1-score are encouraging, and the ablation studies with different LSTM configurations and optimizers are helpful.
4. **Sound Motivation:** The authors present a clear rationale for their approach, grounded in linguistic theory (Neeleman & van de Koot, 2012) and the observed limitations of previous methods. The argument that causality isn't solely determined by syntactic patterns or pre-defined lexical resources is well-articulated.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Novelty of Method:** While the application of LSTMs to this specific task is reasonable, the proposed architecture is quite standard. There’s little discussion of *why* LSTMs are particularly well-suited to this task beyond their ability to encode sequential information. The connection between LSTM architecture and the theoretical motivations (limitations of syntax-based approaches) is weak; it feels like a straightforward application of a powerful tool, rather than a theoretically driven innovation.
2. **Over-reliance on AltLex Corpus:** The entire evaluation is based on a single corpus. While AltLex is a valuable resource, it’s crucial to demonstrate generalization to other datasets or, at the very least, a more rigorous analysis of the corpus’s characteristics and potential biases. The experiments with the reduced dataset are a step in the right direction, but more extensive validation is needed.
3. **Insufficient Error Analysis:** The paper lacks a detailed error analysis. While some illustrative examples are provided, a systematic examination of the types of errors the system makes – and why – would be invaluable. Understanding *where* the system fails will provide insights into its limitations and guide future research. The discussion of the ambiguous connective "which then" is a start, but more is needed.
4. **Lack of Comparison to Stronger Baselines:** While the comparison to Hidey & McKeown (2016) is appropriate, more recent and sophisticated baselines leveraging pre-trained language models (e.g., BERT, RoBERTa) should be considered. These models have demonstrated state-of-the-art performance on a wide range of NLP tasks and would provide a more challenging benchmark.

**Questions to Authors:**

1. Could you provide a more detailed error analysis, categorizing the types of errors the system makes (e.g., misclassifying temporal relations as causal, failing to identify subtle causal cues)?
2. Have you experimented with pre-trained language models (e.g., BERT, RoBERTa) as a baseline or as part of your architecture? If not, can you discuss the potential benefits and challenges of incorporating such models?
3. The paper emphasizes the limitations of syntax-based approaches. However, could syntactic information (e.g., dependency parsing) be integrated into your neural network architecture to potentially improve performance?
4. The choice of LSTM seems somewhat arbitrary. Have you explored other recurrent neural network architectures (e.g., GRUs) or attention mechanisms?
