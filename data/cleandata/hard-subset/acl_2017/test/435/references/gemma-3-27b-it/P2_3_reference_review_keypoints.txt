- Addresses causal relation classification by disambiguating ambiguous lexical markers using LSTM-based contextual encoding.
- Empirically validates that contextual neural models can disambiguate causal relations on the AltLex corpus, outperforming a lexical-resource baseline.
- Applies neural networks specifically to non-explicit causal discourse markers, filling a niche in discourse relation work.
- Provides a thorough analysis and discussion of limitations, including counterfactual and temporal issues.
- Strong experimental results: LSTM model outperforms Hidey & McKeown (2016) baseline on full and reduced AltLex datasets.
- Ablation studies (Stated Pair LSTM vs. Pair LSTM) offer useful insight into model components.
- Clear motivation and problem framing, arguing against reliance on predefined syntax or lexical resources.
- Detailed error analysis with example cases showing where the model succeeds over the baseline.
- Comprehensive related work situating the contribution among rule-based and ML approaches.
- Rigorous experimentation exploring optimizers, padding strategies, and comparing to a strong non-neural baseline.
- Limited dataset size (AltLex) restricts generalizability and raises overfitting concerns.
- Lack of comparison to stronger neural baselines (e.g., BERT/RoBERTa) weakens claims about model superiority.
- Architectural simplicity: reliance on LSTMs without exploring attention or pretrained language models may miss gains.
- Limited discussion of failure cases; more analysis needed on specific linguistic phenomena causing errors.
- Questions: did the authors try pretrained LMs, examples where counterfactual constraint fails, detailed error-type breakdowns, and sensitivity to embedding choice?
- Additional notes: paper is well-written, references are comprehensive, and dataset details are helpful.
- Overall: solid contribution recommending acceptance with minor revisions to address dataset, stronger baselines, and error analysis.
