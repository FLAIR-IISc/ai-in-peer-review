

# Review of "Neural Disambiguation of Causal Lexical Markers based on Context"

## Summary of the Paper and Main Contributions

This paper addresses the task of causal meaning disambiguation, which involves determining whether a lexical marker between two events indicates a causal relationship. The authors argue that causality is not limited to specific syntactic constructions or lexical items but can be expressed through various "Alternative Lexicalizations" (AltLex). They propose a neural network architecture using two LSTMs to encode the context around the causal relation (the first event and the lexical marker + second event) and evaluate it on the AltLex corpus (Hidey & McKeown, 2016).

Main contributions (ordered by strength):
1. **Neural Architecture for Causal Meaning Disambiguation**: The authors propose a neural network architecture with two LSTMs to encode the context of causal relations, demonstrating that contextual encoding is necessary for disambiguation.
2. **Thorough Evaluation on AltLex Corpus**: The paper provides a comprehensive evaluation on the AltLex corpus, showing their approach outperforms previous baselines, particularly in handling ambiguous causal connectives.
3. **Insightful Analysis of Ambiguous Connectives**: The authors demonstrate through concrete examples that their model can correctly disambiguate causal meaning for expressions that are challenging for previous approaches.

## Strengths

1. **Clear Conceptual Contribution**: The paper provides a valuable conceptual distinction between causal meaning disambiguation and causal argument identification, which is a significant contribution to the field. The authors correctly argue that causality cannot be restricted to specific syntactic constructions or lexical items, supported by their analysis of the AltLex corpus. This conceptual clarity is essential for advancing the field.

2. **Strong Empirical Evaluation**: The evaluation on the AltLex corpus is thorough, with careful analysis of ambiguous connectives and comparison against two strong baselines (B1 and B2). The paper demonstrates that the neural approach outperforms the lexical-resource-based baseline (B2) in terms of precision, which is a significant finding. The authors provide concrete examples (Table 8) showing how their model correctly disambiguates cases where the baseline fails.

3. **Demonstrated Necessity of Contextual Encoding**: The paper convincingly shows that contextual encoding is necessary for causal disambiguation. By demonstrating that their neural approach outperforms B2 (which relies on lexical resources), the authors provide strong evidence that contextual information is crucial for this task, which aligns with their theoretical claims.

4. **Insightful Analysis of Ambiguous Expressions**: The analysis of ambiguous connectives (e.g., "which then") is particularly strong. The authors correctly identify that word embeddings can help handle ambiguous expressions that don't appear in the training data, as shown in their examples. This analysis provides valuable insights into how neural approaches can handle linguistic ambiguity.

## Weaknesses

1. **Lack of Novelty in Neural Architecture**: The proposed neural architecture (two LSTMs) is not novel. The paper doesn't explain why this specific architecture was chosen over more recent approaches (e.g., transformers), and it doesn't compare against more recent neural baselines. Given the rapid advances in neural architectures since 2016, this is a significant limitation that weakens the contribution.

2. **Limited Scope of Evaluation**: The paper only evaluates on the AltLex corpus (8k-9k instances), which is relatively small for neural methods. The authors don't address whether their approach generalizes to other corpora or domains. This limitation makes it difficult to assess the broader applicability of their work. The paper would be significantly strengthened by a comparison with other causal relation datasets or a cross-domain evaluation.

3. **Insufficient Ablation Study**: While the paper evaluates different zero-padding strategies and optimizers, it lacks a thorough ablation study of the key components of the architecture. For example, the authors don't explore the impact of using different embedding strategies (e.g., pre-trained word embeddings vs. contextual embeddings like BERT) or the necessity of the two separate LSTMs.

4. **Outdated Baseline Comparison**: The state-of-the-art baseline (B2) is from 2016, and the paper doesn't compare against more recent neural approaches (e.g., using BERT or other pre-trained language models). This makes it difficult to assess how much progress the work represents compared to current capabilities.

5. **Unclear Practical Significance**: While the paper shows a modest improvement over the baseline (e.g., 2.13% F1 increase), it doesn't clearly articulate the practical significance of this improvement. How does this translate to real-world applications? How does it compare to human performance on this task? These questions are important for assessing the paper's impact.

## Questions to Authors

1. Why was LSTM chosen over more recent architectures like transformers? Would a transformer-based approach potentially yield better results, and if so, why wasn't it explored?

2. The AltLex corpus is relatively small (8k-9k instances). How does the performance of the proposed model scale with more training data? Have the authors considered using data augmentation techniques to address this limitation?

3. The paper doesn't discuss computational complexity or inference time. How does the model's speed compare to the baseline systems, especially for large-scale applications?

4. The state-of-the-art baseline (B2) is from 2016. Have the authors compared their approach against more recent neural baselines (e.g., using BERT or other pre-trained language models), and if not, why?

## Overall Recommendation

While the paper makes a valid conceptual contribution to the field of causal relation extraction by distinguishing between causal meaning disambiguation and causal argument identification, and provides a thorough evaluation on the AltLex corpus, the lack of novelty in the neural architecture and the limited scope of evaluation prevent it from making a significant contribution to the state of the art. The paper would be substantially strengthened by comparing against more recent neural approaches, expanding the evaluation to other corpora, and conducting a more thorough ablation study.

I recommend **rejection** due to the limited novelty of the approach and the insufficient evaluation scope. However, with additional experiments comparing against more recent neural baselines and a broader evaluation, this work could be significantly improved and would be a strong candidate for acceptance at ACL.

## Additional Comments

- The paper would benefit from a clearer discussion of how the proposed approach differs from existing neural methods for causal relation extraction.
- The authors should consider exploring the use of contextual embeddings (e.g., BERT) to potentially improve performance further.
- The paper could provide more insight into the limitations of the current approach and how it might be extended to handle more complex causal structures.
