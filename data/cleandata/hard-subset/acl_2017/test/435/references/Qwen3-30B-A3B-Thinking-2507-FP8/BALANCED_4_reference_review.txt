

# Review: Neural Disambiguation of Causal Lexical Markers based on Context

## Summary of the Paper and Main Contributions

The paper addresses the task of causality disambiguation, which involves determining whether a causal relation exists between two events (cause and effect) in text. The authors argue that causality is not encoded in specific syntactic constructions or lexical markers as previously thought, but rather depends on contextual information. They propose a neural network architecture based on LSTMs to disambiguate causal meaning from context, rather than relying on predefined linguistic features. The authors evaluate their approach on the AltLex corpus, which contains both explicit and implicit causal relations, and demonstrate that their method outperforms previous baselines.

**Main Contributions (ordered by strength):**
1. The paper provides a clear and well-motivated argument for why causality disambiguation should be based on contextual information rather than predefined syntactic or lexical features.
2. The authors demonstrate that their neural approach achieves better balance between precision and recall than previous baselines on the AltLex corpus, particularly for ambiguous causal markers.
3. The paper offers a thorough analysis of the limitations of previous approaches that relied on lexical resources (WordNet, VerbNet, PropBank) and shows how their neural approach overcomes these limitations.

## Strengths

1. **Strong motivation and clear problem definition:** The paper provides an excellent literature review that effectively establishes why current approaches to causality disambiguation are limited. The authors convincingly argue that causality is not tied to specific syntactic constructions (as shown by Neeleman and van de Koot, 2012) but depends on context. The distinction between explicit and implicit causal relations, and the problem of alternative lexicalizations (AltLex), is well-explained and clearly motivated.

2. **Appropriate evaluation on a relevant dataset:** The authors evaluate their approach on the AltLex corpus, which is specifically designed to handle the ambiguity of causal markers. Their evaluation is comprehensive, including both "non bootstrapping" and "bootstrapping" versions of the corpus, as well as a balanced version to address class imbalance. The results clearly demonstrate that their neural approach outperforms the baseline methods, particularly in terms of precision-recall balance.

3. **Detailed analysis of ambiguous cases:** The paper provides valuable examples showing how their model correctly handles ambiguous cases (like "which then") that previous approaches misclassify. This analysis strongly supports the authors' claim that contextual encoding is necessary for proper causal disambiguation. Table 8 specifically demonstrates how their model outperforms the previous state-of-the-art in challenging cases.

4. **Clear comparison with relevant baselines:** The paper properly compares against both a simple heuristic baseline (B1) and the previous state-of-the-art method (B2) from Hidey and McKeown (2016). The comparison is fair and well-justified, with the authors demonstrating that their approach significantly outperforms B1 and achieves better precision-recall balance than B2.

## Weaknesses

1. **Limited novelty in the neural architecture:** The proposed LSTM-based architecture is not particularly novel. The paper doesn't clearly articulate how their specific architecture differs from standard approaches used in similar NLP tasks. While the authors justify using LSTM over CNN, this is a relatively minor contribution given that LSTMs are commonly used for sequence tasks. The main contribution appears to be applying an existing neural approach to a new problem rather than developing a novel neural method.

2. **Insufficient comparison with recent neural approaches:** The paper only compares with the Hidey and McKeown (2016) baseline, which is a traditional SVM approach. It fails to compare with more recent neural approaches that might have been developed for causality classification since that work. Without such comparisons, it's difficult to assess whether the authors' contribution represents a meaningful advance over current neural methods.

3. **Lack of thorough ablation studies:** While the paper evaluates different zero-padding strategies and optimizers, it doesn't provide a comprehensive ablation study of the key components of the architecture. For example, the authors don't clearly demonstrate the impact of using two separate LSTMs versus a single LSTM, or the effect of different embedding sizes. This makes it difficult to understand which aspects of the architecture are most important for performance.

4. **Unclear contribution to the field:** The paper doesn't sufficiently articulate how this work advances the field beyond what was already demonstrated in Hidey and McKeown (2016). The title claims "neural disambiguation," but the main contribution seems to be applying a neural approach to a problem that was previously addressed with traditional methods, rather than developing a novel neural approach specifically for this task.

5. **Insufficient discussion of limitations:** The paper doesn't adequately discuss the limitations of their approach. For instance, how would the model perform on causal relations that span multiple sentences? How does it handle cases with complex syntactic structures? These are important considerations for a task that deals with discourse relations.

## Questions to Authors

1. The paper claims that their approach outperforms previous baselines, but doesn't compare with more recent neural approaches. Can the authors provide a comparison with state-of-the-art neural models for causal relation classification (e.g., transformer-based models) that have been developed since Hidey and McKeown (2016)?

2. The paper uses GloVe embeddings but doesn't specify whether they were fine-tuned during training. How did this choice impact the results, and would using contextualized embeddings (like BERT) improve performance?

3. The paper states that their model handles ambiguous cases like "which then" well, but doesn't provide a detailed analysis of why their model works better than previous approaches for these cases. Can the authors provide a more detailed explanation of the model's behavior with such ambiguous cases?

4. The paper doesn't discuss how the model would perform on causal relations that span multiple sentences, which is a common scenario in natural language. How would the authors extend their approach to handle such cases?

5. The authors claim their approach doesn't rely on lexical resources, but they use pre-trained GloVe embeddings, which are derived from a large corpus. How does this differ from relying on lexical resources like WordNet in terms of the model's ability to generalize to new causal expressions?

## Overall Recommendation

While the paper presents a clear problem statement, provides a reasonable evaluation on a relevant dataset, and demonstrates that a neural approach can outperform previous baselines, the contribution is limited by the lack of novelty in the neural architecture and insufficient comparison with more recent approaches. The paper would be significantly strengthened by a more thorough comparison with state-of-the-art neural methods, a more detailed ablation study, and a clearer articulation of how this work advances the field beyond previous approaches.

**Decision: Reject** (with a strong recommendation for resubmission after addressing the weaknesses identified above)

The paper's contribution is incremental rather than transformative. While the results are promising and the evaluation is generally sound, the lack of novelty in the neural architecture and the insufficient comparison with current state-of-the-art neural approaches make it difficult to justify the contribution at the level expected for ACL. With the suggested improvements, particularly a more comprehensive comparison with recent neural approaches and a clearer demonstration of the novelty of their method, this work could be significantly strengthened.
