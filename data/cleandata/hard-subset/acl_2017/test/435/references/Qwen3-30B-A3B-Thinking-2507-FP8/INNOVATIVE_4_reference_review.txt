

# Review of "Neural Disambiguation of Causal Lexical Markers based on Context"

## Summary
This paper tackles causal meaning disambiguation - determining whether a lexical marker (e.g., "because," "so") is being used causally in a sentence. The authors argue that previous work has been limited by syntactic constructions or reliance on lexical resources (like WordNet), and propose a neural network approach that encodes context without such restrictions. They evaluate on the AltLex corpus (Hidey & McKeown, 2016), which contains both explicit and implicit causal relations, and claim that context encoding is necessary for proper causal disambiguation.

## Main Contributions (as I see them)
1. **Theoretical insight**: The claim that causality is not limited to specific syntactic constructions or lexical resources, but requires contextual understanding (a bold conceptual shift challenging previous work).
2. **Novel application**: The first neural approach for causal meaning disambiguation that doesn't rely on lexical resources, directly addressing the AltLex problem.
3. **Empirical validation**: Demonstrating that context encoding outperforms previous feature-based approaches on the AltLex corpus.

## Strengths

1. **Bold theoretical contribution**: The paper makes a significant conceptual contribution by challenging the prevailing view that causality is encoded in specific syntactic constructions or lexical resources. The authors correctly point out that previous work (e.g., Bethard & Martin, 2008; Mirza & Tonelli, 2016) has been restricted to specific syntactic constructions that don't align with human understanding of causality. This theoretical insight that "causality does not have a fixed list of lexical or syntactic constructions" is a major contribution to the field and aligns with the human mental model of causation as noted by Neeleman and van de Koot (2012).

2. **Strong empirical validation**: The paper provides clear evidence that their neural approach outperforms the state-of-the-art (by 2.13% F1 in the Causal class) on the AltLex corpus. Crucially, the paper shows that their approach successfully handles ambiguous lexical markers (e.g., "which then" with one Causal instance and one Non-Causal instance in training) where previous methods fail, as demonstrated in Table 8. This directly validates their core claim that context encoding is necessary for causal disambiguation.

3. **Addresses a real problem in the field**: The paper correctly identifies the AltLex problem (the limited coverage of causal expressions in existing corpora) as a significant limitation in previous work. Hidey and McKeown (2016) already noted this issue, and the authors' solution directly addresses it, making this a meaningful contribution to the field rather than just incremental improvement.

## Weaknesses

1. **Limited novelty in neural architecture**: While the application to causal disambiguation is novel, the neural architecture itself (two LSTMs with dense layers) is quite standard and doesn't introduce any novel neural components. The paper would be stronger if they had developed a more specialized architecture for causal reasoning.

2. **Evaluation limited to one corpus**: The paper only evaluates on the AltLex corpus, which is relatively small (8,214 instances for the "non bootstrapping" version). The paper doesn't discuss how their approach would scale to larger corpora or perform on other causal datasets. While the AltLex corpus is the most suitable for this task, a more comprehensive evaluation would strengthen the claims.

3. **Lack of deeper analysis**: The paper shows that their model outperforms baselines but doesn't provide a deep analysis of *why* it works better. For example, they don't explore how different parts of the context contribute to the disambiguation decision. A more detailed analysis would strengthen the theoretical contribution.

## Questions to Authors

1. Have you considered using pre-trained language models (like BERT) instead of GloVe embeddings? Would this improve performance, and if so, why?

2. Could you provide more detailed analysis of the cases where your model outperforms the baseline, beyond the examples in Table 8? For instance, what specific linguistic phenomena does your model handle better?

3. How would your approach scale to larger datasets, and what would be the computational requirements for training on significantly more data?

## Overall Recommendation

I recommend acceptance. This paper makes a significant conceptual contribution by challenging the prevailing view of causality in computational linguistics and demonstrating that context encoding is necessary for causal disambiguation. The authors successfully validate their claim through empirical results that clearly outperform previous work. While the neural architecture itself is standard, the *application* to causal disambiguation represents a novel and important direction for the field. As an ACL reviewer who prioritizes novel ideas over methodological perfection, I believe the theoretical contribution of this paper outweighs the minor weaknesses in the neural architecture and evaluation scope. The paper makes a strong case for a new direction in causal reasoning research that is grounded in human understanding rather than syntactic constructions.
