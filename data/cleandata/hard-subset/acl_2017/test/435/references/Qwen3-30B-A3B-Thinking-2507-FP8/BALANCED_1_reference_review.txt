

# Review for ACL Submission: "Neural Disambiguation of Causal Lexical Markers based on Context"

## Summary
The paper addresses the task of causal meaning disambiguation, which involves determining whether a relation between two events (cause and effect) is causal or non-causal. The authors argue that previous approaches to causality classification have been limited by their reliance on specific syntactic constructions or lexical resources, and propose a neural network architecture that encodes the context of the relation between events to disambiguate causal meaning. They evaluate their approach on the AltLex corpus (Hidey and McKeown, 2016), which contains a variety of causal expressions, and demonstrate that their method outperforms two baselines, including the state-of-the-art system from Hidey and McKeown (2016).

## Main Contributions (as I see them)
1. **A neural network approach for causal meaning disambiguation** that focuses on context encoding rather than relying on specific syntactic constructions or lexical resources.
2. **A thorough evaluation on the AltLex corpus** demonstrating that their approach outperforms previous methods, with their best model improving the state-of-the-art by 2.13% in F1 for the causal class.
3. **Analysis of ambiguous causal expressions** (e.g., "which then") showing that context encoding is crucial for accurate disambiguation.

## Strengths

1. **Clear identification of a key limitation in prior work**: The paper effectively identifies that previous approaches to causality classification have been constrained by their reliance on specific syntactic constructions or lexical resources (e.g., WordNet, VerbNet), which limits their coverage and generalization. This is a well-motivated problem that aligns with the growing trend in NLP toward context-based approaches.

2. **Strong empirical results with statistical significance**: The authors demonstrate that their neural approach outperforms both baselines on the AltLex corpus, with statistically significant improvements (as shown by McNemar's test). The best model ("Pair LSTM Mean Adam") achieves 75.40% F1 for the causal class on the non-bootstrapping version, improving the state-of-the-art by 2.13%. The results are well-presented with comprehensive metrics (Precision, Recall, F1, Accuracy).

3. **Insightful analysis of ambiguous cases**: The paper provides compelling examples (Table 8) where their model correctly disambiguates causal meaning where previous work failed, particularly for ambiguous expressions like "which then" that are context-dependent. This analysis strongly supports the paper's core claim that context encoding is essential for causal disambiguation.

4. **Clear task definition and distinction of subtasks**: The paper clearly defines causality classification as comprising two subtasks (causal meaning classification and causal argument identification), which is a valuable conceptual contribution. This distinction helps clarify the research landscape and sets the stage for future work.

5. **Appropriate use of the AltLex corpus**: The authors correctly identify the AltLex corpus as the most suitable resource for this task, given its focus on Alternative Lexicalizations (AltLex) and its larger coverage of causal expressions compared to other corpora. The paper provides a thorough analysis of the corpus's characteristics, including the class distribution of lexical markers.

## Weaknesses

1. **Lack of architectural novelty**: While the approach is practical, the proposed neural architecture (two LSTMs with dense layers) is relatively straightforward and doesn't introduce a fundamentally new neural architecture or theoretical insight. The paper doesn't clearly articulate how this specific architecture differs from or improves upon existing approaches in the literature, such as the Multi-column CNN used by Kruengkrai et al. (2017) for a similar task.

2. **Limited exploration of model variants and hyperparameters**: The paper evaluates only two main architectural variants (Stated Pair LSTM and Pair LSTM) and a few hyperparameter combinations. There's no thorough exploration of how different neural network components (e.g., attention mechanisms, different types of RNNs) might impact performance. The paper also doesn't compare against other neural architectures (e.g., transformers) that might be better suited for this task.

3. **Insufficient ablation studies**: The paper lacks ablation studies to isolate the contribution of specific components of the architecture (e.g., the effect of using two separate LSTMs vs. a single LSTM, the impact of different padding strategies). Without these, it's difficult to determine which aspects of their approach are most important for the performance gains.

4. **Potential data leakage concerns with bootstrapped corpus**: The paper notes that the "bootstrapping" version of the corpus has class distribution differences between training and test sets (Table 4). This could lead to potential data leakage or overestimation of performance. The authors don't adequately address how they handled this issue or whether their model's performance on this version is truly indicative of generalization.

5. **Lack of failure case analysis**: While the paper shows some correctly classified examples, it doesn't provide examples of cases where their model failed. Understanding failure modes is crucial for assessing the limitations of the approach and identifying directions for future work.

## Questions to Authors

1. The paper mentions that the "bootstrapping" version of the corpus has class distribution differences between training and test sets (Table 4). How did the authors ensure that their model wasn't simply memorizing these differences, and how does this affect the interpretation of the results on this version of the corpus?

2. The paper doesn't compare against other neural architectures (e.g., transformers). Why did the authors choose LSTMs over these more recent architectures, and how would the performance compare if they used, for example, a transformer-based model?

3. The paper doesn't provide ablation studies to isolate the contribution of specific components of the architecture. Could the authors provide these results to help clarify which aspects of their approach are most important (e.g., the effect of using two separate LSTMs vs. a single LSTM)?

4. The paper states that "Pair LSTM Mean Adam" reached the best balance between Precision and Recall. However, it doesn't explain why this specific strategy outperformed others. Could the authors provide more insight into this finding?

## Additional Comments

- The paper would benefit from a more detailed discussion of related work, particularly how their approach relates to recent neural approaches for semantic relation classification.
- The paper should clarify how the "non bootstrapping" and "bootstrapping" versions of the corpus were constructed and how they differ beyond the class distribution issue.
- The claim about the superiority of context encoding over syntactic constructions could be strengthened with more direct comparisons to previous work that used syntactic features.
- The paper mentions that "the system has a high Precision with lower Recall, indicating it mainly classifies correctly sentences with unambiguous connectives." This is a limitation that should be discussed more explicitly in the conclusion.

## Overall Recommendation

The paper addresses a meaningful problem in computational linguistics with a practical approach that demonstrates clear improvements over the state-of-the-art. However, the lack of architectural novelty, limited exploration of model variants, and insufficient ablation studies prevent this from being a groundbreaking contribution. The empirical results are strong, but the theoretical contribution is modest.

I recommend **weak acceptance** with the following conditions for revision:
1. Provide ablation studies to isolate the contribution of specific components of the architecture.
2. Address the potential data leakage concern with the bootstrapped corpus.
3. Add a discussion of failure cases to provide a more balanced assessment of the model's capabilities.
4. Include a comparison with at least one more recent neural architecture (e.g., transformer-based) to strengthen the methodological justification.

With these revisions, the paper would make a solid contribution to the field of computational linguistics and causality analysis.
