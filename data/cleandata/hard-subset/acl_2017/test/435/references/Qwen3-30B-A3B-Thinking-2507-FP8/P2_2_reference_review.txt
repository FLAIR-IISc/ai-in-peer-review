

# Review of "Neural Disambiguation of Causal Lexical Markers based on Context"

## Summary and Main Contributions
This paper addresses the task of causality disambiguation - determining whether a relation between two events is causal or not, particularly when the causal relation is not signaled by a clear lexical marker but by alternative lexicalizations (AltLex). The authors propose a neural network architecture based on LSTM to encode the context of the relation rather than relying on fixed syntactic constructions or lexical resources. Their main contributions are: (1) a neural approach to causality disambiguation that doesn't depend on predefined lexical resources; (2) empirical validation showing their model outperforms previous baselines on the AltLex corpus; and (3) analysis demonstrating that context is crucial for disambiguating ambiguous causal markers.

## Strengths

1. **Significant Gap Addressed**: The paper effectively identifies a critical gap in prior work on causality classification. Previous approaches (e.g., Bethard & Martin, 2008; Hidey & McKeown, 2016) relied on lexical resources or syntactic constructions that don't fully capture the contextual nature of causality. The authors rightly point out that causality isn't defined by fixed linguistic patterns (as shown in Neeleman & van de Koot, 2012), and their neural approach that encodes context rather than relying on predefined features is well-motivated.

2. **Well-Designed Evaluation**: The experimental setup is strong, using two versions of the AltLex corpus (non-bootstrapping and bootstrapping) to test model performance on both unambiguous and ambiguous cases. The comparison against two meaningful baselines (B1 based on majority class and B2 from Hidey & McKeown, 2016) provides clear evidence of their model's superiority. Their analysis of specific ambiguous cases (Table 8) effectively demonstrates the model's ability to handle challenging examples that previous systems miss.

3. **Sound Model Architecture**: The authors correctly argue that LSTM is more appropriate than CNN for this task, as causality doesn't have a fixed syntactic structure. Their "Pair LSTM" architecture with two inputs (first event and lexical marker + second event) makes intuitive sense for the task. Their thorough exploration of padding strategies and optimizers (Adadelta vs. Adam) adds significant value to the paper.

4. **Clear Demonstration of Contextual Importance**: The paper effectively demonstrates that context is crucial for causality disambiguation through their analysis of ambiguous markers like "since" and "after" (Table 2) and their examples of correctly handling ambiguous cases (Table 8). This is a valuable contribution to the field, as it moves beyond previous work that assumed causality is defined by fixed lexical or syntactic patterns.

## Weaknesses

1. **Limited Novelty Claim**: While the neural approach is sound, the paper overstates the novelty of their LSTM-based architecture. Previous work has used neural networks for semantic relation classification (e.g., Melamud et al., 2016), and the authors don't sufficiently distinguish their specific contribution from existing neural approaches to relation classification. The paper would have been stronger if they had explicitly compared with recent neural models for semantic relation classification rather than just the specific baselines they chose.

2. **Insufficient Comparison with State-of-the-Art**: The paper states "As far as we know, the AltLex corpus has been only used in the work in which it was presented," but this is a weak justification for not comparing with more recent neural approaches to semantic relation classification. The authors should have at least compared with contemporary neural models (e.g., using transformers) to better establish the novelty and significance of their contribution.

3. **Ambiguous Task Definition**: The paper's definition of "causality disambiguation" is somewhat unclear. Is the task simply to determine whether a causal relation exists between two events, or does it also include identifying which event is the cause and which is the effect? The paper seems to focus on the former, but this ambiguity affects the evaluation and interpretation of results. A clearer task definition would strengthen the paper's contribution.

4. **Limited Analysis of Model Failures**: The paper presents strong results but doesn't sufficiently analyze where their model fails. Understanding the limitations of their approach would be valuable for future work, especially given the complexity of causality in natural language. The authors should provide examples of cases where their model fails to better contextualize their results.

## Questions to Authors

1. How does your model compare to more recent neural approaches to semantic relation classification (e.g., transformer-based models) that have been developed since the Hidey & McKeown (2016) baseline?

2. The paper argues that causality doesn't have a fixed syntactic structure, but does your model implicitly learn to rely on syntactic patterns despite this claim? Could you provide examples of cases where your model correctly identifies causality despite syntactic differences?

3. How would your model handle cases where the causal relation spans more than the two events mentioned in your input format (e.g., when the cause is mentioned earlier in the paragraph)?

## Overall Assessment

This is a solid paper that makes a valuable contribution by demonstrating the importance of context for causality disambiguation and providing an effective neural approach to the task. While the novelty claim is somewhat overstated, the empirical results are convincing and the work addresses a meaningful gap in the literature. With some clarification of the task definition and more comprehensive comparison to contemporary neural approaches, this paper would be a strong candidate for ACL. The authors have made a clear case that context is essential for causality disambiguation, which is an important insight for the field. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions to address the weaknesses noted above.
