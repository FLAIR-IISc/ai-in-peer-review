

# Review of "Neural Disambiguation of Causal Lexical Markers based on Context"

## Summary
This paper addresses the task of causal meaning disambiguation, where the goal is to determine whether a lexical marker (e.g., "because," "so," "since") expresses a causal relationship between two events in context. The authors argue that previous approaches either rely on syntactic constructions or lexical resources (like WordNet), which limits coverage and fails to capture context-dependent causal meaning. They propose a neural network architecture using two LSTMs to encode the cause and effect events along with the lexical marker, and evaluate it on the AltLex corpus, which contains causal expressions with ambiguous markers.

## Main Contributions
1. **The AltLex corpus**: The paper effectively leverages the AltLex corpus (a relatively new resource for causal expressions with ambiguous markers), which is well-suited for the task of context-dependent causal disambiguation.
2. **Neural context encoding approach**: The authors demonstrate that neural networks can effectively encode contextual information for causal disambiguation without relying on predefined syntactic patterns or lexical resources.
3. **Empirical evaluation**: The paper provides thorough evaluation across multiple neural network configurations and compares against two strong baselines, showing clear performance gains.

## Strengths
1. **Meaningful problem statement**: The paper correctly identifies a significant challenge in computational linguistics - that causal markers like "since" or "after" can have ambiguous meanings depending on context (temporal vs. causal), and previous approaches fail to adequately handle this ambiguity. The authors provide strong theoretical grounding from linguistic literature to justify their approach.

2. **Appropriate corpus usage**: The AltLex corpus is well-suited for this task, as it was specifically designed to capture context-dependent causal expressions. The authors' thorough evaluation on both versions of the corpus (non-bootstrapping and bootstrapping) demonstrates a comprehensive understanding of the data and its challenges.

3. **Clear empirical results**: The paper provides detailed evaluation metrics (precision, recall, F1, accuracy) across multiple configurations. The results show consistent improvement over baselines, particularly in balancing precision and recall (a key metric for this task). The analysis of specific lexical markers like "break" and "which then" demonstrates the model's ability to handle ambiguous cases.

## Weaknesses
1. **Lack of architectural novelty**: The proposed neural architecture (two connected LSTMs followed by dense layers) is relatively standard and not particularly innovative. Many recent papers use similar approaches for relation classification tasks. The paper doesn't sufficiently justify why this specific architecture is better than simpler alternatives (e.g., a single LSTM on concatenated text), which would strengthen the contribution.

2. **Limited task scope**: The paper frames causality as a binary classification task (causal vs. non-causal), but the problem of causal relation identification is more complex. The authors mention "causal meaning classification" and "causal argument identification" as two subtasks, but they only address the first one. A more comprehensive approach would be to address both subtasks, which would significantly increase the paper's impact.

3. **Insufficient analysis of failure cases**: While the paper provides examples of correctly classified cases, it lacks a thorough analysis of where the model fails. Understanding failure modes would be valuable for future work and would strengthen the paper's contribution. The authors mention that "which then" is ambiguous, but don't analyze why the model sometimes fails to disambiguate it correctly.

## Questions to Authors
1. How does your proposed architecture compare to a simpler approach that concatenates the cause, lexical marker, and effect into a single input for a single LSTM? This would provide a clearer baseline for the architectural contribution.

2. The paper claims that causality "does not have a fixed list of lexical or syntactic constructions." However, the task definition assumes the presence of a lexical marker. How would your model handle cases where the causal relation is entirely implicit (no lexical marker), which appears to be a common scenario in natural language?

3. The paper states that "the use of pre-trained embeddings has a higher ability of generalization" compared to lexical resources. However, the paper doesn't explicitly quantify how much of the performance gain comes from the neural architecture versus the use of GloVe embeddings. Could you clarify this distinction?

## Overall Assessment
This paper makes a solid contribution to the field of computational linguistics by demonstrating that neural context encoding can effectively handle the ambiguity of causal lexical markers. The use of the AltLex corpus is well-justified, and the empirical results are convincing. However, the architectural novelty is limited, and the task scope is narrow. The paper would benefit from addressing the weaknesses noted above, particularly by expanding the task scope to include causal argument identification and providing a more thorough analysis of failure cases.

The paper is well-written, the claims are supported by evidence, and the evaluation is thorough. With minor revisions to address the weaknesses, this could be a strong contribution to ACL. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions.

**Recommendation**: Accept with minor revisions.
