

# Peer Review: "Neural Disambiguation of Causal Lexical Markers based on Context"

## Summary and Main Contributions

This paper addresses the task of causal meaning disambiguation - determining whether a lexical marker (e.g., "since," "after") signals a causal relation between two events in a sentence. The authors argue that causality is not fixed by syntax or lexical resources but depends on context, making it a disambiguation problem rather than a syntactic one. They propose a neural network architecture using LSTMs to encode the context of causal relations, avoiding reliance on lexical resources or syntactic features.

Main contributions (ordered by strength):
1. A compelling theoretical argument that causality is context-dependent rather than fixed by syntax or lexical resources, effectively challenging previous approaches in the field.
2. A neural network architecture for causal meaning disambiguation that demonstrates consistent improvement over state-of-the-art baselines, particularly in precision (reducing false positives).
3. A thorough evaluation on the AltLex corpus showing the effectiveness of context encoding for disambiguating causal meaning, with analysis of ambiguous lexical markers.

## Strengths

1. **Strong theoretical foundation**: The paper effectively challenges previous approaches that relied on syntax or lexical resources by citing Neeleman and van de Koot (2012), showing that causality isn't encoded in syntactic constituents. The argument that "causality does not have a fixed list of lexical or syntactic constructions" is well-supported and represents a significant contribution to the theoretical understanding of causality in language.

2. **Comprehensive evaluation**: The paper provides a thorough evaluation of multiple model configurations, including ablation studies comparing "Stated Pair LSTM" and "Pair LSTM" architectures. The results consistently show that neural approaches outperform baselines, with the best configuration ("Pair LSTM Mean Adam") improving precision by 3.89% and achieving a better balance between precision and recall compared to the state-of-the-art (B2).

3. **Practical demonstration of context importance**: The paper effectively demonstrates the necessity of context encoding through examples where the system correctly disambiguates ambiguous markers like "which then" that B2 fails on. The analysis in Section 4.3 provides concrete evidence that context encoding is essential for causal disambiguation, addressing a key limitation of previous work.

## Weaknesses

1. **Lack of discussion on limitations**: The paper fails to address critical limitations of the approach. It doesn't discuss how the model would handle cases where causal relations span multiple sentences or where the context is very long (beyond the LSTM's capacity). The paper also doesn't analyze whether the model's success is due to learning specific linguistic patterns or merely memorizing the training data. This omission significantly limits the paper's contribution.

2. **Corpus limitations and scalability concerns**: The AltLex corpus is relatively small (8k-9k examples), and the paper doesn't discuss how the model would scale to larger datasets or different domains. The "bootstrapping" version has instances that don't follow the training/test class distribution, but the paper doesn't address how this affects generalizability or whether the model would fail on unseen lexical markers. This is a significant limitation for a paper claiming to address the "limited coverage" problem.

3. **Insufficient analysis of model behavior**: While the paper shows improved performance, it lacks detailed analysis of *why* the model works better. For instance, it doesn't explain what specific contextual features the model is learning that allow it to disambiguate causal meaning. The paper mentions that "the system learns beyond the class distribution of the lexical markers in the training data" but provides no evidence or analysis to support this claim. This weakens the paper's contribution to the field.

## Questions to Authors

1. The paper shows that your model has higher precision but slightly lower recall than B2. How would you address the precision-recall trade-off in practical applications where recall might be more important than precision (e.g., information extraction systems)?

2. The paper demonstrates success on the AltLex corpus, but how would your model handle cases where the causal relation is not explicitly signaled by any lexical marker (e.g., "The leak caused injuries, resulting in 558,125 injuries")? Would you need to modify the architecture to handle such implicit causality?

3. In Table 8, you show examples where your model correctly classifies "break" as non-causal, while B2 classifies it as causal. Could you provide more examples where your model correctly handles lexical markers that are primarily causative in the training data but have non-causal uses in the test data?

## Overall Assessment

This paper makes a valuable contribution by demonstrating the importance of context encoding for causal meaning disambiguation. The theoretical argument is strong, the evaluation is thorough, and the results show consistent improvement over baselines. However, the lack of discussion about limitations and the insufficient analysis of *why* the model works better significantly reduce the paper's impact. With stronger analysis of model behavior and clearer discussion of limitations, this would be a strong contribution to the field. As it stands, the paper has merit but requires substantial revision to address the identified weaknesses. I recommend acceptance with major revision.
