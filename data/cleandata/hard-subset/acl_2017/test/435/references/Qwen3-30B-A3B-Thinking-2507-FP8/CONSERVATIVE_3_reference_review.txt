

# ACL Review

## Summary of the Paper

This paper proposes a neural network approach for disambiguating causal lexical markers in context. The authors argue that causality in natural language is not limited to specific syntactic constructions or lexical resources, and that neural networks can better capture the contextual meaning required for causal disambiguation. They introduce two LSTM-based architectures ("Stated Pair LSTM" and "Pair LSTM") for processing cause-effect event pairs with a lexical marker. The approach is evaluated on the AltLex corpus (Hidey and McKeown, 2016), comparing against two baselines: a simple heuristic (B1) that classifies based on the most common class for each lexical marker, and the state-of-the-art system from Hidey and McKeown (2016).

## Main Contributions (as I see them)

1. Application of standard LSTM networks to causal meaning disambiguation
2. Thorough empirical evaluation on the AltLex corpus
3. Comparison of different zero-padding strategies and optimizers for this task

## Strengths

1. **Clear problem definition and task distinction**: The paper clearly defines the task of causal meaning disambiguation as distinct from related tasks like causal argument identification. The distinction between explicit and implicit causal relations (Table 1) and the discussion of temporal vs. causal interpretations (Table 2) provide a solid foundation for the work.

2. **Thorough evaluation on a relevant corpus**: The evaluation on the AltLex corpus is comprehensive, with careful attention to the class distribution of lexical markers (Table 4). The paper provides detailed results across multiple configurations (Table 5), which is valuable for understanding the behavior of the proposed approach.

3. **Demonstrated improvement over baselines**: The proposed neural approach consistently outperforms the baselines, with the best configuration improving the state-of-the-art by 2.13% in F1 score on the "non bootstrapping" version of the corpus. This is a meaningful improvement that suggests the approach has merit.

## Weaknesses

1. **Lack of novelty in the core method**: The paper proposes an LSTM-based approach for causal disambiguation, but this is not a novel neural architecture. The core method is a standard application of established NLP techniques (LSTMs for text classification), and the paper doesn't provide sufficient justification for why LSTM is better than other neural approaches (like transformers or CNNs) for this specific task. The claim that "causation does not have a particular syntactic structure" is presented without empirical evidence.

2. **Inadequate comparison with contemporary neural baselines**: The paper only compares against the Hidey and McKeown (2016) system and a simple heuristic baseline. Given that transformer-based models (BERT, etc.) have become standard in NLP, the absence of comparison against these more recent approaches is a significant weakness. This is especially concerning as the authors claim their approach "outperforms the state of the art," but the state of the art has likely evolved since the Hidey and McKeown paper.

3. **Insufficient analysis of results**: While the paper provides quantitative results, it lacks a thorough qualitative analysis of *why* the proposed approach works better. The paper mentions that the system handles ambiguous lexical markers better (Table 8), but doesn't provide enough examples or error analysis to demonstrate the specific advantages of the neural approach over the baselines. The claim that "the encoding of the context is required" is made without sufficient evidence.

4. **Potential data leakage issue**: The paper notes that the "bootstrapping" version of the corpus has differences in class distribution between training and test sets (Table 4), which creates a risk of data leakage. The authors claim good performance on these challenging instances, but this could simply be due to the model learning the distributional differences rather than truly understanding causal meaning.

5. **Overstated claims about the limitations of prior work**: The paper claims that previous approaches were "limited by syntactic patterns or the coverage of semantic resources," but doesn't provide sufficient evidence to support this claim. The paper itself relies on pre-trained word embeddings (GloVe), which are a form of semantic resource, so the claim that they avoid "the coverage of semantic resources" is inconsistent.

## Questions to Authors

1. Why did you choose LSTM over other neural architectures (e.g., transformers) without providing empirical evidence that LSTM is superior for this specific task?

2. Why didn't you compare against more recent neural baselines (e.g., BERT-based models) that have become standard in NLP, given your claim that your approach "outperforms the state of the art"?

3. Can you provide a more detailed error analysis showing specific examples where your model succeeds where the Hidey and McKeown (2016) system fails, particularly for ambiguous lexical markers?

4. How do you address the potential data leakage issue in the "bootstrapping" version of the corpus, where the training and test sets have different class distributions?

5. Did you evaluate the impact of using pre-trained embeddings versus training embeddings from scratch, and how would this affect your results?

## Overall Recommendation

This paper presents a reasonable application of established neural methods to a well-defined NLP task. However, the lack of novelty in the core approach, the inadequate comparison with contemporary neural baselines, and the insufficient analysis of results are significant weaknesses that prevent it from making a strong contribution to the field. While the results are promising, the paper doesn't sufficiently demonstrate why the specific neural architecture proposed is better than other established approaches for this task.

I recommend rejection as submitted, but would consider a major revision addressing the weaknesses outlined above, particularly by comparing against more recent neural baselines and providing a more thorough analysis of the results. The paper has potential but needs significant strengthening to meet ACL's standards for novelty and rigor.
