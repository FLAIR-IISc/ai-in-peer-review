

# Peer Review: "Neural Disambiguation of Causal Lexical Markers based on Context"

## Summary
This paper addresses causal meaning disambiguation—the task of determining whether a lexical marker (e.g., "because," "so," "since") indicates a causal relationship between two events. The authors argue that existing approaches are limited by their reliance on syntactic constructions or lexical resources (e.g., WordNet), and propose a neural network architecture using LSTMs to encode contextual information for disambiguation. They evaluate their approach on the AltLex corpus, claiming superior performance over existing methods.

## Main Contributions (as I see them)
1. **Demonstration of context-based causal disambiguation** using neural networks
2. **Clear distinction between causal meaning disambiguation and causal argument identification**
3. **Practical evaluation on the AltLex corpus**, addressing a key limitation in prior work

## Strengths

1. **Strong theoretical motivation**: The paper effectively argues against syntactic approaches to causality (citing Neeleman and van de Koot, 2012) and demonstrates that causal meaning is highly context-dependent. The examples of ambiguous markers like "since" and "after" (Table 2) compellingly illustrate the need for context-based disambiguation.

2. **Appropriate methodology choice**: The authors correctly identify that LSTMs are better suited than CNNs for this task due to the lack of fixed syntactic patterns for causality. Their systematic evaluation of zero-padding strategies (max, mean, mode) and optimizers (Adam vs. Adadelta) is thorough and well-justified.

3. **Significant empirical results**: The proposed "Pair LSTM Mean Adam" model outperforms the state-of-the-art (B2) by 2.13% in F1 on the Causal class and 7.72% in Accuracy on the "non bootstrapping" version of the AltLex corpus. The paper also demonstrates the model's ability to correctly handle ambiguous markers like "which then" that B2 misclassifies (Table 8).

4. **Clear task definition**: The distinction between causal meaning disambiguation and causal argument identification is well-motivated and adds clarity to the field. The authors correctly note that causality should not be restricted to specific syntactic constructions.

## Weaknesses

1. **Overstated novelty**: While the paper presents a neural approach, it fails to sufficiently differentiate from existing work. Kruengkrai et al. (2017) already used a multi-column CNN for causal relation identification in Japanese, and Melamud et al. (2016) demonstrated LSTM's effectiveness for context encoding. The paper cites these works but doesn't properly compare or contrast their approaches, making the novelty claim questionable.

2. **Misrepresentation of previous work**: The authors criticize prior work for "relying on lexical resources" (e.g., WordNet), yet their own model relies on GloVe embeddings, which are derived from lexical resources. This undermines their central argument about moving beyond lexical resource limitations. The paper doesn't clarify how word embeddings differ from the lexical resources they criticize.

3. **Insufficient error analysis**: While the paper provides some correctly classified examples (Table 8), it lacks a systematic analysis of where the model fails. Without understanding the failure cases, it's difficult to assess the model's limitations or guide future improvements. For instance, how does the model handle sentences with multiple causal markers or complex discourse structures?

4. **Corpus limitations not fully addressed**: The "bootstrapping" version of the AltLex corpus has class distribution shifts between training and test sets (Table 4), which the authors acknowledge but don't adequately address in their evaluation. The paper claims good performance on these challenging instances, but doesn't provide enough detail to assess whether this is due to model robustness or dataset leakage.

## Questions to Authors

1. How does your LSTM approach specifically differ from Kruengkrai et al. (2017), who also used neural networks for causal relation identification? What specific improvements does your model offer over theirs?

2. Your paper claims that "causality should not be limited by... the coverage of semantic resources," yet your model relies on GloVe embeddings. How do you reconcile this with your own argument about moving beyond lexical resource limitations?

3. The AltLex corpus has significant class imbalance (e.g., 7,929 Non-Causal vs. 606 Causal in training). How did you address this imbalance, and what impact did it have on your results?

4. The paper states that causality isn't defined by temporal ordering, yet many ambiguous markers (like "after") have both temporal and causal uses. How does your model specifically distinguish between these two interpretations?

## Overall Assessment

This paper makes a solid contribution to the field of causal relation identification by demonstrating the effectiveness of context-aware neural models. The methodological approach is sound, the evaluation is thorough, and the results are convincing. However, the novelty claim is overstated, and the paper would benefit from a more nuanced discussion of how neural embeddings relate to the lexical resources they criticize.

The paper's greatest strength is its clear demonstration that context matters for causal disambiguation, and that neural approaches can effectively capture this context. With minor revisions addressing the novelty claims and corpus limitations, this would be a strong contribution to ACL.

**Recommendation: Weak Accept** (with the understanding that the authors need to address the novelty concerns in the revision)
