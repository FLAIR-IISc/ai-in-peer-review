

# Peer Review: "Neural Disambiguation of Causal Lexical Markers based on Context"

## Summary and Contributions

This paper addresses causality disambiguation - determining whether a causal relationship exists between two events, especially when signaled by ambiguous lexical markers (e.g., "since" which can be temporal or causal). The authors argue that previous approaches have been limited by reliance on syntactic constructions or lexical resources, and propose a neural network architecture using LSTMs to encode context for causal disambiguation. They evaluate their approach on the AltLex corpus, claiming it outperforms previous baselines.

The primary contributions are: 1) A neural architecture for causal meaning disambiguation that doesn't rely on syntactic features or lexical resources; 2) A thorough evaluation demonstrating superior performance over previous state-of-the-art; and 3) Demonstration of the model's ability to handle ambiguous lexical markers that previous approaches struggle with.

## Strengths

1. **Appropriate Dataset Selection and Justification**: The paper correctly identifies the AltLex corpus as the most suitable resource for this task, providing a strong rationale for why previous corpora (PDTB, Causal-TimeBank) are inadequate due to size and coverage limitations. The authors' analysis of the corpus's class distribution and ambiguous markers is thorough and well-justified.

2. **Strong Baseline Comparison**: The authors compare their approach to two well-chosen baselines: a simple majority-class baseline (B1) and the previous state-of-the-art system (B2) by Hidey and McKeown (2016). This provides a solid foundation for evaluating their approach. The results showing consistent improvements in Precision (with a 7.72% accuracy improvement over B2) are convincing.

3. **Insightful Analysis of Ambiguous Markers**: The paper provides valuable analysis of how the model handles ambiguous markers like "which then" and "break," demonstrating the model's ability to capture context-dependent meaning. The examples in Table 8 effectively show cases where the authors' model succeeds where B2 fails.

4. **Practical Impact**: The work has clear practical applications in natural language understanding systems that need to accurately identify causal relationships, which is important for applications like information extraction, question answering, and discourse analysis.

## Weaknesses

1. **Lack of Theoretical Contribution and Overstated Problem**: The paper claims causality "does not have a fixed list of lexical or syntactic constructions," which overstates the problem. While some markers are ambiguous, many causal markers (like "because") are largely unambiguous. The paper doesn't provide sufficient evidence to support this claim, and the motivation for their approach is weakened by this overstatement. The authors could have focused on the *ambiguity* problem rather than claiming causality has no fixed linguistic form.

2. **Insufficient Comparison to Modern Neural Methods**: The paper compares to Melamud et al. (2016) but doesn't compare to more recent neural approaches using contextual embeddings like BERT, which have become the standard for contextual semantic tasks. Given that the authors use pre-trained GloVe embeddings, it's surprising they don't compare to a BERT-based baseline, which would provide a much stronger evaluation of their approach.

3. **Limited Error Analysis**: While the paper shows some correctly classified examples, it lacks a thorough error analysis. It would be valuable to understand what types of causal relations the model struggles with (e.g., implicit vs. explicit, different syntactic constructions) and why. The authors should analyze the specific cases where their model fails compared to B2.

4. **Corpus Size and Generalizability Concerns**: The AltLex corpus, while larger than previous resources, is still relatively small (around 20k examples). The paper doesn't adequately address how this size might limit the model's generalizability or whether the model is overfitting to the specific patterns in this corpus. The authors should discuss how their model would perform on larger, more diverse corpora.

5. **Weak Justification for LSTM over Other Architectures**: The authors claim LSTMs are better than CNNs for this task because causality "does not have a particular syntactic structure." However, they don't provide evidence that LSTMs outperform CNNs on this specific task, nor do they acknowledge that more recent architectures like transformers might be better suited for this context.

## Questions to Authors

1. How does your model compare to a simple BERT-based approach for this task? Given that BERT has become the standard for contextualized embeddings, it would be valuable to see how your LSTM-based approach compares.

2. The paper claims causality "does not have a fixed list of lexical or syntactic constructions," but some causal markers like "because" are largely unambiguous. How does your model handle these unambiguous cases, and what is the performance difference between ambiguous and unambiguous markers?

3. The paper mentions "the lack of resources" as a limitation. Given this, why didn't you create a larger, more diverse corpus to train your model on, rather than relying on the existing AltLex corpus?

4. The paper states that "causation is concerned with the human understanding of the world and not with the world itself." How does this philosophical stance influence your approach to the task, and how do you ensure your model aligns with human causal understanding?

## Overall Assessment

This paper presents a solid empirical contribution to the task of causal disambiguation, with a well-justified dataset and strong results over previous baselines. However, the theoretical contribution is limited, and the paper would be significantly strengthened by comparing to more recent neural approaches, providing a thorough error analysis, and addressing corpus size limitations. The overstatement of the problem (claiming causality has no fixed linguistic construction) weakens the motivation for the work.

While the results are promising, the paper doesn't push the field forward in terms of methodology or theoretical understanding. With additional comparisons to modern neural approaches and a more nuanced discussion of the linguistic problem, this work could be significantly stronger. As it stands, it's a solid empirical contribution but lacks the theoretical or methodological innovation expected for ACL acceptance.
