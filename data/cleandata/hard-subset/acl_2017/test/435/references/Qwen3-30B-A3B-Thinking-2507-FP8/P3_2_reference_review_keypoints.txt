- Paper tackles causal disambiguation for ambiguous lexical markers using an LSTM-based neural architecture without relying on syntactic features or lexical resources.  
- Evaluated on the AltLex corpus and claims improved performance over prior baselines.

- Contribution: neural architecture for causal meaning disambiguation that avoids syntactic/lexical resources.  
- Contribution: thorough evaluation showing higher precision and ~7.72% accuracy improvement over previous SOTA (Hidey & McKeown 2016).  
- Contribution: analysis demonstrating handling of ambiguous markers difficult for prior methods.

- Strength: appropriate dataset choice with solid rationale and analysis of class distribution and ambiguous markers.  
- Strength: strong baseline comparisons (majority baseline and prior SOTA) and convincing precision improvements.  
- Strength: insightful analysis of ambiguous markers with illustrative examples where the model succeeds.  
- Strength: clear practical impact for NLU applications (information extraction, QA, discourse analysis).

- Weakness: overstates the linguistic problem by claiming causality lacks fixed lexical/syntactic forms; should focus on ambiguity instead.  
- Weakness: insufficient comparison to modern contextual embedding methods (e.g., BERT); uses GloVe instead.  
- Weakness: limited error analysis; lacks detailed examination of failure cases and relation types the model struggles with.  
- Weakness: corpus size and generalizability concerns — AltLex (~20k examples) may be too small and risk overfitting.  
- Weakness: weak justification for choosing LSTMs over CNNs or transformers, with no comparative evidence.

- Question: how does the model compare to a simple BERT-based approach?  
- Question: how does performance differ between ambiguous and unambiguous markers (e.g., “because”)?  
- Question: given resource limitations, why not create a larger, more diverse corpus instead of relying on AltLex?  
- Question: how does the stated philosophical view of causation influence model design and alignment with human causal understanding?

- Overall: solid empirical contribution with promising results but limited theoretical/methodological innovation; needs comparisons to modern neural methods, deeper error analysis, and discussion of dataset limitations to be stronger for ACL-level acceptance.
