

# ACL Review: "Neural Disambiguation of Causal Lexical Markers based on Context"

## Summary and Main Contributions

This paper proposes a neural network approach for disambiguating causal lexical markers in text, specifically addressing the challenge that some lexical expressions (like "since" or "after") can have both causal and temporal meanings depending on context. The main contributions are:

1. A neural architecture (using LSTM) for causal meaning disambiguation that does not rely on syntactic features or lexical resources, which the authors argue is more generalizable than previous approaches.
2. An evaluation of their approach on the AltLex corpus (Hidey and McKeown, 2016), showing clear improvements over previous baselines.
3. Analysis demonstrating that their model can correctly disambiguate causal meaning for ambiguous lexical markers (like "which then") where previous approaches failed.

## Strengths

1. **Clear problem definition and justification**: The paper provides a thorough linguistic background on causal relations, clearly explaining why current approaches based on syntactic features or lexical resources are insufficient. The authors properly frame the task as a binary classification problem between two events (cause and effect), which is well-motivated and clearly explained. They effectively address the limitations of previous work (e.g., "the syntax-grounded construction of causality defined by those theories is far away from the human mental model of causation").

2. **Strong empirical results**: The paper demonstrates clear improvements over previous baselines (B1 and B2), with statistically significant gains (p=0.005 and 0.05). For the "non bootstrapping" version of the corpus, the best model ("Pair LSTM Mean Adam") improves F1 by 2.13% and Accuracy by 7.72% over the state-of-the-art (B2). The results are presented clearly in Table 5 and supported by detailed analysis in Table 8 showing specific cases where the model outperforms previous work.

3. **Appropriate methodological choice**: The authors make a well-reasoned argument for using LSTM over CNN for this task, citing the lack of fixed syntactic structure for causality and the need for sequential context encoding. The ablation study comparing "Stated Pair LSTM" vs. "Pair LSTM" shows that encoding the two events independently leads to better performance, which is a useful finding. The paper also provides a clear description of the model architecture, including padding strategies and the use of pre-trained word embeddings.

## Weaknesses

1. **Limited novelty**: While the approach is well-executed, the core idea (using neural networks for causal relation disambiguation) isn't particularly novel. Previous work like Kruengkrai et al. (2017) also used neural networks for causal relation identification, though in a different context (Japanese). The paper doesn't clearly differentiate its approach from these prior works, and the novelty claim is overstated. The main contribution appears to be applying LSTM to this specific task rather than a fundamentally new method.

2. **Lack of comparison to more recent neural approaches**: The paper doesn't compare to more recent neural approaches (e.g., BERT-based models), which is a significant omission given the current state of the art in NLP. Without this comparison, it's unclear if the proposed LSTM-based approach is still competitive with the latest methods. This is especially problematic since the AltLex corpus is relatively small, where transformer-based approaches might have shown better performance.

3. **Insufficient analysis of failure cases**: The paper provides some examples of correctly classified cases but doesn't analyze cases where the model fails. Understanding the limitations of the model (e.g., when it misclassifies ambiguous expressions) would strengthen the paper and provide clearer directions for future work. The authors mention that some configurations have "a large number of false positives," but they don't explore why this happens or how to address it.

## Questions to Authors

1. How does your model compare to more recent neural approaches (e.g., BERT-based models) that have been applied to causal relation identification? Have you considered these approaches, and if so, why did you choose LSTM over them?

2. The paper states that "the task of causality classification lacks of representative or large enough corpus that covers a wide range of causal expressions." However, the AltLex corpus used in this paper is already relatively large (21,354 instances for the bootstrapping version). Why do you believe this corpus is still insufficient for training neural methods, and what specific limitations does it have that prevent it from being used as a standard benchmark?

3. The paper mentions that the authors reduced the number of instances of the Non Causal class by a factor of ten to create a more balanced dataset. How does this affect the generalizability of your results, and what would be the impact of using the original imbalanced dataset for training and evaluation?

## Additional Comments

The paper is well-written and clearly structured, with a strong empirical evaluation. However, the novelty claim is overstated, and the lack of comparison to more recent neural approaches is a significant weakness. The authors should address these issues in their revision to strengthen the contribution of their work.
