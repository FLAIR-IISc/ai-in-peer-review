

# Peer Review: Neural Disambiguation of Causal Lexical Markers based on Context

## Summary and Main Contributions

This paper addresses the problem of causal meaning disambiguation, specifically identifying whether a lexical marker (e.g., "because," "so," "since") signals a causal relation between two events. The authors argue that causality is not fixed to specific syntactic constructions but is context-dependent, and propose a neural network architecture that encodes the context of causal relations to disambiguate causal meaning. Their main contributions are: (1) a strong theoretical argument that causality is context-dependent rather than fixed to specific lexical or syntactic constructions; (2) a neural architecture for causal meaning disambiguation that outperforms previous methods; and (3) empirical validation of their approach on the AltLex corpus.

## Main Contributions (as I see them)

1. **Theoretical justification for context-dependence of causality**: The paper provides a compelling review of linguistic literature that demonstrates causality is not fixed to specific lexical or syntactic constructions, which is the strongest contribution.
2. **Empirical validation of neural context encoding**: The paper demonstrates that neural encoding of context (rather than relying on lexical resources) is effective for causal meaning disambiguation, with clear performance improvements over previous methods.
3. **Analysis of ambiguous lexical markers**: The paper provides insightful analysis of how the model handles ambiguous markers (e.g., "which then"), showing the importance of context.

## Strengths

1. **Strong theoretical foundation**: The paper's literature review thoroughly establishes the context-dependence of causality, correctly identifying the limitations of previous syntactically grounded approaches. The authors effectively argue that causality is not encoded in specific verbs or syntactic structures but is instead context-dependent, which is well-supported by Neeleman and van de Koot (2012).

2. **Effective empirical results**: The paper demonstrates clear performance improvements over the state-of-the-art (B2) with a 2.13% absolute F1 improvement in the causal class on the non-bootstrapping corpus. The authors provide a thoughtful analysis of precision-recall trade-offs, showing their model achieves a better balance than B2, which has high recall but low precision.

3. **Insightful analysis of ambiguous markers**: The analysis of the "which then" marker (Table 8) is particularly strong, demonstrating the model's ability to handle ambiguity through context encoding rather than relying on lexical resources. This directly supports the paper's core claim about the necessity of context.

## Weaknesses

1. **Limited scope of the task**: The paper frames the problem as a binary classification task (causal vs. non-causal) but ignores the broader challenge of causal relation extraction, which includes identifying the two events themselves (e1 and e2) and their direction. This is a significant limitation, as the authors themselves acknowledge the need for a two-step task (causal meaning classification and causal argument identification), but focus only on the first step.

2. **Lack of novelty in architecture**: The proposed "Pair LSTM" architecture is not novel; similar approaches have been used for relation classification tasks in NLP. The paper doesn't clearly differentiate their approach from previous work on relation classification, which weakens the contribution claim.

3. **Insufficient evaluation of model limitations**: The paper provides examples where their model outperforms the baseline but doesn't analyze cases where their model fails. A thorough failure analysis would have strengthened the paper by identifying the limitations of the approach.

4. **Corpus limitations**: The AltLex corpus is relatively small (8k examples in the non-bootstrapping version), and the paper doesn't address whether the model would generalize to larger or more diverse corpora. The bootstrapping version has class distribution issues between training and test sets, which the paper acknowledges but doesn't properly address.

## Questions to Authors

1. The paper defines causality as a binary classification task, but the field typically treats causal relation extraction as a multi-step process (identifying events, then determining their relationship). How would your approach integrate with a full causal relation extraction pipeline?

2. The paper states that the "causal meaning classification" task is the focus, but the examples (Table 1) suggest that the lexical markers themselves are often ambiguous. Why not approach this as a lexical disambiguation task (e.g., "because" can be causal or temporal) rather than a binary classification?

3. The paper claims the model handles ambiguous markers like "which then" through context encoding, but the word "which" doesn't appear in the training corpus. How does the model generalize to such unseen combinations of words?

## Overall Assessment

This is a well-executed paper that makes a strong theoretical case for context-dependent causality and demonstrates empirical improvements over previous methods. The theoretical foundation is excellent, and the results are convincing. However, the limited scope of the task (binary classification rather than full causal relation extraction) and lack of novelty in the architecture weaken the contribution. The paper would be significantly stronger with a more comprehensive evaluation of the model's limitations and a clearer connection to the broader task of causal relation extraction.

I recommend acceptance with minor revisions to address the scope limitations and provide a more thorough analysis of the model's failures. The paper makes a valuable contribution to the understanding of context-dependent causality, but should clarify the scope of its contribution to avoid overstating its novelty.
