

# Review of "Neural Disambiguation of Causal Lexical Markers based on Context"

## Summary of the Paper and Main Contributions

This paper addresses the task of causal meaning disambiguation, specifically determining whether a lexical marker (e.g., "since," "so," "because") is being used in a causal sense or another sense (e.g., temporal) within a given context. The authors argue that previous approaches to causal relation identification have been limited by their reliance on syntactic constructions or lexical resources, which restricts their coverage and generalization ability.

The main contributions of this work (as objectively assessed):

1. **A neural network architecture for causal meaning disambiguation that does not rely on predefined syntactic constructions or lexical resources**, instead encoding context through LSTM networks to capture the meaning of lexical markers in context. The authors propose two variants of this architecture: "Stated Pair LSTM" and "Pair LSTM."

2. **Empirical demonstration that their neural approach outperforms previous baselines** on the AltLex corpus (Hidey and McKeown, 2016), with improvements of up to 7.72% in Accuracy on the "non bootstrapping" version of the corpus.

3. **Demonstration of the system's ability to handle ambiguous lexical markers** (e.g., "since," "so," "which then") that have multiple meanings depending on context, showing the neural approach's advantage over previous methods that relied on fixed lexical resources.

## Strengths

1. **Strong motivation and clear problem definition**: The paper provides a thorough and well-structured justification for the task of causal meaning disambiguation, distinguishing it from causal relation identification. The authors clearly articulate the limitations of previous work (reliance on syntactic constructions, restricted coverage of lexical resources) and provide a solid theoretical foundation for their approach based on Neeleman and van de Koot (2012). The distinction between causal meaning disambiguation and causal argument identification is particularly well-motivated and clarifies the scope of their contribution.

2. **Comprehensive evaluation with appropriate baselines**: The authors conduct a thorough empirical evaluation on the AltLex corpus, comparing their approach against two well-chosen baselines: B1 (a simple majority class baseline) and B2 (the system from Hidey and McKeown, 2016). The evaluation includes detailed analysis of performance across different lexical markers, with particular attention to ambiguous cases (e.g., "since," "so," "which then"). The results consistently show that the proposed neural approach outperforms both baselines, with statistically significant improvements (e.g., McNemar's test with P-value 0.005).

3. **Technical soundness of the approach**: The neural architecture is well-motivated (LSTM for sequential context encoding, which is appropriate for this task), and the authors carefully evaluate different design choices (zero-padding strategies, optimizers, dropout rates). The paper provides sufficient technical detail about the implementation and experimental setup, including the use of pre-trained GloVe embeddings and the specific hyperparameters used. The ablation studies comparing "Stated Pair LSTM" and "Pair LSTM" are particularly valuable for understanding the design choices.

## Weaknesses

1. **Limited novelty of the neural approach**: While the application to causal meaning disambiguation is valid, the neural architecture itself (LSTM-based sentence pair classification) is relatively straightforward and not particularly novel. Previous work has already applied similar neural approaches to related tasks (e.g., Melamud et al., 2016; Kruengkrai et al., 2017). The paper does not clearly articulate how their specific architecture differs from or improves upon existing neural approaches for sentence pair classification tasks, which weakens the novelty claim.

2. **Insufficient comparison to more recent neural approaches**: The paper compares only against the system from Hidey and McKeown (2016), which is a traditional machine learning approach (SVM with hand-crafted features). It does not compare against more recent neural approaches that have become standard for NLP tasks, such as transformer-based models (e.g., BERT, RoBERTa). This omission is significant because the paper claims to "outperform the state-of-the-art," but the state-of-the-art for sentence pair classification tasks has evolved considerably since 2016.

3. **Limited analysis of failure cases**: The paper provides examples of correctly classified instances by their system that were misclassified by the baseline, but it does not systematically analyze the cases where their own system fails. Understanding these failure cases would provide valuable insights for future work and would strengthen the paper's contribution by showing a more complete understanding of the limitations of the approach.

## Questions to Authors

1. The paper claims that their neural approach "outperforms the state-of-the-art" but only compares against B2 (the system from Hidey and McKeown, 2016). Could you clarify why this is considered the state-of-the-art for this specific task, and why more recent neural approaches (e.g., transformer-based models) were not included in the comparison?

2. The authors mention that their "Pair LSTM" architecture outperforms the "Stated Pair LSTM" architecture. How does this architecture differ from standard sentence pair classification approaches (e.g., using BERT for sentence pairs), and what specific advantages does it offer for this causal meaning disambiguation task?

3. The paper states that the "bootstrapping" version of the AltLex corpus has instances where the class distribution differs between training and test sets. How did your system perform specifically on these challenging instances, and what insights did you gain about the robustness of your approach to distribution shifts?
