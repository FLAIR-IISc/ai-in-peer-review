

# Review of "Neural Disambiguation of Causal Lexical Markers based on Context"

## Summary and Main Contributions

This paper addresses the task of causal meaning disambiguation, specifically determining whether a relation between two events (e1, e2) is causal or non-causal, with particular attention to lexical markers that can have ambiguous meanings (e.g., "since" or "after" which can be temporal or causal). The authors argue that previous approaches to causality classification have been limited by reliance on specific syntactic constructions or lexical resources, and propose a neural network architecture that encodes the context of the two events to disambiguate causal meaning.

As I see them, the main contributions of this work are:

1. **Demonstrating that neural networks can effectively disambiguate causal meaning based on context** without relying on syntactic constructions or lexical resources. This is the most significant contribution as it shows a more generalizable approach to causality classification that doesn't suffer from the limitations of previous work.

2. **Thorough analysis and application of the AltLex corpus**, including detailed examination of ambiguous lexical markers and class distribution issues. This provides valuable insights for future work in the area.

3. **Clear conceptual distinction** between causal meaning disambiguation and causal argument identification, which is an important theoretical contribution to the field.

## Strengths

1. **Clear problem statement and motivation**: The paper provides an excellent and thorough discussion of the limitations of previous approaches to causality classification, particularly highlighting how prior work has been constrained by syntactic constructions and lexical resources. The authors effectively establish the need for a more context-based approach to causal meaning disambiguation, which is well-motivated and clearly presented.

2. **Strong empirical evaluation**: The authors conduct a comprehensive evaluation of their neural architecture against relevant baselines (B1 and B2), including a detailed analysis of performance on ambiguous lexical markers. Their analysis of how their model handles specific ambiguous cases (e.g., "which then" in Table 8) provides compelling evidence for the value of their context-based approach.

3. **Important conceptual contribution**: The distinction between causal meaning disambiguation and causal argument identification is a valuable theoretical contribution that clarifies the task landscape for future work. This conceptual framing helps position their work within the broader literature and makes their contributions more precise.

4. **Careful corpus analysis**: The paper provides a detailed analysis of the AltLex corpus, including the distribution of ambiguous lexical markers and the class imbalance issues. This is particularly valuable as the authors acknowledge the limitations of existing corpora and show how their approach can work within these constraints.

5. **Good baseline comparison**: The authors compare their approach to a strong baseline (B2) that was specifically designed for this task by the original corpus authors. The fact that their neural approach outperforms B2 (by 2.13% F1 on the non-bootstrapping version) is a significant empirical contribution.

## Weaknesses

1. **Limited novelty of the approach**: The paper's core methodology (using LSTMs for context encoding) is not particularly novel in NLP, as many prior works have successfully applied similar architectures to various classification tasks. The paper doesn't sufficiently emphasize how their specific application to causal disambiguation advances the field beyond what would be expected from applying a standard neural architecture to a new problem. This is a significant limitation given ACL's high standards for novelty.

2. **Small dataset size and limited generalizability**: The AltLex corpus is relatively small (around 8k examples for the non-bootstrapped version), which limits the strength of the empirical claims. The authors acknowledge this but don't adequately address how such a small dataset affects the generalizability of their findings or whether their approach would scale to larger corpora. This is particularly concerning given the current trend toward larger datasets in NLP.

3. **Lack of detailed error analysis**: While the paper provides some examples of cases where their model outperforms the baseline (Table 8), it lacks a systematic error analysis that would help identify the specific limitations of their approach. For instance, it would be valuable to know what types of causal relations the model struggles with most, or whether certain types of ambiguous markers are consistently misclassified.

4. **Limited discussion of broader impact**: The paper doesn't sufficiently contextualize its contribution within the broader landscape of NLP tasks. It's not clear how this work compares to state-of-the-art approaches on other related tasks (e.g., event extraction, relation extraction), which would help establish the significance of their contribution.

5. **Insufficient discussion of limitations**: The paper doesn't adequately address the limitations of their approach. For example, it doesn't discuss how their model would perform on causal relations that are not captured in the AltLex corpus, or how it would handle more complex causal structures beyond binary classification. This omission weakens the paper's contribution.

## Questions to Authors

1. How do you plan to address the limited size of the AltLex corpus in future work? Given the small dataset size (around 8k examples), how confident are you that the performance gains you observed are not due to overfitting to this specific corpus?

2. The paper claims that their approach outperforms the state-of-the-art, but the absolute performance (around 80% F1) is not particularly high for a neural approach. How do you contextualize this performance against other NLP tasks or similar classification problems? Is this a reasonable performance for the task, or does it indicate a fundamental limitation of the approach?

3. The paper mentions that the corpus has an imbalance between causal and non-causal examples. Did you try any techniques to address this imbalance (e.g., class weighting, oversampling) beyond reducing the non-causal class by a factor of ten? If not, why not?

4. The authors state that their approach doesn't rely on lexical resources, but they use pre-trained GloVe embeddings. How do you ensure that the embeddings themselves don't encode lexical bias that might affect the performance on ambiguous markers? Could this potentially be a limitation of the approach?

## Overall Recommendation

This paper makes a valuable contribution to the problem of causal meaning disambiguation, particularly through its clear conceptual framing and empirical demonstration of a context-based approach. However, the limited novelty of the core methodology and the small dataset size are significant weaknesses that prevent this work from meeting ACL's high standards for novelty and impact. While the results are promising, they don't represent a substantial enough advance to warrant acceptance at ACL.

**Decision: Reject**

The paper has interesting ideas and good empirical results, but the lack of sufficient novelty and the limited scope of the contribution (compared to the standard for ACL) make it unsuitable for publication. The authors would need to substantially strengthen the novelty of their approach and provide more convincing evidence of its broader impact to be considered for acceptance. I would encourage them to pursue this line of work with a more novel architecture or by addressing a more significant limitation of current approaches.
