

# Review of "Neural Disambiguation of Causal Lexical Markers based on Context"

## Summary and Main Contributions

The paper proposes a neural network architecture for disambiguating causal lexical markers (e.g., "because," "so," "since") in text. The authors claim that existing approaches rely too heavily on linguistic rules, lexical resources, or syntactic structures, while their approach uses neural networks to encode contextual information for causal meaning disambiguation. They evaluate their approach on the AltLex corpus introduced by Hidey and McKeown (2016).

Main contributions (in order of strength):
1. A neural network architecture for causal meaning disambiguation based on contextual encoding of events and lexical markers.
2. Evaluation on the AltLex corpus, showing improved performance over previous state-of-the-art methods.
3. Analysis of the performance on ambiguous causal markers like "which then" that are challenging for previous approaches.

## Strengths

1. The paper identifies a meaningful gap in the literature regarding causal relation disambiguation and provides a compelling argument for why neural approaches might be more suitable than previous rule-based or feature-engineering approaches. The authors correctly note that the lack of fixed syntactic constructions for causality makes neural approaches more promising.

2. The evaluation on the AltLex corpus is well-designed, and the authors provide a thorough analysis of how their model handles ambiguous causal markers like "which then" that are challenging for previous approaches. The examples in Table 8 demonstrate their model's ability to handle these edge cases better than the baseline.

3. The paper provides a detailed comparison with the state-of-the-art method by Hidey and McKeown (2016), showing that their neural approach achieves better performance on the causal class (F1) while maintaining a good balance between precision and recall.

## Weaknesses

1. **Consistent citation and reference formatting errors**: The paper contains numerous inconsistent citation formats throughout. For example, "Neeleman and van de Koot (2012)" is sometimes written as "Neeleman and Koot (2012)" (dropping "van de"), "Bethard and Martin (2008)" is sometimes written as "Bethard and Martin.,? 2008", and "Hidey and McKeown (2016)" is sometimes written as "Hidey and McKeown.,? 2016". The references section also contains errors like "Neeleman and Koot.,? 2012" instead of the correct "Neeleman and van de Koot, 2012" and "Prasad et al\.,? \Q2008\E" with backslashes and Q/E markers. These formatting errors undermine the paper's credibility and suggest a lack of careful proofreading.

2. **Technical errors in methodology**: The paper contains multiple technical errors that significantly undermine the credibility of the proposed method. Most notably:
   - Equation (1) states "pad(x) = {max(x),mean(x),mode(x)}" but x is a vector, so max(x) is a scalar value, not a padding strategy. This is mathematically incorrect.
   - Equation (2) claims "vec(x) : IRn×d → IRnd" but the output should be IR^100 (not IR^nd) as stated in the text.
   - The paper uses softmax for binary classification (Causal vs. Non-Causal), which is incorrect. For binary classification, sigmoid activation should be used, not softmax, which is designed for multi-class problems.
   - The paper states "the first input of the system is the first event of the relation, and the second input is the concatenation of the lexical marker and the second event" but then claims the LSTM for the second event is "initialized with the last state of the first LSTM" - this is confusing and inconsistent with the description of the input structure.

3. **Inconsistent and contradictory results presentation**: The paper contains multiple contradictions in its presentation of results. For example:
   - The paper claims "the best configuration ("Pair LSTM Max Adadelta")" for the non bootstrapping version, but in Table 5, "Pair LSTM Max Adadelta" has F1 75.40% while "Pair LSTM Mean Adam" has F1 74.44% (so "Max Adadelta" actually has higher F1).
   - The paper states "all the configurations in Table 5 outperform the baseline B1" but for the "bootstrapping" version, "Stated Pair LSTM Max Adadelta" has Precision 78.69% which is lower than B1's 74.38% (a contradiction).
   - For the non bootstrapping version, the paper claims "Pair LSTM Mean Adam" has the highest performance but the table shows "Pair LSTM Max Adadelta" has the highest F1 (75.40% vs 74.44%).

4. **Lack of clarity in key concepts**: The paper's definition of causality is inconsistent. It states "causing event (cause, e1) and caused event (effect, e2)" but then refers to "the causing event (e1) and caused event (e2)" without the "cause" and "effect" labels. This inconsistency is confusing for readers. Additionally, the paper states "the causing event does not always appear before the caused event in the sentence" but then uses examples where the order is clear, without addressing the more complex cases where the order is ambiguous.

5. **Inconsistent terminology and typos**: The paper contains numerous typos and inconsistent terminology that make it difficult to follow:
   - "Stated Pair LSTM" (should be "Stateful Pair LSTM" or "State Pair LSTM" - "Stated" is likely a typo)
   - "syntaxgrounded construction" (should be "syntax-grounded")
   - "Pe-t-er" instead of "Peter" in the example
   - "the later sentence" instead of "the latter sentence"
   - "Bootstrapping" written as "Bootstrapping" in the table headers
   - "zero-padding strategies" written as "zero-padding" in the equation
   - Inconsistent use of "Causal" vs "Causal class" in tables

## Questions to Authors

1. In Table 5, the "Pair LSTM Mean Adam" and "Pair LSTM Mean Adadelta" have identical results for the non bootstrapping version. Can you explain this discrepancy?

2. The paper states that "the system that reached the highest performance is the one that used the mean strategy for zero-padding and the optimizer Adam" but for the non bootstrapping version, "Pair LSTM Max Adadelta" has the highest F1 (75.40%) while "Pair LSTM Mean Adam" has 74.44%. Can you clarify which configuration actually performed best?

3. The paper uses softmax for binary classification (Causal vs. Non-Causal), but softmax is designed for multi-class classification. Why not use sigmoid activation for binary classification, which is the standard approach?

## Overall Recommendation

**Reject**

While the paper addresses an important problem in computational linguistics and makes a reasonable argument for the need for contextual encoding in causal relation disambiguation, the numerous technical errors, inconsistent results presentation, and formatting issues undermine the credibility of the work. The paper contains multiple mathematical errors, incorrect technical claims, and inconsistent terminology that would require substantial revision before publication. The technical flaws are too significant to ignore, even though the overall research direction is promising. The paper would benefit from a complete rewrite with careful attention to technical details and consistent formatting.
