

# Review of "Neural Disambiguation of Causal Lexical Markers based on Context"

## Summary of the Paper
This paper addresses the task of causality disambiguation, defined as determining whether a relation between two events (cause and effect) is causal. The authors argue that causality is not encoded in specific syntactic structures or lexical items as previously thought, but rather depends on context. They propose a neural network approach using LSTMs to encode the context of two events and a lexical marker to disambiguate causal meaning. The work is evaluated on the AltLex corpus, which contains both explicit and implicit causal expressions with ambiguous lexical markers.

## Main Contributions
1. A neural network architecture for causality disambiguation that doesn't rely on predefined syntactic structures or lexical resources, but rather encodes contextual information through LSTMs.
2. Empirical demonstration that context encoding effectively handles ambiguous causal lexical markers, outperforming previous baselines on the AltLex corpus.
3. Analysis of how the model handles ambiguous lexical markers (e.g., "since", "which then") that can have causal or non-causal meanings depending on context.

## Strengths
1. **Strong justification for the problem and approach**: The paper provides a thorough literature review of causality in linguistics, clearly demonstrating the limitations of previous syntactic and lexical approaches (citing Neeleman and van de Koot, 2012). The authors make a compelling case that causality depends on context rather than fixed linguistic constructions, which justifies their neural approach.

2. **Comprehensive empirical evaluation**: The authors conduct a thorough evaluation on the AltLex corpus, comparing their approach against two baselines (B1: majority class baseline, B2: Hidey and McKeown's SVM system). Their results (Table 5) show consistent improvement over baselines, with statistical significance (p=0.005) for their best configuration. The analysis of different architectural choices (Stated Pair LSTM vs. Pair LSTM, zero-padding strategies, optimizers) demonstrates careful model design.

3. **Effective handling of ambiguous lexical markers**: The paper provides concrete examples (Table 8) showing how their model correctly disambiguates causal meaning for ambiguous markers like "break" and "which then" that confuse previous approaches. This demonstrates the value of context encoding for causal disambiguation.

4. **Clear technical implementation details**: The paper provides sufficient detail about the neural architecture, data preprocessing, and experimental setup to allow for reproducibility. The discussion of zero-padding strategies (Section 3.3) and comparative analysis of different optimizers (Adadelta vs. Adam) shows methodological rigor.

## Weaknesses
1. **Limited novelty claim**: While the authors claim their approach is novel, they don't sufficiently differentiate it from prior neural approaches. The paper references Kruengkrai et al. (2017) who used CNN for causal meaning identification, but doesn't clearly explain how their LSTM-based approach represents a meaningful advance over existing neural methods. The novelty appears incremental rather than transformative.

2. **Insufficient comparison with recent neural approaches**: The paper only compares against Hidey and McKeown (2016) and a simple baseline, but doesn't compare against more recent neural approaches to causality (e.g., transformer-based models). This makes it difficult to assess whether the proposed method is competitive with current state-of-the-art neural models for this task.

3. **Limited analysis of failure cases**: The paper provides examples of correctly classified instances (Table 8) but doesn't include a thorough analysis of failure cases. Understanding where the model fails is crucial for assessing its limitations and potential for improvement.

4. **Corpus limitations not fully addressed**: While the authors acknowledge the AltLex corpus's limitations (small size, class imbalance), they don't sufficiently discuss how these limitations affect the generalizability of their results. The paper doesn't address whether their approach would work on larger, more diverse corpora or whether the corpus biases might affect model performance.

## Questions to Authors
1. How does your approach compare to recent neural approaches to causality that use transformer-based architectures (e.g., BERT), which have shown strong performance on many NLP tasks? Have you considered using such architectures?

2. The paper mentions that the "bootstrapping" version of the corpus has instances where training and test sets have different class distributions (Table 4). How does this affect your model's performance, and what strategies could be used to address this issue in future work?

3. The authors claim that "causality is not defined by a specific syntactic construction or the coverage of semantic resources" (Section 2), yet the AltLex corpus was built using a bootstrapping method based on known causal markers from PDTB. How does this approach align with your claim that causality shouldn't be limited by specific linguistic constructions?

4. The paper states that "the encoding of the context is required for the disambiguation of the causal meaning" (Section 4.3), but doesn't provide a detailed analysis of what specific contextual features the model is learning. Could you elaborate on what aspects of the context are most important for the model's performance?
