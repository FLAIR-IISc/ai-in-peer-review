

# Review of "Neural Disambiguation of Causal Lexical Markers based on Context"

## Summary of the Paper and Main Contributions

This paper addresses the task of causality disambiguation, which involves determining whether a relation between two events is causal. The authors argue that previous approaches to causality classification have been limited by reliance on syntactic constructions or lexical resources, and they propose a neural network-based approach that encodes the context of the relation to disambiguate causal meaning.

The main contributions of this work, as I see them, are:

1. A neural network architecture (specifically, a Pair LSTM with specific configurations) for causality disambiguation that outperforms existing baselines on the AltLex corpus.
2. A detailed analysis showing that the proposed neural approach can correctly disambiguate causal meaning for ambiguous lexical markers (like "which then" which has both causal and non-causal uses) where previous methods fail.
3. The demonstration that the neural approach generalizes better than previous methods that rely on lexical resources (like WordNet, VerbNet, PropBank), which restrict the system to the linguistic coverage of those resources.

## Strengths

1. **Clear Problem Definition and Motivation**: The paper provides a thorough literature review of previous work on causality classification, highlighting the limitations of previous approaches (reliance on syntactic constructions, lexical resources, and restricted coverage). The authors clearly articulate why neural approaches might be better suited for this task, particularly because causality doesn't have a fixed linguistic construction. This is well-justified and sets up a strong foundation for their work. The paper's argument against syntactic grounding for causality (citing Neeleman and van de Koot, 2012) is particularly strong and well-supported.

2. **Appropriate Dataset and Baselines**: The paper uses the AltLex corpus, which is the largest available corpus for this task (to the best of their knowledge), and they clearly explain why previous corpora are insufficient. They establish two strong baselines: a simple heuristic baseline (B1) and the state-of-the-art method from the corpus authors (B2). The results show that their neural approach outperforms both baselines, with their best configuration ("Pair LSTM Mean Adam") achieving 81.94% F1 in the Causal class on the "bootstrapping" version of the corpus, which is 1.04% higher than B2's 80.90%.

3. **Technical Soundness and Clear Experiments**: The paper provides a detailed description of their neural network architecture (Pair LSTM vs. Stated Pair LSTM), including their reasoning for choosing LSTM over CNN. They systematically evaluate different hyperparameters (zero-padding strategies, optimizers, dropout rates) and show that their best configuration outperforms all other configurations. The evaluation metrics (Precision, Recall, F1, Accuracy) are appropriate for the task, and they provide detailed analysis of the results, including the behavior of the model on ambiguous lexical markers like "which then" (Table 8).

## Weaknesses

1. **Lack of Novelty in Neural Architecture**: The proposed neural architecture (Pair LSTM) is not particularly novel. The use of LSTMs for sentence classification tasks is well-established in NLP. The authors claim that their architecture is novel because it's designed specifically for causality disambiguation, but the core components (LSTM, dense layers) are standard. The paper doesn't clearly articulate how their architecture differs from other neural approaches to similar tasks. For example, the paper doesn't compare with other neural approaches to causal relation extraction (e.g., Kruengkrai et al., 2017) that might have been developed for similar tasks.

2. **Limited Scope and Generalizability**: The paper only evaluates their approach on the AltLex corpus, which is the only large corpus available for this task. However, the AltLex corpus has limitations (it's a bootstrapped corpus with class imbalance issues). The paper doesn't discuss whether their approach would generalize to other corpora or tasks beyond the specific AltLex dataset. Without testing on a different dataset, it's hard to claim that their approach is broadly applicable. The paper would be stronger if they could demonstrate generalization to other causal relation datasets.

3. **Insufficient Analysis of Failure Cases**: The paper provides some examples of correctly classified cases (Table 8) but doesn't provide examples of cases where their model fails. Without understanding the failure cases, it's difficult to assess the limitations of their approach and where further improvements might be needed. The paper mentions that "the system has a large number of false positives" but doesn't analyze why these occur.

## Questions to Authors

1. The paper claims that causality "does not have a fixed list of lexical or syntactic constructions," but they still use a specific format for their input (two events and a lexical marker). How do they handle cases where the causal relation is expressed without a clear lexical marker (e.g., in implicit causal relations not covered by the AltLex corpus)?

2. The paper mentions that the AltLex corpus has a class imbalance, and they tested with a reduced version. However, they don't discuss whether their model's performance would change if they used more sophisticated techniques to handle class imbalance (e.g., oversampling, class weighting). Could they provide more details on how they handled class imbalance?

3. The paper states that their approach outperforms previous work on the AltLex corpus, but they don't compare with other neural network approaches that might have been developed for similar tasks. Could they clarify why they chose to compare only with the two baselines (B1 and B2) rather than with other neural methods?

## Additional Comments

- The paper would benefit from a more detailed discussion of how their approach differs from related work on causal relation extraction, particularly from the work by Kruengkrai et al. (2017) which also uses neural networks for causal relation tasks.

- The paper should clarify the exact definition of "causal meaning disambiguation" in relation to other related tasks (e.g., causal relation extraction, causal event identification) to avoid confusion.

- The paper mentions that "the causal meaning of some lexical markers totally depends on the context," but it doesn't provide a clear definition of what constitutes the "context" for their model. It would be helpful to clarify whether the context is limited to the immediate sentence or includes broader discourse context.
