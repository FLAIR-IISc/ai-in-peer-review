

# ACL Review

## Summary of the Paper
The paper proposes a neural network architecture for causal meaning disambiguation, focusing on identifying whether a relation between two events is causal. The authors claim that their approach outperforms previous work on the AltLex corpus. They define the task as distinguishing between causal and non-causal relations, with the input consisting of two events and a lexical marker that may signal causality.

## Main Contributions (in order of strength)
1. The authors propose a neural network architecture (using LSTM) for causal meaning disambiguation, which they claim outperforms previous methods on the AltLex corpus.
2. They provide an analysis of the AltLex corpus, highlighting the ambiguity of certain causal markers and the need for context-based disambiguation.
3. The paper clearly distinguishes between causal meaning disambiguation and causal argument identification as two subtasks of causality classification.

## Strengths
1. **Clear task definition**: The authors provide a well-defined task for causal meaning disambiguation, distinguishing it from causal argument identification, which is a useful contribution to the field.
2. **Novel approach to context encoding**: The paper makes a valid point that context encoding is important for causal meaning disambiguation, and the neural approach offers a promising alternative to feature engineering-based methods.
3. **Thorough evaluation**: The authors compare their approach with two strong baselines and provide detailed analysis of the results, including examples of correctly classified cases.

## Weaknesses

1. **Major formatting and presentation errors**: The paper contains numerous critical formatting errors that undermine its credibility. Most notably, the paper states "The corpus size is shown in Table 3" but there is no Table 3. Similarly, the paper references "Table 5.1" instead of "Table 5." These are not minor errors but serious issues that suggest the paper was not carefully proofread. Additionally, the references have inconsistent formatting (e.g., "Neeleman and Koot.,? 2012" should be "Neeleman and van de Koot, 2012") and contain typos like "as well as the the number."

2. **Misrepresentation of prior work**: The paper misrepresents PropBank's annotation scheme, claiming that "John" and "the hammer" are "cause arguments" of the verb "broke." In reality, PropBank annotates semantic roles (e.g., ARG0 for the agent), not specifically "cause arguments." This misrepresentation is serious because it undermines the paper's foundation. Additionally, the paper misinterprets Neeleman and van de Koot (2012) by claiming they "concluded that the linguistic approximation of causation by culmination of events is not exclusive of causal verbs," when the cited work actually argues against syntax-grounded constructions of causality.

3. **Poorly constructed examples**: The counterexample "A hammer eventually killed John" is grammatically problematic and fails to make the point the authors are trying to make. Hammers don't "eventually" kill people; it's the action of hitting with a hammer that causes death. The example should be "The hammer eventually killed John" (which is still awkward) or "A hammer hit John, eventually killing him." The example is so poorly constructed that it undermines the paper's credibility.

4. **Mathematical notation errors**: The paper contains multiple errors in mathematical notation, including inconsistent dimensionality (e.g., "W ∈ IR100×nd" should be "W ∈ ℝ^100×(n×d)"), unclear function definitions, and inconsistent use of mathematical symbols. These errors make it difficult to understand the proposed architecture.

5. **Inconsistent terminology**: The paper uses inconsistent capitalization (e.g., "Causal" vs. "causal," "Bootstrapping" vs. "bootstrapping") and spelling ("bootstrapping" vs. "bootstrapping"), which makes it difficult to follow the arguments and undermines the paper's professionalism.

## Questions to Authors

1. In Section 3.1, you state that "the caused event (e2) is before the causing event (e1) in the following example: (This water was diverted)e2 ([due to]l a combination of improper maintenance, leaking and clogging, and eventually ended up in the MIC storage tank.)e1." This example is confusing because the "due to" phrase should be introducing the cause of the diversion (improper maintenance, etc.), meaning the cause should precede the effect. Can you clarify this example and how it demonstrates your point?

2. In the introduction, you claim that "the syntax-grounded construction of causality defined by those theories is far away from the human mental model of causation." However, your counterexample "A hammer eventually killed John" is grammatically questionable. Can you provide a better counterexample that clearly demonstrates the point you're making?

3. The paper states that "the corpus size is shown in Table 3," but there is no Table 3 in the paper. Please clarify where this table should be and provide the missing information.

4. You use "l" to denote lexical markers (e.g., "([due to]l"). This is confusing as it looks like a lowercase L. Please clarify the notation and consider using a different symbol to avoid confusion.

5. In Section 4.2, you state that "the system that reached the highest performance is the one that used the mean strategy for zero-padding and the optimizer Adam." However, Table 5 shows that "Pair LSTM Mean Adam" reached 81.56% F1 in the "bootstrapping" version, while "Pair LSTM Mean Adadelta" reached 81.94% F1. This is a contradiction. Please clarify which configuration actually achieved the highest performance.

## Recommendation
Reject

The paper presents a potentially interesting approach to causal meaning disambiguation, but the numerous formatting errors, misrepresentations of prior work, and poorly constructed examples are too severe to overlook. The paper's credibility is significantly undermined by these issues, and the technical content suffers from inconsistent notation and unclear explanations. The paper would benefit from a complete rewrite to address these fundamental issues before it could be considered for publication.
