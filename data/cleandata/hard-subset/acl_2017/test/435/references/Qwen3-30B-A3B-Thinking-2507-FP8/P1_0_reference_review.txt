

# Peer Review

## Summary

This paper addresses causal meaning disambiguation, the task of determining whether a lexical marker (e.g., "because," "since," "so") signals a causal relation between two events. The authors argue that previous approaches rely too heavily on lexical resources and specific syntactic patterns, which limits their coverage of the diverse ways causality can be expressed in natural language. They propose a neural network architecture based on LSTMs to encode the context of the two events and the lexical marker, claiming it can disambiguate causal meaning without relying on lexical resources. The paper evaluates this approach on the AltLex corpus (Hidey and McKeown, 2016), demonstrating improved performance over previous baselines.

## Primary Contributions

1. **Demonstration that context encoding via neural networks is sufficient for causal meaning disambiguation**: The authors convincingly show that their LSTM-based approach outperforms previous methods that rely on lexical resources, supporting their claim that context encoding alone can capture causal meaning.

2. **Effective use of the AltLex corpus**: The paper makes a valuable contribution by applying and evaluating a novel approach on the AltLex corpus, which contains a diverse set of causal expressions beyond standard lexical markers.

3. **Detailed analysis of ambiguous lexical markers**: The authors provide insightful examples showing how their model correctly disambiguates causal meaning for ambiguous markers (e.g., "which then") where previous methods failed.

## Strengths

1. **Strong theoretical grounding**: The paper provides an excellent review of linguistic theories of causation (Neeleman and van de Koot, 2012), correctly identifying the key limitation of previous computational approaches that rely on fixed syntactic constructions. This theoretical foundation makes the paper's argument compelling.

2. **Clear empirical validation**: The evaluation on the AltLex corpus is thorough, with careful analysis of specific cases where the proposed model outperforms previous baselines. The authors demonstrate that their model correctly handles ambiguous lexical markers (e.g., "which then") where the previous system (B2) fails.

3. **Practical contribution to resource development**: The paper makes a valuable contribution to the field by using and evaluating on the AltLex corpus, which is a significant resource for causal relation research that has not been widely adopted in prior work.

## Weaknesses

1. **Overstated novelty claim**: The paper claims their neural approach is novel for causal meaning disambiguation, but Kruengkrai et al. (2017) already used a multi-column CNN for causal relation identification in Japanese. The paper fails to adequately distinguish its approach from prior neural work, weakening the novelty claim.

2. **Limited baseline comparison**: The baselines (B1 and B2) are insufficiently strong. B1 is a simple majority-class baseline, and B2 is the previous state-of-the-art, but there's no comparison to more recent neural baselines. The paper claims "higher ability of generalization" without providing evidence beyond comparison to B2.

3. **Lack of discussion on limitations**: The paper fails to address critical limitations of their approach, particularly that it requires pre-identified events. This is a major constraint in real-world applications where identifying the causal events themselves is a non-trivial part of the task. The paper doesn't discuss how their approach would integrate into a full causal relation extraction pipeline.

4. **Insufficient analysis of model behavior**: While the paper shows the model outperforms B2, it doesn't provide enough insight into *why* the model works. For example, they don't analyze which parts of the context are most important for disambiguation or how the model handles different types of ambiguous markers.

5. **Corpus limitations**: The evaluation is limited to the AltLex corpus, which is built from Wikipedia. The paper doesn't discuss how the model would perform on other domains (news, scientific texts) or languages, limiting the perceived generalizability of the approach.

## Questions to Authors

1. How does your approach compare to more recent neural approaches for causal relation extraction? For example, have you considered using transformers or other more advanced architectures that have shown promise in related NLP tasks?

2. The paper claims that causal relations don't have fixed syntactic constructions, yet your system requires the events to be pre-identified. How would you address the problem of identifying the causal events themselves, which is a critical step in causal relation extraction?

3. The AltLex corpus is built from Wikipedia. How would your model perform on other domains, such as news articles or scientific texts, which may have different patterns of causal expression?

## Recommendation

I recommend acceptance with minor revisions. The paper makes a valuable contribution to the field by demonstrating that neural context encoding can effectively disambiguate causal meaning without relying on lexical resources, and the evaluation on the AltLex corpus is well-executed. However, the authors should address the weaknesses noted above, particularly by clarifying the novelty of their approach, providing a more comprehensive comparison to baselines, and discussing the limitations of their method more thoroughly.

The paper has significant potential to advance the field of causal relation extraction, and with the suggested revisions, it would be a strong contribution to ACL.
