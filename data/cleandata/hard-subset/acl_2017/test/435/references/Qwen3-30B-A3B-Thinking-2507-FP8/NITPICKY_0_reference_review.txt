

# ACL Review: Neural Disambiguation of Causal Lexical Markers based on Context

## Summary of the Paper
The paper proposes a neural network architecture for causality disambiguation, specifically for determining whether a lexical marker between two events expresses a causal relationship. The authors argue that previous work has been limited by reliance on syntactic constructions or lexical resources, and they claim that their neural approach, which encodes contextual information using LSTMs, outperforms previous methods.

## Main Contributions (as I see them)
1. A neural network architecture (Pair LSTM) for causality disambiguation that uses contextual information rather than relying on syntactic constructions or lexical resources
2. Evaluation on the AltLex corpus, with detailed analysis of ambiguous lexical markers
3. Demonstration that context encoding outperforms previous methods on ambiguous cases

## Strengths
1. The paper identifies a genuine limitation in previous work on causality disambiguation (reliance on syntactic constructions and lexical resources) and proposes a clear alternative approach using neural networks to encode contextual information.

2. The authors provide a thorough evaluation of the AltLex corpus, including detailed analysis of ambiguous lexical markers (Table 4) and their impact on classification performance.

3. The paper demonstrates that their model outperforms the baseline B2 on the AltLex corpus, particularly for ambiguous cases like "which then" (Table 8), which supports their main claim about the importance of contextual encoding.

## Weaknesses

### 1. Methodological flaws in the neural architecture

The paper claims that LSTM was chosen over CNN because "CNN requires the definition of the kernel size, which means that we should know beforehand the length of the context that projects the causal meaning of the sentence." This is a weak justification. The authors could have used a CNN with multiple kernel sizes (a common practice) to handle different context lengths. They provide no evidence that their specific architecture is superior to a well-designed CNN with multiple kernel sizes.

The paper also fails to justify why they concatenate the lexical marker with the second event rather than treating it as a separate input. This is a critical design choice that should have been properly motivated.

### 2. Inconsistent and insufficient evaluation methodology

The paper claims that "Pair LSTM Max Adadelta" is the best configuration, but Table 5 shows that "Pair LSTM Max Adadelta" has an F1 of 75.40% for the non-bootstrapping version, while "Pair LSTM Mean Adam" has an F1 of 74.44%. This is a contradiction that undermines the paper's credibility. The authors should have carefully verified these numbers before publication.

The paper also uses inconsistent evaluation metrics: Precision, Recall, and F1 for the Causal class in the main results, but Accuracy as the overall metric for the balanced dataset evaluation. This makes it difficult to compare results across experiments.

### 3. Insufficient corpus analysis and data leakage concerns

The paper claims that "there are no differences between the training and the test set in the 'non bootstrapping' version, but there are in the 'bootstrapping' one." However, they don't explain how the train-test split was performed for the bootstrapping version. If the test set contains instances from classes not present in the training set (as suggested by Table 4), this represents a form of data leakage that invalidates the evaluation.

The paper states the AltLex corpus is "freely released" but provides no link to access the corpus, making the work non-reproducible - a critical issue for computational linguistics papers.

### 4. Minor formatting and presentation issues

The paper contains numerous minor but significant formatting inconsistencies:
- Inconsistent capitalization of terms ("Causal" vs. "causal," "Non Causal" vs. "Non-causal")
- Grammatical errors throughout (e.g., "the later sentence" should be "the latter sentence")
- Inconsistencies in reference formatting (e.g., "Neeleman and Koot.,? 2012" vs. "Neeleman and van de Koot (2012)")
- Typos in Table 8 ("Non Causal" should be "Non-causal")
- In Table 5, the paper claims an improvement of "2.13% according to F1" but the actual difference is 1.64% (75.40% - 73.76%)

### 5. Limited novelty

The paper claims "neural networks are able to encode the meaning of those events, and discover whether the underlying relation between them is causal," but this is a standard application of neural networks to classification tasks. The novelty appears to be limited to the specific application to causality disambiguation, not the neural architecture itself. The paper fails to clearly distinguish their approach from previous neural network approaches to causality disambiguation (e.g., Kruengkrai et al., 2017).

## Questions to Authors

1. Can you clarify why you concatenate the lexical marker with the second event rather than treating it as a separate input? How does this design choice specifically improve causality disambiguation?

2. The paper claims that "Pair LSTM Max Adadelta" is the best configuration, but Table 5 shows "Pair LSTM Mean Adam" has a higher F1 (74.44% vs 75.40%? Wait, 75.40% for "Pair LSTM Max Adadelta" and 74.44% for "Pair LSTM Mean Adadelta" - this contradicts your claim). Can you explain this discrepancy?

3. How was the train-test split performed for the "bootstrapping" version of the corpus, given that Table 4 indicates differences between training and test sets? Did this create a data leakage issue?

4. Can you provide examples of cases where your model fails, particularly for ambiguous lexical markers like "which then" that the paper claims to handle well?

5. How does your model compare to a simple baseline that uses the average of word embeddings for the entire sentence (e.g., using the entire sentence as input to a single LSTM)? This would be a more direct comparison to the "common average-of-words-embeddings representation" mentioned in the paper.

## Overall Recommendation

I recommend rejection of this paper. While the paper addresses a meaningful problem in computational linguistics, the methodological flaws, inconsistent results, insufficient novelty, and numerous minor formatting issues significantly undermine the paper's credibility. As a perfectionist reviewer, I find these issues too substantial to overlook, even though the paper makes a reasonable claim about the importance of contextual encoding for causality disambiguation. The authors need to thoroughly revise the paper to address these fundamental issues before it could be considered for publication.
