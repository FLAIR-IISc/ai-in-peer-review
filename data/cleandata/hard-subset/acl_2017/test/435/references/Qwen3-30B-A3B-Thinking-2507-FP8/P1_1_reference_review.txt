

# Peer Review: "Neural Disambiguation of Causal Lexical Markers based on Context"

## Summary and Contributions

This paper addresses the task of causal meaning disambiguation, where the goal is to determine whether a lexical marker (e.g., "because," "since") signals a causal relationship between two events. The authors argue that prior work has been limited by reliance on specific syntactic constructions or lexical resources, and they propose a neural network approach using LSTM to encode contextual information for disambiguation.

The primary contributions as I see them (in order of strength):
1. **Demonstration that neural approaches can effectively handle causal meaning disambiguation without lexical resources** - The authors show that their LSTM-based model outperforms previous feature-based systems, suggesting context encoding is sufficient for this task.
2. **Analysis of ambiguous causal markers** - The paper provides valuable insights into how the same lexical markers ("since," "after") can have causal or temporal interpretations based on context.
3. **Application of the AltLex corpus** - The authors are the first to use this corpus for neural causality disambiguation, demonstrating its utility for the task.

## Strengths

1. **Clear demonstration of neural approach superiority**: The authors provide compelling evidence that their neural model outperforms the previous state-of-the-art (Hidey and McKeown, 2016) by 2.13% F1 in the Causal class on the non-bootstrapping corpus. Their analysis showing that the neural model correctly handles ambiguous markers like "which then" (which the baseline misclassifies) demonstrates the value of context encoding.

2. **Insightful analysis of ambiguous markers**: The paper effectively demonstrates that context is crucial for disambiguating causal meaning. Figure 2 and Table 8 provide clear evidence that lexical markers like "since" can be ambiguous, and the neural model successfully handles these cases where the baseline fails. This supports their core claim that causality is context-dependent rather than syntactically fixed.

3. **Practical contribution to a neglected task**: While causality has been studied in NLP, the specific task of causal meaning disambiguation (distinguishing causal from temporal/other relations) has received less attention. The authors clearly define this task and show that neural approaches can make progress, which is valuable for downstream applications like discourse analysis and event extraction.

## Weaknesses

1. **Insufficient novelty compared to related work**: The paper doesn't adequately distinguish its neural approach from prior neural work. Kruengkrai et al. (2017) already used a multi-column CNN for causal relation identification in Japanese. The authors' LSTM approach is similar to Melamud et al. (2016) which showed LSTMs outperform word embeddings for context encoding. The paper fails to clearly establish why their specific architecture (Pair LSTM vs. Stated Pair LSTM) is novel or superior to these existing approaches.

2. **Limited corpus size and evaluation**: The AltLex corpus is relatively small (8k-9k examples), which is problematic for neural methods that typically require larger datasets. The paper doesn't explore data augmentation or transfer learning to address this limitation. Additionally, the evaluation is limited to only two versions of the same corpus without testing on other datasets or using cross-validation, which weakens the generalizability claims.

3. **Inadequate comparison to recent neural approaches**: The paper only compares to Hidey and McKeown (2016), which is a feature-based system. It fails to compare with more recent neural approaches for similar tasks (e.g., using transformers or BERT for causal relation classification), making it difficult to assess whether the authors' method represents a significant advancement over current neural techniques.

4. **Lack of ablation studies**: The paper evaluates different zero-padding strategies and optimizers, but doesn't provide a thorough ablation study to understand which components of their architecture are most important. For instance, they don't show how much the LSTM component contributes versus the dense layers, making it hard to understand what truly drives their performance gains.

## Questions to Authors

1. The paper claims their approach is "neural" but doesn't explicitly compare to recent neural methods for causality classification. Could you clarify how your approach differs from or improves upon existing neural baselines (e.g., using BERT for this task)?

2. The AltLex corpus has a significant imbalance between causal and non-causal examples (e.g., 8,821 non-causal vs. 12,534 causal in bootstrapping version). How did you handle this class imbalance in your experiments, and what would happen if you used a more balanced dataset?

3. The paper mentions that their system correctly handles "which then" (Table 8), but the word "which" doesn't appear in the training corpus. How do you explain this success given the out-of-vocabulary issue, and how would you handle such cases in production?

## Overall Assessment

This paper makes a reasonable contribution to the task of causal meaning disambiguation by demonstrating that neural context encoding can outperform previous feature-based approaches. The analysis of ambiguous markers is particularly strong. However, the novelty claim is weakened by the lack of comparison to recent neural methods, and the small dataset size limits the significance of the results. With stronger comparisons to recent neural approaches and a more thorough analysis of their model's components, this work could be significantly strengthened.

The paper is well-written and addresses an important NLP problem. With the suggested improvements, it would be a solid contribution to the field. As it stands, it's a marginal accept with the understanding that the authors need to address the novelty and evaluation concerns in the revision.

**Rating: Weak Accept** (with the understanding that the authors need to address the weaknesses in their revision)
