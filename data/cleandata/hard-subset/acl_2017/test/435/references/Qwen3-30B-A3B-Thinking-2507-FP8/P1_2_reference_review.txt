

# Peer Review

## Summary

This paper proposes a neural network approach for causal meaning disambiguation, focusing on identifying whether a lexical marker between two events signals a causal relation. The authors argue that previous approaches have been limited by syntactic constructions or lexical resources, and they propose to use neural networks to encode the context around the lexical marker to disambiguate causal meaning. They evaluate their approach on the AltLex corpus, which contains a wider variety of causal expressions than previous resources like PDTB.

## Main Contributions

1. The authors propose a neural network architecture (based on LSTMs) for causal meaning disambiguation that encodes the context of two events and the lexical marker between them, addressing the context-dependent nature of causal markers.

2. They demonstrate that their neural approach outperforms previous methods on the AltLex corpus, particularly in achieving a better balance between precision and recall for causal classification.

3. The paper provides a valuable contribution to the scarcity of resources for causal relation analysis by using and evaluating on the AltLex corpus, which captures a wider variety of causal expressions than previous resources.

## Strengths

1. **Strong Motivation and Problem Statement**: The paper provides a compelling argument for why previous approaches to causal relation identification are limited. The authors effectively cite Neeleman and van de Koot (2012) to argue that causality doesn't have a fixed syntactic structure, which is a significant theoretical contribution to the field. Their critique of prior work that relies on syntactic features or lexical resources is well-supported and clearly articulated.

2. **Thorough Evaluation on a Meaningful Resource**: The authors use the AltLex corpus, which is a recently released resource that addresses the limited coverage of causal expressions in previous corpora. Their evaluation demonstrates that their neural approach consistently outperforms the previous state-of-the-art (B2) across both versions of the corpus, with improvements of up to 2.13% in F1 for the Causal class. The results are well-presented in Table 5, and the authors provide a detailed analysis of the most frequent connectives in Table 8, showing specific cases where their model outperforms the baseline.

3. **Clear Demonstration of Contextual Understanding**: The paper effectively demonstrates that their model can handle ambiguous lexical markers (e.g., "which then" in Table 8) that previous methods fail to disambiguate. This is a significant contribution as it shows the model truly learns the contextual nature of causal relations rather than simply memorizing the most frequent connective types.

4. **Practical Implementation Details**: The authors provide comprehensive details about their model architecture, including the use of different zero-padding strategies, optimizers (Adadelta and Adam), and regularization techniques. This level of detail is valuable for reproducibility and for the community to build upon their work.

5. **Balanced Performance**: The authors demonstrate that their model achieves a better balance between precision and recall than previous approaches, which is particularly important for practical applications. The "Pair LSTM Mean Adam" configuration achieves the best balance (Precision 72.65%, Recall 88.57% for the bootstrapping version), showing that the model can accurately identify causal relations without overgeneralizing.

## Weaknesses

1. **Limited Novelty of Approach**: While the application of LSTMs to causal disambiguation is valuable, the paper doesn't sufficiently differentiate this work from existing neural approaches. Melamud et al. (2016) already demonstrated the effectiveness of LSTMs for context encoding in similar tasks. The proposed architecture (Pair LSTM vs. Stated Pair LSTM) is a relatively minor variation on existing neural network designs for sentence pair classification, and the paper doesn't clearly articulate how this differs from other neural approaches to relation classification.

2. **Lack of Comparison to Other Neural Architectures**: The paper only compares to previous rule-based and SVM-based approaches, but doesn't provide a comparison to other neural architectures (e.g., transformers, BERT) that might be more appropriate for this task. Given the current state of the field, this omission is significant as it limits the reader's ability to understand the relative contribution of the proposed approach.

3. **Misleading Claim About Lexical Resources**: The authors claim their approach "does not rely on lexical resources," but they actually use GloVe embeddings, which are a form of lexical resource. They don't clarify this distinction, which could mislead readers about the nature of their contribution. The paper would be stronger if they acknowledged that they're using a different kind of lexical resource (pre-trained embeddings) rather than lexical databases like WordNet.

4. **Limited Analysis of Failure Cases**: While the paper provides examples of correctly classified cases, it doesn't thoroughly analyze cases where their model fails. Understanding failure modes would be valuable for future work and would strengthen the evaluation.

5. **Insufficient Discussion of Generalization**: The paper doesn't address how well the model would generalize to out-of-domain text or to causal relations expressed in different linguistic contexts. This is particularly important given the authors' claim that causality is not restricted to specific syntactic constructions.

## Questions to Authors

1. How does your model handle cases where the causal relation spans multiple sentences rather than being contained within a single sentence?

2. The paper mentions that the "bootstrapping" version of the AltLex corpus has some instances with different class distributions between training and test sets. How did this affect your model's performance, and what strategies did you use to mitigate this issue?

3. The authors claim that their approach doesn't rely on lexical resources, but they use GloVe embeddings. How does this differ from the lexical resource dependency of previous approaches, and what are the implications for the model's generalizability?

4. The paper focuses on disambiguating causal meaning, but doesn't address the broader task of causal argument identification (which the authors mention as a future direction). How would you extend your model to handle this more complex task?

## Overall Recommendation

This paper makes a meaningful contribution to the field by demonstrating that neural networks can effectively disambiguate causal meaning through context encoding, outperforming previous approaches on a relevant corpus. While the novelty of the architecture is somewhat limited, the thorough evaluation and clear demonstration of contextual understanding make this a valuable contribution. The paper is well-written, the methodology is sound, and the results are significant for the field. With minor revisions addressing the weaknesses noted above, this paper would be a strong candidate for publication at ACL.

**Final Recommendation: Accept**
