- Paper addresses disambiguating whether lexical markers (e.g., “because,” “so”) signal causal relations, arguing causality is context-dependent.  
- Proposes a neural architecture that encodes context to disambiguate causal meaning and evaluates on the AltLex corpus.  
- Main contributions: theoretical argument for context-dependent causality, a neural model that outperforms prior methods, and analysis of ambiguous markers.  
- Strong theoretical foundation with a thorough literature review supporting context-dependence of causality.  
- Empirical results show clear improvement (≈2.13% absolute F1 on causal class) and a better precision-recall balance than prior work.  
- Insightful analysis of ambiguous markers (e.g., “which then”) demonstrating the model’s context sensitivity.  
- Task scope is limited to binary causal vs. non-causal classification and does not address event identification or relation direction.  
- Proposed Pair LSTM architecture lacks novelty relative to existing relation classification approaches.  
- Paper lacks a thorough failure analysis showing where the model fails compared to baselines.  
- Corpus limitations: AltLex is small (~8k examples) and bootstrapping introduces train/test class distribution issues not fully addressed.  
- Question: how would this approach integrate into a full causal relation extraction pipeline (event identification + relation)?  
- Question: why treat the problem as binary classification rather than lexical disambiguation of ambiguous markers?  
- Question: how does the model generalize to unseen or rare marker combinations (e.g., “which then” not in training data)?  
- Overall: well-executed with strong theory and convincing results, but weakened by limited scope and limited novelty in architecture.  
- Recommendation: accept with minor revisions to address scope, provide failure analysis, and clarify contribution boundaries to avoid overstating novelty.
