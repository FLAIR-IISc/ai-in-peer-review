

# ACL Review: "Neural Disambiguation of Causal Lexical Markers based on Context"

## Summary
This paper proposes a neural network approach for causality disambiguation, specifically addressing whether a relation between two events (e1, e2) is causal or non-causal. The authors claim their approach outperforms previous work on the AltLex corpus, introducing two LSTM-based architectures ("Stated Pair LSTM" and "Pair LSTM") and demonstrating improvements over the state-of-the-art.

## Main Contributions (in order of strength)
1. The paper provides a detailed analysis of the AltLex corpus, including its class distribution and challenges with ambiguous lexical markers, which is valuable for future work in causality disambiguation.
2. The authors introduce a neural network approach for causality disambiguation, applying LSTMs to encode contextual information for causal meaning, which represents a novel application in this specific domain.
3. The paper demonstrates empirical improvements over previous methods on the AltLex corpus, with the best configuration achieving 75.40% F1 in the Causal class for the "non bootstrapping" version.

## Strengths
1. **Novel Application of Neural Networks**: The paper's application of LSTMs to causality disambiguation is a reasonable and timely approach, as neural methods have shown promise for context-dependent tasks. The authors correctly identify that traditional rule-based and feature-based approaches (like those of Hidey and McKeown) rely on limited linguistic coverage, which is a valid critique.

2. **Comprehensive Corpus Analysis**: The paper provides a thorough analysis of the AltLex corpus, including the distribution of lexical markers, class imbalance, and the challenges posed by ambiguous expressions (e.g., "since" and "after"). The detailed discussion of the corpus construction and its limitations is well-executed.

3. **Empirical Validation**: The paper demonstrates that neural approaches can outperform previous work on the AltLex corpus, with the "Pair LSTM" architecture consistently outperforming the "Stated Pair LSTM" architecture. The analysis of specific ambiguous cases (like "which then") provides concrete evidence supporting the need for contextual encoding.

## Weaknesses
1. **Technical Inconsistencies and Mathematical Errors**: The paper contains multiple technical inconsistencies that undermine its credibility:
   - Equation (1) incorrectly defines `pad(x) = {max(x),mean(x),mode(x)}` - this should be a vector or list, not a set.
   - Equation (2) has inconsistent notation: `vec(x) : IRn×d → IRnd` should be `vec(x) : IRn×d → IR100` since the output is a vector of length 100.
   - The paper claims a 2.13% F1 improvement over the state-of-the-art, but the actual difference in Table 5 is 1.64% (75.40% - 73.76%).
   - The paper states "the system that reached the highest performance is the one that used the mean strategy for zero-padding and the optimizer Adam," but for the "non bootstrapping" version, the best configuration is "Pair LSTM Max Adadelta" (75.40% F1), not the mean strategy with Adam.

2. **Formatting and Presentation Issues**: The paper suffers from numerous formatting inconsistencies that detract from its professionalism:
   - Inconsistent citation styles (e.g., "Neeleman and van de Koot (2012)" vs. "Neeleman and Koot (2012)" in references).
   - Table 5 contains incorrect formatting (e.g., "69,84%" instead of "69.84%").
   - References are poorly formatted (e.g., "Kruengkrai et al\.,? \Q2017\E").
   - Table 4 contains a grammatical error: "the the number of unarguable ones."

3. **Lack of Clear Methodological Justification**: The paper fails to clearly justify why the "Stated Pair LSTM" architecture (where the second LSTM is initialized with the last state of the first LSTM) was proposed, given that the results consistently show that "Pair LSTM" (without this connection) performs better. The paper claims "our assumption about the relation between the meanings of the two input events does not hold," but this assumption was not properly established in the first place.

4. **Insufficient Analysis of Baselines**: The paper does not provide a thorough comparison with simpler baselines, such as using pre-trained embeddings with a linear classifier or simple bag-of-words approaches. The claim that "neural networks are able to encode the meaning of those events" is not adequately supported by comparison with simpler methods that might achieve similar performance.

5. **Inconsistent Claims about Corpus Handling**: The paper states "there are no differences between the training and the test set in the 'non bootstrapping' version," but Table 4 shows "Causal in train, Non Causal in test" as 0 for non bootstrapping and 27 for bootstrapping. This is confusing and contradictory, as the text suggests the bootstrapping version has differences between training and test sets, but the table shows that the non bootstrapping version actually has no differences (all zeros), while the bootstrapping version has differences.

## Questions to Authors
1. Can you clarify the discrepancy between the claimed 2.13% improvement in F1 and the actual difference of 1.64% shown in Table 5? This is a significant inconsistency that needs to be resolved.

2. Why did you choose to use "Stated Pair LSTM" as a model architecture, given that the results consistently show it underperforms compared to "Pair LSTM"? What was the theoretical motivation for this architecture?

3. The paper claims "the individual term which does not appear in any instance of the training corpus," but "which" is a word, not a term. Can you clarify what you mean by "term" and provide evidence for your claim about "which" not appearing in the training corpus?

4. For the "bootstrapping" version of the corpus, Table 5 shows that "Pair LSTM Mean Adam" achieved the best performance (81.56% F1), but the paper states "the system that reached the highest performance is the one that used the mean strategy for zero-padding and the optimizer Adam." However, "Pair LSTM Mean Adam" is not listed in Table 5. Can you explain why this configuration is not included in the table?

5. The paper mentions "the counterfactual constraint" but doesn't properly define it. In the standard definition, the counterfactual is "if the cause did not occur, the effect would not have occurred." However, the paper's example ("The argument between John's parents broke the window") doesn't fit this definition. Can you clarify your definition of the counterfactual constraint and how it differs from the standard definition?

## Overall Recommendation
**Reject**

While the paper makes a reasonable attempt at applying neural networks to causality disambiguation and provides a useful analysis of the AltLex corpus, the numerous technical inconsistencies, formatting errors, and methodological weaknesses significantly undermine the paper's credibility. The discrepancies between claimed results and actual data (e.g., the 2.13% vs. 1.64% F1 improvement) are particularly concerning for a paper at ACL. The paper would benefit from a thorough revision to correct these issues before being considered for publication. The minor flaws identified here are substantial enough to warrant rejection, as they suggest a lack of careful attention to detail that is expected in ACL publications.
