

# Review for ACL

## Summary
This paper addresses the task of causal meaning disambiguation, which involves determining whether a lexical marker (e.g., "because," "so") signals a causal relationship between two events in a sentence. The authors propose a neural network architecture that encodes the context of the two events and the lexical marker to disambiguate causal meaning. They evaluate their approach on the AltLex corpus, which contains both explicit and implicit causal relations with a variety of lexical markers. The paper argues that previous work has been limited by reliance on specific syntactic constructions or lexical resources, and their neural approach encodes contextual information to overcome these limitations.

## Main Contributions
1. A neural network architecture that encodes the context of two events and the lexical marker for causal meaning disambiguation, without relying on predefined syntactic constructions or lexical resources.
2. Demonstration that their approach outperforms previous state-of-the-art methods on the AltLex corpus, particularly for ambiguous lexical markers (e.g., "since" or "after" that can have both causal and temporal meanings).
3. Analysis showing that their model correctly disambiguates causal meaning for lexical markers with ambiguous usage (e.g., "which then"), where previous methods failed.

## Strengths

1. **Clear articulation of the problem and limitations of prior work**: The paper effectively identifies a significant gap in causal relation identification research - the reliance on specific lexical markers or syntactic constructions. The authors provide strong theoretical grounding for their approach by referencing linguistic work (Neeleman and van de Koot, 2012) that challenges syntactic approaches to causality. Their critique of previous methods (which rely on lexical resources like WordNet) is well-supported and makes a compelling case for a context-based neural approach.

2. **Thorough evaluation on a challenging corpus**: The authors carefully analyze the AltLex corpus, noting the presence of ambiguous lexical markers and the importance of context for disambiguation. Their evaluation demonstrates that their model outperforms the baseline (B1) and state-of-the-art method (B2) across multiple metrics, particularly for ambiguous markers. The detailed examples in Table 8 provide strong evidence of the model's ability to correctly disambiguate causal meaning where previous methods failed.

3. **Effective demonstration of model capabilities**: The paper provides a detailed analysis of the model's performance on ambiguous markers like "which then," which has only one causal instance in the training data. The authors correctly identify that their model's success with this marker demonstrates the value of context encoding, which is a significant contribution to the field. The comparison between their model and B2 (which relies on lexical resources) clearly shows the advantage of context-based approaches.

## Weaknesses

1. **Limited novelty of the neural architecture**: The paper proposes a neural network architecture using LSTM for context encoding, but this is a standard approach in NLP (as cited by the authors, e.g., Melamud et al., 2016). The paper does not clearly articulate how their specific architecture (with two separate LSTMs for the two events) differs significantly from prior work in neural sequence modeling. The authors claim novelty in "neural encoding of the context," but this is a common technique rather than a novel contribution.

2. **Insufficient justification for architecture choice**: The paper states that they chose LSTM over CNN because "CNN requires the definition of the kernel size," but fails to provide evidence that LSTM is particularly suited for this task compared to other sequence models. The authors don't compare their approach against more recent neural architectures (like Transformers or attention-based models), which would strengthen their claim about the suitability of their architecture.

3. **Limited evaluation scope**: The paper evaluates exclusively on the AltLex corpus, which was introduced in the same work as their baseline (Hidey and McKeown, 2016). This creates a potential evaluation bias, as the corpus was developed with the same authors who created the baseline method. The paper doesn't provide evidence that the model generalizes to other datasets or that the AltLex corpus represents the broader landscape of causal relation identification.

## Questions to Authors

1. The paper states that "the task of causality classification has not to be restricted to a syntactic construction," but it's not clear how the proposed model handles cases where the causal relation spans multiple sentences or where the two events are not immediately adjacent in the text. How would your model perform in such scenarios?

2. The paper claims that "the system that reached the highest performance is the one that used the mean strategy for zero-padding and the optimizer Adam," but doesn't provide a detailed analysis of the most common error cases. What are the most frequent types of errors made by your model, and how do they differ from the errors made by the baseline method (B2)?

3. The paper states that "the representation of causality should not be limited by syntactic patterns or the coverage of semantic resources," but doesn't provide evidence that their model generalizes better to unseen causal expressions than previous methods. Can you provide examples of causal expressions not present in the AltLex corpus that your model would correctly disambiguate?

## Overall Assessment
The paper makes a clear contribution to the field by addressing a significant limitation in causal relation identification through a context-based neural approach. The evaluation on the AltLex corpus is thorough and well-executed, with strong evidence of the model's ability to handle ambiguous lexical markers. However, the novelty of the neural architecture is limited, and the evaluation is restricted to a single corpus that was developed alongside the baseline method. While the work is technically sound and addresses an important problem, the contribution is incremental rather than transformative. The paper would be strengthened by a more thorough comparison with recent neural architectures and evidence of generalization to other datasets.
