

# Peer Review: "Neural Disambiguation of Causal Lexical Markers based on Context"

## Summary and Main Contributions

This paper addresses the task of causal meaning disambiguation, specifically determining whether a lexical marker (e.g., "because," "since") signals a causal relation between two events in context. The authors correctly identify a key limitation in prior work: existing approaches rely heavily on syntactic constructions or lexical resources (like WordNet), which restricts their coverage. Their main contribution is a neural network architecture (based on LSTMs) that encodes the contextual meaning of events and lexical markers to disambiguate causal relations, evaluated on the AltLex corpus. The paper makes two additional valuable contributions: a thorough analysis of lexical marker ambiguity in the corpus, and demonstrating that neural approaches can outperform feature-based baselines.

## Strengths

1. **Strong Motivation for Neural Approach**: The paper correctly identifies a critical limitation in prior work - the reliance on syntactic constructions and lexical resources that restrict coverage of causal expressions. The authors' argument that causality is not encoded in a fixed set of lexical/syntactic patterns (citing Neeleman & van de Koot, 2012) is well-supported and well-motivated. Their claim that neural networks can better capture the contextual nature of causality is compelling and addresses a real gap in the literature.

2. **Thorough Corpus Analysis**: The paper provides an excellent analysis of the AltLex corpus, particularly the class distribution of lexical markers (Table 4) and the problem of ambiguous markers like "since" and "after" (Table 2). This analysis is crucial for understanding the task's challenges and for future work, as it reveals that even seemingly unambiguous markers can have multiple interpretations depending on context.

3. **Significant Performance Improvement**: The experimental results demonstrate a meaningful improvement over the state-of-the-art (B2) on the AltLex corpus. The best model ("Pair LSTM Mean Adam") achieves a 2.13% higher F1 score on the Causal class and 7.72% higher Accuracy compared to B2. The ablation studies (comparing architectures, padding strategies, and optimizers) are well-designed and provide valuable insights into the model's behavior.

## Weaknesses

1. **Narrow Task Definition**: The paper focuses narrowly on disambiguating the causal meaning of lexical markers, but this is just one aspect of causality in natural language. The authors acknowledge that causality involves identifying causal relations between events (not just disambiguating markers), yet they don't address the more fundamental task of *detecting* causal relations when no explicit marker is present. This limits the paper's contribution to the broader field of causality detection.

2. **Lack of Comparison to Modern Neural Approaches**: Given that this paper is submitted to ACL, the absence of comparison to transformer-based models (e.g., BERT, RoBERTa) is a significant oversight. These models have become the standard for most NLP tasks, including relation extraction. The authors' LSTM approach, while reasonable for 2016, is now outdated for this task. The paper should at least discuss why they didn't use these more powerful models.

3. **Insufficient Discussion of Class Imbalance**: The AltLex corpus is highly imbalanced (see Table 3), which can lead to biased models. While the authors attempt to address this by creating a balanced version of the corpus (Table 6), they don't adequately discuss how their model performs on the imbalanced data (the more realistic scenario). The fact that B1 (majority class baseline) performs relatively well (e.g., 74.38% Precision on the "bootstrapping" version) suggests the class imbalance is a major factor, but this isn't properly analyzed.

4. **Weak Justification for LSTM over CNN**: The authors argue that LSTM is better than CNN for this task because causation doesn't have a fixed syntactic structure, and CNN requires knowing the kernel size. However, they don't provide evidence that kernel size is a meaningful limitation for this task. More importantly, they don't explain why they didn't use a more recent architecture like a bidirectional LSTM or transformer, which would likely outperform their proposed architecture.

5. **Limited Analysis of Failure Cases**: The paper presents some correctly classified examples (Table 8), but doesn't provide a thorough analysis of failure cases. Understanding why the model fails (e.g., for ambiguous markers like "which then") would be valuable for future work. Without this, it's hard to assess the model's limitations and how it might be improved.

## Questions to Authors

1. The paper focuses on disambiguating lexical markers, but how would your approach extend to detecting causal relations without any explicit marker (i.e., implicit causality)? Would your model need significant modification?

2. Given the availability of pre-trained language models like BERT, why didn't you compare your approach to these more recent architectures? How would you expect your model to perform against them?

3. The paper mentions that the counterfactual constraint (if the cause didn't occur, the effect wouldn't happen) is not always valid. How does your model handle cases where the causal relation is not counterfactual (e.g., "The argument between John's parents broke the window")?

## Overall Assessment

This paper makes a meaningful contribution to the specific task of causal lexical marker disambiguation, demonstrating that neural approaches can outperform previous feature-based methods. The corpus analysis is particularly strong. However, the narrow focus on disambiguating lexical markers rather than detecting causality more broadly limits the paper's significance. The lack of comparison to modern neural approaches and insufficient handling of class imbalance are significant weaknesses for a paper submitted to ACL. With revisions addressing these issues, the paper would make a strong contribution to the field. As it stands, it's a solid but somewhat incremental advance that would benefit from a broader perspective on causality detection.
