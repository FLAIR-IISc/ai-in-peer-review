- Proposes an LSTM-based neural model to disambiguate whether lexical markers (e.g., "because", "since") signal causal relations, evaluated on the AltLex corpus; includes corpus analysis and shows neural models can beat feature-based baselines.

- Strong motivation: correctly identifies limitations of prior syntactic/lexical-resource approaches and argues neural methods better capture contextual causality.

- Thorough corpus analysis: detailed examination of marker class distributions and ambiguity (e.g., "since", "after") that highlights task challenges.

- Empirical gains: achieves meaningful improvements over prior state-of-the-art (2.13% F1 on Causal, 7.72% accuracy) with well-designed ablation studies.

- Narrow task focus: only handles disambiguating explicit lexical markers and does not address detecting implicit causal relations without markers.

- Missing modern baselines: no comparison to transformer-based models (BERT/RoBERTa), which are now standard for relation tasks.

- Insufficient treatment of class imbalance: limited analysis of realistic (imbalanced) corpus performance despite creating a balanced variant; majority baseline performance is worrisome.

- Weak architectural justification: argument for LSTM over CNN is not well supported and authors do not consider bidirectional LSTMs or transformers.

- Limited error analysis: few failure-case analyses are provided, reducing insight into model weaknesses (especially for ambiguous markers).

- Question: How would the approach extend to detecting implicit causal relations when no explicit marker is present?

- Question: Why were pre-trained transformer models not used or compared to, and how would the authors expect their model to fare against them?

- Question: How does the model handle non-counterfactual causal statements (cases where counterfactual constraint doesn't hold)?

- Overall: A meaningful, well-analyzed contribution to lexical-marker disambiguation that is nonetheless narrow and would be stronger with broader causality coverage, transformer comparisons, and better handling of class imbalance.
