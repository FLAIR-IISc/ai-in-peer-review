

# Review of "Neural Disambiguation of Causal Lexical Markers based on Context"

## Summary and Main Contributions
The paper addresses the task of causal meaning disambiguation, which involves determining whether a relation between two events (cause and effect) is causal or non-causal. The authors argue that causality is not limited to specific syntactic constructions or lexical markers but depends on contextual interpretation. They propose a neural network architecture using LSTMs to encode the context of two events (cause and effect) for disambiguation, evaluated on the AltLex corpus.

**Main Contributions (in order of strength):**
1. **A neural approach to causal meaning disambiguation that avoids reliance on syntactic features or lexical resources:** The authors correctly identify a limitation in existing work that depends on predefined linguistic features and propose a context-based neural solution.
2. **Empirical validation on the AltLex corpus showing improved performance:** The authors demonstrate that their neural model outperforms previous baselines, particularly in handling ambiguous causal markers.
3. **Analysis of how the model handles ambiguous linguistic constructions:** The paper provides detailed examples showing the model's ability to correctly disambiguate causal meaning for markers like "break" and "which then" where previous methods fail.

## Strengths
1. **Clear identification of the research gap:** The paper effectively argues that existing approaches to causal relation identification are limited by their reliance on syntactic constructions or lexical resources (e.g., WordNet, PropBank). The authors correctly point out that causality is not fixed to specific linguistic forms but depends on context, which is well-supported by the cited literature (Neeleman and van de Koot, 2012; Hidey and McKeown, 2016). This is a strong theoretical foundation for their work.

2. **Relevant and comprehensive evaluation:** The authors use the AltLex corpus, which is the most comprehensive resource for causal disambiguation according to their analysis. The evaluation is thorough, with careful analysis of ambiguous cases (e.g., "break" and "which then") where their model outperforms previous baselines. The paper demonstrates that their approach can correctly disambiguate causal meaning even when lexical markers are ambiguous or have predominantly non-causal usage in the training data.

3. **Strong empirical results:** The results clearly show that the proposed neural approach outperforms both baselines (B1 and B2) on the AltLex corpus. The best configuration ("Pair LSTM Mean Adam") achieves 81.96% F1 in the Causal class on the bootstrapping version of the corpus, which is a meaningful improvement over the previous state-of-the-art (80.90% F1). The paper also provides a thoughtful analysis of precision-recall tradeoffs, showing that their model achieves a better balance than previous approaches.

## Weaknesses
1. **Lack of clear task definition and limited novelty of the neural architecture:** The paper does not sufficiently distinguish the proposed "causal meaning disambiguation" task from related tasks like causal relation identification or causal argument identification. The task is presented as a binary classification problem (causal vs. non-causal), but it's unclear how this fits into the broader landscape of causal reasoning in NLP. More critically, the proposed "Pair LSTM" architecture is a straightforward application of existing techniques (LSTMs with concatenation and dense layers) rather than a novel neural architecture. The paper doesn't justify why LSTM was chosen over more recent transformer-based approaches, which would be the natural baseline for this kind of task today.

2. **Inadequate comparison with current state-of-the-art:** The paper claims to outperform the "state-of-the-art" but only compares against two baselines (B1 and B2), where B2 is the system from the AltLex corpus paper itself (Hidey and McKeown, 2016). This is not a strong comparison to the current state-of-the-art. The authors don't compare against more recent neural approaches to causal relation detection, such as those using transformer-based models or pre-trained language models (e.g., BERT, RoBERTa), which would be the natural comparison for a 2023 submission. Without this comparison, it's difficult to assess the true novelty and significance of their contribution.

3. **Limited discussion of practical implications and broader impact:** While the paper demonstrates improved performance on a specific corpus, it doesn't discuss how this work would be integrated into larger causal reasoning pipelines or its potential applications. The paper mentions future work on creating a new corpus for "causality disambiguation and causality argument classification," but it doesn't explain why this is needed when the AltLex corpus is already available. The paper also doesn't address the computational cost of their approach compared to simpler baselines.

## Questions to Authors
1. How does the proposed task of "causal meaning disambiguation" specifically differ from causal relation identification or causal argument identification? What is the precise definition of the task that distinguishes it from related work in the literature?

2. Why was LSTM chosen over more recent transformer-based architectures (e.g., BERT) for this task? Would a pre-trained language model fine-tuned on this task provide better performance than the proposed Pair LSTM? If so, why wasn't this comparison included?

3. The paper claims to outperform the state-of-the-art but only compares against two baselines, one of which is the system from the corpus paper itself. What are the most relevant recent approaches to causal relation identification that should be compared against, and why weren't they included?

## Additional Comments
The paper would benefit from a clearer definition of the task and a more thorough comparison with current neural approaches to causal relation detection. The authors should also provide a direct link to the AltLex corpus for reproducibility. The writing is generally clear but could be improved by more precise definitions of key terms like "causal meaning disambiguation" and "lexical markers."

## Recommendation
I recommend **weak rejection**. The paper makes a valid point about the limitations of existing approaches to causal disambiguation and demonstrates that a neural context-based approach can improve performance on the AltLex corpus. However, the contribution lacks sufficient novelty (the neural architecture is straightforward application of existing techniques), the comparison with state-of-the-art is inadequate, and the task definition is not clearly distinguished from related work. The authors could strengthen the paper significantly by comparing against more recent neural approaches and providing a clearer task definition.

If the authors address the weaknesses identified above (particularly by comparing against more recent neural approaches and clarifying the task definition), this could become a strong contribution to the field.
