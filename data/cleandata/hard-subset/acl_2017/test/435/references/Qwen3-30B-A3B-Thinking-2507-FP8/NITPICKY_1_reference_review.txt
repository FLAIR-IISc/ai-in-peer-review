

# ACL Review: "Neural Disambiguation of Causal Lexical Markers based on Context"

## Summary of the Paper
The paper proposes a neural network architecture for causality disambiguation, focusing on identifying whether a relation between two events is causal or non-causal. The authors argue that causality should not be limited by syntactic patterns or lexical resources, and they propose using LSTMs to encode context for disambiguation. They evaluate their approach on the AltLex corpus introduced by Hidey and McKeown (2016), claiming improvements over previous baselines.

## Main Contributions (as I see them)
1. Application of LSTM-based neural networks to causality disambiguation (though not particularly novel)
2. Evaluation on the AltLex corpus, which captures a wider range of causal expressions
3. Analysis of ambiguous lexical markers in causal relations

## Strengths

1. **Appropriate Corpus Selection**: The AltLex corpus is indeed a valuable resource that captures causal expressions beyond standard lexical markers. Using it for evaluation is appropriate and shows the potential of their approach on a more challenging dataset than standard corpora like PDTB.

2. **Clear Evaluation Framework**: The paper provides a thorough comparison against two well-defined baselines (B1 and B2), and the results show consistent improvements over previous work. The analysis of different architectures and hyperparameters is well-presented.

3. **Insightful Analysis of Ambiguous Cases**: The paper correctly identifies that some lexical markers (like "since" and "after") can have both causal and temporal interpretations, and they provide some examples where their model successfully disambiguates these cases.

## Weaknesses

1. **Lack of Novelty in Approach**: The paper's main contribution is applying a standard LSTM architecture to a new problem, not proposing any novel neural network architecture or method. The justification for using LSTM over CNN ("LSTM has a higher capacity of encoding meaning for the task of causality classification") is weak and unsupported by evidence. The paper doesn't demonstrate that LSTM is inherently better for this specific task than other methods. This is a significant weakness for an ACL paper, which typically requires substantial methodological innovation.

2. **Inconsistent and Poorly Defined Task**: The paper claims to focus on "causal meaning classification" (a binary classification task), but it also mentions "causal argument identification" as a separate task without evaluating it. The definition of the task is inconsistent throughout the paper (e.g., "causal meaning disambiguation" vs. "causal relation classification"). The paper also fails to clearly define what constitutes a "causal meaning" or how it differs from other semantic relations. This lack of clarity undermines the paper's contribution.

3. **Methodological Inconsistencies and Missing Details**: The paper lacks critical methodological details needed for reproducibility, including:
   - Exact model architecture (number of LSTM layers, hidden units)
   - Training details (batch size, number of epochs)
   - Data split details (how training, validation, and test sets were created)
   - No ablation studies to show the contribution of different components
   - The paper states "the system architecture 'Pair LSTM' tends to reach better results than the architecture 'Stated LSTM,'" but the naming is inconsistent (previously "Stated Pair LSTM" was used), creating confusion.

4. **Inconsistent Results and Claims**: The paper makes a specific claim about performance improvement ("improves the state of the art by 2.13% according to F1 in the Causal class"), but the numbers don't add up. For the "non bootstrapping" version, B2 has F1 73.76% and the best configuration (Pair LSTM Max Adadelta) has F1 75.40%, which is a 1.64% improvement, not 2.13%. This discrepancy suggests a lack of attention to detail.

5. **Formatting and Presentation Issues**: The paper contains numerous formatting inconsistencies that undermine its credibility:
   - Inconsistent use of "AltLex" (sometimes "AltLex," sometimes "AltLex")
   - Inconsistent capitalization of "Causal" vs "causal"
   - In Table 5, precision and recall values are formatted with commas instead of decimal points in some places ("69,84%")
   - Inconsistent citation formats (e.g., "Neeleman and van de Koot (2012)" vs. "Neeleman and Koot (2012)")
   - The paper mentions Figure 1 but doesn't include it
   - The paper uses "stated pair LSTM" and "stated LSTM" interchangeably, creating confusion

## Questions to Authors

1. The paper claims that "the representation of causality should not be limited by syntactic patterns or the coverage of semantic resources, due to the lack of a particular linguistic construction for causality." What evidence do you provide to support this claim? The paper doesn't provide any linguistic evidence for the absence of a particular linguistic construction for causality.

2. The paper states "the system architecture 'Pair LSTM' tends to reach better results than the architecture 'Stated LSTM'." However, the paper previously used "Stated Pair LSTM" for the connected LSTM architecture. Can you clarify the naming convention and the architectural difference between these two approaches?

3. The paper claims a 2.13% improvement in F1, but the numbers show only 1.64%. Please clarify this discrepancy and provide the correct calculation.

4. The paper uses "the" before "AltLex corpus" without defining what AltLex is. What does "AltLex" stand for, and how is it different from the standard causal lexical markers in PDTB?

5. The paper mentions "the counterfactual constraint" but doesn't clearly define it. What is the exact definition of this constraint in the context of your task, and why did you decide not to include it in your definition of causality?

## Final Recommendation

**Reject**

While the paper addresses a meaningful problem in computational linguistics and uses a relevant corpus, the lack of methodological novelty, inconsistent task definition, and numerous methodological and formatting inconsistencies are substantial weaknesses that outweigh the paper's modest empirical results. The paper fails to provide sufficient evidence that its approach is genuinely novel or superior to existing methods beyond a small performance improvement. The numerous inconsistencies and errors in presentation suggest a lack of attention to detail, which is particularly concerning for an ACL submission. For these reasons, I recommend rejection.
