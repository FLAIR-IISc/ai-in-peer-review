- Paper proposes a two-input LSTM binary classifier to disambiguate causal vs. non-causal uses of lexical markers based on context.  
- Demonstrates that context is crucial for disambiguating ambiguous causal markers (e.g., "since").  
- Clearly defines the task as causal meaning disambiguation, distinct from causal argument identification.  

- Strong empirical validation showing context-sensitive cases where the neural model outperforms prior methods.  
- Clear task motivation and critique of approaches relying on fixed syntactic constructions or lexical resources.  
- Thorough corpus analysis of the AltLex dataset, including class distribution, ambiguous markers, and bootstrapping effects.  
- Reasonable choice and justification for LSTMs over CNNs given lack of fixed syntactic structure.  

- Methodological novelty is limited: model is a straightforward use of two LSTMs with dense layers, lacking new neural contributions.  
- Evaluation is limited to the relatively small AltLex corpus (8–9k examples), raising generalizability concerns.  
- Inadequate comparison to recent neural methods (e.g., transformer-based or advanced contextual encoders).  
- Binary causal vs. non-causal labeling oversimplifies nuanced types of causality (direct/indirect, necessary/sufficient, probabilistic).  
- Lacks deeper theoretical grounding tying model design to linguistic or psychological theories of causality.  

- Overall: empirical evidence for context importance is solid, but limited novelty and evaluation weaken the paper’s contribution for ACL.  

- Q1: How does your approach compare to recent transformer-based neural methods for causal relation extraction?  
- Q2: Why not incorporate broader context (full sentence or surrounding sentences) into the model for disambiguation?  
- Q3: How will you extend the approach to handle different types of causal relations beyond binary classification?  

- Recommendation: Reject due to insufficient methodological novelty and limited, single-dataset evaluation.
