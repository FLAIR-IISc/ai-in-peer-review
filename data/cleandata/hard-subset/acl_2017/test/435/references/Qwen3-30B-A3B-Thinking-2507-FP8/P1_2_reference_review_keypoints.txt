- Proposes an LSTM-based neural approach to disambiguate whether a lexical marker signals a causal relation by encoding context around the marker.  
- Evaluates on the AltLex corpus to capture a wider variety of causal expressions than prior resources.

- Contribution: Introduces a Pair LSTM architecture for encoding two events and the connecting lexical marker for causal disambiguation.  
- Contribution: Achieves better balance of precision and recall and outperforms prior state-of-the-art (B2) on AltLex.  
- Contribution: Uses and highlights the AltLex corpus, addressing scarcity and broader coverage of causal expressions.

- Strength: Strong motivation arguing causality lacks fixed syntactic structure and prior syntactic/lexical approaches are limited.  
- Strength: Thorough evaluation on AltLex with clear performance improvements and detailed per-connective analysis.  
- Strength: Demonstrates contextual understanding by correctly disambiguating ambiguous markers (e.g., "which then").  
- Strength: Provides practical implementation details (padding, optimizers, regularization) aiding reproducibility.  
- Strength: Models achieve balanced precision and recall (best config: Pair LSTM Mean Adam with 72.65% P, 88.57% R).

- Weakness: Limited novelty—LSTM application and architecture variants are incremental compared to prior neural work (e.g., Melamud et al.).  
- Weakness: No comparison to modern neural architectures (transformers, BERT), limiting assessment of relative contribution.  
- Weakness: Misleading claim of not relying on lexical resources despite using GloVe embeddings.  
- Weakness: Insufficient analysis of failure cases to understand model limitations.  
- Weakness: Lacks discussion on generalization to out-of-domain text or different linguistic contexts.

- Question: How does the model handle causal relations that span multiple sentences?  
- Question: How did differing class distributions in the bootstrapped AltLex affect performance and what mitigation was used?  
- Question: Clarify the distinction between using pre-trained embeddings (GloVe) and reliance on lexical resources in prior work.  
- Question: How would the approach be extended to the broader task of causal argument identification?

- Overall recommendation: Accept — meaningful contribution with strong evaluation and contextual understanding despite limited architectural novelty.
