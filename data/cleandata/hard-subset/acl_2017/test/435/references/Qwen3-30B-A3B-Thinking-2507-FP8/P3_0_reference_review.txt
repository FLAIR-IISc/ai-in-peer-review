

# Peer Review: Neural Disambiguation of Causal Lexical Markers based on Context

## Summary and Main Contributions

This paper proposes a neural network approach to disambiguate causal meaning in linguistic expressions, arguing that causal relations are not limited to specific syntactic constructions or lexical markers but depend on contextual information. The authors define a binary classification task (causal vs. non-causal) and propose a two-input LSTM architecture that encodes the causing event and the lexical marker plus caused event. They evaluate their approach on the AltLex corpus, claiming it outperforms previous baselines.

My assessment of the paper's main contributions (in order of significance):
1. **Demonstration of context's importance for causal disambiguation**: The paper provides compelling empirical evidence that context is crucial for handling ambiguous lexical markers (e.g., "since" as temporal vs. causal).
2. **Clear task definition**: The paper effectively distinguishes causal meaning disambiguation from causal argument identification, clarifying the scope of the task.
3. **Empirical validation on a relevant corpus**: The AltLex corpus is well-suited for this task, and the paper provides thorough evaluation on it.

## Strengths

1. **Strong empirical validation of context importance**: The paper convincingly demonstrates that context is necessary for disambiguating causal meaning, particularly for ambiguous markers like "which then" and "break." The analysis in Table 8 shows specific cases where the neural approach correctly handles ambiguity while previous methods fail, providing strong evidence for the authors' claim.

2. **Clear task definition and motivation**: The paper effectively establishes the problem by critiquing previous approaches that rely on fixed syntactic constructions or lexical resources. The distinction between causal meaning disambiguation and argument identification is particularly valuable for the field.

3. **Careful corpus analysis**: The authors provide thorough analysis of the AltLex corpus, including class distribution, ambiguous markers, and the impact of bootstrapping. This demonstrates a deep understanding of the data challenges in this domain.

4. **Appropriate neural architecture choice**: The justification for using LSTMs over CNNs (due to the lack of fixed syntactic structure for causality) is well-reasoned and aligns with the problem statement.

## Weaknesses

1. **Lack of methodological novelty**: The core neural architecture (two LSTMs with dense layers) is a straightforward application of established techniques rather than a novel contribution. The paper doesn't introduce any new neural network components, training techniques, or architectural innovations. For ACL, methodological novelty is typically expected, and this paper falls short in this regard.

2. **Limited evaluation scope**: The paper only evaluates on the AltLex corpus, which is relatively small (8k-9k examples). Without testing on additional datasets or larger corpora, the generalizability claim is unsupported. The authors acknowledge the lack of larger resources, but this limitation isn't adequately addressed.

3. **Inadequate comparison with recent neural approaches**: The paper compares only to the SVM-based system from Hidey and McKeown (2016), which is outdated. It fails to compare with more recent neural approaches to causal relation extraction (e.g., using transformers or more sophisticated contextual encoders), which would better establish the significance of their contribution.

4. **Oversimplification of causality**: The binary classification (causal vs. non-causal) fails to capture the nuanced nature of causal relations in language. Causality can be direct, indirect, necessary, sufficient, or probabilistic, and the paper doesn't address how their approach might handle these distinctions.

5. **Lack of theoretical grounding**: While the paper cites Neeleman and van de Koot (2012) about the limitations of syntactic approaches, it doesn't deeply integrate these insights into the model design. The authors could have designed their model to better align with psychological theories of causality rather than just using a standard neural architecture.

## Overall Assessment

The paper makes a valid point about the importance of context for causal disambiguation, and the empirical results are promising. However, the lack of methodological novelty and limited evaluation scope significantly weaken the contribution. For an ACL submission, the paper should have either introduced a novel neural architecture or demonstrated generalizability across multiple datasets and tasks.

The authors' claim that neural networks "are able to encode the meaning of those events, and discover whether the underlying relation between them is causal" is supported by their results but lacks the methodological innovation expected at ACL. The paper would have been stronger if it had proposed a genuinely novel architecture or addressed the limitations of the binary classification approach.

## Questions to Authors

1. How does your approach compare to recent neural methods for causal relation extraction (e.g., those using transformer-based architectures)?

2. Why didn't you incorporate broader context (e.g., the full sentence or surrounding sentences) into your model, which might be crucial for disambiguation?

3. How do you plan to extend your approach to handle different types of causal relations (e.g., direct vs. indirect causation) rather than just binary classification?

## Recommendation

I recommend rejection. While the paper makes a reasonable empirical contribution to the field, it lacks the methodological novelty and comprehensive evaluation expected for ACL. The authors' claim that neural networks "are able to encode the meaning of those events" is well-supported, but this is not a significant enough contribution for ACL without a novel approach or broader validation.
