

# Review of "Neural Disambiguation of Causal Lexical Markers based on Context"

## Summary
This paper proposes a neural network approach for disambiguating causal lexical markers (e.g., "because," "so," "after") in natural language texts. The authors claim their method can determine whether a lexical marker indicates a causal relationship between two events, and they evaluate it on the AltLex corpus. They compare their approach to two baselines: a simple heuristic based on the most frequent class for each lexical marker (B1), and the original method from Hidey and McKeown (2016) (B2).

## Main Contributions (as I see them)
1. Application of LSTM-based neural networks to causal lexical marker disambiguation
2. Empirical demonstration that their approach outperforms the original AltLex corpus creators' method on their corpus
3. Analysis of zero-padding strategies for handling variable-length input sequences

## Strengths

1. **Appropriate corpus selection**: The authors use the AltLex corpus, which is the most comprehensive resource for this specific task, and they properly acknowledge its limitations and characteristics.

2. **Thorough baseline comparison**: The paper includes two meaningful baselines (B1 and B2) that provide a reasonable comparison point for the proposed method, with B2 representing the state of the art at the time of the corpus release.

3. **Good empirical results**: The authors demonstrate consistent improvements over baselines, particularly in Precision (a critical metric for causal disambiguation), with the best configuration outperforming B2 by 2.13% F1 in the Causal class on the non-bootstrapping corpus.

## Weaknesses

1. **Lack of strong justification for neural approach**: The authors claim LSTMs are superior to CNNs because "causation does not have a particular syntactic structure," but provide no evidence for this claim. They don't compare with other neural architectures (e.g., simple RNNs, transformers) or provide ablation studies to demonstrate why their specific architecture is better. This is a classic case of proposing a new neural architecture without sufficient justification for why it should outperform simpler alternatives.

2. **Inadequate baseline comparison**: The baselines are too weak to support the authors' claims of "outperforming the state of the art." B1 is a trivial heuristic, and B2 is the original method from the corpus creators without any explanation of why it should be considered the current state-of-the-art. The authors don't compare with more recent neural methods for similar tasks that would have been available at the time of writing (2017), such as early transformer-based approaches.

3. **Overstated novelty and claims**: The paper claims "neural networks are able to encode the meaning of those events, and discover whether the underlying relation between them is causal" as if this were a novel discovery. However, this is a standard application of neural networks to text classification, not a meaningful innovation. The authors fail to demonstrate why their specific approach to encoding context is substantially different from prior work or why it should be expected to work better.

4. **Insufficient analysis of ambiguous cases**: The paper mentions lexical markers like "since" and "after" that can have both causal and temporal meanings, but provides minimal analysis of how the model handles these ambiguities. The examples in Table 8 are limited and don't cover the full spectrum of challenges. A proper analysis of error cases would be essential to understand the limitations of the approach.

5. **Lack of connection to broader NLP applications**: The paper doesn't explain why this specific disambiguation task is important for natural language processing or how it would be used in downstream applications. Without this context, it's difficult to assess the practical significance of the work.

## Questions to Authors

1. The paper claims that "causation does not have a particular syntactic structure" as justification for using LSTMs instead of CNNs, but provides no evidence for this claim. Can you provide evidence from the linguistic literature or from your own analysis of the data that supports this assertion?

2. The paper doesn't compare with more recent neural approaches that would have been available around 2017 (e.g., early transformer models). Why didn't you compare your method with these approaches, which would have provided a more meaningful baseline?

3. The authors state that "neural networks are able to encode the meaning of those events," but this is a standard capability of neural networks for text classification. Can you clarify what specific innovation your method brings beyond the standard application of neural networks to this task?

4. The paper shows that the "Pair LSTM Mean Adam" configuration performs best, but doesn't provide sufficient explanation for why this particular configuration works better. What specific characteristics of the causal relationships are being captured by this particular architecture and hyperparameter combination?

5. The paper doesn't address how the results would change if the model were trained on a larger, more diverse corpus. Given that the AltLex corpus is relatively small (21,354 instances in the bootstrapping version), how would your model perform with more training data?

## Overall Recommendation

I am unable to recommend acceptance of this paper. While the paper presents some empirical results that show modest improvements over baselines, the methodological weaknesses are significant. The authors fail to provide sufficient justification for their neural approach, compare against meaningful baselines, or demonstrate any meaningful innovation beyond the standard application of neural networks to a text classification task. The claims of novelty and significance are overstated, and the paper lacks the methodological rigor expected for ACL.

The paper would need to substantially revise its claims, provide a more rigorous comparison with stronger baselines (including more recent neural methods), and provide a deeper analysis of the linguistic phenomena they're addressing before it could be considered for publication.
