

# Peer Review: "Neural Disambiguation of Causal Lexical Markers based on Context"

## Summary and Main Contributions

This paper addresses causal meaning disambiguation—the task of determining whether a relation between two events is causal or non-causal. The authors correctly identify a key limitation in previous approaches: most prior work relies on syntactic constructions or lexical resources with limited coverage, which fails to capture the context-dependent nature of causal expressions. The main contributions are:

1. A neural network architecture (based on LSTM) that encodes contextual information for causal meaning disambiguation, rather than relying on predefined syntactic or lexical features.
2. A thorough evaluation showing that their approach outperforms previous state-of-the-art methods on the AltLex corpus, particularly for ambiguous causal markers like "since" and "after."
3. A detailed analysis of how the model handles ambiguous expressions that cannot be resolved by simple lexical or syntactic rules.

## Strengths

1. **Strong identification of a real limitation in the field:** The paper effectively argues that previous causal classification approaches are constrained by their reliance on specific syntactic constructions or lexical resources, which fails to capture the context-dependent nature of causal expressions. This is a well-supported claim based on linguistic literature (e.g., Neeleman and van de Koot, 2012) and the limitations of prior work.

2. **Compelling empirical results on a challenging dataset:** The AltLex corpus is particularly relevant because it contains causal expressions beyond standard markers like "because." The authors' demonstration that their model outperforms previous baselines (by 2.13% F1 on the "non bootstrapping" version) is convincing. The detailed analysis of ambiguous markers (like "which then" in Table 8) shows the model's ability to handle cases that previous methods fail at.

3. **Clear experimental design and ablation studies:** The paper carefully evaluates different neural architectures (Stated Pair LSTM vs. Pair LSTM), zero-padding strategies, and optimization methods. The comparison to baselines B1 (class distribution) and B2 (Hidey and McKeown, 2016) is appropriate, and the analysis of precision/recall trade-offs provides valuable insights into the model's behavior.

## Weaknesses

1. **Lack of comparison to recent neural approaches:** The paper compares to Hidey and McKeown (2016) but fails to compare to more recent neural approaches to causality classification. Given the rapid advances in contextual embeddings and transformer models, the authors should have compared their LSTM-based approach to modern alternatives like BERT or RoBERTa, which have become standard in NLP. This omission significantly weakens the paper's claim of being state-of-the-art.

2. **Superficial analysis of failure cases:** While the paper provides examples of correctly classified ambiguous cases, it lacks a detailed analysis of where the model fails. Understanding failure modes is crucial for assessing the limitations of the approach and guiding future work. The paper mentions "some instances" in the bootstrapping corpus that are harder for the model, but doesn't provide examples or analyze why these are challenging.

3. **Limited engagement with the linguistic theory:** The paper references linguistic theories of causality (e.g., Neeleman and van de Koot, 2012) but doesn't deeply connect their computational approach to these theories. For example, how does the model's contextual encoding align with the human mental model of causality that the paper references? This theoretical grounding is underdeveloped.

4. **Insufficient discussion of corpus limitations:** The paper assumes the AltLex corpus is the best available resource but doesn't critically analyze its limitations. The bootstrapping method used to create the corpus may introduce biases, and the significant class imbalance (with many more non-causal examples) could affect the model's performance. The authors should have discussed how these factors might influence their results.

## Questions to Authors

1. Have you considered using contextual embeddings (e.g., BERT) instead of GloVe? Given that the paper's main claim is about contextual encoding, it would be valuable to see how a more advanced contextual embedding approach compares to your LSTM-based method.

2. Can you provide more details about the bootstrapping process used to create the AltLex corpus? How might this process introduce biases that affect the model's performance?

3. The paper mentions that the bootstrapping version has instances where the class distribution differs between training and test sets. Could you provide specific examples of these challenging cases and explain why they are difficult for the model?

## Overall Recommendation

This paper makes a valuable contribution by highlighting the context-dependent nature of causal expressions and proposing a neural approach that successfully handles ambiguous causal markers. The empirical results are strong and well-presented. However, the lack of comparison to recent neural approaches and the superficial engagement with linguistic theory are significant weaknesses that prevent this paper from being a truly impactful contribution to the field. With revisions addressing these issues, the paper could be strengthened considerably.

**Overall Rating: Weak Accept** (The paper has merit but requires significant revisions to address the weaknesses noted above, particularly the comparison to recent neural approaches and deeper engagement with linguistic theory.)
