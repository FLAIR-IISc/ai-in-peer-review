## Review of "Neural Disambiguation of Causal Lexical Markers based on Context"

This paper addresses the task of causal relation classification, arguing that encoding sentence meaning via neural networks is crucial for disambiguating causal interpretations, particularly when explicit causal markers are absent or ambiguous. The authors propose a neural network architecture utilizing LSTMs and evaluate it on the AltLex corpus, demonstrating performance improvements over a baseline SVM system.

The primary contribution of this work lies in the application of LSTMs to the specific problem of causal relation disambiguation, moving beyond feature engineering-heavy approaches. While the idea of using neural networks for this task isn’t entirely novel, the focus on contextual encoding and the empirical validation on the AltLex corpus, which specifically targets ambiguous causal expressions, is valuable. A secondary contribution is the thorough experimentation with different LSTM configurations (stated vs. paired) and optimization strategies (Adadelta vs. Adam), providing insights into the optimal setup for this task. 

The strengths of the paper are its clear problem definition, comprehensive related work review, and rigorous experimental evaluation. The authors demonstrate a good understanding of the challenges in causality classification, particularly the limitations of relying solely on lexical cues. The ablation studies with different LSTM architectures and optimizers are well-executed and provide valuable insights. The inclusion of a comparison against a strong baseline (Hidey & McKeown, 2016) further strengthens the validity of the results. The analysis of misclassified examples, highlighting the difficulties with ambiguous connectives, is also a positive aspect.

However, the paper suffers from several weaknesses. The novelty of the proposed approach is incremental; applying LSTMs to text classification is well-established. The paper doesn’t sufficiently motivate *why* LSTMs are particularly well-suited for this task beyond a general statement about encoding sequential information. Furthermore, the improvements over the baseline are modest, and the paper lacks a deeper analysis of *what* the LSTM is learning to disambiguate causality. The discussion of the counterfactual constraint feels somewhat tangential and could be streamlined. Finally, the presentation could benefit from more visual aids, such as diagrams illustrating the LSTM architecture and data flow.

**Questions to Authors:**

1.  Could you elaborate on *why* you believe LSTMs are superior to other neural network architectures (e.g., CNNs) for capturing the nuances of causal relations in text?
2.  What specific linguistic features or patterns do you believe the LSTM is learning to differentiate between causal and non-causal relations? Can you provide examples from the learned weights or hidden states?
3.  The paper mentions the difficulty with ambiguous connectives. Could you provide a more detailed error analysis focusing on these cases, perhaps identifying specific types of ambiguity that the model struggles with?
