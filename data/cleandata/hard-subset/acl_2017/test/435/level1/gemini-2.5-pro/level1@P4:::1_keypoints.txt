- Task and claim: paper studies causal meaning disambiguation and claims an end-to-end neural model can disambiguate causality from event/context without hand-crafted features.
- Model and dataset: proposes a dual-encoder LSTM that encodes two event spans and achieves state-of-the-art on the AltLex corpus.
- Contribution 1: shows a feature-light dual-encoder LSTM is effective for causal relation classification.
- Contribution 2: reports strong empirical gains over a feature-based SVM baseline, improving F1 for causal class and overall accuracy.
- Contribution 3: provides analysis suggesting broader semantic context matters more than lexical/syntactic markers for causality disambiguation.
- Strength 1: strong empirical performance with consistent, significant improvements over baselines including prior SOTA.
- Strength 2: method is simple and general, relying only on pre-trained embeddings rather than heavy linguistic resources.
- Strength 3: paper is clearly written with reproducible experimental details (architecture, hyperparameters, data splits).
- Strength 4: qualitative analysis gives concrete examples where the model leverages context beyond lexical cues.
- Weakness 1: limited architectural novelty—dual-encoder LSTM is a standard design and model variation exploration is minimal.
- Weakness 2: ambiguity in input representation—unclear whether LSTMs receive only short event spans or surrounding sentence context.
- Weakness 3: insufficient justification for choosing LSTMs over CNNs; no empirical CNN comparison and arguments are underdeveloped.
- Weakness 4: simplified task formulation assumes pre-identified event spans and markers, limiting real-world applicability and analysis of marker vs. event contributions.
- Question 1: clarify exactly what text is input to each LSTM—event spans only or event plus surrounding context, and how any window is defined.
- Question 2: have you tried alternative ways to incorporate the lexical marker (separate channel, concatenation with outputs) to isolate its contribution?
