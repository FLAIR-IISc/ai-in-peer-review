**Summary and Contributions**

This paper addresses the task of causal meaning disambiguation, which it frames as a binary classification problem: given two text spans (events e1 and e2) linked by a potential causal lexical marker (l), determine if the relationship is causal. The authors hypothesize that a deep learning model can encode the necessary contextual meaning to perform this disambiguation without relying on hand-crafted linguistic features. They propose a dual-input LSTM architecture that processes the two events, concatenates their representations, and feeds them through dense layers for classification. The model is evaluated on the AltLex corpus, demonstrating superior performance over a strong, feature-engineered SVM-based system, which is considered the state-of-the-art on this dataset.

In my view, the main contributions of this work are:

1.  **A strong empirical result demonstrating the effectiveness of a neural, feature-free approach for causal marker disambiguation.** The primary contribution is showing that a relatively standard LSTM architecture, using only pre-trained word embeddings, can outperform a complex, feature-engineered system on a challenging and relevant task. This aligns with the broader trend in NLP at the time, validating the power of learned representations over manual feature design for semantic tasks.
2.  **Establishment of a new, strong baseline for the AltLex corpus.** The paper provides a thorough experimental evaluation of different neural configurations (e.g., padding strategies, optimizers, architectural variants) on the AltLex dataset. The results and analysis serve as a valuable benchmark for future research on this specific task and dataset.

**Strengths**

1.  **Significant Performance Improvement over State-of-the-Art:** The proposed model achieves clear and consistent improvements over the previous state-of-the-art system of Hidey and McKeown (2016). On the "non bootstrapping" version of the AltLex corpus, the best model configuration improves F1-score by 2.13% and accuracy by a substantial 7.72%. Similar gains are observed on the more challenging "bootstrapping" version. This strong empirical result is a compelling argument for the method's effectiveness and the paper's acceptance.
2.  **Methodological Simplicity and Generality:** A major strength of the proposed approach is its independence from complex feature engineering and external linguistic resources (e.g., WordNet, PropBank, syntactic parsers), which were central to most prior work in this area. By relying solely on pre-trained word embeddings and a standard neural architecture, the model is simpler, more generalizable, and less brittle. This is a significant advantage, as it avoids the domain/resource dependency and development overhead associated with feature-based systems.
3.  **Thorough Experimentation and Analysis:** The authors conduct a solid experimental study. They compare their model against two sensible baselines: a majority-class heuristic (B1) and the prior SOTA (B2). They systematically explore variations in their model, including two LSTM architectures ("Pair" vs. "Stated Pair"), different padding strategies, and optimizers, providing a clear picture of what works. Furthermore, the qualitative analysis in Section 4.3, with concrete examples where their model succeeds and the baseline fails, provides valuable insight into the model's ability to handle lexical ambiguity (e.g., with the verb "break" or the connective "which then"). The additional experiment on a class-balanced version of the dataset further strengthens the empirical claims.

**Weaknesses**

1.  **Limited Architectural Novelty:** The main weakness of the paper is the lack of novelty in the proposed neural architecture. A dual-input LSTM model where representations are concatenated and passed to a classifier was a well-established and standard approach for sentence-pair classification tasks (e.g., NLI, paraphrase detection) by 2017. While its application to causal disambiguation is new and effective, the architecture itself does not represent a significant technical or methodological advance. The contribution is therefore more in the application and empirical result than in novel model design.
2.  **Motivation and Justification of Model Choices:** The paper introduces two architectural variants: "Stated Pair LSTM" and "Pair LSTM". The former is motivated by the idea that the events are sequentially related, yet the latter, simpler model without the state connection performs better in most cases. The paper notes this outcome but does not provide a deep analysis or compelling hypothesis for why the more direct modeling of a sequential dependency fails to help. This makes the architectural exploration feel somewhat post-hoc rather than driven by a clear, validated hypothesis about the nature of the task.
3.  **Mismatch between Ambitious Framing and Narrow Task Definition:** The introduction frames the problem within the broad and challenging context of human causal reasoning and linguistic theories of causation. However, the actual task tackled is a much narrower, supervised classification problem where the candidate events (e1, e2) and the linking marker (l) are already identified. The model does not perform event extraction or relation extraction in a broader sense; it only classifies pre-defined relations. While this is a valid and useful sub-problem, the framing may overstate the scope of the contribution.

**Questions to Authors**

1.  The "Pair LSTM" architecture, which encodes the two events independently, generally outperforms the "Stated Pair LSTM," which initializes the second LSTM with the final state of the first. This result seems to counter the intuition that there is a strong sequential or logical dependency between the cause and effect spans that would benefit from direct state transfer. Could you elaborate on why you believe the independent encoding proves more effective for this task? Is it possible that the direct state transfer introduces noise or makes optimization more difficult?
2.  Given the time of this work, attention mechanisms were becoming a standard component for highlighting relevant parts of an input sequence in relation to another. Did you consider using an attention mechanism (e.g., allowing the model to attend to specific words in e1 while processing e2)? Do you believe this could have further improved performance by helping the model focus on the most salient words for determining causality, rather than relying on a fixed-size LSTM hidden state?
