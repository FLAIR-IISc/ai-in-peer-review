- Summary: Proposes a dual-LSTM model encoding two events (with lexical marker) to classify Causal vs Non-Causal on the AltLex corpus, outperforming a feature-based SVM.

- Contribution: Demonstrates that standard LSTMs with pre-trained embeddings can beat complex feature-engineered systems on causality classification.

- Contribution: Provides a thorough empirical benchmark on AltLex, testing architectural variants, optimizers, padding strategies, and offering qualitative analysis.

- Strength: Significant empirical gains over prior state-of-the-art (e.g., F1 75.40%, accuracy 77.90% on non-bootstrapped data).

- Strength: Methodological simplicity and generalizability — end-to-end model relying only on pre-trained embeddings, avoiding heavy linguistic resources.

- Strength: Well-motivated rationale arguing context-aware semantic encoding is needed over syntactic/lexical rules for disambiguation.

- Strength: Informative analysis comparing Pair vs Stated Pair LSTMs and giving qualitative examples where the model corrects baseline errors.

- Weakness: Limited architectural novelty — a standard siamese-style LSTM without newer mechanisms like attention.

- Weakness: Ambiguity in input representation — unclear how e1, l, e2 map to AltLex Arg1/Arg2 and whether surrounding context is included.

- Weakness: Insufficient baselines and ablations — missing simpler neural baselines (e.g., bag-of-words) and ablation removing the lexical marker.

- Weakness: Superficial discussion of dataset differences — large baseline performance jump between non-bootstrapped and bootstrapped sets is noted but not analyzed.

- Question: Please clarify precise mapping from AltLex Arg1/Arg2/Connective to model inputs and whether extra context is fed to LSTMs.

- Question: Why choose LSTMs over CNNs (e.g., multi-filter CNNs); were CNN experiments conducted?

- Question: Why does the Stated Pair LSTM (sequential hidden-state passing) perform worse than independent encoding — any analysis linking performance to textual cause/effect order?
