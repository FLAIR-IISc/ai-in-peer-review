- Task & approach: Paper studies causal meaning disambiguation (Causal vs Non-Causal) using LSTM-based contextual encoders on event pairs with a lexical marker.
- Evaluation & dataset: Experiments on the AltLex corpus comparing to per-connective majority and a feature-rich SVM, showing modest F1/accuracy gains.
- Model variants: Two architectures tested — Pair LSTM (independent encoders for e1 and [l+e2]) and Stated Pair LSTM (second LSTM initialized from the first).
- Analysis & control: Includes a rebalanced-data experiment and small qualitative analysis claiming gains stem from context encoding rather than resource features.
- Contribution (empirical): Simple LSTM encoders yield small but consistent improvements, especially in precision for the Causal class.
- Contribution (architectural): Independent encoding (Pair LSTM) outperforms state-initialized sharing (Stated Pair LSTM), suggesting separate argument encodings work better.
- Contribution (dataset insight): Paper highlights class imbalance and distributional issues in the AltLex bootstrapped split and provides a rebalanced experiment.
- Strength: Focuses on contextual modeling using only pretrained embeddings, avoiding reliance on lexical resources and helping generalization to rare markers.
- Strength: Empirical gains are consistent across configurations and splits, though modest in magnitude.
- Strength: Useful negative result about cross-LSTM state initialization warns against naïve encoder tying.
- Strength: Several configurations improve Causal precision, desirable for downstream systems that favor fewer false positives.
- Weakness: Risk of conflating marker identity with causality since the marker token is included; lacks masked-marker or ambiguous-marker-only evaluations.
- Weakness: Limited baselines and ablations—no bag-of-words/logistic, averaged embeddings, single BiLSTM over [e1 l e2], attention models, or ablations excluding l or varying BiLSTM vs LSTM.
- Weakness: Methodological/reproducibility gaps—unclear sequence representation (pooling vs last state), padding/pooling mechanisms, hyperparameters, batch/epoch/early stopping, seeds, and potential use of test data in tuning.
- Weakness: Metrics and statistical testing insufficient—overemphasis on accuracy despite class imbalance, lacking macro-F1/per-class F1/PR curves and significance tests for main results.
- Weakness: Limited generality—experiments only on AltLex, no tests on PDTB, Causal-TimeBank, or other domains.
- Question: How do results change if lexical markers are masked, evaluated only on ambiguous markers, or in leave-one-marker-out cross-lexical tests?
- Question: Clarify “zero-padding strategies” vs pooling (max/mean/mode): what is pooled and what is the final sentence representation?
- Question: Provide exact model specs—uni vs bi-LSTM, hidden sizes, layers, dropout, batch size, epochs, early stopping, dev split, hyperparameter tuning, and seed averaging.
- Question: Can you compare to a simple BiLSTM over [e1; l; e2] and a bag-of-ngrams logistic regression to contextualize gains?
- Question: Do analyses stratified by connective ambiguity and frequency buckets exist to support claims of contextual causality learning?
- Additional suggestions: Report macro-averaged and per-class F1, PR curves, significance tests, analyze encoder similarity/attention, improve clarity, and release code and hyperparameters for reproducibility.
