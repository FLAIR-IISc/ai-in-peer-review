Summary: The paper tackles causal meaning disambiguation between two events signaled by potentially ambiguous lexical markers. It proposes a two-input LSTM architecture (Pair LSTM and a variant with state transfer, “Stated Pair LSTM”) that encodes the event contexts (with GloVe embeddings) and classifies the relation as causal/non-causal. Experiments on the AltLex corpus (bootstrapping and non-bootstrapping versions) show improvements over (i) a majority-per-marker baseline and (ii) a feature-rich SVM (Hidey & McKeown, 2016). The authors also analyze padding strategies and optimizers, and provide qualitative examples for ambiguous expressions.

Contributions (as I see them, strongest first):
1) Empirical demonstration that context-encoding neural models outperform feature-engineered approaches and marker-majority heuristics for causal disambiguation on AltLex.
2) Architectural/optimization insights: encoding events independently (Pair LSTM) outperforms state-initialized LSTMs; optimizer and padding choices materially affect precision–recall trade-offs.
3) Corpus-level analysis of ambiguity and train–test distribution shifts in AltLex (bootstrapping vs non-bootstrapping) with illustrative case studies.

Strengths:
- Solid empirical gains over prior work: On the non-bootstrapping set, the best model improves F1 for the causal class by +2.13 and accuracy by +7.72 over the SVM baseline; on bootstrapping, several configs match or exceed the SVM with better precision, indicating fewer false positives.
- Careful ablations lend insight: Pair vs Stated LSTM, optimizer (Adam vs Adadelta), and padding strategies are examined with clear implications (e.g., independent encoding is better, Adam yields a more balanced P/R).
- Qualitative analysis is convincing: The model correctly handles ambiguous markers (e.g., “which then”) and non-causative uses of causative verbs (“break”), supporting the claim that context encoding matters.
- Task formulation clarity: The split between causal meaning classification and argument identification is reasonable and helps scope the problem.

Weaknesses:
- Limited novelty: The architecture (two LSTMs + dense layers with GloVe) is standard; no attention mechanisms, contextualized embeddings, or more recent architectures are considered.
- Narrow evaluation: Results are only on AltLex; no tests on PDTB causal relations or Causal-TimeBank (even as out-of-domain), limiting claims of generality.
- Methodological details are under-specified: The “max/mean/mode” padding strategy is unclear; preprocessing, tokenization, event span extraction, and train/dev/test protocol are not fully described.
- Baselines could be stronger: No comparison to BiLSTM with attention or contextual encoders; significance testing is limited and inconsistently reported; some tables contain typos/inconsistencies.

Questions to Authors (optional):
- What are the exact splits and tuning protocol? Is there a dev set?
- How are events and markers supplied at test time—gold spans or detected automatically?
- Please clarify the padding operations and sequence lengths handled by the LSTMs.
- How does the model perform without the lexical marker input, and on AltLex items with unseen markers?
- Any results on other datasets (even zero-shot/transfer) to assess robustness?
