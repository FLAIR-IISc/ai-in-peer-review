Summary
The paper addresses causal meaning disambiguation for pairs of event spans connected by a lexical marker (explicit or AltLex), proposing LSTM-based models that encode (e1) and (l + e2) and classify the relation as Causal vs Non-Causal. Experiments on the AltLex corpus (Hidey & McKeown, 2016) show that the proposed models outperform a strong per-marker majority baseline and a feature-rich SVM baseline, particularly on the non-bootstrapped split; results on the bootstrapped split are competitive and sometimes better, with improved precision/recall balance in the best configurations. The authors argue that encoding contextual meaning is necessary for disambiguation beyond lexical lists and patterns.

Main contributions (as I see them)
1) Empirical finding that a simple two-input LSTM encoder operating only on pre-trained word embeddings outperforms or matches a feature-intensive SVM across two AltLex splits, establishing a new state-of-the-art on the non-bootstrapped split.
2) Architectural comparison showing that independently encoding the two arguments (“Pair LSTM”) is more effective than initializing the second LSTM with the state of the first (“Stated Pair LSTM”), suggesting limited benefit of sequential dependency between arguments for this task.
3) Analysis indicating improved handling of ambiguous or distribution-shifted markers (e.g., “which then”), and a balanced-data experiment with significance testing (McNemar) that supports robustness beyond class skew.

Strengths
- Clear motivation and problem setup. The paper argues persuasively that causality is not rigidly tied to fixed syntactic patterns or a small set of connectives and that contextual semantics should be modeled. The experimental design (no reliance on lexicons, only embeddings) directly tests this premise.

- Competitive to state-of-the-art with a simple model. On the non-bootstrapped AltLex split, “Pair LSTM Max Adadelta” improves F1 for the Causal class by 2.13 points and accuracy by 7.72 points over the SVM baseline. On the bootstrapped split, several configurations are on par or slightly better in F1 and yield a more desirable precision/recall balance (e.g., “Pair LSTM Mean Adam”).

- Resource-light and generalizable. Using only pre-trained GloVe vectors and no handcrafted features or external lexicons increases portability across domains and languages and reduces engineering effort, addressing a practical limitation of prior work.

- Sensible ablations and negative results. The comparison between “Pair” and “Stated Pair” LSTM variants and among optimizers and padding strategies adds credibility and provides actionable guidance (independent encoders with Adam and mean padding often best).

- Qualitative analysis and robustness checks. The paper includes instructive examples where the neural model succeeds and the SVM errs, including ambiguous lexicalizations; and it evaluates on a reduced, more balanced dataset with statistical testing.

Weaknesses
- Limited novelty in modeling. Using LSTMs to encode sentence or argument-level context for relation/sense classification was common by 2016–2017. The proposed architectures (two separate LSTMs with concatenation vs state initialization) are straightforward; no attention, pooling analysis, or interaction mechanisms are explored. Stronger baselines (e.g., biLSTM over the full span, attention between arguments, CNNs, or even averaged-embedding MLP) are missing.

- Insufficient methodological clarity and reproducibility. Key details are unclear: LSTM hidden sizes, whether last state or pooling is used, batch size, epochs, early stopping, learning rate schedules, dropout placement, fine-tuning of embeddings, OOV handling, and tokenization. The “padding strategy” is confusing: Eq. (1) suggests applying max/mean/mode to matrices, which is nonsensical; presumably the authors choose the padded sequence length as max/mean/mode over length distributions, but this must be clarified. Figure 1 is referenced but not included. Without code or complete hyperparameters, replication is hard.

- Evaluation scope and controls. All results are on AltLex; there is no evaluation on other corpora (e.g., PDTB explicit/AltLex subsets, Causal-TimeBank) to test generalization. The model assumes gold (e1, l, e2) boundaries, which are non-trivial to obtain in practice; there is no end-to-end pipeline evaluation or discussion of argument detection sensitivity.

- Class skew and metric reporting. The main results emphasize F1 on the Causal class and overall accuracy; macro-F1 or per-class F1 would offer a fairer view under class imbalance. Significance testing is only reported for the reduced dataset; the main improvements over B2 on the original splits are not tested for significance. The improvements on the bootstrapped split are small, and it is unclear whether they are statistically meaningful.

- Causal signal attribution not disentangled. The claim that “encoding the meaning of a sentence is required” is plausible but not empirically isolated. Missing controls include ablating the connective identity (mask l), testing only ambiguous markers, shuffling e1/e2, or using per-marker majority as a feature. Without these, it is difficult to quantify how much gain comes from context vs marker identity and frequency.

Questions to Authors
- Please precisely define the padding strategy and correct Eq. (1). Are you choosing the padded sequence length as the maximum/mean/mode of input lengths? How do you handle truncation vs padding per batch?

- What are the LSTM hyperparameters (hidden dimension, number of layers, bidirectionality?), pooling strategy (last state vs max/mean pooling), training epochs, batch size, early stopping, and whether embeddings are fine-tuned?

- How are e1 and e2 spans obtained from AltLex? Are they gold spans? If not, what heuristic or parser is used?

- Can you report macro-F1 and per-class F1, and provide significance tests for the main tables? Also, report results restricted to ambiguous markers to isolate contextual gains.

- How does a simpler baseline (averaged word embeddings of e1, l, e2 concatenated + MLP or logistic regression) perform? A CNN baseline would also strengthen the comparison.

Additional Comments
- Several typos, inconsistent table references, and notation issues (e.g., the “pad” equation) should be fixed. Ensure Figure 1 is present.

- Discuss whether the model is symmetric or sensitive to e1/e2 order; consider an experiment swapping arguments.

- The analysis on “which then” is interesting; a broader per-marker breakdown (precision/recall) would be informative.

Overall, the paper presents a solid, simple neural baseline that improves over feature-heavy prior work on AltLex, with useful ablations. Its novelty is limited, and clarity and evaluation breadth need strengthening. With additional controls, clearer methodology, and broader comparisons, this would be a stronger contribution.
