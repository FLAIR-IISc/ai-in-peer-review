Summary
The paper addresses causal meaning disambiguation: given two events (e1, e2) and a potential causal lexical marker (l), determine whether the relation is causal. The authors argue that encoding sentence meaning via neural models is necessary for disambiguation. They propose two LSTM-based architectures: (i) Pair LSTM, which independently encodes the two inputs (e1) and (l+e2) with LSTMs and combines them through dense layers; and (ii) Stated Pair LSTM, which initializes the second LSTM with the final state of the first. They evaluate on the AltLex corpus (non-bootstrapping and bootstrapping versions) and compare against (B1) a per-marker majority baseline and (B2) the feature-based SVM of Hidey and McKeown (2016). On both corpus versions, several neural configurations outperform B1 and slightly outperform or match B2, with the best gains on the non-bootstrapping split. They also report results with a downsampled, more balanced training set and provide qualitative analyses of ambiguous markers.

Main contributions (as assessed)
- Empirical finding that an LSTM-based contextual encoder for (e1) and (l+e2) improves causal disambiguation on AltLex over a strong feature-based SVM baseline, especially in accuracy and precision (e.g., non-bootstrapping: F1 75.40% vs 73.76%; Acc 77.90% vs 71.86%).
- Architectural comparison showing that independently encoding the two inputs (Pair LSTM) tends to outperform state-initialized encoding (Stated Pair LSTM), and analysis of optimizer/pooling choices.
- Error analysis demonstrating cases where contextual modeling handles ambiguous and non-causative uses of commonly causative words (e.g., “break”, “which then”).

Strengths
- Empirical improvements over prior work: The best model outperforms B2 on the non-bootstrapping AltLex set (Pair LSTM Max + Adadelta: F1 75.40% vs 73.76%; Acc 77.90% vs 71.86%). On the bootstrapping set, the best models are marginally better in F1 and accuracy (e.g., Pair LSTM Mode + Adam: F1 81.96% vs 80.90%; Acc 80.19% vs 79.58%). These results support the claim that contextual encoding improves disambiguation beyond resource-driven features.
- Sound task decomposition and motivation: The paper clearly separates causal meaning classification from causal argument identification, focuses on the former, and motivates why context is needed given the ambiguity of many markers (e.g., since, after, AltLex).
- Sensible model comparisons and analysis: The Pair vs Stated Pair comparison is informative; the observation that independent encodings perform better is useful. The optimizer comparison (Adam vs Adadelta) and pooling choices provide additional insights into model behavior (precision–recall trade-offs).
- Reduced reliance on hand-crafted features/resources: Using only pre-trained embeddings avoids the coverage limitations of lexical resources and appears to generalize to less frequent/ambiguous markers (e.g., “which then” case).
- Qualitative examples: The case studies (Table 8) illustrate where contextual modeling helps, especially with ambiguous markers and verbs that are causative in some contexts but not others.

Weaknesses
- Limited novelty: The core contribution is an application of standard LSTM encoders to a binary classification problem with two inputs. The architectures and training components (GloVe embeddings, LSTMs, dense layers, Adam/Adadelta) are conventional; the novelty is mainly empirical rather than methodological.
- Clarity and technical detail issues: Several descriptions are confusing or incomplete. The “zero-padding strategies” are described via max/mean/mode operations, which are pooling operations, not padding; the exact sequence encoding pathway (e.g., last hidden state vs pooled embeddings) is unclear. Key hyperparameters (LSTM hidden size, number of layers, batch size, epochs, learning rate, whether embeddings are fine-tuned) and the selection protocol (dev set) are not specified. The “Stated Pair LSTM” terminology is ambiguous (stateful vs initialized). Some tables and counts appear inconsistent/garbled.
- Evaluation scope is narrow: Results are only on AltLex; no tests on other resources (e.g., PDTB explicit/implicit subsets, Causal-TimeBank) or cross-domain transfers to assess generalization. No comparison to simpler neural baselines (e.g., BiLSTM over the full sentence, or a classifier on averaged embeddings) to isolate the benefit of the two-input design.
- Limited analysis of confounds: The model may benefit from lexical-marker identity; there is no control experiment removing marker identity or reporting per-marker performance to quantify generalization to unseen/rare markers. The bootstrapping split has distribution shifts; robustness is not systematically quantified.
- Statistical testing and reproducibility: Significance is only reported for improvements over B1 on a reduced dataset; no statistical test is provided for differences from B2 on the main results. Code, training protocol, and random seed details are not provided, limiting reproducibility.

Questions to authors
- Architecture clarification: Are sequences fed to LSTMs and the LSTM outputs reduced via taking the final hidden state, or are you pooling word embeddings directly via max/mean/mode before the dense layers? Please clarify the exact computation graph and the role of “padding” vs “pooling.”
- Hyperparameters and training: What are the LSTM hidden sizes, number of layers, batch size, number of epochs, learning rate schedule, and early stopping criteria? Are GloVe embeddings fine-tuned during training?
- Data splits and validation: How are train/dev/test splits defined for both corpus versions? Is a development set used to select hyperparameters? Are results averaged over multiple random seeds?
- Statistical significance: Are improvements over B2 in Table 5 statistically significant (e.g., McNemar’s test on the official test sets)?
- Ablations and baselines: How does the model perform when (a) using only e1, (b) only l+e2, or (c) masking/removing the lexical marker? How does it compare to a BiLSTM over the full sentence or a classifier on averaged embeddings?
- Per-marker and OOD analysis: Can you report per-marker metrics and performance on markers unseen or rare in training? Do results differ for explicit vs AltLex cases?
- Resource release: Will you release code and trained models to support reproducibility?

Overall, the paper presents a clear empirical advance on a useful task with a straightforward neural approach, but the methodological novelty is limited and important implementation and evaluation details are missing. Substantially improving clarity, adding ablations and stronger baselines, and expanding evaluation would strengthen the contribution.
