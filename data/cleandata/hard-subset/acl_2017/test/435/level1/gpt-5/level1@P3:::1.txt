Summary and claimed contributions
- The paper addresses causal meaning disambiguation: given two events (e1, e2) and a lexical marker l in context, predict whether the relation is Causal vs Non-Causal. The authors argue that causality is not tied to fixed lexical/syntactic patterns and propose LSTM-based architectures to encode contextual meaning.
- They evaluate on the AltLex corpus (Hidey & McKeown, 2016), compare against (i) a per-connective majority baseline and (ii) the feature-rich SVM from Hidey & McKeown, and report modest improvements in F1/accuracy. They explore two variants: Pair LSTM (independent encoders for e1 and [l+e2]) and Stated Pair LSTM (second LSTM initialized from the first), along with optimizer and “padding” strategies.
- They provide a small analysis and a rebalanced-data experiment, arguing that gains come from context encoding rather than resource-driven features.

Contributions as I see them
1) Empirical finding: Simple LSTM encoders over events and marker context provide small but consistent improvements over a strong feature-based SVM on AltLex, especially in precision on the causal class.
2) Architectural observation: Encoding each argument independently (Pair LSTM) performs better than initializing the second with the first (Stated Pair LSTM), suggesting independent context encodings are preferable for this task.
3) Dataset-centric insight: The paper surfaces the effect of class imbalance and distributional differences in the bootstrapped AltLex split, and includes a rebalanced experiment and limited qualitative examples.

Strengths
- Focus on context over handcrafted lexical resources: The model relies only on pre-trained word embeddings, avoiding dependence on WordNet/VerbNet/PropBank coverage. This aligns with the motivation that causal meaning is contextually expressed and improves generalization to less frequent markers (e.g., “which then”), as shown in examples.
- Consistent, albeit modest, empirical gains: On AltLex non-bootstrapping, best F1 (Causal) improves from 73.76 to 75.40 and accuracy from 71.86 to 77.90. On bootstrapping, the best neural model edges the SVM by ~1 point F1. While not large, these gains are consistent across multiple configurations and splits.
- Useful negative result about cross-LSTM state initialization: The “Stated Pair LSTM” underperforms “Pair LSTM,” cautioning against naïvely tying the encoders; this is a practical architectural lesson for future work on event-relation modeling.
- High-precision operating point: Several configurations significantly improve precision for the Causal class relative to B2 (e.g., +3–4%), an attractive property for downstream applications that prefer fewer false positives (e.g., IE pipelines).

Weaknesses
- Evaluation design risks conflating marker identity with causality: The input includes the lexical marker l concatenated with e2, and many connectives are unambiguous or highly skewed. Although B1 is strong, the LSTM can still overfit to marker identity. The paper lacks a targeted evaluation on ambiguous markers only, masked-marker experiments (e.g., replacing l with a placeholder), or cross-lexical generalization (train without a marker, test on it). Without this, the core claim that “encoding of sentence meaning is required” remains insufficiently isolated from connective identity cues.
- Limited baselines and ablations: No comparison to simple neural baselines such as bag-of-words logistic regression, averaged embeddings, a single BiLSTM over the concatenated sequence [e1 l e2], or attention-based models. There is no ablation on including vs excluding l, or on using BiLSTM vs unidirectional LSTM. The “zero-padding strategies” (max/mean/mode) appear to be pooling operations rather than padding, but their role is not clearly specified or ablated.
- Methodological clarity and reproducibility: Key details are missing or ambiguous: how sequences are represented to the LSTM (last hidden state vs pooling), actual padding/pooling mechanism (Eq. 1 suggests pooling, not padding), batch size, number of epochs, early stopping, learning rate, final chosen hyperparameters, and random seeds. Hyperparameter search appears to use the test set (multiple configurations reported), with no clear development split, raising concerns about overfitting to the test data.
- Metrics and statistical testing: Accuracy is emphasized despite severe class imbalance. Macro-F1, per-class F1 (both classes), and ROC/PR analysis for the causal class would be more appropriate. Statistical testing is only reported for the rebalanced experiment and not for the main results; multiple configuration comparisons risk selection bias.
- Corpus coverage and generality: Results are only on AltLex; no experiments on PDTB explicit connective disambiguation, Causal-TimeBank, or other domains. The model’s applicability beyond AltLex (and beyond sentences paired via Wikipedia/Simple Wikipedia alignment) remains uncertain.

Questions to authors
- How much of the gain remains if the lexical marker token(s) are masked or replaced with a generic placeholder? Can you report results on (a) ambiguous markers only, and (b) a leave-one-marker-out setup to test cross-lexical generalization?
- Please clarify the “zero-padding strategies.” Eq. 1 looks like temporal pooling (max/mean/mode) rather than padding. What exactly is pooled (embeddings, LSTM outputs)? What is the final sentence representation (last hidden state vs pooled states)?
- What is the precise model architecture: unidirectional vs bidirectional LSTMs? Hidden sizes, number of layers, dropout rates actually used, batch size, epochs, early stopping criterion, and development split? Were hyperparameters tuned on a dev set, and were the reported test results from a single run or averaged across seeds?
- Can you compare to a simple BiLSTM over the full sequence [e1; l; e2] and to a bag-of-ngrams logistic regression? These would help contextualize where the gains come from (sequence modeling vs feature engineering).
- Do you have an analysis stratified by connective ambiguity (unambiguous vs ambiguous), as well as by frequency buckets? This would substantiate the claim that the model learns contextual causality beyond lexicon identity.

Additional comments
- Consider reporting macro-averaged F1 and per-class F1 for both classes, and PR curves for the Causal class. Provide significance tests for the main results, not just the rebalanced setting.
- The “Stated Pair LSTM” negative result is interesting; analyzing attention or similarity between the two encoded arguments could provide further insight.
- Editing for clarity would help: several tables/figures and references have inconsistencies; ensure reproducibility by releasing code and final hyperparameters.

Overall, the paper tackles an important problem and shows encouraging gains with a simple neural approach. However, stronger experimental controls to isolate contextual learning from marker identity and clearer methodology are needed to substantiate the central claim and improve reproducibility.
