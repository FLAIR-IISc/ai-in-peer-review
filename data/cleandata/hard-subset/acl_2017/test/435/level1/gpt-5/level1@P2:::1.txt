Summary and contributions
This paper addresses causal meaning disambiguation for lexical markers (e.g., since, after) by proposing a two-input LSTM architecture that encodes the context of two events and the connective, and evaluates it on the AltLex corpus. The authors compare against a majority-per-marker baseline and the feature-rich SVM system of Hidey and McKeown (2016), reporting small but consistent gains.

Contributions (as I see them), in order of strength:
1) An empirical study showing that a simple LSTM-based contextual encoder can match or outperform a strong feature-based SVM on AltLex for causal vs. non-causal disambiguation, with improved precision and overall accuracy on both corpus versions.
2) An analysis indicating that encoding event context (rather than relying solely on connective priors) improves robustness on some ambiguous markers and out-of-distribution marker senses, with illustrative examples.
3) A modest exploration of design choices (two-branch vs. state-initialized LSTMs, optimizer, and padding length heuristics).

Strengths
- Empirical improvement over a strong prior system: On the non-bootstrapping AltLex, the best “Pair LSTM Max Adadelta” improves F1 for the Causal class by +2.13 points (75.40 vs. 73.76) and Accuracy by +7.72 points (77.90 vs. 71.86) over Hidey & McKeown (B2). On the bootstrapping variant, several LSTM configurations slightly exceed or match B2 in F1 (e.g., 81.96 vs. 80.90) and accuracy (80.52 vs. 79.58). These are credible gains given the competitive baselines and the dataset’s difficulty (marker ambiguity and class imbalance).
- Context encoding helps on ambiguous cases: The qualitative analysis (Table 8) includes examples where the LSTM correctly flips away from the training majority class (e.g., “which then”, “break off … which means”) while B2 fails. This supports the central claim that contextual encoding beyond connective identity is beneficial.
- Sensible architectural choice with compact input design: Modeling the two events as separate inputs and concatenating l with e2 is simple and aligns with the task formulation. The negative result for “state-initialized” LSTMs is informative: independent encoding plus MLP interaction outperforms tied states, suggesting event-level features are best combined at a higher layer.
- Some robustness checks: The paper reports results across two corpus variants (bootstrapping vs. non-bootstrapping) and considers imbalance by evaluating on a reduced Non-Causal subset; it also probes optimizers and padding-length heuristics. The reported McNemar test suggests improvements over the majority-per-marker baseline are statistically meaningful in the reduced setting.

Weaknesses
- Evaluation does not properly isolate reliance on the connective: Because the second input is “marker + e2” and many connectives are near-unambiguous, the model may largely key on l. The strong performance of the per-marker majority baseline (B1) corroborates this risk. The paper lacks a critical ablation: performance when the connective is masked or removed, and/or results on the subset of genuinely ambiguous markers. Without that, it is unclear how much improvement comes from contextual understanding vs. connective priors captured by embeddings.
- Methodological and mathematical clarity issues: The padding-length discussion is confusing and appears incorrect. Defining pad(x) = {max(x), mean(x), mode(x)} with x ∈ R^{n×d} conflates vector-valued sequences and length selection; it is unclear what “mode” of length means (lengths are integers; mean often non-integer). More generally, preprocessing details (tokenization, truncation strategy, max length, OOV handling) and training details (epochs, batch size, early stopping, selection on dev) are missing, which hinders reproducibility and interpretation.
- Limited baselines and ablations: There is no CNN baseline (despite arguing LSTM > CNN), nor simpler neural/text baselines (bag-of-embeddings + logistic regression, BiLSTM sentence encoders, attention over context windows). There is no ablation on embeddings (random vs. GloVe), on feeding e2 without the marker, or on combining e1/e2 without any marker. These are important to support the claim that encoding sentence meaning (not lexicalized heuristics) drives the gains.
- Narrow evaluation scope and practical setup: Experiments rely solely on AltLex; Causal-TimeBank and other corpora are dismissed as “too small,” but even small-sample evaluation or cross-corpus tests would inform generalization. The task setup assumes gold event spans and known marker l; the paper does not discuss event detection or connective identification, which limits applicability and understates end-to-end difficulty.
- Writing quality and referencing: The paper contains numerous grammatical issues and inconsistent citations; some references are incomplete. Section 3.1 mixes philosophical discussion (counterfactuality) with task definition without operationalizing these distinctions in the model or evaluation. Figures/tables are referenced, but finer-grained breakdowns (per-marker results, especially for ambiguous markers) are missing.

Questions to authors
- How does the model perform when the connective is masked or removed from the input? Please report results on: (a) e1 + e2 only; (b) e1 + e2 with a MASK token in place of l; (c) l only (no context), to quantify dependency on the marker.
- What is performance on the subset of instances where the connective is ambiguous across the training set (and/or across train vs. test as in Table 4)? Please provide per-marker or aggregated results for that subset.
- Please clarify the padding-length procedure precisely. What are the maximum sequence lengths for e1 and l+e2? How do “mean” and “mode” produce integer lengths? Are sequences truncated or padded to those lengths?
- What train/dev/test split was used? Were hyperparameters tuned on a dev set? How many epochs, batch size, early stopping criterion, and total parameter count?
- Did you reimplement B2 or use authors’ code? Were features and resources identical to Hidey & McKeown (2016)? How did you ensure comparable preprocessing/splits?
- How are OOV tokens handled with GloVe 840B? Are embeddings updated during training?
- Can you release code and scripts to ensure reproducibility?

Overall assessment
This work addresses an important subproblem and shows promising, mostly small improvements over a strong feature-based baseline. However, the lack of ablations controlling for connective reliance, methodological ambiguities (especially padding), missing baselines, and limited evaluation breadth weaken the paper’s claims about contextual meaning encoding. With additional analyses (masking l, ambiguous-only results), clearer methodology, and stronger baselines, the paper would be significantly stronger. In its current form, I lean toward rejection, but see a viable path to a solid contribution with the requested clarifications and experiments.
