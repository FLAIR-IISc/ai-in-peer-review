

Summary: The paper proposes a neural network architecture for causality classification, specifically focusing on disambiguuation of causal meaning in linguistic expressions. The authors claim that encoding sentence meaning is necessary for causal disambiguation, and present a model using two LSTMs to process the cause and effect events. Their main contributions are: (1) demonstrating that neural context encoding outperforms feature-based approaches on ambiguous causal lexical markers, (2) providing a thorough analysis of the AltLex corpus's challenges with ambiguous causal markers, and (3) showing that their model generalizes better than previous approaches that rely on lexical resources.

Strengths: The paper makes a meaningful contribution to a poorly studied NLP task with clear practical relevance for discourse analysis. The empirical evaluation on the AltLex corpus is comprehensive, with careful analysis of ambiguous markers like "since" and "after" that previous methods struggle with. The model's ability to outperform the baseline on ambiguous cases (e.g., "which then" in Table 8) demonstrates genuine progress beyond simple class distribution learning. The authors also provide valuable insights about the limitations of previous approaches that rely on lexical resources like WordNet.

Weaknesses: The paper's claim of "outperforming state-of-the-art" is questionable since the only previous work on the AltLex corpus is the one that introduced it (Hidey and McKeown, 2016), making this a limited comparison. The evaluation is restricted to a single corpus with limited size (21k sentences), raising concerns about generalizability. The paper fails to justify why LSTM was chosen over more modern architectures like transformers, which might better capture long-range dependencies in causal relations. Additionally, the paper lacks a detailed error analysis to understand the model's limitations with complex causal expressions.

Questions to Authors: How would your approach scale to larger, more diverse corpora beyond AltLex? What specific linguistic phenomena does your model struggle with that could inform future work? Could you provide more details on how your model handles cases where the causal relation spans multiple sentences?
