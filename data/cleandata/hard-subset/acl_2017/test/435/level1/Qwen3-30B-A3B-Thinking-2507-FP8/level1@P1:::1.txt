

# Peer Review for ACL 2017

## Summary and Contributions

This paper proposes a neural network architecture for causality classification, specifically focusing on the disambiguation of causal lexical markers based on context. The authors argue that causal meaning is not fixed to specific syntactic constructions or lexical resources, but rather depends on context, and therefore neural networks that can encode contextual meaning are better suited for this task. They evaluate their approach on the AltLex corpus (a recently released corpus with a larger coverage of causal expressions than PDTB) and claim to outperform the state-of-the-art.

As I see them, the main contributions of this work are:
1. A thorough evaluation of neural approaches for causal meaning disambiguation on the AltLex corpus, which is a significant resource for the field.
2. Demonstration that contextual encoding (via LSTM) outperforms previous methods that rely on lexical resources and syntactic features.
3. Analysis showing the model's ability to handle ambiguous causal markers that previous approaches fail to disambiguate correctly.

## Strengths

1. **Significant problem addressed with clear motivation**: The paper correctly identifies a key limitation in prior work - that causal expressions are not limited to specific syntactic constructions or lexical resources. The authors effectively argue against the "syntax-grounded construction of causality" approach and provide evidence from Neeleman and van de Koot (2012) that causality isn't captured by standard syntactic tests. This is a strong theoretical foundation for their approach.

2. **Use of the AltLex corpus and thorough analysis**: The AltLex corpus is a valuable resource that addresses the limited coverage problem noted by Hidey and McKeown (2016). The authors provide a careful analysis of the corpus (Tables 3-4), particularly noting the class distribution of ambiguous causal markers, which demonstrates a deep understanding of the task's challenges.

3. **Strong empirical results on ambiguous cases**: The paper's greatest strength is its demonstration of the model's ability to handle ambiguous causal markers that the state-of-the-art fails on (Table 8). For instance, the model correctly disambiguates "which then" as causal (despite the training data having only one causal instance), while B2 misclassifies it as non-causal. This is compelling evidence supporting their core claim that contextual encoding is necessary for causal disambiguation.

4. **Clear task definition and distinction**: The paper clearly distinguishes between causal meaning classification and causal argument identification, which helps clarify the field's challenges. Their focus on the former task is well-justified and appropriately scoped.

5. **Comparative analysis of neural architectures**: The authors properly evaluate different neural architectures (Pair LSTM vs. Stated Pair LSTM) and zero-padding strategies, showing that independent encoding of events followed by dense layers outperforms the more complex connection between event encoders. This careful comparison adds credibility to their approach.

## Weaknesses

1. **Lack of novelty in neural architecture**: The proposed architecture (LSTM-based with two inputs) is not particularly novel in the context of modern NLP. While the application to causal disambiguation is interesting, the architecture itself is a standard approach that has been used in many other NLP tasks. The paper doesn't sufficiently justify why this specific architecture is better suited for causality than other standard neural approaches.

2. **Insufficient discussion of limitations**: The paper fails to address important limitations of their approach. For instance, they don't discuss how the model would perform on causal expressions outside the AltLex corpus, or how it handles different domains (e.g., medical vs. news text). The claim that "our claim holds" is somewhat vague without addressing these boundaries.

3. **Class imbalance not adequately addressed**: The AltLex corpus is highly imbalanced (Tables 3-4), with a significant majority of non-causal examples. While the authors note this and perform some experiments with a reduced corpus, they don't discuss how this imbalance affected their model's performance or how they might mitigate it in future work. This is a significant oversight for a classification task.

4. **Weak baselines**: The baselines (B1 and B2) are not particularly strong. B1 is a simple majority-class classifier, and B2 is the system from the AltLex corpus authors, which the authors themselves note has limitations (reliance on lexical resources). A more comprehensive comparison with recent neural baselines would strengthen the paper's claim of outperforming the state-of-the-art.

5. **Lack of detailed implementation information**: The paper lacks sufficient detail to replicate the experiments (e.g., exact LSTM dimensions, dropout values, batch size). While the paper mentions "different values for dropout ([0.5, 0.75]) and regularization," it doesn't specify which values were used in the best-performing model, making it difficult to assess the robustness of the results.

## Questions to Authors

1. The paper notes that the "bootstrapping" version of the AltLex corpus has instances where the training and test sets have different class distributions (Table 4). How did this affect your model's performance, and what strategies did you use to handle this distribution shift?

2. The paper mentions that the model achieved 81.96% F1 in the Causal class on the bootstrapping corpus with the Pair LSTM Mean Adam configuration. Could you provide the exact hyperparameters (LSTM units, dropout, batch size) used in this configuration to ensure reproducibility?

3. The paper claims the model outperforms the state-of-the-art, but the baselines are not particularly strong. How would your model perform against more recent neural approaches for causal classification, such as those using transformers or attention mechanisms?

4. The authors note that the AltLex corpus has limited coverage of causal expressions. How would you expect your model to generalize to causal expressions not covered in this corpus, and what additional data would you need to improve coverage?
