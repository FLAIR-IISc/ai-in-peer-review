- Proposes a neural network for causality classification using two LSTMs to encode cause and effect events.
- Argues that encoding sentence meaning is necessary for disambiguating causal expressions.
- Shows neural context encoding outperforms feature-based approaches on ambiguous causal lexical markers.
- Provides a thorough analysis of the AltLex corpus and challenges with ambiguous markers.
- Claims the model generalizes better than prior approaches that rely on lexical resources.
- Makes a meaningful contribution to an understudied NLP task with practical discourse-analysis relevance.
- Empirical evaluation on AltLex is comprehensive, with careful analysis of markers like "since" and "after".
- Model outperforms baseline on ambiguous cases (e.g., "which then"), indicating progress beyond simple class-distribution learning.
- Highlights limitations of lexical-resource-based methods such as WordNet.
- “State-of-the-art” claim is weak because comparison is limited to the single prior AltLex paper (Hidey & McKeown, 2016).
- Evaluation is restricted to one modest-sized corpus (21k sentences), raising generalizability concerns.
- No rationale given for choosing LSTMs over modern architectures (e.g., transformers) that may capture long-range dependencies better.
- Paper lacks detailed error analysis to pinpoint failures on complex causal expressions.
- Questions: how the approach scales to larger, diverse corpora; which linguistic phenomena the model struggles with; and how it handles cross-sentence causal relations.
