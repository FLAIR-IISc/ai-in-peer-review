

# Review for ACL 2017

## Summary and Contributions
This paper proposes a neural network architecture for the task of causal meaning disambiguation, arguing that context encoding is essential for correctly identifying causal relations between events. The authors claim that causality is not limited to specific lexical markers or syntactic constructions, and that previous approaches relying on lexical resources restrict coverage. Their main contributions are: (1) a neural architecture using LSTM to encode contextual information for causal disambiguation; (2) empirical demonstration that this approach outperforms previous state-of-the-art methods on the AltLex corpus; and (3) analysis showing the model successfully handles ambiguous lexical markers that previous approaches misclassified.

## Strengths

1. **Clear identification of a significant limitation in prior work**: The paper correctly identifies a critical flaw in previous causal classification approaches - their reliance on lexical resources and syntactic features, which restricts coverage to the limited set of causal constructions represented in those resources. The authors effectively demonstrate this limitation through examples like "since" and "after" having both causal and temporal meanings, which previous methods fail to disambiguate correctly.

2. **Strong empirical results on a relevant dataset**: The authors demonstrate consistent improvements over the state-of-the-art (Hidey and McKeown, 2016) on both versions of the AltLex corpus. The best configuration ("Pair LSTM Mean Adam") achieves 81.94% F1 for the causal class in the bootstrapping version, outperforming the previous best (B2) by 1.04% F1. This represents a meaningful improvement in a challenging task with limited resources.

3. **Demonstration of context encoding's necessity**: The paper provides compelling evidence that context encoding is crucial for causal disambiguation. Their analysis of ambiguous cases (like "which then" and verbs like "break" that can have causal or non-causal meanings) shows their model correctly classifies examples where previous methods fail, directly supporting their core claim about the importance of contextual information.

4. **Appropriate evaluation design**: The authors carefully evaluate their approach against relevant baselines (B1 and B2), analyze the impact of different padding strategies and optimizers, and provide a balanced assessment of precision/recall tradeoffs. Their analysis of the most frequent connectives (Figure 2) effectively demonstrates their model's ability to handle ambiguous markers.

## Weaknesses

1. **Insufficient justification for architectural choices**: The paper claims LSTM was chosen "because we argue that LSTM has a higher capacity of encoding meaning" but provides no empirical comparison with other architectures (e.g., CNNs, attention mechanisms, or early transformer variants). The authors cite Kruengkrai et al. (2017) as using CNNs, but don't justify why LSTM was superior to other alternatives in their specific task. This weakens the contribution of the proposed architecture.

2. **Inadequate handling of dataset imbalance**: The paper notes the AltLex corpus is imbalanced (Causal:Non-Causal ratios around 1:1.5), yet the evaluation doesn't adequately address how this imbalance affects the results. While they attempt to balance the dataset in Section 4.2, the analysis of this effect is superficial and doesn't provide statistical significance testing for the improvements observed.

3. **Limited comparison to contemporary methods**: The paper only compares against B2 (Hidey and McKeown, 2016) as the "state-of-the-art," but doesn't place their work in the broader context of other recent approaches to causal classification. This makes it difficult to assess the significance of their contribution relative to other methods published around the same time.

4. **Superficial analysis of failure cases**: While the paper provides some examples of correctly classified instances, it lacks a systematic analysis of where the model fails. Understanding the types of errors (e.g., specific lexical markers, syntactic structures, or contextual features that cause misclassification) would provide valuable insights for future work and strengthen the paper's contribution.

## Questions to Authors

1. Could you provide more details about the comparison between LSTM and other neural architectures (e.g., CNNs, attention-based models) that you considered for this task? Why did you conclude LSTM was the most appropriate choice?

2. In Table 7, you report improvements with the balanced dataset, but the McNemar's test only shows significance for the "non bootstrapping" version (p=0.005). Could you elaborate on why the "bootstrapping" version didn't reach statistical significance despite showing a 1.25% improvement in F1?

3. Could you provide more details about the "bootstrapping" process used to create the AltLex corpus? The paper mentions it was created by aligning sentences from Wikipedia, but doesn't explain how they identified causal relations for the bootstrapping phase.
