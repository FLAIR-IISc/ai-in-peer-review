

# Peer Review: "Neural Disambiguation of Causal Lexical Markers based on Context"

## Summary and Main Contributions

This paper addresses the task of causality classification, specifically focusing on disambiguating causal meaning when lexical markers (e.g., "because," "since") can have multiple interpretations. The authors claim that neural networks can effectively encode contextual meaning for causal disambiguation, outperforming previous approaches that relied on syntactic patterns or lexical resources. The main contributions, in order of significance, are:

1. **Insight into the limitations of existing causality classification approaches**: The paper correctly identifies that prior work (e.g., Bethard & Martin, 2008; Mirza & Tonelli, 2016) is constrained by syntactic constructions or lexical resources, which cannot capture the full range of causal expressions. This is a valuable theoretical contribution that correctly challenges the field's assumptions.

2. **Demonstration of neural networks' effectiveness for causal disambiguation**: The authors present a novel LSTM-based architecture that processes causal relations as two events plus a lexical marker, showing that context encoding is sufficient for disambiguation without relying on predefined linguistic features.

3. **Empirical validation on the AltLex corpus**: The paper provides a thorough evaluation showing that their neural approach outperforms the state-of-the-art on both versions of the AltLex corpus, particularly with ambiguous causal markers.

## Strengths

1. **Theoretical insight about causality representation**: The paper's most significant contribution is its critical analysis of prior work, correctly identifying that causality isn't tied to specific syntactic constructions or lexical resources (as shown through the counterexamples in Section 1). This insight is well-supported by the literature review and provides a strong foundation for the authors' neural approach.

2. **Strong empirical results with detailed analysis**: The authors provide a thorough evaluation against relevant baselines (including the method from the corpus creators), with careful attention to the corpus's properties (e.g., class imbalance, bootstrapping effects). The analysis of ambiguous markers like "which then" (Table 8) convincingly demonstrates their model's ability to handle cases that previous methods fail on.

3. **Methodological rigor in architecture design**: The paper properly evaluates multiple neural configurations (Stated Pair LSTM vs. Pair LSTM, different padding strategies, optimizers), providing a solid basis for their final architecture choice. The use of GloVe embeddings without additional linguistic features is particularly compelling as it demonstrates the model's ability to learn from raw contextual information.

## Weaknesses

1. **Lack of statistical significance testing**: The paper claims improvements of 2.13% (F1) and 7.72% (Accuracy) over the state-of-the-art, but provides no statistical testing to confirm these differences are meaningful. Without McNemar's test or similar, the claimed improvements could be due to chance, especially with the relatively small AltLex corpus.

2. **Insufficient handling of class imbalance**: The paper acknowledges the corpus's imbalance (Table 3) but only addresses it through an ad hoc reduction of non-causal instances (Table 6). This approach doesn't reflect real-world scenarios and limits the practical relevance of the results. The authors should have used techniques like stratified sampling or class-weighted loss instead.

3. **Limited interpretability of the neural model**: The paper claims the model "encodes the meaning of the sentence" but provides no evidence of how the model actually learns to represent causal relations. Without attention visualization, feature attribution, or case studies showing what aspects of the input the model attends to, the claim remains unsubstantiated.

4. **Overstatement of novelty**: The paper claims their approach "outperforms the state-of-the-art" but doesn't adequately compare to recent neural approaches for related tasks (e.g., discourse relation classification or temporal relation extraction). The architecture (LSTM with two inputs) is fairly standard, and the paper doesn't clearly distinguish their contribution from existing neural methods for similar problems.

## Questions to Authors

1. Can you provide statistical significance testing (e.g., McNemar's test) for the claimed performance improvements over the state-of-the-art? The current results don't establish whether the differences are meaningful.

2. How did you handle the specific challenge of the "bootstrapping" corpus version, where some lexical markers have different class distributions between train and test? What performance metrics can you provide for these challenging cases?

3. The paper claims the model "encodes the meaning" of causal relations. Can you provide specific examples showing what aspects of the input the model is attending to when making classification decisions (e.g., through attention visualization or feature attribution)?

This paper makes a valuable contribution to understanding that causality classification should not be constrained by syntactic patterns or lexical resources. While the empirical results are promising, the paper would be significantly strengthened by addressing the statistical significance of results, providing more robust handling of class imbalance, and offering interpretability for the neural model. With these improvements, the work would make a strong contribution to computational linguistics and discourse analysis.
