- Paper identifies limitations of prior causality classification approaches that rely on syntactic patterns or lexical resources, arguing these miss many causal expressions.
- Introduces an LSTM-based model that inputs two events plus a lexical marker to disambiguate causal vs. non-causal uses via contextual encoding.
- Empirically outperforms prior work on both versions of the AltLex corpus, especially on ambiguous markers.

- Strong theoretical insight that causality is not tied to fixed syntactic constructions or lexical resources, supported by counterexamples and literature review.
- Thorough empirical evaluation with careful baseline comparisons and analysis of corpus properties (class imbalance, bootstrapping), showing robustness on ambiguous markers.
- Methodological rigor shown by testing multiple neural configurations and demonstrating effectiveness using only GloVe embeddings without added linguistic features.

- No statistical significance testing reported for claimed F1 and accuracy improvements, so gains may be due to chance.
- Class imbalance addressed only via ad hoc reduction of non-causal instances, which undermines real-world applicability and robustness.
- Model interpretability is limited; no attention visualizations, feature attributions, or case studies demonstrate what the model learns about causality.
- Novelty is overstated given the relatively standard LSTM architecture and lack of comparison to recent neural methods for related tasks.

- Provide statistical significance tests (e.g., McNemar) for performance differences versus prior work.
- Clarify handling and per-marker performance for the bootstrapped corpus where training and test distributions differ.
- Supply interpretability analyses (attention maps, feature attributions, or illustrative examples) showing what input aspects drive model decisions.
