

# Review for ACL 2017

## Summary and Main Contributions

This paper proposes a neural network architecture for causality classification, specifically focusing on the disambiguation of causal meaning based on context. The authors argue that causality is not limited to specific syntactic constructions or a fixed set of lexical markers, but depends on contextual interpretation. The main contributions of the work are:

1. A neural network architecture using LSTM for causal meaning disambiguation that outperforms previous state-of-the-art methods on the AltLex corpus.
2. Empirical demonstration that context encoding is necessary for disambiguating causal meaning, particularly for ambiguous lexical markers that previous approaches struggled with.
3. Detailed analysis of the AltLex corpus, highlighting challenges in causal relation classification due to ambiguous lexical markers and the importance of context.

## Strengths

1. The paper addresses a well-defined problem in computational linguistics with clear motivation. The authors effectively demonstrate that causal relations cannot be restricted to specific syntactic constructions or lexical resources, as previously assumed, which provides a strong theoretical foundation for their approach. Their analysis of ambiguous lexical markers (e.g., "since," "after") and the AltLex corpus provides valuable insights into the challenges of causal relation classification.

2. The experimental results are well-presented and demonstrate clear improvements over the state-of-the-art (Hidey and McKeown, 2016). The best configuration achieves a 2.13% improvement in F1 score (Causal class) and 7.72% in Accuracy on the "non bootstrapping" version of the AltLex corpus. The results are statistically significant according to McNemar's test (p=0.005).

3. The paper provides a thorough analysis of cases where their model outperforms the baseline, particularly for ambiguous lexical markers like "which then" that previous approaches failed to handle correctly. The examples in Table 8 clearly demonstrate the model's ability to correctly disambiguate causal meaning in challenging cases where the lexical marker is ambiguous.

## Weaknesses

1. The novelty of the approach is limited. While the authors claim their neural architecture is novel, the use of LSTMs for sequence modeling in classification tasks was well-established by 2017. The specific architecture (two LSTMs with concatenation) is a standard approach that does not provide significant methodological innovation compared to existing neural models for similar tasks.

2. The paper lacks sufficient comparison with alternative neural architectures. The authors claim LSTM is better than CNN (citing Kruengkrai et al., 2017), but they do not provide empirical comparisons with other standard neural approaches (e.g., a single LSTM on the combined input). Without these comparisons, it's unclear whether the specific two-LSTM architecture is necessary or if simpler approaches would suffice.

3. The paper does not adequately address the limitations of the AltLex corpus as a benchmark. The corpus has significant class imbalance (Non Causal instances outnumber Causal instances by 5:1 in the "non bootstrapping" version) and contains some ambiguous examples that may not generalize well to other domains. The paper's success on this specific corpus does not necessarily indicate generalizability to other causal relation classification tasks.

4. The paper's claim that "neural networks are able to encode the meaning of those events, and discover whether the underlying relation between them is causal" is not sufficiently substantiated. The paper does not provide any analysis of what the neural model has actually learned about causal relations, nor does it provide visualizations or interpretability analysis of the encoded representations.

5. The paper does not clearly differentiate their "causal meaning disambiguation" task from previous work on causal relation classification. This makes it difficult to assess the novelty of the problem formulation and whether the paper truly advances the field beyond existing approaches.

## Questions to Authors

1. Could you provide a comparison between your "Pair LSTM" architecture and a simpler approach that uses a single LSTM on the combined input (events + lexical marker)? Would this simpler approach achieve comparable performance?

2. How do you address the class imbalance in the AltLex corpus, and what would be the impact on your model's performance if the corpus were balanced? The paper mentions reducing the Non Causal instances by a factor of ten, but this seems like an artificial adjustment.

3. Could you provide more detailed analysis of what aspects of the context the neural model is actually using to make causal disambiguation decisions? For example, do the learned representations correlate with specific semantic features or lexical properties?
