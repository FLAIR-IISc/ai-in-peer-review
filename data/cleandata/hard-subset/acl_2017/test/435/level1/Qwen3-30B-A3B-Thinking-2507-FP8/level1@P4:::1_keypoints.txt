- Proposes an LSTM-based neural architecture for causal meaning disambiguation that outperforms prior state-of-the-art on the AltLex corpus.  
- Demonstrates that encoding context is necessary to disambiguate causal meaning, especially for ambiguous lexical markers.  
- Provides a detailed analysis of the AltLex corpus, highlighting challenges from ambiguous markers and the importance of context.  

- Well-motivated problem with strong theoretical grounding and useful analysis of ambiguous lexical markers (e.g., "since," "after").  
- Experimental results show statistically significant improvements over Hidey & McKeown (2016) (±2.13% F1 on Causal class, 7.72% Accuracy; McNemar p=0.005).  
- Thorough error analysis with examples (e.g., "which then") where the model better disambiguates causal meaning than the baseline.  

- Limited novelty: two-LSTM with concatenation is a standard architecture and not a major methodological innovation.  
- Lacks empirical comparison with alternative neural architectures (e.g., single LSTM on combined input).  
- Reliance on the AltLex corpus is problematic given severe class imbalance and ambiguous examples that may hurt generalizability.  
- No interpretability analysis or visualization to show what the model has learned about causal relations.  
- Task formulation ("causal meaning disambiguation") is not clearly differentiated from prior causal relation classification work.  

- Could you compare the Pair LSTM to a simpler single-LSTM on combined input to assess necessity of the architecture?  
- How do you handle class imbalance in AltLex and how would balancing the corpus affect performance (is the 10x reduction of Non Causal instances justified)?  
- Can you provide analysis of which contextual features the model uses (e.g., do learned representations correlate with specific semantic or lexical properties)?
