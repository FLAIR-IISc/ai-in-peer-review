**Summary and Contributions**

This paper proposes a neural network architecture for the task of causality classification, specifically focusing on the disambiguation of causal lexical markers based on context. The authors argue that encoding the meaning of the sentence is necessary for disambiguating its causal meaning. They evaluate their system on the AltLex corpus, outperforming the state-of-the-art method.

The main contributions of this work are:

1. A neural network architecture for causality classification that effectively encodes the meaning of the sentence to disambiguate its causal meaning.
2. A comprehensive evaluation of the proposed system on the AltLex corpus, demonstrating its superiority over the state-of-the-art method.
3. An analysis of the performance of the system on different types of causal lexical markers, highlighting its ability to handle ambiguous and unambiguous markers.

**Strengths**

1. The paper presents a well-motivated and clearly explained argument for the importance of encoding the meaning of the sentence in causality classification.
2. The proposed neural network architecture is carefully designed and evaluated, with a thorough analysis of its performance on different types of causal lexical markers.
3. The authors provide a comprehensive comparison with the state-of-the-art method, demonstrating the superiority of their approach.
4. The paper includes a detailed analysis of the results, highlighting the strengths and weaknesses of the proposed system.

**Weaknesses**

1. The paper relies heavily on the AltLex corpus, which may not be representative of all types of causal relations. It would be beneficial to evaluate the system on other corpora to demonstrate its generalizability.
2. The authors do not provide a detailed analysis of the errors made by their system, which could provide valuable insights into its limitations and areas for improvement.
3. The paper could benefit from a more detailed discussion of the implications of the results, particularly in terms of the potential applications of the proposed system.
4. The authors do not provide a clear explanation of how their system handles out-of-vocabulary words, which could be a limitation of the approach.

**Questions to Authors**

1. How do you plan to address the issue of out-of-vocabulary words in your system?
2. Can you provide a more detailed analysis of the errors made by your system, including examples of misclassified instances?
3. How do you plan to extend your system to handle more complex types of causal relations, such as those involving multiple events or entities?
4. Can you provide a more detailed discussion of the potential applications of your system, particularly in terms of its potential impact on natural language processing and artificial intelligence?

**Additional Comments**

The paper is well-written and easy to follow, with clear explanations of the proposed system and its evaluation. The authors provide a thorough analysis of the results, highlighting the strengths and weaknesses of their approach. However, there are some areas for improvement, including the need for a more detailed analysis of errors and a clearer explanation of how the system handles out-of-vocabulary words. Overall, the paper presents a significant contribution to the field of natural language processing and has the potential to impact a wide range of applications.
