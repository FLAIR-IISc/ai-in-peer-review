**Summary of the Paper**

The paper proposes a neural network architecture for the task of causality classification, which involves identifying whether there is a causal relation between two events. The authors argue that the encoding of the meaning of the events is required for disambiguating their causal meaning. They evaluate their system on the AltLex corpus, which is a freely available corpus for causality classification. The system uses a pair of LSTMs to encode the two events of the relation and several dense layers with a tanh activation function to classify the relation as causal or non-causal.

**Contributions of the Paper**

1. **Neural Network Architecture for Causality Classification**: The authors propose a novel neural network architecture for causality classification, which uses a pair of LSTMs to encode the two events of the relation.
2. **Use of Word Embeddings**: The authors use pre-trained word embeddings (GloVe) to represent the input events, which allows the system to generalize to unseen words and contexts.
3. **Evaluation on AltLex Corpus**: The authors evaluate their system on the AltLex corpus, which is a freely available corpus for causality classification.

**Strengths of the Paper**

1. **Improvement over State-of-the-Art**: The authors' system outperforms the state-of-the-art system on the AltLex corpus, demonstrating the effectiveness of their approach.
2. **Ability to Generalize**: The use of word embeddings and the neural network architecture allows the system to generalize to unseen words and contexts.
3. **Robustness to Noise**: The authors' system is robust to noise in the input data, as demonstrated by its ability to correctly classify examples with ambiguous connectives.

**Weaknesses of the Paper**

1. **Limited Evaluation**: The authors only evaluate their system on the AltLex corpus, which may not be representative of all types of causality classification tasks.
2. **Lack of Analysis of Errors**: The authors do not provide a detailed analysis of the errors made by their system, which could provide insights into its limitations.
3. **Dependence on Pre-Trained Embeddings**: The authors' system relies on pre-trained word embeddings, which may not always be available or suitable for all tasks.

**Questions to Authors**

1. How do you plan to extend your system to handle more complex types of causality classification tasks, such as those involving multiple events or entities?
2. Can you provide a more detailed analysis of the errors made by your system, including examples of incorrect classifications and an explanation of why they occurred?
3. How do you plan to address the issue of out-of-vocabulary words, which may not have pre-trained embeddings available?

**Additional Comments**

The authors' system is a promising approach to causality classification, and their evaluation on the AltLex corpus demonstrates its effectiveness. However, further evaluation on more diverse datasets and a more detailed analysis of errors are needed to fully understand the strengths and limitations of their approach.
