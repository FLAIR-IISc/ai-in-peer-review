**Summary of the Paper**

This paper proposes a neural network architecture for the task of causality classification, specifically focusing on the disambiguation of causal lexical markers based on context. The authors argue that the encoding of the meaning of a sentence is required for the disambiguation of its causal meaning. They evaluate their approach on the AltLex corpus and demonstrate that their system outperforms the state-of-the-art.

**Main Contributions**

1. **Novel neural network architecture**: The authors propose a neural network architecture with two inputs, which encodes the two events of a causal relation using LSTM and uses dense layers with a tanh activation function.
2. **Effective use of word embeddings**: The authors demonstrate the effectiveness of using word embeddings (specifically GloVe) to represent the input events and lexical markers.
3. **Improved performance on the AltLex corpus**: The authors show that their system outperforms the state-of-the-art on the AltLex corpus, achieving a higher F1 score and accuracy.

**Strengths**

1. **Innovative approach to causality classification**: The authors' approach to causality classification is novel and effective, demonstrating the potential of neural networks in this task.
2. **Thorough evaluation**: The authors conduct a thorough evaluation of their system, comparing it to two baselines and analyzing the results in detail.
3. **Use of word embeddings**: The authors' use of word embeddings is effective in capturing the semantic meaning of the input events and lexical markers.

**Weaknesses**

1. **Limited corpus size**: The authors' evaluation is limited to the AltLex corpus, which may not be representative of other datasets or domains.
2. **Class imbalance**: The authors' dataset is imbalanced, with a significant difference in the number of instances between the Causal and Non-Causal classes.
3. **Lack of interpretability**: The authors' neural network architecture is complex, and it is difficult to interpret the results or understand why the system makes certain predictions.

**Questions to Authors**

1. How do you plan to address the class imbalance issue in future work?
2. Can you provide more details on the hyperparameter tuning process for the neural network architecture?
3. How do you think the system would perform on other datasets or domains, and what modifications would be necessary to adapt it to these new contexts?

**Additional Comments**

The authors' work is a significant contribution to the field of causality classification, and their approach has the potential to be applied to other tasks in natural language processing. However, further work is needed to address the limitations mentioned above and to evaluate the system's performance on a broader range of datasets and domains.
