## Review of "Neural Disambiguation of Causal Lexical Markers based on Context"

This paper addresses the challenging problem of causal relation classification, specifically focusing on disambiguating the causal meaning of discourse markers. The authors argue that encoding the contextual meaning of a sentence is crucial for this task and propose a neural network architecture based on LSTMs to achieve this. They evaluate their approach on the AltLex corpus, reporting improved performance compared to a baseline system.

The primary contribution of this work, as I see it, lies in the application of a relatively simple LSTM-based architecture to the AltLex corpus, demonstrating its effectiveness for causal disambiguation. While the idea of using neural networks for this task isn't novel, the direct application to this specific dataset, along with a reasonably thorough experimental setup comparing different configurations (stated vs. paired LSTMs, optimizers, padding strategies), provides a valuable baseline and empirical evidence supporting the importance of contextual encoding. A secondary contribution is the insightful discussion of the limitations of existing approaches relying on lexical resources and syntactic constraints, motivating the need for a more flexible, context-aware solution.

**Strengths:**

1.  **Empirical Validation:** The paper provides clear experimental results on a publicly available dataset (AltLex), demonstrating that the proposed LSTM-based model outperforms a strong baseline (Hidey & McKeown, 2016). This provides concrete evidence supporting the authors’ claim.
2.  **Thorough Experimentation:** The authors explore various configurations of their model, including different LSTM architectures, optimizers, and padding strategies. This demonstrates a commitment to optimizing the approach and provides valuable insights into the impact of different design choices.
3.  **Clear Problem Definition & Motivation:** The paper clearly articulates the challenges of causal relation classification and convincingly argues for the need to move beyond lexical and syntactic cues. The discussion of the limitations of previous work is well-articulated.
4. **Analysis of Results**: The analysis section discusses the performance of the model with a particular focus on the challenging cases, providing insight on how to improve the system in the future.

**Weaknesses:**

1.  **Limited Novelty:** The use of LSTMs for sequence encoding is a well-established technique. The novelty primarily resides in the application to the specific AltLex task and dataset, rather than a groundbreaking architectural innovation.
2.  **Lack of Ablation Study:** While various configurations are tested, a systematic ablation study (e.g., removing layers, varying embedding dimensions) would provide a more granular understanding of the contribution of each component.
3. **Data Limitations:** The authors acknowledge the limited size and potential biases of the AltLex corpus. The results may not generalize well to other datasets or domains. Further exploration of the impact of data characteristics is needed.



**Questions to Authors:**

1.  Could you provide more details about the hyperparameter tuning process? Specifically, how were the dropout rates and regularization parameters selected?
2.  The paper mentions difficulties with certain ambiguous connectives. Could you provide specific examples of these cases and analyze why the model struggles with them?
3.  Have you considered incorporating attention mechanisms to allow the model to focus on the most relevant parts of the input sentences?
