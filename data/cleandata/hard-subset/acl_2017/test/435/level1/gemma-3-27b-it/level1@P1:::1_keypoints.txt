- Paper proposes an LSTM-based neural architecture for causal relation classification, claiming sentence-level encoding disambiguates causal meaning and outperforming prior SVM-based work on the AltLex corpus.

Main contributions:
- Empirical demonstration that LSTMs effectively identify causal relations and outperform a previous SVM baseline.
- Emphasis on encoding entire sentence context (events + connecting lexical markers) with LSTMs rather than hand-crafted features.
- Evaluation on the recent, challenging AltLex corpus that tests diverse causal expressions.

Strengths:
- Strong empirical results with consistent and significant F1 improvements, especially in the non-bootstrapping setup.
- Clear motivation and problem framing, arguing limitations of syntactic/lexical-resource approaches.
- Thorough experimentation across LSTM configurations, padding strategies, optimizers, and dropout rates.
- Insightful analysis linking model behavior to specific challenging examples and baseline limitations.

Weaknesses:
- Limited architectural novelty—the contribution is mainly application and empirical validation rather than a new model.
- Lack of a systematic ablation study quantifying individual components’ contributions (e.g., each LSTM in the Stated Pair LSTM).
- Limited error analysis; more comprehensive categorization of failure modes is needed.
- Dependence on pre-trained GloVe embeddings without exploration of alternative embedding strategies or training from scratch.

Questions to authors:
- Can you provide a detailed error analysis categorizing error types and probable causes?
- What is the intuition for connecting the LSTMs in the Stated Pair LSTM, and why didn’t it consistently outperform the Pair LSTM?
- How sensitive is performance to the choice of pre-trained embeddings, and were embeddings trained from scratch considered?
- Do you plan to tackle causal argument identification (not just binary causality classification) in future work?

Additional comments:
- Table 5 is long and hard to read; consider splitting it into smaller, focused tables.
- Reference list is extensive but needs more consistent formatting.
- Introduction would benefit from a more concise summary of related literature.
