This paper presents a neural network approach to causal discourse relation classification, arguing that encoding sentence meaning is key to disambiguating causal markers. The authors claim to outperform the state-of-the-art on the AltLex corpus.

**Contributions:**

1. **Empirical Validation of Contextual Encoding:** The most significant contribution is the demonstration, through empirical results, that a neural network capable of encoding sentence context *does* improve performance on causal relation classification compared to a strong baseline (Hidey & McKeown, 2016). While the idea of leveraging contextual information isn't novel, its successful application and validation on this specific task and dataset is valuable.
2. **Systematic Evaluation of LSTM Architectures:** The paper provides a reasonably thorough comparison of different LSTM configurations (stated vs. paired, different optimizers, padding strategies) for this task. This experimentation, and the resulting insights into the impact of these choices, is a positive contribution.
3. **Focus on a Challenging Dataset:** The use of the AltLex corpus, which explicitly targets the issue of non-canonical causal expressions, is a strength. This dataset provides a good testbed for methods that move beyond relying on a fixed set of causal connectives.

**Strengths:**

1. **Strong Empirical Results:** The reported improvements over the Hidey & McKeown (2016) baseline are significant, especially in terms of F1 score on the causal class. This suggests the proposed method is genuinely effective. The ablation study across different LSTM configurations also helps solidify these findings.
2. **Clear Motivation & Problem Definition:** The paper clearly articulates the problem of causal relation classification and highlights the limitations of previous approaches that rely heavily on lexical resources or syntactic patterns. The argument for the importance of contextual understanding is well-motivated.
3. **Appropriate Methodology:** The use of LSTMs is a reasonable choice given the sequential nature of language and the need to capture long-range dependencies. The experimental setup, including the comparison to a strong baseline and the use of standard evaluation metrics, is sound.
4. **Detailed Analysis:** The inclusion of a detailed analysis section (4.3), with specific examples of correctly and incorrectly classified instances, provides valuable insights into the strengths and weaknesses of the proposed system. The discussion of edge cases, such as the connective "which then," is particularly insightful.
5. **Reproducibility:** The authors provide sufficient details regarding the data preprocessing, model architecture, and training parameters to allow for reasonably easy replication of the results.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Novelty of the Architecture:** While the experimental results are strong, the proposed neural network architecture (LSTMs with dense layers) is relatively standard. The novelty lies more in the *application* of this architecture to the specific task and dataset, rather than in the architecture itself. This doesn't necessarily invalidate the contribution, but it limits the paper's impact.
2. **Insufficient Comparison to Stronger Neural Baselines:** While Hidey & McKeown (2016) is a good baseline, the field of NLP has advanced significantly since that work. It would be stronger to compare the proposed system to more recent, state-of-the-art neural models specifically designed for relation classification or discourse parsing (e.g., transformers-based models). This would better establish the true contribution of the work.
3. **Data Imbalance:** The authors acknowledge the class imbalance in the AltLex corpus but only address it with a post-hoc reduction in the non-causal examples. This could introduce bias. More sophisticated techniques for handling class imbalance (e.g., weighted loss functions, oversampling, or undersampling) might yield further improvements.
4. **Lack of Error Analysis Detail:** The error analysis in section 4.3 is helpful, but it could be more systematic. For example, categorizing the types of errors (e.g., errors due to ambiguous connectives, errors due to complex syntactic structures) would provide a more nuanced understanding of the system's limitations.
5. **Limited Discussion of Counterfactual Reasoning:** While the paper correctly dismisses the strict counterfactual constraint, it doesn’t explore how counterfactual reasoning *might* be incorporated into the model. Even a brief discussion of this would strengthen the paper.

**Questions to Authors:**

1.  Have you experimented with transformer-based models (e.g., BERT, RoBERTa) for this task? If so, how do their performance compare to the LSTM-based model?
2.  Did you explore any techniques for handling the class imbalance in the AltLex corpus beyond simply reducing the number of non-causal examples? If not, why?
3.  Can you provide a more detailed breakdown of the types of errors made by the system? For example, what percentage of errors are due to ambiguous connectives, and what percentage are due to other factors?
4.  The paper argues against requiring a strict counterfactual constraint. However, could a model benefit from *some* understanding of counterfactual relationships, even if it's not a hard requirement? Have you considered incorporating any features or mechanisms to capture this information?

**Additional Comments:**

*   The writing quality is generally good, but some sentences are a bit verbose.
*   Figure 1 is helpful, but the text references it before the figure is presented.
*   The references section is extensive and well-organized.



Overall, this is a solid paper that makes a valuable contribution to the field of causal discourse relation classification. The empirical results are compelling, and the analysis is insightful. While the architecture itself is not highly novel, the successful application of LSTMs to this challenging task and dataset is a positive achievement. Addressing the weaknesses outlined above would further strengthen the paper and increase its impact. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions.
