Contributions:
- Empirical validation that contextual sentence encoding with a neural network improves causal relation classification over a strong baseline.
- Systematic evaluation of different LSTM configurations (stated vs paired, optimizers, padding) with insights on their impact.
- Focused use of the AltLex corpus to address non-canonical causal expressions beyond fixed causal connectives.

Strengths:
- Strong empirical results with significant F1 gains on the causal class and supportive ablation studies.
- Clear motivation and problem definition highlighting limits of lexical/syntactic approaches.
- Appropriate methodology: LSTMs fit the task and experiments use sound baselines and metrics.
- Detailed analysis including concrete examples and discussion of edge cases (e.g., "which then").
- Good reproducibility: sufficient preprocessing, architecture, and training detail provided.

Weaknesses:
- Limited novelty: the architecture (LSTMs + dense layers) is standard; novelty is mainly in application.
- Insufficient comparison to recent neural baselines (e.g., transformer-based models).
- Class imbalance handled only by reducing non-causal examples, which may introduce bias.
- Error analysis is helpful but not systematic; lacks categorized error breakdowns.
- Little discussion or exploration of incorporating counterfactual reasoning into the model.

Questions to authors:
- Have you experimented with transformer-based models (BERT/RoBERTa) and how do they compare?
- Did you try other class-imbalance techniques (weighted loss, oversampling, undersampling) beyond reduction?
- Can you provide a detailed breakdown of error types (e.g., ambiguous connectives vs syntactic issues)?
- Could partial counterfactual understanding benefit the model, and was any mechanism for this considered?

Additional comments:
- Writing is generally good but sometimes verbose.
- Figure 1 is referenced before it appears.
- References are extensive and well-organized.

Overall recommendation:
- Solid paper with compelling empirical contributions; recommend acceptance with minor revisions.
