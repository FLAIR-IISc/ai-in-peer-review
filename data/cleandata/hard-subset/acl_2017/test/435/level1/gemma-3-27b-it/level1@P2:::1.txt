This paper proposes a neural network architecture for causal discourse relation classification, arguing that encoding sentence meaning is crucial for disambiguating causal meanings. The authors evaluate their approach on the AltLex corpus, reporting state-of-the-art results.

**Contributions:**

1. **Empirical Performance on AltLex:** The primary contribution is demonstrating strong performance on the challenging AltLex corpus, outperforming the previous state-of-the-art (Hidey & McKeown, 2016). This is significant as AltLex specifically targets the issue of non-explicit causal markers, a known weakness in prior work.
2. **Justification for Contextual Encoding:** The results support the authors’ hypothesis that encoding the full sentence context is valuable for causal disambiguation, moving beyond reliance on specific lexical markers or syntactic patterns. This reinforces the idea that causal reasoning requires a deeper understanding of the conveyed meaning.
3. **Exploration of LSTM Architectures:** The paper offers a solid comparison of different LSTM configurations (stated pair vs. pair) and optimization algorithms (Adadelta vs. Adam), providing insights into architectural choices for this task.

**Strengths:**

1. **Strong Empirical Results:** The consistent outperformance of the proposed models compared to the baseline and the state-of-the-art (as reported in Table 5) is a compelling argument for the effectiveness of the approach. The reported improvements, while not massive, are statistically significant and demonstrate a clear advancement.
2. **Relevant Task and Dataset:** The focus on causal discourse relation classification, particularly with the use of the AltLex corpus, addresses a crucial challenge in natural language understanding. AltLex is a valuable resource specifically designed to tackle the limitations of relying solely on explicit causal markers.
3. **Clear Motivation and Hypothesis:** The paper articulates a clear motivation for the work—the limitations of existing approaches to causality—and presents a well-defined hypothesis about the importance of contextual encoding.
4. **Thorough Experimentation:** The authors conduct a relatively comprehensive set of experiments, varying LSTM architectures, optimizers, and zero-padding strategies. This demonstrates a commitment to rigorous evaluation and understanding the impact of different design choices.
5. **Analysis of Results:** The inclusion of analysis, particularly the discussion of results on specific challenging cases (e.g., the “which then” example in Section 4.3), strengthens the paper by providing insights into *why* the system performs well (or struggles).

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Novelty of the Architecture:** The proposed LSTM-based architecture, while effective, isn't particularly novel. LSTMs are a standard choice for sequence modeling, and the specific configuration doesn't introduce groundbreaking new techniques. The novelty primarily lies in the application to this specific task and dataset.
2. **Insufficient Discussion of Error Analysis:** While some examples of correctly/incorrectly classified instances are provided, a more in-depth error analysis would be beneficial. Understanding *where* the model still fails and *why* could suggest avenues for future improvement. What types of causal relations are particularly challenging?
3. **Limited Comparison to More Recent Models:** The state-of-the-art comparison is based on a 2016 paper. A more comprehensive evaluation would include comparisons to more recent advancements in neural discourse relation classification, potentially leveraging pre-trained language models (e.g., BERT, RoBERTa).
4. **Dependence on Word Embeddings:** The performance is highly reliant on the quality of the pre-trained word embeddings (GloVe). The paper doesn’t explore the impact of using different embedding types or fine-tuning the embeddings during training.
5. **Lack of Ablation Study:** An ablation study, systematically removing components of the architecture to assess their individual contributions, would further clarify the effectiveness of different design choices.

**Questions to Authors:**

1. Have you experimented with more recent pre-trained language models (e.g., BERT, RoBERTa) to represent the input sentences? If so, what were the results?
2. Can you provide a more detailed error analysis, categorizing the types of errors the system makes and offering potential explanations for those errors?
3. What is the computational cost of your proposed architecture compared to the baseline method (Hidey & McKeown, 2016)?
4. Did you consider adding an attention mechanism to the LSTM layers to allow the model to focus on the most relevant parts of the input?

**Additional Comments:**

The paper is well-written and clearly presented. The motivation for the work is well-established, and the experimental results are convincing. While the architectural novelty is limited, the empirical performance and the focused application to the challenging AltLex corpus make this a worthwhile contribution. The suggested improvements would further strengthen the paper.
