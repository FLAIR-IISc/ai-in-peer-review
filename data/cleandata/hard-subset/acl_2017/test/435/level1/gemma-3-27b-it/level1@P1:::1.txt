This paper proposes a neural network architecture for causal relation classification, arguing that encoding sentence meaning is key to disambiguating causal meaning. The authors outperform the state-of-the-art on the AltLex corpus.

**Main Contributions:**

1. **Empirical Demonstration of Neural Networks for Causal Disambiguation:** The paper provides strong empirical evidence that neural networks, specifically LSTMs, are effective in identifying causal relations, outperforming a previous SVM-based approach. This demonstrates the viability of a neural approach to a challenging NLP task.
2. **Focus on Contextual Encoding:** The core contribution lies in the explicit focus on encoding the entire sentence context (both events and connecting lexical markers) using LSTMs, as opposed to relying on feature engineering or lexical resources. This highlights the importance of holistic sentence representation for understanding causality.
3. **Evaluation on a Recent, Challenging Dataset:** The evaluation is performed on the AltLex corpus, a relatively new dataset specifically designed to address the limitations of previous corpora regarding coverage of diverse causal expressions. This provides a more realistic and demanding testbed for the proposed approach.

**Strengths:**

1. **Strong Empirical Results:** The paper consistently outperforms the baseline (Hidey & McKeown, 2016) on the AltLex corpus, demonstrating the effectiveness of the proposed LSTM-based architecture.  The F1 score improvements, particularly in the “non bootstrapping” version, are significant.
2. **Clear Motivation and Problem Definition:** The paper effectively articulates the challenges in causality classification, highlighting the limitations of earlier approaches that relied on syntactic structures or predefined lexical resources. The motivation for exploring a neural network approach is well justified.
3. **Thorough Experimentation:** The authors explore various configurations of the LSTM network (stated vs. paired, different padding strategies, optimizers, dropout rates) demonstrating a diligent effort to optimize performance. The ablation studies (implicitly through comparison of different configurations) provide insights into the importance of different design choices.
4. **Insightful Analysis of Results:** The analysis of results goes beyond simply reporting numbers. The authors discuss the performance on challenging cases (e.g., the "which then" example) and relate it back to the limitations of the baseline approach and the strengths of their model. This shows a deep understanding of the task and the data.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Novelty in Architecture:** While the application to causal disambiguation is valuable, the core LSTM architecture itself is not particularly novel. The contribution resides more in the application and the demonstration of its effectiveness in this specific task, rather than a fundamentally new architectural design.
2. **Lack of Detailed Ablation Study:** While several configurations are tested, a more systematic ablation study would strengthen the paper. For example, it would be valuable to analyze the individual contributions of the two LSTMs in the “Stated Pair LSTM” architecture more explicitly. Quantifying how much each LSTM contributes to the final performance would be helpful.
3. **Limited Error Analysis:** While the authors present some examples of misclassifications, a more comprehensive error analysis would be beneficial.  Identifying the types of errors the model makes (e.g., misclassifying specific lexical markers, failing to capture long-range dependencies) could reveal potential areas for future improvement.
4. **Dependence on Pre-trained Word Embeddings:** The reliance on pre-trained word embeddings (GloVe) introduces a potential dependency on the quality and coverage of those embeddings. Exploring the impact of different embedding strategies (e.g., training embeddings from scratch on the AltLex corpus) could be worthwhile.



**Questions to Authors:**

1.  Could you provide a more detailed error analysis, categorizing the types of errors your model makes and identifying potential reasons for those errors?
2.  You mention the “Stated Pair LSTM” and “Pair LSTM” architectures. Could you elaborate on the intuition behind connecting the LSTMs in the former architecture, and why it didn't consistently outperform the latter?
3.  How sensitive is the performance of your model to the choice of pre-trained word embeddings? Have you experimented with training embeddings from scratch on the AltLex corpus?
4.  The paper focuses on the binary classification of causal meaning. Do you have any plans to address the causal argument identification subtask in future work?
