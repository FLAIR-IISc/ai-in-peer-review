### Summary and Contributions

This paper addresses the problem of mention ambiguity in joint embedding models that learn representations for words, entities, and mentions from text and a knowledge base (KB). The core issue is that a single mention phrase (e.g., "Apple") can refer to multiple distinct entities (Apple Inc., apple fruit). The authors propose a novel "Multi-Prototype Mention Embedding" (MPME) model to tackle this. The model learns multiple vector representations (prototypes or senses) for each ambiguous mention by jointly optimizing objectives based on textual context (word co-occurrence) and KB structure (entity relations). Additionally, the paper presents a language model-based approach to disambiguate a mention in a given context to its most appropriate learned sense. The authors evaluate their learned embeddings both intrinsically and extrinsically, demonstrating state-of-the-art performance on a benchmark entity linking task.

In my view, the main contributions of this work are:

1.  **A Novel Multi-Prototype Joint Embedding Model:** The primary contribution is the MPME model, which explicitly models mention ambiguity in a unified text-KB semantic space. It extends the ideas from multi-prototype word embedding models to the more complex setting of jointly learning from unstructured text and a structured KB, which is a logical and valuable step forward for this line of research.
2.  **An Integrated Disambiguation Method:** The paper provides a complete solution by not only learning ambiguous representations but also proposing a concrete method for disambiguating mentions in context. This demonstrates the practical utility of the learned embeddings for downstream tasks like entity linking.

### Strengths

1.  **Well-Motivated and Timely Problem:** The ambiguity of mentions is a well-known and critical bottleneck in entity linking and knowledge base population. While prior work has focused on joint embeddings of text and KBs, most models implicitly learn a single, averaged representation for each mention, conflating its different meanings. Directly addressing this limitation by introducing multiple prototypes is a significant and timely contribution that could benefit many downstream applications.
2.  **Novel and Sound Approach:** The proposed solution is a clever synthesis of two important research threads: multi-prototype word embeddings (e.g., Neelakantan et al., 2014) and joint text-KB embedding models (e.g., Wang et al., 2014; Han et al., 2016). Applying the multi-prototype concept to mentions, which are explicitly linked to KB entities, is a non-trivial extension. The joint objective function, which models both textual context and KB relations to inform the sense induction, is a sound and powerful approach to learning high-quality, disambiguated representations.
3.  **Comprehensive Evaluation Plan:** The authors claim to evaluate their model with both qualitative analysis (presumably visualizing the learned sense clusters) and quantitative analysis of the embeddings themselves. More importantly, they demonstrate the model's effectiveness on a challenging downstream task, entity linking, and claim state-of-the-art results. This extrinsic evaluation is crucial as it grounds the work in a practical application and provides strong evidence for the utility of the proposed method.

### Weaknesses

1.  **Lack of Clarity on the Disambiguation Mechanism:** The abstract describes the disambiguation component as an "efficient language model based approach." This description is too vague. It is unclear how this model works. Is it trained jointly with the embedding model or separately? How does it select the appropriate sense vector for a mention given its context? Is it a simple skip-gram-style context scoring, or something more sophisticated? The effectiveness of the entire system hinges on this component, and its details are critical for understanding and replicating the work.
2.  **Insufficient Positioning Against Related Work:** While the core idea seems novel, the paper needs to be very clear about how it builds upon and differs from its predecessors. For instance, is the model a straightforward application of the non-parametric method from Neelakantan et al. (2014) on top of an existing joint model like that of Han et al. (2016), or is the integration more deeply coupled? How does this work compare to other entity linking models that use neural methods to model context (e.g., Yamada et al., 2016) but may not learn explicit prototypes? A more detailed comparison is needed to precisely situate the paper's contribution.
3.  **Potential Scalability and Hyperparameter Concerns:** Learning multiple prototypes for every mention in a large vocabulary could be computationally intensive. The paper should address the model's scalability. A key detail missing from the abstract is how the number of prototypes per mention is determined. Is it a fixed hyperparameter that requires tuning? Or is it learned dynamically from the data? The choice has significant implications for the model's complexity and practicality.
4.  **Presentation and Formatting Issues:** The provided reference list is severely corrupted, with repeated entries, garbled text, and inconsistent formatting. This suggests a lack of care in the preparation of the manuscript and makes it very difficult for a reviewer to verify citations and understand the context of the work. While this may be a technical artifact, it is a serious issue that must be fixed.

### Questions to Authors

1.  Could you please provide a more detailed description of the "language model based approach" for disambiguation? Specifically, what is the model architecture, what is its objective function, and how does it utilize the learned multi-prototype embeddings at inference time?
2.  How do you determine the number of senses/prototypes for each mention? Is this a global hyperparameter `K`, or is it variable and determined automatically by the model during training? If it is a hyperparameter, how sensitive are the final entity linking results to its value?
3.  To better isolate the contribution of the multi-prototype approach, could you provide an ablation study or comparison against a strong baseline that uses your same joint embedding framework but with only a *single* prototype per mention? This would clearly demonstrate the performance gain attributable to your core idea.
