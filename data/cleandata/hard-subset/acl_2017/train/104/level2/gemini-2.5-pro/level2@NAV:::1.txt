**Summary and Contributions**

This paper addresses the key challenge of mention ambiguity in models that jointly embed text and knowledge bases. The authors propose a novel Multi-Prototype Mention Embedding model that learns multiple sense embeddings for each mention phrase by integrating information from both textual contexts and a knowledge base. The main contributions of this work are:

1.  A new model for learning multi-prototype embeddings for entity mentions, which jointly leverages textual context and knowledge base structure to create distinct representations for different senses of a mention.
2.  An efficient language model-based disambiguation method that utilizes these learned embeddings to select the correct sense in a given context, demonstrating state-of-the-art performance on a downstream entity linking task.

**Strengths**

1.  **Significant Problem Formulation:** The paper tackles a well-recognized and important problem. While multi-prototype embeddings for general words and joint text-KB embeddings exist, this work provides a well-motivated synthesis of these ideas to specifically address the nuanced problem of mention ambiguity, which is a critical bottleneck in entity linking and knowledge base population.
2.  **Novel and Sound Approach:** The core idea of learning distinct sense embeddings for mentions by jointly optimizing over textual and knowledge-based signals is novel and technically sound. This approach has the potential to produce more robust and context-aware representations than methods that learn a single, averaged embedding for an ambiguous mention.
3.  **Comprehensive Evaluation:** The authors claim a thorough evaluation strategy, including both intrinsic (qualitative and quantitative analysis of the embeddings) and extrinsic (application to entity linking) validation. Achieving state-of-the-art results on a standard benchmark task is a strong signal of the method's effectiveness and practical utility.

**Weaknesses**

1.  **Lack of Architectural Detail (in Abstract):** The abstract provides a high-level overview but lacks crucial details about the model architecture and training process. Key aspects, such as how the number of prototypes per mention is determined and the precise objective function for joint learning, are unclear. These details are essential for assessing the novelty and reproducibility of the work.
2.  **Ambiguity in Disambiguation Method:** The proposed language model-based disambiguation method is described as "efficient" but not explained. Its novelty and specific mechanism are not detailed, making it difficult to evaluate its contribution relative to the multi-prototype embeddings themselves.
3.  **Unclear Comparison to Baselines:** While "state-of-the-art" is claimed, the abstract does not specify which recent models were used for comparison. A convincing SOTA claim requires comparison against strong, contemporary baselines that also attempt to address entity ambiguity.

**Questions to Authors**

1.  Could you elaborate on the architecture of the language model used for disambiguation and how it specifically incorporates the learned multi-prototype embeddings to make its predictions?
2.  How is the number of prototypes for each mention determined? Is it a fixed hyperparameter, or is it learned from the data (e.g., based on the number of distinct entities a mention is linked to in the knowledge base)?
