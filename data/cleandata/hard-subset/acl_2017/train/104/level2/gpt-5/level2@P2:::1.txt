Summary and contributions
- This paper proposes Multi-Prototype Mention Embeddings (MPME): for each surface form (mention), the model learns multiple sense-specific embeddings by jointly modeling textual context words and entities from a KB in a unified space. A language-model-based disambiguation method is used to select the appropriate sense for a mention in context. The approach is evaluated on entity linking (EL), with claims of state-of-the-art results.
- As I see it, the main contributions are:
  1) A joint embedding framework that induces multiple sense embeddings per mention by integrating distributional context with KB entity signals.
  2) An efficient disambiguation procedure that maps a mention occurrence to one of its induced prototypes using a language-model/scoring mechanism.
  3) An empirical demonstration on EL that suggests gains over existing methods (pending clarity on datasets and baselines).

Strengths
- Clear problem focus on mention ambiguity: Many prior joint text–KB embedding works treat words and entities, but surface forms are often neglected or treated monosemously. Explicitly modeling mentions with multiple prototypes is well-motivated and can benefit EL and other tasks that must bridge text to KB entities.
- Joint training across words, mentions, and entities is potentially impactful: Unifying these three types in one embedding space could improve candidate ranking and contextual scoring. If done efficiently at scale, this is a practical contribution for downstream systems.
- Simplicity/efficiency of disambiguation: A language-model-based scoring that selects among learned mention senses may be fast and easy to integrate into existing EL pipelines, which often suffer from heavy collective inference.
- Reported strong empirical results: The abstract claims state-of-the-art EL performance. If corroborated with robust baselines on standard datasets (e.g., AIDA-CoNLL), this would be a significant result.
- Potential generality: Multi-prototype mention representations might transfer to related tasks (NER+linking, query understanding, QA), which increases the paper’s significance.

Weaknesses
- Novelty relative to prior work is unclear: Multi-prototype embeddings are well-studied (e.g., Reisinger & Mooney 2010; Huang et al. 2012; Neelakantan et al. 2014; Tian et al. 2014). Joint embeddings of words/entities have multiple precedents (Weston et al. 2013; Wang et al. 2014; Toutanova et al. 2015; Yamada et al. 2016; Mancini et al. 2016; Chen et al. 2014). The paper should make precise what is technically new: Is the novelty the sense induction over mentions specifically (distinct from words), the joint objective tying mention-senses to entities, or the LM-based selection? As written, the contribution risks appearing incremental without clearer distinctions and ablations.
- Disambiguation seems local-only and may ignore global coherence: Many strong EL systems leverage collective inference/graph coherence among entities in a document (Kulkarni et al. 2009; Hoffart et al. 2011; Alhelbawy & Gaizauskas 2014; Pershina et al. 2015). If the proposed LM-based method scores each mention independently, it may underperform on documents with multiple mutually constraining mentions. The paper should either incorporate a coherence term or demonstrate strong performance in scenarios where coherence matters, with comparisons to collective baselines.
- Empirical setup is under-specified in the submission: The abstract mentions “benchmark dataset” (singular) and “state-of-the-art,” but it is essential to evaluate on standard EL benchmarks (AIDA-CoNLL test splits, TAC KBP if applicable) with common metrics, report micro/macro accuracy or in-KB accuracy, and compare to strong baselines (Hoffart 2011; Ceccarelli 2013; Yamada 2016/2017; Huang et al. 2015). Without details, the strength of the empirical claim cannot be assessed.
- Sense inventory and model capacity: How many prototypes per mention? Is K fixed per mention, learned, or determined nonparametrically? Mention frequency is highly skewed; a fixed K risks overfitting common mentions and noise for rare ones. The paper should detail the clustering/assignment mechanism, regularization to prevent sense proliferation, and handling of tail mentions and NIL cases.
- Objective and training details are insufficiently clear: The references suggest a skip-gram/negative-sampling style objective, but the paper should precisely define how words, mentions, and entities co-train (which co-occurrences are optimized, how entities are injected, negative sampling strategy, candidate generation pipeline). Without these, it’s hard to reproduce or to attribute the gains to the proposed components.
- Presentation and citation quality: The references list contains many duplicates and typographical errors; some entries are garbled. This detracts from clarity and makes it difficult to verify positioning versus prior work.

Questions to authors
- How is the number of prototypes per mention determined? Fixed K for all mentions, frequency-dependent, or nonparametric (e.g., online clustering with a threshold)? What hyperparameters control sense splitting/merging?
- Are mention prototypes explicitly tied to KB entities (one prototype per entity) or are they latent clusters that may cover multiple entities? If the latter, how do you bridge from prototype to entity candidates at test time?
- Please provide the exact training objective(s): which triples/pairs are optimized (word–mention, mention–entity, word–entity?), the sampling scheme, and how contexts are constructed. Do you pretrain any components?
- Disambiguation: What is the “language model” concretely? Is it a simple context-embedding scoring function or a trained LM? Does it incorporate document-level coherence across multiple mentions? If not, can your scoring be combined with a global coherence term, and do you report such results?
- Datasets and baselines: Which datasets (AIDA-CoNLL test A/B), which metrics, and which baselines (Hoffart 2011; Ceccarelli 2013; Pershina 2015; Yamada 2016/2017; Huang 2015) are included? Do you compare to joint word-entity embedding baselines and to multi-sense word embedding baselines? Please include significance testing.
- Ablations: What is the gain from multi-prototype vs single-prototype mentions? From joint training vs training mentions alone on text? From the proposed LM-based disambiguation vs a standard cosine/softmax scoring?
- Efficiency: What are training/inference complexities and wall-clock times? How does candidate generation work (Aho–Corasick/dictionary spotting?), and does it limit recall?

Additional comments
- Please fix the references and ensure complete, deduplicated, and correctly formatted citations; add missing recent EL baselines if omitted.
- Include error analysis to characterize when multi-prototype helps (e.g., highly ambiguous mentions) and when it fails (rare entities, NILs, long-tail contexts).
- Consider adding a simple document-level coherence module to complement your efficient local scorer; even a lightweight pairwise coherence term could strengthen results.
- If possible, release code/embeddings to facilitate reproducibility.

Overall, the paper targets an important problem and proposes a plausible, practical approach. With clearer methodological details, stronger positioning vs prior work, comprehensive evaluation with ablations and collective baselines, and improved presentation, this submission could be significantly strengthened.
