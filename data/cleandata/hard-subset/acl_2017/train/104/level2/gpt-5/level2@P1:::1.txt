Summary and claimed contributions
This paper proposes Multi-Prototype Mention Embeddings (MPME), a model that learns multiple sense embeddings for each surface mention by jointly modeling its textual contexts and linked entities from a knowledge base. A lightweight language-model-based disambiguator selects the appropriate sense for a new mention occurrence, and the authors demonstrate the approach on entity linking (EL), reporting state-of-the-art performance along with qualitative analyses of the learned word/entity/mention-sense spaces.

As I see it, the main contributions are:
1) A mention-centric multi-prototype embedding framework that integrates textual context and KB entity signals into a single space, enabling fine-grained representations of ambiguous mention surfaces.
2) An efficient context language model for mention sense selection/disambiguation that pairs naturally with the learned embeddings.
3) An empirical study on EL showing improved accuracy over strong baselines and prior joint text-KB or multi-sense embedding methods, with qualitative evidence of coherent sense clusters.

Strengths
- Clear problem focus and useful abstraction: modeling ambiguity at the mention level. Prior work predominantly addresses multi-sense at the word type level (e.g., Huang et al., Neelakantan et al., Tian et al.) or joint text–KB embeddings (e.g., Wang et al., Toutanova et al., Han et al.) without explicitly creating multiple prototypes tied to the same surface mention form. The explicit mention-level sense inventory fills a practical gap for EL, where surface forms are the primary interface.
- Empirical gains on a key downstream task. The paper reports state-of-the-art performance on standard EL benchmarks (details needed, see Questions), which is a strong signal of utility. The improvements seem particularly meaningful for highly ambiguous mentions, aligning with the model’s design. The approach compares against competitive baselines (e.g., graph/collective methods and neural EL systems) and appears to offer favorable speed-accuracy tradeoffs via the LM-based disambiguator.
- Joint use of textual and KB signals. The training ties context words with candidate entities, helping anchor sense clusters to real-world entities and reducing purely distributional drift. The qualitative analyses (nearest neighbors, sense visualizations) suggest the learned space captures both semantic and entity-level relatedness, which can benefit other tasks beyond EL.
- Efficient inference. The disambiguation component appears simple and fast, which is attractive for deployment, especially compared to heavier collective inference. If the reported speedups hold, the method could be a practical alternative in high-throughput settings.
- Sensible and extensible design. The framework is conceptually compatible with many candidate generation strategies and could incorporate richer global constraints or priors if desired, making it a promising foundation.

Weaknesses
- Novelty relative to prior multi-sense and joint embeddings needs sharper positioning. While the mention-centric lens is compelling, the technical differences from earlier multi-prototype embeddings (e.g., clustering, nonparametric senses, parametric sense selection) and from joint text-KB models are not fully clear from the current description. It would help to articulate what is fundamentally new in the objective/architecture and why that difference matters empirically (beyond applying prior ideas at the mention level).
- Sense inventory specification and learning dynamics are under-specified. Is the number of senses per mention fixed or adaptive? How do you avoid sense collapse or proliferation, especially for frequent mentions with many candidate entities? What regularization or priors control granularity? Without such details, it is hard to assess robustness across head/tail mentions.
- Disambiguation method clarity and the role of global coherence. The “language model based” disambiguator needs more detail: context windowing, smoothing, negative sampling, and how candidate entity priors are incorporated. EL benefits from document-level coherence; if the method is purely local, please clarify, and quantify the gap to collective/global methods. If pseudo-global signals (e.g., context pooling) are used, they should be described and ablated.
- Ablations and diagnostics are insufficient. To understand what drives the gains, please include: single- vs multi-prototype mentions; text-only vs KB-augmented training; varying the number of senses; removing the LM vs using standard dot-product similarity; and swapping candidate generation. Also report performance across ambiguity buckets, head vs tail mentions, and NIL cases if applicable. Statistical significance would strengthen SOTA claims.
- Evaluation scope and generalization. The method is motivated broadly as unifying text and knowledge, but evaluation is limited to EL. A small supplemental study on a second task (e.g., word sense disambiguation, fine-grained entity typing, or relation extraction) would help establish broader value and when multi-prototype mentions help (cf. Li & Jurafsky, 2015).
- Presentation and references. The reference list in the review copy contains numerous duplicates and garbled entries. While this is a minor issue, it impedes readers from verifying related work and suggests the need for a careful pass on formatting.

Questions to authors
1) How is the number of senses per mention determined? Fixed K per mention, shared K across mentions, or adaptive (e.g., nonparametric)? What criteria stop new senses from being created?
2) What is the exact training objective? Is it skip-gram/CBOW-like with negative sampling? How are entities incorporated—are they predicted like context words, or coupled via a joint loss? How do you prevent sense collapse?
3) Candidate generation and mention detection: which resources and algorithms are used (the references suggest dictionary-based matching with Aho–Corasick/double-array)? What are recall/precision tradeoffs and their impact on final EL scores?
4) Disambiguation details: please specify the language model (unigram/bigram/NN-based), context selection, smoothing, and whether entity prior (p(entity|mention)) is used. How are candidate senses scored and mapped to entities at test time?
5) Do you model document-level coherence (e.g., global or collective inference)? If not, how does your method compare to collective systems under a controlled setting? Could your embeddings be plugged into a collective reranker?
6) Datasets, baselines, and metrics: which EL benchmarks (e.g., AIDA-CoNLL, TAC, WNED) are used? Are results micro/macro, in-KB-only vs NIL-aware? Which baselines were tuned, and did you run significance tests?
7) Resource footprint and scalability: approximate number of mention senses learned, memory usage, and training/inference time. How do you handle rare mentions and OOV surface forms?

Additional comments
- Please add ablations and error analysis to clarify when multi-prototype mentions help most (e.g., fine-grained senses, long contexts, tail entities).
- Consider reporting transfer to non-Wikipedia text to assess robustness.
- The manuscript would benefit from a careful pass on formatting and references to remove duplicates/corruption and to ensure all baselines are properly cited.
- If possible, releasing code and pretrained embeddings would materially increase impact.

Overall evaluation
A well-motivated and practically useful approach with promising empirical results on EL. With clearer positioning relative to prior multi-sense/joint embedding work, stronger ablations, and improved presentation, this paper would be a strong contribution. I lean positive, contingent on addressing the methodological clarity and evaluation depth.
