Summary and claimed contributions
This paper proposes Multi-Prototype Mention Embeddings (MPME), which learn multiple sense-specific embeddings for each surface-form mention by jointly modeling textual contexts and knowledge base (KB) entities. A lightweight language-model-based disambiguator assigns each mention occurrence to a learned sense. The approach is evaluated qualitatively and quantitatively, with an entity linking (EL) study claiming state-of-the-art performance on a benchmark dataset.

Contributions (as I see them), ordered by strength:
1) A joint representation space that includes words, entities, and multi-prototype mention embeddings, designed to address mention ambiguity in EL.
2) An efficient disambiguation procedure that selects a mention sense using local language modeling.
3) An empirical study indicating improvements on a standard EL benchmark.

Strengths
- Problem focus and practicality: Explicitly modeling mention-level ambiguity is well-motivated for EL, where surface forms (not just words) are inherently polysemous. The proposed representation could be immediately useful to practitioners building EL systems.
- Joint learning with KB supervision: Integrating entity signals into multi-prototype learning is a sensible extension beyond classic multi-sense word embeddings, likely improving alignment between textual context and entity semantics.
- Efficiency considerations: The inclusion of dictionary-based matching and a simple language model disambiguator suggests a deployment-friendly path, potentially offering good accuracy–efficiency trade-offs.
- Empirical promise: The claim of state-of-the-art EL results—if substantiated on widely used datasets and metrics—indicates potential impact.

Weaknesses
- Novelty relative to prior work: Multi-prototype embeddings and joint text–KB embeddings are well-studied. The paper needs to clarify what is substantively new versus prior sense/mention embedding and neural EL methods, and provide a stronger positioning.
- Incomplete empirical detail: The abstract does not specify datasets (e.g., AIDA-CoNLL), metrics (e.g., micro accuracy, In-KB F1, nDCG), baselines (strong collective and neural EL models), or significance tests. Ablations isolating the benefit of multi-prototype mentions, KB supervision, and the disambiguator are essential.
- Reproducibility: It is unclear how the number of prototypes per mention is chosen (fixed K vs. nonparametric), how candidates are generated, training objectives, hyperparameters, and efficiency metrics. Code/data release would help.
- Bibliography quality: The references appear duplicated and error-prone, and seem to omit several relevant recent EL/embedding works, suggesting gaps in literature coverage.

Questions to authors
- Which EL benchmarks and metrics were used, and how do you compare to strong recent baselines? Please report statistical significance.
- How is the number of prototypes per mention determined? Is there a nonparametric mechanism?
- What is the exact training objective and negative sampling strategy for jointly learning words, mentions, and entities?
- How does the language-model disambiguator interact with priors like p(e|m) and with global coherence signals?
- Can you provide ablations and an error analysis, and commit to releasing code and pretrained embeddings?
