**Summary of the Paper**

The paper proposes a novel Multi-Prototype Mention Embedding model to deal with the ambiguity of entity mentions in a unified semantic space. The model learns multiple sense embeddings for each mention by jointly modeling words from textual contexts and entities derived from a knowledge base. An efficient language model-based approach is also designed to disambiguate each mention to a specific sense. The authors evaluate their model on entity linking tasks and achieve state-of-the-art performance.

**Main Contributions**

1. **Multi-Prototype Mention Embedding model**: The paper proposes a novel model that learns multiple sense embeddings for each mention, which is a significant contribution to the field of natural language processing.
2. **Efficient disambiguation method**: The authors design an efficient language model-based approach to disambiguate each mention to a specific sense, which is a crucial step in entity linking tasks.
3. **State-of-the-art performance on entity linking**: The authors evaluate their model on entity linking tasks and achieve state-of-the-art performance, which demonstrates the effectiveness of their approach.

**Strengths**

1. **Novelty of the model**: The Multi-Prototype Mention Embedding model is a novel approach to dealing with the ambiguity of entity mentions, and it shows promising results in entity linking tasks.
2. **Efficiency of the disambiguation method**: The language model-based approach to disambiguation is efficient and effective, which is a significant advantage in real-world applications.
3. **Comprehensive evaluation**: The authors evaluate their model on a benchmark dataset and provide a comprehensive analysis of the results, which demonstrates the robustness of their approach.

**Weaknesses**

1. **Limited scalability**: The paper does not provide a clear discussion on the scalability of the model, which is a crucial aspect in real-world applications.
2. **Lack of comparison with other models**: The authors do not provide a comprehensive comparison of their model with other state-of-the-art models, which makes it difficult to assess the significance of their contribution.
3. **Overemphasis on entity linking**: The paper focuses primarily on entity linking tasks, which may limit the applicability of the model to other natural language processing tasks.

**Questions to Authors**

1. How does the model handle out-of-vocabulary entities, and what strategies can be employed to improve its performance in such cases?
2. Can the authors provide a more detailed analysis of the computational complexity of the model and its disambiguation method?
3. How does the model perform on other natural language processing tasks, such as question answering or text classification?
