**Summary of the Paper**

This paper proposes a novel Multi-Prototype Mention Embedding model to address the ambiguity of entity mentions in a unified semantic space. The model jointly learns multiple sense embeddings for each mention by incorporating words from textual contexts and entities derived from a knowledge base. An efficient language model-based approach is also designed to disambiguate each mention to a specific sense. The proposed method is evaluated on the benchmark dataset for entity linking, achieving state-of-the-art results.

**Contributions of the Work**

1. **Multi-Prototype Mention Embedding model**: The paper introduces a novel model that learns multiple sense embeddings for each mention, addressing the ambiguity of entity mentions in a unified semantic space.
2. **Joint learning of words and entities**: The model incorporates words from textual contexts and entities derived from a knowledge base, enabling a more comprehensive understanding of entity mentions.
3. **Efficient disambiguation approach**: The paper proposes an efficient language model-based approach to disambiguate each mention to a specific sense, demonstrating its effectiveness in entity linking tasks.

**Strengths of the Submission**

1. **Novelty of the proposed model**: The Multi-Prototype Mention Embedding model addresses a significant challenge in entity disambiguation and provides a new perspective on incorporating knowledge bases into the embedding space.
2. **Comprehensive evaluation**: The paper provides a thorough evaluation of the proposed method on the benchmark dataset for entity linking, demonstrating its effectiveness and achieving state-of-the-art results.
3. **Clear presentation**: The paper is well-written, and the authors provide a clear explanation of their proposed method, making it easy to follow and understand.

**Weaknesses of the Submission**

1. **Limited discussion of related work**: While the paper mentions several related works, it would be beneficial to provide a more comprehensive discussion of existing methods and how they differ from the proposed approach.
2. **Insufficient analysis of the proposed model's limitations**: The paper could benefit from a more detailed analysis of the proposed model's limitations and potential areas for improvement.
3. **Lack of qualitative analysis**: While the paper provides quantitative results, it would be helpful to include more qualitative analysis of the proposed method's performance, such as examples of correct and incorrect disambiguations.

**Questions to Authors**

1. How do the authors plan to address the issue of scalability, as the proposed model may become computationally expensive for large datasets?
2. Can the authors provide more insights into the choice of hyperparameters and their impact on the model's performance?
3. How do the authors plan to extend the proposed model to other tasks, such as entity recognition or relation extraction?

**Additional Comments**

The paper is well-written, and the authors provide a clear explanation of their proposed method. However, it would be beneficial to address the weaknesses mentioned above to strengthen the submission.
