**Summary of the Paper**

This paper proposes a novel Multi-Prototype Mention Embedding model that integrates text and knowledge into a unified semantic space to deal with the ambiguity of entity mentions. The model learns multiple sense embeddings for each mention by jointly modeling words from textual contexts and entities derived from a knowledge base. An efficient language model-based approach is also designed to disambiguate each mention to a specific sense. The authors evaluate their model using entity linking as a study case and achieve state-of-the-art results on a benchmark dataset.

**Contributions**

1. A novel Multi-Prototype Mention Embedding model that effectively deals with the ambiguity of entity mentions by learning multiple sense embeddings for each mention.
2. An efficient language model-based approach for disambiguating each mention to a specific sense.
3. State-of-the-art results on a benchmark dataset for entity linking, demonstrating the effectiveness of the proposed model.

**Strengths**

1. The paper addresses a significant challenge in natural language processing, namely the ambiguity of entity mentions, and proposes a novel solution that effectively deals with this issue.
2. The authors provide a thorough evaluation of their model using entity linking as a study case and demonstrate state-of-the-art results on a benchmark dataset.
3. The paper is well-organized, and the authors provide a clear and concise explanation of their model and its components.

**Weaknesses**

1. The paper relies heavily on existing work in the field, and the authors could have provided more insight into how their model differs from previous approaches.
2. The evaluation of the model is limited to entity linking, and it would be beneficial to see how the model performs on other tasks, such as question answering or text classification.
3. The authors could have provided more details on the computational complexity of their model and its scalability to large datasets.

**Questions to Authors**

1. How does the proposed model handle out-of-vocabulary (OOV) words, and are there any plans to extend the model to handle OOV words?
2. Can the authors provide more insight into how the model deals with homographs, which are words with multiple meanings but the same spelling?
3. How does the model perform on datasets with limited training data, and are there any plans to explore few-shot learning or transfer learning approaches?
