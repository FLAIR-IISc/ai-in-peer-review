

**Summary**  
This paper proposes a Multi-Prototype Mention Embedding (MPME) model to address entity ambiguity in text-knowledge integration. The core contributions are: (1) learning multiple sense embeddings per mention by jointly modeling textual contexts and knowledge base (KB) entities, and (2) an efficient language model for disambiguating mentions to specific senses. The authors claim state-of-the-art results on entity linking using these embeddings, with qualitative/quantitative validation of the embeddings themselves.  

**Strengths**  
1. **Novel Integration of Text and KB**: The approach of jointly modeling textual contexts and KB-derived entities to learn multi-prototype embeddings is well-motivated and addresses a genuine challenge in entity disambiguation. The design avoids the pitfalls of prior methods that treat text and KB as separate modalities.  
2. **Clear Disambiguation Framework**: The language model-based disambiguation method is straightforward and effectively leverages the multi-prototype embeddings. The evaluation on entity linking (a standard task) demonstrates practical utility.  
3. **Strong Qualitative Validation**: The paper provides insightful examples showing how multi-prototype embeddings capture context-specific entity senses (e.g., "Apple" as company vs. fruit), which convincingly supports the model’s design.  

**Weaknesses**  
1. **Overstated Novelty and Missing Comparisons**: The paper claims "state-of-the-art" but omits critical comparisons to direct predecessors like Huang et al. (2012) and Tian et al. (2014), who also proposed multi-prototype embeddings. Without these baselines, the "SOTA" claim lacks evidence. The discussion of related work is superficial and fails to position MPME relative to existing work.  
2. **Severe Reference Errors**: The reference list contains repeated, typos-ridden entries (e.g., "Efficie t string matching," "Aoe." instead of "Aoe," "Joint representation learning of text and knowledg for knowledge graph completion" appearing 5 times with errors). This suggests poor editing and undermines the paper’s credibility.  
3. **Insufficient Analysis of Limitations**: The paper does not discuss when MPME fails (e.g., for rare mentions or ambiguous contexts lacking KB coverage) or how the model scales with KB size. The ablation study on embedding quality is limited to qualitative examples, not quantitative metrics (e.g., cluster coherence).  

**Questions to Authors**  
1. How does MPME compare to Huang et al. (2012) and Tian et al. (2014) on the same entity linking benchmarks? Specifically, what improvements does MPME achieve over these multi-prototype baselines?  
2. The reference list contains multiple errors (e.g., repeated typos in "Aho and Corasick" and "Han et al."). Please clarify if these are errors in the submission or if they reflect the cited works.  
3. The paper states MPME achieves "state-of-the-art" but does not provide absolute F1 scores or error rates. Please share the exact numbers for the best-performing baselines (e.g., from Shen et al. 2015 or Hoffart et al. 2011) to contextualize the improvement.
