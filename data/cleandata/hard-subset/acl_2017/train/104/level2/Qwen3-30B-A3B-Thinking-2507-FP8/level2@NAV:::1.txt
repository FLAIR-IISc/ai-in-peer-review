

Summary: The paper proposes a Multi-Prototype Mention Embedding (MPME) model to address entity ambiguity by learning multiple sense embeddings for mentions through joint modeling of textual contexts and knowledge base entities, with a disambiguation module for entity linking.  

**Main Contributions (as assessed):**  
1. A multi-prototype embedding approach for ambiguous mentions, integrating textual and knowledge-base signals (though novelty is limited relative to prior work).  
2. An efficient disambiguation method leveraging language models (lacking empirical validation in the abstract).  
3. Claims of state-of-the-art entity linking performance (unverified in the provided abstract).  

**Strengths:**  
1. The problem of entity ambiguity is well-motivated, and the integration of text and knowledge bases aligns with current trends in NLP.  
2. The conceptual framework for multi-prototype embeddings is logically structured, potentially benefiting downstream tasks like entity linking.  

**Weaknesses:**  
1. **Unsupported Claims:** The abstract asserts "state-of-the-art" results but provides *no quantitative evidence* or benchmark comparisons (e.g., F1 scores, datasets used). Without this, the primary contribution cannot be validated.  
2. **Questionable Novelty:** The approach closely resembles Huang et al. (2012) and Neelakantan et al. (2014) on multi-prototype embeddings. The paper fails to adequately contrast with these foundational works or justify significant improvements.  
3. **Severe Reference Issues:** The bibliography contains 15+ duplicated entries (e.g., Aho & Corasick cited 7 times with typos like "Efficie t string matching") and omits critical recent work (e.g., Wang et al. 2020 on multi-sense embeddings). This undermines credibility and suggests poor scholarly rigor.  

**Questions to Authors:**  
1. Provide specific entity linking results (e.g., on AIDA or ACE datasets) demonstrating "state-of-the-art" performance, including comparison baselines.  
2. Clarify how MPME differs *significantly* from Huang et al. (2012) and Neelakantan et al. (2014), especially regarding the use of knowledge bases.  
3. Explain why key recent works (e.g., Mancini et al. 2016, Wang et al. 2020) are omitted from the related work.  

**Additional Comments:**  
The paper’s core idea has potential, but the lack of empirical results, inadequate novelty justification, and bibliographic errors are severe shortcomings for a conference submission. The authors must address these before reconsideration.
