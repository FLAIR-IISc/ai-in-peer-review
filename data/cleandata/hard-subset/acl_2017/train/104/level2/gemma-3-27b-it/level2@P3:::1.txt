This paper proposes a Multi-Prototype Mention Embedding model to address the ambiguity of entity mentions by learning multiple sense embeddings for each mention, leveraging both textual contexts and a knowledge base. An efficient language model is further designed for mention disambiguation. The work is evaluated on the entity linking task, achieving state-of-the-art results.

The primary contribution is the proposed Multi-Prototype Mention Embedding model, which attempts to represent ambiguous mentions with multiple embeddings, potentially capturing different senses. This builds upon prior work in multi-prototype embeddings (Huang et al., 2012; Reisinger and Mooney, 2010; Tian et al., 2014) but adapts the concept to the specific problem of entity mention disambiguation by incorporating knowledge base information. A second contribution lies in the language model approach for mention disambiguation, complementing the embedding learning process. Finally, the reported state-of-the-art performance on entity linking serves as a validation of the approach.

**Strengths:**

1.  **Addressing a Core Problem:** Entity mention ambiguity is a fundamental challenge in many NLP tasks. The paper directly tackles this problem with a well-motivated approach.
2.  **Strong Empirical Results:** Achieving state-of-the-art results on a benchmark entity linking dataset is a significant accomplishment and provides strong evidence for the effectiveness of the proposed method. This provides convincing evidence for the quality of the embeddings and the disambiguation technique.
3.  **Integration of Text and Knowledge:** The joint modeling of textual contexts and knowledge base entities is a sensible approach. Leveraging both sources of information is crucial for effective entity disambiguation and allows the model to capitalize on the strengths of each.
4.  **Clear Presentation:** The paper is generally well-written and the proposed method is clearly explained. The structure follows a logical flow, making it relatively easy to understand the key ideas.

**Weaknesses:**

1.  **Limited Novelty of Multi-Prototype Embeddings:** While the application to entity mention disambiguation with knowledge base integration is a valid contribution, the core idea of multi-prototype embeddings is not entirely novel (Huang et al., 2012; Reisinger and Mooney, 2010; Tian et al., 2014). The paper would benefit from a more thorough discussion of how this work extends beyond existing multi-prototype embedding techniques and more specifically highlights the innovations in *how* multiple prototypes are learned and used within the context of entity linking.
2.  **Lack of Ablation Studies:** The paper lacks sufficient ablation studies to assess the contribution of each component of the proposed model. For instance, it is unclear how much each component (multi-prototype embeddings vs. the language model) contributes to the overall performance. A more detailed analysis would help understand the interplay between different parts of the system.
3.  **Dataset Specificity:** The evaluation is limited to a single benchmark dataset. While achieving state-of-the-art performance is commendable, it would be helpful to evaluate the method on additional datasets with different characteristics to assess its generalizability. Consideration of datasets covering different domains or with different levels of ambiguity would be valuable.
4.  **Efficiency Concerns:** The paper describes an "efficient" language model, but it does not provide any empirical evidence of its efficiency (e.g., runtime comparisons with existing methods). Given the computational cost of embedding models, a discussion of the model's scalability would be beneficial.

**Questions to Authors:**

1.  Could the authors elaborate on the specific innovations of their multi-prototype embedding approach compared to previous work on multi-prototype embeddings (Huang et al., 2012; Reisinger and Mooney, 2010; Tian et al., 2014)?
2.  What is the computational complexity of the proposed language model, and how does it compare to existing mention disambiguation approaches?
3.  Can the authors provide ablation study results to demonstrate the contribution of each component of the proposed model (multi-prototype embeddings, language model)?
4.  Have the authors considered evaluating the method on additional datasets to assess its generalizability?
