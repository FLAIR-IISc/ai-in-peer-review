## Review for Multi-Prototype Mention Embedding Model

This paper addresses the challenge of ambiguity in representing entity mentions within a unified text and knowledge space. The authors propose a Multi-Prototype Mention Embedding model that learns multiple sense embeddings for each mention by integrating textual context and knowledge base entities, coupled with a language model-based disambiguation approach.

The primary contribution of this work is the demonstration of improved entity linking performance through the use of multi-prototype mention embeddings. While multi-prototype embeddings are not entirely novel (as evidenced by citations like Huang et al., 2012 and Neelakantan et al., 2014), the adaptation and application of this concept to jointly model textual and knowledge-based information for mention disambiguation is a significant step. A secondary contribution lies in the reported state-of-the-art results on an entity linking benchmark dataset, indicating a practical benefit to the proposed method. Finally, the paper provides a clear and concise explanation of the model and disambiguation approach, making it accessible to researchers in the field.

**Strengths:**

1. **Strong Empirical Results:** Achieving state-of-the-art performance on a standard entity linking dataset provides concrete evidence of the method's effectiveness. This is a strong indicator of practical value.
2. **Clear Problem Definition:** The paper clearly articulates the problem of mention ambiguity and motivates the need for a more nuanced representation of mentions.
3. **Sound Methodology:** The joint modeling of textual contexts and knowledge base entities appears to be a reasonable approach to address the identified ambiguity.
4. **Well-Written and Organized:** The paper is generally well-written and logically organized, making it easy to understand the proposed approach.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Novelty:** The core idea of multi-prototype embeddings is not entirely new. The paper would benefit from a more in-depth discussion of how this work differs from, and improves upon, existing multi-prototype embedding approaches.
2. **Insufficient Ablation Study:** While the paper reports overall performance gains, it lacks a detailed ablation study to isolate the impact of each component of the model (e.g., the multi-prototype embedding vs. the disambiguation module). 
3. **Dataset Specificity:** The reported results are limited to a single benchmark dataset. Evaluating the model on a wider range of datasets with different characteristics would strengthen the conclusions.
4. **Reference Formatting:** The reference list is poorly formatted and contains numerous errors (repeated entries, incomplete information). This needs substantial cleaning.

**Questions to Authors:**

1. Can you provide a more detailed comparison of your multi-prototype embedding approach with existing methods, highlighting the specific innovations and advantages of your model?
2. What is the computational overhead introduced by using multi-prototype embeddings compared to single-embedding approaches? 
3. Could you provide more details about the language model used for disambiguation? What specific architecture and training data were employed?
