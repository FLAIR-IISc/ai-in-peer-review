This paper proposes a Multi-Prototype Mention Embedding model to address the ambiguity of entity mentions when integrating text and knowledge. The core idea is to learn multiple sense embeddings for each mention by combining textual context and knowledge base entities, and then employing a language model for disambiguation. The approach is evaluated on the entity linking task, achieving state-of-the-art results.

**Contributions:**

1. **Multi-Prototype Mention Embeddings:** The primary contribution is the introduction of a novel embedding approach capable of representing multiple senses for a single mention. This is achieved by jointly modeling textual contexts and knowledge base entities, allowing the model to capture the nuanced meanings of ambiguous mentions.
2. **Language Model for Disambiguation:**  The paper proposes an efficient language model-based approach to disambiguate mentions to specific senses. This provides a mechanism to select the appropriate embedding for a given mention in context, further improving performance.
3. **State-of-the-Art Results on Entity Linking:** The experimental results demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed model, achieving state-of-the-art performance on a benchmark entity linking dataset. This empirically validates the approach and showcases its practical value.

**Strengths:**

1. **Addresses a Core Problem:** Entity mention ambiguity is a significant challenge in integrating text and knowledge, and this work directly tackles this issue. The proposed approach is well-motivated and addresses a practical limitation of existing methods.
2. **Sound Technical Approach:** The combination of multi-prototype embeddings and a language model for disambiguation is a logically sound and potentially powerful approach. The joint modeling of textual and knowledge-based information is particularly appealing.
3. **Strong Empirical Validation:** The state-of-the-art results on entity linking provide compelling evidence of the effectiveness of the proposed model. The authors demonstrate a clear improvement over existing methods on a standard benchmark.
4. **Clear Presentation:** The paper is generally well-written and easy to follow. The technical details are explained clearly, and the experimental setup is well-described. The motivation and contributions are clearly stated in the abstract and introduction.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Novelty in Components:** While the combination of techniques is valuable, the individual components – multi-prototype embeddings and language models – are not entirely novel. Previous work (e.g., Huang et al., 2012; Neelakantan et al., 2014; Chen et al., 2014) has explored similar ideas.  The paper would benefit from a more thorough discussion of how this work builds upon and differentiates itself from existing approaches.
2. **Disambiguation Model Details:** The description of the language model for disambiguation is somewhat brief. More detail on the model architecture, training procedure, and specific features used would be helpful for understanding its effectiveness.  Is it a standard language model, or are there specific modifications tailored to this task?
3. **Knowledge Base Dependency:** The model's performance is likely sensitive to the quality and coverage of the knowledge base used. The paper does not sufficiently discuss the potential impact of knowledge base limitations on the results or explore strategies for mitigating this dependency (e.g., using multiple KBs or incorporating external knowledge sources).
4. **Error Analysis:** While the quantitative results are strong, a more detailed error analysis would be beneficial. Understanding *where* the model fails (e.g., types of ambiguous mentions, specific entities) could provide insights for future improvements.



**Questions to Authors:**

1. Could you provide more detail on the language model used for disambiguation? Specifically, what is the architecture of the model, what features are used, and how is it trained?
2. How sensitive is the performance of the model to the choice of knowledge base? Have you experimented with different knowledge bases or considered strategies for dealing with incomplete knowledge?
3. Could you provide an error analysis of the results? What types of ambiguous mentions are most challenging for the model? Are there specific entities that are frequently mis-disambiguated?
4.  How does the proposed approach compare to recent end-to-end neural entity linking models that do not rely on explicit mention disambiguation?
