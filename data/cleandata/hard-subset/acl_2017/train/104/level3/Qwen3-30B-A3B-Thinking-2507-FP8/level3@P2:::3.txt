

**Summary and Contributions**  
This paper proposes a Multi-Prototype Mention Embedding (MPME) model to address ambiguity in entity mentions by jointly learning sense-specific embeddings from textual contexts and knowledge base entities. The core contribution lies in the *evaluation framework*—the paper demonstrates robust performance on entity linking via a simple disambiguation method, with thorough qualitative/quantitative analysis. The *joint modeling approach* (integrating text, entities, and sense embeddings) is presented as a key contribution, though it builds incrementally on prior work. The *disambiguation method* (an efficient language model) is secondary, as it relies on the embeddings rather than introducing novel techniques.  

**Strengths**  
1. **Solid empirical evaluation**: The paper provides thorough analysis of embeddings (word, entity, multi-prototype) and achieves state-of-the-art results on entity linking. The ablation studies and qualitative examples convincingly validate the embeddings’ quality, even if absolute gains are modest (e.g., +1.2% F1 on AIDA). This rigor outweighs the incremental nature of the core method.  
2. **Practical training procedure**: The model uses a straightforward neural architecture (e.g., skip-gram-like objective) that avoids complex optimization, enabling broad applicability beyond entity linking (e.g., to coreference or SRL). The authors demonstrate this via a simple disambiguation pipeline, which is a strength for real-world adoption.  
3. **Clear presentation in key sections**: The problem formulation (Section 2) and results discussion (Section 4) are well-structured, with precise metrics and intuitive examples (e.g., Figure 1’s mention-sense visualization). This clarity aids reproducibility and understanding of the contributions.  

**Weaknesses**  
1. **Lack of substantive novelty**: The MPME model is *overlapping* with Yamada et al. (2016), which also proposes multi-prototype embeddings for entity disambiguation using a similar joint text-knowledge framework. The paper fails to sufficiently differentiate its approach (e.g., no analysis of architectural differences or performance gaps between the methods). This undermines the primary contribution claim.  
2. **Inconsistent exposition**: Section 3 (model details) and parts of Figure 2 lack critical implementation details (e.g., how sense prototypes are derived from knowledge base entities). The description of the "dictionary of mentions" vs. "dictionary of entity-mention pairs" is confusing and inconsistent, hindering reproducibility.  
3. **Redundant and ambiguous terminology**: Terms like "text anchors," "dictionary of mentions," and "sense embeddings" are used interchangeably without clear definitions. For instance, Section 3.1 refers to "entity-mention pairs" but later treats them as "mentions," creating confusion about the input representation.  

**Questions to Authors**  
1. How does MPME fundamentally differ from Yamada et al. (2016) in terms of architecture, training, or theoretical grounding? Please provide a detailed comparison (e.g., loss functions, prototype derivation) to clarify novelty.  
2. Could the multi-prototype embeddings be evaluated on *other NLP tasks* (e.g., semantic role labeling or dependency parsing) to demonstrate broader utility beyond entity linking? This would strengthen the claim that the embeddings are "high quality."  
3. Please clarify the relationship between "dictionary of mentions" (Section 3.1) and "entity-mention pairs" (Section 3.2). Are they distinct data structures, and if so, why?  

**Additional Comments**  
The reference list contains multiple typos (e.g., "Efficient string matching: an aid to bibliographic search" listed four times with errors), which should be corrected. The paper’s core idea is sound but not sufficiently novel for ACL, especially given the proximity to Yamada et al. (2016). The empirical validation is strong, but the limited novelty and exposition issues prevent it from being a compelling contribution.
