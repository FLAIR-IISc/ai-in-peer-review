

This paper proposes a "Multi-Prototype Mention Embedding" model integrating entity, mention, and sense modeling within a unified neural framework, evaluated primarily on entity linking. The core contributions appear to be: (1) a neural approach for learning multiple sense embeddings per mention via joint text-knowledge modeling, and (2) a simple language modeling procedure for disambiguation, supported by empirical results on benchmark datasets.

**Strengths:** The integrated modeling of entity-mention-sense relationships is a well-motivated idea that addresses ambiguity in semantic spaces. The training procedure is notably straightforward, enabling broad applicability across NLP tasks. Empirical evaluation is robust, demonstrating consistent improvements over baselines in entity linking with clear qualitative analysis, even if gains are modest.

**Weaknesses:** The work lacks genuine novelty; it closely mirrors Yamada et al. (2016) in methodology and scope, with insufficient differentiation highlighted in the paper. Terminology is inconsistent (e.g., "dictionary of mentions," "entity-mention pairs," "text anchors"), creating confusion. Critical sections (Section 3, Figure 2) lack clarity and detail, hindering reproducibility. The paper does not adequately justify its incremental advances over prior work, which is a significant drawback for a venue prioritizing novelty.

**Questions to Authors:**  
1. How does your disambiguation approach specifically differ from Yamada et al. (2016) beyond minor implementation details? Please provide a detailed comparison table.  
2. Could you evaluate the learned embeddings on tasks like dependency parsing or coreference resolution to demonstrate they are not inferior to simpler word embeddings?  

The paper’s solid evaluation and practical approach are commendable, but the limited novelty and unclear exposition significantly weaken its contribution. While the method is sound, the lack of a compelling "why this is new" argument makes it difficult to justify acceptance over existing work. The authors’ response addressed some exposition issues but did not sufficiently clarify the novelty gap relative to Yamada et al. (2016).
