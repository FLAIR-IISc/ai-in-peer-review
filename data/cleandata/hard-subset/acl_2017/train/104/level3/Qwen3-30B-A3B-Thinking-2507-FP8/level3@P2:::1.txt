

**Summary and Contributions**  
This paper proposes a Multi-Prototype Mention Embedding (MPME) model to address entity mention ambiguity by learning multiple sense embeddings per mention via joint modeling of textual contexts and knowledge base entities. The authors further introduce a language model-based disambiguation approach. The primary contribution is the MPME framework itself, which enables context-aware sense-specific embeddings. A secondary contribution is the empirical analysis of the smoothing parameter’s impact on entity linking performance. The third contribution—state-of-the-art entity linking results—appears partially undermined by methodological issues in comparisons.

**Strengths**  
1. **Novel multi-prototype embedding approach**: The model’s core innovation—learning multiple embeddings per mention by leveraging textual contexts and knowledge graph entities—provides a principled solution to ambiguity. Qualitative analysis demonstrates distinct sense representations, validating the model’s ability to capture contextual meaning.  
2. **Insightful smoothing parameter analysis**: The study of the smoothing parameter (λ) reveals critical trade-offs between entity popularity and disambiguation accuracy. For instance, higher λ values (prioritizing popular entities) improve performance on common mentions but degrade results for rare entities, offering actionable guidance for real-world deployment.  
3. **Strong empirical results on entity linking**: The model achieves competitive results on benchmark datasets (e.g., AIDA-CoNLL), outperforming ALIGN in supervised settings. This demonstrates the practical utility of MPME embeddings for downstream tasks, particularly when combined with the disambiguation module.

**Weaknesses**  
1. **Confounded comparison with ALIGN**: The claimed SOTA result is weakened by inconsistent hyperparameters (context window size: 10 vs. 5; embedding dimensions: 500 vs. 200). This makes it impossible to attribute performance gains solely to the MPME model, as differences in setup could explain the margin. A fair comparison requires identical settings.  
2. **Ambiguous technical definitions**: The paper lacks clarity on the neighborhood definition *N(e_j)* (e.g., whether Wikipedia outlinks are treated as directed or undirected) and the scope of "mention" (string type vs. document-specific instance). This obscures how entity contexts are constructed, risking misinterpretation of the embedding methodology.  
3. **Inadequate disambiguation order evaluation**: The paper does not compare against unsupervised baselines (e.g., graph-based methods like Pershina et al. 2015) to assess whether the *order* of sense disambiguation (e.g., before or after embedding) impacts results. Without this, the claimed superiority of the proposed disambiguation step remains unverified.  

**Questions to Authors**  
1. Please clarify the hyperparameters used for ALIGN in the comparison (e.g., context window, embedding dimensions). If the settings differ, provide a fair comparison with identical configurations.  
2. Define *N(e_j)* precisely: Is the Wikipedia outlink graph treated as directed or undirected? How does this choice affect the learned entity embeddings?  
3. Why was the disambiguation module not evaluated against unsupervised baselines? How does the proposed method’s disambiguation order (vs. alternatives like *pre*-embedding disambiguation) impact performance?  
4. The term "mention embedding" is used for sense-specific vectors, but "mention" typically refers to the text span. Please clarify whether embeddings are for *senses* (not mentions) and justify the terminology.  

**Additional Comments**  
The paper’s reference list contains multiple typos and duplicate entries (e.g., repeated Aho & Corasick citations), which should be corrected. The analysis of smoothing parameter λ is a valuable contribution but would benefit from ablation studies on λ across different entity types (e.g., rare vs. common). The model’s computational efficiency (e.g., inference time vs. ALIGN) is not addressed, despite the authors’ claim of "efficiency" in the title.
