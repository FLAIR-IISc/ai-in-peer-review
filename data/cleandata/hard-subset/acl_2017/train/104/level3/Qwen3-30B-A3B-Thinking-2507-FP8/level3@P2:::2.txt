

**Summary**  
This paper proposes a Multi-Prototype Mention Embedding (MPME) model for entity disambiguation (EDL), framing it as a two-step process: (1) mapping mentions to sense-specific embeddings via joint text-knowledge modeling, and (2) disambiguating senses to entities. The authors claim state-of-the-art results on EDL benchmarks using this approach. However, the paper overstates its scope by positioning the work as a "unified semantic space" bridge for text-knowledge integration, while narrowly addressing EDL.  

**Main Contributions**  
1. **Embedding technique for mention senses**: A method to learn multiple sense embeddings per mention by integrating textual context and knowledge base (KB) entities.  
2. **Smoothing parameter for disambiguation**: An efficient language model approach with a tunable smoothing parameter to resolve sense-to-entity mapping.  

**Strengths**  
1. **Methodological clarity**: The two-step pipeline (mention→sense→entity) is well-motivated and technically sound, adhering to established embedding best practices. The use of distributional context (from text) and entity profiles (from KB) is logically structured.  
2. **Effective smoothing parameter**: The proposed smoothing technique (Section 6.4.2) is a practical and well-justified contribution that improves disambiguation robustness without excessive complexity.  
3. **Comprehensive evaluation**: The experiments include both qualitative analysis of embeddings and strong quantitative results on EDL benchmarks, demonstrating consistent improvements over baselines like ALIGN and Yamada et al. (2016).  

**Weaknesses**  
1. **Overstated scope and misleading novelty**: The title and introduction frame the work as a general "text-knowledge semantic space" solution, yet it exclusively targets EDL. Crucially, the claimed novelty (multi-prototype mentions) is not distinct from prior work:  
   - *Reisinger & Mooney (2010)* and *Tian et al. (2014)* explicitly proposed multi-prototype embeddings for word senses, with *Mancini et al. (2016)* extending this to entities.  
   - The paper fails to address how *mention senses* differ from *entity senses* or justify an intermediate layer when entities themselves could be directly disambiguated (e.g., via *Wu et al. (2016)* or *Huang et al. (2015)*).  
2. **Unexplained empirical superiority**: The paper states MPME outperforms direct mention→entity mapping (e.g., ALIGN) but provides no analysis of *why*. The reviewer’s assumption—*that mention senses are distributional profiles derived from entity text*—implies the intermediate layer adds no new signal beyond what entities already encode. The authors must clarify:  
   - How mention senses are created (e.g., seeded from entity text? Clustered from context?),  
   - Whether senses map bijectively to KB entities, and  
   - What *additional signal* the sense layer provides over direct entity representations.  
3. **Critical omission of relevant baselines**: The evaluation ignores standard EDL benchmarks like TAC KBP 2016, which is a major oversight. The authors’ dismissal of KBP as "not applicable" is untenable; embedding quality must be validated via *application performance* (e.g., EDL accuracy), not just conceptual elegance.  

**Questions to Authors**  
1. **Role of mention senses**: The paper assumes mention senses are context-disambiguated distributional profiles (e.g., derived from entity text). If so, why is this not redundant with entity embeddings? If senses are created differently (e.g., via clustering), please clarify the process and provide evidence of added signal.  
2. **KBP 2016 evaluation**: Why were TAC KBP 2016 results omitted? Given that KBP is the standard EDL benchmark, its exclusion undermines the claim of "state-of-the-art" results. Please justify and include comparisons.  
3. **Sense-to-entity mapping**: Do mention senses map bijectively to KB entities? If not, how are non-bijective mappings resolved during disambiguation (e.g., via entity popularity), and how does this affect the model’s robustness?  

**Additional Comments**  
The paper’s methodological rigor is commendable, but the misalignment between claimed scope and actual contribution is a significant flaw. The smoothing parameter is a strong point, but the core novelty must be re-evaluated against existing multi-prototype work. The authors should revise Section 5 to explicitly clarify the *novel role* of mention senses (e.g., "senses capture context-specific usage patterns not reflected in static entity pages") or acknowledge the overlap with prior work. Without this, the contribution appears incremental.
