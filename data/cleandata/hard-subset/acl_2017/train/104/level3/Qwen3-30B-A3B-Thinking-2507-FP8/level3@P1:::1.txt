

This paper proposes a Multi-Prototype Mention Embedding (MPME) model for resolving entity mention ambiguity by learning multiple sense embeddings per mention through joint modeling of textual contexts and knowledge base entities. The core contribution is a framework that generates context-aware, sense-specific embeddings for ambiguous mentions, enabling improved entity linking. The secondary contribution is an efficient language model for disambiguating mentions to specific senses, validated on entity linking benchmarks. The tertiary contribution is an analysis of the smoothing parameter's impact on entity linking performance, revealing insights into how entity popularity affects disambiguation.

Strengths:  
1. The MPME model achieves state-of-the-art results on standard entity linking benchmarks, outperforming ALIGN by 1.8–3.2 F1 points. This demonstrates the efficacy of multi-prototype embeddings in capturing sense-specific semantics, directly addressing the core challenge of mention ambiguity. The quantitative gains are significant and consistently observed across datasets, providing strong empirical validation for the proposed approach.  
2. The paper offers a valuable analysis of the smoothing parameter α in the entity context generation process. By showing that α=0.5 optimizes performance across datasets, the authors reveal how entity popularity influences disambiguation accuracy—a practical insight for real-world applications where entity frequency varies. This analysis is methodologically sound and directly informs model configuration.  
3. The direct comparison with ALIGN (a closely related model using similar input data) is methodologically rigorous. Unlike prior work that compares with older baselines, this comparison isolates the contribution of the multi-prototype mechanism, strengthening the claim that the proposed embedding framework drives the performance gains.

Weaknesses:  
1. The comparison with ALIGN is confounded by inconsistent hyperparameters: the paper uses a context window of 10 (vs. ALIGN’s 5) and vector dimensions of 500 (vs. 200). This invalidates the claim of a "fair comparison," as the performance gap could stem from these settings rather than the model architecture alone. The authors should re-run ALIGN with identical hyperparameters to isolate the contribution of MPME.  
2. The definition of N(e_j) (the neighborhood of entity e_j) is ambiguous. The paper states it includes "entities linked to e_j via Wikipedia outlinks" but does not clarify whether the Wikipedia outlink graph is treated as directed or undirected. This ambiguity affects the entity context construction—directed vs. undirected graphs yield different neighbor sets—and undermines reproducibility.  
3. The terminology around "mention" is inconsistent and confusing. The paper refers to "mention embeddings" but later specifies these represent "sense embeddings" (not surface-form mentions). This conflates standard NLP terminology (where "mention" denotes a string instance in text) with the authors’ sense-specific embeddings, muddying the contribution and potentially misleading readers.

Questions to Authors:  
1. Please clarify whether the Wikipedia outlink graph used to define N(e_j) is directed or undirected, and how this choice impacts the generated entity contexts.  
2. The paper claims MPME embeddings are "for mention senses," but the training data uses document-level mentions. How are these embeddings applied to new mentions in unseen documents, and does the model disambiguate at the document level or mention instance level?
