- Summary: Proposes a Multi-Prototype Mention Embedding model that learns multiple sense embeddings per mention by jointly modeling textual contexts and KB entities, applied to entity linking with strong benchmark results.

- Contribution 1: Presents a unified neural language modeling framework integrating entity, mention, and sense modeling.

- Contribution 2: Offers a simple, efficient learning procedure intended to be broadly applicable across NLP tasks.

- Contribution 3: Provides empirical validation (qualitative and quantitative) showing the quality of learned embeddings.

- Strength 1: Unified modeling coherently addresses mention ambiguity by combining entity, mention, and sense representations.

- Strength 2: Method is simple and easy to implement, facilitating adaptation to other NLP tasks.

- Strength 3: Solid experimental evaluation on entity linking that demonstrates effectiveness, albeit with incremental gains.

- Weakness 1: Limited novelty — approach is very similar to Yamada et al. (2016) and differences are not clearly articulated.

- Weakness 2: Inconsistent exposition and confusing terminology (e.g., varying phrases for mention/entity dictionaries) hinder clarity.

- Weakness 3: Improvements over prior work are incremental and not striking, reducing perceived impact.

- Weakness 4: Lack of broader validation — embeddings are not tested on other NLP tasks to show general utility.

- Question 1: Please provide a detailed technical comparison to Yamada et al. (2016) explaining concrete differences and benefits.

- Question 2: How will you resolve terminology inconsistencies to improve clarity (e.g., standardize dictionary/anchor terms)?

- Question 3: Can you provide preliminary results or insight on how these embeddings perform on other tasks (parsing, coreference, SRL)?

- Overall assessment: Solid, well-executed paper with acceptable empirical results but limited novelty and scope; suitable for ACL after minor revisions (clearer exposition, stronger differentiation, broader validation).
