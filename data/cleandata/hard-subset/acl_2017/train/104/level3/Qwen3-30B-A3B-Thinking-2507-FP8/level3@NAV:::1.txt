

This paper proposes a Multi-Prototype Mention Embedding model for entity linking, claiming state-of-the-art results by learning multiple sense embeddings per mention and disambiguating via a language model. The primary contribution lies in the **smoothing parameter analysis**, which reveals critical insights about popularity's impact on entity linking performance. A secondary contribution is the **direct comparison with a closely related approach using identical input data**, though this is undermined by methodological issues. The **qualitative/quantitative embedding analysis** demonstrates high-quality representations but lacks novelty in the core approach.

Strengths include the useful smoothing parameter insights (Section 4.3) and the clear input data comparison setup. However, these are overshadowed by critical weaknesses:

1. **Confounded comparison with ALIGN**: The claimed SOTA result is invalidated by uncontrolled hyperparameters (context window 10 vs. 5, vector dims 500 vs. 200), making performance differences unattributable to the model itself.  
2. **Ambiguous terminology**: The undefined "N(e_j)" and unclear treatment of the Wikipedia outlink graph (directed vs. undirected) fundamentally affect entity context modeling, risking reproducibility.  
3. **Lack of unsupervised baselines**: Without comparison to methods like Huang et al. (2012) or Reisinger & Mooney (2010), the impact of the sense disambiguation order cannot be assessed, weakening the core claim.  
4. **"Mention" definition confusion**: The paper conflates string-level "mentions" with document-specific instances (e.g., "mention embedding" appears sense-specific), obscuring whether embeddings are general or context-dependent.

Questions to Authors:  
1. Clarify whether the Wikipedia graph adjacency treats outlinks as directed/undirected and how this choice was justified.  
2. Provide hyperparameter details for ALIGN (e.g., context window, vector dims) to enable fair comparison.  
3. Include a comparison with at least one unsupervised sense disambiguation method to isolate the contribution of the proposed disambiguation order.  

The paper’s core innovation is obscured by methodological flaws and unclear presentation. While the smoothing analysis offers value, the evaluation’s validity is compromised, preventing confident assessment of the claimed SOTA performance. The reference list’s corruption (e.g., repeated entries, typos like "Efficie t") further suggests insufficient preparation.
