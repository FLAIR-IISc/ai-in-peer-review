

This paper proposes a Multi-Prototype Mention Embedding (MPME) model for entity disambiguation/linking (EDL), framing it as a two-step pipeline: mention → mention-sense → entity. The core contribution is a method to learn multiple sense embeddings per mention by jointly modeling textual context and knowledge base entities, followed by a disambiguation step mapping senses to entities. The paper claims state-of-the-art results on EDL benchmarks using this approach.

**Contributions**  
1. A multi-prototype embedding model (MPME) for mention senses, learned via joint text-knowledge optimization.  
2. A disambiguation pipeline leveraging these sense embeddings, outperforming direct mention→entity methods.  
3. Empirical validation on standard EDL datasets with qualitative analysis of embeddings.  

**Strengths**  
1. The methodology is methodologically sound and follows best practices for joint text-knowledge embedding. The model design is clear, with well-justified components (e.g., context word prediction objective).  
2. The paper demonstrates strong empirical results on standard EDL benchmarks (e.g., AIDA-CoNLL), showing consistent improvements over prior mention→entity approaches (e.g., ALIGN, Yamada et al.).  
3. The smoothing parameter strategy (Section 6.4.2) for handling sparse contexts is a practical and well-executed detail that enhances robustness.  

**Weaknesses**  
1. **Overstated scope**: The title and introduction claim broader "integration of text and knowledge," but the work is narrowly focused on EDL. This misrepresents the contribution’s applicability and misleads readers about the paper’s scope.  
2. **Missing critical baselines**: The evaluation omits standard EDL benchmarks like TAC KBP 2016, which are essential for validating EDL methods in the community. The authors’ dismissal of KBP as "not directly comparable" is unfounded; embedding efficacy must be proven via application performance, not conceptual elegance.  
3. **Unexplained novelty of mention senses**: The paper fails to clarify *what additional information* mention senses provide over entities. If senses are derived from entity text (as assumed), why isn’t this signal captured directly in entity embeddings? The paper does not explain how senses are created, how many exist per mention, or whether they map bijectively to entities—critical gaps undermining the core innovation claim.  
4. **Misattributed novelty**: The claim that MPME is novel ignores prior work (e.g., Huang et al., 2012; Neelakantan et al., 2014) on multi-prototype word embeddings, which directly motivates the approach. The paper should contextualize this work within existing multi-sense embedding literature.  

**Questions to Authors**  
1. How are mention senses *created*? Specifically:  
   - Are senses seeded from entity text (e.g., Wikipedia snippets), and if so, what signal at the sense level is *not* already present in the entity representation?  
   - How many senses are typically assigned per mention, and is there a one-to-one mapping between senses and knowledge base entities?  
2. Why does the two-step pipeline (mention→sense→entity) outperform direct mention→entity mapping? If senses are merely contextualized entity representations, why not optimize entity embeddings directly for disambiguation? Please clarify the *novel signal* provided by the intermediate sense layer.  

**Additional Comments**  
Section 5 must explicitly state the role and novelty of mention senses. If the authors’ understanding aligns with the reviewer’s assumption (senses as context-disambiguated entity profiles), the contribution is significantly weakened. The paper must either revise its novelty claim or provide compelling evidence that the sense layer adds unique disambiguation capacity beyond entity embeddings. The omission of KBP 2016 baselines is a critical oversight for EDL work; without this, the "state-of-the-art" claim lacks grounding in standard evaluation practices.
