

Summary and main contributions: The paper proposes a Multi-Prototype Mention Embedding model that learns multiple sense embeddings for each mention by jointly modeling textual contexts and knowledge base entities. The authors claim three primary contributions: (1) a novel multi-prototype approach for handling mention ambiguity, (2) an efficient disambiguation method for linking mentions to specific entity senses, and (3) analysis of the smoothing parameter's impact on entity linking performance. As I see them, the most significant contribution is the analysis of the smoothing parameter (contribution #3), followed by the direct comparison with a closely related method (contribution #2), with the multi-prototype embedding approach (contribution #1) being less novel given prior work on multi-sense embeddings.

Strengths: 
1. The analysis of the smoothing parameter provides valuable insight into how entity popularity affects linking performance. The authors demonstrate that their method effectively handles both popular and rare entities, which is a critical consideration for practical entity linking systems. This analysis goes beyond typical performance reporting and offers actionable guidance for practitioners.

2. The direct comparison with a closely related approach using similar input data is well-executed and provides clear evidence of the method's advantages. The comparison with a method that uses the same Wikipedia graph structure but different embedding approaches helps isolate the contribution of their multi-prototype approach.

3. The qualitative analysis of the embeddings provides useful evidence that the multi-prototype embeddings capture meaningful semantic distinctions between different entity senses, supporting the technical claims of the paper.

Weaknesses:
1. The comparison with ALIGN is confounded by different hyperparameters (context window 10 vs 5, vector dimensions 500 vs 200), making it impossible to determine whether the performance difference stems from the method itself or the parameter choices. This is a significant methodological flaw that undermines the main claim of outperforming ALIGN.

2. The paper's unclear definition of N(e_j) and whether the Wikipedia outlink graph adjacency is treated as directed or undirected creates ambiguity about the model's actual implementation. The graph structure fundamentally affects entity contexts and vectors, yet the paper fails to clarify this critical detail.

3. The ambiguity around "mention" terminology is problematic. It's unclear whether the embeddings are for string types (e.g., "Paris" as a concept) or specific document mentions (e.g., "Paris" in a particular sentence). This ambiguity makes it difficult to assess the scope and applicability of the method, particularly since the paper claims to handle "mention sense disambiguation."

4. The lack of comparison with other unsupervised entity linking methods makes it impossible to assess the impact of their sense disambiguation order. Without such comparisons, it's unclear whether the improvements are due to their specific disambiguation approach or simply the multi-prototype representation.

Questions to Authors:
1. Please clarify whether the Wikipedia outlink graph adjacency is treated as directed or undirected in the model, and explain how this choice affects the entity context vectors.

2. Please specify exactly what "mention" refers to in your framework - are the embeddings for general string types (e.g., "Paris" as a concept) or for specific document mentions (e.g., "Paris" in a particular sentence)?

3. Can you provide a fair comparison with ALIGN using the same hyperparameters (context window 5 and vector dimension 200) to isolate the contribution of your method?

4. How do you interpret the relationship between the smoothing parameter and entity popularity, and how might this inform the design of entity linking systems for domains with different entity frequency distributions?
