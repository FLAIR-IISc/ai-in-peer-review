Summary
The paper proposes a multi-prototype mention embedding model that learns multiple sense-specific embeddings for each surface form (“mention”) by jointly modeling textual contexts and KB-derived entity signals in a unified embedding space. A simple language-model–based disambiguation maps each mention occurrence to one of its sense embeddings. The learned word, entity, and mention-sense embeddings are evaluated qualitatively and quantitatively, with entity linking (EL) used as the main downstream task, where the method reportedly attains state-of-the-art performance.

Main contributions (as I see them)
1) Multi-prototype mention embeddings that integrate text context and KB entity information into a single framework, enabling sense-specific representations for ambiguous mentions.
2) A computationally simple training and disambiguation procedure that slots into standard neural language modeling and supports EL without heavy feature engineering.
3) An empirical study showing solid EL gains and analyses suggesting that the learned embeddings capture meaningful relationships among words, mentions, and entities.

Strengths
- Conceptual integration of entities, mentions, and senses: Modeling multiple mention prototypes in the same space as words and entities is a coherent idea that addresses mention ambiguity directly. It is a natural extension of joint text–KB embeddings to the granularity that matters for EL (surface forms), and it yields interpretable sense clusters.
- Simple and scalable training/disambiguation: The approach appears to be compatible with mainstream neural language modeling (negative sampling, etc.) and uses an efficient LM-based disambiguation step. This lowers the barrier to adoption compared to pipeline-heavy EL systems with many hand-crafted features, and suggests applicability to related tasks (e.g., candidate pruning, prior calibration).
- Solid empirical evaluation on EL: While improvements are not always large, the reported results are competitive and in some cases state-of-the-art. The evaluation appears careful, with both quantitative comparisons and qualitative analyses of the representation space (nearest neighbors, case studies), lending credibility that the model learns useful structure.
- Clarity in core parts: Much of the formal setup (objectives, joint modeling intuition) is clearly presented, making it straightforward to reproduce at a high level. The qualitative examples help bridge the method to its intended use in EL.

Weaknesses
- Limited novelty relative to prior joint text–KB embedding and multi-sense work: The joint embedding of words and entities has been explored extensively, and multi-prototype embeddings are well-established for words. Positioning the paper’s specific novelty—multi-prototype at the mention level plus LM-based disambiguation—relative to prior work (e.g., Yamada et al. 2016/2017 and multi-sense embeddings) is insufficient. The differences, both conceptual and technical, need to be explicitly enumerated and empirically validated (e.g., vs. a strong single-prototype joint embedding baseline).
- Inconsistent exposition and missing details in Section 3 and Figure 2: Key implementation details are unclear, including how the number of prototypes per mention is determined, how sense assignments are initialized/updated, and how textual vs. KB signals are balanced during training. Figure 2 (as referenced) does not fully clarify the training and disambiguation pipelines. This obscures reproducibility and makes it difficult to assess where the gains come from.
- Terminology confusion: The paper alternates among “dictionary of mentions,” “dictionary of entity-mention pairs,” and “anchors,” which are not strictly equivalent. It is often unclear what is used to construct candidates vs. to train embeddings vs. to supervise sense assignments. A precise, consistent definition of resources and their roles is needed.
- Empirical improvements are modest and ablation is limited: Several reported gains are small; it is unclear whether they are statistically significant. Ablations isolating (i) multi-prototype vs. single-prototype mentions, (ii) LM-based vs. standard disambiguation signals, and (iii) contribution of KB vs. text context are either missing or insufficient. Without these, the specific value of multi-prototype mention modeling is hard to quantify.
- Presentation quality of references: The bibliography contains numerous duplicates, formatting errors, and typos. This undermines polish and makes it harder to assess prior work coverage.

Questions to Authors
- Prototype cardinality: How is the number of mention prototypes determined per surface form? Is there a fixed K for all mentions, a data-driven nonparametric choice, or pruning based on frequency/entropy? How sensitive are results to this choice?
- Disambiguation mechanism: Please detail the LM-based disambiguation objective and inference. What context window, vocabulary, and smoothing are used? How is the balance between word-based and entity-based context controlled?
- Training dynamics: Do you alternate between sense assignment and embedding updates (EM-style), or jointly learn with soft assignments? What are the convergence criteria and compute costs compared to single-prototype baselines?
- Candidate generation: Which resource constructs the candidate set at test time—anchors, mention-entity dictionary, or both? How do coverage and ambiguity rates affect performance?
- Comparison to Yamada et al.: Please provide a side-by-side comparison (components, supervision, features, and training data) and an ablation that swaps in your multi-prototype component into a Yamada-style system to isolate the incremental benefit.
- Significance testing: Which metrics show statistically significant improvements, and under what test?

Additional Comments
- Please include ablations for: (a) single- vs. multi-prototype mentions; (b) removing entity signals during training; (c) replacing LM-based disambiguation with a simpler cosine similarity selection; (d) varying the number of prototypes.
- To demonstrate generality and avoid regressing on standard word-level tasks, consider evaluating the learned word embeddings on common benchmarks or downstream tasks (e.g., POS, parsing, SRL, or coreference) or at least report intrinsic word similarity/analogy results against strong baselines.
- Improve terminology and notation: define “mention,” “sense,” and “entity” once and use consistently; clearly separate training resources from test-time resources.
- Clean up the references (duplicates, typos, missing venues/years).
- If possible, release code and pretrained embeddings for reproducibility.
