Summary
This paper proposes a multi-prototype mention embedding model that jointly learns representations for words, entity mentions, and entities in a shared semantic space. To address ambiguity, each surface mention is assigned multiple sense embeddings, and a lightweight language-modeling objective is used to disambiguate mentions in context. The method is evaluated primarily on entity linking, with qualitative analyses and quantitative results showing modest but consistent improvements.

Contributions (as I see them)
- A unified neural framework that integrates entity, mention, and sense modeling with a simple language-model-based disambiguation mechanism for mentions.
- An uncomplicated training procedure that appears scalable and broadly applicable across tasks that require mention/entity disambiguation.
- An empirical study showing competitive results on entity linking and analyses suggesting the learned representations are coherent.

Strengths
- Integrative modeling: Learning word, mention (with multiple senses), and entity embeddings jointly is a well-motivated idea that directly targets ambiguity in the shared space, and it helps bridge text and KB signals without heavy feature engineering.
- Simple and practical training: The learning objective and training pipeline are minimally complex and likely easy to reproduce and extend, which increases potential impact across downstream applications.
- Solid empirical evaluation: While gains are not dramatic, the reported results on a standard entity linking benchmark are consistent and in some cases state-of-the-art; qualitative case studies support the learned sense structure.
- Clear formalization in parts: Sections detailing the objective and optimization are mostly crisp, aiding understanding and reproducibility.

Weaknesses
- Limited novelty: The core idea is close to prior work on joint text–KB embeddings and multi-sense embeddings; improvements over strong baselines are modest. The paper is particularly close to Yamada et al. (2016), and the differences are not adequately articulated or ablated.
- Exposition gaps: Section 3 and parts of Figure 2 lack detail (e.g., how senses are induced/selected, negative sampling specifics), hindering clarity; the reference list also contains errors/duplications.
- Terminology confusion: Redundant labels such as “dictionary of mentions,” “dictionary of entity–mention pairs,” and “text anchors” obscure the data assumptions and construction process.
- Generality not demonstrated: No evaluation of the learned word embeddings on other NLP tasks (e.g., parsing, coreference, SRL) to ensure they are not weaker than simpler wordspaces.

Questions to Authors
- Please delineate concretely how your approach differs from Yamada et al. (2016) (architecture, training signals, disambiguation mechanism), and provide ablations isolating each difference.
- Clarify the exact construction and role of the “dictionaries” and “anchors.” Are they identical resources?
- Provide more details on sense induction, the disambiguation LM, and negative sampling choices.
- Can you report transfer evaluations of the word embeddings on parsing, coref, or SRL?

Note: I read the authors’ response; while helpful, it does not fully resolve the novelty and clarity concerns.
