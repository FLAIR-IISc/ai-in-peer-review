Summary
The paper proposes a multi-prototype mention embedding model that jointly learns representations for words, entities, and multiple senses per mention by integrating textual context and knowledge-base signals. A simple language-model-based disambiguation is used to select a sense for each mention. Applied to entity linking (EL), the method reportedly achieves state-of-the-art performance, and includes analyses on a smoothing parameter that balances prior popularity and context evidence. The paper offers a direct comparison to ALIGN, a closely related joint text–knowledge approach.

Contributions (as I see them)
- Primary: Empirical gains over ALIGN on supervised EL with very similar input data, suggesting improved joint text–knowledge representations specifically for ambiguous mentions.
- Secondary: Analysis of the smoothing/popularity parameter that yields actionable insight into how priors affect EL accuracy across mention frequencies.
- Tertiary: An apples-to-apples baseline setup against a strong, closely related model (ALIGN), facilitating clearer attribution of gains to modeling choices.

Strengths
- Strong empirical result: The method outperforms ALIGN in supervised EL, indicating that multi-prototype mention modeling adds value over single-prototype or entity-only embeddings when ambiguity is prominent.
- Useful parameter study: The smoothing parameter analysis is informative, clarifying the trade-off between popularity priors and contextual evidence—especially important for tail entities and noisy contexts.
- Comparative framing: A direct comparison with a closely related approach using similar inputs strengthens the empirical narrative and reduces dataset/feature confounds that often plague EL evaluations.
- Practicality: The proposed disambiguation mechanism is efficient and integrates naturally with the learned mention senses, suggesting a path to deployment.

Weaknesses
- Fairness of comparison: The ALIGN comparison is confounded by different hyperparameters (context window 10 vs 5, vector dims 500 vs 200). Without matched settings or sensitivity analyses, it is hard to ascribe gains to the model rather than capacity or context breadth.
- Graph/context ambiguity: N(e_j) is not clearly defined, and it is unclear whether the Wikipedia outlink graph is treated as directed or undirected. This choice can materially change entity contexts and learned vectors, affecting reproducibility and conclusions.
- Terminology and scope: “Mention” is ambiguously used; it is unclear whether embeddings represent surface-form types or document-specific tokens. The “mention embedding” seems to be for senses only, leaving the status of surface-form/type embeddings uncertain.
- Limited positioning: The impact of disambiguation order and the LM approach is hard to assess without comparisons to other unsupervised EL methods (e.g., graph-based/PPR variants), or ablations isolating the disambiguation component.

Questions to Authors (Optional)
- Please provide ALIGN results with matched hyperparameters (window size, dimensionality) or report sensitivity curves for both methods.
- Precisely define N(e_j). Is the outlink graph treated as directed, undirected, or symmetrized? How does this choice affect contexts?
- What exactly is embedded: mention surface-form types, token-level mentions, or only mention senses? How are senses tied back to surface forms?
- Can you compare your LM-based disambiguation to standard unsupervised EL baselines or provide ablations on disambiguation order?
