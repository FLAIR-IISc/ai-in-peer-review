Summary:
The paper addresses entity disambiguation/linking (EDL) to Wikipedia by introducing a two-step pipeline: (1) learn multi-prototype mention embeddings (MPME) that capture distinct “senses” for each surface form by jointly modeling textual context and KB entities; (2) disambiguate a mention in context to one of its mention-sense embeddings and then map that sense to a KB entity. An efficient language-model-based disambiguator with smoothing is proposed. The approach is evaluated both intrinsically (embedding quality) and extrinsically on an EDL benchmark, with reported improvements over direct mention→entity mapping baselines.

Main contributions (as I see them):
1) Empirical finding that inserting an explicit mention-sense layer (multi-prototype mention embeddings) in a joint text–KB embedding framework yields better EDL accuracy than direct mention→entity mapping (including a strong ALIGN/Yamada-style baseline).
2) A practical, efficient language-model-based disambiguation method with a well-motivated smoothing scheme for selecting mention senses in context.
3) A coherent joint training setup that produces compatible word, entity, and mention-sense representations, facilitating simple, fast scoring at test time.

Strengths:
- Clear and practical modeling: The joint embedding framework is methodologically clean, follows best practices (distributional context, negative sampling, shared space for words/entities), and integrates naturally with a simple disambiguation objective. This makes replication and adoption feasible.
- Empirical improvements over direct mapping: The core result—MPME/SPME outperforming direct mention→entity mapping—is important and practically relevant for EDL, where ambiguity is concentrated at the surface-form level. The ablation between single- and multi-prototype variants is especially useful for demonstrating the incremental benefit of multiple senses.
- Efficient disambiguation with smoothing: The language-model-based disambiguator is both simple and effective. The smoothing parameter in Section 6.4.2 is a nice, pragmatic addition that stabilizes decisions when context is sparse or noisy; this is a common pain point in EDL and the paper addresses it well.
- Joint space is broadly useful: The qualitative analyses suggest that word, entity, and mention-sense embeddings are mutually consistent. This supports downstream extensibility (e.g., to related tasks such as query expansion or candidate generation) without additional engineering.
- Clarity of presentation: The paper is generally well written and the pipeline is easy to follow. The separation between learning the space and performing disambiguation is crisp.

Weaknesses:
- Under-explained mechanism behind the gains: The paper shows that MPME/SPME beat direct mapping but does not convincingly explain why. Is the improvement due to capacity (additional parameters/intermediate layer), better inductive bias (surface-form-level prototypes), or better data utilization? Capacity-controlled ablations (e.g., enlarge entity embeddings or add entity sub-prototypes) are needed to isolate the effect of the sense layer itself.
- Missing details on the sense inventory: It is unclear how mention senses are created and differentiated, how many senses are learned per mention, whether sense counts are fixed or data-driven, and whether senses map bijectively to entities. Without this, it is hard to assess novelty and to reproduce the approach or interpret the results.
- Ambiguity about what is unique at the mention-sense level: If mention senses are seeded or supervised by entity pages/anchors, what signal exists at the mention-sense level that is not already present in entity representations? If the learned sense vectors mostly mirror entity vectors, the intermediate layer could be redundant; if not, the paper should articulate the complementary information (e.g., context-profile clusters that cut across entities or capture regularities shared by subsets of entities).
- Scope overclaim: The title/intro frame the work as broadly “bridging text and knowledge,” but the method and evaluation are narrowly focused on EDL. The paper would benefit from tightening claims to EDL or adding evidence for broader impact (e.g., improvements on KB completion or relation extraction).
- Related work and evaluation coverage: Claims of novelty should be calibrated against prior EDL evaluations (e.g., TAC KBP EL tasks up to 2016). These should be cited and, ideally, used as comparative baselines or datasets. Several strong graph-based and neural EDL systems are cited but not clearly compared under matched settings, limiting the strength of the SOTA claim.

Questions to authors:
- Sense inventory:
  - How are the number of senses per mention determined (fixed K, data-driven clustering, nonparametric)? What is the distribution of senses/mention?
  - Are mention senses in 1–1 correspondence with entities for that surface form, many–one, or many–many? Do senses ever aggregate multiple entities?
- Training signals:
  - What precisely supervises the mention-sense vectors: context words only, linked entity signals, or both? If both, how are they weighted?
  - Are senses initialized from entity text/anchors or from unsupervised clustering of mention contexts?
- Why the intermediate layer helps:
  - Can you provide capacity-controlled ablations (e.g., wider entity embeddings, entity sub-prototypes) showing that gains persist beyond parameter count?
  - What additional information do mention senses capture beyond entity pages? Any analysis of sense–entity cosine similarities, cluster coherence, or examples where sense≠entity?
- Disambiguation:
  - Please detail the language model: vocabulary, context window, smoothing formula, and complexity. How sensitive are results to the smoothing hyperparameter?
  - How do you combine sense selection with candidate entity selection? Is there a fallback when the best sense has no candidate mapping?
- Evaluation:
  - Which benchmark(s) are used? How do you handle NIL entities? Please compare to strong TAC KBP EL baselines or justify their omission.
  - Provide error analysis categorizing where MPME/SPME help most (polysemous mentions, rare entities, short contexts).

Additional comments:
- Please update Section 5 to explicitly state the role and novelty of mention senses and clarify whether they are merely surrogates for entities or a genuinely distinct level of representation.
- Calibrate claims of novelty; cite and discuss TAC KBP EL evaluations and competitive baselines. Even if not reproducing those setups, acknowledging them will strengthen the paper.
- The reference list has numerous duplications and typos; please clean it.
- The smoothing idea in Section 6.4.2 is a highlight—consider a small ablation across context sparsity regimes to showcase its effect.
- Post-response note: Dismissing KBP-style evaluations is unconvincing. Joint embeddings should be validated via downstream task performance; EDL is the right proving ground. Strengthening empirical comparisons will materially improve the paper.
