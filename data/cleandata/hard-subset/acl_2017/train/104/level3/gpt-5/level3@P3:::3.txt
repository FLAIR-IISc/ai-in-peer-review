Summary
The paper proposes a Multi-Prototype Mention Embedding model that jointly learns embeddings for words, mentions (with multiple senses), and entities in a shared space. Ambiguity of surface forms is addressed by inducing several sense embeddings per mention and by using a simple language-model-based disambiguation procedure to select a sense in context. The model is trained from text and a KB-derived anchor dictionary, and is evaluated both qualitatively (nearest neighbors) and quantitatively on entity linking, reporting state-of-the-art results on a benchmark. I read the authors’ response; while some clarifications were helpful, the main concerns around novelty and exposition remain.

Contributions (as I see them; ordered by importance)
1) A unified neural language modeling framework that integrates words, entities, and multi-prototype mention embeddings, enabling sense-specific mention representations aligned with entities.
2) A simple, efficient disambiguation mechanism (language-model scoring) that leverages the learned space for entity linking without heavy feature engineering.
3) An empirical study showing competitive-to-state-of-the-art entity linking performance and reasonable qualitative analyses of the learned spaces.

Strengths
1) Unified modeling of entities, mentions, and senses: The idea to learn multi-prototype mention vectors jointly with words and entities in one space is conceptually clean and practically useful. It addresses mention ambiguity directly while keeping entities and words comparable, which benefits EL and potentially other tasks involving surface forms and KB entries.
2) Simple, scalable training and inference: The training objective is straightforward (word/mention-context prediction with entity signals), and the disambiguation relies on a light-weight language-model score rather than complex global inference. This simplicity makes the approach easier to reproduce and to plug into other applications.
3) Solid empirical evaluation on EL: The paper reports state-of-the-art or near SOTA EL scores using mostly the learned embeddings plus a simple resolver, demonstrating that the representation quality is competitive. The qualitative nearest-neighbor examples for mentions, senses, and entities are consistent with the intended behavior.
4) Clear formalization in parts: Despite some issues noted below, much of the modeling section is precise (e.g., objectives and training signals), which helps the reader reconstruct the approach.

Weaknesses
1) Limited novelty relative to prior work; incremental gains: The core elements—joint text+KB embedding, multi-prototype representations, and simple context-based disambiguation—have strong precedents (e.g., multi-sense word embeddings, joint word–entity spaces, and EL via embedding similarity). The paper feels close to Yamada et al. (2016/2017) in spirit and technique, and it does not sufficiently articulate what is fundamentally new versus reconfigured. Reported improvements, while solid, are not striking.
2) Exposition inconsistencies and missing details (esp. Section 3 and Figure 2): Important aspects of the training/inference pipeline are under-specified: how senses are induced and updated, how the number of prototypes per mention is determined, and how the LM-based disambiguation integrates with the embedding space at inference. Figure 2 and the surrounding text leave unclear data flow and parameter sharing. The response clarifies some notation but not all procedural details.
3) Terminology and object definitions are confusing: The paper alternates between “dictionary of mentions,” “dictionary of entity–mention pairs,” and “text anchors,” which appear to denote overlapping or distinct resources. This redundancy makes it hard to follow how candidates are constructed, how anchors supervise senses, and what exactly is used during training vs. inference.
4) Insufficient differentiation from Yamada et al. (2016): The paper should give a crisp, technical comparison (objective, supervision, negative sampling, candidate generation, inference) and a controlled empirical ablation showing where gains come from. As-is, the relation is relegated to related work without demonstrating substantive differences in method or results.
5) Limited downstream validation of word embeddings: The proposed training could hurt the pure word space if entity/mention signals dominate. Evaluating the word embeddings on standard tasks (e.g., dependency parsing, SRL, coreference, NER) would bolster the claim that the model is a general-purpose language representation learner and not narrowly optimized for EL.

Questions to Authors (to be addressed)
- Precise novelty: In technical terms, how does your objective and training pipeline differ from Yamada et al. (2016/2017) and other joint word–entity embedding work? Please provide a side-by-side comparison and, if possible, an ablation that isolates the multi-prototype mention layer.
- Sense induction: How do you determine the number of prototypes per mention (fixed K, adaptive, nonparametric)? What initialization, assignment, and update rules are used, and how do you prevent sense collapse or proliferation?
- Disambiguation model: Please detail the LM-based disambiguation—what exact score is computed, what context window, and how candidate priors are incorporated? Is the selection local or does it use any global coherence?
- Resources and terminology: Define precisely the “dictionary of mentions,” “dictionary of entity–mention pairs,” and “text anchors.” Which of these supervise training, which drive candidate generation, and how are they constructed (e.g., from Wikipedia anchors, redirects)?
- Computational complexity: What are training time, memory, and corpus scale? How does complexity grow with number of mentions and senses? Any tricks (e.g., Aho–Corasick, double-array tries) used at scale?
- Word-space quality: Can you report results on at least one or two standard downstream tasks (parsing, SRL, coreference, or NER) to confirm that word embeddings are not worse than strong baselines (e.g., word2vec, fastText)?

Additional Comments
- Please improve Section 3 and Figure 2: add a clear diagram of the training loop, parameter matrices (word, mention-sense, entity), and the disambiguation pipeline, with symbols aligned to equations.
- Provide stronger ablations: remove entity signals; replace multi-prototype with single-prototype mentions; vary candidate priors; evaluate with/without the LM scorer.
- Clarify hyperparameters and training details: negative sampling strategy, frequency thresholds, handling of rare mentions/entities, and sense pruning/merging criteria.
