Summary
The paper addresses entity disambiguation/linking (EDL) to Wikipedia via a two-step pipeline: a mention is first mapped to a mention-sense embedding (multi-prototype mention embedding, MPME/SPME), then to an entity in a joint embedding space where words, mention-senses, and entities are co-trained. A simple language-model-based disambiguator selects the sense, and the authors report improvements over direct mention→entity mapping.

Main contributions (as I see them)
1) Empirical finding: a two-step mention→sense→entity pipeline outperforms direct mention→entity baselines (e.g., ALIGN/Yamada et al.) on standard EDL benchmarks.
2) A clear joint training recipe for words, entities, and mention-senses, with a useful smoothing parameter that improves robustness (Sec. 6.4.2).
3) A practical, efficient disambiguation module that integrates with the joint space.

Strengths
- Empirical improvement over strong direct-mapping baselines. The core result—that MPME/SPME outperforms ALIGN/Yamada—appears consistent across datasets and is practically relevant for EDL.
- Methodological clarity and sound engineering. The joint embedding setup follows best practices, is easy to understand, and seems reproducible; the LM-based sense selector is efficient.
- Sensible modeling tweak. The smoothing parameter in Sec. 6.4.2 is a neat idea that stabilizes estimation and may generalize to related joint-embedding tasks.
- Potentially reusable representations. A shared space for words, senses, and entities can support other knowledge-intensive NLP tasks.

Weaknesses
- Missing core details about mention-senses. The paper does not specify how senses are created/differentiated, how many senses per mention, or whether senses map bijectively to KB entities. This is central to the contribution.
- Lack of explanation for why two-step helps. The authors claim MPME/SPME > direct mapping but do not analyze what additional signal mention-senses capture beyond entity embeddings, nor provide capacity-matched ablations.
- Overclaiming scope. Title/intro promise broad “bridging text and knowledge,” but the work is narrowly about EDL; framing should be tightened.
- Incomplete related work and novelty claim. Prior TAC KBP EDL evaluations (e.g., KBP 2016) are not acknowledged; these should be cited and used as baselines.
- Post-rebuttal concern: Dismissing KBP evaluation is not persuasive; embedding quality should be validated by downstream application performance.

Questions to Authors (to be addressed in rebuttal)
- Precisely define how mention-senses are induced: initialization, training signal, number per mention, selection criterion, and whether senses are tied one-to-one with entities.
- Confirm or correct the following assumptions: entities are represented from a neighboring-entity graph; words from distributional context; mention-senses are context-disambiguated distributional profiles. How are entity profiles built?
- If mention-senses are seeded from entity text, what signal exists at the sense level not already in entity pages? Why can’t it be merged into entity embeddings?
- Provide ablations: (a) direct vs two-step with matched parameters; (b) removing senses but enriching entity representations; (c) varying number of senses per mention.
- Update Section 5 to explicitly state the role and novelty of mention-senses and add TAC KBP baselines (e.g., 2016 task) and Yamada et al. comparisons.
