Summary
This paper tackles entity disambiguation/linking (EDL) to Wikipedia by introducing an intermediate mention-sense layer between surface mentions and KB entities. The core idea is to learn joint embeddings of words, entities, and multiple prototypes for each mention (Multi-Prototype Mention Embedding, MPME). A language-model-based disambiguation mechanism assigns each mention occurrence to one of its sense embeddings; a subsequent step maps the selected mention-sense to an entity. The authors claim that this two-step pipeline (mention → mention-sense → entity) outperforms direct mention → entity models and produces qualitatively interpretable embeddings.

Main contributions (as I see them)
1) Empirical finding: Introducing an intermediate mention-sense layer and learning multi-prototype mention embeddings yields stronger EDL accuracy than direct mention→entity alignment baselines (e.g., ALIGN/Yamada et al.). This is the primary contribution because the methodological novelty is incremental but the performance gain is the most concrete result.
2) Modeling/engineering: A joint embedding framework that simultaneously learns representations for words, entities, and multiple mention-senses, combined with an efficient language-model-based sense assignment and a smoothing strategy (Section 6.4.2).
3) Analysis: Qualitative evidence that the learned word/entity/mention-sense embeddings are coherent.

Strengths
- Clear and largely standard modeling recipe: The paper builds on well-understood embedding techniques (skip-gram style training, joint text–KB objectives), which makes the approach easy to follow and, in principle, reproducible. The use of a language-model-based disambiguation procedure is sensible and efficient.
- Empirical improvements over direct alignment: The main quantitative result—that the two-step MPME pipeline beats direct mention→entity mapping (e.g., Yamada et al.)—is valuable. Given the strong baseline, this supports the claim that an intermediate sense layer can help EDL.
- Interpretable embedding space: The qualitative analyses for words, entities, and mention-senses help build confidence that the learned space captures meaningful relations across modalities (text and KB).
- Practical design detail: The smoothing parameter in Section 6.4.2 is a useful, well-motivated tweak that appears to improve robustness in disambiguation, and could be adopted by others irrespective of the broader framework.

Weaknesses
- Overclaiming scope: Title and introduction frame the work as broadly “bridging text and knowledge,” but the approach and evaluation focus narrowly on EDL. This mismatch makes it harder to assess the generality of the contribution and should be corrected in positioning and claims.
- Missing core methodological details on mention-senses: The paper does not specify how mention-senses are created/differentiated, how many senses per mention are used, nor whether senses correspond bijectively to KB entities. It is unclear whether senses are seeded/clusters from textual contexts, from entity pages, or both; how sense proliferation is controlled; and what objective/criterion decides when a mention requires multiple prototypes. Without this, the approach is difficult to reproduce and interpret.
- Insufficient justification for the intermediate layer: While the two-step approach outperforms direct mapping, the paper does not explain why. What information do mention-senses capture that entity embeddings do not? If mention-senses are largely induced from entity texts or profiles, why can’t that signal be folded directly into entity representations? The paper needs ablations and analysis (e.g., with/without sense layer; single- vs multi-prototype; varying number of prototypes) to tease out causal factors.
- Incomplete evaluation landscape: Strong prior EDL evaluations and systems exist under TAC KBP (e.g., 2016 task). The paper does not compare against or even properly contextualize these, weakening the claim of state-of-the-art. At minimum, related work and a discussion of how the proposed setup aligns with KBP settings, metrics (e.g., micro-accuracy, NIL handling), and constraints are needed; ideally, results on a KBP benchmark should be included.
- Limited exploration of design alternatives: The paper “follows best practices” but does not probe alternatives (e.g., clustering algorithms for sense induction, parameterization of the sense prior, contrastive vs LM-based disambiguation, different ways to construct entity profiles/contexts). This limits insight into what aspects are essential vs incidental.

Questions to authors
- Pipeline confirmation: Please confirm that the pipeline is (i) learn joint embeddings for words/entities/mention-senses; (ii) use a language-model-based method to assign each mention occurrence to a sense; (iii) then map the sense to an entity. If this is inaccurate, provide the exact sequence.
- Definition and induction of mention-senses:
  - How are mention-senses initialized and differentiated? Are they induced via clustering of mention contexts, via entity text, or another procedure?
  - How many senses per mention are allowed and how is this number chosen or adapted?
  - Do senses correspond one-to-one with entities for a given mention, many-to-one, or can multiple senses map to the same entity?
- Unique signal at the mention-sense level: What information exists at the mention-sense layer that is not already encoded in entity embeddings or entity pages? Please provide concrete examples where the sense layer resolves an ambiguity that entity-only models cannot.
- Entity and context construction: How are entity “profiles” built (neighbors in the KB graph, Wikipedia pages, anchor contexts)? How are word contexts sampled and weighted? What is the negative sampling strategy?
- Ablations and sensitivity: Please provide results for (a) removing the sense layer, (b) single- vs multi-prototype per mention, (c) varying the number of prototypes, (d) different smoothing values and the LM vs simpler disambiguation heuristics.
- Evaluation breadth: Can you report results or at least discuss applicability under TAC KBP (including NIL detection/cluster metrics)? If out of scope, explain why that setting is not appropriate for the claimed contribution.

Additional comments
- Please revise the title/intro to align claims with the actual scope (EDL). Make the role and novelty of mention-senses explicit in Section 5.
- The references contain numerous duplicates and typos; please clean and normalize.
- Report training/inference efficiency (time, memory) and complexity added by the sense layer at test time.

Post-response note
If the authors argue that TAC KBP evaluation is irrelevant, I disagree: joint embeddings are best validated by downstream application performance, not only by conceptual elegance or intrinsic analyses. Comparative results or a clear rationale for omitting such evaluation are necessary to substantiate the paper’s impact.
