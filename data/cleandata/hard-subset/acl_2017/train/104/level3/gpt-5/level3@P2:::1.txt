Summary and contributions
This paper proposes a joint text–knowledge representation framework that learns multiple sense (prototype) embeddings for ambiguous mention strings. The model leverages textual contexts and knowledge base (KB) entity neighborhoods to induce sense-specific representations, and uses an efficient language-model-based procedure to disambiguate a mention to a sense at inference time. The authors evaluate primarily on entity linking (EL), providing both qualitative analyses of the learned spaces and quantitative results.

As I see it, the main contributions are:
1) Empirical gains over a closely related joint text–KB baseline (ALIGN) on supervised EL with similar input data, indicating improved joint representations for ambiguous mentions.
2) A multi-prototype representation for surface-form mentions that couples textual and KB entity contexts, bringing sense-aware modeling to EL in a unified embedding space.
3) An analysis of a smoothing parameter that modulates popularity priors in EL, offering practical insight into the popularity–context trade-off.

Strengths
- Strong empirical improvements over a close baseline: The head-to-head comparison with ALIGN—arguably the most directly comparable prior approach using similar inputs—shows consistent improvements in supervised EL. This is a compelling indicator that the proposed representations capture mention–entity semantics better than prior joint embedding methods.

- Direct and focused comparison setup: By aligning input data and pipeline components with ALIGN where possible, the paper reduces many confounding variables typical in EL comparisons (annotation coverage, KB scope, etc.), making the observed gains more believable. The emphasis on a closely related approach (rather than unrelated pipelines) strengthens the claim that the added value comes from the representation and disambiguation choices.

- Insightful analysis of popularity smoothing: The study of the smoothing parameter clarifies how entity popularity interacts with contextual evidence in EL. The reported sensitivity offers practical guidance for deployment and illuminates why certain errors occur when popularity overwhelms context or vice versa.

- Conceptually sound multi-prototype design: Treating mentions as polysemous surface forms and learning multiple sense embeddings by jointly modeling textual contexts and KB neighbors is a natural and well-motivated extension of both multi-sense word embeddings and joint text–KB learning. The approach directly addresses the core ambiguity challenge in EL.

- Simple, seemingly efficient disambiguation: The language-model-based sense selection procedure appears straightforward to implement and efficient at inference, making the approach more practical for large-scale EL than heavier collective inference methods.

Weaknesses
- Fairness of the ALIGN comparison is confounded by hyperparameter discrepancies: The reported comparison uses different context window sizes (10 vs. 5) and vector dimensionalities (500 vs. 200). Both choices are known to materially affect embedding quality and downstream EL. Without matched settings or sensitivity analyses for both systems, it is difficult to attribute the gains to the proposed modeling rather than to more favorable hyperparameters.

- Underspecified graph neighborhood N(ej): It is unclear whether the Wikipedia outlink graph is treated as directed or undirected, and whether N(ej) includes outgoing links, incoming links, or both. These choices directly affect entity contexts, relatedness, and ultimately the learned embeddings and EL performance. The ambiguity hinders reproducibility and makes it hard to interpret where improvements come from.

- Ambiguity in “mention” terminology and granularity: The paper alternates between “mention” as a surface-form type and as a document-level occurrence. From the text, “mention embeddings” appear to be sense prototypes at the type level, not token-level vectors. The training/inference pipeline (what is learned vs. inferred per token, whether a sense-agnostic string embedding exists, how senses are instantiated) needs clearer exposition.

- Limited assessment of the disambiguation component and ordering: The effectiveness of the language-model-based sense disambiguation and the impact of disambiguation order are hard to gauge. Comparisons with other unsupervised or lightly supervised EL methods (e.g., prior-only, popularity+context baselines, or graph-based PPR variants) are missing, making it unclear how much of the gain stems from the new embeddings versus the disambiguation strategy or the popularity smoothing.

- Missing ablations to isolate contributions: It would strengthen the paper to quantify (a) single- vs. multi-prototype mentions, (b) text-only vs. KB-only vs. joint training, (c) the effect of the number of prototypes per mention, and (d) robustness of the smoothing parameter across datasets. These would clarify which components are essential and how general the findings are.

Questions to authors
- Please define N(ej) precisely. Is the Wikipedia graph treated as directed? Do you include outgoing links, incoming links, or both? How are redirects, disambiguation pages, and template links handled?

- What exactly is a “mention” in your embedding space? Do you learn a fixed set of sense prototypes per surface-form type corpus-wide, or per document? Do you also maintain a sense-agnostic surface-form embedding? How are the number of prototypes chosen/learned and how are senses initialized?

- Can you re-run ALIGN with the same context window and vector dimensionality as your model (and vice versa), or provide sensitivity plots for both systems to demonstrate the improvements are not due to hyperparameter advantages?

- What is the computational complexity and runtime of the disambiguation step per mention and per document? Does your approach perform any collective/global reasoning, or is it purely local? If ordering influences results, how is the order determined and how sensitive are results to it?

- Could you compare your disambiguation module to standard unsupervised EL baselines (prior-only, prior+context, or PPR-based approaches) while holding embeddings fixed to disentangle the contributions?

- How stable is the smoothing parameter across datasets and domains? Do you tune it per dataset, and if so, how?

Additional comments
- Writing/notation: Several definitions (e.g., N(ej), directedness) and the “mention” terminology would benefit from clearer, earlier definitions. Consider renaming “mention embedding” to “surface-form sense embedding” to reduce ambiguity.

- Reproducibility: Please release code, pre-trained embeddings, and detailed hyperparameters/preprocessing steps (vocabulary sizes, graph statistics, windowing, negatives).

- References contain many duplicates and typos; please clean the bibliography.
