Summary: The paper proposes a Multi-Prototype Mention Embedding model that learns multiple sense embeddings per surface mention by jointly leveraging textual context (words) and a knowledge base (entities). An efficient language-model-based disambiguation procedure then assigns each mention occurrence to a specific sense and entity. The authors provide qualitative analyses of the learned spaces and evaluate on a standard entity linking benchmark, reporting state-of-the-art performance with modest but consistent gains.

Contributions (as I see them, strongest first):
1) A simple joint training framework that induces multiple embeddings per mention by integrating word and entity signals, reducing ambiguity inherent in surface forms shared by multiple entities.
2) An efficient disambiguation scheme based on a language model over contexts that selects among the learned mention senses/entities without heavy global inference.
3) An empirical study demonstrating competitive EL performance and qualitative evidence that word, entity, and mention-sense embeddings are coherent.

Strengths:
- Conceptual integration of mentions, entities, and senses within one neural framework. Treating mentions as first-class objects with multiple sense prototypes bridges multi-sense word representation work and joint text–KB representation work. This directly addresses the core ambiguity of shared surface forms and yields more interpretable neighborhoods in the embedding space.
- Simplicity and scalability. The learning procedure closely follows standard neural language modeling (negative sampling/contrastive estimation style) and should scale to large corpora and KBs. The local LM-based disambiguation is lightweight compared to graph-based collective inference, making the approach attractive for practical deployment and potentially applicable beyond EL.
- Solid, if not spectacular, empirical support. On the reported EL benchmark(s), the method achieves state-of-the-art accuracy with consistent gains over reasonable baselines. The ablations (as described) suggest the multi-prototype component contributes beyond single-prototype mention embeddings. Qualitative analyses (nearest neighbors/visualizations) reinforce that the learned spaces capture intuitive semantics.
- Reasonably clear formalization in parts. The optimization objective and training signals (words vs. entities) are presented clearly in places, making it easier to reason about the method and potentially reproduce it.

Weaknesses:
- Limited novelty relative to prior work. Multi-prototype embeddings (e.g., Reisinger & Mooney; Huang et al.; Neelakantan et al.; Tian et al.) and joint text–KB embeddings for EL (e.g., Wang et al.; Toutanova et al.; Han et al.) are well-studied. The closest prior (Yamada et al., 2016) jointly learns word and entity embeddings for EL. The main delta here—multiple prototypes for mentions plus a local LM disambiguation—feels incremental, and the empirical improvements, while positive, are not striking.
- Insufficient differentiation and comparison to Yamada et al. (2016). The paper does not thoroughly articulate architectural/training differences (e.g., how anchors are used, negative sampling strategies, treatment of context words vs. entities) nor provide an apples-to-apples comparison under identical data and candidate generation. It remains unclear how much of the gain stems from multi-prototype modeling vs. other implementation details.
- Exposition and terminology issues limit clarity. Section 3 and parts of Figure 2 do not fully specify how senses are created/updated, the number of prototypes per mention (fixed vs. dynamic), or the exact training-time sense assignment. The description of the “language model” used for disambiguation is too high-level to reimplement. Terminology such as “dictionary of mentions,” “dictionary of entity–mention pairs,” and “anchors” is used inconsistently, making it hard to follow the data pipeline. The references list contains duplicates and typographical errors.
- Narrow evaluation scope. The claim that the learned embeddings are broadly useful would be stronger with intrinsic evaluation (e.g., word similarity/analogy) and at least one non-EL downstream task (e.g., NER, parsing, coreference, SRL) to show that word embeddings are not weakened by the joint training. On EL, comparisons to strong global/collective disambiguation systems (e.g., Hoffart et al.; Pershina et al.) would clarify the trade-off between local LM disambiguation and global coherence.
- Missing analysis of limits and costs. There is little discussion of the computational/memory footprint of maintaining multiple prototypes per mention, sensitivity to the number of prototypes and context window, and performance on rare/long-tail mentions. An error analysis would help diagnose when multi-prototype modeling helps or hurts.

Questions to authors:
- How are multiple prototypes per mention induced and maintained during training? Is the number of senses fixed a priori or dynamically allocated/merged? What heuristic or probabilistic criterion governs splitting/merging?
- Is the LM-based disambiguation trained jointly with the embeddings or post hoc? What is the exact scoring function and its complexity per candidate? How does it differ from standard context-similarity scoring in EL?
- Can you provide a controlled comparison with Yamada et al. (2016): same training corpus, candidate generator, and evaluation protocol? When you disable multi-prototype modeling, how close are you to their numbers?
- How sensitive are results to the number of prototypes per mention, context window, and negative sampling rate? Please include sensitivity plots.
- Do the learned word embeddings retain competitiveness on intrinsic tests or a representative downstream task? If not, what trade-offs are observed?
- What is the coverage of your mention/anchor dictionary and KB? How do you handle NIL entities and unseen surface forms at test time?
