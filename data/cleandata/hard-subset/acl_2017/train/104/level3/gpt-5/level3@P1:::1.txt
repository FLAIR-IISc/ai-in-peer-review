Summary:
The paper proposes a Multi-Prototype Mention Embedding (MPME) model that learns multiple sense embeddings for ambiguous mention strings by jointly modeling textual context (words) and knowledge base (KB) entities. A language-model-based disambiguation step maps a mention occurrence to one of its learned sense embeddings. The learned representations are evaluated qualitatively and quantitatively, and the model is applied to entity linking (EL), where it outperforms prior joint text–KB embedding approaches, including ALIGN, on supervised EL benchmarks. The paper also studies a smoothing parameter governing popularity priors in EL.

Contributions (as I see them, in order of strength):
1) Demonstration that multi-prototype mention embeddings jointly trained with words and entities yield consistent gains for supervised entity linking over a strong, closely related joint embedding baseline (ALIGN).
2) An efficient language-model-based disambiguation method that operationalizes the use of multi-sense mention embeddings at inference time.
3) Empirical analysis of a smoothing parameter controlling popularity, shedding light on the trade-off between contextual evidence and prior frequency in EL.

Strengths:
- Strong empirical improvement over a closely related baseline: The paper reports clear gains over ALIGN in supervised EL using very similar input resources. This direct head-to-head is valuable because it isolates the effect of the multi-prototype modeling for mentions within a comparable training paradigm (joint text–KB embeddings), strengthening the claim that improved mention representations matter for EL.
- Useful analysis of popularity smoothing: The exploration of the smoothing parameter quantifies how popularity interacts with context-based scoring, offering practical guidance for EL practitioners. The analysis goes beyond one-off tuning by showing performance trends as the parameter varies, making the results actionable.
- Sensible modeling choice tailored to mention ambiguity: Extending multi-sense embedding ideas (well-studied for words) to mentions is a natural but non-trivial step in the EL setting, where ambiguity and context granularity differ from general word sense. The joint optimization with entity representations plausibly encourages sense clusters that are discriminative for EL.
- Practical inference method: The language-model-based disambiguation is conceptually simple and likely efficient, which increases the deployability of the approach. The method integrates learned embeddings with a standard EL scoring pipeline without expensive global inference.
- Qualitative and quantitative evidence on embedding quality: The paper presents both intrinsic analyses and downstream EL evaluation, providing a more rounded assessment of the learned representations than many prior works that report only task metrics.

Weaknesses:
- Fairness of comparison with ALIGN is confounded: The reported comparison uses different hyperparameters (e.g., window size 10 vs. 5, embedding dimension 500 vs. 200). Given the sensitivity of embedding methods to such choices, it is hard to attribute improvements solely to the multi-prototype mention modeling. A controlled study using matched hyperparameters or cross-checked settings (ALIGN at 500-d/10 and MPME at 200-d/5) would strengthen the claim.
- Graph adjacency definition is underspecified: The notation N(e_j) is not clearly defined, and it is unclear whether the Wikipedia outlink graph is treated as directed or symmetrized. This materially affects entity contexts and learned entity vectors, and thus EL performance. Without this detail, reproduction and interpretation of the gains are difficult.
- Ambiguity in “mention” terminology and granularity: It is unclear whether “mention embeddings” are trained for mention string types (e.g., “Washington”) with multiple sense prototypes, or for individual document-level mentions. The text suggests prototypes correspond to senses of a string, but the terminology blurs type vs. token. This affects how one should use or evaluate the embeddings (e.g., reusability across documents).
- Limited positioning vs. unsupervised EL and disambiguation order: The paper emphasizes a language-model-based disambiguation but does not compare to unsupervised EL baselines (e.g., popularity-only, local context-only, standard collective graph-based methods without supervision) under the same candidate generation. It is therefore hard to assess the incremental value of the proposed disambiguation order/strategy independent of supervision.
- Missing ablations on model components: The paper would benefit from isolating the effect of multi-prototype vs. single-prototype mention representations, the contribution of entity–entity graph context vs. word–entity context, and the number of prototypes per mention (fixed vs. adaptive). These ablations would clarify where the gains come from.

Questions to Authors:
- Please precisely define N(e_j): What neighbors are included (outlinks, inlinks, both)? Is the graph treated as directed or undirected? Are links weighted, and are categories or redirects handled?
- What exactly is a “mention” in the embedding space? Are prototypes tied to surface forms (type-level) with sense clusters induced from all their occurrences, or are they document-specific? If type-level, how do you handle orthographic variants and alias consolidation?
- How are the number of prototypes per mention determined (fixed K, nonparametric, or clustering with threshold)? How sensitive is EL performance to K and to the clustering method or initialization?
- Can you report a controlled comparison with ALIGN using matched hyperparameters (window size, dimensionality, negative sampling, training steps), and/or a small grid to show robustness of your gains across such choices?
- What is the computational cost of the disambiguation step (time per mention, with and without popularity smoothing), and how does it scale with candidate set size?
- What candidate generation is used across systems? Is it identical between your model and ALIGN? Please clarify coverage (recall@K) to ensure differences are not due to upstream candidate quality.
- Do you evaluate with and without supervision in the EL classifier to isolate the contribution of the embeddings and the language-model disambiguation from task-specific training?

Additional comments:
- Please include an ablation showing a single-prototype version of your model to quantify the value of multi-prototype learning for mentions.
- Clarify training data overlap with evaluation (e.g., Wikipedia snapshot, anchor coverage) and whether any test leakage could occur via popularity statistics.
- Consider reporting results using standard collective EL baselines (Hoffart et al., Alhelbawy & Gaizauskas, Pershina et al.) under the same candidate generator to position your method beyond ALIGN.
- Minor: The references list includes many duplicates and typographical issues; please clean for the camera-ready.
