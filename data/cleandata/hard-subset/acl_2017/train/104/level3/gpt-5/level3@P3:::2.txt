Summary
The paper proposes a two-step pipeline for entity disambiguation/linking (EDL) to Wikipedia that first maps a surface form (mention) to a learned “mention sense” (multi-prototype mention embedding; MPME/SPME), and then maps that sense to a KB entity. All components (words, entities, mention senses) are learned jointly in a shared embedding space. A lightweight language-model-based disambiguator assigns contexts to mention senses. On standard EDL benchmarks, the approach outperforms direct mention→entity alignment models (e.g., ALIGN/Yamada et al.). The paper also reports qualitative analyses of the learned embeddings and introduces a smoothing parameter that stabilizes disambiguation.

Contributions (as I see them)
1) Empirical finding: An intermediate mention-sense layer (MPME/SPME) in a joint embedding framework improves EDL performance over direct mention→entity mappings.
2) A practical, efficient language-model-based disambiguation procedure and a smoothing parameter that improves robustness (Section 6.4.2).
3) A clear engineering recipe for joint training of words, entities, and (multi-prototype) mentions within one space.

Strengths
- Methodological clarity and solid engineering: The training objective and the joint embedding setup follow best practices for distributional and knowledge-enhanced embeddings. The model is easy to reason about and to reproduce given more detail.
- Empirical gains over strong direct-alignment baselines: The central result—two-step MPME/SPME beats ALIGN/Yamada et al.—is practically important for EDL, where ambiguity at the mention level is a core challenge.
- Sensible disambiguation design: The LM-based assignment of contexts to mention senses is simple, scalable, and appears to work well in practice. The smoothing parameter in Section 6.4.2 is a thoughtful, well-motivated tweak that measurably improves stability.
- Joint space quality: Qualitative analyses indicate coherent neighborhoods for words/entities/mentions, suggesting the learned space captures useful cross-modal relatedness.
- Clear separation of components: The paper cleanly delineates representation learning versus inference (disambiguation), aiding interpretability and potential reuse.

Weaknesses
- Overclaiming in title/introduction: The paper positions itself as “bridging text and knowledge,” but the technical focus and evaluation are narrowly on EDL. Please calibrate claims to the actual scope.
- Missing explanation for the key empirical finding: The paper does not explain why an intermediate mention-sense layer outperforms direct mention→entity mapping. What information do mention senses encode that entities lack, and why is that not better folded into entity representations?
- Underspecified construction of mention senses: It is unclear how senses are created/differentiated, how many senses per mention are learned, whether the number is fixed or data-driven, and whether senses map bijectively to entities. Without this, reproducibility and interpretability suffer.
- Limited exploration of alternatives and ablations: The paper does not compare against strong alternatives such as (i) richer entity embeddings with context-sensitive features, (ii) collective inference baselines, or (iii) direct mention→entity models augmented with context attention, prior link probabilities, or surface-form features. Ablations isolating the impact of the intermediate layer, the LM disambiguator, and the smoothing parameter are needed.
- Related work and evaluation gaps (TAC KBP): The claim to novelty ignores prior TAC KBP EDL evaluations (e.g., KBP 2016) and widely-used benchmarks/protocols. These should be cited and, ideally, used as baselines/evaluation to validate real-world utility. Post-response, I remain unsatisfied with dismissing KBP: embedding quality must be validated via application performance, not just conceptual elegance.
- Minor: References contain duplications and typos; Section 5 does not clearly articulate the role/novelty of mention senses; limited error analysis and discussion of pipeline error propagation.

Questions to Authors (to be addressed in rebuttal)
1) Role of mention senses: Concretely, what additional signal do mention senses capture beyond entity embeddings? Why can’t that signal be merged into enhanced entity representations (e.g., entity senses or context-conditioned entity encoders)?
2) Construction of mention senses:
   - How are senses induced (clustering of contexts, nonparametric methods, fixed k per mention, or learned allocation)? Any initialization from entity pages?
   - How many senses per mention on average; distribution across frequent/rare mentions?
   - Is there a bijective mapping between senses and KB entities? Can multiple senses map to the same entity (polysemy in context) or vice versa?
3) Assumed setup (please confirm/correct):
   - Entity profiles: entity embeddings are learned from the KB graph (neighboring entities/relations) and possibly from entity page text.
   - Word embeddings: trained from distributional context windows over text.
   - Mention senses: context-disambiguated distributional profiles specific to surface forms, learned jointly with entities/words; disambiguation assigns mention occurrences to senses via the LM.
   If this is inaccurate, please provide the precise data sources and training signals per component.
4) If senses are seeded from entity text, what signal exists at the mention-sense level that is not already present in entity pages? Why is a separate embedding layer preferable to augmenting entity embeddings with mention-conditioned parameters or attention to local context?
5) Section 5 request: Please explicitly state the role and novelty of mention senses, including how they interact with words/entities during training and at inference; add ablations proving the intermediate layer is the cause of the gains.
6) Ablations and diagnostics:
   - Compare direct mention→entity with identical training data/hyperparameters to isolate the contribution of the intermediate layer.
   - Report sensitivity to number of senses per mention; show performance vs. k and with/without nonparametric growth.
   - Replace the LM disambiguator with a simple cosine or attention-based scorer; quantify the delta.
   - Analyze error propagation across the two steps; provide per-category errors (homographs, rare entities, NILs).
7) TAC KBP: Please cite TAC KBP EDL (e.g., 2016) and, if possible, evaluate or align your setup with these protocols. If not feasible, justify rigorously why, and provide alternative external validation that approximates application conditions.

Additional comments
- The smoothing parameter in Section 6.4.2 is a nice, generalizable idea; consider highlighting it as a standalone takeaway with broader applicability.
- Please clean the references (duplicates/typos) and temper the abstract/title to reflect the EDL-centric scope.
