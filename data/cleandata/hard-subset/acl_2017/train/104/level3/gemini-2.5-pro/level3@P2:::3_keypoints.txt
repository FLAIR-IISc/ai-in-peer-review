- Summary: Proposes a joint model that learns word, entity, and multi-prototype mention embeddings to handle mention ambiguity, trained from text and a knowledge base and applied to entity linking with state-of-the-art results.

- Contribution 1: Introduces a unified framework that jointly learns multiple sense vectors per ambiguous mention alongside word and entity embeddings in a shared space.

- Contribution 2: Demonstrates solid empirical gains on a standard entity linking benchmark using the learned multi-prototype embeddings and a disambiguation method.

- Strength 1: Conceptually elegant — explicitly modeling multiple prototypes per mention integrates text and KB signals to directly address mention ambiguity.

- Strength 2: Simple, scalable, and generalizable — builds on familiar language-modeling techniques and yields embeddings useful for many downstream tasks.

- Strength 3: Thorough evaluation — quantitative and qualitative analyses show improvements over strong baselines and provide insight into embedding quality.

- Weakness 1: Limited novelty — combines existing ideas (multi-prototype embeddings and text–KB joint embedding) with incremental empirical gains.

- Weakness 2: Insufficient comparison to closely related work — lacks a detailed technical differentiation from very similar prior work (e.g., Yamada et al., 2016).

- Weakness 3: Clarity and reproducibility issues — Section 3 and Figure 2 are hard to follow, omit key details, and use inconsistent terminology.

- Question 1: Please detail the key technical differences between your model and Yamada et al. (2016).

- Question 2: Clarify the training use of multiple sense embeddings — are senses selected per instance or aggregated, and how does this enter the objective?

- Question 3: Have you evaluated the quality of the learned word embeddings on intrinsic or extrinsic tasks to show joint modeling doesn’t degrade (and may improve) word representations?
