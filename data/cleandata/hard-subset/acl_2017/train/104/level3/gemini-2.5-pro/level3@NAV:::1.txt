**Summary and Contributions**

This paper introduces a Multi-Prototype Mention Embedding model to address ambiguity in entity linking by jointly learning representations for words, entities, and multiple senses for each mention. The model combines textual contexts with a knowledge base to generate these embeddings. A language model-based approach is then used for disambiguation.

The primary contributions of this work are:
1.  An empirical demonstration that a multi-prototype model for mention senses can outperform a strong single-prototype baseline (ALIGN) on a supervised entity linking task, suggesting the value of modeling ambiguity directly in the joint embedding space.
2.  A useful analysis of a smoothing parameter that provides insight into balancing contextual information with entity popularity, a key challenge in entity linking.

**Strengths**

1.  The model achieves superior performance over ALIGN in supervised entity linking. This is a strong result, providing evidence that jointly learning multi-prototype representations for mentions improves the quality of text-knowledge embeddings for downstream tasks.
2.  The paper includes a direct comparison with ALIGN, a highly relevant and closely related method. By using similar input data, the authors provide a focused evaluation that effectively highlights the potential benefits of their multi-prototype approach over a single-prototype one.
3.  The analysis of the smoothing parameter is a valuable addition, yielding practical insights into the impact of entity popularity on linking accuracy. This exploration helps understand the model's behavior and offers guidance for tuning similar systems.

**Weaknesses**

1.  The experimental comparison with the primary baseline, ALIGN, is confounded. The authors report using different hyperparameters for their model (context window=10, dimensions=500) and the baseline (context window=5, dimensions=200). This makes it impossible to determine if the performance gains stem from the novel architecture or simply from a more favorable hyperparameter configuration. This is a critical flaw that undermines the central claim of superiority.
2.  Key methodological details are ambiguous, hindering clarity and reproducibility. For instance, the definition of N(e_j) (an entity’s neighborhood) is unclear regarding whether the Wikipedia outlink graph is treated as directed or undirected, a choice that significantly alters entity contexts. Furthermore, the terminology around "mention" is inconsistent; it is difficult to discern if embeddings represent mention strings (types) or are only learned for their disambiguated senses.
3.  The evaluation of the disambiguation component is insufficient. By not comparing against other unsupervised entity linking methods, it is hard to assess the standalone contribution and effectiveness of the proposed sense disambiguation ordering strategy.

**Questions to Authors**

1.  Could you justify the use of different hyperparameters for your model and the ALIGN baseline? To isolate the architectural contribution, could you provide results where hyperparameters like the context window size and embedding dimensions are held constant across both models?
2.  Please clarify whether the entity neighborhood N(e_j) is derived from a directed or undirected view of the Wikipedia link graph.
