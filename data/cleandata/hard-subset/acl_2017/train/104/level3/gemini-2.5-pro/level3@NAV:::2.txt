This paper presents a novel approach to entity disambiguation and linking (EDL) using a two-step pipeline: mapping a textual mention to an intermediate “mention sense,” and then mapping that sense to a knowledge base entity. The method relies on jointly learned embeddings for words, mention senses, and entities. The primary contribution is the empirical result showing that this two-step architecture (MPME/SPME) outperforms strong direct mention-to-entity mapping baselines on an EDL benchmark. A secondary contribution is the methodologically clear implementation, which follows standard embedding practices.

**Strengths**

1.  The paper addresses the important and challenging problem of lexical ambiguity in linking unstructured text to structured knowledge bases. The proposed architecture is intuitive and tackles the problem from a novel angle.
2.  The empirical results are the strongest part of the paper. The demonstrated improvement over direct-mapping models like Yamada et al. provides compelling evidence that the intermediate sense layer has practical value, motivating further investigation into this type of architecture.
3.  The methodology is clearly explained, and the inclusion of a smoothing parameter (Section 6.4.2) is a thoughtful technical detail that likely improves model robustness.

**Weaknesses**

1.  **Critical Lack of Justification for the Core Contribution:** While the paper shows *that* the intermediate sense layer works, it fails to explain *why*. What specific information does a “mention sense” capture that cannot be integrated into a richer entity representation directly? This is the central question of the paper, and its absence leaves the core contribution feeling like an engineering result without a clear scientific insight. The authors must provide a concrete explanation to justify this additional layer of complexity.
2.  **Missing Methodological Details and Reproducibility:** The paper omits crucial details about the mention senses, making the work difficult to reproduce or build upon. Key questions are unanswered: How are senses created or differentiated? How many senses are learned per mention? Is there a bijective mapping between senses and entities? I assume entity embeddings derive from the KB graph, word embeddings from distributional context, and mention-sense embeddings are context-disambiguated distributional profiles. The authors must confirm this and add these details.
3.  **Overclaiming and Missing Citations:** The title’s claim of “bridging text and knowledge” is too broad for a paper focused specifically on EDL. Furthermore, the paper incorrectly implies novelty in its evaluation, completely omitting citations to extensive prior art from shared tasks like the TAC KBP EDL evaluations (e.g., KBP 2016). The value of an embedding model must be validated through performance on established application benchmarks, not just its conceptual elegance, and this prior work must be acknowledged and discussed.
