**Summary of the Paper**

The paper proposes a novel Multi-Prototype Mention Embedding (MPME) model to tackle entity disambiguation/linking (EDL) to Wikipedia. The model learns multiple sense embeddings for each mention by jointly modeling words from textual contexts and entities derived from a knowledge base. The authors claim that their approach outperforms direct mention→entity mapping methods, such as ALIGN and Yamada et al.

**Main Contributions**

Based on the paper, I identify the following main contributions, in order of strength:

1. **Novel MPME model**: The authors propose a new approach to EDL by learning multiple sense embeddings for each mention, which is a significant contribution to the field.
2. **Joint embedding of text and knowledge**: The paper explores the integration of text and knowledge into a unified semantic space, which is a promising direction for future research.
3. **Experimental evaluation**: The authors conduct a thorough experimental evaluation of their approach, including qualitative and quantitative analysis, which provides valuable insights into the performance of their model.

**Strengths**

Here are my strongest arguments supporting the acceptance of this submission, in order of importance:

1. **Methodological clarity**: The paper is well-written and provides a clear explanation of the proposed MPME model, making it easy to follow and understand.
2. **Standard embedding best practices**: The authors follow standard embedding best practices, which ensures that their approach is robust and reliable.
3. **Experimental evaluation**: The paper provides a thorough experimental evaluation, including qualitative and quantitative analysis, which demonstrates the effectiveness of the proposed approach.
4. **Smoothing parameter idea**: The authors propose a novel smoothing parameter idea in Section 6.4.2, which is a valuable contribution to the field.

**Weaknesses**

Here are my strongest arguments against the acceptance of this submission, in order of importance:

1. **Overclaiming**: The title and introduction overclaim the broader impact of the paper, focusing narrowly on EDL while claiming to "bridge text and knowledge."
2. **Lack of explanation**: The authors do not provide a clear explanation of why their two-step MPME/SPME approach outperforms direct mention→entity mapping methods.
3. **Missing details**: The paper lacks important details, such as how mention senses are created/differentiated, how many senses per mention, and whether senses map bijectively to KB entities.
4. **Incorrect novelty claim**: The authors claim novelty for their approach, but prior TAC KBP EDL evaluations exist and should be cited as related baselines.

**Questions to Authors**

To clarify the contributions of this submission, I have the following questions for the authors:

1. Can you provide a concrete explanation of what additional information mention senses provide over entities and why that justifies an intermediate layer?
2. Can you confirm or correct the assumed setups outlined in the review, including entity profiles from neighboring-entity graph, words from distributional profiles, and mention senses as context-disambiguated distributional profiles?
3. Can you provide more details on how mention senses are created/differentiated, how many senses per mention, and whether senses map bijectively to KB entities?
4. If mention senses are seeded from entity text, can you explain what signal exists at the mention-sense level that is not already present in entity pages and whether that could instead be merged into entity representations?
