**Summary of the Paper**

The paper proposes a novel Multi-Prototype Mention Embedding (MPME) model to tackle entity disambiguation/linking (EDL) to Wikipedia. The model learns multiple sense embeddings for each mention by jointly modeling words from textual contexts and entities derived from a knowledge base. The authors also design an efficient language model-based approach to disambiguate each mention to a specific sense. The proposed method is evaluated on the benchmark dataset, achieving state-of-the-art performance.

**Contributions of the Work**

1. **Multi-Prototype Mention Embedding (MPME) model**: The authors propose a novel model that learns multiple sense embeddings for each mention, which is a significant contribution to the field of entity disambiguation/linking.
2. **Efficient language model-based disambiguation approach**: The authors design an efficient language model-based approach to disambiguate each mention to a specific sense, which is a practical contribution to the field.
3. **Evaluation on benchmark dataset**: The authors evaluate their proposed method on the benchmark dataset, achieving state-of-the-art performance, which demonstrates the effectiveness of their approach.

**Strengths**

1. **Methodologically clear and follows standard embedding best practices**: The authors' method is well-explained, and they follow standard embedding best practices, making it easy to understand and replicate their work.
2. **Novelty of the MPME model**: The authors propose a novel model that learns multiple sense embeddings for each mention, which is a significant contribution to the field of entity disambiguation/linking.
3. **Efficient language model-based disambiguation approach**: The authors design an efficient language model-based approach to disambiguate each mention to a specific sense, which is a practical contribution to the field.

**Weaknesses**

1. **Overclaiming in the title and introduction**: The title and introduction overclaim broader "bridging text and knowledge" while focusing narrowly on EDL, which may be misleading.
2. **Lack of explanation for the two-step pipeline**: The authors do not explain why their two-step MPME/SPME approach outperforms direct mention→entity mapping (ALIGN/Yamada et al.), which is a significant omission.
3. **Missing details on mention senses**: The authors do not provide sufficient details on how mention senses are created/differentiated, how many senses per mention, and whether senses map bijectively to KB entities, which makes it difficult to understand the novelty of their approach.
4. **Incorrect claim of novelty**: The authors claim novelty for their approach, but prior TAC KBP EDL evaluations exist and should be cited as related baselines (KBP 2016 task).

**Questions to Authors**

1. Can you provide a concrete explanation of what additional information mention senses provide over entities and why that justifies an intermediate layer?
2. Can you confirm or correct the assumed setups (entity profiles from neighboring-entity graph, words from distributional profiles, mention senses as context-disambiguated distributional profiles)?
3. How are mention senses created/differentiated, and how many senses per mention?
4. Do senses map bijectively to KB entities?
5. If mention senses are seeded from entity text, what signal exists at the mention-sense level that is not already present in entity pages, and could that instead be merged into entity representations?
6. Can you update Section 5 to explicitly state the role and novelty of mention senses if our understanding is correct?

**Additional Comments**

The reviewer likes the smoothing parameter idea in Section 6.4.2, which is a positive aspect of the paper. However, the reviewer is unsatisfied with the authors dismissing KBP evaluation, arguing that embeddings must be validated via application performance, not just conceptual elegance.
