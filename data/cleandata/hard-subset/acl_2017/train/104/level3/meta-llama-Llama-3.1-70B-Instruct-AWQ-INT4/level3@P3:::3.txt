**Paper Summary**

The paper proposes a novel Multi-Prototype Mention Embedding model that integrates entity, mention, and sense modeling within the same neural language modeling technique. The model learns multiple sense embeddings for each mention by jointly modeling words from textual contexts and entities derived from a knowledge base. The authors also design an efficient language model-based approach to disambiguate each mention to a specific sense. The proposed model is evaluated on the task of entity linking, and the results demonstrate the high quality of the word, entity, and multi-prototype mention embeddings.

**Main Contributions**

1. **Integrating entity, mention, and sense modeling**: The paper proposes a novel approach that integrates entity, mention, and sense modeling within the same neural language modeling technique. This is a significant contribution, as it addresses the challenge of ambiguity in the common space.
2. **Multi-Prototype Mention Embedding model**: The authors propose a new model that learns multiple sense embeddings for each mention by jointly modeling words from textual contexts and entities derived from a knowledge base. This model is a significant improvement over previous approaches that only consider a single sense embedding for each mention.
3. **Efficient language model-based approach**: The paper proposes an efficient language model-based approach to disambiguate each mention to a specific sense. This approach is simple and supports a large set of applications.

**Strengths**

1. **Good idea**: The paper proposes a good idea integrating entity, mention, and sense modeling within the same neural language modeling technique. This approach addresses the challenge of ambiguity in the common space.
2. **Simple neural learning and training procedure**: The authors use a simple neural learning and training procedure that supports a large set of applications. This makes the model more practical and applicable to various tasks.
3. **Solid empirical evaluation**: The paper presents solid empirical evaluation results, demonstrating the high quality of the word, entity, and multi-prototype mention embeddings.

**Weaknesses**

1. **Limited novel content**: The amount of genuinely novel content in the paper is limited. The improvements over previous approaches are not striking, and the paper does not significantly advance the state-of-the-art.
2. **Inconsistent prose and exposition**: The paper's prose and exposition are inconsistent. Section 3 and parts of Figure 2 lack clarity and detail, making it difficult to understand the model and its components.
3. **Redundant/confusing terminology**: The paper uses redundant and confusing terminology, such as "dictionary of mentions" vs "dictionary of entity-mention pairs" vs text anchors. This makes it difficult to understand the model and its components.
4. **Lack of differentiation from previous work**: The paper is quite close to Yamada et al. (2016) and does not sufficiently outline the differences between the two approaches.

**Suggestions**

1. **Evaluate the learned word embeddings on other NLP tasks**: The authors should evaluate the learned word embeddings on other NLP tasks, such as parsing, coreference, and semantic role labeling, to show that they are not weaker than simpler word spaces.

**Additional Comments**

The authors' response to the reviewer's comments is appreciated. However, the reviewer still has concerns about the paper's novelty and the lack of differentiation from previous work. The authors should address these concerns in their final submission.
