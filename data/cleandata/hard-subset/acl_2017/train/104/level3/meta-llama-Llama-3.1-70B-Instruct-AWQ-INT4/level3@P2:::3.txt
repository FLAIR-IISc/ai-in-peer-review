**Summary and Contributions**

This paper proposes a novel Multi-Prototype Mention Embedding model that integrates text and knowledge into a unified semantic space. The model learns multiple sense embeddings for each mention by jointly modeling words from textual contexts and entities derived from a knowledge base. The authors also design an efficient language model-based approach to disambiguate each mention to a specific sense. The main contributions of this work are:

1. A unified neural language modeling technique that integrates entity, mention, and sense modeling.
2. A simple and efficient learning and training procedure that supports a large set of applications.
3. Solid empirical evaluation and comparison with state-of-the-art methods.

**Strengths**

1. The paper proposes a good idea of integrating entity, mention, and sense modeling within the same neural language modeling technique. This approach has the potential to improve the performance of various natural language processing tasks.
2. The authors use a simple and efficient neural learning and training procedure that supports a large set of applications. This makes the model more practical and easier to implement.
3. The formal presentation is clear in places, and the empirical evaluation is solid, even if the improvements are not large. The authors provide a thorough comparison with state-of-the-art methods and demonstrate the effectiveness of their approach.

**Weaknesses**

1. The amount of genuinely novel content is limited. The paper builds upon existing work, and the improvements are not striking. The authors could have provided more innovative solutions or explored new research directions.
2. The prose and exposition are inconsistent. Section 3 and parts of Figure 2 lack clarity and detail, making it difficult to understand the model architecture and training procedure.
3. The terminology is redundant and confusing. For example, the authors use "dictionary of mentions," "dictionary of entity-mention pairs," and "text anchors" interchangeably, which can be confusing for readers.
4. The paper is quite close to Yamada et al. (2016) and does not sufficiently outline the differences. The authors should have provided a more detailed comparison with existing work and highlighted the unique contributions of their approach.

**Questions to Authors**

1. Can you provide more details on the model architecture and training procedure, especially in Section 3 and Figure 2?
2. How do you plan to address the issue of redundant and confusing terminology?
3. Can you provide a more detailed comparison with Yamada et al. (2016) and highlight the unique contributions of your approach?
4. Have you evaluated the learned word embeddings on other NLP tasks, such as parsing, coreference, and SRL, to show that they are not weaker than simpler word spaces?

**Additional Comments**

The authors' response to the reviewer's comments is appreciated. However, some of the weaknesses mentioned above still need to be addressed. The authors should consider providing more innovative solutions, exploring new research directions, and improving the clarity and consistency of their presentation.
