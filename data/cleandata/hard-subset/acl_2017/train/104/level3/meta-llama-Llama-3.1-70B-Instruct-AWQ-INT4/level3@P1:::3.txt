This paper proposes a novel Multi-Prototype Mention Embedding model that integrates text and knowledge into a unified semantic space, addressing the challenge of ambiguity in entity mentions. The model learns multiple sense embeddings for each mention by jointly modeling words from textual contexts and entities derived from a knowledge base. Additionally, the authors design an efficient language model-based approach to disambiguate each mention to a specific sense. The paper demonstrates the high quality of the word, entity, and multi-prototype mention embeddings through both qualitative and quantitative analysis.

The main contributions of this work are:

1. A novel Multi-Prototype Mention Embedding model that effectively integrates entity, mention, and sense modeling within the same neural language modeling technique.
2. A simple neural learning and training procedure that supports a large set of applications.
3. A solid empirical evaluation that demonstrates the high quality of the learned embeddings.

Strengths:

1. The paper proposes a good idea integrating entity, mention, and sense modeling within the same neural language modeling technique. This approach has the potential to improve the performance of various NLP tasks, such as entity linking and question answering.
2. The neural learning and training procedure is simple and efficient, making it suitable for a wide range of applications.
3. The formal presentation is clear in places, and the empirical evaluation is solid, even if the improvements are not large. The authors provide a thorough analysis of the results, including both quantitative and qualitative evaluations.

Weaknesses:

1. The amount of genuinely novel content is limited, and the improvements over existing methods are not striking. While the paper builds upon previous work, it does not significantly advance the state-of-the-art.
2. The prose and exposition are inconsistent, with some sections (e.g., Section 3) and parts of Figure 2 lacking clarity and detail. This makes it difficult to fully understand the proposed model and its components.
3. The terminology used in the paper is sometimes redundant or confusing, such as the use of "dictionary of mentions" vs "dictionary of entity-mention pairs" vs text anchors. This can lead to confusion and make it harder to follow the paper.
4. The paper is quite close to Yamada et al. (2016) and does not sufficiently outline the differences between the two approaches. This makes it difficult to understand the unique contributions of the proposed model.

Suggestions:

1. Evaluate the learned word embeddings on other NLP tasks, such as parsing, coreference, and semantic role labeling (SRL), to show that they are not weaker than simpler wordspaces. This would provide a more comprehensive understanding of the strengths and limitations of the proposed model.
2. Consider providing a more detailed comparison with Yamada et al. (2016) to highlight the differences and unique contributions of the proposed model.
