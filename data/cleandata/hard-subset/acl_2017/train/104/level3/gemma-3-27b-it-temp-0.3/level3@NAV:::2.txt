This paper proposes a Multi-Prototype Mention Embedding (MPME) model for entity disambiguation (EDL), framing it as a step towards “bridging text and knowledge.” The core idea is to learn multiple sense embeddings for each mention by jointly modeling textual contexts and entities from a knowledge base, utilizing a two-step pipeline: mention → mention-sense → entity. The authors claim state-of-the-art performance on EDL benchmarks.

I see the primary contribution as the exploration of an intermediate “mention-sense” layer within a joint embedding framework for EDL. While multi-prototype embeddings are not entirely novel (Huang et al., 2012; Reisinger & Mooney, 2010; Tian et al., 2014), applying this specifically to the mention-disambiguation pipeline and demonstrating performance gains is a valuable contribution. A secondary contribution lies in the smoothing parameter introduced in Section 6.4.2, which appears to be a practical improvement to the embedding process.

**Strengths:**

1. **Methodological Soundness:** The paper follows standard embedding practices and presents a clear methodology. The use of joint embeddings is well-motivated and the implementation appears technically competent.
2. **Empirical Results:** The authors demonstrate improved performance over a direct mention-to-entity mapping approach (ALIGN/Yamada et al., 2017). This suggests the intermediate mention-sense layer is providing a beneficial representation.
3. **Smoothing Parameter:** The smoothing technique described in Section 6.4.2 is a clever addition that addresses potential issues with sparse data and improves embedding quality. 

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Overstated Claims:** The title and introduction present the work as a broader contribution to “bridging text and knowledge,” which is a significant overreach given the narrow focus on EDL.
2. **Lack of Justification for Mention Senses:** The paper fails to adequately explain *why* the mention-sense layer improves performance. What specific information does it capture that isn't already present in the entity representations? Is it capturing contextual nuances? The authors need to articulate the value proposition of this intermediate layer.
3. **Missing Implementation Details:** Crucially, the paper lacks details on how mention senses are *created* and *differentiated*. How many senses are generated per mention? Do these senses map bijectively to KB entities? If senses are seeded from entity text, what novel signal exists at the mention-sense level that isn’t already in the entity pages?
4. **Incomplete Baselines:** The authors claim novelty without acknowledging prior work in EDL evaluations, specifically the TAC KBP tasks (e.g., KBP 2016). This omission undermines the claim of state-of-the-art performance.

**Questions to Authors:**

1. Please provide a detailed explanation of the process used to generate and differentiate mention senses. How many senses are typically created per mention?
2. Can you articulate specifically what information the mention-sense layer captures that is not already present in the entity representations?
3. Why wasn’t the TAC KBP EDL evaluation included as a baseline?
4. If the mention senses are derived from entity text, could this information be directly incorporated into the entity representations instead of introducing a separate layer?
