This paper proposes a Multi-Prototype Mention Embedding model to address the ambiguity of entity mentions when integrating text and knowledge. The core idea is to learn multiple sense embeddings for each mention by jointly modeling textual contexts and entities from a knowledge base, followed by a language model-based disambiguation step. The authors demonstrate state-of-the-art performance on entity linking, specifically outperforming the ALIGN model.

The primary contribution of this work, as I see it, is the demonstration of improved performance in entity linking through the combination of multi-prototype mention embeddings and a language model-based disambiguation approach. While multi-prototype embeddings have been explored previously (Huang et al., 2012; Reisinger and Mooney, 2010; Tian et al., 2014), the specific application to joint text-knowledge representation and entity linking, combined with the disambiguation component, provides a practical advancement. A second notable contribution is the analysis of the smoothing parameter in the entity linking task, providing insights into the role of entity popularity. Finally, the direct comparison to a closely related model, ALIGN, despite its limitations (discussed below), offers a valuable benchmark and demonstrates the effectiveness of the proposed approach.

**Strengths:**

1. **Strong Empirical Results:** The paper achieves state-of-the-art results on the entity linking benchmark, demonstrably improving upon the performance of the ALIGN model. This is a significant accomplishment, indicating the practical effectiveness of the proposed approach. The quantitative results presented in the paper clearly support this claim.
2. **Insightful Parameter Analysis:** The authors perform an analysis of the smoothing parameter in the entity linking task. This analysis sheds light on the influence of entity popularity on disambiguation performance, offering valuable insights for the broader field of entity linking and knowledge base completion.
3. **Direct Comparison to Related Work:**  The direct comparison to ALIGN, a closely related method that also aims to integrate text and knowledge, is valuable.  This allows for a more nuanced understanding of the contributions of the proposed model, showcasing its advantages in the context of existing approaches.
4. **Well-Defined Problem & Clear Motivation:** The problem of mention ambiguity in joint text-knowledge spaces is clearly articulated and the motivation for addressing it is well-established. The paper effectively frames the problem and presents a logical solution.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Incomplete/Confounded Comparison with ALIGN:** The comparison with ALIGN appears incomplete and potentially confounded by differing hyperparameters. Specifically, the different context window sizes (10 vs 5) and vector dimensions (500 vs 200) raise concerns about a fair comparison. It is difficult to isolate the contribution of the Multi-Prototype Mention Embedding model versus the impact of these parameter choices. A more rigorous comparison with carefully controlled hyperparameters is needed.
2. **Lack of Clarity Regarding Graph Structure:** The paper’s description of N(e_j) and the adjacency treatment within the Wikipedia graph is unclear.  Specifying whether the graph is treated as directed or undirected is crucial, as this directly impacts the entity contexts used for vectorization. This ambiguity affects the reproducibility and interpretability of the results.
3. **Ambiguous "Mention" Terminology:** The definition of “mention” is ambiguous. Is the embedding learned for the string type (e.g., "Washington") or for specific document mentions? The description suggests the embedding is for mention *senses* only, but this needs to be explicitly clarified. This impacts our understanding of the model's scope and application.
4. **Limited Comparison to Unsupervised Baselines:**  While the comparison to ALIGN is valuable, assessing the impact of the sense disambiguation component is difficult without comparison to other unsupervised entity linking methods. It’s unclear how much of the performance gain is due to the disambiguation step versus the multi-prototype embeddings themselves.  Including comparisons to relevant, strong unsupervised baselines would strengthen the evaluation.



**Questions to Authors:**

1. Could you clarify the treatment of the Wikipedia graph (directed vs. undirected) when constructing the entity contexts?
2. Please clarify whether the learned mention embeddings represent string types, specific document mentions, or mention senses.
3.  Would you consider adding a comparison to other unsupervised entity linking methods to better isolate the contribution of the sense disambiguation component?
4. Can you provide a more detailed explanation of the hyperparameter tuning process, especially regarding the context window size and vector dimension choices for both your model and ALIGN?



**Additional Comments:**

The references list contains multiple repetitions of the same entries, particularly those for Aho and Corasick. This should be cleaned up for clarity. The notation used throughout the paper is generally clear, but more explicit definitions of symbols would be helpful.
