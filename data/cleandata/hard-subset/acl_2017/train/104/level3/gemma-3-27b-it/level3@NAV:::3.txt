This paper proposes a Multi-Prototype Mention Embedding model to address ambiguity in entity mentions within a unified semantic space. The core idea is to learn multiple sense embeddings for each mention by jointly modeling textual contexts and entities from a knowledge base, leveraging a language model for disambiguation. The authors demonstrate the effectiveness of their approach through entity linking, achieving state-of-the-art results on a benchmark dataset.

The primary contribution, as I see it, is the straightforward integration of entity, mention, and sense modeling within a neural language modeling framework. This allows for joint learning and disambiguation in a relatively simple manner. A secondary contribution is the practical demonstration of this approach applied to the entity linking task, showcasing solid empirical results. Finally, the method's simplicity is a benefit, potentially enabling application to a wide range of NLP tasks.

However, the novelty of the work is limited. The method builds heavily on existing techniques for embedding learning and knowledge integration, and the improvements reported are not substantial. The prose is inconsistent; Section 3, detailing the model, and parts of Figure 2 are particularly unclear and lack sufficient detail to fully understand the implementation. Furthermore, the authors employ confusing and redundant terminology – the distinctions between “dictionary of mentions,” “dictionary of entity-mention pairs,” and “text anchors” are not well-defined. 

Critically, the paper does not adequately differentiate itself from Yamada et al. (2016), which also tackles multi-prototype embeddings. The authors need to explicitly highlight the unique aspects of their approach compared to this closely related work. The authors’ response acknowledges the connection to Yamada et al. but does not sufficiently address the comparative limitations.

To further validate the quality of the learned embeddings, I suggest evaluating them on downstream tasks such as parsing, coreference resolution, and semantic role labeling. This would demonstrate whether the embeddings offer advantages beyond the entity linking task and prove they aren't weaker than those produced by simpler wordspace models.



**Questions to Authors:**

1.  Can you provide a more detailed explanation of the differences between your approach and the work of Yamada et al. (2016), particularly regarding the learning objective and disambiguation process?
2.  Could you clarify the distinctions between the terms “dictionary of mentions”, “dictionary of entity-mention pairs”, and “text anchors”?
3.  What specific architectural choices or hyperparameters were most critical for achieving the reported performance, and why?
