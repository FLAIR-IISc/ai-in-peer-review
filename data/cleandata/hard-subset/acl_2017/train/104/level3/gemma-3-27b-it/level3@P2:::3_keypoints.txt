- Primary contribution: practical Multi-Prototype Mention Embedding model that jointly learns multiple sense embeddings per mention by integrating textual context and knowledge-base entities.  
- Secondary contributions: language-model-based disambiguation approach, state-of-the-art entity linking results, and clear empirical evaluation.

- Strength 1: Well-motivated unified framework that jointly models words, entities, and multiple mention senses to address mention ambiguity.  
- Strength 2: Simple, generalizable neural architecture and training procedure that can be adapted beyond entity linking.  
- Strength 3: Thorough quantitative and qualitative empirical evaluation demonstrating strong performance.  
- Strength 4: Generally clear presentation in parts with logical flow.

- Weakness 1: Limited novelty—builds on existing embedding, KB-integration, and language-model techniques with modest improvements.  
- Weakness 2: Clarity and detail issues in Section 3 and Figure 2, leaving component interactions and design rationale underspecified.  
- Weakness 3: Inconsistent and confusing terminology (e.g., “dictionary of mentions” vs. “entity-mention pairs” vs. “text anchors”).  
- Weakness 4: Insufficient explicit differentiation from closely related work, especially Yamada et al. (2016).

- Question 1: Please clarify the model architecture—how textual context embeddings and KB entity embeddings interact to produce mention sense embeddings.  
- Question 2: Provide a detailed comparison to Yamada et al. (2016), highlighting key differences and advantages.  
- Question 3: Report ablation studies showing the contributions of components (KB integration, multi-prototype embeddings, etc.).

- Additional comment: Evaluate learned embeddings on other NLP tasks (parsing, coreference, SRL) to demonstrate generalizability.  
- Additional comment: Discuss the computational complexity of the proposed model.

- Overall assessment: Solid, practical approach with promising results but limited novelty and clarity issues; recommend Accept with minor revisions to improve explanation, terminology, and related-work differentiation.
