## Review for ACL 2017

This paper proposes a Multi-Prototype Mention Embedding model to address the ambiguity of entity mentions by learning multiple sense embeddings for each mention, jointly modeling textual contexts and knowledge base entities. The authors also present a language model-based approach for mention disambiguation. The model is evaluated on the entity linking task, achieving state-of-the-art results.

**Contributions:**

The primary contribution of this work is a practical approach to learning multi-prototype mention embeddings by integrating textual and knowledge-based information within a neural language modeling framework. While the individual components aren't entirely novel, the specific combination and implementation provide a functional and reasonably effective solution to the mention disambiguation problem. A secondary contribution is the demonstration of the effectiveness of this approach on the challenging entity linking task, achieving state-of-the-art performance. Finally, the paper provides a clear empirical evaluation, showcasing the quality of the learned embeddings.

**Strengths:**

1. **Sound Idea and Integration:** The core idea of jointly modeling words, entities, and multiple mention senses within a unified framework is well-motivated and addresses a known challenge in semantic representation learning. The authors effectively integrate knowledge base information with textual context to resolve ambiguity, which is a crucial step for many downstream NLP tasks.

2. **Simplicity and Generalizability:** The proposed model utilizes a relatively simple neural network architecture and training procedure. This simplicity is a strength, as it makes the approach more accessible and potentially adaptable to a wider range of applications beyond entity linking. The authors suggest the model can be used in other applications, and this is a valid point.

3. **Solid Empirical Evaluation:** The paper presents a thorough empirical evaluation on the entity linking task. The results demonstrate that the proposed model achieves state-of-the-art performance, indicating its practical effectiveness. The inclusion of both qualitative and quantitative analysis strengthens the evaluation.

4. **Clear Presentation (in parts):** Certain sections of the paper are well-written and clearly explain the methodology. The overall flow of the paper is logical, even if some specific sections require improvement (see Weaknesses).



**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Novelty:** While the proposed approach is effective, the amount of genuinely novel content is limited. The paper builds upon existing techniques in word embeddings, knowledge base integration, and language modeling. The improvements achieved over existing methods, while statistically significant, are not substantial, raising questions about the overall impact of the work.

2. **Clarity and Detail Issues:** Section 3, describing the multi-prototype mention embedding model, and parts of Figure 2, are lacking in clarity and detail. The explanation of how the different components interact and the rationale behind specific design choices are not fully developed. This makes it difficult to fully understand and appreciate the nuances of the proposed model. 

3. **Confusing Terminology:** The paper suffers from inconsistent and sometimes confusing terminology. The use of terms like "dictionary of mentions" versus "dictionary of entity-mention pairs" versus "text anchors" creates ambiguity and hinders understanding. A more consistent and precise use of terminology is crucial.

4. **Insufficient Differentiation from Related Work:** The paper does not adequately differentiate itself from closely related work, particularly Yamada et al. (2016). The authors need to more explicitly highlight the differences between their approach and existing methods, and justify the added value of their contribution. The reviewer's notes indicate this point was raised in the author response, but the paper itself still lacks this critical discussion.

**Questions to Authors:**

1. **Clarification of Model Architecture:** Could you provide a more detailed explanation of the interaction between the different components of the multi-prototype mention embedding model? Specifically, how are the textual context embeddings and knowledge base entity embeddings combined to generate the mention sense embeddings?

2. **Comparison to Yamada et al. (2016):** Please provide a detailed comparison of your approach to the work of Yamada et al. (2016), clearly outlining the key differences and advantages of your model.

3. **Ablation Studies:** Did you perform any ablation studies to evaluate the contribution of each component of the model (e.g., the knowledge base integration, the multi-prototype embeddings)? If so, could you share the results?

**Additional Comments:**

The suggestion to evaluate the learned word embeddings on other NLP tasks (parsing, coreference, SRL) is a good one. This would provide further evidence of the quality and generalizability of the embeddings and demonstrate their utility beyond entity linking. It would be helpful to see a discussion of the computational complexity of the proposed model.



**Overall Assessment:**

The paper presents a solid and practical approach to mention disambiguation. While the novelty is limited and there are some clarity issues, the empirical results are promising and the proposed model shows potential for wider applicability. I recommend **Accept** with minor revisions to address the clarity and differentiation issues. The authors should focus on providing a more detailed explanation of the model architecture, clarifying the terminology, and more explicitly contrasting their work with related research.
