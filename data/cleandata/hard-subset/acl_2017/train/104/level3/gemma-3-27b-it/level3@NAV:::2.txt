## Review

This paper proposes a Multi-Prototype Mention Embedding (MPME) model for entity disambiguation (EDL) utilizing a two-step pipeline: mention → mention-sense → entity. The core idea is to learn multiple embeddings for each mention, representing different senses, by jointly modeling textual contexts and knowledge base entities. The authors claim state-of-the-art performance on entity linking tasks. 

I see the primary contribution as the exploration of an intermediate “mention-sense” layer within an embedding framework for EDL, and the associated language model for disambiguation. While multi-prototype embeddings are not novel (see Huang et al., 2012; Reisinger & Mooney, 2010; Neelakantan et al., 2014), applying them specifically to the mention-sense disambiguation step in EDL is a reasonable approach. A secondary contribution lies in the smoothing parameter introduced in Section 6.4.2, which seems potentially beneficial. 

However, the paper suffers from several weaknesses. The title and introduction significantly overstate the scope, framing the work as broadly “bridging text and knowledge” when it is narrowly focused on EDL. More critically, the authors fail to adequately justify the introduction of mention senses. What *additional* information do these senses provide that is not already present in the entity embeddings themselves? The authors need to clearly articulate this, and the role of mention senses, perhaps by detailing how these senses are constructed—how many senses per mention are created, and whether there's a bijective mapping to KB entities. If senses are seeded from entity text, what signal justifies a separate layer, rather than incorporating that information directly into entity representations? Section 5 should be revised to explicitly address this if my understanding is correct.

The empirical claim that MPME/SPME outperforms direct mention-entity mapping (ALIGN/Yamada et al., 2017) lacks explanation. *Why* does the intermediate step improve performance? This is a crucial point needing elaboration. Furthermore, the authors ignore relevant prior work. The TAC KBP EDL evaluations (e.g., KBP 2016 task) provide established baselines and should be cited and compared against. Dismissing such evaluations, as occurred during the rebuttal, is unsatisfactory; embedding quality must be demonstrated through downstream application performance, not merely conceptual appeal.

**Strengths:**

*   The smoothing parameter idea presented in Section 6.4.2 is well-motivated and could be a useful contribution.
*   The method is clearly described and follows standard embedding best practices.
*   The empirical results demonstrate competitive performance on the chosen dataset.

**Weaknesses:**

*   The title and introduction are misleadingly broad.
*   The justification for the mention-sense layer is weak and requires significant elaboration.
*   The paper lacks a comparison to relevant prior work, specifically the TAC KBP EDL evaluations.
*   The reason for the performance improvement over direct mapping is not explained.



**Questions to Authors:**

1.  Please detail the process of creating mention senses. How are they generated, how many are created per mention, and do they map bijectively to KB entities?
2.  Can you explain what specific information the mention senses provide *over* and above what is already represented in the entity embeddings?
3.  Why does the two-step MPME/SPME approach outperform direct mention-entity mapping? 
4.  Will you include a comparison to the TAC KBP EDL baselines in a revised version?
