## Review

This paper introduces a Multi-Prototype Mention Embedding model to address the ambiguity inherent in entity mentions within a unified text-knowledge semantic space. The core idea is to learn multiple sense embeddings for each mention by jointly modeling textual contexts and entities from a knowledge base, coupled with a language model for sense disambiguation. The authors demonstrate state-of-the-art results on an entity linking benchmark.

**Contributions:**

1. **Empirical Demonstration of Multi-Prototype Embeddings for Entity Linking:** The primary contribution lies in demonstrating the effectiveness of learning multiple embeddings per mention for improving entity linking performance. The paper provides strong quantitative results showing that this approach outperforms ALIGN on the task, suggesting a more nuanced representation of mention ambiguity.
2. **Analysis of Smoothing Parameter Impact:** The authors provide valuable insights into the impact of a smoothing parameter (lambda) used in their model, showcasing how it balances popularity bias in entity linking. This analysis sheds light on a key trade-off in entity linking systems.
3. **Direct Comparison & Baseline:** The paper presents a direct comparison against a closely related approach (ALIGN) using similar input data, offering a valuable baseline for evaluating their proposed method. 

**Strengths:**

1. **Strong Empirical Results:** The paper achieves state-of-the-art performance on the chosen entity linking benchmark dataset. This demonstrates the practical effectiveness of the proposed Multi-Prototype Mention Embedding model.  The gains over ALIGN are noteworthy and provide convincing evidence of improvement.
2. **Relevant Problem & Motivation:** Entity mention ambiguity is a fundamental challenge in natural language processing and knowledge base integration.  The authors clearly articulate the problem and present a compelling motivation for their approach.
3. **Insightful Analysis of Smoothing Parameter:** The detailed analysis of the smoothing parameter (lambda) is a significant strength. The authors not only present results but also interpret them in terms of the inherent trade-off between leveraging popularity and capturing less frequent, but potentially correct, entity assignments. This contributes to a deeper understanding of the factors influencing entity linking performance.
4. **Clear Presentation of the Model:** The paper is generally well-written and clearly explains the core components of the Multi-Prototype Mention Embedding model. The process of generating mention embeddings and performing sense disambiguation is logically presented.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Confounded Comparison with ALIGN:** The comparison with ALIGN, while seemingly strong, is compromised by differing hyperparameters. The authors use a context window of 10, while ALIGN uses 5. They also utilize vector dimensions of 500 versus 200 for ALIGN. These differences make it difficult to attribute the performance gains solely to the multi-prototype embeddings, as they could also be influenced by these architectural choices. A fairer comparison with consistent hyperparameter settings is needed.
2. **Ambiguous Graph Definition:** The description of N(e_j) – the neighborhood of entity e_j – is unclear. It's unclear whether the Wikipedia outlink graph is treated as directed or undirected when defining adjacency. This distinction is crucial as it significantly impacts the resulting entity contexts and, subsequently, the generated entity vectors. A precise definition is required.
3. **Terminology Ambiguity ("Mention"):** The term "mention" is used inconsistently. It is unclear whether the embeddings are generated for string types (e.g., "Washington") or for specific document mentions (e.g., the instance of "Washington" in a particular sentence). The text suggests "mention embedding" refers only to mention *senses*, further complicating the understanding of what is being embedded.
4. **Limited Context for Disambiguation Evaluation:** While the authors demonstrate improvements in entity linking, the evaluation of the sense disambiguation component itself feels somewhat isolated. It's difficult to assess the true impact of the disambiguation order without comparison to other unsupervised entity linking methods that employ different disambiguation strategies. The paper doesn’t provide evidence that the proposed disambiguation method is superior to existing approaches in isolation.



**Questions to Authors:**

1.  Could you clarify whether the Wikipedia outlink graph is treated as directed or undirected when constructing the neighborhood N(e_j)? If directed, how does this affect the entity embeddings?
2.  Regarding the term "mention," are the embeddings generated for string types or for specific document mentions? Please clarify the scope of the "mention embedding."
3.  Do you have results comparing the performance of your sense disambiguation method against other unsupervised entity linking approaches that utilize alternative disambiguation strategies (e.g., graph-based methods)?
4.  Were experiments conducted with ALIGN using the same hyperparameter settings (context window size of 10 and vector dimension of 500) to ensure a fairer comparison? If so, could you please present those results?

**Additional Comments:**

*   The references are somewhat messy and repetitive, with multiple entries for the same papers. Cleaning this up would improve readability.
*   A more detailed ablation study, examining the contribution of each component (multi-prototype embeddings vs. disambiguation method) would be valuable.
