This paper proposes a Multi-Prototype Mention Embedding (MPME) model for entity disambiguation (EDL) by learning multiple sense embeddings for each mention, jointly modeling textual contexts and entities from a knowledge base. The authors also introduce a language model-based approach for disambiguating mentions to specific senses. Experiments on entity linking demonstrate state-of-the-art performance.

The primary contribution of this work, as I see it, is a practical and well-implemented approach to incorporating multiple senses into mention embeddings for the task of entity disambiguation. While the idea of multi-sense embeddings is not entirely novel, this work provides a clear and effective instantiation within a joint text-knowledge embedding framework, achieving strong empirical results. A secondary contribution is the identification and effective mitigation of ambiguity in mention representations, leading to improved performance in the entity linking task. Finally, the language model component for sense disambiguation represents a useful addition to the overall system.

**Strengths:**

1. **Strong Empirical Performance:** The paper demonstrates state-of-the-art results on the entity linking benchmark. This is a significant achievement and suggests the proposed method is effective in practice. The quantitative results clearly show an improvement over existing baselines, including strong recent work like ALIGN and Yamada et al.
2. **Clear and Methodologically Sound Approach:** The MPME model is well-defined and logically structured. The authors leverage established embedding techniques and present a clear methodology for integrating textual and knowledge-based information. The use of a language model for sense disambiguation is a sensible and well-motivated addition.
3. **Practical Implementation Details:** The inclusion of details like the smoothing parameter (Section 6.4.2) demonstrates attention to practical implementation concerns. This level of detail is helpful for reproducibility and potential adaptation of the method.
4. **Well-Written and Accessible:** The paper is generally well-written and easy to understand. The technical concepts are explained clearly, and the experimental setup is described in sufficient detail.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Lack of Insight into Intermediate Representation:** The core novelty – the mention-sense embeddings – lacks a clear justification for *why* they improve performance. The paper demonstrates *that* they do, but offers little insight into *how* the intermediate layer provides additional information compared to directly mapping mentions to entities. What specific information does a “mention sense” capture that isn’t already available in the corresponding entity’s knowledge base representation? Without addressing this, the contribution feels somewhat empirical rather than theoretically grounded.
2. **Missing Related Work & Baselines:** The paper overlooks relevant prior work in the area of entity disambiguation, specifically the TAC KBP (Knowledge Base Population) evaluations. These evaluations provide established datasets and baselines for EDL and should have been included for a more thorough comparison. Dismissing the relevance of these baselines in the author response is unconvincing – performance on established benchmarks is critical for evaluating the effectiveness of embedding models.
3. **Unclear Specification of Mention Sense Creation:**  The paper lacks a detailed explanation of how the mention senses are created and differentiated. Key questions remain unanswered: How many senses are generated per mention? Is there a clear mapping between mention senses and entities in the knowledge base? If senses are seeded from entity text, what novel signal do they provide beyond the entity representation itself? This ambiguity hinders understanding of the model’s inner workings and potential limitations.
4. **Overly Broad Framing:** The introduction frames the work as broadly “bridging text and knowledge,” while the technical contribution is narrowly focused on entity disambiguation. This overclaiming of scope detracts from the paper’s impact.



**Questions to Authors:**

1.  Could you elaborate on the underlying motivation for introducing mention-sense embeddings? What specific information do they capture that is not already present in the entity representations, and how does this contribute to improved disambiguation performance?
2.  Please clarify the process of creating mention senses. How many senses are generated for each mention, and is there a one-to-one correspondence between mention senses and entities in the knowledge base?
3.  Why were the TAC KBP EDL evaluations not included as baselines in the experiments?
4.  The paper assumes certain configurations (entity profiles, word distributions, mention sense representations). Could you explicitly state these assumptions and confirm if my understanding is correct?



**Additional Comments:**

The smoothing parameter described in Section 6.4.2 is a welcome detail and demonstrates a practical approach to model optimization. If the authors could address the concerns regarding the role and justification of mention senses, and include a comparison to relevant KBP baselines, the paper would be significantly strengthened. Ultimately, the value of these embeddings must be demonstrated through downstream task performance – not merely conceptual elegance.
