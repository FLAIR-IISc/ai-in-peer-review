This paper introduces a Multi-Prototype Mention Embedding model to address ambiguity in entity linking by learning multiple sense embeddings for each mention, integrating textual contexts and knowledge base entities. The core idea is to move beyond single embeddings per mention, allowing for nuanced representation of polysemous terms. An efficient language model is also proposed for mention sense disambiguation. The authors demonstrate state-of-the-art performance on an entity linking benchmark, and provide analysis of the smoothing parameter used in their approach.

The primary contribution of this work, in my view, lies in the explicit modeling of multiple senses for mentions within a joint text-knowledge embedding space. While multi-prototype embeddings aren't entirely novel (as evidenced by the references), the application to entity linking with a clear focus on leveraging both textual *and* knowledge-based context is a valuable step. A secondary contribution is the analysis of the smoothing parameter, offering insight into the trade-off between popularity bias and accurate entity linking. Finally, the direct comparison against a closely related approach, despite limitations (see weaknesses), provides a concrete evaluation of the proposed method.

**Strengths:**

1.  **Strong Empirical Results:** The paper reports state-of-the-art results on an entity linking task, indicating the effectiveness of the proposed method.
2.  **Clear Motivation:** The problem of mention ambiguity is well-defined, and the proposed solution directly addresses this challenge.
3.  **Insightful Analysis:** The smoothing parameter analysis offers valuable understanding of a key hyperparameter and its impact on performance.
4. **Direct Comparison:** The inclusion of a direct comparison against a related approach allows for a focused evaluation of the method's improvements.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Incomplete Comparison to ALIGN:** The comparison to ALIGN is confounded by differing hyperparameters (context window size and vector dimensions), making it difficult to draw definitive conclusions about superiority. A fairer comparison with optimized hyperparameters for both models is needed.
2. **Ambiguous Definitions:** The definition of N(e_j) is unclear, and it's vital to clarify whether the Wikipedia adjacency graph is treated as directed or undirected, as this significantly impacts entity context.
3. **Mention Embedding Scope:** The term "mention embedding" is ambiguous. Is it referring to string-type embeddings, document-specific mention embeddings, or exclusively the sense embeddings? This requires clarification.
4. **Limited Baselines:** The lack of comparison against a wider range of unsupervised entity linking methods limits assessment of the contribution of the sense disambiguation component. How does this compare to, for instance, simpler unsupervised approaches like those relying on graph-based methods?



**Questions to Authors:**

1.  Could you please clarify the definition of N(e_j) and whether the Wikipedia adjacency graph is treated as directed or undirected?
2.  Please elaborate on the scope of the "mention embedding"—does it refer to string types, document mentions, or mention senses?
3.  Would you consider including a comparison against a broader set of unsupervised entity linking methods to better contextualize the impact of your sense disambiguation approach?
4.  Could you present results with ALIGN using the same hyperparameters as your model (context window 10, vector dims 500) to provide a more direct comparison?
