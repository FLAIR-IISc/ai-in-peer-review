## Review for ACL 2017

This paper proposes a Multi-Prototype Mention Embedding (MPME) model for entity disambiguation (EDL) that learns multiple sense embeddings for each mention by jointly modeling textual contexts and entities from a knowledge base. A language model is then used to disambiguate each mention to a specific sense. The authors demonstrate state-of-the-art performance on entity linking using this approach.

**Contributions:**

The primary contribution of this work is the exploration of an intermediate “mention sense” layer in a joint embedding space for text and knowledge. By explicitly modeling multiple senses for each mention, the authors attempt to capture ambiguity inherent in natural language and improve the accuracy of entity linking. A secondary contribution lies in the practical demonstration of this approach, achieving strong results on a standard entity linking benchmark. Finally, the smoothing technique described in Section 6.4.2, while a relatively minor component, presents a potentially valuable improvement to language model training.

**Strengths:**

1. **Strong Empirical Results:** The paper reports state-of-the-art performance on the entity linking task. This demonstrates the practical effectiveness of the proposed MPME model and suggests a tangible benefit to incorporating mention-sense embeddings. The quantitative results presented in the tables clearly indicate an improvement over strong baselines.

2. **Methodological Soundness:** The methodology employed is well-defined and aligns with current best practices in embedding-based approaches to NLP. The use of joint learning and the integration of textual and knowledge-based information are sensible and well-motivated choices. The model architecture is clearly explained, and the implementation details are sufficient for reproducibility.

3. **Clear Presentation:** The paper is generally well-written and easy to follow. The authors clearly articulate the problem they are addressing and the proposed solution. The structure of the paper is logical, and the experiments are described in a straightforward manner.

4. **Useful Smoothing Technique:** The introduction of the smoothing parameter in the language model training process is a clever idea. It potentially addresses the issue of data sparsity and improves the generalization ability of the model, which is a valuable contribution.



**Weaknesses:**

1. **Unjustified Intermediate Layer:** The core claim of the paper – that an intermediate “mention sense” layer improves performance – lacks a sufficiently compelling explanation. While the authors demonstrate *that* it works, they do not adequately explain *why*. What additional information does the mention-sense layer provide that is not already captured in either the entity embeddings or the contextual word embeddings? Is it capturing nuances of usage beyond what is present in the entity’s Wikipedia page? Without a clearer articulation of this benefit, the addition of this layer feels somewhat ad-hoc.

2. **Missing Details Regarding Mention Sense Creation:** The paper lacks crucial details regarding the creation and representation of mention senses. How many senses are generated per mention? Are these senses predefined, or are they learned from the data? Do these senses map bijectively to entities in the knowledge base? A clearer explanation of this process is essential for understanding the model’s functionality. My understanding is that the mention senses are formed by context-disambiguated distributional profiles. Is this correct?

3. **Incomplete Baseline Comparison:** The authors fail to acknowledge and compare against relevant prior work in the entity disambiguation space, specifically the TAC KBP EDL evaluations. These evaluations represent a significant body of work in this area and provide a crucial context for assessing the novelty and significance of the proposed approach. Dismissing their relevance (as was done in the author response) is unsatisfactory; validation should come from application performance on standard tasks, not merely conceptual elegance.

4. **Overstated Scope:** The title and introduction suggest a broader contribution to “bridging text and knowledge” than is actually delivered. The paper focuses almost exclusively on entity disambiguation and does not explore the broader implications of the proposed approach for other NLP tasks. This discrepancy between the claimed scope and the actual contributions weakens the overall impact of the paper.



**Questions to Authors:**

1.  Could you provide a more detailed explanation of the information captured by the mention-sense layer that is not already present in the entity embeddings or contextual word embeddings?
2.  Please clarify the process by which mention senses are created and differentiated. How many senses are generated per mention, and how are these senses represented?
3.  How does the number of senses affect the performance of the model? Have you experimented with different numbers of senses?
4.  Given that the mention senses seem to rely on distributional profiles, what signal exists at the mention-sense level that is not already present in the corresponding entity pages, and how can this be better utilized or merged into the entity representations?
5.  Could you please provide a comparative analysis against existing entity disambiguation systems evaluated on the TAC KBP datasets?



**Additional Comments:**

Please update Section 5 to explicitly state the role and novelty of mention senses, assuming my interpretation of their creation is correct. Consider including a visualization of the embedding space to illustrate the relationship between mentions, senses, and entities. The references are somewhat disorganized and contain many duplicates. Please clean them up.
