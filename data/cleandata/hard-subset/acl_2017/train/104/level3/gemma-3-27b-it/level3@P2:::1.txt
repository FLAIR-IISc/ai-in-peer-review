## Review for ACL 2017

This paper proposes a Multi-Prototype Mention Embedding model to address the ambiguity of entity mentions when integrating text and knowledge. The core idea is to learn multiple sense embeddings for each mention by jointly modeling textual contexts and entities from a knowledge base, coupled with a language model-based disambiguation approach. The model is evaluated on the entity linking task, achieving state-of-the-art results.

**Contributions:**

The primary contribution of this work is the demonstration that learning multiple sense embeddings for mentions, informed by both textual context and a knowledge base, improves performance on entity linking. While the idea of multi-prototype embeddings is not entirely novel (see, e.g., Reisinger & Mooney, 2010; Huang et al., 2012; Tian et al., 2014), the specific combination of jointly modeling textual contexts *and* knowledge base entities within this framework, and its effective application to entity linking, is a valuable contribution. A secondary contribution lies in the analysis of the smoothing parameter used in the disambiguation process, offering insights into the trade-off between contextual information and entity popularity. Finally, the paper provides a direct comparison to a recent and closely related approach (ALIGN), which, despite being somewhat confounded (see Weaknesses), offers a useful benchmark.

**Strengths:**

1. **Strong Empirical Results:** The paper demonstrates state-of-the-art performance on the entity linking benchmark. Achieving superior results to existing methods provides strong evidence for the effectiveness of the proposed approach. The reported improvements, while perhaps not overwhelmingly large, are significant and suggest a genuine advance in the field.
2. **Relevant Problem and Motivation:** Entity mention ambiguity is a well-recognized challenge in integrating textual and knowledge-based information. The proposed approach directly addresses this problem, providing a theoretically sound and practically effective solution.
3. **Clear Presentation of the Model:** The paper provides a relatively clear and concise description of the Multi-Prototype Mention Embedding model and the disambiguation process. The mathematical formulations are presented in a readable manner, aiding in understanding the technical details.
4. **Insightful Analysis of Smoothing Parameter:** The analysis of the smoothing parameter reveals a non-trivial relationship between context and popularity in entity linking. This provides a valuable understanding of the model's behavior and potential avenues for further improvement. 
5. **Direct Comparison to Related Work:** The comparison to ALIGN offers a useful point of reference and highlights the benefits of the proposed approach. The fact that the authors explicitly acknowledge the limitations of the comparison (see Weaknesses) further strengthens this aspect of the work.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Confounded Comparison with ALIGN:** The comparison to ALIGN is not entirely fair due to differences in hyperparameters – specifically the context window size (10 vs. 5) and vector dimensions (500 vs. 200). These differences could significantly impact performance, making it difficult to definitively attribute the observed improvements solely to the proposed Multi-Prototype Mention Embedding model. A more controlled comparison with consistent hyperparameters would strengthen the claims.
2. **Ambiguity in Terminology and Scope:** The paper's terminology regarding “mention” is unclear. Is the embedding learned for string types or specific document mentions? The description suggests it’s for mention senses only, but this should be explicitly stated. This ambiguity makes it challenging to fully grasp the scope of the model’s contributions.
3. **Unclear Knowledge Graph Context:** The definition of N(e_j) is not fully clear. More importantly, the paper doesn't specify whether the adjacency relationships used to define entity context within the knowledge graph are treated as directed or undirected. This choice has a significant impact on the resulting entity contexts and, consequently, the learned embeddings. This detail needs to be clarified.
4. **Limited Comparison to Unsupervised Methods:** While the comparison to ALIGN (a supervised approach) is valuable, the paper lacks a comprehensive comparison to other unsupervised entity linking methods. This makes it difficult to assess the independent impact of the sense disambiguation component of the model. It’s unclear how much of the performance gain is attributable to the multi-prototype embeddings versus the disambiguation step.
5. **Repetitive References:** The reference list contains a significant number of duplicated entries.



**Questions to Authors:**

1. Could you clarify whether the learned mention embeddings are for string types, specific document mentions, or mention senses?
2. Please specify whether the adjacency relationships in the knowledge graph are treated as directed or undirected when defining entity context.
3.  Could you provide a more detailed explanation of the rationale behind the choice of hyperparameters for ALIGN? Would the results change significantly if ALIGN were run with the same hyperparameters as your model?
4.  How does the performance of the model compare to other unsupervised entity linking methods, beyond ALIGN? Specifically, could you include results from approaches like Pershina et al. (2015) or Cucerzan (2007)?

**Additional Comments:**

- The repeated references should be cleaned up in the final version of the paper.
- Providing ablation studies to isolate the contribution of each component (multi-prototype embeddings vs. disambiguation) would be beneficial.
