- Strengths:
The paper presents a simple yet effective neural learning approach for entity disambiguation and linking, with a large set of potential applications. The integration of entity, mention, and sense modeling within the same neural language modeling technique is a notable contribution.

- Weaknesses: 
The paper's novelty is somewhat limited, and clarity is occasionally compromised in certain sections.

The paper proposes a neural learning method that combines entity, mention, and sense modeling within a unified neural language modeling framework. This approach enables a straightforward training procedure, making it suitable for a wide range of applications. Although the paper's formal presentation is clear, the discussion does not consistently match the technical sophistication of the ideas.

The empirical evaluation is satisfactory, albeit without striking performance improvements. While the work appears to be an extension of (Yamada et al., CoNLL 2016), it introduces novel ideas and is of relevant interest.

The weaker aspects of the paper are:

- The writing is not always clear. Section 3, in particular, could benefit from clarification. Certain details in Figure 2 are not adequately explained, and some terminology is redundant. For instance, the distinction between the "dictionary of mentions," "dictionary of entity-mention pairs," and "text anchors" and "types for annotated text anchors" is unclear.
- The paper bears a strong resemblance to (Yamada et al., 2016), and the authors should explicitly outline the differences between the two.

A general observation about the current version is that the paper primarily evaluates the Multiple Embedding model on entity linking/disambiguation tasks. However, word embeddings are also used in tasks unrelated to entities in the knowledge base, such as parsing, coreference, or semantic role labeling. The authors should demonstrate that the word embeddings generated by the proposed MPME method are not inferior to those produced by simpler word space models in these other semantic tasks, particularly those involving direct entity mentions.
