**Review**

**Strengths:** The paper presents good ideas, employs simple neural learning, and demonstrates interesting performance, albeit not striking, with a large set of potential applications.

**Weaknesses:** The amount of novel content and clarity in some sections are concerns.

The paper introduces a neural learning method for entity disambiguation and linking, integrating entity, mention, and sense modeling within the same neural language modeling technique. This approach is commendable, and the simple training procedure allows for a wide range of applications. While the paper is formally clear, the discussion does not consistently match the level of technical sophistication.

The empirical evaluation is satisfactory, although the reported performance improvements are not remarkable. Despite seeming to extend the work of Yamada et al. (CoNLL 2016), the paper contributes novel ideas and is of relevant interest.

However, there are several weaker points to address:

* The writing is not always clear, particularly in Section 3. Some details in Figure 2 are not explained, and the terminology is occasionally redundant. For instance, the distinction between the "dictionary of mentions" and the "dictionary of entity-mention pairs" is unclear, as they appear to be similar to "text anchors" and "types for annotated text anchors."
* The paper bears a strong resemblance to Yamada et al. (2016), and the authors should explicitly outline the differences.

A general observation about the current version is that the Multiple Embedding model is primarily tested on entity linking/disambiguation tasks. However, word embeddings are also used in tasks unrelated to entities in the knowledge base, such as parsing, coreference, or semantic role labeling. The authors should demonstrate that the word embeddings provided by the proposed MPME method are not inferior to simpler wordspaces in these other semantic tasks, particularly those involving entity mentions directly.
