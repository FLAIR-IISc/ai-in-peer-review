This paper addresses the problem of disambiguating and linking textual entity mentions to a given background knowledge base, specifically English Wikipedia. Although the title and introduction may be slightly misleading, as they imply that the proposed method solves the broader problem of bridging text and knowledge, the Entity Disambiguation and Linking (EDL) task is a crucial part of this overall goal.

The proposed method involves an intermediate layer of representation, namely mention senses, which enables a two-step process: (1) mapping mentions to mention senses, and (2) mapping mention senses to entities. The approach learns various embedding representations for words, mention senses, and entities, which are then jointly trained to maximize a single overall objective function that equally optimizes all three types of embeddings.

From a technical standpoint, the approach is clear and aligns with current deep processing trends and best practices regarding embeddings. While alternative methods can be suggested, it is unclear whether they would significantly improve the results. My comments focus on the fundamental approach, which raises a crucial question: why is a two-step process involving mention senses more effective than a simple direct mapping from word mentions to entities, as proposed by Yamada et al. (ALIGN algorithm)? Although Table 2 shows that the two-step MPME (and its simplification SPME) outperform the ALIGN algorithm, the exact difference and additional information provided by the mention senses compared to the entities are not clearly explained.

To clarify this, it appears that the profiles for entities consist of neighboring entities in a relatedness graph, built by examining word-level relatedness in entity definitions (Wikipedia pages). These profiles are extended skip-gram-based embeddings. In contrast, word profiles follow the standard distributional semantics approach without sense disambiguation. Mention sense profiles, however, incorporate sense disambiguation using a sense-based profile ('language model') from local context words and neighboring mentions. This sense disambiguation process is briefly mentioned before Section 4 but lacks details. This is a critical point in the approach, as it is unclear how the senses are created and differentiated, and who defines the number of senses a mention string can have.

If the sense collection's definitional profiles are built using entity text as 'seed words,' this would imply a bijective mapping between mention senses and entities, with exactly one entity for each mention sense. In this case, the information used at the mention sense level that is not used at the entity level would be the words in the texts that reliably associate with the mention sense but do not occur in the equivalent entity webpage in Wikipedia. It is essential to understand how many such words are there, on average, for a mention sense, and how powerful or necessary it is to maintain this extra differentiation information in a separate space (the mention sense space) rather than loading these additional words into the Entity space (by adding them to the Wikipedia entity pages).

If the above understanding is correct, it would be beneficial to update Section 5 to clearly explain this point, as it appears to be the primary new information in the paper. Furthermore, the claim in Section 6 that this is the first work to address mention ambiguity in the integration of text and knowledge representations is incorrect. The TAC KBP evaluations for the past two years have included EDL tasks, involving eight or nine systems, all performing this task against Freebase, which is considerably larger and more noisy than Wikipedia.

On a positive note, the idea of the smoothing parameter in Section 6.4.2 is well-received. However, after reading the authors' responses, I remain unsatisfied with their explanation regarding the KBP evaluation. They argue that the evaluation is not relevant, as they focus on the goodness of the embeddings instead. Nevertheless, the only way to evaluate such 'goodness' is through an application, as the conceptual elegance of an embedding is secondary to its performance.
