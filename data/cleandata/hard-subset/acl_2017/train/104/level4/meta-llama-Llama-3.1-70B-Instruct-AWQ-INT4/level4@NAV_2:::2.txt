This paper addresses the problem of disambiguating/linking textual entity mentions into a given background knowledge base, specifically English Wikipedia. Although its title and introduction may be somewhat misleading, as bridging text and knowledge encompasses more than the entity disambiguation and linking (EDL) task, EDL is a crucial part of the overall task. The proposed method involves a two-step process: (1) mapping mentions to mention senses, and (2) mapping mention senses to entities. This is achieved through the learning of various embedding representations for words, mention senses, and entities, which are then jointly trained to maximize a single overall objective function.

Technically, the approach is fairly clear and aligns with current deep processing trends and best practices regarding embeddings. However, it is unclear why a two-step process, involving mention senses, is superior to a simple direct one-step mapping from word mentions to their entities, as seen in the ALIGN algorithm proposed by Yamada et al. Table 2 suggests that the two-step Multi-Prototype Mention Embedding (MPME) approach, and even its simplification Single-Prototype Mention Embedding (SPME), outperform the one-step approach, but the exact reason for this improvement is not provided.

The main contributions of this work, as I see them, are:

1. The proposal of a novel Multi-Prototype Mention Embedding model that learns multiple sense embeddings for each mention by jointly modeling words from textual contexts and entities derived from a knowledge base.
2. The design of an efficient language model-based approach to disambiguate each mention to a specific sense.
3. The achievement of state-of-the-art results on the entity linking task using the proposed disambiguation method and multi-prototype mention embeddings.

The strengths of this submission are:

1. The proposed approach is technically sound and well-motivated, addressing a crucial problem in the integration of text and knowledge representations.
2. The experimental results demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed method, achieving state-of-the-art performance on the entity linking task.
3. The idea of using a smoothing parameter to balance the influence of different types of embeddings is innovative and worth exploring further.

The weaknesses of this submission are:

1. The paper lacks a clear explanation of why a two-step process, involving mention senses, is better than a simple direct one-step mapping from word mentions to their entities.
2. The authors' response to the KBP evaluation not being relevant is unsatisfactory, as the evaluation of the 'goodness' of embeddings is inherently tied to their application.
3. The paper could benefit from a more detailed explanation of how the senses are created and differentiated, and how the sense collection's definitional profiles are built.

Questions to Authors:

1. Can you provide a more detailed explanation of how the senses are created and differentiated, and how the sense collection's definitional profiles are built?
2. How do you respond to the criticism that the KBP evaluation is not relevant to your work, and how do you plan to evaluate the 'goodness' of your embeddings in a more meaningful way?
3. Can you provide more information on the smoothing parameter and its impact on the performance of the proposed method?
