**Summary of the Paper and Main Contributions**

The paper proposes a novel Multi-Prototype Mention Embedding model that integrates text and knowledge into a unified semantic space. The model learns multiple sense embeddings for each mention by jointly modeling words from textual contexts and entities derived from a knowledge base. Additionally, the authors design an efficient language model-based approach to disambiguate each mention to a specific sense. The contributions of this work, in order of strength, are:

1. The proposal of a novel Multi-Prototype Mention Embedding model that effectively captures the ambiguity of entity mentions in a unified semantic space.
2. The design of an efficient language model-based approach for disambiguating entity mentions.
3. The application of the proposed model to the task of entity linking, achieving state-of-the-art results on a benchmark dataset.

**Strengths**

1. The paper presents a thorough evaluation of the proposed model, including both qualitative and quantitative analyses, demonstrating the high quality of the word, entity, and multi-prototype mention embeddings.
2. The direct comparison with a closely related approach (ALIGN) provides a useful baseline for evaluating the effectiveness of the proposed model.
3. The analysis of the smoothing parameter offers valuable insights into the impact of popularity on entity linking, a persistent issue in this task.

**Weaknesses**

1. The comparison with ALIGN could be improved by providing more details on the experimental setup, such as the content window size, vector dimension, and adjacency definition in the graph. This would help to clarify the impact of these differences on the learned vectors and the entity similarity task.
2. The terminology used in the paper, particularly the distinction between "mention" as a string type and a particular mention in a document, is sometimes unclear. This ambiguity makes it difficult to follow the discussion of "mention embedding" and its relationship to sense embeddings.
3. The impact of sense disambiguation order on the performance of the proposed model is not fully explored, and a comparison with other unsupervised entity linking methods would be beneficial in evaluating the effectiveness of the proposed approach.

**Questions to Authors**

1. Could you provide more details on the experimental setup for the comparison with ALIGN, including the content window size, vector dimension, and adjacency definition in the graph?
2. How do you distinguish between "mention" as a string type and a particular mention in a document, and how does this affect the learning of sense embeddings?
3. Have you considered comparing the proposed model with other unsupervised entity linking methods to evaluate the impact of sense disambiguation order on performance?
