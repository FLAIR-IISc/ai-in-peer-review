This paper addresses the problem of disambiguating/linking textual entity mentions into a given background knowledge base, specifically English Wikipedia. Although the title and introduction may be somewhat misleading, as they imply a broader scope than the Entity Disambiguation and Linking (EDL) task, EDL is indeed a crucial part of bridging text and knowledge.

The proposed method employs an intermediate layer of representation, namely mention senses, to perform this bridging in two steps: (1) mention to mention sense, and (2) mention sense to entity. The approach learns various embedding representations for words, mention senses, and entities, which are then jointly trained to maximize a single overall objective function that equally optimizes all three types of embeddings.

Technically, the approach is clear and follows current deep processing trends and best practices regarding embeddings. While alternative methods could be suggested, it is unclear whether they would make a significant difference. My comments focus on the basic approach, which lacks a clear explanation for why a two-step process involving mention senses is superior to a simple direct one-step mapping from word mentions to their entities, as proposed by Yamada et al. in the ALIGN algorithm. Table 2 shows that the two-step MPME (and its simplification SPME) outperform the ALIGN algorithm, but the exact difference and additional information that mention senses provide compared to entities are unclear.

To clarify, the profiles for entities consist of neighboring entities in a relatedness graph, which is built by examining word-level relatedness in entity definitions (Wikipedia pages). These profiles are extended skip-gram-based embeddings. In contrast, word profiles are based on the standard distributional semantics approach without sense disambiguation. Mention sense profiles, however, employ the standard distributional semantics approach with sense disambiguation, using a sense-based profile ('language model') from local context words and neighboring mentions.

The sense disambiguation process is briefly mentioned before Section 4 but lacks details. This is a problem point in the approach, as it is unclear how senses are created and differentiated, who defines the number of senses a mention string can have, and whether the sense collection's definitional profiles are built starting with entity text as 'seed words.' If the latter is the case, what information is used at the mention sense level that is not used at the entity level? Is it simply the words in the texts that reliably associate with the mention sense but do not occur in the equivalent entity webpage in Wikipedia? How many such words are there, on average, for a mention sense, and how powerful or necessary is it to maintain this extra differentiation information in a separate space (the mention sense space) rather than loading these additional words into the entity space?

If the above understanding is correct, it would be beneficial to update Section 5 to clarify this point, as it appears to be the main contribution of the paper. Furthermore, the claim in Section 6 that this is the first work to address mention ambiguity in the integration of text and knowledge representations is incorrect. The TAC KBP evaluations for the past two years have hosted EDL tasks, involving eight or nine systems, all performing this task against Freebase, which is considerably larger and more noisy than Wikipedia.

On a positive note, the idea of the smoothing parameter in Section 6.4.2 is commendable. However, after reading the authors' responses, I remain unsatisfied with their explanation regarding the KBP evaluation not being relevant, as they focus on the goodness of the embeddings instead. In reality, the only way to evaluate such 'goodness' is through an application, as the conceptual elegance of an embedding is secondary to its performance.
