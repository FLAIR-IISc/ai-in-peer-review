This paper addresses the problem of disambiguating/linking textual entity mentions into a given background knowledge base, specifically English Wikipedia. Although the title and introduction are somewhat misleading, as they imply a broader scope than the actual task of entity disambiguation and linking (EDL), EDL is indeed a core part of the overall task of bridging text and knowledge.

The proposed method involves an intermediate layer of representation, namely mention senses, which follows a two-step process: (1) mention to mention sense, and (2) mention sense to entity. Various embedding representations are learned for the words, the mention senses, and the entities, which are then jointly trained to maximize a single overall objective function that maximizes all three types of embedding equally.

Technically, the approach is fairly clear and conforms to current deep processing fashion and known best practices regarding embeddings. However, it is not explained why a two-step process involving mention senses is better than a simple direct one-step mapping from word mentions to their entities, as proposed by Yamada et al. in the ALIGN algorithm. Table 2 shows that the two-step MPME (and even its simplification SPME) perform better, but the exact difference and additional information that the mention senses provide compared to the entities are not clear.

To clarify, the following points need to be addressed:

1. For entities: Their profiles consist of neighboring entities in a relatedness graph built by looking at word-level relatedness of entity definitions (pages in Wikipedia). The profiles are (extended skip-gram-based) embeddings.
2. For words: Their profiles are the standard distributional semantics approach, without sense disambiguation.
3. For mention senses: Their profiles are the standard distributional semantics approach, but with sense disambiguation. Sense disambiguation is performed using a sense-based profile ('language model') from local context words and neighboring mentions.

However, it is unclear how exactly the senses are created and differentiated, who defines how many senses a mention string can have, and whether this is done by looking at the knowledge base. If this is the case, it would result in a bijective mapping between mention senses and entities, with exactly one entity for each mention sense. Additionally, it is unclear what information is used at the mention sense level that is not used at the entity level and whether this extra differentiation information is necessary to keep in a separate space.

**Contributions:**

1. The proposed Multi-Prototype Mention Embedding model learns multiple sense embeddings for each mention by jointly modeling words from textual contexts and entities derived from a knowledge base.
2. The model uses an efficient language model-based approach to disambiguate each mention to a specific sense.
3. The paper demonstrates the high quality of the word, entity, and multi-prototype mention embeddings through qualitative and quantitative analysis.

**Strengths:**

1. The proposed model achieves state-of-the-art performance on the benchmark dataset for entity linking.
2. The use of a smoothing parameter in Section 6.4.2 is a positive aspect of the paper.
3. The paper provides a clear and well-structured presentation of the proposed method.

**Weaknesses:**

1. The paper does not provide a clear explanation of why a two-step process involving mention senses is better than a simple direct one-step mapping from word mentions to their entities.
2. The paper does not adequately address the creation and differentiation of senses and how they are used in the model.
3. The paper claims that this is the first work to deal with mention ambiguity in the integration of text and knowledge representations, which is not accurate.

**Questions to Authors:**

1. Can you provide a clear explanation of why a two-step process involving mention senses is better than a simple direct one-step mapping from word mentions to their entities?
2. How exactly are the senses created and differentiated, and who defines how many senses a mention string can have?
3. Can you provide more details on the sense-based profile ('language model') used for sense disambiguation and how it is trained?
4. How do you respond to the criticism that the TAC KBP evaluations for the past two years have hosted EDL tasks, involving eight or nine systems, all performing exactly this task, albeit against Freebase, which is considerably larger and more noisy than Wikipedia?
