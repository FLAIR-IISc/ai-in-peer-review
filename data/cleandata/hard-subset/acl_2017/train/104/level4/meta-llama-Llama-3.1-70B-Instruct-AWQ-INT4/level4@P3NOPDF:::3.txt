This paper proposes a neural learning method for entity disambiguation and linking, integrating entity, mention, and sense modeling within the same neural language modeling technique. The approach is built upon a simple training procedure, allowing for a wide range of applications. The paper is formally well-structured, but the discussion could be improved in certain sections to match the level of the technical ideas presented.

The paper's strengths lie in its good ideas, straightforward neural learning approach, and interesting performance, albeit not striking. Additionally, the method's potential for application in various areas is notable.

However, there are some weaknesses that need to be addressed. The amount of novel content in the paper could be improved, as it appears to be an extension of previous work (Yamada et al., CoNLL 2016). Furthermore, clarity is an issue in some sections, particularly Section 3, which requires more explanation and elaboration. Certain details in Figure 2, such as the terminology used, are not adequately explained. For instance, the distinction between the "dictionary of mentions" and the "dictionary of entity-mention pairs" is unclear, and their relationship to "text anchors" and "types for annotated text anchors" needs to be clarified.

Another concern is the paper's similarity to Yamada et al. (2016). While the authors do introduce some novel ideas, they should explicitly outline the differences between their work and the previous study.

A general observation about the current version of the paper is that it primarily evaluates the Multiple Embedding model against entity linking/disambiguation tasks. However, word embeddings are also used in other semantic tasks that do not directly depend on entities in the knowledge base, such as parsing, coreference, or semantic role labeling. To strengthen the paper, the authors should demonstrate that the word embeddings provided by the proposed MPME method are not weaker than those obtained from simpler word spaces in these tasks, particularly those involving entity mentions.

In response to the authors' rebuttal, it is essential to address these concerns to improve the overall quality and impact of the paper.
