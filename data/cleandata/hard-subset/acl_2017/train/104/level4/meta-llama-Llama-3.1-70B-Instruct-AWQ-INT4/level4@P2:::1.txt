**Summary and Main Contributions**

This paper proposes a novel Multi-Prototype Mention Embedding model that integrates text and knowledge into a unified semantic space. The model learns multiple sense embeddings for each mention by jointly modeling words from textual contexts and entities derived from a knowledge base. The authors also design an efficient language model-based approach to disambiguate each mention to a specific sense. The main contributions of this work are:

1. A novel Multi-Prototype Mention Embedding model that effectively captures the ambiguity of entity mentions.
2. An efficient language model-based approach for sense disambiguation.
3. State-of-the-art performance on the entity linking task using the proposed framework.

**Strengths**

1. The proposed framework outperforms the ALIGN method in the supervised entity linking task, suggesting that it improves representations of text and knowledge learned jointly.
2. The paper provides a direct comparison with a closely related approach using similar input data, which helps to evaluate the effectiveness of the proposed model.
3. The analysis of the smoothing parameter provides useful insights into the impact of popularity on entity linking, a persistent issue in this task.

**Weaknesses**

1. The comparison with ALIGN could be improved. Specifically, the paper should clarify the differences in the input data and model settings, such as the content window size, vector dimension, and adjacency definition in the graph.
2. The terminology used in the paper is sometimes ambiguous, making it difficult to follow whether "mention" refers to a string type or a particular mention in a document. The phrase "mention embedding" is used, but it appears that embeddings are only learned for mention senses.
3. The impact of sense disambiguation order is not clearly evaluated, and a comparison to other unsupervised entity linking methods would be helpful to assess the effectiveness of the proposed approach.

**Questions to Authors**

1. Can you clarify the differences in the input data and model settings between the proposed framework and the ALIGN method?
2. How do you define "mention" in the context of your paper, and can you provide more details on the distinction between mention types and mention senses?
3. Can you provide a more detailed analysis of the impact of sense disambiguation order on the performance of the proposed framework, and compare it to other unsupervised entity linking methods?
