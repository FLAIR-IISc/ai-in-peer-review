This paper addresses the problem of disambiguating/linking textual entity mentions into a given background knowledge base, specifically English Wikipedia. While the title and introduction are somewhat exaggerated, as bridging text and knowledge encompasses more than the Entity Disambiguation and Linking (EDL) task, EDL is indeed a crucial part of this overall task.

The proposed method involves an intermediate layer of representation, namely mention senses, which is used to perform the bridging in two steps: (1) mention to mention sense, and (2) mention sense to entity. Various embedding representations are learned for words, mention senses, and entities, which are then jointly trained to maximize a single overall objective function that treats all three types of embeddings equally.

Technically, the approach is fairly clear and aligns with current deep processing trends and best practices regarding embeddings. While alternative methods could be suggested, it is unclear whether they would make a significant difference. My comments focus on the basic approach, which is not entirely clear in its justification for using a two-step process involving mention senses instead of a simple direct one-step mapping from word mentions to their entities, as in the ALIGN algorithm proposed by Yamada et al. Table 2 shows that the two-step MPME (and even its simplification SPME) perform better, but the exact reason for this improvement is unclear. What additional information do mention senses provide compared to entities?

To understand this, I have attempted to break down the profiles of entities, words, and mention senses. For entities, their profiles consist of neighboring entities in a relatedness graph, which is presumably built by examining word-level relatedness in entity definitions (Wikipedia pages). These profiles are extended skip-gram-based embeddings. For words, their profiles follow the standard distributional semantics approach without sense disambiguation. For mention senses, their profiles also follow the standard distributional semantics approach but with sense disambiguation, which is performed using a sense-based profile ('language model') from local context words and neighboring mentions. However, this process is not thoroughly explained, and it is unclear how senses are created and differentiated. Who determines the number of senses a mention string can have? If this is done by looking at the knowledge base, then we get a bijective mapping between mention senses and entities, which raises further questions.

If the sense collection's definitional profiles are built starting with entity text as 'seed words,' what information is used at the mention sense level that is not used at the entity level? Is it simply the words in the texts that reliably associate with the mention sense but do not occur in the equivalent entity webpage in Wikipedia? How many such words are there, on average, for a mention sense? How powerful or necessary is it to keep this extra differentiation information in a separate space (the mention sense space) as opposed to just loading these additional words into the Entity space (by adding these words into the Wikipedia entity pages)? If my understanding is correct, I suggest updating Section 5 to clarify this point, as it seems to be the main contribution of the paper.

It is also worth noting that the paper's claim in Section 6 that "…this is the first work to deal with mention ambiguity in the integration of text and knowledge representations, so there is no exact baselines for comparison" is not accurate. The TAC KBP evaluations for the past two years have hosted EDL tasks, involving eight or nine systems, all performing exactly this task, albeit against Freebase, which is considerably larger and more noisy than Wikipedia. Please see http://nlp.cs.rpi.edu/kbp/2016/. I appreciate the idea of the smoothing parameter in Section 6.4.2, which I found to be a positive contribution.

After reading the authors' responses, I remain unsatisfied with their explanation regarding the KBP evaluation not being relevant, as they are more interested in the 'goodness' of the embeddings. However, the only way to evaluate such 'goodness' is through an application. Ultimately, the question is whether the embedding performs better, not how conceptually elegant it is.
