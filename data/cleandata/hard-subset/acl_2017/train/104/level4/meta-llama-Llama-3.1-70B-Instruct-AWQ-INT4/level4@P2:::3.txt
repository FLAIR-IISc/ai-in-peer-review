**Summary of the Paper**

The paper proposes a novel Multi-Prototype Mention Embedding (MPME) model that integrates text and knowledge into a unified semantic space. The model learns multiple sense embeddings for each mention by jointly modeling words from textual contexts and entities derived from a knowledge base. An efficient language model-based approach is also designed to disambiguate each mention to a specific sense. The authors evaluate the quality of the word, entity, and multi-prototype mention embeddings using both qualitative and quantitative analysis. They also apply the disambiguation method and the MPME model to the entity linking task on a benchmark dataset, achieving state-of-the-art performance.

**Main Contributions**

Based on my understanding, the main contributions of this work are:

1. **Novel MPME model**: The authors propose a new model that integrates entity, mention, and sense modeling within the same neural language modeling technique.
2. **Efficient language model-based disambiguation**: The authors design an efficient approach to disambiguate each mention to a specific sense.
3. **State-of-the-art performance on entity linking**: The authors achieve state-of-the-art performance on the entity linking task using the proposed MPME model.

**Strengths**

1. **Good ideas and simple neural learning**: The paper presents a novel idea and uses simple neural learning techniques, making it easy to understand and implement.
2. **Interesting performance**: Although the performance improvements are not striking, the results are still interesting and demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed model.
3. **Large set of applications**: The paper highlights the potential applications of the proposed model, making it relevant to a broad audience.

**Weaknesses**

1. **Amount of novel content**: The paper is quite close in nature to Yamada et al. (2016), and the authors should at least outline the differences.
2. **Clarity in some sections**: The paper is clear formally, but the discussion is not always at the same level of the technical ideas. Some sections, such as Section 3, are not clear, and some details of Figure 2 are not explained.
3. **Redundant terminology**: The terminology is somehow redundant, with terms like "dictionary of mentions" and "dictionary of entity-mention pairs" that are not clearly explained.

**Questions to Authors**

1. Can you provide more details on the differences between the proposed MPME model and Yamada et al. (2016)?
2. Can you clarify the terminology used in the paper, such as "dictionary of mentions" and "dictionary of entity-mention pairs"?
3. Can you provide more insights on the potential applications of the proposed model, beyond entity linking?

**Additional Comments**

The paper tests the Multiple Embedding model against entity linking/disambiguation tasks. However, word embeddings are not only used to model such tasks, but also some processes not directly depending on entities of the KB, e.g., parsing, coreference, or semantic role labeling. The authors should show that the word embeddings provided by the proposed MPME method are not weaker wrt to simpler wordspaces in such other semantic tasks, i.e., those involving directly entity mentions.
