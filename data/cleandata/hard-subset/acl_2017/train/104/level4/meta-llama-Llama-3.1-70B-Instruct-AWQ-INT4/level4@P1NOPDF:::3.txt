- Strengths:
The paper presents a novel neural learning method for entity disambiguation and linking, integrating entity, mention, and sense modeling within the same neural language modeling technique. The simple training procedure allows for a large set of applications. Although the performance improvements reported are not striking, the ideas are interesting and the empirical evaluation is good.

- Weaknesses: The amount of novel content is limited, and some sections lack clarity.

The paper formally presents a clear neural learning method, but the discussion does not always match the level of technical sophistication. The authors introduce a good idea by combining entity, mention, and sense modeling within the same neural language modeling technique. This integration allows for a simple training procedure, which in turn supports a wide range of applications.

The empirical evaluation is thorough, although the performance improvements reported are not remarkable. While the paper appears to be an extension of (Yamada et al., CoNLL 2016), it contributes novel ideas and is of relevant interest.

However, the paper has some weaker points. The writing is not always clear, and some sections require improvement. For instance, Section 3 is not as clear as other parts of the paper. Some details of Figure 2 are not explained, and the terminology is occasionally redundant. For example, the distinction between the "dictionary of mentions" and the "dictionary of entity-mention pairs" is unclear. Are these different from "text anchors" and "types for annotated text anchors"? Clarification on these points would improve the paper's readability.

Furthermore, the paper bears a strong resemblance to (Yamada et al., 2016), and the authors should at least outline the key differences between the two works.

One general observation about the current version is that the paper primarily tests the Multiple Embedding model against entity linking/disambiguation tasks. However, word embeddings are not only used for tasks directly dependent on entities in the knowledge base (KB) but also for other semantic tasks, such as parsing, coreference, or semantic role labeling. The authors should demonstrate that the word embeddings provided by the proposed MPME method are not weaker than simpler word spaces in these other semantic tasks, particularly those involving direct entity mentions.
