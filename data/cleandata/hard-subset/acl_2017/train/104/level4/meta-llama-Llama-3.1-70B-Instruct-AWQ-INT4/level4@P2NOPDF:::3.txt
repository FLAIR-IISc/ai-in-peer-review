**Strengths:**

The paper presents a novel neural learning method for entity disambiguation and linking, integrating entity, mention, and sense modeling within the same neural language modeling technique. This approach is supported by a simple training procedure, allowing for a wide range of applications. The empirical evaluation is thorough, and the paper's formal clarity is commendable.

**Weaknesses:**

The paper's main weaknesses lie in the amount of novel content and clarity in certain sections. While the technical ideas are well-presented, the discussion sometimes falls short of the same level of clarity.

The paper's contributions, although not revolutionary, are relevant and interesting. However, the performance improvements reported in the empirical evaluation are not striking. Furthermore, the paper's similarity to Yamada et al. (CoNLL 2016) raises concerns about the amount of novel content.

Upon closer examination, some sections of the paper, particularly Section 3, lack clarity. Certain details in Figure 2 are not adequately explained, and the terminology is sometimes redundant. For instance, the distinction between the dictionary of mentions, dictionary of entity-mention pairs, text anchors, and types for annotated text anchors is unclear.

Additionally, the paper's similarity to Yamada et al. (2016) is not adequately addressed. The authors should explicitly outline the differences between their work and the previous study.

A general observation about the current version of the paper is that it primarily tests the Multiple Embedding model against entity linking/disambiguation tasks. However, word embeddings have applications beyond these tasks, such as parsing, coreference, or semantic role labeling. The authors should demonstrate that the word embeddings provided by the proposed MPME method are not weaker than simpler word spaces in these other semantic tasks, particularly those involving direct entity mentions.
