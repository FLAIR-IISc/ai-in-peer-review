This paper tackles the problem of disambiguating and linking textual entity mentions to a given background knowledge base, specifically English Wikipedia. Although the title and introduction may be somewhat misleading, as they imply a more comprehensive approach to bridging text and knowledge, the Entity Disambiguation and Linking (EDL) task is indeed a crucial component of this broader objective.

The proposed method employs an intermediate layer of representation, namely mention senses, to facilitate the bridging process through two steps: (1) mapping mentions to mention senses, and (2) mapping mention senses to entities. The approach learns various embedding representations for words, mention senses, and entities, which are then jointly trained to optimize a single overall objective function that equally maximizes all three types of embeddings.

From a technical standpoint, the approach is relatively clear and adheres to current deep processing trends and best practices regarding embeddings. While alternative methods could be proposed, it is unclear whether they would yield significantly different results. My comments focus on the fundamental approach, which raises a key question: why is a two-step process involving mention senses more effective than a direct one-step mapping from word mentions to entities, as in the ALIGN algorithm proposed by Yamada et al.? Although Table 2 shows that the two-step MPME (and its simplification SPME) outperform the ALIGN algorithm, the exact difference and additional information provided by the mention senses compared to the entities are not explicitly explained.

To better understand this aspect, it appears that the profiles for entities consist of neighboring entities in a relatedness graph, which is constructed by examining word-level relatedness in entity definitions (Wikipedia pages). These profiles are based on extended skip-gram-based embeddings. In contrast, word profiles follow the standard distributional semantics approach without sense disambiguation. Mention sense profiles, on the other hand, employ the standard distributional semantics approach with sense disambiguation, which is performed using a sense-based profile ('language model') from local context words and neighboring mentions. However, the details of this process are not provided, and it is unclear how the senses are created and differentiated, and who determines the number of senses a mention string can have.

If the sense collection's definitional profiles are built starting with entity text as 'seed words,' this would imply a bijective mapping between mention senses and entities, where each mention sense corresponds to exactly one entity. In this case, the primary information used at the mention sense level that is not used at the entity level would be the words in the texts that reliably associate with the mention sense but do not occur in the equivalent entity webpage in Wikipedia. It is essential to clarify how many such words are there, on average, for a mention sense, and how crucial it is to maintain this extra differentiation information in a separate space (the mention sense space) rather than incorporating these additional words into the Entity space (by adding them to the Wikipedia entity pages).

If the above understanding is correct, it would be beneficial to update Section 5 of the paper to explicitly state this, as it appears to be the primary novel contribution of the paper. Furthermore, the claim in Section 6 that this work is the first to address mention ambiguity in the integration of text and knowledge representations is inaccurate. The TAC KBP evaluations for the past two years have featured EDL tasks, involving eight or nine systems, all performing this task against Freebase, which is considerably larger and noisier than Wikipedia.

On a positive note, the idea of the smoothing parameter in Section 6.4.2 is well-received. However, after reading the authors' responses, I remain unsatisfied with their explanation regarding the KBP evaluation's relevance. They argue that the focus is on the goodness of the embeddings, but the only way to evaluate such 'goodness' is through an application. Ultimately, the question is whether the embedding performs better, rather than its conceptual elegance.
