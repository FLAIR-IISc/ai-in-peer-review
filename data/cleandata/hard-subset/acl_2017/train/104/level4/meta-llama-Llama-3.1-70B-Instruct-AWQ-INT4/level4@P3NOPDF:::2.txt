This paper addresses the problem of disambiguating/linking textual entity mentions into a given background knowledge base, specifically English Wikipedia. Although the title and introduction may be somewhat misleading, as they imply a more comprehensive approach to bridging text and knowledge, the EDL task is indeed a core part of this overall task.

The proposed method involves an intermediate layer of representation, namely mention senses, which follows a two-step process: (1) mention to mention sense, and (2) mention sense to entity. Various embedding representations are learned for words, mention senses, and entities, which are then jointly trained to maximize a single overall objective function that equally optimizes all three types of embeddings.

From a technical standpoint, the approach is fairly clear and adheres to current deep processing trends and best practices regarding embeddings. While alternative methods can be suggested, it is unclear whether they would significantly impact the outcome. My comments focus on the fundamental approach, particularly the two-step process involving mention senses.

A crucial aspect that is not explicitly explained is why this two-step process is superior to a direct one-step mapping from word mentions to their entities, as proposed by Yamada et al. in the ALIGN algorithm. Table 2 demonstrates that the two-step MPME (and its simplification SPME) outperform the ALIGN algorithm, but the exact reason for this improvement is unclear. What additional information do mention senses provide compared to entities? To clarify this, I have attempted to understand the differences in the profiles of entities, words, and mention senses.

Entity profiles consist of neighboring entities in a relatedness graph, which is built by examining word-level relatedness in entity definitions (Wikipedia pages). These profiles are extended skip-gram-based embeddings. Word profiles, on the other hand, are based on the standard distributional semantics approach without sense disambiguation. Mention sense profiles, however, employ the standard distributional semantics approach with sense disambiguation, using a sense-based profile ('language model') from local context words and neighboring mentions.

This sense disambiguation process is a critical aspect of the approach, but it lacks detailed explanation. How exactly are senses created and differentiated? Who defines the number of senses a mention string can have? If this is determined by the knowledge base, then a bijective mapping between mention senses and entities is established, resulting in exactly one entity for each mention sense. In this case, are the sense collection's definitional profiles built starting with entity text as 'seed words'? If so, what information is used at the mention sense level that is not used at the entity level? Is it merely the words in the texts that reliably associate with the mention sense but do not occur in the equivalent entity webpage in Wikipedia? How many such words are there, on average, for a mention sense? Is it powerful/necessary to maintain this extra differentiation information in a separate space (the mention sense space) rather than loading these additional words into the entity space (by adding them to the Wikipedia entity pages)?

If my understanding is correct, I suggest updating Section 5 of the paper to clearly explain this key aspect. This is, in my opinion, the primary new information presented in the paper.

I must correct the statement in Section 6 that "…this is the first work to deal with mention ambiguity in the integration of text and knowledge representations, so there is no exact baselines for comparison." The TAC KBP evaluations for the past two years have hosted EDL tasks, involving eight or nine systems, all performing this task against Freebase, which is considerably larger and more noisy than Wikipedia. Please refer to http://nlp.cs.rpi.edu/kbp/2016/ for more information.

On a positive note, I appreciate the idea of the smoothing parameter in Section 6.4.2.

Post-response: After reading the authors' responses, I remain unsatisfied with their explanation regarding the KBP evaluation. They claim that the evaluation is not relevant, as they are interested in the goodness of the embeddings instead. However, the only way to evaluate the 'goodness' of embeddings is through an application. The conceptual elegance of an embedding is secondary to its performance.
