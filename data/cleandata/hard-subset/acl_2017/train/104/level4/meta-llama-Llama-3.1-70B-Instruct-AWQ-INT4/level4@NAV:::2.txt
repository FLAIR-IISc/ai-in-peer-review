This paper tackles the problem of disambiguating textual entity mentions by linking them to a given background knowledge base, specifically English Wikipedia. While the title and introduction may be somewhat misleading, as they imply a more comprehensive approach to bridging text and knowledge, the Entity Disambiguation with Linking (EDL) task is indeed a crucial component of this overall objective.

The proposed method employs an intermediate representation layer, namely mention senses, which involves a two-step process: (1) mapping mentions to mention senses, and (2) mapping mention senses to entities. The approach utilizes various embedding representations for words, mention senses, and entities, which are jointly trained to optimize a single objective function that equally maximizes all three types of embeddings.

From a technical standpoint, the approach is clear and adheres to current deep processing trends and best practices regarding embeddings. However, the rationale behind using a two-step process involving mention senses, rather than a direct one-step mapping from word mentions to entities, is not explicitly explained. The ALIGN algorithm, proposed by Yamada et al., uses a one-step approach, yet the results in Table 2 suggest that the two-step MPME (and its simplified version, SPME) perform better. It is unclear what additional information or differences the mention senses provide compared to the entities.

To clarify, the profiles for entities appear to consist of neighboring entities in a relatedness graph, built by examining word-level relatedness in entity definitions (Wikipedia pages). These profiles are based on extended skip-gram-based embeddings. In contrast, word profiles are derived from standard distributional semantics without sense disambiguation. Mention sense profiles, on the other hand, employ standard distributional semantics with sense disambiguation, using a sense-based profile ('language model') from local context words and neighboring mentions. However, the process of creating and differentiating senses is not thoroughly explained, leaving questions about how senses are defined and who determines the number of senses a mention string can have.

If the sense collection's definitional profiles are built using entity text as 'seed words,' it is unclear what information is used at the mention sense level that is not used at the entity level. Specifically, what words in the texts reliably associate with the mention sense but do not occur in the equivalent entity webpage in Wikipedia? How many such words are there, on average, for a mention sense? This raises questions about the necessity of maintaining this extra differentiation information in a separate space (the mention sense space) rather than incorporating these additional words into the Entity space.

The paper's claim that it is the first work to address mention ambiguity in the integration of text and knowledge representations is inaccurate. The TAC KBP evaluations have hosted EDL tasks for the past two years, involving multiple systems performing this task against Freebase, which is larger and noisier than Wikipedia.

On a positive note, the idea of the smoothing parameter in Section 6.4.2 is commendable. However, in response to the authors' reply, it is essential to note that the only way to evaluate the 'goodness' of embeddings is through an application. The conceptual elegance of an embedding is secondary to its performance.
