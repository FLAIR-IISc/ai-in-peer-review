**Summary of the Paper and Main Contributions**

The paper proposes a novel Multi-Prototype Mention Embedding (MPME) model for entity disambiguation and linking. The model integrates entity, mention, and sense modeling within a unified neural language modeling technique. The main contributions of this work, in order of strength, are:

1. A novel approach to entity disambiguation and linking using a multi-prototype mention embedding model.
2. A simple and efficient training procedure that allows for a large set of applications.
3. An empirical evaluation demonstrating the effectiveness of the proposed model in entity linking and disambiguation tasks.

**Strengths**

The paper presents a good idea for integrating entity, mention, and sense modeling within a unified neural language modeling technique. The simple training procedure connected with the modeling allows for a large set of applications. The empirical evaluation is good, although not striking improvements in performance are reported. The paper is clear formally, and the discussion is generally at a good level.

**Weaknesses**

The weaker points of the paper are:

1. **Clarity**: The prose is not always clear, particularly in Section 3. Some details of Figure 2 are not explained, and the terminology is sometimes redundant. For example, the distinction between the dictionary of mentions and the dictionary of entity-mention pairs is unclear.
2. **Novelty**: The paper is quite close in nature to Yamada et al. (2016), and the authors should at least outline the differences.
3. **Scope of evaluation**: The paper only tests the Multiple Embedding model against entity linking/disambiguation tasks. However, word embeddings are used in a broader range of semantic tasks, such as parsing, coreference, or semantic role labeling. The authors should demonstrate that the word embeddings provided by the proposed MPME method are not weaker than simpler word spaces in these tasks.

**Additional Comments**

One general observation is that the paper could benefit from a more detailed comparison with related work, particularly Yamada et al. (2016). Additionally, the authors should consider evaluating the proposed model on a broader range of semantic tasks to demonstrate its effectiveness and versatility.

**Questions to Authors**

1. Can you clarify the distinction between the dictionary of mentions and the dictionary of entity-mention pairs?
2. How does the proposed MPME model differ from Yamada et al. (2016), and what are the key advantages of the proposed approach?
3. Can you provide additional evaluation results demonstrating the effectiveness of the proposed model in a broader range of semantic tasks, such as parsing, coreference, or semantic role labeling?
