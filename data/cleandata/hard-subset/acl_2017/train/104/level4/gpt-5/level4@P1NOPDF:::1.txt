- Strengths:
* Outperforms ALIGN on a supervised entity linking task, suggesting that the proposed framework improves jointly learned representations of text and knowledge.
* Provides a direct comparison with a closely related approach using very similar input data.
* The analysis of the smoothing parameter is useful, as popularity bias is a persistent issue in entity linking.

- Weaknesses:
* The comparison with ALIGN could be more rigorous. ALIGN uses a content window size of 10, while this paper uses 5; ALIGN uses 500-dimensional vectors, while this paper uses 200. It is also unclear whether N(ej) includes only entities that link to ej. The graph is directed and consists of Wikipedia outlinks, but is adjacency defined as it would be for an undirected graph? For ALIGN, the context of an entity is the set of entities that link to that entity. If N(ej) is defined differently here, it is difficult to assess how much impact this change has on the learned vectors, and it could contribute to the difference in scores on the entity similarity task.
* It is sometimes unclear whether “mention” refers to a string type or a specific mention occurrence in a particular document. The phrase “mention embedding” is used, but it appears that embeddings are learned only for mention senses.
* The impact of the sense disambiguation order is hard to evaluate without comparison to other unsupervised entity linking methods.

- General Discussion:
The paper presents promising results and a thoughtful analysis of popularity smoothing, but differences in setup and unclear definitions limit the interpretability of the comparison to ALIGN and the conclusions about sense disambiguation. Clarifying the definition of N(ej), aligning hyperparameters where possible, and comparing sense disambiguation order to other unsupervised baselines would strengthen the claims.
