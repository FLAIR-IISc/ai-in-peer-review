Summary and contributions
This paper proposes a Multi-Prototype Mention Embedding (MPME) model that learns multiple sense-specific embeddings for each mention phrase by jointly modeling textual context words and knowledge-base (KB) entities in a unified vector space. A lightweight language-model-based disambiguation component assigns a mention occurrence to one of its learned sense prototypes. The approach is evaluated qualitatively (inspecting learned word/entity/mention representations) and quantitatively on entity linking (EL), where the authors report state-of-the-art results.

As I see it, the main contributions are:
1) A multi-prototype embedding formulation at the level of mention strings that jointly leverages text and KB signals, addressing mention ambiguity in a unified embedding space.
2) A simple, efficient disambiguation mechanism that uses the learned prototypes without heavy feature engineering or complex inference.
3) An empirically validated training pipeline that scales and yields competitive EL performance, along with qualitative analyses of the learned representations.

Strengths
1) Conceptually clean integration of text and KB supervision: Modeling mention ambiguity directly via multiple prototypes, while jointly embedding words and entities, is a natural and appealing way to bridge textual and KB contexts. The design aligns with prior joint embedding work but adds an explicit handle on polysemy at the mention level, which is practically important for EL.

2) Simple and efficient learning and inference: The training objective and the disambiguation step are easy to implement and appear to scale to large corpora and KBs. Avoiding complex global inference or heavy handcrafted features increases reproducibility and deployment potential.

3) Solid empirical results on entity linking: The method achieves state-of-the-art performance on a standard EL benchmark. Even if the margins are not dramatic, improvements are consistent and obtained with a relatively simple architecture, which is noteworthy given the competitive landscape of EL methods.

4) Qualitative analyses support the claims: The paper provides case studies and visualization-style evidence that the model learns sensible word, entity, and mention-sense representations. These analyses help interpret how multiple prototypes capture mention ambiguity.

5) Broad applicability: Because the training objective unifies words, entities, and mentions, the learned representations could be reused across a variety of entity-aware applications (e.g., EL, ERD, KB completion, possibly entity-aware IR). The generality of the setup is a practical advantage.

Weaknesses
1) Limited novelty relative to prior work, especially Yamada et al. (2016): The manuscript feels close in spirit to recent joint text–KB embedding approaches. While the multi-prototype mention focus is a meaningful extension, the paper does not delineate sharply how the modeling, training signals, and disambiguation differ from Yamada et al. (2016) and related systems. A clearer comparison (both conceptual and empirical) is needed to substantiate novelty.

2) Exposition and terminology issues: Section 3 is hard to follow in places. Figure 2 contains components that are not fully explained. Terminology is sometimes redundant or ambiguous (e.g., “dictionary of mentions,” “dictionary of entity–mention pairs,” “text anchors,” “types for annotated text anchors”). It is not always clear whether these refer to distinct resources or the same artifact with different names. These issues obscure the otherwise sound technical content.

3) Narrow evaluation scope: The paper’s experiments focus on EL. Given that the training also produces word embeddings, the authors should demonstrate that these word vectors are not degraded compared to simpler baselines on standard intrinsic evaluations (e.g., word similarity/analogy) or extrinsic tasks that do not rely on KB entities (e.g., POS tagging, parsing, NER, coreference, or SRL). Without this, it is unclear whether the joint objective sacrifices general-purpose word quality.

4) Modest gains and limited ablations: Reported improvements over strong baselines are not striking. The paper would be stronger with thorough ablations: single- vs multi-prototype mentions; text-only vs text+KB signals; effect of the disambiguation component; and sensitivity to the number of prototypes. These would clarify where the gains come from and how robust the approach is.

5) Presentation and reference quality: The references contain multiple duplicates and typographical errors, and some citations appear corrupted. Cleaning up the bibliography and ensuring accurate, consistent citations is necessary for a camera-ready version and currently detracts from the paper’s polish.

Questions to authors
- Differentiation from prior work: Please give a precise, itemized comparison to Yamada et al. (2016) and other joint embedding baselines. What is new in the objective, architecture, negative sampling, training data, and disambiguation? Can you provide a head-to-head experiment under identical settings?

- Number of prototypes and assignment: How is the number of mention prototypes chosen (fixed per mention, global hyperparameter, or data-driven)? How are senses initialized and maintained during training? Is there a clustering or nonparametric mechanism (e.g., online sense induction), and how do you avoid sense proliferation?

- Disambiguation details: Please clarify the language-model-based disambiguation procedure, including the exact scoring function, context windowing, and computational complexity at inference time. How does it scale with candidate set size per mention?

- Rare and unseen mentions: How does the model handle low-frequency mentions and zero-shot cases? Do you back off to single-prototype representations or rely on surface-form similarity and entity priors?

- Evaluation breadth: Can you report results showing that your word embeddings remain competitive on standard intrinsic benchmarks or one extrinsic non-entity task? Even a small study would help establish that the joint objective does not harm general-purpose word semantics.

- Ablations and error analysis: Please add ablation studies (single vs multi-prototype; text-only vs joint; with/without the disambiguation component), and provide an error analysis highlighting typical failure modes (e.g., highly polysemous mentions, tail entities).

- Resource clarity: Please define precisely the “dictionary of mentions,” “dictionary of entity–mention pairs,” and “text anchors.” Are these distinct? How are types used? A schematic table mapping each term to its source, format, and role in training would resolve confusion.

Overall evaluation
This is a solid and practical contribution with a clean idea and competitive results. Its main limitations are the unclear differentiation from closely related work, uneven exposition, and a narrow evaluation scope. With clearer comparisons, stronger ablations, and modest additional evaluation of the learned word embeddings, this paper would be a strong accept. As it stands, it is promising but would benefit from revisions to strengthen novelty claims and clarity. I did read the author response.
