Summary and Contributions
- The paper proposes a Multi-Prototype Mention Embedding model that learns multiple sense-specific embeddings for each mention by jointly modeling textual context words and entities from a knowledge base.
- It introduces an efficient language-model-based disambiguation procedure that assigns each mention occurrence to a specific sense embedding.
- Empirically, the paper reports state-of-the-art results on entity linking and presents qualitative and quantitative analyses of the learned word, entity, and mention-sense embeddings, including a study on popularity smoothing.

Strengths
- Strong empirical results on supervised entity linking: The method outperforms ALIGN on a standard benchmark, suggesting that the joint text–KB representation and the multi-prototype treatment of mentions improve downstream EL performance.
- Direct head-to-head comparison: The evaluation includes a comparison with a closely related approach using very similar input sources, which makes the contrast informative.
- Useful analysis of popularity effects: The exploration of the smoothing parameter sheds light on how popularity biases influence entity linking, addressing a known challenge in the EL literature.

Weaknesses
- Fairness of the comparison with ALIGN is unclear. Key settings differ (e.g., window size 5 vs. 10; embedding dimension 200 vs. 500), and the definition of the entity neighborhood N(ej) is not precisely specified. The paper states the graph is directed and built from Wikipedia outlinks, but it is not clear whether adjacency is treated as directed or symmetrized, and whether N(ej) contains inlinks, outlinks, or both. ALIGN defines entity context as entities that link to the target entity (inlinks). If N(ej) differs here, this could materially affect both the learned entity contexts and the results on entity similarity and EL. Without controlling for these differences, it is hard to attribute the gains.
- Ambiguity in terminology and representation granularity. “Mention” sometimes appears to refer to a surface form/type and other times to a specific occurrence. The paper uses “mention embedding,” yet the evidence suggests embeddings are learned for mention senses (prototypes), not for surface forms or individual tokens. The mapping between surface forms, sense inventories, and learned prototypes needs clarification.
- Limited isolation of the disambiguation component. The impact of the proposed language-model-based disambiguation is not disentangled from the representation learning. There is no comparison to other unsupervised EL/WSD-style disambiguation baselines, and no ablations that swap in a standard disambiguation method or use the proposed disambiguator with alternative embeddings. This makes it difficult to assess the relative contribution of the disambiguation step.

Questions to Authors (for author response)
- How is N(ej) defined exactly? Does it include only entities that link to ej (inlinks), only outlinks from ej, or both? Is the graph treated as directed or undirected when constructing entity contexts?
- Can you provide a controlled comparison with ALIGN by matching its hyperparameters (e.g., window size = 10, dimension = 500)? Conversely, how does ALIGN perform under your settings? Please include entity similarity and EL metrics under matched conditions.
- What is the precise definition of “mention” in your model: surface string type, token occurrence, or sense? Do you learn embeddings for surface forms independent of sense prototypes? How are sense prototypes induced, how many per surface form, and are they shared across documents?
- Can you isolate the contribution of the disambiguation method? For example, (a) use your embeddings with a standard prior+context disambiguation baseline, and (b) use your disambiguator with ALIGN (or other) embeddings.
- Do you compare the unsupervised disambiguation to established baselines (e.g., prior-based, PageRank/graph-based, or WSD-style methods)? If not, can you add such comparisons?

Additional Comments
- The popularity smoothing analysis is valuable; consider reporting sensitivity curves across additional datasets or domains.
- The bibliography contains multiple duplicates and corrupted entries; please clean and de-duplicate the references.
- Ensure consistent use of terminology (mention vs. sense vs. entity) throughout to improve readability.
