Review- 
- Strengths:
Solid ideas, straightforward neural learning, interesting—though not standout—performance, and a broad range of applications.
- Weaknesses: degree of novelty; clarity in certain sections.

The paper proposes a neural learning approach for entity disambiguation and linking. It presents a strong idea to unify entity, mention, and sense modeling within the same neural language modeling framework. The simple training procedure associated with the model enables support for a wide set of applications.

The formal presentation is clear, but the discussion does not consistently match the level of the technical content.

The empirical evaluation is good, although the gains are not dramatic. While it appears to extend Yamada et al. (CoNLL 2016), it introduces new ideas and is of clear interest.

The weaker points of the paper are:
- The writing is not always clear. Section 3, in particular, is hard to follow. Some details of Figure 2 are not explained, and the terminology feels redundant: for example, why refer to a “dictionary of mentions” or a “dictionary of entity–mention pairs”? Are these different from text anchors and the types for annotated text anchors?
- The paper is quite close in nature to Yamada et al. (2016), and the authors should at least outline the differences.

One general observation on the current version is:
The paper evaluates the Multiple Embedding model on entity linking/disambiguation tasks. However, word embeddings are also used for processes not directly dependent on KB entities, e.g., parsing, coreference, or semantic role labeling. The authors should demonstrate that the word embeddings produced by the proposed MPME method are not weaker, with respect to simpler word spaces, on such other semantic tasks, i.e., those that do not directly involve entity mentions.
