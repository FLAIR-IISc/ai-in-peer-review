Summary:
The paper presents a neural learning method for entity disambiguation and linking. It proposes a sound idea: integrating entity, mention, and sense modeling within the same neural language modeling framework. The simple training procedure tied to the modeling supports a large set of applications. The paper is formally clear, though the discussion does not always match the level of the technical ideas.

Strengths:
- Good ideas and a simple neural learning approach.
- Interesting performance (although not striking).
- Broad applicability across a large set of applications.

Weaknesses:
- Limited amount of novel content.
- Clarity issues in some sections.

Detailed comments:
- Empirical evaluation: The experiments are good, though they do not show striking improvements. While the work appears to be an extension of Yamada et al. (CoNLL 2016), it adds novel ideas and is of relevant interest.
- Clarity and presentation: The prose is not always clear. In particular:
  - Section 3 is not as clear as it should be.
  - Some details of Figure 2 are not explained.
  - The terminology feels redundant and potentially confusing. For example, what exactly is meant by the “dictionary of mentions” versus the “dictionary of entity–mention pairs”? How do these differ from text anchors and types for annotated text anchors?
- Relation to prior work: The paper is quite close in nature to Yamada et al. (2016). The authors should explicitly outline the differences.
- Scope of evaluation: The paper evaluates the Multiple Embedding model (MPME) on entity linking/disambiguation tasks. However, word embeddings are also widely used in tasks that do not directly depend on KB entities (e.g., parsing, coreference resolution, semantic role labeling). The authors should demonstrate that the word embeddings produced by MPME are not weaker than simpler word spaces on such tasks—i.e., on semantic tasks that do not directly involve entity mentions.
