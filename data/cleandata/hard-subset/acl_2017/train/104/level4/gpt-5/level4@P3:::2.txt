Summary and contributions
This paper proposes a two-step approach to entity disambiguation/linking (EDL) via Multi-Prototype Mention Embeddings (MPME). The key idea is to model ambiguous mention strings with multiple sense embeddings learned jointly with word and entity embeddings, and to use an efficient language-model–style disambiguation procedure to assign each mention in context to a sense, and then to an entity. The model is evaluated qualitatively and quantitatively, including an EDL study on a Wikipedia-based benchmark.

As I see it, the main contributions are:
1) A joint embedding framework that learns multiple sense embeddings per mention string alongside word and entity embeddings, with a disambiguation model to select the appropriate sense in context.
2) An application of these embeddings to entity linking that improves over strong baselines on a standard dataset, with a smoothing mechanism that stabilizes disambiguation.
3) Qualitative analyses suggesting that learned sense, word, and entity representations capture meaningful distinctions.

Strengths
- Clear, modular formulation of the two-step pipeline. Separating mention-to-sense and sense-to-entity is intuitively appealing for handling mention ambiguity. The paper integrates this with standard embedding objectives in a way that is easy to follow and generally consistent with best practices.
- Strong empirical results on EL. The method outperforms the ALIGN-style direct mention-to-entity approach and, according to Table 2, both MPME and SPME variants deliver gains. This supports the claim that explicitly modeling multiple mention senses adds value.
- Practical disambiguation model with effective smoothing. The language-model–based disambiguator and the smoothing parameter (Sec. 6.4.2) are well-motivated and appear to contribute to robustness. This is a thoughtful design choice for noisy local context.
- Joint learning of words, senses, and entities. Training all three in a common space facilitates information transfer between textual context and KB structure. The qualitative examples indicate sensible neighborhoods, suggesting the representations are coherent.
- Scope and positioning. The work sits at a useful intersection of multi-prototype word embeddings and knowledge-enhanced embeddings, and frames ambiguity at the mention level—closer to the EL problem than generic word-sense modeling.

Weaknesses
- Insufficient justification and analysis of the two-step design. While the two-step models outperform the one-step ALIGN baseline, the paper does not explain why. What information do mention senses capture that entities alone do not? Are gains due to increased model capacity, better inductive bias, or extra context harvested during sense induction? An ablation controlling for parameter count and training data would clarify whether the two-step architecture itself drives improvements.
- Under-specified sense induction and mapping. Key questions remain unanswered: How are senses created and differentiated for each mention string? Who decides the number of senses per mention (fixed, learned, or based on KB alias inventory)? Is there a one-to-one mapping between mention senses and entities, or can a sense cover multiple entities? What contexts are used to seed or update sense profiles, and how is the disambiguation “language model” trained? Without these details, it is hard to assess novelty, reproducibility, and the true information advantage of the sense layer.
- Construction of entity profiles is unclear. The paper refers to an entity relatedness graph and entity embeddings but does not specify how the graph is built (e.g., anchor-link co-occurrence, category links, in/outlinks, text co-mentions) or how text vs. graph signals are weighted. This matters for interpreting why sense-level signals would add information not already captured at the entity level.
- Baseline framing is problematic. The claim that there are “no exact baselines” is not accurate. The TAC KBP EDL evaluations have featured multiple systems solving essentially the same problem (albeit on Freebase). Even if the focus here is representation learning, EL performance is the primary extrinsic metric of “embedding goodness.” At minimum, a discussion and, if feasible, a comparison to TAC KBP systems (or careful adaptation of the dataset) would strengthen the evaluation. Relying primarily on ALIGN as the direct baseline risks understating the state of the art.
- Clarity and reproducibility issues. Important implementation details are missing: objective weighting across words/senses/entities (the paper says “equally” but precise coefficients matter), negative sampling, hyperparameters, training data sizes and preprocessing, and runtime/memory. The disambiguation component is called “efficient,” yet no timing or complexity comparisons are provided. The references section also contains many duplicates and typographical errors, which detract from professionalism.

Questions to authors
- Sense induction:
  - How do you determine the number of senses per mention string? Is it fixed, data-driven (e.g., clustering), or derived from the KB candidate set?
  - Is there a bijection between mention senses and entities, or can multiple senses map to the same entity (or vice versa)?
  - What contexts initialize and update sense embeddings: only local sentence context, neighboring mentions, or KB-derived text? Please detail the algorithm and training schedule.
- Disambiguation model:
  - Please specify the exact form of the “language model,” the features used (context words, neighboring mentions), training objective, and how the smoothing parameter is set. Provide sensitivity analysis to the smoothing value.
- Entity representations:
  - How is the entity relatedness graph constructed? Which signals are used (links, co-mentions, categories), and how are they weighted? How do you combine graph and text for entity embeddings?
- Source of the gains:
  - Can you provide an ablation comparing: (a) direct mention-to-entity mapping with matched parameter count; (b) two-step with senses but no extra sense-specific context; and (c) entities augmented with the same extra context used to build sense profiles? This would isolate the benefit of the intermediate sense layer versus simply enriching entity representations.
  - On average, how many context words associated with a learned mention sense do not appear on the corresponding Wikipedia entity page? This would quantify the additional signal provided by the sense layer.
- Evaluation:
  - Can you comment on the feasibility of evaluating on TAC KBP EDL (2015/2016) or, at least, on a subset adapted to Wikipedia? If not, please justify and discuss how your benchmark approximates real-world EL challenges.

Additional comments
- The title and introduction suggest a broader “text–knowledge integration” story than the experiments demonstrate. Framing the work more narrowly around EL with multi-prototype mention modeling would better match the actual contributions.
- Please clean and deduplicate the references; several entries are repeated with typographical errors.
- Consider releasing code and trained embeddings to facilitate reproducibility.

Overall assessment
The idea of modeling ambiguous mention strings with multiple learned senses, jointly with words and entities, is promising and empirically effective on the reported benchmark. However, the paper currently under-explains the core sense-induction mechanism and the origin of the two-step gains, and it overlooks important baselines from the EL community. With stronger analysis, clearer methodology, and a more comprehensive evaluation, this work could make a solid contribution to entity disambiguation and representation learning.
