Summary and contributions
This paper tackles entity disambiguation/linking (EDL) to a background knowledge base (English Wikipedia). While the title and introduction suggest a broader “text–knowledge integration,” the core contribution is squarely in EDL, which is indeed central to bridging text and knowledge. The key idea is to introduce an intermediate representation—mention senses—and perform disambiguation in two steps: (1) mention → mention sense, and (2) mention sense → entity. The model jointly learns embeddings for words, mention senses, and entities under a single objective that treats all three equally. An efficient language-model-based method is proposed to assign each mention occurrence to a specific sense. Experiments report qualitative/quantitative evidence of embedding quality and state-of-the-art results on an EDL benchmark.

My assessment of the main contributions (in order of importance):
1) An empirical finding that a two-step pipeline with multi-prototype mention embeddings (MPME, and even SPME) outperforms a direct mention→entity approach (e.g., Yamada et al.’s ALIGN) on EDL.
2) A joint training framework that learns word, mention-sense, and entity embeddings in a unified space with a single objective.
3) A simple smoothing parameter in training (Section 6.4.2) that appears effective and practically useful.

Strengths
- Technically sound and consistent with current best practices. The model design, joint objective, and training scheme are clear and align with established embedding methodologies. While alternative architectures could be imagined, it is not obvious they would materially change outcomes.
- Empirical improvements over a strong direct-mapping baseline. Table 2 shows that the two-step sense-based approach (MPME/SPME) beats ALIGN. This is an important and practically relevant result for EDL.
- Sensible unification of words, mention senses, and entities. Learning these jointly is a natural way to integrate distributional text signals and KB-derived signals, and the paper articulates this unification well.
- Smoothing parameter (Section 6.4.2). I found this idea elegant and effective. It is a small, clear intervention that yields measurable gains, and it is easy for others to adopt.

Weaknesses
- Insufficient justification and analysis of why two-step disambiguation helps. The paper shows MPME/SPME outperform a one-step ALIGN mapping, but does not explain why the sense layer is beneficial. What information is captured at the mention-sense level that is not already available at the entity level? A careful analysis is needed to substantiate the conceptual value of the intermediate space beyond the empirical delta in Table 2.
- Unclear sense induction and disambiguation procedure. The paper briefly references a language-model-based approach using local context words and neighboring mentions, but key details are missing:
  • How are senses created and differentiated per mention string?
  • Who determines the number of senses per mention? Is it learned, fixed, or derived from the KB?
  • If the KB drives sense enumeration, is there effectively a bijection between mention senses and entities (i.e., one entity per sense)? If so, how does this differ from a direct entity mapping?
  • What is the precise role of neighboring mentions in the language model?
Without clarity here, it is difficult to understand the mechanism by which the sense layer yields gains.
- Potential redundancy between mention-sense and entity profiles. My current understanding (please confirm) is:
  • Entity profiles: embeddings derived from a relatedness graph over entities, likely built from word-level relatedness of their Wikipedia pages.
  • Word profiles: standard distributional embeddings without sense disambiguation.
  • Mention-sense profiles: distributional embeddings with sense disambiguation via the proposed language model.
If mention senses are seeded or constrained by entity pages, what evidence shows that the mention-sense space contains discriminative textual signals not already captured in the entity space? For instance:
  • Are there context words reliably associated with a mention sense that do not appear in the entity page? How many, on average?
  • How much do these additional words contribute to the observed performance gains?
  • Could the same benefit be achieved by enriching the entity space with these words (e.g., augmenting entity pages), avoiding a separate sense layer?
- Inaccurate claim of novelty relative to prior EDL work. The paper states there are no exact baselines because this is the first work to handle mention ambiguity during text–knowledge integration. However, the TAC KBP evaluations (e.g., 2015–2016) hosted EDL tasks with several systems tackling the same problem, albeit against Freebase (larger and noisier than Wikipedia). While the KB differs, these are directly relevant baselines for entity disambiguation with ambiguous mentions. See http://nlp.cs.rpi.edu/kbp/2016/.
- Overstated scope in title/intro. “Bridging text and knowledge” encompasses more than EDL. The framing could be tempered or the contributions broadened, but as written it feels misaligned with the actual scope.
- Evaluation framing. The rebuttal suggests a focus on “embedding goodness” rather than application performance. In practice, embedding quality is best demonstrated through downstream tasks. Conceptual elegance matters less than realized gains; hence, broader or stronger application-based evaluation would strengthen the claims.

Questions to authors
- Please clarify the construction of entity profiles and the relatedness graph: how is relatedness computed, and from which textual sources?
- Provide details of the language-model-based sense disambiguation: exact features, training/inference procedure, and the role of neighboring mentions.
- How are the number of senses per mention determined? If KB-derived, is there a one-to-one mapping between senses and entities?
- If senses are tied to entities, what novel textual evidence do sense profiles capture that entity profiles do not? Please quantify (e.g., average number of additional context words per sense not in the entity page; ablations showing their impact).
- Why does the two-step pipeline outperform ALIGN? Beyond Table 2, please include an analysis isolating the contribution of the sense layer (e.g., ablations comparing 1-step vs 2-step with matched training data; sensitivity to sense count).
- The joint objective weights words, mention senses, and entities equally. Did you explore different weightings? If so, how sensitive are results?

Additional suggestions
- Please revise Section 5 to explicitly detail how mention senses are created, differentiated, and trained, including the relationship to entities and the textual sources used.
- Temper or qualify the novelty claim regarding handling mention ambiguity by citing relevant EDL work (e.g., TAC KBP) and clarifying differences.

Post-response note
I appreciate the authors’ reply. I remain unconvinced by the argument that KBP EDL is not relevant while the focus is on embedding quality. Ultimately, embedding quality should be validated via downstream performance. The key question is not conceptual elegance but empirical gains in applications.
