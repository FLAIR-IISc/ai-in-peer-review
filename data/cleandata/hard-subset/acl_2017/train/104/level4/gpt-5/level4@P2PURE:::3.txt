- Strengths:
Good ideas, a straightforward neural approach, interesting (though not dramatic) performance, and a broad range of applications.

- Weaknesses:
Limited novelty; clarity issues in some sections.

The paper presents a neural method for entity disambiguation and linking. It proposes a sound idea: integrate entity, mention, and sense modeling within the same neural language modeling framework. The simple training procedure, aligned with the modeling, enables support for a wide range of applications. Formally, the paper is clear, but the exposition does not always match the quality of the technical ideas.

The empirical evaluation is solid, though the performance gains are not striking. While the work appears to extend Yamada et al. (CoNLL 2016), it introduces new ideas and is of clear relevance.

Weaker points:
- The prose is sometimes unclear. I found Section 3 particularly hard to follow. Several details in Figure 2 are not explained, and some terminology feels redundant or confusing. For example: Why do you refer to a “dictionary of mentions” or a “dictionary of entity–mention pairs”? How do these differ from text anchors and the types for annotated text anchors?
- The paper is quite close in spirit to Yamada et al. (2016), and the authors should explicitly outline the differences.

General observation:
The paper evaluates the Multiple Embedding model primarily on entity linking/disambiguation. However, word embeddings are also widely used in tasks that do not directly depend on KB entities, such as parsing, coreference resolution, or semantic role labeling. The authors should demonstrate that the word embeddings produced by the proposed MPME method are not inferior to simpler word spaces on such downstream semantic tasks (i.e., tasks that do not directly involve entity mentions).
