This paper tackles entity disambiguation/linking (EDL) of textual mentions to a background knowledge base (English Wikipedia). The title and introduction overstate the scope: bridging text and knowledge is broader than EDL, though EDL is indeed a central component of that larger goal.

The core idea is to insert an intermediate “mention sense” layer between mentions and entities, yielding a two-step pipeline: (1) mention → mention sense, and (2) mention sense → entity. The authors learn embeddings for words, mention senses, and entities, and train them jointly under a single objective that treats all three types symmetrically.

The technical presentation is clear and follows current best practices for embedding-based models. My main concerns are conceptual. The paper does not convincingly explain why the two-step modeling via mention senses should outperform a direct mention→entity mapping (e.g., Yamada et al., the ALIGN baseline). Table 2 shows MPME (and even SPME) outperform ALIGN, but the mechanism is unclear. What specific information do mention senses capture that entities do not?

To check my understanding, here is how I interpret the profiles and training signals used:
- Entities: Profiles are defined via neighboring entities in a relatedness graph, presumably constructed from word-level relatedness derived from the entities’ Wikipedia pages. Entity representations are extended skip-gram-style embeddings over this graph.
- Words: Standard distributional semantics without sense disambiguation.
- Mention senses: Distributional semantics with sense disambiguation. The sense assignment appears to rely on a sense-based profile or language model derived from local context words and neighboring mentions (briefly mentioned before Section 4, but not detailed).

This last point is under-specified and is critical to the contribution. How exactly are mention senses created and distinguished? Who determines the number of senses per surface form? If the sense inventory is derived from the KB, does each mention sense map one-to-one to a KB entity (i.e., each sense corresponds to exactly one entity), even though a surface form may have multiple candidate entities? If so, are the mention-sense profiles initialized or “seeded” from the corresponding entity page text?

If mention senses are seeded from entity pages, what additional information exists at the mention-sense level that is absent from the entity level? Is it the set of contextual words that reliably co-occur with that sense in text but do not appear on the entity’s Wikipedia page? If that is the case, how large is this set on average, and how discriminative is it? Why is it necessary to maintain a separate mention-sense space for this information, rather than enriching the entity representation directly by incorporating these context-derived words into the entity profile?

If my reconstruction is roughly correct, please clarify this explicitly in Section 5. In my view, this is the central novel aspect of the paper and deserves a precise, transparent explanation of where the performance gains originate.

I also disagree with the claim in Section 6 that “this is the first work to deal with mention ambiguity in the integration of text and knowledge representations, so there is no exact baselines for comparison.” The TAC KBP evaluations have hosted EDL tasks for multiple years, with numerous systems tackling exactly this problem, albeit over Freebase (larger and noisier than Wikipedia). See http://nlp.cs.rpi.edu/kbp/2016/.

On the positive side, the smoothing parameter in Section 6.4.2 is a nice, pragmatic addition.

Post-response: I have read the authors’ rebuttal. I remain unconvinced by the argument that the TAC KBP EDL evaluations are irrelevant and that the focus should be on the intrinsic “goodness” of the embeddings. Ultimately, embedding quality must be established through downstream performance. Conceptual elegance is secondary to empirical gains: the key question is whether the proposed representations deliver consistently better end-task results.
