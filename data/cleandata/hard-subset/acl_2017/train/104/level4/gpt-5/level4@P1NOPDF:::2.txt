This paper addresses the problem of disambiguating/linking textual entity mentions to a background knowledge base (here, English Wikipedia). The title and introduction feel somewhat overstated/misleading—bridging text and knowledge involves more than EDL—but EDL is indeed a core component. The method introduces an intermediate representation, “mention senses,” yielding a two-step pipeline: (1) mention → mention sense, and (2) mention sense → entity. The model learns embeddings for words, mention senses, and entities, trained jointly under a single objective that weights all three equally.

Technically, the approach is clear and aligns with current deep learning practice for embeddings. While many alternatives could be suggested, it is not obvious they would materially change the outcome. My comments therefore focus on the core design. The paper does not explain why the two-step approach via mention senses should outperform a direct one-step mapping from mention to entity (as in Yamada et al., the ALIGN algorithm). Table 2 shows MPME (and even the simplified SPME) outperform ALIGN, but why, precisely? What additional signal do mention senses capture compared to entities? To clarify, please confirm whether the following understanding is correct (and, if so, revise the paper to make this explicit):

- Entities: Their profiles consist of neighboring entities in a relatedness graph. I assume this graph is constructed via word-level relatedness computed from entity definitions (Wikipedia pages). The profiles are embeddings learned with an extended skip-gram objective.

- Words: Their profiles follow standard distributional semantics without sense disambiguation.

- Mention senses: Their profiles follow standard distributional semantics with sense disambiguation. Disambiguation uses a sense-based profile (“language model”) derived from local context words and neighboring mentions, as briefly noted just before Section 4, but without details.

This last point is a key weakness: the paper does not specify how senses are created and differentiated. Who determines how many senses a mention string has? If you derive senses from the knowledge base, this seems to induce a bijection between mention senses and entities—i.e., one entity per sense—even though a mention string may map to multiple entities. In that case, are the definitional profiles for mention-sense clusters initialized using the entity text as seed words?

If so, what information is available at the mention-sense level that is not already captured at the entity level? Is it exactly the set of words in text that reliably associate with a mention sense but do not appear on the corresponding Wikipedia entity page? How many such words exist on average per mention sense? How strong is the case for keeping this differentiation in a separate mention-sense space, rather than simply injecting those additional words into the entity space (e.g., by augmenting the entity page text)?

If this reading is essentially correct, please update Section 5 accordingly; in my view, this is the paper’s primary new contribution.

It is not accurate, as stated in Section 6, that “…this is the first work to deal with mention ambiguity in the integration of text and knowledge representations, so there is no exact baselines for comparison.” The TAC KBP evaluations in the past two years have included EDL tasks, with eight or nine systems tackling exactly this problem, albeit on Freebase, which is substantially larger and noisier than Wikipedia. Please see http://nlp.cs.rpi.edu/kbp/2016/.

On a positive note, I found the smoothing parameter in Section 6.4.2 to be a well-motivated idea.

Post-response: I have read the authors’ responses. I remain unconvinced by the claim that the KBP evaluation is not relevant because the paper focuses on the “goodness” of embeddings. Ultimately, the only meaningful way to assess embedding quality is through application performance. Conceptual elegance aside, the central question is whether the method leads to better results.
