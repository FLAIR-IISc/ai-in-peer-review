This paper tackles the task of disambiguating/linking textual entity mentions to a background knowledge base (here, English Wikipedia). The title and introduction oversell/mischaracterize the scope somewhat, since bridging text and knowledge encompasses far more than EDL, though EDL is undeniably central to the broader problem. The proposed approach inserts an intermediate representation layer—mention senses—yielding a two-stage pipeline: (1) mention to mention sense, and (2) mention sense to entity. The authors learn embedding representations for words, mention senses, and entities, and jointly optimize them under a single objective that equally emphasizes all three embedding types.

Methodologically, the work is well presented and aligns with current deep learning practice and established embedding techniques; while many alternatives could be suggested, it’s not obvious they would materially change outcomes. My concerns center on the conceptual design. The paper does not clearly justify why a two-step pathway via mention senses should outperform a direct mention-to-entity mapping (as in Yamada et al., referred to here as ALIGN). Table 2 shows the two-step MPME (and even the simplified SPME) outperforming ALIGN. But why, precisely? What distinct signal do mention senses capture relative to entities? To clarify, please verify whether the following understanding is correct (and revise the paper accordingly to make this explicit).

For entities: their profiles comprise neighboring entities in a relatedness graph. This graph is constructed (presumably) by measuring word-level relatedness across entity definitions (Wikipedia pages). The resulting profiles are embeddings trained in an extended skip-gram framework.

For words: their profiles follow the standard distributional semantics paradigm without sense disambiguation.

For mention senses: their profiles apply standard distributional semantics but with sense disambiguation. Disambiguation leverages a sense-specific profile (“language model”) derived from local context words and neighboring mentions, as briefly noted just before Section 4, but without sufficient detail. This is a key methodological gap. How are senses generated and distinguished? Who determines how many senses a given mention string can have? If this is driven by the knowledge base, then we effectively have a bijection between mention senses and entities—i.e., exactly one entity per mention sense (even if multiple entities might be possible for the surface form). If so, are the definitional profiles for the sense inventory seeded from the corresponding entity texts? In that case, what information exists at the mention-sense level that is not already present at the entity level? Is it precisely the set of context words that reliably co-occur with the mention sense but do not appear on the entity’s Wikipedia page? How many such words are there on average per mention sense? How critical is it to maintain this extra differentiation in a separate “mention sense” space rather than simply augmenting the entity space by adding these discriminative words to the entity pages?

If this reading is essentially correct, please revise Section 5 to state this explicitly, as it seems to be the paper’s primary novel contribution.

Contrary to the claim in Section 6 that “this is the first work to deal with mention ambiguity in the integration of text and knowledge representations, so there is no exact baselines for comparison,” the TAC KBP evaluations over the past two years have included EDL tasks, with eight or nine systems addressing exactly this problem, albeit against Freebase, which is substantially larger and noisier than Wikipedia. See http://nlp.cs.rpi.edu/kbp/2016/ .

On the positive side: I found the smoothing parameter in Section 6.4.2 to be a particularly nice idea.

Post-response: I have reviewed the authors’ rebuttal. I remain unconvinced by the argument that the KBP evaluation is irrelevant in favor of intrinsic embedding “quality.” Ultimately, the only credible way to assess such quality is through downstream performance. Conceptual elegance is secondary; what matters is whether the method achieves superior results.
