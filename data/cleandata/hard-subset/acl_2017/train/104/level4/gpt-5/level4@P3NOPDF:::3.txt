Summary
This paper proposes a neural method for entity disambiguation and linking that integrates entity, mention, and sense modeling within a single neural language modeling framework. The approach uses a simple training procedure and is positioned as broadly applicable across multiple downstream tasks. The empirical evaluation shows competitive results, though the gains are not dramatic. While the work is close in spirit to Yamada et al. (CoNLL 2016), it introduces additional ideas that make it relevant to the ACL audience. I have read the author response.

Strengths
- Clear, coherent technical idea: unifying entity, mention, and sense modeling in a single neural framework.
- Simple, efficient training that appears scalable and easy to reproduce.
- Broad potential applicability to entity-centric NLP tasks.
- Formal presentation is generally sound, and the empirical results are solid, if not transformative.

Weaknesses and points needing clarification
- Limited novelty relative to closely related prior work (notably Yamada et al., 2016). The paper should more explicitly articulate what is new: the modeling assumptions, training objectives, resources, or integration strategy that go beyond prior methods.
- Exposition issues reduce accessibility, especially in Section 3. Important terminology is introduced but not crisply defined or consistently used.
- Figure 2 is under-explained. Several components and connections are not described in the text or the caption, making it hard to map the figure to the formal equations and training procedure.
- Redundant or confusing terminology: “dictionary of mentions,” “dictionary of entity–mention pairs,” “text anchors,” and “types for annotated text anchors” are referenced without clear distinctions. If these are separate resources, please define each, explain how they are constructed, and describe how they are used differently in the model and training data.
- Reported improvements are incremental. The paper would benefit from deeper analysis demonstrating when and why the unified modeling helps (e.g., specific phenomena or error categories).
- The evaluation focuses on entity-centric tasks. Since the method induces word embeddings as a byproduct, it would be helpful to show that these embeddings are not weaker than simpler baselines on tasks that do not directly involve KB entities (e.g., parsing, coreference, or semantic role labeling), or at least on standard intrinsic word similarity/analogy benchmarks.

Detailed comments and suggestions
- Clearly position the contribution relative to Yamada et al. (2016). A concise subsection contrasting objectives, architecture, training data, negative sampling schemes, and the handling of senses would help. If the “multiple embedding” aspect is the key innovation, specify precisely how multiple embeddings are constructed, tied, or regularized, and why this leads to better disambiguation.
- Improve Section 3’s clarity:
  - Provide a clean notation table or paragraph that defines all objects (mentions, entities, senses, anchors, types), their spaces, and their relationships.
  - Walk through a single training instance end-to-end, showing how the loss is computed and how each embedding is updated.
  - State all assumptions about availability and noise in the supervision (e.g., how anchors are extracted, how mention–entity pairs are curated).
- Expand Figure 2’s caption and cross-reference it explicitly from the main text. Label all nodes and edges, indicate which parts correspond to observed vs. latent variables, and link elements to loss terms.
- Add ablation studies:
  - Remove or disable each component (mention modeling, sense modeling, entity modeling) to quantify its contribution.
  - Compare single-embedding vs. multiple-embedding variants to isolate the value of the proposed multiple-embedding mechanism.
  - Vary the amount of supervision from entity–mention dictionaries or anchors to test robustness to resource sparsity.
- Provide more analysis:
  - Error typology: where does the model fail (rare entities, polysemous mentions, long-tail senses)?
  - Case studies illustrating how the joint modeling corrects mistakes that a baseline would make.
  - Sensitivity to hyperparameters and training data size.
- If running full downstream tasks like parsing/coreference is out of scope, provide lighter-weight evidence about the general utility of the learned word embeddings (e.g., intrinsic evaluation, or transfer to a simple sentence classification task). This would support the claim of broad applicability and help rule out degradation relative to simpler word-space models.
- Report computational cost (training time, memory, throughput) and discuss scalability to larger KBs and corpora.

Empirical evaluation
- The reported results are competitive and demonstrate that the approach is viable. However, given the modest improvements, stronger support through ablations and error analysis is important to establish the value of the unified modeling choice.
- Please verify that comparisons use the strongest available baselines under comparable settings and resources, including Yamada et al. (2016). If your training data or dictionaries differ, note the differences and, if possible, normalize for them.

Overall assessment
This paper presents a clean and practically appealing approach with a wide range of potential applications. Its main limitations are clarity of exposition and the degree of novelty relative to closely related work. With a clearer presentation (especially Section 3 and Figure 2), an explicit contrast to Yamada et al. (2016), and additional evidence via ablations and broader embedding evaluation, the paper would make a solid contribution. As it stands, I view it as borderline, leaning positive if the authors can address the clarity issues and better articulate and validate the incremental contributions.
