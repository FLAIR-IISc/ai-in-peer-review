Summary and contributions (as I see them)
The paper proposes a neural method for entity disambiguation and linking that integrates entities, mentions, and their senses within a single language-modeling framework. Specifically, it learns multi-prototype mention embeddings (MPME), i.e., multiple sense embeddings per mention, by jointly modeling words in textual context and entities from a knowledge base. It also introduces an efficient language-model-based approach to disambiguate mentions to specific senses. The overall training is simple and appears broadly applicable.

Contributions, in order of perceived strength:
1) A unified neural framework that jointly models entities, mentions (with multiple senses), and words, enabling multi-prototype mention embeddings.
2) An efficient disambiguation method that leverages the learned embeddings within a language modeling objective.
3) An empirical study demonstrating competitive performance on entity linking/disambiguation, suggesting potential applicability to a range of tasks.

Strengths
1) Conceptually sound integration of entities, mentions, and multiple senses. Modeling mentions with multiple prototypes within a unified semantic space is a good idea that aligns well with the ambiguity issues in entity linking.
2) Simple and practical learning procedure. The training setup is straightforward, which makes the approach accessible and potentially useful for many applications.
3) Empirical results are interesting, though not striking. The evaluation supports the method’s viability for entity linking/disambiguation and indicates that the approach can compete with strong baselines.
4) Broad applicability. By design, the framework could support a wide set of downstream applications beyond the specific entity-centric evaluation presented here.

Weaknesses
1) Limited novelty relative to prior work (especially Yamada et al., 2016). The paper appears close in spirit and methodology to Yamada et al. The submission should clearly delineate what is genuinely new, beyond incremental extensions, and quantify how each new component contributes to the observed performance.
2) Clarity issues, particularly in Section 3. The exposition does not consistently match the level of the underlying technical ideas. For example, Figure 2 lacks explanation of some details, and the terminology is occasionally redundant or confusing. The paper refers to a “dictionary of mentions” and a “dictionary of entity-mention pairs,” but it is unclear how these differ from text anchors and types for annotated text anchors. Clear definitions and consistent terminology are needed to avoid ambiguity.
3) Evaluation scope is narrow. The method is tested on entity linking/disambiguation, which is appropriate, but word embeddings are also widely used for tasks not directly dependent on knowledge base entities (e.g., parsing, coreference, semantic role labeling). The authors should demonstrate that the word embeddings produced by MPME are not weaker than simpler word embedding spaces on such tasks, i.e., tasks not directly involving entity mentions. Without this, it is hard to assess the generality of the approach.
4) Magnitude of improvement. While the results are solid, the gains are not striking. Given the claimed state-of-the-art positioning in the abstract, the paper should better contextualize the improvements, perhaps via stronger ablations or analysis that clarify when and why the method excels.

Questions to authors
1) Novelty vs. Yamada et al. (2016): Please explicitly enumerate the differences from Yamada et al. (modeling assumptions, training objective, use of multi-prototype mentions, disambiguation mechanism, data/resources). Which components are novel, and how do they each contribute in ablation studies?
2) Terminology and data resources: What precisely is the “dictionary of mentions,” and how does it differ from the “dictionary of entity-mention pairs”? How do these relate to text anchors and their types for annotated text anchors? Please define these terms formally and ensure they are used consistently throughout.
3) Figure 2: Several elements in Figure 2 are not explained. Please add a detailed caption or text description specifying the role of each component and how it connects to the training and disambiguation procedures.
4) Generalization beyond entity-centric tasks: Do you have results showing that the word embeddings from MPME perform on par with standard baselines on tasks such as dependency parsing, coreference, or SRL? If not, can you comment on expected behavior and plans to evaluate this? Even a small-scale comparison would strengthen the case for general utility.
5) Performance context: Given that improvements are not large, can you provide more error analysis or case studies to illustrate where multi-prototype mention modeling offers clear benefits over single-prototype or entity-only baselines?

Additional comments
- The paper is formally solid overall, but tightening the exposition—especially in Section 3 and around Figure 2—would substantially improve clarity.
- The method appears to be a meaningful extension of previous work and is of genuine interest, even if some improvements are modest. A clearer articulation of novelty and stronger empirical breadth would further strengthen the submission.
