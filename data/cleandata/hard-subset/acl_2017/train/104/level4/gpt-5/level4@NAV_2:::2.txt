This paper tackles the problem of disambiguating/linking textual entity mentions to a background knowledge base (here, English Wikipedia). While the title and introduction oversell the broader “text–knowledge integration” theme (there is more to that than EDL), entity discovery and linking is indeed a central component. The proposed approach introduces an intermediate representation layer of mention senses, yielding a two-stage pipeline: (1) map a surface mention to a mention sense; (2) map that mention sense to an entity. The model learns embeddings for words, mention senses, and entities, and jointly optimizes them under a single objective that treats all three embedding types symmetrically.

Methodologically, the design is clear and aligned with contemporary embedding-based practice; many plausible alternatives exist, but it is not obvious they would materially change outcomes. My comments target the core modeling choice. The paper does not convincingly justify why a two-step route via mention senses should outperform a direct mention-to-entity mapping (as in Yamada et al., termed ALIGN here). Table 2 reports that the two-step MPME (and even the simplified SPME) surpass ALIGN, but the reason is not articulated. What additional information or representational advantage do mention senses provide over entities?

To check my understanding (and to suggest clarifications in the paper), I believe the following is intended:
- Entities: their representations are derived from neighboring entities in a relatedness graph. This graph is (presumably) built from word-level relatedness computed over entity definitions (Wikipedia pages). The resulting entity profiles are learned via extended skip-gram style embeddings.
- Words: their profiles follow standard distributional semantics without sense disambiguation.
- Mention senses: their profiles also follow distributional semantics, but with sense disambiguation. Disambiguation uses a sense-based “language model” informed by local context words and neighboring mentions (briefly noted just before Section 4), but key details are missing.

This raises several questions. How are mention senses induced and distinguished? Who determines the number of senses per mention string? If this is derived from the KB, it implies a bijection between mention senses and entities—i.e., one entity per mention sense (even if multiple entities share the same surface form). If so, are the sense profiles initialized from or seeded by the entity text? In that case, what information is introduced at the mention-sense level that is not already present at the entity level? Is it exactly the set of context words that reliably co-occur with the sense in text but are absent from the corresponding Wikipedia page? How many such words exist per sense on average? How critical is it to maintain this extra differentiation in a separate mention-sense space rather than augmenting entity representations directly (e.g., by incorporating these context words into entity profiles)?

If this reading is accurate, please revise Section 5 to make it explicit, as this seems to be the central novel insight of the work.

It is also not accurate, as stated in Section 6, that this is the first work to handle mention ambiguity in joint text–knowledge representations, leaving no direct baselines. The TAC KBP evaluations in recent years have included EDL tasks with multiple systems addressing precisely this problem (albeit on Freebase, which is larger and noisier than Wikipedia). See http://nlp.cs.rpi.edu/kbp/2016/.

On the positive side, the smoothing parameter discussed in Section 6.4.2 is a thoughtful addition.

Post-response: I read the authors’ rebuttal. I remain unconvinced by the claim that KBP-style evaluations are irrelevant because the focus is on embedding quality. In practice, the only compelling way to assess the “quality” of embeddings is via extrinsic application performance. Conceptual elegance alone is insufficient; demonstrable gains on real tasks are what ultimately matter.
