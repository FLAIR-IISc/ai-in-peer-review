Summary
This paper tackles entity disambiguation/linking (EDL) from text to a knowledge base (Wikipedia). It proposes an intermediate representation—mention senses—so the task decomposes into (1) mention to mention-sense disambiguation and (2) mention-sense to entity mapping. The authors jointly learn embeddings for words, mention senses, and entities under a single objective, and introduce a language-model-based method for mention-sense disambiguation. Qualitative and quantitative analyses of the learned embeddings are presented, and the method achieves strong results on an entity linking benchmark.

Contributions (as I see them)
1) Introducing multi-prototype mention embeddings (MPME) as an explicit intermediate layer between surface mentions and entities, jointly trained with word and entity embeddings.
2) A simple, efficient language-model-style disambiguation mechanism with a smoothing parameter that improves robustness.
3) Empirical improvements over a strong direct-mapping baseline (ALIGN, Yamada et al.) on a standard Wikipedia-based EL benchmark.

Strengths
- Clear, modern modeling: The joint embedding framework is technically sound and consistent with best practices for distributional training. The overall approach is understandable and appears straightforward to reproduce.
- Empirical gains: Table 2 shows MPME (and even SPME) outperforming a direct mention-to-entity baseline (ALIGN). This supports the claim that an intermediate sense layer can be beneficial.
- Joint learning of three spaces: Training words, entities, and mention-senses together is a clean design that potentially captures complementary signals. The qualitative analyses suggest the embeddings are coherent.
- Practical disambiguation: The language-model-based disambiguation with a smoothing parameter (Section 6.4.2) is a nice, pragmatic idea that likely improves robustness to sparse context.
- Relevance: EDL is a central component in bridging text and KBs, and the paper targets a key pain point—mention ambiguity.

Weaknesses
- Insufficient justification for the two-step design: The core question—why is the mention-sense intermediate layer better than a direct mention-to-entity mapping—is not explained in depth. The empirical gains are clear, but the mechanism is not. What information do mention senses capture that entities do not?
- Unclear sense induction: The paper lacks detail on how senses are created and differentiated. How is the number of senses per mention determined? Is it tied to the KB’s candidate entities (leading to a near-bijective mapping), or discovered from text? If senses mirror entities, what value does the added space provide beyond re-weighting textual evidence already present for entities?
- Ambiguity in data sources and profiles: It is unclear how entity profiles are built (e.g., via Wikipedia link graph, co-mentions, or textual definitions) and how the relatedness graph is constructed. Similarly, how are mention-sense profiles initialized and updated? Are “seed” words derived from entity pages, local contexts, or both?
- Missing comparisons to broader EDL literature: The paper states there are no exact baselines, but prior TAC KBP EDL evaluations (e.g., 2015–2016) include multiple systems that address mention ambiguity at large scale (albeit on Freebase). While domain differences exist, at minimum a discussion or partial comparison is warranted to contextualize the contribution and avoid overclaiming novelty.
- Framing and claims: The title and introduction suggest a broader “text–knowledge integration” contribution, but the work is primarily an EDL method with joint embeddings. This overstates scope. Also, the claim of being the first to address mention ambiguity in text–KB integration is not accurate given prior EDL work.

Questions to Authors
1) Sense inventory:
- How do you determine the number of senses per surface form? Is it fixed, learned, or tied to the number of candidate entities in the KB?
- Is there ever a many-to-one or many-to-many mapping between mention senses and entities, or are senses effectively proxies for entities?

2) Profiles and training signals:
- How are entity profiles constructed? What defines edges in the entity relatedness graph (links, co-citations, textual similarity)?
- How are mention-sense profiles initialized and updated? Do you use entity page text as seeds, purely local contexts, or both?
- What textual evidence is used at the mention-sense level that is not already used at the entity level? Can you quantify how often sense-specific context words do not appear in the corresponding entity page?

3) Disambiguation details:
- Please provide the full formulation of the language-model-based disambiguation (smoothing, context window, handling of neighboring mentions).
- What is the computational complexity of sense disambiguation at inference time, and how does it scale with the number of senses per mention?

4) Objective and ablations:
- Are the three embedding components (words, mention senses, entities) weighted equally in the objective? If not, how are weights chosen?
- Please add ablations isolating the contributions of mention-sense embeddings, entity relatedness, and the smoothing parameter.

5) Evaluation scope:
- Could you evaluate or at least discuss performance on TAC KBP EDL datasets to bolster generality? If not, please justify and consider a transfer or zero-shot analysis.

Suggestions
- Clarify Section 5 with a concrete description of the sense inventory construction, the mapping cardinalities, and the distinct evidence captured by mention-sense embeddings vs entity embeddings. This explanation is central to the paper’s novelty claim.
- Calibrate the title/intro to reflect the scope (EDL via joint embeddings with a sense layer).

Post-response comment
I appreciate the response but remain unconvinced that KBP EDL evaluations are irrelevant. Ultimately, embedding “quality” should be judged by downstream performance. Conceptual elegance is useful, but what matters is whether the method delivers stronger task outcomes.
