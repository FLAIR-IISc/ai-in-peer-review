Summary
The paper addresses ambiguity in learning a unified semantic space for text and knowledge bases by proposing Multi-Prototype Mention Embeddings: multiple sense embeddings per mention string, learned jointly with word and entity embeddings. A language-model-based disambiguation method assigns each mention occurrence to a specific sense. The authors evaluate the quality of the learned representations and apply the approach to entity linking (EL), reporting state-of-the-art results and a direct comparison to ALIGN.

Contributions (as I see them)
1) A joint learning framework that produces multiple sense embeddings for mentions alongside word and entity embeddings in the same space.
2) An efficient language model approach for disambiguating mention occurrences to sense embeddings.
3) Empirical evidence on a standard EL benchmark that, under a broadly comparable setup, the proposed framework outperforms ALIGN, accompanied by an informative analysis of a smoothing parameter that controls popularity bias.

Strengths
1) Competitive EL performance against a strong, closely related baseline. The method outperforms ALIGN in supervised EL on a standard benchmark, suggesting that jointly learning multi-prototype mention embeddings alongside word and entity representations improves downstream disambiguation. Because ALIGN is a particularly relevant comparator for joint text–KB embedding, the positive gap is meaningful.
2) Direct comparison under similar inputs. The paper compares to ALIGN using closely matched input resources, which reduces data-driven confounds and makes the comparison more informative than comparisons to unrelated systems or datasets. This helps isolate the effect of the proposed modeling choices.
3) Useful analysis of popularity smoothing. The exploration of the smoothing parameter and its effect on disambiguation is valuable, as popularity bias is a long-standing challenge in EL. The analysis helps diagnose when popularity dominates and how tuning can rebalance contextual evidence versus priors.

Weaknesses
1) Potentially confounded comparison to ALIGN. Several key hyperparameters differ: ALIGN used a content window size of 10 versus 5 here, and a 500-dimensional space versus 200 dimensions here. These choices materially affect capacity, contextualization, and ultimately performance. Without controlling for these hyperparameters—either by (a) matching them across methods or (b) providing sensitivity analyses showing robustness—the reported gains may reflect setup differences rather than core modeling advances. Relatedly, the definition of N(ej) appears to differ: the graph is directed (Wikipedia outlinks), but it is unclear whether adjacency is treated as directed (outlinks only), reversed (inlinks only), or symmetrized. ALIGN defines an entity’s context as the set of entities that link to it (inlinks). If N(ej) here includes outlinks, both in- and outlinks, or is otherwise constructed differently, the entity similarity and EL results are not strictly comparable. This ambiguity weakens the strength of the empirical claim.
2) Ambiguity in the notion of “mention.” The paper alternates between using “mention” to mean a surface string type and an individual mention occurrence in context. The term “mention embedding” is used, but from the description it seems embeddings are learned for mention senses (clusters) rather than for the surface forms themselves. This terminological ambiguity makes it difficult to interpret the training objective, what exactly is embedded, and how disambiguation maps tokens to prototypes, which in turn affects reproducibility.
3) Limited assessment of the disambiguation component. The impact of the language-model-based disambiguation and its ordering (if disambiguation is performed sequentially or collectively) is hard to gauge without comparisons to other unsupervised EL strategies or alternative disambiguation modules. An ablation or head-to-head comparison would clarify whether gains stem from the multi-prototype embeddings, the disambiguation procedure, or their interaction.

Questions to Authors
- Please precisely define N(ej). Does it include only entities that link to ej (inlinks), only entities linked from ej (outlinks), both (symmetrized), or some other neighborhood? Is the graph treated as directed or undirected in practice?
- To strengthen the comparison with ALIGN, could you either (a) match window size and embedding dimensionality to ALIGN, or (b) report sensitivity analyses showing that your gains persist across these hyperparameters for both methods?
- What exactly is embedded under “mention embedding”? Are embeddings parameterized per surface string type, per sense/cluster, or both? How are individual mention tokens mapped to prototypes during training and inference?
- Can you quantify the contribution of the language-model-based disambiguation relative to the multi-prototype representation? For example, provide an ablation with a simpler unsupervised disambiguation baseline or compare to standard unsupervised EL methods.
- How does the popularity smoothing parameter interact with context strength? Do you observe regimes where popularity dominates even with strong contextual signals, and how does tuning mitigate this?

Overall, the paper tackles an important problem with a sensible joint modeling approach and provides promising results. Clarifying the evaluation setup—especially the construction of N(ej) and matching key hyperparameters—would substantially strengthen the empirical claims and make the contribution more convincing.
