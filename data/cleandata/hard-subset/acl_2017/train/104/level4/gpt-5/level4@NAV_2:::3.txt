- Strengths:
Sound ideas, straightforward neural training, competitive (though not dramatic) results, and a broad range of potential applications.

- Weaknesses:
Limited novelty; uneven clarity in certain sections.

The paper proposes a neural approach to entity disambiguation and linking. It puts forward a compelling idea to jointly model entities, mentions, and senses within a single neural language modeling framework. The simplicity of the training procedure, together with the modeling choice, makes the approach applicable to many tasks.

The paper is formally well-structured, but parts of the exposition do not consistently match the rigor of the technical content.

The empirical evaluation is solid, though the improvements are not dramatic. While the work appears to extend Yamada et al. (CoNLL 2016), it offers additional ideas and is of clear interest.

The weaker points of the paper are:
- The writing is at times opaque. Section 3 is not as clear as it could be. Some aspects of Figure 2 are left unspecified, and the terminology feels redundant: for instance, why distinguish a “dictionary of mentions” from a “dictionary of entity–mention pairs”? How do these differ from text anchors and from types for annotated anchors?
- The paper is quite close in nature to Yamada et al. (2016); the authors should at least delineate the differences.

One general observation on the current version is:
The Multiple-Prototype Mention Embedding model is evaluated primarily on entity linking/disambiguation. However, word embeddings are broadly used for tasks not directly dependent on KB entities, such as parsing, coreference, and semantic role labeling. The authors should demonstrate that the word embeddings produced by MPME are not weaker than simpler embedding spaces on such other semantic tasks, particularly those not explicitly grounded in entity mentions.
