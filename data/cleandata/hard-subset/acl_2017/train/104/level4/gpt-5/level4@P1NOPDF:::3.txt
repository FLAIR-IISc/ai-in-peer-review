- Strengths:
  - Good core ideas: integrating entity, mention, and sense modeling within the same neural language modeling framework.
  - Simple neural training procedure.
  - Interesting (though not dramatic) empirical performance.
  - Broad range of potential applications.

- Weaknesses:
  - Limited amount of genuinely novel content relative to prior work.
  - Clarity issues in several sections.

The paper presents a neural learning method for entity disambiguation and linking. The central idea—using a single neural language modeling technique to jointly model entities, mentions, and senses—is sound. The simplicity of the training procedure, together with the modeling choices, appears to support a wide array of applications.

The formal presentation is clear, but the narrative discussion does not consistently match the rigor of the technical ideas. The empirical evaluation is solid, although it does not deliver striking performance gains. While the work seems to extend Yamada et al. (CoNLL 2016), it introduces some new ideas and is of relevant interest.

Weaker points and suggestions for improvement:
- Exposition and clarity: Section 3 is not always clear. Some details of Figure 2 are not explained. The terminology also feels redundant or insufficiently distinguished. For example:
  - What exactly do you mean by a “dictionary of mentions” versus a “dictionary of entity–mention pairs”?
  - How do these differ from “text anchors” and “types for annotated text anchors”?
  - Clarifying the relationships among these resources (and using consistent naming) would reduce confusion.
- Relation to prior work: The paper is quite close in nature to Yamada et al. (2016). Please more explicitly outline the differences in modeling assumptions, training data, objective(s), and resulting capabilities, and quantify where possible how each change contributes to performance or coverage.
- Evaluation scope: The Multiple Embedding model is evaluated primarily on entity linking/disambiguation. However, word embeddings are also widely used in tasks that do not directly depend on KB entities (e.g., parsing, coreference, semantic role labeling). The authors should demonstrate that the word embeddings produced by the proposed MPME method are not weaker, with respect to simpler word spaces, on such tasks. This would help establish that the approach does not trade off general-purpose lexical semantics for entity-aware modeling.

Overall, the paper is technically sound and potentially useful, with a compelling unification of entity, mention, and sense modeling. Improving the clarity of Section 3 and Figure 2, tightening the terminology, and more explicitly differentiating the contributions from Yamada et al. (2016) would strengthen the paper. Broadening the evaluation to include standard non-KB-dependent NLP tasks would further substantiate the general utility of the learned embeddings.
