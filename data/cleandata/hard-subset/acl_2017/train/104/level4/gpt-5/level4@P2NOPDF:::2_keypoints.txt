- Title/intro overstate scope: bridging text and knowledge is broader than the paper’s actual focus on entity disambiguation/linking (EDL).
- Core idea: insert an intermediate “mention sense” layer yielding a two-step pipeline (mention → mention-sense → entity) with joint embeddings for words, mention-senses, and entities.
- Technical presentation is clear and follows embedding best practices; objective treats all three types symmetrically.
- Main conceptual concern: paper fails to convincingly explain why the two-step mention-sense model should outperform direct mention→entity mapping (e.g., ALIGN).
- Empirical result note: Table 2 shows MPME/SPME outperform ALIGN, but the mechanism for this improvement is unclear.
- Reviewer’s reconstruction of profiles: entities via relatedness graph (neighbor-based skip-gram), words via standard distributional semantics, mention-senses via context- and neighbor-based distributional signals—this needs confirmation.
- Critical under-specification: how mention-senses are created, distinguished, and how many senses per surface form are allowed is not explained.
- Question whether mention-senses map one-to-one to KB entities and whether senses are seeded/initialized from entity page text.
- If senses are seeded from entities, unclear what additional information mention-senses capture beyond entity representations (e.g., context words not on the entity page), and how large/discriminative that set is.
- Design question: why maintain a separate mention-sense space instead of enriching entity representations with context-derived words?
- Request to clarify the above (mention-sense construction and the source of performance gains) explicitly in Section 5, as it’s the central novel aspect.
- Disagreement with claim of novelty: TAC KBP EDL evaluations and prior systems address mention ambiguity in text–KB integration and should be cited/compared.
- Positive note: the smoothing parameter in Section 6.4.2 is a useful pragmatic addition.
- Post-rebuttal stance: reviewer remains unconvinced that focusing on intrinsic embedding “goodness” suffices—embedding quality must be proven with consistent downstream task improvements.
