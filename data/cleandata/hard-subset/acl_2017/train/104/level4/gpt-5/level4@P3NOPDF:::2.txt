Summary
This paper tackles entity disambiguation/linking (EDL) to English Wikipedia by introducing an intermediate layer of “mention senses” between textual mentions and entities. The model jointly learns embeddings for words, mention senses, and entities under a unified objective. The overall approach is clearly described and consistent with current embedding-based practice.

Strengths
- Clear formulation of a two-step pipeline (mention → sense → entity) with joint training.
- Sensible use of embedding methods and relatedness graphs; technically sound.
- Empirical results (e.g., Table 2) suggest the two-step approach (MPME/SPME) outperforms a direct mapping (ALIGN).
- The smoothing parameter in Section 6.4.2 is a thoughtful and effective idea.

Main concerns and requests for clarification
1) Why does the two-step approach help?
- The paper shows MPME/SPME outperform ALIGN, but it does not explain mechanistically why the intermediate mention-sense layer yields gains. Please provide a concrete account and supporting analysis (e.g., sparsity reduction, better parameter sharing across aliases, denoising of local context, or more robust candidate pruning).
- An ablation or controlled comparison would help: hold candidate generation and training data constant, and vary only the presence of the sense layer.

2) Construction and role of mention senses
- How are mention senses created and differentiated? Who determines the number of senses per surface form? Is the inventory induced from text, derived from the KB, or both?
- If the sense inventory is KB-derived, is there a bijection between mention senses and entities (i.e., exactly one entity per sense)? If not, what is the mapping?
- How is sense assignment performed during training/inference? The paper briefly mentions a sense-based profile (language model) using local context words and neighboring mentions; please specify the algorithm, supervision signal, and negative sampling or regularization used.
- What information exists at the mention-sense level that is not already captured at the entity level? For example, do sense profiles incorporate contextual words that reliably co-occur with a mention sense but are absent from the corresponding entity page? If so, please quantify: on average, how many such distinctive words per sense, and how much do they contribute (e.g., via a feature ablation)?
- If sense profiles are built using entity text as “seeds,” what prevents simply augmenting entity embeddings with these additional contextual words? Please justify the need for a separate representation space (mention-sense space) versus enriching entity representations directly, and consider an experiment where enriched entity embeddings replace the sense layer.

3) Clarify representation learning for each layer
- Entities: You state that entity profiles consist of neighboring entities in a relatedness graph. How is this graph constructed and weighted (e.g., hyperlink co-occurrence, in- and out-links, categories, anchor statistics)? Is the graph derived from word-level relatedness in entity pages, and how are edges normalized?
- Words: Confirm that standard distributional embeddings are used without sense disambiguation; specify corpus, windowing, and training details.
- Mention senses: Provide full details of the sense-disambiguated distributional model, including training data generation, context windowing, and how ambiguous contexts are handled.

4) Objective and training
- The paper states that the three embedding types are “maximized equally.” Please justify this design choice and provide a sensitivity analysis over different loss weightings. How does the smoothing parameter interact with these weights?

Evaluation and baselines
- The claim in Section 6 that “this is the first work to deal with mention ambiguity in the integration of text and knowledge representations” overlooks the TAC KBP EDL evaluations (e.g., 2015–2016), which include multiple systems addressing precisely this task (albeit against Freebase). See http://nlp.cs.rpi.edu/kbp/2016/. While the KB differs, these are meaningful baselines or at least relevant points of comparison.
- If direct comparison is infeasible, please explain clearly why (e.g., KB mismatch, annotation differences), and provide a principled mapping or a discussion that positions your contributions relative to those results. At minimum, include a downstream EDL evaluation with standard precision/recall/F1 on widely used datasets to demonstrate practical performance gains.
- Provide error analysis that contrasts ALIGN vs MPME/SPME, highlighting where the sense layer helps or hurts (e.g., rare mentions, long mentions, noisy context).
- Report candidate generation recall, linking accuracy by mention frequency/type, and the impact of the smoothing parameter on these metrics.

Presentation and reproducibility
- The title and introduction could more accurately reflect the scope: bridging text and knowledge is broader than EDL, and the paper focuses on the EDL component via mention senses.
- Section 5 should be expanded to fully detail the construction of mention senses, the relatedness graph, and training data, as this appears to be the core novelty in the method.
- Please release code, learned embeddings, and preprocessing scripts to facilitate replication.

Post-rebuttal comments
I appreciate the authors’ response but remain unconvinced by the assertion that TAC KBP EDL is not relevant because the paper focuses on embedding “goodness.” Ultimately, embedding quality should be demonstrated through downstream tasks; conceptual elegance is secondary to empirical utility. Presenting strong application-level results and competitive comparisons is essential to substantiate the contribution.

Overall assessment
A solid and technically competent paper with a promising idea—the mention-sense layer—and encouraging results. However, the paper needs a clearer justification for why the two-step design helps, substantially more detail on how mention senses are constructed and used, and stronger empirical positioning against established EDL evaluations. Addressing these points would significantly strengthen the contribution.
