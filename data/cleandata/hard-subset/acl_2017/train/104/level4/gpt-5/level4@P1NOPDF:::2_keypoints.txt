- Title/introduction are overstated/misleading; bridging text and knowledge involves more than entity disambiguation (EDL).  
- Paper proposes a two-step pipeline (mention → mention sense → entity) with jointly learned embeddings for words, mention senses, and entities.  
- Technical approach is clear and consistent with embedding methods, but core design choices need justification.  
- The paper fails to explain why the two-step mention-sense approach should outperform direct mention→entity methods like ALIGN.  
- Request confirmation and clarification of representations: entities (neighbor-based profiles from a relatedness graph), words (standard distributional profiles), and mention senses (sense-disambiguated distributional profiles).  
- Key weakness: the paper does not specify how mention senses are created, how many senses per mention, or how they are differentiated.  
- If mention senses are derived from the KB, this may create a near-bijection to entities; the paper must clarify initialization and relation to entity text.  
- Need explicit explanation of what information mention-sense representations capture that entities do not, and whether augmenting entity pages would suffice instead.  
- Empirical questions: how many unique context words per mention sense exist on average, and how strong is the justification for a separate mention-sense space?  
- Section 5 should be updated if the above reading (KB-derived mention senses and their novelty) is correct, as it appears to be the main contribution.  
- The claim of being the “first” to handle mention ambiguity is incorrect; TAC KBP EDL systems provide relevant baselines (see KBP 2016).  
- Positive: the smoothing parameter in Section 6.4.2 is well motivated.  
- After author response, reviewer remains unconvinced about dismissing KBP relevance and stresses that embedding quality should be measured by application performance.
