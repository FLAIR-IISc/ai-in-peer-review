Summary and contributions (as I see them)
- The paper proposes a Multi-Prototype Mention Embedding (MPME) model that learns multiple sense embeddings for ambiguous mention strings by jointly modeling words from textual context and entities from a knowledge base in a shared space.
- It introduces an efficient language-model-based disambiguation module to assign a mention occurrence to a specific sense embedding at inference time.
- Empirically, it evaluates the embeddings qualitatively and quantitatively and applies the model to entity linking, reporting competitive, sometimes state-of-the-art, performance.

Strengths
- Conceptual integration of mentions, entities, and words: Modeling multiple mention senses within a joint text–KB space is a sound idea that addresses a practical pain point in entity-centric NLP, where the same surface form maps to different entities. The design naturally supports downstream EL as well as other entity-aware tasks.
- Simple and pragmatic learning framework: The training objective and architecture are straightforward and seem easy to implement and scale. This simplicity increases the likelihood of adoption and reduces engineering overhead compared to heavier joint inference pipelines.
- Broad applicability: By producing representations for words, entities, and mentions together, the approach can, in principle, benefit a wide range of applications beyond EL (e.g., retrieval, QA, or ER), which strengthens its potential impact.
- Solid empirical grounding: The paper provides both qualitative analyses (e.g., nearest neighbors) and quantitative evaluations, and the reported EL results are competitive, with some improvements reaching the state of the art on a benchmark dataset.

Weaknesses
- Limited novelty relative to prior work: The approach appears close in spirit to Yamada et al. (2016). The paper should more explicitly delineate technical differences (objectives, architectures, resources, training data, negative sampling, or disambiguation mechanisms) and explain why those differences matter. As written, the contribution feels incremental.
- Clarity and terminology: Parts of Section 3 are difficult to follow, and several terms seem redundant or ill-defined. For instance, the “dictionary of mentions,” “dictionary of entity–mention pairs,” “text anchors,” and “types for annotated text anchors” are used without clear distinctions or definitions. Figure 2 contains unlabeled or underexplained components; readers need a more precise description of the training flow and modules.
- Evaluation scope and external validity: The experiments focus on entity linking/disambiguation. However, embeddings are used broadly in NLP. The paper should demonstrate that the learned word embeddings are not inferior to standard baselines on tasks that do not directly involve KB entities (e.g., parsing, coreference, SRL) or at least include widely used intrinsic/extrinsic word embedding benchmarks to show no regression.
- Magnitude of gains: While results are competitive, improvements are not uniformly large or “striking.” A more thorough ablation (e.g., effect of multiple prototypes vs. single-prototype, role of the disambiguation module, contribution of KB vs. text) would help isolate where the gains come from and justify the added complexity.
- Exposition: Although the formalism is generally sound, the prose occasionally lags behind the technical content, which hinders readability and reproducibility (especially in the model and disambiguation sections).

Questions to authors
- What are the precise differences from Yamada et al. (2016)? Please compare objectives, architectures, training data, negative sampling strategies, and the disambiguation module. If possible, include a controlled comparison where only the new components are varied.
- Terminology and resources: Please define “dictionary of mentions,” “dictionary of entity–mention pairs,” “text anchors,” and “types for annotated text anchors,” and clarify how they are constructed and related. Are these distinct resources or different views of the same annotations?
- Figure 2 clarification: Please label all components and data flows. How are contexts sampled, how are negatives generated, and where do gradients flow for mention vs. entity updates? Is the disambiguation model trained jointly or separately?
- Prototype selection: How is the number of sense prototypes per mention determined? Is it fixed or adaptive? What is the computational overhead and how does performance vary with this hyperparameter?
- General-purpose quality: Can you report results on standard word embedding evaluations (e.g., word similarity/analogy) or extrinsic tasks (e.g., parsing, NER, SRL) to show that word representations from MPME do not degrade compared to simpler baselines?
- Error analysis: What types of mentions benefit most from multiple prototypes (e.g., highly polysemous vs. rare mentions), and where does the system fail (e.g., limited context, tail entities)?
