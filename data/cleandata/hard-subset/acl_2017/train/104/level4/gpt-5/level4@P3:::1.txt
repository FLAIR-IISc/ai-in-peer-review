Summary
This paper proposes a Multi-Prototype Mention Embedding model that learns multiple sense embeddings for each mention surface form by jointly modeling textual context words and knowledge-base entities. A language-model-based disambiguation component assigns each mention occurrence to a specific sense embedding, with a smoothing parameter to control popularity bias. The learned word, entity, and mention-sense embeddings are evaluated qualitatively and quantitatively. Applied to entity linking, the approach achieves strong results and outperforms a closely related joint text–KB embedding method (ALIGN) on a supervised entity linking benchmark.

Main contributions (as I see them)
- A joint learning framework for multi-prototype (sense-specific) embeddings of mention surface forms, integrating textual contexts and KB entity signals.
- A simple, efficient language-model-based disambiguation method with a tunable popularity smoothing mechanism, used both to induce/assign mention senses and to perform entity linking.
- An empirical demonstration that the resulting embeddings and disambiguation improve supervised entity linking performance over a strong, closely related baseline using similar input data.

Strengths
- Strong empirical gains over a closely related baseline. The method outperforms ALIGN on supervised entity linking, suggesting the proposed multi-prototype mention representation plus disambiguation improves joint text–KB representations in a way that translates to downstream task gains. The comparison to a method trained over similar sources of supervision makes the improvements more credible than comparisons to distant baselines.
- Useful analysis of popularity smoothing. The study of the smoothing parameter illuminates how popularity biases affect disambiguation quality—a persistent issue in entity linking—and provides practical guidance on tuning this factor. This analysis adds value beyond raw accuracy numbers.
- Joint modeling of mentions, words, and entities. The framework produces embeddings for words, entities, and mention senses in a unified space, enabling both qualitative inspection (nearest neighbors) and quantitative evaluation (entity similarity and EL). This is a compelling design that can be reused in other entity-centric tasks.
- Methodological simplicity and efficiency. The language-model-based disambiguation is conceptually simple and efficient to implement, making the approach attractive for practitioners who need robust performance without complex inference.

Weaknesses
- Fairness and clarity of the comparison to ALIGN. Important training hyperparameters differ between systems (ALIGN reportedly used window size 10 and 500 dimensions vs. 5 and 200 here). Without matched settings or sensitivity analyses (e.g., performance as a function of window size and dimensionality), it is hard to ascribe gains to modeling innovations rather than capacity or context breadth. Additionally, the definition of entity context/adjacency N(ej) is unclear: the paper mentions a directed Wikipedia outlink graph, but does N(ej) include only inlinks, only outlinks, both, or an undirected neighborhood? ALIGN defines entity context as the set of entities that link to the target (incoming links). A different neighborhood definition could meaningfully affect entity similarity and downstream results.
- Ambiguity around “mention” vs. “mention sense.” The paper alternates between “mention” as a surface form type and “mention” as a token occurrence. “Mention embedding” appears to refer to embeddings of mention senses (prototypes), not of the surface form itself or its token-level contextualized representation. The terminology and notation should be clarified: what exactly is embedded (surface form, sense prototype tied to an entity, or latent sense cluster), and at what stage is a token occurrence mapped to a prototype?
- Limited ablations isolating the contributions. It is difficult to quantify the benefit of multi-prototype mention embeddings versus the disambiguation model or other design choices. Key ablations that seem missing include: (a) single-prototype mention embeddings with the same training pipeline; (b) using the same embeddings with/without the language-model disambiguation; and (c) text-only vs. KB-only vs. joint training. Such ablations would clarify which component drives the gains.
- Lack of comparison to unsupervised/graph-based EL methods for disambiguation order. The paper claims an efficient disambiguation approach but does not compare to unsupervised entity linking baselines (e.g., graph/ranking methods leveraging coherence) to contextualize the contribution of the disambiguation model itself. Even a limited comparison on a standard subset would help establish whether the proposed LM-based scoring is competitive in the unsupervised setting.
- Reproducibility and evaluation details. The paper would benefit from clearer specification of the training corpus, candidate generation, and mention detection protocol used in evaluation. If Aho–Corasick or other dictionary-based matching is used, please detail dictionaries, pruning, and thresholds. Report whether reported gains are statistically significant, and provide confidence intervals or significance tests where appropriate.

Questions to authors
- Entity graph neighborhood: How is N(ej) defined exactly? Are neighbors the entities that link to ej (incoming links), those that ej links to (outgoing), both (undirected), or something else? If this differs from ALIGN, please quantify the impact on entity similarity and EL performance.
- Hyperparameters in comparisons: Did you run ALIGN with matched window size and dimensionality, or run your model with ALIGN’s settings (window 10, dim 500)? If not, can you provide a sensitivity analysis to rule out capacity/context-size effects?
- Sense granularity and assignment: How many sense prototypes per mention do you allow? Is the number fixed per mention, globally fixed, or determined nonparametrically? Are mention senses directly tied one-to-one to KB entities, or can multiple prototypes map to the same entity (or represent latent senses without an entity)?
- Training and disambiguation pipeline: At what stage is a mention occurrence assigned to a sense prototype—online during training, or post hoc with the LM-based scorer? Is there a risk of circularity if the same LM signal is used for both training assignments and test-time disambiguation?
- Evaluation protocol: Which supervised EL dataset(s) are used, and what is the mention detection and candidate generation setup? Are gold mentions provided or automatically detected? Do you use prior probabilities/popularity in candidate generation, and how does the smoothing parameter interact with those priors?
- Statistical significance: Are improvements over ALIGN and other baselines statistically significant? Please report tests and p-values.

Additional comments
- Consider adding ablations for single- vs. multi-prototype mentions, and text-only/KB-only/joint training to isolate contributions.
- Provide qualitative error analysis showing when multi-prototype modeling helps (e.g., “Washington” as person/state/team) and when it fails, to guide future work.
- The references section appears to contain numerous duplicates, typos, and malformed entries. Please clean and deduplicate the bibliography.
