- Strength: Proposes a unified neural framework integrating entity, mention, and sense modeling within a single language-modeling architecture.  
- Strength: Training procedure is simple, scalable, and practically appealing for broad applications.  
- Strength: Empirical results are positive, though not dramatically better than baselines.  
- Weakness: Limited novelty relative to prior work, especially insufficient distinction from Yamada et al. (CoNLL 2016).  
- Weakness: Clarity issues in exposition, notably Section 3 and the explanation of Figure 2.  
- Contribution: Main contribution is integrating entity, mention, and sense representations in a single neural language-modeling framework with straightforward training.  
- Clarity issue: Section 3 is difficult to follow; variables, components, and data structures need precise definitions.  
- Clarity issue: Figure 2 lacks detail on inputs/outputs and information flow between modules.  
- Terminology problem: Distinctions among “dictionary of mentions,” “dictionary of entity–mention pairs,” “text anchors,” and “types” are unclear and sometimes redundant.  
- Empirical critique: Improvements over baselines are modest; evaluation is focused mainly on entity linking/disambiguation.  
- Empirical suggestion: Need auxiliary evaluations (e.g., parsing, coreference, SRL) to show MPME embeddings are competitive for non-entity-centric tasks.  
- Relation to prior work: Authors should explicitly outline conceptual and technical differences from Yamada et al. (2016), including what modeling/training changes yield gains.  
- Recommendations: Revise Section 3, expand Figure 2 explanation, disambiguate terminology with a glossary or unified notation, and provide clearer comparisons to Yamada et al.
