- Strengths:
  - Presents a sound idea: a unified neural framework that integrates entity, mention, and sense modeling within the same language-modeling architecture.
  - Simple training procedure with practical appeal; the approach appears scalable and supports a broad set of applications.
  - Empirical results are positive, if not dramatic.

- Weaknesses:
  - Limited novelty relative to prior work; the relationship to Yamada et al. (CoNLL 2016) is not sufficiently distinguished.
  - Clarity issues in the exposition, particularly in Section 3 and in the explanation of Figure 2.

Summary and contributions:
The paper proposes a neural learning method for entity disambiguation and linking. Its main contribution is to integrate entity, mention, and sense representations in a single neural language-modeling framework. The training is straightforward and aligns well with the modeling choices, enabling broad applicability across tasks that involve entities and mentions.

Clarity and presentation:
The paper is formally well specified, but the narrative does not consistently match the quality of the technical content. Section 3 is difficult to follow, and Figure 2 lacks sufficient detail. Terminology is sometimes redundant or unclear. For example:
- The distinction between “dictionary of mentions” and “dictionary of entity–mention pairs” is not explicit.
- It is unclear how these differ from “text anchors” and “types” in annotated corpora.
Clarifying these terms and their roles would improve readability and reproducibility.

Empirical evaluation:
The empirical results are solid, though improvements over baselines are not striking. While the work appears to extend Yamada et al. (2016), the paper does introduce additional ideas and remains of clear interest to the community. However, the evaluation focuses primarily on entity linking/disambiguation. Since word embeddings are widely used in tasks that do not directly involve KB entities (e.g., parsing, coreference resolution, semantic role labeling), it would strengthen the paper to demonstrate that the proposed MPME embeddings are not weaker than simpler word embedding spaces on such tasks. Even a targeted subset of these evaluations would help establish the general utility of the learned representations.

Relation to prior work:
The paper seems closely related in spirit to Yamada et al. (2016). The authors should explicitly outline conceptual and technical differences, including:
- What is new in the modeling (beyond integrating mentions, entities, and senses)?
- How the training regime and data differ.
- Which components drive the observed gains.

Recommendations for improvement:
- Revise Section 3 for clarity; define all components, variables, and data structures precisely.
- Expand the explanation of Figure 2, including all inputs/outputs and the flow of information between modules.
- Disambiguate terminology around mentions, entity–mention pairs, anchors, and types, preferably with a concise glossary or a unifying notation.
- Provide a clearer comparison to Yamada et al. (2016), highlighting both technical distinctions and empirical differences.
- If possible, include auxiliary evaluations to show that MPME embeddings remain competitive on non-entity-centric NLP tasks.
