This paper tackles the problem of disambiguating/linking textual entity mentions to a background knowledge base (here, English Wikipedia). While the title and introduction are somewhat overblown/misleading—bridging text and knowledge involves more than the EDL task—EDL is indeed a core component of that larger problem. The proposed approach introduces an intermediate representation layer of mention senses, yielding a two-step pipeline: (1) mention → mention sense, and (2) mention sense → entity. The authors learn embeddings for words, mention senses, and entities, and jointly optimize a single objective that treats the three embedding types equally.

Technically, the method is clearly described and follows current deep-learning practice and known best practices for embeddings. Although one could propose many alternatives, it is not obvious they would materially change the outcomes. My main comments concern the core approach. The paper does not explain why the two-step pipeline with mention senses is preferable to a direct mapping from mentions to entities (as in Yamada et al., referred to here as ALIGN). Table 2 shows that the two-step MPME (and even the simpler SPME) outperform ALIGN. But why, exactly? What specific additional information do mention senses capture compared to entities?

As I understand it (please confirm or clarify, and consider updating the paper accordingly):
- Entities: Their profiles consist of neighboring entities in a relatedness graph. I assume this graph is built by examining word-level relatedness in the entity definitions (Wikipedia pages). The profiles are embeddings learned with an extended skip-gram approach.
- Words: Their profiles follow standard distributional semantics without sense disambiguation.
- Mention senses: Their profiles follow standard distributional semantics with sense disambiguation. The disambiguation uses a sense-based profile (“language model”) derived from local context words and neighboring mentions, as briefly noted just before Section 4, but without sufficient detail.

This last point is problematic. Please clarify the following:
- How exactly are mention senses created and differentiated?
- Who (or what) determines how many senses a given mention string can have?
- If this determination relies on the KB, does that imply a bijective mapping between mention senses and entities—i.e., exactly one entity per mention sense (even if there may be more entities)?
- Are the definitional profiles for sense collections built starting from entity texts as “seed words”?
- If so, what information is used at the mention-sense level that is not already used at the entity level?
- Is it precisely the set of words reliably associated with a mention sense in text that do not occur on the corresponding Wikipedia entity page?
- On average, how many such words are there per mention sense?
- How powerful or necessary is it to maintain this extra differentiation in a separate mention-sense space, as opposed to injecting these additional words directly into the entity space (e.g., by augmenting the Wikipedia entity pages)?

If the above understanding is essentially correct, please update Section 5 to make it explicit; in my view, this is the most important new information in the paper.

It is also not accurate, as stated in Section 6, that “this is the first work to deal with mention ambiguity in the integration of text and knowledge representations, so there is no exact baselines for comparison.” The TAC KBP evaluations over the past two years have included EDL tasks, with eight or nine participating systems performing exactly this task (albeit against Freebase, which is larger and noisier than Wikipedia). See http://nlp.cs.rpi.edu/kbp/2016/ .

On a positive note, I found the smoothing parameter in Section 6.4.2 to be a good idea.

Post-response: I have read the authors’ responses. I remain unconvinced by the argument that the KBP evaluation is not relevant and that the primary interest is in the “goodness” of the embeddings instead. In practice, the only meaningful way to assess such “goodness” is via an application. Conceptual elegance alone is not sufficient; the key question is whether the method delivers better performance.
