This paper tackles the task of disambiguating/linking textual entity mentions to a background knowledge base (here, English Wikipedia). The title and introduction feel somewhat overstated/misleading—bridging text and knowledge involves more than EDL—but EDL is indeed a core component of that broader goal.

The proposed method inserts an intermediate representation layer of mention senses, yielding a two-step pipeline: (1) map mention to mention sense, then (2) map mention sense to entity. The authors learn embeddings for words, mention senses, and entities, and jointly train them with a single objective that gives equal weight to all three embedding types.

Technically, the approach is clear and aligns with current deep learning practice and known best practices for embeddings. While many variations could be suggested, it is unclear they would materially change outcomes. My comments therefore focus on the core design choice. The paper does not clearly explain why this two-step design (mention → sense → entity) should outperform a direct one-step mapping from mentions to entities (as in Yamada et al., referred to here as ALIGN). Table 2 shows that the two-step MPME (and even the simpler SPME) perform better. But why, exactly? What specific information do mention senses capture that entities do not? To diagnose this, please verify whether the following understanding is correct (and, if it is, revise the paper to make it explicit):

- Entities: Their profiles consist of neighboring entities in a relatedness graph. This graph is (I assume) built by measuring word-level relatedness from the entity definitions (Wikipedia pages). The profiles are embeddings learned via an extended skip-gram approach.
- Words: Their profiles follow standard distributional semantics, without sense disambiguation.
- Mention senses: Their profiles follow standard distributional semantics with sense disambiguation. Disambiguation is performed using a sense-based profile (“language model”) derived from local context words and neighboring mentions, as briefly noted just before Section 4, but without details. This lack of detail is problematic. How are senses constructed and differentiated? Who determines how many senses a surface form can have? If this is driven by the knowledge base, then there would be a bijection between mention senses and entities—exactly one entity per mention sense (even though a given surface form may map to multiple entities overall). In that scenario, are the definitional profiles for the sense collection initialized from entity text as “seed words”? If so, what information at the mention-sense level is not already present at the entity level? Is it merely the words that reliably co-occur with the mention sense in text but do not appear on the corresponding Wikipedia entity page? How many such words are there on average per mention sense? In other words, how strong/necessary is it to maintain this extra differentiation in a separate mention-sense space, rather than just incorporating these additional words into the entity space (e.g., by augmenting the Wikipedia entity pages)?

If the above reading is essentially correct, please revise Section 5 accordingly; to me, this is the key new insight in the paper.

It is also not accurate, as stated in Section 6, to claim this is “the first work to deal with mention ambiguity in the integration of text and knowledge representations, so there is no exact baselines for comparison.” The TAC KBP evaluations over the past two years have included EDL tasks, with eight or nine systems tackling precisely this problem—albeit on Freebase, which is larger and noisier than Wikipedia. See http://nlp.cs.rpi.edu/kbp/2016/ .

On the positive side, I very much liked the smoothing parameter in Section 6.4.2.

Post-response: I have read the authors’ responses. I remain unconvinced by the argument that the KBP evaluation is not relevant and that the focus should instead be on the intrinsic “goodness” of the embeddings. In practice, the only meaningful way to assess such “goodness” is via an application. Conceptual elegance alone does not matter; what matters is whether it improves performance.
