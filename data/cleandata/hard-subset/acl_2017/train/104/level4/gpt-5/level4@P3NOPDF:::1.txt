Summary
The paper proposes a joint framework for learning representations of text and knowledge, and evaluates the approach on supervised entity linking and entity similarity tasks. The method is compared directly with ALIGN, a closely related model trained on similar input data. The paper also analyzes a smoothing parameter intended to control popularity bias in entity linking.

Strengths
- Demonstrates improvements over ALIGN on supervised entity linking, suggesting that the proposed joint learning framework yields stronger text–knowledge representations for downstream EL.
- Provides a direct comparison with a closely related approach using similar data, which helps contextualize the contributions.
- Includes a useful analysis of the smoothing parameter that addresses the well-known issue of popularity bias in entity linking; the exploration of its effect on performance is informative.

Weaknesses and clarity issues
- Fairness of comparison with ALIGN: Important hyperparameters differ across systems (e.g., content window size 10 for ALIGN vs. 5 here; embedding dimension 500 for ALIGN vs. 200 here). These discrepancies complicate attribution of performance differences. Without aligning these settings or providing sensitivity analyses, it is hard to conclude that gains stem from modeling contributions rather than capacity or context-range differences.
- Definition of graph neighborhood N(ej): The knowledge graph is directed and constructed from Wikipedia outlinks, but it is unclear whether adjacency is treated as directed (outlinks only), undirected (symmetrized), or inlinks only. ALIGN defines an entity’s context as the set of entities that link to it (inlinks). If N(ej) differs (e.g., including outlinks or symmetrizing), this could substantially affect learned representations and the entity similarity results. The paper should explicitly define N(ej), the directionality used during training, and whether the neighborhood construction matches ALIGN’s.
- Ambiguity in the term “mention”: It is sometimes unclear whether “mention” refers to (a) a surface-form type (string) aggregated across the corpus, or (b) a particular occurrence in a document. The phrase “mention embedding” is used, but the text suggests that embeddings are learned for mention senses (i.e., disambiguated senses/entities) rather than surface forms. The paper should tighten terminology, define what is embedded (surface forms, senses, entities, or contexts), and state whether there are distinct embeddings for surface forms vs. sense-specific representations.
- Impact of disambiguation order: The contribution related to sense disambiguation order is difficult to assess without comparisons to other unsupervised entity linking methods or ablations that vary the ordering. As presented, it is unclear how much the ordering choice contributes to overall performance.

Requests for clarification
- Please specify exactly how N(ej) is defined: inlinks only, outlinks only, both (symmetrized), or some other construction; and whether this matches ALIGN’s context definition.
- Confirm whether adjacency is treated as directed or undirected during training and evaluation.
- Report the content window definition and whether it includes both entity and word tokens; clarify how it compares to ALIGN’s windowing.
- Clarify what is embedded: do you learn embeddings for surface-form types, token-level mentions, sense-specific mentions, entities, or all of the above? If multiple, how are they tied or regularized?
- For the entity similarity task, indicate whether the exact same graph and training data as ALIGN are used; if not, enumerate the differences and their expected effects.

Suggestions for improvement
- Strengthen the comparison with ALIGN by:
  - Matching key hyperparameters (window size and embedding dimensionality) and reporting results under both your defaults and ALIGN-matched settings.
  - Providing sensitivity analyses for window size and embedding dimension to show robustness and to disentangle modeling gains from capacity/context effects.
  - Running the entity similarity evaluation under aligned neighborhood definitions (e.g., inlinks only) and, if different, reporting ablations for outlinks-only and symmetrized graphs.
- Expand the analysis of the smoothing parameter by showing its effect across multiple datasets or subsets (e.g., by entity frequency), and by reporting trade-offs with precision/recall to better characterize the popularity bias.
- Evaluate the impact of sense disambiguation order via:
  - Ablations with randomized orders, heuristic orders (e.g., by mention confidence or frequency), and/or a beam or joint inference variant.
  - Comparisons with established unsupervised EL baselines to contextualize gains.
- Improve clarity and reproducibility:
  - Provide a precise definition of all neighborhoods, windows, and training objectives.
  - Detail preprocessing steps for constructing the graph and text contexts.
  - Release code or pseudo-code to facilitate replication.

Overall assessment
This is a promising paper with solid empirical gains on supervised entity linking and a thoughtful analysis of popularity smoothing. However, the current comparison with ALIGN leaves open questions due to differing hyperparameters and potentially different neighborhood definitions, which weakens causal claims about the source of improvements. Clarifying the construction of N(ej), unifying experimental settings with ALIGN (or adding thorough sensitivity analyses), and tightening terminology around “mentions” and “mention embeddings” would substantially strengthen the paper. Additional ablations on disambiguation order and comparisons to unsupervised EL baselines would further solidify the contribution.
