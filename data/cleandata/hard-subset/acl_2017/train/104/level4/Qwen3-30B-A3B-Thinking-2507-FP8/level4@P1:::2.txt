

This paper tackles entity disambiguation/linking (EDL) by proposing a two-step approach: (1) mapping mentions to sense-specific embeddings, and (2) mapping these senses to entities. The authors claim novelty in their "multi-prototype mention embedding" (MPME) model, which jointly learns word, sense, and entity embeddings to resolve mention ambiguity. However, the primary contribution is not the method itself but the empirical evaluation framework for embedding-based EDL, as the core technique resembles prior work. The paper’s strongest contribution is its comprehensive benchmarking of embedding quality and EDL performance on a standard dataset, demonstrating state-of-the-art results.  

**Strengths**  
1. **Rigorous evaluation framework**: The authors provide thorough qualitative and quantitative analysis of word, entity, and multi-prototype embeddings, moving beyond typical accuracy metrics to assess embedding quality. This framework is valuable for the community, as it establishes clear benchmarks for future work.  
2. **Strong empirical results**: The MPME model achieves new SOTA on the standard EDL benchmark, outperforming prior embedding-based approaches (e.g., Han et al. 2016, Huang et al. 2012). This validates the practical utility of their method.  
3. **Effective smoothing parameter**: The introduction of a context-aware smoothing parameter (Section 6.4.2) is a thoughtful technical contribution that improves disambiguation robustness without adding complexity.  

**Weaknesses**  
1. **Unjustified two-step approach**: The paper claims novelty for using "mention senses" as an intermediate layer but fails to explain *why* this is superior to direct mention-to-entity mapping (e.g., Yamada et al.’s ALIGN). Table 2 shows MPME outperforms single-step methods, yet the paper does not clarify what unique information the sense layer captures. Specifically:  
   - Are mention sense profiles built from *additional* context words not present in entity Wikipedia pages (e.g., local context words that co-occur with the mention but not the entity)? If so, how many such words exist per sense, and why can’t these be incorporated directly into entity embeddings?  
   - If sense definitions are derived from entity pages (e.g., using Wikipedia text as "seed words"), the sense-to-entity mapping becomes bijective, making the sense layer redundant. The paper must clarify this distinction to justify the two-step design.  

2. **Misleading baseline claim**: The paper states, "this is the first work to deal with mention ambiguity... so no exact baselines exist," which is inaccurate. TAC KBP 2015–2016 featured EDL tasks with 8–9 systems (e.g., Hoffart et al. 2011, Ceccarelli et al. 2013), all competing on similar mention disambiguation against Freebase. The authors should cite these works and acknowledge the existing EDL benchmarking landscape.  

3. **Insufficient technical detail**: The disambiguation step (Section 4) lacks clarity on how mention senses are defined and differentiated. Key questions remain:  
   - Who determines the number of senses per mention?  
   - How are senses created (e.g., via clustering, manual annotation, or knowledge base constraints)?  
   - What is the *informational gain* of maintaining a separate sense space versus enriching entity embeddings with context words? Without this, the method’s novelty is questionable.  

**Questions to Authors**  
1. The paper claims "multi-prototype" embeddings for mentions but does not specify how many prototypes per mention are used. Please clarify: Is this fixed (e.g., 3 prototypes) or dynamic (e.g., based on context)? How does this compare to prior multi-prototype work (e.g., Neelakantan et al. 2014)?  
2. In Table 2, MPME outperforms SPME (single-prototype). Does this imply that the *number* of prototypes (not just the multi-prototype design) is critical? If so, what is the optimal prototype count?  
3. The paper states that "entity profiles are built from Wikipedia pages." How are these profiles constructed (e.g., skip-gram on page text)? Crucially, do they *exclude* words that appear in the mention’s local context but not the entity page? If not, how does the sense layer avoid redundancy with entity embeddings?  

**Additional Comments**  
The title and introduction overstate the scope (e.g., "integrating text and knowledge" is too broad for an EDL-focused paper). The method’s alignment with current embedding best practices is sound, but the core innovation hinges on the two-step rationale, which requires stronger justification. The authors’ response to the baseline issue (citing TAC KBP as irrelevant because it uses Freebase) is unpersuasive: the *task* (mention disambiguation) is identical, and Freebase’s noise does not invalidate the comparison. Embedding quality must be evaluated *through application* (e.g., EDL performance), as the authors acknowledge in their response. The smoothing parameter is a redeeming technical detail, but it cannot offset the lack of clarity on the method’s novelty.
