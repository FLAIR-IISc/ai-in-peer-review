- Proposes MPME: a multi-prototype mention embedding model that learns multiple sense embeddings per mention via joint text and KB entity modeling.  
- Introduces a unified training procedure that integrates text, entity, and sense modeling within a single neural language model for disambiguation.  
- Shows empirical validation with state-of-the-art entity linking results (e.g., +0.8% F1 on AIDA-CoNLL).  
- Strength: Clear, well-motivated multi-prototype approach that handles polysemy without explicit sense inventories.  
- Strength: Strong qualitative and quantitative analyses, with convincing ablations showing multi-prototypes outperform single-embedding baselines.  
- Strength: Practically applicable—compatible with LM pipelines, leverages pre-trained embeddings, and offers linear-time disambiguation.  
- Weakness: Novelty is unclear—approach closely parallels Yamada et al. (2016) and lacks a clear technical distinction from prior sense-aware models.  
- Weakness: Evaluation is limited to entity linking; no results on non-EL tasks (e.g., parsing, coreference, SRL) to support claims of broad utility.  
- Weakness: Method exposition is ambiguous—key constructs like “dictionary of mentions” and “entity-mention pairs” are undefined and Figure 2 uses inconsistent terminology (“sense” vs. “prototype”).  
- Weakness: Bibliography and typos contain errors and duplicates, undermining scholarly rigor (e.g., repeated Aho & Corasick entries, misprints).  
- Question: Please clarify exactly how MPME’s multi-prototype mechanism differs from Yamada et al. (2016), ideally with a comparative table.  
- Question: Provide results (or justification for omission) on non-entity-linking downstream tasks to substantiate claims of broad applicability.  
- Additional: Empirical gains are solid but impact is weakened by the weak novelty argument; revise prose for precision and compare to more recent baselines (e.g., Huang et al. 2015).
