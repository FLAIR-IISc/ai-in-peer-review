

This paper addresses the problem of disambiguating/linking textual entity mentions to a background knowledge base (English Wikipedia). While the title and introduction slightly overstate the scope (as entity disambiguation is only one component of bridging text and knowledge), EDL constitutes a core aspect of the broader challenge. The approach employs an intermediate representation layer—mention senses—to perform the task in two steps: (1) mapping mentions to mention senses, and (2) mapping mention senses to entities. Embeddings for words, mention senses, and entities are jointly trained via a single objective function that equally optimizes all three embedding types.  

The technical method is clear and aligns with current deep learning practices and embedding best practices; alternative approaches are unlikely to yield significant improvements. The primary concern lies with the fundamental design choice: why is a two-step process (via mention senses) superior to a direct one-step mapping from mentions to entities (as in Yamada et al.’s ALIGN algorithm)? Table 2 demonstrates that MPME (and its simplification SPME) outperform direct methods, but the paper does not clarify *why*. Specifically:  
- For entities, profiles derive from neighboring entities in a relatedness graph built using word-level semantic relatedness of Wikipedia entity definitions (pages), represented as skip-gram embeddings.  
- For words, profiles follow standard distributional semantics without sense disambiguation.  
- For mention senses, profiles use distributional semantics *with* sense disambiguation, performed via a sense-based profile (language model) from local context words and neighboring mentions (briefly noted before Section 4, but without details).  
The critical gap is explaining *how* mention senses are defined, differentiated, and why they require a separate space. If senses are derived from the knowledge base (implying a bijective mapping to entities), then:  
1. Are sense profiles built using entity page text as "seed words"?  
2. What *unique information* do sense profiles contain that is absent from entity profiles? Specifically, do they incorporate words reliably associated with a mention sense but *not* present in the corresponding entity Wikipedia page?  
3. How many such words exist per mention sense on average?  
4. How essential is retaining this differentiation in a dedicated mention sense space versus augmenting entity profiles with these words (e.g., by adding them to Wikipedia entity pages)?  
This clarification is the paper’s key contribution; Section 5 must explicitly address these points.  

The claim in Section 6 that "this is the first work to address mention ambiguity in text-knowledge integration, with no exact baselines" is incorrect. TAC KBP evaluations over the past two years have included EDL tasks with eight to nine systems (e.g., http://nlp.cs.rpi.edu/kbp/2016/), albeit using Freebase (larger/noisier than Wikipedia).  

On a positive note, the smoothing parameter in Section 6.4.2 is well-conceived.  

Post-response: The authors’ rebuttal regarding TAC KBP irrelevance (citing their focus on embedding "goodness") is insufficient. Embedding quality is only meaningful through application performance; conceptual elegance is irrelevant if it does not improve downstream results.
