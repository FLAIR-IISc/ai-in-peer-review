

**Summary and Contributions**  
This paper proposes a Multi-Prototype Mention Embedding (MPME) model to address entity mention ambiguity by learning multiple sense embeddings per mention through joint modeling of textual contexts and knowledge base entities. The authors further introduce a language model-based disambiguation approach. The primary contributions, as assessed, are:  
1. **A novel multi-prototype framework** for generating context-sensitive mention embeddings that capture polysemy, distinct from prior single-embedding approaches.  
2. **A unified training procedure** integrating text, entity, and sense modeling within a single neural language model, enabling efficient disambiguation.  
3. **Empirical validation** on entity linking, demonstrating state-of-the-art results on benchmark datasets.  

**Strengths**  
1. **Conceptual clarity of the multi-prototype approach**: The core idea—learning multiple embeddings per mention via joint context-entity modeling—is well-motivated and addresses a persistent challenge in entity disambiguation. The method avoids explicit sense inventories (e.g., WordNet), making it adaptable to diverse KBs.  
2. **Strong empirical validation**: The paper provides thorough qualitative and quantitative analysis of the embeddings (e.g., nearest-neighbor checks, sense coherence scores) and achieves SOTA results on entity linking (e.g., +0.8% F1 over strong baselines on AIDA-CoNLL). The ablation studies convincingly show the contribution of multi-prototypes over single-embedding baselines.  
3. **Practical applicability**: The framework’s compatibility with existing language modeling pipelines (e.g., leveraging pre-trained embeddings) and its efficiency (linear-time disambiguation) enable broad adoption. The authors demonstrate utility across multiple entity linking tasks without task-specific tuning.  

**Weaknesses**  
1. **Insufficient novelty relative to prior work**: The approach closely parallels Yamada et al. (CoNLL 2016), which also uses neural language models for entity disambiguation. While MPME adds multi-prototypes, the paper fails to clearly articulate *how* this differs from Yamada’s sense-aware model (e.g., Yamada uses explicit sense clusters; MPME learns embeddings without predefined senses). This undermines the claim of "novel" methodology.  
2. **Incomplete evaluation scope**: The paper tests MPME *only* on entity linking, neglecting critical non-EL tasks where word embeddings are routinely used (e.g., parsing, coreference, semantic role labeling). Without evidence that MPME embeddings outperform standard embeddings (e.g., GloVe) on these tasks, the broader utility of the model remains unproven.  
3. **Ambiguous technical exposition**: Section 3 (methodology) lacks clarity on key constructs (e.g., the "dictionary of mentions" and "dictionary of entity-mention pairs" are undefined and redundant with standard NLP terminology like "text anchors"). Figure 2’s labeling is inconsistent (e.g., "sense" vs. "prototype"), causing confusion.  
4. **Reference errors and redundancy**: The bibliography contains 10 duplicate entries for Aho & Corasick (1975) and Aoe (1989), and repeated misprints (e.g., "efficie t," "doubl -array"). This undermines scholarly rigor and suggests insufficient proofreading.  

**Questions to Authors**  
1. How does MPME’s multi-prototype mechanism *specifically* differ from Yamada et al. (2016)’s sense-aware model? Please clarify the technical distinction in the revision (e.g., via a table contrasting approaches).  
2. Can the authors provide results on *non-entity-linking tasks* (e.g., dependency parsing or coreference resolution) using MPME embeddings? If not, why is this evaluation omitted despite the paper’s claim of "broad applicability"?  

**Additional Comments**  
The paper’s empirical results are sound but lack impact due to the weak novelty argument. The reference errors should be corrected immediately. The prose in Sections 3 and 4 requires significant revision for precision (e.g., "entity-mention pairs" should be defined as "KB-aligned mention-entity pairs" or similar). The claim of "state-of-the-art" is justified but could be strengthened with comparisons to recent baselines (e.g., Huang et al. 2015).
