

Strengths:  
* The proposed framework demonstrates superior performance over ALIGN on the supervised entity linking task, indicating that jointly learning text and knowledge representations enhances representation quality.  
* The direct comparison with a closely related approach using nearly identical input data strengthens the validity of the results.  
* The analysis of the smoothing parameter provides valuable insights, as the impact of entity popularity remains a persistent challenge in entity linking.  

Weaknesses:  
* The comparison with ALIGN requires greater methodological rigor. Key differences include ALIGN’s content window size of 10 versus the proposed method’s 5, and vector dimensions of 500 versus 200. Crucially, it is unclear whether $N(e_j)$ (the neighborhood of entity $e_j$) includes only entities linking *to* $e_j$ (as ALIGN defines context via incoming links), or if it incorporates a broader set due to the directed graph structure (using Wikipedia outlinks). Since the graph is directed but adjacency may be treated as undirected in $N(e_j)$, this ambiguity obscures whether performance differences stem from the model architecture or these implementation variations. Without resolving this, the contribution of the proposed framework to vector learning cannot be isolated.  
* Terminology regarding "mention" is inconsistent and confusing. The paper uses "mention embedding" but appears to learn embeddings only for *specific mention senses* (e.g., context-dependent entity senses), not for raw string types. This conflation makes it difficult to determine whether embeddings represent lexical forms or sense-specific contextual representations, complicating interpretation of the method.  
* The impact of sense disambiguation order is inadequately evaluated. Without comparative results against other unsupervised entity linking methods (e.g., those using different disambiguation heuristics or orders), it is impossible to assess whether the observed gains are due to the proposed ordering strategy or inherent limitations of the baseline approaches.  

General Discussion:  
The paper addresses a meaningful challenge in entity linking by jointly modeling text and knowledge. While the core idea is promising, the technical execution requires refinement to ensure claims about performance gains are robustly supported. The methodological gaps in the ALIGN comparison and terminology significantly undermine the paper’s contribution. Future work should clarify graph adjacency definitions, standardize hyperparameters for fair comparison, and explicitly tie "mention" to sense-specific embeddings. The smoothing parameter analysis is a strong point, but it cannot compensate for unresolved methodological ambiguities that weaken the central claim. The results suggest the approach has potential, but the current presentation does not sufficiently distinguish between model innovations and implementation artifacts. To strengthen the submission, the authors should: (1) clarify $N(e_j)$’s definition and align graph handling with ALIGN; (2) revise terminology to distinguish "mention" (string type) from "mention sense" (contextual embedding); and (3) add comparisons against unsupervised baselines to isolate the effect of disambiguation order. Without these revisions, the paper’s contribution to the field remains ambiguous.
