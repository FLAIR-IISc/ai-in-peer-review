

Strengths:
The paper presents a novel neural learning approach for entity disambiguation and linking, integrating entity, mention, and sense modeling within a unified neural language modeling framework. The method demonstrates good ideas, a simple training procedure, and broad applicability across a large set of tasks. The empirical evaluation is sound, though performance improvements are modest rather than striking.

Weaknesses:
1.  **Novelty and Comparison:** While the paper introduces some novel elements beyond Yamada et al. (CoNLL 2016), the connection to their work is not sufficiently articulated. The authors must clearly articulate the key differences and contributions relative to this prior work.
2.  **Clarity and Terminology:** The prose is not consistently clear. Section 3 requires significant improvement in exposition. Certain details in Figure 2 are unexplained, and terminology is redundant or ambiguous. For instance, the paper refers to a "dictionary of mentions" and a "dictionary of entity-mention pairs" – it is unclear how these differ from standard concepts like text anchors or types for annotated text anchors. This lack of clarity hinders understanding.
3.  **Evaluation Scope:** The paper evaluates the Multiple Embedding model (MPME) solely on entity linking/disambiguation tasks. However, word embeddings are also crucial for semantic tasks not directly dependent on KB entities (e.g., parsing, coreference, semantic role labeling). The authors should demonstrate that the MPME word embeddings are not weaker than simpler word spaces on such tasks, particularly those involving entity mentions, to establish broader utility.
