

Strengths:  
Good ideas, simple neural learning approach, interesting performance (though not striking), and a broad range of applications.  

Weaknesses: Limited novelty and clarity issues in certain sections.  

The paper proposes a neural learning method for entity disambiguation and linking, introducing a sound idea to integrate entity, mention, and sense modeling within a unified neural language modeling framework. The straightforward training procedure enables support for a wide range of applications.  

The paper is formally clear, but the discussion does not consistently match the technical depth of the proposed methods. The empirical evaluation is solid, though performance gains are not particularly striking. While it builds upon Yamada et al. (CoNLL 2016), the work contributes novel elements and holds relevant interest.  

Key weaknesses include:  
- Unclear prose, particularly in Section 3; unexplained details in Figure 2; and redundant terminology (e.g., why distinguish "dictionary of mentions" from "dictionary of entity-mention pairs" when these appear equivalent to text anchors and types for annotated text anchors?).  
- Insufficient delineation of differences from Yamada et al. (2016), which the paper closely resembles.  

A general observation: The paper evaluates the Multiple Embedding model (MPME) solely on entity linking/disambiguation tasks. However, word embeddings are also used in non-entity-dependent semantic tasks (e.g., parsing, coreference, semantic role labeling). The authors should demonstrate that MPME embeddings are not inferior to simpler wordspaces in these broader semantic tasks involving entity mentions.
