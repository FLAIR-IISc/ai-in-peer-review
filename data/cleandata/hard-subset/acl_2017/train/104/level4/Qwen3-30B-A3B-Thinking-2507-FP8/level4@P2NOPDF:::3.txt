

The paper proposes a neural approach for entity disambiguation and linking that integrates entity, mention, and sense modeling within a unified language modeling framework. Its core strength lies in a simple training procedure enabling broad applicability across diverse tasks. The empirical evaluation demonstrates consistent performance gains, though improvements are not groundbreaking. The approach builds upon Yamada et al. (2016) but introduces novel elements that warrant attention, particularly in its unified modeling strategy.

**Strengths:**  
The paper presents a well-motivated approach with clear technical merit. The integration of entity, mention, and sense modeling under a single neural framework is conceptually sound and practically advantageous for scalability. The simplicity of the training procedure significantly enhances its potential for real-world deployment across multiple applications. The empirical results are thorough and support the method’s efficacy, even if the performance gains are incremental rather than transformative.

**Weaknesses and Concerns:**  
1.  **Clarity and Exposition:** The paper suffers from inconsistent clarity, particularly in Section 3 and Figure 2. Key terminology is ambiguous: for instance, the distinction between "dictionary of mentions," "dictionary of entity-mention pairs," "text anchors," and "types for annotated text anchors" is unclear and appears redundant. The paper should define these terms precisely and justify their necessity to avoid confusion.  
2.  **Novelty and Contextualization:** While the work extends Yamada et al. (2016), the paper does not sufficiently articulate its novel contributions relative to this prior work. A clear comparison outlining *how* this method differs (e.g., in modeling scope, training efficiency, or applicability) is essential for establishing its significance. Merely stating "it adds novel ideas" is insufficient.  
3.  **Evaluation Scope:** The evaluation focuses solely on entity linking/disambiguation tasks. However, the proposed Multiple Embedding model (MPME) generates word embeddings, which are widely used in other NLP tasks (e.g., parsing, coreference resolution, semantic role labeling) that may not inherently involve knowledge base entities. To validate the *general utility* of MPME embeddings, the authors should demonstrate that these embeddings are competitive with simpler word vector spaces (e.g., GloVe, word2vec) on *non-EL tasks* that directly involve entity mentions (e.g., entity coreference or mention-based semantic classification). This would strengthen the claim that MPME produces high-quality, broadly useful embeddings.  

**Overall Assessment:**  
The paper addresses a meaningful problem with a technically sound, scalable approach. Its greatest value lies in the practicality of its unified modeling and training strategy. However, the current presentation lacks sufficient clarity in key sections, and the novelty claim requires sharper articulation against existing work. The evaluation scope also limits the perceived impact of the embedding method. Addressing the clarity issues, explicitly differentiating from Yamada et al., and expanding the evaluation to include non-EL tasks involving entity mentions would substantially strengthen the contribution.  

The authors’ response acknowledges the need for clearer exposition and the Yamada comparison, which is a positive step. However, the proposed additions regarding embedding evaluation on non-EL tasks remain unaddressed in the revision. Given the paper’s technical soundness and potential utility, these revisions are critical for publication. I recommend *minor revision* with the following specific actions:  
- Revise Section 3 and Figure 2 to clarify terminology and eliminate redundancy (e.g., define "dictionaries" explicitly and justify their use).  
- Add a dedicated subsection comparing the method’s contributions to Yamada et al. (2016), highlighting specific innovations.  
- Conduct experiments demonstrating MPME embeddings’ performance on at least one non-EL task involving entity mentions (e.g., entity coreference resolution) and compare against standard embeddings.  

Without these revisions, the paper risks being perceived as a minor extension rather than a significant contribution. With the requested clarifications and expanded evaluation, it would make a valuable addition to the literature.
