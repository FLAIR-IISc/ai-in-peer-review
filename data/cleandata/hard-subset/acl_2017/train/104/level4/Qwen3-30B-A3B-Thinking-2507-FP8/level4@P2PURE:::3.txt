

- Strengths:  
The paper presents good ideas, a simple neural learning approach, and interesting (though not striking) performance across a broad range of applications. The integration of entity, mention, and sense modeling within a unified neural language modeling framework is particularly noteworthy.

- Weaknesses:  
The paper lacks sufficient novelty and suffers from clarity issues in certain sections. While the formal presentation is clear, the discussion does not consistently match the technical depth of the contributions. The empirical evaluation is adequate, but reported performance improvements are not striking. Although the work appears to extend Yamada et al. (CoNLL 2016), it introduces novel elements of relevance.  

The key weaknesses are:  
- Prose clarity: Section 3 is unclear, and Figure 2 lacks sufficient explanation of details. Terminology is redundant (e.g., why distinguish between a "dictionary of mentions" and a "dictionary of entity-mention pairs" when these concepts appear equivalent to text anchors and annotated anchor types?).  
- Insufficient differentiation from Yamada et al. (2016): The paper must clearly outline its technical distinctions from this prior work.  

Additional concern:  
The paper evaluates the multiple embedding (MPME) model solely on entity linking/disambiguation tasks. However, word embeddings are also used in non-entity-dependent semantic tasks (e.g., parsing, coreference, semantic role labeling). The authors should demonstrate that MPME-derived embeddings are not inferior to simpler word spaces in these broader semantic tasks, particularly those involving entity mentions.
