

This paper addresses the problem of disambiguating and linking textual entity mentions to a background knowledge base (English Wikipedia). While the title and introduction slightly overstate the scope (as bridging text and knowledge involves more than the Entity Disambiguation and Linking (EDL) task, though EDL is a core component), the method is sound. It employs an intermediate layer of representation—mention senses—to perform bridging in two steps: (1) mention to mention sense, and (2) mention sense to entity. The approach learns embedding representations for words, mention senses, and entities, jointly training them via a single objective function that equally optimizes all three embedding types.  

Technically, the method is clear, aligns with current deep learning practices, and follows established embedding best practices. While alternative approaches could be proposed, it is unclear they would yield significant improvements. My primary critique concerns the justification for the two-step process (mention sense intermediate layer) over a direct one-step mapping from mentions to entities (as in Yamada et al.’s ALIGN algorithm). Table 2 demonstrates that the two-step MPME (and its simplification SPME) outperform direct methods, but the paper does not explain *why* this is the case. Specifically:  
- What is the exact additional information provided by mention senses compared to entities?  
- How are mention senses defined, differentiated, and constrained (e.g., who determines the number of senses per mention string)?  
- If senses are derived from the knowledge base, does this create a bijective mapping between senses and entities (one sense per entity, even if multiple entities exist)?  
- If sense profiles are built using entity page text as "seed words," what unique information is captured at the mention sense level that is absent at the entity level? Is it solely words reliably associated with the mention sense but *not* appearing in the corresponding entity Wikipedia page?  
- How many such distinct words exist per mention sense on average?  
- How necessary is it to maintain this differentiation in a separate mention sense space, versus incorporating these words directly into the entity space (e.g., by augmenting Wikipedia entity pages)?  

If this interpretation is accurate, Section 5 must be revised to explicitly clarify these points, as this constitutes the paper’s primary novel contribution.  

The claim in Section 6 that "this is the first work to deal with mention ambiguity in text-knowledge integration, so there are no exact baselines" is incorrect. The TAC KBP evaluations for the past two years have included EDL tasks with eight to nine systems, all performing this exact task (though against Freebase, which is larger and noisier than Wikipedia). See http://nlp.cs.rpi.edu/kbp/2016/.  

On a positive note, the smoothing parameter in Section 6.4.2 is well-conceived.  

Post-response: I have reviewed the authors’ response. I remain unconvinced by their dismissal of KBP relevance (citing a focus on embedding "goodness" instead of application). The sole meaningful evaluation of embedding quality is through downstream performance; no one cares about conceptual elegance if the embeddings do not improve results.
