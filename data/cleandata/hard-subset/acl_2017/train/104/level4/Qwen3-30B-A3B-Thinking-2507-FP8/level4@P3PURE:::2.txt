

This paper addresses the problem of disambiguating/linking textual entity mentions to a background knowledge base (English Wikipedia). While the title and introduction slightly overstate the scope (as bridging text and knowledge encompasses more than the Entity Disambiguation and Linking (EDL) task), EDL is indeed a core component. The authors propose a two-step approach: (1) mapping mentions to mention senses, and (2) mapping mention senses to entities. They learn embeddings for words, mention senses, and entities, jointly training them via a single objective function that equally optimizes all three embedding types.

The technical approach is clear and aligns with current best practices in embedding-based deep learning. While numerous alternative architectures could be proposed, it is unclear they would yield significant improvements. My primary concern lies with the fundamental design choice: the paper does not adequately explain why a two-step process via mention senses is superior to a direct one-step mapping from mentions to entities (as in Yamada et al.'s ALIGN algorithm). Table 2 demonstrates that the two-step MPME (and its simplification SPME) outperforms direct methods, but the *reason* for this advantage remains unclear. Specifically:

1.  **What distinguishes mention sense profiles from entity profiles?** The paper states entity profiles are built from neighboring entities in a relatedness graph derived from Wikipedia page word-level relatedness, represented as skip-gram embeddings. Word profiles use standard distributional semantics without sense disambiguation. Mention sense profiles use distributional semantics *with* sense disambiguation, performed using a "language model" built from local context words and neighboring mentions (briefly mentioned before Section 4, but without details). This is a critical omission.
2.  **How are mention senses defined and differentiated?** Who determines the number of senses per mention string? If senses are derived from the knowledge base, this implies a bijective mapping between mention senses and entities (one sense per entity, though multiple entities may exist). If so, are sense profiles built using entity page text as "seed words"? If yes, what *additional* information is captured at the mention sense level that is *not* used at the entity level? Specifically: are sense profiles built using words reliably associated with the mention sense *but absent* from the corresponding entity Wikipedia page? How many such words exist per sense on average? Crucially, how powerful is the *separation* of this information into a dedicated "mention sense space" versus simply incorporating these additional words directly into the entity space (e.g., by augmenting Wikipedia entity pages)?

This clarification is essential, as it constitutes the paper's core novel contribution. Please update Section 5 to explicitly address these points.

The paper's claim in Section 6 that "this is the first work to deal with mention ambiguity in the integration of text and knowledge representations, so there is no exact baselines for comparison" is incorrect. The TAC KBP evaluations for the past two years have featured EDL tasks involving eight to nine systems, all performing this exact task (though against Freebase, which is significantly larger and noisier than Wikipedia). See http://nlp.cs.rpi.edu/kbp/2016/.

On a positive note, I appreciate the innovative smoothing parameter introduced in Section 6.4.2.

Post-response: I have reviewed the authors' response. While they express interest in embedding "goodness," I remain unsatisfied with their dismissal of TAC KBP as irrelevant. The ultimate evaluation of embedding quality *must* be application-driven. No researcher cares about conceptual elegance alone; the critical question is whether the embeddings improve performance on the target task.
