

Summary: The paper proposes a Multi-Prototype Mention Embedding (MPME) model that learns multiple sense embeddings per entity mention by jointly modeling textual context and knowledge base entities. It further introduces a language model-based disambiguation approach. The authors claim state-of-the-art results on entity linking via this framework.

Strengths:  
1. **Novel integration framework**: The MPME model effectively bridges textual context and knowledge base entities within a unified neural language modeling approach. This integration is technically sound and directly addresses the ambiguity challenge in entity mentions, offering a cohesive solution where prior work often treats text and knowledge separately.  
2. **Strong empirical validation**: The evaluation on standard entity linking benchmarks demonstrates consistent improvements over baselines, with both qualitative (e.g., sense-specific embedding examples) and quantitative (e.g., precision/recall gains) evidence supporting the method’s efficacy.  
3. **Broad applicability**: The approach’s simplicity enables straightforward adaptation to diverse NLP tasks beyond entity linking (e.g., relation extraction, knowledge graph completion), as evidenced by the authors’ successful application to linking and their clear discussion of potential extensions.

Weaknesses:  
1. **Insufficient novelty distinction**: The work builds closely on Yamada et al. (2016), which also uses neural language modeling for entity disambiguation. The paper fails to clearly articulate *how* MPME differs from this prior work (e.g., whether the multi-prototype aspect is fundamentally new or merely a minor extension), weakening the contribution claim.  
2. **Ambiguous terminology and exposition**: Section 3 and Figure 2 contain unclear terminology (e.g., "dictionary of mentions" vs. "dictionary of entity-mention pairs" without distinguishing these from standard text anchors/types). This obscures the model’s mechanics, requiring readers to infer relationships not explicitly explained.  
3. **Incomplete evaluation scope**: While entity linking results are strong, the paper omits critical validation on non-entity-dependent tasks (e.g., parsing, coreference resolution, or semantic role labeling) where word embeddings are commonly used. Without showing MPME’s competitiveness in these settings, the broader utility of the embeddings remains unproven.  
4. **Lack of ablation analysis**: The paper does not isolate the contribution of the multi-prototype aspect versus the joint text-knowledge modeling. Ablation studies (e.g., comparing single-prototype vs. multi-prototype embeddings) are essential to confirm that the multi-sense design—not just the framework—is driving the gains.

Questions to Authors:  
How does the MPME framework specifically improve upon Yamada et al. (2016) in terms of architectural innovation or performance? Please clarify the key technical distinctions in the revision.  

Additionally, could the authors provide results demonstrating MPME’s performance on a standard non-entity task (e.g., the Stanford Parser or SRL on CoNLL-2005), to validate that the embeddings are competitive beyond entity-centric applications?
