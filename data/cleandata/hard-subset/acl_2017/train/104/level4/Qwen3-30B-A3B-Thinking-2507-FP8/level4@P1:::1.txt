

**Summary and Contributions**  
This paper proposes a Multi-Prototype Mention Embedding (MPME) model to address entity mention ambiguity by learning multiple sense embeddings per mention through joint modeling of textual contexts and knowledge base entities. The primary contributions are: (1) a novel framework for multi-prototype mention embeddings that explicitly disentangles semantic ambiguity; (2) a language-model-based disambiguation method leveraging these embeddings; and (3) empirical validation on entity linking, achieving state-of-the-art results. The core innovation lies in the multi-prototype embeddings, not the disambiguation approach.

**Strengths**  
1. **Strong empirical results on entity linking**: The model outperforms ALIGN (a strong baseline) by 1.8% in F1 on the AIDA-CoNLL dataset, demonstrating that joint text-knowledge representations with multi-prototype embeddings effectively capture semantic ambiguity. This is the most compelling evidence of the approach’s value.  
2. **Rigorous comparison with a direct baseline**: The comparison with ALIGN uses identical input data (same Wikipedia graph, entity types, and training/test splits), making the performance gap attributable to the proposed framework rather than data differences.  
3. **Insightful analysis of hyperparameters**: The smoothing parameter analysis (e.g., varying α) effectively shows how entity popularity impacts embedding quality—a persistent challenge in entity linking—and provides actionable guidance for practitioners.

**Weaknesses**  
1. **Incomparable baseline comparison**: The comparison with ALIGN is undermined by unreported hyperparameters: ALIGN uses a context window of size 10 vs. MPME’s 5, and vector dimensions of 500 vs. 200. Crucially, the definition of $N(e_j)$ (neighborhood set) is ambiguous—does it include *only* entities linking *to* $e_j$ (as in ALIGN) or all Wikipedia outlinks? Since the graph is directed, adjacency definitions differ, making it unclear whether performance differences stem from the model or implementation choices.  
2. **Terminology inconsistency**: The paper repeatedly uses "mention embedding" generically, but embeddings are learned *per sense*, not per mention string. For example, "mention embeddings for each sense" (Section 3.2) contradicts the title "Multi-Prototype Mention Embedding." This confusion risks misrepresenting the method’s scope.  
3. **Unverified advantage of disambiguation order**: The paper claims the disambiguation method improves results over unsupervised baselines, but no comparison is made to unsupervised entity linking approaches (e.g., graph-based methods like Pershina et al. 2015). Without this, the contribution of the *order* of disambiguation (e.g., context-first vs. entity-first) remains unproven.

**Questions to Authors**  
1. Please clarify the definition of $N(e_j)$ in Equation (3). Specifically:  
   - Does $N(e_j)$ include *only* entities with Wikipedia links *to* $e_j$ (as in ALIGN), or all entities connected via Wikipedia outlinks?  
   - If the latter, how does this affect the directionality of the graph compared to ALIGN’s undirected adjacency?  
   This is critical for reproducibility and interpreting the performance gap.  

2. The term "mention embedding" is used throughout, but embeddings are sense-specific. Please revise terminology to explicitly state that embeddings are learned per *sense* (not per mention string) to avoid confusion.  

**Additional Comments**  
The paper’s focus on multi-prototype embeddings is valuable, but the disambiguation method’s novelty is overstated. The core contribution is the embedding framework, not the disambiguation pipeline. The results on entity linking are compelling, but the baseline comparison must be made rigorous before the contribution can be fully assessed.
