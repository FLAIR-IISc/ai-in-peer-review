

This paper addresses the problem of disambiguating and linking textual entity mentions to a background knowledge base (English Wikipedia). While the title and introduction somewhat overstate the scope—since entity linking (EL) is only one component of the broader text-knowledge integration challenge—the paper makes a valuable contribution to this core task. The method proposes a two-step approach: first mapping mentions to mention senses, then mapping those senses to entities. It learns embeddings for words, mention senses, and entities, jointly optimizing them under a single objective function to maximize all three embedding types equally.  

The technical approach is well-structured and aligns with current deep learning best practices for embeddings. While alternative methods might be proposed, it is unclear whether they would yield significant improvements. My primary concern, however, is the lack of justification for the two-step process over a direct one-step mapping (e.g., Yamada et al.’s ALIGN algorithm). Table 2 demonstrates that the two-step MPME (and its simplified variant SPME) outperforms direct mapping, but the paper does not explain *why*. Specifically:  

What distinguishes mention sense representations from entity representations? The paper implies that mention senses incorporate sense-specific contextual information not present in entity profiles. To clarify:  
- **Entity profiles** are built from neighboring entities in a relatedness graph derived from Wikipedia page text (using word-level relatedness), represented as skip-gram embeddings.  
- **Word profiles** use standard distributional semantics (without sense disambiguation).  
- **Mention sense profiles** use distributional semantics *with* sense disambiguation, leveraging local context words and neighboring mentions (briefly mentioned before Section 4 but without details).  

This is the critical gap. How are mention senses *defined*? Who determines the number of senses per mention string? If senses are derived from the knowledge base, a bijection between senses and entities would arise (one sense per entity, even if multiple entities exist for a string). In that case:  
- Are sense profiles built using entity page text as "seed words"?  
- What *additional* information does the mention sense space provide beyond the entity space? Specifically, do sense profiles include words reliably associated with the sense but *not* appearing in the corresponding entity Wikipedia page?  
- How many such words exist per sense on average? If minimal, is retaining this differentiation in a separate "mention sense" space truly necessary, or could these words simply be added to the entity page embeddings?  

The paper must explicitly clarify this mechanism in Section 5. If the interpretation above is accurate, this distinction constitutes the paper’s key novelty, yet it remains obscured.  

Additionally, Section 6’s claim that "this is the first work to address mention ambiguity in text-knowledge integration, so no exact baselines exist" is inaccurate. The TAC KBP evaluations for 2015–2016 included Entity Disambiguation Linking (EDL) tasks with eight to nine systems, all performing this exact task (albeit on Freebase, which is larger and noisier than Wikipedia). See http://nlp.cs.rpi.edu/kbp/2016/.  

On a positive note, the smoothing parameter in Section 6.4.2 is a compelling and well-motivated contribution.  

**Post-response note:** I have reviewed the authors’ response. While they clarify their focus on embedding quality, I remain unconvinced that the KBP evaluation is irrelevant. Embedding quality must ultimately be validated through downstream performance—no one cares about conceptual elegance if it does not improve task outcomes. The authors should address this by demonstrating how their embeddings enhance EL performance in a practical setting, rather than relying solely on abstract quality metrics.
