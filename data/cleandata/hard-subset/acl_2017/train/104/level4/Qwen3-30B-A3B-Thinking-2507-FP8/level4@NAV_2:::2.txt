

Summary:
This paper tackles entity disambiguation and linking (EDL) by proposing a two-step framework: first mapping textual mentions to sense-specific embeddings (mention senses), then mapping these senses to entities. It introduces a Multi-Prototype Mention Embedding (MPME) model that jointly learns word, mention sense, and entity embeddings through a unified objective function, demonstrating state-of-the-art results on entity linking.

Main Contributions:
1. The empirical demonstration of state-of-the-art entity linking performance using the proposed multi-prototype mention embeddings, representing a significant practical contribution.
2. The implementation of the multi-prototype embedding model, which effectively leverages context and knowledge base information despite limited novelty in the core approach.

Strengths:
1. The method achieves state-of-the-art results on entity linking benchmarks, providing robust empirical validation of the embedding approach's effectiveness.
2. The smoothing parameter design in Section 6.4.2 is a thoughtful technical contribution that enhances model robustness and performance.

Weaknesses:
1. The two-step process (mention → sense → entity) lacks a clear justification for superiority over direct entity linking (e.g., Yamada et al.'s ALIGN algorithm), undermining the paper's core motivation.
2. The methodology for defining and differentiating mention senses is inadequately explained, leaving unclear what unique information the sense layer provides beyond the entity layer (e.g., whether sense-specific context words are absent from entity Wikipedia pages).
3. The claim of "no exact baselines for comparison" is factually incorrect, as TAC KBP entity linking tasks (e.g., 2016 evaluation) provide established baselines with multiple competing systems.

Questions to Authors:
1. How are the number of senses per mention and their definitions determined? Is this process knowledge-base-dependent, and if so, how does it avoid a bijective mapping between senses and entities?
2. What specific context words are used to define mention senses that do not appear in the corresponding entity's Wikipedia page, and how many such words exist on average per sense?
3. How does the intermediate sense layer provide information not already captured in the entity embeddings (e.g., via Wikipedia page content), and why is this information better preserved in a separate sense space?
