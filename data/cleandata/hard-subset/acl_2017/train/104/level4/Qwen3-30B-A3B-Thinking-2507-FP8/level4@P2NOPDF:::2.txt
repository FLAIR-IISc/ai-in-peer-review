

This paper tackles entity disambiguation/linking (EDL) by mapping textual mentions to entities in English Wikipedia. While the title and introduction overstate the scope (EDL is a component of broader text-knowledge integration, not the whole problem), the core task is well-defined. The authors propose a two-step approach: (1) mapping mentions to intermediate "mention senses," then (2) mapping those senses to entities. They learn embeddings for words, mention senses, and entities, jointly optimizing a unified objective to balance all three.  

The technical approach is sound, aligning with current embedding best practices. However, the paper lacks a clear justification for the two-step process over direct mention-to-entity mapping (e.g., Yamada et al.’s ALIGN algorithm). Table 2 demonstrates superior performance for MPME and SPME (the two-step variants), but the *why* remains unclear. The authors must explicitly address:  
- What unique information does the "mention sense" layer provide beyond the entity layer?  
- How are mention senses defined? Specifically:  
  - Do they derive from sense-disambiguated context (e.g., using local words/neighboring mentions), as hinted in Section 4?  
  - If senses are KB-derived, does this create a one-to-one mapping between senses and entities (i.e., one sense per entity)?  
  - If so, what *additional* contextual words are captured at the mention-sense level that *do not appear* in the corresponding Wikipedia entity page? How many such words exist per sense on average?  
  - Crucially: Why is this extra differentiation *necessary* in a separate "mention sense" space rather than being incorporated directly into the entity embeddings (e.g., by augmenting Wikipedia entity pages with these context-specific words)?  

The paper must clarify this in Section 5. Without this, the method’s novelty is obscured. The current description implies mention-sense profiles use "sense-disambiguated" distributional semantics, but the mechanism is under-specified. For instance, if sense definitions are seeded from entity text, the distinction from the entity layer becomes unclear.  

Regarding baselines, Section 6’s claim that "no exact baselines exist" is incorrect. TAC KBP 2015–2016 featured explicit EDL tasks with 8–9 systems (e.g., [1]), though they used Freebase instead of Wikipedia. Freebase is larger and noisier, but the task is identical. The authors should cite these evaluations (http://nlp.cs.rpi.edu/kbp/2016/) and acknowledge them as relevant baselines.  

Positively, the smoothing parameter in Section 6.4.2 is well-motivated and adds value.  

Post-response note: The authors’ rebuttal—that KBP results are irrelevant because they focus on embedding *goodness*—is unpersuasive. Embedding quality *must* be validated through application performance; conceptual elegance alone is insufficient. The KBP results directly measure the task’s success, making them highly relevant.  

**Summary of Required Revisions:**  
1. **Clarify the mention-sense layer’s purpose** (Section 5): Explicitly state what information is captured at this level that isn’t in the entity layer, and justify why it requires a separate space.  
2. **Correct the baseline claim** (Section 6): Acknowledge TAC KBP EDL tasks as relevant baselines.  
3. **Refine the title/introduction** to avoid overstating the contribution.  

The paper’s core contribution (the two-step framework) is promising but lacks sufficient justification for its design. Addressing these points would significantly strengthen the work. The method is technically solid, but the *necessity* of the intermediate layer must be unambiguously demonstrated. Without this, the approach risks appearing as a minor variation rather than a substantive advance.
