

Strengths:  
The paper presents a compelling framework for joint learning of text and knowledge representations, demonstrating superior performance over ALIGN on the supervised entity linking task. This improvement suggests the proposed method effectively enhances the quality of representations by integrating text and knowledge more cohesively. The direct comparison with ALIGN using identical input data is a significant strength, providing a clear baseline for evaluating the approach. Additionally, the analysis of the smoothing parameter offers valuable insights into mitigating the persistent challenge of entity popularity bias in entity linking, which is often overlooked in prior work.

Weaknesses:  
1. **Inconsistent Comparison with ALIGN**: The comparison with ALIGN is undermined by critical methodological differences. ALIGN uses a context window size of 10 and vector dimension 500, while this paper employs a window size of 5 and dimension 200. Crucially, it is unclear whether the neighborhood definition \(N(e_j)\) in the proposed method aligns with ALIGN’s usage. ALIGN defines the context of an entity \(e_j\) as the set of entities linking *to* \(e_j\) (in-neighbors in the Wikipedia link graph), whereas the paper’s graph uses directed outlinks and does not specify if adjacency is treated as undirected. If \(N(e_j)\) instead represents out-neighbors (as implied by the graph’s directed nature), this fundamentally alters the context used for representation learning. Without identical experimental setups, it is impossible to attribute the performance difference solely to the proposed framework, as the parameter choices and neighborhood definitions may confound the results.  

2. **Ambiguous Terminology**: The paper frequently conflates "mention" as a string type (e.g., "Paris") versus a specific mention instance (e.g., "Paris" in a particular document). This is particularly problematic when discussing "mention embeddings," which appear to be learned only for sense-level mentions (e.g., "Paris" as capital of France), not raw strings. Such ambiguity obscures whether the model generalizes to unseen string forms or requires sense disambiguation at inference time. The paper should clarify this distinction throughout and explicitly define "mention" in context.  

3. **Lack of Baseline for Sense Disambiguation Order**: The paper’s approach to sense disambiguation order (e.g., processing mentions sequentially) is not evaluated against unsupervised baselines (e.g., graph-based methods or unsupervised clustering). Without such comparisons, the contribution of the proposed order to performance remains unquantified. The authors should benchmark against at least one unsupervised EL method to isolate the impact of their ordering strategy.  

Recommendations:  
- **Re-run ALIGN with identical parameters** (window size 10, dimension 500) to ensure a fair comparison. Clarify whether \(N(e_j)\) uses in-neighbors (as in ALIGN) or out-neighbors, and justify this choice. If the graph is directed, explicitly state that adjacency is not symmetric.  
- **Revise terminology** to consistently distinguish between mention *types* (string-level) and *instances* (document-specific). Replace "mention embeddings" with "sense embeddings" where appropriate.  
- **Add an ablation study** comparing the proposed sense disambiguation order against a simple baseline (e.g., random order or greedy disambiguation) using an unsupervised EL method. This would clarify the methodological contribution.  

Overall, the paper addresses a meaningful problem with a well-motivated approach. However, the methodological inconsistencies in the comparison with ALIGN and the lack of clarity around key terms weaken the empirical claims. Addressing these issues would significantly strengthen the contribution. The core idea of jointly learning text and knowledge representations is promising and merits publication, provided the technical gaps are resolved. The analysis of the smoothing parameter is particularly noteworthy and should be emphasized in the revision.
