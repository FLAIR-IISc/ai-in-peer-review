

Review  

This paper tackles entity disambiguation/linking (EDL) by mapping textual mentions to English Wikipedia entities via an intermediate mention-sense layer. While the core EDL task is well-motivated, the title and introduction overstate the contribution—EDL is a component of broader text-knowledge integration, not the entirety of it. The method proceeds in two steps: (1) mapping mentions to sense representations, and (2) mapping these senses to entities. It learns embeddings for words, mention senses, and entities, jointly optimizing a single objective to balance all three.  

The technical approach is sound and aligns with current best practices in embedding-based methods. However, the paper lacks a clear justification for the two-step process over a direct one-step mapping (e.g., Yamada et al.’s ALIGN). Table 2 shows the two-step models (MPME, SPME) outperform direct approaches, but the *reason* for this improvement is unexplained. Specifically:  
- How do mention senses encode information *not present* in entity-level representations?  
- What unique contextual or linguistic features are captured at the sense level versus the entity level?  

The paper implies that mention-sense profiles incorporate sense-disambiguated context (via local words and neighboring mentions), while entity profiles derive from Wikipedia page text. Yet it fails to clarify:  
1. How senses are defined (e.g., is the number of senses fixed per mention? Are they KB-driven, creating a bijection between senses and entities?).  
2. Whether sense profiles *exclude* words present in entity pages (e.g., words uniquely associated with a sense but absent from the target entity’s Wikipedia page). If so, how many such words exist on average per sense, and why are they better handled in a separate sense space rather than integrated into entity embeddings (e.g., by augmenting Wikipedia pages)?  

The authors should explicitly address these points in Section 5. The current description suggests sense profiles are built *without* entity page text, but this is ambiguous. Clarifying the *informational difference* between the sense and entity spaces is critical—it is the paper’s core innovation, yet it remains obscured.  

Additionally, the claim in Section 6 that "there are no exact baselines for comparison" is inaccurate. The TAC KBP 2015–2016 evaluations included EDL tasks with multiple systems (e.g., 8–9 teams) using Freebase, a larger and noisier KB than Wikipedia. These provide direct, relevant baselines (see [nlp.cs.rpi.edu/kbp/2016](http://nlp.cs.rpi.edu/kbp/2016/)). The authors’ dismissal of KBP as irrelevant is unwarranted; practical performance on established benchmarks is the standard for EDL evaluation.  

On a positive note, the smoothing parameter in Section 6.4.2 is a thoughtful contribution that merits emphasis.  

**Post-response clarification**: The authors’ rebuttal—that KBP is irrelevant because they prioritize "embedding goodness" over application—misses the point. Embedding quality *must* be evaluated through downstream tasks; conceptual elegance alone is insufficient. The KBP benchmarks directly measure EDL performance, which is the paper’s focus. Without comparable results on such benchmarks, the claimed improvements lack empirical grounding. The authors’ response fails to address the core gap: *why* the two-step approach works better, and *how* it outperforms existing EDL systems.  

**Summary**: The paper presents a technically plausible EDL method but obscures its key innovation (the sense layer’s role) and misrepresents the baseline landscape. To strengthen the contribution:  
1. Explicitly define the informational distinction between sense and entity profiles in Section 5.  
2. Provide a clear, concise justification for the two-step design (e.g., "Senses capture context-specific usage patterns absent from static entity pages").  
3. Compare against TAC KBP results to contextualize performance.  

Without these revisions, the paper’s novelty and significance remain unclear. The method is solid, but the argument for its superiority is incomplete. With targeted clarifications, this could be a valuable contribution to the field.
