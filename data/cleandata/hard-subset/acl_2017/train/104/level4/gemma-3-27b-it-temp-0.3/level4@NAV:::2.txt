This paper tackles the problem of entity disambiguation and linking (EDL), mapping textual mentions to a knowledge base (English Wikipedia). While the title and introduction present a broad scope, the work focuses specifically on EDL, a crucial component of broader text-to-knowledge bridging. The proposed method employs an intermediate representation layer of mention senses, decomposing the task into two stages: (1) mention to mention sense, and (2) mention sense to entity. The authors learn embeddings for words, mention senses, and entities, jointly training them to maximize a unified objective function with equal weighting for all three embedding types.

The technical approach is sound, aligning with current deep learning practices and established embedding techniques. While alternative methods could be considered, their potential impact is unclear. My primary concerns relate to the fundamental rationale for the two-step process utilizing mention senses, rather than a direct mapping from mentions to entities (as seen in the ALIGN algorithm by Yamada et al.). The results in Table 2 indicate that the two-step MPME (and SPME) outperform ALIGN, but the underlying reason for this improvement remains unexplained. Specifically, what additional information do mention senses provide compared to entities? 

My understanding of the profiles used for each component is as follows: entity profiles are based on neighboring entities within a relatedness graph constructed from word-level relatedness of entity definitions (Wikipedia pages) and represented as extended skip-gram embeddings. Word profiles utilize standard distributional semantics without sense disambiguation. Mention sense profiles also employ distributional semantics, but *with* sense disambiguation, leveraging a sense-based profile derived from local context and neighboring mentions (briefly mentioned before Section 4), though details are lacking. This lack of detail regarding sense creation and differentiation is a key weakness. How are senses defined, and how is the number of senses per mention string determined? If the knowledge base dictates this mapping, does this result in a bijective relationship between mention senses and entities? If so, are sense profiles initialized using entity text as seed words?  Ultimately, what information is captured at the mention sense level that isn’t already present at the entity level? Specifically, are there words reliably associated with a mention sense that *do not* appear on the corresponding Wikipedia page? What is the average number of such words, and how significant is this additional differentiation for justifying a separate mention sense space versus simply incorporating these words into the entity profiles?

If my interpretation is correct, Section 5 should be revised to explicitly state this, as it represents the paper’s core contribution. The claim in Section 6 that this is the first work to address mention ambiguity in text-to-knowledge integration is inaccurate. The TAC KBP evaluations over the past two years have featured EDL tasks with eight to nine participating systems, albeit evaluated against Freebase (a larger and noisier knowledge base). See http://nlp.cs.rpi.edu/kbp/2016/ for details.

Positively, I found the smoothing parameter introduced in Section 6.4.2 to be a valuable addition.

**Post-Response:** I remain unconvinced by the authors’ response dismissing the relevance of the KBP evaluation, citing a focus on embedding quality. Embedding quality is ultimately judged by its performance in downstream applications. Conceptual elegance is secondary to demonstrable improvement.
