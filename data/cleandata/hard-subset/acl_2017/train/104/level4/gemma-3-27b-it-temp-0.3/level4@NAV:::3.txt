## Review:

This paper presents a neural learning method for entity disambiguation and linking, and demonstrates several strengths. The core idea of integrating entity, mention, and sense modeling within a unified neural language modeling framework is promising. The simplicity of the training procedure effectively enables support for a wide range of applications. The paper is formally well-structured, and the empirical evaluation is sound, though performance improvements are moderate. While building upon prior work such as Yamada et al. (CoNLL 2016), the contribution introduces novel concepts and remains relevant to the field.

However, the paper also has areas for improvement. The level of detail in the discussion does not consistently match the technical sophistication of the ideas presented. Specifically, Section 3 lacks clarity, and certain details within Figure 2 require further explanation. The terminology employed could be streamlined; the distinction between the “dictionary of mentions” and the “dictionary of entity-mention pairs” is unclear, and their relationship to “text anchors and types for annotated text anchors” should be clarified. 

Furthermore, the work closely resembles Yamada et al. (2016), and a more explicit discussion of the key differences would strengthen the contribution.

A broader consideration regarding the evaluation is that the Multiple Embedding model is assessed solely on entity linking/disambiguation tasks. Given that word embeddings are utilized in a wider array of NLP tasks – including parsing, coreference resolution, and semantic role labeling – that do not directly rely on knowledge base entities, it would be beneficial to demonstrate that the word embeddings generated by the proposed MPME method do not degrade performance on these other semantic tasks involving entity mentions.

I have reviewed the author’s response.
