Strengths: Good ideas, simple neural learning, interesting demonstrations (although not very effective), and finally, a large set of applications.
Weaknesses: Limited amount of new material. Lack of clarity in some sections.
This paper presents a neural learning method for unit ambiguity and linking. It presents a good idea of integrating unit, mention, and meaning modeling within a single neural language modeling technique. The simple training process associated with modeling allows it to support a large set of applications.
The paper is formally clear, but the discussion is not always at the same level as technical ideas.
The empirical evaluation is good, although no significant improvement in performance is reported. Although it appears to be an extension of (Yamada et al., CoNLL 2016), it adds new ideas and is of relevant interest.
The weak points of the paper are:
The prose is not always clear. I found section 3 to be not very clear. Some details in Figure 2 are not clarified, and the terminology is somehow unnecessary: for example, why do you refer to a dictionary of mentions? Or a dictionary of unit-mention pairs? Are these different from the types of text anchors and annotated text anchors?
This paper is quite close in nature to Yamada et al., 2016, and the authors should at least outline the differences.
A general overview of the current version is:
This paper presents a multi-embedding model against unit linking/ambiguity tasks. However, word embedding is used not only to model such tasks, but also for some processes that do not directly depend on the unit of KB, such as parsing, coreference, or semantic role labeling. The authors should show that the word embedding provided by the proposed MPME method is not weaker in relation to simple word locations in other semantic tasks, i.e., those that directly involve unit mentions.
