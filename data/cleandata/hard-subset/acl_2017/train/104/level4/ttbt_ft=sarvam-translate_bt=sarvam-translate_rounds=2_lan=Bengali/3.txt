- Strengths: Good idea, simple neural learning, impressive performance (although not particularly noteworthy), and finally, a wide range of applications.
- Weaknesses: Lack of new content. Some parts lack clarity.

This paper presents a neural learning method for entity disambiguation and connection. It introduces a good idea of combining entity, reference, and comprehension modeling within the same neural language modeling technique. The simple training method associated with the modeling can support a wide range of applications.

The paper is formally clear, but the discussion does not always align with the same level of technical concepts.

The empirical evaluation is good, although there are no significant reports of performance improvements. Although it seems to be an extension of (Yamada et al., CoNLL 2016), it adds new concepts and is a subject of relevant interest.

The weak points of the paper are:

- The prose is not always clear. I did not find section 3 very clear. Some details in Figure 2 are not explained, and the terminology is somewhat redundant: for example, why are you talking about a reference dictionary? Or a reference-entity pair dictionary? Are these different from the types of text anchors and annotated text anchors?
- The paper is very close in nature to Yamada et al., 2016, and the authors should at least highlight the differences.

A general observation in the current version is:

The paper tests multiple embedding models against entity connection/disambiguation tasks. However, word embedding is not only used to model such tasks, but also some processes that are not directly dependent on KB entities, such as parsing, coreference, or semantic role labeling.

The authors should show that word embedding provided by the proposed MPME method is not weaker than simple word space in other semantic tasks, i.e., those that directly involve entity reference.
