- Paper tackles Entity Disambiguation and Linking (EDL) using a two-step approach that maps mentions to intermediate "mention senses" then to KB entities, learning joint embeddings for words, mention senses, and entities.
- Main contributions: a two-step model with an intermediate mention sense representation, and a joint training framework for unified embeddings of words, senses, and entities.
- Strength: technically sound design that follows established embedding training practices.
- Strength: introduction of a smoothing parameter to combine language model and embedding scores.
- Weakness: insufficient motivation/explanation for why the intermediate mention sense layer is necessary or what information it captures beyond entity embeddings.
- Weakness: missing crucial implementation details about how mention senses are identified, enumerated, and differentiated during training, harming reproducibility.
- Weakness: inaccurate novelty claim and missing comparisons to existing EDL baselines (e.g., TAC KBP / Freebase-based systems) that address mention ambiguity.
- Question: confirm profile constructions—entity profiles from neighboring entities, word profiles from corpus context without disambiguation, mention sense profiles from corpus context with disambiguation.
- Question: how is the number of senses per mention determined; are senses derived from candidate KB entities implying one-to-one mapping between sense and entity?
- Question: if senses map one-to-one to entities, what extra information makes the mention sense layer useful—does it capture corpus-only contextual words, and can you quantify (e.g., average number of such words)?
- Question: why model extra information in a separate mention-sense space rather than augmenting entity representations directly?
