This paper presents a method for Entity Disambiguation and Linking, mapping textual mentions to entities within a knowledge base, specifically English Wikipedia. While the title and introduction suggest a broader scope for bridging text and knowledge, the work focuses on the core EDL task. The proposed approach decomposes this process into two stages by introducing an intermediate representation layer: 1) mapping a mention to a "mention sense," and 2) mapping the mention sense to an entity. The methodology involves learning embedding representations for words, mention senses, and entities, which are then jointly optimized via a single, unified objective function.

From a technical standpoint, the methodology is sound, aligning with contemporary deep learning practices and established best practices for generating embeddings. While alternative architectural choices could be proposed, it is not obvious that they would yield substantially different results. My primary concerns, therefore, relate to the fundamental motivation for the proposed architecture. The paper lacks a clear justification for why a two-step process involving mention senses is superior to a direct, one-step mapping from mentions to entities, as employed by the ALIGN algorithm (Yamada et al.).

While the results in Table 2 indicate that the proposed two-step models (MPME and SPME) outperform the direct mapping, the paper does not sufficiently explain the underlying reasons for this improvement. Specifically, what unique and additional information does the mention sense layer contribute compared to the entity layer? To clarify my understanding, I request the authors confirm the following interpretation:
*   **Entity profiles** are embeddings derived from a relatedness graph of neighboring entities, which I assume is constructed based on the content of their Wikipedia pages.
*   **Word profiles** are generated using a standard distributional semantics approach, without sense disambiguation.
*   **Mention sense profiles** also use distributional semantics but incorporate sense disambiguation.

The mechanism for this sense disambiguation, briefly described before Section 4 as using a sense-based profile from the local context, is insufficiently detailed. This lack of clarity is a significant weakness. Crucial questions remain unanswered: How are these mention senses defined and distinguished? What determines the number of possible senses for a given mention string?

If the senses are derived from the knowledge base, it implies a one-to-one mapping between a mention sense and its corresponding entity. If this is the case, are the definitional profiles for these senses initialized using text from the corresponding entity pages as "seeds"? The central question then becomes: what information does the mention sense level capture that is absent from the entity level? Is it merely the set of words from the source corpus that co-occur with a mention but are not present in the corresponding Wikipedia article? An analysis of the magnitude and impact of this differential information would be valuable. The paper should justify why this information warrants a separate representation space rather than being incorporated directly into the entity representations.

If my interpretation is accurate, this conceptual contribution should be explicitly stated and elaborated upon in the paper, as it constitutes the primary novelty of the work.

Furthermore, the claim in Section 6 that “…this is the first work to deal with mention ambiguity in the integration of text and knowledge representations, so there is no exact baselines for comparison” is inaccurate. The Entity Discovery and Linking (EDL) track at the Text Analysis Conference Knowledge Base Population (TAC KBP) evaluations has featured numerous systems performing this exact task for several years. While these systems target Freebase rather than Wikipedia, they represent highly relevant and established baselines that should be acknowledged and discussed (e.g., http://nlp.cs.rpi.edu/kbp/2016/).

On a minor but positive note, the inclusion of the smoothing parameter described in Section 6.4.2 is a thoughtful addition.

**Post-response:** I have reviewed the author response. Their justification for omitting the KBP evaluation—that their focus is on the quality of the embeddings rather than the task itself—is unconvincing. The practical utility of an embedding space can only be demonstrated through its performance on a downstream application. The central question is not the conceptual elegance of the representation, but its empirical effectiveness, which necessitates comparison with task-oriented benchmarks.
