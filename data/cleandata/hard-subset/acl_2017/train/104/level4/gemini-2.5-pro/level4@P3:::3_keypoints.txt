- Paper introduces MPME, a model that learns multiple sense-specific embeddings for ambiguous mention phrases by jointly embedding words, entities, and mention senses using text and a knowledge base.
- Training uses a skip-gram–style objective and a language-model–based disambiguation method to select the appropriate sense in context.
- Evaluated on entity linking and achieves state-of-the-art performance on a benchmark dataset.

Contributions:
- Proposes Multi-Prototype Mention Embeddings (MPME) to model multiple distinct embeddings per mention, integrating textual context and KB information in one objective.
- Presents a language model–based disambiguation technique that scores sense coherence in local context for efficient mention disambiguation.
- Provides strong empirical validation showing SOTA results on an entity linking benchmark.

Strengths:
- Well-motivated, conceptually clean model that directly addresses mention ambiguity and integrates text and KB signals with a simple training procedure.
- Strong quantitative and qualitative results demonstrating meaningful sense-specific embeddings and high entity linking performance.
- Learned multi-prototype embeddings have potential applicability to other NLP tasks (relation extraction, QA, coreference).

Weaknesses:
- Presentation clarity is uneven (Section 3 and Figure 2 are confusing); key terms like “dictionary of mentions” vs “dictionary of entity-mention pairs” are not well defined.
- Insufficient discussion of novelty relative to closely related work (notably Yamada et al. 2016); lacks explicit comparison of technical innovations.
- Evaluation is limited to entity linking, leaving open whether word embeddings suffer degradation on general NLP or intrinsic tasks.

Questions for authors:
- Please clarify terminology in Section 3 and provide a detailed walkthrough of Figure 2 and each component’s role.
- Explicitly detail the main technical and conceptual differences between your approach and Yamada et al. (2016).
- Have you evaluated the quality of the learned word embeddings on standard benchmarks (e.g., word similarity) to ensure they remain semantically rich compared to word2vec/GloVe?
