### Summary and Contributions

This paper addresses the problem of ambiguity in joint semantic representations of text and knowledge bases, where a single mention phrase can refer to multiple distinct entities. The authors propose a "Multi-Prototype Mention Embedding" model that learns multiple sense-specific embeddings for each ambiguous mention. This is achieved by jointly modeling textual contexts (using a word embedding objective) and entity contexts from a knowledge graph. Furthermore, the paper introduces a language model-based method to disambiguate a mention in a given context by selecting the most appropriate sense embedding.

In my view, the main contributions of this work are:

1.  **A Novel Model for Ambiguity in Joint Embeddings:** The primary contribution is the model itself, which extends existing joint text-knowledge embedding frameworks by explicitly learning multiple, distinct vector representations (prototypes) for the different senses of a single mention phrase. This directly tackles a known limitation in prior work.
2.  **State-of-the-Art Empirical Results:** The method is evaluated on a supervised entity linking task, where it achieves state-of-the-art performance, notably outperforming a strong and highly related baseline (ALIGN). This result provides compelling evidence for the effectiveness of the proposed approach.

### Strengths

1.  **Strong Empirical Performance:** The proposed model achieves state-of-the-art results on a standard supervised entity linking benchmark. Outperforming a closely related baseline like ALIGN suggests that the explicit modeling of mention ambiguity through multi-prototype embeddings leads to more effective and robust joint representations of text and knowledge. This is a significant and convincing result.
2.  **Direct Comparison to a Relevant Baseline:** The paper's experimental design includes a direct comparison with ALIGN, a very similar approach that also learns joint embeddings from text and a knowledge graph. By using comparable input data, the authors provide a focused evaluation that strengthens the claim that their specific modeling innovation is responsible for the observed performance gains.
3.  **Insightful Analysis of Popularity Bias:** The authors include an analysis of the smoothing parameter used to mitigate entity popularity bias. This is a valuable contribution, as popularity is a persistent and challenging issue in entity linking. This analysis demonstrates a thoughtful consideration of practical challenges and provides useful insights into the model's behavior.

### Weaknesses

1.  **Potentially Unfair Comparison to the ALIGN Baseline:** The experimental comparison to ALIGN, while a key strength, may be weakened by several confounding factors that are not fully addressed. This makes it difficult to attribute the performance improvement solely to the proposed multi-prototype model. Specifically:
    *   **Mismatched Hyperparameters:** The paper uses a context window of 5 and a vector dimension of 200, whereas the ALIGN model was originally evaluated with a window of 10 and a dimension of 500. These differences in hyperparameters can have a substantial impact on performance.
    *   **Ambiguous Definition of Knowledge Graph Context:** It is unclear how the entity neighborhood, N(e_j), is defined. The paper mentions the graph is directed (Wikipedia outlinks), but does not specify if adjacency is treated as undirected. For comparison, ALIGN defines an entity's context as the set of entities that *link to* that entity (in-links). If the authors' definition of N(e_j) is different (e.g., based on out-links or both), this constitutes a significant difference in the input signals provided to the models, which could contribute to the performance gap.
2.  **Imprecise Terminology:** The paper's use of terminology is sometimes confusing, particularly regarding the term "mention." It is difficult to follow whether "mention" refers to a surface string (e.g., "apple"), or a specific instance of that string in a particular document. The phrase "mention embedding" is used throughout, but the model appears to learn embeddings only for the pre-defined *senses* of a mention string (e.g., 'Apple Inc.', 'apple fruit'), rather than for the mention string itself. This lack of clarity hinders a precise understanding of the model.
3.  **Limited Evaluation of the Disambiguation Component:** The impact and novelty of the language model-based sense disambiguation method are difficult to assess. The primary evaluation is on a supervised entity linking task, which does not isolate the performance of this unsupervised disambiguation step. To properly demonstrate its contribution, the method should be compared against other established unsupervised entity linking methods on a relevant benchmark. Without this, it is unclear how this component stands relative to prior work.

### Questions to Authors

1.  Regarding the comparison with ALIGN, could you clarify the precise definition of the entity neighborhood N(e_j)? Does it consist of entities with in-links to e_j, out-links from e_j, or both? How does this definition compare to that used in the original ALIGN paper?
2.  Could you also comment on the choice of different hyperparameters (context window size, vector dimension) from those used in ALIGN? Have you conducted any experiments with matched hyperparameters to ensure that the observed performance gains are primarily due to the proposed modeling approach rather than the hyperparameter settings?
3.  Could you please clarify your terminology? Does the model learn a single embedding for a mention string (e.g., "Paris") in addition to the multiple embeddings for its senses (e.g., 'Paris, France', 'Paris Hilton')? Or are embeddings only learned for the senses?
