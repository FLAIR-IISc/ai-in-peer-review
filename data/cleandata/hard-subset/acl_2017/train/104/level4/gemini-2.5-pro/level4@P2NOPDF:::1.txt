This paper proposes a framework for jointly learning representations of text and a knowledge graph for the task of entity linking. The model demonstrates promising results, particularly in its supervised setting, but the clarity of the experimental comparison and certain methodological details could be improved.

**Strengths:**

*   The proposed framework demonstrates superior performance compared to the ALIGN model on the supervised entity linking task. This result suggests that the joint learning approach effectively improves the learned representations of text and knowledge.
*   The authors provide a valuable direct comparison against a closely related approach (ALIGN), leveraging highly similar input data. This strengthens the validity of their experimental findings and allows for a more focused analysis of the proposed model's contributions.
*   The paper includes a thoughtful analysis of the smoothing parameter used to mitigate popularity bias. This is a welcome contribution, as popularity bias is a persistent and significant challenge in the field of entity linking.

**Weaknesses:**

*   The experimental comparison with the ALIGN baseline could be strengthened by addressing inconsistencies in key hyperparameters. For instance, this work uses a content window of size 5 and a vector dimension of 200, whereas ALIGN employed a window of size 10 and a dimension of 500. These discrepancies make it difficult to isolate the source of the performance improvement and attribute it solely to the proposed model architecture, as opposed to parameter tuning.
*   There is a lack of clarity regarding the definition of an entity's neighborhood, denoted as `N(e_j)`. The paper states the knowledge graph is directed and based on Wikipedia outlinks, but it is not specified whether adjacency is defined based on these directed outlinks, inlinks, or as an undirected graph. The ALIGN model, for instance, defines an entity's context using its in-links. If the definition of `N(e_j)` in this work differs, it represents a significant methodological divergence that could substantially impact the learned entity representations and their performance on the entity similarity task.
*   The terminology used in the paper could be more precise. Specifically, the term "mention" is used ambiguously, at times referring to a surface string (a type) and at other times to a specific instance of that string in a document (a token). The phrase "mention embedding" is also potentially confusing, as the model appears to learn embeddings for mention *senses* (i.e., candidate entities for a mention) rather than for the mention string itself. Clarifying this distinction would improve the reader's understanding of the model.
*   The paper introduces a novel sense disambiguation ordering strategy. However, its impact is difficult to ascertain, as the experiments lack a direct comparison with other unsupervised entity linking systems that employ different ordering strategies or none at all. Including such a comparison would provide a clearer measure of this component's contribution to overall performance.

**General Discussion:**

Overall, this paper presents a promising direction for entity linking by jointly modeling text and knowledge. The empirical results, which show an improvement over the ALIGN model, are a clear strength. However, the work would be significantly stronger if the authors addressed the weaknesses outlined above. In particular, ensuring a more direct, apples-to-apples comparison with the ALIGN baseline by aligning hyperparameters would provide a much clearer picture of the model's architectural benefits. Furthermore, providing precise definitions for the entity neighborhood `N(e_j)` and clarifying the terminology around "mentions" and "mention embeddings" would greatly improve the paper's clarity and reproducibility. Finally, experimentally justifying the contribution of the proposed disambiguation ordering against other methods would solidify its claimed benefits. While the core ideas are valuable, these clarifications are essential for properly assessing the paper's contributions to the field.
