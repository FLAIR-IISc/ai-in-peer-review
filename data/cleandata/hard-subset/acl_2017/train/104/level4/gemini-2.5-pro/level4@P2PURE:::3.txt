This review has been structured to provide a summary followed by a detailed analysis of the paper's strengths and weaknesses.

### Summary
The paper presents a neural learning method for entity disambiguation and linking. A key strength is the idea of integrating entity, mention, and sense modeling within a single neural language modeling framework. The simplicity of the modeling and its associated training procedure allows the method to support a wide range of applications. The formal presentation is clear, but the accompanying discussion could be improved. The empirical evaluation is solid, though the performance improvements reported are not dramatic. While the work appears to be an extension of Yamada et al. (CoNLL 2016), it introduces novel ideas and is of relevant interest to the community.

### Strengths
*   **Novel Idea:** The core concept of integrating entity, mention, and sense modeling within the same neural framework is a strong contribution.
*   **Simplicity:** The proposed neural learning method is straightforward and elegant.
*   **Versatility:** The model's design and training procedure enable its application to a large set of tasks.
*   **Performance:** The paper reports interesting empirical results.

### Weaknesses
*   **Clarity:** The prose is occasionally unclear, particularly in Section 3, which makes the technical contributions harder to fully appreciate.
*   **Novelty:** The paper's contribution relative to prior work, specifically Yamada et al. (2016), is not clearly articulated.

### Detailed Comments
1.  **Clarity and Terminology:** The clarity of the writing could be improved. Section 3, in particular, was difficult to follow. Furthermore, some details in Figure 2 are not explained. The terminology also seems redundant at times. For instance, what is the distinction between the "dictionary of mentions" and "text anchors," or between the "dictionary of entity-mention pairs" and "types for annotated text anchors"? Clarifying these terms would strengthen the paper.

2.  **Comparison to Prior Work:** The proposed method is very similar in nature to that of Yamada et al. (2016). The authors should explicitly and clearly outline the key differences and novel contributions of their work in comparison.

### General Observation
The paper primarily evaluates the proposed Multiple Embedding (MPME) model on entity linking and disambiguation tasks. However, the resulting word embeddings could potentially be used for a variety of other NLP tasks that do not directly depend on entities from a knowledge base, such as parsing, coreference resolution, or semantic role labeling. It would significantly strengthen the paper if the authors could demonstrate that the specialized word embeddings produced by the MPME method do not degrade performance on these general semantic tasks when compared to standard, more broadly trained word embeddings. This would show that the specialization for entities does not come at the cost of general linguistic utility.
