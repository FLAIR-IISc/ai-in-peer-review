- Proposes a joint embedding framework for words, entities, and mention senses that integrates document text and knowledge-graph structure.
- Evaluated on supervised entity linking and an entity similarity task, showing improved performance over a strong ALIGN baseline.
- Direct, focused comparison to ALIGN is a methodological strength that supports the claimed architectural improvements.
- Analysis of the smoothing parameter λ provides useful insight into mitigating popularity bias in entity linking.
- Experimental comparison clarity is limited by hyperparameter discrepancies (e.g., window size 10 vs 5, vector dim 500 vs 200) that could confound performance differences.
- The definition of an entity's neighborhood N(e_j) is ambiguous (inlinks vs outlinks vs undirected), which can materially affect learned entity representations and similarity results.
- Terminology is imprecise: "mention" is used inconsistently (surface string type vs specific document instance).
- The phrase "mention embedding" is unclear—paper appears to learn embeddings for mention senses (candidate entities), not for mention strings; consistent terms like "mention string," "mention instance," and "mention sense embedding" are needed.
- The paper emphasizes supervised evaluation; its effectiveness in purely unsupervised entity linking is not well assessed and should be compared to established unsupervised methods.
- Overall idea and results are promising but would be strengthened by clarifying experimental details and refining terminology.
