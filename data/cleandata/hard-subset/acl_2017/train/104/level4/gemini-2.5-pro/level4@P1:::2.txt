### Summary of the Paper

This paper tackles the problem of Entity Disambiguation and Linking (EDL), mapping textual mentions to entities in a knowledge base (in this case, English Wikipedia). While the title and introduction suggest a broader scope of integrating text and knowledge, the work's primary focus is on the EDL task. The proposed method introduces an intermediate representational layer of "mention senses" to bridge mentions and entities in a two-step process: (1) mapping a mention to a specific sense, and (2) linking that sense to a knowledge base entity. To achieve this, the authors learn embedding representations for words, mention senses, and entities, which are jointly trained under a unified objective function.

### Contributions

As I see them, the main contributions of this work are:

1.  A novel two-step model for entity linking that introduces an intermediate "mention sense" representation to explicitly handle the ambiguity of textual mentions.
2.  A joint training framework that learns unified embeddings for words from a text corpus, entities from a knowledge base, and the proposed multi-prototype mention senses.

### Strengths

1.  The technical approach is sound and aligns well with current deep learning methodologies and established best practices for training embedding models. The design is clear and well-motivated within this paradigm.
2.  The introduction of a smoothing parameter (Section 6.4.2) for combining the language model and embedding model scores is a thoughtful and interesting contribution.

### Weaknesses

1.  **Lack of Motivation for the Mention Sense Layer:** The central weakness of the paper is the insufficient justification for its core contribution: the two-step process involving an intermediate mention sense layer. While Table 2 shows that this approach (MPME) outperforms a direct one-step mapping (the ALIGN baseline), the paper does not explain *why*. What specific information is captured by the mention sense embeddings that cannot be captured by entity embeddings alone? The paper needs to provide a clearer rationale for why this additional layer of complexity is necessary and effective.

2.  **Unclear Implementation of Mention Senses:** Crucial details about the creation and representation of mention senses are missing. The process for sense disambiguation, which uses a language model based on local context, is mentioned only briefly before Section 4. This leaves several key questions unanswered:
    *   How are the senses for a given mention string initially identified and enumerated?
    *   How are these senses differentiated from one another during training?
    This lack of detail makes it difficult to fully understand the model's mechanics and hinders the work's reproducibility.

3.  **Inaccurate Claims of Novelty and Missing Baselines:** The paper claims in Section 6 that it is "...the first work to deal with mention ambiguity in the integration of text and knowledge representations, so there is no exact baselines for comparison." This claim is incorrect. The EDL task has been a central focus of shared task evaluations like the TAC Knowledge Base Population (KBP) track for several years, with numerous systems addressing this exact problem. While these systems may use a different knowledge base (e.g., Freebase), they represent highly relevant prior art and should be acknowledged and discussed. The omission of this context weakens the paper's positioning within the field.

### Questions to Authors

To clarify the core mechanism of your model, could you please address the following points?

1.  Could you confirm if my understanding of how the different profiles are constructed is correct?
    *   **Entity profiles:** Based on neighboring entities in a relatedness graph derived from Wikipedia.
    *   **Word profiles:** Based on standard distributional context from a text corpus, without sense disambiguation.
    *   **Mention sense profiles:** Based on distributional context from a text corpus, but with sense disambiguation applied.

2.  Regarding the creation of mention senses: How is the number of senses for each mention string determined? If the senses are derived directly from the set of possible entities in the knowledge base for a given mention, this would imply a one-to-one mapping between a mention sense and a KB entity. Is this the case?

3.  If there is indeed a one-to-one mapping between senses and entities, what is the source of the additional information that makes the mention sense layer beneficial?
    *   Is the sense embedding primarily capturing contextual words from the training corpus that are associated with the mention but are *not* present in the corresponding entity's Wikipedia page?
    *   If so, could you provide some analysis to quantify this? For example, what is the average number of such "corpus-only" words associated with a mention sense?
    *   Why is it better to model this information in a separate mention sense space rather than simply augmenting the entity representations with these additional words directly?

Clarifying these points, particularly the motivation for the separate mention sense layer, is critical. If my understanding is correct, this mechanism appears to be the main novelty of the paper, and it should be explained more explicitly in the text.
