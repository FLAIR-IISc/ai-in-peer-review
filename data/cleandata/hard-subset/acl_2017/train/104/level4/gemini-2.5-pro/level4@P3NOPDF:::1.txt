This paper proposes a framework for jointly learning embeddings for words, entities, and mention senses in a shared vector space. The primary contribution is a model that integrates textual context from documents with structural information from a knowledge graph to create unified representations. The authors evaluate their model on supervised entity linking and an entity similarity task, demonstrating improved performance over a strong, related baseline. While the core idea is compelling and the results are promising, the paper could be strengthened by clarifying key aspects of the experimental setup and refining its terminology.

**Strengths**

*   **Strong Empirical Performance:** A key strength of this work is its performance on the supervised entity linking task, where it outperforms the closely related ALIGN model. The direct comparison using very similar input data provides compelling evidence that the proposed joint learning framework successfully improves the representations of text and knowledge.
*   **Rigorous Comparison:** The decision to compare directly and extensively with ALIGN is commendable, as it allows for a focused evaluation of the proposed model's architectural contributions against a highly relevant baseline.
*   **Thoughtful Analysis of Popularity Bias:** The analysis of the smoothing parameter λ is a valuable contribution. By explicitly modeling and analyzing its effect, the authors thoughtfully address the persistent issue of popularity bias in entity linking, which is a significant practical challenge. This analysis provides useful insights for practitioners.

**Weaknesses and Questions for the Authors**

The primary weaknesses of the paper relate to the clarity of the experimental comparison and certain terminological ambiguities, which, if addressed, would significantly strengthen the paper's claims.

*   **Clarity of Experimental Comparison with ALIGN:** While the comparison to ALIGN is a strength, several details obscure a direct, apples-to-apples evaluation, making it difficult to isolate the precise source of the performance gains.
    *   **Hyperparameter Discrepancies:** The baseline model (ALIGN) and the proposed model are evaluated with different hyperparameters. For instance, ALIGN used a content window size of 10 and a vector dimension of 500, whereas this paper uses a window size of 5 and a dimension of 200. Could the authors comment on the potential impact of these differences or, ideally, report results with aligned hyperparameters to ensure the comparison is fair?
    *   **Graph Context Definition:** There is an ambiguity in the definition of an entity's neighborhood, N(e_j). The paper states the graph is constructed from Wikipedia outlinks (which are directed), but it is unclear if adjacency is treated symmetrically, as it would be for an undirected graph. For context, ALIGN defines an entity's neighborhood as the set of entities that *link to* it. If the definition of N(e_j) used here differs (e.g., by including out-links or treating edges as undirected), this could significantly impact the learned entity representations and contribute to the observed performance differences on the entity similarity task. Clarification on this point is essential for interpreting the results.

*   **Terminological Precision:** The manuscript would benefit from more precise and consistent terminology, which would improve overall readability and clarity.
    *   The term "mention" is used ambiguously. At times it seems to refer to a surface string type (e.g., "Washington"), while at other times it refers to a specific instance of that string in a document.
    *   Similarly, the paper uses the phrase "mention embedding," but the model description suggests that embeddings are learned for "mention senses" (i.e., candidate entities for a mention string), not for the mention string itself. Consistently using more precise terms like "mention string," "mention instance," and "mention sense embedding" would greatly clarify the exposition.

*   **Contextualizing Unsupervised Performance:** The evaluation focuses heavily on the supervised setting. While the model includes mechanisms relevant to unsupervised linking, such as the sense disambiguation order, its effectiveness in a purely unsupervised scenario is difficult to assess from the current experiments. Including a comparison against other established unsupervised entity linking methods would provide a more complete picture of the model's capabilities and better situate its contribution within the broader literature.
