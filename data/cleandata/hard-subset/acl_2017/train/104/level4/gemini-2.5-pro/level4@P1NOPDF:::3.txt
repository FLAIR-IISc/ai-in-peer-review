This paper presents a simple yet effective neural learning method for entity disambiguation and linking. Its primary strengths are its novel integration of entity, mention, and sense modeling within a single framework and a simple training procedure that supports a wide range of applications. The empirical results are promising, though not groundbreaking. The main weaknesses are a lack of clarity in some sections and concerns about the degree of novelty over prior work.

The paper introduces an interesting approach that integrates entity, mention, and sense modeling within a unified neural language modeling framework. The simplicity of the training procedure is a key advantage, enabling its application to a broad set of tasks. While the formal presentation is clear, the surrounding discussion does not always match the quality of the technical ideas. The empirical evaluation is solid, but the performance improvements reported are not striking. Despite its similarity to Yamada et al. (2016), the paper introduces novel contributions of relevant interest to the community.

The weaker points of the paper are as follows:

- The prose is occasionally unclear, with Section 3 being a particular example. Some details in Figure 2 are not adequately explained, and the terminology appears redundant at times. For instance, the authors should clarify the distinction between the "dictionary of mentions," the "dictionary of entity-mention pairs," and "text anchors," and explain how these relate to the "types" for annotated text anchors.

- The proposed method is very similar in nature to the work of Yamada et al. (2016). The authors should more explicitly outline the key differences and novel contributions of their work in relation to this prior publication.

One general observation on the current version:
The paper evaluates the proposed embedding model on entity linking and disambiguation tasks. However, word embeddings are also critical for a variety of other NLP tasks that do not directly depend on knowledge base entities, such as parsing, coreference resolution, and semantic role labeling. To demonstrate the robustness of their method, the authors should include an evaluation showing that the word embeddings produced by their model are not degraded for these general semantic tasks when compared to embeddings generated by standard models.
