**Summary and Contributions**

This paper addresses the challenge of mention ambiguity in models that jointly embed text and knowledge graphs. The authors propose a Multi-Prototype Mention Embedding model to learn multiple, distinct vector representations (senses) for each potentially ambiguous mention. The learning process is unified, modeling both textual contexts and entities from a knowledge base. To apply these learned embeddings, the paper also introduces a language model-based method to disambiguate a mention in a given context to its most appropriate sense. The effectiveness of the model is demonstrated through strong performance on the entity linking task.

The primary contributions of this work are:

1.  A novel model for learning multi-sense embeddings for entity mentions by jointly leveraging information from both a text corpus and a knowledge graph. This approach directly tackles the problem of polysemy for mentions in the shared semantic space.
2.  An efficient language model-based disambiguation method that utilizes the learned multi-prototype embeddings to select the correct entity sense for a given mention context.
3.  Strong empirical results on a benchmark entity linking dataset, where the proposed method achieves state-of-the-art performance and outperforms a highly relevant baseline, thereby validating the quality of the learned representations.

**Strengths**

1.  The proposed model achieves state-of-the-art performance on a standard supervised entity linking benchmark, notably outperforming the strong ALIGN baseline. This result provides compelling evidence that the multi-prototype approach effectively improves the quality of jointly learned representations for text and knowledge base entities.
2.  The paper provides a direct and valuable comparison against ALIGN, a highly relevant prior work. By using a similar experimental setup, the authors make a convincing case for the specific advantages of their proposed multi-prototype mention embedding technique over existing joint embedding methods.
3.  The inclusion of an analysis of the smoothing parameter is a notable strength. This analysis offers valuable insights into how the model can mitigate entity popularity bias, a well-known and persistent challenge in entity linking. This demonstrates a thoughtful consideration of practical issues beyond raw performance metrics.

**Weaknesses**

1.  The experimental comparison with the ALIGN model could be more rigorous, as several differences in the setup may confound the results. Specifically, this work uses a context window size of 5 and a vector dimension of 200, whereas ALIGN reportedly used a window of 10 and dimensions of 500. Furthermore, there is ambiguity in the definition of an entity's neighborhood, N(ej). It is unclear if this paper uses the same definition as ALIGN (incoming links) or a different one (e.g., based on undirected adjacency). These discrepancies make it difficult to isolate the impact of the proposed model versus the impact of these differing experimental choices.
2.  The paper's terminology regarding "mentions" is at times ambiguous. It is not always clear whether "mention" refers to a surface string type or a specific instance of that string in a document. The term "mention embedding" is used throughout, but the model appears to learn embeddings for *mention senses* rather than for the ambiguous mention string itself. Clarifying this distinction would improve the paper's precision and readability.
3.  The evaluation focuses exclusively on a supervised entity linking setting. While the results are strong, the paper would benefit from a more comprehensive evaluation of the disambiguation component. Without comparison to other unsupervised entity linking methods, it is difficult to assess the standalone contribution and robustness of the proposed language model-based sense disambiguation strategy.

**Questions to Authors**

1.  Could you clarify the differences in hyperparameters (e.g., context window size, vector dimension) and the precise definition of an entity's neighborhood N(e_j) between your experimental setup and that of the ALIGN model? Can you comment on how these differences might affect the performance comparison?
2.  Could you please clarify the terminology used for "mentions"? Does the model learn an embedding for an ambiguous mention string directly, or does it only learn embeddings for the different senses associated with that string?
3.  Have you considered evaluating your disambiguation method in an unsupervised setting or comparing it against other unsupervised entity linking baselines? Such an experiment would help to better isolate and understand the performance of the sense selection mechanism.
