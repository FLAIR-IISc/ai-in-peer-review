This paper addresses the problem of disambiguating and linking textual entity mentions to a given knowledge base (specifically, English Wikipedia). The title and introduction slightly overstate the paper's scope, as the broader task of bridging text and knowledge encompasses more than just Entity Discovery and Linking (EDL), but EDL is undeniably a core component. The proposed method performs this linking via an intermediate layer of "mention senses," following a two-step process: (1) mapping a mention to a mention sense, and (2) mapping the mention sense to an entity. The model learns embedding representations for words, mention senses, and entities, which are jointly trained to optimize a single objective function that integrates all three embedding types.

The technical approach is sound and aligns with current deep learning methodologies and best practices for embeddings. While alternative architectural choices are possible, it is not immediately obvious that they would yield substantially different results. My primary concern, however, lies with the core motivation for the two-step process. The paper does not adequately explain why this intermediate layer of mention senses is superior to a direct one-step mapping from textual mentions to entities, such as the ALIGN algorithm from Yamada et al. While Table 2 demonstrates that the proposed two-step models (MPME and its simplification SPME) outperform this direct approach, the underlying reasons for this improvement are not elucidated. Specifically, what distinct information do the mention sense representations capture that is not already present in the entity representations?

To better understand this, I have tried to reconstruct the process, and I ask the authors to clarify if the following interpretation is correct.
*   **Entity representations** are learned from their neighboring entities in a relatedness graph, which is presumably constructed based on the textual content of their Wikipedia pages.
*   **Word representations** are learned using a standard distributional semantics approach on a large corpus, without sense disambiguation.
*   **Mention sense representations** are also learned distributionally, but critically, *with* sense disambiguation applied.

However, the details of this sense disambiguation process—mentioned briefly before Section 4 as using a sense-based profile from local context words and neighboring mentions—are a critical but underspecified part of the paper. This raises several questions:
*   How are senses defined and enumerated for a given mention string?
*   If the set of senses is derived from the knowledge base, does this result in a bijective mapping between mention senses and entities?
*   If so, what information is leveraged at the mention sense level that is not already available at the entity level? Is the key difference simply the contextual words from a large text corpus that are associated with a mention sense but do not appear on the corresponding entity's Wikipedia page?
*   A quantitative analysis would be helpful: on average, how large and informative is this set of non-KB words? This is crucial for understanding the necessity of a separate sense space. Could this additional textual evidence not be integrated directly into the entity representations by augmenting the content of the entity pages?

If my understanding is correct, this distinction is the core contribution of the paper. I strongly urge the authors to clarify these points in the final version, as this would significantly improve the paper's clarity and impact.

Furthermore, the claim in Section 6 that "...this is the first work to deal with mention ambiguity in the integration of text and knowledge representations, so there is no exact baselines for comparison" is incorrect. The TAC KBP evaluations have featured an Entity Discovery and Linking (EDL) track for several years (e.g., http://nlp.cs.rpi.edu/kbp/2016/), with numerous participating systems performing this exact task, albeit against Freebase, which is a larger and noisier knowledge base than Wikipedia.

On a positive note, the introduction of the smoothing parameter in Section 6.4.2 is a thoughtful and interesting contribution.

**Post-Rebuttal Comments:**
Having read the author response, I remain unconvinced by their argument regarding the relevance of the KBP evaluations. The authors' claim that their focus is on the intrinsic "goodness" of the embeddings, rather than task performance, misses a crucial point. The quality of representation models like these can only be meaningfully evaluated extrinsically, through their performance on a downstream task. Conceptual elegance is secondary to empirical results; the fundamental question is whether the proposed representations lead to better performance on the task they are designed for.
