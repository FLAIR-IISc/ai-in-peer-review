This paper presents a method for Entity Disambiguation and Linking (EDL), the task of mapping textual entity mentions to entities in a knowledge base (here, English Wikipedia). While the paper's framing suggests a broad solution for bridging text and knowledge, its core contribution is focused on the specific, albeit crucial, task of EDL. The proposed approach introduces an intermediate representation layer of "mention senses" to create a two-step linking process: (1) mapping a textual mention to a mention sense, and (2) mapping the mention sense to a knowledge base entity. The model learns embeddings for words, mention senses, and entities, which are trained jointly through a unified objective function.

The technical approach is sound and aligns well with current deep learning methodologies for representation learning. The use of joint training for the different embedding spaces is a well-motivated choice, and the overall method is presented with reasonable clarity. The introduction of a smoothing parameter in Section 6.4.2 to balance global and local information is also a thoughtful and interesting contribution.

However, the main weakness of the paper is the lack of a clear justification for its core architectural choice: the two-step mapping via an intermediate "mention sense" layer. While the experiments in Table 2 show that the proposed two-step models (MPME and SPME) outperform the direct one-step mapping of Yamada et al. (the ALIGN algorithm), the paper does not provide a clear intuition or analysis as to *why* this is the case.

The key question is: what distinct and necessary information is captured by the mention sense embeddings that is not already present in the word or entity embeddings? To clarify this, the authors should elaborate on the following points, which are currently underspecified:

1.  **Creation and Nature of Mention Senses:** The process for defining and differentiating mention senses is not detailed. The paper briefly mentions a sense-based profile using local context words, but the mechanism for its creation is omitted. How are the senses for a given mention string enumerated? Is the set of possible senses derived from the target knowledge base? If so, this suggests a bijective mapping between the senses of a mention and the candidate entities it can link to.

2.  **Differential Information Content:** If there is a direct correspondence between mention senses and entities, what is the source of the additional information that makes the separate sense embeddings beneficial? My current interpretation is as follows:
    *   **Entity Embeddings** are derived from the structured information and text of Wikipedia pages (i.e., from the knowledge base itself).
    *   **Word Embeddings** are derived via standard distributional semantics from a large corpus, without sense disambiguation.
    *   **Mention Sense Embeddings** are also derived from distributional semantics, but critically, *with* sense disambiguation applied to the contexts in which the mention appears.

    If this understanding is correct, the key contribution is the model's ability to separate two distinct sources of information: knowledge base content (in entity embeddings) and corpus-derived contextual information (in mention sense embeddings). The paper should state this explicitly. More importantly, it should analyze the value of this separation. What kinds of contextual words associated with a mention sense are *not* found in the corresponding entity's Wikipedia page? An analysis of the average size and nature of this differential information would significantly strengthen the paper and justify the model's complexity. If this is indeed the central new idea, it should be clearly articulated as the paper's main conceptual contribution.

Another significant issue is the claim in Section 6 that "...this is the first work to deal with mention ambiguity in the integration of text and knowledge representations, so there is no exact baselines for comparison." This claim is inaccurate. The task of Entity Linking, which inherently deals with mention ambiguity, has been a central focus of shared tasks for many years, most notably the TAC KBP evaluations. These evaluations involve numerous systems performing precisely this task, albeit often with different and more challenging knowledge bases like Freebase. The authors should revise this claim and situate their work within this broader context of existing EDL research.

**Post-Rebuttal Comments:**
Having reviewed the author response, I remain unconvinced by the argument that the work should be evaluated on the "goodness of the embeddings" rather than on end-task performance. In the context of tasks like EDL, the practical utility of an embedding is its primary measure of quality. An embedding is "good" precisely because it enables better performance on a downstream application. It is difficult to evaluate the conceptual elegance of a representation in isolation. The performance on the EDL task *is* the evaluation of the embedding quality, and dismissing benchmark evaluations like KBP on this basis seems misguided.
