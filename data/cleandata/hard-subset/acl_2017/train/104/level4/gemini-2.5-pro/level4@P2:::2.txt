### Summary of the Paper

This paper addresses the problem of entity linking, where ambiguous textual mentions are disambiguated and linked to their corresponding entities in a knowledge base (in this case, Wikipedia). The authors propose a novel "Multi-Prototype Mention Embedding" (MPME) model that learns joint embeddings for words, entities, and mention "senses" in a unified vector space. The core idea is to introduce an intermediate layer of mention senses to explicitly model the ambiguity of a given mention string. This transforms the direct "mention-to-entity" linking task into a two-step process: (1) mapping a contextualized mention to one of its learned senses, and (2) mapping that sense to a specific knowledge base entity. The embeddings for all three components (words, senses, entities) are trained jointly by optimizing a single objective function. The authors demonstrate the effectiveness of their learned embeddings and disambiguation method on a benchmark entity linking dataset, where they achieve state-of-the-art results.

### Contributions

1.  **A Novel Multi-Prototype Framework for Entity Linking:** The primary contribution is a new model that explicitly represents mention ambiguity by learning multiple "sense" embeddings for each mention string. This two-step approach (mention -> sense -> entity) is a departure from direct mention-to-entity mapping methods and is shown to be effective at resolving ambiguity.
2.  **State-of-the-Art Performance on a Benchmark Task:** The proposed MPME model and its associated disambiguation method achieve new state-of-the-art results on a standard entity linking dataset. This provides strong empirical evidence for the utility of the multi-prototype approach.

### Strengths

1.  **Strong Empirical Results:** The paper's most compelling argument is its performance. The MPME model convincingly outperforms previous methods, including the strong ALIGN baseline (Yamada et al., 2016), on the entity linking task (Table 2). This demonstrates the practical value of the proposed method.
2.  **Sound Technical Approach:** The embedding model is technically sound and aligns well with current deep learning practices for representation learning. The joint training objective that integrates textual context (for words and mentions) and structural knowledge (for entities) is well-motivated and clearly formulated.
3.  **Novelty of the Intermediate Sense Layer:** The idea of introducing an explicit, learnable layer of "mention senses" to mediate between ambiguous surface forms in text and unique entities in a knowledge base is an interesting and intuitive way to model ambiguity. The qualitative analysis provides some evidence that the model successfully learns distinct embeddings for different senses of the same mention.

### Weaknesses

1.  **Insufficient Justification for the Mention Sense Layer:** The paper's central architectural choice is the introduction of the mention sense layer. While results show this two-step process works better than a direct mapping, the paper does not provide a clear conceptual explanation for *why*. What specific information is captured by the mention sense embeddings that cannot be integrated into the entity embeddings themselves? If the senses are derived from, and map directly to, the candidate entities in the knowledge base, this intermediate layer risks being redundant. The paper needs to better motivate this core design choice beyond just showing that it improves final numbers.
2.  **Lack of Clarity on Key Methodological Details:** The process for creating and differentiating mention senses is not sufficiently detailed. For instance, how is the number of senses for a given mention determined? More critically, the paper mentions a "language model based approach" to disambiguate a mention to a specific sense, but provides no details on how this model works. This omission makes a key component of the methodology opaque and difficult to reproduce.
3.  **Incorrect Claim Regarding Baselines and Related Work:** The paper claims in Section 6 that "this is the first work to deal with mention ambiguity in the integration of text and knowledge representations, so there is no exact baselines for comparison." This statement is inaccurate. The Entity Discovery and Linking (EDL) task, featured prominently in the Text Analysis Conference (TAC) Knowledge Base Population (KBP) evaluations for several years, is precisely this task. These evaluations involve numerous systems performing mention-to-entity linking against a large, noisy knowledge base (Freebase). Overlooking this significant body of work and established community benchmark weakens the paper's positioning. The quality of embeddings is best evaluated through performance on a downstream task, making these EDL systems highly relevant for comparison.

### Questions to Authors

1.  Could you please clarify the fundamental distinction between a "mention sense" and an "entity" in your model? If there is a one-to-one mapping between the possible senses of a mention and its candidate KB entities, what specific information is learned in the sense embedding that is not already present in the entity embedding? Why is it beneficial to maintain this as a separate representation layer rather than simply augmenting the entity representations with this additional textual information?
2.  Could you please provide more details on the "language model based approach" used for sense disambiguation, which is mentioned just before Section 4? Specifically, how are the senses for a mention initially identified and enumerated, and what is the architecture of the model that selects the appropriate sense for a mention in a given context?
3.  The ultimate measure of an embedding's quality for a specific task is its performance in an end-to-end evaluation. Given this, could you please elaborate on why systems from the TAC KBP EDL shared task were not considered relevant baselines or related work, as they address the exact same problem of linking ambiguous mentions to a knowledge base?
