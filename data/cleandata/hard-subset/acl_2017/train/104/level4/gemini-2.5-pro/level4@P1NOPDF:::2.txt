This paper addresses the problem of linking textual entity mentions to a knowledge base (in this case, English Wikipedia). While the title and introduction frame the work broadly as 'bridging text and knowledge,' the specific task is Entity Linking, a core component of this larger challenge. The proposed method introduces an intermediate layer of 'mention senses,' decomposing the linking task into two steps: 1) mapping a mention to a mention sense, and 2) mapping the mention sense to an entity. The model learns embedding representations for words, mention senses, and entities, which are jointly optimized within a single objective function.

The technical approach is sound and aligns with current deep learning practices for learning embeddings. While minor variations could be explored, my main concerns relate to the core motivation of the model. The paper does not adequately explain why a two-step process using an intermediate mention sense layer should be superior to a direct, one-step mapping from mentions to entities (as in the ALIGN baseline). The results in Table 2 clearly show that the two-step models (MPME and SPME) perform better, but the paper offers little intuition as to why this is the case. What additional information does the mention sense layer provide that is not already present in the entity representations?

To clarify this crucial point, I have tried to reconstruct the process, and I ask the authors to confirm my understanding and elaborate on these details in the paper. My understanding is as follows:
*   **Entity profiles** are embeddings derived from a relatedness graph of co-occurring entities in Wikipedia.
*   **Word profiles** are standard distributional embeddings, without sense disambiguation.
*   **Mention sense profiles** are also distributional embeddings, but they are sense-disambiguated.

The critical component—and a weakness in the paper's current description—is the creation of these mention senses. The process is mentioned only briefly before Section 4. How are these senses defined and differentiated? What determines the number of senses for a given mention string? If the senses are derived from the knowledge base (i.e., the set of possible entities a mention can link to), this would seem to create a bijective mapping between mention senses and entities. If that is the case, what is the source of the information that makes the mention sense profiles different from the entity profiles? Is it that the sense profiles incorporate words from the training corpus that are associated with a mention but do not appear in the corresponding Wikipedia page for the entity? If so, it would be helpful to quantify this difference. On average, how much new information (i.e., how many words) does a sense profile contain compared to its corresponding entity profile?

In essence, the key question is: why is it necessary to model this supplementary textual information in a separate mention sense space? What is the advantage of this approach over simply augmenting the entity representations with this additional textual evidence?

If my understanding is correct, this distinction is the core contribution of the paper. I strongly recommend that the authors revise the paper to make this motivation and the underlying mechanism explicit, as it is currently difficult for the reader to grasp the model's primary innovation.

The claim in Section 6 that "...this is the first work to deal with mention ambiguity in the integration of text and knowledge representations, so there is no exact baselines for comparison" is inaccurate. The TAC KBP evaluations have hosted Entity Discovery and Linking (EDL) tracks for several years (e.g., 2015, 2016), featuring numerous systems that perform this exact task. While these evaluations use Freebase rather than Wikipedia, they are directly relevant prior work and constitute a clear set of established baselines in the field. See, for example, the 2016 overview: http://nlp.cs.rpi.edu/kbp/2016/.

On a positive note, the introduction of the smoothing parameter in Section 6.4.2 is a thoughtful and interesting contribution.

Post-response: I have read the authors' response. I am not convinced by their argument that the KBP evaluation is irrelevant because their primary interest is in the 'goodness' of the embeddings themselves. The quality of a representation like an embedding can only be meaningfully evaluated extrinsically, through its performance on a downstream task. The ultimate measure of an embedding's value is its ability to improve performance on an application, not its conceptual elegance.
