**Strengths:**
The paper is built on several strong concepts, proposing an elegant and straightforward neural training procedure. The model achieves promising, albeit not groundbreaking, performance and, importantly, its design supports a wide range of applications.

**Weaknesses:**
The primary concerns relate to the degree of novelty and the clarity of the exposition in certain sections.

This work introduces a neural approach for entity disambiguation and linking. A central contribution is the novel integration of entity, mention, and sense representations within a single, unified neural language modeling framework. This unified modeling, combined with a simple training methodology, enables the method to be applied across a diverse set of applications.

While the formal aspects of the paper are presented clearly, the accompanying discussion sometimes lacks the same level of precision as the technical contributions. The experimental results are solid; however, they do not demonstrate substantial performance gains over existing methods. Despite its resemblance to an extension of Yamada et al. (2016), the paper introduces valuable new concepts that are of significant interest to the community.

The weaker points of the paper are:
- The clarity of the writing could be improved, with Section 3 being particularly difficult to follow. Several components of Figure 2 are not adequately explained in the text. Furthermore, the terminology used can be redundant. For instance, the distinction between a "dictionary of mentions" or a "dictionary of entity-mention pairs" and concepts like "text anchors" and their "types" is unclear.
- The proposed work is very similar in spirit to Yamada et al. (2016). The authors must more explicitly delineate the key differences and novel contributions of their method compared to this prior work.

One general observation on the current version is:
The proposed Multiple Embedding model is evaluated exclusively on entity linking and disambiguation tasks. However, the utility of word embeddings extends to numerous other NLP tasks that are not directly dependent on a knowledge base, such as parsing, coreference resolution, and semantic role labeling. The authors should demonstrate that the embeddings produced by their MPME method do not degrade performance on these other semantic tasks when compared to standard word spaces. This would confirm that the specialization for entity-related tasks does not come at the cost of general linguistic representation.
