This paper presents a neural method for entity disambiguation and linking, introducing the compelling idea of integrating entity, mention, and sense modeling within a single, unified neural language modeling framework. The simplicity of the training procedure and modeling allows the method to support a large set of applications. The paper is formally clear, and the empirical evaluation is solid, demonstrating interesting, albeit not striking, performance improvements. Although it appears to be an extension of Yamada et al. (2016), it introduces novel ideas of relevant interest to the community.

The main weaknesses of the paper relate to the clarity of the presentation and the discussion of its novelty with respect to prior work.

Specific points for improvement are listed below:

*   **Clarity and Prose:** The prose is not always clear, which sometimes makes the technical contributions difficult to follow. Section 3, in particular, would benefit from significant revision for clarity. Additionally, some details in Figure 2 are not adequately explained in the text. The terminology also seems redundant in places. For example, the authors should clarify the distinction between the "dictionary of mentions" or the "dictionary of entity-mention pairs" and concepts like "text anchors" or "types for annotated text anchors."

*   **Comparison to Prior Work:** The proposed method is very similar in nature to Yamada et al. (2016). The authors should include a section that explicitly and clearly delineates the differences and novel contributions of their work compared to this prior approach.

*   **Evaluation of Word Embeddings:** The paper tests the proposed Multiple Embedding (MPME) model on entity linking and disambiguation tasks. However, word embeddings are also fundamental components for many other NLP tasks that do not directly depend on knowledge base entities, such as parsing, coreference resolution, and semantic role labeling. A significant question is whether the specialized word embeddings produced by MPME degrade performance on these other semantic tasks compared to standard word embeddings. The authors should demonstrate that their proposed embeddings are not weaker in these general tasks, as this would greatly strengthen the claims about the overall utility of the method.
