- Proposes MPME: a two-step EDL approach mapping mention -> mention-sense -> KB entity using an intermediate "mention senses" layer.  
- Jointly learns embeddings for words, mention senses, and entities via a unified objective.  
- Contribution: demonstrates that the two-step mention→sense→entity framework achieves state-of-the-art results on the evaluated benchmark.  
- Contribution: introduces a multi-prototype joint embedding model capturing multiple contextual usages per mention.  
- Strength: strong empirical performance—MPME (and SPME) outperform ALIGN and set new benchmark results (Table 2).  
- Strength: method is technically sound, leveraging skip-gram embeddings and a coherent joint training objective.  
- Strength: novelty in explicitly modeling mention senses and using a smoothing parameter to balance information sources.  
- Weakness: lacks conceptual justification for why the intermediate mention-sense layer captures information entities alone cannot.  
- Weakness: key methodological details on creating and differentiating mention senses are missing, harming reproducibility.  
- Weakness: unclear how number of senses per mention is determined and whether senses map bijectively to KB entities.  
- Weakness: incorrect/overstated novelty claims—prior EDL work (e.g., TAC KBP) addressing text–knowledge integration is not cited.  
- Weakness: framing is overly broad (claims to solve text–knowledge integration) while the contribution is narrowly an EDL method.  
- Question: please clarify the construction, definition, and count of mention senses and their precise relation to KB entities.  
- Question: explain what unique information sense profiles contain versus entity profiles and why textual context cannot simply enrich entity embeddings.  
- Question: revise novelty claims and explicitly compare/contrast with TAC KBP and related prior EDL systems.
