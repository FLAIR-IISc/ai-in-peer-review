- Addresses mention ambiguity by learning multiple sense (prototype) embeddings for each ambiguous mention string.
- Proposes a Multi-Prototype Mention Embedding model trained jointly on a large text corpus and a knowledge base to embed words, entities, and mention senses in one semantic space.
- Leverages both textual context and knowledge-graph (neighboring-entity) context to inform sense embeddings.
- Introduces a language-model–based iterative disambiguation method that assigns mention instances to learned sense embeddings.
- Evaluates embeddings intrinsically on word/entity similarity tasks and extrinsically on entity linking, reporting state-of-the-art results on a standard benchmark.
- Contribution: a novel framework that explicitly models mention ambiguity with multiple sense embeddings using joint text + KB objectives.
- Contribution: strong empirical gains on supervised entity linking, outperforming a closely related baseline (ALIGN).
- Strength: demonstrates strong empirical performance, supporting the value of multi-prototype modeling for downstream tasks.
- Strength: includes a direct, relevant experimental comparison with the ALIGN model, controlling for motivation and input data.
- Strength: provides insightful analysis of the smoothing parameter that balances model confidence with entity popularity.
- Weakness: baseline comparison lacks sufficient detail / controlled “apples-to-apples” hyperparameter matching (e.g., window size, embedding dim).
- Weakness: unclear definition of entity neighborhood N(e_j) (incoming vs. outgoing vs. undirected links), which could confound comparisons with ALIGN.
- Weakness: imprecise terminology around “mention” vs. mention string type vs. mention instance; should use clearer terms like “mention sense” or “prototype embeddings.”
- Question: clarify exact definition of entity neighborhood N(e_j) and how it compares to ALIGN’s definition.
- Question: explain choice of differing hyperparameters vs. ALIGN and whether cross-hyperparameter experiments were run for a fairer comparison.
- Question: justify using the iterative language-model–based disambiguation over other collective methods (e.g., graph-ranking) that capture inter-entity dependencies.
