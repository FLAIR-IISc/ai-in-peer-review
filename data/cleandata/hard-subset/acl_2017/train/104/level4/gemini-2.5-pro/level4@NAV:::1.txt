- Strengths:
* The proposed model achieves superior performance over ALIGN on the supervised entity linking benchmark, which supports the claim that the framework yields improved, jointly learned representations for text and knowledge.
* The inclusion of a direct comparison to a closely related method, conducted with highly similar input data, is a notable strength.
* The paper provides a useful analysis of the smoothing parameter, which is particularly insightful given that entity popularity bias remains a significant challenge in the field of entity linking.
- Weaknesses:
* The comparison to ALIGN could be strengthened. There are notable discrepancies in the experimental setup, such as the content window size (5 vs. 10 in ALIGN) and the vector dimensionality (200 vs. 500). Furthermore, the definition of an entity's neighborhood, N(ej), lacks clarity. It is unclear if adjacency is treated as in an undirected graph, despite the underlying graph of Wikipedia outlinks being directed. This detail is significant because ALIGN defines an entity's context as the set of entities with inbound links. If the definition of N(e_j) used here differs, it becomes challenging to isolate the impact of this change on the learned embeddings and could account for some of the observed performance differences on the entity similarity task.
* The paper's use of the term "mention" is occasionally ambiguous. It is not always clear whether it refers to a string type or a specific instance within a document. For example, the authors use the phrase "mention embedding," yet the model appears to learn embeddings exclusively for mention senses, not for the mentions themselves.
* The impact of the sense disambiguation ordering is difficult to assess in isolation. A comparison against other unsupervised entity linking approaches would be necessary to properly contextualize and validate the significance of this design choice.
