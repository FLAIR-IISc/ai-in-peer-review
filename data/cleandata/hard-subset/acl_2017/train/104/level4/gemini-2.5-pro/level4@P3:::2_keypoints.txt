- Proposes MPME: a two-step entity linking method using an intermediate "mention sense" representation and joint embeddings for words, entities, and senses.
- Main contributions: multi-prototype mention sense embeddings and empirical validation that mention→sense→entity outperforms direct mention→entity, achieving state-of-the-art on the benchmark.

- Strength: achieves strong empirical (SOTA) results on the entity linking benchmark.
- Strength: intermediate "mention sense" layer is intuitive and allows complementary use of textual and KB signals.
- Strength: method is technically sound, using skip-gram objectives and joint training aligned with representation learning best practices.
- Strength: includes pragmatic design details (e.g., smoothing parameter) to balance context-based and embedding-based scores.

- Weakness: unclear definition, motivation, and construction of "mention senses," and insufficient analysis of what they capture versus entities.
- Weakness: overstates novelty by ignoring extensive prior work in Entity Disambiguation/Linking (e.g., TAC KBP and other EDL systems).
- Weakness: framing as broad "integration of text and knowledge" is misleading; contribution is specific to entity linking.

- Question: How are "mention senses" defined/enumerated—are they predetermined by candidate KB entities and is there a one-to-one mapping to entities?
- Question: What distinct information do sense embeddings capture versus entity embeddings, and could augmenting entity representations with textual context obviate senses?
- Question: Why not evaluate or compare against large-scale EDL shared-task systems (e.g., TAC KBP) to better situate performance?
