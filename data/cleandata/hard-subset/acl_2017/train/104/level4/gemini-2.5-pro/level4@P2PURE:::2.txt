This paper addresses the task of linking entity mentions in text to a knowledge base (English Wikipedia). While the title and introduction frame the work broadly as bridging text and knowledge, the specific focus is on Entity Linking (EL), a core component of this larger goal. The proposed method introduces an intermediate representation layer of 'mention senses,' breaking the linking process into two steps: (1) mapping a mention to a mention sense, and (2) mapping the mention sense to an entity. Embeddings are learned for words, mention senses, and entities, and are jointly optimized under a single objective function.

The technical approach is sound, employing modern deep learning techniques and best practices for embeddings. While alternative architectures could be explored, the core of my feedback concerns the fundamental motivation for the proposed model.

The paper's central weakness is the lack of a clear justification for why a two-step process using mention senses is superior to a direct one-step mapping from mentions to entities (as in the ALIGN baseline). While Table 2 demonstrates that the proposed two-step models (MPME and SPME) outperform the one-step approach, the paper does not explain the underlying reason for this performance gain.

Specifically, what additional information is captured by the mention sense representations that is not already present in the entity representations? My understanding of the distinction is as follows, and I would ask the authors to clarify this in the paper:

*   **Entity representations** are embeddings derived from a graph of related entities, which seems to be constructed from the textual content of Wikipedia pages.
*   **Word representations** are standard, non-sense-disambiguated distributional embeddings.
*   **Mention sense representations** are also distributional embeddings, but they *are* sense-disambiguated. The paper states this is done using local context, but provides insufficient detail on the mechanism, which is a key weakness.

This leads to several critical questions:
1.  How are mention senses defined and differentiated? Is the set of senses for a mention string derived from the knowledge base itself?
2.  If so, does this create a one-to-one mapping between a mention sense and a KB entity?
3.  If the mapping is one-to-one, what is the source of the additional information that makes the mention sense representation distinct from the entity representation? Is it derived from words in the training corpus that are contextually associated with the mention but do not appear in the corresponding Wikipedia entity page?
4.  If this is the case, the paper should quantify this informational difference. How much unique, non-KB information does the mention sense space capture on average? Is this information significant enough to justify a separate representation space, rather than simply augmenting the entity representations with these corpus-derived terms?

If my understanding is correct, this distinction is the paper's primary contribution, and it should be explicitly and clearly articulated in Section 5.

Furthermore, the claim in Section 6 that "...this is the first work to deal with mention ambiguity in the integration of text and knowledge representations..." is inaccurate. The TAC KBP evaluations have featured an Entity Discovery and Linking (EDL) task for several years, with numerous participating systems performing this exact task. While KBP uses Freebase rather than Wikipedia, it is a highly relevant and established line of research that should be acknowledged and discussed. For reference, see the 2016 KBP overview: http://nlp.cs.rpi.edu/kbp/2016/.

On a positive note, the introduction of the smoothing parameter in Section 6.4.2 is a thoughtful and valuable contribution.

**Post-Rebuttal Comments:** I have read the author response. I am not satisfied with the argument that dismisses the KBP evaluation as irrelevant because the paper's focus is on the "goodness" of the embeddings. The quality of an embedding representation can only be meaningfully evaluated through its performance on a downstream task. Conceptual elegance is secondary to empirical performance; the crucial question is whether the proposed embeddings lead to better results on the task they were designed for.
