This paper proposes a method for Entity Disambiguation and Linking (EDL), the task of mapping textual entity mentions to a canonical entity in a knowledge base (in this case, English Wikipedia). While the title and introduction frame the work broadly as "bridging text and knowledge," the paper's specific focus is on the core EDL task. The proposed approach introduces an intermediate layer of "mention senses," breaking the linking process into two stages: (1) mapping a textual mention to a mention sense, and (2) mapping the mention sense to a canonical entity. The core of the method involves jointly learning embedding representations for words, mention senses, and entities within a unified framework designed to optimize a single objective function.

From a technical standpoint, the method is sound and aligns with current best practices in deep learning and embedding-based models. My primary concerns, however, relate to the conceptual motivation behind the core architectural choice.

The main conceptual weakness of the paper is the lack of a clear justification for the two-step process involving an intermediate sense layer. Why is this two-step approach superior to a direct, one-step mapping from mentions to entities, such as the ALIGN method (Yamada et al., 2016) used as a baseline? While the empirical results in Table 2 suggest that the two-step models (MPME and SPME) outperform the direct baseline, the paper does not provide a clear theoretical explanation for this performance gain. What additional information does the mention sense layer provide that is not already captured by the entity representations?

To better understand this, I have formulated a hypothesis about how the model components are constructed, and I would ask the authors to clarify if this is correct, and if so, to make these details explicit in the paper. My understanding is as follows:

*   **Entity Representations:** These are embeddings derived from a relatedness graph, where entity profiles are defined by their neighboring entities in the knowledge base (Wikipedia).
*   **Word Representations:** These are standard distributional embeddings learned from a text corpus, without any sense disambiguation.
*   **Mention Sense Representations:** These are also distributional embeddings, but they are sense-disambiguated. The paper briefly mentions that this disambiguation uses a sense-based profile derived from local context words and neighboring mentions, but the details of this critical step are omitted.

This interpretation raises several crucial questions that are not addressed in the paper:
1.  **Sense Inventory:** How are the mention senses defined and enumerated? Who determines the number of senses a given mention string can have?
2.  **Sense-Entity Mapping:** If the sense inventory is derived from the knowledge base, does this result in a bijective mapping where each mention sense corresponds to exactly one KB entity?
3.  **Information Gain:** If there is a one-to-one mapping between senses and entities, what distinct information does the mention sense representation capture that is not already present in the entity representation? Is it primarily textual evidence from the corpus that is reliably associated with the mention but does not appear on the entity's corresponding Wikipedia page? If so, what is the scale of this additional information (e.g., average number of words per sense)?
4.  **Model Simplification:** Could this additional information not be incorporated directly into the entity representation (e.g., by augmenting the entity's Wikipedia page text with these terms), thereby simplifying the model back to a more direct one-step process?

If my understanding above is largely correct, then the nature of this supplementary information at the sense level constitutes the paper's main contribution. This point is currently unclear and should be explicitly detailed, ideally with analysis, in Section 5.

Furthermore, the paper's claim in Section 6 that "...this is the first work to deal with mention ambiguity in the integration of text and knowledge representations, so there is no exact baselines for comparison" is inaccurate. The Text Analysis Conference (TAC) Knowledge Base Population (KBP) track has hosted an EDL shared task for several years (e.g., 2015, 2016), featuring numerous systems performing this exact task against a large and noisy knowledge base (Freebase). While the target KB and datasets differ, this represents a significant body of prior and concurrent work that should be acknowledged and discussed to properly contextualize the paper's contributions.

On a more positive note, the introduction of the smoothing parameter in Section 6.4.2 is a clever and well-motivated addition to the model.

**Post-Rebuttal Comments:**
I have read the author response. The authors argue that the TAC KBP evaluation is not directly relevant because their focus is on the quality of the learned embeddings rather than task performance. I find this argument unconvincing. The intrinsic "goodness" of an embedding is a vague concept; its value can only be demonstrated extrinsically through improved performance on a downstream task, such as EDL. The ultimate measure of a representation is its utility. Therefore, comparison against state-of-the-art systems on established benchmarks like KBP is not only relevant but essential for properly situating the contributions of this work.
