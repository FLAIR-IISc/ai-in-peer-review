- Proposes MPME: learns multiple sense vectors per mention by jointly training on text and a KB, integrating words, entities, and mention senses into one space and using an LM for disambiguation.
- Claims state-of-the-art performance on a standard entity linking benchmark, validating the approach.

- Contribution: a unified multi-prototype neural model that jointly learns representations for words, entities, and multiple mention senses.
- Contribution: strong empirical result—SOTA entity linking performance demonstrating the utility of the embeddings and disambiguation method.

- Strength: novel, coherent architecture that elegantly integrates mentions, entities, and multiple senses in a single neural framework.
- Strength: strong empirical results—competitive, state-of-the-art performance on entity linking.
- Strength: embeddings have broad potential applicability to downstream NLP tasks (relation extraction, QA, coreference, etc.).

- Weakness: model description lacks clarity (Section 3 and Figure 2 are hard to follow) and terminology (e.g., “dictionary of mentions” vs “dictionary of entity-mention pairs”) is ambiguous.
- Weakness: insufficient differentiation from prior work (notably Yamada et al. 2016); lacks explicit comparison of technical novelties.
- Weakness: limited evaluation scope—focuses only on entity linking and omits intrinsic evaluations to verify word embedding quality after joint training.

- Question: please clarify the definitions, construction, and usage of “dictionary of mentions” and “dictionary of entity-mention pairs” in Section 3/Figure 2.
- Question: explicitly articulate the primary technical and conceptual differences between this model and Yamada et al. (2016).
- Question: have you evaluated the model’s word embeddings on standard intrinsic tasks (e.g., WordSim-353, SimLex-999, analogies) to ensure joint training doesn’t degrade general word representation quality?
