This paper tackles the problem of entity disambiguation and linking (EDL) – mapping textual mentions to entities in a knowledge base (English Wikipedia). While the title and introduction present the work as a broad contribution to bridging text and knowledge, the paper’s core focus is specifically on EDL. The proposed method utilizes an intermediate layer of “mention senses” to facilitate this mapping, proceeding in two steps: (1) mention to mention sense, and (2) mention sense to entity. The authors learn embeddings for words, mention senses, and entities, jointly trained to maximize a unified objective function that gives equal weight to all three embedding types. 

Technically, the approach is sound, aligning with current deep learning practices and established embedding techniques. While alternative methods could be considered, their potential impact isn't immediately obvious. My primary concerns lie with the fundamental rationale behind the two-step process and the role of the mention sense layer. The paper doesn’t clearly articulate *why* introducing mention senses improves performance over a direct, one-step mapping from mentions to entities (as seen in the ALIGN algorithm by Yamada et al.). Table 2 demonstrates performance gains with the two-step MPME (and its simplification, SPME), but the underlying reasons remain unclear. What specific information do mention senses provide that entities do not? 

My understanding of the profiles used for each component is as follows: entity profiles are based on neighboring entities within a relatedness graph constructed from word-level similarities of entity definitions (Wikipedia pages), represented as extended skip-gram embeddings. Word profiles utilize standard distributional semantics without sense disambiguation. Mention sense profiles also employ distributional semantics, but *with* sense disambiguation. This disambiguation leverages a sense-based profile (‘language model’) derived from local context words and neighboring mentions, briefly described before Section 4, but lacking detailed explanation. This lack of detail is a significant weakness. How are these senses created and differentiated? What determines the number of senses associated with a mention string? If sense creation relies on the knowledge base, does this result in a one-to-one mapping between mention senses and entities? If so, are the definitional profiles for mention senses initialized with entity text? Ultimately, what information is present at the mention sense level that isn’t already available at the entity level? Specifically, are these the words reliably associated with a particular mention sense but absent from the corresponding Wikipedia entity page? What is the average number of such words per mention sense? Is maintaining this differentiation in a separate space (the mention sense space) truly necessary, or could this information be effectively incorporated directly into the entity profiles by augmenting the Wikipedia entity pages?

If my interpretation is correct, Section 5 of the paper should explicitly state this, as it represents the core novel contribution. The claim in Section 6 that “this is the first work to deal with mention ambiguity…” is inaccurate. The Text Analysis Conference (TAC) Knowledge Base Population (KBP) evaluations over the past two years have featured EDL tasks with eight to nine participating systems, all tackling the same problem, albeit using Freebase instead of Wikipedia. Relevant information can be found at http://nlp.cs.rpi.edu/kbp/2016/. 

On a positive note, I appreciated the smoothing parameter introduced in Section 6.4.2.

Following the author’s response, I remain unconvinced by their argument that the TAC KBP evaluations are irrelevant because their focus is on embedding quality. Embedding quality is inherently application-dependent; conceptual elegance is secondary to demonstrable performance improvements. The ultimate measure of an embedding’s value is its effectiveness in downstream tasks.
