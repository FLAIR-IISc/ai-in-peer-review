**Review:**

**Strengths:**

* The proposed framework outperforms ALIGN on the supervised entity linking task, suggesting improved representations of text and knowledge learned through their joint embedding.
* The paper provides a direct comparison with a closely related approach using highly similar input data, allowing for a nuanced evaluation.
* The analysis of the smoothing parameter is particularly insightful, as addressing the impact of entity popularity is a persistent challenge in entity linking.

**Weaknesses:**

* The comparison with ALIGN could be strengthened. Key differences in experimental setup exist, including ALIGN’s use of a content window size of 10 versus the 5 used here, and a vector dimension of 500 compared to 200. Furthermore, it is unclear whether N(e<sub>j</sub>) includes only entities that link *to* e<sub>j</sub>. Given that the graph is directed, constructed from Wikipedia outlinks, it’s not immediately apparent if adjacency is treated as in an undirected graph. ALIGN defines entity context as the set of entities linking *to* it; if N(e<sub>j</sub>) is defined differently, it’s difficult to ascertain the impact of this change on the learned vectors, and this could significantly contribute to the observed differences in entity similarity scores.
* The terminology surrounding “mention” is occasionally ambiguous. It is sometimes unclear whether “mention” refers to a string-type entity mention or a specific instance of that mention within a document. While the phrase "mention embedding" is used, the embeddings appear to be learned only for mention *senses*, not the mentions themselves.
* Evaluating the impact of sense disambiguation order is difficult without comparison to other unsupervised entity linking methods.
