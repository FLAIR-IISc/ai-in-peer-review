This paper addresses the problem of entity disambiguation and linking (EDL) – mapping textual entity mentions to their corresponding entries in a knowledge base (English Wikipedia). While the title and introduction suggest a broader scope of bridging text and knowledge, the work focuses specifically on EDL, a core component of that larger task. The proposed method utilizes an intermediate representation layer of mention senses, decomposing the problem into two steps: (1) mapping mentions to mention senses, and (2) mapping mention senses to entities. The authors learn embeddings for words, mention senses, and entities, jointly training them to maximize a unified objective function that equally weights all three embedding types.

Technically, the approach is sound and aligns with current deep learning best practices for embeddings. While alternative architectures could be considered, it's unclear if they would lead to substantial improvements. My primary concerns relate to the fundamental motivation for the two-step process involving mention senses. The paper does not adequately explain *why* this approach is superior to a direct, one-step mapping from mentions to entities, as exemplified by Yamada et al.’s ALIGN algorithm. Table 2 demonstrates that the two-step MPME (and its simplified version SPME) achieve better performance, but the underlying reason for this improvement remains unclear. What specific information do mention senses capture that entities do not? 

To clarify this, I believe the following understanding is critical (and should be explicitly stated in the paper): entity profiles are constructed from neighboring entities in a relatedness graph built using word-level relatedness of entity definitions (Wikipedia pages), and represented as extended skip-gram embeddings. Word profiles employ standard distributional semantics *without* sense disambiguation. Crucially, mention sense profiles utilize distributional semantics *with* sense disambiguation, leveraging a sense-based profile ('language model') derived from local context words and neighboring mentions (briefly mentioned before Section 4). This sense disambiguation process is a key point of concern. The paper lacks details regarding how these senses are created and differentiated. How is the number of senses per mention string determined? If the knowledge base dictates this, it implies a bijective mapping between mention senses and entities – each sense corresponds to exactly one entity, even if multiple entities exist for the mention string. In this scenario, are sense profiles initialized using entity text as “seed words”? If so, what information at the mention sense level is *not* present at the entity level? Specifically, are these the context words reliably associated with a particular mention sense but absent from the corresponding Wikipedia entity page? Quantifying this – the average number of such words per mention sense – would be valuable. Ultimately, the paper needs to justify why maintaining this differentiation in a separate space (the mention sense space) is more effective than incorporating these additional words directly into the entity representations within Wikipedia.

If my understanding is largely correct, Section 5 should be revised to clearly articulate this as the paper’s core contribution. The statement in Section 6 that “this is the first work to deal with mention ambiguity in the integration of text and knowledge representations” is inaccurate. The Text Analysis Conference (TAC) Knowledge Base Population (KBP) evaluations over the past two years have featured EDL tasks with eight to nine participating systems, all addressing this problem, albeit against the Freebase knowledge base (which is admittedly larger and noisier than Wikipedia). Details are available at http://nlp.cs.rpi.edu/kbp/2016/.

On a positive note, the smoothing parameter introduced in Section 6.4.2 is a clever and potentially beneficial addition.

**Post-Response Evaluation:** I am not convinced by the authors’ response regarding the irrelevance of the TAC KBP evaluations, and their assertion that their focus is solely on the quality of the embeddings. The utility of embeddings can *only* be evaluated through their application in downstream tasks. Conceptual elegance is secondary; performance is paramount.
