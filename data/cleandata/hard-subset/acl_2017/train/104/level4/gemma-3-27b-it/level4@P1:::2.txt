This paper addresses the problem of disambiguating and linking textual entity mentions to a background knowledge base (in this case, English Wikipedia). While the title and introduction suggest a broad approach to bridging text and knowledge, the work focuses specifically on the entity disambiguation and linking (EDL) task, which is a core component of this broader challenge. The method introduces an intermediate representation layer of mention senses, employing a two-step process: (1) mapping mentions to mention senses, and (2) mapping mention senses to entities. Embeddings are learned for words, mention senses, and entities, and jointly trained to maximize an overall objective function.

Technically, the approach is sound and aligns with current deep learning practices and established best practices for embeddings. While alternative approaches could be considered, it’s unclear if they would yield substantial improvements. My comments primarily address the fundamental approach. The paper does not fully explain why a two-step process, utilizing mention senses, is superior to a direct one-step mapping from word mentions to entities—an approach exemplified by the ALIGN algorithm from Yamada et al. Table 2 demonstrates that the proposed Multi-Prototype Mention Embedding (MPME) and its simplified version (SPME) outperform ALIGN, but the underlying reasons remain unclear. What specific information do mention senses provide that is not already present in the entity representations? To clarify this, I would appreciate the authors addressing the following points (and updating the paper accordingly):

Regarding entities: their profiles are constructed using neighboring entities within a relatedness graph, built (presumably) based on word-level relatedness extracted from entity definitions (Wikipedia pages). These profiles are represented as embeddings derived from an extended skip-gram model. 

Regarding words: their profiles leverage standard distributional semantics, without incorporating sense disambiguation.

Regarding mention senses: their profiles also employ distributional semantics, but *with* sense disambiguation. This disambiguation relies on a sense-based profile—a “language model”—derived from local context words and neighboring mentions, as briefly mentioned before Section 4, though without detailed explanation. This lack of detail is a key concern. How exactly are these senses created and differentiated? What determines the number of senses assigned to a mention string? If sense creation relies on the knowledge base, does this result in a bijective mapping between mention senses and entities – meaning each mention sense corresponds to exactly one entity (though an entity might have multiple senses)?  If so, are the sense profiles initially built using entity text as “seed words”? If that’s the case, what unique information is captured at the mention sense level that isn't already present at the entity level? Specifically, are there words consistently associated with a mention sense that do *not* appear in the corresponding Wikipedia entity page?  On average, how many such words exist for each mention sense?  How significant is it to maintain this differentiating information in a separate space (the mention sense space) rather than simply incorporating it into the Entity space by adding these words to the Wikipedia entity pages?

If my understanding is largely correct, please clarify this in Section 5 of the paper, as it represents the core novel contribution of this work.

Furthermore, the claim in Section 6 that “this is the first work to deal with mention ambiguity in the integration of text and knowledge representations, so there is no exact baselines for comparison” is inaccurate. The Text Analysis Conference (TAC) Knowledge Base Population (KBP) evaluations over the past two years have featured EDL tasks involving eight to nine systems, all performing this task – albeit against Freebase, which is larger and noisier than Wikipedia. See http://nlp.cs.rpi.edu/kbp/2016/ for details.

On a positive note, I found the smoothing parameter introduced in Section 6.4.2 to be a valuable addition.
