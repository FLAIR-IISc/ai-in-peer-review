## Review:

This paper presents a neural learning method for entity disambiguation and linking, and demonstrates a valuable approach to integrating entity, mention, and sense modeling within a unified neural language modeling framework. The simplicity of the training process is a key strength, enabling support for a wide range of applications. The work exhibits interesting performance, though improvements are not dramatic. Overall, the paper addresses a relevant problem and presents ideas of genuine interest.

The paper is formally well-structured, but the depth of discussion does not consistently match the sophistication of the technical concepts. The empirical evaluation is sound, although the reported performance gains are moderate. While building upon prior work such as Yamada et al. (CoNLL 2016), the paper introduces novel contributions.

However, the paper would benefit from several improvements:

- Clarity of presentation needs refinement. Specifically, Section 3 requires greater clarity, and details within Figure 2 are insufficiently explained. The terminology employed also feels somewhat redundant; the distinction between the “dictionary of mentions” and the “dictionary of entity-mention pairs,” for instance, is unclear, and their relationship to “text anchors” and “types for annotated text anchors” should be explicitly defined.
- The relationship to Yamada et al. (2016) should be more thoroughly addressed. A clearer outline of the differences between the two approaches would strengthen the paper’s contribution. 

A broader consideration regarding the evaluation is that the Multiple Embedding model is assessed solely on entity linking/disambiguation tasks. Given that word embeddings are utilized in a much wider array of NLP tasks—including parsing, coreference resolution, and semantic role labeling—that do *not* directly rely on knowledge base entities, it’s important to demonstrate that the word embeddings produced by the proposed MPME method do not perform worse than simpler word embedding spaces on these more general semantic tasks involving mention processing. 

I have reviewed the authors’ response.
