This paper addresses the problem of disambiguating/linking textual entity mentions into a given background knowledge base (in this case, English Wikipedia). (Its title and introduction are somewhat ambitious, as bridging text and knowledge encompasses more than just entity disambiguation/linking (EDL), though EDL is a core component.) The method involves bridging this gap via an intermediate layer of representation – mention senses – implemented in two steps: (1) mention to mention sense, and (2) mention sense to entity. Embedding representations are learned for words, mention senses, and entities, and jointly trained to maximize an overall objective function that balances all three embedding types.

Technically, the approach is sound and aligns with current deep learning practices and established best practices for embeddings; alternative approaches exist but may not yield substantial improvements. My comments primarily concern the foundational approach. The rationale behind the two-step process, utilizing mention senses, isn't fully explained, particularly compared to a simpler, direct one-step mapping from word mentions to their entities (e.g., the ALIGN algorithm by Yamada et al.). Table 2 indicates that the two-step MPME (and SPME) perform better, but the reason for this improvement remains unclear. What specific additional information do mention senses provide compared to entities? To clarify, I understand the following:

For entities: profiles are constructed from neighboring entities in a relatedness graph, built (presumably) using word-level relatedness of entity definitions (Wikipedia pages). These profiles utilize extended skip-gram-based embeddings.

For words: profiles are based on standard distributional semantics, without sense disambiguation.

For mention senses: profiles also employ standard distributional semantics, but *with* sense disambiguation. This disambiguation leverages a sense-based profile ('language model') derived from local context words and neighboring mentions, briefly mentioned before Section 4, though lacking detail. This is a key area of concern. How are senses created and differentiated? How is the number of senses per mention string determined? If based on the knowledge base, this implies a bijective mapping between mention senses and entities – one entity per mention sense (though multiple entities may exist). Consequently, are the sense profiles initially built using entity text as 'seed words'? If so, what information is unique to the mention sense level that isn't present at the entity level? Specifically, are these words that reliably associate with the mention sense but are absent from the corresponding Wikipedia entity page? What is the average number of such words per mention sense? How crucial is maintaining this differentiating information in a separate space (the mention sense space) versus incorporating these additional words directly into the entity space (by augmenting Wikipedia entity pages)?

If my understanding is correct, please revise Section 5 to reflect this, as it represents the primary novelty of the paper. 

Furthermore, the claim in Section 6 that "…this is the first work to deal with mention ambiguity in the integration of text and knowledge representations, so there is no exact baselines for comparison" is inaccurate. The TAC KBP evaluations over the past two years have featured EDL tasks, involving eight to nine systems, all addressing this challenge, though against Freebase, which is larger and more noisy than Wikipedia. See http://nlp.cs.rpi.edu/kbp/2016/.

On a positive note, I appreciate the smoothing parameter described in Section 6.4.2.
