This paper proposes a Multi-Prototype Mention Embedding model to address the ambiguity of entity mentions by learning multiple sense embeddings for each mention, leveraging both textual contexts and a knowledge base. The authors also present a language model-based approach for mention disambiguation. They demonstrate strong performance on entity linking, achieving state-of-the-art results on a benchmark dataset.

**Contributions:**

1. **Novel Multi-Prototype Mention Embedding:** The core contribution is the proposed method for learning multiple sense embeddings for each mention, effectively capturing the polysemous nature of entity mentions. This is a significant step towards more nuanced and accurate entity representation.
2. **Effective Disambiguation Approach:** The language model-based mention disambiguation method appears to be a key component, successfully leveraging contextual information to select the correct sense of a mention.
3. **Strong Empirical Results:** Achieving state-of-the-art results on a standard entity linking benchmark provides strong evidence for the effectiveness of the proposed approach.

**Strengths:**

* **State-of-the-art Performance:** The reported state-of-the-art results on entity linking are a compelling argument for acceptance. This demonstrates a clear practical benefit of the proposed method. The authors convincingly show that their approach outperforms existing methods, indicating a significant improvement in the field.
* **Well-Motivated Approach:** The problem of mention ambiguity is well-recognized in the field, and the proposed solution of multi-prototype embeddings is a logical and promising approach to address it. The work clearly articulates the challenges of representing ambiguous mentions in a unified semantic space.
* **Thorough Analysis of Smoothing Parameter:** The analysis of the smoothing parameter is a valuable contribution. Understanding the impact of popularity bias is crucial in entity linking, and this analysis provides useful insights. This demonstrates a thoughtful consideration of practical challenges in the task.
* **Direct Comparison to Related Work:** The direct comparison with ALIGN is valuable, providing a clear benchmark for the proposed approach. This allows for a direct assessment of the benefits of the introduced methodology.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Inconsistent Experimental Setup & Fair Comparison:** The comparison with ALIGN suffers from inconsistencies in experimental setup (context window size, vector dimension). This makes it difficult to isolate the impact of the proposed method versus the effect of these differing hyperparameters. A more controlled comparison, ideally with both systems trained and evaluated using identical settings, is needed to draw definitive conclusions. The description of N(ej) and the nature of the graph (directed vs. undirected) lacks sufficient clarity, further complicating the interpretation of the results.
* **Terminology Ambiguity:** The inconsistent use of "mention" (referring to a string type vs. a specific instance in a document) creates confusion. This ambiguity hinders the clarity of the explanation and the understanding of how the embeddings are learned. Clarifying this terminology is crucial.
* **Limited Comparison to Unsupervised Baselines:** While the comparison to ALIGN is valuable, evaluating the impact of the sense disambiguation order in isolation requires comparison to other unsupervised entity linking methods. Demonstrating an improvement *relative* to other unsupervised baselines would strengthen the claim of the disambiguation method's effectiveness.
* **References Formatting:** The references section contains several inconsistencies in formatting and repeated entries. This impacts the readability and professional appearance of the paper.

**Questions to Authors:**

1. Could you please provide results comparing your approach to ALIGN using a consistent experimental setup (e.g., same context window size and vector dimension)?
2. Could you clarify the definition of N(ej) and whether the graph used for constructing the knowledge graph is treated as directed or undirected?
3.  To address the terminology ambiguity, could you consistently use distinct terms to differentiate between a mention *type* and a specific mention *instance* within a document?
4.  Could you present a comparison of your sense disambiguation method to other unsupervised entity linking baselines, to assess its independent contribution?

**Additional Comments:**

* Please carefully review and standardize the formatting of the references section.
* Consider adding a qualitative example showcasing the benefits of multi-prototype embeddings for a particularly ambiguous mention. This would further illustrate the effectiveness of your approach.
