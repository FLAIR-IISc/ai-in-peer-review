This paper tackles the problem of entity disambiguation and linking (EDL), mapping textual mentions to a knowledge base (English Wikipedia). While the title and introduction present the work as a broader solution for bridging text and knowledge, EDL forms a core component of this effort. The proposed method utilizes an intermediate representation layer of mention senses, proceeding in two stages: (1) mention to mention sense, and (2) mention sense to entity. The authors learn embeddings for words, mention senses, and entities, jointly training them to maximize a unified objective function that equally prioritizes all three embedding types.

The technical approach is sound and aligns with current deep learning practices and established embedding techniques. While alternative approaches could be considered, their potential impact isn’t immediately obvious. My primary concern lies with the fundamental rationale for the two-step process involving mention senses, compared to a direct, single-step mapping from mentions to entities, as seen in approaches like Yamada et al.’s ALIGN.  Table 2 demonstrates the superiority of the two-step MPME (and its simplified version, SPME), but the underlying reason remains unclear. What specific additional information do mention senses provide compared to entities? 

My understanding of the profiles used for each component is as follows – please confirm and clarify in the paper: Entities are represented by embeddings built from neighboring entities within a relatedness graph, constructed based on word-level relatedness of their Wikipedia page definitions. Words utilize standard distributional semantics *without* sense disambiguation. Mention senses, however, employ distributional semantics *with* sense disambiguation, leveraging a sense-based profile derived from local context words and neighboring mentions (briefly noted before Section 4), though without detailed explanation. This lack of detail regarding sense creation and differentiation is a critical issue.  How are these senses determined, and what governs the number of senses assigned to a mention string?  If the knowledge base dictates this, does this result in a bijective mapping between mention senses and entities, effectively one entity per sense?  If so, are the sense profiles initialized using entity text as seed words? If so, what information resides at the mention sense level that isn’t already present at the entity level – specifically, what words reliably associate with a mention sense but *don’t* appear on the corresponding Wikipedia page? What is the average number of these differentiating words per mention sense? Is maintaining this extra information in a separate space (the mention sense space) truly necessary, or could it be integrated directly into the entity representations by augmenting the Wikipedia entity pages?

If my understanding is accurate, please explicitly state this in Section 5, as I believe this represents the paper’s main contribution.  Furthermore, the claim in Section 6 that "…this is the first work to deal with mention ambiguity in the integration of text and knowledge representations" is inaccurate. The Text Analysis Conference (TAC) Knowledge Base Population (KBP) evaluations over the past two years have included EDL tasks, featuring eight to nine competing systems addressing the same problem, albeit using Freebase as the knowledge base.  See http://nlp.cs.rpi.edu/kbp/2016/ for more information.

A noteworthy positive aspect of the work is the smoothing parameter introduced in Section 6.4.2.

**Post-response:** I have reviewed the authors’ response. I remain unconvinced by their argument regarding the irrelevance of the KBP evaluations, and their focus on embedding quality in isolation. Embedding quality can *only* be evaluated through performance on a downstream application. The conceptual elegance of an embedding is secondary to its practical utility: does it improve performance?
