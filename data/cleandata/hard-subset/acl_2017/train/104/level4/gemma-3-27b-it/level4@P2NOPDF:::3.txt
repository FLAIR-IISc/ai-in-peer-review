This paper presents a neural approach to entity disambiguation and linking, integrating entity, mention, and sense modeling within a neural language modeling framework. The core idea is sound, and the resulting simplicity of the training procedure allows for application to a wide range of tasks. The empirical evaluation is solid, demonstrating a reasonable, though not groundbreaking, performance improvement. While the work builds upon prior research, specifically Yamada et al. (2016), it introduces novel elements and remains a relevant contribution to the field.

However, the paper suffers from a lack of clarity in certain sections and a limited amount of truly novel content. While formally well-structured, the depth of discussion doesn't consistently match the sophistication of the technical ideas presented. 

Specifically, Section 3 requires clarification. Several details within Figure 2 are unexplained, and the terminology used feels somewhat redundant. For instance, the purpose of referencing a “dictionary of mentions” and a “dictionary of entity-mention pairs” is unclear – how do these differ from established concepts like text anchors and types associated with annotated text anchors?

The relationship to Yamada et al. (2016) also needs further elaboration. While the authors acknowledge this prior work, a more detailed outline of the key differences would strengthen the paper’s contribution.

A broader observation concerns the evaluation. The paper focuses on entity linking/disambiguation tasks, but the utility of word embeddings extends beyond knowledge base-reliant processes to areas like parsing, coreference resolution, and semantic role labeling. To fully demonstrate the value of the proposed Multiple Path Multi-Embedding (MPME) method, the authors should demonstrate that the resulting word embeddings do not perform *worse* than simpler wordspaces on these more general semantic tasks – those involving, but not exclusively dependent on, entity mentions. Establishing competitive performance across a wider range of tasks would significantly bolster the paper’s impact.
