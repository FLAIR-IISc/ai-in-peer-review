This paper proposes a Multi-Prototype Mention Embedding model to address ambiguity in jointly representing text and knowledge. The core idea is to learn multiple sense embeddings for each mention by combining textual contexts and knowledge base entities, coupled with a language model-based disambiguation approach. The primary contributions, as I see them, are: (1) a novel approach to learning multiple sense embeddings for mentions, effectively capturing polysemy by integrating textual and knowledge-based information; (2) an empirical demonstration, through entity linking experiments, that this approach leads to state-of-the-art performance; and (3) an analysis of the impact of a smoothing parameter, offering insight into the trade-off between popularity and specificity in entity linking.

**Strengths:**

*   The method achieves state-of-the-art results on a benchmark entity linking dataset, demonstrably improving upon existing techniques. This strongly suggests the proposed framework effectively learns more informative representations of text and knowledge when used jointly.
*   The paper provides a direct comparison to a closely related approach (ALIGN), allowing for a focused evaluation of the proposed method's advantages. The quantitative results presented offer compelling evidence of its superiority in the tested setting.
*   The analysis of the smoothing parameter is valuable. Addressing the influence of entity popularity is a persistent challenge in entity linking, and this work offers practical insights into managing that influence.
*   The approach to modeling multiple senses for each mention is well-motivated and contributes to a more nuanced representation of entity mentions within a unified semantic space.

**Weaknesses:**

*   The comparison with ALIGN is not entirely fair due to differences in hyperparameters and data handling. ALIGN utilized a larger context window (10 vs. 5) and a higher vector dimension (500 vs. 200). Moreover, the definition of the neighborhood function N(ej) requires clarification. It is unclear whether it considers only entities linking *to* ej, and whether the graph structure is treated as directed or undirected, as ALIGN uses incoming links to define context. These differences make it difficult to isolate the impact of the proposed method.
*   The terminology around "mention" is occasionally ambiguous. It's not always clear whether "mention" refers to a string type or a specific instance of that string within a document. This lack of clarity sometimes hinders understanding of the embedding process, particularly regarding whether embeddings are learned for all mentions or only for distinct senses.
*   Determining the true impact of the sense disambiguation component is challenging without comparison to other unsupervised entity linking methods. While the reported results are strong, it is difficult to assess how much of the performance gain is attributable specifically to the disambiguation step versus the multi-prototype embeddings themselves.

**Questions to Authors:**

*   Could you clarify the precise definition of N(ej) and whether the graph structure used is directed or undirected?
*   Could you provide a more detailed ablation study to isolate the contribution of the sense disambiguation module from that of the multi-prototype embeddings?
*   How sensitive are the results to the choice of hyperparameters, particularly the context window size and vector dimension?  Would a fairer comparison to ALIGN involve matching these parameters?
