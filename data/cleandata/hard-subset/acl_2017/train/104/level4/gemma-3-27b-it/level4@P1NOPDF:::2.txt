This paper addresses the problem of disambiguating and linking textual entity mentions to a given background knowledge base (in this case, English Wikipedia). While the title and introduction are somewhat ambitious – bridging text and knowledge is a broader task than entity disambiguation (EDL) – EDL is certainly a core component of the overall goal. The method employs an intermediate layer of representation, namely mention senses, to perform this linking in two steps: (1) mapping mentions to mention senses, and (2) mapping mention senses to entities. The authors learn embedding representations for words, mention senses, and entities, and jointly train them to maximize a single objective function that equally prioritizes all three types of embeddings.

Technically, the approach is sound and aligns with current deep learning practices and established best practices for embeddings. While alternative approaches could be considered, it’s unclear whether they would lead to significant improvements. My primary concerns relate to the fundamental approach itself. The paper doesn’t clearly explain why a two-step process, incorporating mention senses, is superior to a direct, one-step mapping from word mentions to entities, as used in the ALIGN algorithm (Yamada et al.). Table 2 demonstrates that the two-step MPME (and its simplified version SPME) achieve better results, but the *reason* for this improvement remains unclear. What additional information do mention senses provide compared to entities? To clarify, please verify the following understanding (and update the paper to accurately reflect the process):

Entities are represented by profiles consisting of related entities in a knowledge graph. This graph is built (presumably) by analyzing the word-level relatedness of entity definitions (Wikipedia pages), and the profiles are implemented as extended skip-gram embeddings. 

Words are represented using standard distributional semantics, without sense disambiguation.

Mention senses are also represented using distributional semantics, but *with* sense disambiguation. This disambiguation relies on a sense-based profile ('language model') derived from local context words and neighboring mentions, as briefly mentioned before Section 4, though without detailed explanation. This lack of detail is a key weakness. How exactly are these senses created and differentiated? What determines the number of senses a given mention string can have? If the number of senses is determined by the knowledge base, this creates a bijective mapping between mention senses and entities – meaning each mention sense corresponds to exactly one entity, even if multiple entities exist for a mention. In this case, are the sense profiles initially built using entity text as ‘seed words’? If so, what information is incorporated at the mention sense level that isn’t already present at the entity level? Specifically, are these the words that reliably co-occur with the mention sense but do *not* appear on the corresponding Wikipedia entity page? On average, how many such words exist for a given mention sense? How significant is it to maintain this extra differentiation in a separate space (the mention sense space) instead of simply adding these additional words to the entity profiles within Wikipedia?

If this understanding is accurate, please emphasize this point in Section 5, as it represents the paper's main contribution.

The claim in Section 6 that “…this is the first work to deal with mention ambiguity in the integration of text and knowledge representations, so there is no exact baseline for comparison” is incorrect. The Text Analysis Conference (TAC) Knowledge Base Population (KBP) evaluations for the past two years have included EDL tasks involving eight or nine systems, all tackling this same challenge, albeit using Freebase – a knowledge base that is larger and potentially noisier than Wikipedia. See http://nlp.cs.rpi.edu/kbp/2016/ .

On a positive note, I appreciated the smoothing parameter introduced in Section 6.4.2.

Post-response: I have reviewed the authors’ response. I remain unconvinced by their argument that the TAC KBP evaluation is irrelevant because they focus on the quality of the embeddings themselves. Ultimately, the only way to assess the quality of embeddings is through their performance in a practical application. Conceptual elegance alone is insufficient; the critical question is whether the embeddings lead to improved results.
