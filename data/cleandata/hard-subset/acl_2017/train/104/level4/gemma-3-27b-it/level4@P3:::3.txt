This paper proposes a Multi-Prototype Mention Embedding (MPME) model to address the ambiguity of entity mentions by learning multiple sense embeddings for each mention, leveraging both textual context and knowledge base entities. The authors also present a language model-based approach for mention disambiguation. They demonstrate the effectiveness of their approach through entity linking experiments, achieving state-of-the-art results.

**Contributions:**

1. **Multi-Prototype Mention Embeddings:** The core contribution lies in the development of MPME, which effectively captures multiple senses of an entity mention using a joint modeling approach of textual context and knowledge base entities. This allows for more nuanced representations compared to traditional single-embedding approaches.
2. **Effective Disambiguation Method:** The proposed language model-based approach to disambiguate mentions to specific senses complements the MPME and provides a practical solution for resolving ambiguity during entity linking.
3. **Strong Empirical Results:** The paper demonstrates competitive performance on a standard entity linking benchmark, achieving state-of-the-art results. This provides empirical validation of the proposed method’s effectiveness.

**Strengths:**

1. **Novelty and Relevance:** While building upon prior work in joint representation learning of text and knowledge, the MPME model introduces a valuable extension by explicitly modeling multiple senses for each mention. This addresses a significant challenge in semantic representation learning and is highly relevant to the field.
2. **Solid Empirical Evaluation:** The authors provide a thorough empirical evaluation on a standard entity linking dataset. Achieving state-of-the-art performance provides strong evidence for the effectiveness of the proposed method. The quantitative results are clearly presented and analyzed.
3. **Clear Problem Formulation:** The paper clearly articulates the problem of mention ambiguity and motivates the need for a multi-prototype embedding approach. The connection to real-world applications like entity linking is well-established.
4. **Practical Approach:** The method appears relatively straightforward to implement and integrate into existing systems, due to the use of a language model framework. The training procedure is described as simple, which suggests potential for scalability.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Clarity and Redundancy:** Certain sections, particularly Section 3, lack clarity. The descriptions of Figure 2 and associated terminology (e.g., "dictionary of mentions" vs. "dictionary of entity-mention pairs") are confusing and require further explanation. There is some redundancy in the terminology used.
2. **Limited Differentiation from Prior Work:** The paper could benefit from a more detailed comparison to related work, specifically Yamada et al. (2016). A clearer articulation of the novel contributions beyond this previous work is needed. The authors should explicitly highlight the key differences and improvements offered by their approach.
3. **Narrow Evaluation Scope:** The evaluation focuses solely on entity linking. While this demonstrates the effectiveness of the method in a specific task, it doesn't provide insights into the broader impact of the learned embeddings on other NLP tasks. As noted, the usefulness of word embeddings extends beyond entity-focused tasks like parsing, coreference resolution, and semantic role labeling. Demonstrating the quality of the embeddings in these broader contexts would strengthen the paper.
4. **Missing Ablation Study:** An ablation study to analyze the contribution of individual components (e.g., the multi-prototype aspect, the knowledge base integration, the language model) would be helpful in understanding the effectiveness of each element of the proposed model.

**Questions to Authors:**

1. Could you provide a more detailed explanation of the terminology used in Section 3, specifically clarifying the distinctions between "dictionary of mentions," "dictionary of entity-mention pairs," "text anchors," and "types for annotated text anchors"?
2.  Can you elaborate on the key differences between your approach and the work of Yamada et al. (2016)? Please provide a more specific comparison of the methods, highlighting the unique aspects of your contribution.
3.  Have you considered evaluating the quality of the learned word embeddings on tasks beyond entity linking, such as parsing, coreference resolution, or semantic role labeling? If so, what were the results? If not, what are the challenges in doing so?
4.  Could you provide an ablation study to demonstrate the contribution of each component of the MPME model (e.g., multi-prototype learning, knowledge base integration, language model)?
