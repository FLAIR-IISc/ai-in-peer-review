This paper addresses the problem of entity disambiguation and linking (EDL) – mapping textual entity mentions to a knowledge base (English Wikipedia in this case). While the title and introduction present a broad scope for bridging text and knowledge, EDL represents a core component of this larger task. The proposed method leverages an intermediate representation layer of mention senses, decomposing the linking process into two steps: (1) mention to mention sense, and (2) mention sense to entity. The authors learn embedding representations for words, mention senses, and entities, jointly training them to maximize a unified objective function that equally weights all three embedding types.

Technically, the approach is well-defined and aligns with current deep learning practices and established embedding techniques. While alternative methods could be explored, their impact is uncertain. My primary concerns relate to the fundamental approach. The paper does not adequately explain the rationale for a two-step process utilizing mention senses, as opposed to a direct one-step mapping from mentions to entities (as demonstrated by Yamada et al.’s ALIGN algorithm). Table 2 indicates that the two-step MPME (and its simplification, SPME) achieve superior performance, but the underlying reason remains unclear. What specific information do mention senses provide that is not available at the entity level? To clarify, I believe the following description accurately reflects the methodology (and suggest the authors confirm and articulate this in the paper):

Entity profiles are constructed from neighboring entities within a relatedness graph, presumably built using word-level relatedness of entity definitions (Wikipedia pages), and represented as extended skip-gram embeddings. 
Word profiles employ standard distributional semantics without sense disambiguation.
Mention sense profiles also utilize standard distributional semantics, but *with* sense disambiguation. This disambiguation leverages a sense-based profile (a ‘language model’) derived from local context words and neighboring mentions, as briefly mentioned before Section 4, though lacking detail. This lack of detail is a critical weakness. How are senses created and differentiated? What determines the number of senses associated with a mention string? If this determination relies on the knowledge base, it implies a bijective mapping between mention senses and entities – a one-to-one correspondence, even if multiple entities exist. Consequently, are the definitional profiles for these sense collections initialized with entity text as ‘seed words’? If so, what information present at the mention sense level is *not* incorporated at the entity level? Specifically, are these additional words that reliably co-occur with the mention sense but are absent from the corresponding Wikipedia entity page? What is the average number of such words per mention sense? Ultimately, how significant and necessary is maintaining this differentiating information in a separate space (the mention sense space) versus directly incorporating these words into the Entity space by augmenting the Wikipedia entity pages?

If my understanding is accurate, the authors should explicitly state this in Section 5, as it represents the paper’s core contribution. The claim in Section 6 that “this is the first work to deal with mention ambiguity in the integration of text and knowledge representations” is incorrect. The TAC KBP evaluations over the past two years have featured EDL tasks involving eight to nine systems, operating on Freebase (a considerably larger and noisier knowledge base than Wikipedia). Refer to http://nlp.cs.rpi.edu/kbp/2016/ for more information. 

Positively, I appreciated the smoothing parameter introduced in Section 6.4.2.

Post-response: I have reviewed the authors’ response. I remain unpersuaded by their argument regarding the irrelevance of the KBP evaluation, particularly their assertion that their focus lies on the quality of the embeddings themselves. The validity of embedding quality can only be established through application-based evaluation; conceptual elegance alone is insufficient. The critical question remains: does this approach lead to improved performance?
