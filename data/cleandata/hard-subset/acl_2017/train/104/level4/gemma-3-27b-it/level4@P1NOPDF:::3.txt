## Review

**Strengths:** The paper presents good ideas, employs a simple neural learning approach, demonstrates interesting performance (though not groundbreaking), and offers a large set of potential applications.

**Weaknesses:** The amount of novel content is somewhat limited, and the clarity could be improved in certain sections.

This paper introduces a neural learning method for entity disambiguation and linking. A key strength is the idea of integrating entity, mention, and sense modeling within a single neural language modeling framework. This simplicity in training, coupled with the modeling approach, enables support for a wide range of applications.

The paper is formally clear, but the depth of discussion doesn’t consistently match the sophistication of the technical ideas. 

The empirical evaluation is sound, though the reported performance improvements are not dramatic. While the work appears to build upon Yamada et al. (CoNLL 2016), it introduces novel concepts and is therefore of relevant interest to the community.

The paper’s weaker points include:

*   **Clarity of Presentation:** The prose isn't always clear. I found Section 3 particularly difficult to follow. Some details in Figure 2 are unexplained, and the terminology feels somewhat redundant. For example, what is the justification for distinguishing between a “dictionary of mentions” and a “dictionary of entity-mention pairs”? Are these concepts distinct from text anchors and types for annotated text anchors?
*   **Related Work Discussion:** The paper is closely related to Yamada et al. (2016), and the authors should more explicitly outline the key differences between the two approaches.

**General Observation:**

The paper evaluates the Multiple Embedding model on entity linking/disambiguation tasks. However, word embeddings are utilized in a much broader range of tasks than just those directly involving knowledge base entities – for instance, parsing, coreference resolution, and semantic role labeling. The authors should demonstrate that the word embeddings produced by their proposed MPME method are not inferior to those generated by simpler word embedding spaces when applied to these other semantic tasks, specifically those involving entity mentions.
