This paper addresses the problem of disambiguating and linking textual entity mentions into a given background knowledge base (English Wikipedia). While the title and introduction may be somewhat overblown – bridging text and knowledge encompasses more than just the Entity Disambiguation (EDL) task – EDL is nonetheless a core component of the broader challenge. The method proposes bridging this gap via an intermediate layer of representation, specifically mention senses, following a two-step process: (1) mention to mention sense, and (2) mention sense to entity. Various embedding representations are learned for words, mention senses, and entities, and jointly trained to maximize a single objective function, equally weighting all three embedding types.

Technically, the approach is sound and aligns with current deep learning practices and established best practices regarding embeddings. While alternative approaches could be considered, it’s unclear if they would yield substantial improvements. My primary concerns lie with the fundamental approach itself. The paper does not adequately explain *why* a two-step process, involving mention senses, is superior to a simpler, direct one-step mapping from word mentions to their entities – an approach exemplified by Yamada et al.’s ALIGN algorithm. Table 2 demonstrates that the two-step MPME (and its simplification, SPME) outperform ALIGN, but the rationale for this improvement remains unclear. What specific additional information do mention senses provide compared to entities? To clarify, please verify the following understanding and update the paper accordingly.

The entity profiles consist of neighboring entities within a relatedness graph, presumably constructed by analyzing word-level relatedness within entity definitions (Wikipedia pages). These profiles are represented as (extended skip-gram-based) embeddings. Word profiles utilize standard distributional semantics without sense disambiguation. Mention sense profiles, conversely, employ standard distributional semantics *with* sense disambiguation, leveraging a sense-based profile (a ‘language model’) derived from local context words and neighboring mentions, as briefly noted before Section 4, but without sufficient detail. This lack of detail is a critical weakness. How are senses created and differentiated? What determines the number of senses a mention string can have? If this determination relies on the knowledge base, it implies a bijective mapping between mention senses and entities – meaning each mention sense corresponds to exactly one entity (even if multiple entities exist for the mention string). If so, are the sense profiles initially seeded with entity text? If that's the case, what information is present at the mention sense level that isn’t already available at the entity level? Specifically, are these simply the words that reliably associate with the mention sense but do not appear on the corresponding Wikipedia page? On average, how many such words exist for a given mention sense? How significant and necessary is maintaining this extra differentiation information in a separate space (the mention sense space) versus integrating these additional words directly into the Entity space by augmenting the Wikipedia entity pages?

If this understanding is accurate, please explicitly state it in Section 5 of the paper, as it represents the primary novel contribution. The claim in Section 6 that “this is the first work to deal with mention ambiguity in the integration of text and knowledge representations, so there is no exact baseline for comparison” is inaccurate. The Text Analysis Conference (TAC) Knowledge Base Population (KBP) evaluations over the past two years have featured EDL tasks, involving eight to nine systems performing precisely this task, albeit against Freebase, a knowledge base considerably larger and more noisy than Wikipedia. See http://nlp.cs.rpi.edu/kbp/2016/.

On a positive note, I appreciated the smoothing parameter introduced in Section 6.4.2.

Post-response: I have reviewed the authors’ response. I remain unconvinced by their argument that the TAC KBP evaluation is irrelevant because they prioritize embedding quality. The only meaningful way to assess embedding ‘quality’ is through downstream applications. Conceptual elegance is insufficient; the crucial question is whether the embeddings lead to improved performance.
