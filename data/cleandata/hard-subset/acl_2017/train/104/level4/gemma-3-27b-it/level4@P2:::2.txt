This paper tackles the problem of entity disambiguation and linking (EDL) – mapping textual mentions to entities in a knowledge base (English Wikipedia). While the paper frames this as a broad challenge of integrating text and knowledge, the core contribution lies within the EDL task. The proposed method employs a two-step process: (1) mapping mentions to mention senses, and (2) mapping mention senses to entities. It learns embeddings for words, mention senses, and entities, jointly trained to optimize a single objective function. 

The technical approach is sound, utilizing current deep learning practices for embeddings. However, the core justification for the two-step process, involving intermediate mention senses, isn’t clearly articulated. The paper demonstrates (Table 2) that this approach (MPME and SPME) outperforms a direct one-step mapping (like the ALIGN algorithm by Yamada et al.). The key question is *why*? What unique information do mention senses provide compared to entities?

My understanding of the approach, and a potential key contribution of the paper, is as follows: entity profiles are built from related entities in a knowledge graph, derived from word-level relatedness of entity definitions (Wikipedia pages) and represented as embeddings. Word profiles utilize standard distributional semantics *without* sense disambiguation. Mention sense profiles, however, employ distributional semantics *with* sense disambiguation, using a language model based on local context words and neighboring mentions (though details on this are limited). The critical point is understanding how these senses are created and differentiated – how many senses are allowed per mention, and how are they defined? If the senses are primarily derived from entities, the mapping becomes largely bijective, raising the question of what novel information the mention sense level provides. Specifically, does the mention sense level capture words associated with the mention that *aren’t* present on the corresponding Wikipedia page? If so, how significant is this additional information, and would simply incorporating those words into the entity profiles be sufficient? Clarification on these points would significantly strengthen the paper.

Furthermore, the claim in Section 6 that this work is the first to address mention ambiguity in text-knowledge integration is inaccurate. The TAC KBP evaluations for the past two years have featured EDL tasks with numerous participating systems (albeit against Freebase), providing relevant baselines for comparison. (See http://nlp.cs.rpi.edu/kbp/2016/.)

Positively, the smoothing parameter described in Section 6.4.2 is a valuable addition.

**Contributions (as I see them):**

1.  **Empirical Demonstration of Multi-Prototype Mention Embeddings:** The paper presents a strong empirical result demonstrating that learning multiple sense embeddings for mentions, combined with a disambiguation method, improves entity linking performance, achieving state-of-the-art results on a benchmark dataset.
2.  **A Joint Learning Framework for Text and Knowledge:** The framework for jointly learning word, entity, and mention sense embeddings is well-defined and contributes to a unified representation space.
3.  **Practical Disambiguation Approach:** The language model-based approach for disambiguating mentions to specific senses, though requiring further detail, presents a practical solution to a key challenge in entity linking.

**Strengths:**

1.  **Strong Empirical Results:** The paper demonstrates state-of-the-art performance on the entity linking task, providing compelling evidence for the effectiveness of the proposed approach. The quantitative results in Table 2 are significant.
2.  **Clear Technical Presentation:** The methodology is clearly described, and the use of embeddings aligns with current best practices in the field.
3.  **Well-Defined Objective Function:** The use of a single objective function to jointly train all embedding types simplifies the training process and encourages a cohesive representation space.
4.  **Practical Innovation:** The smoothing parameter introduced in Section 6.4.2 demonstrates a thoughtful consideration of practical challenges in the application of the model.

**Weaknesses:**

1.  **Lack of Justification for Two-Step Process:** The core innovation of using intermediate mention senses lacks a clear theoretical or empirical justification. The paper needs to explain *why* this two-step approach is superior to a direct mapping from mentions to entities.
2.  **Insufficient Detail on Sense Disambiguation:** The description of the sense disambiguation process (using a sense-based profile and local context) is vague and lacks critical details, hindering reproducibility and understanding. Specifically, how are senses created, and what criteria are used to determine the number of senses per mention?
3.  **Ignoring Relevant Prior Work:** The claim of novelty regarding addressing mention ambiguity overlooks the substantial body of work in the TAC KBP evaluations, which have tackled the same problem for several years. This oversight undermines the paper’s positioning within the existing literature.
4.  **Unclear Contribution of Mention Sense Level:** It is unclear what unique information is captured at the mention sense level that is not already present in the entity representations. If the senses are strongly tied to entities, the added complexity may not be justified.

**Questions to Authors:**

1.  Could you please provide a more detailed explanation of the sense disambiguation process, including the criteria for determining the number of senses per mention and the algorithm used to create the sense-based profiles?
2.  What specific information is captured at the mention sense level that is *not* present at the entity level? Please quantify this, for example, by providing the average number of unique words associated with a mention sense that are not found on its corresponding Wikipedia page.
3.  How does the performance of your system compare to the top-performing systems from the TAC KBP entity linking tasks (e.g., those described at http://nlp.cs.rpi.edu/kbp/2016/)?
4.  Can you provide a more intuitive explanation of why the two-step process with mention senses improves performance compared to a direct mapping from mentions to entities? What specific challenges does this approach address?
