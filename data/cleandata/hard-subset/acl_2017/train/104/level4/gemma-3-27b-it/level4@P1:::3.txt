The paper proposes a Multi-Prototype Mention Embedding (MPME) model to address ambiguity in jointly embedding text and knowledge. The core idea is to learn multiple sense embeddings for each mention by combining textual contexts and entities from a knowledge base, along with a language model-based disambiguation approach.

**Contributions:**

1.  **A novel approach to mention embedding:** The paper introduces a method for creating multiple embeddings per mention, capturing different senses based on both textual context and knowledge base entities. This is a significant step towards more nuanced representation of ambiguous mentions.
2.  **Effective disambiguation method:** The proposed language model-based approach to disambiguate mentions to a specific sense demonstrates a practical application of the multi-prototype embeddings and achieves strong performance on entity linking.
3.  **Strong empirical results:** The authors demonstrate state-of-the-art results on entity linking, validating the effectiveness of their approach.

**Strengths:**

1.  **Sound technical approach:** The integration of textual and knowledge-based information into a multi-prototype embedding space is a conceptually strong and well-motivated approach. The use of a language model for disambiguation is a natural and effective choice.
2.  **State-of-the-art performance:** Achieving state-of-the-art results on the entity linking task provides compelling evidence for the effectiveness of the proposed model. This is a significant accomplishment.
3.  **Clear formal presentation:** The paper is generally well-structured and formally clear, making it easy to understand the proposed method. The neural network architecture is described in a straightforward manner.
4. **Practical applicability:** The simplicity of the training procedure allows for the application of the model to a wide range of downstream tasks, increasing its potential impact.

**Weaknesses:**

1.  **Clarity of specific details:** Section 3 could benefit from more detailed explanations. Certain aspects of Figure 2 are unclear, and the terminology (e.g., “dictionary of mentions”, “dictionary of entity-mention pairs”) feels redundant. A clarification of the relationship between these dictionaries and concepts like “text anchors” and “annotated text anchors” would be helpful.
2.  **Limited novelty discussion:** The paper would be strengthened by a more thorough comparison to related work, particularly Yamada et al. (2016). Explicitly outlining the differences and advancements offered by the MPME model is crucial.
3.  **Scope of evaluation:** The current evaluation focuses primarily on entity linking/disambiguation. While this is a strong validation, the authors should consider demonstrating the quality of the learned word embeddings on tasks that *don't* directly rely on knowledge base entities, such as parsing, coreference resolution, or semantic role labeling. This would provide a more comprehensive assessment of the embeddings’ general quality.

**Questions to Authors:**

1.  Could you provide a more detailed explanation of the different dictionaries used in Section 3 (e.g., dictionary of mentions, dictionary of entity-mention pairs) and their relationship to other concepts like text anchors?
2.  What are the key distinctions between your proposed MPME model and the work presented in Yamada et al. (2016)? A detailed comparison would be valuable.
3.  Have you considered evaluating the quality of the learned word embeddings on tasks beyond entity linking, such as parsing or coreference resolution? If so, what were the results? If not, are you planning to do so?
