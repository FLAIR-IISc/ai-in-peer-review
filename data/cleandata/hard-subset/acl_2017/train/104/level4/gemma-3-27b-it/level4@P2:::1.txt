This paper proposes a Multi-Prototype Mention Embedding model to address ambiguity in jointly embedding text and knowledge. The core idea is to learn multiple sense embeddings for each mention by integrating textual contexts and knowledge base entities, followed by a language model-based disambiguation step. The authors demonstrate state-of-the-art performance on entity linking.

**Contributions:**

1. **Novel Multi-Prototype Mention Embedding:** The paper introduces a method for generating multiple embeddings per mention, effectively capturing different senses based on both textual context and knowledge base information. This is a significant approach to handling the inherent ambiguity of entity mentions.
2. **Effective Disambiguation via Language Models:** The use of a language model to disambiguate mentions to specific senses appears to be a strong component of the framework, and contributes to the overall performance.
3. **Strong Empirical Results:** The paper achieves state-of-the-art results on a benchmark entity linking dataset, demonstrating the practical effectiveness of the proposed approach.

**Strengths:**

* **Superior Performance:** The reported performance improvement over ALIGN on the supervised entity linking task suggests a meaningful advancement in jointly representing text and knowledge. This provides strong evidence for the effectiveness of the proposed framework.
* **Direct Comparative Analysis:** The paper includes a direct comparison to a closely related method (ALIGN), facilitating a better understanding of the benefits of the proposed approach. 
* **Insightful Parameter Analysis:** The analysis of the smoothing parameter provides valuable insight into the role of entity popularity, a recurring challenge in entity linking, and demonstrates a thoughtful approach to model design.
* **Clear Presentation of Entity Linking Results:** The paper clearly demonstrates improvements in entity linking, a relevant and challenging NLP task.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Incomplete Comparison to ALIGN:** The comparison with ALIGN is hampered by differing hyperparameters (content window size, vector dimension). It's unclear how much of the performance difference stems from the proposed method versus these implementation details. Furthermore, the definition of the neighborhood N(ej) isn't entirely clear; specifically, whether it represents incoming or outgoing links in the Wikipedia graph, and whether the graph is treated as directed or undirected. This ambiguity makes it difficult to assess the impact of differences in graph construction.
* **Terminology Ambiguity:** The inconsistent use of "mention" – sometimes referring to a string type and other times a specific instance in a document – can lead to confusion.  The phrase "mention embedding" specifically suggests embeddings for senses, but it is not always clear.
* **Limited Disambiguation Benchmarking:** The paper lacks a thorough comparison to other unsupervised entity linking methods, making it challenging to determine the unique contribution of the proposed sense disambiguation order.  The effectiveness of the disambiguation component would be better established with broader benchmarking.

**Questions to Authors:**

1. Could you clarify the definition of N(ej) and whether the Wikipedia graph is treated as directed or undirected in your implementation?
2.  How much of the performance difference between your model and ALIGN can be attributed to the differing hyperparameters (window size, vector dimension) rather than the proposed methodology?  Have you considered conducting experiments with aligned hyperparameters?
3.  Could you provide more precise definitions of "mention" throughout the paper to avoid ambiguity?
4.  How does the performance of your disambiguation method compare to other state-of-the-art unsupervised entity linking methods that do *not* utilize multi-prototype embeddings?

**Additional Comments:**

The references appear to be formatted inconsistently and contain numerous errors/repetitions. Please review and standardize the bibliography.
