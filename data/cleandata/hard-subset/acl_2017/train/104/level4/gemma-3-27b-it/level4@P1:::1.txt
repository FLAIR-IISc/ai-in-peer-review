The paper proposes a Multi-Prototype Mention Embedding model to address the ambiguity of entity mentions when integrating text and knowledge. The core idea is to learn multiple sense embeddings for each mention by jointly modeling textual contexts and entities from a knowledge base, and then employ a language model-based approach for disambiguation.

The primary contributions of this work, as I see them, are: (1) the introduction of a multi-prototype mention embedding approach that effectively captures multiple senses of entity mentions by integrating textual and knowledge-based information, and (2) a demonstration of the effectiveness of this approach in the entity linking task, achieving state-of-the-art results on a benchmark dataset. The analysis of the smoothing parameter and its impact on entity linking performance is also a valuable addition.

**Strengths:**

*   The paper demonstrates strong performance in a supervised entity linking task, outperforming the ALIGN model. This suggests the proposed framework learns improved representations of text and knowledge when used jointly.
*   The authors provide a direct comparison to a closely related approach (ALIGN), using similar input data. This allows for a focused evaluation of the proposed method's advantages.
*   The analysis of the smoothing parameter is insightful, addressing the common challenge of popularity bias in entity linking. Understanding the impact of this parameter is valuable for practical applications.
*   The qualitative analysis strengthens the findings and provides further insights into the learned embeddings and disambiguation process.

**Weaknesses:**

*   The comparison with ALIGN could be more thorough. There are differences in hyperparameters (content window size of 10 vs. 5, vector dimension of 500 vs. 200) and potentially in the definition of the neighborhood graph N(ej). It’s unclear if N(ej) includes only entities that link *to* ej, and whether the graph is treated as directed or undirected, which significantly impacts the learned vectors. A more controlled comparison addressing these factors would strengthen the claim of improvement.
*   The terminology surrounding “mention” is occasionally ambiguous. It’s not always clear whether "mention" refers to a string type or a specific instance of that string within a document. The phrase "mention embedding" implies embeddings are learned for all mentions, but the paper focuses on learning embeddings for mention *senses*. Clarification is needed.
*   It's difficult to assess the contribution of the sense disambiguation component in isolation. A comparison with other unsupervised entity linking methods would help establish the value added by the proposed disambiguation approach.

**Questions to Authors:**

*   Could you clarify the precise definition of N(ej) and whether it considers only entities linking *to* ej? Is the graph treated as directed or undirected?
*   Can you provide a more detailed ablation study that isolates the contributions of the multi-prototype embedding and the sense disambiguation component?
*   How sensitive are the results to the choice of hyperparameters, particularly the window size and embedding dimension?

**Additional Comments:**

*   The reference list contains numerous repetitions and inconsistencies in formatting. Please review and standardize the citations.
*   The paper would benefit from a more intuitive explanation of the language model used for disambiguation.
