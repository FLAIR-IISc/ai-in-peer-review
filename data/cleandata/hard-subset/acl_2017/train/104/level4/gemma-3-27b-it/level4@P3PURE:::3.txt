**Review:**

**Strengths:** This paper presents good ideas, employs a simple neural learning approach, demonstrates interesting performance (although not dramatic), and boasts a large set of potential applications.

**Weaknesses:** The amount of novel content is relatively limited, and clarity could be improved in certain sections.

The paper introduces a neural learning method for entity disambiguation and linking. A key idea is the integration of entity, mention, and sense modeling within a unified neural language modeling framework. This simplicity in training, coupled with the modeling approach, enables support for a wide range of applications. 

The paper is formally clear, but the depth of discussion does not consistently match the technical sophistication of the ideas. The empirical evaluation is sound, although the performance improvements reported are not striking. While appearing to extend the work of Yamada et al. (CoNLL 2016), the paper does contribute novel ideas and is of relevant interest to the community.

The weaker aspects of the paper include:

*   The prose is not always clear. Specifically, Section 3 lacks clarity in places. Certain details within Figure 2 are unexplained, and the terminology feels somewhat redundant; for example, the distinction between a "dictionary of mentions" and a “dictionary of entity-mention pairs” is unclear, as is their relationship to text anchors and types for annotated text anchors.
*   The work is closely related to Yamada et al. (2016), and the authors should more explicitly outline the key differences.

A general observation regarding the current version is:

The paper evaluates the Multiple Embedding model on entity linking/disambiguation tasks. However, word embeddings are employed in tasks beyond entity linking, including processes not directly reliant on knowledge base entities such as parsing, coreference resolution, and semantic role labeling. The authors should demonstrate that the word embeddings produced by the proposed MPME method do not perform *worse* than simpler wordspaces on these other semantic tasks—specifically, those involving direct entity mentions.
