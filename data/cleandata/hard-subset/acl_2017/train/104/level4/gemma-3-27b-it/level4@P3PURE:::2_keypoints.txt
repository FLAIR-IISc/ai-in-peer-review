- Paper tackles entity disambiguation/linking to Wikipedia using an intermediate “mention sense” layer, but the title/intro overstate scope beyond EDL.  
- Method: two-step pipeline (mention → mention sense → entity) with jointly trained embeddings for words, mention senses, and entities, equally weighted.  
- Approach is technically sound and follows established embedding/deep-learning practices.  
- Main concern: paper fails to justify why the two-step mention-sense intermediary is superior to a direct mention→entity mapping (e.g., ALIGN).  
- Empirical gains over ALIGN are shown (Table 2) but the paper does not explain what extra information mention senses provide.  
- Entity profiles appear to be neighbor-based embeddings from a relatedness graph; word profiles use standard distributional semantics.  
- Mention-sense profiles allegedly use sense-disambiguated distributional profiles, but details are lacking.  
- Key methodological questions: how are senses created/differentiated, how many senses per mention, and do senses map bijectively to entities?  
- If sense profiles are seeded from entity text, what unique information do they capture beyond entity pages—are they just additional context words, and how many on average?  
- Need justification whether maintaining a separate mention-sense space is necessary versus augmenting entity representations with those additional words.  
- Claim of being “first” to handle mention ambiguity in text–KB integration is incorrect; prior TAC KBP EDL evaluations and systems should be cited and compared.  
- Positive: the smoothing parameter in Section 6.4.2 is a useful addition.  
- Post-response critique: reviewer remains unconvinced; argues embedding quality must be validated by downstream tasks, not just conceptual arguments.
