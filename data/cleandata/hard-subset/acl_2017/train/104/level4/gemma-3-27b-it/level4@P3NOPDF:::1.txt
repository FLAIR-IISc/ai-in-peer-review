## Review for ACL 2017

This paper presents a joint learning framework for entity linking that simultaneously learns representations for text and knowledge graph entities. The approach leverages both local context and global graph structure to generate embeddings, aiming to improve performance on entity linking tasks. Overall, the paper demonstrates promising results and offers insightful analysis. However, some aspects of the experimental setup and presentation could be clarified to strengthen the contribution.

**Strengths:**

The key strength of this work lies in its ability to outperform the ALIGN model on a supervised entity linking task. This performance gain strongly suggests that the proposed framework effectively learns improved representations of both text and knowledge graph entities through their joint embedding. The direct comparison to ALIGN, a closely related approach, is valuable and allows for a nuanced assessment of the presented method. Furthermore, the analysis of the smoothing parameter is a particularly insightful contribution. Addressing the impact of entity popularity is a persistent challenge in entity linking, and providing analysis on this aspect is both relevant and useful to the community.

**Weaknesses:**

While the comparison with ALIGN is appreciated, its fairness and interpretability could be improved. Significant differences in hyperparameters exist between the two models – ALIGN utilizes a content window size of 10 while this work employs a window size of 5, and the vector dimensions differ (500 for ALIGN versus 200 in this paper). These discrepancies make it challenging to isolate the impact of the proposed joint learning framework. 

A key point of confusion arises in the definition of N(ej). It is unclear whether this neighborhood includes *only* entities that link *to* ej, or whether it is a more comprehensive set. Given the directed nature of the Wikipedia outlink graph used, clarifying whether adjacency is treated as in an undirected graph is crucial. ALIGN defines the context of an entity as the set of entities that link to it. If N(e_j) is defined differently, it is difficult to ascertain the contribution of this change to the observed performance differences, particularly on the entity similarity task. A more thorough ablation study or sensitivity analysis addressing these hyperparameters and the neighborhood definition would be beneficial.

The terminology used throughout the paper could also benefit from greater precision. The term “mention” is occasionally ambiguous, oscillating between referring to a string and a specific instance of that string within a document. While “mention embedding” is employed, the paper seems to learn embeddings for mention *senses* rather than mentions themselves. Clarifying this distinction would improve readability and prevent potential misunderstandings.

Finally, assessing the impact of the proposed sense disambiguation order is difficult without a more comprehensive comparison to other established unsupervised entity linking methods. While ALIGN provides a relevant baseline, contrasting the method against other popular unsupervised approaches (e.g., those based on distant supervision or clustering) would better contextualize its performance and highlight the advantages of the proposed order.

**General Discussion:**

This paper presents a solid contribution to the field of entity linking. The joint learning framework offers a promising approach for improving entity representations, and the demonstrated performance gains are encouraging. However, addressing the weaknesses outlined above – particularly regarding the fairness of the comparison with ALIGN and the clarity of terminology – would significantly strengthen the paper. The analysis of the smoothing parameter is a valuable addition, and further exploration of this aspect, alongside a broader comparison with existing unsupervised methods, would increase the impact of this work. 

I recommend acceptance with minor revisions addressing the points raised above. Specifically, the authors should:

*   Provide a more detailed discussion of the hyperparameter choices and their potential impact on the comparison with ALIGN. Consider conducting sensitivity analyses.
*   Clarify the definition of N(ej) and whether adjacency is treated as directed or undirected.
*   Ensure consistent and precise use of the term “mention.”
*   Include a comparison with additional unsupervised entity linking baselines.
