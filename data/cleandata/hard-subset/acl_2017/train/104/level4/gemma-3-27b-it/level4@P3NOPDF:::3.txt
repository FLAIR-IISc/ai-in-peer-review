## Review for ACL 2017

This paper presents a neural learning method for entity disambiguation and linking, integrating entity, mention, and sense modeling within a unified neural language modeling framework. The core idea is sound and the simplicity of the training procedure is a notable strength, enabling application to a diverse range of tasks. The empirical evaluation is solid, though the performance improvements, while positive, are not substantial. Overall, this work builds upon prior research (specifically Yamada et al., CoNLL 2016) and introduces novel elements that are of relevant interest to the ACL community.

**Strengths:**

The paper’s strengths lie in its conceptual simplicity and broad applicability. The proposed approach leverages neural learning in a straightforward manner, making it relatively easy to understand and potentially adapt. The ability to support a large set of applications is a significant benefit, demonstrating the versatility of the model. The authors correctly identify the benefits of a joint modeling approach for entity disambiguation and linking.

**Weaknesses:**

The primary weaknesses of this paper relate to clarity and the extent of novel contribution. While formally presented, the discussion occasionally lacks the same level of technical rigor as the method itself. Specifically, Section 3 could benefit from a more detailed explanation, and certain elements of Figure 2 require further clarification. The terminology used is sometimes redundant; for example, the distinction between the “dictionary of mentions” and the “dictionary of entity-mention pairs” is unclear, as is their relationship to “text anchors and types for annotated text anchors.” 

Furthermore, the paper's relationship to Yamada et al. (2016) is not adequately addressed. A clearer outline of the differences and advancements over this prior work would strengthen the contribution. The authors should explicitly highlight *what* is novel in their approach beyond simply extending the previous model.

**Detailed Comments & Questions:**

*   **Clarity:** Section 3 requires more elaboration on the specific modeling choices and their rationale. The connection between the different components should be made more explicit.
*   **Figure 2:** Several details in Figure 2 are not sufficiently explained. Providing a more detailed caption or expanding on the relevant sections in the text would improve comprehension.
*   **Terminology:** As mentioned above, the various dictionaries and anchor terms are confusingly defined. A consistent and precise terminology is crucial.
*   **Related Work:** A more thorough discussion of related work, particularly Yamada et al. (2016), is necessary. The authors should explicitly position their work within the existing literature and delineate its unique contributions.

**General Observation & Important Consideration:**

The paper evaluates the Multiple Embedding model on entity linking and disambiguation tasks. However, word embeddings generated by such models are frequently used in other NLP tasks *not* directly reliant on knowledge base entities, such as parsing, coreference resolution, and semantic role labeling. Consequently, it's crucial to demonstrate that the word embeddings produced by the proposed MPME method are not inferior to those obtained from simpler word embedding spaces (e.g., Word2Vec, GloVe) in these broader semantic tasks – specifically, tasks that involve processing entity mentions but aren’t solely focused on entity linking. Establishing this would further validate the overall utility of the MPME approach. Without this demonstration, it’s difficult to assess whether the benefits of the joint modeling outweigh potential drawbacks in terms of general-purpose word representation quality.

**Overall Assessment:**

This paper presents a promising approach to entity disambiguation and linking. While the performance gains are modest, the conceptual clarity and potential for broad application are noteworthy. Addressing the weaknesses related to clarity, terminology, and novelty will significantly strengthen the paper. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions, contingent upon the authors addressing the points raised above, particularly a clearer comparison to Yamada et al. (2016) and a demonstration of the general-purpose utility of the generated word embeddings.
