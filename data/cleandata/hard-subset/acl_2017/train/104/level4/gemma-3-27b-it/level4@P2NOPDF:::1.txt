This paper presents a novel framework for jointly learning text and knowledge representations for entity linking. The approach demonstrates promising results and offers valuable insights into the challenges of entity linking, particularly concerning the impact of entity popularity.

**Strengths:**

The paper’s key strength lies in its performance, surpassing that of the ALIGN model on a supervised entity linking task. This improvement strongly suggests that the proposed joint learning framework effectively enhances the quality of both text and knowledge representations. Furthermore, the authors provide a direct comparison to a highly relevant, closely related approach using similar input data, which is commendable. The analysis of the smoothing parameter is particularly insightful, as effectively managing the influence of entity popularity is a persistent and crucial issue in entity linking research. This analysis adds significant value to the work.

**Weaknesses:**

While the comparison to ALIGN demonstrates an improvement, certain aspects of it could be clarified to strengthen the conclusions. Specifically, there are differences in hyperparameters – ALIGN utilizes a content window size of 10 versus 5 in this paper, and a vector dimension of 500 compared to 200 here. These differing settings make it difficult to isolate the impact of the proposed framework from the impact of these parameter choices. Additionally, the definition of N(e<sub>j</sub>) requires clarification. It’s unclear whether N(e<sub>j</sub>) encompasses solely entities *linking to* e<sub>j</sub>. Given that the knowledge graph is directed (based on Wikipedia outlinks), the paper should explicitly state whether adjacency is treated as it would be in an undirected graph.  ALIGN defines the context of an entity as the set of entities linking *to* it.  If N(e<sub>j</sub>) is defined differently, the contribution of this difference to the observed improvements in entity similarity scores remains unclear and should be addressed.

Another area for improvement concerns the terminology used throughout the paper. The meaning of “mention” sometimes feels ambiguous, shifting between referring to a string of text and a specific mention instance within a document. While the term “mention embedding” is used, the paper seems to learn embeddings for mention *senses* rather than mentions themselves. This inconsistency could be addressed through more precise language. 

Finally, evaluating the impact of the proposed sense disambiguation order is challenging without a comparison to other established unsupervised entity linking methods. A more comprehensive comparison would better highlight the benefits, or potential drawbacks, of this specific approach to sense disambiguation. 

**General Discussion:**

Overall, this paper presents a valuable contribution to the field of entity linking. The proposed framework shows promising results and the analysis of the smoothing parameter is particularly strong. Addressing the weaknesses outlined above – specifically clarifying the comparison with ALIGN, ensuring consistent terminology, and providing a more thorough evaluation of the sense disambiguation order – would significantly strengthen the paper and its conclusions. The work is well-motivated, and the findings are relevant to the wider research community working on entity linking and knowledge representation learning.
