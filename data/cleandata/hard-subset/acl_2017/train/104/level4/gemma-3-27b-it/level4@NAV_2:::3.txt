This paper proposes a Multi-Prototype Mention Embedding (MPME) model to address ambiguity in semantic spaces arising from multiple senses of entity mentions. The core idea is to learn multiple sense embeddings for each mention by jointly modeling textual contexts and knowledge base entities, coupled with a language model-based disambiguation approach. The main contributions, as I see them, are: (1) a framework for learning multiple, context-aware embeddings for entity mentions, effectively capturing polysemy; (2) an efficient language model-based disambiguation method to select the appropriate sense embedding for a given mention; and (3) a demonstration of the model’s effectiveness through state-of-the-art results on the entity linking task.

**Strengths:**

The approach presents a compelling method for handling mention ambiguity, which is a significant challenge in integrating text and knowledge. The integration of textual context and knowledge base information within a unified neural framework is well-motivated and executed. The simplicity of the training procedure is a notable strength, allowing for scalability and broad applicability. The empirical results, while not demonstrating dramatic improvements, are solid and establish the effectiveness of the proposed method on a standard benchmark. Finally, the potential for application to a variety of downstream tasks leveraging improved mention representations is significant.

**Weaknesses:**

The clarity of the presentation could be improved. Specifically, Section 3 lacks sufficient detail, and certain aspects of Figure 2 require further explanation. The terminology used, particularly regarding dictionaries of mentions and entity-mention pairs versus text anchors, feels redundant and potentially confusing. The paper’s relationship to prior work, especially Yamada et al. (2016), isn't clearly delineated; a more explicit discussion of the differences is needed. A crucial omission is an evaluation of the quality of the resulting word embeddings on tasks *not* directly tied to entity disambiguation or linking, such as parsing, coreference resolution, or semantic role labeling. Demonstrating that the MPME method doesn't degrade performance on these broader semantic tasks would significantly strengthen the contribution.

**Questions to Authors:**

1.  Could you provide a more detailed explanation of Section 3, specifically elaborating on the implementation details of the multi-prototype mention embedding learning process?
2.  Please clarify the distinction between the “dictionary of mentions” and the “dictionary of entity-mention pairs,” and how these relate to concepts like “text anchors” and “types for annotated text anchors.”
3.  Could you explicitly outline the key differences between your approach and the work presented in Yamada et al. (2016)?
4.  Do you have any plans to evaluate the quality of the learned word embeddings on tasks beyond entity linking and disambiguation, such as parsing or coreference resolution? If not, could you discuss the potential impact of the multi-prototype embedding on such tasks?
