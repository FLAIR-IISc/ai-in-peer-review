Summary and contributions
Based on the references alone (the body of the manuscript is missing/corrupted), the submission appears to be about named entity recognition/linking (NER/EL) or entity disambiguation, combining efficient dictionary-based mention detection (Aho–Corasick; double-array trie) with joint embedding of text and knowledge graphs for disambiguation, possibly with multi-sense word/entity representations and collective graph-based ranking. Because the main text is absent, concrete contributions are not identifiable; the list below reflects what I infer from the bibliography.

- An efficient mention-detection component using classical string-matching (Aho–Corasick) and double-array tries to support large dictionaries at high throughput.
- A joint embedding model that learns representations of words and entities from text and a KG for entity disambiguation, potentially leveraging multi-prototype embeddings for polysemy and collective coherence signals.
- An evaluation framework for entity linking/disambiguation (citing standard datasets and metrics is unclear; DCG is cited).

Strengths
- Practicality and potential impact of an efficient mention detector: If the authors indeed combine Aho–Corasick and double-array structures, that can deliver O(n) matching with low constants and compact memory, a well-known but still practical engineering choice for high-throughput mention detection from large lexicons (e.g., Wikipedia titles/aliases). In production EL systems this matters; a careful implementation can outperform neural taggers for recall while remaining fast.
- Intuitive integration of text-KG joint embeddings for EL: The references to Weston et al. (2013), Wang et al. (2014), Toutanova et al. (2015), Wang & Li (2016), and Han et al. (2016) suggest a unified embedding space for words and entities. This is a sensible foundation for local compatibility scoring (context-to-entity similarity), and, if done well, can simplify candidate ranking and lend itself to efficient retrieval.
- Consideration of collective disambiguation: Citations to graph-ranking and PPR-based methods (Kulkarni et al., Alhelbawy & Gaizauskas, Pershina et al.) hint at global coherence modeling. Combining local embedding-based scores with global coherence often yields measurable gains on AIDA-CoNLL and WNED datasets.
- Awareness of polysemy and multi-sense embeddings: Citing Reisinger & Mooney, Huang et al., Neelakantan et al., Tian et al., Li & Jurafsky indicates an attempt to mitigate word sense conflation. If the method models multiple prototypes or senses, it could improve EL in ambiguous contexts.

Weaknesses
- Incomplete/corrupted submission precludes scientific evaluation: The manuscript body is missing. The “References” section is heavily duplicated, contains severe typographical errors, truncated lines, and extraneous strings (“Confidential Review Copy…”, “played it during public”). Without a clear problem statement, method description, experimental setup, results, or analysis, the paper cannot be assessed for correctness, novelty, or significance. As-is, it is unreviewable.
- Unclear novelty relative to strong baselines: Even if we infer the intended approach, the literature already contains local neural models and collective EL with joint text-entity embeddings (e.g., Ganea & Hofmann 2017; Globerson et al. 2016; Le & Titov 2018; Yamada et al. 2016/2017). More recent methods (BLINK 2020; GENRE/mGENRE 2021–2022; Bootleg 2020; ReFinED 2022; REL 2021; SapBERT-based linkers) have set strong bars. The references stop around 2016–2017 and miss these developments, making it likely that the claimed contributions are incremental unless clearly differentiated.
- No experimental evidence, metrics, or datasets: There is no description of datasets (AIDA-CoNLL, TAC KBP, WNED, GERBIL benchmarks), evaluation metrics (in-KB micro accuracy, micro/macro F1 for mention detection, NIL prediction), or comparisons to recent baselines. The single IR metric citation (DCG) is unusual for EL and suggests potential metric misalignment. Without results, claims of effectiveness or efficiency cannot be validated.
- Reproducibility and training details absent: Critical details such as candidate generation (alias tables, priors), negative sampling, training objectives (skip-gram variants, margin losses), architectural choices, hyperparameters, pretraining corpora, KG coverage, and computational resources are missing. No ablations (e.g., with/without collective inference, with/without multi-sense), no error analysis, and no runtime/memory measurements are provided.
- Related work quality and scholarly presentation: The references contain duplicates, OCR-like errors, missing venues/years, and misattributions. Foundational EL papers (e.g., Ganea & Hofmann 2017) and more recent neural retriever/generator-based linkers are omitted. This undermines credibility and makes it difficult to place the work in context.
- Potential ethical and practical considerations unaddressed: EL systems can propagate biases from KGs and Wikipedia (coverage skew, gender/geography), and raise privacy concerns in some domains. There is no indication these are considered. Practical deployment aspects (domain adaptation, robustness, multilinguality) are also unclear.

Questions to authors
- Please provide the complete manuscript. What is the precise task scope (EL only, NER+EL, cross-lingual EL)? What are the concrete technical contributions beyond prior joint text-KG embedding approaches?
- How is mention detection performed? If dictionary-based, how are aliases curated, lowercased/cased variants handled, and what is the trade-off vs. NER taggers? Do you handle overlapping mentions and nested entities? What are precision/recall figures for mention detection alone?
- Describe candidate generation and priors. Do you use mention-entity prior p(e|m) from Wikipedia hyperlinks/redirects? How do you ensure high recall with manageable candidate set sizes?
- Detail the disambiguation model. What is the embedding objective (skip-gram, joint KG-text, translational constraints)? How do you incorporate context (bag-of-words, attention, transformers), and do you use multi-prototype embeddings? How is global coherence modeled (graph edges, objective, inference algorithm)?
- What datasets and metrics do you use? Please report results on standard EL benchmarks (AIDA-CoNLL test A/B, WNED-ClueWeb/ACE/MSNBC/AQUAINT, TAC KBP if applicable) with standard metrics, and compare to strong baselines: Ganea & Hofmann (2017), Le & Titov (2018), BLINK, GENRE/mGENRE, Bootleg, ReFinED, REL. Include ablations for each component and runtime/memory comparisons for the efficient mention detector.
- How does your approach handle NIL/KB-unknown entities and domain shift? Any experiments on out-of-domain corpora? Multilingual capabilities?
- Will you release code, models, and data preprocessing scripts to ensure reproducibility?

Given the current state (references-only, corrupted), I cannot recommend acceptance. If the authors can submit the full, corrected manuscript with rigorous experiments and clear novelty over recent EL advances, the idea of combining efficient lexicon-based detection with modern disambiguation remains practically interesting.
