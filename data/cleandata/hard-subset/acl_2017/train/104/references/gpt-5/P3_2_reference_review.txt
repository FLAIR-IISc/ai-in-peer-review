Summary
Based on the fragmentary text and the reference list, the submission appears to address named entity linking/disambiguation (EL/NED) by combining:
- Fast dictionary-based candidate generation using classical string-matching data structures (Aho–Corasick automaton and a double-array trie).
- Joint representation learning of words and KB entities with a skip-gram/word2vec-style objective, potentially augmented with knowledge graph structure.
- A downstream ranking and possibly collective disambiguation component (references to graph ranking and personalized PageRank suggest a global coherence stage).
The paper cites a broad set of related work on EL, joint text–KG embedding, and multi-sense embeddings, but the main body (method, experiments) is largely missing from the provided material.

Main contributions (as I see them; inferred)
1) An efficient candidate generation module for EL using an Aho–Corasick-based dictionary matcher instantiated via a double-array trie to scale to very large mention–entity lexicons.
2) A joint embedding model for words and entities trained on text and knowledge graph signals with a word2vec-like objective to produce features for entity ranking/disambiguation.
3) An end-to-end EL system that integrates the above with graph-based global inference.

Strengths
- Practical focus on efficiency: If realized as claimed, combining Aho–Corasick with a double-array trie for mention matching can deliver very high-throughput candidate generation with compact memory, a critical bottleneck in production EL systems. This is well-motivated by the Aho–Corasick and Aoe double-array literature cited and could be a useful engineering contribution to the community if benchmarked rigorously against alternatives.
- Coherent pipeline design: The proposed architecture—dictionary-based candidates, embedding-based local scoring, and graph-based global coherence—is consistent with best practices in EL. The referenced methods (e.g., Hoffart et al., Pershina et al., Cucerzan) suggest the authors are aware of the trade-offs among priors, context models, and graph coherence and are combining them thoughtfully.
- Appropriate learning signal: Using a skip-gram-style objective to tie words and entities together has proven effective in prior work (Toutanova et al., Wang et al., Weston et al., Yamada et al.). If the model integrates KB structure (e.g., via relation-based edges or co-link structure) and textual context, it can provide robust signals for ambiguous mentions and long-tail entities.
- Potential impact and reproducibility: If code and resources for the candidate generator and embedding training are released, this could lower the barrier for building scalable EL systems and support reproducibility.

Weaknesses
- Incomplete/garbled submission: The core scientific content (model specification, training details, datasets, experimental setup, quantitative results) is absent or unreadable in the provided copy. The reference list includes many duplicates, corrupted entries, and inconsistent metadata. As it stands, it is impossible to verify claims, assess novelty, or judge empirical quality. This is a primary reason to reject.
- Unclear novelty relative to prior art: Joint text–KB embeddings for EL and graph-aware disambiguation are well-explored (e.g., Toutanova et al. 2015; Wang et al. 2014; Wang & Li 2016; Han et al. 2016; Yamada et al. 2016/2017; Globerson et al. 2016). Without a precise description, it appears the method may be an incremental combination of known components rather than a fundamentally new model. The efficiency angle is promising, but double-array tries and Aho–Corasick are established; the paper must show substantive new engineering or empirical advances (e.g., orders-of-magnitude speedups at fixed recall).
- Methodological ambiguity: The brief phrase “predict the context words by maximizing…” suggests a standard skip-gram objective. It is unclear how entities are incorporated (linked anchors? joint negatives?), how KG structure is encoded (TransE-like constraints vs. random walks vs. co-link/co-mention), whether multi-sense/multi-prototype embeddings are used, how NIL detection is handled, and how priors (p(entity|mention)) interact with learned scores. Without these details, the contribution cannot be evaluated.
- Missing evaluation protocol: There is no evidence of evaluation on standard EL benchmarks (AIDA-CoNLL, TAC KBP, WNED-wiki), no baselines (Hoffart, Pershina, Yamada, Globerson, Ganea & Hofmann), no metrics (in-KB accuracy, micro/macro, NDCG for ranking), no ablations (e.g., with/without KG edges, with/without coherence), no error analysis, and no efficiency measurements (throughput, latency, memory) for candidate generation and end-to-end inference. These are essential for ACL.
- Writing and presentation quality: Numerous typos, duplicated references, and corrupted entries (e.g., repeated Aho–Corasick and Aoe citations, inconsistent author names and venues) significantly detract from clarity and raise concerns about the care taken in preparing the submission.

Questions to authors
- What is the precise novelty over prior joint text–entity embedding approaches (e.g., Toutanova et al. 2015; Yamada et al. 2016/2017; Wang & Li 2016)? Please specify objective functions, negative sampling schemes, how entities are connected to words, and how KG structure is injected.
- How is candidate generation implemented and evaluated? Please provide recall@K for candidate lists on standard datasets, as well as throughput and memory comparisons against simple hash/dictionary baselines and other trie implementations. What is the maximum supported lexicon size?
- Describe the disambiguation model: features used (priors, embedding similarities, context windows), training regime, and whether you use global coherence (e.g., PPR, loopy belief propagation, pairwise coherence). How do you balance local vs. global signals?
- What datasets and metrics are used? Please report results on AIDA-CoNLL (test A/B), TAC KBP (if applicable), and WNED benchmarks, with standard metrics. Include strong baselines and ablations.
- How do you handle NIL entities and out-of-KB mentions? Is there a calibrated rejection strategy?
- Can you release code/models and the mention–entity lexicon to support reproducibility?

Recommendation
Reject in the current form due to incomplete/garbled content and unclear novelty. The topic and high-level system design are relevant and potentially impactful, particularly the emphasis on efficient candidate generation. I encourage the authors to resubmit with a complete, carefully edited manuscript that clearly articulates methodological contributions and provides rigorous, reproducible empirical validation against strong baselines.
