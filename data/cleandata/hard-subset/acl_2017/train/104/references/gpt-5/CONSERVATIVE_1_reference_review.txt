Summary and claimed contributions
Based on the (garbled) reference list and scattered phrases, the submission appears to target named entity linking/disambiguation (EL/ED). It seems to combine:
- Efficient dictionary-based candidate generation (Aho–Corasick and double-array tries) for high-recall mention-to-entity lookup.
- Joint representation learning of words/senses/entities from text and knowledge graphs, possibly used to rank candidates.
- A graph-based ranking/collective disambiguation component.

Given the absence of a clear method description or results in the provided text, I infer the intended contributions are:
1) An integrated EL pipeline coupling classical, efficient candidate generation with joint text–KG embeddings.
2) A training scheme for joint embeddings oriented toward ED (unclear how it differs from prior art).
3) Empirical improvements in ED quality and/or efficiency (not evidenced in the submission as provided).

Strengths
- Practical direction: Combining fast, deterministic candidate generation (Aho–Corasick; double-array tries) with neural ranking is a sensible, engineering-focused approach. These data structures are well-established and can yield low-latency, high-recall candidate sets in production EL systems.
- Relevant problem and literature grounding: The references (despite being corrupted) suggest the authors are aware of core EL, multi-sense embedding, and joint text–KG embedding works (Hoffart et al., Pershina et al., Kulkarni et al., Toutanova et al., Wang & Li, Han et al., Mikolov, etc.).
- Potential efficiency benefits: If implemented carefully, the proposed candidate generation could be memory- and time-efficient across languages, and amenable to large KBs. Reporting candidate recall vs. dictionary size and latency would be useful to practitioners.

Weaknesses
- Incomplete and non-compliant submission: The provided material appears to be an unedited, corrupted references section, with numerous duplicates, typos, placeholders (e.g., “\Q2017\E”), and stray text (“played it during public”). There is no clear problem statement, method, experimental setup, or results. As it stands, the work is not reviewable for technical correctness, novelty, or empirical validity.
- Novelty is unclear and likely limited: The core ideas—dictionary-based candidate generation, joint word/entity embeddings, and graph-based collective disambiguation—are well-trodden. Prior works (e.g., Yamada et al. 2016/2017, Toutanova et al. 2015, Wang & Li 2016, Han et al. 2016, Ganea & Hofmann 2017) already integrate neural representations with EL pipelines. Without a precise algorithmic contribution and head-to-head comparisons, it is difficult to see what is new.
- Missing rigorous evaluation against established baselines: There is no evidence of experiments on standard datasets (AIDA-CoNLL, TAC KBP/WNED, MSNBC/AQUAINT, GERBIL benchmarks), no comparisons to strong baselines (AIDA, PPR-based systems, TagMe, Babelfy, neural SOTA), and no ablations isolating the impact of candidate generation vs. embedding vs. graph components. Absent such evaluations, claims of effectiveness or efficiency cannot be substantiated.
- Insufficient methodological detail: Key details are absent—how the dictionary is constructed and maintained (redirects, aliases, priors), how NIL/unknown entities are handled, how the joint embedding objective is defined relative to prior work, how negative sampling and sense granularity are managed, what graph signals are used (coherence, priors, PPR), and training data/KB versions. Without these, the work is not reproducible.
- Bibliography and presentation quality: The references contain many duplicates, misspellings, incomplete venues/years, and inconsistent formatting. This falls below ACL standards and raises concerns about overall rigor and care.

Questions to authors
- Scope and novelty: What precisely is the new technical contribution beyond established pipelines that combine dictionary candidate generation and joint text–KG embeddings? How does your embedding objective differ from Toutanova et al. (2015), Wang & Li (2016), Yamada et al. (2016/2017), Han et al. (2016)? Please give a formal definition and theoretical/empirical motivation.
- Candidate generation: How is the mention–entity dictionary built (surface form extraction, redirects, aliases, disambiguation pages, priors)? What are the recall and average candidate set sizes on AIDA-CoNLL dev/test? What is the latency and memory footprint of the double-array trie vs. alternative gazetteer implementations?
- Disambiguation model: What features/signals are used for ranking (contextual similarity, prior p(e|m), coherence via graph edges, document-level constraints)? Is there a collective inference component (e.g., PPR/ILP/CRF)? Please provide ablations for each module.
- Evaluation protocol: Which datasets, splits, and metrics do you use (micro/macro accuracy, in-KB accuracy, NIL detection/linking F1, NDCG)? Do you evaluate both mention detection and disambiguation, or ED given gold mentions? Please compare to strong baselines (AIDA, PPR-based, TagMe, Babelfy, neural models such as Ganea & Hofmann 2017, Le & Titov 2018, Yamada et al. 2016/2017) with identical KB versions.
- Training details: What corpora and KB versions are used (Wikipedia dump date, KG triples)? How are entities initialized and tied to words/senses? What are hyperparameters, optimization settings, and training compute?
- Generalization and robustness: How does the method handle NIL entities, tail entities, and domain shift (news vs. web vs. biomedical)? Do you report per-genre performance and OOV behavior?
- Reproducibility: Will you release code, dictionaries, and trained embeddings? Are there licensing constraints?

Given my preference for rigor and established baselines, I would need a substantially revised, complete submission with clear methodological novelty and thorough, controlled evaluations before considering acceptance. At present, the work is not assessable and appears incremental relative to prior art.
