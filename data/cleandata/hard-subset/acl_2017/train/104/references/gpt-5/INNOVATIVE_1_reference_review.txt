Summary and contributions
Based on the (heavily corrupted) reference list and scattered phrases, the submission appears to address named entity disambiguation/linking (NED/EL) by jointly learning embeddings of words and knowledge base entities from Wikipedia text and links, and coupling this with efficient dictionary-based candidate generation (Aho–Corasick and double-array trie). It likely uses a skip-gram-style objective and simple context scoring, possibly with collective/graph-based ranking references.

As I see them, the primary contributions would be:
1) A joint word–entity embedding model trained on Wikipedia text and anchor links for NED, with a skip-gram-like objective.
2) A practical, scalable candidate generation mechanism using Aho–Corasick/double-array tries to match mention strings against a large lexicon.
3) A straightforward scoring or graph-based ranking method to select entities given context.

Strengths
1) Practical efficiency focus. Leveraging Aho–Corasick/double-array tries for mention detection and candidate lookup is a solid engineering choice that enables low-latency, large-scale EL. If the paper includes empirical throughput/latency numbers on web-scale text, that would be a meaningful practical contribution.
2) Simple, unified representation. Joint word–entity embeddings can reduce feature engineering and enable compact, fast scoring. This line of work is attractive for deployment due to its simplicity (unsupervised pretraining with a small discriminative layer).
3) Literature grounding. The references span core EL baselines (Hoffart et al., Cucerzan, Kulkarni et al., Pershina), multi-sense embeddings, and joint text–KG embedding work (Wang et al., Toutanova et al., Han et al.), suggesting the authors are aware of the field’s foundations.
4) Potential accessibility. If the authors release trained embeddings, a mention–entity dictionary, and code for fast candidate generation, the package could have downstream utility beyond EL.

Weaknesses
1) Limited novelty relative to established work. The central ideas—joint word–entity embeddings trained on Wikipedia links for EL, plus dictionary-based candidate generation—are well-established since at least Yamada et al. (ACL 2017) and multiple successors (e.g., Wikipedia2Vec, LUKE, REL/ReFinED, BLINK-style dense retrieval + cross-encoders, GENRE/mGENRE seq2seq EL). As framed, the submission appears incremental, without a clear, bold conceptual advance. Given my emphasis on novelty, this is the main concern.
2) Missing or outdated comparisons. There is no evidence in the provided text of rigorous evaluation against modern EL baselines and settings (e.g., BLINK, GENRE/mGENRE, Bootleg, ReFinED, Cross-encoder BERT, KILT benchmarks). Without strong, contemporary comparisons and ablations, it is hard to assess significance.
3) Incomplete and low-quality presentation. The provided material is essentially a corrupted, duplicated reference list with typos and placeholders (“Confidential Review Copy,” broken author names/venues, repeated Aho–Corasick entries). This raises concerns about the completeness of the submission and overall polish. Important details (model architecture, training objective, data, hyperparameters, inference, error analysis) are not visible.
4) Unclear technical novelty in the embedding objective. If the training objective is a standard skip-gram with anchors, the paper needs to concretely articulate what is new: e.g., new negative sampling strategies, link-graph regularization, multi-prototype entities, multilingual alignment, or training efficiency improvements. None are evident.
5) Scope of evaluation and generality. It is unclear how the method handles NIL/KB-missing entities, cross-domain and multilingual settings, or robustness under mention noise—common requirements for modern EL. Without these, the impact is limited.

Questions to authors
- What is the precise novelty versus Yamada et al. (2017) and subsequent joint word–entity embedding methods (e.g., Wikipedia2Vec, LUKE)? Please enumerate the differences in objective, architecture, training data, or inference.
- What datasets and benchmarks are used? Please evaluate on standard EL datasets (AIDA-CoNLL/CoNLL-YAGO, WNED, TAC/KILT EL tasks), and include contemporary baselines (BLINK, GENRE/mGENRE, ReFinED, Bootleg, cross-encoder rerankers).
- How are candidates generated and scored? Provide details for the Aho–Corasick/double-array implementation, dictionary size, ambiguity handling, and the source/estimation of p(e|m). Is there collective/global inference or only local scoring?
- What is the training objective? If skip-gram-like, what are the contexts, negative sampling scheme, and any graph regularization or joint text–KG constraints? Any architectural components beyond dot-product (e.g., attention over context, multi-prototype entities)?
- Ablations and efficiency: Provide ablations for candidate prior vs context-only vs joint; embedding dimensionality; graph-based vs local scoring. Report training time, inference throughput, and memory footprint, and compare to dense retrieval/cross-encoder pipelines.
- How do you handle NIL prediction, domain shift (news/web/biomedical), and multilingual/multi-script mentions? Any results beyond English Wikipedia?
- Are resources released (code, embeddings, mention dictionary)? If so, what are the licensing and maintenance plans?

Overall assessment
Given the material available, the submission appears to repackage a well-trodden approach to EL without a clearly articulated, substantive new idea or comprehensive modern evaluation. I am willing to overlook minor flaws for a bold conceptual advance, but here the novelty seems limited and the presentation incomplete. I would encourage the authors to (1) clearly state the new technical ideas, (2) position against strong 2020–2024 baselines on standard benchmarks, and (3) improve clarity, polish, and reproducibility. In its current form, I cannot recommend acceptance.
