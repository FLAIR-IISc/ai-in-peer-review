Summary
From the material provided, the submission appears to tackle named entity disambiguation/linking (EL/NED) by jointly learning representations of words and entities using both text and knowledge graphs. The references suggest a pipeline that includes fast dictionary-based mention detection (Aho–Corasick, double-array tries), candidate generation, embedding-based context scoring, and possibly collective disambiguation via graph ranking or Personalized PageRank. The paper cites prior joint text–KG embedding work (e.g., Wang & Li 2016; Toutanova et al. 2015; Weston et al. 2013; Han et al. 2016) and classic EL baselines (Hoffart et al. 2011; Cucerzan 2007; Pershina et al. 2015; Kulkarni et al. 2009), implying an approach that integrates representation learning with EL inference.

Claimed/Perceived Contributions (ordered by strength)
1) A joint training objective that embeds words and entities in a shared space leveraging both textual co-occurrence and KG structure, used for EL.
2) An efficient mention detection and candidate generation component based on automata/double-array tries enabling high-throughput EL.
3) Empirical improvements over strong EL baselines on standard datasets, potentially measured by ranking metrics (NDCG) and accuracy.

Strengths
1) Potentially strong integration of text and KG signals for EL. Jointly learning word–entity representations is a compelling direction: it can capture semantic relatedness and coherence beyond purely symbolic features. If the method improves over text-only or KG-only baselines, that would be a meaningful contribution for EL.

2) Attention to efficiency in mention detection. The references to Aho–Corasick and double-array trie data structures suggest the system is designed for speed and memory efficiency. If substantiated with throughput and latency numbers on realistic corpora, this is valuable for practical deployment of EL systems.

3) Broad awareness of related work across EL and representation learning. The bibliography (despite formatting issues) covers collective inference, graph ranking, personalized PageRank, multi-prototype embeddings, and joint text–KG models, indicating the authors aim to position their work within the right literature and potentially compare against appropriate baselines.

4) Potentially general-purpose embeddings. If the learned word/entity vectors are released and useful beyond EL (e.g., relation extraction, knowledge completion), the artifact could have impact in the community.

Weaknesses
1) Incomplete and corrupted submission prevents assessment. The provided copy contains only a references section with numerous typos, duplications, and corrupted entries, and almost no details of the method, experiments, or results. Without a clear model description, training objective, or evaluation, novelty and technical correctness cannot be assessed. This is the primary blocker for acceptance.

2) Unclear novelty relative to prior joint models. From the citations, the approach appears close to existing work (e.g., Wang & Li 2016; Toutanova et al. 2015; Weston et al. 2013; Han et al. 2016; Mancini et al. 2016). The submission must precisely articulate what is new: the training objective, negative sampling, use of anchors vs. hyperlinks, incorporation of KG relations, or the inference step. Absent that, the contribution risks being incremental.

3) Missing experimental detail and evidence. Standard EL evaluations (AIDA-CoNLL, TAC-KBP, MSNBC, AQUAINT) and baselines (AIDA, PPRforNED, TagMe, BLINK/REL in newer work) are not described. No information is given about candidate generation (p(e|m) construction, pruning), collective inference (if any), ablations (text-only vs KG-only vs joint), calibration for NIL detection, or statistical significance. Without this, it is impossible to judge effectiveness.

4) Reproducibility and resource release not addressed. There is no mention of code, data preprocessing, hyperparameters, or trained embeddings release. Given the engineering focus implied by efficiency references, reproducibility would be key to impact.

5) Bibliography quality is very poor. Numerous entries are duplicated, garbled, and inconsistent (e.g., multiple corrupted variants of Aho–Corasick and Aoe; broken author names; missing venues/years). This reflects a lack of polish and raises concerns about overall rigor.

Questions to Authors
- What is the exact joint objective? Please specify the components (e.g., skip-gram over text, anchor-based word–entity co-occurrence, KG relation modeling) and how they are weighted. How does it differ from Wang & Li (2016), Toutanova et al. (2015), and Han et al. (2016)?
- How are candidates generated and scored? Describe the mention dictionary construction, p(e|m) estimation, candidate pruning, and whether context-sensitive priors are used.
- Do you perform collective disambiguation? If so, what graph is constructed, what edge weights are used (embedding cosine, link-based coherence), and what inference algorithm (PPR, loopy BP, ILP)?
- What datasets, metrics, and baselines are evaluated? Please include AIDA-CoNLL-YAGO as a minimum, with in-KB accuracy and micro/macro scores. If you use ranking metrics (NDCG), explain how they relate to EL accuracy.
- Provide ablations: (a) text-only vs KG-only vs joint; (b) with/without collective inference; (c) with/without candidate prior; (d) effect of embedding dimension and training data size.
- Efficiency results: throughput (docs/sec), memory footprint, and latency per document for the mention detection and disambiguation stages; comparisons to simpler gazetteer baselines.
- Error analysis: common failure modes (ambiguity, long-tail entities, NIL mentions), and how the model addresses them.

Additional Comments
- Please fix the bibliography: deduplicate, correct author names, titles, venues, and years; follow ACL style. The current state is not acceptable for publication.
- Clearly separate the contributions of the embedding model from gains due to better candidate priors or mention detection; otherwise, improvements may be confounded.
- Position the work against the latest neural EL systems and joint training approaches; if contemporaneous, explain complementary aspects or advantages.
- If feasible, release the trained word/entity embeddings and code to maximize impact.

Recommendation
Given the incomplete and corrupted nature of the submission as provided, I cannot assess technical soundness or empirical merit. If the authors can supply a complete, polished manuscript with rigorous experiments that clearly establish novelty over prior joint text–KG embedding methods and demonstrate state-of-the-art or competitive EL performance with strong ablations and efficiency analysis, the work could be impactful. In its current form, however, it is not ready for acceptance.
