Summary and claimed contributions (as I see them)
- The paper appears to tackle named entity disambiguation/linking by combining efficient dictionary-based candidate generation (citing Aho–Corasick and double-array tries) with joint representation learning of text and knowledge graphs/entities (citing Wang et al., Toutanova et al., Weston et al., Han et al., Yamada et al.). Evaluation seems to use ranking metrics such as NDCG and standard EL datasets (based on references).
- Claimed contributions, as inferred:
  1) A practical, efficient candidate generation module for EL using automata/trie-based string matching at scale.
  2) A joint embedding approach that leverages text and knowledge graph signals for better disambiguation, potentially addressing polysemy via multi-prototype embeddings.
  3) An empirical study comparing to classic collective EL methods and embedding-based baselines.

Strengths
- Practical focus on efficiency: The emphasis on Aho–Corasick and double-array tries suggests attention to high-recall, low-latency candidate generation, which is often the throughput bottleneck in EL systems. If accompanied by quantitative recall and speed benchmarks (e.g., alias recall at K, throughput, memory footprint), this is a meaningful contribution for real-world deployment.
- Integrating text and KG evidence: The related work indicates awareness of, and likely use of, joint text–KG embeddings. If the proposed method cleanly fuses contextual text with KG structure (e.g., through joint losses or multi-task learning) and demonstrates gains over text-only or KG-only baselines, that is a solid modeling contribution.
- Consideration of sense ambiguity: Citations to multi-sense embedding literature (Huang et al., Reisinger & Mooney, Neelakantan et al., Li & Jurafsky, Tian et al.) suggest the model may explicitly address polysemy. If validated with ablations showing improved disambiguation for highly ambiguous mentions, that would strengthen the paper’s impact.
- Potentially comprehensive evaluation: The presence of classic EL references (Cucerzan, Hoffart et al., Kulkarni et al., Pershina et al.) and IR metrics (Järvelin & Kekäläinen) hints at a reasonably broad evaluation protocol. If the paper reports both ranking and entity-level accuracy metrics across multiple datasets, that breadth is a plus.

Weaknesses
- Serious bibliographic and presentation issues: The reference section is rife with typos, duplications, malformed venues/years, truncated titles, inconsistent author attributions, and leakage of internal text (“Confidential Review Copy. DO NOT DISTRIBUTE,” “played it during public”). This level of noise undermines the paper’s professionalism and raises concerns about attention to detail elsewhere (e.g., data, code, reporting). It must be corrected.
- Novelty unclear vs. prior art: The core ideas suggested by the references—efficient dictionary matching for candidate generation and joint text–KG embeddings for EL—have substantial prior work (e.g., Yamada et al. 2017 ACL, Wang et al. 2014, Toutanova et al. 2015; also strong neural EL methods from 2017 onward). The paper needs to articulate a precise, testable novelty claim and back it with ablations. As it stands (from what can be inferred), the methodological contribution risks being incremental.
- Potentially outdated related work and baselines: The bibliography appears to stop around 2016–2017 and omits later influential EL frameworks (bi-encoder/cross-encoder architectures, dense retrieval-based EL, autoregressive generation for EL). If this is a current submission, missing comparisons to stronger modern baselines would limit the paper’s significance.
- Reproducibility risks: For EL, details such as alias dictionary construction (anchors, redirects, variants), KB version, candidate recall at K, negative sampling strategies, and training corpora are critical. Absent clear, complete reporting and artifacts (code, models, dictionaries), it will be hard for others to validate or build upon the work.
- Clarity and polish: Beyond references, the fragments suggest possible copy-editing issues in the main text (truncations like “predict the context words by maximizing the following objective functi”). If representative, clarity is a concern and may impede reader comprehension.

Questions to authors
- Technical novelty: What exactly is new relative to Yamada et al. (ACL 2017), Toutanova et al. (2015), Wang et al. (2014), and classic collective EL systems (Hoffart et al., Pershina et al.)? Please enumerate the novel components and provide ablations isolating their impact.
- Candidate generation: How is the mention–entity dictionary built (anchors, redirects, disambiguation pages, morphological variants)? Why choose a double-array trie versus alternatives? Report candidate recall at K and throughput/memory for large KBs.
- Modeling: How are text and KG signals combined (losses, sampling, architectural fusion)? Do you induce multi-prototype embeddings or otherwise handle polysemy? How do you train negatives and avoid embedding collapse?
- Evaluation: Which datasets and splits are used (e.g., AIDA-CoNLL, TAC KBP, MSNBC, WNED)? What metrics (micro accuracy, in-KB accuracy, NDCG/MRR)? What baselines are included, and do they reflect current state of the art? If not, why?
- Error analysis: What are the dominant failure modes (rare entities, NIL detection, long-tail aliases, domain shift)? How does the method perform on highly ambiguous mentions?
- Reproducibility: Will you release code, pre-trained embeddings, and the mention–entity dictionary? If not, provide enough detail to reimplement.

Overall assessment against ACL criteria
- Originality: Low to moderate as currently positioned; needs a clearer, stronger novelty claim and comparisons.
- Quality: Unclear; depends on thoroughness of experiments and ablations. Presentational issues reduce confidence.
- Clarity: Currently weak due to substantial bibliographic and apparent copy-editing problems; must be improved.
- Significance: Potentially moderate if efficiency/accuracy trade-offs are convincingly superior on strong benchmarks; otherwise limited.

Recommendation: Leaning reject unless the authors (a) clearly articulate and demonstrate methodological novelty over prior joint embedding and modern EL systems, (b) provide strong, up-to-date empirical comparisons and ablations, and (c) substantially fix clarity and bibliographic issues. If these are addressed and results are compelling, the work could be competitive given its practical focus on efficiency.
