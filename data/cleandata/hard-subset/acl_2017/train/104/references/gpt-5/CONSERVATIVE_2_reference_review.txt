Summary
Based on the provided material, the submission appears to target named entity linking/disambiguation (EL), combining efficient dictionary-based mention detection (citing Aho–Corasick and double-array tries) with joint text–knowledge graph embedding for candidate ranking (citing Wang & Li 2016; Toutanova et al. 2015; Han et al. 2016; Yamada et al. 2017). The references also suggest awareness of collective inference (e.g., graph ranking/PageRank) and multi-prototype/word-sense embeddings. However, the main body of the paper is effectively missing: beyond a corrupted/duplicated bibliography with many typos, I cannot find a coherent description of the method, experiments, or results. As such, the claims are impossible to verify.

Contributions (as I see them)
- An engineering pipeline that leverages established dictionary matching (Aho–Corasick with a double-array structure) for efficient mention detection in EL. This, if implemented and measured rigorously, could be a useful efficiency contribution.
- Application of joint text–KG embeddings to entity ranking within the EL pipeline, potentially with collective graph-based reranking. The cited works indicate strong prior art; any novelty would have to be clearly articulated and empirically validated.
- Empirical evaluation on standard EL benchmarks using appropriate metrics (e.g., accuracy/F1/NDCG), although no evidence is provided in the submitted materials.

Strengths
- Relevance and grounding in established literature: The references indicate the authors are aware of core EL methods (Hoffart et al., Pershina et al., Cucerzan, Kulkarni et al.) and joint embedding approaches (Toutanova et al., Wang & Li, Han et al., Weston et al.), which suggests the intended approach is built on a solid foundation rather than purely speculative.
- Potential practicality: If the system indeed integrates double-array trie/Aho–Corasick for candidate mention detection over large lexicons, it could offer meaningful speedups and memory efficiency for large-scale EL—an engineering contribution valued in practice.
- Sensible modular design (inferred): A pipeline of fast candidate generation + embedding-based ranking + optional graph-based collective inference is a proven design that can perform competitively when executed well. Leveraging established methods increases reproducibility and reliability, if appropriately documented and evaluated.

Weaknesses
- Incomplete/Corrupted submission: The manuscript provided is not reviewable in its current state. The text is missing beyond a damaged reference list with duplicated and malformed entries. There is no clear problem statement, methodology, experimental setup, results, or analysis. This alone precludes acceptance.
- Unclear novelty: From the references, the proposed components are well established in the EL literature. Without a precise description of what is new—e.g., a novel training objective, a new way to couple mention detection with entity embeddings, or a demonstrably superior inference scheme—the contribution appears incremental at best. A clear differentiation from Yamada et al., Toutanova et al., Wang & Li, Han et al., etc., is essential.
- No empirical evidence: There are no reported datasets, baselines, metrics, or numerical results. A rigorous EL paper should evaluate on standard benchmarks (e.g., AIDA-CoNLL/YAGO, MSNBC/ACE/TAC-KBP), compare to strong baselines, include ablations isolating each component’s contribution, and report significance testing. None of this is available.
- Bibliography and writing quality: The reference list is heavily corrupted (typos, duplicates, inconsistent venues/years, spurious text such as “Confidential Review Copy. DO NOT DISTRIBUTE”). This raises concerns about the care taken in preparing the submission and suggests the main text may also be error-prone.
- Reproducibility and clarity: No information about code availability, data preprocessing, hyperparameters, training regimes, resource usage, or inference time/memory is provided. For a primarily engineering-style contribution, these details are critical.

Questions to Authors
- What is the exact novel contribution compared to prior joint text–KG embedding approaches (e.g., Toutanova et al. 2015; Wang & Li 2016; Han et al. 2016; Yamada et al. 2017)? Please state the novelty precisely and provide ablations that isolate it.
- How are mention candidates generated? Are you using Aho–Corasick with a double-array trie over Wikipedia anchor texts/redirects? What are the coverage, precision, and throughput compared to more standard dictionary matchers? Please provide speed/memory benchmarks.
- What datasets do you evaluate on? Please include AIDA-CoNLL (standard splits), and at least one out-of-domain benchmark (e.g., MSNBC/ACE/TAC-KBP/GERBIL-supported sets). How do you handle NIL mentions?
- What baselines are compared? Include strong EL systems (collective and local) and prior embedding-based methods. Report standard metrics (micro/macro accuracy/F1, in-KB accuracy, NDCG@k where applicable), with statistical significance.
- Do you perform collective disambiguation (e.g., Personalized PageRank/graph optimization), and if so, how is the graph constructed and weighted? Provide an ablation to quantify its impact.
- What is the training objective for the joint embeddings? How are entities linked to text (anchors, hyperlinks, descriptions)? What negative sampling, context windows, and model sizes are used? How does your training differ from Wikipedia2Vec or similar methods?
- Will you release code, pretrained embeddings, and lexicons? What are the runtime and memory footprints for training and inference?
