Summary
Based on the reference list and limited context, the paper appears to tackle named entity linking/disambiguation (EL/NED) with a focus on:
- Efficient mention detection via classic dictionary-based string matching (Aho–Corasick and double-array trie structures).
- Joint representation learning of text and knowledge (entities/relations), possibly leveraging multi-sense/ multi-prototype embeddings, to improve entity disambiguation.
- Evaluation using ranking-oriented IR metrics (e.g., NDCG) on standard EL datasets.

Because only the references are provided and the manuscript body is missing, my summary and subsequent assessment are necessarily inferred and may not align with the actual content. Please clarify during rebuttal.

Main contributions (as I see them, in order of potential strength)
1) Engineering contribution: an efficient, scalable mention detection pipeline built on Aho–Corasick with a double-array trie for large dictionary lookup. This is a valuable systems contribution if accompanied by rigorous memory/speed analyses and practical deployment considerations.
2) Modeling contribution: joint embedding of words/entities/senses integrating textual and KG signals for improved disambiguation. The novelty would hinge on how it differs from prior joint text–KG and multi-sense embedding work.
3) Empirical contribution: a comprehensive evaluation on established EL benchmarks with strong baselines and ablations, including effectiveness–efficiency trade-offs.

Strengths
- Relevance and potential impact: EL remains central to many NLP/IR applications (search, QA, IE). A strong end-to-end system that improves scalability without sacrificing accuracy would be practically impactful.
- Sensible pipeline decomposition: Efficient candidate generation (via dictionary matching) combined with a learned disambiguation model is a well-motivated design reflecting how many high-performing EL systems balance recall and precision. If executed well, this can yield robust real-world performance.
- Awareness of foundational literature: The references suggest familiarity with classic string matching (Aho–Corasick; double-array tries), seminal EL work (Cucerzan; Hoffart et al.; Shen et al.), graph-based approaches (Alhelbawy & Gaizauskas; Pershina et al.), and joint text–KG embeddings (Wang et al.; Toutanova et al.; Han et al.). This breadth is a good starting point for positioning the work.
- Emphasis on efficiency: If the paper provides concrete throughput/latency/memory results at various KB scales and languages, this could be a substantive contribution often under-reported in EL research.

Weaknesses
- Severe issues with the bibliography quality: The references are riddled with typos, duplicates, truncations, wrong venues/years, and placeholder text (e.g., “Confidential Review Copy,” “Submission”). Many entries are repeated in corrupted variants. This undermines the paper’s presentation quality and makes it difficult to verify related work. ACL standards require clean, accurate, and complete citations.
- Apparent lack of novelty in the core methods: Aho–Corasick and double-array tries are decades-old, standard techniques; dictionary-based candidate generation for EL is also established. Similarly, joint text–KG embedding for EL has substantial prior art (e.g., Ganea & Hofmann 2017; Yamada et al. 2016/2017; Toutanova et al. 2015; Wang & Li 2016; Han et al. 2016). The paper must articulate a clear, novel modeling or systems advance beyond these works (e.g., a new integration strategy, training objective, or a provably better efficiency–accuracy frontier).
- Missing comparisons to recent state of the art: The reference list appears to stop around 2016–2017. Modern EL systems and datasets are not cited or (presumably) compared, e.g., strong bi-encoder/cross-encoder pipelines (BLINK), sequence-to-sequence generation approaches (GENRE/mGENRE), recent toolkits (REL, ReFinED), and datasets emphasizing generalization (ZESHEL) and large-KB settings. Without comparisons and analysis against recent strong baselines, the contribution’s significance is unclear.
- Evaluation design unclear: With only references, it is impossible to assess datasets, metrics, baselines, or experimental rigor. For EL, typical expectations include AIDA-CoNLL, TAC-KBP, WNED, and GERBIL benchmarking; reporting in-KB accuracy, micro/macro, and ranking metrics for candidate reranking. Ablations for (a) candidate generation recall/precision, (b) disambiguation model components, and (c) efficiency–effectiveness trade-offs are essential. Lack of these would be a major weakness.
- Clarity and reproducibility risk: The messy references raise concerns about overall writing quality, clarity of the method description, and availability of resources (code, trained models, dictionaries). For acceptance, the paper should provide clear algorithmic details, hyperparameters, and release code/models to support reproducibility.

Questions to Authors (to clarify during rebuttal)
- Problem and contribution clarity:
  - What exactly is the novel contribution beyond established Aho–Corasick/double-array tries and prior joint text–KG embedding for EL? Is it a new objective, architecture, training strategy, candidate integration scheme, or efficiency analysis at unprecedented scales?
- Candidate generation:
  - How are surface forms constructed (redirects, aliases, inflections, acronyms, capitalization variants, cross-lingual variants)? How do you handle overlapping/ambiguous spans and long mentions? Provide candidate generation recall on standard datasets.
  - What are the memory footprint and build/query times for your double-array trie at different KB sizes? How do you handle updates/incremental additions?
- Disambiguation model:
  - Detail the representation learning: what sources (text, KG structure, descriptions) are used? How are senses/entities tied to words? How do you train (loss functions, negatives, alignment strategies)? How does the model differ from Yamada et al. (2016/2017), Ganea & Hofmann (2017), and subsequent neural EL frameworks?
  - Do you incorporate graph signals (PPR, coherence) at inference? Any joint/global inference or only local scoring?
- Evaluation:
  - Which datasets and splits? What metrics? What are your baselines (including recent ones such as BLINK-like bi-encoder + cross-encoder rerankers and GENRE-style generators)? Include ablations isolating the gain from efficient mention detection vs. the learned disambiguator.
  - Provide efficiency numbers (throughput/latency on CPU/GPU, memory use) and accuracy–latency trade-offs. Compare against modern systems’ inference costs.
- Reproducibility:
  - Will you release code, pretrained models, and the mention dictionary? Any constraints on licensing?
