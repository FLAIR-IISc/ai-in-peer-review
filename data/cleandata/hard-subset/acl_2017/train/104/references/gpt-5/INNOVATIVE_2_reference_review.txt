Summary and contributions
The submitted material appears to be an incomplete or corrupted manuscript focused on named entity recognition/linking and joint representation learning of text and knowledge graphs. From the references and stray text fragments (e.g., “predict the context words by maximizing the following objective…”, repeated citations to Han et al., Toutanova et al., Wang & Li, Weston et al., and classic EL work), I infer the paper may propose:
1) A joint embedding model of words and entities (possibly Wikipedia-based, word2vec-like) for entity disambiguation.
2) An efficient mention detection pipeline using Aho–Corasick/double-array trie for large-scale string matching.
3) An application to entity disambiguation with claimed improvements.

However, the body of the paper is missing, and the reference section is severely corrupted and duplicative, preventing a reliable assessment of novelty, methodology, and empirical validation.

Strengths
- Potentially bold, practical unification of fast lexicon-based mention detection (Aho–Corasick/double array) with neural joint embeddings of words/entities. If realized well, this combination can yield both speed and accuracy in end-to-end EL systems, which is valuable in production settings.
- The reference list (despite corruption) indicates awareness of and engagement with multiple relevant strands: multi-prototype/sense embeddings (Reisinger & Mooney, Neelakantan et al., Li & Jurafsky), joint text–KG learning (Toutanova et al., Wang & Li, Weston et al., Han et al.), and collective disambiguation (Hoffart et al., Pershina et al., Kulkarni et al.). A contribution that synthesizes these strands in a simple training objective and strong runtime profile could be impactful.
- If the work includes public release of embeddings, code, or a fast mention detector, it would have high practical utility and likely community uptake.

Weaknesses
- Incomplete/corrupted submission. The main paper text is not provided or is unreadable, and the references are riddled with duplications, truncations, and typographical noise. As-is, I cannot evaluate the technical approach, training objective, datasets, baselines, or results. This is not a minor flaw; it precludes meaningful review.
- Anonymity and formatting issues. The text contains “Confidential Review Copy” strings and explicit author names (e.g., “Ikuya Yamada… ACL”) embedded in ways that may violate anonymity and reviewing guidelines. The reference section also contains errant phrases (“played it during public”), suggesting serious preparation errors.
- Novelty unclear relative to prior art. Without a clear method description, it is impossible to assess differentiation from prior joint word–entity embedding work (e.g., Toutanova et al. 2015; Wang & Li 2016; Han et al. 2016; Wu et al. 2016) and from Wikipedia-based word/entity embedding toolkits that already exist. Likewise, efficient mention detection via Aho–Corasick/double-array tries is long-established; the paper would need to articulate what is new beyond standard practice.
- Empirical validation absent. There is no visible evaluation protocol, datasets (AIDA-CoNLL, TAC, Wikipedia hyperlinks, etc.), baselines, or metrics (e.g., micro/macro accuracy for EL, MRR/NDCG). Claims of speed/accuracy trade-offs, domain robustness, and ablations cannot be verified.
- Reproducibility and resources are unspecified. There is no information about model sizes, training data, hyperparameters, inference complexity, or whether code/embeddings will be released. This undermines potential impact even if the idea were strong.

Questions to authors
- Scope and novelty: What is the precise training objective and model architecture? How does it differ from prior joint embedding approaches (e.g., Toutanova et al. 2015; Han et al. 2016; Wang & Li 2016; Wikipedia2Vec-like methods)? What is the key conceptual advance?
- Mention detection: Do you rely solely on Aho–Corasick/double-array lexicon matching? How do you handle redirects, aliases, overlap, and NIL detection? Any new algorithms or data structures beyond standard double-array tries?
- Disambiguation: Is the entity prediction done via nearest neighbor in a shared embedding space, a learned classifier/ranker, or a collective graph model? Do you incorporate coherence features (e.g., Personalized PageRank) or purely rely on embeddings?
- Evaluation: Which datasets and splits do you use (AIDA-CoNLL, TAC-KBP, MSNBC/AQUAINT/ACE, WikilinksNED)? What are the main baselines (Hoffart/YAGO, Pershina, Ceccarelli, neural EL baselines), and how do you ensure fair comparison? Please report accuracy/F1 and ranking metrics (MRR/NDCG) where appropriate, plus runtime.
- Efficiency: What are the indexing and inference latencies and memory footprints for mention detection and disambiguation at web scale? Any batching/ANN methods for fast entity retrieval?
- Resources: Will you release pretrained embeddings, code, and mention lexicons? If not, why?
- Paper integrity: Can you provide a clean, complete manuscript during the rebuttal that adheres to anonymity and formatting guidelines, and a corrected reference list?

Overall assessment
I am generally enthusiastic about bold, integrative ideas that move the EL community forward, even if not methodologically perfect. The likely direction here—fast lexicon-based mention detection married to joint word–entity embeddings—could be impactful if convincingly demonstrated. Unfortunately, the submission is incomplete/corrupted to the point that I cannot judge novelty, correctness, or empirical merit. This is not a minor shortcoming.

Recommendation: Reject. I would welcome a resubmission with a complete, properly anonymized manuscript that clearly articulates the method’s novelty and provides rigorous, reproducible evaluation. If, during rebuttal, the authors can clarify the core ideas and demonstrate clear differentiation and strong results, I would be inclined to reassess in a future cycle.
