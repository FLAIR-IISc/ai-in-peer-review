Summary and perceived contributions:
Based on the (noisy and partially corrupted) reference list and scattered text fragments, the submission appears to address named entity recognition/linking (NER/EL) with an emphasis on efficiency in mention detection (citing Aho–Corasick and double-array tries) and disambiguation via joint embeddings of words/entities/knowledge graphs (citations to Yamada et al., Toutanova et al., Wang et al., Han et al., etc.). Mentions of nDCG suggest ranking-oriented evaluation; references to PPR and graph-based methods (Pershina; Alhelbawy & Gaizauskas) indicate incorporation of global coherence or graph signals.

Interpreting the likely intent, I infer the following contributions (to be confirmed; many details are missing):
- An engineering contribution: a high-throughput dictionary-based mention detection module using Aho–Corasick and/or a double-array trie to support large alias dictionaries efficiently.
- A modeling contribution: a joint embedding framework that learns representations of words and entities from text and possibly knowledge graphs, used to score mention–entity candidates; potentially incorporating multi-prototype/sense-aware embeddings.
- An empirical contribution: reported gains on standard EL datasets with ranking-based metrics and some ablations; efficiency improvements in latency/memory.

Strengths:
- Practical focus on efficiency: If the system indeed combines compact trie-based mention detection with neural scoring, this is a sensible and impactful design for production EL pipelines where throughput and memory footprint are critical. The cited Aho–Corasick/double-array work is appropriate for scaling.
- Integration of text and KG signals: Joint training of words and entities is a well-motivated direction (Yamada; Toutanova; Wang & Li), and applying it to EL can improve candidate scoring and coverage, especially for sparse entities or long-tail mentions.
- Awareness of core EL literature: The references cover several foundational approaches (Cucerzan; Hoffart et al.; Pershina et al.; Kulkarni et al.) and related embedding work (Mikolov; multi-sense embeddings; joint text–KG methods), suggesting the authors situate their approach within the right context.
- Potentially broad applicability: A fast, accurate EL module with modular candidate generation and neural ranking could be valuable across information extraction, QA, and retrieval systems.

Questions to authors (to clarify during rebuttal):
- Problem scope: Is the task EL only, or end-to-end NER+EL? Do you handle NIL entities and cross-document/coreference? Any multilingual support?
- Candidate generation: Do you use Aho–Corasick or double-array tries for mention spotting only, or also for alias lookup and candidate retrieval? What are the alias sources (Wikipedia anchors, redirects, titles, disambiguation pages)? What is the candidate recall at K?
- Modeling novelty: How does your joint embedding objective differ from Yamada et al. and Toutanova et al.? Do you incorporate KG structure (relations/paths) or only text co-occurrence? Are multi-prototype word/sense embeddings actually used at inference time for EL?
- Global coherence: Do you use graph-based collective disambiguation (PPR, graph ranking) or purely local scoring? If global, how is the graph constructed and optimized efficiently?
- Datasets and baselines: Which benchmarks do you evaluate on? Do you compare against strong neural EL systems (e.g., Ganea & Hofmann 2017; Kolitsas 2018; BLINK) and recent joint word–entity embedding methods? How do you ensure fair candidate sets?
- Efficiency: What are the latency and memory footprints per document/entity candidate under realistic loads? How does your mention detection throughput compare with standard tokenization+NER pipelines?
- Analysis: Provide ablations isolating the effect of candidate generation, embedding components, global coherence, and KG integration. Include error typology and performance across head vs tail entities.
