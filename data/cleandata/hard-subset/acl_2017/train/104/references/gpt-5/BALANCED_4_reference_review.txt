Summary
From the material provided, the submission appears to address named entity disambiguation (NED)/entity linking, combining efficient dictionary-based candidate generation (citing Aho–Corasick and double-array tries) with representation learning that jointly embeds words/entities and leverages knowledge graphs (citing Wang et al., Weston et al., Toutanova et al., Han et al., Yamada et al.). The reference list suggests goals of improving both effectiveness and efficiency, possibly via joint training and graph-based collective inference.

Unfortunately, the submission text seems largely missing; the provided content is almost entirely a corrupted, heavily duplicated references section interleaved with fragments of text (“predict the context words by maximizing…”, “Confidential Review Copy”). As submitted, the paper is not assessable for technical soundness, novelty, or empirical quality.

Main contributions (as I see them)
Given the limited information, the likely intended contributions are:
1) An efficient candidate generation module for entity linking using Aho–Corasick/double-array tries to support large mention dictionaries at low latency.
2) A joint embedding approach integrating text and knowledge graph signals for NED, possibly trained with a context-prediction objective and used for ranking candidates (and potentially collective disambiguation via graph ranking).
3) An empirical evaluation on standard NED datasets with gains in effectiveness and/or speed.

Strengths
- Practical problem focus and plausible system design: Using Aho–Corasick/double-array structures for mention matching is a well-motivated choice for high-throughput candidate generation. Joint word/entity embeddings are a reasonable backbone for NED candidate scoring. The references indicate awareness of core components of modern NED pipelines (dictionary-based candidate recall, priors, contextual scoring, and collective inference).
- Breadth of related work: The references span classic string matching, multi-prototype/sense embeddings, joint text-KB embedding, and NED methods (e.g., Hoffart et al., Pershina et al., Cucerzan), suggesting the authors are engaging with the right literature.
- Potential efficiency angle: If the work truly demonstrates substantial speedups or memory savings without sacrificing accuracy, that would be of interest to practitioners, especially for large-scale EL systems.

Weaknesses
- Incomplete and corrupted submission: The manuscript as provided is not readable as a scientific paper. The references section is heavily duplicated and garbled (numerous typos, repeated entries, incomplete metadata). There is no accessible abstract, method, experiments, or conclusions. This alone makes the paper unreviewable and not suitable for publication.
- Unclear novelty: Even assuming the intended contributions, the high-level pipeline (Aho–Corasick candidate generation + joint embeddings + graph-based ranking) is well established. Without precise problem formulation, modeling choices, and ablations, it is hard to identify what is new beyond engineering integration. Prior art includes Wikipedia2Vec/Yamada et al., joint text-KB embedding (Wang et al., Toutanova et al.), and strong neural EL systems; the paper must articulate a clear technical innovation.
- Missing empirical evidence: There are no experimental details. For NED, standard practice requires reporting In-KB and NIL handling, accuracy/Micro-F1 on AIDA-CoNLL and other benchmarks (e.g., TAC KBP, KORE50), candidate recall at K, P@1/MRR/nDCG, latency/memory, and comparisons to competitive baselines. No such evidence is present, so claims (if any) cannot be substantiated.
- Reference quality and scholarly presentation: Beyond duplication and corruption, some citations are misattributed, incomplete, or contain placeholder text (“played it during public,” “Submission”). This undermines credibility and suggests the manuscript has not undergone basic proofreading or formatting checks.
- Reproducibility and transparency: There is no indication of code/data release, training data sources (e.g., Wikipedia anchors vs. KB triples), negative sampling strategies, hyperparameters, or implementation details (e.g., trie construction, handling of overlapping mentions, disambiguation graph construction). Without this, results would be hard to verify even if experiments were provided.

Questions to Authors
- Problem setup: What exact EL setting do you target (document-level/entity disambiguation without NIL vs. with NIL, zero-shot entities, multilingual)? Which KB (e.g., Wikipedia, Wikidata, Freebase) and which language(s)?
- Novelty: What is the core technical innovation over prior joint word/entity embedding methods and over state-of-the-art EL systems? Is it a new training objective, a new way to integrate graph structure/context, or an efficiency technique? Please delineate contributions precisely.
- Candidate generation: How is the mention lexicon built (anchors, redirects, aliases)? How do you handle overlapping mentions, case/diacritics, tokenization, and normalization? What are the candidate recall@K and throughput/latency versus alternatives?
- Modeling: What is the exact objective (“predict the context words by maximizing…” suggests a Skip-gram-like loss). How are entity–entity and entity–word interactions modeled? Do you incorporate KB relations (TransE/DistMult/ComplEx-style losses) or only text co-occurrences?
- Disambiguation: Do you perform local scoring only, or collective global inference? If collective, what graph is built and what ranking/inference method is used? How do you incorporate priors (p(entity|mention))?
- Evaluation: Which datasets and splits? What metrics (accuracy, micro/macro F1, MRR, nDCG)? How do you handle NIL? What baselines do you compare to (please include strong neural baselines such as BLINK, GENRE/REL/Bootleg, and Wikipedia2Vec-based methods), and do you include statistical significance tests?
- Efficiency: Please report memory usage, preprocessing/build times for the trie, and end-to-end latency on standard hardware, with ablations isolating the impact of your efficiency components.

Additional comments and suggestions for improvement
- Substantial rewriting is needed. Ensure the submission includes an abstract, clear problem definition, method section with equations/algorithms, experiments, analysis, and conclusions. Thoroughly fix the references (deduplicate, correct metadata, adhere to ACL format).
- Provide an ablation study to separate the contributions of candidate generation, priors, embeddings, and collective inference. Include error analysis to identify common failure modes (e.g., rare entities, long mentions, cross-document consistency).
- If efficiency is a key claim, compare to alternative candidate generation strategies (e.g., hash-based gazetteers, DAWGs/marisa-trie, ANN-based candidate retrieval) and report end-to-end system performance.
- For fairness, control pretraining data across methods where possible, or report performance with/without additional supervision.

Recommendation
Reject. The submission in its current form is incomplete and unreviewable, with unclear novelty and no empirical evidence. The topic is relevant and the intended system could be valuable, but the paper must be rewritten, clearly articulate its innovations, and present rigorous experiments and analyses to be considered for ACL.
