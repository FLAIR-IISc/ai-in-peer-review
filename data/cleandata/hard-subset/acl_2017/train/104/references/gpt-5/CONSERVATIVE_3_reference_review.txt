Summary
Based on the manuscript and its references, the paper appears to propose a joint representation learning approach that learns embeddings for words and entities from text and a knowledge graph, and applies these embeddings to named entity disambiguation/linking. The system likely includes an efficient dictionary-based candidate generation component (citing Aho–Corasick and double-array tries), and a scoring/disambiguation module that uses the learned representations (possibly with a graph/coherence component). The work claims efficiency and effectiveness, with evaluation using standard IR/EL metrics.

Main contributions (as I see them)
1) An integrated pipeline for entity linking that combines (a) efficient candidate generation via classical string matching structures and (b) joint text–KG embeddings for candidate ranking. This is primarily an engineering integration of established components rather than a fundamentally new model.
2) A training objective that couples a skip-gram-style word objective with entity/graph signals for improved entity disambiguation. Relative to prior joint embedding work (e.g., Toutanova et al. 2015; Wang et al. 2014; Han et al. 2016; Yamada et al.), the novelty seems incremental.
3) An empirical evaluation suggesting competitive performance, possibly with improved throughput due to the use of Aho–Corasick/double-array structures.

Strengths
1) Practical efficiency focus. Leveraging Aho–Corasick and double-array tries for candidate generation is sound, well-established, and appropriate for high-throughput EL. If the paper provides latency and memory profiles, this is a valuable contribution for real systems.
2) Sensible modeling direction. Joint learning of words and entities from text and KG has proven effective in prior work; building on this line is methodologically reasonable. Using established objectives (word2vec-like) and KG signals reduces risk and increases reproducibility.
3) End-to-end framing. A complete pipeline from mention detection/candidate generation to disambiguation is useful. If the authors evaluate both candidate recall and final linking accuracy, this would offer a clearer picture than ranking-only studies.
4) Potentially broad applicability. If the embeddings are reusable beyond EL (e.g., relation extraction, retrieval), releasing them could benefit the community.
5) Use of established IR metrics. Referencing nDCG and related measures suggests some attention to ranking quality in addition to accuracy.

Weaknesses
1) Limited novelty relative to substantial prior art. The joint text–KG embedding paradigm for EL has been explored extensively (Toutanova et al. 2015; Wang et al. 2014; Weston et al. 2013; Han et al. 2016; Yamada et al.). The manuscript as presented does not clearly articulate what is conceptually new (beyond engineering choices), nor why it significantly advances the state of the art.
2) Missing comparisons to strong modern baselines. The references do not include recent transformer-based EL systems (e.g., BLINK, GENRE/mGENRE, Bootleg, REL, end-to-end EL with BERT). Without head-to-head comparisons on standard datasets (AIDA-CoNLL, TAC-KBP, WNED, etc.), it is difficult to assess competitiveness. This is a major gap for ACL standards.
3) Insufficient methodological clarity and ablations. The draft hints at a skip-gram-like objective “predict the context words …” but lacks precise loss definitions, negative sampling strategy, how KG structure is incorporated (translational constraints? graph context?), and how global coherence is modeled in disambiguation. Ablations removing KG edges, text-only training, and coherence terms are necessary to isolate contributions.
4) Evaluation design concerns. It is unclear whether mention detection is gold or predicted, whether NIL handling is supported, and what candidate generation recall is at K. Without reporting candidate recall and analyzing errors across frequency buckets (head vs tail entities), ambiguity levels, and unseen mentions, the results may overstate effectiveness. Statistical significance testing is not mentioned.
5) Presentation and citation quality. The bibliography has numerous duplicated and corrupted entries (typos, partial lines, inconsistent venues/years). This raises concerns about rigor and care in preparation and may reflect similar issues elsewhere in the paper. Key related work is omitted, which exacerbates the novelty and evaluation concerns.

Questions to Authors
1) Novelty: Precisely define what is new versus Toutanova et al. (2015), Wang et al. (2014), Han et al. (2016), and Yamada et al. (2016/2017). What modeling component or training signal is unique, and why does it matter empirically?
2) Baselines: Please include comparisons to current strong EL systems (e.g., BLINK, GENRE/mGENRE, Bootleg, REL, E2E-BERT EL), with the same datasets and standard protocols. If you cannot, justify why and provide best-effort comparisons (e.g., re-running official models or using reported numbers with matched settings).
3) Objective: Provide the exact training losses (mathematical form), negative sampling scheme, how entities interact with words, and how KG information is injected (transE-style constraints? random walks? relation-specific projections?). How are hyperparameters tuned?
4) Disambiguation: Describe the inference/ranking model. Do you use only local context scores from embeddings, or also global coherence (graph-based, PPR)? If coherence is used, give the exact formulation and optimization/inference details.
5) Candidate generation: Report candidate recall at K (e.g., K=30, 50) and latency/memory for Aho–Corasick vs double-array trie. How do you handle surface form normalization, redirects, anchors, and ambiguity? What is the OOV strategy?
6) Datasets and metrics: Specify datasets, splits, and metrics (micro/macro accuracy, in-KB accuracy, nDCG, mention-level F1). Are significance tests performed? Are results reported with confidence intervals?
7) Robustness: Provide analyses by entity frequency, mention length, capitalization/diacritics noise, and domain shift (e.g., from newswire to web). Do you support NIL prediction? How does the system perform on unseen entities/mentions?
8) Resources: Report training time, hardware, and model size. If claiming efficiency, compare end-to-end throughput (doc/sec) against at least one modern baseline.

Overall, while the paper’s direction is reasonable and the efficiency angle is practical, the current submission appears incremental and underspecified, with missing strong baselines and limited clarity on novelty. Substantial additions in experimental rigor, comparisons, and clear exposition are needed to meet ACL standards.
