Summary and contributions
Based on the submission provided, only a references section is available (with numerous duplications and severe typographical errors), and there is no accessible abstract, method, experiments, or results. From the references and scattered cues (Aho–Corasick, double-array trie; joint text–KG embeddings; collective entity disambiguation; PPR; knowledge graph completion), the intended topic appears to be named entity linking/disambiguation leveraging efficient dictionary-based mention detection and joint text–knowledge embeddings.

Given the lack of substantive content, I can only infer the following potential contributions:
1) A system-level pipeline that combines efficient dictionary-based mention detection (e.g., Aho–Corasick/double-array) with neural/joint text–KG embeddings for disambiguation.
2) Possibly a collective disambiguation component using graph ranking or Personalized PageRank, integrated with the learned representations.

These are common directions in the EL literature; without the body, novelty is impossible to establish.

Strengths
- Relevance and potential impact of the problem: Entity linking/disambiguation with joint text–KG signals and scalable mention detection is an important and practical area with broad applicability in NLP systems. If the paper offered a bold, conceptually new integration or training objective, it could be impactful.
- Awareness of prior work: The bibliography (despite being heavily corrupted) cites a reasonably comprehensive set of foundational and recent works in EL, multi-sense embeddings, and joint text–KG representation learning (e.g., Hoffart et al., Pershina et al., Toutanova et al., Wang et al., Yamada et al.). This suggests the authors know the landscape they need to position against.
- Potential for efficiency: References to Aho–Corasick and double-array tries suggest attention to high-throughput mention detection, which is a real bottleneck in large-scale EL systems.

Weaknesses
- Incomplete submission: The paper body is missing. There is no abstract, problem statement, methodology, experimental setup, results, or discussion to review. This makes it impossible to assess novelty, technical soundness, or empirical validity.
- Corrupted and duplicated references: The reference list is riddled with typographical errors, partial author names, repeated entries, malformed venues, and inconsistent years. This undermines clarity and professionalism and makes it difficult to determine exact prior art for comparison.
- Novelty unclear and likely incremental: Given the references, the plausible approach—dictionary-based mention detection plus joint text–KG embeddings and graph-based collective inference—has been explored in various forms (e.g., Yamada et al., Toutanova et al., Wang et al., Weston et al.). Without a clear statement of what is fundamentally new (e.g., a new training objective that unifies senses and entities, a principled collective inference mechanism leveraging learned structure, or a new evaluation setting), the work appears incremental.
- No experimental evidence or evaluation: There are no datasets, baselines, metrics, or results shown. For EL, standard practice includes evaluations on AIDA-CoNLL, TAC, Wikipedia-based corpora, with strong baselines and ablations. The absence of experiments precludes any assessment of effectiveness, robustness, or efficiency.
- Reproducibility and clarity: No code, data, training details, or hyperparameters are provided. Important choices (candidate generation, priors, graph construction, negative sampling, sense/entity alignment, decoding) are unknown. This would make the work non-reproducible even if a method were described.

Questions to authors
- What is the core novel idea? Please state concisely what is conceptually new compared to: (a) Yamada et al. (joint learning of words and entities), (b) Toutanova et al. (text–KB joint embeddings), (c) Wang et al. (joint text–KG models), and (d) standard collective EL methods (Hoffart et al., Pershina et al., Kulkarni et al.).
- How exactly are Aho–Corasick/double-array structures used? Only for mention detection, or also for efficient candidate retrieval or disambiguation? What are the memory/latency trade-offs and throughput on large corpora?
- What is the disambiguation objective? Do you use attention over context, coherence terms over the entity graph, or a joint decoding objective? If collective, how is the graph built and optimized?
- Datasets and baselines: Which benchmarks (e.g., AIDA, TAC KBP, MSNBC, ACE2004, AQUAINT, WNED) and which baselines (BLINK, GENRE, PPR-based, prior embedding-based methods) do you compare against? What metrics (accuracy, micro/macro F1, in-KB accuracy, nDCG) do you report?
- Ablations and analysis: What is the contribution of joint text–KG training vs. text-only or KG-only, and of collective inference vs. local? Provide error analysis (mention detection vs. candidate generation vs. disambiguation).
- Resources: Will you release code/models/dictionaries? What are the training corpora (Wikipedia, Wikidata/Freebase), negative sampling strategies, and computational budget?

Additional comments and suggestions
- Please submit the full paper. As it stands, the submission is unreviewable.
- Fix and de-duplicate the bibliography; conform to ACL formatting. Ensure all entries include full author lists, correct titles, venues, and years.
- If your contribution is primarily systems-oriented (e.g., industrial-scale EL), emphasize the novelty (e.g., a new learnable candidate generator, a unified loss tying senses and entities, a principled, efficient collective decoder) and provide rigorous empirical evidence and efficiency benchmarks.
- Given the crowded space, a bold idea would help: for example, a single model that jointly induces multi-prototype word senses aligned with KG entities, disambiguates mentions end-to-end without hand-crafted candidate generation, and scales to web-scale corpora. If that is your direction, make it explicit and validate accordingly.

Recommendation
Reject. I value bold, novel contributions, but the current submission lacks the essential content to evaluate novelty or validity. If the authors can provide a complete, clearly positioned paper with strong empirical evidence and a well-articulated new idea, I would be open to reassessing in a future cycle.
