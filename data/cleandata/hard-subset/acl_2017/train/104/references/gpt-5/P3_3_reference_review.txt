Summary and contributions
Based on the provided material (which consists almost entirely of a noisy, duplicated references list and a few fragmentary sentences), I infer that the submission likely proposes an entity linking/disambiguation approach that combines:
- Efficient mention detection/candidate generation via classic dictionary matching and trie-based data structures (Aho–Corasick; double-array tries).
- Joint representation learning of words and entities leveraging text and a knowledge base (akin to joint text–KG embedding; references include Wang et al. 2014; Toutanova et al. 2015; Han et al. 2016; Yamada et al. 2017).
- Application to named entity disambiguation with graph/coherence or ranking components (citing Hoffart et al. 2011; Kulkarni et al. 2009; Pershina et al. 2015; Alhelbawy and Gaizauskas 2014).

If this interpretation is correct, the main contributions as I see them would be:
1) A practical, efficient pipeline that integrates deterministic high-throughput mention detection with joint word–entity embeddings for entity disambiguation.
2) An efficiency contribution (via Aho–Corasick and double-array structures) that enables large-scale inference without sacrificing accuracy.
3) An empirical evaluation on standard EL benchmarks showing competitive accuracy and/or latency improvements.

However, the submission text is missing; thus, I cannot verify methodology, novelty, or results. The rest of my review is contingent on the above interpretation.

Strengths
- Practical integration of known but complementary components: If the paper cleanly bridges high-recall dictionary-based mention detection with neural joint embeddings and a coherence-aware disambiguation objective, that end-to-end integration is practically valuable for large-scale EL systems used in industry and research.
- Attention to efficiency: The references to Aho–Corasick and double-array tries suggest the authors care about throughput and memory footprint. If accompanied by solid engineering and measured wall-clock gains (e.g., QPS, latency distributions, memory usage) at comparable accuracy, this is a strong contribution for ACL.
- Alignment with established and recent literature: The references cover key strands: classical EL (Hoffart; Cucerzan; Kulkarni; Pershina), word/sense multi-prototype embeddings (Reisinger & Mooney; Neelakantan; Tian; Li & Jurafsky; Mancini et al.), and joint text–KG embeddings (Wang et al.; Weston et al.; Toutanova et al.; Han et al.; Wang & Li). If the paper situates itself properly and makes clear what is new, it could be a timely synthesis.
- Potentially reusable resources: If the authors release trained word–entity embeddings, code, and mention lexicons, the work could have high utility regardless of incremental novelty.

Weaknesses
- Missing paper content; unassessable claims: Only a severely corrupted references list is provided. I cannot evaluate the method, experiments, ablations, or conclusions. This alone prevents acceptance.
- Novelty is unclear relative to prior joint word–entity embedding and EL work: The likely core idea (joint training of words and entities using Wikipedia anchors + KG edges; using the embedding space for EL) overlaps heavily with prior work such as Wikipedia2Vec/Yamada et al. (2016/2017), Toutanova et al. (2015), Wang et al. (2014), Wang & Li (2016), Weston et al. (2013), and more recent neural EL models (e.g., Ganea & Hofmann, 2017; not cited). The paper must articulate what is fundamentally new (objective, training signals, negative sampling, coherence modeling, or decoding) and demonstrate consistent gains over these strong baselines.
- Reference quality and scholarly rigor: The bibliography is rife with duplicates, typos, corrupted venue names, and nonsensical fragments (e.g., “played it during public,” “Confid tia Review Copy”). This undermines credibility and suggests inadequate proofreading. Several key works appear missing or inconsistently cited; publication details are inconsistent.
- Evaluation details and baselines likely insufficient: To be competitive, an EL paper should evaluate on AIDA-CoNLL (testA/B), WNED datasets, and possibly TAC-KBP or GERBIL settings; report accuracy, micro/macro metrics, MRR/NDCG (you cite Järvelin & Kekäläinen); and compare against strong recent baselines (Hoffart; Pershina; Kolitsas et al.; Ganea & Hofmann), including collective/distributional models. Ablations should isolate the impact of joint training vs. candidate generation vs. coherence. Without this, it is difficult to assess significance.
- Ambiguity in efficiency claims: If efficiency is a claimed contribution, it must be backed by careful profiling: trie build time/space; mention extraction throughput; end-to-end latency with/without coherence; and comparisons to alternative candidate generators (e.g., probabilistic gazetteers, fuzzy matching). Aho–Corasick/double-array tries are well-known; the novelty must be in system design and measured trade-offs, not the use of these algorithms per se.
- Reproducibility risks: Joint training on Wikipedia/KG is sensitive to preprocessing (anchor priors, redirect handling, NILs, pruning), negative sampling, and hyperparameters. The paper should provide full details, code, and pre-trained models to ensure repeatability and guard against inadvertent train-test leakage (e.g., test mentions seen via anchors).

Questions to authors
- What is the precise novelty over Yamada et al. (2016/2017) and Wikipedia2Vec-style training? Do you introduce a new objective, new negative sampling, new context modeling, or a new way to inject KG structure? Please provide a side-by-side comparison and ablation.
- How are candidates generated and filtered? Do you rely solely on dictionary matching via Aho–Corasick, or do you incorporate priors (p(entity|mention)), surface form normalization, or neural span scoring? What is the recall of the candidate generator on AIDA/WNED?
- How do you model global coherence (if at all)? Graph-based reranking (PPR), CRF/ILP decoding, or neural attention over document entities? What is the overhead and its effect on latency?
- What datasets and splits are used? Do you evaluate on AIDA-CoNLL (standard testA/testB with validation), and do you include WNED (ACE2004, AQUAINT, MSNBC, CWEB, WIKI)? How do your results compare to Hoffart (2011), Pershina (2015), Ganea & Hofmann (2017), and more recent neural EL methods?
- What metrics do you report? In-KB accuracy, micro/macro F1, MRR/NDCG@k (you cite NDCG), and NIL handling? How do you ensure no overlap between training anchors and test gold annotations beyond what is standard?
- Efficiency: Please report memory use of the double-array trie, QPS and latency (p50/p95/p99) for mention detection and end-to-end EL, and compare to alternative implementations. What is the training time and hardware?
- Resources: Will you release code, models, and lexicons? If not, why?
- Error analysis: What are the dominant failure modes (rare entities, long-tail senses, cross-sentence coherence)? How does performance vary with prior entropy of mentions?

Additional comments
- The references need a thorough cleanup: deduplicate, fix typos, provide correct venues/years, and add missing seminal/recent EL papers to properly situate the work.
- If the submission instead targets knowledge graph completion using joint text–KG embeddings (as some citations suggest), please clarify the task, benchmarks (FB15k-237, WN18RR, etc.), and novelty relative to Toutanova et al. (2015), Wang & Li (2016), Han et al. (2016).

Overall assessment
As submitted, the paper is unreviewable due to missing content and severely corrupted references. If the intended contribution is an efficient, joint embedding-based EL system, it could be valuable, but the authors must provide a clear exposition, establish novelty over close prior work, and supply strong, reproducible evaluations before it can be considered for acceptance.
