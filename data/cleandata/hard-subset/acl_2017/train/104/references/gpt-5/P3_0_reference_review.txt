Summary
This submission appears to concern named entity recognition/linking and possibly knowledge graph completion via joint representation learning of text and knowledge, with an emphasis on efficient dictionary-based mention detection (Aho–Corasick; double-array tries) and collective disambiguation (graph ranking, Personalized PageRank). The reference list is the only content provided and is heavily corrupted (duplicates, typos, truncated entries), so my assessment necessarily infers scope and methodology from citations rather than a complete paper.

Contributions (as I see them)
- A scalable mention-detection component using automata/trie-based dictionary matching (Aho–Corasick; double-array trie), presumably for high-recall candidate generation in entity linking.
- A joint embedding approach for words/entities (and possibly relations) leveraging both text and knowledge graph structure for disambiguation and/or knowledge completion, building on prior work in joint text–KG representation learning and multi-sense embeddings.
- A collective disambiguation module that ranks candidates using graph-based relatedness (e.g., Personalized PageRank/graph ranking), potentially improving global coherence.

Strengths
- Strong motivation and relevance: Efficient candidate generation and joint text–KG representations remain central challenges in entity linking and KG completion. Combining fast dictionary matching with neural representations and graph coherence is a well-motivated pipeline architecture.
- Awareness of key classical lines of work: The references cover foundational EL work (collective disambiguation, Wikipedia-based methods), joint text–KG models, multi-prototype word embeddings, and IR evaluation (e.g., NDCG), suggesting an intent to position the work within established methodological families.
- Scalability angle: If the system indeed uses Aho–Corasick/double-array tries, this is a practical strength. Many modern EL pipelines suffer from mention detection bottlenecks; carefully engineered automata with compact data structures can materially improve throughput and memory footprint in production.
- Potentially unified modeling across tasks: If the joint representation also supports KG completion, there is value in demonstrating transfer/compatibility between EL and KGC, as training signals from text and KG can mutually reinforce.
- Practicality: A pipeline combining robust candidate generation, embedding-based local scores, and graph-based global consistency is pragmatically useful and has a track record of strong performance when well-executed.

Weaknesses
- Incomplete/invalid submission: The paper body is missing. The reference section is severely corrupted (duplicated entries, misspellings, malformed venues/years, stray text such as “Confidential Review Copy,” “played it during public,” and inconsistent author strings). In its current form, the submission is not reviewable for technical correctness, novelty, or empirical rigor.
- Unclear novelty: Based on the references, the proposed components are well-established: dictionary-based mention detection; embedding-based entity representations; collective disambiguation via graph ranking. Without concrete innovations (e.g., a new training objective, better negative sampling or alignment strategies, improved candidate recall/precision trade-offs, or novel graph formulations), the contribution risks being incremental relative to extensive prior art.
- Likely outdated related work: The citations largely stop around 2016–2017 and omit major advances in transformer-based entity linking and retrieval, cross-encoder reranking, end-to-end/generative EL, and modern open-source systems/benchmarks developed in the last several years. Any serious EL/KGC paper must position itself against these methods, both in discussion and experiments.
- Missing experimental evidence and details: No datasets, metrics, baselines, or ablations are provided. For the described scope, one would expect comprehensive evaluation on standard EL benchmarks (including mention detection vs linking performance, NIL handling), rigorous candidate-generation recall studies, runtime/memory profiling of the automata/trie structures, and ablations separating local vs global components and text vs KG training signals.
- Clarity and reproducibility risks: Even if the method mirrors the references, key design choices matter: alias table construction, longest-match vs overlapping mentions, pruning heuristics, embedding architectures and losses, negative sampling, disambiguation/reranking features, and graph construction. None are available for assessment. The poor state of the bibliography also raises concerns about overall presentation quality.

Questions to Authors
- Scope and tasks: Is the paper about entity linking only, or does it also include knowledge graph completion? If both, how are the models shared across tasks, and do improvements in one transfer to the other?
- Mention detection: How is the dictionary built and maintained (aliases, redirects, surface-form mining)? How do you handle overlapping/ nested mentions (e.g., longest-match vs context-aware resolution)? What are recall/precision trade-offs vs NER-based detection? Provide runtime and memory profiles of Aho–Corasick vs double-array implementations.
- Joint embeddings: What model architecture and training objectives are used? How do you align tokens to entities (distant supervision, hyperlinks)? How is multi-sense handled (if at all)? Please detail negative sampling strategies, balancing text vs KG losses, and hyperparameters.
- Disambiguation: Describe the local and global scoring functions. Do you use bi-encoder retrieval plus cross-encoder reranking, or purely embedding similarity? How is the entity graph constructed (coherence edges, priors), and how is graph ranking integrated with local scores?
- Evaluation: Which datasets and metrics are used? Please include both mention detection and linking metrics, NIL detection, and overall EL quality. Compare against strong contemporary baselines (including transformer-based retrieval/reranking and generative EL), and report thorough ablations.
- Generalization: Do you support multilingual or cross-domain EL? How does the approach scale with KB size? Are there analyses across entity frequency/ambiguity buckets and long-tail entities?

Additional comments
- The reference section must be cleaned: deduplicate entries; correct author names, titles, venues, and years; remove stray/confidential text; ensure consistent formatting. Include recent, relevant work from the past several years across EL, dense retrieval, cross-encoder reranking, and generative approaches, as well as modern benchmarks.
- If the novelty lies primarily in engineering/scalability, make that explicit and quantify gains (throughput, latency, memory) alongside accuracy trade-offs. If the novelty lies in joint training, provide clear evidence that your objective yields consistent improvements over strong baselines.

Overall, while the inferred direction is relevant and potentially useful, the submission is currently unreviewable in substance. A complete, clearly written paper with modern baselines and rigorous experiments is necessary to assess contribution and significance.
