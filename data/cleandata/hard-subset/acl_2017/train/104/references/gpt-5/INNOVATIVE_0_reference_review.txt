Summary and contributions
The submission appears to target entity linking/disambiguation with a focus on efficiency and joint representation learning. From the reference list, I infer two likely pillars: (a) lexicon-based mention detection using classical string-matching machinery (Aho–Corasick, double-array tries), and (b) joint embedding of text and knowledge graphs for candidate ranking/disambiguation (citing Wang et al., Toutanova et al., Weston et al., Han et al., Wu et al., Yamada et al.). However, the manuscript provided is effectively a garbled reference section; there is no abstract, method, experiments, or claims. As a result, concrete contributions cannot be verified.

Tentative contributions as I see them (if these are indeed what the paper attempts):
1) An efficiency-centric EL pipeline that combines automata-based mention detection (Aho–Corasick/double-array) with neural joint text–KG embeddings for disambiguation.
2) A unified training/representation scheme for words, mentions, and entities, leveraging multi-sense/ multi-prototype ideas to improve EL.
3) Practical system design for large-scale EL with attention to throughput and memory.

Strengths
- Problem importance and potential impact: Efficient, high-quality entity linking that bridges symbolic (string/lexicon) and neural (joint embedding) components is impactful for real-world NLP systems. If the paper indeed proposes a tight integration, this direction could be valuable to the community.
- Breadth of cited prior work: The references cover core EL methods (Cucerzan; Hoffart et al.; Pershina et al.; Shen et al.), joint text–KG models (Wang et al.; Weston et al.; Toutanova et al.; Wang & Li; Han et al.; Wu et al.), and multi-sense embeddings (Reisinger & Mooney; Huang et al.; Neelakantan et al.; Tian et al.; Li & Jurafsky), suggesting awareness of relevant literature.
- Attention to efficiency: The repeated citations of Aho–Corasick and double-array tries indicate a principled starting point for fast mention detection, which is often the throughput bottleneck in EL. An efficiency-first design, if properly evaluated, would be a useful contribution.

Weaknesses
- Incomplete and corrupted submission: The provided file is essentially a reference list with extensive duplication, typographical errors, malformed entries, and “Confidential Review Copy” artifacts embedded in references. There is no title, abstract, method, results, analysis, or discussion. This makes it impossible to assess novelty, correctness, or empirical merit.
- Unclear novelty relative to strong prior art: Even if I infer the intended approach, dictionary-based mention detection via Aho–Corasick/double-array is standard in NER/EL pipelines, and joint text–KG embeddings for EL are well-trodden (Toutanova et al. 2015; Wang et al. 2014; Wang & Li 2016; Han et al. 2016; Yamada et al. 2016/2017; Wu et al. 2016). The paper would need to articulate a bold, clearly new idea (e.g., a fundamentally new training objective, a jointly learned finite-state/neural system, or a demonstrably superior representation) to rise above incrementalism. No such claim is visible.
- No empirical evidence: There are no datasets, baselines, metrics, or results. Without rigorous comparisons to established EL systems and joint-embedding baselines (and without efficiency benchmarks), the contribution cannot be validated.
- Scholarly presentation issues: The bibliography contains many duplicated and corrupted entries (e.g., “Efficie t string matching…”, “played it during public”), missing venues/years, inconsistent author names, and spurious placeholders. This undermines confidence in the care and rigor of the work and would not meet ACL standards.
- Reproducibility and scope: There is no information about resources (code/models), training data, KB coverage, or ethical considerations (e.g., biases in KBs), all necessary for a credible systems paper.

Questions to authors
- What is the core conceptual novelty in one sentence? Please contrast with Toutanova et al. (2015), Wang & Li (2016), Han et al. (2016), and Yamada et al. (2016/2017). What is fundamentally new beyond combining standard mention detection with joint embeddings?
- Method: How are mentions, contexts, and entities represented and trained jointly? What are the objectives (e.g., margin losses, softmax over candidates, mutual information surrogates)? How do you integrate KB structure (relations, paths) with text signals?
- Efficiency: What are the throughput and memory trade-offs of your mention detector (Aho–Corasick vs. double-array vs. alternative tries)? Do you support overlapping/longest-match mentions and multilingual tokenization? Provide end-to-end EL latency and throughput on standard corpora.
- Evaluation: Which datasets and metrics do you use (AIDA-CoNLL, TAC KBP, GERBIL benchmarks; micro/macro accuracy, nDCG, strong/weak matching)? What baselines are included (including recent neural EL models)? Show ablations isolating the impact of (a) your mention detector, (b) joint embeddings, and (c) any novel training components.
- Analysis: Provide calibration/error analysis, entity prior vs. context contributions, rare-entity performance, and robustness to noisy mentions.
- Resources: Will you release code, models, and dictionaries? Clarify licensing and preprocessing to ensure reproducibility.
- Presentation: Please fix the bibliography (deduplicate, correct typos and venues) and remove internal placeholders.

Recommendation
Reject. I prioritize bold, novel ideas even with minor imperfections, but the submission in its current form is incomplete and does not allow evaluation of novelty, correctness, or empirical value. If the intended contribution is a substantively new integration or learning formulation that moves beyond established joint embedding EL pipelines and standard mention detection, I encourage a resubmission with a complete, carefully written paper, strong empirical comparisons, and clear articulation of the conceptual advance.
