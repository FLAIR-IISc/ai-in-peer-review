Summary
The submission appears to target named entity linking/disambiguation (EL), combining efficient dictionary-based mention detection (Aho–Corasick automata and double-array tries) with joint text–knowledge graph (KG) representation learning for candidate ranking. The bibliography suggests evaluation with IR-style metrics (e.g., NDCG) on standard EL datasets and comparisons to graph-based and neural baselines. However, the paper text is missing or severely corrupted; only an error-ridden references section is provided, with truncated phrases from the main text. As submitted, the manuscript is incomplete.

Contributions (as I see them)
- A practical EL pipeline that uses fast lexicon-based mention detection (Aho–Corasick/double-array trie) with joint embeddings of text and KG for ranking.
- An empirical study (implied by references) benchmarking against graph-ranking and neural EL systems with IR metrics.
- An engineering optimization for memory/time-efficient dictionary matching (implied by the double-array trie references).

Strengths
- Sensible pipeline design: Using Aho–Corasick/double-array tries for high-recall mention detection is a well-validated engineering choice for EL, and combining this with joint text–KG embeddings is a reasonable architecture that balances speed and accuracy.
- Awareness of core related work: The bibliography (despite being corrupted) touches on key strands: classical EL (Hoffart et al., Pershina et al., Kulkarni et al., Cucerzan), joint text–KG methods (Toutanova et al., Wang & Li, Weston et al., Wu et al.), multi-sense embeddings (Reisinger & Mooney; Huang et al.; Neelakantan et al.; Tian et al.), and evaluation metrics (Järvelin & Kekäläinen). This coverage suggests the authors intended a comprehensive positioning.
- Potential practical impact: If the method truly couples fast dictionary matching with strong neural ranking and is evaluated at scale, it could be useful for production EL systems that need both throughput and accuracy.

Weaknesses
- Incomplete/invalid submission: The manuscript content is missing; the only material provided is an extremely corrupted references section interleaved with stray fragments of paper text (e.g., “predict the context words by maximizing the following objective functi”). This precludes assessing methodology, experiments, or conclusions. As submitted, this is not reviewable.
- Severe reference quality issues: The bibliography contains pervasive errors:
  - Massive duplication (e.g., Aho–Corasick and Aoe 1989 appear many times).
  - Extensive typos and mutilations (“EEE Transactions on Software Engine ring,” “Efficie t string match ng,” “knowledg graph,” “digit l,” “CoRR abs/1611.04125. played it during public”).
  - Inconsistent and incorrect venues/metadata (e.g., Schütze 2008 listed as “Proceedings of the international communication of association for computing machinery conference” instead of a textbook; missing conference names, pages; improper “CoRR”/arXiv formatting; “Proceedings of the Conference” for Hoffart et al. with no venue).
  - Spurious tokens and LaTeX/escape artifacts (“\Q2017\E,” “et al\.”).
  - Placeholder or nonsense author fields (“n et al.”, “e.”, “k.”).
  These issues alone fail basic scholarly standards and would block publication without major correction.
- Anonymity/confidentiality and policy concerns: The references include strings like “Confid tia[l] Review Copy. DO NOT DISTRIBUTE” and “Confidential Review Copy, DISTRIBUTE,” which indicates mishandling of submission headers/footers and raises process concerns. The ref “Ikuya Yamada, … ACL, \Q2017\E” is oddly marked “Confidential Review Copy,” suggesting confusion between prior work and the present submission. Such artifacts compromise professionalism and potentially double-blind integrity.
- Novelty unclear relative to prior art: Even if the intended method is as inferred, many works already combine high-recall mention detection with neural/joint embedding ranking for EL (e.g., Yamada et al. 2016/2017, Huang et al. 2015, Toutanova et al. 2015, Wang & Li 2016). Without a clear methodological differentiation (new training objective, better negative sampling, improved graph modeling, or demonstrably superior speed–accuracy tradeoffs), novelty appears incremental.
- Likely outdated framing/baselines: The bibliography is largely pre-2017. Modern EL methods (bi-encoder/cross-encoder retrieval-rerankers, sequence-to-entity generators, multilingual/multi-mention models) are absent. If the experiments mirror the references, the evaluation would not be competitive with recent systems and would miss strong baselines (e.g., BLINK-style architectures, GENRE/mGENRE, bootstrapped entity encoders, dual-encoder + in-batch negatives, entity-aware LMs).

Questions to Authors
- Please provide the complete paper. As currently submitted, the manuscript is incomplete. Without the body (method, experiments, analysis), the work cannot be evaluated.
- Clarify the core novelty over prior EL systems that already combine lexicon-based mention detection and joint text–KG embeddings. What is new in your model, training objective, or inference algorithm?
- Detail the candidate generation pipeline: dictionary construction (redirects, disambiguation pages), handling of overlapping mentions, morphological variants, and multilinguality. How are the tries built (double-array specifics), and what are memory and latency figures at Wikipedia scale?
- Describe the embedding training: corpora, preprocessing, negative sampling, alignment between text and KG, treatment of multi-sense entities/words, and how ambiguity is handled. What is the exact objective (the truncated “predict the context words …” suggests word2vec-like training)?
- Provide datasets, splits, and metrics. Which benchmarks (AIDA-CoNLL, MSNBC, AQUAINT, TAC, GERBIL datasets)? How do you evaluate NIL detection? Which metrics (micro/macro accuracy, in-KB accuracy, NDCG, MRR)?
- Compare against recent strong baselines and ablate each component (mention detector, candidate generator, ranker, graph propagation). Provide statistical significance and error analysis.
- Release code, model checkpoints, and dictionaries for reproducibility. Are there licensing constraints for Wikipedia/Wikidata use?

Given the incomplete and corrupted submission and the extensive scholarly/formatting issues, I recommend rejection in its current form. If the authors can provide a complete, polished manuscript with clear novelty and strong modern comparisons, a resubmission could be considered.
