Summary
The submission appears to propose an end-to-end entity linking pipeline that combines (i) efficient dictionary-based mention detection and candidate generation via classical string-matching data structures (Aho–Corasick automata and double-array tries) with (ii) a neural ranking component that leverages joint embeddings of text and knowledge bases for entity disambiguation. The paper draws on work in joint text–KG representation learning and multi-sense word embeddings, and it evaluates with ranking metrics such as NDCG.

Claimed contributions (as I see them)
1) An integrated, high-throughput candidate generation module using Aho–Corasick and double-array tries for large lexicons, intended to yield high recall at low latency for entity linking.
2) A neural disambiguation model that uses joint text–KG embeddings to score mention–entity pairs, inspired by prior joint embedding work.
3) An empirical evaluation of the combined system on entity linking benchmarks (unclear from the provided text which datasets), with ranking-based metrics.

Strengths
- Practical integration of classic string-matching with neural EL: Combining Aho–Corasick and double-array tries for candidate generation with a neural ranker is a sensible engineering choice for production-quality EL systems. If the system demonstrates high recall and throughput at scale, this is practically valuable and aligns with real-world needs.
- Use of joint text–KG representations: Building on prior work (e.g., Toutanova et al., Wang & Li, Han et al.) to jointly model textual and KB signals for disambiguation is methodologically appropriate; such models can capture semantic relatedness beyond surface-form similarity, which is important for difficult EL cases.
- Attention to efficiency: The references to double-array tries suggest attention to memory-efficient implementations. If accompanied by quantitative speed/memory comparisons, this could be a strong contribution for large-vocabulary EL.
- Evaluation with ranking metrics: The use of NDCG (Järvelin & Kekäläinen) suggests an awareness of graded relevance and ranked candidate evaluation, which is appropriate for EL candidate ranking.

Weaknesses
- Insufficient clarity and completeness of the submission: Based on the provided material, the core method, model architecture, training objective, datasets, and results are not described with enough detail to assess novelty or validity. The brief mention of “predict the context words by maximizing the objective” hints at a skip-gram-style loss, but the specifics (negative sampling, context window, entity–word co-occurrence modeling) are missing. Without full experimental details and ablations, the contribution cannot be adequately evaluated.
- Novelty appears limited: The overall design—dictionary/AC-based mention detection + neural ranker with joint embeddings—has been explored by numerous EL systems circa 2014–2019 (e.g., Cucerzan 2007; Hoffart et al. 2011; Pershina et al. 2015; Yamada et al. 2016/2017; and later neural global models). The paper needs to articulate what is materially new (e.g., a novel training objective; a new way to fuse text–KG signals; a principled global inference scheme; or measurable efficiency gains) and provide strong empirical evidence against competitive baselines.
- Evaluation design and baselines unclear: It is not clear which datasets (e.g., AIDA-CoNLL, TAC KBP, WNED) are used, what the train/dev/test splits are, and how NIL/UNK mentions are handled. Strong, recent baselines for EL are not clearly cited or compared against (e.g., neural global models, cross-encoder rankers). Without rigorous comparisons and standardized splits, it is difficult to judge significance.
- References and scholarship issues: The references are heavily corrupted: duplicated entries, misspellings, missing venues, stray phrases (“played it during public,” “Confidential Review Copy”), inconsistent formatting, and incomplete metadata. This raises concerns about care in scholarship and, potentially, about the reliability of experimental setup and claims.
- Missing analyses that are critical for EL: There is no explicit report (in the provided text) of candidate recall, precision of mention detection, throughput (docs/sec), memory footprint of the automata/tries, or ablations quantifying the impact of each component (AC-based detection, embedding choices, global coherence, priors). Error analysis (e.g., head vs tail entities, surface-form ambiguity, cross-domain generalization) is also absent.

Questions to Authors
- Datasets and splits: Which EL datasets do you evaluate on? Are you using standard AIDA-CoNLL splits? How do you handle NIL/Out-of-KB mentions?
- Candidate generation: What is the recall@K of your candidate generator per mention, and how does it trade off with lexicon size and memory usage? Please provide throughput (docs/sec) and peak memory for the AC automaton and the double-array trie, and compare to alternative implementations (e.g., standard trie, DAWG, FlashText/Hyperscan if applicable).
- Disambiguation model: Please detail the architecture (bi-encoder vs cross-encoder vs hybrid), input representations (mention/context windows, entity descriptions, priors), and the training objective. If you use joint text–KG embeddings, how exactly are entity–word and entity–entity co-occurrences modeled? What negative sampling strategy do you use?
- Global coherence and priors: Do you incorporate global document-level coherence (e.g., entity coherence graphs, PPR) or only local scoring? How are popularity/entity priors used or calibrated?
- Baselines: Which strong, recent EL baselines do you compare against? Please include both feature-based (e.g., AIDA/collective models) and neural models (e.g., global neural EL, cross-encoder rankers), and ensure comparisons on standardized splits.
- Ablations: Please provide ablations isolating (i) mention detection vs disambiguation, (ii) embedding choices (word-only vs entity-only vs joint), (iii) use of priors, (iv) global coherence, and (v) candidate set size.
- Generalization: How does the system perform on out-of-domain text (e.g., news vs Wikipedia vs web forums)? Do you report zero-shot performance for unseen entities or robustness to surface-form noise?
- Reproducibility: Will you release code, pre-trained embeddings, the mention lexicon, and scripts to build the AC/trie structures? Provide enough detail to reproduce your measurements.

Additional comments and suggestions
- Clean and de-duplicate the references, fix typos, and ensure complete metadata (authors, title, venue, year). Several entries are repeated or corrupted; this must be corrected for publication.
- Provide a clear problem statement and system overview diagram. Precisely define mention detection, candidate generation, and ranking modules, with complexity analysis.
- Report comprehensive metrics: micro/macro accuracy, precision/recall/F1, recall@K for candidates, MRR/NDCG for ranking, end-to-end EL accuracy including NIL detection.
- Include strong baselines and recent methods; if your contribution is primarily efficiency, demonstrate clear wins in latency and memory at comparable accuracy, and provide a cost–quality trade-off analysis.
- Add qualitative error analysis and case studies showing where joint text–KG embeddings help or fail, especially for polysemous mentions and rare entities.

Decision
Based on the information available, I do not recommend acceptance at this time. The idea is practical, but the novelty is unclear, the paper as provided lacks methodological and experimental detail, and the references indicate significant issues with presentation. With a clearer articulation of novel contributions, rigorous evaluation against strong baselines, thorough ablations, and corrected scholarship, the work could be substantially strengthened.
