Summary and Contributions
Based on the manuscript’s citations and snippets, the paper appears to present an entity linking/disambiguation system that combines: (i) efficient dictionary-based mention detection using Aho–Corasick and/or double-array tries; (ii) joint embedding of words and entities trained with a skip-gram–style objective and possibly augmented with knowledge graph structure; and (iii) candidate ranking using graph-based methods (e.g., Personalized PageRank) and/or embedding similarity. The goal is to improve both accuracy and efficiency of entity linking.

As I see them, the main contributions are:
1) An efficient mention detection component using classical multi-pattern string matching (Aho–Corasick) and double-array tries to scale to large dictionaries with low latency and memory footprint.
2) A joint text–knowledge embedding for entities and words tailored to entity disambiguation, trained with a predictive objective over context words (and possibly KG relations).
3) An end-to-end evaluation of the resulting EL pipeline against established baselines on standard datasets (implied by the references), with attention to both accuracy and efficiency.

Strengths
- Relevance and potential impact: Entity linking remains a core task in NLP applications (IR, QA, IE). A system that emphasizes both accuracy and runtime efficiency is of practical value. The references indicate the paper situates itself at the intersection of fast mention detection and modern embedding-based disambiguation, a useful combination for large-scale deployment.

- Methodological grounding in established techniques: The mention detection approach (Aho–Corasick, double-array tries) is well-suited for high-throughput dictionary matching, and the embedding component (skip-gram–style objectives, joint text–KG training) is consistent with prior work on word/sense/entity representations. This combination is technically plausible and sound if implemented and evaluated carefully.

- Coverage of prior literature (breadth): The references span core EL methods (Hoffart et al., Pershina et al., Kulkarni et al., Shen et al.), representation learning (Mikolov et al., multi-prototype embeddings), and joint text–KG models (Toutanova et al., Wang & Li, Han et al.). This suggests awareness of the main strands relevant to the proposed approach.

- Emphasis on efficiency: If the paper provides quantitative measurements of indexing/memory footprint, throughput (mentions/sec), and latency improvements, the engineering contribution around double-array tries and Aho–Corasick could be a practical strength, particularly for industrial-scale EL.

Weaknesses
- Clarity and presentation issues in the bibliography: The reference list contains numerous typographical errors, duplications, incomplete fields, and corrupted lines (e.g., repeated Aho–Corasick entries with misspellings; “Confidential Review Copy” fragments inside references). This undermines clarity and raises concerns about editorial rigor. A clean, deduplicated, and accurate bibliography is necessary.

- Ambiguity about novelty: The core components—dictionary-based mention detection, joint word–entity embeddings, and graph-based/candidate-ranking methods—are well established before 2016–2017. The manuscript’s unique algorithmic contributions are not clearly distinguishable from prior art based on the materials available. Without a precise description of what is algorithmically new (objective functions, architectures, training schemes, inference), the novelty appears incremental.

- Unclear experimental design: The materials do not specify datasets, metrics, or baselines. For EL, standard datasets include AIDA-CoNLL, MSNBC, AQUAINT, ACE, and TAC-KBP variants; standard metrics include micro/macro accuracy, in-KB accuracy, precision/recall/F1 for mention detection, and speed/latency. It is not clear whether strong baselines from the same period (e.g., neural/local–global EL methods) are included, whether candidate recall is reported, or whether ablations isolate the effect of each component (dictionary trie, embeddings, graph inference).

- Integration details missing: The way knowledge graph structure is incorporated into the embeddings (e.g., via relation-specific objectives, joint training vs. post-hoc retrofitting) is not described. Similarly, the interface between mention detection, candidate generation, and disambiguation (e.g., how priors, popularity, or coherence signals are combined) is unclear. This limits assessment of technical soundness.

- Reproducibility concerns: There is no clear indication of released code/models, training data preprocessing, hyperparameters, or hardware/runtime. Given the focus on efficiency, such details are essential for verifying claims. Absence of error analysis also limits interpretability and practical insights.

Questions to Authors
- Novelty and technical specifics:
  - What is the precise novelty beyond existing joint text–KG embedding approaches (e.g., objective functions, negative sampling strategies, incorporation of relations/paths, training curricula)?
  - How does your disambiguation scoring function combine local context signals (from embeddings) with global coherence or graph-based signals? Please specify the exact formula/model.

- Mention detection:
  - What is the size of the mention dictionary, and how is it constructed (surface forms, redirects, aliases, heuristics)? What is the memory footprint and build time of the double-array trie?
  - What are the mention detection precision/recall/F1 on standard benchmarks, and how do they trade off with speed? Do you handle case-folding, tokenization mismatches, nested or overlapping mentions?

- Candidate generation and recall:
  - What is the average number of candidates per mention, and what is the candidate recall at K? How are candidates sourced (prior/probability tables, string similarity, redirects)?

- Embedding training:
  - What corpora and knowledge graphs are used for training? How are sentences and entity annotations obtained/aligned? What are the training hyperparameters and objective variants?
  - Do you train words and entities in the same space jointly, or do you align pretrained spaces? Any constraints or regularizers to tie entities to their surface words?

- Evaluation:
  - Which datasets and splits are used (e.g., AIDA CoNLL train/dev/test)? What metrics are reported? Please include statistical significance testing where applicable.
  - Which baselines are compared, and are they re-run under the same candidate sets and mention spans? Include both classic (Hoffart, Pershina) and contemporary neural baselines from the same period.
  - Provide ablations isolating the contributions of (i) mention detection method, (ii) joint embeddings vs. text-only embeddings, (iii) graph/collective inference.

- Efficiency:
  - Report end-to-end throughput and latency, with and without collective inference, and for different dictionary sizes. Provide hardware specs and asymptotic/empirical complexity.

- Analysis:
  - Include qualitative/error analyses highlighting common failure modes (rare entities, NIL cases, long mentions, ambiguous contexts) and show how each component mitigates them.

Overall, the topic is important and the proposed combination of efficient mention detection with joint embeddings is potentially useful. However, the current clarity (especially bibliographic quality), the unclear extent of novelty, and the missing methodological and evaluation details need to be addressed to enable a thorough assessment of technical soundness and significance.
