Summary and claimed contributions
From the references and snippets, the paper appears to propose a unified framework that (a) jointly learns embeddings of words, word senses, and entities using text context and knowledge-graph signals, and (b) couples this with a highly efficient dictionary-based mention detection pipeline implemented via Aho–Corasick and a double-array trie. The model is evaluated on entity linking/disambiguation (and possibly KG completion) with metrics such as NDCG.

As I see them, the main contributions are:
1) A unified training objective that combines skip-gram-style context prediction with KG-based constraints to jointly embed words, multi-prototype word senses, and entities for entity linking. This aims to bridge multi-sense word representations and entity embeddings in a single space.
2) A scalable, engineering-focused contribution: high-throughput mention spotting via Aho–Corasick with a double-array structure, enabling training/inference over full Wikipedia-scale data on modest hardware.
3) Empirical gains on entity linking benchmarks (and possibly KG completion), and potential release of resources.

Strengths
- Conceptual unification across words, senses, and entities: Bringing multi-prototype word embeddings together with entity embeddings and KG structure in one objective is a bold direction that could simplify pipelines and enable cross-task transfer. If the model indeed learns a shared space where sense-specific word vectors and entities align, this is a meaningful step beyond task-specific embeddings.
- Emphasis on scalability and practicality: The use of Aho–Corasick with a double-array trie for mention spotting is a strong engineering choice. If the paper demonstrates concrete throughput/memory numbers and shows that these choices enable training over the entirety of Wikipedia with stable latency during inference, that is of high practical value for the community.
- Multi-signal training: Leveraging both textual context and KG constraints often improves robustness and generalization. A clean formulation that balances these signals and yields consistent improvements on EL metrics (e.g., AIDA/CoNLL) would substantiate the approach’s utility beyond a single dataset.
- Potentially broad impact: A released toolkit/resources (if provided) that integrate fast mention detection with unified embeddings could be widely adopted and lower the barrier to building EL systems.

Weaknesses
- Novelty relative to prior art is unclear: Very closely related work already exists on joint text–KG embedding (e.g., Wang et al., 2014; Toutanova et al., 2015; Han et al., 2016; Wu et al., 2016) and on joint word–entity embeddings for EL (Yamada et al., 2016/2017). Multi-prototype embeddings are also well studied (Reisinger & Mooney, 2010; Neelakantan et al., 2014; Tian et al., 2014; Mancini et al., 2016). The paper needs to articulate precisely what is new in its objective/architecture beyond these, and to isolate that novelty with targeted ablations.
- Underspecified methodology in the submission: The excerpt shows “predict the context words by maximizing...” but the full loss, the interaction between text and KG signals, and how sense induction is performed (inventory, alignment, disambiguation during training) are not clearly described here. Without exact formulations and training details, it is hard to assess rigor or reproducibility.
- EL pipeline details are missing/unclear: Given references to graph-based disambiguation (e.g., Personalized PageRank, collective coherence), the actual candidate generation, prior estimation, global inference, and the role of the learned embeddings in the scoring function are not specified. Are priors and coherence features used? How are they combined with embeddings?
- Evaluation coverage and baselines: It is unclear which datasets are used (AIDA/CoNLL, TAC-KBP, Wikipedia hyperlink prediction, etc.), how hyperparameters are chosen, whether results control for mention prior and candidate dictionaries, and whether comparisons are against the strongest EL systems. Ablations for each proposed component (KG loss, sense splitting, mention detector) are essential to support the claims.
- Presentation quality: The reference list has numerous corruptions/duplicates/typos, and there are “Confidential Review Copy” artefacts. This detracts from clarity and raises concerns about carefulness in reporting.

Questions to authors
- What is the concrete novelty versus Yamada et al. (2016/2017) and Toutanova et al. (2015)? Please provide a side-by-side comparison of the training objective, the inventory used (entities vs. senses), and the inference pipeline, plus ablations that isolate your new component(s).
- How are multiple prototypes (senses) induced? Do you use an external sense inventory (e.g., WordNet), Wikipedia/KB-derived senses, or unsupervised clustering? How are senses aligned with entities, and how is sense disambiguation handled during training?
- Please provide the exact loss(es) used, including weighting between text and KG terms, negative sampling strategy, and how entity–relation structure is incorporated (e.g., translational vs. bilinear vs. contrastive objectives).
- For mention detection: What dictionary is used, how large is it, how are redirects/aliases handled, and what are the throughput and memory footprints of the Aho–Corasick + double-array implementation? Can you release the code?
- What is the candidate generation strategy and global disambiguation algorithm? Do you use priors (P(entity|mention)), coherence signals, or graph propagation (e.g., PPR)? How do embeddings integrate into the final scoring?
- Evaluation: Which datasets and metrics are used? Do you compare against strong EL baselines (including those using priors and coherence)? Please include ablations for: removing KG loss; removing sense splitting; replacing your mention detector with a standard dictionary lookup; and replacing your embeddings with off-the-shelf word/entity embeddings.
- Generalization: How does the model handle NIL/unknown entities? Any zero-shot or tail-entity analysis?

Overall, I am enthusiastic about the high-level ambition to unify words, senses, and entities with an efficient end-to-end pipeline. However, given the strong overlap with prior art and the current lack of clarity around the exact methodological novelty and evaluation, I cannot judge the contribution as clearly new and significant. If the authors can precisely delineate and empirically validate the novel pieces and improve presentation, this could become a compelling submission.
