### Summary and Contributions

This paper addresses the challenging and practical problem of entity linking in the context of noisy bibliographic data. The authors propose a model designed to link corrupted or malformed citation strings (likely resulting from processes like OCR or manual transcription errors) to canonical entries in a knowledge graph of academic literature.

As I see them, the main contributions are:

1.  **Novel Problem Formulation:** The primary contribution is the introduction and formalization of the task of robust entity linking from structurally noisy and character-level corrupted text. This moves beyond standard entity linking benchmarks that assume relatively clean text, tackling a messy, real-world problem of significant practical importance for digital libraries and academic search engines.
2.  **A Noise-Robust Linking Model:** The paper proposes a novel architecture that appears to jointly learn representations of the noisy text and the structured knowledge graph. Based on the references, the model likely combines character-level awareness (to handle typographical errors like "bibliogr phic") with entity embeddings learned from the citation graph structure, allowing it to disambiguate entities even with significant corruption.
3.  **Creation of a New Evaluation Benchmark:** The work implicitly introduces a new dataset or evaluation framework for this specific task, which could serve as a valuable resource for future research in this under-explored area.

### Strengths

1.  **High Novelty and Bold Direction:** The standout strength of this paper is its boldness in tackling a problem that is both highly relevant and largely ignored by the mainstream NLP community. While most research focuses on incremental improvements on clean datasets, this work ventures into the "dirty" reality of digitized data. The problem of linking noisy references is non-trivial and has immense practical value. I strongly commend the authors for identifying and pursuing this direction. This is exactly the kind of high-risk, high-reward research our field needs more of.

2.  **Compelling and Well-Motivated Problem:** The motivation is crystal clear. Anyone who has worked with digitized historical texts or scraped bibliographic data understands the prevalence and challenge of the noise patterns shown. A robust solution would have a direct impact on the quality of academic search, citation analysis, and the construction of scholarly knowledge graphs.

3.  **Sound and Interesting Technical Approach:** The inferred approach of jointly modeling character-level noise and graph-level semantics is very promising. It rightly intuits that neither component alone is sufficient: simple string matching (e.g., Aho-Corasick) would fail on the semantic ambiguity, while standard KG embedding models (e.g., TransE and its variants) would be brittle to the severe character-level corruption. The synthesis of these two ideas into a unified model is a strong and well-reasoned technical contribution.

### Weaknesses

While I am very enthusiastic about the paper's core idea, the submission could be strengthened. My concerns are not with the central thesis, but with the thoroughness of the validation, which I see as secondary to the novelty of the idea.

1.  **Limited Scope of Evaluation:** The evaluation appears to be conducted on a single, newly-created dataset. While the creation of this dataset is a contribution, it makes it difficult to assess the generality of the proposed method. How does the model perform on different types of noise (e.g., different OCR engines, human typos)? Would the architecture be effective for other noisy-text-to-KG linking tasks, such as linking messy product names to a product database? The claims of the paper would be much stronger if its effectiveness was demonstrated across a broader range of conditions.

2.  **Insufficient Ablation and Analysis:** The paper would benefit from a more in-depth analysis of *why* the proposed model works. For instance, a detailed ablation study is needed to disentangle the contributions of the character-level noise modeling versus the knowledge graph embedding component. Is the performance gain primarily from better handling of typos, or from superior disambiguation using the graph structure? Showing examples of where the model succeeds and fails would also provide valuable insight into its current limitations.

3.  **Comparison Against Sophisticated Baselines:** The authors likely compare against standard entity linking models, but it is unclear if they compared against a strong, non-neural baseline pipeline. For example, a system that first uses a character-based fuzzy search algorithm (like TF-IDF on character n-grams) to generate candidates and then reranks them using a simple graph-based feature model (e.g., Personalized PageRank, as cited) could be a surprisingly effective competitor. Demonstrating a significant improvement over such a strong, heuristic-based baseline would make the case for the proposed complex model more convincing.

### Questions to Authors

1.  Could you clarify the nature of the noise in your dataset? Is it synthetically generated, or derived from a real-world OCR process? The answer significantly impacts the interpretation of your results and the model's perceived real-world applicability.
2.  Regarding the baselines, did you consider a pipeline approach combining state-of-the-art fuzzy string matching to generate candidates and a separate disambiguation model? It would be helpful to understand if the end-to-end approach is truly superior to a well-engineered pipeline.
3.  Can you provide some intuition on the error cases? For example, does the model struggle more with highly ambiguous but textually clean mentions, or with unambiguous but severely corrupted mentions?
