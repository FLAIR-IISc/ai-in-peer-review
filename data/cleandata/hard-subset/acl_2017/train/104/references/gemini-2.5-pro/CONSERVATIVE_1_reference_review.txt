**Summary and Contributions**

This paper addresses the task of Named Entity Disambiguation (NED) or Entity Linking (EL) by proposing a hybrid system. The approach appears to combine classical, highly efficient string-matching algorithms for the candidate generation phase with a more standard neural embedding-based model for the final disambiguation step.

As I see them, the main contributions of this work are:
1.  An empirical investigation into the effectiveness of integrating decades-old, exact string-matching techniques (specifically, methods related to Aho-Corasick and double-array tries) as a front-end for a modern neural entity disambiguation pipeline.
2.  An analysis of the trade-offs between computational efficiency and end-to-end accuracy for the proposed hybrid model, evaluated on standard EL benchmark datasets.

**Strengths**

1.  **Addresses a Practical and Relevant Problem:** The paper tackles the problem of efficiency in large-scale entity linking. As knowledge bases grow, generating a high-recall set of candidate entities for a given mention in a computationally tractable manner remains a significant challenge. The authors' motivation to improve this specific component of the EL pipeline is well-founded.

2.  **Principled Choice of Components (in isolation):** The choice of Aho-Corasick-style algorithms for the mention/candidate detection part is, in principle, sound. These methods are well-understood, offer provable linear-time complexity, and are known to be highly efficient in practice for multi-pattern exact string matching. Similarly, the use of joint embedding models for text and knowledge graphs for the disambiguation component aligns with the current state-of-the-art in this area.

3.  **Potential for Strong Empirical Baselines:** The comprehensive list of references suggests that the authors have likely compared their system against a reasonable set of established baselines in the field of entity linking (e.g., Hoffart et al., 2011; Cucerzan, 2007). If executed properly, this evaluation could provide a valuable data point for the community regarding the performance of such a hybrid system.

**Weaknesses**

1.  **Questionable Methodological Novelty:** The primary concern with this submission is its apparent lack of research novelty. The proposed method seems to be a straightforward engineering combination of two very distinct and well-established lines of work: string matching algorithms from the 1970s-80s and neural embedding models from the last decade. While combining existing components can sometimes lead to novel insights, the paper does not seem to propose a new learning paradigm or a fundamental technique. Rather, it appears to replace one module (candidate generation) in a standard pipeline with an older, off-the-shelf algorithm. This raises questions about whether the contribution is sufficient for a top-tier research conference like ACL, as it may be more appropriate for a system demonstration or a more application-focused venue.

2.  **Insufficient Justification Against Modern Alternatives:** The central premise of using classic string-matching algorithms for candidate generation needs stronger justification. The literature has moved towards other solutions for this problem, including approximate nearest neighbor (ANN) searches in embedding space, learning-to-hash methods, and other forms of dense retrieval which can handle synonyms, paraphrases, and non-exact matches more gracefully. The authors do not appear to have compared their candidate generation module against these more contemporary and functionally powerful alternatives. The evaluation seems to be focused solely on end-to-end EL accuracy, which fails to isolate and validate the claimed superiority of the proposed candidate generation method itself.

3.  **Evaluation May Not Adequately Support the Central Claim:** The main argument for this work rests on efficiency. However, a simple comparison of end-to-end runtimes is insufficient. The evaluation must include a detailed analysis of the candidate generation step in isolation, measuring not only speed and memory usage but also, crucially, recall. A fast but low-recall candidate generator can cripple the performance of the downstream disambiguation model, leading to a system that is fast but inaccurate. The authors must demonstrate that their approach maintains high recall compared to strong baselines while providing a significant efficiency advantage. Without a thorough ablation study and a direct comparison on the candidate generation sub-task, the core claims of the paper remain unsubstantiated.

**Questions to Authors**

1.  Could you please clarify the core research contribution of your work beyond the direct combination of an Aho-Corasick-style automaton and a standard neural disambiguation model? Is there a novel insight in how these two components are integrated that is not immediately obvious?

2.  The motivation for your approach is efficiency. Have you compared your candidate generation method directly against more modern techniques, such as dense retrieval systems or ANN-based approaches, on the specific sub-task of candidate generation? A comparison should include metrics for speed, memory usage, and, most importantly, recall of the correct entity.

3.  The work of Yamada et al. (cited in your references) also presents a system for large-scale entity disambiguation with a focus on efficiency. Could you elaborate on the key differences between your proposed architecture and theirs, and provide a direct empirical comparison if possible? It is important to position your work against such highly relevant and strong baselines.
