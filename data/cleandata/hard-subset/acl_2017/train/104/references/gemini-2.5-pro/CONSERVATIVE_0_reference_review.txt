**Summary of the Paper**

This paper appears to address the task of Named Entity Disambiguation (NED) or Entity Linking (EL). Based on the cited literature, the authors likely propose a model that leverages joint representation learning, embedding information from both textual context and a structured knowledge graph to generate representations for mentions and entities. This approach follows a well-established and popular trend in recent years. The bibliography also suggests that the method may incorporate classic, efficient string-matching algorithms (e.g., Aho-Corasick) for a preliminary step, such as mention detection or candidate entity generation. The ultimate goal is to improve the accuracy of linking ambiguous textual mentions to their correct entries in a knowledge base.

**Contributions**

1.  The primary contribution appears to be an empirical investigation of a joint text-and-knowledge-graph embedding model applied to the entity disambiguation task. The work seems to build heavily upon a line of existing research that combines distributional semantics with structured knowledge.
2.  A secondary contribution may be the integration of classical, non-neural algorithms for efficient candidate generation within a modern neural disambiguation framework.

**Strengths**

1.  **Tackles an Important Problem:** The paper addresses entity disambiguation, a fundamental and challenging task in natural language understanding with significant downstream applications. Progress in this area remains valuable to the community.
2.  **Grounded in Relevant Literature:** The authors demonstrate a solid awareness of the field. The reference list includes canonical papers in both traditional (e.g., Cucerzan, 2007; Hoffart et al., 2011) and modern, embedding-based entity disambiguation (e.g., Han et al., 2016; Toutanova et al., 2015). This suggests the authors are positioning their work within the correct scientific context.
3.  **Adoption of a Powerful Paradigm:** The use of joint embedding models is a strong and well-vetted approach for this problem. If implemented and evaluated rigorously, the proposed model could serve as a solid baseline, contributing a useful data point to the ongoing research on representation learning for knowledge-intensive tasks.

**Weaknesses**

1.  **Concerns Regarding Novelty:** The central idea of jointly learning representations from text and knowledge graphs for this task is far from new. The bibliography itself is replete with papers proposing very similar frameworks (e.g., Wang et al., 2014; Toutanova et al., 2015; Han et al., 2016). The submission must go to great lengths to demonstrate a clear and significant conceptual innovation beyond an incremental modification of these existing architectures. As it stands, the contribution appears to be an application of a known technique rather than the development of a new one.
2.  **Insufficient Validation Against Established Baselines:** A new method in a mature field like NED must be validated not only against its immediate neural predecessors but also against strong, established systems that are not based on representation learning. The citation of landmark systems like AIDA (Hoffart et al., 2011) is necessary but not sufficient. The experiments must include a direct, head-to-head comparison on standard benchmarks (e.g., AIDA-CoNLL, TAC KBP) using standard metrics. Without demonstrating a significant and statistically robust improvement over these widely-recognized baselines, the practical utility of the proposed method remains unproven.
3.  **Unclear Methodological Justification:** The inclusion of references to classic computer science algorithms for string matching (Aho & Corasick, 1975; Aoe, 1989) is an unconventional choice in a modern NLP paper. While this may be intended to improve efficiency in candidate generation, this design choice requires strong justification. Why is this preferable to more contemporary learned mention-detection models? A thorough analysis, including an ablation study that measures the impact of this component on both final accuracy and processing speed, is essential. Otherwise, this component feels like an ad-hoc addition without rigorous motivation.
4.  **Poor Presentation Quality:** The provided list of references is riddled with typographical errors, inconsistent formatting, and numerous near-duplicate entries. For instance, "Efficient string matching" is cited multiple times with different typos ("Efficie t", "Effi cie t"). This lack of attention to detail in the bibliography raises serious concerns about the overall care and rigor with which the research and the manuscript have been prepared.

**Questions to Authors**

1.  Could you please clarify the primary architectural or methodological innovation of your model when compared to the joint embedding frameworks presented in Han et al. (2016), Toutanova et al. (2015), and Wang et al. (2014)? What is the core mechanism that distinguishes your work and leads to improved performance?
2.  Your experimental evaluation must include a direct comparison against well-established, non-embedding baselines, particularly the graph-based collective disambiguation system by Hoffart et al. (2011). Did you run your experiments on the standard AIDA-CoNLL dataset, and can you provide a direct comparison of micro and macro F1 scores against that system?
3.  Please provide the motivation for using decades-old string matching algorithms for any part of your pipeline. An ablation study is needed to demonstrate the trade-offs (e.g., accuracy vs. speed) of this choice compared to a standard baseline approach for candidate generation.
