### Summary of the submission

Based on the provided list of references, this paper appears to present a method for Named Entity Disambiguation (NED), also known as Entity Linking. The core of the proposed method likely involves a joint learning framework that creates embedding representations for both textual mentions (and their contexts) and entities from a Knowledge Graph (KG). The extensive citation of works on multi-prototype/multi-sense embeddings suggests that a key aspect of the model is its ability to handle the ambiguity of entity names and mentions by learning multiple representations per entity. Furthermore, the inclusion of classic computer science papers on efficient string matching (Aho-Corasick) and data structures (double-array tries) implies a focus on building a complete and efficient end-to-end system, including a fast candidate generation step.

The main contributions of this work, as I infer them, are:

1.  A joint embedding model for NED that explicitly tackles entity and mention ambiguity by incorporating multi-prototype representation learning. This is likely the primary research contribution.
2.  The design of an efficient, end-to-end NED system that integrates a fast dictionary-based candidate generation module with a deep learning-based disambiguation model.
3.  An empirical evaluation of this system on standard NED benchmarks, likely demonstrating improvements over previous joint-learning and feature-based methods.

### Strengths

1.  **Tackling a Core Problem with a Sophisticated Approach:** The paper addresses the fundamental challenge of ambiguity in NED. While many models learn a single vector per entity, the reference list (e.g., Neelakantan et al., 2014; Li and Jurafsky, 2015; Mancini et al., 2016) strongly suggests the authors are advancing beyond this limitation by developing multi-prototype embeddings. This is a well-motivated and promising direction, as many entities (e.g., "Apple") have distinct meanings that cannot be effectively captured by a single vector. This focus on polysemy is a significant strength.

2.  **Focus on Practical System Efficiency:** The inclusion of references like Aho-Corasick (1975) and Aoe (1989) is commendable. Many academic papers on NED focus solely on the ranking/disambiguation model, assuming a pre-computed set of candidates. By apparently building and integrating an efficient candidate generation component, the authors are addressing the full pipeline. This makes the work more relevant for real-world applications and demonstrates a thoroughness that is often lacking in purely model-focused research.

3.  **Solid Grounding in Established Literature:** The authors demonstrate a strong command of the relevant literature up to circa 2016. The bibliography correctly identifies the key research threads in NED: early feature-based and graph-based methods (e.g., Hoffart et al., 2011; Kulkarni et al., 2009), the rise of joint embedding models for text and KGs (e.g., Wang et al., 2014; Toutanova et al., 2015), and the parallel development of multi-sense word embeddings. The proposed work appears to be a thoughtful synthesis and extension of these powerful ideas.

### Weaknesses

1.  **Potential Lack of Novelty in the Core Model:** The central idea of jointly embedding text and knowledge graphs for KG-related tasks, including entity linking, was well-established by 2016 (e.g., Wang et al., 2014; Toutanova et al., 2015; Han et al., 2016). Similarly, applying multi-prototype learning to word embeddings was also an active area of research (Neelakantan et al., 2014). The critical question, which is not answerable from the references alone, is whether this paper presents a truly novel fusion of these ideas or simply applies an existing multi-prototype technique (like Neelakantan's) to an existing joint-learning framework (like Han et al.'s). The novelty of the contribution hinges on the specifics of the model architecture and learning objective, which must be a significant step beyond a straightforward combination of prior work.

2.  **The Reference List is Severely Outdated:** The bibliography appears to stop around 2016-2017. The field of NLP has been revolutionized since then by large pre-trained language models (e.g., BERT, RoBERTa, T5). State-of-the-art NED systems today almost universally leverage these models to obtain rich, contextualized mention representations, leading to significant performance gains. By not comparing against or even acknowledging these transformer-based architectures, the paper risks being obsolete on arrival. The experimental comparisons are likely against systems that are no longer competitive, which would significantly overstate the current relevance of the proposed method's performance.

3.  **Unclear Disentanglement of Contributions:** The paper seems to combine two components: an efficient candidate generator and a multi-prototype disambiguation model. For the evaluation to be meaningful, these contributions must be clearly separated. The authors need to demonstrate through ablation studies how much of the performance gain comes from the disambiguation model itself, versus how the candidate generator might be impacting the final results (e.g., by providing a better recall of candidates). Without this, it's hard to assess the specific merit of the core research contribution (the disambiguation model).

4.  **Minor Concern: Presentation Quality:** The provided reference list contains numerous typos and what appear to be OCR errors (e.g., "Efficie t string match ng", "doub e-array structure", multiple garbled versions of the Han et al. citation). While this does not affect the scientific content, it can be an indicator of a lack of care in the paper's preparation and suggests the manuscript may contain other clarity or quality issues.

### Questions to Authors

1.  Could you please clarify the primary technical novelty of your disambiguation model compared to prior joint text-KG embedding models like those of Wang et al. (2014) and Han et al. (2016)? Specifically, how does your integration of multi-prototype learning differ from a direct application of a method like Neelakantan et al. (2014) within such a framework?

2.  The most significant concern is the outdated nature of the comparisons. Given that transformer-based models have been the state-of-the-art in NED for several years, could you justify why no such models were included as baselines? How do you expect your model would compare to a standard BERT-based entity linking system?

3.  Could you provide an ablation analysis that separates the impact of your candidate generation module from your disambiguation model? For instance, what is the performance of your disambiguation model when using a standard candidate generator, and conversely, how does a baseline disambiguation model perform when using your efficient candidate generator? This would help clarify the distinct benefits of each component.
