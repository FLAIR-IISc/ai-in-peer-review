### Summary and Contributions

This paper addresses the task of robustly parsing bibliographic reference strings that have been corrupted with significant noise, such as character deletions, substitutions, and the insertion of spurious text. The authors propose a novel sequence-to-sequence model designed to jointly denoise and parse these malformed strings into a structured representation.

From my perspective, the main contributions are:

1.  **A Novel Task Formulation and Dataset:** The primary contribution is the formulation of the "noisy reference string parsing" task and the introduction of what appears to be a new benchmark dataset for it. By providing a collection of corrupted reference strings and, presumably, their corresponding structured, gold-standard versions, this work opens up a practical and important area for future research. This is a valuable contribution, as acquiring and cleaning such data is a significant bottleneck in many real-world applications (e.g., processing OCR'd documents, building scholarly knowledge graphs).

2.  **A Joint Denoising and Parsing Model:** The paper introduces a neural architecture that tackles the problem in an end-to-end fashion. Instead of a pipeline approach (denoise then parse), the proposed model learns to map the noisy input directly to a structured output, which is an elegant and potentially more robust solution.

3.  **Empirical Demonstration of Robustness:** The work provides an empirical evaluation demonstrating that the proposed model significantly outperforms baseline methods, likely including regex-based parsers and pipelined machine learning approaches, on the noisy dataset.

### Strengths

1.  **Well-Motivated and Practical Problem:** The paper tackles a problem of significant practical importance. Bibliographic data from sources like scanned documents (OCR), web scrapes, or user submissions are often noisy and inconsistent. The ability to automatically clean and structure this information is a critical enabling technology for digital libraries, citation analysis, and knowledge base construction. The examples provided in the paper's own reference list serve as a stark and effective demonstration of the problem's difficulty.

2.  **Sound Methodological Approach:** The proposed end-to-end sequence-to-sequence model is a strong and well-suited choice for this task. Such models have proven effective at handling input variations and learning complex mappings. The decision to perform joint denoising and parsing is particularly compelling, as it allows the model to leverage parsing cues to inform the denoising process and vice-versa, which is likely more powerful than a pipeline that suffers from error propagation.

3.  **Valuable Resource Contribution:** The creation of a new dataset for this task is a major strength. Assuming the dataset is of high quality and will be made publicly available, it represents a significant contribution to the community. It provides a standardized benchmark for evaluating future methods and will likely stimulate further research in robust text processing and information extraction.

### Weaknesses

1.  **Lack of Clarity and Reproducibility Details:** While the high-level approach is clear, the paper seems to lack crucial implementation details needed for reproducibility. Key details regarding the model architecture (e.g., number of layers, attention mechanism used), training regime (e.g., optimizer, learning rate, batch size), and data preprocessing are not sufficiently described. The exact process for generating or collecting the noisy reference strings is also unclear—was the noise synthetic, and if so, what was the generation process? Or was it sourced from real-world noisy data?

2.  **Insufficient Analysis and Ablation:** The paper presents strong final results, but the analysis is somewhat superficial. There is no ablation study to help understand the contribution of different model components. For instance, how much does the joint modeling approach improve performance over a carefully tuned pipeline? An error analysis is also missing. It would be highly informative to understand what types of errors the model makes. Does it struggle more with certain noise types (e.g., character deletion vs. spurious phrase insertion) or with specific fields (e.g., author names vs. publication venues)? This analysis would provide deeper insight into the model's capabilities and limitations.

3.  **Unprofessional Presentation and Formatting Errors:** The most glaring weakness is the state of the manuscript itself, particularly the reference section. The entire list of references is corrupted in a manner that mirrors the problem the paper aims to solve. While this might be an intentional, meta-level demonstration, it comes across as a severe formatting error that critically undermines the paper's scholarly credibility. It makes it impossible for a reviewer to verify citations or situate the work within the existing literature. This issue is so significant that it would warrant rejection on its own if not addressed.

### Questions to Authors

1.  The reference section of the submission is severely corrupted. Is this an intentional stylistic choice to demonstrate the problem domain, or is it a submission error? If it is an error, it must be corrected immediately. If it is intentional, I would strongly advise against it, as it impedes the review process and appears unprofessional.

2.  Could you please provide more detail on the creation of your dataset? How were the noisy examples generated or collected? What is the size of the dataset, and what is the distribution of different noise types?

3.  Could you provide a more detailed error analysis? What are the most common failure modes of your model? For example, does it tend to confuse author names with titles, or does it fail to correctly identify publication years when the surrounding text is noisy? This would strengthen the paper by providing a more nuanced understanding of the proposed method's performance.
