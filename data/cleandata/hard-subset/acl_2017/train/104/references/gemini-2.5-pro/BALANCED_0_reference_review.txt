**Summary and Contributions**

This paper presents a joint learning framework for end-to-end named entity disambiguation (NED). The proposed model learns unified vector representations for words and entities by leveraging both a large text corpus and the structure and textual descriptions from a knowledge graph (e.g., Wikipedia). A key feature of the model is its use of multi-prototype embeddings for entities, allowing it to represent different senses of an ambiguous entity (e.g., "Apple" the company vs. "apple" the fruit). For mention detection and candidate generation, the system employs an efficient string matching approach based on the Aho-Corasick algorithm. The authors conduct experiments on standard NED benchmarks, including AIDA-CoNLL, and report state-of-the-art performance compared to several strong baselines.

As I see them, the main contributions of this work are:
1.  **An effective, well-integrated system for entity disambiguation.** The paper's primary strength is not in a single groundbreaking component, but in the thoughtful synthesis and successful implementation of several powerful ideas (joint text-KG embeddings, entity descriptions, multi-prototype representations) into a cohesive, high-performing NED system. The strong empirical results validate this engineering effort.
2.  **A successful application of multi-prototype embeddings to the entity level.** While multi-prototype models have been explored for word sense disambiguation, this paper effectively adapts and demonstrates their utility for modeling polysemous entities in a large-scale NED task, supported by strong results in the ablation study.

**Strengths**

1.  **Strong Empirical Performance:** The paper achieves state-of-the-art or competitive results on several widely-recognized and challenging NED datasets. The reported improvements over a range of recent and established baselines are consistent and appear significant. This provides compelling evidence for the effectiveness of the proposed model and its architecture.
2.  **Well-motivated Model Design:** The architectural choices are sound and well-justified. Integrating entity descriptions from the knowledge graph to inform entity embeddings is a proven technique for enriching representations, and the joint learning objective that combines corpus-level co-occurrence with knowledge graph structure is elegant. The decision to explicitly model entity ambiguity via multiple prototypes is a natural fit for the NED task and directly addresses a known challenge in the field.
3.  **Thorough Ablation Study:** The authors provide a valuable ablation analysis that systematically removes key components of their model (e.g., the KG structural information, the entity descriptions, the multi-prototype mechanism). This analysis provides clear insights into the relative importance of each component and convincingly demonstrates that each part contributes positively to the model's overall performance. This strengthens the paper's conclusions significantly.

**Weaknesses**

1.  **Limited Novelty of Core Technical Components:** The main weakness of the paper is that the constituent parts of the model are largely based on existing work. The concept of jointly embedding text and knowledge graphs has been explored extensively (e.g., Wang et al., 2014; Toutanova et al., 2015; Han et al., 2016). Similarly, multi-prototype embeddings for words are not new (e.g., Neelakantan et al., 2014; Reisinger and Mooney, 2010). While the application to entity prototypes is well-executed, the paper would be stronger if it more clearly articulated the conceptual novelty of its approach beyond combining these existing ideas. As it stands, the contribution feels more incremental and focused on engineering than on fundamental methodological advances.
2.  **Insufficient Analysis of the Candidate Generation Module:** The paper introduces the use of the Aho-Corasick algorithm for efficient candidate generation as a feature of the system. However, the impact and significance of this choice are not analyzed. Standard NED systems use various methods for candidate generation (e.g., dictionary lookups, search engine queries), and it is unclear how the proposed method compares in terms of the trade-off between speed, recall, and precision. Without a direct comparison or analysis, it is difficult to assess whether this is a minor implementation detail or a significant contribution to building practical, efficient NED systems.
3.  **Potential Scalability and Generalizability Issues:** The proposed method is evaluated on datasets derived from Wikipedia, which is a high-quality, densely linked, and well-curated knowledge resource with rich textual descriptions for most entities. It is unclear how robust the model would be in settings with sparser or noisier knowledge graphs, or in specialized domains where such rich descriptions may not be available. A discussion of these limitations and the model's dependency on high-quality KG data would make the paper more complete.

**Questions to Authors**

1.  The joint learning objective appears conceptually similar to several prior works that embed text and KGs. Could you provide a more detailed comparison with the objective functions in Wang et al. (2014) and Toutanova et al. (2015) and highlight the key differences that lead to your model's improved performance?
2.  How do you determine the number of prototypes for each entity? Is this a fixed hyperparameter for all entities, or is it determined dynamically (e.g., based on the number of entries on a Wikipedia disambiguation page)? How sensitive is the model's performance to this choice?
3.  Could you provide a quantitative analysis of your candidate generation step? For example, what is the recall of the generated candidates on the AIDA-CoNLL test set, and how does the processing time compare to a standard baseline method for candidate generation? This would help clarify the practical benefits of your approach.
