### **Summary and Contributions**

This paper appears to address the task of Named Entity Disambiguation (NED) or Entity Linking (EL), which involves linking ambiguous mentions in text to their correct entries in a knowledge graph (KG). Judging from the cited literature, the authors likely propose a neural model that jointly learns vector representations for words in a text corpus and entities in a KG.

The main novelty seems to be the adaptation of multi-prototype embedding models—previously used for word sense disambiguation—to the problem of entity ambiguity. The core idea is likely that a single entity mention (e.g., "Apple") can refer to multiple distinct entities (Apple Inc., apple fruit) and that the model should learn a separate vector representation (a "prototype") for each of these potential referents. The model would then use the surrounding text context to select the most appropriate prototype for disambiguation. The citation of classic string-matching algorithms suggests these might be used for an efficient candidate generation step.

As I see them, the main contributions are:
1.  A novel neural architecture for entity linking that explicitly models entity ambiguity by learning multiple, context-aware prototype embeddings for each entity mention.
2.  The integration of this multi-prototype entity representation model into a joint text-KG embedding framework, enabling the model to leverage both textual context and KG structure for disambiguation.

### **Strengths**

1.  **Tackles a well-motivated and central problem in NED:** The paper addresses the fundamental challenge of polysemy in entity linking. Many entity mentions are highly ambiguous, and existing models that assign a single static vector to an entity (e.g., "Washington") cannot easily distinguish between its different real-world referents (the person, the state, the D.C.). Applying multi-prototype embeddings to this problem is an intuitive, well-motivated, and promising direction.

2.  **Builds on a solid foundation of successful research paradigms:** The approach appears to synthesize two powerful and highly relevant lines of NLP research: joint representation learning of text and knowledge graphs (e.g., Wang et al., 2014; Toutanova et al., 2015) and multi-sense/multi-prototype word embeddings (e.g., Reisinger and Mooney, 2010; Neelakantan et al., 2014). Combining these paradigms is a logical next step for the field, and this work appears to be one of the first to do so explicitly for the NED task.

3.  **Demonstrates awareness of a broad range of related work:** The authors cite a comprehensive set of references, including classic EL systems (e.g., Cucerzan, 2007; Hoffart et al., 2011), foundational representation learning models (Mikolov et al., 2013), and the latest work on joint KB-text embeddings. This suggests that the authors have a strong grasp of the literature and that their work is likely well-positioned and compared against a strong set of baselines.

### **Weaknesses**

1.  **Potential for limited technical novelty:** The primary concern is whether the contribution is a simple application of existing multi-prototype word embedding techniques to entities, or if it involves significant technical innovation. For example, methods like Neelakantan et al. (2014) learn word senses from textual co-occurrence. Entities, however, also have rich structural information from the knowledge graph. A key weakness would be if the proposed model fails to meaningfully integrate this structural information into the prototype learning process itself, making it a superficial adaptation of a known method to a new domain. The authors need to clearly distinguish their method from prior work on multi-sense embeddings.

2.  **Unclear justification for using classic string-matching algorithms:** The repeated citation of Aho and Corasick (1975) and Aoe (1989) is conspicuous in a paper that is otherwise focused on modern representation learning. While these are likely used for candidate generation (i.e., finding all potential mentions in text), it is unclear if this is a novel part of the contribution or simply a standard pre-processing choice. If it is the latter, it is not a contribution and its prominent citation is odd. The paper needs to strongly justify why this specific, older approach is used and how it compares to or integrates with the neural components, especially when more recent EL systems often use different candidate generation strategies.

3.  **Significant issues with presentation and attention to detail:** The reference list provided is poorly formatted, containing numerous typographical errors (e.g., "Efficie t string matching," "knowledg"), inconsistent formatting, repeated entries, and apparent copy-paste artifacts (e.g., "? \Q2017\E"). This lack of care in the bibliography raises serious concerns about the overall quality and clarity of the manuscript. If the main body of the paper suffers from similar imprecision, it could obscure the technical details, undermine the credibility of the results, and impede reproducibility.

### **Questions to Authors**

1.  Could you please clarify the specific technical novelty of your multi-prototype model compared to prior work on multi-sense word embeddings (e.g., Neelakantan et al., 2014; Tian et al., 2014)? What architectural or objective function modifications were required to adapt this concept from words in free text to structured entities in a knowledge graph?

2.  What is the precise role of the Aho-Corasick and Aoe algorithms in your system? Are they part of a novel contribution to candidate generation, or a standard implementation choice? If the latter, could you provide a brief justification for this choice over other methods commonly used in state-of-the-art EL pipelines?

3.  How does the proposed model scale with the size of the knowledge graph? Learning multiple prototypes for potentially millions of entities seems computationally demanding. Could you comment on the training time and memory complexity of your approach, and whether any specific strategies were employed to ensure its feasibility on large-scale KGs?
