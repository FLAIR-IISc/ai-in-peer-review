**Summary and Contributions**

This paper appears to present a model for Named Entity Disambiguation (NED) that learns joint vector representations of text and entities from a knowledge graph. Based on the cited literature, the proposed method likely combines contextual information derived from text corpora with structural information from a knowledge base to produce embeddings that are sensitive to entity identity. This is a well-established paradigm for this task.

The primary contribution, as I see it, is an integrated model for NED that builds upon the now-common approach of jointly embedding text and knowledge graphs. While the authors may claim significant novelty in their model architecture, the reference list suggests this work is situated in a very crowded field, and the contribution is more likely an incremental refinement of existing techniques rather than a fundamental breakthrough.

**Strengths**

1.  **Addresses a Core NLP Task:** Named Entity Disambiguation is a fundamental and challenging problem in natural language understanding. Rigorous work that advances the state of the art on this task is valuable to the community.
2.  **Grounded in Established Literature:** The authors demonstrate a comprehensive awareness of the relevant background literature. The bibliography includes seminal works on word embeddings (Mikolov et al., 2013), multi-prototype embeddings (Neelakantan et al., 2014), entity linking systems (Hoffart et al., 2011; Kulkarni et al., 2009), and, most critically, a wide range of recent papers on joint representation learning for text and knowledge graphs (Wang et al., 2014; Toutanova et al., 2015; Han et al., 2016). This suggests the work is well-positioned to compare against relevant prior art.
3.  **Focus on Joint Learning:** The approach of jointly leveraging unstructured text and structured knowledge graphs is a powerful and proven direction for knowledge-intensive NLP tasks. Pursuing this direction is methodologically sound and has the potential to yield strong empirical results.

**Weaknesses**

1.  **Questionable Novelty:** My primary concern is the potential lack of novelty. The problem of jointly embedding text and knowledge graphs for disambiguation has been extensively studied, as evidenced by the authors' own citations (e.g., Wang et al., 2014; Toutanova et al., 2015; Han et al., 2016; Wu et al., 2016). It is unclear from the bibliography what fundamentally new principle or technique this paper introduces that distinguishes it from this large body of highly related work. The submission risks being another minor variation on a well-known theme without a strong, clear statement of its unique contribution.
2.  **Risk of Insufficient Empirical Comparison:** Given the maturity of this research area, a very high standard for empirical evaluation is required. The paper must not only demonstrate improvement over older systems but also rigorously compare against the state-of-the-art joint embedding models it cites. Anything less than a direct, head-to-head comparison with several recent and strong baselines on standard, widely-used datasets would be a significant flaw. The burden of proof is on the authors to show that their proposed method provides a substantial and statistically significant improvement over these established approaches.
3.  **Unclear Methodological Motivation:** The inclusion of foundational string matching algorithms (Aho and Corasick, 1975; Aoe, 1989) is puzzling. While such methods are used in the candidate generation phase of many NED systems, this is a standard, solved component. If their use here is standard, citing them so prominently seems unnecessary. If the authors are proposing a novel integration of these classic algorithms with modern neural embedding techniques, the motivation is not immediately obvious and risks appearing ad-hoc. A strong theoretical or empirical justification for such a hybrid approach would be essential.
4.  **Presentation Quality:** The provided reference list is replete with typographical and formatting errors (e.g., "knowledg," "efficie t string match ng," "doubl -array structure"). While this may seem minor, it suggests a lack of attention to detail that can undermine confidence in the overall rigor and quality of the research itself. A submission to a top-tier venue like ACL is expected to be polished and meticulously prepared.

**Questions to Authors**

1.  Could you please explicitly articulate the primary methodological innovation of your model compared to the joint embedding frameworks proposed by Wang et al. (2014), Toutanova et al. (2015), and Han et al. (2016)? What is the key insight that leads to improved performance?
2.  The field of NED has many strong, publicly available systems that serve as standard baselines (e.g., the system of Hoffart et al., 2011). Can you confirm that your experimental evaluation includes direct comparisons against these and other recent state-of-the-art models on standard benchmark datasets (e.g., AIDA-CoNLL, TAC KBP)?
3.  What is the specific role of the classic string matching algorithms (Aho-Corasick, Aoe) in your system? If they are used for a standard candidate generation step, please clarify this. If they are part of a novel contribution, please explain the motivation for integrating them into a modern neural architecture.
