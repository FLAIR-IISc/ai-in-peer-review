### Summary and Contributions

Based on the provided list of references, this paper appears to address the task of Named Entity Disambiguation (NED), also known as Entity Linking (EL). The proposed method is likely a neural embedding-based model that jointly learns representations for words (from a text corpus) and entities (from a knowledge graph) in a shared vector space. The core idea seems to be that by learning to align these representations, the model can disambiguate a textual mention of an entity by finding the knowledge graph entity whose embedding is most compatible with the mention's context.

From my perspective, the main contributions of this work are likely:

1.  **A Unified Model for NED Integrating Multi-Prototype Embeddings:** The key contribution appears to be the synthesis of two major lines of research: joint text/KG embedding models (e.g., Toutanova et al., 2015; Han et al., 2016) and multi-prototype word/sense embeddings (e.g., Neelakantan et al., 2014; Li and Jurafsky, 2015). The paper likely proposes a model where entities are not represented by a single vector but by multiple prototype vectors, which could help capture different facets or senses of an entity, thereby improving disambiguation accuracy for polysemous entities.
2.  **A Comprehensive Neural Architecture for NED:** The paper seems to present a complete end-to-end system. The citation of classic string matching algorithms (Aho and Corasick, 1975) suggests a well-defined candidate generation phase, while the bulk of the citations point to a sophisticated neural model for the re-ranking/disambiguation phase that leverages both local textual context (via models inspired by Mikolov et al., 2013) and global graph-based information (implicit in the KG embedding literature).

### Strengths

1.  **Tackles a Fundamental and Relevant Problem:** Named Entity Disambiguation is a critical upstream task for many NLP applications, including information extraction, question answering, and semantic search. Developing more accurate and robust models for NED is of significant interest to the ACL community. The approach of using joint embedding models represents the current state-of-the-art paradigm for this task.
2.  **Sound Methodological Grounding and Promising Direction:** The paper builds upon a very solid foundation of well-established and highly-cited work in distributional semantics, knowledge graph embeddings, and their joint modeling. The decision to integrate concepts from multi-prototype embeddings is a logical and promising next step. Standard entity embedding models often struggle with entities that have diverse contexts (e.g., a person who is a politician, an author, and an actor). A multi-prototype approach could theoretically model these different roles more effectively, leading to more nuanced and context-sensitive disambiguation.
3.  **Potential for Strong Empirical Performance:** A well-executed model combining state-of-the-art techniques for text representation, knowledge graph embedding, and multi-sense modeling has a high potential to achieve state-of-the-art results on standard NED benchmarks (e.g., AIDA-CoNLL, TAC KBP). The synthesis of strong local context models with global knowledge from a KG is a powerful combination that has proven effective in many recent works.

### Weaknesses

1.  **Potential Lack of Clear Novelty Over Existing Work:** My primary concern is the potential for insufficient novelty given the very strong and recent related work cited. Several papers have already proposed joint embedding models for text and knowledge graphs, some specifically for entity-related tasks (Wang et al., 2014; Toutanova et al., 2015; Han et al., 2016). The authors must very clearly articulate how their proposed architecture, training objective, or modeling assumptions differ from and improve upon these existing frameworks. If the sole contribution is the addition of a multi-prototype mechanism on top of an existing joint model, the conceptual novelty might be considered incremental unless it requires a non-trivial reformulation of the training process and yields significant performance gains.
2.  **Insufficient Justification and Analysis:** A complex model like the one inferred from the references requires extensive ablation studies and analysis to be convincing. The authors would need to demonstrate the specific impact of each component. For instance, how much of the performance gain comes from the multi-prototype representation versus the joint training objective itself? How does the model perform on entities with high vs. low polysemy? Without such analysis, the paper risks being a "kitchen sink" approach where the reasons for its success (or failure) are not well understood.
3.  **Scalability and Efficiency Concerns:** Training joint embedding models on large-scale text corpora (like Wikipedia) and knowledge graphs (like Freebase or Wikidata) is computationally expensive. While the citation to Aho-Corasick suggests efficiency in the candidate generation step, the core training loop for the neural model could be a significant bottleneck. The paper needs to address the computational complexity, training time, and memory requirements of their method to assess its practicality for real-world, large-scale applications.
4.  **Minor Presentation Issues:** The provided reference list contains numerous typos, OCR-like errors (e.g., "Efficie t string match ng," "J int representation l arning"), and inconsistent formatting. While this does not affect the scientific content, it suggests a lack of careful proofreading and detracts from the overall quality of the submission. The authors should be urged to correct these throughout the paper.

### Questions to Authors

1.  Could you please explicitly delineate the primary technical novelty of your model compared to the closely related joint embedding frameworks of Toutanova et al. (2015) and Han et al. (2016)? Is it a novel objective function, a new way of integrating graph structure, or a different attention mechanism?
2.  The citation of multi-prototype embedding literature (e.g., Neelakantan et al., 2014) is a key point of interest. How are multiple prototypes for a single entity formally defined and learned in your model? Is the number of prototypes a fixed hyperparameter, and how do you associate a specific context with a specific prototype during disambiguation?
3.  Could you provide details on the computational cost of your model? Specifically, what is the training time on the datasets used, and how does its complexity scale with the size of the vocabulary and the number of entities in the knowledge graph?
