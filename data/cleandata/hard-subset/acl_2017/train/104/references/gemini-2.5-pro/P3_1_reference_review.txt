### **Summary and Contributions**

This paper addresses the task of entity disambiguation (ED), also known as entity linking (EL), by proposing a method that jointly learns representations of text and entities from a knowledge graph (KG). Based on the cited literature, the work appears to combine classic, efficient algorithms for candidate generation with a modern neural embedding-based model for the final disambiguation step.

The primary contributions of this work, as I see them, are:

1.  **A Hybrid Architecture for Entity Disambiguation:** The paper seems to propose a two-stage pipeline. The citation of classic string matching algorithms (Aho and Corasick, 1975; Aoe, 1989) is highly unusual for a modern deep learning paper. This suggests the authors use these techniques for an efficient, exact-match-based candidate generation phase to identify potential entity mentions and retrieve an initial set of candidate entities from the KG. This is then followed by a more sophisticated deep learning model for ranking/disambiguation, which builds on the extensive literature on joint text-KG embeddings (e.g., Wang et al., 2014; Toutanova et al., 2015; Han et al., 2016).
2.  **A Novel Joint Embedding Model for Context-Entity Compatibility:** While building on prior work, the paper likely introduces a specific innovation in its joint learning model. The model likely learns low-dimensional vectors for words and entities in a shared space, such that the representation of a mention's context is close to the representation of the correct target entity. The novelty probably lies in the specific objective function or architectural design used to integrate information from the textual context and the KG's structural properties.

### **Strengths**

1.  **Addresses a Fundamental and Challenging NLP Problem:** Entity disambiguation is a cornerstone task for natural language understanding, crucial for downstream applications like information extraction, question answering, and semantic search. The paper tackles a well-motivated and significant problem.
2.  **Thorough Grounding in Relevant Literature:** The authors demonstrate a comprehensive understanding of the field. They cite foundational work on word embeddings (Mikolov et al., 2013a,b), multi-prototype embeddings for word sense disambiguation (Reisinger and Mooney, 2010; Neelakantan et al., 2014), and, most importantly, the key recent line of research on jointly embedding text and knowledge graphs for various tasks, including ED (Wang et al., 2014; Toutanova et al., 2015; Han et al., 2016). This situates their work firmly within the state of the art.
3.  **Potential for a Pragmatic and Efficient System:** The inclusion of Aho-Corasick and double-array tries (Aoe, 1989) is a compelling feature. While most contemporary work focuses solely on the neural model, this paper seems to consider the full pipeline. Using these highly optimized, classic data structures for the initial mention detection and candidate retrieval stage could lead to a system that is not only accurate but also very fast, which is a significant practical advantage often overlooked in research settings. This combination of classic algorithmic efficiency with modern representation learning is a notable strength.

### **Weaknesses**

1.  **Poor Presentation and Lack of Attention to Detail:** The single most glaring weakness is the state of the reference list. It is riddled with typos, formatting errors, repetitions, and extraneous, nonsensical text (e.g., "played it during public", "Confid tia Review Copy"). This sloppiness severely undermines the credibility of the submission and suggests a rushed and careless preparation process. While this may not reflect on the core ideas, it makes the paper difficult to parse and trust. This must be corrected.
2.  **Potential Lack of Novelty in the Core Disambiguation Model:** The central idea of jointly embedding text and KGs for disambiguation is well-established. Numerous prior works cited by the authors (e.g., Wang et al., 2014; Han et al., 2016) have explored this space. The paper must do an exceptional job of articulating precisely what its novel contribution is beyond these existing methods. Is it a new loss function? A different way of modeling KG structure? An attention mechanism? Without a clear and significant point of departure from this prior work, the contribution risks being merely incremental.
3.  **Unclear Justification for Using Older Algorithms:** While the use of Aho-Corasick is interesting, its justification is not immediately obvious in the context of modern systems. Current state-of-the-art candidate generators often rely on learned alias tables, approximate nearest neighbor search over embeddings, or personalized PageRank. The authors need to provide a compelling argument and empirical evidence that their choice of these classic, exact-match algorithms is superior to or complementary to these more common techniques, especially for handling mention variations and aliases.

### **Questions to Authors**

1.  The core idea of your work appears to be similar to several prior papers on joint text-KG embeddings for ED (e.g., Han et al., 2016; Toutanova et al., 2015). Could you please succinctly state the primary technical novelty of your embedding and disambiguation model that distinguishes it from this body of work?
2.  Could you elaborate on the precise role of the Aho-Corasick and Aoe algorithms in your system? What is your motivation for using these specific algorithms for candidate generation over more recent methods that can handle surface-form variations and are commonly used in state-of-the-art EL systems?
3.  The reference list provided is severely corrupted, which makes it difficult to assess the paper's scholarly basis. Can you confirm that this is an artifact of the submission process and will be fully rectified in any future version?
