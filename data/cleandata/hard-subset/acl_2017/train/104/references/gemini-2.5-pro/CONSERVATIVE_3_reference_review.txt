**Summary and Contributions**

This paper presents a model for Named Entity Disambiguation (NED) that jointly learns representations for textual entity mentions and entities from a knowledge graph. The core idea is to embed both the local context of a mention and the structural information of candidate entities from the knowledge graph into a common vector space, where disambiguation is performed by finding the nearest entity neighbor to a given mention. The model also appears to draw inspiration from multi-prototype word embedding methods to better handle polysemous entities.

As I see them, the contributions are:
1.  The application of a joint text-and-knowledge-graph embedding framework, previously explored for tasks like knowledge graph completion, to the problem of Named Entity Disambiguation.
2.  An empirical evaluation of this approach on standard NED benchmarks, comparing it against several other embedding-based models from recent years.

**Strengths**

1.  The paper tackles a well-established and important problem in NLP. Entity disambiguation is a critical upstream task for information extraction, question answering, and semantic search. The general direction of leveraging both unstructured text and structured knowledge bases is a sound and well-motivated approach that aligns with a significant body of recent work (e.g., Wang et al., 2014; Toutanova et al., 2015).

2.  The exploration of multi-prototype embeddings (inspired by Neelakantan et al., 2014; Tian et al., 2014) to model different senses or facets of an entity is a reasonable idea. Standard models that assign a single vector to an entity like "Apple" struggle to capture its distinct meanings (the company vs. the fruit). Attempting to address this limitation is a commendable goal.

3.  The proposed method is evaluated against a number of other representation learning-based approaches. This provides some context for the model's performance within the sub-field of neural NED methods.

**Weaknesses**

1.  **Insufficient Comparison to Established Baselines:** The primary weakness of this submission is its failure to benchmark against strong, established, and often simpler baselines. The evaluation appears to focus exclusively on other recent embedding-based models. However, the field of NED has a long history, and highly robust systems based on rich features, graph-based collective inference, and probabilistic models have set high standards. Omission of comparisons to influential systems like AIDA (Hoffart et al., 2011), the collective annotation model of Kulkarni et al. (2009), or even simpler Personalized PageRank approaches (Pershina et al., 2015) is a major flaw. Without demonstrating a significant improvement over these well-understood and powerful methods, it is difficult to assess the practical utility of the proposed complex neural architecture. New models must justify their existence by outperforming not just their contemporaries, but also the proven workhorses of the field.

2.  **Lack of Methodological Justification and Ablation Studies:** The proposed model seems to be a complex combination of several components: a text encoder, a knowledge graph embedding module, and a multi-prototype mechanism. The paper does not provide a clear justification for this complexity through rigorous ablation studies. It is unclear how much each component contributes to the final performance. For example, how much does the multi-prototype component improve results over a simpler, single-vector-per-entity baseline using the same framework? How does the joint model compare to a pipeline approach using pre-trained word and entity embeddings? A convincing research paper must dissect its own model to show that its added complexity provides a tangible and significant benefit.

3.  **Limited Novelty:** The fundamental concept of jointly learning representations for text and knowledge graphs is not new. Several cited papers (Wang et al., 2014; Toutanova et al., 2015; Han et al., 2016) have already established this paradigm for tasks like relation extraction and knowledge graph completion. While applying this to NED is a valid research direction, the submission does not sufficiently articulate its core technical novelty beyond this direct application. The contribution feels more incremental than foundational. The paper would be much stronger if it could pinpoint a novel architectural insight or learning objective that is fundamentally different from prior joint-embedding work and is specifically tailored to the challenges of disambiguation.

4.  **Presentation and Attention to Detail:** The manuscript, particularly the bibliography, contains numerous typographical and formatting errors (e.g., "Efficie t string matching," "knowledg for knowledge graph," inconsistent citation styles). While this may seem minor, it reflects a lack of care and rigor in the paper's preparation. Such issues undermine confidence in the technical details and the soundness of the reported results.

**Questions to Authors**

1.  Could you please elaborate on the rationale for omitting comparisons to prominent and strong non-embedding-based baselines such as AIDA (Hoffart et al., 2011) and the work of Kulkarni et al. (2009)? Providing these comparisons is critical for contextualizing the performance of your proposed model.

2.  Can you provide a thorough ablation study that isolates the performance contribution of each key component of your model? Specifically, what is the performance gain attributable to the multi-prototype mechanism compared to a single-prototype version of your own model?

3.  Given the extensive prior work on joint text and knowledge graph embeddings, could you more precisely define the primary technical novelty of your proposed method compared to frameworks like that of Wang et al. (2014) or Toutanova et al. (2015)? What is the key innovation beyond applying a similar paradigm to a different task?
