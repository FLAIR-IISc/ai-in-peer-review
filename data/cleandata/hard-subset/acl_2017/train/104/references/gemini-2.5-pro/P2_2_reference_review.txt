**Summary and Contributions**

This paper appears to address the task of entity linking or knowledge graph completion by proposing a method that jointly learns representations from text and a knowledge graph. Based on the provided reference list, the paper seems to be situated within the popular line of work on embedding-based models for these tasks.

The main contributions of this work, as I interpret them from the citations, are:

1.  **A Model for Jointly Embedding Text and Knowledge Graphs:** The core of the paper seems to be a new or modified model for learning low-dimensional vector representations for entities and words, drawing heavily on prior work such as Han et al. (2016), Wang et al. (2014), and Toutanova et al. (2015).
2.  **An Efficient Implementation for Candidate Generation/Matching:** The repeated, albeit corrupted, citations to classic computer science papers on efficient string matching (Aho & Corasick, 1975) and data structures (Aoe, 1989) strongly suggest that a key aspect of this work is a novel or particularly efficient implementation of a component of the entity linking pipeline, likely the initial step of identifying potential entity mentions in text.

**Strengths**

1.  **Addresses a Relevant and Important Problem:** Entity linking and knowledge graph completion are central tasks in natural language understanding, with significant downstream applications. Work that improves the state-of-the-art, particularly in terms of efficiency and scalability, is of high interest to the ACL community.
2.  **Potential for High Efficiency and Scalability:** The most unique aspect suggested by the bibliography is the focus on efficiency, highlighted by references to Aho-Corasick and double-array tries. Modern entity linking systems often rely on massive knowledge bases (e.g., Wikipedia) and process large text corpora. A method that integrates classic, highly-optimized algorithms for string matching into a modern neural architecture could represent a significant practical contribution, addressing a major bottleneck in existing systems. If the authors successfully demonstrate a substantial improvement in speed or memory usage without sacrificing accuracy, this would be a strong point in favor of the paper.
3.  **Grounded in a Strong Body of Prior Work:** The authors cite a comprehensive list of foundational and recent papers in the areas of word embeddings (Mikolov et al., 2013), multi-prototype embeddings (Neelakantan et al., 2014), and joint text/knowledge graph representation learning (Han et al., 2016; Wang et al., 2014). This indicates that the authors are well-versed in the relevant literature and are likely positioning their work correctly within the current research landscape.

**Weaknesses**

1.  **Extremely Poor Manuscript Preparation:** The single most critical weakness of this submission is its presentation. The provided text, specifically the reference section, is riddled with severe formatting and typographical errors. Examples include "Efficie t string matching," "knowledg for knowledge graph completion," and random characters/spacing throughout nearly every entry. This level of carelessness makes the paper exceptionally difficult to read and suggests a rushed and unprofessional submission process. It fundamentally undermines confidence in the scientific rigor and correctness of the work itself. For a top-tier venue like ACL, this is a major issue that would typically warrant an immediate rejection.
2.  **Unclear Novelty of the Core Model:** While the efficiency aspect is potentially novel, the core modeling approach appears to be heavily based on a well-established paradigm. Numerous papers cited (e.g., Han et al., 2016; Wang et al., 2014; Toutanova et al., 2015) have already explored the joint embedding of text and knowledge graphs. Without a clear and compelling explanation of how the proposed model architecturally differs from or improves upon these existing methods, the contribution may be perceived as incremental. The novelty seems to hinge entirely on the integration of efficient data structures, which might be viewed as more of an engineering contribution than a fundamental scientific one, depending on the depth of the integration.
3.  **Ambiguity of the Contribution's Scope:** It is difficult to determine from the provided information whether the main claim is a new model that is also efficient, or an efficiency-focused reimplementation of an existing model. The paper needs to clearly articulate its primary contribution. If the model is not novel, the paper must provide a thorough justification for why an efficiency improvement alone warrants publication at ACL, including detailed complexity analysis and extensive empirical timing comparisons against strong baselines. The snippet "predict the context words by maximizing the following objective functi" suggests a standard skip-gram-like objective, which would further point towards the novelty being in implementation rather than the core learning paradigm.

**Questions to Authors**

1.  The reference section of the manuscript is severely corrupted with formatting errors, making it very difficult to parse and verify citations. Could you please confirm whether this was a file conversion or submission error? I strongly urge you to correct this for any future version of the paper, as in its current state, it significantly detracts from the credibility of the submission.
2.  Could you please clarify the primary novelty of your work? Is it a new neural architecture for joint text/KG embedding, or is it the application of efficient algorithms like Aho-Corasick to an existing architectural framework? Please explicitly differentiate your proposed model from closely related works like Han et al. (2016) and Wang et al. (2014).
3.  Assuming the efficiency improvement is a key contribution, could you elaborate on how the Aho-Corasick algorithm and/or double-array structures are integrated into your pipeline? At what specific stage do they provide a speedup (e.g., initial mention detection, candidate entity generation from the knowledge base)? What are the theoretical and, more importantly, the empirical performance gains (in terms of processing time and/or memory) over standard approaches?
