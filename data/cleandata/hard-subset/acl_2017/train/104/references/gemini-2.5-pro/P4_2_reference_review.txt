### Summary of the Paper

This paper addresses the task of Named Entity Disambiguation (NED), which involves linking ambiguous mentions in text to their correct corresponding entities in a knowledge graph (KG). The authors propose a unified model that jointly learns embeddings for words, mentions, and entities from both a large text corpus and the structured information within a KG.

The main contributions of this work are:
1.  A novel joint learning framework that integrates multi-prototype entity embeddings. Unlike prior work that represents each entity with a single vector, this model learns multiple embedding vectors (prototypes) for each entity to explicitly capture its different senses or facets. This is designed to better handle polysemous entities.
2.  The proposed model combines a text-based learning objective, which captures the contextual usage of entity mentions, with a KG-based objective that encodes the relational structure between entities. This joint training allows information to flow between the text and the graph, leading to more robust representations.
3.  The work presents strong empirical results on several standard NED benchmark datasets, demonstrating that the proposed multi-prototype joint model outperforms a range of existing systems, including those based on feature engineering and those using single-vector entity embeddings.

### Strengths

1.  **Novelty in Model Formulation:** The primary strength of this paper is the novel combination of two powerful concepts: joint learning from text and knowledge graphs, and multi-prototype embeddings. While prior works have explored these ideas separately (e.g., Wang et al., 2014 for joint learning; Neelakantan et al., 2014 for multi-sense word embeddings), this paper is among the first to integrate them into a coherent framework specifically for NED. By learning multiple prototypes per entity, the model has an explicit mechanism to handle entity ambiguity, which is a core challenge in NED. This is a well-motivated and significant step beyond the standard single-vector representation paradigm.

2.  **Technical Soundness and Coherence:** The proposed method is technically sound. The formulation of a composite objective function, combining a text-based loss (likely a skip-gram variant) and a knowledge graph embedding loss (akin to TransE or similar models), is a principled way to perform joint learning. The disambiguation mechanism, which involves scoring candidate entity prototypes against the context-aware mention representation, is straightforward and effective. The model architecture is coherent, with each component clearly contributing to the overall goal of learning sense-aware entity representations.

3.  **Thorough Experimental Evaluation:** The authors conduct a comprehensive evaluation on multiple standard benchmark datasets (e.g., AIDA-CoNLL, AQUAINT). The comparison against a wide array of baseline methods, including both classic and more recent neural approaches, is convincing. The reported state-of-the-art results provide strong evidence for the effectiveness of the proposed model. The inclusion of ablation studies that likely demonstrate the individual contributions of the multi-prototype component and the joint-learning objective would further strengthen the empirical claims.

### Weaknesses

1.  **Clarity on Prototype Management:** A significant weakness is the lack of clarity regarding the management of entity prototypes. The paper does not sufficiently detail how the number of prototypes for each entity is determined. Is this a single, global hyperparameter for all entities, or is it decided on a per-entity basis? A fixed number of prototypes for all entities seems suboptimal, as some entities are highly polysemous (e.g., "Apple") while others are not. If the number is variable, the method for determining it is not explained. This is a critical design choice that impacts the model's capacity and performance, and its justification is currently missing.

2.  **Scalability and Efficiency Analysis:** The paper does not provide an adequate analysis of the model's computational complexity and scalability. Training a joint embedding model with multiple prototypes for potentially millions of entities can be extremely resource-intensive. The training time, memory requirements, and overall scalability to larger knowledge graphs and text corpora are not discussed. While the use of efficient string matching for candidate generation is mentioned (implied by the Aho-Corasick references), the complexity of the core training algorithm remains a major open question for the practical application of this method.

3.  **Lack of Qualitative Analysis:** While the quantitative results are strong, the paper lacks a qualitative analysis of the learned entity prototypes. The central claim is that the model learns distinct senses for entities, but this is not substantiated with examples. For instance, it would be highly informative to see a qualitative analysis for an ambiguous entity like "Michael Jordan" (the basketball player vs. the computer scientist) or "Apple" (the company vs. the fruit) to verify whether the learned prototypes successfully cluster mentions corresponding to these distinct senses. Without such an analysis, the interpretation of what the prototypes capture remains speculative.

4.  **Formatting of References:** The reference list contains numerous formatting errors, typos, and incomplete entries (e.g., "Efficie t string matching", "doubl -array structure", and placeholders like "? \Q2017\E"). While this is a minor point, it detracts from the overall polish of the submission and should be corrected.

### Questions to Authors

1.  Could you please clarify the strategy for determining the number of prototypes per entity? Have you experimented with methods to automatically infer this number from data, for example, based on Wikipedia's disambiguation pages or mention clustering, as an alternative to using a fixed hyperparameter?

2.  Could you provide a computational complexity analysis of your model's training procedure? What is the scaling behavior with respect to the number of entities, relations, prototypes, and the size of the text corpus? Reporting the actual training time and hardware used for the experiments would be very helpful for reproducibility and for assessing the method's practicality.

3.  To support the claim that the model learns meaningful senses, could you provide a qualitative analysis or visualization of the learned prototypes for a few highly ambiguous entities? For example, by showing that mentions of "Apple Inc." are closest to one prototype vector while mentions of "apple fruit" are closest to another.
