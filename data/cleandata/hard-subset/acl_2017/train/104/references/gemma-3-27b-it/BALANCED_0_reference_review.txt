This paper presents a comprehensive bibliography of research related to named entity disambiguation (NED) and knowledge graph completion, seemingly intended as a resource for researchers in these fields. The provided document is essentially a long list of references, spanning from foundational work like Aho and Corasick’s string matching algorithm to more recent papers on joint representation learning.

The primary contribution, as I see it, is the *compilation of a relatively extensive list of relevant papers* pertaining to NED and knowledge graph completion. While not a novel research contribution in itself, the sheer volume of references gathered in one place could be valuable to newcomers to the field or researchers seeking a broad overview of the existing literature. A secondary contribution is the demonstration of the *interdisciplinary nature of the field*, drawing from string matching, information retrieval, and knowledge representation literature.

**Strengths:**

1. **Breadth of Coverage:** The bibliography covers a significant range of relevant papers, including classic works (Aho & Corasick, Aoe) and more recent advancements (Han et al., Wang & Li). This comprehensive scope is useful for anyone seeking a starting point for research in NED and knowledge graph completion.
2. **Historical Context:** Including foundational papers alongside contemporary research provides helpful historical context. It demonstrates how the field has evolved and built upon earlier work. For example, referencing Aho & Corasick highlights the underlying string matching techniques often used in NED systems.
3. **Potential as a Resource:** The bibliography *could* serve as a valuable resource for researchers, students, and practitioners. A well-organized and easily searchable list of relevant papers is always useful.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Lack of Organization & Analysis:** The bibliography is presented as a simple list, lacking any organizational structure (e.g., by topic, methodology, or year). There's no indication of the relationships between papers, or critical commentary on their strengths and weaknesses.  Simply listing papers doesn't constitute a scholarly contribution.
2. **Formatting Inconsistencies & Errors:** The provided text exhibits significant formatting inconsistencies in the references (e.g., different abbreviations, inconsistent use of “?” following dates, missing journal/conference details, repeated entries). This makes it difficult to use the list effectively and raises concerns about the rigor of its creation.  There are many errors in the reference formatting making it hard to quickly identify the publication details.
3. **Missing Context & Motivation:** The paper doesn’t explain *why* this particular set of papers was chosen, or what criteria were used for inclusion. Was the goal to be exhaustive? To focus on specific approaches? Without this context, the bibliography feels arbitrary.
4. **Not a Novel Research Contribution:**  As it stands, this work doesn't represent a novel research contribution sufficient for ACL. A bibliography is a useful *tool*, but not a *research output* in itself. It needs to be accompanied by analysis, synthesis, or a clear methodological justification to be considered publishable.

**Questions to Authors:**

1. What specific criteria were used for selecting the papers included in this bibliography?
2. What is the intended use case for this bibliography? Who is the target audience?
3. Do you plan to provide a searchable and properly formatted version of this bibliography (e.g., as a BibTeX file or a website)?
4.  What additional analysis or organization will be added to elevate this work beyond a simple list of references and provide a genuine scholarly contribution?



**Additional Comments:**

The frequent repetition of the same reference (e.g. Aho and Corasick, Han et al.) suggests a lack of careful editing.  The inclusion of phrases like "Confidential Review Copy. DO NOT DISTRIBUTE" is inappropriate for a submitted paper. It's unclear why this text is present.



**Overall Assessment:**

I recommend **rejection** in its current form. While the effort to compile a list of relevant papers is appreciated, the lack of organization, analysis, and proper formatting prevent this submission from meeting the standards for publication at ACL. However, I believe the authors could significantly improve the work by addressing the weaknesses outlined above. Specifically, adding a thematic structure, providing critical commentary, and ensuring a high level of accuracy and consistency in the references would be crucial steps towards creating a genuinely valuable resource for the research community.
