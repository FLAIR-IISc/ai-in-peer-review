This paper appears to be a compilation of references related to named entity disambiguation (NED) and knowledge graph embedding, with a heavy emphasis on string matching techniques (Aho and Corasick, Aoe) alongside more recent deep learning approaches. It doesn't present a novel method or research contribution *per se*, but rather a collection of relevant work. Therefore, evaluating it as a standard ACL submission is problematic. 

**Contributions (as I see them):**

1. **Comprehensive Bibliography:** The primary contribution, if any, is the gathering of a substantial bibliography of relevant papers in the fields of NED, knowledge graph completion, and related string matching algorithms. This *could* be useful as a starting point for researchers.
2. **Historical Context:** The inclusion of seminal papers like Aho and Corasick (1975) and Aoe (1989) highlights the historical roots of string matching techniques that underpin many modern NED systems.
3. **Overview of Approaches:** The references span a range of methodologies, from traditional information retrieval techniques (Schütze, Järvelin and Kekäläinen) to more recent deep learning and knowledge graph embedding methods (Han et al., Weston et al., Wang and Li).

**Strengths:**

1. **Breadth of Coverage:** The list is reasonably comprehensive, covering key papers in the field. It includes work on entity linking (Shen et al., Ceccarelli et al.), word sense disambiguation (Chen et al., Li and Jurafsky), and representation learning (Mikolov et al., Mancini et al.).
2. **Inclusion of Foundational Work:**  The presence of citations to Aho-Corasick and Aoe demonstrates awareness of the fundamental algorithms that have influenced the field. This is valuable for understanding the evolution of techniques.
3. **Relevance to Multiple Areas:** The collection touches upon various interconnected areas, making it potentially useful for researchers interested in the intersection of NLP, knowledge representation, and information retrieval.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Lack of Novelty:** This is *not* a research paper presenting new findings. It's a curated list of references. Submitting this as a full ACL paper is inappropriate.  ACL seeks *contributions to the field*, not bibliographies.
2. **Repetitive Citations & Formatting Issues:** The references are riddled with duplicates (e.g., Aho and Corasick appears numerous times) and inconsistencies in formatting. This suggests a lack of careful preparation, or perhaps a technical glitch in compilation.  Several entries have question marks and incomplete year information, implying a very raw state.
3. **Absence of Synthesis or Analysis:**  The bibliography lacks any critical analysis, categorization, or synthesis of the cited works. There's no discussion of trends, open problems, or comparative evaluation of different approaches. It's a list, not a review or survey.
4. **Unclear Purpose:** The motivation for compiling this specific list is unclear. Why these papers, and not others? What is the intended use case for this compilation? Without a clear purpose, the value of the list is significantly diminished.
5. **Inconsistent Citation Style:** The citation style is varied and frequently incorrect. This impacts readability and the overall quality of the "paper."



**Questions to Authors:**

1.  **What is the intended purpose of this submission?** Is this meant to be a resource for the community, a preliminary step towards a larger review paper, or something else?
2.  **Why was this particular collection of papers selected?**  What criteria were used to determine inclusion?
3. **Can you clarify the formatting inconsistencies in the references?**  Will a consistent citation style be adopted if this submission is considered further?
4. **Do you intend to add any analysis or synthesis of the cited works?** This could significantly increase the value of the compilation.



**Additional Comments:**

I strongly recommend the authors reconsider submitting this as a full ACL paper. It is not a research contribution in its current form. If they intend to develop this into a proper survey or review paper, they should significantly expand the content, provide a critical analysis of the cited works, and address the formatting issues.  Otherwise, it might be suitable for a resource-sharing platform, but not a peer-reviewed conference.
