This paper presents a survey of techniques for Named Entity Disambiguation (NED), drawing heavily on established methods in Information Retrieval and Knowledge Representation. The core of the paper is a comprehensive literature review, categorizing existing approaches to NED and highlighting key developments. The authors claim contributions in providing a structured overview of the field, identifying current trends, and outlining potential future research directions.

As I see it, the primary contribution is indeed the extensive compilation of related work. The paper serves as a useful, if lengthy, bibliography for anyone entering the field of NED. A secondary contribution lies in the categorization of approaches, which provides a relatively clear taxonomy. However, the "trends" and "future directions" identified are largely obvious to anyone with even a passing familiarity with the field.

**Strengths:**

1. **Comprehensive Literature Review:** The paper genuinely covers a large swathe of relevant literature. The breadth of references, spanning string matching algorithms (Aho and Corasick, Aoe) to more recent deep learning approaches (Han et al., Wang and Li), is commendable. This thoroughness is valuable for researchers seeking a starting point for NED.
2. **Clear Categorization:** The organization of different NED techniques into distinct categories (e.g., graph-based, embedding-based) provides a helpful structure for understanding the field. This allows readers to quickly grasp the different methodologies employed and their relative strengths and weaknesses.
3. **Established Foundations:** The inclusion of foundational work in Information Retrieval (Schütze, Järvelin and Kekäläinen) demonstrates an understanding of the historical context of NED and its roots in related disciplines. This grounding in fundamental principles is a positive aspect.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Lack of Novelty:** The paper presents almost entirely a survey of *existing* work. There's a near-complete absence of novel ideas, methods, or analyses. While comprehensive surveys are valuable, this paper doesn't offer any new technical contributions beyond organization and summarization.
2. **Superficial Analysis:** Despite the length of the paper, the analysis of different techniques feels superficial. The authors often describe methods without critically evaluating their limitations or providing insightful comparisons. The categorization, while helpful, lacks depth. For example, simply grouping methods as "embedding-based" doesn’t adequately capture the nuances of different embedding strategies (e.g., word vs. sense embeddings, joint vs. separate training).
3. **Over-Reliance on Citations without Critical Evaluation:** The sheer number of citations is impressive, but it also feels somewhat indiscriminate. Many cited papers are simply listed without a clear justification of their relevance or a discussion of their impact. The paper feels more like a catalog of papers than a critical review. Many citations are repeated with slightly different formatting which is distracting.
4. **Missing Evaluation Context:** The paper fails to adequately contextualize the performance of different methods. There’s no discussion of commonly used datasets, evaluation metrics, or state-of-the-art results. This makes it difficult to assess the practical effectiveness of the reviewed techniques.
5. **Formatting and Presentation:** The reference list is messy and inconsistent in formatting. Frequent repetition of references with minor variations is unprofessional and hinders readability.

**Questions to Authors:**

1. Could you articulate a clear thesis statement beyond simply "to survey the literature on NED"? What specific gap in understanding does this survey aim to fill?
2. How did you select the papers included in this survey? What criteria were used to determine relevance and inclusion?
3. Can you provide a more detailed comparative analysis of the different techniques discussed, highlighting their strengths, weaknesses, and applicability to different scenarios?
4. Given the established nature of many of the cited techniques, what specific opportunities for future research beyond the broadly stated "future directions" do you foresee?
