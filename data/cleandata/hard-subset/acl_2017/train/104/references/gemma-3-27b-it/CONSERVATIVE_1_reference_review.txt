This paper appears to be a collection of references related to named entity disambiguation (NED), knowledge graph completion, and related areas of information retrieval and natural language processing. It does *not* present a novel research contribution. It’s essentially a bibliography, albeit a rather extensive one.

**Contributions (as I see them):**

1.  **Compilation of Relevant Literature:** The primary contribution, albeit a trivial one, is the gathering of a substantial list of papers relevant to the fields of NED and knowledge graph integration. This *could* be useful as a starting point for researchers entering the field, but its value is limited by the lack of organization or annotation.
2.  **Demonstration of Research Landscape:** The sheer volume of references indicates the breadth and activity within these research areas. While not a novel insight, it highlights the existing body of work.

**Strengths:**

1.  **Breadth of Coverage:** The list includes seminal works (Aho & Corasick, Aoe) alongside more recent publications (Han et al., Wu et al.). This demonstrates a reasonable awareness of the history and current trends in the field.
2.  **Relevance to Core Topics:** The included papers generally address relevant themes: string matching (as a foundational element), entity linking, knowledge graph embedding, and joint representation learning.
3.  **Potential Utility as a Starting Point:** For a researcher completely new to NED and related fields, this list offers a place to begin exploring the literature.

**Weaknesses:**

1.  **Lack of Novelty:** This is not a research paper; it presents no new method, analysis, or results. It is purely a compilation of existing work. Submitting this as a full paper to a competitive conference like ACL is inappropriate.
2.  **Disorganized Presentation:** The references are presented in a seemingly arbitrary order, with frequent repetitions (multiple entries for the same paper with slight variations in formatting). There is no clear categorization or annotation to guide the reader.
3.  **Inconsistent Formatting:** The reference formatting is extremely inconsistent (e.g., differing use of “?” versus year, abbreviations like “CoRR” vs. full URLs). This reflects poorly on the effort invested and reduces the usability of the list.
4.  **Missing Context:**  The list provides no indication of *why* these papers were chosen or their relative importance.  A simple categorization (e.g., by task: Entity Linking, Knowledge Graph Completion) would significantly increase the value.
5. **Duplication and Errors:** The list contains numerous duplicated entries, and some entries have apparent errors or inconsistencies in author names or publication details.



**Questions to Authors:**

1.  What is the intended contribution of this submission? Is it meant to be a review article, a survey, or something else entirely?
2.  If the intention is to provide a resource for the community, why was no effort made to organize, categorize, and consistently format the references?
3.  Was any attempt made to remove duplicate entries and correct errors in the bibliographic information?

**Additional Comments:**

This submission, in its current form, is not suitable for acceptance at ACL. It requires substantial revision to become a meaningful contribution, and even then, would be better suited as a supplementary resource rather than a standalone paper. The authors should consider carefully if the effort required to address the weaknesses is justified, given the lack of novel research presented.
