## Review for ACL Submission

**Summary:**

This submission appears to be a collection of references related to Named Entity Disambiguation (NED) and Knowledge Graph (KG) completion, with a strong emphasis on joint representation learning of text and knowledge. The provided document *is not a paper itself*, but rather a bibliography, likely used as background material for another work, or as a reference list for a survey. It’s difficult to assess a 'paper' consisting solely of references; therefore, the review will focus on the *collection itself* – its comprehensiveness, relevance to current research, and potential utility to the field.

**Main Contributions (as I see them, given the nature of the submission):**

1. **Curated Bibliography:** The primary contribution is the compilation of a significant number of relevant papers in the areas of NED, entity linking, knowledge graph embedding, and joint representation learning.  This saves researchers considerable time in building a foundational understanding of the field.
2. **Historical Coverage:** The bibliography spans a good range of years (1975-2017), including foundational work (Aho & Corasick, Aoe) alongside more recent advances (Han et al., Wu et al.). This provides a sense of the evolution of techniques.
3. **Focus on Joint Learning:**  The clear emphasis on joint representation learning of text and knowledge highlights a key research direction within the broader field.



**Strengths:**

1. **Broad Coverage of Key Areas:** The bibliography effectively covers major themes in NED and KG completion, including string matching algorithms, early entity linking approaches, word embedding techniques, and more recent deep learning-based methods leveraging KGs.  This breadth is valuable.
2. **Inclusion of Foundational Work:**  The inclusion of classic papers like Aho & Corasick and Aoe demonstrates an awareness of the historical context of the field. While seemingly distant from modern deep learning, understanding these foundational algorithms is crucial for informed innovation.
3. **Timeliness (up to 2017):**  The list is current up to 2017, capturing a significant wave of research in joint representation learning.  It serves as a useful snapshot of the state-of-the-art *at that time*.
4. **Relevance to Current Research:** The papers cited are generally highly relevant to ongoing research in areas like information retrieval, knowledge graph reasoning, and natural language understanding. Many papers are frequently cited.
5. **Potential Utility for Survey/Review Papers:** This bibliography is ideally suited as a starting point for researchers conducting surveys or writing review papers in these areas.



**Weaknesses:**

1. **Not a Research Paper:**  Critically, this submission *isn’t a paper*. It's a list of references.  Therefore, concepts like novelty, methodology, and results are entirely absent. Its acceptance as a standalone ACL submission is highly questionable.  A proper submission should *analyze* these papers, synthesize insights, or present a new research contribution *based on* these references.
2. **Inconsistent Formatting & Duplication:**  There are numerous instances of duplicate entries (e.g., Aho & Corasick is listed multiple times) and inconsistencies in formatting (varying abbreviation styles, date formats, etc.). This suggests a lack of careful curation. While a bibliography doesn't *need* to be perfect, the sloppiness detracts from its usability.
3. **Limited Scope – Missing Recent Work:** While current to 2017, the list does not include work from the last 7+ years. The field of KG completion and joint embedding has rapidly advanced since then.  A truly comprehensive resource would incorporate recent developments.
4. **Lack of Categorization/Annotation:** The bibliography is a simple list. It would be significantly more useful if papers were categorized (e.g., by task – entity linking, KG completion, relation extraction – or by technique – deep learning, graph embeddings, probabilistic models) and potentially annotated with short summaries of their key contributions.
5. **Unclear Purpose:** The document lacks any introductory text explaining its purpose or intended audience. Why was this bibliography compiled? What is its intended use?




**Questions to Authors:**

1. **What is the intended purpose of this document?**  Is it meant to accompany another submission, serve as a standalone resource, or something else?
2. **If this is intended as a standalone resource, do you plan to update it to include more recent publications (post-2017)?**
3. **Do you intend to add any categorization or annotation to the list to make it more accessible and useful?**
4. **Can you explain the rationale for including some of the older papers (e.g., Aho & Corasick) in a bibliography focused on modern techniques?**  While valuable for historical context, their direct relevance to current research might be limited.
5. **How was this bibliography compiled?** What criteria were used for inclusion/exclusion of papers?




**Additional Comments:**

The repeated typos ("d knowledge", "id to bibliog phic earch", etc.) throughout the references suggest a lack of attention to detail.  A careful proofread is essential. Given that this is a reference list and not a full paper, I am not evaluating the "experimental setup" or "results," as those sections are absent. 

**Overall Recommendation:**

**Reject**. As it stands, this submission is not a research paper suitable for ACL.  It is a basic bibliography. It *could* be a useful resource if significantly improved (cleaned, expanded, categorized, annotated, and accompanied by a clear statement of purpose). However, in its current form, it does not meet the standards for publication at a major NLP conference.  If the authors intend to submit a *paper* that leverages this bibliography to analyze trends or present a novel contribution, that would be a more appropriate submission.
