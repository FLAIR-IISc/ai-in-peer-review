This submission presents a bibliography of research papers relevant to named entity disambiguation, knowledge graph completion, and related areas of natural language processing. The primary purported contribution is the compilation of a comprehensive list of references.

**Contributions (as I see them):**

1.  **Compilation of References:** The paper does, in fact, list a significant number of relevant papers. This *could* be useful as a starting point for researchers entering the field. However, the execution is deeply flawed (see Weaknesses).
2.  **Demonstration of Topic Relevance:** The collected references, despite their problematic presentation, demonstrate a clear focus on the stated areas of NLP.

**Strengths:**

1.  **Broad Coverage (Potential):** The list *attempts* to cover a range of important publications. If correctly formatted and curated, this breadth could be valuable.
2.  **Relevant Topic:** The chosen area of research is active and important within the NLP community.

**Weaknesses:**

1.  **Formatting Catastrophe:** The formatting of the bibliography is abysmal. There are inconsistent citation styles *within* single entries (e.g., “abs/1611.04125.” vs. “abs/1611.04125”), missing punctuation, and variations in author name presentation (e.g., “Han et al\.” vs. “Han et al.”). The consistent use of question marks in place of years is inexplicable and unacceptable. This lack of uniformity renders the bibliography nearly unusable.
2.  **Duplication and Redundancy:** The bibliography contains numerous duplicate entries, often with minor variations in formatting. For example, the Aho and Corasick paper appears *multiple* times with slight differences. This suggests a lack of careful curation.
3.  **Incomplete Information:** Some entries lack complete information. The entry "Confid tia Review Copy. DO NOT DISTRIBUTE." is entirely inappropriate and adds no scholarly value. Other entries are missing volume or issue numbers where applicable. The “\Q2017\E” and similar year representations are non-standard and ambiguous.
4.  **Ambiguous Authorship/Attribution:** Several entries use abbreviations like “Corasick.”, “Aoe.”, and “Cucerzan.” These are insufficient for proper citation and require full author names. The use of “k.” after Corasick is particularly perplexing.
5.  **Lack of Categorization/Organization:** The bibliography is presented as a simple, unorganized list. It would be far more valuable if the papers were categorized by topic or methodology. The complete lack of any structure hinders its usefulness.
6.  **Questionable Inclusion Criteria:** The inclusion of phrases like “played it during public” within a citation raises serious concerns about the rigor of the selection process. This is not a scholarly reference.

**Questions to Authors:**

1.  What systematic method was used to compile this bibliography, and how was redundancy addressed?
2.  Why was a consistent citation style not used throughout the bibliography? Please specify the style (e.g., ACL, IEEE) that was intended.
3.  What is the justification for the use of question marks in place of publication years?
4.  What criteria were used to determine which papers were included, and why were entries like "Confid tia Review Copy. DO NOT DISTRIBUTE." included?
5.  Do you intend to provide a categorized and properly formatted version of this bibliography?

**Additional Comments:**

The sheer number of formatting errors and inconsistencies demonstrates a lack of attention to detail that is unacceptable for a scholarly submission. While compiling a list of references is a valid starting point, the current state of this work is far below the standards required for acceptance. A complete overhaul of the formatting, deduplication, and content is necessary. Without significant revisions, this submission does not represent a meaningful contribution to the field.



**Recommendation:** Reject. The numerous and fundamental flaws in this submission outweigh any potential benefits. The work, as presented, is simply not usable or scholarly.
