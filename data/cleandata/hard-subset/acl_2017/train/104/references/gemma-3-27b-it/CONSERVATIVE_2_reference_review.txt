This submission appears to be a bibliographic collection related to named entity disambiguation and knowledge graph embedding, rather than a novel research paper. The references span a considerable period, from the foundational work of Aho and Corasick (1975) on string matching, through to more recent developments in joint representation learning (Han et al., 2016; Wu et al., 2016).  The overwhelming majority of entries are simply citations to established work.  Any "contribution" is merely the curation of this list, and its value is questionable without a clear rationale or organizing principle.

**Contributions (as I see them):**

1. **Compilation of Relevant Literature:** The primary (and perhaps only) contribution is the assembly of a fairly comprehensive bibliography on entity disambiguation and related topics.
2. **Historical Perspective:**  The inclusion of seminal papers like Aho & Corasick and Aoe demonstrates an awareness of the historical roots of string matching techniques, which, while not directly central to modern entity disambiguation, provide a broader context. 

**Strengths:**

1. **Breadth of Coverage:** The bibliography touches upon a wide range of relevant techniques, including string matching, knowledge graph embedding, word sense disambiguation, and various entity linking approaches. This suggests the curator has a good understanding of the field.
2. **Inclusion of Foundational Work:**  The reference list isn’t solely focused on recent deep learning approaches. It incorporates classic IR and NLP papers, offering a more complete picture of the evolution of the field.
3. **Comprehensive Citation of Key Players:** Many prominent researchers in the field (e.g., Liu, Sun, Mikolov, Weston) are well-represented, indicating the bibliography isn't missing core contributions.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Lack of Novelty:**  This is not a research paper. It presents no new method, analysis, or experimental results. It's a collection of references, and a simple list, however extensive, does not constitute a scientific contribution suitable for ACL.
2. **Absence of Organizing Principle/Rationale:** The references appear to be listed somewhat haphazardly. There's no clear indication of *why* these specific papers were selected, or how they relate to each other. Is it a list of papers used in a survey? Papers influencing a particular method?  Without this context, the list is less useful.
3. **Repetitive and Inconsistent Citations:** Several references are duplicated (e.g., Aho and Corasick, Aoe, Han et al.).  Furthermore, the formatting is inconsistent and contains errors. (e.g., "? 2017", "? \Q2017\E"). This suggests a lack of careful attention to detail.
4. **Missing Context & Annotation:** The bibliography lacks any annotation or summary of the papers. A useful bibliography would ideally include brief descriptions of each paper's key contributions or relevance.

**Questions to Authors:**

1. What is the specific purpose of this bibliography? What gap in the literature does it address?
2. What criteria were used to select the papers included in the list?
3. Will the formatting inconsistencies and duplicate entries be addressed?
4. Do you intend to add annotations or summaries to each citation to explain its relevance?



**Additional Comments:**

If the intention is to create a resource for researchers, this would be better suited to a platform like GitHub or a dedicated website, rather than a peer-reviewed conference submission.  As it stands, this is not a research contribution and is not appropriate for ACL.
