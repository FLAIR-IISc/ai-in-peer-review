This paper appears to be a bibliographic collection – a listing of references related to Named Entity Disambiguation (NED) and related areas like knowledge graph embedding and word sense disambiguation. There’s no novel research presented; it's essentially a curated list of papers. The primary 'contribution', if it can be called that, is the *compilation* of these references. A secondary contribution might be the sheer breadth of the included papers, touching upon many relevant subfields.

**Strengths:**

1. **Comprehensive Coverage:** The list is remarkably extensive, covering foundational work (Aho & Corasick, Aoe) through to more recent papers (Wu et al., 2016; Yamada et al., 2017). It showcases a good understanding of the historical development and current state-of-the-art in NED and related fields. This could be a useful resource for researchers entering the field.
2. **Interdisciplinary Connections:** The bibliography successfully links work from information retrieval (Schütze, Järvelin & Kekäläinen), knowledge representation (Weston et al., Shen et al.), and natural language processing (Mikolov et al., Chen et al.). Demonstrating these connections is valuable.
3. **Historical Perspective:** Including classic papers alongside more contemporary ones provides a valuable historical perspective. The inclusion of Aho-Corasick and Aoe's work, while seemingly distant from modern neural NED, underscores the roots of string matching techniques that still inform the field.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Lack of Novelty:** This is not a research paper in any meaningful sense. It doesn't present new methods, analyses, or insights. It's a collection, and while a *good* collection, it doesn’t warrant acceptance to a research conference like ACL. ACL prioritizes novel contributions, and this work offers none.
2. **Formatting and Consistency Issues:** The reference list is riddled with inconsistencies. Some authors are fully named, others are abbreviated ("Han et al."). Date formats vary ("\Q2017\E", "2013a", etc.). There are duplicated entries, and errors within entries (e.g. misspellings like "bibliogr phic"). This suggests a lack of careful curation and attention to detail.
3. **Missing Context and Categorization:** The list is simply a sequence of references. There's no organization, categorization, or commentary to guide the reader. A helpful bibliography would group papers by theme (e.g., knowledge graph embeddings, contextualized embeddings, supervised vs. unsupervised methods) and perhaps offer brief summaries or annotations. The current form makes it difficult to utilize efficiently.
4. **Redundancy/Duplication:** Numerous references are repeated verbatim, which indicates a problem with the curation process. This does not enhance the value of the bibliography.



**Questions to Authors:**

1. What is the specific *research question* this work addresses?  If the goal is simply to provide a list of papers, why is this suitable for a conference like ACL?
2. What criteria were used for selecting the included papers? Was there a specific scope or methodology guiding the selection process?
3. Do you plan to address the inconsistencies in the reference formatting?
4. Do you envision any future work building upon this bibliography (e.g., adding annotations, developing a search interface)?

**Additional Comments:**

This submission, in its current form, is not appropriate for ACL. It could potentially be a useful resource if published as a technical report or made available as a community-maintained resource (e.g., on GitHub). However, as a conference submission, it falls far short of the expected standards of novelty and rigor.
