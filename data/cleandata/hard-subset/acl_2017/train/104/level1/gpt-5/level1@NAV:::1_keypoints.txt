- Summary: Learns multi-prototype mention embeddings from text and KB signals and uses an LM-based disambiguator; reports SOTA on a standard EL benchmark.
- Contribution 1: Demonstrates mention-level multi-prototype embeddings plus a simple disambiguator improve EL accuracy to SOTA.
- Contribution 2: Presents a practical framework to learn multi-prototype mention embeddings aligned with entities, bridging text and KBs.
- Contribution 3: Proposes an efficient LM-based disambiguation method with favorable speed–accuracy trade-offs.
- Strength 1: Modeling ambiguity at the mention level is well-motivated and directly aligned with entity linking needs.
- Strength 2: Joint text–KB training is principled for reducing sparsity and grounding senses.
- Strength 3: The approach appears practical and scalable, with attention to efficiency and string-matching.
- Strength 4: Uses both qualitative (embedding inspections) and quantitative evaluations to support claims.
- Weakness 1: Novelty is incremental—multi-prototype and joint text–KB embeddings are established; claimed novelty needs clearer articulation.
- Weakness 2: Evaluation clarity is lacking—datasets, baselines, and ablation details behind the “SOTA” claim are insufficient.
- Weakness 3: Methodological specifics are underspecified (number of senses per mention, training objective, negative sampling, LM integration).
- Weakness 4: Scholarly presentation issues (duplicate/typo-ridden references) undermine rigor and related-work assessment.
- Question 1: How is the number of prototypes per mention determined and how is sense proliferation prevented?
- Question 2: Which datasets and baselines support the “SOTA” claim, and what are the absolute/relative improvements?
- Question 3: Please provide ablations isolating the effects of joint training versus the LM disambiguator.
- Question 4: How does the method handle rare/unseen mentions and NIL entities?
