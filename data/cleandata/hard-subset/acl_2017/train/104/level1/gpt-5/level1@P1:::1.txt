Summary
The paper addresses ambiguity when integrating text and knowledge into a shared embedding space: a surface mention (e.g., “Washington”) can denote multiple entities. It proposes Multi-Prototype Mention Embeddings (MPME), learning multiple sense vectors per mention by jointly modeling textual context (words) and knowledge-derived signals (entities). At inference, an efficient language-model-based disambiguator selects the appropriate mention sense using context. The authors evaluate intrinsic quality of word/entity/mention embeddings and apply the approach to entity linking (EL), claiming state-of-the-art performance on a benchmark.

Contributions (as I see them)
1) A multi-prototype embedding model specifically for mention strings that jointly learns with word and entity embeddings, tying senses to KB entities through shared training. This extends multi-sense representations from words to ambiguous mention forms used in EL.
2) A simple, efficient disambiguation mechanism that uses a language modeling objective to select the most plausible mention-sense given local context.
3) An empirical study showing that using MPME in EL yields competitive or state-of-the-art performance.

Strengths
- Clear and timely problem formulation: Modeling multiple senses for mentions (as opposed to only words or only entities) directly targets the primary ambiguity in EL. The idea of learning mention-sense vectors in the same space as words and entities is natural and compelling and could be reused in other tasks (e.g., NED, query understanding).
- Joint text–KB training: Leveraging both textual contexts and KB-derived signals in a single embedding space is a strong design choice supported by prior work; extending this to multi-prototype mentions plausibly improves compatibility between context words and candidate entities. Qualitative analyses described in the abstract suggest meaningful neighborhoods for word/entity/mention vectors.
- Practical disambiguation: A language-model style scoring function for sense selection promises efficiency and easy integration with standard candidate generation pipelines. If the model scores context likelihood under each mention-sense and chooses the argmax, it avoids heavy global inference and could be attractive in production.
- Reported gains on EL: Applying the disambiguator with MPME to a standard dataset and achieving state-of-the-art indicates practical value beyond intrinsic embedding quality. If the experiments are on AIDA-CoNLL and include micro/macro accuracy or in-KB metrics, this would be a strong result.
- Potentially low supervision: If training primarily relies on naturally occurring hyperlinks/anchors and KB alignments, this is scalable and reduces annotation cost, which is a pragmatic advantage over fully supervised end-to-end EL architectures.

Weaknesses
- Incomplete/messy presentation and missing details: The submission contains numerous formatting issues and duplicated/corrupted references; key sections (objective function, training details) appear truncated. Without a precise learning objective, optimization procedure, and disambiguation formula, the work is not reproducible. Please specify loss functions (e.g., skip-gram/negative sampling variants), how entities are incorporated (context windows, anchor-based contexts), and exact disambiguation scoring.
- Ambiguity about sense induction and number of prototypes: It is unclear how many sense embeddings are learned per mention, how senses are induced, and whether the number is fixed or non-parametric (as in Neelakantan et al., 2014). How are assignments of training contexts to senses handled (EM? online clustering? hard vs soft)? What prevents sense proliferation for frequent mentions and underfitting for rare ones?
- Novelty relative to prior multi-sense and EL work: Multi-prototype embeddings for words (Reisinger & Mooney, Huang et al., Tian et al., Neelakantan et al.) and joint word–entity embeddings (Toutanova et al., Yamada et al.) are established. The paper’s novelty appears to be focusing prototypes on mentions and tying them to entities with a LM-based disambiguation. This is an incremental step unless the method introduces a substantially new training or disambiguation mechanism. A stronger positioning and comparisons to recent neural EL models (e.g., attention-based local models and global coherence, circa 2016–2017) are needed.
- Evaluation clarity and baselines: The abstract claims state-of-the-art but the submission lacks specifics about datasets, metrics, and baselines. For EL, comparisons should include strong systems with local+global inference and recent embedding-based methods. Ablations isolating the value of (a) multi-prototypes vs single mention vector, (b) joint text+KB vs text-only, and (c) the proposed LM disambiguation vs standard cosine similarity or classifier, are needed to attribute gains.
- Disambiguation scope and coherence: A purely local LM-based selector may underperform in documents where global topical coherence is essential. If no document-level joint inference is used, experiments should quantify the gap vs collective methods and perhaps show how MPME features can be plugged into a global model. Runtime and memory costs for inference and training are also not reported.

Questions to Authors
- Model specifics: What is the exact training objective? Do you use skip-gram with negative sampling over a mixed vocabulary of words, mentions, and entities? How are KB signals incorporated (e.g., contexts from entity descriptions, graph proximity, anchor contexts)?
- Sense induction: How many prototypes per mention? Fixed K per mention or adaptive? How are training contexts assigned to prototypes during learning? Any regularization to avoid redundant senses?
- Disambiguation: Please provide the exact LM scoring function used at inference. Is it simply P(context | mention-sense) approximated via dot-products with surrounding words/entities? How are candidates enumerated?
- Datasets and baselines: Which EL benchmarks were used (e.g., AIDA-CoNLL, TAC KBP, MSNBC, AQUAINT)? What metrics (micro/macro accuracy, InKB, NDCG) and which baselines (including recent neural EL models) are compared? Are candidate generation and mention detection standardized across systems?
- Ablations and analysis: What is the relative contribution of multi-prototypes vs a single mention vector? How sensitive are results to the number of senses, context window size, and negative sampling rate? Do you evaluate NIL detection or only in-KB linking?
- Efficiency: What are training time, inference latency per mention, and memory footprint as a function of vocabulary size and number of prototypes?

Additional comments
- The reference list needs significant cleaning; many entries are duplicated and corrupted. Ensure all compared methods are properly cited and that datasets and code (if available) are referenced.
- Include qualitative examples showing different sense prototypes for the same mention and nearest neighbors across words/entities to substantiate the interpretability claims.

Overall recommendation
The core idea—multi-prototype embeddings for mentions jointly trained with words and entities—is interesting and practically relevant to EL. However, the submission in its current form is underspecified and does not allow a fair assessment of novelty or reproducibility; the evaluation section appears incomplete. With clearer methodological details, stronger baselines, and thorough ablations, this work could make a solid contribution. As-is, I lean toward rejection, but I encourage resubmission after substantial revision.
