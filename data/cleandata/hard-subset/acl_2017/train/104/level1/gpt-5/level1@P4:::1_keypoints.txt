- Summary: Proposes Multi-Prototype Mention Embedding (MPME) to assign multiple sense embeddings per surface-form mention by jointly modeling textual context and KB entities, with an LM-based disambiguator; evaluated intrinsically and on entity linking claiming SOTA.

- Contribution 1: Joint embedding framework learning multiple sense embeddings per mention aligned with word and entity vectors in a shared space.

- Contribution 2: Efficient language-model-based disambiguation mechanism to select mention senses in context for use in EL.

- Contribution 3: Empirical study including intrinsic analyses of learned vectors and extrinsic EL evaluation.

- Strength: Clear motivation addressing mention-level ambiguity as a meaningful gap complementary to multi-sense word and joint text–KB embeddings.

- Strength: Coherent integration of signals by jointly learning words, entities, and mention-sense vectors to align textual and KB semantics.

- Strength: Promising empirical impact on entity linking with claimed state-of-the-art results on a standard benchmark.

- Strength: Efficiency emphasis—LM-based disambiguator may be faster than graph-based collective methods if supported by runtime/memory measurements.

- Strength: Qualitative analyses (nearest neighbors) can provide interpretable evidence of distinct senses and cross-modal alignment.

- Weakness: Incremental novelty—shifting multi-prototype learning to surface-form mentions appears reasonable but limited relative to prior multi-sense and joint embedding work.

- Weakness: Insufficient technical detail about number of prototypes, training objective, disambiguator formulation, handling of rare mentions, and control of sense proliferation.

- Weakness: Evaluation scope and baselines unclear; needs more intrinsic/transfer evaluations, ablations, and comparison to strong global/coherent EL systems.

- Weakness: Efficiency claims are unsubstantiated without quantitative training/inference complexity, memory footprint, or runtime comparisons.

- Weakness: Presentation issues—references contain duplicates, typos, and corrupted entries, undermining clarity and related-work coverage.

- Question 1: Specify datasets/splits, candidate generation, mention detection assumptions, and whether AIDA-CoNLL or other benchmarks were used.

- Question 2: How many prototypes per mention are learned—fixed K, per-mention, or dynamically induced—and how are redundant senses prevented?

- Question 3: Provide the exact joint objective with equations, training details (sampling, optimization, hyperparameters), and how KB structure is used beyond anchors.

- Question 4: Define the LM-based disambiguator—local vs. document-level/global coherence—and comparison to collective EL methods on hard documents.

- Question 5: Provide ablations quantifying contributions of multi-prototype vs. single-prototype, joint training vs. text-only, and LM disambiguator vs. simpler methods.

- Question 6: Report efficiency metrics: training time, inference latency per mention, and memory usage compared to strong baselines.

- Question 7: Will code, trained embeddings, and preprocessing scripts be released for reproducibility?

- Question 8: Provide additional intrinsic evaluations of mention-sense quality (e.g., clustering purity, sense separation for high-frequency ambiguous mentions).

- Question 9: Correct the bibliography to remove duplicates/typos and ensure key related works are covered.
