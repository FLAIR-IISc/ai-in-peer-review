Summary
The paper addresses ambiguity when integrating text and knowledge into a shared embedding space. It proposes a Multi-Prototype Mention Embedding (MPME) model that assigns multiple sense embeddings to each surface-form mention by jointly modeling textual context words and entities from a knowledge base. A language-model-based disambiguation method selects the appropriate sense for a mention in context. The approach is evaluated intrinsically (quality of word/entity/mention vectors) and extrinsically on entity linking, where the authors claim state-of-the-art performance.

Main contributions (as I see them)
1) A joint embedding framework that learns multiple sense embeddings per surface-form mention, aligned with word and entity representations in a unified space.
2) An efficient, language-model-based disambiguation mechanism to select mention senses in context and use them for entity linking.
3) An empirical study on entity linking, including intrinsic analyses of the learned vectors.

Strengths
- Clear problem focus and motivation: Ambiguity at the mention level is a practical bottleneck in joint text–KB embedding and entity linking. Modeling multi-prototype representations at the mention (surface form) level is a natural but under-explored complement to prior multi-sense embeddings for words (e.g., Reisinger & Mooney 2010; Huang et al. 2012; Neelakantan et al. 2014; Tian et al. 2014) and joint text–KB embeddings (e.g., Wang et al. 2014; Toutanova et al. 2015; Wang & Li 2016). The paper targets a meaningful gap.
- Coherent integration of signals: Jointly learning word, entity, and mention-sense vectors promises to align textual and KB semantics and reduce error propagation between mention disambiguation and entity modeling. If implemented as described, this design is technically sensible and leverages available supervision from contexts and KB anchors.
- Promising empirical impact on EL: The paper claims state-of-the-art results on a standard entity linking benchmark, which, if shown against strong baselines and with proper splits (e.g., AIDA-CoNLL), would establish the practical value of learning multi-prototype mention embeddings. Using the disambiguation mechanism within an EL pipeline is a good testbed.
- Efficiency emphasis: A language-model-based disambiguator may offer faster inference than graph-based collective methods. If accompanied by runtime and memory measurements, this would be attractive for large-scale EL systems.
- Qualitative analyses: Inspecting nearest neighbors across words, entities, and mention senses can provide interpretable evidence that the model captures distinct senses for ambiguous mentions and aligns modalities.

Weaknesses
- Limited novelty relative to prior art: Multi-prototype representations are well-studied for words/senses, and joint text–KB embedding is established. The main novelty here is shifting multi-prototype learning to surface-form mentions. This is a reasonable extension but appears incremental. A careful comparison to strong multi-sense and EL embedding baselines (e.g., Yamada et al. 2016; Ganea & Hofmann 2017) is necessary to substantiate novelty.
- Insufficient technical detail (as presented): The abstract and references do not clarify key aspects needed to assess soundness:
  • How many prototypes per mention are used, and how is this chosen (fixed K vs. adaptive sense induction)?  
  • What is the exact training objective that couples words, entities, and mention senses (loss terms, negative sampling, optimization)?  
  • How the language-model-based disambiguator is formulated and how candidates are scored; whether it accounts for global coherence across a document.  
  • How sense proliferation/duplication is controlled and how rare mentions are handled.
  Without these, it is difficult to judge whether the approach is robust and reproducible.
- Evaluation scope and baselines unclear: The paper appears to evaluate primarily on entity linking. For an embedding contribution, additional intrinsic or transfer evaluations (e.g., mention sense clustering quality, ablations on single- vs. multi-prototype mentions, removal of KB signals, removal of LM disambiguation) are important. It is also important to compare against recent strong EL systems with global coherence and neural scoring. Lack of thorough ablations would weaken the attribution of gains to multi-prototype mention modeling.
- Efficiency claims not substantiated: The abstract mentions an “efficient” language model approach, but there is no indication of training/inference complexity, memory footprint (potentially large if storing multiple vectors per mention), or runtime comparisons to graph-based EL. Without quantitative efficiency metrics, the claim remains anecdotal.
- Presentation quality and references: The reference list contains numerous duplicates, typographical errors, and corrupted entries (e.g., multiple repeated Aho–Corasick, misspellings, malformed years/venues). This undermines clarity and raises concerns about proofreading and completeness of related work coverage.

Questions to Authors
1) What datasets and splits are used for entity linking? Please specify candidate generation, mention detection assumptions, and whether you evaluate on AIDA-CoNLL (train/dev/test A/B/C) or other benchmarks (TAC-KBP, MSNBC, AQUAINT).
2) How many prototypes per mention are learned? Is K fixed globally, per-mention, or induced dynamically (e.g., nonparametric clustering)? How do you prevent redundant senses?
3) What is the exact joint objective for learning word, entity, and mention-sense embeddings? Please provide equations, training details (sampling strategies, optimization, hyperparameters), and how KB structure (links/relations) is used beyond anchor co-occurrences.
4) How is the language-model-based disambiguation defined? Is it purely local (context window) or does it incorporate document-level global coherence? If purely local, how does the method compare to collective EL (e.g., Hoffart et al. 2011; Pershina et al. 2015; Yamada et al. 2016; Ganea & Hofmann 2017) on difficult documents?
5) What are the main ablation results quantifying the contribution of: (a) multi-prototype mentions vs. single-prototype, (b) joint training with entities vs. text-only, and (c) the LM disambiguator vs. a simpler nearest-neighbor or classifier?
6) Please report efficiency metrics: training time, inference latency per mention, and memory usage (number of stored mention-sense vectors) compared to strong baselines.
7) Do you release code, trained embeddings, and data preprocessing scripts to ensure reproducibility?
8) Can you provide additional intrinsic evaluation of mention-sense quality (e.g., clustering purity against gold EL annotations, sense separation analyses for high-frequency ambiguous mentions)?
9) The references contain many duplicates and typos. Will you provide a corrected bibliography and ensure key related works are covered?
