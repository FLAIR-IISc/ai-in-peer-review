Summary:
The paper tackles ambiguity of surface mentions by learning multi-prototype embeddings per mention string, jointly informed by textual context words and knowledge-base (KB) entities. It further proposes an efficient language-model-based disambiguation module that selects the appropriate mention sense at inference. The approach is evaluated qualitatively and quantitatively and reportedly attains state-of-the-art performance on a standard entity linking benchmark.

Contributions (as I see them, strongest first):
1) Demonstration that mention-level multi-prototype embeddings, when jointly trained on text and KB signals and coupled with a simple disambiguator, improve entity linking accuracy to SOTA.
2) A practical framework for learning multi-prototype mention embeddings aligned with entities, bridging text and KBs in a unified space.
3) An efficient disambiguation method based on a language model that appears to offer favorable speed-accuracy trade-offs.

Strengths:
1) Problem alignment and relevance: Modeling ambiguity at the mention level is directly aligned with entity linking, addressing a gap left by multi-sense word embeddings and pure entity embeddings. This design choice is compelling and well-motivated by the task.
2) Joint text–KB training: Leveraging both contextual words and entity supervision is a principled way to reduce sparsity and ground senses. If the reported gains hold across datasets, this is a solid empirical contribution.
3) Practicality and efficiency: The language-model-based disambiguator and references to efficient string matching suggest a system that can scale to large mention inventories, which is valuable for real-world EL.
4) Evidence breadth: The combination of qualitative (embedding inspections) and quantitative evaluations strengthens the claim that the learned space is meaningful, beyond just end-task numbers.

Weaknesses:
1) Novelty is incremental: Multi-prototype embeddings and joint text–KB embedding are established; the main novelty seems to be focusing on mentions. The paper should more clearly articulate what is fundamentally new in modeling or objective design.
2) Evaluation clarity and coverage: The abstract mentions a single benchmark and “SOTA” without detailing datasets, baselines, or ablations. It is unclear whether strong modern EL baselines are included and how much each component contributes.
3) Methodological specifics are underspecified: How are the number of senses per mention chosen (fixed vs nonparametric)? What is the precise training objective and negative sampling strategy? How is the LM disambiguator trained and integrated?
4) Scholarly presentation: The reference list contains substantial duplication and typographical errors, which undermines rigor and makes it hard to assess related work coverage.

Questions to Authors:
- How is the number of prototypes per mention determined, and how do you prevent sense proliferation for frequent mentions?
- What datasets and baselines are used for “SOTA,” and what is the absolute/relative improvement?
- Provide ablations isolating the effects of joint training vs. the LM disambiguator.
- How does the method handle rare/unseen mentions and NIL entities?
