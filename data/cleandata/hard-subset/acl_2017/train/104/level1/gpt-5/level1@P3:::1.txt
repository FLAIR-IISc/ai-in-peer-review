Summary and contributions
The paper proposes a Multi-Prototype Mention Embedding model to address lexical ambiguity of entity mentions (e.g., “Washington”) by learning multiple sense-specific embeddings per mention surface form. The model jointly leverages textual context (words around mentions) and knowledge base supervision (entities) to induce mention-sense vectors. An efficient, language-model-based procedure is introduced to disambiguate a test-time mention occurrence to one of its learned senses. The authors provide qualitative analyses of the embeddings and report state-of-the-art results on an entity linking (EL) benchmark.

As I see them, the main contributions are:
1) A mention-centric multi-prototype embedding framework that integrates text and KB signals to produce sense-specific vectors tied to ambiguous surface forms, bridging word-sense induction and entity representations.
2) An efficient disambiguation method that selects a mention sense using a language-model-style scoring of local context.
3) Empirical evidence on EL that multi-prototype mention representations and the proposed disambiguation improve over prior work.

Strengths
1) Clear problem motivation and sensible modeling choice: Modeling ambiguity at the mention level complements both entity embeddings and word multi-sense embeddings. Unlike classic word-sense induction (Reisinger & Mooney; Huang et al.; Neelakantan et al.), this work targets the specific ambiguity structure of surface forms in EL, where multiple entities share a surface and distributional signals from both context words and KB anchors are informative. This framing is appropriate and practically impactful for EL and related tasks.

2) Joint use of text and KB supervision: Integrating context words and KB-derived entity signals in a unified objective follows a successful line (Wang et al., Toutanova et al., Wang & Li) but focusing the representation on mention senses is novel in emphasis. If the training leverages hyperlinks/anchors to tie mentions to candidate entities, the model can exploit typed co-occurrence patterns that are often missed by purely textual multi-sense models, improving sense separability.

3) Empirical gains on EL: Reporting state-of-the-art on a standard benchmark is significant for ACL. If the comparison includes strong graph-based and embedding-based systems (e.g., Hoffart et al., Pershina et al., Yamada et al.), this demonstrates the practical relevance beyond qualitative nearest-neighbor analyses. The tie to a scalable candidate detection pipeline (Aho–Corasick/double-array trie) underscores applicability.

4) Efficiency and scalability: The use of trie-based mention detection and a lightweight LM-style sense selection is attractive for large-scale deployment. Many EL systems trade accuracy for heavy inference (collective global inference, graph optimization); a fast local disambiguation that remains competitive is valuable.

5) Analysis across representation types: The paper claims qualitative and quantitative analyses for words, entities, and mention senses. Triangulating these gives a richer picture (e.g., nearest neighbors show whether mention senses sit between word context and entity semantics), which is informative for the community exploring joint embedding spaces.

Weaknesses
1) Limited novelty relative to prior multi-sense and joint text-KB embedding: Multi-prototype representations (Reisinger & Mooney; Huang et al.; Neelakantan et al.; Tian et al.) and joint text-KB embeddings (Wang et al.; Toutanova et al.; Wang & Li; Weston et al.) are well-studied. The central new element is placing multi-prototype on mention strings instead of words or entities. It is not fully clear conceptually how this differs from learning entity embeddings plus a learned mapping from context to entity space. Without deeper theoretical or methodological distinction (e.g., a principled objective tying mention senses to a calibrated mixture over entity embeddings), the novelty may be incremental.

2) Methodological clarity is insufficient: The abstract and references suggest a skip-gram-style objective, but key details are missing to assess soundness:
- How many senses per mention? Fixed K, frequency-dependent, or non-parametric (as in Neelakantan et al.)?
- How are mention senses initialized and updated? Is there hard EM-style assignment of contexts to senses during training, or a soft mixture?
- How are entity signals incorporated (explicit alignment to anchors, negative sampling over candidates, weighting between word and entity terms)?
- What exactly is the “language model based” disambiguation? N-gram LM over contexts, or a neural LM scoring sense-context compatibility? How are prototypes used at inference time?
- How is candidate generation performed and filtered before disambiguation?

3) Evaluation incompleteness and baselines: Claiming SOTA requires careful comparison. It is not clear which datasets and metrics are used (AIDA-CoNLL, TAC KBP, WNED/Gerbil?), whether prior strong embedding-based (Yamada et al. 2016/2017; Huang et al. 2015) and graph-based (Hoffart; Pershina; Alhelbawy) baselines are included, and whether candidate generation is standardized across systems. Without standardized candidate sets and the same KB, improvements may reflect better mention detection rather than better disambiguation. NDCG is cited; EL typically reports micro accuracy on mentions with gold entities—please clarify.

4) Ablation and analysis gaps: The core claims hinge on the value of multi-prototype mention embeddings and the LM disambiguator. Missing ablations would weaken the case:
- Single-prototype mention embeddings vs multi-prototype.
- Without KB signals vs with KB signals.
- Using only entity embeddings with a context-to-entity scoring vs using mention-sense intermediates.
- Varying number of senses K and analyzing frequency vs performance.
- Local vs collective inference comparisons, and robustness on NIL/“unlinkable” cases.

5) Writing and reference quality: The references list contains numerous duplications, typos, and malformed entries (e.g., repeated Aho–Corasick, broken author/year fields). This detracts from the paper’s polish and hinders verifying related work coverage. It also raises concerns about careful positioning relative to closely related works on word/sense/entity joint embeddings (e.g., Chen et al. 2014; Mancini et al. 2016).

Questions to authors
- How is the number of senses per mention determined? If fixed, what value is used and how sensitive is performance? If adaptive, what criterion controls new-sense creation?
- Describe the training objective precisely. How are word-context and entity components combined and weighted? What negative sampling or noise-contrastive scheme is used?
- How are training signals for entities obtained (Wikipedia anchors only, or additional KB triples)? Do you backpropagate from entity nodes to mention senses directly, or via co-occurrence in anchored contexts?
- What is the exact disambiguation algorithm at test time? Provide the scoring function, features used, and computational complexity. How do you handle unseen mentions or mentions with senses unseen in training?
- Which EL datasets, KB versions, and metrics are used? Are candidate generators and priors (e.g., P(entity|mention) from anchors) standardized across baselines?
- Provide ablations isolating: multi- vs single-prototype; with vs without KB signals; with vs without the LM step; and a direct context-to-entity model without mention-sense intermediates.
- How do results vary with mention frequency and ambiguity (e.g., number of candidate entities)? Please include an error analysis on frequent ambiguous mentions (e.g., “Washington”).
- Are the learned mention-sense embeddings closer to their predominant entities or to context word centroids? A quantitative alignment analysis would clarify what the model captures.

Overall, the paper tackles a relevant problem with a promising representation and reports strong results. With tighter methodological exposition, stronger baselines, and thorough ablations, it could make a solid ACL contribution.
