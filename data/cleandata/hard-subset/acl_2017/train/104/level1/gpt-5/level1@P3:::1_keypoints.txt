- Proposal: Multi-Prototype Mention Embedding model that learns multiple sense-specific embeddings per mention by jointly leveraging textual context and KB supervision, plus an LM-style disambiguation at test time.  
- Contribution 1: Mention-centric multi-prototype framework integrating text and KB signals to produce sense-specific vectors tied to surface forms.  
- Contribution 2: Efficient LM-style disambiguation method to select a mention sense from learned prototypes.  
- Contribution 3: Empirical claim of state-of-the-art results on an entity linking benchmark showing benefits of multi-prototype mention representations.

- Strength 1: Clear motivation and sensible modeling choice targeting mention-level ambiguity that complements entity and word-sense embeddings.  
- Strength 2: Joint use of textual context and KB anchor supervision can improve sense separability beyond purely textual multi-sense models.  
- Strength 3: Reports SOTA on a standard EL benchmark, demonstrating practical relevance if compared to strong baselines.  
- Strength 4: Efficient, scalable pipeline (trie-based mention detection and lightweight local disambiguation) attractive for large-scale deployment.  
- Strength 5: Qualitative and quantitative analyses across words, entities, and mention senses provide useful triangulation of learned representations.

- Weakness 1: Novelty may be incremental given prior multi-prototype and joint text-KB embedding work; distinction from mapping context to entity space is unclear.  
- Weakness 2: Methodological details are insufficient (number of senses, initialization/assignment, training procedure, exact LM disambiguation, candidate generation).  
- Weakness 3: Evaluation details and baselines are unclear—dataset, metrics, KB version, and whether comparisons and candidate sets are standardized.  
- Weakness 4: Missing key ablations (single vs multi-prototype, with/without KB, varying K, local vs collective inference, NIL handling) weaken causal claims.  
- Weakness 5: Writing and references need cleanup—duplicates, typos, and malformed entries hinder verification and positioning.

- Question 1: How is the number of senses per mention determined (fixed K, frequency-dependent, or adaptive) and how sensitive is performance?  
- Question 2: Precisely state the training objective and how word-context and entity components are combined, including negative sampling details.  
- Question 3: How are entity training signals obtained (Wikipedia anchors only or additional KB triples) and how are gradients propagated to mention senses?  
- Question 4: What is the exact test-time disambiguation algorithm, its scoring function, features, and computational complexity; how are unseen mentions/senses handled?  
- Question 5: Which EL datasets, KB versions, candidate generators, and metrics are used, and are priors standardized across baselines?  
- Question 6: Provide ablations isolating multi- vs single-prototype, with/without KB signals, with/without the LM step, and a direct context-to-entity model.  
- Question 7: How do results vary with mention frequency and ambiguity; include error analysis on high-ambiguity mentions (e.g., “Washington”)?  
- Question 8: Are mention-sense embeddings closer to predominant entities or to context word centroids—provide quantitative alignment analysis.

- Overall: Promising and relevant approach with strong empirical claims, but needs clearer methodology, stronger/standardized baselines, and thorough ablations to be a solid contribution.
