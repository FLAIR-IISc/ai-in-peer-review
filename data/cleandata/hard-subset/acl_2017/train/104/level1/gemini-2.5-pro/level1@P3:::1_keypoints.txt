- Proposes Multi-Prototype Mention Embedding: learns multiple vector prototypes per ambiguous mention by jointly embedding text and KBs, plus an LM-based disambiguation step and SOTA entity linking results.

- Contribution: introduces a joint learning framework that models mention ambiguity with multiple prototypes grounded in KB entities.

- Contribution: presents a practical two-stage pipeline—offline multi-prototype learning, then efficient online contextual disambiguation.

- Contribution: reports state-of-the-art empirical performance on a standard entity linking benchmark.

- Strength: novel synthesis of multi-prototype representation learning and joint text–KB embedding, grounding prototypes in KB entities for interpretability.

- Strength: methodologically sound joint training using both unstructured text and structured KB signals, with sensible separation of learning and disambiguation for efficiency.

- Strength: comprehensive evaluation combining intrinsic analyses and extrinsic entity linking results, convincingly demonstrating utility if claims hold.

- Weakness: vague description of the objective and learning process—unclear how text and KB losses are combined and how prototypes are correctly updated from context.

- Weakness: scalability and training efficiency concerns—no details on prototype initialization, prototype count per mention, training time, or memory footprint.

- Weakness: unclear disambiguation model—“language model based” is underspecified (type of LM, integration with prototypes, role of context).

- Question: provide a detailed formulation of the joint objective and mechanism for selecting/updating the correct mention prototype during training.

- Question: how are prototype sets determined per mention—predefined from KB candidates or learned/hyperparameterized?

- Question: elaborate the language-model-based disambiguation—its exact formulation and how it scores candidate prototypes given context.

- Question: report computational complexity, training time, and model size comparisons versus baseline joint-embedding methods.
