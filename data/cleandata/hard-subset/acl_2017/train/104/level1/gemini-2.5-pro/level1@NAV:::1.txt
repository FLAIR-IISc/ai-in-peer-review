**Summary of the Paper**
This paper addresses the problem of mention ambiguity in joint text and knowledge base embedding models. The authors propose a "Multi-Prototype Mention Embedding" model that learns multiple sense-specific vector representations for each entity mention. This is achieved by jointly training on textual context and entities from a knowledge base. The paper also introduces a language model-based method to disambiguate a mention to its correct sense in a given context. The authors evaluate their approach on an entity linking task, claiming to achieve state-of-the-art performance on a benchmark dataset.

The main contributions of this work are:
1.  A novel method for learning multi-prototype embeddings for entity mentions by jointly leveraging information from unstructured text corpora and a structured knowledge base.
2.  An empirical demonstration of the effectiveness of these embeddings, showing state-of-the-art results on a downstream entity linking task.

**Strengths**
1.  The paper tackles a significant and well-known problem. Mention ambiguity is a critical bottleneck in many NLP tasks, including entity linking and relation extraction. A robust method for learning and disambiguating mention senses is of high value to the community.
2.  The proposed approach is intuitive and technically sound. Using a knowledge base to help ground the different senses (prototypes) of an ambiguous mention is a logical extension of prior work on multi-prototype word embeddings (e.g., Neelakantan et al., 2014), adapting it to the knowledge-aware setting.
3.  The claimed state-of-the-art result on a standard entity linking benchmark is a strong argument for acceptance. This provides concrete evidence that the proposed embeddings and disambiguation method are not just theoretically interesting but also practically effective.

**Weaknesses**
1.  The novelty of the contribution needs to be better situated with respect to prior work. The idea of multi-prototype embeddings is not new, nor is the joint embedding of text and knowledge graphs. The paper must clarify how the proposed joint modeling objective is a non-trivial combination of these two lines of research.
2.  The abstract lacks crucial details about the model architecture and disambiguation method. Phrases like "jointly modeling" and "language model based approach" are too vague. A more precise description of the objective function and the disambiguation mechanism is needed to properly assess the technical contribution.
3.  The manuscript quality is concerning, as evidenced by the bibliography. The reference list is plagued with numerous duplications, typos, and severe formatting errors. This lack of attention to detail raises concerns about the care taken in the experimental setup and analysis.

**Questions to Authors**
1.  Could you please elaborate on the specific architectural novelty of your model? How does your joint objective function for learning multi-prototype embeddings differ from simply applying existing multi-prototype methods (e.g., Neelakantan et al., 2014) to the output of a standard text+KB embedding model (e.g., Han et al., 2016)?
2.  The bibliography requires significant correction. Can you confirm this will be addressed in the final version?
