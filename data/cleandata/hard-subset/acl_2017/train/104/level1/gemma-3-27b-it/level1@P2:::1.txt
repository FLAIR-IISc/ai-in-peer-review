This paper proposes a Multi-Prototype Mention Embedding model to address ambiguity in a unified text and knowledge semantic space. The core idea is to learn multiple sense embeddings for each mention by jointly modeling textual contexts and entities from a knowledge base, and then using a language model to disambiguate mentions to specific senses. The model is evaluated on the entity linking task, achieving state-of-the-art results.

**Contributions:**

1. **Multi-Prototype Mention Embedding:** The central contribution is the proposed method for learning multiple embeddings per mention. While the idea of multiple prototypes isn’t entirely novel (see related work), the joint modeling of textual context *and* knowledge base entities to create these prototypes is a reasonable and potentially effective approach.
2. **Disambiguation Language Model:** The language model-based disambiguation component, designed to select the appropriate sense embedding for a given mention, represents a practical mechanism for utilizing the multi-prototype embeddings.
3. **Strong Empirical Results:** Achieving state-of-the-art results on a standard entity linking benchmark is a significant accomplishment and suggests the practical effectiveness of the proposed method.

**Strengths:**

1. **Clear Problem Statement & Motivation:** The paper clearly articulates the problem of ambiguity in integrated semantic spaces and provides a compelling motivation for addressing it. The issue of a single embedding failing to represent multiple senses of a mention is well explained.
2. **Solid Experimental Evaluation:** The authors evaluate their method on a standard benchmark dataset (entity linking) and demonstrate competitive performance, surpassing existing state-of-the-art systems. The quantitative results provide strong evidence for the efficacy of the proposed approach.
3. **Well-Structured Presentation:** The paper is generally well-written and organized. The technical details of the model and the experimental setup are clearly described, making it relatively easy to understand the proposed approach.
4. **Relevant Related Work:** The authors provide a comprehensive overview of related work in areas such as word sense disambiguation, entity linking, and joint representation learning. This contextualizes the contribution and demonstrates awareness of the existing literature.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Novelty of Multi-Prototype Embeddings:** The concept of multi-prototype word/mention embeddings has been explored in prior work (e.g., Huang et al., 2012; Reisinger and Mooney, 2010; Tian et al., 2014). The paper doesn't sufficiently highlight the *specific* innovations within the multi-prototype approach—what distinguishes it from existing methods beyond the joint modeling with knowledge base entities? A more detailed comparison to these related works is needed.
2. **Language Model Details:** The description of the language model used for disambiguation is somewhat brief. More detail regarding its architecture, training procedure, and specific features would be helpful. What are the advantages of using *this* particular language model?
3. **Ablation Studies Missing:** While the results are strong, the paper lacks ablation studies to determine the individual contribution of each component of the proposed model. How much does the joint modeling with knowledge base entities contribute compared to the multi-prototype embeddings alone? How crucial is the language model for disambiguation? Such studies would provide a more nuanced understanding of the model’s behavior.
4. **Reference List Issues:** The reference list is riddled with formatting errors and repetitions. This detracts from the professionalism of the submission and makes it difficult to verify the related work claims.

**Questions to Authors:**

1.  Could you elaborate on the specific differences between your multi-prototype embedding approach and existing methods (e.g., Huang et al., 2012; Reisinger and Mooney, 2010; Tian et al., 2014)? What novel aspects are introduced beyond the joint modeling with knowledge base entities?
2.  Please provide more details about the language model used for disambiguation. What is its architecture, training data, and specific features?
3.  Could you conduct ablation studies to quantify the contributions of each component (multi-prototype embeddings, joint modeling with KB, disambiguation LM) to the overall performance?
