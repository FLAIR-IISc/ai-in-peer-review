This paper proposes a Multi-Prototype Mention Embedding model to address the ambiguity of entity mentions when integrating text and knowledge. The core idea is to learn multiple sense embeddings for each mention, leveraging both textual contexts and information from a knowledge base, and then employing a language model for disambiguation. The authors demonstrate the effectiveness of their approach through entity linking experiments, achieving state-of-the-art results on a benchmark dataset.

The primary contribution is the proposed multi-prototype mention embedding approach. While multi-prototype embeddings are not entirely novel (as evidenced by the references – Huang et al., 2012; Reisinger and Mooney, 2010; Tian et al., 2014), the application to jointly model textual contexts *and* knowledge base entities for mention disambiguation is a significant refinement. A second contribution lies in the language model-based disambiguation method, though its specific novelty isn’t fully elucidated – a clearer comparison to existing disambiguation techniques would be beneficial. Finally, the strong performance on the entity linking task, achieving state-of-the-art results, serves as a practical demonstration of the model’s efficacy.

**Strengths:**

1. **Strong Empirical Results:** The authors convincingly demonstrate the effectiveness of their model through state-of-the-art results on a standard entity linking benchmark. This is a strong indicator of practical utility.
2. **Joint Modeling:**  The joint consideration of textual context and knowledge base entities within the embedding process is a well-motivated approach to tackle the inherent ambiguity of mentions, and represents a clear improvement over methods that treat these sources in isolation.
3. **Clear Presentation:** The paper is generally well-written and clearly articulates the motivation, proposed method, and experimental setup. The structure facilitates understanding of the technical details.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Novelty Discussion:** The paper doesn’t sufficiently differentiate its multi-prototype approach from prior work in multi-sense embeddings. A more thorough discussion of existing methods and a clear justification of the proposed model’s advantages are needed.
2. **Disambiguation Method Detail:** The language model used for disambiguation is described at a high level. More specifics about its architecture, training procedure, and comparison to alternative disambiguation methods would strengthen the contribution.
3. **Reference List Issues:** The reference list is extremely messy and contains numerous repetitions and formatting inconsistencies. This detracts from the professionalism of the submission.



**Questions to Authors:**

1. Could you provide a more detailed comparison of your language model-based disambiguation method with existing state-of-the-art disambiguation techniques (e.g., those based on graph ranking or deep learning)?
2. How does your approach specifically address the “cold start” problem for entities not well-represented in the knowledge base?
3. Can you elaborate on the computational complexity of training and applying the multi-prototype embeddings, particularly compared to single-embedding approaches?
