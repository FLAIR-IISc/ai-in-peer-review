## Review for ACL 2017 Submission

This paper proposes a Multi-Prototype Mention Embedding model to address the ambiguity of entity mentions by learning multiple sense embeddings for each mention, leveraging both textual contexts and knowledge base entities. An efficient language model is then used for mention disambiguation. The model is evaluated on the entity linking task, achieving state-of-the-art results.

**Contributions:**

1. **Multi-Prototype Mention Embeddings:** The core contribution lies in the idea of representing ambiguous mentions with multiple embeddings, each capturing a different sense derived from textual and knowledge-based contexts. This is a reasonable approach to tackle the inherent ambiguity in natural language.
2. **Joint Modeling of Text and Knowledge:** The authors successfully integrate information from both textual contexts and a knowledge base to generate these embeddings, allowing for a more informed representation of mention senses.
3. **State-of-the-Art Results on Entity Linking:** The application of the proposed model to entity linking demonstrates its effectiveness and practical utility, achieving improvements over existing methods on a benchmark dataset.

**Strengths:**

1. **Clear Problem Definition & Motivation:** The paper clearly articulates the problem of mention ambiguity and its impact on downstream tasks like entity linking. The motivation for exploring multi-prototype embeddings is well-justified.
2. **Solid Experimental Evaluation:** The authors demonstrate the effectiveness of their model through a thorough experimental evaluation on a standard entity linking dataset. The achieved state-of-the-art results provide strong evidence of the model's performance.
3. **Integration of Knowledge and Text:** Successfully combining textual context and knowledge base information to learn more robust mention representations is a significant strength. This addresses a crucial challenge in semantic understanding.
4. **Efficient Disambiguation Method:** The proposed language model-based disambiguation approach appears to be efficient and effective in selecting the appropriate sense embedding for a given mention in context.
5. **Well-Related Work:** The paper provides a comprehensive overview of related work in areas like word sense disambiguation, entity linking, and joint representation learning.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Novelty in Core Idea:** While the implementation and integration are well-executed, the core idea of using multiple embeddings per mention is not entirely novel. Prior work (e.g., Huang et al., 2012; Reisinger and Mooney, 2010; Neelakantan et al., 2014) has already explored similar concepts. The paper doesn't sufficiently emphasize the specific innovations beyond simply applying this idea to the combined textual and knowledge base setting.
2. **Lack of Ablation Study:** The paper lacks a detailed ablation study to dissect the contribution of each component of the proposed model. For example, it's unclear how much each of the textual context, knowledge base entities, and the language model contribute to the overall performance. This makes it difficult to assess the true value of each component.
3. **Dataset Specificity:** The results are reported on a single benchmark dataset. Demonstrating performance on a more diverse set of datasets (e.g., different domains, knowledge bases) would strengthen the generalizability claims.
4. **Computational Complexity:** The paper does not discuss the computational cost of the proposed model, particularly regarding the efficient language model and the learning of multiple prototypes. This information is important for assessing its practicality for large-scale applications.



**Questions to Authors:**

1. How does the proposed model compare to existing multi-prototype embedding methods (e.g., Huang et al., 2012; Neelakantan et al., 2014) when applied to the same entity linking task? Could you provide a direct comparison?
2. Can you provide a more detailed breakdown of the computational cost associated with training and inference of the model? Specifically, how does the number of prototypes affect performance and computational demands?
3.  What are the limitations of the knowledge base used in your experiments? How would the model perform with different or less comprehensive knowledge bases?
4. Could you elaborate on the details of the language model used for disambiguation? Is it a pre-trained model, or is it trained jointly with the embedding learning process?

**Additional Comments:**

* The references section contains multiple repeated entries and formatting inconsistencies. Please ensure a clean and standardized reference list.
* The abstract is well-written and concise.
* The paper is generally well-organized and easy to follow.
