This paper addresses the long-standing problem of word sense disambiguation (WSD) in the context of entity linking, arguing that ambiguity arises because the same mention can refer to multiple entities in a knowledge base. The authors propose a Multi-Prototype Mention Embedding model, learning multiple embeddings per mention based on textual context and knowledge base entities, coupled with a language model for disambiguation.

The primary contribution of this work is a practical and effective method for generating and utilizing multi-prototype mention embeddings for improved entity linking performance. While the core idea of multiple embeddings isn't entirely novel (see related work), the specific joint modeling of textual context and knowledge base entities within a single framework, and the subsequent language model-based disambiguation, represent a solid engineering achievement. A secondary contribution is the demonstration of state-of-the-art results on a standard entity linking benchmark, providing empirical validation of the proposed approach. Finally, the thorough experimental analysis, including both quantitative and qualitative results, strengthens the credibility of the findings.

**Strengths:**

1. **Strong Empirical Results:** The paper achieves state-of-the-art performance on a standard entity linking dataset. This is a compelling argument for acceptance, as it demonstrates the practical effectiveness of the proposed approach. The results are clearly presented and compared to existing methods.
2. **Well-Motivated Problem:** The problem of mention ambiguity in entity linking is clearly articulated and well-motivated. The authors correctly identify this as a key challenge hindering accurate knowledge integration.
3. **Technically Sound Approach:** The proposed Multi-Prototype Mention Embedding model is technically sound and logically presented. The joint modeling of textual and knowledge-based information is a sensible strategy for capturing the nuanced meaning of mentions. The language model component further refines the disambiguation process.
4. **Comprehensive Evaluation:** The authors provide a comprehensive evaluation, including both quantitative metrics (accuracy, F1-score) and qualitative analysis (examples of correctly and incorrectly disambiguated mentions). This holistic assessment builds confidence in the reliability of the results.
5. **Clear Presentation:** The paper is generally well-written and easy to understand. The technical details are explained clearly, and the figures and tables are informative.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Novelty in Core Idea:** While the implementation is strong, the core idea of using multiple embeddings for a single mention isn’t groundbreaking. Several prior works (Huang et al., 2012; Neelakantan et al., 2014; Tian et al., 2014) have explored similar concepts. The paper needs to more clearly delineate how its approach differs and improves upon these existing methods beyond simply the joint modeling aspect. Is the joint modeling significantly more effective than other approaches to learning multiple embeddings?
2. **Language Model Details are Sparse:** The paper mentions using a language model for disambiguation, but the details of this language model are somewhat vague. What type of language model is used (e.g., LSTM, Transformer)? How is it trained? What features does it utilize? Providing more specifics about the language model would strengthen the paper.
3. **Knowledge Base Specificity:** The experiments are performed using a single knowledge base. It is unclear how well the proposed approach would generalize to other knowledge bases with different structures or levels of granularity.  An analysis of the influence of knowledge base characteristics on performance would be valuable.
4. **Computational Cost:**  The use of multiple embeddings inherently increases computational complexity. The paper doesn’t discuss the computational cost of the proposed approach compared to other entity linking methods. A discussion of efficiency and scalability would be beneficial.



**Questions to Authors:**

1.  Can you provide a more detailed comparison of your joint modeling approach to previous work on multi-prototype embeddings (e.g., Huang et al., 2012; Neelakantan et al., 2014)? What specific architectural or training differences lead to the improved performance observed in your experiments?
2.  Please provide more details about the language model used for disambiguation, including its architecture, training procedure, and input features.
3.  Have you investigated the performance of your approach on knowledge bases other than the one used in your experiments? If so, what were the results? If not, what are the potential challenges of applying your approach to different knowledge bases?
4.  What is the computational overhead associated with using multi-prototype embeddings compared to traditional single-embedding approaches? How does this affect the scalability of your method?
