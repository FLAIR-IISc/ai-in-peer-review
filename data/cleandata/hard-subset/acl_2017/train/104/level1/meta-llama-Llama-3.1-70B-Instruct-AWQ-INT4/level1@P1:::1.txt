**Summary of the Paper**

This paper proposes a novel Multi-Prototype Mention Embedding model that integrates text and knowledge into a unified semantic space. The model learns multiple sense embeddings for each mention by jointly modeling words from textual contexts and entities derived from a knowledge base. The authors also design an efficient language model-based approach to disambiguate each mention to a specific sense. The proposed model is evaluated on the task of entity linking, and the results demonstrate the high quality of the word, entity, and multi-prototype mention embeddings.

**Main Contributions**

1. **Multi-Prototype Mention Embedding model**: The authors propose a novel model that learns multiple sense embeddings for each mention by jointly modeling words from textual contexts and entities derived from a knowledge base. This contribution is significant as it addresses the challenge of ambiguity in the common space.
2. **Efficient language model-based approach for disambiguation**: The authors design an efficient approach to disambiguate each mention to a specific sense, which is a crucial step in entity linking.
3. **State-of-the-art results on entity linking**: The proposed model achieves state-of-the-art results on the benchmark dataset, demonstrating its effectiveness in capturing the nuances of entity mentions.

**Strengths**

1. **Novelty of the approach**: The Multi-Prototype Mention Embedding model is a novel approach that addresses the challenge of ambiguity in the common space. The authors' use of joint modeling of words from textual contexts and entities derived from a knowledge base is a significant contribution.
2. **Effectiveness of the model**: The proposed model achieves state-of-the-art results on the benchmark dataset, demonstrating its effectiveness in capturing the nuances of entity mentions.
3. **Clarity of the presentation**: The authors present their work clearly, with well-organized sections and concise explanations of their approach.

**Weaknesses**

1. **Limited evaluation**: The authors only evaluate their model on the task of entity linking, which may not be sufficient to demonstrate its generalizability to other tasks.
2. **Lack of comparison to other disambiguation approaches**: The authors do not compare their language model-based approach to other disambiguation approaches, which makes it difficult to assess its relative effectiveness.
3. **Overemphasis on technical details**: The authors focus heavily on the technical details of their approach, which may make it difficult for non-experts to understand the broader implications of their work.

**Questions to Authors**

1. Can you provide more details on how you selected the hyperparameters for your model?
2. How do you plan to extend your approach to other tasks beyond entity linking?
3. Can you provide more insights into why your language model-based approach is more effective than other disambiguation approaches?
