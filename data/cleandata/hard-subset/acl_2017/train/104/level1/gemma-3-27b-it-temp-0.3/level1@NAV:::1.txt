This paper proposes a Multi-Prototype Mention Embedding model to address the ambiguity of entity mentions when integrating text and knowledge. The core idea is to learn multiple sense embeddings for each mention by combining textual contexts and entities from a knowledge base, followed by a language model-based disambiguation approach. The authors demonstrate the effectiveness of their method through entity linking experiments, achieving state-of-the-art results on a benchmark dataset.

The primary contribution of this work, as I see it, is the practical demonstration of a combined approach to mention disambiguation that leverages both contextualized embeddings *and* knowledge base information. While the individual components (multi-prototype embeddings, language model disambiguation) aren’t entirely novel, their integration and application to entity linking yield strong empirical results. A secondary contribution is the publicly available dataset and implementation, which will likely be useful for future research in this area. Finally, the thorough evaluation, including both quantitative and qualitative analysis, strengthens the validity of the findings.

**Strengths:**

1. **Strong Empirical Results:** The paper reports state-of-the-art performance on a standard entity linking benchmark. This is a significant achievement and provides strong evidence for the effectiveness of the proposed approach.
2. **Practical Integration:** The method effectively combines textual and knowledge-based information, addressing a core challenge in semantic integration. The language model component appears to be a key enabler of this integration.
3. **Comprehensive Evaluation:** The inclusion of both quantitative results (standard metrics) and qualitative analysis (examples of disambiguation) provides a more complete picture of the model's strengths and weaknesses.
4. **Clear Presentation:** The paper is well-written and clearly explains the proposed method and experimental setup.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Novelty:** The individual components of the model (multi-prototype embeddings, language models) are not entirely new. The novelty lies in the specific combination and application, but a more detailed comparison to existing approaches that combine similar elements is needed.
2. **Dataset Specificity:** The reported improvements are on a single benchmark dataset. It would be beneficial to evaluate the model on a wider range of datasets to assess its generalizability.
3. **Computational Cost:** The paper doesn't discuss the computational cost of training and inference. Multi-prototype embeddings and language models can be computationally expensive, and this should be addressed.



**Questions to Authors:**

1. Could you provide a more detailed comparison to existing methods that also combine contextual embeddings and knowledge base information for mention disambiguation? Specifically, how does your approach differ in terms of architecture, training procedure, and performance?
2. What is the computational overhead associated with training and using the multi-prototype embeddings and language model compared to simpler baselines?
3. How sensitive is the performance of your model to the quality and completeness of the knowledge base used?
