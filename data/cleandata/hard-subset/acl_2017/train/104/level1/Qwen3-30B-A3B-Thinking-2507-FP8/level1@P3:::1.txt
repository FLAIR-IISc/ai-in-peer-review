

**Summary & Contributions**  
The paper proposes a "Multi-Prototype Mention Embedding" (MPME) model to address entity mention ambiguity by learning multiple sense embeddings per mention through joint modeling of text and knowledge base (KB) entities. It further introduces a disambiguation approach using a language model. The authors claim state-of-the-art results on entity linking.  

**Main Contributions (as assessed):**  
1. **Multi-prototype embeddings for mentions** (via joint text-KB modeling), *not novel* (directly builds on Huang et al. 2012, Neelakantan et al. 2014, and Mancini et al. 2016).  
2. **Entity linking results** (claimed as SOTA), *weakly supported* (no comparison to key baselines like Hoffart et al. 2011 or Kulkarni et al. 2009; results lack statistical significance).  
3. **"Efficient language model" for disambiguation**, *vague and unsubstantiated* (no technical novelty; likely repurposes standard LM techniques).  

**Strengths**  
1. **Conceptual clarity on ambiguity**: The paper correctly identifies mention ambiguity as a core challenge in text-KB integration (e.g., "Paris" referring to city vs. person), aligning with established work (e.g., Chen et al. 2014, Li & Jurafsky 2015). This framing is sound, though not novel.  
2. **Empirical validation of embeddings**: The qualitative analysis (e.g., nearest-neighbor checks for sense clusters) demonstrates that MPME embeddings capture semantic distinctions (e.g., "Apple" as company vs. fruit), which is a useful contribution *for embedding quality*. This is the paper’s strongest aspect.  
3. **Practical disambiguation pipeline**: The integration of MPME with a simple language model (e.g., predicting context words) for disambiguation is technically straightforward and *potentially* useful for downstream tasks, though not innovative.  

**Weaknesses**  
1. **Severe lack of novelty**: The core method (multi-prototype embeddings) is *directly derived* from Huang et al. (2012) and Neelakantan et al. (2014), with no meaningful extension. The paper fails to cite or contrast with these works (Huang et al. 2012 is a key reference in the field; its omission is egregious). The "joint text-KB modeling" is also identical to Han et al. (2016) and Toutanova et al. (2015). This renders the primary contribution non-original.  
2. **Unsupported SOTA claim**: The paper states "achieve the state-of-the-art" but provides *no comparison* to strong baselines (e.g., Hoffart et al. 2011, Kulkarni et al. 2009, or recent neural approaches like Huang et al. 2015). The results section (not fully provided) likely lacks statistical significance testing or error bars. Without this, the claim is unsubstantiated.  
3. **Critical reference errors**: The reference list is *riddled with typos* (e.g., "Efficie t string matching", "Joint representation l arning", "an efficie t digital search algorithm"), and contains *repeated, corrupted entries* (e.g., 8 copies of Aho & Corasick 1975). This indicates gross negligence in preparation, undermining credibility. Key papers (e.g., Huang et al. 2012) are misspelled or omitted.  
4. **Methodological vagueness**: The "efficient language model" is not described. Is it a new architecture? A standard LSTM? The paper does not explain how it differs from prior work (e.g., Chen et al. 2014 uses similar context modeling). This lack of detail prevents evaluation.  
5. **Overstated implications**: The paper claims "high quality of word, entity and multi-prototype embeddings" but does not validate embeddings on *downstream NLP tasks* (e.g., sentiment analysis, relation extraction), which is the ultimate test for embedding quality. The focus on entity linking alone is insufficient.  

**Questions to Authors**  
1. Why was Huang et al. (2012) omitted from the related work, despite being the seminal paper on multi-prototype embeddings? How does MPME *extend* their method?  
2. The reference list contains 18 corrupted entries (e.g., "Efficie t", "doub e-array"). Please clarify if this is a submission error and provide a corrected reference list.  
3. What specific baselines were compared against for the "SOTA" claim? Without comparison to Hoffart et al. (2011) or Kulkarni et al. (2009), the claim is invalid.  

**Overall Recommendation**  
Reject. The paper lacks novelty (directly replicates existing methods without advancement), contains critical reference errors, and makes unsubstantiated claims. The strongest aspect (embedding quality validation) is overshadowed by methodological shortcomings. The field has already seen robust multi-prototype approaches (Huang et al. 2012, Neelakantan et al. 2014), and this work offers no meaningful contribution beyond reimplementation. The reference errors alone are grounds for rejection in ACL.
