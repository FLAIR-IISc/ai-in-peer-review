

# Review for ACL 2017

## Summary and Contributions

This paper proposes a Multi-Prototype Mention Embedding (MPME) model to address the ambiguity of entity mentions in text. The authors claim to learn multiple sense embeddings for each mention by jointly modeling words from textual contexts and entities from a knowledge base, and design an efficient language model for disambiguation. The main contributions as assessed objectively are:

1. A multi-prototype embedding approach for entity mentions that jointly models textual context and knowledge base entities.
2. Empirical demonstration of improved entity linking performance using the proposed embeddings and disambiguation method.
3. A demonstration of high-quality word, entity, and multi-prototype mention embeddings through qualitative and quantitative analysis.

## Strengths

1. The problem addressed (entity mention ambiguity) is well-motivated and significant in NLP. Entity linking is a fundamental task with practical applications, and ambiguity resolution is a core challenge.

2. The empirical results demonstrate clear improvements over prior work on entity linking benchmarks. The paper reports state-of-the-art results, providing strong evidence that the proposed method is effective for the specific task of entity linking.

3. The joint modeling of textual context and knowledge base entities is a reasonable approach to the problem of entity ambiguity, and the paper provides thorough qualitative analysis of the embeddings, which helps validate the quality of the representations.

## Weaknesses

1. The novelty claim is questionable. The paper builds on existing work on multi-prototype embeddings (Huang et al., 2012; Reisinger & Mooney, 2010; Neelakantan et al., 2014; Tian et al., 2014) but fails to clearly articulate how their approach differs from these prior works or why the entity mention problem requires a fundamentally different solution than the word sense problem. The method appears to be a straightforward extension of existing techniques to entity mentions rather than a novel contribution.

2. The paper lacks adequate comparison with recent entity disambiguation approaches that use similar techniques. For instance, it does not properly compare with Huang et al. (2015) or Hoffart et al. (2011), both of which use knowledge graphs and neural approaches for entity disambiguation. Without such comparison, it's difficult to assess whether the claimed "state-of-the-art" results represent a meaningful advancement.

3. The evaluation is limited to entity linking without demonstrating broader applicability. The paper does not show how the embeddings would benefit other NLP tasks that could leverage multi-prototype representations (e.g., relation extraction, question answering), which would strengthen the case for the method's significance.

4. The paper contains significant reference duplication and formatting errors (e.g., multiple identical entries for Aho & Corasick, repeated misprints in references), suggesting poor quality control in preparation. This undermines the paper's professionalism and attention to detail.

## Questions to Authors

1. How does your multi-prototype mention embedding model differ from prior work on multi-prototype word embeddings (e.g., Huang et al., 2012; Reisinger & Mooney, 2010; Neelakantan et al., 2014; Tian et al., 2014)? Specifically, what novel techniques do you employ to handle the unique challenges of entity mentions compared to word senses?

2. The paper claims to achieve state-of-the-art results on entity linking. How does your method compare to recent entity disambiguation approaches that also use knowledge bases and language models (e.g., Huang et al., 2015; Hoffart et al., 2011)? What specific advantages does your approach offer over these methods?

3. The paper demonstrates improved entity linking performance but does not show how the embeddings benefit other NLP tasks. Could you provide examples of how these embeddings would improve performance on other tasks such as relation extraction or question answering?
