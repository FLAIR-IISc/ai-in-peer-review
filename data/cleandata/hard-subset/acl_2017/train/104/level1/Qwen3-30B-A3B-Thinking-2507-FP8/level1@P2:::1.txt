

# Review for ACL 2017

## Summary and Main Contributions

This paper proposes a Multi-Prototype Mention Embedding model that learns multiple sense embeddings for entity mentions by jointly modeling textual contexts and knowledge base entities. The authors also introduce an efficient language model-based approach for disambiguating mentions to specific senses. The paper claims to achieve state-of-the-art results on entity linking using their method.

As I see them, the main contributions of this work are:

1. Application of multi-prototype embedding techniques to entity mentions rather than words, specifically designed for the ambiguity challenge in entity linking.
2. Joint modeling of textual context and knowledge base entities to create more semantically rich embeddings for mentions.

## Strengths

1. **Addresses a meaningful problem**: Entity ambiguity is a well-known challenge in NLP, particularly in entity linking and knowledge base population. The paper correctly identifies that the same mention phrase can refer to multiple entities, which is a significant obstacle for many NLP applications. This is a relevant and important problem that warrants attention.

2. **Practical validation**: The paper demonstrates empirical results on a standard entity linking benchmark, showing that their approach achieves state-of-the-art performance. This practical validation is important for establishing the utility of the proposed method in real-world applications.

3. **Joint modeling approach**: The idea of jointly modeling textual context and knowledge base entities is reasonable and potentially effective. By leveraging both sources of information, the model can potentially capture more nuanced semantic relationships than approaches that rely solely on text or knowledge bases.

## Weaknesses

1. **Lack of clear novelty**: The paper fails to adequately distinguish its approach from prior work on multi-prototype embeddings. The authors cite Huang et al. (2012), Neelakantan et al. (2014), and Reisinger and Mooney (2010), but don't clearly explain how their method differs or improves upon these approaches. For instance, Huang et al. (2012) already proposed "multiple word prototypes" for improving word representations, and Neelakantan et al. (2014) developed "nonparametric estimation of multiple embeddings per word." The paper doesn't convincingly argue why their entity-specific approach is sufficiently novel.

2. **Insufficient comparison with state-of-the-art**: The paper claims "state-of-the-art" results but provides minimal details about how their method compares to the most recent approaches. The abstract mentions "benchmark dataset" but doesn't specify which one or provide comparative numbers. Without specific performance metrics and comparisons to recent methods (e.g., those from ACL 2016 or EMNLP 2016), the claim of state-of-the-art results is unsubstantiated and difficult to evaluate.

3. **Poorly proofread references**: The reference list contains numerous typos and inconsistencies (e.g., "Efficie t string matching," "An efficie t digital search algorithm," "Joint representation l arning"). This suggests the paper may not have been carefully prepared, which undermines the perceived quality of the work.

4. **Lack of technical detail**: The paper is vague about the "efficient language model-based approach" for disambiguation. It doesn't explain why this approach is more efficient than standard methods or how it differs from typical language modeling approaches. Without these details, it's impossible to assess the claimed efficiency or novelty of this component.

## Questions to Authors

1. The paper cites Huang et al. (2012) and Neelakantan et al. (2014) as prior work on multi-prototype embeddings. How does your approach to multi-prototype mention embedding specifically differ from these methods, and what are the key innovations that make your method more suitable for entity mentions rather than words?

2. Can you provide specific performance metrics showing how your method compares to the most recent state-of-the-art methods for entity linking (e.g., those published at ACL 2016 or EMNLP 2016)? What is the exact improvement over previous approaches?

3. The paper claims an "efficient language model-based approach" for disambiguation. Please clarify how this approach differs from standard language modeling techniques and why it is specifically more efficient for this task.

## Overall Assessment

This paper addresses a relevant problem in NLP with a potentially useful approach, but it suffers from significant weaknesses in novelty and presentation. The lack of clear differentiation from prior work on multi-prototype embeddings is a major concern, as the paper doesn't convincingly establish what makes their approach novel or superior. The unsubstantiated claim of "state-of-the-art" results and the poorly proofread references further undermine the paper's credibility.

While the joint modeling of textual context and knowledge base entities is a reasonable idea, it doesn't represent a significant advance over existing work. The paper would need to provide a more thorough comparison with prior multi-prototype methods and demonstrate clear improvements over the current state of the art to be considered for publication at ACL.

Given these concerns, I would recommend rejection of this paper in its current form. The authors would need to substantially revise the paper to clearly establish novelty, provide detailed comparisons with recent state-of-the-art methods, and properly proofread the manuscript.
