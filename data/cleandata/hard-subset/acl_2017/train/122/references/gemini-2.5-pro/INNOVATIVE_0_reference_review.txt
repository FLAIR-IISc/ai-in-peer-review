### Summary and Contributions

This paper introduces the Neural Belief Tracker (NBT), a novel framework for dialogue state tracking (DST) that operates directly on pre-trained word embeddings, eliminating the need for hand-crafted semantic dictionaries. The core idea is to move away from delexicalization and exact string matching, instead learning to recognize user intents by modeling semantic similarity in a vector space. The authors propose two model variants, NBT-DNN and NBT-CNN, which encode the user utterance, system acts, and a candidate slot-value pair into vector representations. These vectors interact through dedicated semantic decoding and context modeling components to produce a final binary decision for each candidate pair. Experiments on the DSTC2 and WOZ 2.0 datasets show that NBT matches the performance of strong baseline models that use manually created lexicons and significantly outperforms them when these resources are unavailable.

In my view, the main contributions are:

1.  **A new paradigm for DST:** The primary contribution is the conceptual shift from delexicalization-based DST to a fully data-driven approach that reasons over continuous semantic representations. This is a bold and important step towards building more scalable dialogue systems for complex domains where creating manual lexicons is infeasible.
2.  **An effective neural architecture (NBT):** The paper proposes a concrete and effective architecture that successfully implements this new paradigm. The model's design, which separates semantic decoding from context modeling, is clean and demonstrates strong empirical performance.
3.  **A convincing empirical validation:** The experimental setup, particularly the comparison against baselines with and without semantic dictionaries, provides compelling evidence for the proposed approach's effectiveness and scalability advantages. The analysis of different word vector spaces further strengthens the paper's conclusions.

### Strengths

1.  **High Novelty and Potential Impact:** The paper's core proposal—to replace hand-crafted delexicalization with learned semantic matching via word embeddings—is highly novel in the context of dialogue state tracking. This directly addresses a major bottleneck in scaling dialogue systems to new and more complex domains. This work opens up a promising new research direction that could have a significant impact on how dialogue systems are developed in the future. I value this kind of forward-looking, high-risk, high-reward research.

2.  **Excellent Experimental Design:** The evaluation is thoughtfully designed and very convincing. By testing on two distinct datasets (DSTC2 with ASR noise vs. WOZ 2.0 with richer language), the authors demonstrate the model's robustness in different conditions. Critically, the comparison against baseline models both with and without access to semantic dictionaries perfectly isolates the contribution of the NBT framework. The result that NBT can learn what previously had to be hand-coded is a powerful statement.

3.  **Strong Empirical Results:** The NBT models not only achieve state-of-the-art or competitive results but do so without the crutch of manual resources. The fact that NBT matches the performance of lexicon-augmented systems on DSTC2 and *surpasses* them on the more linguistically varied WOZ 2.0 dataset strongly supports the authors' claims about the model's ability to handle lexical variation.

4.  **Insightful Analysis of Word Representations:** The ablation study in Section 6.2, which compares randomly initialized, GloVe, and semantically-specialized Paragram vectors, is a valuable addition. It provides one of the clearer demonstrations in the literature of a downstream task directly benefiting from semantically-specialized word embeddings. This finding is of interest beyond the immediate scope of dialogue state tracking.

### Weaknesses

While I am very enthusiastic about this paper, there are some aspects that could be improved. However, in my view, these are minor compared to the significance of the core contribution.

1.  **Scalability Concerns Regarding the Candidate Set:** The proposed method requires iterating over every possible slot-value pair defined in the ontology for each turn. This is feasible for the tested domains (which have a small, closed-class ontology) but would be computationally prohibitive for domains with a very large number of possible values (e.g., tracking restaurant names, movie titles, or contact names). The paper does not discuss this limitation or potential solutions, such as candidate generation or retrieval-based methods.

2.  **Simplicity of Architectural Components:** While the overall framework is novel, some of the specific architectural choices are relatively simple. For instance, the NBT-DNN's method of summing n-gram vectors is a coarse way to represent an utterance, and the context modeling relies on simple dot-product gating. While the results show this is effective, the work could be strengthened by exploring or at least discussing more sophisticated mechanisms like attention to model the interactions between utterance, context, and candidate pairs. This is not a major flaw, as the simple model's success is itself a strong result, but it leaves obvious room for improvement.

3.  **Belief Update Mechanism is Ad-Hoc:** The belief update mechanism described in Section 4 is a simple, rule-based linear interpolation with a hand-tuned decay factor (λ). This component feels disconnected from the core neural model. A more elegant and potentially more robust solution would be to integrate this temporal belief update step directly into the model architecture, for example, by using a recurrent structure over dialogue turns.

### Questions to Authors

1.  The model's scalability seems to be its most significant practical limitation. How do the authors envision this approach scaling to domains with very large or open-ended value sets (e.g., tracking a specific song title from a catalogue of millions)? Have you considered combining NBT with a candidate generation step?

2.  The context modeling in Equations 9 and 10 is an interesting gating mechanism. Did you experiment with alternative, more expressive ways to model the three-way interaction between the user utterance, system act, and candidate pair, such as using an attention mechanism?

3.  The belief update mechanism uses a fixed λ tuned on the development set. Do you believe this parameter could be learned, or have you considered replacing this rule-based update with an end-to-end recurrent model across turns to manage the belief state more dynamically?
