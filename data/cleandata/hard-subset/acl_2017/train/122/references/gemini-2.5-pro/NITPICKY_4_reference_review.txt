### Summary of the Paper and Main Contributions

This paper introduces the Neural Belief Tracker (NBT), a framework for dialogue state tracking that operates directly on pre-trained word vector representations. The primary goal is to create a scalable model that couples Spoken Language Understanding (SLU) and Dialogue State Tracking (DST) without relying on hand-crafted semantic dictionaries, a key limitation of previous "delexicalisation"-based approaches. The model processes the user utterance and preceding system act to make a binary decision for each candidate slot-value pair defined in the domain ontology. The authors propose two variants, NBT-DNN and NBT-CNN, and evaluate them on the DSTC2 and WOZ 2.0 datasets.

As I see them, the main contributions are:
1.  A convincing empirical demonstration that a DST model using pre-trained word vectors can match the performance of strong baseline systems that depend on manually curated semantic dictionaries. This provides a clear path toward more scalable DST systems.
2.  A valuable analysis directly linking the semantic quality of the underlying word embeddings (random vs. distributional vs. semantically specialised) to downstream task performance in belief tracking.
3.  The introduction of a clean, delexicalisation-free neural architecture for DST that, while composed of existing components, is effectively applied and serves as a strong new baseline for future work in this area.

### Strengths

1.  **Excellent Motivation and Problem Formulation:** The paper clearly identifies a significant and practical limitation in existing state-of-the-art DST models: their reliance on non-scalable, hand-crafted semantic dictionaries for delexicalisation. The proposed NBT framework is a direct and well-reasoned solution to this problem, aiming to learn lexical variation from data via word embeddings. This is a strong and important research direction.

2.  **Strong and Convincing Experimental Results:** The empirical evaluation is thorough and its main conclusion is well-supported. The NBT models not only significantly outperform baselines when semantic dictionaries are withheld (a fair comparison of data-driven capabilities) but also match their performance when the baselines are given these handcrafted resources (Table 1). This is a powerful result that validates the central hypothesis of the paper.

3.  **Insightful Ablation Study on Word Vectors:** The experiment reported in Table 2, which compares randomly initialized vectors against GloVe and the specialised Paragram-SL999 vectors, is a key strength. It provides direct evidence that the semantic content captured in pre-trained embeddings is crucial for the model's success and that higher-quality semantic representations lead to better DST performance. This moves beyond intrinsic word vector evaluations and demonstrates their impact on a complex downstream task.

4.  **Careful Evaluation on Multiple Datasets:** The use of both DSTC2 (with ASR noise) and WOZ 2.0 (cleaner, more complex language) allows the authors to analyze the model's robustness to recognition errors and its capacity for semantic understanding, respectively. The conclusion that performance gains are larger on WOZ 2.0 correctly identifies that the model's key strength lies in handling linguistic variation.

### Weaknesses

1.  **Numerous and Severe Formatting Issues:** The paper suffers from a startling lack of polish, particularly in the bibliography. Nearly every entry in the References section is malformed, containing artifacts like ".,?", repeated author names (e.g., "Bohus and Rudnicky.,? 2006, Bohus and Rudnicky.,"), extraneous characters ("et al\.,?"), and apparent BibTeX compilation errors ("\Q2015\E"). This level of carelessness in the presentation is unacceptable for a top-tier publication and severely undermines the professionalism of the work. Further, minor but noticeable inconsistencies exist in the main text, such as the use of the `RATING` slot in the Figure 1 example, which is not part of the ontology used in the experiments.

2.  **Methodological Simplicity and Decoupling:** While the overall framework is effective, the belief update mechanism described in Section 4 is a simple, non-learned heuristic. It consists of a weighted average over N-best lists and a linear interpolation with the previous belief state using a tuned hyperparameter `λ`. This decouples the turn-level neural understanding from the cross-turn belief accumulation. This feels like a step back from more integrated approaches, such as the recurrent connections used in Henderson et al. (2014c), which learn the state update dynamics. The simplicity of this component weakens the claim of a fully data-driven model.

3.  **Concerns Regarding Comparability and Reproducibility:** The authors state they have modified both datasets: DSTC2 was manually cleaned of spelling errors, and the WOZ 2.0 dataset was expanded. While improving data quality is good practice, this prevents a direct, apples-to-apples comparison with the large body of previously published results on these standard benchmarks. The paper would be much stronger if it also reported results on the original, unmodified test sets to properly contextualize its performance gains against prior art.

4.  **Unsubstantiated Scalability Claims:** The primary motivation for this work is to build a more scalable DST model. However, the experiments are conducted on a single, relatively small domain (restaurant search) with only three informable slots. While the approach is *conceptually* more scalable than methods requiring manual lexicons, the paper provides no empirical evidence that the model actually scales effectively to domains with significantly larger and more complex ontologies. The claim of scalability therefore remains an assertion rather than a demonstrated property.

### Questions to Authors

1.  The belief update mechanism is a fixed heuristic. Did you experiment with a learned update mechanism, for instance, by using an RNN to model the sequence of turn-level representations and predict the cumulative belief state? If so, how did it perform compared to the linear interpolation approach?
2.  Could you please report the performance of your NBT models on the original, un-cleaned DSTC2 test set? This would be invaluable for comparing your results directly against those reported in Henderson et al. (2014a) and Williams (2014), and for understanding the impact of your cleaning process.
3.  In the context modelling (Section 3.3), `dr` and `dc` are calculated by scaling the entire utterance representation `r` with a scalar similarity score. This seems to imply that if the system asks about `price`, the entire utterance representation is passed through, without focusing on the parts of the utterance relevant to the price. Have you considered more nuanced attention-like mechanisms for context modelling?
