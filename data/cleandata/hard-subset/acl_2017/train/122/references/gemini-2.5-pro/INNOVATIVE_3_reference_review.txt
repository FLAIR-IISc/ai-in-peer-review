### Summary of the Paper
This paper introduces the Neural Belief Tracker (NBT), a novel framework for dialogue state tracking (DST) that moves away from traditional delexicalisation-based methods. Instead of relying on hand-crafted semantic dictionaries to handle lexical variation, the NBT operates directly on pre-trained word embeddings. The model learns to detect user intents by reasoning in a continuous vector space. For each dialogue turn, it processes the user utterance, the preceding system act, and a candidate slot-value pair, producing a binary decision on whether the user has expressed that specific intent. The authors propose two architectural variants, NBT-DNN and NBT-CNN, and evaluate them on two restaurant-domain datasets (DSTC2 and WOZ 2.0). Their results show that the NBT, without any domain-specific lexicons, performs on par with or better than strong baseline systems that depend on such hand-crafted resources.

### Main Contributions
1.  **A New Paradigm for Dialogue State Tracking:** The paper's primary contribution is a significant conceptual shift in DST. By replacing the brittle, labor-intensive process of delexicalisation with a fully data-driven model that operates on word embeddings, the authors propose a more elegant, scalable, and robust solution to handling linguistic variation. This is a bold and important new direction for the field.
2.  **Effective Application of Word Embeddings for SLU/DST:** This work is a pioneering demonstration of how pre-trained word embeddings can be effectively leveraged to replace hand-engineered features for the specific, challenging task of joint Spoken Language Understanding (SLU) and DST. The model successfully learns to recognize paraphrases and semantic similarity (e.g., "pricey" for `price=expensive`) directly from data.
3.  **Task-Based Validation of Semantically Specialized Embeddings:** The paper provides compelling downstream task evidence for the superiority of semantically specialized word vectors (Paragram-SL999) over standard distributional vectors (GloVe) and random initializations for this task. This analysis adds valuable insight into the properties of word embeddings that are most crucial for language understanding components.

### Strengths
1.  **Exceptional Novelty and Potential Impact:** The core idea of this paper is highly novel and constitutes a major advance. The reliance on hand-crafted semantic dictionaries was a well-known bottleneck preventing DST systems from scaling to new, complex domains. This paper directly attacks that problem with a clean and principled neural approach. The proposed framework has the potential to fundamentally change how DST systems are built, making them more adaptable and easier to deploy. This is exactly the kind of ambitious, forward-looking research that moves the field forward.
2.  **Strong and Convincing Experimental Results:** The empirical validation is a key strength. The central claim—that a data-driven approach can replace manual lexicons—is convincingly supported by the results in Table 1. The NBT models, without access to any semantic dictionaries, achieve performance comparable to the lexicon-supplemented baselines. Furthermore, they significantly outperform the baselines when those systems are also deprived of the dictionaries. This is a powerful and unambiguous demonstration of the proposed method's effectiveness.
3.  **Thoughtful and Well-Motivated Model Architecture:** The architecture is intelligently designed for the task. It correctly identifies and models the three key inputs: the user utterance, the candidate slot-value pair, and the dialogue context (previous system turn). The explicit modeling of interactions between these components, such as the element-wise multiplication for semantic decoding and the gating mechanisms for context modeling, are well-justified and appear effective.
4.  **Insightful Analysis of Word Vector Representations:** The ablation study in Section 6.2 is excellent. It goes beyond simply using off-the-shelf embeddings and investigates *why* certain embeddings work better than others. The conclusion that semantically specialized vectors outperform distributional ones (which conflate similarity and relatedness) provides a valuable lesson for the community and strengthens the paper's scientific contribution.

### Weaknesses
1.  **Simplistic Belief Update Mechanism:** The mechanism for updating the belief state across turns (Section 4) is a simple, rule-based linear interpolation. This component feels somewhat disconnected from the otherwise sophisticated neural architecture of the turn-level model. While it is clearly effective enough for the experiments presented, a more integrated, end-to-end differentiable approach (e.g., using a turn-level RNN) could be more powerful. However, I consider this a minor point, as the paper's main contribution lies in the novel turn-level understanding model, which is successfully isolated and evaluated.
2.  **Potential Scalability Concerns for Large Ontologies:** The proposed model works by iterating through every possible slot-value pair defined in the ontology for each turn. This is perfectly feasible for the evaluated domains, which have a small, closed set of values. It is less clear how this approach would scale to domains with very large or open-ended value sets (e.g., tracking movie titles, contact names, or street addresses). This is an important consideration for real-world deployment, though it is an acceptable limitation for a paper introducing a foundational new method.
3.  **Evaluation Limited to a Single Domain:** Both DSTC2 and WOZ 2.0 datasets are situated in the restaurant-finding domain. While the linguistic style differs between them, demonstrating the NBT's effectiveness on a thematically different domain (e.g., flight booking, technical support) would have provided stronger evidence for the generality of the approach.

Despite these weaknesses, I believe they are minor in the face of the paper's significant conceptual contribution. The limitations primarily point towards clear avenues for future work building upon this strong foundation, rather than fundamental flaws in the proposed approach.

### Questions to Authors
1.  Regarding the scalability issue for large ontologies: Have you considered alternative approaches to the final decision-making step that would not require iterating over all possible slot-value pairs? For instance, could the model be trained to directly generate the expressed value's vector representation, which could then be matched against known values using nearest-neighbor search?
