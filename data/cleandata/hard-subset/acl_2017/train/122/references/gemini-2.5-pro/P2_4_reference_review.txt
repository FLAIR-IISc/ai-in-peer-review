### Summary of the Paper
This paper introduces the Neural Belief Tracker (NBT), a novel framework for dialogue state tracking (DST) in task-oriented dialogue systems. The primary motivation is to overcome the scalability limitations of previous state-of-the-art models, which often rely on a process called "delexicalisation." This process requires hand-crafted semantic dictionaries to map various linguistic expressions to canonical slot-value pairs, a dependency that hinders deployment in new or more complex domains. The proposed NBT model avoids this dependency by operating directly on pre-trained word vector representations. It jointly models Spoken Language Understanding (SLU) and DST by iterating through all possible slot-value pairs in the ontology and making a binary classification for each, based on the user utterance and the preceding system act. The authors propose two variants, NBT-DNN and NBT-CNN, which differ in how they compose the user utterance representation. Through experiments on two restaurant-finding datasets (DSTC2 and WOZ 2.0), the paper demonstrates that NBT can match the performance of models that use hand-crafted lexicons and significantly outperform them when such resources are unavailable.

### Contributions
1.  **A Novel DST Framework Avoiding Hand-Crafted Lexicons:** The main contribution is the NBT model itself, which presents a principled way to perform joint SLU and DST without relying on delexicalisation or manually curated semantic dictionaries. By leveraging the semantic information encoded in pre-trained word vectors, the model can learn to handle lexical variation in a data-driven manner, representing a significant step towards more scalable and portable DST systems.

2.  **Strong Empirical Validation and Analysis:** The paper provides a thorough and convincing empirical evaluation. The experimental design is a key contribution, as it directly tests the central hypothesis by comparing NBT against strong baselines both with and without access to semantic dictionaries. This comparison clearly isolates and demonstrates the value of the proposed approach. Furthermore, the analysis in Table 2, which investigates the impact of different word vector types (random, distributional, and semantically specialised), offers valuable insights into the model's properties and provides strong evidence that the semantic quality of embeddings is crucial for this downstream task.

### Strengths
1.  **Clear Problem Motivation and Significance:** The paper does an excellent job of identifying a critical bottleneck in existing DST research—the reliance on hand-crafted resources for delexicalisation. The introduction clearly explains why this is a scalability problem and positions the NBT as a direct and well-motivated solution. The work is timely and addresses a highly relevant issue for the field of conversational AI.

2.  **Rigorous and Well-Designed Experiments:** The experimental setup is a major strength. Using two datasets (DSTC2 with ASR noise and the cleaner but more linguistically diverse WOZ 2.0) allows for a nuanced evaluation of the model's capabilities. The choice of baselines is appropriate, representing competitive prior work. The core comparison against baseline models in two settings (+/- semantic dictionary) provides compelling evidence for the paper's main claim. The results are statistically significant and convincingly demonstrate that NBT effectively learns the semantic variations that previously had to be hard-coded.

3.  **Sound and Well-Explained Model Architecture:** The NBT architecture is logical and well-justified. While the individual components (e.g., using a CNN for sentence representation) are established techniques, their composition into a framework for this specific DST task is novel and effective. The paper clearly explains the role of each submodule—representation learning, semantic decoding, and context modelling—with helpful diagrams (Figures 2-4). The design choices, such as using element-wise multiplication for feature interaction, are explained and motivated.

4.  **Clarity and Readability:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and easy to follow. The problem, proposed solution, and experimental validation are presented in a clear, logical progression. The background section effectively contextualizes the work within the existing literature. This high level of clarity makes the paper's contributions accessible and impactful.

### Weaknesses
1.  **Limited Novelty of Individual Neural Components:** While the overall application and framework are novel for DST, the core neural building blocks are standard. The use of a Kim (2014)-style CNN for sentence encoding or summing n-gram representations are common techniques in NLP. The paper would be slightly stronger if it more explicitly acknowledged this, framing its novelty more precisely around the *integration* of these components to solve the specific problem of replacing delexicalisation in belief tracking.

2.  **Potential Scalability Concerns with the Ontology Size:** The proposed model works by iterating over every candidate slot-value pair in the domain ontology and performing a separate binary classification. While this is computationally feasible for the ontologies in the DSTC2 and WOZ 2.0 datasets, this approach does not scale well computationally as the number of slots and, particularly, the number of possible values for each slot grows. For open-ended slots like `restaurant-name` or `movie-title` with thousands of potential values, this approach would be impractical. The paper's claim of improved scalability focuses on the human effort of lexicon creation, but it does not fully address this potential computational bottleneck.

3.  **Simplicity of the Context Modelling:** The context modelling component is relatively simple, as it only considers the immediately preceding system act and uses dot products as gating functions. This makes a strong Markovian assumption that might not hold in more complex dialogues where context from several turns prior is necessary for interpretation. A more sophisticated context encoder (e.g., an RNN over the dialogue history) could potentially capture longer-term dependencies more effectively, though this would add complexity.

### Questions to Authors
1.  Regarding the scalability concern mentioned in the weaknesses: Have you considered the computational performance of the NBT as the ontology size increases? Could you comment on the trade-offs between your approach and a generative one that might predict the value directly rather than classifying from a pre-defined list, especially for domains with very large sets of possible values?

2.  The context modelling component is effective but simple. Did you experiment with incorporating a more extensive dialogue history, for instance, by feeding the representations of previous turns into an RNN? If so, did this provide any benefit over only using the last system act?
