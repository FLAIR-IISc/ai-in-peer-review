**Summary of the paper**
This paper introduces the Neural Belief Tracker (NBT), a novel framework for dialogue state tracking (DST) that aims to eliminate the need for hand-crafted semantic dictionaries. The core idea is to replace traditional delexicalization techniques with a neural model that operates directly on pre-trained word embeddings. The model takes the user utterance, the previous system act, and a candidate slot-value pair as input. It then uses one of two proposed architectures (NBT-DNN or NBT-CNN) to learn vector representations and predict whether the given slot-value pair was expressed. The authors evaluate their approach on the DSTC2 and WOZ 2.0 datasets, comparing it against established delexicalization-based baselines.

**Contributions**
1.  The primary contribution is a neural architecture for DST that leverages pre-trained word embeddings to handle lexical variation, thereby avoiding the need for manually curated semantic dictionaries which are common in established joint SLU/DST models.
2.  The paper provides an empirical evaluation on two datasets demonstrating that the proposed NBT models can match the performance of strong baseline systems that use hand-crafted dictionaries, and significantly outperform them when such dictionaries are unavailable.
3.  A secondary contribution is an analysis showing the impact of different types of word embeddings on DST performance, concluding that semantically-specialized embeddings provide a significant advantage.

**Strengths**
1.  **Strong and Fair Experimental Setup:** The evaluation is methodologically sound. The authors compare their method against well-established and competitive baselines (Henderson et al., 2014d; Wen et al., 2017). Crucially, they evaluate the baselines both with and without the task-specific semantic dictionaries, providing a clear and direct assessment of the NBT's ability to learn semantic variation without these hand-crafted resources. This rigorous comparison is a major strength of the paper.
2.  **Clear Motivation and Problem Formulation:** The paper does an excellent job of motivating the work by clearly identifying a key limitation of existing delexicalization-based DST models: their reliance on manually constructed semantic dictionaries, which hinders scalability. The proposed NBT framework is presented as a direct and principled solution to this problem.
3.  **Demonstrably Effective Results:** The results presented in Table 1 are compelling. The NBT models consistently achieve state-of-the-art or competitive performance on both datasets. The fact that NBT matches the performance of the lexicon-supplemented baselines provides strong evidence for the central claim of the paper—that semantic knowledge can be effectively captured from pre-trained embeddings, obviating the need for manual lexicon creation in these tasks.
4.  **Informative Ablation Study on Word Embeddings:** The analysis in Section 6.2 is valuable. It not only supports the model design but also provides broader insights for the community. By showing that semantically specialized Paragram-SL999 vectors significantly outperform standard GloVe and random vectors, the authors reinforce the claim that the model's success is indeed tied to the semantic quality of the underlying embeddings, rather than just the neural architecture itself.

**Weaknesses**
1.  **Untested Scalability Claims:** The primary motivation for this work is to improve scalability to larger, more complex domains where creating semantic dictionaries is infeasible. However, the experiments are conducted on two relatively small-scale, single-domain datasets (restaurant finding) which share the same ontology of only three informable slots. While the method shows promise, the paper provides no direct evidence that this approach would indeed scale effectively to domains with tens or hundreds of slots and values, where the complexity of semantic relationships increases dramatically. The claims of scalability thus remain speculative.
2.  **Architectural Novelty is Limited:** The core components of the NBT models are based on established neural architectures. The NBT-CNN is a fairly standard application of a convolutional network for sentence classification (e.g., Kim, 2014), and the NBT-DNN is a simpler bag-of-n-grams model. While the application of these techniques to the problem of dictionary-free DST is novel, the underlying neural building blocks are not. The contribution lies more in the problem formulation and application than in fundamental architectural innovation.
3.  **Dependency is Shifted, Not Eliminated:** The paper argues that it removes the dependency on hand-crafted resources (semantic dictionaries). However, it introduces a new, strong dependency on high-quality, pre-trained, and *semantically specialized* word embeddings. The results in Table 2 show a marked performance drop when using more standard GloVe vectors and an even larger drop with random initialization. High-quality, semantically specialized embeddings like Paragram-SL999 are not available for all languages or specialized domains. Therefore, the work trades one form of resource dependency for another, which may still pose a bottleneck for deployment in new settings.
4.  **Simplistic Belief Update Mechanism:** The belief state update rule described in Section 4 is a simple linear interpolation between the current turn's belief and the previous state's belief. This is a conventional but somewhat rudimentary approach. More established and recent work has explored more sophisticated recurrent mechanisms for belief updating that can potentially capture more complex temporal dependencies in dialogue. The focus on the turn-level SLU component leaves the inter-turn DST aspect feeling less developed.

**Questions to Authors**
1.  The central motivation of your work is scalability. Could you comment on how you expect the NBT model to perform in a much larger domain, such as the multi-domain WOZ 2.0 dataset or the DSTC3 dataset, which features a significantly larger number of slots and values? How does the "iterate over all candidate slot-value pairs" approach scale computationally as the ontology size grows?
2.  Your results show a strong dependence on Paragram-SL999 vectors. In a new domain or a lower-resource language where only standard distributional vectors (like GloVe or word2vec) are available, your results (Table 2, WOZ 2.0) suggest that NBT may not outperform the baseline. Does this undermine the claim that NBT is a more general and scalable solution than delexicalization, which can be bootstrapped with a small, manually-created dictionary?
