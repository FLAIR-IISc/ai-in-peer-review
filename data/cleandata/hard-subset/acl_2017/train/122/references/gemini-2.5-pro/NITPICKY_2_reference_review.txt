### Summary and Contributions

This paper introduces the Neural Belief Tracker (NBT), a framework for dialogue state tracking that operates directly on pre-trained word embeddings. The core idea is to replace the conventional delexicalisation pipeline, which relies on hand-crafted semantic dictionaries, with a neural model that learns to recognize mentions of slot-value pairs from their semantic representations. The authors present two model variants, NBT-DNN and NBT-CNN, and evaluate them on the DSTC2 and WOZ 2.0 datasets. The main contributions of this work, as I assess them, are:

1.  A novel approach for joint SLU and DST that reasons over dense word vectors instead of sparse, delexicalised features. This is a well-motivated step towards building more scalable DST systems that do not depend on domain-specific manual resources.
2.  A compelling empirical demonstration that semantically specialized word vectors (Paragram-SL999) provide a significant performance boost over both general-purpose distributional vectors (GloVe) and random initialization in a downstream dialogue task.
3.  The release of improved versions of two standard datasets (a manually corrected DSTC2 and an expanded WOZ 2.0), which is a valuable service to the community.

### Strengths

1.  **Novel and Well-Motivated Method:** The paper's primary strength lies in its conceptual shift away from delexicalisation. The motivation to handle lexical variation via pre-trained embeddings rather than manually curated dictionaries is clear, sound, and addresses a major bottleneck in scaling dialogue systems.
2.  **Strong Empirical Results and Comparisons:** The experimental setup is solid. The NBT models are compared against strong, relevant baselines. The results convincingly show that the proposed approach can match the performance of models that use hand-crafted lexicons, and substantially outperform them when those lexicons are not available. This directly validates the central hypothesis of the paper.
3.  **Insightful Analysis of Word Embeddings:** The ablation study in Section 6.2, which compares different word vector initializations, is a highlight. It provides concrete, downstream evidence for the value of semantically specialized embeddings, moving beyond intrinsic evaluation metrics and demonstrating their tangible benefit for a complex language understanding task.
4.  **Good Use of Multiple Datasets:** The evaluation on both DSTC2 (with ASR noise) and WOZ 2.0 (cleaner, more complex language) allows for a more nuanced understanding of the model's capabilities and limitations, particularly regarding its ability to handle linguistic variety versus ASR errors.

### Weaknesses

Despite its novel ideas and strong results, the paper is marred by methodological simplifications, a lack of crucial technical details, and a general lack of polish that detracts from its overall quality.

1.  **Crude and Ad-Hoc Architectural Choices:** Several key components of the NBT model seem overly simplistic or arbitrary.
    *   The **NBT-DNN** model's sentence representation is a simple, unweighted sum of n-gram vectors (Eq. 2), which completely discards sequential information. This is a surprisingly naive approach for sentence modelling.
    *   The **Context Modelling** module (Sec 3.3) is a particularly weak point. It is a hard-coded heuristic for handling only two specific system acts (`request` and `confirm`) using a non-obvious dot-product gating mechanism. The paper does not explain how other system acts are handled, nor does it justify this specific mathematical formulation over more principled, learned approaches like attention. This component feels brittle and not generalizable.
    *   The **Belief State Update Mechanism** (Sec 4) is a basic linear interpolation, a method that has been superseded by more powerful recurrent update mechanisms that can model dialogue history more effectively. The choice of this simple rule feels dated and inadequate.
2.  **Insufficient Detail for Reproducibility:** The paper omits essential hyperparameters and training details. While the optimizer and loss function are mentioned, there is no information on the learning rate, batch size, regularization methods (e.g., dropout, L2), hidden layer dimensions, or convergence criteria. This lack of detail makes the experiments impossible to reproduce and constitutes a significant flaw in a computational paper.
3.  **Poor Presentation and Formatting Errors:** The paper suffers from a striking lack of attention to detail.
    *   The **References section is riddled with formatting and BibTeX parsing errors** (e.g., `.,?`, `et al\.,`, `\Q2015\E`). This level of sloppiness is unacceptable for a top-tier conference submission and severely undermines the credibility of the work.
    *   The main architectural diagram (Figure 2) is misleading. It depicts the system act vectors (`tq`, `ts`, `tv`) as inputs to the "Representation Learning" block, whereas Section 3.3 describes them interacting with representations *after* this stage.
    *   Footnote 2 provides a post-hoc justification for using element-wise multiplication over concatenation, stating that the dataset was too small for the latter to learn the interaction. This is an empirical claim that should be substantiated with results, not presented as an unsubstantiated aside.

### Questions to Authors

1.  Regarding the Context Modelling (Sec 3.3): How are system dialogue acts other than `request` and `confirm` handled by the model? Could you provide the rationale for the specific dot-product gating formulation and state whether you explored more standard, learned alternatives like an attention mechanism?
2.  Could you please provide the full set of hyperparameters and training details necessary to reproduce your results, including learning rate, batch size, all hidden layer sizes, and any regularization used?
3.  The belief update mechanism is a simple linear interpolation. Did you experiment with recurrent update mechanisms (e.g., an LSTM/GRU over turns)? If so, how did they compare to the simple rule described in Section 4? If not, why did you opt for the simpler method?
