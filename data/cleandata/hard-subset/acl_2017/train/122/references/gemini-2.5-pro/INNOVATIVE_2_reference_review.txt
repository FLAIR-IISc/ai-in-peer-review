### Summary and Contributions

This paper introduces the Neural Belief Tracker (NBT), a novel framework for dialogue state tracking that moves away from the dominant paradigm of delexicalization. Instead of relying on hand-crafted semantic dictionaries to map textual mentions to ontology items, the NBT operates directly on pre-trained word embeddings. It formulates DST as a semantic matching problem in a continuous vector space, where it learns to decide if a candidate slot-value pair is expressed in a user utterance, conditioned on the preceding system turn.

In my view, the primary contributions of this work are:

1.  **A Conceptual Shift in Dialogue State Tracking:** The most significant contribution is the bold move to replace the discrete, lexicon-dependent delexicalization process with a fully data-driven, vector-space model. This reframes the core challenge of DST from one of string matching and feature engineering to one of learned semantic similarity, which represents a promising and more scalable direction for the field.
2.  **Strong Empirical Demonstration of Viability:** The paper provides compelling evidence that this new approach is not just conceptually elegant but also practically effective. By showing that the NBT can match the performance of strong baselines that use hand-crafted lexicons, and significantly outperform them when those lexicons are removed, the authors validate their core hypothesis.
3.  **Insightful Analysis on the Role of Word Embeddings:** The ablation study comparing different word vector initializations provides a valuable, extrinsic validation of semantically-specialized embeddings. It demonstrates that the quality of the underlying semantic space is crucial for the model's success, reinforcing the paper's central premise.

### Strengths

1.  **High Novelty and Potential Impact:** This paper's main strength is its novelty. It directly confronts a major bottleneck in scaling dialogue systems: the reliance on manually curated resources like semantic dictionaries. The proposed vector-space approach is a fundamental departure from prior work and has the potential to make DST models far more robust to linguistic variation and easier to adapt to new, complex domains. This is precisely the kind of bold, high-risk, high-reward research that moves the field forward.
2.  **Elegant and Principled Approach:** The NBT framework is clean and intuitive. The idea of composing representations for the utterance, context, and a candidate pair, and then learning a matching function is a principled way to tackle the problem. By doing so, it maximizes parameter sharing across the ontology and leverages the rich prior knowledge encoded in pre-trained embeddings to handle unseen paraphrases and synonyms naturally.
3.  **Convincing Experimental Results:** The experiments are well-designed and the results are strong. The comparison on two different datasets (one with ASR noise, one with richer language) is effective. The key result—that NBT without a lexicon performs on par with a state-of-the-art system *with* a lexicon—is a powerful statement. It effectively proves that the semantic knowledge required for the task can be learned from data and general-purpose embeddings, rather than needing to be manually encoded.

### Weaknesses

While I am very enthusiastic about this work, I will note a few areas that could be strengthened, though I do not see them as grounds for rejection given the paper's significant conceptual contribution.

1.  **Simplistic Context Modeling:** The context modeling component (Section 3.3) is quite basic, relying on dot-product gates and only considering the immediately preceding system turn. While sufficient to prove the paper's main point about semantic matching, this mechanism may not be robust enough for more complex conversational phenomena where context builds over multiple turns. This feels like a clear area for future improvement rather than a critical flaw in the current work.
2.  **Limited Scope of Evaluation:** The experiments are confined to a single, relatively simple restaurant-finding domain. A central motivation for this work is scalability to "richer" and "larger" domains, but the current evaluation does not directly test this claim. While it is understandable for a first paper on a new method, demonstrating the approach on at least one other, more complex domain would have made the scalability argument much more concrete.
3.  **Potential Scalability Concerns:** The model iterates over all possible slot-value pairs in the ontology at each turn to make a decision. This is feasible for the DSTC2 domain, but could become a computational bottleneck for domains with very large ontologies (e.g., thousands of possible values for a slot). The paper does not address this potential limitation of its inference procedure.

### Questions to Authors

1.  The current inference method requires scoring every slot-value pair in the ontology. How do you see this approach scaling to domains with thousands of possible values (e.g., tracking a specific movie title or artist name)? Would the framework need to be modified, for instance by incorporating a candidate generation step, to remain practical?
2.  The context model is an interesting first step. Did you experiment with more sophisticated context representations, such as using an RNN to encode the full dialogue history, rather than just the last system act? If so, did it provide any benefit, or did the model overfit on these small datasets?
