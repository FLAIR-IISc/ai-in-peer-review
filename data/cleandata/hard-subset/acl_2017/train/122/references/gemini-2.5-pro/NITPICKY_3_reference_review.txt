### Summary of the Paper

This paper introduces the Neural Belief Tracker (NBT), a dialogue state tracking (DST) model designed to operate directly on natural language without requiring delexicalization or hand-crafted semantic dictionaries. The core idea is to leverage pre-trained word embeddings to represent user utterances, system acts, and candidate slot-value pairs. The model uses these representations to compute semantic similarity and contextual relevance, making a binary decision for each possible slot-value pair at each turn. Two architectural variants are proposed: NBT-DNN, which uses simple summation of n-gram vectors, and NBT-CNN, which employs a convolutional neural network for utterance representation. The authors evaluate their models on the DSTC2 and WOZ 2.0 datasets, demonstrating that the NBT framework matches the performance of strong baselines that use manually created lexicons and significantly outperforms them when such resources are unavailable.

### Contributions

From my perspective, the main contributions of this work are:

1.  **A scalable DST framework without manual lexicons:** The primary contribution is a compelling demonstration of a neural DST model that successfully replaces the brittle process of delexicalization with learned representations from word embeddings. This is a valuable step towards building more scalable and portable dialogue systems.
2.  **Strong empirical validation:** The paper provides a thorough empirical evaluation on two datasets. The experimental design, which includes comparisons to strong baselines both with and without their corresponding semantic dictionaries, effectively isolates and validates the contribution of the proposed vector-based approach.
3.  **Analysis of word embedding quality:** The ablation study on different types of word vectors (random, GloVe, Paragram-SL999) provides a useful, task-based (extrinsic) validation that semantically specialized embeddings can yield significant performance gains in downstream applications, confirming findings from prior intrinsic evaluations.

### Strengths

1.  **Well-motivated and impactful problem:** The paper addresses a clear and significant limitation of many previous DST models: their reliance on hand-crafted resources for delexicalization, which hinders scalability. The proposed solution of using word embeddings is elegant and directly tackles this core problem.
2.  **Solid experimental results:** The NBT models achieve state-of-the-art performance on two established benchmarks. The gains are particularly notable on the WOZ 2.0 dataset, which features more linguistic variety, corroborating the authors' hypothesis that their model is better equipped to handle lexical variation.
3.  **Thorough comparisons:** The choice of baselines is appropriate, representing strong contemporary models for each dataset. Comparing the performance of these baselines with and without their semantic dictionaries provides a clear and convincing benchmark for the NBT's capabilities.

### Weaknesses

Despite its promising direction, the paper suffers from a lack of methodological rigor, poor presentation quality, and several underdeveloped technical details.

1.  **Questionable and poorly justified architectural choices:** Several key components of the model seem ad-hoc and lack sufficient justification.
    *   The context modeling mechanism in Section 3.3 (Equations 9 and 10) is particularly crude. Using a scalar dot product (e.g., `cs · tq`) to gate the entire utterance vector `r` is a simplistic formulation that pales in comparison to more standard and expressive attention mechanisms. The paper provides no justification for this choice over more established alternatives.
    *   The belief update mechanism described in Section 4 is a simple linear interpolation controlled by a single hyperparameter `λ`. This is a non-learned, rule-based component that feels disconnected from the "neural" philosophy of the model. This simplistic approach to handling turn-level accumulation and ASR uncertainty may be a key performance bottleneck, as suggested by the large performance gap between ASR and transcribed inputs (Section 6.1).
2.  **Extremely poor formatting and lack of professional polish:** The paper is marred by an unacceptable number of formatting errors, which severely undermines its credibility. The entire references section is riddled with errors: stray question marks, incorrect author formatting (e.g., `Celikyilmaz and Hakkani.Tur.,?`), inconsistent capitalization, and raw BibTeX artifacts (e.g., `Collobert et al\.,?`, `\Q2015\E`). This level of sloppiness is inexcusable for a top-tier conference submission and suggests a rushed and careless preparation process.
3.  **Insufficient explanation of key design decisions:** The authors state that pre-trained word vectors are kept fixed during training to handle unseen words. While this is a plausible reason, the trade-off is significant, as it prevents the model from adapting the embeddings to the specific domain. This decision warrants a more detailed discussion and, ideally, an ablation study comparing fixed vs. fine-tuned embeddings. Furthermore, an important finding—that concatenating `r` and `c` representations led to "very weak performance"—is relegated to a footnote. This suggests a potential fragility in the model or a difficulty in learning from concatenated features on smaller datasets, a point that should be explored more transparently in the main text.
4.  **Overstated novelty claims:** The claim of being "the first to successfully use pre-trained word vector spaces to improve the language understanding capability of belief tracking models" is very strong and may not be entirely accurate. While the specific NBT architecture is novel, neural methods using embeddings have been explored in the broader SLU space. A more careful and nuanced positioning with respect to related work is required.

### Questions to Authors

1.  Could you provide a detailed justification for the context modeling formulation used in Equations (9) and (10)? Have you experimented with more standard, learnable attention mechanisms? If so, how did they compare?
2.  The belief update mechanism in Section 4 is a simple, rule-based interpolation. Why was this chosen over an integrated, learnable component (e.g., an RNN over turns)? Do you agree that this component is likely a primary source of error when dealing with noisy ASR N-best lists?
3.  The decision to freeze word embeddings is a critical one. Could you elaborate on the trade-offs involved? What was the performance impact of fine-tuning the embeddings during training? Did it improve in-domain performance at the cost of generalizing to unseen paraphrases?
