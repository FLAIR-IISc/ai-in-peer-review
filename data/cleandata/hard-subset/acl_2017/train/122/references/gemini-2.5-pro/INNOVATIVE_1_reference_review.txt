### Summary and Contributions

This paper introduces the Neural Belief Tracker (NBT), a novel framework for dialogue state tracking that moves away from the dominant paradigm of delexicalisation. Instead of relying on hand-crafted semantic dictionaries to map textual mentions to ontology items, NBT operates directly on pre-trained word embeddings. The model takes a user utterance, the preceding system act, and a candidate slot-value pair, and uses a neural architecture to decide if the pair was expressed. By reasoning in a continuous semantic space, the model aims to handle lexical variation in a more scalable and data-driven manner. Experiments on two restaurant-domain datasets show that NBT can match the performance of strong delexicalisation-based baselines that use manually created dictionaries, and significantly outperforms them when such resources are unavailable.

In my view, the main contributions of this work are:

1.  **A new conceptual paradigm for belief tracking:** The primary contribution is the bold shift from discrete, delexicalised features to continuous semantic representations for joint Spoken Language Understanding and Dialogue State Tracking. This addresses a fundamental scalability bottleneck—the need for domain-specific, hand-crafted lexicons—and charts a promising new direction for the field.
2.  **Strong empirical evidence for replacing manual lexicons with word embeddings:** The paper provides a compelling demonstration that semantically-aware word vectors can effectively stand in for manually curated synonym lists. The result that NBT (without a lexicon) matches a strong baseline (with a lexicon) is a powerful validation of the core hypothesis.
3.  **A clear demonstration of the downstream value of semantically-specialized word vectors:** The analysis in Section 6.2 offers valuable insight by showing that the quality and nature of word embeddings have a direct and significant impact on this complex downstream task. This provides important practical evidence for the utility of recent work on improving word representations.

### Strengths

1.  **High Novelty and Conceptual Significance:** The most impressive aspect of this paper is its willingness to challenge the established delexicalisation approach. By building a model that reasons directly over semantic vector spaces, the authors tackle a core problem of scalability and brittleness in dialogue systems. This is not an incremental refinement of an existing method but a fundamentally new and promising way of thinking about the problem. The potential to build robust dialogue systems without the laborious process of creating semantic dictionaries for every new domain is a significant step forward.
2.  **Elegant and Convincing Experimental Design:** The experiments are well-designed to isolate and validate the paper's central claim. Comparing the proposed NBT model against strong baselines both with and without the hand-crafted semantic dictionary provides a perfectly clear and convincing ablation. The key result in Table 1—showing that NBT closes the performance gap that the dictionary provides for the baseline—is a powerful piece of evidence for the approach's effectiveness.
3.  **Highlights the Practical Impact of Word Representation Quality:** The ablation study on different word vector initializations (Table 2) is a valuable contribution in its own right. It provides a clear, quantitative demonstration that semantically specialized vectors (Paragram-SL999) offer a tangible performance benefit over standard distributional vectors (GloVe) and random initializations on a complex, task-oriented problem. This moves the evaluation of word vectors beyond intrinsic tasks and underscores their practical importance.
4.  **Simplicity and Clarity of the Model:** Despite its conceptual novelty, the proposed NBT architecture is refreshingly simple and elegant. It combines standard, well-understood neural components (feed-forward layers, convolutions, gating mechanisms) into a coherent and effective model. This clarity makes the work easy to understand, appreciate, and likely reproduce or build upon.

### Weaknesses

While I am very enthusiastic about this paper's direction, there are a few areas where the approach could be strengthened or its limitations explored further.

1.  **Simplistic Belief Update Mechanism:** The core of the paper is the innovative turn-level neural model. However, the mechanism for updating the belief state across turns is a simple linear interpolation with a fixed `λ` hyperparameter. This feels like a disconnect from the data-driven philosophy of the rest of the model. A more principled, learned update mechanism (e.g., using a turn-level RNN) could potentially handle more complex conversational phenomena and might be more robust than this heuristic-based approach.
2.  **Scalability Concerns for Large Ontologies:** The authors motivate their work by the need for scalability, but the proposed inference method—iterating over every possible slot-value pair in the ontology—presents a potential computational bottleneck. This is feasible for the domains tested, but it is unclear how it would perform in domains with slots that can take on thousands or millions of values (e.g., tracking a specific movie title, song, or product). The paper does not address this potential limitation of its inference procedure.
3.  **Limited Context Modelling:** The model's context is restricted to the immediately preceding system turn. While this is a reasonable simplification and captures important phenomena like answering direct questions, real dialogues often have longer-range dependencies. A user's utterance might refer to something discussed several turns earlier. The current gating mechanism cannot capture such interactions, which may limit the model's effectiveness in more complex, multi-turn conversations.

### Questions to Authors

1.  The inference process requires iterating through all slot-value pairs. Could you comment on the computational cost and feasibility of this approach for domains with very large value sets (e.g., tracking a contact name from a user's address book)? Have you considered alternative inference strategies, such as using approximate nearest neighbor search in the embedding space to identify a smaller set of candidate values to score?
2.  Your model treats the binary classification for each slot-value pair as an independent decision. Does this ever lead to inconsistent belief states, for example, by assigning high confidence to two mutually exclusive values for the same slot (e.g., `price=cheap` and `price=expensive`)? Would a joint model over the values of a single slot, for instance using a softmax layer, be a more robust approach?
3.  The belief update relies on a fixed `λ=0.55`. Did you experiment with a learned or dynamic update mechanism? It seems plausible that the weight given to the current turn's evidence versus the historical belief state should vary depending on the context, for example, based on the system's dialogue act or the model's confidence.
