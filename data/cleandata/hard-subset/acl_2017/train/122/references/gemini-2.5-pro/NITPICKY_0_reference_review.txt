### Summary and Contributions

This paper introduces the Neural Belief Tracker (NBT), a framework for dialogue state tracking that aims to overcome the scalability limitations of previous methods, particularly their reliance on hand-crafted semantic dictionaries. The proposed models (NBT-DNN and NBT-CNN) jointly perform SLU and DST by operating directly on pre-trained word vectors. For each turn, the model iterates through all ontology-defined slot-value pairs and makes a binary classification decision. The core idea is that semantic similarities captured in word embeddings can handle lexical variation (e.g., "cheap" vs. "inexpensive") without explicit delexicalisation rules. Experiments on the DSTC2 and WOZ 2.0 datasets show that NBT matches the performance of strong baselines that use semantic dictionaries and significantly outperforms them when such resources are unavailable.

From my perspective, the main contributions of this work are:
1.  A convincing demonstration that a neural DST model can successfully leverage pre-trained word embeddings to handle lexical variation, thereby obviating the need for hand-crafted, non-scalable semantic dictionaries.
2.  A valuable empirical study showing that the quality of the word vector space is crucial for this task. Specifically, the paper shows that semantically specialized vectors (Paragram-SL999) lead to significant performance gains over both standard distributional vectors (GloVe) and random initializations, providing a rare and useful result for a downstream application.

### Strengths

1.  **Well-Motivated Problem and Solution:** The paper clearly articulates a significant limitation of prior work in DST—the reliance on delexicalisation and manually curated semantic lexicons. The proposed NBT framework is a direct and well-reasoned approach to solving this problem by replacing brittle string matching with semantic reasoning in a vector space. This is a clear and important step towards building more scalable and robust dialogue systems.

2.  **Thorough and Rigorous Evaluation:** The experimental design is sound. The authors evaluate their models on two different datasets that test complementary aspects: DSTC2 tests robustness to ASR noise, while WOZ 2.0, with its richer, typed language, tests semantic understanding. The choice of baselines is appropriate, and the comparison against versions both with and without the semantic dictionary provides a direct and compelling validation of the paper's central claim.

3.  **Insightful Analysis of Word Embeddings:** The ablation study in Section 6.2, which compares random, GloVe, and Paragram-SL999 vectors, is a key strength. It not only reinforces the design of the NBT model but also provides a concrete, task-based justification for using semantically-specialized embeddings. This finding is of interest to the broader NLP community beyond the immediate scope of dialogue state tracking.

### Weaknesses

Despite the merits of the core idea, the paper is marred by a substantial number of methodological and presentational flaws that undermine its quality and reproducibility.

1.  **Inadequate Justification of Architectural Choices:** The paper presents several key design decisions with little to no justification.
    *   **Context Modelling (Sec 3.3):** The formulation in Equations 9 and 10 is an ad-hoc gating mechanism based on dot products. It is not clear why this specific form was chosen over other, potentially more powerful methods like attention. The claim that it is "particularly useful for the confirm system act" is asserted but not supported with any analysis or examples.
    *   **Belief Update (Sec 4):** The "tracking" component of this "Neural Belief Tracker" is a simple, non-learned linear interpolation rule. This is a significant weakness. The model's primary contribution is in turn-level intent detection, but the temporal belief tracking aspect is rudimentary. Using a fixed, hand-tuned `λ` is a step back from more sophisticated, learned update mechanisms (e.g., RNN-based trackers).
    *   **Semantic Decoding (Sec 3.2):** Footnote 2 dismisses the use of vector concatenation—a standard technique—by stating it "led to very weak performance." This is insufficient. An analysis of *why* it failed is necessary for the reader to understand the design space and trust the authors' choice of element-wise multiplication.

2.  **Lack of Critical Details for Reproducibility:** Several key details are omitted. The paper does not specify crucial hyperparameters for training, such as learning rate, batch size, or regularization methods used with the Adam optimizer. While the hidden layer size for the final decision network is mentioned (100), other architectural details are sparse. This lack of transparency makes it difficult to reproduce the results or build upon the work.

3.  **Overstated Scalability Claims:** The authors claim their framework is "better-suited to scaling." While it successfully avoids the need for manual lexicons, it introduces a different, and potentially more severe, scalability bottleneck. The model must iterate over every single slot-value pair in the ontology for each turn. This is feasible for the small toy domains of DSTC2 and WOZ 2.0, but it is computationally intractable for real-world domains with slots that can take thousands or millions of values (e.g., song titles, business names). The paper fails to acknowledge or discuss this critical limitation.

4.  **Numerous and Severe Formatting Issues:** The paper exhibits a concerning lack of attention to detail, particularly in the bibliography. The reference section is riddled with errors: spurious question marks, inconsistent author formatting (`et al\.,`), repeated entries (`Bohus and Rudnicky`), and leftover BibTeX macros (`\Q2015\E`). This level of sloppiness in a submitted manuscript is unacceptable for a top-tier conference and casts doubt on the rigor applied to the research itself. Furthermore, Figure 2 is overly abstract, and the mathematical notation in Equation 5 is imprecise (describing `mn` as a "list" being used in what appears to be a matrix multiplication).

### Questions to Authors

1.  Regarding the belief update mechanism in Section 4: Given the paper's title, did you experiment with a learned, neural component for the cross-turn belief update (e.g., an GRU/LSTM), rather than the simple linear interpolation rule? How do you justify this simplistic approach in a model presented as a "Neural Belief Tracker"?

2.  Your model's inference time scales linearly with the size of the ontology (i.e., the total number of slot-value pairs). How do you envision this approach scaling to domains with very large or open-ended value sets (e.g., tracking a specific movie title)? Do you consider this a major limitation of the proposed framework?

3.  Could you provide a more detailed justification for the context modeling formulation in Equations 9 and 10? Please compare it to alternative approaches you considered (e.g., attention) and explain why the proposed dot-product gating was superior.
