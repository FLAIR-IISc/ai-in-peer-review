Summary
This paper proposes Neural Belief Tracker (NBT), a joint SLU/DST framework that reasons directly in pre-trained word embedding space to detect slot-value mentions without hand-crafted semantic lexicons or delexicalization. Two instantiations (NBT-DNN and NBT-CNN) compose utterance representations; a simple, interpretable interaction (element-wise r ⊗ c) aligns utterance and candidate slot-value, and shallow context gating incorporates the last system act (request/confirm). A pragmatic belief update combines ASR N-best with historical state. Experiments on DSTC2 (ASR) and WOZ 2.0 (typed) show consistent gains over delexicalization-based baselines, and an ablation demonstrates that semantically specialized embeddings (Paragram-SL999) substantially improve DST.

Contributions
- A simple, scalable DST framework that eliminates semantic dictionaries by operating purely in embedding space, coupling SLU and DST with minimal supervision and extensive parameter sharing across slots/values.
- Empirical evidence that semantically specialized word vectors (vs. distributional or random) materially improve downstream DST, moving beyond intrinsic similarity to task impact.
- Competitive-to-SOTA results on DSTC2 and WOZ 2.0 without lexicons, plus a clean/expanded WOZ dataset and a practical ASR N-best fusion scheme.

Strengths
- Conceptual novelty and practicality: Replacing delexicalization with embedding-space reasoning is a clear step-change for scalability to larger ontologies and richer paraphrase/inflectional variation. The approach directly targets a long-standing bottleneck (manual lexicons) with a lightweight, broadly applicable solution.
- Strong, statistically significant improvements: On both datasets, NBT outperforms robust delexicalization baselines; importantly, it matches or exceeds systems that depend on hand-crafted lexicons, validating the central claim. Gains are largest on WOZ (richer language), aligning with the method’s strengths.
- Clean, modular architecture with interpretable interactions: The r ⊗ c similarity and context gating via dot-product produce understandable decision signals. No word-level labels are needed; embeddings are fixed to generalize to unseen rephrasings, enabling parameter sharing across slots/values and good data efficiency.
- Useful analysis on word vector quality: The comparison among random, GloVe, and Paragram-SL999 is compelling and shows that semantic specialization translates to downstream benefits, particularly under ASR noise. This is an impactful methodological insight for the community.
- Deployment-oriented choices: The ASR N-best integration and simple temporal interpolation are easy to adopt. The cleaned DSTC2 transcriptions and expanded WOZ 2.0 dataset improve reproducibility and study validity.

Weaknesses
- Shallow context modeling: Only the immediate previous system act is used with simple gating. This likely misses longer-range phenomena (corrections, multi-acts, anaphora, negation), limiting applicability in more complex dialogues. A comparison to RNN/Transformer-based dialogue context encoders would strengthen claims.
- Heuristic belief update: The state update is rule-based with a tuned λ; no learned calibration or end-to-end training with ASR confusions. A learned update (or lattice/ConfNet use) may yield further gains; at minimum, sensitivity to λ and N-best size should be presented.
- Limited domain breadth: Evaluation is confined to a single restaurant domain with a small ontology. Claims about scalability and unseen rephrasings would be more convincing with cross-domain or multi-domain tests, leave-one-value-out generalization, or open-vocabulary value experiments.
- Incomplete ablations: Critical design choices (element-wise product vs concatenation/bilinear, embedding freezing vs limited fine-tuning, parameter sharing across slots, CNN hyperparameters) are not systematically ablated. This hinders understanding of which components are essential.
- Handling of negation and conflict across turns: The paper does not analyze cases like “not Italian” or corrections (“Actually, make it Chinese”), where embedding similarity alone could be misleading without robust discourse modeling.

Questions to Authors
- How sensitive is performance to λ and the ASR N-best size? Can you provide a sensitivity analysis and WER-stratified results?
- Can NBT generalize to unseen values at test time using only embeddings (e.g., leave-one-value-out)? Any experiments on open-vocabulary values?
- How are multiple system acts handled within a turn? If multiple requests/confirms occur, how are tq, ts, tv aggregated?
- How does the model handle negation and corrections across turns? Any explicit treatment or error analysis?
- Did you try limited fine-tuning of embeddings with regularization (e.g., small LR or retrofitting during training)? How does that compare to fixed embeddings?
- What class balancing or sampling do you use when generating negative examples over all slot-value pairs? Any impact on calibration and thresholding (0.5)?

Overall, this is a bold, timely idea with clear empirical benefits and high potential for scaling DST without manual lexicons. The context modeling and belief update are rudimentary, but given the novelty and demonstrated gains, I lean positive.
