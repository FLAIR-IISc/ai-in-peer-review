Summary
This paper proposes Neural Belief Tracker (NBT) models for dialogue state tracking that jointly handle SLU and DST without hand-crafted semantic dictionaries. The key idea is to operate entirely in a pre-trained word vector space (notably, semantically specialized Paragram-SL999), composing utterance, candidate slot–value, and last system act representations and then deciding, for each slot–value candidate, whether it is expressed. Two encoder variants are presented (NBT-DNN with summed n-grams and NBT-CNN with convolution + max pooling). Context is modeled via simple gating using the last system act (request/confirm). A simple rule-based belief update over ASR N-best is used. The models are evaluated on DSTC2 (ASR) and WOZ 2.0 (typed), outperforming delexicalization-based baselines with and without semantic dictionaries, with largest gains when no dictionaries are available. The paper further shows that semantically specialized embeddings help more than generic distributional vectors.

Main contributions (as I see them)
1) A simple, modular DST architecture that replaces delexicalization and hand-crafted semantic dictionaries with pre-trained word embeddings and a learned interaction between utterance and slot–value vectors, plus lightweight context gating.
2) Empirical evidence that semantically specialized vectors (Paragram-SL999) improve DST over GloVe/random, and that NBT matches or outperforms delexicalized baselines, especially when semantic dictionaries are unavailable.
3) Two encoder variants (DNN/CNN) with largely shared parameters across slots/values, suggesting potential for better parameter efficiency than per-value SLU classifiers.

Strengths
- Removes hand-crafted semantic dictionaries while remaining competitive: On both DSTC2 and WOZ 2.0, NBT matches or slightly exceeds delexicalization+dictionary systems and clearly outperforms dictionary-free baselines. This is an impactful practical advantage for scaling to domains where dictionaries are costly.
- Clear, simple modeling with good parameter sharing: Encoding utterances and slot–values in the same vector space and using element-wise interactions is straightforward, easy to implement, and appears data-efficient (useful in small DST datasets).
- Evidence on the role of embedding quality: The comparison among random, GloVe, and Paragram-SL999 is valuable and supports the claim that semantic specialization (similarity vs. relatedness) matters for DST. This is a useful takeaway beyond this specific model.
- Sensible context handling for confirm/request acts: The gating with last system act is simple yet targeted to common dialogue phenomena (short yes/no answers), and likely explains some robustness without complex dialogue encoders.
- Reproducibility gestures: Use of public datasets and indication that cleaned DSTC2 and expanded WOZ 2.0 would be released are positive, and the architecture is described with enough detail to re-implement.

Weaknesses
- Limited empirical scope and modest gains over strongest baselines: Improvements on DSTC2 over dictionary-augmented delexicalization are small (e.g., 72.9 to 73.4 joint goals). The evaluation is confined to a single, small, slot-categorical domain with three informable slots. Claims of better scalability are not validated on larger ontologies or multi-domain settings.
- Inference scales linearly with ontology size: The model iterates over all slot–value pairs each turn. While parameters may be shared, the runtime cost still grows with ontology size. No analysis of computational efficiency or pruning strategies is provided, undermining the scalability claim.
- Shallow context modeling and hand-tuned belief update: Only the last system act is used (Markov assumption), and the belief update is a hand-tuned convex interpolation with λ tuned on dev. There is no ablation quantifying the contribution of context gating versus semantic decoding, nor comparison to learned belief updates or stronger dialogue-history models.
- Baseline coverage and training regime for ASR: The DSTC2 setup trains on transcriptions but evaluates on ASR; many prior systems train with ASR features (confusion networks, etc.). The paper acknowledges ASR robustness issues but does not either (a) train with ASR features, or (b) compare to DSTC2 systems that used ASR-rich inputs. This weakens the robustness claims.
- Insufficient ablations and analysis: It is unclear how much each component contributes (element-wise product vs concatenation, role of cs vs cv, effect of keeping embeddings fixed vs fine-tuning). The two NBT variants perform similarly; no discussion on when CNN helps, or on utterance length sensitivity. Error analysis is minimal, and statistical testing details (unit of analysis, handling of turn correlation) are not specified.

Questions to Authors
- Scalability: What is the per-turn inference cost in terms of |S| and |Vs|? Have you profiled NBT on a larger ontology (e.g., 10–20 slots, hundreds of values)? Any approximate candidate generation or pruning?
- ASR robustness: Why train on transcriptions rather than ASR/confusion networks for DSTC2? Can you report results when training with ASR hypotheses or confidence features? How does NBT compare to top DSTC2 systems that leveraged ASR features directly?
- Ablations: Please provide ablations for (i) removing context gating, (ii) replacing element-wise product with concatenation/bilinear, (iii) fine-tuning embeddings vs freezing, and (iv) DNN vs CNN across utterance lengths. Also quantify the impact of Paragram vs GloVe per slot.
- Belief update: How sensitive are results to λ? Have you tried a learned update or a simple HMM/RNN over turns to replace the hand-tuned interpolation?
- OOV/coverage: How are OOV words handled at test time? What is the OOV rate on DSTC2/WOZ, and how does it affect performance, especially for unseen rephrasings?

Overall, the paper presents a clean, practical approach with some positive results. However, given the narrow domain, small gains over strong baselines, reliance on per-candidate iteration, and limited analysis, more rigorous validation (scalability, ASR robustness, and comprehensive ablations) would strengthen the case.
