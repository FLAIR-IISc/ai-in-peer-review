Summary
The paper introduces Neural Belief Tracker (NBT), a family of joint SLU/DST models that operate directly on pre-trained word vectors rather than delexicalised features or hand-crafted semantic dictionaries. Two variants are proposed: NBT-DNN (sum of n-gram embeddings with slot-specific nonlinear projections) and NBT-CNN (convolutions over 1–3-grams with ReLU and max-pooling). A semantic decoding module combines utterance and candidate slot–value representations via element-wise multiplication; a simple context gating models last system acts (request/confirm). A rule-based belief update merges ASR N-best turn-level predictions with a tuned λ. Experiments on DSTC2 (ASR) and an expanded WOZ 2.0 (typed) show that NBT matches or exceeds delexicalisation-based baselines, particularly when no semantic dictionaries are available. An ablation demonstrates that semantically specialized Paragram-SL999 vectors yield the best performance.

Main contributions (as assessed)
1) Empirical finding: Pre-trained, semantically specialized word vectors enable DST models to robustly handle lexical variation and paraphrase, removing the need for semantic dictionaries and improving performance, especially on richer text (WOZ 2.0).
2) A simple, parameter-sharing NBT architecture that couples SLU and DST via vector-space interactions and shallow context gating over the last system act, achieving competitive results with limited supervision and without handcrafted resources.
3) A controlled comparison of different embedding initializations (random, GloVe, Paragram) linking intrinsic semantic quality to downstream DST performance.

Strengths
- Resource efficiency and scalability of supervision: The model avoids manual semantic dictionaries and fine-grained token-level labels. It shares parameters across slots/values and leverages fixed embeddings, offering a practical path to larger domains with limited annotation.
- Empirical gains without dictionaries: On both datasets, NBT significantly outperforms delexicalised baselines when no lexicons are provided; on WOZ 2.0, joint goal accuracy improves from 70.8 (baseline) to 84.2–84.4 (NBT), indicating strong handling of paraphrases and varied language.
- Robustness to embedding choice and clear benefit of semantic specialization: The ablation attributing gains to Paragram-SL999 over GloVe/random provides evidence that embedding semantic quality matters for DST. This supports the core claim and offers guidance for practitioners.
- Clear, modular design: The architecture and components (representation learning, semantic decoding, context gating, decision layer) are described with sufficient detail, including key hyperparameters (e.g., L=300 filters, hidden size 100), aiding reproducibility.
- Context-sensitive gating for confirm/request: The simple gating using dot-products between system act arguments and candidate slot/value is well-motivated and addresses cases where the user replies with short affirmations/negations.

Weaknesses
- Scalability to large ontologies: The model performs a binary decision for each candidate slot–value pair, leading to inference cost linear in ontology size. The paper does not analyze computational complexity, pruning, or candidate generation strategies for larger, realistic ontologies.
- Limited context modeling: Only the last system act is used (Markov assumption). The approach may miss multi-turn phenomena (ellipsis, coreference, corrections) and longer-range dependencies. No ablation quantifies the contribution of the context gating or explores richer context encoders.
- Dependence on specialized embeddings and language constraints: Performance relies on fixed, high-quality, English embeddings (Paragram-SL999). Portability to low-resource languages or domains without strong paraphrase resources is not evaluated. The claim of better generalization to unseen words is plausible but not explicitly tested (e.g., OOV paraphrase evaluation).
- Rule-based belief update and training/evaluation mismatch: The belief update uses a tuned λ rather than being learned end-to-end, and DSTC2 models are trained on transcripts but tested on ASR, potentially overstating robustness. More principled handling of ASR uncertainty (e.g., lattices/confusion networks) is not investigated.
- Experimental scope and analysis: Comparisons are against delexicalised baselines; stronger contemporary joint/end-to-end trackers (e.g., memory networks/attention models cited) are not included in head-to-head evaluations. Error analysis is minimal; the paper claims 0.96 joint goal on DSTC2 transcripts but offers limited breakdowns by slot/value, utterance length, or error types. Statistical testing details (unit of analysis) and effect sizes are not discussed.

Questions to Authors (optional)
- Scalability: What is the inference/training time as a function of number of slots/values? Have you tried candidate pruning (e.g., via retrieval) to reduce the number of slot–value evaluations per turn?
- Context: What is the impact of including more than the last system act or adding previous user turns via a lightweight RNN? Can you provide an ablation isolating the gains from request/confirm gating?
- ASR handling: Why not train on ASR hypotheses or confusion networks for DSTC2 to reduce the transcript-ASR mismatch? Did you try learning λ or integrating the update into the network?
- Embeddings: How sensitive are results to fine-tuning embeddings versus keeping them fixed? Can you report performance under constrained embedding settings (e.g., domain-only training) to assess portability to low-resource scenarios?
- Class imbalance and calibration: How do you handle the imbalance between positive and negative examples per slot–value during training? Is the 0.5 decision threshold optimal across slots, or did you calibrate per-slot thresholds?
- Reproducibility: Will you release code and the corrected DSTC2 and expanded WOZ 2.0 datasets/splits used in the paper? If so, what license and documentation will be provided?

Overall, the paper is clearly written and technically sound. The main novelty lies in validating that semantically specialized embeddings can replace manual lexicons for DST within a simple joint architecture. The contributions are incremental but practically valuable, with solid empirical evidence.
