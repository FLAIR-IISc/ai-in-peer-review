Summary and main contributions
This paper proposes Neural Belief Tracker (NBT), a lexicon-free approach to dialogue state tracking that operates entirely in a pre-trained semantic vector space and jointly handles SLU/DST without delexicalisation. The model instantiates two utterance encoders (simple n-gram DNN and a CNN), a semantic decoding module that matches utterance and candidate slot–value via element-wise interaction in embedding space, and a lightweight context gating mechanism keyed to the last system act. Evaluation on DSTC2 and WOZ 2.0 shows NBT outperforms delexicalisation-based baselines and matches or surpasses systems augmented with hand-crafted semantic dictionaries.

Contributions (in order of significance):
1) A conceptually novel DST framework that eliminates hand-crafted semantic dictionaries by reasoning over semantically specialised pre-trained word vectors and sharing parameters across slots/values.
2) An empirical demonstration that semantic specialisation of embeddings (Paragram-SL999) materially improves DST, moving beyond intrinsic metrics to a downstream task.
3) A simple but effective context gating mechanism that exploits the last system act to disambiguate short answers and confirmations without explicit slot/value surface matching.

Strengths
- Conceptual novelty with clear practical impact: Moving from delexicalisation to an embedding-space matcher for DST is a meaningful shift. It addresses a known scalability bottleneck (hand-built lexicons), and the parameter sharing across values/slots is elegant and likely to generalize to richer domains.

- Solid empirical gains and clean ablations: On both DSTC2 and WOZ 2.0, NBT consistently outperforms delex baselines and matches/surpasses lexicon-enhanced systems, which is the right comparator. The ablation showing Paragram > GloVe/random substantiates the core claim that semantic specialisation matters for DST.

- Simple, interpretable architecture: Element-wise interaction between utterance and candidate slot–value vectors and the gating with last system act are easy to implement and reason about. The design choices (fixed embeddings to retain semantic geometry, per-slot parameter sharing) are coherent and well-motivated.

- Robust handling of lexical/morphological variation: The model’s ability to recognize paraphrases without dictionaries (e.g., pricey ≈ expensive, inexpensive ≈ cheap) is demonstrated and precisely the kind of generalization we want in scalable DST.

- Clarity and reproducibility: The paper is well written, with explicit training details, public word vectors, a promise to release cleaned DSTC2 and expanded WOZ 2.0 data, and significance testing.

Weaknesses
- Limited context modeling: The Markov assumption that only the last system act matters is restrictive. Many real dialogues require longer-range context, coreference, and multi-turn disambiguation. The authors acknowledge that DSTC2 results jump to 0.96 on transcriptions, implying that the current handling of ASR and multi-turn phenomena is the main bottleneck; nonetheless, a more integrated context model (e.g., recurrent over turns or attention over prior acts) would strengthen the contribution.

- Scalability to large/complex ontologies: While the approach avoids dictionaries, it still enumerates all slot–value candidates and performs a binary decision per pair, which is O(|S||V|) per turn. There is no analysis of computational cost, pruning, or retrieval to make this feasible for large multi-domain ontologies.

- ASR integration and belief update are simplistic: Training on transcripts and evaluating on ASR (DSTC2) creates a mismatch; ASR is incorporated only via an N-best weighted sum, and the belief update uses a hand-tuned λ. A learned update or direct modeling over confusion networks/lattices would better support the claim of robustness in noisy settings.

- Evaluation scope: Results are confined to a single domain (Cambridge restaurants). The paper argues for better scalability to real-world domains, but there’s no cross-domain or zero-shot evaluation, nor analysis of cases where embeddings might mislead (antonyms, related-but-different values). Error analysis is limited.

- Dependence on specialized embeddings and OOV handling: The method’s success hinges on Paragram-SL999; GloVe helps less or not at all on WOZ, suggesting brittleness to the choice of embedding space. The paper does not discuss OOV/misspelling handling, coverage for domain-specific values, or whether limited fine-tuning could help without destroying semantic geometry.

Questions to authors (optional)
- Complexity/scalability: What is the per-turn runtime as a function of slot/value inventory? Do you employ any candidate pruning or retrieval (e.g., nearest neighbors in embedding space) before classification?

- ASR integration: Can λ be learned jointly, or can you directly train on N-best/confusion networks? Did you experiment with features from CNs/lattices or joint modeling of ASR confidence?

- OOV and domain terms: How do you handle OOVs, misspellings, or rare multiword values? Did you consider subword/character models to cover unseen forms while keeping the semantic geometry?

- Embeddings: Why fix the embeddings rather than allow limited fine-tuning with a regularizer that preserves specialisation? Did you try retrofitting during training or a bilinear similarity instead of element-wise product?

- Generalization: Any preliminary results for multi-domain or zero-shot values (e.g., unseen slot values that are semantically close to seen ones) to support the scalability claim?

Overall recommendation
I lean toward acceptance. The core idea—DST entirely in a semantically specialised embedding space without delexicalisation—is both novel and practically important, and the empirical evidence is convincing for the stated scope. The limitations (context modeling, ASR integration, scalability) are addressable and do not detract from the conceptual contribution.
