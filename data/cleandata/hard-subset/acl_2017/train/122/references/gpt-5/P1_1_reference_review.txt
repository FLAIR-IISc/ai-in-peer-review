Summary and contributions
This paper proposes Neural Belief Tracker (NBT), a family of neural models for dialogue state tracking that jointly perform SLU/DST without relying on delexicalization and hand-crafted semantic dictionaries. The key idea is to operate directly in a pre-trained, semantically specialized word embedding space, composing utterances and candidate slot-value pairs and using simple context gating based on the last system act. Two variants (NBT-DNN and NBT-CNN) differ in utterance encoding. The models are evaluated on DSTC2 (ASR) and WOZ 2.0 (typed), and an analysis shows the importance of semantic specialization of embeddings.

Main contributions (as I see them):
1) A simple, effective DST architecture that dispenses with delexicalization and hand-crafted semantic dictionaries by leveraging semantically specialized word vectors and a candidate-pair matching formulation.
2) Empirical evidence on two benchmarks that NBT matches or outperforms strong delexicalization-based models and significantly outperforms them when lexicons are unavailable, with additional analysis demonstrating the impact of embedding quality.
3) A lightweight context modeling mechanism (gating via similarity to last system act) that improves handling of confirmations and requests without heavy sequence modeling.

Strengths
- Removes reliance on semantic dictionaries without sacrificing accuracy: On both datasets, NBT achieves parity with or better than dictionary-augmented baselines, while substantially outperforming baselines without lexicons (especially on WOZ 2.0: +13.4 joint goal over the delex baseline). This addresses a key scalability bottleneck in practical DST.
- Solid, interpretable architecture with good parameter sharing: The candidate pair formulation allows most parameters to be shared across values (and largely across slots), avoiding per-value models typical in SLU and making the approach more data-efficient. The element-wise interaction between utterance and candidate vectors is simple and effective.
- Useful context handling with minimal machinery: The dot-product gating with the last system act (request/confirm) addresses a critical DST need (e.g., “yes/no” confirmations) without complex dialogue-level recurrence. The model design aligns with practical SDS pipelines that often condition strongly on the last system act.
- Careful analysis of embeddings: The comparison of random vs GloVe vs Paragram-SL999 strengthens claims about the necessity of semantic similarity (vs relatedness) for this task. The observed behavior across noisy (DSTC2) and clean (WOZ) settings is informative and practically relevant.
- Broad evaluation: Results on both ASR-based (DSTC2) and typed (WOZ 2.0) corpora demonstrate robustness to different noise/linguistic characteristics. Reporting joint goal and requests, plus significance testing against baselines, is appreciated.

Weaknesses
- Novelty is incremental: The main technical ingredients (pre-trained embeddings, shallow composition, element-wise interactions, CNN/DNN encoders) are standard; the novelty lies in applying them to DST to avoid delexicalization. While impactful, the conceptual advance is modest relative to prior joint SLU/DST and CNN/RNN-based trackers.
- Scalability and computational cost: The approach requires iterating over all slot-value pairs per turn (O(|S||V|)), which may become expensive in larger ontologies. The paper argues parameters can be shared, but does not quantify runtime/latency or memory for larger domains, nor propose pruning/approximation strategies.
- Limited context modeling: Only the last system act is used (Markov assumption). This is a strong heuristic; many dialogues require longer-range context (e.g., implicit references, corrections). There is no ablation quantifying the contribution of context gating, nor a comparison to models that incorporate more of the dialogue history.
- ASR robustness remains shallow: The belief update is rule-based over N-best with a tuned λ. There is no evaluation on confusion networks/lattices or direct modeling of ASR uncertainty. Given the DSTC2 remarks (0.96 on transcripts), a deeper ASR-aware integration would strengthen claims for noisy settings.
- Missing training details and imbalance handling: The candidate-pair dataset construction implies heavy class imbalance (many negatives per positive). The paper does not detail sampling strategies, class weighting, batch composition, or early stopping/regularization. Without this, reproducibility and fairness are harder to assess.
- Dependency on specialized embeddings: Results rely on Paragram-SL999; gains weaken with GloVe on WOZ. It would help to show robustness across other specialized embeddings, or whether light fine-tuning achieves similar effects. Also, multi-word value representation as simple summation is naive; alternatives (e.g., attention) are not explored.
- Limited ablations: Beyond embedding comparisons, the paper lacks ablations for key components (context gating on/off; r⊗c vs concatenation with increased capacity; fixed vs fine-tuned embeddings; NBT-DNN vs NBT-CNN under length-controlled subsets). This would clarify what drives gains.

Questions to authors
- Can you provide an ablation of the context modeling (remove dr and dc; request vs confirm separately) and quantify its impact on affirmations/negations and requests?
- How do you address class imbalance during training? Any negative sampling, class weighting, or threshold tuning beyond 0.5?
- What is the computational cost per turn as a function of ontology size? Any batching or pruning strategies for larger domains?
- Sensitivity to λ for belief updating: please report a small sweep and performance curves.
- Did you try fine-tuning embeddings (perhaps with a low learning rate) vs keeping them fixed? Any effect on generalization to unseen synonyms?
- How many runs/seeds are your results averaged over? Please report variance and significance vs dictionary-augmented baselines where relevant.
- For multi-word slot values, did you compare sum vs mean vs a small CNN/attention? Any degradation for longer values?
- Clarify which parameters are shared across slots/values (e.g., in NBT-DNN you keep separate slot-specific parameters; how much sharing is actually used in your reported models?).

Additional comments
- The small improvements over strong dictionary-augmented baselines on DSTC2 are still valuable given the removal of manual resources; emphasizing this “no lexicon” setting as the primary contribution would strengthen framing.
- The cleaned DSTC2 transcriptions are mentioned; please clarify precisely where they were used (training only?) and ensure fair comparability to prior work.
- A qualitative error analysis (e.g., synonym handling, antonyms, long-distance references) would add insight.
- Release of code and the cleaned data would materially improve reproducibility.
