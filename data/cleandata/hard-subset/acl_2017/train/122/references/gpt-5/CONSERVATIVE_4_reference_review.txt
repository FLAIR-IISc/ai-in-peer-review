Summary and contributions
This paper proposes Neural Belief Tracker (NBT), a joint SLU/DST framework that dispenses with delexicalized templates and hand-crafted semantic dictionaries by operating directly over pre-trained word vectors. Two instantiations are presented (DNN with cumulative n-grams; CNN over 1–3-grams), along with a simple last-system-act context gate and a rule-based belief update over ASR N-best lists. Experiments on DSTC2 (ASR) and WOZ 2.0 (typed) show statistically significant improvements over strong delexicalization baselines, and an analysis of different embeddings highlights benefits of semantically specialized vectors.

As I see them, the main contributions are:
1) Empirical finding: a simple, dictionary-free joint SLU/DST architecture using pre-trained, semantically specialized embeddings can match or exceed delexicalized systems on two benchmarks.
2) A pragmatic architecture that combines utterance–slot/value interaction via element-wise product with a lightweight last-system-act gating mechanism.
3) Dataset contribution: cleaned DSTC2 transcripts and an expanded WOZ 2.0 set (if released as claimed), which are useful to the community.

Strengths
1) Clear empirical demonstration that high-quality semantic embeddings reduce reliance on hand-crafted lexicons. On WOZ 2.0, NBT significantly outperforms the delexicalized baseline even when the latter is augmented with a semantic dictionary (goals 84.2–84.4 vs 83.7; requests 91.2–91.6 vs 87.6), supporting the central claim that dictionary-free tracking is feasible.
2) Competitive performance without domain-specific lexicons. On DSTC2, NBT-CNN (73.4 goals, 96.5 requests) matches the delexicalized+lexicon model (72.9, 95.7) and beats the plain delexicalized baseline (69.1, 95.7), indicating robustness to vocabulary variation.
3) Sensible probing of embedding choices. The comparison between random, GloVe, and Paragram-SL999 provides useful insight: semantically specialized vectors materially help, especially under ASR noise. This is a concrete, reproducible takeaway for practitioners.
4) Simplicity and transparency. The architecture, context gating, and rule-based belief update are straightforward, making the approach relatively easy to implement and to analyze. The per-candidate binary formulation also simplifies training data construction.
5) Potentially valuable data resources. The cleaned DSTC2 and expanded WOZ 2.0 are practically useful; if released, this would be an enduring contribution independent of model novelty.

Weaknesses
1) Limited validation scope and scalability concerns. Both datasets are within the same narrow restaurant domain and share the same ontology. The model still enumerates all slot–value pairs and learns slot-specific parameters (for NBT-DNN/CNN), so scalability to larger ontologies or multi-domain settings is untested. Claims about better scalability than delexicalization are not substantiated with experiments on larger ontologies or cross-domain transfer.
2) Incomplete baseline coverage and fairness issues. Comparisons are limited to two delexicalized trackers (Henderson et al. 2014; Wen et al. 2017). There is no head-to-head evaluation against contemporary joint neural DST models that do not rely on delexicalization (e.g., memory-network-based trackers, attention-based joint SLU/DST, sequence-labelling plus learned belief updates). Moreover, the baselines cannot exploit pre-trained embeddings, while NBT relies crucially on Paragram-SL999; this raises fairness concerns. A stronger baseline would incorporate embeddings as features or use subword models.
3) Weak context modelling and turn-level independence. The model only considers the last system act and uses simple dot-product gates. This ignores longer dialogue dependencies, corrections, co-reference, and multi-act/system phenomena. The belief update is rule-based with a tuned λ rather than learned jointly. The independence across candidate pairs can also yield poorly calibrated decisions; using a flat 0.5 threshold for requests is arbitrary and unvalidated.
4) Insufficient ablations beyond embeddings. While the embedding analysis is helpful, other core design choices are not rigorously probed: effect of context gating (remove/alter gates), element-wise product vs alternative interaction functions (numbers are not reported), freezing vs fine-tuning embeddings, per-slot parameter sharing vs full sharing, and the impact of CNN vs DNN as a function of utterance length. Given the modest gains on DSTC2 over the lexicon-augmented baseline, stronger ablations are needed to isolate what really drives improvements.
5) ASR robustness and training/testing mismatch. Models are trained on transcriptions but evaluated on ASR for DSTC2, which may disadvantage NBT or, at minimum, complicate comparisons to baselines that incorporate ASR features. The claim that “goal accuracy rises to 0.96 on transcriptions” is not informative for ASR settings and risks overclaiming. There is no analysis of N-best depth effects, ASR posterior calibration, or confusion network features—all standard in established pipelines.

Questions to authors
1) Scalability: What is the computational complexity as ontology size grows (number of values per slot, number of slots)? Have you profiled inference time and memory usage for larger ontologies? Can you share results on a larger or multi-domain ontology, or at least synthetic scaling experiments?
2) Baselines: Can you compare against at least one non-delexicalized joint neural DST (e.g., attention/memory-network-based) and/or a strong sequence labelling SLU + learned belief updater? Also, can you augment the delexicalized baseline with pre-trained embeddings to address fairness?
3) Context: How much does the last-act gating contribute? Please report ablations removing tq/ts/tv terms, and results using longer context (e.g., RNN over system acts). How does the model handle corrections and co-reference?
4) Calibration and coupling: Decisions are made independently per candidate and thresholded. Did you try a softmax over values per slot or a CRF-style global coupling to ensure mutual exclusivity? Any evidence of inconsistencies or calibration issues across values?
5) Embeddings: What is OOV coverage on DSTC2/WOZ for Paragram-SL999? Did you try fine-tuning embeddings or subword models to handle morphology and rare words? How sensitive are results to the specific semantic specialization method (e.g., other PPDB sizes)?
6) ASR: Have you trained on ASR N-best or confusion networks instead of transcriptions? How do results change with different N-best depths and λ? Please clarify how statistical significance was computed (per turn vs per dialogue).

Overall, the paper is well written and makes a practical, incremental contribution. However, given the narrow evaluation scope, limited baselines, and modest gains over lexicon-augmented delexicalized systems, I would need stronger empirical validation (scalability, broader baselines, and ablations) to be fully convinced.
