Summary and main contributions
- This paper proposes Neural Belief Tracker (NBT), a joint SLU/DST framework that operates entirely in pretrained word-vector space rather than via delexicalization and semantic dictionaries. It introduces two realizations (NBT-DNN and NBT-CNN), a lightweight context-gating mechanism tied to last system acts (request/confirm), and a simple ASR N-best belief update.
- Primary contribution: A dictionary-free, vector-space formulation of belief tracking that leverages semantically specialized embeddings to recognize paraphrases and lexical/morphological variation, removing a key scalability bottleneck of prior delexicalization-based DST.
- Secondary contribution: Empirical evidence on DSTC2 and WOZ 2.0, including an informative ablation on embedding types (random vs GloVe vs Paragram-SL999), demonstrating that semantic specialization of embeddings materially improves DST performance.
- Tertiary contribution: Practical, simple architectures with parameter efficiency that couple SLU and DST and yield statistically significant improvements over strong delexicalization baselines; release of cleaned DSTC2 transcriptions and an expanded WOZ dataset is also valuable.

Strengths
- Novel and impactful shift away from delexicalization/dictionaries: Replacing exact-match templates with vector-space reasoning is an important conceptual step for scalable DST. It directly addresses a known pain point—manual semantic dictionaries—and shows that semantically informed embeddings can substitute for hand-engineered paraphrase resources.
- Solid empirical gains, especially on richer text (WOZ 2.0): Both NBT variants significantly improve joint goal accuracy and request accuracy over competitive baselines. Improvements are largest in WOZ, where language variation is greater, aligning with the paper’s central thesis.
- Clear demonstration of the importance of semantically specialized embeddings: The ablation showing Paragram-SL999 > GloVe > random (especially on noisy DSTC2) connects intrinsic embedding properties to downstream DST performance. This is a useful and broadly relevant result for the community.
- Simple, efficient modeling with interpretable components: The architecture is easy to implement and train, and the element-wise interaction plus context gating provides a transparent mechanism for how slot/value semantics and last system acts influence decisions. This lowers barriers to adoption in production systems.
- Practicality in noisy settings: The ASR N-best weighted combination is simple yet sensible, and the approach maintains strong performance under ASR noise relative to baselines, with promising transcription-only upper bound (0.96 joint goals) indicating modeling headroom.

Weaknesses
- Limited scope and generality: Experiments are confined to a single domain (Cambridge restaurants) with three informable slots. There is no cross-domain evaluation or tests on large ontologies to substantiate scalability claims beyond removing dictionaries. Demonstrating zero-shot generalization to unseen values/slots would strongly reinforce the core premise.
- Shallow context modeling: The model only conditions on the last system acts via dot-product gates. It does not track longer histories, anaphora, or multi-turn ellipsis, which commonly occur in real dialogs. A comparison against models with richer state encoders (RNN/Transformer over dialog turns) would contextualize the trade-off between simplicity and coverage.
- Heuristic belief update and ASR handling: The belief update is a hand-tuned interpolation (λ) over N-best lists. There is no end-to-end learning of turn accumulation, no use of lattice/confusion network structure, and no uncertainty-aware modeling beyond N-best weighting. This may constrain performance in harder ASR conditions.
- Incomplete comparisons and ablations: Baselines are primarily delexicalization-based. Missing are comparisons to contemporary joint SLU/DST sequence-labeling or attention-based models that don’t rely on dictionaries. Ablations on key NBT choices (element-wise product vs dot/concat, removal of context gates, CNN vs DNN under varied utterance lengths) would isolate what drives gains besides embeddings.
- Parameter sharing claims vs implementation details: The paper argues for sharing across slots/values, but NBT-DNN maintains slot-specific parameters for representation learning (W^s_n, b^s_n). The extent and effect of sharing are unclear. Additionally, fixing embeddings shifts burden to coverage; OOV handling and morphological robustness without subword modeling are not addressed.

Questions to authors
- Parameter sharing: Do you use separate representation parameters per slot throughout, or only for some components? Did you try full parameter sharing across slots, and how did it affect performance and sample efficiency?
- OOV and morphology: How are tokens not present in Paragram handled at test time? Any fallback (UNK, subword/char composition)? How sensitive is performance to OOV rate?
- Zero-/few-shot generalization: Can NBT detect values unseen during training but present in the embedding vocabulary, given only their names? Any empirical evidence on unseen values/slots or cross-domain transfer?
- Context ablation: What is the quantitative impact of the request/confirm gating terms? How does performance change if you remove or vary the context window beyond the last system act?
- Interaction function: You report that concat underperformed. Can you provide numbers comparing element-wise product vs dot-product vs concat, holding other factors fixed?
- Reproducibility: Will you release code in addition to the cleaned DSTC2 and expanded WOZ 2.0 datasets, and the exact preprocessing needed to reproduce the results (including the orthographic corrections)?

Overall, this paper introduces a clear and compelling new direction for DST by moving to vector-space reasoning with semantically specialized embeddings, backed by solid evidence. Despite limitations in scope and some missing comparisons, the conceptual contribution is significant and timely. I lean toward acceptance.
