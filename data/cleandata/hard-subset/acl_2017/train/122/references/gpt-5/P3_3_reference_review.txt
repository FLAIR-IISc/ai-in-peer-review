Summary and main contributions
This paper proposes the Neural Belief Tracker (NBT), a joint SLU/DST model that replaces delexicalisation and hand-crafted semantic dictionaries with reasoning over pre-trained word embeddings. The model scores each candidate slot-value pair given the current user utterance and preceding system acts, with two alternative utterance encoders (sum-over-ngrams MLP and CNN), a semantic “decoder” based on element-wise interaction with the candidate pair, and a simple context gating using the last system acts (request/confirm). A rule-based belief update combines ASR N-best posteriors with a moving-average over turns. The work evaluates on DSTC2 (ASR) and WOZ 2.0 (typed), reporting statistically significant improvements over delexicalisation-based baselines, and shows that semantically specialized embeddings (Paragram-SL999) outperform random/GloVe.

As I see them, the main contributions are:
1) A data-driven DST framework that removes reliance on hand-crafted semantic dictionaries by leveraging fixed, semantically specialized word embeddings and candidate-wise interaction (primary).
2) An effective, lightweight context gating mechanism that incorporates the last system acts into turn-level decisions without heavy sequence modeling (secondary).
3) An empirical demonstration that semantic specialization of embeddings improves downstream DST performance, beyond intrinsic evaluations (secondary).

Strengths
- Clear advance beyond delexicalisation/dictionaries: The approach directly addresses a widely acknowledged scalability bottleneck in joint SLU/DST—manual semantic lexicons—by operating entirely in an embedding space. The reported gains on WOZ 2.0 (joint goals +13.4 over baseline; comparable to or better than baseline+lexicon) are especially compelling for richer, paraphrase-heavy language where delexicalisation is brittle.

- Solid, interpretable architecture with principled interactions: The element-wise product between utterance and candidate slot-value representations is a simple, parameter-efficient way to induce a learned similarity metric. The context gating (dot products between slot/value vectors and system act arguments) is intuitive and explains improvements with confirm/request behaviors, where user evidence can be implicit.

- Careful analysis of embedding choice: The study isolates the effect of embeddings (random vs GloVe vs Paragram-SL999) and demonstrates consistent gains for specialized vectors, particularly on noisy ASR data. This is a meaningful empirical contribution for the DST community.

- Broadly applicable and efficient: The candidate-wise binary formulation with shared parameters and fixed embeddings allows handling unseen paraphrases and should transfer across domains where ontologies are enumerated but lexicons are not. The model’s simplicity suggests practicality in resource-constrained settings.

- Strong performance on both noisy and clean settings: The model is competitive on DSTC2 and substantially stronger on WOZ 2.0. The reported 0.96 joint goal accuracy on DSTC2 transcriptions indicates the approach captures semantic variation well; remaining errors are driven by ASR, aligning with the paper’s claims.

Weaknesses
- Limited dialog context modeling: The model only conditions on the immediately preceding system acts (request/confirm) via scalar gates and ignores the broader dialog history and user turns. This Markov assumption may mis-handle context-dependent phenomena (e.g., coreference, corrections) beyond these two acts. An ablation showing the quantitative contribution of the context module would help; likewise, comparisons with recurrent/attention-based history models would clarify the trade-offs.

- Independence across values and heuristic belief update: Scoring is performed independently per candidate, followed by per-slot selection with a threshold and “choose max,” which can yield uncalibrated probabilities and inconsistencies. A per-slot normalized objective (e.g., softmax over values + none) or a learned recurrent belief updater could reduce error propagation and better capture persistence and corrections. The current mixing coefficient λ and N-best marginalization are rule-based and tuned on dev; learned updates could be stronger and more principled.

- Partial parameter sharing and unclear generalization claims: While the paper emphasizes sharing across values/slots, several components are slot-specific (superscript s for encoders). This weakens the claim of maximum sharing and raises questions about scalability to larger ontologies and multi-domain scenarios. A variant with full sharing across slots and an empirical cost/benefit analysis would strengthen the scalability argument.

- Limited comparison and analysis depth: The baselines are delexicalisation-based (Henderson et al., Wen et al.) and do not include more recent jointly trained end-to-end trackers or strong SLU sequence taggers coupled to DST (even at the time, several RNN/CRF SLU + DST combinations existed). The paper would benefit from (i) ablations (context gating on/off; element-wise vs concatenation; DNN vs CNN beyond aggregate metrics), (ii) error analysis (negation, coreference, rare values), and (iii) coverage/OOV statistics for embeddings.

- Training/testing mismatch and fairness concerns on DSTC2: Models are trained on transcriptions but evaluated on ASR hypotheses. Some DSTC2 systems trained directly on ASR/confusion networks, and best-performing challenge systems often exploited both SLU and raw ASR features. Clarifying training conditions for baselines and whether NBT uses any ASR-level features beyond N-best would ensure fair comparison.

Questions to authors
- How large is the OOV rate for slots/values and user tokens in DSTC2/WOZ 2.0 under Paragram-SL999? How are OOVs handled, and how sensitive is performance to OOV rate?

- What is the quantitative impact of the context gating? Please provide an ablation removing the context module or using only request or only confirm gates.

- Did you try fine-tuning embeddings (perhaps with a low LR) or counter-fitting/specializing them on in-domain ontologies? How does performance change versus freezing?

- Can you report per-slot performance and calibration diagnostics? For informable slots, how often do multiple values surpass 0.5, and how often does “choose max” override a correct multi-valued hypothesis?

- How does the model scale to larger ontologies (number of candidate pairs, training time, memory)? Any preliminary evidence on multi-domain settings?

Additional comments
- Clarify whether slot-specific parameters are necessary; a fully shared encoder might improve generalization and reduce memory. 
- It would be helpful to release code and the cleaned DSTC2 text to facilitate reproducibility.
