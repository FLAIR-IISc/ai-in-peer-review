Summary and Contributions
- This paper proposes the Neural Belief Tracker (NBT), a neural dialogue state tracker that dispenses with hand-crafted semantic dictionaries by operating directly over pre-trained, semantically specialized word vectors. NBT couples SLU and DST, using learned utterance, candidate slot-value, and context representations to make per-turn binary decisions for each ontology item, followed by a simple ASR N-best belief update.
- The paper provides two instantiated architectures (NBT-DNN and NBT-CNN) with a lightweight context mechanism based on the last system acts (request/confirm) and an element-wise interaction between utterance and candidate pair. It reports significant gains over strong delexicalisation-based baselines on DSTC2 and WOZ 2.0, especially when semantic lexicons are not available.
- The work empirically isolates the effect of word vector quality, showing consistent gains from semantically specialized vectors (Paragram-SL999) over GloVe and random initialization, thereby providing evidence that semantic specialization improves downstream DST.

Strengths
- Clear empirical gains without hand-crafted lexicons: On both DSTC2 and WOZ 2.0, NBT models match or surpass delexicalisation-based trackers augmented with semantic dictionaries and significantly outperform baselines without such resources. This directly addresses scalability concerns and reduces domain engineering effort.
- Principled use of semantically specialized embeddings: The comparison across random, GloVe, and Paragram vectors demonstrates that semantic similarity (as opposed to mere relatedness) measurably improves belief tracking, especially under ASR noise (DSTC2). This isolates a concrete, generalizable design choice.
- Simple, data-efficient architecture: The models use fixed pre-trained embeddings, shallow composition (DNN/CNN) over n-grams, an element-wise interaction for semantic decoding, and a minimal context gate tied to last system acts. This design is technically sound, easy to reproduce, and likely to be robust with modest data.
- Sound evaluation setup and reporting: The work evaluates both noisy (DSTC2) and text (WOZ 2.0) settings, reports joint goal and request accuracy, includes statistical significance testing, and presents an ablation on word vectors. The belief update over ASR N-best lists is explicit and standard.
- Clarity and presentation: The architecture is clearly described with equations and figures. The problem formulation, training setup, datasets, and update mechanism are well specified, facilitating replication.

Weaknesses
- Limited context modeling: The model uses only the last system act as context via simple dot-product gates. It does not incorporate richer multi-turn user history, coreference, or longer-range dependencies, which can be important in realistic dialogues. This choice may limit performance in more complex conversational settings.
- Narrow domain and ontology scale: Experiments are restricted to a single, small domain (restaurants) with three informable slots. Although the approach is claimed to scale, no empirical evidence is given on larger ontologies or multi-domain setups. The per-turn iteration over all slot-value pairs could become costly as ontologies grow.
- Dependence on specialized embeddings and language coverage: The strongest results rely on semantically specialized Paragram vectors. The approach assumes good coverage of domain vocabulary; OOV handling and cross-lingual applicability are not evaluated. It is unclear how performance holds in low-resource languages or domains with sparse paraphrastic coverage.
- Partial parameter sharing: While the text argues for broad parameter sharing, the implementation maintains separate parameter sets per slot in the representation learning components. Cross-slot sharing is not evaluated, and the claimed potential for sharing “across all slots” is not demonstrated, leaving open questions about scalability and sample efficiency.
- Baseline coverage and ablations: While comparisons to strong delexicalisation-based trackers are provided, there is no head-to-head evaluation against more recent attention/memory-network-based DST models in the same setting. Ablations on the context gates, the element-wise interaction vs concatenation, and the belief-update coefficient are limited, making it hard to attribute gains precisely among components.

Questions to Authors (Optional)
- Parameter sharing: You maintain separate parameters per slot (superscript s). Did you attempt full cross-slot sharing, and how did it affect performance and data efficiency?
- OOV and coverage: How are tokens not present in the pre-trained embedding vocabulary handled at test time? What proportion of OOVs occurred in DSTC2/WOZ, and what is the sensitivity to OOV rates?
- Context ablations: What is the performance impact of removing the request/confirm context gates or replacing them with a learned attention over the last K turns? Any evidence on multi-turn user-context use?
- Computational scalability: What is the runtime per turn as a function of ontology size? Have you profiled iteration over all slot-value pairs for larger ontologies?
- Belief update: The update uses a fixed λ tuned on development data. How sensitive are results to λ, and how do N-best list lengths affect performance? Have you tried using ASR confusion networks?
- Embedding specialization: Did you try fine-tuning embeddings during training, possibly with constraints to preserve semantic similarity, and how did that compare to keeping them fixed?

Additional comments
- The paper mentions manual correction of DSTC2 transcriptions; please clarify whether this affects training only, and confirm that test-time evaluation uses the official ASR outputs to maintain comparability.
