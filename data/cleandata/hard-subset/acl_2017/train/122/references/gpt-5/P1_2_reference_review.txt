Summary
This paper proposes Neural Belief Tracker (NBT), a family of neural models for dialogue state tracking that jointly performs SLU/DST without delexicalization or hand-crafted semantic dictionaries. The core idea is to operate entirely in a pre-trained word vector space, composing representations of the user utterance, the preceding system acts, and a candidate slot–value pair. Two variants are presented: NBT-DNN (summed n-grams + MLP) and NBT-CNN (convolutions over 1–3-grams + max pooling). A semantic decoder computes an interaction r ⊙ c between utterance and candidate pair, and a simple context module gates the utterance based on last-turn system acts (request/confirm). A rule-based belief update aggregates N-best ASR hypotheses and prior belief. Experiments on DSTC2 and an expanded WOZ 2.0 dataset show that NBT outperforms strong delexicalization-based baselines, especially when no semantic dictionaries are available, and that semantically specialized word vectors (Paragram-SL999) yield consistent gains over GloVe/random.

Main contributions (as I see them)
1) A practical, dictionary-free joint SLU/DST approach that reasons over pre-trained word vectors, avoiding delexicalization, with demonstrated improvements on two benchmarks.
2) A clear demonstration that semantically specialized embeddings materially improve downstream DST performance, including ablations comparing Paragram vs GloVe vs random.
3) A simple yet effective context-gating mechanism for incorporating last system acts (request/confirm) into the decision process.

Strengths
- Removes reliance on semantic dictionaries while maintaining or improving accuracy. The NBT variants match or outperform dictionary-augmented baselines: on WOZ 2.0, joint goal 84.2–84.4 vs 83.7 and requests 91.2–91.6 vs 87.6; on DSTC2, joint goal 73.4 vs 72.9 and requests 96.5 vs 95.7. This supports the claim of better scalability to real-world domains.
- Methodologically simple and modular. The architecture is straightforward (r ⊙ c similarity, last-turn context gating, per-candidate binary decision) and can be trained with standard cross-entropy. The design makes the approach reproducible and easy to adapt.
- Strong evidence for the value of semantic specialization. The ablation showing Paragram > GloVe > random (especially on noisy DSTC2) makes a compelling case that injecting semantic similarity into embeddings benefits DST, moving beyond intrinsic evaluations.
- Robustness to paraphrasing without large supervision. Fixing pre-trained vectors and sharing parameters across values leverages lexical generalization (e.g., “inexpensive” ~ “cheap”) without word-level annotations or value-specific parameters, addressing a known bottleneck of earlier SLU or delexicalized systems.
- Sensible treatment of ASR uncertainty and context. The N-best weighted aggregation and gating by system request/confirm addresses two critical practical issues in SDS; the authors also identify that ASR errors are the dominant failure mode (with reported 0.96 joint goal on DSTC2 transcripts), guiding future work.

Weaknesses
- Limited context modeling and dialogue dynamics. The model only uses the previous system acts (Markov assumption) and ignores longer-range dialogue phenomena (e.g., multi-turn references, corrections, anaphora). This likely caps performance in more complex settings and could be contrasted against RNN/attention-based dialogue context models.
- Scalability to large ontologies is not analyzed. The model iterates over all slot–value pairs and uses per-slot parameters in representation layers (superscript s), limiting cross-slot sharing despite the stated goal. There is no complexity/latency analysis or experiments on larger ontologies, which is crucial to the paper’s scalability claim.
- Belief update is heuristic and decoupled. The turn-level classifier and the temporal smoothing (single λ) are not learned jointly; no calibration analysis is provided. This may yield suboptimal belief dynamics and fragile behavior across domains or acoustic conditions.
- Evaluation breadth and novelty scope are constrained. Both datasets are single-domain Cambridge restaurant tasks; improvements on DSTC2 are modest (73.4 vs 72.9) and significance relies on paired t-tests without reporting variance across random seeds. Claims of being the first to use pre-trained vectors for DST are plausible, but comparisons to then-contemporary end-to-end neural DST or attention-based joint models could be expanded.
- Limited ablation and error analysis beyond embeddings. It is unclear how much each component contributes (semantic decoder vs context gating vs CNN vs DNN). There is no analysis on handling negation and contradictions (e.g., “not expensive”), despite known pitfalls of distributional similarity. The paper also notes distributional antonymy issues; specialized vectors mitigate but may not fully resolve this.

Questions to Authors
- Context and dynamics: How does performance change if you incorporate more than the last system act (e.g., attention over several turns or an RNN over dialogue context)? Did you attempt multiple-turn context and, if so, why was it not adopted?
- Sharing vs per-slot parameters: You state parameters can be shared across slots/values, yet representation layers are per-slot. What is the impact of full sharing across slots? Can you report results with shared parameters and discuss the trade-offs?
- Scalability and efficiency: What are the computational costs (per turn, per dialogue) as ontology size grows? Do you prune candidate slot–value pairs, and what is the inference time on typical ontologies larger than DSTC2?
- Belief update calibration: Did you try learning λ or making it slot-specific? Alternatively, can you integrate the temporal smoothing into the neural architecture (e.g., recurrent belief updates) and does that improve calibration/accuracy?
- Robustness and seeds: How sensitive are results to random initialization? Please report mean/std over multiple runs for the main metrics to substantiate significance claims.
- Negation and polarity: How does the model handle negation (e.g., “not pricey”), and is this explicitly modeled beyond relying on embeddings? Can you provide targeted evaluation or examples?

Additional comments
- Releasing the cleaned DSTC2 transcriptions and the expanded WOZ 2.0 would be highly valuable. A qualitative error analysis illustrating success/failure on paraphrases, antonyms, and confirm/request cases would strengthen the paper.
