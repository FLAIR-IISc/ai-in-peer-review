Summary and main contributions
- Proposes the Neural Belief Tracker (NBT), a slot-value binary classifier that eschews delexicalization and semantic dictionaries by operating directly on pre-trained word embeddings. Two variants are presented: NBT-DNN (sum-of-ngrams + MLP) and NBT-CNN (convolution + max-pooling), both with a simple semantic decoder (element-wise product between utterance and slot-value embeddings) and minimal context gating based on the last system act.
- Demonstrates that semantically-specialized word vectors (Paragram-SL999) materially improve DST performance, outperforming delexicalized baselines with and without hand-built lexicons on DSTC2 and WOZ 2.0; includes a word-vector ablation (random vs GloVe vs Paragram).
- Provides a simple belief update rule over ASR N-best lists and reports joint goal and request accuracy gains on standard datasets (including an expanded WOZ 2.0).

Strengths
- Clear, simple architecture with reasonable inductive bias: using a shared embedding-based interaction to avoid heavy ontology-specific engineering is compelling. The element-wise interaction d = r ⊙ c and the small decision head reduce overfitting on small DST datasets.
- Empirical demonstration that semantically specialized embeddings provide tangible gains over standard distributional vectors and random initialization. The targeted ablation strengthens the claim that semantic similarity (not just relatedness) matters for DST.
- Competitive results without semantic dictionaries. On both DSTC2 and WOZ 2.0, NBT variants match or exceed delexicalized baselines and retain performance when lexicon resources are removed, supporting the claim of better scalability.
- Practical handling of ASR via N-best posterior-weighted averaging is straightforward and effective; improvements on noisy DSTC2 are reported and the model can exploit clean text (WOZ) even more strongly.
- Potential for parameter sharing and deployment: the approach is ontology-agnostic at the feature level and could be extended to larger domains.

Weaknesses
- Limited novelty and modeling depth. The core components (sum/CNN encoders, element-wise product similarity, last-utterance gating, independent binary classifiers per slot-value) are standard. The context model is extremely shallow (Markov, last system act only) and cannot capture cross-turn phenomena (coreference, corrections, implicit constraints) common in DST. The “joint SLU/DST” claim is somewhat overstated: the model is essentially a turn-level classifier with a post-hoc rule-based belief update.
- Evaluation breadth and baselines are insufficient. Comparisons omit stronger contemporary end-to-end trackers that operate over word embeddings and longer context (e.g., attention-based RNNs, memory networks, incremental LSTMs cited in the related work). The strongest reported DSTC2 systems leveraged both SLU and ASR features; it is unclear whether the delexicalized baselines here are the best available variants. No ablations of key NBT components (context gating, element-wise product vs bilinear/dot/concat, per-slot parameterization) are provided.
- Training/testing mismatch and data usage. Models are trained on transcriptions but tested on ASR for DSTC2, likely disadvantaging both NBT and baselines in unclear ways; no experiments training on ASR n-best or lattices. The manual “cleaning” of DSTC2 transcriptions is mentioned but not quantified; it threatens reproducibility. The expanded WOZ 2.0 split is new and only promised for release; without immediate availability, the claims are hard to verify.
- Methodological and statistical concerns. The belief update mixes independent probabilities, applies a 0.5 threshold to define Vt and then separately chooses the argmax for informable slots—this is ad hoc and not properly normalized. λ is tuned on dev, but no sensitivity analysis is shown. Statistical testing details are missing (unit of analysis, independence assumptions for a paired t-test over turns are questionable). Class imbalance (many negatives per positive per slot-value) is not addressed (no reweighting, sampling strategy, or calibration analysis).
- Inconsistencies and missing details. The text claims parameters “can be shared across all slots,” yet Eq. (3)/(6) use slot-specific parameters (superscript s). How much sharing is actually used? Hyperparameters and training details are sparse: learning rate, batch size, epochs/early stopping, regularization, initialization, and thresholding are not described; OOV handling and multiword value composition (summing) are simplistic and unvalidated. The model assumes availability of system acts for context; for WOZ 2.0, how are these acts obtained?
- Presentation quality issues. Numerous typos/formatting artifacts (dangling figure captions mid-text, missing footnotes, inconsistent variable names: syst vs syst−1; errors in references with “?”, escaped characters, inconsistent author names/diacritics). These hinder clarity and reproducibility.

Questions to authors
- What is the exact unit for the paired t-test (turns, dialogues), and how do you address dependence between turns within a dialogue? Please report p-values and effect sizes.
- Do you train on ASR hypotheses or only on transcriptions? If only transcriptions, what is the gap when training on N-best with posterior weighting vs transcriptions only?
- Clarify parameter sharing: which components are slot-specific (superscript s) vs globally shared? Did you try fully shared parameters across slots?
- How are system dialogue acts produced for WOZ 2.0? Manual annotation or automatic derivation? What is annotation quality/agreement?
- How do you handle OOV tokens and multiword slot values beyond summation? Any subword modeling attempted?
- Provide an ablation comparing element-wise product to dot product, bilinear, and concatenation+MLP under matched parameter budgets.
- Sensitivity of performance to λ and to the 0.5 threshold; calibration plots or Expected Calibration Error would help.
- Can you compare against at least one stronger non-delexicalized baseline (e.g., attention-based RNN or memory network) on the same splits?

Additional comments
- Fix belief update equations (typo “syst” vs “syst−1”) and clarify normalization across values per slot.
- Report per-slot accuracies and confusion patterns; include dev set results and early stopping criteria.
- Correct references (years, venues, escaping), ensure all footnotes are present, and remove mid-text figure artifacts.
- Temper the “first to” claim or precisely scope it (e.g., first to show extrinsic gains from semantically specialized vectors in DST).
- Please release code, cleaned DSTC2, and the expanded WOZ 2.0 split to support reproducibility.

Overall, while the idea is sensible and results are promising, the limited novelty, shallow context modeling, insufficient baselines/ablations, and numerous methodological/presentation issues collectively undermine the contribution.
