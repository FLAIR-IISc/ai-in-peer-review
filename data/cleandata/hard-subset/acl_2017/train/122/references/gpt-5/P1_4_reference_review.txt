Summary
This paper proposes Neural Belief Tracker (NBT), a family of joint SLU/DST models that eliminate the need for hand-crafted semantic dictionaries by reasoning entirely in pre-trained word embedding spaces. Two variants are introduced: NBT-DNN (sum of n-grams with MLP) and NBT-CNN (convolutional filters over n-grams). The model composes representations of the user utterance, a candidate slot-value pair, and the last system act (request/confirm), fuses them via element-wise similarity, and applies a small decision network. A simple rule-based belief update combines ASR N-best and prior state. On two restaurant-domain datasets (DSTC2 and WOZ 2.0), NBT outperforms delexicalization-based baselines without semantic lexicons and matches/surpasses them with lexicons. Ablations show semantically specialized Paragram vectors outperform GloVe and random.

Main contributions (as I see them)
1) A practical, dictionary-free joint SLU/DST architecture that leverages pre-trained, semantically specialized word vectors and simple interaction mechanisms (element-wise product + last-system-act gating) to handle lexical/morphological variation.
2) Empirical evidence that specialized embedding spaces materially improve downstream DST performance, especially under ASR noise (DSTC2), and that NBT matches or beats strong delexicalized baselines without hand-crafted resources.
3) A clear, reproducible framework (two instantiations, DNN and CNN) with minimal hand-engineering and a simple belief update mechanism that performs competitively.

Strengths
- Removes reliance on semantic dictionaries while retaining or surpassing performance: On DSTC2, NBT-CNN (73.4 joint goal, 96.5 requests) beats a delexicalized baseline (69.1/95.7) and is competitive with/above the baseline augmented with dictionaries (72.9). On WOZ 2.0 (typed), NBT (~84/92) substantially improves over the baseline (70.8/87.1) and even exceeds the lexicon-augmented baseline on both goals and requests. This directly addresses a known scalability bottleneck.
- Demonstrates the value of semantically specialized embeddings in DST: The ablation showing Paragram-SL999 > GloVe > random (especially under ASR noise) is convincing, moves beyond intrinsic evaluations, and provides a practical takeaway for the community.
- Simple and interpretable architecture: The explicit interaction between utterance and candidate pair via element-wise multiplication, and context gating that encodes the last system request/confirm act, are elegant, low-parameter choices that likely contribute to robustness on small datasets.
- Sensible treatment of ASR with N-best fusion: The belief update is transparent and easy to integrate with existing SDS pipelines, and yields strong results on DSTC2.
- Reproducibility and clarity: Model details (filter sizes, hidden layer dimensions, fixed embeddings), datasets, and baselines are described clearly; the plan to release cleaned DSTC2 transcriptions and WOZ 2.0 expansion increases impact.

Weaknesses
- Limited domain and ontology scope: All experiments are on a single small restaurant domain. The claim of scalability (parameter sharing, dictionary-free operation) would be stronger with larger ontologies or multi-domain settings. The current results may not generalize to complex schemas, many values, or compositional constraints.
- Shallow context modelling: The “Markov” assumption that only the last system act matters could fail in dialogues with longer-range dependencies, corrections, or implicit grounding. The paper lacks ablations quantifying how much of the gain comes from context gating vs. semantic decoding, and whether deeper discourse modeling (e.g., RNNs over turns) would help or is unnecessary.
- Dependence on high-quality pre-trained embeddings: The approach hinges on coverage and quality of pre-trained vectors (and specifically specialized ones). OOV handling, domain shift, and low-resource languages are not addressed. Keeping embeddings fixed may limit adaptation; conversely, fine-tuning might harm generalization—this trade-off is not explored.
- Baseline competitiveness and fairness: While the baselines are reasonable delexicalized systems, the DSTC2 literature includes stronger systems that combine SLU and rich ASR features. It is unclear whether the comparison uses the best available configurations or recent end-to-end trackers. Also, the NBT belief update uses a tuned λ and thresholding; the sensitivity of results to these choices is not reported.
- Incomplete analysis: No per-slot results, error breakdowns, or complexity/runtime analysis. The claim that DSTC2 goal accuracy reaches 0.96 on transcriptions is striking; more detail (e.g., per-turn vs. dialogue, effect of update rule) would help assess whether the core semantic model or the update heuristic drives this number.

Questions to Authors
- Ablations: What is the contribution of each component? (a) without context gating, (b) replacing element-wise product by dot product or concatenation, (c) sharing vs. per-slot parameterization in the utterance encoder, (d) fine-tuning vs. freezing embeddings.
- How does NBT scale with ontology size? What are the computational/latency implications of iterating over all slot-value pairs? Any pruning strategies?
- OOV and multiword handling: How are unseen words/values handled at test time? Does summing multiword value vectors perform better than alternative composition (e.g., averaging, attention)?
- ASR settings: What N-best size is used, and how sensitive are results to N? Have you tried confusion networks or lattices?
- Generalization: Have you tried training on one dataset and testing on the other, or cross-domain adaptation? Any preliminary multi-domain results?

Additional comments
- Clarify whether system acts are assumed known (gold) at test time and whether this assumption holds in deployed systems.
- Consider reporting per-slot accuracies and calibration of P(s,v) to facilitate downstream policy learning.

Overall, this is a solid, practical contribution with clear empirical benefits and minimal reliance on hand-crafted resources. With stronger ablations and broader evaluation, it would be a compelling advance for DST. I lean toward acceptance.
