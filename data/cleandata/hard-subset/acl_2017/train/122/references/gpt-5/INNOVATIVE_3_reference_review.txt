Summary and main contributions
This paper proposes Neural Belief Tracker (NBT), a family of models for dialogue state tracking that abandons delexicalization and hand-crafted semantic dictionaries, and instead performs joint SLU/DST entirely in a pre-trained word vector space. Two instantiations (NBT-DNN, NBT-CNN) compose utterance and system act representations, compare them to candidate slot-value pairs via element-wise interactions, and integrate minimal context via gating on the previous system act. On DSTC2 and WOZ 2.0, NBT matches or surpasses dictionary-augmented delexicalized baselines and clearly outperforms them without lexicons; ablations show that semantically specialized embeddings (Paragram-SL999) are key.

Contributions (as I see them, strongest first)
1) Conceptual shift: A dictionary-free, embedding-based joint SLU/DST framework that reasons directly in a semantically specialized vector space rather than relying on delexicalization and hand-crafted lexicons.
2) Empirical evidence that specialized word vectors materially improve downstream DST, not just intrinsic similarity metrics, including an ablation vs. random and GloVe.
3) A simple, scalable architecture (candidate-pair scoring with parameter sharing and minimal context gating) that achieves strong results on two benchmarks, especially when lexicons are unavailable.

Strengths
- Novelty and direction-setting: Moving belief tracking away from fragile delexicalization to vector-space reasoning is a clear conceptual advance with strong practical implications for scalability to richer language and larger ontologies. The use of semantically specialized embeddings in DST is original and impactful.

- Strong and clear empirical wins without hand-crafted resources: On both DSTC2 and WOZ 2.0, NBT outperforms delexicalized baselines without semantic dictionaries, and it matches/surpasses the dictionary-augmented variants (e.g., WOZ goal accuracy ~84% vs. 83.7% with lexicon; DSTC2 ~73.4% vs. 72.9%). This directly supports the central claim that NBT removes dependence on brittle lexicons.

- Insightful ablation on word vector quality: Demonstrating that Paragram-SL999 > GloVe > random (especially on noisy DSTC2) is both informative and novel, providing compelling evidence that semantic specialization benefits a real downstream task.

- Elegant context handling for confirmations/requests: The gating mechanism that conditions on the last system act (dot-product gates between system act arguments and candidate slot/value) is simple, interpretable, and effective for phenomena like “yes/no” confirmations.

- Practicality and simplicity: The training setup is straightforward (binary decisions over candidate pairs, frozen pretrained vectors) and shares parameters across values; this is attractive for domains lacking extensive annotated SLU resources or lexicons.

Weaknesses
- Limited domain/generalization evidence: All experiments are within a single, small ontology (Cambridge restaurants). Claims about scalability to larger/multi-domain settings are plausible but unvalidated. Iterating over all slot-value pairs is O(|S||V|) per turn; practical runtime and memory footprints for larger ontologies are not reported.

- Simplistic context modeling: Only the last system act is considered (Markovian assumption), with no modeling of longer user/system history, coreference, or discourse phenomena beyond confirmations/requests. This may cap performance in more complex dialogues.

- Dependence on a fixed ontology and closed-world candidate enumeration: The model presupposes an ontology and cannot propose unseen values, handle open vocabularies, or perform value tracking when users mention out-of-ontology entities. This limits applicability in more open-ended settings.

- Embedding reliance and freezing: The approach hinges on high-quality, specialized embeddings and freezes them. This may hinder adaptation to domain-specific jargon or other languages without comparable specialized resources; the trade-off between freezing vs. fine-tuning is not explored.

- Belief update and ASR robustness: The belief update is a rule with a tuned λ and a fixed 0.5 threshold; calibration and sensitivity analyses are missing. On DSTC2, improvements over baselines are modest with ASR, and the reported near-ceiling performance (0.96) with transcripts highlights a remaining gap in robustness to ASR errors.

Questions to authors
- Scalability: What is the computational cost per turn as a function of |S||V|? Can you share throughput numbers (utterances/sec) and memory usage for NBT-CNN? Any pruning strategies for candidate enumeration?

- Parameter sharing: You state vectors can be shared across slots, but also mention separate parameter sets per slot for some components. Which parts are shared vs. slot-specific in the final model, and how does this choice affect performance?

- Handling unseen values: Can NBT score values not seen during training but present in the ontology at test time? How does it generalize to multi-word paraphrases unseen during training beyond embedding composition by summation?

- Embedding strategy: Did you try fine-tuning embeddings or adapting specialized vectors to the domain? How does performance change if you unfreeze embeddings or continue specialization on in-domain text?

- Belief update: Why 0.5 as the decision threshold, and how sensitive are results to λ and the threshold? Any calibration assessment (e.g., reliability curves) for turn-level probabilities?

- Data cleanliness: You corrected DSTC2 transcripts; can you clarify whether corrections were applied only to training transcriptions and whether this affects comparability to prior work?

Overall recommendation
Accept. The paper introduces a meaningful and timely shift in dialogue state tracking—replacing delexicalization and manual lexicons with semantically specialized embeddings and a simple, effective neural architecture. Despite limitations in breadth of evaluation and context modeling, the core idea is both novel and pragmatically impactful, with solid empirical support and a compelling ablation on vector semantics.
