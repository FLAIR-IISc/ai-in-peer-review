Summary and contributions
- This paper introduces the Neural Belief Tracker (NBT), a joint SLU/DST framework that operates entirely over pre-trained, semantically specialized word embeddings rather than delexicalized features and hand-crafted semantic dictionaries. Two instantiations are proposed (NBT-DNN and NBT-CNN) that compose utterance n-grams, perform semantic decoding via element-wise interactions with candidate slot–value vectors, and incorporate limited context via gating with the last system acts.
- The method includes a simple belief state update that aggregates over ASR N-best lists and smooths with the previous belief.
- Empirically, NBT matches or surpasses strong delexicalization-based baselines with and without semantic dictionaries on DSTC2 and WOZ 2.0, and the paper demonstrates that semantically specialized embeddings (Paragram-SL999) provide consistent gains over distributional vectors and random initializations.

Strengths
- Clear and impactful reframing of delexicalization: The paper convincingly argues and demonstrates that operating directly in a high-quality semantic vector space can obviate costly lexicons. This is a meaningful step toward scalability and robustness to paraphrase.
- Simple, well-motivated architecture with effective components: The combination of (i) n-gram composition (DNN or CNN), (ii) element-wise interaction between utterance and candidate slot–value vectors, and (iii) lightweight context gating for request/confirm acts is conceptually clean and computationally straightforward, yet empirically strong.
- Solid empirical evidence: Results on two widely used datasets show statistically significant gains over baselines; importantly, NBT matches or beats models that use handcrafted dictionaries and substantially outperforms baselines when such resources are unavailable. The effect is particularly strong on WOZ, supporting the claim about handling paraphrase and richer language.
- Insightful analysis of word vector quality: The comparison among random, GloVe, and Paragram-SL999 is a useful and nontrivial contribution, connecting intrinsic semantic specialization to downstream DST performance. The trends (especially on noisy DSTC2) are informative.
- Reproducibility and clarity: The model is specified with sufficient detail (including architectural choices, optimization, and belief update), and the cleaned DSTC2 transcriptions and expanded WOZ 2.0 are noted for release.

Weaknesses
- Limited context modeling and discourse phenomena: The model only conditions on the last system acts (request/confirm) and ignores broader user/system history (e.g., corrections, negations beyond confirm scenarios, co-reference). This Markovian simplification is a strong assumption; no ablation is provided to quantify the contribution of the context module, and no analysis of known hard phenomena (e.g., “anything but X”, “not cheap”, revisions) is presented.
- Scalability and parameter sharing claims: While the paper claims extensive parameter sharing, the implementation maintains separate parameters per slot for the representation learning layers, and the decision process iterates over all slot–value pairs. This raises questions about scalability to large ontologies and cross-slot generalization; empirical evidence is limited to a small, single-domain ontology (three informable slots).
- ASR robustness and fairness of training: For DSTC2, the model is trained on transcriptions and evaluated on ASR hypotheses, while baselines often leverage ASR/SLU features. The belief update is a simple rule with a tuned λ; there is no training on ASR N-best/confusion networks nor analysis of sensitivity to λ or the decision threshold. Reported gains on DSTC2 are modest and may be partly confounded by these choices.
- Ablations and error analysis are sparse: Beyond the word vector study, there is little component-level ablation (e.g., removing context gating, changing interaction functions, comparing NBT-DNN vs NBT-CNN under controlled settings). No qualitative error analysis is provided to illuminate failure modes (antonymy, negation, rare values, long-range dependencies).
- Evaluation scope: Only a single, small domain is considered. Request accuracy is already near-ceiling for baselines, limiting insight; more challenging settings (larger ontologies, multi-domain, unseen values/slots at test time) would better test the claimed scalability and reliance on semantic spaces.

Questions to authors
- Training data for DSTC2: Did you train NBT on ASR N-best/hypotheses or only on (cleaned) transcriptions? If only transcripts, what happens when training on N-best or confusion networks? Please report that variant for fairness and to isolate ASR robustness.
- Context module ablation: What is the impact on joint goal and request accuracy when removing dr and dc or when using only one of them? Do you have results beyond the Markov assumption (e.g., incorporating more than the last system act)?
- Interaction function: You mention concatenation performed poorly. Can you report results for a bilinear term (r^T W c) or attention-based interaction to support the choice of element-wise product?
- Parameter sharing: How many parameters are slot-specific vs shared? Can you evaluate zero-shot generalization to unseen values (or slots) at test time to substantiate the sharing/generalization claims?
- Class imbalance: Given the per-turn, per-slot–value binary labeling, what is the positive:negative ratio, and did you use any loss weighting or sampling strategies? If not, how sensitive are results to imbalance?
- Sensitivity to λ and threshold: How sensitive are DSTC2 results to the smoothing coefficient and the 0.5 decision threshold? Any calibration analysis?

Additional comments
- A qualitative analysis (e.g., nearest-neighbor paraphrases that NBT captures; failure modes involving negation/polysemy) would significantly strengthen the claims about semantic handling.
- Reporting per-slot performance and per-turn length breakdowns would illuminate where CNN vs DNN helps.
- While fixing embeddings aids generalization, a comparison with light fine-tuning under a semantic regularizer could be informative.
