- Summary: Proposes Neural Belief Tracker (NBT) family (NBT-DNN, NBT-CNN) that removes hand-crafted semantic dictionaries by operating in pre-trained embedding space, fusing utterance, candidate slot-value, and last system act via element-wise similarity plus a decision network, with a simple rule-based belief update; outperforms delexicalized baselines on DSTC2 and WOZ 2.0 and shows Paragram embeddings > GloVe > random.

- Contribution 1: Presents a practical, dictionary-free joint SLU/DST architecture using pre-trained, semantically specialized vectors and simple interaction mechanisms to handle lexical/morphological variation.

- Contribution 2: Provides empirical evidence that semantically specialized embeddings materially improve DST performance, especially under ASR noise, and that NBT matches or exceeds strong delexicalized baselines without hand-crafted lexicons.

- Contribution 3: Offers a clear, reproducible framework (DNN and CNN instantiations) with minimal hand-engineering and a simple belief update that performs competitively.

- Strength: Eliminates reliance on semantic dictionaries while retaining or surpassing baseline performance on DSTC2 and WOZ 2.0, addressing a scalability bottleneck.

- Strength: Ablations convincingly demonstrate the value of semantically specialized embeddings (Paragram-SL999) over GloVe and random vectors, especially with ASR noise.

- Strength: Architecture is simple and interpretable—element-wise multiplication for utterance/candidate interaction and gating for last system act are low-parameter, robust choices.

- Strength: ASR handling via N-best fusion is transparent and integrates easily with SDS pipelines, yielding strong DSTC2 results.

- Strength: Paper is reproducible and clear, with detailed model settings and a plan to release cleaned transcriptions and expansions.

- Weakness: Experiments are limited to a single small restaurant domain, so scalability to larger ontologies, many values, or multi-domain settings is unproven.

- Weakness: Context modeling is shallow (only last system act); longer-range dependencies, corrections, or discourse effects are not addressed or ablated.

- Weakness: Heavy dependence on high-quality, specialized pre-trained embeddings; OOVs, domain shift, low-resource languages, and the freeze vs. fine-tune trade-off are not explored.

- Weakness: Baseline comparisons may be incomplete—stronger DSTC2 systems and end-to-end trackers are not clearly compared, and sensitivity to tuned belief-update hyperparameters is unreported.

- Weakness: Analysis is incomplete—no per-slot results, error breakdowns, or runtime analysis; surprising high transcription accuracy needs more detail on what drives it.

- Question: Ablation breakdown requested—impact of removing context gating, replacing element-wise product, shared vs. per-slot encoders, and freezing vs. fine-tuning embeddings.

- Question: How does NBT scale with ontology size and what are the computational/latency costs of iterating over all slot-value pairs; any pruning strategies?

- Question: How are OOV words and multiword values handled at test time; is summing multiword vectors optimal versus averaging or attention?

- Question: What N-best size is used for ASR fusion, how sensitive are results to N, and were confusion networks or lattices tried?

- Question: How well does NBT generalize across datasets or domains—any cross-dataset or multi-domain experiments?

- Additional comment: Clarify whether system acts are assumed gold at test time and whether that assumption holds in deployed settings.

- Additional comment: Recommend reporting per-slot accuracies and calibration of P(s,v) to aid downstream policy learning.

- Overall: Solid, practical contribution with clear empirical benefits and minimal hand-engineering; acceptance recommended pending stronger ablations and broader evaluation.
