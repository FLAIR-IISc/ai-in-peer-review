Summary
This paper proposes the Neural Belief Tracker (NBT), a joint SLU/DST model that avoids delexicalisation and handcrafted semantic dictionaries by operating over pre-trained word vectors. Two variants are presented: NBT-DNN (sum of n-gram embeddings with MLP) and NBT-CNN (CNN+ReLU+max-pooling over 1–3-grams). A semantic decoder composes candidate slot/value embeddings and interacts with the utterance representation via element-wise multiplication; a simple context gate leverages the last system act (request/confirm). A rule-based belief update mixes N-best ASR turn-level predictions with the previous belief using a tuned coefficient. Experiments on DSTC2 (ASR) and WOZ 2.0 (typed) show improved joint goal and request accuracies over delexicalisation baselines, with gains attributed to semantically-specialised embeddings (Paragram-SL999).

Main contributions (as I see them)
1) A simple, parameter-efficient joint SLU/DST architecture that replaces delexicalisation with pre-trained semantic word vectors and demonstrates competitive performance without handcrafted semantic dictionaries.
2) An empirical study showing that semantically specialised word vectors (Paragram-SL999) yield significant gains over random/GloVe for belief tracking.
3) A lightweight context-gating mechanism using the last system act, integrated into the belief tracker.

Strengths
- Clear motivation and simplicity: The model eliminates reliance on brittle semantic dictionaries and exact matching, which is appealing for scalability and domain adaptation. The NBT design is easy to implement and train, enabling broad applicability.
- Empirical improvements on two benchmarks: On both DSTC2 and WOZ 2.0, NBT variants outperform delexicalisation-based baselines on joint goal and requests. Gains are larger on WOZ, aligning with the claim that richer semantics benefit from vector-based reasoning.
- Ablation w.r.t. word vectors: The comparison of random vs GloVe vs Paragram-SL999 supports the central claim that semantically-specialised vectors are beneficial, especially under ASR noise.
- Context integration that matters for confirms: The gating tailored to confirm/request acts is a reasonable, targeted inductive bias that likely contributes to robustness for short “yes/no” responses.
- Parameter sharing potential: Operating in a common embedding space encourages sharing across values and potentially across slots, addressing data sparsity.

Weaknesses
- Novelty and scope are limited: The core ideas (pre-trained embeddings, CNN sentence encoders, element-wise interaction with candidate embeddings) are standard; the architectural novelty is modest. Claims of scalability are not substantiated beyond a single, small ontology (3 informable slots) and two datasets within the same domain. No evidence for large ontologies, new slots/values, or multi-domain transfer.
- Contradictory and underspecified parameter sharing: The paper claims parameters “can be shared across all slots,” yet Equation (3) explicitly uses slot-specific parameters (“we maintain a separate set of parameters for each slot”). Clarify what is shared in practice and whether sharing across slots was tried; otherwise the scalability argument weakens.
- Incomplete methodological details and ablations: Critical training details are missing (batch size, epochs, learning rate/Adam settings, dropout/regularization, early stopping criteria, random seeds). No ablation on context gating, gating similarity choices, or the semantic decoder (e.g., dot product vs element-wise vs bi-affine; sum vs concatenation vs MLP over [cs, cv]). No analysis of the belief update coefficient λ sensitivity. No report of N for N-best ASR. These omissions hurt reproducibility and attribution of gains.
- Statistical testing and reporting issues: Table 1’s asterisk notation is confusing/inconsistent (e.g., “+ sem. dict.” entries marked with asterisks; legend says significance is vs baseline; text claims “no significant variation between NBT and lexicon-supplemented”). The unit of analysis for the paired t-test (turns or dialogs?) and multiple comparison corrections are not provided.
- Potential unfairness and de-anonymization: The DSTC2 transcriptions were “corrected manually” and “will be made available on the first author’s website,” which both violates anonymity and complicates comparability to prior work (were baselines retrained on the corrected data? are results comparable to published DSTC2 numbers?). This is a serious procedural flaw for ACL.
- Semantic design choices are questionable: The gating uses raw dot products (scale-sensitive, unnormalized) and the candidate vector uses c = σ(W(cs+cv)+b), which may lose slot/value interactions; no normalization or calibration is discussed. Multi-word expressions are summed (not normalized), and OOV handling is unspecified. Fixed embeddings may limit domain adaptation; no comparison to fine-tuning.
- Belief update mechanism: The rule-based mixing (λ=0.55) is ad-hoc; no comparison to stronger learned updates (e.g., RNNs) or calibration analysis. It is unclear if baselines used comparable ASR posterior aggregation, raising fairness concerns.

Questions to Authors
1) What exactly is shared across slots/values? Did you try a single parameter set across all slots, and with what effect? Please resolve the inconsistency between the claim of sharing and Eq. (3) stating slot-specific parameters.
2) Provide full training details: tokenization, casing, OOV strategy, vocabulary thresholds, optimizer hyperparameters, regularization, early stopping, seeds, and number of epochs. How stable are results across seeds?
3) How large is N in the ASR N-best lists? Do baselines aggregate over N-best with posteriors in the same way? If not, please align or report both settings.
4) Statistical testing: What is the unit for the paired t-test (turns vs dialogs)? How many comparisons were tested and were corrections applied? Please fix the asterisk annotations and clarify significance claims vs which baselines.
5) Ablations: Can you report performance without context gating, with cosine similarity (normalized) instead of dot products, and with alternative composition for c (e.g., concatenation + MLP, bi-affine scoring)?
6) On DSTC2 “corrected” transcripts: Were baselines and your models trained/evaluated on identical corrected resources? How do results compare to using the official (uncorrected) transcriptions? Also, the statement about “first author’s website” violates anonymity—please address.
7) OOV and multi-word handling: How do you handle unseen words at test time (UNK vs random vs zero)? Do you normalize sums/averages for multi-word slot names/values? Any impact on performance?
8) Scalability: What is the computational cost per turn as ontology size grows? Any experiments on larger ontologies, zero-shot values, or cross-domain generalization?

Overall, while the idea is practical and results are promising, the paper has significant methodological, reporting, and procedural issues that must be addressed.
