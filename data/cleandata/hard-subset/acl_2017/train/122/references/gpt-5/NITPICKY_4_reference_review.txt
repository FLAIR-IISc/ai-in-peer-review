Summary and main contributions
This paper proposes Neural Belief Tracker (NBT), a family of models for dialogue state tracking that jointly perform SLU/DST without delexicalisation or hand-crafted semantic dictionaries. Key components include: (i) utterance encoders over pre-trained word vectors (a simple DNN over cumulative n-grams and a CNN with filters over 1–3-grams), (ii) semantic decoding via element-wise interaction between the utterance and slot-value representations, and (iii) lightweight context gating that conditions on the last system act (request/confirm). Experiments on DSTC2 (ASR) and an expanded WOZ 2.0 (typed) show competitive or better accuracy versus delexicalisation-based baselines, especially without semantic lexicons, and further gains with semantically specialised (Paragram-SL999) embeddings.

As I see them, the primary contributions are:
1) A delexicalisation-free DST approach that leverages pre-trained semantically specialised word vectors and simple context gating, matching or surpassing delex baselines.
2) An empirical demonstration that semantically specialised embeddings materially improve DST performance over random/GloVe on two datasets.
3) A simple, reproducible architecture variant (DNN vs CNN) and ablations across word vector initialisations.

Strengths
- Clear, impactful goal of removing the reliance on manual semantic dictionaries. This addresses an acknowledged scalability bottleneck in prior joint SLU/DST systems; the paper demonstrates that high-quality pre-trained embeddings can fill that gap.
- Solid empirical results on two commonly used domains. NBT outperforms baselines without semantic lexicons and remains competitive with the dictionary-augmented variants. The effect is particularly pronounced on WOZ 2.0, which stresses semantic understanding over ASR robustness.
- Careful isolation of word vector quality. The comparison among random, GloVe, and semantically specialised vectors is informative, and the consistent benefits on DSTC2 support the thesis that semantic quality (not mere distributional relatedness) matters for belief tracking.
- Simple, computationally efficient design. The model is straightforward (single hidden layer decision module, element-wise multiplication for interaction, last-turn context gating) and likely easy to deploy; no complex recurrent encoders are required for reasonable performance.
- Context handling tailored to common SDS patterns. The gating mechanisms for system Request/Confirm are intuitive and well-motivated, supporting implicit confirmations and short affirmative/negative user replies.

Weaknesses
- Presentation and formatting are significantly below venue standards. Numerous citation formatting errors (e.g., “? 2016”, escaped punctuation, inconsistent author fields), figures interleaved mid-paragraph, stray footnote markers, and typos reduce clarity. This is not cosmetic: it hinders verification of prior work and undermines reproducibility.
- Evaluation details and statistical rigor are underspecified. The unit of the paired t-test (turns/dialogues) is not stated; no correction for multiple comparisons; confidence intervals are absent. Reported gains over the lexicon-augmented baseline are small (e.g., DSTC2 joint goals 73.4 vs 72.9), and the paper concedes non-significance there; yet the abstract-level claims can be read as stronger than the evidence, especially on DSTC2.
- Ambiguity and inconsistency in parameter sharing and model specification. Early sections claim parameters can be shared across slots, yet Section 3.1 explicitly “maintain a separate set of parameters for each slot.” The decision module description (one hidden layer of size 100) lacks other essential hyperparameters (batch size, epochs, regularisation, dropout, early stopping). OOV handling and multi-word composition (simple summation) are not justified or ablated.
- Dataset handling raises concerns about fairness and anonymity. The authors “corrected” DSTC2 transcriptions; it is unclear precisely what was changed and whether baselines were retrained on the same corrected data. The paper repeatedly states resources “will be made available on the first author’s website,” which violates double-blind review. The expanded WOZ 2.0 collection process is only briefly described; annotation protocol, quality control, and inter-annotator agreement are not reported.
- Limited context modelling and simplistic belief update. Only the last system act is considered (Markov assumption), which is common but should be justified via ablations; there is no analysis of failures with multi-turn dependencies. The belief update uses a fixed λ tuned on dev, binary thresholding at 0.5, and per-slot argmax—no calibration or exploration of alternative updates. No ablations on the contribution of context gates (dr, dc) versus semantic decoding (d).
- Baseline comparison scope and fairness. While delex-based baselines are relevant, it is unclear if they were optimally tuned on the corrected data and the expanded WOZ split. The claim that pre-trained vectors “cannot be incorporated directly” into delex models is too strong; at minimum, feature augmentation or hybrid models could be tested. Run-time/complexity analysis is missing; given O(|S||V|) pairwise decisions per turn, the claim of scalability to large ontologies requires empirical support.

Questions to authors
1) Precisely what edits were made to DSTC2 transcriptions, and were all compared systems retrained on the corrected data? Will you release the exact diffs after the review period, and how do you ensure this adheres to anonymity?
2) What is your OOV handling strategy at test time for words/values missing from Paragram-SL999? What fraction of tokens/values are OOV in each dataset, and how sensitive is performance to OOV rate?
3) Please provide full training details: batch size, number of epochs, optimiser settings beyond Adam defaults, weight decay/dropout, early stopping criteria, and seeds. How robust are results across random seeds?
4) Can you report ablations isolating the contributions of context gates (dr, dc) versus semantic decoding (d)? In particular, what is performance with/without last-system-act gating?
5) Clarify parameter sharing: are parameters shared across values within a slot but not across slots? Did you try full sharing across slots? Provide results.
6) For statistical testing, what is the unit of analysis and number of repeats? Provide confidence intervals and address multiple comparisons.
7) How do you handle “don’t care”/“any” values, negations, and multi-valued goals? Please detail the labelling policy and model behaviour.
8) Runtime and scalability: report per-turn inference time as a function of ontology size, and memory footprint, for both NBT-DNN and NBT-CNN.
