Summary and contributions
This paper proposes Neural Belief Tracker (NBT), a family of neural models for dialogue state tracking that replaces delexicalization and hand-crafted semantic dictionaries with reasoning over pre-trained word embeddings. Two variants (NBT-DNN and NBT-CNN) compose utterance representations, interact them with candidate slot-value representations via element-wise multiplication, and incorporate shallow context via gating based on the last system acts (request/confirm). A simple rule-based mechanism integrates ASR N-best lists and updates beliefs over turns. Evaluation on DSTC2 (ASR) and an expanded WOZ 2.0 (typed) shows significant gains over strong delexicalization-based trackers and highlights the benefit of semantically specialized vectors (Paragram-SL999).

As I see them, the primary contributions are:
1) A simple, parameter-efficient framework that couples SLU and DST using pre-trained semantically specialized word vectors, eliminating the need for semantic dictionaries while achieving state-of-the-art results for the setting.
2) Empirical evidence across two benchmarks, with careful comparisons to delexicalization baselines and an informative study on the impact of different word vector spaces.
3) Practical resources: a cleaned DSTC2 transcription and an expanded WOZ 2.0 dataset, improving reproducibility and enabling richer evaluation.

Strengths
- Strong empirical performance without hand-crafted lexicons. On both DSTC2 and WOZ 2.0, NBT variants significantly outperform competitive delexicalization-based baselines in joint goal and request accuracy, and match or surpass lexicon-augmented baselines. This directly supports the central claim that NBT removes reliance on semantic dictionaries without sacrificing accuracy.

- Sound use of semantically specialized word vectors. The analysis comparing random, GloVe, and Paragram-SL999 embeddings provides clear evidence that semantic specialization (similarity over relatedness) materially improves goal tracking, especially with ASR noise (DSTC2). Freezing embeddings to leverage the geometry for unseen paraphrases is a neat and well-motivated design that improves generalization.

- Simple, modular architecture with effective context incorporation. The element-wise interaction between utterance and candidate pair is a lightweight alternative to more complex attention mechanisms, and the gating with last system acts addresses crucial confirm/request cases elegantly. The approach shares parameters across values and, partially, across slots, enhancing data efficiency.

- Solid experimental setup and metrics. The paper evaluates both goals (joint) and requests, trains on transcriptions but reports on ASR for DSTC2, and controls for significance. Reporting a near-ceiling 0.96 goal accuracy with gold transcripts underscores that performance bottlenecks are largely ASR-related, not semantic modeling.

- Resource contribution. Cleaning DSTC2 transcriptions and expanding WOZ 2.0 is valuable to the community and strengthens the evaluation’s credibility.

Weaknesses
- Scalability to large or multi-domain ontologies remains unclear. The model iterates over all slot-value pairs and maintains slot-specific parameters; this O(|S||V|) scoring may be computationally and memory intensive as domains grow. The claim of scalability would be stronger with complexity analysis, batching strategies, or results on larger ontologies.

- Limited context modeling. Only the immediately preceding system acts are used, via simple dot-product gates. This neglects richer dialogue phenomena (anaphora, coreference, negation patterns spanning multiple turns, corrections). An ablation isolating the context module’s contribution and comparisons to recurrent/attention-based context would clarify its effectiveness.

- Rule-based belief update and thresholds. The belief update is not learned, uses a fixed λ and a 0.5 decision threshold, and assumes independence across slot-value decisions with post-hoc selection (e.g., pick top value per informable slot). This can be suboptimal and ignores mutual exclusivity and cross-slot constraints; a learned update or structured decoding could yield gains, especially under ASR noise.

- Narrow evaluation scope. Both datasets are single-domain Cambridge restaurants. The paper claims potential for multi-domain and non-English settings, but provides no experiments demonstrating cross-domain transfer, handling of larger vocabularies/ontologies, or other languages/morphologies. Generality is asserted rather than empirically shown.

- Dependence on specialized embeddings and freezing. The best results rely on Paragram-SL999; such resources may be unavailable for many languages/domains. Freezing embeddings may hinder adaptation to domain-specific terms, senses, and OOV handling. A sensitivity study on fine-tuning vs freezing and robustness to domain shift would strengthen the claims.

Questions to authors
- Complexity/scalability: What is the per-turn computational cost as a function of slots and values? How does training/inference time grow for larger ontologies? Any batching/tricks to mitigate O(|S||V|) scoring?

- Ablations: What is the quantitative contribution of the context module (dr, dc) vs semantic decoding (d)? How sensitive is performance to the element-wise multiplication choice vs concatenation or bilinear scoring?

- Belief update: Did you try learning λ or the update rule end-to-end? How sensitive are results to λ and the 0.5 threshold? Can you enforce mutual exclusivity during decoding to reduce contradictions?

- Embeddings: How do results change if embeddings are fine-tuned (with small LR) or adapted via domain-specific specialization? What is OOV handling at test time, and how often do OOVs occur?

- Generalization: Any preliminary results on multi-domain or larger ontologies (e.g., adding hotels/attractions) or on non-English languages with richer morphology?

Additional comments
- Provide qualitative error analysis highlighting common failure modes (negation, comparative forms, multi-constraint turns) to guide future improvements.

- Consider replacing slot-specific parameters with shared encoders plus slot/type embeddings to improve scalability and cross-slot transfer.

- Exploring attention over the full dialogue history or lightweight recurrent context could address multi-turn phenomena without heavy complexity.

- Releasing code alongside the cleaned/expanded data would further enhance impact and reproducibility.
