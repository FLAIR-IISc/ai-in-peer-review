Summary and claimed contributions
This paper introduces Neural Belief Tracker (NBT), a joint SLU/DST framework that eschews delexicalisation and hand-crafted semantic dictionaries by operating directly in pre-trained word embedding spaces. Two variants are presented (NBT-DNN and NBT-CNN) that learn utterance representations, combine them with slot–value representations via an element-wise interaction, and incorporate the immediately preceding system acts through a simple gating mechanism. A rule-based belief update aggregates over ASR N-best lists. Experiments on DSTC2 (speech) and WOZ 2.0 (typed) show that NBT matches or outperforms competitive delexicalisation-based baselines, especially when semantic dictionaries are absent, and that semantically specialised embeddings (Paragram-SL999) yield the best performance.

Contributions (as I see them, strongest first):
1) A delexicalisation-free joint SLU/DST model that leverages pre-trained, semantically specialised word vectors and shares parameters across values, reducing reliance on domain-specific lexicons.
2) A simple, effective context gating mechanism that conditions on recent system acts (request/confirm) to disambiguate short affirmative/negative user responses.
3) An empirical analysis demonstrating the importance of embedding quality for DST, with consistent gains from semantically specialised vectors over distributional and random initialisations.

Strengths
- Addresses a real scalability bottleneck: The move away from semantic dictionaries is both practically important and well-justified. The model operates over word vectors and shares parameters across slot-values, which directly targets the combinatorial explosion and annotation costs of prior delexicalisation pipelines. Evidence: On both DSTC2 and WOZ 2.0, NBT matches/exceeds baselines even when baselines have access to curated lexicons, and substantially outperforms them without lexicons.

- Simple, modular architecture with clear inductive bias: The decomposition into utterance representation, semantic decoding via element-wise interaction with slot–value representations, and explicit context gating is easy to understand and implement. The context module neatly captures confirm/request cases that often foil bag-of-ngrams models, and the element-wise interaction retains feature structure that a scalar similarity would lose.

- Solid empirical validation and analysis: Results on two standard datasets with different characteristics (noisy ASR vs typed language) establish robustness. Statistical significance is reported; effect sizes on WOZ are notable for both goals and requests. The embedding ablation cleanly isolates the benefit of semantically specialised vectors, especially for noisy DSTC2, and aligns with the paper’s claims about relatedness vs similarity.

- Data efficiency and parameter sharing: Fixing pre-trained embeddings and sharing parameters across values/slots is appropriate for limited labelled DST data. The model’s performance with fixed embeddings argues for good generalisation to unseen paraphrases (e.g., “inexpensive” ≈ “cheap”).

- Clarity and reproducibility: The paper is well-written with helpful schematics. Many architectural details (filter sizes/counts, hidden dimensions, update rule) are specified, and cleaned transcripts/WOZ 2.0 are promised for release.

Weaknesses
- Limited context modelling: The Markov assumption (only last system acts) ignores broader dialogue history, coreference, and cross-turn dependencies beyond confirm/request. This will miss phenomena like “No, somewhere else” or multi-constraint revisions. An RNN/attention over the full dialogue state or richer structure for system and user histories could strengthen performance, especially in multi-domain or longer dialogs.

- Hand-designed belief update and independence assumptions: The belief update is a simple convex combination with a tuned λ and independent binary decisions per slot–value, followed by thresholds. This ignores mutual exclusivity within slots and inter-slot correlations, and may lead to calibration issues. A learned update, slot-conditional normalisation (e.g., softmax over values + none), or joint decoding with constraints could improve consistency and calibration.

- Modest gains on DSTC2 and fairness of comparison: On DSTC2 (ASR), improvements over delexicalisation+dictionary are small (e.g., 72.9 → 73.4 joint goals). Given that NBT leverages external semantic resources (Paragram), it would help to ensure baselines are as strong as possible (e.g., feature augmentations or alternative SLU front-ends) and report confidence intervals. Also, results using test transcriptions (reported as 0.96) are informative but should be clearly separated from the main evaluation and accompanied by comparable baselines.

- Generalisation and scalability not empirically demonstrated: Claims about scalability to larger ontologies and multi-domain scenarios are not tested. The ontology here is small (3 informable slots). It remains to be shown that the per-candidate evaluation remains efficient and accurate with hundreds of values and cross-domain slots (e.g., zero/few-shot values, rare multi-word values).

- Embedding limitations and OOV handling: Fixing embeddings aids generalisation but can hinder domain adaptation and handling of OOV/multi-word values. The approach sums word vectors for multi-word names/values and does not model subword morphology or compositionality beyond trigrams in NBT-DNN. There is limited discussion of OOV strategies, rare value handling, negation (“not expensive”), and polarity beyond confirm/request contexts.

Questions to authors
- How does performance change if you replace the last-act gating with a learned attention over the full dialogue history (system acts and user turns)? Any ablation beyond removing gating?

- Did you try fine-tuning embeddings with a small learning rate or adapter layers? How does this trade off with generalisation to unseen paraphrases?

- How are OOV words handled at test time? Any subword or character-level augmentation tried, especially for morphologically rich languages (as mentioned in future work)?

- For belief updates, have you tested slot-conditional softmax (value + none) instead of independent binary decisions and a threshold? Any calibration analysis (e.g., ECE) to justify the 0.5 threshold?

- What is the computational cost at inference (per turn) as ontology size grows? Any batching/approximation to avoid iterating over all candidate pairs?

- Error analysis: What are the dominant remaining errors on WOZ (e.g., negation, comparative/ superlative forms, long-distance dependencies)?

Additional comments
- Please provide more training details (batch size, learning rate schedules, regularisation, early stopping criteria) and random seed variance across runs.

- Clarify significance testing setup (number of runs, paired t-test over dialogues/turns).

- Consider reporting per-slot/value performance and confusion matrices, and include a few qualitative examples to illustrate successes/failures of the context gating.
