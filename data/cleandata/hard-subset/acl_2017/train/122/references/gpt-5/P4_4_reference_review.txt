Summary and Contributions
This paper proposes Neural Belief Tracker (NBT), a family of joint SLU/DST models that operate entirely in pre-trained word vector spaces instead of delexicalized features and hand-crafted semantic lexicons. The models learn utterance representations (via n-gram summation with a shallow DNN or a CNN), interact them with candidate slot–value representations through element-wise multiplication, and incorporate immediate context via gating informed by the last system acts. A simple rule-based belief update combines N-best ASR hypotheses with previous belief.

As I see them, the main contributions are:
1) A lexicon-free, embedding-based DST framework (NBT) that jointly models SLU/DST and removes dependency on hand-crafted semantic dictionaries while maintaining or improving accuracy.
2) Two concrete instantiations (NBT-DNN and NBT-CNN) with a semantic decoding mechanism and a light-weight context gating module tailored to confirm/request acts.
3) Empirical evidence across two datasets (DSTC2, WOZ 2.0) that semantically specialized word vectors (Paragram-SL999) materially improve DST, outperforming both delexicalized baselines without lexicons and matching/surpassing lexicon-augmented systems.

Strengths
- Eliminates manual semantic dictionaries with competitive or better performance:
  • On DSTC2 and WOZ 2.0, both NBT variants significantly improve joint goal accuracy over delexicalized baselines; they remain competitive with or surpass baselines augmented with semantic lexicons. This directly addresses scalability limits of delexicalization and demonstrates practical value.
- Clear evidence that semantically specialized embeddings matter:
  • The comparison among random, GloVe, and Paragram-SL999 shows consistent gains from semantically specialized vectors, especially under ASR noise (DSTC2). This is a well-controlled ablation that supports the central claim that embedding semantics enable robust paraphrase handling.
- Simple, modular architecture with parameter sharing:
  • The approach uses fixed pre-trained vectors and shared parameters across values (and largely across slots), reducing data requirements vs per-value classifiers. The element-wise interaction between utterance and candidate pair yields an effective similarity feature without collapsing to a single scalar, and the modules are easy to implement and extend.
- Context modeling aligns with dialog act structure and improves handling of short responses:
  • The gating via similarity to last system acts (request/confirm) plausibly disambiguates elliptical/yes-no user responses without requiring full dialog history modeling, which is effective for the targeted phenomena and reflects SDS interaction patterns.
- Practicality for ASR settings:
  • The N-best posterior-weighted turn probabilities and a mixing coefficient with previous belief are straightforward and improve robustness to ASR errors; the method is simple to reproduce and tune.

Weaknesses
- Limited context modeling (Markov assumption on last system acts):
  • Only the immediately preceding system acts are considered. Dialogs that require integrating longer context, resolving coreference beyond a single turn, or handling multi-turn confirmations/repairs may not be handled well. An ablation quantifying the contribution of the context gate and failure cases would strengthen the claim.
- Strong dependence on high-quality pre-trained vectors:
  • Results are sensitive to embedding choice; performance drops with GloVe (and random), especially on noisy data. This raises questions about portability to new domains/languages lacking high-quality specialized vectors or with many OOV values, and whether fine-tuning embeddings would help or harm.
- Evaluation breadth and reproducibility concerns:
  • Experiments are limited to a single ontology/domain (restaurants). The expanded WOZ 2.0 dataset is newly collected; while the authors state it will be released, broader benchmarks or cross-domain tests would better support scalability claims. The report that goal accuracy reaches 0.96 on DSTC2 transcriptions suggests a large gap vs ASR; a detailed analysis of ASR error modes and robustness would be informative.
- Scalability and parameter sharing claims need clarification:
  • The model iterates over all slot–value pairs per turn; computational cost scales with ontology size. Some parameters are slot-specific (e.g., F^s_n, W^s), which partially undermines “maximizing parameter sharing across slots.” The practical impact on large ontologies is not quantified, nor are strategies to prune candidate pairs.
- Non-learned belief update and fixed thresholds:
  • The belief update uses a manually tuned mixing coefficient λ and a fixed 0.5 decision threshold. This may be suboptimal and dataset-specific; learned calibration (e.g., via a small RNN over turns or learned decay) could improve generalization and remove a hand-tuned component in an otherwise neural pipeline.

Questions to Authors
- Context modeling:
  • What is the empirical contribution of the context gating module? Please provide an ablation (no context, request only, confirm only).
  • How does the model handle negation and corrections beyond single-turn confirm/request (e.g., “not Thai anymore, actually Italian”) when these do not immediately follow a system act?
- Parameter sharing and scalability:
  • Which parameters are slot-specific vs shared? Can parameters be fully shared across slots without loss? What is the computational cost per turn as a function of ontology size, and have you experimented with candidate pruning?
- Embeddings and fine-tuning:
  • Did you try fine-tuning embeddings (perhaps with small LR or projection layers) and what was the effect, especially under ASR noise? How do you handle OOV tokens and multiword values beyond vector summation?
- Belief update:
  • How sensitive is performance to λ and the 0.5 threshold? Would a learned turn-combiner (e.g., logistic regression or a small RNN) improve calibration?
- Evaluation details:
  • For DSTC2, were any ASR features used in training beyond transcriptions? Are the corrected transcriptions used only for training, and is there any risk of mismatch vs the official setup? How are positive/negative examples balanced during training?

Overall, the paper is clear, technically sound, and provides a useful, scalable alternative to delexicalization with convincing empirical support, though broader evaluations and clarifications on context modeling, parameter sharing, and belief updating would strengthen the work.
