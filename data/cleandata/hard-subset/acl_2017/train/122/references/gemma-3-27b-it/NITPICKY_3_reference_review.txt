This paper presents the Neural Belief Tracker (NBT), a model aiming to improve dialogue state tracking (DST) by jointly learning spoken language understanding (SLU) and DST without relying on hand-crafted semantic lexicons. The authors propose two variants, NBT-DNN and NBT-CNN, both leveraging pre-trained word vectors to handle lexical variation. The key claimed contributions are: (1) achieving performance comparable to delexicalization-based models *without* requiring manual lexicons; and (2) outperforming delexicalization models when such resources are unavailable.

**Strengths:**

1. **Addressing a Practical Limitation:** The reliance on manually crafted semantic dictionaries (delexicalization) is a significant bottleneck for scaling DST to new domains. The NBT directly tackles this limitation, offering a more scalable and adaptable approach. This is a genuinely valuable problem to address.
2. **Solid Empirical Evaluation:** The paper presents a reasonably thorough experimental evaluation on two established datasets (DSTC2 and WOZ 2.0), comparing the NBT models against strong baselines, including state-of-the-art delexicalization-based systems. The reported results demonstrate competitive performance and, importantly, highlight the advantage of NBT when lexical resources are limited.
3. **Clear and Well-Structured Presentation:** The paper is generally well-written and logically organized. The architecture of the NBT is clearly explained with accompanying figures, and the experimental setup is described in sufficient detail to allow for replication.
4. **Demonstrated Benefit of Semantic Word Vectors:** The ablation study (Table 2) effectively illustrates the importance of utilizing semantically-specialized word vectors, reinforcing the core idea of leveraging pre-trained knowledge to address lexical variation.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Incremental Improvement, Not a Paradigm Shift:** While the NBT achieves competitive results, the performance gains over strong delexicalization baselines are often marginal. The claim of “significantly outperforming” when resources are unavailable is qualified by the fact that the absolute performance remains relatively modest. The advancements presented feel evolutionary rather than revolutionary.
2. **Insufficient Analysis of Error Cases:** The paper lacks a detailed error analysis. Understanding *where* the NBT models struggle (e.g., specific slot types, complex utterances) would provide valuable insights for future improvements and would strengthen the argument for its benefits.  What types of lexical variations still cause issues?
3. **Hyperparameter Sensitivity and Reproducibility Concerns:** The paper does not provide sufficient details regarding hyperparameter tuning. The choice of parameters can significantly impact performance, and without this information, it is difficult to assess the robustness of the results.  Furthermore, while the authors state they will make the cleaned DSTC2 data available, details on training code and specific configurations are lacking.  Reproducibility is crucial.
4. **Overly Complex Architecture (NBT-CNN):** While the NBT-CNN model incorporates convolutional layers, its effectiveness is not convincingly demonstrated. The performance difference between NBT-DNN and NBT-CNN is minimal, and the added complexity may not be justified. A clearer justification for the CNN architecture would be beneficial. The explanation for why element-wise multiplication was chosen over the dot product for combining representations felt weak.
5. **Limited Discussion of ASR Robustness:** The authors acknowledge the importance of ASR noise but only briefly mention performance gains when using transcriptions. A more detailed analysis of the NBT’s robustness to different levels of ASR error would strengthen the claims regarding real-world applicability.




**Questions to Authors:**

1. Can you provide a more detailed error analysis, outlining the common types of errors made by the NBT models?
2. Please provide specific details regarding hyperparameter tuning (e.g., learning rate, batch size, network architecture) for both NBT-DNN and NBT-CNN.
3. Will you be releasing the training code alongside the cleaned DSTC2 dataset to facilitate reproducibility?
4. What is the computational cost of the NBT-CNN compared to the NBT-DNN? Does the marginal improvement justify the increased complexity?
5. Could you elaborate on the rationale for using element-wise multiplication over the dot product for combining utterance and candidate representations?



**Additional Comments:**

*   The formatting is mostly consistent, but there are some minor inconsistencies in figure captions (e.g., missing periods).
*   The sentence structure in some sections is somewhat convoluted and could be simplified for clarity.
*   The citation style appears generally correct.
