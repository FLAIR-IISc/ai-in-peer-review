This paper presents the Neural Belief Tracker (NBT) family of models, designed to couple SLU and DST and alleviate the need for manually crafted semantic lexicons. The core idea is to leverage pre-trained word vectors for improved language understanding within the belief tracking framework. The authors demonstrate that NBT models achieve performance comparable to delexicalisation-based models (which *use* such lexicons) and significantly outperform them when those resources are unavailable.

**Contributions:**

1. **A novel approach to DST that eschews manual delexicalisation:** This is the primary contribution. The paper convincingly demonstrates a path toward scalable DST by moving away from lexicon-dependent methods and relying instead on the semantic information captured within pre-trained word embeddings.
2. **Effective integration of pre-trained word vectors into a belief tracking architecture:** The authors explore different ways to incorporate these vectors (DNN and CNN variants) and show their importance through ablation studies. Demonstrating *how* to effectively utilize these vectors is a key contribution.
3. **Comprehensive experimental evaluation:** The paper presents a thorough evaluation on two datasets (DSTC2 and WOZ 2.0), comparing the NBT models against strong baselines and providing insightful analysis of the results.

**Strengths:**

1. **Addresses a significant bottleneck in DST:** The reliance on manual lexicons is a known limitation in scaling DST to more complex domains. The NBT framework offers a viable solution to this problem.
2. **Clear and well-motivated design:** The architecture of the NBT is clearly explained and the reasoning behind each component (representation learning, semantic decoding, context modelling) is well-justified. The design choices appear sensible and are grounded in prior work.
3. **Strong experimental results:** The NBT models consistently outperform the baselines, and the results demonstrate the effectiveness of the approach, particularly in the low-resource setting where lexicons are unavailable. The ablation study highlighting the importance of semantically specialized word vectors is particularly valuable.
4. **Reproducibility:** The authors commit to making the datasets and code available, promoting reproducibility and further research in this area.
5. **Thorough comparison to existing methods:**  The careful selection of baseline models, including state-of-the-art delexicalisation techniques, provides a strong basis for comparison.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited novelty in individual components:** While the *combination* of components and the overall approach is novel, the individual components (DNN, CNN, word embeddings) are well-established techniques. The paper would benefit from further emphasizing the novelty of the specific *way* these components are integrated and applied to DST.
2. **Context modelling appears simplistic:** The context modelling component, relying only on the preceding system act, seems potentially limited.  More sophisticated context representations, such as incorporating the entire dialogue history, might improve performance. The motivation for choosing this simple approach isn't fully explained.
3. **Belief state update mechanism is basic:** The rule-based belief state update mechanism based on probabilities and a threshold feels somewhat disconnected from the neural network components and could be a potential area for improvement. Exploring a differentiable belief update process integrated within the NBT framework could be beneficial.
4. **Limited error analysis:** While the results are presented clearly, a more in-depth error analysis could provide valuable insights into the limitations of the model and directions for future work.  Specifically, analyzing the types of errors the model makes could highlight areas where the word vector representations are insufficient.



**Questions to Authors:**

1.  The paper highlights the importance of semantically specialized word vectors (Paragram-SL999).  Could you comment on the computational cost associated with these vectors compared to more standard word embeddings like GloVe, and whether this cost poses a practical limitation for very large ontologies or datasets?
2.  The context modelling component relies solely on the preceding system act.  Did you experiment with incorporating more of the dialogue history, and if so, what were the results?  If not, can you elaborate on the design choices that led you to prioritize simplicity in this component?
3.  The belief state update mechanism uses a fixed threshold (0.5). How sensitive is the performance to changes in this threshold, and did you explore any adaptive thresholding strategies?
4.  Could you provide more details about the types of errors the NBT models make? For example, are there specific slot-value pairs that are consistently misidentified?



**Additional Comments:**

*   The figure captions are a little sparse and could benefit from more descriptive text.
*   Consider adding a table summarizing the hyperparameter settings used for the different models.
*   The paper would benefit from a brief discussion of potential challenges and future directions, such as adapting the NBT framework to multi-domain dialogue systems or incorporating external knowledge sources.



Overall, this is a strong paper that addresses an important problem in dialogue state tracking. The proposed NBT framework is well-motivated, effectively implemented, and thoroughly evaluated. While there are some minor weaknesses, the contributions are significant and the paper is well-written and easy to understand. I recommend acceptance.
