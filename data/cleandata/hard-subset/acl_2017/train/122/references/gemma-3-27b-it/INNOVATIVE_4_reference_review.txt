This paper presents the Neural Belief Tracker (NBT), a model for dialogue state tracking that eschews hand-crafted lexical resources (delexicalisation) in favor of learning directly from pre-trained word vectors. The authors propose two variants, NBT-DNN and NBT-CNN, and demonstrate competitive performance on two restaurant search datasets (DSTC2 and WOZ 2.0), achieving results comparable to delexicalisation-based systems and outperforming them when lexical resources are unavailable.

The primary contribution of this work is the demonstration that effective dialogue state tracking *can* be achieved without relying on extensive manual effort in creating semantic lexicons. This is a significant step towards scaling DST models to more complex domains where such resources are impractical to build. The secondary contribution lies in the thoughtful architecture of the NBT, particularly its use of pre-trained word vectors to handle lexical variation and its context modelling component.

**Strengths:**

1. **Bold and Novel Approach:** The central idea of moving away from delexicalisation and embracing distributional semantics is a genuinely novel and promising direction for DST. The field has long been constrained by the bottleneck of lexicon creation, and this work offers a viable alternative.
2. **Strong Empirical Results:** The NBT models achieve competitive performance, matching or exceeding the results of established delexicalisation-based systems. The performance gap widens when lexical resources are limited, further strengthening the argument for the proposed approach.
3. **Clear and Well-Motivated Design:** The architecture is logically structured and clearly explained. The reasoning behind using pre-trained word vectors and the design choices for the representation learning components (DNN and CNN) are well-justified. The context modelling component, acknowledging the importance of dialogue history, is a thoughtful addition.
4. **Thorough Evaluation:**  Evaluation across two datasets – one with noisy ASR, one with clean text – provides a reasonable assessment of the model’s robustness and generalizability. The comparison against strong baselines is crucial for demonstrating the value of the proposed approach.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Analysis of Error Cases:** While the results are promising, the paper lacks a detailed analysis of *where* the NBT models struggle. Understanding the types of errors made (e.g., misinterpreting complex utterances, failing to handle specific lexical variations) would be valuable for guiding future research.
2. **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** The paper doesn’t delve into the sensitivity of the models to hyperparameter choices. Reporting an ablation study on key hyperparameters (e.g., embedding dimension, filter sizes in the CNN) would increase confidence in the robustness of the results.
3. **Modest Gains in Some Settings:**  While the NBT models perform well, the gains over lexicon-equipped baselines are not overwhelming in all settings. A more detailed discussion of the scenarios where the NBT truly shines would be beneficial.
4. **Simplistic Belief State Update:** The belief state update mechanism (averaging probabilities) is relatively simple. Exploring more sophisticated update rules (e.g., using Kalman filters or more advanced Bayesian methods) could potentially improve performance.



**Questions to Authors:**

1. Can you provide a more detailed error analysis, highlighting the types of utterances/scenarios where the NBT models struggle?  Specifically, are there particular slot-value pairs or linguistic phenomena that pose challenges?
2.  How sensitive are the results to the choice of pre-trained word vectors?  Did you experiment with other word vector spaces (e.g., fastText, word2vec) and if so, what were the results?
3.  Regarding the context modelling component: does the performance significantly degrade when the dialogue history is shortened (e.g., considering only the immediately preceding system turn)?
4.  Can you elaborate on the computational cost associated with the NBT models compared to the delexicalisation-based baselines?  Does the use of pre-trained word vectors introduce a significant overhead?



**Additional Comments:**

*   The paper is well-written and easy to follow.
*   The inclusion of the code and datasets would be highly beneficial for reproducibility.
*   Consider exploring the application of transfer learning to further improve performance, particularly in low-resource scenarios.



Overall, this is a strong paper that presents a compelling alternative to traditional DST approaches. While there are areas for improvement, the novelty and potential impact of the work are substantial. I recommend acceptance.
