This paper proposes the Neural Belief Tracker (NBT), a model designed to jointly perform spoken language understanding and dialogue state tracking without relying on hand-crafted semantic lexicons. The authors present two variants, NBT-DNN and NBT-CNN, which leverage pre-trained word vectors to handle lexical variation. The key claim is that NBT models achieve performance comparable to delexicalization-based models *without* the need for manually constructed resources and outperform them when such resources are unavailable.

The primary contribution is a demonstration that pre-trained word vectors can be effectively incorporated into a joint SLU/DST model, achieving competitive performance. A secondary contribution lies in the empirical validation on two datasets (DSTC2 and WOZ 2.0), showing the method’s robustness and ability to handle varying levels of data quality (noisy ASR vs. typed input).

**Strengths:**

1. **Practicality and Scalability:** The elimination of hand-crafted lexicons is a significant advantage. Delexicalization, as the authors correctly point out, does not scale well to complex domains. A system that can perform reasonably well *without* substantial manual effort is valuable.
2. **Competitive Performance:** The NBT models achieve performance on par with strong baseline methods relying on delexicalization. This demonstrates that the proposed approach is a viable alternative and doesn't suffer a significant performance penalty for avoiding manual lexicon creation.
3. **Clear Presentation:** The paper is generally well-written and clearly explains the NBT architecture and methodology. The inclusion of diagrams (Figures 2, 3, and 4) aids understanding.
4. **Thorough Evaluation:** The authors evaluate the models on two datasets, providing a more comprehensive assessment of their generalization ability. The ablation study examining the impact of different word vector types (Table 2) is particularly insightful.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Novelty:** While the application of pre-trained word vectors to DST is a reasonable approach, the core idea isn't entirely novel. Many NLP tasks have benefited from pre-trained embeddings. The paper doesn't strongly establish *why* this approach is particularly well-suited to DST or what unique challenges DST poses that necessitate this specific implementation. It feels more like a successful application of an established technique than a genuinely groundbreaking contribution.
2. **Insufficient Baseline Comparison:** The baseline models used are somewhat dated. While Henderson et al. (2014) remains a relevant comparison, more recent DST models employing transformer-based architectures (e.g., BERT, RoBERTa) should be included to provide a stronger benchmark. The authors acknowledge the importance of ASR robustness but don’t test against state-of-the-art ASR-robust methods.
3. **Overreliance on Pre-trained Vectors:** The model relies *entirely* on the semantic information contained within pre-trained vectors. While the ablation study shows the importance of vector quality, it doesn’t explore how the model could learn to refine or adapt these embeddings during training. The fixed nature of the embeddings may limit performance in specialized domains.
4. **Simplified Context Modelling:** The context modelling component (Equations 9 and 10) appears rather simplistic. The use of dot products to capture interaction between system acts and candidate slot-value pairs feels limiting. More sophisticated attention mechanisms or recurrent networks could potentially capture more nuanced contextual dependencies.

**Questions to Authors:**

1. Why were more recent, transformer-based DST models not included as baselines? What computational or implementation challenges prevented their inclusion?
2. Have you considered incorporating a mechanism for fine-tuning the pre-trained word vectors during training? If so, what were the results? If not, what are the potential drawbacks of doing so?
3. Could you elaborate on the rationale behind the specific context modelling approach (Equations 9 and 10)? How does this approach compare to more complex context modelling techniques (e.g., using a recurrent neural network to encode the dialogue history)?
4. The paper mentions "a hidden dependency" with delexicalization regarding identifying slot/value mentions. Could you expand on this point? Is the NBT approach inherently better at this identification, or does it simply circumvent the need for it?



**Additional Comments:**

The description of the belief state update mechanism (Section 4) is rather concise. More detail on the weighting coefficient (λ) and its impact on performance would be helpful. Consider including a discussion of potential failure cases and limitations of the NBT models.
