This paper presents the Neural Belief Tracker (NBT) family of models, designed to improve dialogue state tracking (DST) by jointly learning spoken language understanding (SLU) and DST without relying on hand-crafted semantic lexicons. The core idea is to leverage pre-trained word vectors to handle lexical variation and improve language understanding capabilities. The authors claim to achieve performance comparable to delexicalisation-based models (which *do* use handcrafted lexicons) and significantly outperform them when such resources are unavailable.

The primary contribution of this work is the demonstration that pre-trained word vectors can effectively be used within a joint SLU/DST model to achieve competitive performance, and particularly excel in low-resource scenarios where lexicon construction is impractical. This is a valuable contribution as it reduces the reliance on expensive and difficult-to-maintain handcrafted resources. A secondary contribution is the exploration of different architectural choices (NBT-DNN vs. NBT-CNN) for incorporating these word vectors, providing insights into the suitability of different neural network architectures for this task. Finally, the empirical evaluation on two datasets, including a comparison to strong baselines, provides a solid foundation for claiming the effectiveness of the proposed approach.

**Strengths:**

1. **Addresses a significant challenge:** The reliance on manually crafted semantic dictionaries (delexicalisation) is a major bottleneck for scaling DST systems to new domains. The NBT directly addresses this limitation by effectively utilizing pre-trained word embeddings. The experimental results convincingly demonstrate the benefits, especially in the WOZ 2.0 dataset where no lexicon is utilized.
2. **Strong Empirical Validation:** The paper presents a thorough experimental evaluation on two benchmark datasets (DSTC2 and WOZ 2.0), comparing the NBT models against strong baseline systems. The reported results are statistically significant and demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed approach. The ablation study (Table 2) clearly illustrates the importance of using semantically specialized word vectors.
3. **Clear and Well-Structured Presentation:** The paper is well-written and easy to follow. The architecture of the NBT model is clearly explained with the aid of diagrams (Figures 2, 3, and 4). The motivation for the design choices is also clearly articulated.
4. **Reproducibility:** The authors state their intention to make the cleaned datasets available, which promotes reproducibility and further research. 
5. **Contextual understanding:** The inclusion of context modelling through system acts is a beneficial addition, allowing the model to better disambiguate user utterances based on the dialogue history.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Novelty in Architecture:** While the application of pre-trained word vectors to DST is a valuable contribution, the underlying neural network architectures (DNN and CNN) are relatively standard. The novelty primarily lies in *how* these architectures are adapted and combined with word vectors for this specific task, rather than a fundamentally new architectural approach.
2. **Simplified Belief State Update:** The belief state update mechanism is relatively simple (averaging with a fixed weight). While functional, it may not fully leverage the probabilistic nature of the NBT's output. Exploring more sophisticated update strategies, like Kalman filtering or particle filtering, could potentially lead to further improvements.
3. **Focus on Restaurant Domain:** The evaluation is limited to a single domain (restaurant search). It would be valuable to investigate the generalizability of the NBT models to other, more complex domains.
4. **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** The paper lacks detailed discussion on hyperparameter tuning and sensitivity analysis. A more in-depth investigation into the impact of different hyperparameters on performance would strengthen the results.

**Questions to Authors:**

1.  Could you provide a more detailed analysis of the computational cost associated with the NBT models compared to the baseline delexicalisation-based models?
2.  How does the performance of the NBT models degrade with increasing noise in the ASR output? Have you explored any techniques to further improve robustness to ASR errors?
3.  Regarding the choice of Paragram-SL999 vectors, did you experiment with other pre-trained embeddings (e.g., fastText, BERT) and if so, what were the results?
4.  Could you elaborate on the choice of λ = 0.55 for the belief state update mechanism? Was this value determined through a systematic hyperparameter search?

**Additional Comments:**

*   The paper would benefit from a discussion of potential error sources and limitations of the NBT models.
*   The description of the semantic decoding module (Section 3.2) could be more detailed.
*   Consider including a table summarizing the hyperparameters used for all the models.
