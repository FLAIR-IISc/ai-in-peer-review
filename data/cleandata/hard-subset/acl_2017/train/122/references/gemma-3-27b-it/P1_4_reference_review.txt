## Peer Review for "Neural Belief Tracker: Data-Driven Dialogue State Tracking"

**Summary:**

This paper introduces the Neural Belief Tracker (NBT) family of models, aiming to improve dialogue state tracking (DST) by jointly modeling spoken language understanding (SLU) and DST without relying on hand-crafted semantic lexicons (delexicalization). The core idea is to leverage pre-trained word vectors to capture semantic similarity and handle lexical variation, composing these vectors into intermediate representations used for intent recognition. The authors demonstrate that NBT models achieve performance comparable to delexicalization-based methods, and significantly outperform them when no lexicon is available, on two restaurant search datasets (DSTC2 and WOZ 2.0).

**Main Contributions (as I see them):**

1. **Successful Application of Pre-trained Word Vectors to DST:** The paper convincingly demonstrates that using pre-trained word vectors (specifically, semantically specialized ones) *can* effectively replace the need for manually curated lexical resources in DST. This is a significant step towards more scalable and adaptable DST systems.
2. **Joint SLU/DST with Vector Space Reasoning:** The architecture effectively couples SLU and DST, moving away from discrete feature engineering and towards a continuous representation learning approach. This is a solid engineering contribution that simplifies the pipeline.
3. **Empirical Evaluation on Multiple Datasets:** The paper provides a strong empirical validation of the proposed models on both a standard DST benchmark (DSTC2) and a more realistic (Wizard-of-Oz) dataset, demonstrating the robustness of the approach.


**Strengths:**

1. **Addresses a critical limitation of existing DST systems:**  The reliance on hand-crafted lexicons for delexicalization is a major bottleneck for scaling DST to new domains and languages. This paper directly tackles that issue with a compelling alternative.
2. **Strong Empirical Results:**  The NBT models consistently outperform baseline delexicalization models, especially in the low-resource scenario (WOZ 2.0 without a lexicon). The performance is competitive with state-of-the-art lexicon-aware models, showcasing its effectiveness. The ablation study (Table 2) further highlights the importance of semantic specialization in word vectors.
3. **Clear and Well-Structured Presentation:** The paper is well-written and organized. The architecture (Figure 2) is clearly illustrated, and the explanation of the different components is concise and understandable. The motivation and related work are well-presented.
4. **Reproducibility Encouraged:** The authors explicitly state that the cleaned dataset and code will be made available, which is crucial for fostering further research in this area.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Analysis of Error Cases:** While the results are strong, the paper lacks a detailed analysis of *where* the NBT models still struggle. Understanding the types of errors (e.g., ambiguity, complex utterances, ASR errors) would provide valuable insights for future improvements.
2. **Context Modeling Simplification:** The context modeling component (using only the last system act) feels somewhat simplistic. More sophisticated approaches (e.g., incorporating a history of turns or attention mechanisms over the dialogue context) might yield further performance gains.  The reasoning for *why* this simplification was chosen is not fully articulated.
3. **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** The paper doesn’t discuss the sensitivity of the models to hyperparameter choices (e.g., vector dimensionality, learning rate). Understanding the robustness of the performance to different configurations would be beneficial.
4. **ASR Robustness as a Future Direction - Needs More Detail:** While the authors acknowledge ASR errors are a hurdle, the mention of increasing performance with transcriptions feels like an afterthought. A more comprehensive analysis of how the models handle noisy ASR input, beyond simply mentioning the goal accuracy increase, would be valuable.



**Questions to Authors:**

1. **Error Analysis:** Could you provide a more detailed error analysis, identifying common types of errors made by the NBT models? What types of utterances or dialogue states are particularly challenging?
2. **Context Modeling:** Why did you choose to model context using only the last system act?  Have you experimented with incorporating a longer dialogue history, and if so, what were the results?
3. **Hyperparameter Tuning:** How sensitive is the performance of the NBT models to different hyperparameter settings?  Could you provide some insight into the choices made and the rationale behind them?
4. **ASR Robustness:**  Could you elaborate on experiments to mitigate ASR errors?  Did you explore any techniques like data augmentation with ASR simulated errors or using the N-best list of ASR hypotheses (as mentioned in the belief state update)?




**Additional Comments:**

*   The formatting of Figure 3 and Figure 4 is a little inconsistent; consider unifying the style.
*   The notation could be made more consistent throughout the paper. For example, the meaning of 'r' changes slightly between sections.



Overall, this is a strong paper that addresses an important problem in DST. The proposed NBT models are a promising step towards more scalable and adaptable dialogue systems. Addressing the weaknesses outlined above would further strengthen the contribution and impact of this work. I recommend acceptance.
