## Peer Review for ACL Submission: Neural Belief Tracker: Data-Driven Dialogue State Tracking

**Summary:**

This paper introduces the Neural Belief Tracker (NBT) family of models for dialogue state tracking (DST). The core idea is to couple Spoken Language Understanding (SLU) and DST, moving away from reliance on manually crafted delexicalization resources (semantic dictionaries) by instead leveraging pre-trained word vectors to handle lexical variation. The authors present two NBT variants, NBT-DNN and NBT-CNN, and demonstrate through experiments on DSTC2 and WOZ 2.0 datasets that they achieve comparable performance to delexicalization-based models, and significantly outperform them when no such resources are available.

**Main Contributions (as I see them):**

1. **A practical and competitive approach to DST without manual lexicon creation:** The NBT models offer a viable alternative to traditional delexicalization methods, which are known to be a bottleneck for scaling DST to complex domains.  Achieving performance on par with lexicon-based methods *without* the lexicon is a significant practical contribution.
2. **Demonstration of the effectiveness of pre-trained word vectors in DST:** The paper convincingly shows that leveraging semantic information encoded in pre-trained word vectors improves DST performance, particularly in scenarios with limited resources or noisy ASR. This underscores the potential of transfer learning in dialogue systems.
3. **Clear and well-documented implementation:** The paper provides a relatively detailed description of the NBT architecture and experimental setup, making it easier for others to reproduce and build upon their work. The release of the datasets and code would further strengthen this aspect.

**Strengths:**

1. **Addresses a critical bottleneck in DST:** The reliance on manually crafted lexicons is a significant limitation for real-world deployment.  The NBT models directly address this limitation, offering a more scalable solution.
2. **Strong empirical results:** The NBT models consistently outperform baseline models, particularly on the WOZ 2.0 dataset where no lexical resources were available.  The statistical significance reported reinforces the robustness of these findings.
3. **Well-motivated design choices:** The authors provide a clear rationale for their design choices, such as the use of semantically specialized word vectors and the specific architecture of the NBT-DNN and NBT-CNN models.  This enhances the understandability and credibility of the work.
4. **Thorough experimentation:** The experiments across two datasets, with varying levels of noise and resource availability, demonstrate the generalizability of the NBT models. The ablation study highlighting the importance of word vector quality is particularly insightful.
5. **Clear writing and presentation:** The paper is well-organized, clearly written, and easy to follow. The figures and tables are informative and contribute to understanding the proposed approach.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited novelty of individual components:** While the *combination* of techniques is valuable, the individual components (e.g., CNNs, DNNs, word embeddings) are well-established in the NLP literature. The paper would benefit from a more detailed discussion of how these components are specifically tailored to the DST task and how their interaction leads to improved performance.
2. **Lack of comparison to more recent DST models:** The baselines used for comparison are somewhat dated (Henderson et al., 2014).  Comparing the NBT models to more recent state-of-the-art DST approaches, such as those based on transformers (e.g., TRADE, SOM-DST), would provide a more comprehensive evaluation of their performance.
3. **Simplistic belief state update mechanism:** The belief state update mechanism relies on a simple averaging scheme. Exploring more sophisticated methods, such as incorporating a Kalman filter or using a recurrent neural network, could potentially further improve performance.  The impact of the λ parameter could also be discussed in more detail.
4. **Limited analysis of error cases:**  A more detailed error analysis, identifying common failure modes of the NBT models, would provide valuable insights for future research. For example, are there specific slot-value pairs or utterance types that the model struggles with?



**Questions to Authors:**

1.  Could you elaborate on the computational cost of the NBT models compared to the baseline methods? Specifically, what is the training and inference time for each model?
2.  How sensitive are the NBT models to the choice of pre-trained word vectors? Did you experiment with other vector spaces, such as fastText or BERT embeddings?
3.  Could you provide more details on the specific hyperparameters used for training the NBT models (e.g., learning rate, batch size, number of epochs)?
4.  The paper mentions that the NBT model is "the first to successfully use pre-trained word vector spaces to improve the language understanding capability of belief tracking models." Are there any previous attempts to leverage word vectors in DST that might be relevant to discuss?
5.  What is the impact of ASR errors on the performance of the NBT models? Have you considered incorporating techniques for ASR error correction into the NBT framework?



**Additional Comments:**

*   It would be beneficial to make the code and datasets publicly available to facilitate reproducibility and further research.
*   The discussion of related work could be expanded to include more recent advances in DST.
*   Consider adding a table summarizing the key differences between the NBT-DNN and NBT-CNN models.
