This paper introduces the Neural Belief Tracker (NBT), a family of models aiming to improve dialogue state tracking (DST) by jointly modeling spoken language understanding (SLU) and DST, and by eschewing hand-crafted semantic lexicons in favor of learning from pre-trained word vectors. The core contribution lies in demonstrating that comparable performance to delexicalization-based models can be achieved *without* manual lexicon construction, offering a potential scalability benefit. A secondary contribution is the exploration of different representation learning components (DNN and CNN) within the NBT framework, and the finding that semantic specialization of word vectors (Paragram-SL999) is crucial for performance.

**Strengths:**

1. **Addresses a practical limitation of DST:** The reliance on manually crafted delexicalization dictionaries is a significant bottleneck for scaling DST to new domains. The NBT’s ability to achieve competitive performance without them is a valuable step toward practical deployment.
2. **Thorough experimental evaluation:** The authors evaluate the NBT models on two datasets (DSTC2 and WOZ 2.0) and compare them to strong baselines, including state-of-the-art delexicalization-based models. The ablation study regarding word vector initialization is particularly insightful.
3. **Clear and well-structured presentation:** The paper is generally well-written and organized. The architecture of the NBT model is clearly explained with diagrams, and the experimental setup is described in sufficient detail.
4. **Demonstrated benefit of semantically specialized word vectors:** The result showing the superiority of Paragram-SL999 vectors over GloVe and randomly initialized vectors reinforces the importance of semantic representation in this task. This is a valuable finding, highlighting a key design choice.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Incremental Improvement, Not a Paradigm Shift:** While the elimination of manual lexicons is positive, the performance gains are modest. The NBT models match, but do not substantially exceed, the performance of existing delexicalization-based systems *when those systems have access to lexicons*. The core claim of addressing scalability feels less impactful without a demonstrably superior performance profile.
2. **Oversimplified Context Modeling:** The context modeling component (using similarity scores between system acts and candidate slot-value pairs) feels underdeveloped. The reliance solely on the *last* system act seems overly restrictive, potentially overlooking important contextual information from earlier turns. The use of dot product for similarity, while simple, may not capture nuanced relationships.
3. **Lack of Analysis of Failure Cases:** The paper lacks a detailed analysis of the errors made by the NBT models. Understanding *where* the models struggle would provide valuable insights for future improvement. For example, are there specific types of slot-value pairs that are consistently misidentified?
4. **Presentation of Equations & Notation:** Several equations (e.g., Equations 9 & 10) are presented without sufficient explanation of the rationale behind the chosen operations (element-wise multiplication). While technically correct, they lack intuitiveness and make the model more difficult to understand. The repeated use of 's' as a superscript denoting slot is also confusing.
5. **Minor Formatting Inconsistencies:** There are a few minor inconsistencies in formatting throughout the paper (e.g. inconsistent use of bolding). These don’t detract from understanding, but indicate a lack of careful proofreading.

**Questions to Authors:**

1. Can you provide a more detailed analysis of the NBT models’ error cases, specifically identifying types of slot-value pairs where the models consistently underperform?
2.  Could you elaborate on the rationale behind using element-wise multiplication in Equations 9 and 10, and explore alternative interaction functions?
3.  Have you experimented with incorporating more contextual information beyond the last system act (e.g., a history of system and user utterances)? If so, what were the results?
4.  What is the computational cost (training time, inference time) of the NBT models compared to the delexicalization-based baselines?
5.  The paper mentions the potential for scaling to "sophisticated application domains." Can you provide more concrete examples of such domains and explain how NBT specifically addresses the challenges they present?



**Additional Comments:**

*  The figure captions are generally informative, but could benefit from more specific descriptions of the components shown.
*  The reference list is extensive and well-formatted.
*  Consider adding a discussion of the limitations of using pre-trained word vectors, especially the potential for bias.



Despite the contributions, I lean towards **rejection** due to the incremental nature of the improvement and the areas for improvement identified. While the idea of eliminating manual lexicons is valuable, the performance gains are not substantial enough to justify the complexity of the proposed model, particularly given the relative simplicity of the context modeling component. A more thorough analysis of failure cases and exploration of more sophisticated context modeling techniques are needed to strengthen the paper’s claims.
