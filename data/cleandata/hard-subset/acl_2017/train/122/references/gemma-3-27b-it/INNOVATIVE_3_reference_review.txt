This paper introduces the Neural Belief Tracker (NBT), a model aiming to improve dialogue state tracking by jointly learning spoken language understanding and belief state estimation without relying on hand-crafted semantic lexicons. The core idea is to leverage pre-trained word vectors to handle lexical variation and ambiguity, moving away from exact matching approaches. The authors present two variants, NBT-DNN and NBT-CNN, and demonstrate competitive performance on two restaurant search datasets, DSTC2 and WOZ 2.0.

The primary contribution of this work is the **novel approach of directly incorporating pre-trained word vectors into a joint SLU/DST model to address lexical variation without delexicalization**. While joint models exist, and the use of word embeddings is common, the explicit focus on resolving variation *through* vector space reasoning, and demonstrably achieving performance comparable to lexicon-based approaches, is a significant step. This is particularly compelling given the acknowledged scalability issues of manual lexicon creation. A secondary contribution is the **thorough experimental evaluation on two datasets**, demonstrating the effectiveness of the approach, particularly in low-resource scenarios (where lexicons are unavailable). Finally, the **demonstration that semantic specialization of word vectors (Paragram-SL999) yields substantial performance gains** reinforces the importance of semantic information in this task.

**Strengths:**

1. **Novelty and Potential Impact:** The core idea is genuinely novel and addresses a significant pain point in DST: the reliance on manually created lexicons. Scaling DST to more complex domains necessitates moving away from these brittle resources, and NBT offers a promising alternative. The potential for reduced engineering effort and increased robustness is substantial.
2. **Strong Empirical Results:** The NBT models achieve competitive performance against strong baselines, including systems leveraging hand-crafted lexicons. Critically, they *outperform* these baselines when lexicons are unavailable. This is strong evidence of the method’s effectiveness.
3. **Clear and Well-Structured Presentation:** The paper is well-written and clearly explains the model architecture and experimental setup. The motivation is well-articulated, and the contributions are readily apparent. The ablation study regarding word vector types is also insightful.
4. **Practical Relevance:** The problem addressed is directly relevant to building real-world dialogue systems. The focus on scalability and reducing reliance on manual effort is highly valuable. The authors explicitly acknowledge the importance of ASR robustness, signaling awareness of real-world deployment challenges.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Analysis of Error Cases:** While the paper demonstrates good overall performance, it lacks a detailed analysis of *where* the model fails. Understanding the types of errors made by NBT (e.g., specific slot values it struggles with, common sources of misinterpretation) would be valuable for guiding future improvements.
2. **Simplicity of the Belief State Update Mechanism:** The belief state update rule is relatively simple (averaging probabilities). More sophisticated update mechanisms (e.g., incorporating confidence scores, using a more nuanced weighting scheme) might further improve performance. The impact of the lambda parameter seems somewhat arbitrarily chosen, a deeper investigation into optimal update strategies would be beneficial.
3. **Lack of Comparison to More Recent DST Models:** The baselines are reasonably strong, but the field of DST is rapidly evolving. A comparison to more recent, state-of-the-art models (e.g., those utilizing transformers or more sophisticated attention mechanisms) would provide a stronger benchmark.
4. **Limited Discussion of Computational Cost:** The authors don't provide any information on the computational cost of the NBT models compared to the baselines. Understanding the trade-off between performance and efficiency is important for practical applications.



**Questions to Authors:**

1. Can you provide a more detailed analysis of the types of errors made by the NBT models? Specifically, which slot values or utterance patterns are most challenging?
2. Have you explored more sophisticated belief state update mechanisms beyond the simple averaging approach? If so, what were the results?
3. Could you comment on the computational cost (training and inference time) of the NBT models compared to the baselines?
4. How sensitive is the performance to the choice of pre-trained word vectors? Are there other vector spaces that yield comparable results?



**Additional Comments:**

The paper is generally very strong. The lack of in-depth error analysis and comparison to the very latest DST models are the main areas for improvement. However, the novelty and potential impact of the approach are significant enough that I lean towards acceptance despite these minor shortcomings. The focus on removing the bottleneck of manual lexicon creation is a valuable contribution.
