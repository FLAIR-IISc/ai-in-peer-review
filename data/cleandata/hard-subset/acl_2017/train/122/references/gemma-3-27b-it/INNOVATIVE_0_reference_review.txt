This paper presents the Neural Belief Tracker (NBT), a model designed to jointly learn spoken language understanding and dialogue state tracking without relying on hand-crafted lexicons. The core idea is to leverage pre-trained word vectors to reason over semantic similarity, rather than relying on exact matching of slot-value pairs. The authors propose two variants, NBT-DNN and NBT-CNN, and demonstrate their performance on the DSTC2 and WOZ 2.0 datasets.

The primary contribution of this work is the introduction of a novel approach to dialogue state tracking that *completely* eschews delexicalization and hand-crafted resources. While joint SLU/DST models exist, the reliance on pre-defined dictionaries has been a persistent limitation. NBT tackles this head-on, demonstrating that strong performance can be achieved solely through the power of pre-trained word embeddings. A secondary, supporting contribution is the empirical demonstration that semantically specialized word vectors (Paragram-SL999) significantly outperform generic vectors, highlighting the importance of semantic representation in this task. Finally, the architecture, while not groundbreaking in its individual components, presents a reasonable and effective way to integrate word embeddings into a belief tracking framework.

**Strengths:**

1. **Bold and Novel Approach:** The complete removal of delexicalization is a genuinely novel and significant step. It addresses a major bottleneck in scaling DST systems to more complex domains. This is a direction the field desperately needs to explore.
2. **Strong Empirical Results:** The NBT models achieve competitive performance against state-of-the-art delexicalization-based models, and *outperform* them when no semantic dictionaries are available. This convincingly demonstrates the efficacy of the proposed approach. The fact that performance on WOZ 2.0 (a less noisy, more naturally-expressed dataset) is particularly strong supports the claim that NBT excels at semantic understanding.
3. **Insightful Analysis of Word Embeddings:** The ablation study demonstrating the benefit of semantically-specialized word vectors is valuable. It reinforces the importance of high-quality semantic representations for this task, and provides practical guidance for future research.
4. **Clear and Well-Written:** The paper is generally well-written and easy to follow. The architecture is clearly explained, and the experimental setup is well-defined.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Analysis of Failure Cases:** While the results are positive, the paper lacks a detailed analysis of *where* NBT struggles. What types of utterances or slot-value pairs does it misclassify? Understanding these failure cases would provide valuable insights for future improvements.
2. **Context Modelling Simplification:** The context modelling approach (using only the previous system act) feels somewhat simplistic. While acknowledged by the authors, exploring more sophisticated methods for incorporating dialogue history could potentially further improve performance.  The explanation of *why* this specific approach was chosen is thin.
3. **Evaluation Scope:** The evaluation is limited to the restaurant domain and two datasets. While these datasets are standard benchmarks, exploring the generalizability of NBT to other domains would strengthen the claims of scalability.
4. **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** The paper lacks a discussion of hyperparameter tuning and sensitivity. How robust are the results to changes in learning rate, embedding dimension, or network architecture?  Understanding this would give more confidence in the reproducibility of the findings.

**Questions to Authors:**

1. Could you provide a more detailed analysis of the types of errors NBT makes? Specifically, can you identify any recurring patterns or challenging cases?
2.  The context modeling seems simplified. Why was this approach specifically chosen over other methods (e.g., attending over the entire dialogue history)?
3. Do you have any preliminary results on the performance of NBT in other dialogue domains, or plans to evaluate it on additional datasets?
4. How sensitive are the results to the choice of hyperparameters, particularly the learning rate and embedding dimension?
