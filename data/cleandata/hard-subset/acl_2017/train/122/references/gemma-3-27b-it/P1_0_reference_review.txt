## Peer Review for ACL Submission: Neural Belief Tracker: Data-Driven Dialogue State Tracking

**Summary:**

This paper presents the Neural Belief Tracker (NBT) family of models for dialogue state tracking (DST). The core contribution lies in moving away from delexicalization techniques (relying on hand-crafted semantic dictionaries) and instead leveraging pre-trained word vectors to handle lexical variation in user utterances. The authors propose two model variants, NBT-DNN and NBT-CNN, which jointly model SLU and DST. Evaluation on two restaurant search datasets (DSTC2 and WOZ 2.0) demonstrates that NBT models achieve comparable performance to delexicalization-based approaches, and significantly outperform them when semantic lexicons are unavailable.

**Main Contributions (as perceived by this reviewer):**

1. **Demonstration of effective use of pre-trained word vectors for DST:** The paper convincingly shows that pre-trained word embeddings can be a viable alternative to hand-crafted lexicons for handling lexical variation in dialogue state tracking, without a significant performance drop. This is a valuable contribution, particularly for scaling DST to new domains where creating lexicons is costly.
2. **Joint SLU/DST model without delexicalization:**  The proposed NBT models effectively couple SLU and DST without relying on the intermediate step of delexicalization, simplifying the pipeline and potentially improving robustness.
3. **Comprehensive experimental evaluation:** The authors perform a thorough evaluation on two standard datasets, comparing their models against strong baselines and analyzing the impact of different word vector types.

**Strengths:**

1. **Addresses a significant challenge in DST:** The reliance on hand-crafted lexicons is a major bottleneck for scaling DST to new domains. This paper offers a promising solution by leveraging the power of pre-trained word embeddings.
2. **Strong empirical results:** The NBT models achieve competitive performance on both datasets, especially in the low-resource setting where lexicons are not available. The results clearly demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed approach.
3. **Clear and well-structured presentation:** The paper is well-written and organized, with a clear explanation of the models and experiments. The architecture diagrams (Figures 2, 3, and 4) are helpful for understanding the model details.
4. **Thorough ablation study (regarding word vectors):**  The analysis in Section 6.2 showcasing the importance of semantically-specialized word vectors is particularly insightful and highlights a key advantage of this approach.
5. **Reproducibility:** The authors commit to making the code and cleaned datasets publicly available, which enhances the reproducibility and impact of their work.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited novelty of the core architectural components:**  The building blocks of NBT (DNNs, CNNs, pre-trained word vectors) are well-established in NLP. The novelty resides primarily in the *application* of these components to the DST task in a specific way, and the demonstrated effectiveness of that application. While this is a valuable contribution, the architectural novelty is not substantial.
2. **Markovian Assumption for Context Modelling:** The reliance on only the *last* system utterance for context is a simplifying assumption.  More sophisticated methods for incorporating dialogue history (e.g., recurrent models operating over the entire dialogue context) might yield further improvements. The authors acknowledge this, but it's a limitation to consider.
3. **Lack of comparison with more recent DST models:** The comparison is primarily against Henderson et al. (2014) and Wen et al. (2017).  More recent DST models, particularly those based on Transformers, would provide a more challenging benchmark and a better assessment of the NBT's competitiveness.  A brief discussion of how the NBT approach compares to these newer models would be beneficial.
4. **Belief State Update Mechanism is relatively simple:** The rule-based belief state update mechanism with a fixed lambda value feels somewhat ad-hoc. Exploring more sophisticated update mechanisms, potentially learned end-to-end with the belief tracker, could improve performance.



**Questions to Authors:**

1.  How sensitive is the performance of the NBT models to the choice of pre-trained word vectors? Have you explored other types of embeddings (e.g., BERT embeddings) and what were the results?
2.  The paper states the NBT models are "the first to successfully use pre-trained word vector spaces to improve the language understanding capability of belief tracking models".  Could you clarify this claim? Are there previous works that have explored using word embeddings for DST, and if so, how does your approach differ significantly?
3.  Regarding the context modelling, could you elaborate on the rationale behind choosing only the last system utterance? Have you experimented with incorporating more of the dialogue history and what were the results?
4.  Could you provide some insights into the computational cost (training time, inference time) of the NBT models compared to the baseline delexicalization-based approach?




**Additional Comments:**

*   The paper would benefit from a more detailed discussion of the limitations of the proposed approach and potential avenues for future research.
*   Consider adding a small example of the predicted belief states for a sample dialogue turn to illustrate how the NBT models work in practice.



**Overall Assessment:**

This is a solid and well-executed paper that addresses an important problem in dialogue state tracking. The proposed NBT models offer a promising alternative to traditional delexicalization-based approaches and demonstrate strong empirical performance. While the architectural novelty is limited, the demonstrated effectiveness and practical implications of the work are significant. I recommend **Accept** with minor revisions addressing the weaknesses and questions outlined above.
