This paper introduces the Neural Belief Tracker (NBT) family of models for dialogue state tracking, aiming to overcome the limitations of traditional approaches reliant on hand-crafted semantic lexicons. The core contribution lies in directly leveraging pre-trained word vectors to couple SLU and DST, enabling the model to handle lexical variation without delexicalization. A secondary contribution is the empirical demonstration that NBT models achieve comparable performance to delexicalization-based models when lexicons are available, and significantly outperform them when they are not. Finally, the paper provides a thorough experimental evaluation on two datasets, DSTC2 and WOZ 2.0, highlighting the importance of semantic specialization in the word vectors used.

**Strengths:**

1. **Addressing a Significant Problem:** The reliance on hand-crafted lexicons in traditional DST systems is a major bottleneck for scalability and deployment in real-world scenarios. This paper directly tackles this issue with a compelling approach.
2. **Strong Empirical Results:** The NBT models demonstrate competitive performance with existing state-of-the-art methods, particularly in low-resource settings where lexicons are unavailable. The results on both DSTC2 and WOZ 2.0 datasets are convincing.
3. **Thorough Analysis of Word Vector Impact:** The ablation study demonstrating the importance of semantically specialized word vectors (Paragram-SL999) over generic (GloVe) or randomly initialized vectors is a crucial finding and provides valuable insight into the model's behavior. This is a particularly strong point.
4. **Clear and Comprehensive Presentation:** The paper is well-written and clearly explains the model architecture, motivation, and experimental setup. The inclusion of diagrams (Figures 2, 3, and 4) aids in understanding the model's components.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Novelty in Architecture:** While the application of pre-trained word vectors to DST is valuable, the underlying neural network architecture (DNN, CNN) is fairly standard. The novelty primarily resides in *how* these architectures are used, rather than the architectures themselves. The architecture doesn't push the boundaries of neural network design.
2. **Context Modelling Simplification:** The context modelling component, based solely on the last system utterance, appears somewhat simplistic. While acknowledged by the authors, more sophisticated methods for incorporating dialogue history could potentially further improve performance. The assumption of Markovian dependence might be restrictive.
3. **Evaluation Metric Limitations:** While joint goal accuracy and request accuracy are standard metrics, they provide a limited view of the model's performance. Investigating metrics that capture the nuances of belief state representation (e.g., entropy, KL divergence) could offer a more comprehensive evaluation.
4. **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** The paper does not offer extensive analysis of hyperparameter tuning and its effect on performance. Reporting sensitivity analyses would strengthen the robustness of the results.

**Questions to Authors:**

1.  The paper mentions that the NBT models outperform delexicalization-based approaches when lexicons are *not* available. How does the performance gap change as the size and quality of the manually curated lexicons increase? Could you provide a more granular comparison across different lexicon sizes?
2.  The context modelling component currently only considers the last system utterance. Have you explored more sophisticated methods for incorporating longer dialogue history (e.g., hierarchical recurrent networks, attention mechanisms over the entire dialogue history)?
3.  Could you elaborate on the choice of the Adam optimizer and the specific learning rate schedule used during training? Did you experiment with other optimization algorithms?
4.  What is the computational cost (training time, inference time) of the NBT models compared to the baseline delexicalization-based models?



**Additional Comments:**

*   The notation used throughout the paper is consistent and well-defined.
*   The related work section provides a good overview of the existing literature in dialogue state tracking.
*   The authors' commitment to making the datasets and code publicly available is commendable.
