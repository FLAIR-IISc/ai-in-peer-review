- Introduces Neural Belief Tracker (NBT) models that use pre-trained word vectors to avoid hand-crafted delexicalization for dialogue state tracking.
- Proposes two NBT variants (NBT-DNN, NBT-CNN) and shows competitive performance on DSTC2 and WOZ 2.0.
- Demonstrates that pre-trained, semantically specialized word vectors can effectively replace manual semantic dictionaries in DST.
- Shows strong empirical results, outperforming delexicalization approaches especially when lexical resources are unavailable.
- Ablation study highlights the importance of semantically specialized embeddings (Paragram-SL999) for model performance.
- Presentation is clear, well-structured, and supported by helpful architecture diagrams.
- Addresses a practical bottleneck in DST by reducing reliance on hand-crafted lexical resources, aiding scalability.
- Compares against strong baselines and uses standard datasets (DSTC2 with ASR output, WOZ 2.0 clean text) for robust evaluation.
- Core architectural components (DNNs, CNNs, element-wise operations) are standard; novelty lies mainly in their application to DST.
- Context modeling via dot-product similarity with system acts is simplistic and may not capture complex dialogue history.
- Lacks detailed error analysis to identify common failure cases and problematic slot-value or utterance types.
- Asks authors to elaborate on context modeling limitations and whether alternatives (RNNs, attention) were considered.
- Requests examples and analysis of frequent NBT errors and exploration of other semantic knowledge injection methods (e.g., knowledge graph embeddings).
- Notes excellent formatting/readability, commendable code/dataset release plans, and suggests the belief state update discussion be more concise.
- Overall assessment: strong, practical contribution with compelling results; recommend acceptance with minor revisions addressing noted weaknesses.
