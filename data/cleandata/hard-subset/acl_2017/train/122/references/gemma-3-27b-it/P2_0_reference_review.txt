## Review for ACL: Neural Belief Tracker: Data-Driven Dialogue State Tracking

**Summary:**

This paper presents the Neural Belief Tracker (NBT), a family of models designed to improve dialogue state tracking (DST) by jointly learning spoken language understanding (SLU) and DST, without relying on hand-crafted semantic lexicons (delexicalization). The core idea is to leverage pre-trained word vectors to reason about semantic similarity, allowing the model to generalize to variations in user language. The authors demonstrate that NBT models achieve performance comparable to delexicalization-based approaches *and* significantly outperform them when semantic lexicons are unavailable.

**Main Contributions (as I see them):**

1. **Successful Integration of Pre-trained Word Vectors for DST:** The paper convincingly demonstrates that pre-trained word vectors can be effectively utilized within a joint SLU/DST framework, achieving competitive performance without the need for hand-crafted lexicons. This is a significant step towards scaling DST to more complex domains.
2. **Demonstration of the Limitations of Delexicalization:** The paper highlights a critical limitation of delexicalization – its reliance on manually constructed semantic dictionaries, which are expensive to create and do not scale. The NBT models offer a viable alternative.
3. **A Relatively Simple and Effective Architecture:**  The NBT architecture, while not groundbreaking in its individual components, represents a well-engineered approach to combining representation learning, context modeling, and semantic decoding.



**Strengths:**

1. **Strong Empirical Results:** The NBT models consistently outperform baseline delexicalization methods on both the DSTC2 and WOZ 2.0 datasets, especially in the low-resource setting where lexicons are absent.  The reported results (goal accuracy, request accuracy) are compelling and demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed approach.
2. **Clear Motivation and Problem Statement:** The paper clearly articulates the limitations of existing DST approaches, particularly the scalability issues associated with delexicalization. The motivation for the work is well-defined and relevant to the field.
3. **Thorough Ablation Study (Word Vector Choice):** The ablation study on different word vector embeddings (random, GloVe, Paragram-SL999) provides valuable insights into the importance of semantic specialization in the word embeddings. Demonstrating the importance of *how* word vectors are trained, and not just *that* they are used, is a strong point.
4. **Well-Written and Clear Presentation:** The paper is generally well-written and easy to follow. The architecture diagrams are helpful, and the explanations of the different components are clear.
5. **Availability of Code/Data:** The authors state that the cleaned datasets will be made available, which promotes reproducibility and further research.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Novelty in Core Components:** The individual components of the NBT (CNNs, DNNs, word vectors, etc.) are not novel in themselves. The primary contribution lies in their *combination* and application to the DST problem. While effective, the architecture doesn’t push the boundaries of deep learning techniques.
2. **Context Modeling Simplification:** The context modeling component, limited to the immediately preceding system act, seems potentially restrictive.  More sophisticated methods for incorporating dialogue history (e.g., using recurrent networks to encode the entire dialogue history) could potentially improve performance.
3. **Lack of Comparison to More Recent DST Models:** While the comparison to delexicalization-based baselines is valuable, the paper would benefit from a comparison to more recent and advanced DST models, such as those employing transformers or more complex memory networks.  This would better position the NBT within the current landscape of DST research.
4. **Limited Analysis of Failure Cases:** The paper lacks a detailed analysis of cases where the NBT models fail. Understanding the types of errors the models make would provide valuable insights for future improvements.




**Questions to Authors:**

1. **Contextual Information:**  You mention limiting context to the previous system act for simplicity. Have you experimented with incorporating a longer dialogue history (e.g., using a recurrent network to encode the entire dialogue)? If so, what were the results?
2. **Transformer-based Models:**  Have you considered incorporating transformer-based architectures (e.g., BERT) into the NBT framework? Do you anticipate that using a pre-trained transformer would further improve performance, and if so, what challenges might arise?
3. **Error Analysis:** Could you provide some examples of specific failure cases for the NBT models, and discuss potential strategies to address those failures?  For example, are there particular types of utterances or slot-value pairs where the model consistently struggles?
4. **Scalability:** While you address scalability compared to delexicalization, what are the computational costs (training time, inference time) of the NBT models, especially as the ontology size increases?




**Additional Comments:**

*   The choice of the Adam optimizer and learning rate were not explicitly detailed. Providing these details would improve reproducibility.
*   The description of the belief state update mechanism is concise. A slightly more detailed explanation of the weighting parameter (λ) and its effect on performance would be helpful.



**Overall Assessment:**

This is a solid and well-executed paper that addresses an important problem in dialogue state tracking. The NBT models offer a promising alternative to traditional delexicalization-based approaches, particularly in resource-constrained scenarios. While the individual components of the NBT are not groundbreaking, their effective combination and the demonstrated empirical results make this a valuable contribution to the field. I recommend acceptance, with minor revisions addressing the weaknesses outlined above.
