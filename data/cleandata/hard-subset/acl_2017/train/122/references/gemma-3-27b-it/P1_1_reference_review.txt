## Peer Review for "Neural Belief Tracker: Data-Driven Dialogue State Tracking"

**Summary:**

This paper presents the Neural Belief Tracker (NBT) family of models for dialogue state tracking (DST), aiming to overcome the limitations of existing approaches that rely on either separate SLU modules or hand-crafted delexicalization resources. The NBT models jointly learn SLU and DST by leveraging pre-trained word vectors to handle lexical variation without explicit knowledge engineering. The authors demonstrate that NBT models achieve competitive performance with delexicalization-based methods and significantly outperform them when semantic lexicons are unavailable.

**Main Contributions (as I see them):**

1. **Demonstration of effective pre-trained word vector usage in DST:** The paper successfully demonstrates that utilizing pre-trained word vectors for semantic understanding significantly improves DST performance, especially when hand-crafted lexicons aren't available. This is a valuable finding, offering a path toward more scalable DST systems.
2. **A viable joint SLU/DST model without delexicalization:** The proposed NBT models offer a strong alternative to traditional joint models reliant on delexicalization, reducing the need for manual lexicon creation and its associated maintenance costs.
3. **Comprehensive experimental evaluation:** The thorough evaluation on two datasets (DSTC2 and WOZ 2.0) with comparisons to strong baselines provides convincing evidence for the effectiveness of the proposed approach.

**Strengths:**

1. **Strong Empirical Results:** The NBT models consistently outperform baseline delexicalization-based models on both datasets, demonstrating the effectiveness of the proposed approach. The results on WOZ 2.0, where lexical variation is more pronounced, are particularly compelling.
2. **Addresses a Key Limitation:** The paper directly tackles the scalability issue of delexicalization, a major bottleneck in deploying DST systems to new domains. The ability to operate effectively without hand-crafted lexicons is a significant advantage.
3. **Clear and Well-Structured Presentation:** The paper is well-written and logically organized. The architecture of the NBT model is clearly explained with the help of diagrams. The descriptions of the datasets and experimental setup are also comprehensive.
4. **Insightful Analysis of Word Vector Importance:** The ablation study demonstrating the impact of different word vector types (random, GloVe, Paragram-SL999) on performance is insightful and reinforces the importance of semantic richness in the word embeddings.
5. **Robustness to ASR Errors (Implicitly):** While not directly focused on ASR robustness, training on ASR outputs in the DSTC2 dataset suggests the model can handle some level of noise.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Novelty in Core Architecture:** The core architecture – using CNNs or DNNs to process word vectors – isn’t fundamentally novel. The primary contribution lies in the application of these techniques specifically to the DST problem and the demonstration of their effectiveness with pre-trained embeddings.  This should be acknowledged more explicitly.
2. **Context Modeling Could be Enhanced:** The context modeling mechanism (using dot product similarity) feels simplistic. Exploring more sophisticated context representation techniques (e.g., attention mechanisms, recurrent models) might further improve performance. The limited scope of considering only the *last* system utterance also seems restrictive.
3. **Lack of Error Analysis:** The paper would benefit from a more detailed error analysis. Identifying common types of errors made by the NBT models (e.g., misinterpreting specific slot values, struggling with complex user utterances) would provide valuable insights for future improvements.
4. **Parameter Sensitivity:** The paper lacks discussion on the sensitivity of the model to hyperparameter settings. How do different learning rates, embedding dimensions, or network architectures impact performance?  Reporting some sensitivity analysis would increase confidence in the results.



**Questions to Authors:**

1.  **Novelty:**  Given that the core components (CNNs/DNNs and word vectors) are established techniques, could you elaborate on the specific innovations within the NBT architecture that distinguish it from existing approaches and contribute to its improved performance?
2.  **Context Modeling:** You mention the limitation of considering only the last system utterance. Have you experimented with incorporating a broader dialogue history into the context modeling component, and if so, what were the results?
3.  **Error Analysis:** Could you provide some examples of the types of errors the NBT models make in practice, and discuss potential strategies for addressing these errors?
4. **Scalability beyond Restaurant Domain:** While the models avoid manual lexicon creation, how well do you anticipate these models will scale to domains with substantially larger and more complex ontologies? Are there potential bottlenecks related to the computational cost of iterating over all slot-value pairs?




**Additional Comments:**

*   The authors should provide the code and trained models to facilitate reproducibility.
*   Consider mentioning related work on contrastive learning or metric learning for word embeddings as these could be relevant to the success of the Paragram-SL999 vectors.
*   The formatting of the tables could be improved for readability.

**Overall Assessment:**

This is a solid and well-executed paper that addresses a practical challenge in dialogue state tracking. The results are convincing, and the proposed approach offers a promising alternative to traditional methods. While the core architecture isn’t groundbreaking, the effective application of pre-trained word vectors and the elimination of the need for hand-crafted lexicons are significant contributions.  I recommend acceptance with minor revisions addressing the weaknesses outlined above.
