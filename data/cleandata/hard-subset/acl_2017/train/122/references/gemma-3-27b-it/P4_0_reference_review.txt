This paper introduces the Neural Belief Tracker (NBT) family of models for dialogue state tracking (DST), aiming to overcome the limitations of traditional methods relying on hand-crafted semantic lexicons. The core contribution is a novel approach that couples spoken language understanding (SLU) and DST, leveraging pre-trained word vectors to handle lexical variation without explicit delexicalization. The authors present two variants, NBT-DNN and NBT-CNN, and demonstrate their effectiveness on two datasets, DSTC2 and WOZ 2.0. A secondary contribution is a thorough empirical evaluation, demonstrating that NBT models achieve competitive performance with delexicalization-based methods, and significantly outperform them when lexical resources are unavailable. A further, though less central, contribution lies in highlighting the importance of semantically specialized word vectors for this task.

**Strengths:**

1. **Addressing a Key Limitation:** The paper directly tackles a significant bottleneck in DST – the need for extensive, manually curated semantic lexicons. By successfully employing pre-trained word vectors, the NBT models offer a more scalable and practical solution, particularly for complex domains where lexicon creation is infeasible. This is a strong and relevant contribution.
2. **Strong Empirical Results:** The NBT models consistently achieve competitive or superior performance to existing approaches on both datasets.  The results on WOZ 2.0, where the lack of lexical resources is more pronounced, are particularly compelling. The statistical significance tests further strengthen these findings.
3. **Comprehensive Evaluation:** The paper provides a detailed experimental setup, including comparisons against strong baselines (Henderson et al. and Wen et al.) and ablation studies (varying word vector types). This thoroughness builds confidence in the reported results and facilitates reproducibility. The analysis of the impact of different word vector types (random, GloVe, Paragram-SL999) is insightful and reinforces the importance of semantic specialization.
4. **Clear and Well-Structured Presentation:** The paper is well-written and logically organized. The architecture of the NBT models is clearly explained with accompanying figures.  The background section provides sufficient context for readers unfamiliar with DST.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Novelty of Core Architecture:** While the application of pre-trained word vectors to DST is a valuable contribution, the underlying neural network architecture (DNN/CNN with element-wise multiplication) is relatively standard.  The novelty primarily resides in *how* these components are combined and applied to the DST task, rather than a fundamentally new architectural paradigm.
2. **Context Modeling Simplification:** The context modeling component, which focuses only on the immediately preceding system utterance, appears somewhat simplistic.  More sophisticated methods for capturing long-range dependencies within the dialogue history could potentially further improve performance. The justification for this simplification is not fully explored.
3. **Belief State Update Rule:** The rule-based belief state update mechanism, while functional, feels somewhat disconnected from the neural network core of the NBT models.  Integrating the belief state update directly into the neural network framework could lead to more end-to-end learning and potentially better performance.  The hyperparameter λ’s tuning is mentioned, but a justification for its value is missing.
4. **Limited Error Analysis:** The paper lacks a detailed error analysis. Understanding the types of errors the NBT models still make (e.g., misinterpreting specific slot values, failing to capture complex user intentions) would provide valuable insights for future research.

**Questions to Authors:**

1.  The paper mentions that the NBT models are the first to successfully use pre-trained word vectors to improve the language understanding capability of belief tracking models. Could you elaborate on prior attempts or challenges encountered when applying word vectors to this task, and why your approach overcomes these limitations?
2.  The context modeling component only considers the immediately preceding system utterance. Have you experimented with incorporating a longer dialogue history (e.g., using a recurrent neural network to encode the entire conversation)? If so, what were the results?
3.  Could you provide more details regarding the types of errors made by the NBT models, particularly on the WOZ 2.0 dataset? A qualitative analysis of these errors would be beneficial.
4.  How sensitive are the results to the choice of pre-trained word vectors? Are there other semantically specialized vectors that might perform comparably or better than Paragram-SL999?

**Additional Comments:**

*   The notation could be made more consistent throughout the paper (e.g., the use of subscripts and superscripts).
*   It would be helpful to include a table comparing the number of parameters in the NBT models and the baseline models.
*   Consider including discussion of computational cost.
