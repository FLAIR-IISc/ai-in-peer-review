## Review for "Neural Belief Tracker: Data-Driven Dialogue State Tracking"

**Overall Assessment:** This paper presents a solid and well-motivated contribution to the field of dialogue state tracking (DST). The authors address a significant challenge – the reliance on hand-crafted lexical resources in traditional DST systems – with a novel approach that leverages pre-trained word embeddings. The experimental results demonstrate competitive performance against existing methods, and importantly, show a clear advantage when lexical resources are unavailable. The paper is well-written and clearly explains the proposed models. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions.

**Summary & Contributions:**

The paper introduces the Neural Belief Tracker (NBT) family of models, which jointly learn SLU and DST by reasoning directly over pre-trained word vectors instead of relying on delexicalization with manually constructed semantic dictionaries.  

I identify three key contributions:

1. **A novel approach to DST without manual lexical resources:** The core contribution is moving away from delexicalization and utilizing word embeddings to capture semantic similarity, allowing the model to generalize to unseen paraphrases. This is a significant step towards more scalable and robust DST systems.
2. **Effective Joint SLU/DST Architecture:** The NBT architecture is thoughtfully designed, with separate modules for representation learning, context modelling, and semantic decoding, allowing for flexibility and efficient parameter sharing. The exploration of both DNN and CNN based representation learners is also valuable.
3. **Strong Empirical Validation:** The comprehensive evaluation on both DSTC2 and WOZ 2.0 datasets demonstrates the effectiveness of the NBT models, particularly in scenarios where lexical resources are limited. The ablation study highlighting the importance of semantically specialized word vectors is particularly insightful.

**Strengths:**

1. **Addresses a critical limitation of existing DST systems:** The dependence on hand-crafted lexicons is a major bottleneck for scaling DST to new domains. The NBT models directly address this limitation, offering a more practical and adaptable solution.
2. **Clear and well-motivated design:** The architecture of the NBT models is logically structured and clearly explained. The authors provide a strong rationale for each design choice, making the paper easy to follow and understand. The explanation of context modeling is particularly well done.
3. **Comprehensive experimental evaluation:** The authors evaluate their models on two widely used datasets and compare them against strong baselines. The inclusion of both datasets (DSTC2 with ASR noise and WOZ 2.0 without) provides a more complete assessment of the models' strengths and weaknesses.
4. **Insightful ablation study:** The ablation study on word vector types (random, GloVe, Paragram-SL999) convincingly demonstrates the importance of semantic specialization for achieving strong performance. This finding is valuable for future research in this area.
5. **Reproducibility:** The authors explicitly state the intention to release the cleaned datasets used in their experiments, which enhances the reproducibility and impact of their work.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Analysis of Error Cases:** While the quantitative results are strong, the paper lacks a detailed qualitative analysis of the NBT models’ error cases. Understanding *where* the models fail could provide valuable insights for future improvements.  Specifically, it would be useful to see examples where the model struggles with ambiguous utterances or complex contextual dependencies.
2. **Simplicity of Belief State Update:** The rule-based belief state update mechanism (lambda=0.55) feels somewhat simplistic given the sophistication of the core NBT model. While it’s functional, exploring more advanced belief update strategies (e.g., Bayesian filtering) could potentially further improve performance.
3. **Lack of Comparison to State-of-the-Art Neural DST Models:** The baseline models are well-chosen, but a comparison against more recent, sophisticated neural DST models (e.g., those utilizing transformers or more complex attention mechanisms) would strengthen the paper.  While the authors focus on overcoming the lexical resource bottleneck, it’s important to position the NBT models relative to the current state-of-the-art in neural DST.
4. **Limited Discussion of Computational Cost:** The paper does not discuss the computational cost of the NBT models, particularly in comparison to delexicalization-based approaches.  Understanding the trade-offs between performance and computational efficiency is important for real-world deployment.



**Questions to Authors:**

1.  Could you provide some example error cases where the NBT models fail, and discuss the potential reasons for these failures?
2.  Have you explored alternative belief state update mechanisms beyond the rule-based approach presented in the paper? If so, what were the results?
3.  How does the computational cost of the NBT models compare to that of delexicalization-based approaches, both in terms of training and inference time?
4.  Regarding the context modelling – did you explore other ways of encoding the system dialogue acts beyond just dot product similarity?

**Additional Comments:**

*   Figure 2 could benefit from more detailed labeling of the different components, especially within the “Semantic Decoding” and "Context Modeling" modules.
*   The notation is generally clear, but defining 'ku' earlier would be helpful.



In conclusion, this is a strong paper that presents a valuable contribution to the field of dialogue state tracking. The authors successfully address a critical limitation of existing approaches and demonstrate the effectiveness of their proposed models through comprehensive experiments. Addressing the weaknesses outlined above would further strengthen the paper and enhance its impact.
