This paper presents the Neural Belief Tracker (NBT), a model for dialogue state tracking (DST) that aims to bypass the need for manually crafted semantic lexicons by leveraging pre-trained word vectors. The authors propose two variants, NBT-DNN and NBT-CNN, both of which jointly model spoken language understanding and DST. The primary claimed contribution is achieving performance comparable to delexicalization-based models *without* relying on hand-crafted resources, and outperforming them when those resources are unavailable.

**Contributions (as I see them):**

1.  **A conceptually sound approach:** The idea of moving away from explicit lexical matching and toward a representation learning-based approach for DST is reasonable, given the scalability issues with delexicalization.
2.  **Empirical demonstration of viability:** The results show that the NBT models can indeed achieve competitive performance with established methods, particularly in low-resource scenarios.
3. **Thorough experimentation**: The paper presents results across two datasets and a variety of configurations, demonstrating a reasonable level of analysis.

**Strengths:**

1.  **Clear Problem Statement and Motivation:** The paper clearly articulates the limitations of existing DST approaches that rely on manual lexicons, establishing a strong motivation for the proposed work.
2.  **Competitive Performance:**  The NBT models achieve performance on par with strong baselines, even those utilizing manually created lexicons. This demonstrates the viability of the approach and its potential for practical application. The fact that the performance is maintained without lexicons is a positive finding.
3.  **Detailed Model Description:** The architecture of the NBT models is described thoroughly, with clear explanations of each component and their interactions. The inclusion of diagrams is helpful.
4.  **Ablation Study on Word Vectors:** The experiment highlighting the importance of semantically-specialized word vectors (Paragram-SL999) is insightful and demonstrates the value of incorporating prior knowledge into the model. The results convincingly show the benefits of using these vectors over simpler alternatives.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Novelty:** While the idea is reasonable, the core components (DNN/CNN, word embeddings, joint modeling) are not novel. The contribution lies more in the *application* of these techniques to the DST problem in a specific way. The authors acknowledge this, but the paper still reads as an incremental improvement rather than a breakthrough.
2. **Lack of Comparison to State-of-the-Art:** The baselines used (Henderson et al., Wen et al.) are somewhat dated. A comparison to more recent, transformer-based DST models would be crucial to establish the true competitiveness of the NBT. It is possible that more recent methods already address the lexicon issue and achieve superior performance. The authors mention that the model can handle semantic variation, but a more comprehensive comparison is needed to validate this claim.
3.  **Overstated Claims:** The claim that the NBT "efficiently learns to handle variation without requiring any hand-crafted resources" is too strong. While it *avoids* manual lexicons, it still relies on *pre-trained* word embeddings, which are themselves the result of significant human effort and resource investment. This is a semantic point, but important for accurately representing the work’s contribution.
4. **Belief State Update Mechanism is Simplistic:** The described belief state update mechanism is rather basic and could be a bottleneck in overall system performance. A more sophisticated update strategy might be necessary to fully leverage the strengths of the NBT.



**Questions to Authors:**

1.  How do the NBT models compare to more recent, transformer-based DST models (e.g., those using BERT or similar architectures)? Can you provide a comparison on the DSTC2 and WOZ 2.0 datasets?
2.  The paper states that the pre-trained word vectors are "fixed" during training. Have you experimented with fine-tuning these vectors, even with a low learning rate? Could this further improve performance?
3.  The context modeling component appears relatively simple. Have you considered more sophisticated methods for incorporating dialogue context, such as hierarchical recurrent networks or attention mechanisms?
4. Can you elaborate on the sensitivity of the model to the size of the pre-trained word vectors? Would using larger word vector dimensions significantly improve performance, or would it introduce diminishing returns?

**Additional Comments:**

*   The discussion of the semantic dictionary in Figure 1 feels somewhat tangential to the main contribution of the paper.
*   The notation in the equations could be made more consistent. For example, the use of subscripts and superscripts is sometimes unclear.
*   Consider including error analysis to identify specific types of errors that the NBT models still struggle with.
