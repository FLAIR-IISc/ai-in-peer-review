## Review for "Neural Belief Tracker: Data-Driven Dialogue State Tracking"

**Summary:** This paper presents the Neural Belief Tracker (NBT) family of models for dialogue state tracking (DST). The core idea is to jointly learn SLU and DST without relying on hand-crafted delexicalisation resources (like semantic dictionaries) by leveraging pre-trained word vectors. The authors propose two variants, NBT-DNN and NBT-CNN, and demonstrate competitive performance against strong baselines on two restaurant search datasets (DSTC2 and WOZ 2.0), particularly when delexicalisation resources are unavailable.

**Main Contributions (as I see them):**

1. **Demonstration of effective end-to-end DST with pre-trained word vectors:** The paper successfully shows that pre-trained word vectors can be effectively used to improve the language understanding capabilities of DST models, offering a viable alternative to manual delexicalisation. This is the primary contribution.
2. **Competitive performance without hand-crafted resources:** The NBT models achieve performance on par with delexicalisation-based methods *without* requiring the significant effort of creating and maintaining semantic dictionaries, making them more scalable.
3. **Empirical comparison of different word vector types:** The ablation study examining the impact of different pre-trained word vectors highlights the importance of semantic specialisation in this task.


**Strengths:**

1. **Addresses a significant practical challenge:** The reliance on manual delexicalisation is a major bottleneck in scaling DST models to new domains.  The NBT models offer a compelling solution to this problem, making DST more accessible and deployable.
2. **Strong empirical results:** The models consistently outperform baseline methods, especially on the WOZ 2.0 dataset where lexical variation is more prominent. The results provide convincing evidence of the efficacy of the proposed approach.
3. **Clear and well-structured presentation:** The paper is generally well-written and easy to follow. The architecture diagrams (Figures 2, 3 & 4) are particularly helpful in understanding the model components. The motivation is clearly articulated, and the experimental setup is well-defined.
4. **Thorough experimentation:**  The authors evaluate their models on two different datasets with varying characteristics (ASR noise vs. clean text), providing a comprehensive assessment of their performance. The ablation study regarding word vector types is also a strong point.
5. **Emphasis on semantic specialization:**  The investigation into the impact of different word vector types is important. It convincingly demonstrates that vectors pre-trained with semantic constraints (like Paragram-SL999) yield better results than standard distributional embeddings.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited novelty in individual components:** While the *combination* of techniques is valuable, the individual components (DNNs, CNNs, pre-trained word vectors) are not novel. The core innovation lies in applying these components effectively to the DST task without delexicalisation.  The authors acknowledge this, but the paper would benefit from a more nuanced discussion of the novelty in the *way* these components are integrated.
2. **Context modelling feels simplistic:** The context modelling (using dot product similarity with system acts) appears somewhat rudimentary. The paper admits it's a Markovian simplification, but it's unclear how much performance is lost due to this simplification. Exploring more sophisticated context representations (e.g., RNNs over dialogue history) could be beneficial.
3. **Lack of error analysis:** The paper lacks a detailed error analysis. Understanding *where* the models struggle would provide valuable insights for future improvements. What types of slot-value pairs are most often misidentified? What kinds of user utterances cause the most errors? This information could inform the design of more robust models.
4. **Limited comparison to recent state-of-the-art DST models:** While the baselines are reasonable, the paper doesn’t compare against some of the more recent and powerful DST approaches, such as those based on transformers (e.g., TRADE, SOM-DST).  A comparison to these models would provide a better understanding of the current state-of-the-art and the relative strengths of the NBT models.




**Questions to Authors:**

1.  Could you elaborate on the computational cost of NBT-CNN compared to NBT-DNN? Does the increased performance justify the additional computational burden?
2.  The context modelling seems simplified. Have you experimented with more sophisticated methods for incorporating dialogue history, such as recurrent neural networks? If so, what were the results?
3.  Could you provide an error analysis, highlighting the most common types of errors made by the NBT models?  What kinds of utterances or slot-value pairs are particularly challenging?
4.  Have you considered incorporating attention mechanisms into the NBT models, either within the representation learning stage or the semantic decoding stage?
5. The paper mentions scaling to "sophisticated application domains". Can you give examples of domains where the lack of delexicalisation resources would be particularly problematic, and how the NBT would perform compared to existing methods in those scenarios?



**Additional Comments:**

*   The discussion of the belief state update mechanism (Section 4) feels a bit tacked on. It would be helpful to have a more detailed analysis of the impact of the λ parameter on performance.
*   The phrasing "semantic specialisation" is used repeatedly. Perhaps a slightly more descriptive term would be beneficial.
*  It would be helpful to include the size of the NBT models (number of parameters) in the experimental results.




**Overall:** This is a solid and well-executed paper that addresses an important practical challenge in dialogue state tracking. The NBT models offer a promising alternative to delexicalisation-based methods, and the empirical results are convincing. While there are some weaknesses, they are relatively minor. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions, contingent on the authors addressing the questions and concerns raised above.
