This paper presents the Neural Belief Tracker (NBT) family of models for dialogue state tracking (DST), aiming to address the limitations of previous approaches relying on hand-crafted lexicons. The core idea is to couple SLU and DST and leverage pre-trained word vectors to handle lexical variation without manual resource engineering. The authors claim their models achieve performance comparable to delexicalization-based approaches, while significantly outperforming them when such resources are unavailable.

The primary contribution of this work is the demonstration of effective dialogue state tracking using pre-trained word vectors, removing the need for manually constructed semantic dictionaries. While the coupling of SLU and DST is not novel, applying it *with* pre-trained vectors and showing competitive performance is a substantial step.  A second, contributing aspect is the architectural exploration with NBT-DNN and NBT-CNN, offering options for representing user utterances and contexts. Finally, the thorough experimental evaluation on two datasets, including a comparison with strong baselines, provides convincing evidence for the proposed approach's effectiveness.

**Strengths:**

1. **Novelty in Vector Space Utilization:** The most significant strength is the successful application of pre-trained word vectors to DST. Previous work largely depended on delexicalization or separate SLU modules. Showing comparable or superior performance to lexicon-based methods without manual lexicon creation is a noteworthy advancement. This addresses a critical scalability issue for DST in complex domains.
2. **Strong Empirical Results:** The authors provide compelling experimental results on both DSTC2 and WOZ 2.0 datasets. The NBT models consistently outperform the baselines, demonstrating the effectiveness of the proposed approach. The performance gains on WOZ 2.0, where semantic variation is more pronounced, further support the benefits of using pre-trained word vectors.
3. **Comprehensive Evaluation:** The comparison to both delexicalization-based models *with* and *without* semantic dictionaries offers valuable insights. Showing that NBT matches performance when lexicons are available, but excels when they aren’t, clearly highlights the advantage of the proposed method. The ablation study with different word vector collections is also well-executed.
4. **Clear Architectural Description:** The architecture of the NBT models is clearly explained and well-illustrated in Figure 2. The modular design, with distinct representation learning, context modelling, and semantic decoding components, facilitates understanding and potential extension.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Discussion of Error Analysis:** While the quantitative results are strong, the paper lacks a detailed error analysis. Understanding *where* the NBT models still struggle would be valuable for future research. Are there specific types of utterances or slot-value pairs that consistently pose challenges?
2. **Context Modeling Simplification:** The context modeling approach, focusing only on the last system turn, seems somewhat simplistic.  More sophisticated methods that consider the entire dialogue history or employ attention mechanisms could potentially improve performance further. The authors acknowledge this might be a limitation, but a more detailed discussion of potential alternatives would be beneficial.
3. **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** The paper doesn’t delve into the sensitivity of the models to hyperparameter settings. Providing insights into the impact of different learning rates, embedding dimensions, or network architectures would enhance reproducibility and inform future work.
4. **Limited Scalability Discussion:** Although the motivation centers on scalability, the paper doesn’t provide concrete details on computational cost or memory requirements as the ontology size grows. A brief analysis of these aspects would strengthen the argument for the method's practical applicability.

**Questions to Authors:**

1. Could you provide a more detailed error analysis, identifying common types of errors made by the NBT models? Specifically, are there any slot-value pairs or utterance patterns that consistently lead to incorrect predictions?
2. The context modeling component currently only considers the last system turn. Have you experimented with incorporating a longer dialogue history or employing attention mechanisms to weigh different turns based on their relevance? If so, what were the results?
3. What is the computational cost (training time, inference time, memory usage) of the NBT models, especially as the size of the ontology increases?
4.  How would the NBT model handle out-of-vocabulary words that aren't present in the pre-trained word vector space? Is there a fallback mechanism or a strategy for learning representations for novel words?

**Additional Comments:**

* The paper is well-written and clearly organized.
* The references are comprehensive and relevant.
* The authors should consider making the cleaned DSTC2 dataset publicly available, as mentioned in the paper.
