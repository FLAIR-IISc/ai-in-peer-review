This paper proposes the Neural Belief Tracker (NBT), a dialogue state tracking (DST) model that aims to overcome the limitations of traditional methods relying on hand-crafted lexicons by leveraging pre-trained word vectors to jointly model spoken language understanding and DST. The authors present two variants, NBT-DNN and NBT-CNN, and demonstrate their performance on the DSTC2 and WOZ 2.0 datasets.

The primary contribution lies in the attempt to remove the reliance on delexicalisation, a significant bottleneck in scaling DST to more complex domains. While the idea is conceptually sound, the execution and presentation reveal several shortcomings. A secondary contribution is the empirical demonstration that pre-trained word vectors improve performance, though the magnitude of improvement is not particularly striking and contingent on the dataset.

**Strengths:**

1. **Addressing a Real Problem:** The paper tackles a genuine limitation of current DST systems – the difficulty of scaling delexicalisation to large, complex domains. Reducing the need for manual lexicon construction is valuable.
2. **Clear Motivation & Background:** The introduction effectively motivates the problem and provides a concise overview of existing DST approaches, categorizing them helpfully. The inclusion of Figure 1 illustrating delexicalisation is beneficial.
3. **Solid Experimental Setup:** The use of two commonly used datasets (DSTC2 and WOZ 2.0) and comparison against established baselines provides a reasonable evaluation framework. The ablation study (Table 2) regarding word vectors is insightful.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Incremental Improvement & Lack of Robustness:** While the NBT models achieve comparable or slightly better performance than the baselines, the improvements are not substantial. The claim of “significantly outperform[ing]” lexicon-based models when resources are unavailable is misleading; the differences are often marginal and statistically significant only on one metric in one dataset (see Table 1). Moreover, the performance gains are heavily reliant on the specific choice of pre-trained word vectors, and the ablation study reveals a concerning dependence. This suggests the model isn’t particularly robust.
2. **Architectural Details & Justification:** The architecture of both NBT variants (particularly NBT-DNN) feels rather ad-hoc. The motivation for summing cumulative n-gram representations (Equation 4) is weak, and the claim that it's a "simpler" model doesn’t justify the architectural choice. The design decisions lack detailed justification beyond “we tried it and it worked”. Similarly, the explanation for using element-wise multiplication (Equation 8) over the more intuitive dot product is insufficient.
3. **Presentation & Clarity:** The paper suffers from numerous minor presentation issues. Equations are often introduced without sufficient context (e.g., Equation 7 – what is 'W sc'? It's defined only in the text, not alongside the equation). The figures (2, 3, and 4) are overloaded with details and difficult to interpret quickly. The writing style is occasionally verbose and repetitive. Furthermore, the description of the belief state update mechanism (Section 4) is convoluted and requires careful reading.
4. **Insufficient Analysis & Error Analysis:** The paper lacks a thorough analysis of the model’s failures. Where does NBT struggle? Are there specific slot types or user utterances that consistently cause errors? A qualitative error analysis would significantly strengthen the paper.
5. **Overstated Claims:** The phrasing in the conclusion is overly enthusiastic. Claiming the NBT proposes a “framework better-suited to scaling” feels premature given the modest performance gains and the strong dependence on the quality of pre-trained vectors.

**Questions to Authors:**

1.  Can you provide a detailed error analysis illustrating the types of errors NBT makes compared to the baseline models?
2.  What is the sensitivity of your models to different pre-trained word vector initializations beyond Paragram-SL999, GloVe, and random initialization?
3.  The choice of element-wise multiplication in Equation 8 seems unusual. Could you elaborate on why you believe it outperforms a simple dot product?
4.  The belief state update mechanism in Section 4 appears complex. Is there a simpler alternative that achieves similar results?



**Additional Comments:**

*   The citation formatting is inconsistent (e.g., some citations include a year, others don't).
*   The use of abbreviations (e.g., ASR, SLU, DST) is generally acceptable, but ensure they are defined upon first use.
*   Please provide a link to the cleaned DSTC2 dataset and the WOZ 2.0 dataset upon publication.



**Overall Assessment:**

While the paper addresses an important problem in DST, the proposed solution provides only incremental improvements and suffers from several weaknesses in terms of architectural justification, presentation, and analysis. The claims of substantial improvement are overstated. Therefore, I recommend **rejection** at this time. A major revision addressing the weaknesses outlined above, including a more thorough analysis and justification of design choices, might be considered for future submission.
