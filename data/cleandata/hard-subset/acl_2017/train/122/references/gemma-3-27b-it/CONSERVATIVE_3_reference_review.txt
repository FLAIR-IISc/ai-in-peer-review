This paper proposes the Neural Belief Tracker (NBT), a family of models designed to jointly perform spoken language understanding (SLU) and dialogue state tracking (DST) without relying on hand-crafted semantic lexicons. The authors claim novelty in leveraging pre-trained word vectors for improved language understanding within belief tracking, achieving performance comparable to delexicalization-based methods while outperforming them when lexical resources are unavailable. 

The primary contribution is a practical approach to DST that sidesteps the need for extensive manual lexicon creation, a significant bottleneck for scaling to new domains. A secondary contribution is the demonstration that pre-trained word vectors, when appropriately integrated, can effectively capture semantic variation in user utterances. Finally, the extensive experimentation across two datasets provides a reasonable validation of the proposed method.

**Strengths:**

1. **Practicality and Scalability:** The elimination of hand-crafted lexicons is a genuine strength. Delexicalization, while effective, is a laborious and expensive process, limiting the deployment of DST systems to resource-rich domains. The NBT models address this limitation directly, offering a more scalable solution.
2. **Competitive Performance:** The NBT models achieve performance comparable to strong delexicalization baselines on DSTC2 and outperform them on the WOZ 2.0 dataset, particularly when lexical resources are not provided. This suggests that the approach is robust and adaptable.
3. **Sound Methodology:** The authors present a clear and well-defined model architecture (although complex), and the experimental setup is reasonably thorough. The use of established pre-trained word vectors (Paragram-SL999) and standard evaluation metrics (joint goal accuracy, request accuracy) enhances the credibility of the findings.
4. **Demonstrated Benefit of Semantic Vectors:** The ablation study demonstrating the importance of semantically-specialized word vectors is valuable. It reinforces the idea that capturing semantic relationships, rather than just distributional similarity, is crucial for robust DST.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Incremental Novelty:** While the idea of leveraging word vectors for DST is conceptually appealing, the core approach of combining SLU and DST is not novel (Henderson et al., 2014d). The primary novelty lies in *how* this is achieved – by eschewing lexicons and relying on word vectors. However, this is more of an engineering contribution than a fundamental algorithmic breakthrough.  The paper doesn’t convincingly demonstrate why this approach is fundamentally superior to simply training a larger, more expressive delexicalization model.
2. **Limited Error Analysis:** The paper lacks a detailed error analysis. Understanding *where* the NBT models struggle and *why* would be invaluable for guiding future research. For instance, is performance degraded on utterances with complex syntactic structures, or on utterances containing ambiguous references?
3. **Context Modelling Simplification:** The context modelling approach, which focuses solely on the immediately preceding system act, appears overly simplistic. Real-world dialogue involves longer-range dependencies and a more complex interplay of contextual information. The authors acknowledge this limitation but do not offer a clear path towards addressing it.
4. **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** The paper does not provide sufficient detail about hyperparameter tuning. The optimal values for learning rates, embedding dimensions, and other crucial parameters are not discussed, hindering reproducibility.

**Questions to Authors:**

1.  Could you provide a more detailed error analysis, identifying common failure cases and potential areas for improvement?
2.  What is the computational cost (training time, inference time, model size) of the NBT models compared to the delexicalization baselines?
3.  How sensitive are the NBT models to the choice of pre-trained word vectors? Would different embeddings (e.g., fastText) yield comparable results?
4.  What strategies have you considered for improving the context modelling component to capture longer-range dependencies?



**Additional Comments:**

The formatting of some equations is inconsistent (e.g., missing spaces around operators). The discussion of related work is comprehensive but could benefit from a more critical assessment of the limitations of existing approaches. The claim that this work proposes a "framework better-suited to scaling belief tracking models" feels overly strong given the incremental nature of the contribution.
