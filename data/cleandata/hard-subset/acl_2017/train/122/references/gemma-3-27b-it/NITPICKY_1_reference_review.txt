This paper introduces the Neural Belief Tracker (NBT) family of models for dialogue state tracking (DST), aiming to overcome the limitations of traditional approaches reliant on hand-crafted semantic lexicons. The core contribution is a coupled SLU/DST model that leverages pre-trained word vectors to handle lexical variation without explicit delexicalization. The authors present two variants, NBT-DNN and NBT-CNN, and demonstrate their performance on the DSTC2 and WOZ 2.0 datasets.

**Strengths:**

1. **Addresses a Practical Problem:** The reliance on manually crafted semantic dictionaries is a significant bottleneck in scaling DST systems to more complex domains. The NBT’s ability to achieve comparable performance *without* these dictionaries is a substantial benefit, making it more practical for real-world applications.
2. **Sound Methodology:** The approach of utilizing pre-trained word vectors to capture semantic similarity is well-motivated and aligns with current trends in NLP. The architecture, while not radically novel, appears reasonably well-designed, and the comparison between DNN and CNN variants provides valuable insights.
3. **Empirical Validation:** The evaluation on two datasets, including the challenging WOZ 2.0, provides a solid empirical basis for the claims. The results consistently show the NBT models matching or outperforming baseline delexicalization-based models. The ablation study (Table 2) regarding the quality of word vectors is particularly insightful.
4. **Clear Presentation:** The paper is generally well-written and clearly explains the motivation, methodology, and results. The diagrams illustrating the model architecture (Figures 2, 3, and 4) are helpful.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Incremental Improvement, Not Breakthrough:** While the NBT models achieve competitive performance, the gains over strong baselines are relatively modest. The paper doesn’t convincingly demonstrate a paradigm shift in DST capability. The claim of "significantly outperform[ing]" delexicalization models "when such resources are not available" is softened by the fact that performance *with* resources is comparable. This feels less like a breakthrough and more like a well-engineered optimization.
2. **Insufficient Detail on Hyperparameter Tuning:** The paper lacks detailed information about the hyperparameter tuning process. Specific learning rates, batch sizes, and regularization techniques are not mentioned, hindering reproducibility. The choice of λ=0.55 is stated but without explanation of how it was determined.
3. **Limited Error Analysis:** The paper lacks a thorough error analysis. Understanding *where* the NBT models fail would provide valuable insights for future research. Are there specific slot-value pairs that consistently cause problems? What types of utterances are particularly challenging? Identifying these failure cases would strengthen the paper’s contribution.
4. **Context Modeling Simplification:** The context modeling component, while acknowledged as important, is significantly simplified by only considering the last system turn. The authors admit this is a Markovian assumption, but do not explore the impact of incorporating a longer dialogue history.
5. **Formatting Inconsistencies and Typos:** Several minor formatting inconsistencies are present, e.g. inconsistent spacing after periods in figure captions, and slight variations in font size. Additionally, I observed minor typos ("statis-tically" in Table 1). While these are minor, they detract from the overall polish of the submission.

**Questions to Authors:**

1. Could you provide more details on the hyperparameter tuning process, including the ranges of values explored and the criteria used for selection?
2. What types of errors does the NBT model make most frequently? Could you provide examples of utterances where the model fails and explain why?
3. How sensitive is the performance of the NBT models to the choice of pre-trained word vectors? Have you experimented with other vector spaces beyond Paragram-SL999?
4. Did you consider incorporating a longer dialogue history into the context modeling component? If so, what were the results? If not, why not?
5. Regarding the claim of "efficiently learning to handle variation," can you provide quantitative evidence demonstrating the NBT models' ability to generalize to unseen lexical variations compared to the delexicalization baseline?



**Additional Comments:**

*   Please carefully proofread the manuscript for typos and formatting inconsistencies.
*   Consider adding a discussion section that outlines the limitations of the current work and potential directions for future research.
*   While the related work section is reasonably comprehensive, it could benefit from a more nuanced discussion of recent advancements in neural DST models.
* The choice of datasets is reasonable, but a larger and more diverse dataset could further strengthen the evaluation.



Despite its strengths, the incremental nature of the improvements, coupled with the lack of detailed hyperparameter tuning information and error analysis, leads me to recommend **rejection**. While the paper presents a solid contribution, it falls short of the novelty and impact expected for acceptance at ACL. Addressing the weaknesses outlined above could significantly improve the paper's quality and increase its chances of acceptance in a future submission.
