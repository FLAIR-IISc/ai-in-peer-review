This paper introduces the Neural Belief Tracker (NBT), a model designed to jointly learn spoken language understanding and dialogue state tracking without relying on hand-crafted semantic lexicons. The core idea is to leverage pre-trained word vectors to reason about semantic similarity and handle lexical variation. The authors present two variants – NBT-DNN and NBT-CNN – and demonstrate competitive performance on two standard datasets (DSTC2 and WOZ 2.0), matching or exceeding the performance of delexicalization-based models, particularly when semantic dictionaries are unavailable.

The primary contribution of this work is the **novel approach to belief tracking that eschews explicit delexicalization in favor of learning semantic relationships directly from pre-trained word embeddings.** This is a significant step towards scaling dialogue state tracking to more complex domains where manual lexicon creation is infeasible. The second contribution is a **well-engineered model (NBT) exploring different representation learning methods (DNN and CNN) for effectively utilizing these embeddings within the belief tracking framework.** Finally, the **empirical validation on two established datasets, demonstrating competitive performance and highlighting the advantage of NBT when lexical resources are limited**, is a valuable addition to the field.

**Strengths:**

1. **Bold and Novel Idea:** The move away from delexicalization is a genuinely innovative approach.  Delexicalization, while effective, is a brittle and labor-intensive process. This paper directly addresses this limitation with a potentially more robust and scalable solution. This is the strongest aspect of the submission.
2. **Strong Empirical Results:** The NBT models achieve results comparable to, and in some cases exceeding, state-of-the-art delexicalization-based models.  The fact that the NBT performs *better* without lexicons is compelling evidence of its effectiveness.
3. **Clear and Well-Structured Presentation:** The paper is well-written, clearly explaining the motivations, the model architecture, and the experimental setup. The figures are helpful in visualizing the model.
4. **Ablation Study on Word Vectors:** The inclusion of results varying the word vector type (random, GloVe, Paragram-SL999) provides valuable insight into the importance of semantic richness in the embeddings, reinforcing the core idea.
5. **Practical Relevance:** The potential for scaling belief tracking to more complex and real-world domains is a significant advantage, making this work highly relevant to the community.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Analysis of Failure Cases:** While the results are positive overall, the paper lacks a detailed analysis of *where* the NBT struggles. Understanding the types of utterances or slot-value pairs that the model misclassifies would be valuable for future improvements. 
2. **Context Modeling Simplicity:** The context modeling component (using only the previous system act) feels somewhat simplistic. More sophisticated methods for incorporating dialogue history might further improve performance. This isn’t a fatal flaw, but an area for exploration.
3. **Evaluation Focus:** The evaluation primarily focuses on goal and request accuracy. Exploring other metrics (e.g., belief state entropy) or conducting a more qualitative evaluation of the tracked belief states could provide a more comprehensive assessment.
4. **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** The paper doesn't discuss hyperparameter tuning or sensitivity. Understanding how the performance changes with different hyperparameters would strengthen the robustness of the results.

**Questions to Authors:**

1. Can you provide examples of specific utterances or slot-value pairs where the NBT model fails, and discuss potential reasons for these failures?
2. Have you experimented with more sophisticated context modeling approaches (e.g., incorporating multiple previous turns, using attention mechanisms)? If so, what were the results?
3. How sensitive is the performance of the NBT models to the choice of pre-trained word vectors?  Specifically, did you try other pre-trained vectors and what were the results?
4.  Could you comment on the computational cost of the NBT models compared to the delexicalization-based baselines?



**Additional Comments:**

The paper is generally well-written and the idea is compelling. The lack of deep error analysis is a minor drawback, but the overall contribution is significant enough to warrant acceptance.  I am particularly impressed by the authors' willingness to move away from a well-established paradigm (delexicalization) and explore a new, potentially more scalable approach.
