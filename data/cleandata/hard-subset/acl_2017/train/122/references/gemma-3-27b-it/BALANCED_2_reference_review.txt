This paper introduces the Neural Belief Tracker (NBT), a novel approach to dialogue state tracking (DST) that aims to overcome the limitations of traditional methods relying on hand-crafted semantic lexicons. The core idea is to couple Spoken Language Understanding (SLU) and DST, learning to handle lexical variation directly from pre-trained word vectors. The authors present two NBT variants, NBT-DNN and NBT-CNN, and demonstrate competitive performance on two restaurant search datasets, DSTC2 and WOZ 2.0.

**Contributions:**

1. **Elimination of Hand-Crafted Lexicons:** The most significant contribution is the demonstration that strong DST performance can be achieved *without* relying on manually curated semantic dictionaries for delexicalization. This is particularly crucial for scaling DST to more complex and diverse domains.
2. **Effective Use of Pre-trained Word Vectors:** The paper successfully leverages pre-trained word vectors to capture semantic similarity and improve language understanding within the DST framework. The ablation study (Table 2) clearly demonstrates the importance of using semantically specialized word vectors like Paragram-SL999.
3. **Joint SLU/DST Modeling:** The NBT models effectively combine SLU and DST, offering a streamlined approach compared to traditional pipelines with separate modules. This integrated approach simplifies the development process and avoids potential error propagation between stages.

**Strengths:**

1. **Strong Empirical Results:** The NBT models achieve competitive performance, matching or exceeding the performance of delexicalization-based baselines on both datasets. This establishes the viability of the proposed approach.
2. **Clear Motivation and Problem Definition:** The paper clearly articulates the limitations of existing DST methods, specifically the reliance on manually crafted resources, and provides a compelling motivation for the proposed approach.
3. **Thorough Ablation Study:** The ablation study focusing on different word vector types (Table 2) provides valuable insights into the contribution of semantic specialization and validates the design choices.
4. **Well-Structured and Readable:** The paper is well-organized and clearly written, making it easy to understand the proposed method and its experimental setup. The figures and diagrams are helpful in visualizing the model architecture.
5. **Comprehensive Evaluation:** Evaluating on both the DSTC2 (ASR data) and WOZ 2.0 (clean text) datasets provides a more comprehensive assessment of the model’s capabilities.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Novelty in Architecture:** While the combination of pre-trained vectors with a joint SLU/DST model is effective, the underlying neural network architecture (DNN and CNN) is relatively standard. The paper doesn't introduce significant architectural innovations beyond this combination.
2. **Context Modeling Simplification:** The context modeling component only considers the *last* system utterance. While acknowledged as a simplification, it might limit the model’s ability to capture longer-range dependencies in the dialogue history.
3. **Lack of Comparison with More Recent Methods:** The comparison is primarily with Henderson et al. (2014) and Wen et al. (2017). Including comparisons with more recent state-of-the-art DST models (e.g., transformer-based approaches) would provide a more robust evaluation.
4. **Belief State Update Mechanism:** The belief state update mechanism described in Section 4 feels somewhat tacked on and simplistic. The use of a fixed threshold (0.5) and a single lambda value (0.55) might not be optimal for all scenarios. Further exploration of more sophisticated update strategies could be beneficial.
5. **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** The paper doesn’t discuss the sensitivity of the model’s performance to different hyperparameters. Reporting a hyperparameter search or ablation study on key parameters (e.g., embedding size, hidden layer size, learning rate) would strengthen the analysis.

**Questions to Authors:**

1. Have you explored more sophisticated context modeling techniques beyond considering only the last system utterance? If so, what were the results?
2. How sensitive is the model’s performance to the choice of pre-trained word vectors? Are there other vector spaces (e.g., fastText, BERT embeddings) that yield comparable or superior results?
3. Could you elaborate on the limitations of the belief state update mechanism and potential avenues for improvement?
4. How do the models perform when the ASR quality is significantly degraded (e.g., high noise levels)?
5. Have you investigated the use of transformer-based architectures for the NBT model?

**Additional Comments:**

* The paper would benefit from a more detailed discussion of the computational cost of the proposed approach compared to traditional methods.
* Providing example cases where the NBT models successfully handle lexical variations that would be challenging for delexicalization-based systems would further highlight the benefits of the approach.
* It would be helpful to visualize the learned word embeddings to gain insights into how the model captures semantic relationships.



**Overall Assessment:**

This is a solid and well-executed paper that presents a valuable contribution to the field of dialogue state tracking. The NBT models offer a promising alternative to traditional methods, particularly in scenarios where manually curated resources are unavailable or impractical to create. While the architecture is not groundbreaking, the effective integration of pre-trained word vectors and the elimination of hand-crafted lexicons are significant achievements. Despite some minor weaknesses, the paper is of high quality and deserves acceptance. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions addressing the questions and suggestions outlined above.
