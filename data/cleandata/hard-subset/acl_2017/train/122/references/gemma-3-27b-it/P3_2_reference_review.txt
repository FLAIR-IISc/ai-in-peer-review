## Review for "Neural Belief Tracker: Data-Driven Dialogue State Tracking"

**Overall Assessment:** This paper presents a strong and well-motivated approach to dialogue state tracking (DST) that effectively addresses the limitations of traditional delexicalization-based methods. The core idea of leveraging pre-trained word vectors to learn semantic variation without manual lexicon construction is compelling and demonstrably successful. The paper is clearly written, well-structured, and provides a thorough experimental evaluation. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions.

**Summary & Contributions:**

The paper introduces the Neural Belief Tracker (NBT) family of models, designed to jointly perform spoken language understanding (SLU) and DST. The key contribution is a method for learning to track dialogue state *without* relying on hand-crafted semantic dictionaries, a major bottleneck for scaling DST systems to more complex domains.  A secondary contribution lies in demonstrating that the NBT models achieve competitive performance with delexicalization-based methods *and* significantly outperform them when lexical resources are limited.  Finally, the paper highlights the importance of semantically specialized word vectors for achieving strong performance, demonstrating that simply using pre-trained embeddings isn’t enough; the *quality* of those embeddings matters significantly.

**Strengths:**

1. **Addresses a Significant Problem:**  The reliance on manual lexicons in traditional DST is a well-known limitation. This work directly tackles that issue with a data-driven approach, making DST more scalable and adaptable.
2. **Strong Empirical Results:**  The experimental results on both DSTC2 and WOZ 2.0 are convincing. The NBT models achieve comparable or superior performance to strong baselines, demonstrating the effectiveness of the proposed approach. The fact that the NBT models perform particularly well on WOZ 2.0 (where no ASR noise is present) supports the claim that the model is genuinely learning semantic understanding, not simply compensating for ASR errors.
3. **Clear and Well-Explained Methodology:** The NBT architecture is presented with sufficient detail, and the motivation behind each component (representation learning, semantic decoding, context modeling) is clearly articulated. The inclusion of diagrams (Figures 2-4) is helpful.
4. **Insightful Analysis of Word Vector Quality:** The ablation study demonstrating the impact of different word vector types (random, GloVe, Paragram-SL999) is particularly valuable. It reinforces the importance of *semantic* specialization in word embeddings for DST tasks and provides practical guidance for future research.
5. **Reproducibility:** The authors commit to releasing the cleaned datasets used in their experiments, which is a strong positive aspect and contributes to the reproducibility of their results.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Analysis of Error Cases:** While the quantitative results are strong, the paper lacks a qualitative analysis of *where* the NBT models struggle.  Providing examples of challenging utterances and the corresponding errors made by the model would offer valuable insights into its limitations and potential areas for improvement.  Specifically, it would be useful to understand why the performance gain on DSTC2 is smaller than on WOZ 2.0, given the ASR noise in the former.
2. **Context Modeling Could Be More Sophisticated:**  The context modeling component (using only the immediately preceding system act) seems relatively simplistic.  More sophisticated methods for incorporating dialogue history (e.g., using a recurrent neural network to encode the entire dialogue context) might yield further improvements. While the authors acknowledge this, a brief discussion of the trade-offs (computational cost, potential overfitting) would be beneficial.
3. **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** The paper does not provide extensive details on hyperparameter tuning.  It’s unclear how sensitive the model’s performance is to different hyperparameters, and a brief discussion of the optimization process would enhance the reproducibility of the results.



**Questions to Authors:**

1. Can you provide a qualitative analysis of the types of errors that the NBT models make?  Specifically, can you provide example utterances where the model fails and explain why?
2.  You mention that the performance gain on DSTC2 is smaller than on WOZ 2.0.  Is this primarily due to the ASR noise in DSTC2? If so, did you experiment with any techniques to improve the model’s robustness to ASR errors (e.g., using ASR confidence scores)?
3.  What was the hyperparameter tuning process like? How sensitive are the results to changes in key hyperparameters (e.g., learning rate, embedding dimension, hidden layer size)?
4. Could you elaborate on the computational costs associated with the NBT-CNN model compared to the NBT-DNN model? Does the increased complexity of CNN significantly impact inference time?



**Additional Comments:**

*   Consider adding a brief discussion of related work on using transformers for DST. While the focus of this paper is on word vectors, transformers are becoming increasingly popular in this area.
*   The notation used throughout the paper is generally clear, but some equations could benefit from more explicit explanations. For example, what exactly does '⊗' represent in Equation 9 and 10?
*   The formatting of Table 2 could be improved for readability (e.g., using boldface to highlight the best-performing model for each dataset).
