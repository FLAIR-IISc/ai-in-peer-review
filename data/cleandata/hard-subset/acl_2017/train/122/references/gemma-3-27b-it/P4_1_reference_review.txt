This paper introduces the Neural Belief Tracker (NBT), a framework for dialogue state tracking that aims to overcome the limitations of traditional methods reliant on handcrafted semantic lexicons. The authors propose models that couple SLU and DST, leveraging pre-trained word vectors to handle lexical variation without manual resource creation. The main contribution lies in demonstrating that NBT models achieve comparable performance to delexicalization-based models while significantly outperforming them when semantic dictionaries are unavailable. A secondary contribution is the empirical validation of the benefits of using semantically specialized word vectors (Paragram-SL999) for improved belief tracking. Finally, the paper provides a well-defined experimental setup and comparative analysis on two established datasets (DSTC2 and WOZ 2.0).

**Strengths:**

1. **Addressing a Significant Problem:** The reliance on hand-crafted semantic lexicons is a major bottleneck for scaling dialogue state tracking to new domains. The NBT framework directly addresses this issue, offering a promising alternative. The paper clearly articulates this problem and positions its work as a solution.
2. **Strong Empirical Results:** The NBT models demonstrate competitive performance against strong baselines, including those utilizing hand-crafted resources. The statistical significance of the improvements reported, particularly on the WOZ 2.0 dataset, provides strong evidence for the effectiveness of the proposed approach. The ablation study with different word vectors further strengthens these findings.
3. **Clear and Comprehensive Evaluation:** The authors use established datasets (DSTC2, WOZ 2.0) and standard evaluation metrics (joint goal accuracy, request accuracy), allowing for direct comparison with existing work. The detailed experimental setup and clear presentation of results facilitate understanding and reproducibility.
4. **Well-Motivated Design Choices:** The rationale behind key design choices, such as the use of pre-trained word vectors and the architectural components of the NBT model (representation learning, semantic decoding, context modelling), are clearly explained and justified. The discussion on the advantages of element-wise multiplication over dot product in the semantic decoding module is particularly insightful.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Novelty in Core Architecture:** While the application of pre-trained word vectors to belief tracking is a valuable contribution, the core architectural components (DNNs and CNNs) are relatively standard in the field of NLP. The paper does not introduce fundamentally new neural network architectures. The novelty primarily resides in *how* these components are applied within the DST context, rather than the components themselves.
2. **Context Modelling Simplification:** The context modelling component, which only considers the previous system utterance, feels somewhat simplistic. Real-world dialogues involve more complex contextual dependencies. While the authors acknowledge this, a more sophisticated context modelling mechanism could potentially further improve performance.
3. **Lack of Error Analysis:** The paper would benefit from a more detailed error analysis. Identifying specific types of errors made by the NBT models could provide insights for future improvement. For instance, are there specific slot-value pairs that consistently pose challenges? Do the models struggle with certain types of linguistic phenomena?
4. **Belief State Update Mechanism:** The rule-based belief state update mechanism feels disconnected from the neural components of the model. Integrating the belief state update directly into the neural network could potentially lead to a more cohesive and optimized system.



**Questions to Authors:**

1.  Could you elaborate on the computational cost of the NBT models compared to the baseline delexicalization-based models? Specifically, what is the difference in training time and inference time?
2.  The paper mentions that the performance gap between NBT and baseline models is smaller on the DSTC2 dataset compared to WOZ 2.0. Could you attribute this difference to the characteristics of the ASR data in DSTC2 (i.e., does the model's robustness to ASR errors play a role)?
3.  The context modelling module only considers the immediately preceding system turn. Did you experiment with incorporating information from earlier turns in the dialogue history, and if so, what were the results?
4.  How sensitive is the NBT model to the choice of pre-trained word vectors? Have you experimented with other semantically specialized vectors besides Paragram-SL999?

**Additional Comments:**

*   The discussion of related work is comprehensive and provides a good overview of the existing literature.
*   The paper is well-written and easy to follow. The figures and tables are clear and informative.
*   It would be helpful to provide some qualitative examples of dialogues where the NBT model successfully handles lexical variation that would challenge a delexicalization-based approach.
