This paper presents the Neural Belief Tracker (NBT), a model designed to jointly perform spoken language understanding and dialogue state tracking without relying on hand-crafted semantic lexicons. The authors propose two variants, NBT-DNN and NBT-CNN, leveraging pre-trained word vectors to handle lexical variation. The key claim is that NBT models achieve comparable performance to delexicalization-based models, and significantly outperform them when semantic dictionaries are unavailable, offering a more scalable solution for real-world dialogue systems.

The primary contribution of this work, in my assessment, is a demonstration of the viability of directly applying pre-trained word vectors to the dialogue state tracking task *without* extensive hand-engineering of lexical resources. While the architecture itself isn't groundbreaking, the successful integration of word vectors as a replacement for delexicalization is a pragmatic step forward. A secondary contribution is the empirical evaluation on both the DSTC2 and WOZ 2.0 datasets, providing a comparative analysis against established baselines.

**Strengths:**

1. **Addresses a practical problem:** The reliance on manually crafted semantic dictionaries is a significant bottleneck for scaling dialogue systems to new domains. This work directly tackles this issue with a data-driven approach.
2. **Solid Empirical Results:** The NBT models demonstrably achieve performance on par with, and in some cases exceeding, strong baselines – including those utilizing hand-crafted lexicons – on two established datasets. The results on WOZ 2.0, where lexical variation is more prominent, are particularly encouraging.
3. **Clear and Thorough Presentation:** The paper is well-written and provides sufficient detail regarding the model architecture, experimental setup, and results. The inclusion of ablation studies, specifically regarding the choice of word vectors (Table 2), strengthens the conclusions.
4. **Practical Relevance:**  The demonstrated ability to perform reasonably well *without* expert-defined lexicons is a valuable result, suggesting this approach could be more readily deployed in resource-constrained scenarios.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Novelty in Architecture:** The core architecture (DNN and CNN-based feature extraction) is not particularly novel in the context of NLP. The primary innovation lies in *how* these components are applied to DST, rather than the components themselves. This lessens the overall impact of the work.
2. **Insufficient Error Analysis:** While the paper reports performance metrics, it lacks a detailed error analysis. Understanding *where* the NBT models struggle (e.g., specific slot types, complex utterances) would provide valuable insights for future improvement. A qualitative examination of failure cases is missing.
3. **Markovian Assumption & Simplistic Belief Update:** The reliance on only the previous system turn for context, and the relatively simple belief state update mechanism (averaging probabilities), feels limiting. More sophisticated methods for incorporating dialogue history and handling uncertainty could significantly improve performance. The lambda parameter tuning is briefly mentioned but lacks details.
4. **ASR Robustness Concerns:** The authors acknowledge that performance gains are more pronounced on the WOZ dataset (no ASR errors).  The paper doesn’t adequately address the real-world challenge of noisy ASR.  While the ASR N-best list approach is mentioned, its impact isn’t fully explored.



**Questions to Authors:**

1. Could you provide a more detailed error analysis, outlining common failure cases and the types of utterances where the NBT models struggle?
2. How sensitive is the performance of the NBT models to the quality of the pre-trained word vectors? Have you experimented with other pre-trained embeddings beyond Paragram-SL999 and GloVe?
3. What are the computational costs associated with the NBT models compared to the delexicalization-based baselines?
4. Could you elaborate on the rationale behind the choice of the specific architecture components (e.g., CNN filters, DNN hidden layer size)? Was any hyperparameter optimization performed?
5. Regarding the belief state update, what is the justification for the equal weighting of the current and previous turn beliefs (λ = 0.55)? Has a more adaptive weighting scheme been considered?



**Additional Comments:**

The paper would benefit from a discussion of the limitations of relying solely on word vectors for semantic understanding. Contextualized word embeddings (e.g., BERT, RoBERTa) might offer further improvements, although this may increase computational complexity. The reference to "the decoder" (Section 3.2) feels vague and could be clarified.
