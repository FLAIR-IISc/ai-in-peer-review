- Proposes Neural Belief Tracker (NBT) models (NBT-DNN, NBT-CNN) that couple SLU and DST and use pre-trained word vectors to avoid manual semantic lexicons; competitive on DSTC2 and WOZ 2.0.  
- Demonstrates effective removal of lexicon dependency, aiding scalability to diverse domains.  
- Integrates pre-trained word vectors to capture lexical variation and semantic information.  
- Provides thorough empirical evaluation on two standard datasets, showing competitive or superior performance, especially on WOZ 2.0.  
- Addresses a practical scalability bottleneck of delexicalization, important for real-world dialogue systems.  
- Strong empirical results, matching or exceeding lexicon-equipped baselines.  
- Clear, well-structured presentation with helpful diagrams and sufficient model/experiment detail.  
- Ablation study highlights importance of semantically specialized word vectors (random vs GloVe vs Paragram-SL999).  
- Tackles a key limitation of prior joint SLU/DST models that relied on delexicalization.  
- Core components (DNNs, CNNs, word vectors) lack novelty individually; primary novelty is their application/integration for DST.  
- Context modeling (Equations 9 & 10) seems ad-hoc; dot-product similarity and gating may be suboptimal and need better justification or alternatives.  
- Belief state update is simplistic and threshold-based (0.5); could benefit from more sophisticated or probabilistic methods.  
- Paper lacks detailed error analysis to identify remaining failure modes and guide improvements.  
- Questions for authors: justification/alternatives for similarity measure, sensitivity to 0.5 threshold, qualitative error examples, and computational cost vs delexicalization baselines.  
- Suggests adding discussion on adapting NBT to multi-domain dialogue systems.  
- Overall recommendation: Accept — meaningful contribution with clear writing and strong evaluation despite relatively minor, addressable weaknesses.
