- Introduces the Neural Belief Tracker (NBT) that jointly learns SLU and DST using pre-trained word vectors, avoiding hand-crafted lexicons.  
- NBT leverages semantic similarity in embeddings to generalize to varied user language.  
- NBT matches delexicalization approaches and outperforms them when semantic lexicons are unavailable.  
- Main contributions: effective use of pre-trained embeddings for DST, critique of delexicalization’s scalability, and a simple effective architecture.  
- Strong empirical results on DSTC2 and WOZ 2.0, especially in low-resource/lexicon-absent settings.  
- Clear motivation and problem statement highlighting delexicalization’s scalability issues.  
- Ablation study shows the importance of embedding choice and semantic specialization (Paragram-SL999 > GloVe > random).  
- Paper is well-written with helpful diagrams and promises to release cleaned data/code for reproducibility.  
- Weak novelty: individual components (CNNs, DNNs, embeddings) are standard; contribution is their combination.  
- Context modeling is simplistic (only previous system act); encoding longer dialogue history might improve performance.  
- Lacks comparison to more recent DST approaches (e.g., transformers, advanced memory networks).  
- Limited analysis of failure cases and error types.  
- Questions: did authors try incorporating longer dialogue history (e.g., recurrent encoders) and what were the results?  
- Questions: have authors considered transformer-based architectures (e.g., BERT) and anticipated challenges?  
- Questions: request specific failure examples and strategies to address recurring model errors.  
- Questions: ask about computational costs (training/inference) and scalability as ontology size increases.  
- Additional comments: report optimizer and learning rate details for reproducibility and clarify the belief state update weighting parameter (λ).  
- Overall: solid, well-executed contribution offering a promising alternative to delexicalization; recommend acceptance with minor revisions.
