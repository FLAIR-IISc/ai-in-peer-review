## Review for "Neural Belief Tracker: Data-Driven Dialogue State Tracking"

**Summary:**

This paper introduces the Neural Belief Tracker (NBT) family of models for dialogue state tracking (DST). The core idea is to couple spoken language understanding (SLU) and DST, moving away from reliance on manually crafted semantic lexicons (delexicalization) by leveraging pre-trained word vectors to handle lexical variation. The authors present two variants (NBT-DNN and NBT-CNN) and demonstrate competitive performance on two standard datasets (DSTC2 and WOZ 2.0), matching or exceeding delexicalization-based models, particularly when semantic lexicons are unavailable.

**Main Contributions (as I see them):**

1. **Effective Removal of Lexicon Dependency:** The paper successfully demonstrates a viable approach to DST that significantly reduces, and in some cases eliminates, the need for manually created semantic lexicons. This is a crucial step towards scaling DST to more complex and diverse domains.
2. **Integration of Pre-trained Word Vectors:** The innovative use of pre-trained word vectors for semantic understanding within the DST framework is a solid contribution.  The results clearly show the benefit of leveraging semantic information embedded in these vectors.
3. **Comprehensive Evaluation:** The authors provide a thorough empirical evaluation on two established datasets, comparing their approach to strong baselines and demonstrating consistent improvements.

**Strengths:**

1. **Practical Significance:** The problem addressed – the scalability bottleneck of delexicalization – is a real and important one in the field of dialogue systems.  Moving away from hand-crafted resources is essential for deploying DST in more realistic scenarios.
2. **Strong Empirical Results:**  The NBT models achieve competitive or superior performance compared to strong baselines, including lexicon-equipped models. The improvement on the WOZ 2.0 dataset, where lexical variation is more pronounced, is particularly compelling.
3. **Clear and Well-Structured Presentation:** The paper is well-written, logically organized, and provides sufficient detail regarding the model architecture and experimental setup.  The diagrams are clear and helpful.
4. **Ablation Study (Word Vectors):**  The ablation study on different word vector types (random, GloVe, Paragram-SL999) clearly demonstrates the importance of semantic specialization in the word vectors. This adds valuable insight into the model's behavior.
5. **Addresses a key limitation of Joint Models:** The authors directly address the limitations of previous joint SLU/DST models that still relied on delexicalization.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Novelty in Core Components:** While the *combination* of techniques is valuable, the individual components (DNNs, CNNs, word vectors) are not novel in themselves.  The primary novelty lies in the application *to* DST and the specific way these components are integrated. The paper acknowledges this.
2. **Complexity of Context Modelling:** The context modeling component (Equations 9 & 10) seems somewhat ad-hoc and could benefit from more detailed justification. The use of dot product for similarity might be suboptimal, and the gating mechanism feels potentially brittle.  A more comprehensive exploration of context modeling strategies could further improve performance.
3. **Simplistic Belief State Update:** The belief state update mechanism (described in Section 4) is relatively simple and relies heavily on a fixed threshold (0.5). A more sophisticated update rule, perhaps incorporating confidence scores or Bayesian approaches, could be investigated.
4. **Limited Error Analysis:** While the quantitative results are strong, the paper lacks a detailed error analysis. Understanding *where* the NBT models still struggle could provide valuable insights for future work.



**Questions to Authors:**

1. **Context Modeling Justification:**  Could you provide a more detailed rationale for the choice of dot product for calculating similarity in the context modeling component? Have you experimented with other similarity measures?
2. **Belief State Threshold:** How sensitive is the performance of the model to the choice of the 0.5 threshold in the belief state update mechanism? Have you explored alternative methods for determining this threshold?
3. **Error Analysis:** Can you provide some qualitative examples of errors made by the NBT models? Are there specific types of slot-value pairs or user utterances that consistently pose challenges?
4. **Computational Cost:**  How does the computational cost of the NBT models compare to the delexicalization-based baselines, particularly during inference?



**Additional Comments:**

*   The paper would benefit from a discussion of potential challenges in adapting the NBT models to multi-domain dialogue systems.
*   The reference list is extensive and well-maintained.




**Overall Assessment:**

I recommend **Accepting** this paper. While the individual components may not be groundbreaking, the combination of techniques and the demonstrated ability to achieve competitive performance without relying on hand-crafted lexicons represent a significant contribution to the field of dialogue state tracking. The clear writing, thorough evaluation, and insightful analysis make this a valuable addition to the ACL conference.  The weaknesses identified are relatively minor and addressable through future work.
