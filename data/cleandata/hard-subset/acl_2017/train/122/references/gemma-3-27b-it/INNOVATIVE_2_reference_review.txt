This paper introduces the Neural Belief Tracker (NBT), a model for dialogue state tracking that eschews hand-crafted semantic lexicons by leveraging pre-trained word vectors to handle lexical variation. The core idea – coupling SLU and DST and reasoning directly over vector space representations – is a compelling and potentially impactful shift in the field.

**Main Contributions (as I see them):**

1. **Novel Approach to Lexical Variation:** The paper’s primary contribution lies in proposing a viable alternative to delexicalization. Moving away from manually curated dictionaries and relying instead on the semantic capabilities of pre-trained word vectors is a significant conceptual leap, particularly for scaling DST to complex, real-world domains.
2. **Strong Empirical Results:** The NBT models achieve competitive performance with delexicalization-based approaches *without* using hand-crafted resources, and significantly outperform them when such resources are unavailable. This demonstrates the effectiveness of the proposed approach.
3. **Clear Presentation & Thorough Experimentation:** The paper is well-written and clearly explains the NBT architecture and experimental setup. The use of two datasets (DSTC2 and WOZ 2.0) and comparison to strong baselines provide a solid evaluation of the model.

**Strengths:**

1. **Bold Idea with High Potential:** The central idea is genuinely novel and addresses a long-standing bottleneck in DST – the reliance on expensive and brittle hand-crafted lexicons. This is a direction the field needs to explore, and this paper provides a strong starting point.
2. **Effective Use of Pre-trained Embeddings:** The paper demonstrates that pre-trained word vectors are not just useful for initialization, but can be directly integrated into the DST pipeline to handle semantic variation without explicit supervision. The ablation study (Table 2) convincingly shows the importance of semantically specialized vectors.
3. **Competitive Performance:** The NBT models achieve performance comparable to state-of-the-art systems that rely on manual lexicons, showing that the proposed approach doesn't sacrifice accuracy for simplicity. The superiority on the WOZ 2.0 dataset, with its richer language, further highlights the strength of the method.
4. **Well-Designed Experiments:** The choice of datasets and baselines is appropriate and provides a fair comparison. The inclusion of both DSTC2 (with ASR noise) and WOZ 2.0 (clean text) is a good strategy for assessing the model's robustness and generalization ability.
5. **Clear Architecture and Implementation Details:** The paper provides a detailed description of the NBT architecture, making it easy to understand and potentially reproduce.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Analysis of Failure Cases:** While the results are strong overall, the paper lacks a detailed analysis of where the NBT models struggle. Understanding the types of utterances or slot-value pairs that cause errors would be valuable for future improvements.
2. **Simplistic Context Modeling:** The context modeling component, while present, feels somewhat rudimentary. Only considering the immediately preceding system act might be insufficient to capture the full dialogue history. More sophisticated context representations could potentially improve performance.
3. **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** The paper doesn’t provide much detail about hyperparameter tuning. A more thorough discussion of the sensitivity of the model to different hyperparameter settings would strengthen the results.
4. **Lack of Comparison to More Recent DST Models:** While the baselines are strong, more recent DST approaches (e.g., those leveraging transformers) are not considered. A comparison to these state-of-the-art models would provide a more comprehensive evaluation of the NBT’s performance.

**Questions to Authors:**

1. Can you provide a more detailed analysis of the failure cases of the NBT models? What types of utterances or slot-value pairs are most challenging?
2. Have you experimented with more sophisticated context modeling techniques, such as incorporating a longer dialogue history or using a hierarchical representation of the dialogue context?
3. Could you elaborate on the hyperparameter tuning process? Which hyperparameters were most critical for performance, and how did you optimize them?
4. How would the NBT models perform compared to more recent DST models that leverage transformer architectures? Would the benefits of avoiding delexicalization still hold in that context?



**Additional Comments:**

The paper is generally well-written and easy to follow. The figures are clear and helpful. Despite some minor weaknesses, the novelty and potential impact of the proposed approach are significant. I believe this work makes a valuable contribution to the field of dialogue state tracking and deserves acceptance. I'm willing to overlook the incomplete validations (e.g., limited hyperparameter discussion) due to the strength of the core idea and the positive empirical results.
