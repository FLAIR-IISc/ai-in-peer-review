This paper introduces the Neural Belief Tracker (NBT), a model designed to couple Spoken Language Understanding (SLU) and Dialogue State Tracking (DST) without relying on hand-crafted semantic lexicons. The core idea is to leverage pre-trained word vectors to handle lexical variation and improve language understanding within the belief tracking framework. The authors present two variants, NBT-DNN and NBT-CNN, and demonstrate their performance on the DSTC2 and WOZ 2.0 datasets. 

The primary contribution of this work is the demonstration that pre-trained word vectors can effectively replace manually crafted delexicalisation resources in dialogue state tracking. While the idea of leveraging word embeddings is not novel *per se*, its successful application to the specific problem of DST, and its comparable performance to lexicon-based approaches (and superior performance when lexicons are unavailable) is a significant result. A secondary contribution lies in the architecture of the NBT model, particularly the context modelling component which attempts to incorporate dialogue history. Finally, the comprehensive experimental evaluation on two datasets, and the analysis regarding the importance of semantic specialisation in word vectors, adds to the body of knowledge in this field.

**Strengths:**

1. **Addressing a Real-World Problem:** The reliance on manually crafted lexicons is a major bottleneck for scaling DST systems to new domains. The NBT addresses this limitation directly, making it a practically relevant contribution. The performance parity with lexicon-based systems (and superiority without them) strongly supports this claim.
2. **Thorough Experimental Evaluation:** The paper presents a comprehensive evaluation on two well-established datasets (DSTC2 and WOZ 2.0).  The comparison with strong baselines, including lexicon-augmented models, provides a solid foundation for assessing the effectiveness of the proposed approach. The ablation study demonstrating the importance of semantically specialised word vectors is particularly insightful.
3. **Clear and Well-Structured Presentation:** The paper is generally well-written and organized. The architecture of the NBT model is clearly explained with the help of diagrams, and the experimental setup is described in detail, allowing for reproducibility.
4. **Context Modeling:** The inclusion of a context modeling component, while relatively simple, acknowledges the importance of dialogue history in belief tracking and represents a positive design choice. This component appears to improve performance, which is encouraging.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Novelty of Core Components:** While the *combination* of techniques is valuable, the individual components (pre-trained word vectors, CNNs, RNNs) are well-established in the NLP literature. The novelty lies in their specific application to DST and the demonstrated effectiveness, but the paper could benefit from a more nuanced discussion of the prior art and a clearer articulation of what distinguishes the NBT from existing approaches beyond the lexicon-free aspect.
2. **Simplistic Context Modeling:** The context modeling component relies solely on the previous system utterance. More sophisticated approaches, such as incorporating a longer dialogue history or using attention mechanisms, could potentially yield further improvements. The current approach feels somewhat limited.
3. **Lack of Error Analysis:**  The paper focuses primarily on quantitative results. A qualitative error analysis would provide valuable insights into the types of errors the NBT makes and identify areas for future improvement. Understanding *why* the model fails in certain cases would be highly informative.
4. **Performance Gains are Modest:** While statistically significant, the performance improvements over strong baselines are not dramatic. This raises questions about the practical impact of the NBT and whether the benefits outweigh the added complexity.



**Questions to Authors:**

1. The paper mentions using the Adam optimizer. Were other optimizers explored, and if so, what were the results?
2. What is the computational cost associated with the NBT models (training and inference time) compared to the baseline models?
3. Can you elaborate on the challenges encountered when applying the NBT to more complex, multi-domain dialogue systems?
4. The paper mentions “semantic specialisation” of word vectors being important. How sensitive is the performance to the choice of pre-trained word vectors? Have you explored other semantic vector spaces beyond Paragram-SL999?

**Additional Comments:**

*   The notation could be slightly improved for clarity. For example, defining the dimensionality of the vectors used in each component would be helpful.
*   Consider adding a table summarizing the hyperparameters used for each model and dataset.
*   It might be interesting to investigate the impact of different word vector sizes on the performance of the NBT.



Overall, this is a solid paper that addresses a significant challenge in dialogue state tracking. The proposed NBT model offers a promising alternative to lexicon-based approaches, and the thorough experimental evaluation provides strong evidence of its effectiveness. While the core components are not entirely novel, the combination and application to DST are valuable contributions. Addressing the weaknesses identified above, particularly the lack of error analysis and the limited context modeling, would further strengthen the paper. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions.
