This paper proposes the Neural Belief Tracker (NBT), a model designed to jointly learn spoken language understanding and dialogue state tracking without relying on hand-crafted semantic lexicons. The authors claim their model matches the performance of lexicon-based approaches while significantly outperforming them when such resources are unavailable, suggesting a more scalable solution for real-world dialogue systems.

The primary contribution of this work is a demonstration of a viable approach to dialogue state tracking that bypasses the need for delexicalization via manually constructed semantic dictionaries. While joint SLU/DST models are not novel, the reliance on pre-trained word vectors to handle lexical variation *without* augmentation via dictionaries is a step towards reducing the engineering effort associated with deploying dialogue systems in new domains. A secondary contribution is a thorough empirical evaluation on two established datasets, DSTC2 and WOZ 2.0, with comparisons to strong baselines. The inclusion of ablation studies examining the impact of different word vector types is also valuable.

**Strengths:**

1. **Addresses a Practical Problem:** The dependence on manually crafted lexicons is a significant bottleneck for scaling dialogue systems to new domains. This work directly tackles that issue, offering a potentially more practical solution.
2. **Solid Empirical Evaluation:** The authors perform a comprehensive evaluation on standard datasets, comparing against strong baselines. The results consistently demonstrate that NBT matches or exceeds the performance of delexicalization-based models.
3. **Effective Use of Pre-trained Word Vectors:** The results clearly demonstrate the benefit of utilizing semantically specialized word vectors. This highlights the importance of leveraging pre-trained resources for handling lexical variation in dialogue systems.
4. **Clear Presentation:** The paper is well-written and clearly explains the NBT architecture and experimental setup. The inclusion of diagrams is helpful for understanding the model.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Incremental Advancement:** While the work addresses a practical problem, the core ideas – joint SLU/DST and leveraging word embeddings – are not entirely novel. The contribution lies in the specific *combination* of these techniques and the demonstration of its effectiveness without lexical resources, rather than a fundamentally new algorithmic breakthrough. The paper would benefit from a more nuanced discussion of related work.
2. **Limited Error Analysis:** The paper lacks a detailed error analysis. Understanding *where* the NBT struggles would provide valuable insights for future improvements. For example, are there specific types of utterances or slot-value pairs that consistently cause errors?
3. **Sensitivity to Hyperparameters:** The paper mentions tuning a coefficient (λ) for belief state updating but doesn’t provide details on other hyperparameters or a sensitivity analysis. It's unclear how robust the performance is to changes in these settings. A more thorough hyperparameter tuning and sensitivity analysis would strengthen the findings.
4. **ASR Robustness:** The DSTC2 experiments are performed on ASR output, but the impact of ASR errors on NBT's performance is not fully explored. The authors mention that performance improves with transcriptions, but a more detailed analysis of how NBT handles noisy ASR would be beneficial.



**Questions to Authors:**

1. Can you provide a more detailed error analysis, identifying common failure cases for the NBT models?
2. What is the computational cost (training time, inference time) of the NBT models compared to the baseline delexicalization-based approaches?
3. How sensitive is the performance of NBT to different word embedding sizes and types beyond those explored in the paper?
4. Could you elaborate on the choices made for the architecture (e.g., the number of filters in NBT-CNN) and how they were determined?



**Additional Comments:**

The code and datasets used in the experiments should be made publicly available to facilitate reproducibility. The supplementary material should include a more detailed description of the hyperparameter tuning process.
