This paper presents the Neural Belief Tracker (NBT) family of models, which couple SLU and DST and learn to handle lexical variation without relying on hand-crafted resources, specifically semantic dictionaries. The core idea is to leverage pre-trained word vectors to reason over user utterances and system outputs, thereby avoiding the need for exact matching. The authors demonstrate that their models achieve performance comparable to delexicalization-based methods, and significantly outperform them when semantic dictionaries are unavailable. 

**Contributions:**

1. **Novel approach to DST leveraging pre-trained word vectors:** This is the primary contribution. The paper successfully demonstrates that pre-trained word vectors can be effectively used within a belief tracking framework, eliminating the need for manually constructed semantic lexicons. This is a significant step towards more scalable and robust DST systems.
2. **Joint SLU/DST modeling without delexicalization:** The NBT models effectively integrate SLU and DST into a single framework, directly operating on word vectors rather than relying on delexicalization techniques. This simplifies the pipeline and reduces the reliance on external knowledge sources.
3. **Comprehensive experimental evaluation:** The paper presents a thorough evaluation of the NBT models on two benchmark datasets (DSTC2 and WOZ 2.0), comparing them to established baselines, including both delexicalization-based models with and without semantic dictionaries. This provides strong evidence for the effectiveness of the proposed approach.

**Strengths:**

1. **Addressing a critical bottleneck in DST:** The reliance on manually crafted semantic dictionaries is a significant limitation for scaling DST to new domains. This paper directly addresses this issue with a compelling alternative.
2. **Clear and well-motivated design:** The architecture of the NBT models is clearly explained and logically motivated. The separation into representation learning, semantic decoding, and context modeling components makes the model understandable and facilitates analysis.
3. **Strong empirical results:** The results consistently demonstrate that the NBT models perform competitively with existing methods, and excel in scenarios where semantic dictionaries are unavailable. This highlights the practical value of the proposed approach.
4. **Detailed ablation study on word vectors:** The ablation study regarding the different types of word vectors (random, GloVe, Paragram-SL999) is insightful and demonstrates the importance of semantic specialization for this task.
5. **Availability of datasets:** The authors commit to making the cleaned DSTC2 and expanded WOZ 2.0 datasets publicly available, which will further facilitate research in this area.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited discussion of error analysis:** While the results are promising, the paper lacks a detailed error analysis. Understanding the types of errors made by the NBT models could provide valuable insights for future improvements. For example, are there specific slot values or dialogue contexts where the models struggle?
2. **Context modeling simplicity:** The context modeling component, while effective, appears relatively simple. Exploring more sophisticated methods for incorporating dialogue history, such as hierarchical RNNs or attention mechanisms, might further improve performance.
3. **ASR robustness is not fully addressed:** The paper acknowledges the impact of ASR errors but primarily evaluates on transcribed data. A more thorough evaluation on noisy ASR output, and potentially the exploration of techniques for mitigating ASR errors, would strengthen the paper.
4. **Hyperparameter sensitivity:** The paper lacks details on the sensitivity of the models to different hyperparameters. A brief discussion of hyperparameter tuning and its impact on performance would be beneficial.
5. **Lack of comparison to recent end-to-end models:** More recent advances in end-to-end dialogue modeling might provide stronger baselines for comparison. A discussion of how the NBT approach relates to these models would enrich the paper.

**Questions to Authors:**

1. Can you provide a more detailed error analysis, including examples of common errors made by the NBT models?
2. Have you experimented with more sophisticated context modeling techniques, such as hierarchical RNNs or attention mechanisms? If so, what were the results?
3. How sensitive are the NBT models to different hyperparameters (e.g., learning rate, embedding dimension)?
4. How does the NBT approach compare to more recent end-to-end dialogue modeling approaches?
5. Could you elaborate on the computational cost of the NBT models compared to the baseline methods?

**Additional Comments:**

* The notation could be slightly improved for clarity. For example, consistently using subscripts for variables would enhance readability.
* Consider adding a table summarizing the key hyperparameters used for each experiment.
* The figures are well-designed and helpful for understanding the model architecture.



Overall, this is a strong paper that addresses a significant challenge in dialogue state tracking. The proposed NBT models offer a promising alternative to traditional approaches, and the experimental results are compelling. While there are some areas for improvement, the paper makes a valuable contribution to the field and is worthy of acceptance.
