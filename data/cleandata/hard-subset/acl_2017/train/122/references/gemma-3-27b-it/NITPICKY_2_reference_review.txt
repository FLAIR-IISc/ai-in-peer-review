This paper presents the Neural Belief Tracker (NBT) family of models for dialogue state tracking (DST), aiming to overcome the limitations of delexicalisation-based approaches by directly leveraging pre-trained word vectors. The core contribution is the proposal of a joint SLU/DST model that eschews manually crafted semantic dictionaries, relying instead on learning semantic representations from word embeddings. A secondary contribution lies in the demonstration that these models achieve competitive performance with delexicalisation approaches and outperform them when semantic lexicons are unavailable. Finally, the paper offers a detailed architectural breakdown of the NBT model, including variations (NBT-DNN and NBT-CNN) and context modelling techniques.

**Strengths:**

1. **Addresses a Practical Problem:** The reliance on manually crafted semantic dictionaries is a significant bottleneck in scaling DST models to new domains. The NBT models directly tackle this issue, proposing a data-driven alternative. The demonstrated performance parity with delexicalisation methods, without the lexicon, is a positive step.
2. **Thorough Experimental Evaluation:** The paper evaluates the models on two datasets (DSTC2 and WOZ 2.0) using standard metrics (goal accuracy and request accuracy). The comparison against strong baseline models (Henderson et al. and Wen et al.) provides a solid basis for evaluating the NBT’s performance. The ablation study demonstrating the impact of different word vector embeddings is also valuable.
3. **Detailed Model Description:** The paper provides a clear and comprehensive description of the NBT architecture, including the representation learning, semantic decoding, and context modelling components. The figures are helpful in visualizing the model flow. The discussion of design choices (e.g., element-wise multiplication vs. dot product) demonstrates thoughtful consideration.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Incremental Improvements, Limited Novelty:** While addressing a practical problem, the core idea of using pre-trained word vectors within a DST framework isn’t fundamentally novel. Similar approaches have been explored in related fields (e.g., semantic parsing). The paper’s contribution feels incremental rather than groundbreaking; the architectural choices, while well-explained, don't represent a significant leap forward.
2. **Overly Detailed Methodology & Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** The sheer level of detail regarding implementation choices (e.g., specific filter sizes in NBT-CNN, the exact coefficient λ in the belief state update) feels excessive and obscures the broader significance of the work. The hyperparameter tuning is mentioned but lacks sufficient justification for the chosen values. It raises concerns that the reported performance is heavily reliant on careful tuning and may not generalize well.
3. **Insufficient Error Analysis:** The paper lacks a thorough error analysis. While quantitative results are presented, there’s no qualitative discussion of *where* the NBT models struggle. Understanding the types of errors made would provide valuable insights for future improvement and a more nuanced assessment of the model's capabilities. Were the errors related to rare slot values? Ambiguous utterances? Specific types of system acts?
4. **Presentation & Redundancy:** The paper is verbose and contains some redundancy. For example, the explanation of delexicalisation is repeated multiple times. The writing style is occasionally convoluted, making it harder to follow the technical details. A tighter, more focused presentation would significantly improve readability.
5. **Unjustified Claims of Scaling:** The concluding claim that the NBT "proposes a framework better-suited to scaling belief tracking models" feels unsupported by the evidence presented. While the elimination of manual lexicons is helpful, the computational cost of processing word vectors for large ontologies is not addressed. The scalability remains an open question.



**Questions to Authors:**

1. Could you provide a more detailed error analysis, including examples of utterances where the NBT models fail and the reasons for those failures?
2. How does the computational cost of the NBT models scale with the size of the ontology (number of slots and values)? Have you performed any experiments to assess this scalability?
3. The choice of λ = 0.55 is mentioned, but the rationale behind this specific value is unclear. Please provide a more detailed explanation of the hyperparameter tuning process.
4. Are the improvements of NBT-CNN over NBT-DNN statistically significant? If so, please provide the p-values.

**Additional Comments:**

The figures could benefit from clearer labeling and more concise visual representations. Consider streamlining the writing style and removing redundant explanations. The discussion of related work could be more focused and critical, highlighting the specific gaps that the NBT models aim to address.
