## Peer Review for ACL - Neural Belief Tracker: Data-Driven Dialogue State Tracking

**Summary:**

This paper introduces the Neural Belief Tracker (NBT) family of models, designed to improve dialogue state tracking (DST) by jointly modeling Spoken Language Understanding (SLU) and DST without relying on hand-crafted semantic lexicons (delexicalisation). The core idea is to leverage pre-trained word vectors to reason about semantic similarity, effectively handling lexical variation. The authors present two NBT variants, NBT-DNN and NBT-CNN, and demonstrate their performance on the DSTC2 and WOZ 2.0 datasets, showing competitive results against delexicalisation-based baselines, and particularly strong performance when semantic lexicons are unavailable.

**Main Contributions (as I see them):**

1. **Effective Removal of Lexicon Dependency:** The primary contribution is a demonstrated ability to achieve competitive DST performance *without* the need for manually created semantic lexicons. This is a significant step towards more scalable and adaptable DST systems.
2. **Successful Application of Pre-trained Word Vectors:** The paper successfully integrates pre-trained word vectors into a joint SLU/DST model, showing that these vectors can capture semantic relationships crucial for robust belief tracking.  This provides a solid empirical foundation for utilizing semantic information from large-scale pre-trained models in dialogue systems.
3. **Comprehensive Experimental Evaluation:** The authors provide a thorough evaluation on two standard datasets, DSTC2 and WOZ 2.0, comparing against strong baselines and analyzing the impact of different word vector representations.

**Strengths:**

1. **Relevance and Significance:** The problem of DST is core to task-oriented dialogue systems, and the reliance on hand-crafted lexicons is a significant bottleneck for scalability. Addressing this limitation with a data-driven approach is highly relevant and impactful.
2. **Strong Empirical Results:** The NBT models consistently match or outperform existing delexicalisation-based approaches, and crucially, significantly outperform them in the absence of semantic lexicons. This strongly supports the effectiveness of the proposed approach.
3. **Detailed Analysis of Word Vectors:**  The ablation study regarding the type of word vectors used (random, GloVe, Paragram-SL999) is insightful and highlights the importance of semantic specialization in pre-trained embeddings for this task.  This provides valuable guidance for future work.
4. **Clear Presentation:** The paper is generally well-written and clearly explains the model architecture, experimental setup, and results. The inclusion of diagrams (Figures 2, 3, and 4) is particularly helpful.
5. **Reproducibility:** The authors state that the cleaned datasets will be made available, which is critical for reproducibility.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Novelty in Architectural Components:** While the *combination* of techniques (joint SLU/DST with pre-trained word vectors) is valuable, the individual components – DNNs, CNNs, word embeddings – are well-established. The novelty lies primarily in the application and integration rather than fundamentally new architectural innovations.
2. **Lack of Comparison to State-of-the-Art (SOTA) Models:** The comparison is primarily to delexicalisation-based models.  A more compelling evaluation would include a comparison to more recent SOTA DST models that employ more sophisticated techniques (e.g., Transformers, span prediction). It's difficult to assess the true competitive edge of NBT without knowing where it stands relative to the current cutting edge.
3. **Simplistic Belief State Update Mechanism:** The belief state update mechanism described in Section 4 appears relatively basic (averaging probabilities). A more sophisticated update mechanism (e.g., Kalman filtering) might further improve performance and is worth exploring.
4. **Context Modelling Limitations:** While the context modeling is a good start, it's limited to the *previous* system act. Real dialogue context is more complex, and capturing longer-range dependencies might be beneficial.



**Questions to Authors:**

1.  Could you provide a more detailed comparison of the computational cost (training and inference) of NBT-DNN and NBT-CNN?  Is there a significant trade-off between performance and efficiency?
2.  How sensitive is the performance of the NBT models to the choice of pre-trained word vector dimensionality? Did you experiment with different dimensions?
3.  You mention the potential for ASR error compensation. Can you elaborate on potential strategies for incorporating ASR confidence scores or N-best lists more directly into the NBT framework?
4.   How would the NBT models perform in a zero-shot or few-shot setting, where the domain ontology contains new slots or values not seen during training?




**Additional Comments:**

*   The paper would benefit from a discussion of potential error analysis. What types of errors does NBT still make, and what are the likely causes?
*   Consider adding a table summarizing the hyperparameters used for each model variant.
*   The formatting of some equations could be improved for clarity.



**Overall Assessment:**

This is a solid and well-executed paper that addresses an important problem in dialogue state tracking. The results are promising, and the approach has the potential to significantly simplify the development of more scalable and adaptable dialogue systems. While the architectural innovations are not groundbreaking, the effective integration of existing techniques and the thorough experimental evaluation are commendable. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions addressing the weaknesses outlined above.
