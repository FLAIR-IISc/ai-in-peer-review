This paper presents the Neural Belief Tracker (NBT) family of models for dialogue state tracking (DST), aiming to overcome the limitations of traditional delexicalization-based approaches which rely on hand-crafted semantic lexicons. The core idea is to couple SLU and DST and leverage pre-trained word vectors to handle lexical variation without manual resources. The authors propose two model variants, NBT-DNN and NBT-CNN, and demonstrate competitive performance on two datasets, DSTC2 and WOZ 2.0.

**Contributions:**

1. **Effective Use of Pre-trained Word Vectors for DST:** The most significant contribution is demonstrating the effectiveness of using pre-trained word vectors to improve the language understanding capability of belief tracking models. This allows the model to generalize to unseen lexical variations without relying on manually constructed lexicons, a major bottleneck in scaling DST to more complex domains.
2. **Joint SLU/DST Model without Delexicalization:** The NBT models successfully couple SLU and DST in a joint framework, achieving performance comparable to delexicalization-based models *without* the need for manual lexicon creation. This is a valuable contribution, as it simplifies the development process and reduces the reliance on expert knowledge.
3. **Comprehensive Evaluation:** The paper provides a thorough evaluation of the NBT models on two widely-used datasets, DSTC2 and WOZ 2.0, including comparisons to strong baselines and ablation studies showing the importance of semantic specialization in word vectors.

**Strengths:**

1. **Clear Problem Statement and Motivation:** The paper clearly articulates the challenges of traditional DST methods, particularly the reliance on manual lexicons and the difficulties in scaling to larger domains. The motivation for the proposed approach is well-defined and compelling.
2. **Sound Methodology:** The proposed NBT models are well-designed and leverage established techniques such as CNNs and RNNs in a novel way for DST. The use of pre-trained word vectors is a logical and effective approach to address the limitations of lexical variation. The architecture is clearly explained and visualized.
3. **Strong Empirical Results:** The experimental results demonstrate that the NBT models achieve competitive performance on both datasets, matching or exceeding the performance of delexicalization-based baselines. The ablation studies provide valuable insights into the importance of different components of the model.
4. **Detailed Analysis:** The analysis of the results, particularly the comparison of different word vector collections, is insightful and provides a deeper understanding of the model's behavior. The observation that semantically specialized word vectors are crucial for performance is a key finding.
5. **Code Availability:** The authors state that the cleaned datasets will be made available, which is crucial for reproducibility and further research.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Novelty in Model Architecture:** While the application of pre-trained word vectors to DST is novel, the underlying model architectures (DNN and CNN) are relatively standard. The paper could benefit from a more detailed discussion of the design choices and their rationale, highlighting what distinguishes the NBT models from existing neural network-based DST approaches.
2. **Context Modeling Simplicity:** The context modeling component, which considers only the last system act, appears relatively simplistic. More sophisticated methods for capturing dialogue history and context could potentially further improve performance.  The justification for this simplification is not thoroughly explained.
3. **Evaluation Metric Focus:** The paper primarily focuses on joint goal accuracy and request accuracy. While these are standard metrics, they may not fully capture the nuances of DST performance.  Considering metrics like belief state entropy or turn-level accuracy for individual slots could provide a more comprehensive evaluation.
4. **Lack of Error Analysis:** The paper lacks a detailed error analysis to identify the types of errors that the NBT models make. This could provide valuable insights into the model's limitations and guide future research. For example, are there specific slot-value pairs or dialogue contexts where the model consistently struggles?
5. **Ablation Study Depth:** While an ablation study is included, it primarily focuses on the word vector types. Additional ablation studies exploring the impact of different components within the NBT architecture (e.g., the context modeling module, the representation learning submodules) would strengthen the analysis.

**Questions to Authors:**

1.  Could you elaborate on the rationale behind choosing the specific DNN and CNN architectures used in the NBT models? What design choices were made to optimize performance for the DST task?
2.  The context modeling component only considers the last system act. How sensitive is the performance to the amount of dialogue history considered? Have you experimented with incorporating more past turns or using more sophisticated context representations?
3.  Could you provide examples of the types of errors that the NBT models make? This could help identify areas for improvement and guide future research.
4.  How does the performance of the NBT models compare to other recent state-of-the-art DST models that are not based on delexicalization, such as those employing transformers or other advanced architectures?



**Additional Comments:**

*   The paper is well-written and easy to understand.
*   The inclusion of Figure 2, illustrating the NBT architecture, is particularly helpful.
*   The reference list is comprehensive and up-to-date.

**Overall Assessment:**

This is a solid and well-executed paper that presents a valuable contribution to the field of dialogue state tracking. The use of pre-trained word vectors is a novel and effective approach to address the challenges of lexical variation and scalability. While the model architecture is not groundbreaking, the results are strong and the analysis is insightful. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions to address the weaknesses outlined above. Specifically, expanding the discussion of the architecture, exploring more sophisticated context modeling techniques, and performing a more detailed error analysis would significantly strengthen the paper.
