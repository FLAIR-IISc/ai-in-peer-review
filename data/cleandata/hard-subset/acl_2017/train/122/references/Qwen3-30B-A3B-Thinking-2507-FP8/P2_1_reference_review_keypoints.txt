- Proposes Neural Belief Tracker (NBT) integrating SLU and DST without hand-crafted semantic dictionaries.  
- Introduces NBT-DNN and NBT-CNN variants leveraging pre-trained word vectors to handle lexical variation.  
- Claims NBT matches dictionary-based performance and significantly outperforms when dictionaries are unavailable.  
- Demonstrates that semantically-specialized vectors (Paragram-SL999) provide substantial DST performance gains.  
- Practical significance: removes labor-intensive domain-specific dictionary creation, easing real-world deployment.  
- Strong empirical validation: comparable/better results on DSTC2 and WOZ 2.0 with statistical significance.  
- Key insight: semantic quality of embeddings directly affects dialogue state tracking, especially on noisy data.  
- Methodology is clear and well-structured, with appropriate figures and thorough experimental setup.  
- Lacks comparison to recent state-of-the-art methods (transformers, BERT, contextual embeddings).  
- Limited analysis of model limitations and failure cases (rare slots, negation, sarcasm, complex language).  
- Unaddressed computational efficiency: no evidence on training/inference costs or scaling with ontology size.  
- Limited discussion of broader implications (multi-domain transfer, integration with end-to-end systems).  
- Question: How would NBT compare to transformer/BERT-based dialogue state trackers on the same data?  
- Question: How would NBT perform using contextual embeddings like BERT instead of Paragram vectors?  
- Question: How does NBT scale computationally to domains with hundreds/thousands of slot-value pairs?  
- Question: How does the ASR N-best belief update compare to Bayesian or beam-search methods for ASR uncertainty?  
- Overall recommendation: Weak accept — promising and practical, but needs SOTA comparisons and analysis of efficiency/scalability.
