- Introduces the Neural Belief Tracker (NBT) that uses pre-trained word vectors to remove the need for hand-crafted semantic dictionaries in dialogue state tracking.  
- Proposes two NBT variants (NBT-DNN and NBT-CNN) that handle lexical variation via word embeddings.  
- Shows empirically that NBT matches dictionary-based delexicalisation models and outperforms them when dictionaries are unavailable.  
- Demonstrates that semantically specialized vectors (Paragram-SL999) substantially improve DST performance in a downstream task.  
- Addresses a major scalability limitation by removing domain-specific semantic lexicons, supporting practical deployment in complex domains.  
- Provides strong empirical validation on DSTC2 and WOZ 2.0 with ablation studies and statistically significant improvements.  
- Presents a clear, replicable architecture with detailed semantic decoding and context modeling for three-way interactions (utterance, slot-value, system acts).  
- Core idea (using pre-trained vectors for semantic similarity) has limited novelty and overlaps with prior NLP work; claim of being first is overstated.  
- Evaluation is incomplete regarding ASR robustness because DSTC2 experiments use transcriptions rather than ASR hypotheses.  
- Lacks comparison to more recent neural DST approaches that may not rely on semantic dictionaries, making state-of-the-art claims unclear.  
- Question: How practically significant are the modest gains over the best dictionary-enhanced baselines (e.g., 84.2% vs. 83.7%) in real deployments?  
- Question: Can the authors provide results on DSTC2 ASR hypotheses to demonstrate robustness to speech recognition errors?  
- Question: How would NBT perform with standard vectors (e.g., GloVe) in new domains without dictionaries, and what minimal domain data is needed to surpass dictionary methods?  
- Question: How would NBT handle multi-domain adaptation without domain-specific semantic dictionaries — any preliminary results or discussion?
