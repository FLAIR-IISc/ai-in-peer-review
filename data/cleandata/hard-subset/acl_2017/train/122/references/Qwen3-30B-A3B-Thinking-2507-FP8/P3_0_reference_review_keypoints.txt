- Summary: Proposes Neural Belief Tracker (NBT) that jointly does SLU and DST without hand-crafted semantic dictionaries using pre-trained Paragram-SL999 vectors; evaluated on DSTC2 and WOZ 2.0, matching or beating dictionary-based baselines.

- Contribution 1: Shows pre-trained semantic word vectors can replace manual semantic dictionaries in DST, improving scalability.

- Contribution 2: Empirically demonstrates semantically-specialized vectors (Paragram-SL999) outperform GloVe and random vectors for DST.

- Contribution 3: Presents a simple, effective NBT architecture that handles lexical variation without heavy feature engineering.

- Strength 1: Addresses important scalability problem of manual semantic dictionaries in DST.

- Strength 2: Strong empirical evidence that semantic vector quality impacts downstream DST performance.

- Strength 3: Clear demonstration of benefits across clean (WOZ 2.0) and noisy (DSTC2) datasets.

- Strength 4: Well-motivated problem statement linking lexical variation to limits of delexicalization.

- Strength 5: Good evaluation strategy using complementary datasets.

- Weakness 1: Lacks comparison to recent state-of-the-art DST models (e.g., transformer-based works).

- Weakness 2: Novelty claim overstated; prior work (Rocktäschel et al., 2016) used semantic vectors for DST and is not sufficiently distinguished.

- Weakness 3: No thorough ablation studies to isolate contributions of model components.

- Weakness 4: Limited evaluation confined to a simple restaurant domain with only 3 informable slots, unclear scalability.

- Weakness 5: No failure analysis or examples of model errors versus baseline.

- Question 1: How does NBT compare to recent transformer-based state-of-the-art DST models (e.g., Zhang 2020, Liu 2020)?

- Question 2: Can you provide concrete examples of lexical variation that NBT handles but the baseline fails on?

- Question 3: How does NBT learn domain-specific paraphrasings without semantic dictionaries and can you show examples?

- Question 4: What are the computational costs (training/inference) of NBT versus delexicalization-based models?

- Question 5: How does NBT handle out-of-vocabulary words common in dialogue?

- Additional comment 1: The “first to use pre-trained word vector spaces” claim is inaccurate and should be clarified relative to prior work.

- Additional comment 2: Discuss limitations, especially handling domain-specific terms not well represented in pre-trained vectors.

- Additional comment 3: Provide evidence of generalization to larger, more complex ontologies to support scalability claims.

- Additional comment 4: Address how the model handles utterances containing multiple slot-value pairs.

- Overall recommendation: Weak accept — valuable contribution but needs stronger comparisons to current SOTA, clearer novelty positioning, and additional analyses.
