- Contribution: Proposes Neural Belief Tracker (NBT), a data-driven DST that couples SLU and DST to eliminate hand-crafted semantic dictionaries.  
- Contribution: Shows pre-trained, semantically specialized word vectors (Paragram-SL999) can handle lexical variation in DST.  
- Contribution: Empirically demonstrates NBT matches lexicon-based models when dictionaries exist and outperforms them when they don't.

- Strength: Eliminating semantic dictionaries is practically significant—NBT matches dictionary-based performance (72.6% vs 72.9% on DSTC2 goals) while removing costly manual effort.  
- Strength: Clear evidence that semantic vector specialization substantially improves performance over GloVe or random vectors, especially on noisy data (DSTC2).  
- Strength: Evaluation on two datasets (DSTC2, WOZ 2.0) with statistical significance and comparisons to strong baselines increases credibility.

- Weakness: Limited novelty—the core idea of using word vectors for semantic matching is not clearly distinguished from prior work.  
- Weakness: Insufficient ablation studies to isolate contributions of semantic vectors, architecture variants (NBT-DNN vs NBT-CNN), and context modeling.  
- Weakness: Limited evaluation scope (small domain with few slots/values) and unclear scalability to large ontologies where per-candidate iteration is costly.

- Question: How does the model scale to domains with hundreds of slot-value pairs given the need to iterate over all candidates?  
- Question: How was the semantic dictionary for the baseline constructed, and how would that process scale to large domains?  
- Question: Why not compare to more recent transformer-based DST models, and how would NBT perform against them?

- Overall: A solid, practical incremental contribution that addresses a real problem (removing dictionaries) with strong empirical support, but limited novelty and scope; recommendation: Accept (with suggested additional ablation, scalability experiments, and comparisons).
