

# ACL Review

## Summary
This paper proposes the Neural Belief Tracker (NBT), a framework for dialogue state tracking that aims to overcome limitations of existing approaches by using pre-trained word vectors to handle lexical variation without relying on hand-crafted semantic dictionaries. The authors present two model variants (NBT-DNN and NBT-CNN) and evaluate them on DSTC2 and WOZ 2.0 datasets, claiming that NBT matches the performance of delexicalization-based models with semantic dictionaries while outperforming them when such dictionaries are unavailable.

## Main Contributions (as I see them)
1. Application of semantically-specialized word vectors (Paragram-SL999) for dialogue state tracking
2. A neural architecture that processes user utterances, system acts, and candidate slot-value pairs through representation learning, semantic decoding, and context modeling
3. Empirical demonstration that semantic vector quality impacts performance, especially on noisy data

## Strengths
1. **Clear evaluation framework**: The paper uses two standard datasets (DSTC2 and WOZ 2.0) with appropriate evaluation metrics (joint goal accuracy and request accuracy), providing a solid foundation for comparison against baselines.

2. **Semantically-specialized word vectors**: The paper demonstrates that Paragram-SL999 vectors significantly outperform GloVe and randomly initialized vectors, especially on noisy DSTC2 data. This is a valid contribution showing the importance of semantic quality in word vectors for downstream tasks.

3. **Appropriate experimental design**: The paper properly compares against baseline delexicalization-based models and their lexicon-supplemented versions, providing a meaningful evaluation of the NBT's strengths.

## Weaknesses
1. **Serious novelty claim overstatement**: The paper states "To the best of our knowledge, NBT models are the first to successfully use pre-trained word vector spaces to improve the language understanding capability of belief tracking models." However, Rocktäschel et al. (2016) used word vectors for semantic matching in dialogue state tracking at ICLR 2016, which predates this submission. The authors cite Rocktäschel et al. (2016) but fail to acknowledge that they were the first to use pre-trained word vectors for this specific purpose. This is a fundamental flaw that undermines the paper's main contribution claim.

2. **Mathematical errors in key equations**:
   - In Equation (2), the summation should be from i=1 to ku-n+1, but the equation is written as "rn = ku−n+1∑ i=1 vni" which is mathematically incorrect (it should be ∑ from i=1 to ku-n+1).
   - In Section 3.3, the expression "dr = (cs · tq)r" is mathematically problematic. The dot product (cs · tq) is a scalar, and multiplying it by r (a vector) would be scalar-vector multiplication, which isn't standard notation for this context. The authors don't clarify if they mean scalar-vector multiplication or something else.

3. **Incomplete evaluation evidence**: The paper claims NBT "match[es] the performance of delexicalisation-based models which make use of hand-crafted semantic lexicons" but doesn't provide direct comparison numbers. In Table 1, they show "Baseline DST + sem. dict." as 72.9* (DSTC2 goals), but don't provide the exact numbers for the lexicon-supplemented model to verify the claim. The asterisks indicate statistical significance over the baseline, but the paper doesn't show that NBT matches the lexicon-supplemented model's performance.

4. **Lack of evidence for scalability claims**: The paper claims NBT is "better-suited to scaling belief tracking models for deployment in real-world dialogue systems operating over sophisticated application domains," but the evaluation is limited to a very simple restaurant domain with only three informable slots (FOOD TYPE, AREA, PRICE). There's no evidence that the approach would scale to more complex domains with many more slots and values.

5. **Formatting inconsistencies in references**: The references section has inconsistent citation formatting with some entries containing "et al\." with a backslash instead of "et al." and other formatting issues. For example, "Mrkšić et al\.,? 2016" should be "Mrkšić et al., 2016" (without the backslash).

## Questions to Authors
1. How does your work differ from Rocktäschel et al. (2016), which used pre-trained word vectors for dialogue state tracking? Why do you claim to be the first to use pre-trained word vectors for this purpose when Rocktäschel et al. (2016) did it first?

2. Can you provide the exact performance numbers for the lexicon-supplemented baseline model (e.g., "Baseline DST + sem. dict.") to verify that NBT matches its performance as claimed?

3. Can you clarify the mathematical expression "dr = (cs · tq)r" in Section 3.3? Is this intended to be a scalar-vector multiplication, or is there a different interpretation?

4. How did you handle the data augmentation for the WOZ 2.0 dataset? What specific procedure did you use to expand the original WOZ dataset from Wen et al. (2017)?

5. Can you provide evidence or analysis to support the claim that "the NBT model parameters can be shared across all values of the given slot, or even across all slots"?

## Recommendation
**Reject**

This paper has significant methodological flaws and overstates its novelty claim. The mathematical errors in key equations and the serious overstatement of novelty (ignoring Rocktäschel et al. 2016) are particularly concerning. While the empirical evaluation is decent for the limited scope of the paper, the fundamental flaws in the paper's claims and technical details outweigh the modest contributions. The paper would need substantial revisions to address these issues before it could be considered for publication at ACL.
