- Contribution: Proposes the Neural Belief Tracker (NBT), a data-driven DST framework coupling SLU and DST without delexicalization.
- Contribution: Leverages pre-trained semantic-specialized word vectors to handle lexical/semantic variation instead of hand-crafted lexicons.
- Contribution: Empirical validation showing NBT matches or exceeds lexicon-based baselines on DSTC2 and WOZ 2.0 while removing manual lexicon needs.

- Strength: Addresses a major practical scalability bottleneck by eliminating the need for domain-specific semantic dictionaries.
- Strength: Strong empirical results with statistically significant improvements and an ablation showing Paragram-SL999 vectors are important.
- Strength: Well-motivated architecture (NBT-DNN and NBT-CNN) and insightful design choices (element-wise multiplication in semantic decoding).

- Weakness: Evaluation limited to restaurant-search domains, so claimed scalability and cross-domain generalization are not demonstrated.
- Weakness: Lacks comparison to more recent state-of-the-art DST models (e.g., transformer-based or multi-domain approaches).
- Weakness: Insufficient evaluation of ASR robustness since datasets used are transcription- or typed-based, not noisy ASR outputs.

- Question: How would NBT perform on domains with different semantic structures (e.g., flights) without retraining, and what data is needed for adaptation?
- Question: Why does NBT show larger gains on the cleaner WOZ 2.0 than on the noisier DSTC2 despite WOZ having richer vocabulary?
- Question: Were pre-trained word vectors kept fixed or fine-tuned during training, and was any domain-specific adaptation applied?

- Overall assessment: Solid, well-written incremental advance that convincingly removes the need for lexicons but needs broader domain and SOTA comparisons; recommend acceptance with minor revisions.
