- Semantic-specialized word vectors (Paragram-SL999) can replace manual semantic dictionaries, enabling NBT to match lexicon-augmented baselines.
- Ablation shows word-vector choice substantially affects performance; semantic-specialized vectors outperform random and GloVe on DSTC2 and WOZ 2.0.
- NBT is validated on both ASR-hypothesis (DSTC2) and clean text (WOZ 2.0), with larger gains in handling linguistic variation on clean data.

- Strong empirical evidence that semantic-specialized vectors remove the need for hand-crafted lexicons, matching prior performance metrics.
- Thorough ablation demonstrating semantic-specialized vectors are critical, especially for noisy input.
- Clear advantage in clean-text scenarios (WOZ 2.0), supporting claims about handling rich linguistic variation.
- Well-structured, reproducible evaluation using standard datasets, metrics, and released code.

- Limited discussion/evidence of scalability to large, complex real-world domains with many slots and values.
- Insufficient comparison to more recent state-of-the-art DST methods (e.g., transformer-based or newer neural architectures).
- No qualitative analysis of failure cases (negation, implicit references, multi-slot mentions).
- Missing discussion of computational efficiency relative to delexicalization-based models.
- Unclear how the model handles out-of-ontology terms or synonyms not present in the ontology.

- Request concrete examples of paraphrases NBT learned that were not in training data or dictionaries.
- How does NBT handle user terms not in the ontology (e.g., "cost" vs "price"); does it require explicit slot names in the ontology?
- Clarify how much improvement comes from semantic specialization versus vector dimensionality/size and compare to dictionary sizes.
- Explain handling of multi-turn dialogues where user goals change mid-conversation (e.g., reversing preferences).
- Compare NBT to recent neural approaches (attention/contextual embeddings) that also address lexical variation.
