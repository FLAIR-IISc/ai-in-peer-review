- Uses pre-trained word vectors to perform dialogue state tracking without hand-crafted semantic dictionaries, enabling more scalable DST.  
- Demonstrates that semantically specialized word vectors (Paragram-SL999) give large performance gains over standard vectors.  
- Proposes a data-driven NBT architecture that couples SLU and DST to handle lexical variation without manual resources.

- Addresses a practical limitation (manual semantic dictionaries) and shows empirical parity with dictionary-based methods.  
- Provides strong empirical evaluation on DSTC2 and WOZ 2.0 with statistically significant improvements and useful ablation results.  
- Offers the novel insight that word vector quality/semantic specialization critically affects DST performance.

- Claim of being first to use pre-trained word vectors for belief tracking is overstated given prior work (Rocktäschel et al., 2016; Mrkšić et al., 2016).  
- Evaluation is limited to two restaurant-domain datasets, so generalization to other domains or multi-domain settings is untested.  
- Lacks detailed ablation studies to disentangle gains from vector specialization, model architecture (NBT-DNN vs NBT-CNN), and context modeling.

- How does this work specifically differ from Rocktäschel et al. (2016) and Mrkšić et al. (2016); what is the clear novelty?  
- In real-world settings, who would create the semantic dictionaries used by baselines, and would that require domain-specific linguistic expertise?  
- How does NBT compare to more recent end-to-end DST approaches (e.g., Wen et al., 2017)?  
- Can the authors quantify how much of the WOZ 2.0 vs DSTC2 performance difference is due to richer language vs ASR errors?

- Well-written with clear technical exposition, but novelty claims should be revised to acknowledge prior work.  
- Would benefit from broader comparisons to end-to-end state-of-the-art DST systems and more comprehensive ablation/ generalization studies.
