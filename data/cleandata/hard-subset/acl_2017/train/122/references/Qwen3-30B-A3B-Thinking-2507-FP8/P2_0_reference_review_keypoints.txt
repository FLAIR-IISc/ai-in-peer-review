- Introduces the Neural Belief Tracker (NBT) family that uses pre-trained word vectors to learn lexical variation and avoid hand-crafted semantic lexicons.  
- Main claim: NBT matches lexicon-based models when dictionaries exist and outperforms them when dictionaries are unavailable.  
- Contribution: data-driven DST framework that scales better to new/complex domains by removing manual lexicon requirement.  

- Strength: Novel, practical solution addressing the scalability bottleneck of manual semantic lexicons in DST.  
- Strength: Comprehensive evaluation on two datasets (DSTC2 with ASR noise, WOZ 2.0 clean) showing consistent improvements.  
- Strength: Strong justification and ablation evidence that semantically-specialized vectors (Paragram-SL999) outperform generic vectors (GloVe) or random init.  

- Weakness: Limited comparison to recent state-of-the-art DST models (e.g., transformer-based or newer neural approaches).  
- Weakness: Insufficient discussion of practical limitations: handling OOV words, computational costs at scale, and performance on very large ontologies.  
- Weakness: Unclear fairness/justification of the "no lexicon" baseline comparison and lack of detail on how baseline semantic dictionaries were constructed.  

- Question: How were the semantic dictionaries for baselines created (manual vs automated) and were they optimized for the datasets?  
- Question: Can the authors provide concrete examples of domain-specific paraphrases learned by NBT that semantic dictionaries missed?  
- Question: Please provide exact baseline performance numbers without lexicons to quantify the claimed gains.  

- Overall: Novel and valuable approach with strong empirical support, but acceptance should require minor revisions to include comparisons to recent work and a fuller discussion of limitations.
