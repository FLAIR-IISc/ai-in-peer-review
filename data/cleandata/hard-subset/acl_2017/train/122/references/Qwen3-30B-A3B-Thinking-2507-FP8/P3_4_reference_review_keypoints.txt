- Proposes Neural Belief Tracker (NBT) that uses pre-trained word vectors to remove need for hand-crafted semantic dictionaries in dialogue state tracking.  
- Introduces two variants (NBT-DNN, NBT-CNN) that decide whether slot-value pairs are expressed via neural representations of utterances, system acts, and candidates.  

- Shows semantic word vectors can replace semantic dictionaries, improving DST scalability.  
- Demonstrates that higher-quality word vectors (Paragram-SL999) yield better DST performance than GloVe or random init.  
- Proposes a coupled SLU/DST architecture that handles lexical variation without delexicalisation or exact matching.  

- Addresses a major real-world bottleneck by removing manual dictionary construction while matching dictionary-based model performance.  
- Thorough evaluation on DSTC2 (noisy ASR) and WOZ 2.0 (clean NL) shows robustness and strong gains, especially on linguistically varied data.  
- Ablation study highlights the critical impact of word vector semantic quality on DST.  

- No evaluation on truly novel or semantically related slot-values not in training data (e.g., "budget" vs "cheap").  
- Limited comparison to more recent neural DST approaches (e.g., models using contextual embeddings or advanced attention).  
- No empirical evidence or analysis of scalability to large ontologies with many slot-value pairs.  
- Ambiguous handling of multi-word slot names/values (simple summation may ignore word order and nuance).  

- How does NBT handle unseen but semantically related slot-values (e.g., "budget" vs "cheap") and expected performance degradation?  
- Have the authors considered contextual embeddings (e.g., BERT) to handle negation and idiomatic language?  
- Can the authors provide empirical evidence of scalability to large, complex ontologies?  

- Overall: valuable, well-executed incremental contribution that replaces semantic dictionaries with word vectors; accept with minor revisions to address noted weaknesses.
