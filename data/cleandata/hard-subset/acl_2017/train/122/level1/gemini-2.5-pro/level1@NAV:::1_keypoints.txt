- Proposes Neural Belief Tracker (NBT) that uses pre-trained word vectors to replace hand-crafted semantic lexicons for dialogue state tracking, making binary decisions per slot-value pair; two variants (NBT-DNN, NBT-CNN) evaluated on two restaurant datasets and shown to match lexicon-based baselines and outperform them without lexicons.
- Contribution: shows pre-trained embeddings can effectively replace manual semantic dictionaries, improving scalability and generalization for dialogue state tracking.
- Contribution: introduces an NBT architecture that separates semantic decoding of the utterance from dialogue context influence.
- Contribution: provides empirical analysis demonstrating that semantically specialized word vectors directly improve downstream tracking performance.
- Strength: practical significance—removes dependence on manual lexicons and addresses a major scalability bottleneck in dialogue systems.
- Strength: thorough, persuasive evaluation across two datasets (ASR-noisy and richer language) with comparisons to strong baselines both with and without lexicons.
- Strength: strong ablation on word vector quality linking model performance to the semantic content of embeddings.
- Weakness: scalability claim not demonstrated—iterating over every slot-value pair may be computationally prohibitive in large domains (hundreds/thousands of values).
- Weakness: architectural novelty is limited—uses standard components (CNNs, element-wise interactions); novelty is chiefly in formulation and combination rather than new neural methods.
- Weakness: simple belief update (linear interpolation) may be insufficient for complex dialogue phenomena like multi-turn user corrections.
