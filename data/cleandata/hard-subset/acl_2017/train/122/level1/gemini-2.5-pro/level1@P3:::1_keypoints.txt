- Proposes Neural Belief Tracker (NBT) that replaces manual delexicalization by reasoning over pre-trained word embeddings to detect slot-value mentions.  
- NBT takes user utterance, preceding system act, and a candidate slot-value pair as input and outputs a binary decision (two variants: DNN and CNN).  
- Evaluated on DSTC2 and WOZ 2.0; matches state-of-the-art with semantic dictionaries and outperforms them when dictionaries are withheld.

Contributions:
- Demonstrates a practical framework to replace manual delexicalization with learned semantic representations for DST.  
- Provides strong empirical evidence that semantically specialized word embeddings (Paragram-SL999) substantially improve downstream DST performance.

Strengths:
- Addresses a high-impact problem (reducing manual lexicon creation) with a data-driven, scalable-in-principle approach.  
- Thorough and convincing experiments on two diverse datasets with appropriate state-of-the-art baselines.  
- Clear writing, well-explained architecture, and insightful analysis (e.g., differing gains on WOZ vs DSTC2).  
- Commits to reproducibility by detailing training parameters and releasing cleaned datasets.

Weaknesses:
- Scalability concern: binary classification per slot-value pair does not scale to ontologies with thousands of values.  
- Limited architectural novelty: uses standard DNN/CNN components and simple context gating, not fundamentally new neural modules.  
- Poor handling of out-of-ontology values and OOV words—no mechanism for unseen entity detection or OOV embedding handling.

Questions to authors:
- How do you address computational scalability for large ontologies (e.g., candidate generation or single-pass prediction alternatives)?  
- How does NBT handle values not in the ontology or utterance tokens missing from pre-trained embeddings in open-domain scenarios?
