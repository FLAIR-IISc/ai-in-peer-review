This paper presents a novel Neural Belief Tracker (NBT) framework that aims to improve dialogue state tracking by leveraging pre-trained word vectors and avoiding the need for hand-crafted semantic lexicons. The primary contribution is the demonstration that a model relying solely on pre-trained word vectors can achieve performance comparable to, and in some cases surpassing, state-of-the-art delexicalization-based methods, particularly when lexicon resources are unavailable. A secondary contribution lies in the exploration of different neural network architectures (DNN and CNN) for representing user utterances and contextual information within the NBT framework. Finally, the paper highlights the importance of using semantically specialized word vectors, demonstrating their significant impact on performance.

**Strengths:**

1. **Addresses a critical limitation of existing DST systems:** The reliance on hand-crafted lexicons is a significant bottleneck for scaling DST models to new domains. This work directly tackles this problem with a compelling solution – utilizing pre-trained word vectors to capture semantic similarity without explicit knowledge engineering. The results convincingly demonstrate the feasibility of this approach.
2. **Strong empirical results:** The NBT models consistently outperform baseline delexicalization methods on both datasets, especially in the scenario where no semantic dictionary is provided. Achieving comparable performance to lexicon-based systems *without* the lexicons is a strong indicator of the method’s potential. The ablation study confirming the importance of semantically specialized word vectors further strengthens the findings.
3. **Clear and well-structured presentation:** The paper is well-written and easy to follow. The architecture of the NBT model is clearly explained with a helpful diagram (Figure 2). The experimental setup and results are presented in a straightforward manner, allowing for easy comparison with existing methods.
4. **Thorough experimentation:** The authors evaluated their models on two datasets (DSTC2 and WOZ 2.0) using standard evaluation metrics, and performed ablation studies to analyze the impact of different components (word vector types). This rigorous evaluation provides confidence in the robustness of the results.
5. **Practical implications:** This work has significant practical implications for developing dialogue systems in real-world scenarios where creating and maintaining comprehensive semantic lexicons is often infeasible. The NBT framework offers a more scalable and adaptable solution.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited analysis of error cases:** While the paper demonstrates strong overall performance, it lacks a detailed analysis of the types of errors the NBT models make. Understanding *where* the model struggles would provide valuable insights for future improvements. Are there specific slot-value pairs or linguistic phenomena that consistently cause errors?
2. **Context modelling could be more sophisticated:** The context modeling component, while present, appears relatively simple (using only the previous system utterance). Exploring more complex context representations, such as incorporating the entire dialogue history or using attention mechanisms, could potentially further improve performance.
3. **Hyperparameter sensitivity:** The paper doesn't discuss the sensitivity of the model's performance to different hyperparameter settings. A brief discussion of hyperparameter tuning and its impact on results would be beneficial.
4. **Limited comparison to recent advancements:** While the baseline models are appropriate, the field of DST is rapidly evolving. A comparison to more recent methods, such as those incorporating transformer-based architectures, would provide a more comprehensive assessment of the NBT's performance.



**Questions to Authors:**

1. Can you provide a more detailed analysis of the types of errors made by the NBT models? Specifically, are there certain slot-value pairs or linguistic phenomena that are particularly challenging for the model?
2. Did you experiment with more sophisticated context modeling techniques, such as incorporating the entire dialogue history or using attention mechanisms? If so, what were the results?
3. How sensitive is the model’s performance to different hyperparameter settings? Can you provide some insights into the hyperparameter tuning process?
4. Could you comment on the computational cost of the NBT model compared to the baseline delexicalization methods? Does the use of pre-trained word vectors significantly impact the model’s training or inference time?

**Additional Comments:**

* The notation is generally clear, but could be slightly more consistent throughout the paper.
* Consider adding a discussion of the limitations of using pre-trained word vectors (e.g., potential bias, out-of-vocabulary words).
