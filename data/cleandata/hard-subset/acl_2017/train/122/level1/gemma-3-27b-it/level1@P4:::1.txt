This paper proposes a novel Neural Belief Tracker (NBT) framework that aims to overcome limitations of existing dialogue state tracking (DST) approaches, specifically their reliance on large annotated datasets for SLU or hand-crafted semantic lexicons. The core idea is to leverage pre-trained word vectors to compose representations of user utterances and dialogue context, enabling the model to reason about semantic similarity without explicit lexical matching. The authors claim that NBT models match the performance of state-of-the-art delexicalisation-based models and outperform them when semantic lexicons are unavailable.

The primary contribution of this work is the demonstration of a viable approach to DST that effectively utilizes pre-trained word vectors to reduce the dependency on manually crafted resources. The authors successfully show that NBT models can achieve competitive performance with existing methods, especially in scenarios where lexicon creation is impractical. This is a significant step towards more scalable and adaptable DST systems. A second contribution is the detailed exploration of different model architectures (NBT-DNN and NBT-CNN) and representation learning techniques within the NBT framework, offering valuable insights into the impact of various design choices. Finally, the experimental evaluation on two datasets (DSTC2 and WOZ 2.0) provides a solid empirical basis for the proposed approach and demonstrates its effectiveness in different settings.

**Strengths:**

1. **Novelty in utilizing word vectors:** The approach of directly incorporating pre-trained word vectors into a belief tracking framework, circumventing the need for explicit lexical matching or extensive SLU training data, is a novel and promising direction. This addresses a major bottleneck in scaling DST to more complex domains.
2. **Strong empirical results:** The NBT models achieve competitive performance compared to existing state-of-the-art approaches, both with and without access to hand-crafted lexicons. This is evidenced by the reported accuracy scores on both DSTC2 and WOZ 2.0 datasets. The improvement on the WOZ 2.0 dataset is particularly noteworthy, indicating the model's ability to handle more complex and natural language.
3. **Thorough ablation study:** The experiments comparing different word vector collections (random, GloVe, Paragram-SL999) provide valuable insights into the importance of semantic specialization in the word embeddings. The demonstrated performance gains with Paragram-SL999 vectors strongly support the authors' claim about the benefits of using semantically informed representations.
4. **Clear and comprehensive presentation:** The paper is well-written and clearly explains the NBT framework, its components, and the experimental setup. The inclusion of diagrams (Figures 2, 3, and 4) helps to visualize the model architecture and data flow.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited explanation of context modelling:** While the paper introduces context modelling using system acts, the explanation of how these acts interact with the utterance and candidate slot-value representations (Equations 9 and 10) feels somewhat superficial. The choice of dot product for similarity seems potentially limiting, and a more detailed justification for this design decision would be beneficial.
2. **Belief state update mechanism is simplistic:** The belief state update mechanism described in Section 4 relies on a simple averaging approach with a fixed weighting factor (λ). This might not be optimal for capturing the dynamics of dialogue and could benefit from more sophisticated update rules, potentially incorporating confidence scores or other contextual information.
3. **Lack of error analysis:** The paper presents quantitative results but lacks a qualitative error analysis. Examining specific examples where the NBT models fail could provide valuable insights into their limitations and potential areas for improvement. Identifying common error patterns could also guide future research efforts.
4. **Dependence on pre-trained vectors:** While leveraging pre-trained vectors is a strength, the model’s performance is inherently tied to the quality of these vectors. The paper acknowledges this, but further investigation into the robustness of the model to different vector spaces or potential domain adaptation techniques could be worthwhile.

**Questions to Authors:**

1. Could you elaborate on the rationale behind using dot product for calculating the similarity between system acts, candidate pairs, and the utterance representation? Have you explored other similarity measures (e.g., cosine similarity, learned similarity functions)?
2. What is the computational overhead introduced by the NBT framework compared to existing delexicalisation-based approaches?
3. Can you provide some examples of dialogues where the NBT models perform well compared to the baseline, and vice versa? This would help to better understand the strengths and weaknesses of the proposed approach.
