- Introduces Neural Belief Tracker (NBT) that uses pre-trained word vectors to avoid manual lexicons and delexicalization.  
- Composes distributed representations of utterances and context to reason about semantic similarity.  
- Matches state-of-the-art with lexicons and outperforms when lexicons are unavailable on DSTC2 and WOZ 2.0.  
- Main contribution: effective integration of pre-trained embeddings into a joint SLU/DST model to bypass delexicalization.  
- Secondary contribution: NBT-DNN and NBT-CNN architectures capture contextual information without extensive feature engineering.  
- Strong empirical results, notably on the more variable WOZ 2.0 dataset.  
- Eliminates need for manually crafted lexicons, improving scalability to new domains.  
- Clear presentation and motivation with well-explained architecture and methodology.  
- Ablation study shows the importance of semantic specialization in word vectors (random vs GloVe vs Paragram-SL999).  
- Limited novelty in core architecture—DNNs and CNNs are standard components.  
- Context modeling is simplified, considering only the previous system turn.  
- Experiments are restricted to the restaurant domain, leaving generalizability untested.  
- Question: Which semantic relationships in Paragram-SL999 (e.g., synonymy, hypernymy) most benefit DST?  
- Question: What is the computational cost of using pre-trained vectors vs delexicalization and the impact on real-time performance?  
- Question: Have attention mechanisms or other methods been tried to weight relevance across past system turns?
