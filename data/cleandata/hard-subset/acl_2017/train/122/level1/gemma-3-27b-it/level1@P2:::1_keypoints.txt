- Proposes Neural Belief Tracker (NBT) that uses pre-trained word vectors to avoid hand-crafted semantic lexicons for dialogue state tracking.  
- Shows NBT can match or outperform delexicalization-based methods, especially when lexicons are unavailable.  
- Explores different utterance/context representation architectures (DNN and CNN) within the NBT framework.  
- Demonstrates semantically specialized word vectors significantly improve performance.  
- Tackles the scalability bottleneck of DST by removing the need for manual lexicons.  
- Presents strong empirical results on DSTC2 and WOZ 2.0, outperforming baselines without lexicons.  
- Includes ablation studies that validate the importance of word vector types.  
- Paper is clearly written with a helpful architecture diagram and straightforward experimental presentation.  
- Rigorous evaluation across datasets and metrics provides confidence in result robustness.  
- Offers practical, scalable implications for real-world dialogue system deployment.  
- Lacks detailed analysis of error cases to identify which slot-value pairs or linguistic phenomena cause failures.  
- Context modeling is relatively simple (only previous system utterance); more sophisticated history/attention could help.  
- Does not discuss sensitivity to hyperparameter choices or the hyperparameter tuning process.  
- Limited comparison to more recent DST advances (e.g., transformer-based methods).  
- Requests authors to analyze error types and challenging slot-value pairs.  
- Asks whether more advanced context modeling (full dialogue history, attention) was tried and with what results.  
- Seeks information on hyperparameter sensitivity and tuning details.  
- Wants commentary on computational cost versus delexicalization baselines and impact of pre-trained vectors.  
- Notes minor notational inconsistencies and suggests discussing limitations of pre-trained vectors (bias, OOV issues).
