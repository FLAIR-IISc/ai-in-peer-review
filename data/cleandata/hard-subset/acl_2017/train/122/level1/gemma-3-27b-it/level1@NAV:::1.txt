## Review of "Neural Belief Tracker: Data-Driven Dialogue State Tracking"

This paper introduces the Neural Belief Tracker (NBT), a novel framework for dialogue state tracking (DST) that aims to overcome the limitations of existing approaches reliant on either large annotated datasets for SLU or hand-crafted lexicons. The core idea is to leverage pre-trained word vectors to compose distributed representations of utterances and context, enabling the model to reason about semantic similarity without explicit lexical matching. The authors demonstrate that NBT achieves performance comparable to state-of-the-art models using lexicons, and significantly outperforms them when lexicons are unavailable, on two restaurant search datasets (DSTC2 and WOZ 2.0).

The primary contribution of this work lies in the successful application of pre-trained word vectors to improve the language understanding capabilities of belief tracking models. While the concept of utilizing word embeddings isn’t novel in NLP, its effective integration into a joint SLU/DST model, specifically to bypass the need for delexicalization, is a significant step forward. The empirical results convincingly demonstrate the benefits of this approach, especially in scenarios where creating manual lexicons is impractical. A second, notable contribution is the demonstration that a carefully designed neural architecture (NBT-DNN and NBT-CNN) can effectively capture contextual information without requiring complex feature engineering.

**Strengths:**

1. **Strong Empirical Results:** The NBT models consistently outperform baseline delexicalization-based models, especially on the WOZ 2.0 dataset, highlighting the effectiveness of the proposed approach in handling more natural language variations.
2. **Elimination of Lexicons:** The ability to achieve comparable performance *without* relying on manually crafted lexicons is a key advantage. This significantly improves the scalability and applicability of the system to new domains.
3. **Clear Presentation & Motivation:** The paper is well-written and clearly articulates the limitations of existing DST methods and the motivation behind the proposed NBT framework. The architecture and methodology are explained in sufficient detail.
4. **Ablation Study (Word Vectors):**  The comparison of different word vector types (random, GloVe, Paragram-SL999) provides valuable insights into the importance of semantic specialization for the task, strengthening the claims about the effectiveness of the approach. 

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Novelty in Architecture:** While the overall framework is insightful, the core architectural components (DNNs, CNNs) are standard and lack substantial novelty. The novelty resides in *how* these components are applied in the context of DST and the use of pre-trained embeddings.
2. **Context Modeling Simplification:** The context modeling component, which considers only the previous system turn, might be overly simplistic. More sophisticated approaches to capturing dialogue history could potentially further improve performance.
3. **Dataset Limitations:** The experiments are limited to the restaurant domain. Evaluating the NBT models on more diverse and complex domains would further solidify the claims about their generalizability.



**Questions to Authors:**

1.  The paper mentions that performance increases with the semantic quality of word vectors. Could you provide an analysis of which specific semantic relationships (e.g., synonymy, hypernymy) embedded in the Paragram-SL999 vectors are most beneficial for DST performance?
2.  What is the computational cost associated with using pre-trained word vectors compared to traditional delexicalization methods? Does this impact the real-time performance of the system?
3.  Regarding the context modeling, have you experimented with incorporating attention mechanisms to weight the relevance of different past system turns?
