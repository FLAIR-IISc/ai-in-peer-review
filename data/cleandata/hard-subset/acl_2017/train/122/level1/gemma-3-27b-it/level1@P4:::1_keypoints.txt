- Proposes Neural Belief Tracker (NBT) using pre-trained word vectors to represent utterances and context, avoiding SLU and hand-crafted lexicons.  
- Claims NBT matches state-of-the-art delexicalisation models and outperforms them without semantic lexicons (evaluated on DSTC2 and WOZ 2.0).  
- Main contribution: a viable DST approach that reduces dependency on manual resources via pre-trained embeddings.  
- Secondary contribution: detailed exploration of architectures (NBT-DNN, NBT-CNN) and representation learning choices.  
- Empirical evaluation on DSTC2 and WOZ 2.0 provides solid evidence, with notable gains on WOZ 2.0.  
- Novelty: directly incorporating pre-trained word vectors into belief tracking to enable semantic reasoning without explicit lexical matching.  
- Strong empirical results both with and without lexicons, demonstrating competitive performance.  
- Ablation study: comparison of random, GloVe, and Paragram-SL999 vectors shows importance of semantically specialized embeddings.  
- Presentation: paper is clear and well-illustrated with helpful diagrams.  
- Weakness: limited explanation of context modelling and insufficient justification for using dot product similarity.  
- Weakness: belief state update mechanism is simplistic (fixed averaging with λ) and may not capture dialogue dynamics.  
- Weakness: lacks qualitative error analysis to illuminate failure modes and guide improvements.  
- Weakness: performance is tied to quality of pre-trained vectors; robustness and domain adaptation are underexplored.  
- Question: Why use dot product for similarity—were cosine or learned similarity functions explored?  
- Question: What is the computational overhead of NBT compared to delexicalisation-based methods?  
- Question: Can the authors provide example dialogues where NBT outperforms or underperforms the baseline?
