This paper introduces the Neural Belief Tracker (NBT), a novel framework for dialogue state tracking that aims to overcome the limitations of existing approaches relying on large annotated datasets or hand-crafted lexicons. The core idea is to couple SLU and DST and leverage pre-trained word vectors to reason about user utterances and dialogue context, thereby enabling the model to handle lexical variation without explicit resources.

The primary contribution of this work is the successful application of pre-trained word vector spaces to improve the language understanding capability of belief tracking models. While the use of word embeddings is commonplace in NLP, their direct integration into a coupled SLU/DST model without relying on delexicalization or extensive task-specific training is a significant step. The second notable contribution is the demonstration that this approach achieves competitive performance with state-of-the-art models relying on hand-crafted lexicons, and surpasses them when such resources are unavailable. This highlights the potential of NBT for scaling to more complex and resource-scarce dialogue domains. A third contribution, albeit smaller, is the systematic comparison of different word vector initializations, demonstrating the importance of semantically specialized embeddings for this task.

**Strengths:**

1. **Addressing a Key Limitation:** The paper tackles a crucial bottleneck in DST – the reliance on either large annotated datasets or hand-crafted lexicons.  The proposed NBT framework offers a viable alternative by leveraging pre-trained word embeddings, making it more scalable and adaptable to new domains. The motivation is clearly articulated and well-justified.
2. **Strong Empirical Results:** The results on both DSTC2 and WOZ 2.0 datasets are compelling.  The NBT models match or outperform existing state-of-the-art approaches, particularly in the low-resource scenario (WOZ 2.0 without lexicons).  The statistical significance testing further strengthens these findings.
3. **Ablation Study on Word Vectors:** The experiment demonstrating the importance of semantically specialized word vectors (Paragram-SL999) is insightful.  It provides empirical evidence supporting the claim that semantic understanding, encoded within the word vectors, is crucial for the NBT’s success. This adds to the practical value of the work.
4. **Clear and Well-Structured Presentation:** The paper is well-written and easy to follow. The architecture of the NBT model is clearly explained with the help of diagrams (Figures 2, 3, and 4). The experimental setup and results are presented in a concise and organized manner.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Novelty in Model Architecture:** The underlying neural network architectures (DNN and CNN) are relatively standard. While the combination with pre-trained word vectors is novel in this context, the architectural choices themselves don’t represent a significant advancement in deep learning. The authors acknowledge this implicitly but don't fully address how the specific architecture choices were made beyond the initial motivations.
2. **Simplistic Context Modeling:** The context modeling approach, relying solely on the immediately preceding system act, seems potentially limiting. Real-world dialogues often require considering a longer history for accurate state tracking. Investigating more sophisticated context modeling techniques (e.g., recurrent networks, attention mechanisms over dialogue history) could further improve performance.
3. **Belief State Update as a Post-Processing Step:** The belief state update mechanism, relying on a thresholded probability and a weighted average, feels somewhat disconnected from the core NBT model.  Integrating the belief state update directly into the model's training objective might lead to more robust and consistent tracking. The coefficient lambda being tuned, but not further discussed, feels a bit ad-hoc.
4. **Limited Error Analysis:** While the results are positive, the paper lacks a detailed error analysis. Understanding the types of errors the NBT model makes (e.g., misinterpreting specific slot values, failing to handle complex utterances) would provide valuable insights for future improvements.

**Questions to Authors:**

1.  Could you elaborate on the rationale behind choosing DNN and CNN architectures for representation learning? Were other architectures (e.g., LSTMs, Transformers) considered, and if so, why were they rejected?
2.  How sensitive is the performance of the NBT model to the choice of pre-trained word vectors? Have you explored other semantically specialized embeddings beyond Paragram-SL999?
3.  The context modeling currently uses only the previous system act. How does the model perform with longer dialogue histories, and what are the computational challenges associated with incorporating more context?
4. The belief state update step uses a hard threshold (0.5) and a lambda value. Could you provide a more detailed discussion of the sensitivity of the results to these parameters, and potentially explore alternative update mechanisms?

**Additional Comments:**

*   The phrase “delexicalisation” is used frequently. It might be helpful to define it more clearly and consistently throughout the paper.
*   The reference list is comprehensive.
*   Providing code and trained models would significantly enhance the reproducibility and impact of this work.
