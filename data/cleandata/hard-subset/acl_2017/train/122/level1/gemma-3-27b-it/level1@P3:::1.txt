This paper proposes a novel Neural Belief Tracker (NBT) framework that aims to overcome the limitations of existing dialogue state tracking (DST) approaches, specifically their reliance on large annotated datasets for SLU or hand-crafted semantic lexicons for delexicalization. The core idea is to leverage pre-trained word vectors to directly reason over utterance semantics, effectively coupling SLU and DST without explicit lexical resources. The authors present two variants, NBT-DNN and NBT-CNN, and demonstrate competitive performance on two restaurant search datasets, DSTC2 and WOZ 2.0, matching or exceeding delexicalization-based methods, particularly when semantic lexicons are unavailable.

**Contributions:**

1. **Novel NBT Framework:** The primary contribution is the introduction of the NBT framework itself. This approach of directly utilizing pre-trained word vectors for belief tracking, bypassing the need for explicit SLU or extensive delexicalization, represents a significant shift in methodology.
2. **Effective Model Variants:** The authors provide two distinct implementations of the NBT framework (DNN and CNN), offering flexibility and demonstrating that the core concept can be realized with different architectural choices. The CNN variant proves particularly strong.
3. **Empirical Validation:** The thorough evaluation on both DSTC2 and WOZ 2.0 provides strong empirical evidence for the effectiveness of the NBT framework. Importantly, the performance gains are most pronounced on the WOZ 2.0 dataset, highlighting the method’s ability to handle more natural, less constrained language.

**Strengths:**

1. **Addresses a Significant Challenge:** DST remains a critical bottleneck in spoken dialogue systems, and the dependency on annotated data or manual lexicons is a major obstacle to scalability. The NBT framework directly addresses this issue by minimizing the need for these resources.
2. **Strong Experimental Results:** The results are compelling, demonstrating that the NBT framework achieves competitive performance with state-of-the-art delexicalization-based methods. The ablation study regarding the word vector type further strengthens these findings. The performance on WOZ 2.0 is especially convincing, as it shows the model’s ability to generalize beyond the constraints of ASR noise.
3. **Clear and Well-Structured Presentation:** The paper is well-written and clearly explains the motivation, methodology, and experimental setup. The architecture diagrams are helpful, and the results are presented in a concise and understandable manner.
4. **Insightful Analysis:** The discussion of the benefits of semantically specialized word vectors (Paragram-SL999) is insightful and highlights a key factor contributing to the success of the NBT framework.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Error Analysis:** While the results are strong, the paper lacks a detailed error analysis. Understanding *where* the NBT framework still struggles (e.g., specific types of utterances, complex requests) would provide valuable insights for future improvements.
2. **Context Modelling Simplification:** The context modelling approach, relying solely on the previous system utterance, seems somewhat simplistic. More sophisticated methods for incorporating dialogue history (e.g., recurrent models, attention mechanisms) could potentially further improve performance.
3. **Lack of Comparison to End-to-End Models:**  The comparison is primarily focused on delexicalization-based approaches. Including a comparison to recent end-to-end dialogue models (e.g., those employing transformers) would provide a more comprehensive evaluation of the NBT framework’s capabilities.
4. **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** The paper does not discuss the sensitivity of the model to different hyperparameters. A brief analysis of hyperparameter tuning could provide valuable information for reproducibility and practical application.

**Questions to Authors:**

1. Can you provide a more detailed error analysis, identifying specific types of utterances or requests where the NBT framework performs poorly?
2. Have you experimented with more sophisticated context modelling approaches, such as recurrent models or attention mechanisms over the dialogue history? If so, what were the results?
3. How does the performance of NBT compare to recent end-to-end dialogue models on these datasets?
4. How sensitive is the performance of the NBT framework to different hyperparameters (e.g., learning rate, embedding dimension)?

**Additional Comments:**

* It would be helpful to include a table summarizing the hyperparameters used for each experiment.
* The choice of using fixed word embeddings is reasonable, but briefly discussing the potential benefits of fine-tuning them could be interesting.
