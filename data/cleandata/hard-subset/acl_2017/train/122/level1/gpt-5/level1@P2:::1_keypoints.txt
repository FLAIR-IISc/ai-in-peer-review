- Summary: Introduces Neural Belief Tracker (NBT), a joint SLU/DST model using pre-trained word vectors to replace delexicalization and semantic dictionaries, with DNN and CNN variants and a rule-based belief update.

- Contribution 1: Proposes a practical, dictionary-free joint SLU/DST framework that leverages pre-trained embeddings to handle paraphrasing and morphological variation, matching or exceeding delexicalized trackers.

- Contribution 2: Provides an empirical analysis showing that semantically specialized word vectors (Paragram-SL999) improve DST performance over GloVe or random initialization.

- Contribution 3: Introduces a simple context-gating mechanism to incorporate last system request/confirm acts for better turn-level intent detection.

- Strength: Eliminates reliance on hand-crafted semantic dictionaries while maintaining or improving performance, addressing a key scalability bottleneck.

- Strength: Presents a clear, modular architecture (representation, semantic decoding, context gates) with interpretable inductive biases and per-slot-value binary decisions.

- Strength: Evaluates thoroughly across DSTC2 (ASR robustness) and WOZ 2.0 (paraphrasing), showing consistent gains, especially on paraphrase-heavy data.

- Strength: Convincing evidence that semantic specialization of embeddings matters for downstream DST, not just intrinsic metrics.

- Weakness: Scalability claims are limited because inference still scores every slot-value pair and experiments use a small single-domain ontology; no runtime/memory analysis for larger ontologies.

- Weakness: Context modeling is shallow and Markovian (only last system act via dot-product gates), with no multi-turn context modeling or ablations quantifying context contributions.

- Weakness: Training–test mismatch and limited ASR robustness analysis—models trained on transcripts but evaluated on ASR hypotheses, and the hand-tuned belief update coefficient λ is not analyzed for sensitivity.

- Weakness: Comparison fairness and limited ablations—baselines may be disadvantaged, and controlled studies (e.g., freezing vs fine-tuning embeddings, removing context gates) are missing.

- Weakness: Reproducibility concerns due to cleaned/expanded datasets and sparse training details (epochs, early stopping, regularization, learning rates).

- Question (Scalability): What are inference cost, timing, and memory as functions of number of slots and values, and have you explored candidate pruning or retrieval to avoid exhaustive scoring?

- Question (Context): How much do request/confirm gates contribute—please provide ablations removing or replacing gates with learned multi-turn context encoders?

- Question (Embeddings): Did you try fine-tuning embeddings, and how sensitive is performance to OOV handling, subword methods, or different specialized embedding spaces?

- Question (ASR robustness): Have you trained on ASR N-best or confusion networks, and how sensitive is performance to the λ hyperparameter and ASR posterior calibration?

- Question (Generalization): Can the model handle previously unseen slot values at test time, and how does performance change with multiword values or cross-domain slot name differences?

- Overall assessment: A well-motivated, technically sound paper that convincingly shows reasoning over high-quality embeddings can replace delexicalization, but would be strengthened by scalability, richer context modeling, and more thorough ablations.
