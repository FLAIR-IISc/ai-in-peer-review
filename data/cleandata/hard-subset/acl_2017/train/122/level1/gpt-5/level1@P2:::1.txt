Summary and claimed contributions
This paper introduces Neural Belief Tracker (NBT), a joint SLU/DST framework that replaces delexicalization and hand-crafted semantic dictionaries with neural models operating directly over pre-trained word vectors. Two variants are proposed: NBT-DNN (summed n-gram embeddings with a nonlinearity) and NBT-CNN (convolutions over 1–3-grams + ReLU + max-pooling). The model composes utterance, candidate slot-value, and last system act representations; uses an element-wise interaction for semantic decoding; and simple dot-product-based gates to incorporate the immediate context (system request/confirm acts). A rule-based belief update combines per-hypothesis scores across N-best ASR and across turns with a tunable coefficient. On DSTC2 and an expanded WOZ 2.0 dataset, NBT matches or surpasses delexicalization-based trackers, especially when no semantic dictionaries are available. The paper also demonstrates that semantically specialized word vectors (Paragram-SL999) yield better performance than GloVe or random initialization.

Contributions as I see them (ordered by significance)
1) A practical, dictionary-free joint SLU/DST framework that leverages pre-trained word vectors to capture paraphrasing and morphological variation, delivering state-of-the-art performance without hand-crafted lexicons.
2) An explicit, well-justified analysis of the impact of word vector quality on downstream DST, showing that semantically specialized embeddings measurably improve performance.
3) A simple, effective context-gating mechanism for integrating last system acts (request/confirm) into turn-level intent detection, enabling robust handling of yes/no and implicit mentions.

Strengths
- Removes reliance on semantic dictionaries while maintaining or exceeding performance. This addresses a key scalability bottleneck of prior joint trackers: manual lexicon construction does not scale to larger ontologies or diverse user language. Results on DSTC2 and WOZ 2.0 show statistically significant gains over delexicalized baselines without dictionaries, and parity or better when dictionaries are provided. This is strong evidence of practical significance.

- Clear, modular architecture with interpretable inductive biases. The separation into representation learning, semantic decoding (r ⊗ c), and context gates (dot-product-based similarity) is simple yet effective. The model’s per-slot-value binary decisions align with standard DST practice and facilitate sharing parameters across values/slots. The design choice to freeze embeddings to leverage unseen paraphrases is well-motivated.

- Thorough evaluation across two complementary settings with strong baselines. DSTC2 stresses ASR robustness; WOZ 2.0 stresses semantic coverage and paraphrasing. The consistent gains, and especially the larger improvements on WOZ 2.0, substantiate the claim that reasoning over embeddings helps with linguistic variation beyond exact matches.

- Convincing evidence on the role of semantic specialization. The comparison between random, GloVe, and Paragram-SL999 vectors ties the improvements to the semantic content of embeddings (e.g., separating antonyms), moving beyond intrinsic evaluations to a meaningful downstream task. This is a useful contribution for the community.

Weaknesses
- Limited demonstration of scalability to larger ontologies and multi-domain settings. Despite claiming improved scalability, the method still iterates over every slot-value pair in the ontology at inference. The experiments use a small single-domain ontology (3 informable slots). There is no empirical evidence on runtime, memory, or accuracy when the number of values per slot is large, or when multiple domains are present. This weakens the central scalability claim.

- Context modelling is shallow and Markovian. Only the last system act is used, and only for request/confirm types, via simple dot-product gates. Many real dialogues require reasoning over multi-turn context, cross-turn coreference, and system acts beyond request/confirm. There is no ablation quantifying the contribution of each context component, and no exploration of learned context encoders (e.g., RNNs/attention).

- Training–test mismatch and limited ASR robustness analysis. The models are trained on transcriptions but evaluated on ASR hypotheses in DSTC2. While the authors show strong performance on clean transcripts, a more systematic study of training on ASR N-best inputs (or confusion networks) is missing. The belief update uses a hand-tuned λ; its sensitivity and interactions with ASR posterior calibration are not analyzed.

- Fairness of comparisons and ablations. The baselines are delexicalization-based and not designed to use embeddings directly, which could unfairly advantage NBT in settings with rich paraphrasing. The paper does not compare against more recent embedding-aware or attention-based joint trackers. Ablations are limited: we see a brief note about concatenation vs element-wise multiplication, but there is no controlled study on freezing vs fine-tuning embeddings, removing context gates, varying CNN filter sizes, or sharing parameters across slots.

- Reproducibility and dataset changes. The DSTC2 transcriptions were “cleaned,” and WOZ 2.0 was expanded; both are promised for release, but until then, reproducibility is uncertain. Training details (epochs, early stopping, regularization, dropout, learning rate schedules) are sparse, making it harder to replicate exact numbers.

Questions to authors
- Scalability: What is the inference cost as a function of |S| and |V_s|? Can you share timing/memory for a realistic larger ontology? Have you explored candidate pruning or retrieval (e.g., via nearest neighbors in embedding space) to avoid exhaustive scoring?

- Context: How much do the request/confirm gates contribute? Please provide ablations removing tq and (ts, tv), and/or replacing with a learned context encoder over several preceding turns.

- Embeddings: Did you try fine-tuning embeddings (perhaps with a low learning rate or only for frequent tokens)? How sensitive is performance to OOV handling (e.g., subword composition) and to different specialized spaces?

- ASR robustness: Have you trained on ASR N-best or confusion networks? How sensitive is performance to the λ hyperparameter and to ASR posterior calibration errors?

- Generalization: Can the model handle previously unseen slot values at test time (e.g., open sets) if provided only by the ontology string form? How does performance change when values are multiword or when slot names differ across domains?

Overall assessment
The paper is well-motivated, technically sound, and empirically convincing within its scope. Its main idea—ditch delexicalization and reason over high-quality embeddings—addresses a real pain point and yields tangible gains. The claims about scalability would be substantially strengthened by experiments on larger ontologies and richer context modeling. I view this as a solid and useful contribution likely to influence subsequent DST work.
