Summary and claimed contributions
This paper introduces Neural Belief Tracker (NBT), a joint SLU/DST framework that replaces delexicalisation and hand-crafted semantic dictionaries with reasoning over pre-trained word vectors. Two instantiations are proposed: NBT-DNN (sum of n-gram embeddings with a shallow nonlinearity) and NBT-CNN (convolutions over 1–3-grams). The model encodes (i) user utterance, (ii) candidate slot–value pair, and (iii) last system acts, and makes a binary decision per slot–value per turn. Context is incorporated via gating based on similarity between system-act arguments and the candidate pair. A simple rule-based temporal update integrates ASR N-best lists across turns. Experiments on DSTC2 (ASR) and WOZ 2.0 (typed) show significant gains over delexicalisation baselines, and that semantically-specialised Paragram vectors outperform GloVe and random initialisation.

Contributions (as I see them), in order of strength:
1) Empirical demonstration that semantically-specialised word vectors enable lexicon-free joint SLU/DST that matches or exceeds delexicalisation models (with or without semantic dictionaries) on two benchmarks.
2) A simple, scalable architecture that shares parameters across slot values, with a principled interaction between utterance and candidate via element-wise gating, and lightweight context modelling based on last system act.
3) Evidence that semantic specialisation of embeddings (Paragram-SL999) materially improves downstream DST, moving beyond intrinsic evaluations; plus cleaned DSTC2 transcripts and an expanded WOZ 2.0 dataset.

Strengths
- Strong, consistent empirical results without hand-crafted lexicons: On both DSTC2 and WOZ 2.0, NBT significantly improves joint goal accuracy over delexicalisation baselines; it matches or surpasses systems augmented with semantic dictionaries—a key practical advance for scaling to larger ontologies. The larger gains on WOZ (richer language) support the claim that lexical generalisation, not hand-tuned lexicons, drives improvements.
- Clear architectural choices enabling generalisation and sharing: Fixing pre-trained embeddings and using a single classifier over candidates encourages paraphrase robustness (e.g., inexpensive→cheap) and efficient parameter sharing across values (and partially across slots). The element-wise interaction r ⊙ c is a good bias for small data, outperforming naive concatenation.
- Insightful analysis of word vector quality: The comparison between random, GloVe, and Paragram-SL999 shows that semantic specialisation matters for DST—especially in noisy ASR settings. This is a useful and broadly applicable finding for task-oriented dialogue modeling.
- Practicality and simplicity: The model operates on turn-level encodings and a simple Markov context assumption (last system act), and integrates ASR N-best via a convex combination with a tunable λ. This yields a competitive, reproducible system with modest complexity; two alternative encoders (DNN/CNN) allow a trade-off between speed and expressivity.

Weaknesses
- Limited context modeling: The Markov assumption (only last system act) and absence of a discourse-level encoder constrain handling of phenomena like cross-turn references, corrections (“actually, not Thai”), long-distance dependencies, and negation beyond confirm/request templates. The proposed gating dr, dc uses dot products between slot/value and system-act arguments; this may fail when rephrasings are involved or multiple acts occur.
- ASR robustness and update mechanism: The model’s large jump with oracle transcriptions (goal accuracy ≈0.96) versus ASR indicates limited robustness. The N-best integration is a simple weighted sum with a hand-tuned λ; there is no use of token-level confidences or confusion networks, nor learned update dynamics. This likely leaves performance on real noisy conditions below potential and complicates fair comparison to trackers using richer ASR features.
- Scalability to large ontologies: The per-turn iteration over all slot–value pairs scales linearly with ontology size; for large, multi-domain settings this may be prohibitive. There is no candidate pruning strategy, hierarchical value modeling, or analysis of computational cost. Also, training on all negatives introduces severe class imbalance; the paper lacks details on negative sampling or calibration.
- Handling of OOVs, multiword values, and special values: Fixing word vectors helps zero-shot paraphrases but risks brittleness for domain-specific OOVs, ASR-induced variants, or misspellings. Multiword values are represented by simple summation, which may blur compositional meaning. The treatment of special ontology values like dontcare/none is not described; detecting “any”/“no preference” likely needs bespoke handling beyond similarity.
- Comparisons and ablations: While the baselines are established, the paper omits comparison to contemporary attention/memory-based trackers (e.g., attention RNNs, Memory Networks, incremental LSTMs) under identical supervision. Ablations are limited: e.g., the specific contribution of the context gates versus semantic decoding is not quantified, nor is the impact of slot-specific parameterisation (superscript s) on cross-slot generalisation.

Questions to Authors
- How does runtime scale with ontology size (slots × values) and utterance length for NBT-DNN vs NBT-CNN? Any candidate selection or pruning used at inference time?
- How are dontcare/none and negation handled outside the Confirm act (e.g., “I don’t care about price”, “not Thai”)? Is there a special value embedding or rule?
- What is the OOV handling strategy for ASR outputs and domain-specific terms? Did you use subword/character backoff or average unknown token embeddings? How sensitive is performance to OOV rate?
- For requests, what is the candidate representation (REQ=slot)? Are request predictions trained jointly with goals, and do they use the same architecture?
- Could you report an ablation of: (i) context gates removed, (ii) learned c vs using raw (cs+cv), (iii) fixed vs fine-tuned embeddings (with strong regularisation), and (iv) slot-specific vs shared parameters across slots?
- Did you try learned belief updates (e.g., an RNN over turns with inputs P(s,v|ht)) or token-level ASR features (confusion networks)? How much headroom remains versus the λ rule?

Additional comments
- The element-wise interaction is a reasonable inductive bias for small data; a low-rank bilinear form could offer more capacity without excessive parameters.
- The claim of scalability would be strengthened by experiments on a larger ontology or a multi-domain setting, even if synthetic or partial.
- Clarify the unit and procedure for significance testing (dialogue-level vs turn-level, multiple comparisons correction).
