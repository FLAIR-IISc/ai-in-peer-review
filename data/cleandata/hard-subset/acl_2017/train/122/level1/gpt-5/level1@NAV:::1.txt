Summary and contributions:
This paper introduces Neural Belief Tracker (NBT), a joint SLU/DST framework that reasons over pre-trained, semantically specialized word vectors to detect slot–value mentions and update dialogue state without hand-crafted semantic lexicons. Two architectures (NBT-DNN and NBT-CNN) compose utterance representations, combine them with candidate slot–value vectors, and gate decisions with simple context features from the last system acts; a rule-based mechanism aggregates ASR N-best hypotheses. On DSTC2 (speech) and WOZ 2.0 (typed), NBT matches or surpasses delexicalisation-based SOTA, especially when semantic dictionaries are unavailable, and demonstrates that semantically specialized embeddings (Paragram-SL999) materially improve DST.

Contributions (as I see them):
- A lexicon-free, embedding-based DST framework that couples SLU and DST and achieves competitive performance across two datasets (primary).
- An empirical demonstration that semantically specialized word vectors meaningfully boost downstream DST vs. random/GloVe embeddings (secondary).
- A simple, effective context-gating mechanism using last-turn system acts and an ASR N-best aggregation rule (tertiary).

Strengths:
- Strong empirical gains without hand-crafted lexicons: On DSTC2 goals, NBT-CNN reaches 73.4 vs. 69.1 baseline (and comparable to 72.9 with semantic dictionaries); on WOZ goals, 84.2/84.4 vs. 70.8 baseline and 83.7 with dictionaries. Requests also improve (e.g., 91.6 on WOZ).
- Clear evidence for the importance of semantic specialization: Paragram-SL999 significantly outperforms GloVe and random on both datasets for goal tracking, validating the central design choice.
- Methodologically simple yet scalable: Fixed embeddings and parameter sharing across values reduce per-value parameterization and remove domain-specific lexicon engineering.
- Sensible integration of context: The gating by last system Request/Confirm acts captures core pragmatic phenomena (e.g., implicit yes/no confirmations) with minimal overhead.
- Transparent training/evaluation and statistical testing (paired t-tests), with both ASR and transcription analyses.

Weaknesses:
- Limited domain/generalization: Evaluation is confined to a single, small ontology (Cambridge restaurants). Claims about scalability to larger, multi-domain settings remain untested.
- Context modelling is shallow and Markovian: Only the last system act is used; longer-range discourse phenomena and within-dialogue dependencies are not modeled.
- Independent binary decisions per slot–value scale linearly with ontology size and ignore mutual exclusivity/constraints across slots and values.
- Heavy reliance on high-quality English resources (Paragram/PPDB); robustness in low-resource languages or domains without such embeddings is unclear.
- Fixed embeddings may hinder domain adaptation; limited ablations (e.g., fine-tuning, data size sensitivity, error analysis) restrict insight into failure modes.

Questions to authors:
- How does runtime and accuracy scale with ontology size (slots/values in the hundreds or thousands)?
- Can embeddings be fine-tuned without losing the benefits of specialization? Any results on mixed strategies (partial freezing)?
- What is the impact of incorporating more than one previous system turn or user history via RNN/attention?
- How robust is NBT when specialized embeddings are unavailable or noisy (e.g., low-resource locales)?
- Could the belief update (λ) be learned end-to-end, and do you observe calibration differences across domains?
