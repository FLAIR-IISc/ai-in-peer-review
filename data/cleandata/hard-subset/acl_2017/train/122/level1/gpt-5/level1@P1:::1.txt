Summary and claimed contributions
This paper introduces the Neural Belief Tracker (NBT), a joint SLU/DST framework that eliminates hand-crafted semantic lexicons by reasoning directly over pre-trained word vectors. Two turn-level encoders are proposed (NBT-DNN, NBT-CNN), along with a lightweight context gating mechanism using the last system acts (request/confirm) and a simple rule-based belief update over ASR N-best. On DSTC2 and an expanded WOZ 2.0 dataset, NBT matches delexicalisation-based systems augmented with semantic dictionaries and substantially outperforms them without such resources. The paper also shows that semantically specialised embeddings (Paragram-SL999) improve DST beyond distributional vectors.

Contributions (as I see them)
1) A practical, lexicon-free joint SLU/DST architecture that operates entirely on pre-trained word vectors and simple context gating, achieving state-of-the-art results without semantic dictionaries.
2) Empirical demonstration that semantically specialised word vectors (via paraphrase constraints) yield consistent gains on a downstream DST task, surpassing standard distributional embeddings.
3) Resource contribution: cleaned DSTC2 transcripts and an expanded, richer WOZ 2.0 dataset, helping future evaluation of semantic understanding in DST.

Strengths
- Removal of hand-crafted lexicons with competitive accuracy: The core claim is well supported. On DSTC2, NBT-CNN achieves 73.4 joint goal vs. 69.1 baseline without a lexicon and matches the lexicon-enhanced baseline (72.9). On WOZ 2.0 (typed, richer language), NBT’s gains are sizeable: 84.2 vs. 70.8 (baseline) and on par or slightly better than lexicon-enhanced models (83.7). This convincingly shows that vector-space semantics can substitute for delexicalisation dictionaries.
- Clear evidence for the value of semantic specialisation: The ablation contrasting random, GloVe, and Paragram-SL999 embeddings shows consistent gains from semantic specialisation, especially for noisy DSTC2. This is a valuable, transferable insight with broader implications for downstream NLP.
- Simple, effective context modeling: The gating via dot products between candidate slot/value and the last system act (request/confirm) is elegant and addresses common short utterances (e.g., “yes/no”) without heavy sequence models. The improvement in request accuracy (e.g., WOZ 91.6 vs. 87.1 baseline) suggests the design is effective.
- Parameter sharing and ontology-agnostic features: Reasoning over slot/value embeddings allows sharing across values and reduces per-value parameterization, alleviating a key scalability issue of SLU models that learn separate classifiers.
- Solid experimental setup across two complementary datasets: DSTC2 stresses ASR robustness; WOZ 2.0 stresses semantic variation. The consistent improvements and the analysis contrasting the two provide a nuanced understanding of where the model shines.

Weaknesses
- Limited dialogue-level modeling and simplistic belief update: The approach is not end-to-end across turns. It relies on a Markov assumption (only the last system act) and a hand-tuned, rule-based belief update (λ mixing previous state and current N-best). This likely leaves performance on DSTC2 on the table and weakens the claim of joint SLU/DST vs. more fully recurrent trackers across dialogue history.
- ASR robustness is underdeveloped: Models are trained on transcriptions and evaluated on ASR outputs (DSTC2), with only an N-best mixture at inference. There is no use of confusion networks, ASR confidence features, or training-time exposure to ASR hypotheses. The large gap between ASR and transcript performance (reported goal accuracy 0.96 on transcripts) underlines this limitation and complicates fairness against baselines tuned for ASR conditions.
- Scalability to large, multi-domain ontologies remains unclear: Although lexicon design is removed, the model still iterates over all slot-value pairs. This linear scaling with ontology size may be problematic for realistic multi-domain assistants with hundreds/thousands of values (and open-class slots). There is no candidate pruning mechanism, learned pointer to values, or demonstration on larger ontologies.
- Inconsistent parameter sharing story: While the paper motivates sharing across values (and even across slots), NBT-DNN/CNN use slot-specific parameters for the utterance encoder, which may limit generalization and contradict the scalability claim. The impact of per-slot vs. shared encoders is not ablated.
- Analysis and ablations could be deeper: The paper provides a useful embeddings ablation but lacks ablations for key components: context gating (request vs. confirm), element-wise interaction vs. alternatives, belief update λ sensitivity, and the impact of fixing vs. fine-tuning embeddings. Error analyses on paraphrase/antonym confusions and OOV handling are also missing.

Questions to authors
- Training on ASR: Did you experiment with training using ASR N-best or lattices (or confnet features) to reduce the transcript-to-ASR mismatch? How does performance change if trained with N-best (weighted) inputs?
- Parameter sharing: Why are utterance encoders slot-specific? Did you try a shared encoder across all slots, or partial sharing (e.g., shared encoder plus slot adapters)? What is the trade-off empirically?
- Context modeling: How critical is the last-system-act gating? Please provide an ablation showing deltas for request, confirm, and both. Also, does including longer history (beyond last turn) help?
- Interaction function: You chose element-wise multiplication r ⊗ c. Did you try bilinear forms or attention over value descriptions? Any performance differences?
- Scalability: How does runtime scale with ontology size? Any results or heuristics for candidate pruning? Would the approach support open-vocabulary values (e.g., names) and unseen values at test time?
- Embeddings: Are embeddings strictly fixed? Did you try fine-tuning with regularization (e.g., counter-fitting objective) to adapt to domain-specific senses while preserving semantic constraints?

Additional comments and suggestions
- Consider integrating dialogue-level recurrence (e.g., GRU over turns) or memory mechanisms to move beyond the last-system-act assumption.
- Explore ASR-aware training and features (confidences, confusion networks) to close the DSTC2 gap.
- Add subword/character representations to better handle OOVs and morphology if aiming at other languages.
- Report per-slot performance and qualitative error analysis (e.g., antonym traps expensive vs. inexpensive; area north vs. south).
- Clarify dataset release details (cleaned DSTC2, WOZ 2.0) and ensure exact splits for reproducibility.

Overall evaluation
A strong, timely paper with clear practical impact: it removes a major bottleneck (semantic lexicons) while maintaining or improving accuracy. The methodological novelty is moderate but well executed; the empirical insights about semantic specialisation are valuable. Addressing dialogue-level modeling and ASR robustness would further strengthen the work. I lean towards acceptance.
