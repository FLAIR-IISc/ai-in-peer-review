- Introduces Neural Belief Tracker (NBT): lexicon-free joint SLU/DST using pre-trained word vectors with two encoders (NBT-DNN, NBT-CNN) and simple context gating.
- Uses a lightweight rule-based belief update over ASR N-best and last-system-act gating (request/confirm).
- Matches lexicon-enhanced systems on DSTC2 and substantially outperforms them on expanded WOZ 2.0.
- Shows semantically specialised embeddings (Paragram-SL999) yield consistent DST gains over standard distributional vectors.
- Contribution: practical lexicon-free joint SLU/DST architecture achieving state-of-the-art without semantic dictionaries.
- Contribution: empirical evidence that semantic specialisation of embeddings helps downstream DST.
- Contribution: releases cleaned DSTC2 transcripts and an expanded WOZ 2.0 dataset for future evaluation.
- Strength: convincingly removes hand-crafted lexicons while maintaining or improving accuracy across datasets.
- Strength: clear ablation showing value of semantic specialisation, especially for noisy ASR settings.
- Strength: simple, effective context modeling via dot-product gating addresses short utterances like yes/no.
- Strength: reasoning over slot/value embeddings enables parameter sharing and reduces per-value parameters.
- Strength: solid experimental setup using complementary datasets (DSTC2 for ASR, WOZ 2.0 for semantic variation).
- Weakness: limited dialogue-level modeling — relies on last-system-act and a hand-tuned, rule-based belief update.
- Weakness: ASR robustness underdeveloped — trained on transcripts, not ASR N-best/lattices, lacks ASR-aware features.
- Weakness: unclear scalability — iterates over all slot-value pairs, problematic for large multi-domain ontologies and open-class slots.
- Weakness: inconsistent parameter-sharing — utterance encoders are slot-specific, contradicting claimed sharing benefits.
- Weakness: insufficient ablations and error analysis (context gating, interaction function, λ sensitivity, embedding fine-tuning, OOV handling).
- Question: did you train with ASR N-best or lattices, and how does ASR-aware training affect performance?
- Question: why are utterance encoders slot-specific; any experiments with shared encoders or adapters?
- Question: provide ablations for last-system-act gating (request vs. confirm) and longer history inclusion.
- Question: did you try alternative interaction functions (bilinear, attention) and how do they perform?
- Question: how does runtime scale with ontology size and can the method handle open-vocabulary or unseen values?
- Question: are embeddings fixed or fine-tuned, and did you try regularized fine-tuning (e.g., counter-fitting)?
- Suggestion: integrate dialogue-level recurrence or memory to move beyond last-act assumption.
- Suggestion: explore ASR-aware training/features (confidences, confusion networks) to close DSTC2 gap.
- Suggestion: add subword/character representations to handle OOVs and morphology.
- Suggestion: report per-slot performance, qualitative error analysis, and clarify dataset release/splits for reproducibility.
- Overall: strong, practical paper with moderate novelty and valuable empirical insights; leans toward acceptance if dialogue-level and ASR robustness issues are addressed.
