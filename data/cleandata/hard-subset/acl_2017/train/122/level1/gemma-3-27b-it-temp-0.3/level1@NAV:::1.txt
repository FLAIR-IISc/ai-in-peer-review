## Review of "Neural Belief Tracker: Data-Driven Dialogue State Tracking"

This paper presents a novel Neural Belief Tracker (NBT) framework designed to overcome limitations of existing dialogue state tracking (DST) approaches, specifically their reliance on large annotated datasets for SLU or hand-crafted semantic lexicons. The core idea is to leverage pre-trained word vectors to reason over user utterances and dialogue context, effectively learning to compose representations without explicit lexical matching. The authors claim their approach matches state-of-the-art performance when lexicons are available and significantly outperforms them when they are not.

The primary contribution of this work, in my view, is the demonstration of the effectiveness of utilizing pre-trained word vectors *directly* within a belief tracking framework, moving beyond traditional delexicalization techniques. While the idea of leveraging word embeddings isn’t novel in NLP, its application to DST in this manner, and the resulting performance gains, are significant. A secondary contribution lies in the two model variants (NBT-DNN and NBT-CNN) and the exploration of different representation learning techniques within the NBT framework. The ablation study on word vector types (Table 2) further strengthens this contribution by highlighting the importance of semantically specialized embeddings.

**Strengths:**

1. **Strong Empirical Results:** The NBT models consistently outperform baseline delexicalization-based trackers on both the DSTC2 and WOZ 2.0 datasets, demonstrating the practical effectiveness of the proposed approach. The results are statistically significant, bolstering the claims.
2. **Addresses a Key Limitation:** The paper directly tackles the scalability issue of traditional DST methods that rely on manual lexicon creation. This is a crucial step towards deploying DST systems in more complex and diverse domains.
3. **Clear and Well-Structured Presentation:** The paper is well-written and clearly explains the NBT architecture, the different components, and the experimental setup. The inclusion of diagrams (Figures 2, 3, and 4) aids understanding.
4. **Thorough Evaluation:** The authors evaluate their models on two different datasets, including one with noisy ASR data, providing a comprehensive assessment of their robustness.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Novelty in Core Components:** While the *combination* of techniques is valuable, the individual components (DNNs, CNNs, word embeddings) are well-established. The novelty lies in their specific application and integration within the DST context, which could be more explicitly highlighted.
2. **Context Modeling Simplification:** The context modeling component, focusing solely on the previous system turn, feels somewhat limited. More sophisticated approaches to incorporating dialogue history could potentially further improve performance.
3. **Lack of Error Analysis:** The paper lacks a detailed error analysis. Understanding *where* the NBT models struggle would provide valuable insights for future improvements.



**Questions to Authors:**

1.  Could you elaborate on the computational cost of the NBT models compared to the baseline delexicalization-based trackers?
2.  How sensitive is the performance of the NBT models to the choice of pre-trained word vectors? Are there specific characteristics of Paragram-SL999 that make it particularly well-suited for this task?
3.  What are the potential benefits of exploring more sophisticated context modeling techniques, such as incorporating attention mechanisms over the entire dialogue history?
