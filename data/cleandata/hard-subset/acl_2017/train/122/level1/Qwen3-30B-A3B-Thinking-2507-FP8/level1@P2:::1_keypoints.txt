- Proposes Neural Belief Tracker (NBT) that removes dependency on hand-crafted semantic lexicons for dialogue state tracking.  
- NBT leverages pre-trained word vectors to compose utterance and context representations.  
- Empirically shows NBT matches lexicon-based models and outperforms them when lexicons are unavailable.  
- Contribution: novel framework addressing scalability barrier by eliminating manual semantic lexicons.  
- Contribution: demonstrates semantic-specialized word vectors (Paragram-SL999) substantially improve tracking performance.  
- Contribution: comprehensive evaluation across DSTC2 and WOZ 2.0, including ASR/noise conditions.  
- Strength: effectively tackles a critical real-world scalability limitation in dialogue systems.  
- Strength: provides clear evidence that semantic quality of pre-trained vectors matters for downstream tasks.  
- Strength: rigorous, statistically significant evaluation across multiple datasets and conditions.  
- Weakness: limited demonstration of scalability beyond a single domain with only three informable slots.  
- Weakness: lacks analysis of computational efficiency (training/inference time, memory) versus baselines.  
- Weakness: insufficient discussion of handling out-of-vocabulary words and domain-specific terms.  
- Question: how does NBT scale to much larger ontologies (e.g., 50+ slots)?  
- Question: what are the comparative computational requirements of NBT models versus baselines?  
- Question: what strategies do the authors recommend for OOV handling and integration with NBT?  
- Overall: compelling, well-supported approach that outweighs limitations; recommend acceptance with minor revisions.
