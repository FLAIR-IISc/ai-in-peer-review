

This paper proposes the Neural Belief Tracker (NBT), a framework for dialogue state tracking that eliminates reliance on hand-crafted semantic lexicons by leveraging pre-trained word vectors. The main contributions, as I see them, are: 1) Demonstrating that semantic-specialized word vectors (Paragram-SL999) significantly improve dialogue state tracking performance without domain-specific lexicons; 2) Establishing that neural models can effectively handle lexical variation in user utterances through distributed representations rather than exact matching; and 3) Providing a scalable architecture that avoids the manual effort required to create semantic dictionaries for new domains.

The strongest strengths of this work are: First, the empirical evidence showing NBT matches lexicon-enhanced baselines while significantly outperforming them when lexicons are unavailable (e.g., +10.9% on WOZ 2.0 goals over baseline without semantic dictionary). Second, the thorough analysis demonstrating that semantic-specialized word vectors are crucial for performance, with Paragram-SL999 outperforming GloVe and random vectors by up to 8.5% on DSTC2. Third, the clear demonstration of scalability - the NBT framework requires no domain-specific lexicon creation, making it suitable for real-world deployment in complex domains.

The primary weaknesses include: First, the novelty is limited - while the application to dialogue state tracking is novel, the core technique (using word vectors for semantic matching) has been explored in other NLP tasks. Second, the evaluation doesn't sufficiently address ASR robustness, which is critical for spoken dialogue systems; the model's performance drops significantly when using ASR hypotheses instead of clean transcriptions. Third, the baseline comparison is limited to specific models (Henderson et al. 2014, Wen et al. 2017) rather than more recent state-of-the-art approaches like those using transformer architectures.

The authors should clarify: How does the NBT framework specifically handle morphological variations (e.g., "expensive" vs. "inexpensive") compared to semantic dictionaries that would need to explicitly list such variants? Additionally, what is the computational complexity of the NBT model compared to standard baselines, particularly regarding the use of pre-trained word vectors?
