- Proposes Neural Belief Tracker (NBT) that removes need for hand-crafted semantic lexicons for DST.
- Claims pre-trained, semantically-specialized word vectors substantially improve DST performance.
- Shows NBT better handles lexical variation and is applicable across datasets (DSTC2, WOZ 2.0).

- Practical contribution: matches lexicon-enhanced models without lexicons, with statistically significant results.
- Demonstrates semantic vector quality (Paragram-SL999 vs GloVe/random) strongly impacts DST, especially on noisy data.
- Evaluation on two diverse datasets supports generalizability and highlights greater gains in lexically variable domains.
- Clear model presentation (NBT-DNN vs NBT-CNN) and justified design choices (element-wise multiplication).

- Overstates novelty by not clearly distinguishing from prior work using semantic vectors (e.g., Rocktäschel et al., Wieting et al.).
- Scalability claims are unsupported: evaluated only on a single small restaurant domain with three informable slots.
- Insufficient analysis of ASR error impact—no diagnosis or mitigation strategies for worse DSTC2 performance.
- Evaluation is incomplete: reports only joint goal/request accuracy and lacks slot-level or error-type analyses.

- How does NBT specifically differ from prior uses of semantic vectors (Rocktäschel et al., Wieting et al.)?
- How is parameter sharing across slots/values implemented, and what evidence shows it benefits scalability/performance?
- Why do GloVe vectors fail to help on WOZ 2.0 (performing like random initialization)—is this dataset-specific?
