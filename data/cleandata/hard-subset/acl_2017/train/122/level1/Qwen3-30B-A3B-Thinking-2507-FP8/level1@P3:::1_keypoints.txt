- Proposes Neural Belief Tracker (NBT) that uses pre-trained word vectors to avoid hand-crafted semantic lexicons for dialogue state tracking.  
- Introduces two NBT variants (NBT-DNN, NBT-CNN) that compose distributed representations of utterances and context to detect slot-value pairs.  
- Shows empirically that semantic-specialized word vectors (Paragram-SL999) significantly improve performance.  
- Claims the approach scales better to larger domains where lexicon creation is infeasible.  

- Addresses the scalability and lexicon/data limitations of prior dialogue state trackers.  
- Provides strong empirical validation on DSTC2 and WOZ 2.0 with statistically significant improvements (notably NBT-CNN on WOZ 2.0).  
- Offers insightful analysis comparing different word vector spaces, highlighting Paragram-SL999’s advantages, especially on noisy data.  

- Insufficient handling of ASR errors: large performance drop on ASR vs transcriptions and only a simple rule-based belief update proposed.  
- Outdated baseline comparison: missing recent neural approaches (e.g., attention, memory networks) as baselines.  
- No analysis of computational efficiency: training time, model size, and inference speed are not reported.  
- Limited discussion of model limitations: English-centric vectors, unclear handling of morphological variation and rare slot values.  

- Which ASR error patterns most degrade NBT and how can the model be adapted to handle them?  
- How does NBT perform in much larger domains with hundreds of slots/values?  
- How do Paragram-SL999 vectors compare practically to larger pre-trained vectors like GloVe-300 for deployment?  

- Overall: valuable contribution by removing reliance on lexicons, but impact limited by ASR handling, outdated baselines, and missing computational deployment analysis.
