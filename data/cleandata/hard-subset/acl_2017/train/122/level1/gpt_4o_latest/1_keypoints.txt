- Introduces Neural Belief Tracker (NBT), a framework for belief tracking in SDS that removes need for hand-crafted semantic lexicons.  
- Proposes two NBT variants (NBT-DNN and NBT-CNN) differing in representation learning.  
- Leverages pre-trained semantically-specialized word vectors (e.g., Paragram-SL999) to handle lexical variation and noisy ASR.  
- Evaluates on DSTC2 (noisy) and WOZ 2.0 (noise-free), showing NBT outperforms delexicalisation baselines.  
- Main contribution: couples SLU and DST without hand-crafted resources for scalable dialogue state tracking.  
- Demonstrates that specialized pre-trained embeddings improve belief tracking, especially under ASR noise.  
- Empirical results claim state-of-the-art performance and robustness across datasets.  
- Strength: scalability and generalization by removing semantic lexicon dependency.  
- Strength: consistent performance gains over baselines, particularly without semantic dictionaries.  
- Strength: convincing use and analysis of different word vector spaces.  
- Strength: thorough experiments with statistical significance testing.  
- Weakness: lacks empirical evaluation on multi-domain dialogue systems for broader generalizability.  
- Weakness: heavy reliance on quality of pre-trained embeddings may limit low-resource languages/domains.  
- Weakness: ASR error handling uses a simplistic rule-based belief update; more advanced methods not explored.  
- Questions for authors: performance in multi-domain settings and extension plans?  
- Questions for authors: handling of morphologically complex or low-resource languages given embedding reliance?  
- Questions for authors: any exploration of non-rule-based ASR error compensation methods?  
- Overall: significant and well-supported advancement in belief tracking, though multi-domain and ASR handling deserve further work.
