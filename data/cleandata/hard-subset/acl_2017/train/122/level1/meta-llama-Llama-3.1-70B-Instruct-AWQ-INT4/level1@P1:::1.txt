**Summary of the Paper**

This paper proposes a novel Neural Belief Tracking (NBT) framework for dialogue state tracking, which overcomes the limitations of current approaches by leveraging pre-trained word vectors to handle lexical and morphological variation. The NBT model couples Spoken Language Understanding (SLU) and Dialogue State Tracking (DST), eliminating the need for hand-crafted semantic lexicons. The authors evaluate the NBT model on two datasets, DSTC2 and WOZ 2.0, and demonstrate its ability to match the performance of state-of-the-art models that rely on hand-crafted semantic lexicons and outperform them when such resources are not available.

**Main Contributions**

1. **Novel NBT framework**: The authors propose a new framework that integrates SLU and DST using pre-trained word vectors, eliminating the need for hand-crafted semantic lexicons.
2. **Improved performance**: The NBT model demonstrates improved performance on two datasets, DSTC2 and WOZ 2.0, compared to state-of-the-art models that rely on hand-crafted semantic lexicons.
3. **Scalability**: The NBT model is designed to be scalable to larger, more complex dialogue domains, where the creation of hand-crafted semantic lexicons is infeasible.

**Strengths**

1. **Innovative use of pre-trained word vectors**: The authors effectively utilize pre-trained word vectors to handle lexical and morphological variation, eliminating the need for hand-crafted semantic lexicons.
2. **Improved performance**: The NBT model demonstrates improved performance on two datasets, DSTC2 and WOZ 2.0, compared to state-of-the-art models that rely on hand-crafted semantic lexicons.
3. **Scalability**: The NBT model is designed to be scalable to larger, more complex dialogue domains, where the creation of hand-crafted semantic lexicons is infeasible.
4. **Robustness to ASR errors**: The NBT model is robust to ASR errors, as demonstrated by its performance on the DSTC2 dataset.
5. **Flexibility**: The NBT model can be easily adapted to different dialogue domains and languages.

**Weaknesses**

1. **Limited evaluation**: The authors only evaluate the NBT model on two datasets, DSTC2 and WOZ 2.0, which may not be representative of all possible dialogue domains.
2. **Dependence on pre-trained word vectors**: The NBT model relies heavily on pre-trained word vectors, which may not always be available or effective for all languages and domains.
3. **Limited analysis of model components**: The authors do not provide a detailed analysis of the individual components of the NBT model, making it difficult to understand the contributions of each component to the overall performance.
4. **Lack of comparison to other state-of-the-art models**: The authors only compare the NBT model to a limited set of state-of-the-art models, which may not provide a comprehensive understanding of its performance relative to other approaches.
5. **Limited discussion of potential applications**: The authors do not provide a detailed discussion of potential applications of the NBT model, which may limit its impact and adoption.

**Questions to Authors**

1. How do the authors plan to address the limitation of the NBT model's dependence on pre-trained word vectors, particularly for languages and domains where such resources are not available?
2. Can the authors provide a more detailed analysis of the individual components of the NBT model, including the representation learning, semantic decoding, and context modeling submodules?
3. How do the authors plan to extend the NBT model to more complex dialogue domains, such as multi-domain dialogue systems or dialogue systems that require handling of complex morphological variation?
4. Can the authors provide a more comprehensive comparison of the NBT model to other state-of-the-art models, including those that use different approaches to SLU and DST?
5. What are the potential applications of the NBT model, and how do the authors plan to demonstrate its impact and adoption in real-world dialogue systems?
