**Summary**

The paper proposes a novel Neural Belief Tracking (NBT) framework for dialogue state tracking, which overcomes the limitations of current approaches by leveraging pre-trained word vectors to handle lexical and morphological variation. The NBT model couples Spoken Language Understanding (SLU) and Dialogue State Tracking (DST), eliminating the need for hand-crafted semantic lexicons. The authors evaluate the NBT model on two datasets, demonstrating its ability to match the performance of state-of-the-art models that rely on hand-crafted semantic lexicons and outperform them when such resources are not available.

**Main Contributions**

1. **Novel NBT framework**: The authors propose a new framework for dialogue state tracking that leverages pre-trained word vectors to handle lexical and morphological variation, eliminating the need for hand-crafted semantic lexicons.
2. **Coupling SLU and DST**: The NBT model couples SLU and DST, allowing it to reason about the user's intent and update the belief state in a more robust and accurate manner.
3. **Improved performance**: The authors demonstrate the effectiveness of the NBT model on two datasets, showing that it can match the performance of state-of-the-art models that rely on hand-crafted semantic lexicons and outperform them when such resources are not available.

**Strengths**

1. **Robust handling of lexical variation**: The NBT model's use of pre-trained word vectors allows it to handle lexical and morphological variation in a more robust and accurate manner than traditional approaches.
2. **Elimination of hand-crafted semantic lexicons**: The NBT model eliminates the need for hand-crafted semantic lexicons, making it more scalable and applicable to larger, more complex dialogue domains.
3. **Improved performance on noisy data**: The authors demonstrate that the NBT model can outperform traditional approaches on noisy data, such as the DSTC2 dataset, which contains ASR errors.

**Weaknesses**

1. **Dependence on pre-trained word vectors**: The NBT model's performance is dependent on the quality of the pre-trained word vectors used, which may not always be available or accurate for specific domains or languages.
2. **Limited evaluation**: The authors only evaluate the NBT model on two datasets, which may not be representative of all possible dialogue domains or scenarios.
3. **Lack of comparison to other neural models**: The authors only compare the NBT model to traditional approaches and do not evaluate its performance relative to other neural models, such as those using attention mechanisms or memory networks.

**Questions to Authors**

1. How do the authors plan to address the issue of pre-trained word vectors not being available or accurate for specific domains or languages?
2. Can the authors provide more details on the hyperparameter tuning process for the NBT model, including the choice of optimizer, learning rate, and number of hidden layers?
3. How do the authors plan to extend the NBT model to handle more complex dialogue scenarios, such as multi-domain dialogue systems or dialogue systems that require handling of complex morphological variation?
