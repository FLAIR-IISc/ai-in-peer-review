**Summary**

The paper proposes a novel Neural Belief Tracking (NBT) framework for dialogue state tracking, which overcomes the limitations of traditional approaches by leveraging pre-trained word vectors and learning to compose them into distributed representations of user utterances and dialogue context. The authors evaluate the NBT framework on two datasets and show that it matches the performance of state-of-the-art models that rely on hand-crafted semantic lexicons and outperforms them when such lexicons are not provided.

**Contributions**

1. **Novel NBT framework**: The authors propose a new framework for dialogue state tracking that uses pre-trained word vectors to improve language understanding capability.
2. **Efficient learning**: The NBT framework efficiently learns from available data by leveraging semantic information from pre-trained word vectors and maximizing the number of parameters shared across ontology values.
3. **Robust performance**: The authors demonstrate the robust performance of the NBT framework on two datasets, showing that it can handle semantic relations and linguistic variation without relying on hand-crafted semantic lexicons.

**Strengths**

1. **Improved language understanding**: The use of pre-trained word vectors enables the NBT framework to better understand user utterances and dialogue context, leading to improved performance on dialogue state tracking tasks.
2. **Efficient learning**: The NBT framework efficiently learns from available data, making it a promising approach for dialogue state tracking in real-world applications.
3. **Robust performance**: The authors demonstrate the robust performance of the NBT framework on two datasets, showing that it can handle semantic relations and linguistic variation without relying on hand-crafted semantic lexicons.

**Weaknesses**

1. **Dependence on pre-trained word vectors**: The NBT framework relies heavily on pre-trained word vectors, which may not always be available or of high quality for all languages and domains.
2. **Limited evaluation**: The authors only evaluate the NBT framework on two datasets, which may not be representative of all possible dialogue state tracking tasks and domains.
3. **Lack of comparison to other deep learning models**: The authors only compare the NBT framework to traditional delexicalisation-based models, but not to other deep learning models that may also be applicable to dialogue state tracking tasks.

**Questions to Authors**

1. How do the authors plan to extend the NBT framework to handle multi-domain dialogue systems and languages other than English?
2. Can the authors provide more details on the pre-trained word vectors used in the NBT framework and how they were trained?
3. How do the authors plan to address the potential limitations of the NBT framework, such as its dependence on pre-trained word vectors and limited evaluation on only two datasets?
