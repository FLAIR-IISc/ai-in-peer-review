**Summary of the Paper**

The paper proposes a novel Neural Belief Tracker (NBT) framework for dialogue state tracking, which overcomes the limitations of current approaches by leveraging pre-trained word vectors to handle lexical and morphological variation. The NBT model couples Spoken Language Understanding (SLU) and Dialogue State Tracking (DST) without relying on hand-crafted semantic lexicons. The authors evaluate the NBT model on two datasets, DSTC2 and WOZ 2.0, and demonstrate its ability to match the performance of state-of-the-art models that rely on hand-crafted semantic lexicons and outperform them when such lexicons are not available.

**Main Contributions**

1. **Novel NBT framework**: The paper proposes a new framework for dialogue state tracking that leverages pre-trained word vectors to handle lexical and morphological variation.
2. **Coupling SLU and DST**: The NBT model couples SLU and DST, which has been shown to improve belief tracking performance.
3. **Eliminating the need for hand-crafted semantic lexicons**: The NBT model does not rely on hand-crafted semantic lexicons, making it more scalable to larger and more complex dialogue domains.

**Strengths**

1. **Improved performance**: The NBT model demonstrates improved performance on two datasets, DSTC2 and WOZ 2.0, compared to baseline models.
2. **Robustness to lexical and morphological variation**: The NBT model's use of pre-trained word vectors allows it to handle lexical and morphological variation, making it more robust to different linguistic expressions.
3. **Scalability**: The NBT model's ability to eliminate the need for hand-crafted semantic lexicons makes it more scalable to larger and more complex dialogue domains.

**Weaknesses**

1. **Dependence on pre-trained word vectors**: The NBT model's performance relies heavily on the quality of the pre-trained word vectors used.
2. **Limited evaluation**: The paper only evaluates the NBT model on two datasets, which may not be representative of all possible dialogue domains.
3. **Lack of comparison to other state-of-the-art models**: The paper only compares the NBT model to a limited set of baseline models and does not evaluate its performance against other state-of-the-art models.

**Questions to Authors**

1. How do the authors plan to extend the NBT model to handle multi-domain dialogue systems?
2. Can the authors provide more details on the pre-training process for the word vectors used in the NBT model?
3. How do the authors plan to address the issue of out-of-vocabulary words in the NBT model?
