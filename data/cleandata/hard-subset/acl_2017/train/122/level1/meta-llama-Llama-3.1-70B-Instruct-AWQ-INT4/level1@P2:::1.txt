**Summary of the Paper and Main Contributions**

The paper proposes a novel Neural Belief Tracker (NBT) framework for dialogue state tracking, which overcomes the limitations of current approaches by leveraging pre-trained word vectors and learning to compose them into distributed representations of user utterances and dialogue context. The main contributions of this work are:

1. **A novel neural architecture for belief tracking**: The NBT framework couples Spoken Language Understanding (SLU) and Dialogue State Tracking (DST) without relying on hand-crafted semantic lexicons.
2. **Efficient learning from data**: The NBT models learn to handle variation without requiring large amounts of annotated training data or manually constructed semantic dictionaries.
3. **Improved performance on downstream tasks**: The evaluation demonstrates that the NBT models match the performance of state-of-the-art models that rely on hand-crafted semantic lexicons and outperform them when such lexicons are not provided.

**Strengths of the Paper**

1. **Novel approach to belief tracking**: The NBT framework offers a new perspective on belief tracking by leveraging pre-trained word vectors and learning to compose them into distributed representations of user utterances and dialogue context.
2. **Improved performance on downstream tasks**: The evaluation demonstrates that the NBT models achieve state-of-the-art performance on two datasets, surpassing the performance of delexicalisation-based models.
3. **Efficient learning from data**: The NBT models learn to handle variation without requiring large amounts of annotated training data or manually constructed semantic dictionaries.
4. **Flexibility and scalability**: The NBT framework can be applied to different dialogue domains and can handle complex linguistic variation.

**Weaknesses of the Paper**

1. **Limited evaluation**: The evaluation is limited to two datasets, and it would be beneficial to evaluate the NBT models on more datasets to demonstrate their generalizability.
2. **Comparison to other models**: The paper only compares the NBT models to delexicalisation-based models, and it would be beneficial to compare them to other state-of-the-art models, such as those using recurrent neural networks or transformer architectures.
3. **Analysis of the NBT models**: The paper could benefit from a more in-depth analysis of the NBT models, including an examination of the learned representations and the impact of different hyperparameters on performance.

**Questions to Authors**

1. How do the NBT models handle out-of-vocabulary words or words that are not present in the pre-trained word vector space?
2. Can the NBT models be applied to dialogue domains with complex morphological variation, such as languages with rich inflectional morphology?
3. How do the NBT models handle dialogue context that is longer than a single turn, such as conversations that span multiple turns?
