This paper proposes a Neural Belief Tracker (NBT) framework that aims to improve dialogue state tracking by leveraging pre-trained word vectors, thereby reducing the reliance on hand-crafted lexicons and large amounts of annotated data. The core idea is to couple SLU and DST, reasoning over word vector representations of utterances and dialogue context. The authors evaluate NBT on two datasets, demonstrating performance comparable to delexicalization-based models when lexicons are available and significant improvements when they are not.

The primary contribution of this work is the demonstration that pre-trained word vectors can be effectively utilized within a coupled SLU/DST framework for dialogue state tracking, achieving competitive performance without the need for manually crafted lexical resources. This is a significant step towards more scalable and adaptable belief tracking systems. A second, related contribution lies in the detailed exploration of different representation learning techniques (DNN and CNN) within the NBT framework and the analysis of the importance of semantically specialized word vectors (Paragram-SL999) versus general purpose vectors (GloVe, random initialization). The ablation study offers valuable insights into the effectiveness of different components. Finally, the clear articulation of the limitations of existing approaches (dependence on SLU, delexicalization) and positioning of NBT as a solution to those challenges provides a useful framing for the field.

**Strengths:**

1. **Strong Motivation and Problem Definition:** The paper clearly articulates the challenges of scaling dialogue state tracking to more complex domains due to the limitations of existing approaches – reliance on extensive annotation or hand-crafted lexicons. This provides a compelling motivation for the proposed NBT framework.
2. **Novelty and Technical Soundness:** The idea of directly incorporating pre-trained word vectors into a belief tracking model, rather than relying on intermediate SLU stages or delexicalization, is novel and technically sound. The design choices (DNN/CNN architectures, context modeling) are well-justified.
3. **Comprehensive Evaluation:** The experiments on two datasets (DSTC2, WOZ 2.0) provide a solid evaluation of the proposed approach. The comparison with strong baselines, including lexicon-augmented models, is crucial for demonstrating the benefits of NBT. The ablation study regarding the word vector type further strengthens the evaluation.
4. **Clear and Well-Written Presentation:** The paper is generally well-written and easy to follow. The architecture diagrams (Figures 2, 3, and 4) are helpful in understanding the model. The explanations of the key concepts and techniques are clear and concise.
5. **Reproducibility:** The authors state that the cleaned datasets and code will be made available, which is highly appreciated and contributes to the reproducibility of the results.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Error Analysis:** While the quantitative results are strong, a more detailed error analysis would be beneficial. Understanding the specific types of errors the NBT makes (e.g., misinterpreting specific slot values, failing to capture context) would provide valuable insights for future improvements.
2. **Context Modeling Simplification:** The context modeling component, which only considers the immediately preceding system act, might be overly simplistic. Exploring more sophisticated context representations (e.g., incorporating a longer dialogue history, attention mechanisms) could potentially further improve performance.
3. **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** The paper doesn’t explicitly discuss the sensitivity of the model's performance to hyperparameter choices. Addressing this aspect would strengthen the robustness of the findings. It would be helpful to briefly mention a hyperparameter search strategy.
4. **Lack of Comparison to End-to-End Models:** While the comparison to delexicalized models is relevant, a comparison to more recent end-to-end dialogue models (e.g., models that directly map utterances to belief states without explicit SLU) could provide further context.



**Questions to Authors:**

1. Could you provide more details about the error analysis conducted? Specifically, what types of errors does the NBT model frequently make?
2.  The context modeling approach considers only the last system turn. Have you experimented with incorporating more of the dialogue history, and if so, what were the results?
3.  How sensitive is the model performance to the choice of hyperparameters, such as the learning rate, embedding dimension, and hidden layer size?
4. How does NBT perform compared to more recent end-to-end dialogue models that directly predict the belief state from the user utterance?

**Additional Comments:**

*   The notation could be slightly improved for clarity (e.g., consistently using subscripts or superscripts to denote different slots/values).
*   A brief discussion of the computational cost of the NBT model compared to existing approaches would be valuable.
