This paper proposes a Neural Belief Tracker (NBT) framework that aims to improve dialogue state tracking by leveraging pre-trained word vectors to overcome limitations of traditional approaches relying on either large annotated datasets for SLU or hand-crafted lexicons. The core idea is to couple SLU and DST and learn to represent user utterances and dialogue context using distributed representations derived from pre-trained word vectors, avoiding exact matching and delexicalisation. The main contributions are: 1) A novel NBT framework that successfully utilizes pre-trained word vectors for improved language understanding in belief tracking; 2) Demonstration that NBT models achieve performance comparable to state-of-the-art delexicalisation-based models; and 3) Showing significant performance gains of NBT models when hand-crafted lexicons are unavailable.

**Strengths:**

1. **Addresses a Significant Problem:** The paper tackles a crucial challenge in dialogue state tracking: scaling to complex domains without requiring extensive annotated data or laborious lexicon creation. This is a practical and important issue for real-world deployment.
2. **Novel Approach:** The use of pre-trained word vectors to directly reason over semantic similarity, rather than relying on exact matching or delexicalisation, is a novel and promising approach. This allows the model to generalize to unseen variations in user language.
3. **Strong Empirical Results:** The experimental results on both DSTC2 and WOZ 2.0 datasets are compelling. The NBT models consistently match or outperform existing delexicalisation-based approaches, particularly when semantic lexicons are not provided.  The ablation study demonstrating the importance of semantically specialized word vectors (Paragram-SL999) is particularly insightful.
4. **Clear and Well-Structured Presentation:** The paper is well-written and logically organized. The architecture of the NBT model is clearly explained with a helpful diagram (Figure 2). The experimental setup and results are presented in a concise and understandable manner.
5. **Comprehensive Baselines:** The comparison to strong baseline models (Henderson et al. and Wen et al.) provides a solid benchmark for evaluating the NBT framework's effectiveness.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Analysis of Error Cases:** While the overall results are positive, the paper lacks detailed error analysis. Understanding *where* the NBT models still struggle (e.g., specific slot types, complex utterances) would provide valuable insights for future improvement.
2. **Context Modeling Simplification:** The context modeling component relies on only the *last* system utterance. While this is a common simplification, it may limit the model's ability to capture longer-range dependencies in the dialogue history. Exploring more sophisticated context modeling techniques (e.g., using recurrent networks over the entire dialogue history) could be beneficial.
3. **Hyperparameter Tuning Details:** The paper mentions a tuned value for lambda (0.55) but doesn't provide details about the hyperparameter tuning process. A more thorough description of the tuning methodology (e.g., search space, validation set) would enhance reproducibility.
4. **Computational Cost:** While the paper highlights the benefit of avoiding lexicon creation, it doesn’t discuss the computational cost of using the NBT model, particularly during training and inference. Providing some indication of the model's resource requirements would be helpful.
5. **Assumptions about Pre-trained Vectors:** The paper heavily relies on the quality of pre-trained word vectors. While Paragram-SL999 performs well, its performance might be sensitive to the domain or language. Investigating the robustness of the NBT framework to different types of pre-trained vectors would strengthen the findings.

**Questions to Authors:**

1. Could you provide a more detailed error analysis, identifying specific cases where the NBT models fail and potential reasons for these failures?
2. Have you experimented with more sophisticated context modeling techniques beyond the last system utterance? If so, what were the results?
3.  Can you provide more details about the hyperparameter tuning process, including the search space and validation set used?
4.  What is the computational cost (training and inference time) of the NBT models compared to the baseline models?
5.  How sensitive is the performance of the NBT framework to the choice of pre-trained word vectors, and have you evaluated its performance with other vector types?
