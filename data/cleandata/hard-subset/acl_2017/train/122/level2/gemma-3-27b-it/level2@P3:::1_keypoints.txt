- Introduces the Neural Belief Tracker (NBT) that couples SLU and DST by reasoning over pre-trained word vectors to remove the need for hand-crafted lexicons.
- NBT matches state-of-the-art when lexicons are available and outperforms them when lexicons are absent; evaluated on DSTC2 and WOZ 2.0.

- Addresses the critical problem of scaling belief tracking and reducing reliance on hand-crafted lexicons.
- Strong empirical results: consistently outperforms delexicalization-based baselines with statistically significant improvements, especially on WOZ 2.0.
- Ablation shows semantically specialized word vectors (Paragram-SL999) are important; provides insight on why GloVe underperforms.
- Clear, comprehensive presentation with effective diagrams and useful background context.

- Lacks detailed analysis of specific error cases and where the NBT fails compared to baselines.
- Context modeling is simplified to the previous system act, ignoring longer dialogue history and more advanced techniques.
- Insufficient details on hyperparameter tuning and sensitivity, which hurts reproducibility and robustness claims.
- No comparison to recent end-to-end dialogue models that map utterances directly to belief states.

- Please provide examples of consistent error cases and potential fixes.
- How was the weighting factor λ = 0.55 chosen; was it optimized on a development set and what were its characteristics?
- What is the computational cost of NBTs versus baselines for training time and inference speed?
- Were alternative context modeling techniques beyond the previous system act tried, and what were the results?

- Notation could be clearer (e.g., consistently define vector dimensionalities).
- Include a table summarizing key hyperparameters for each model variant.
- Release code and trained models to facilitate reproducibility.
