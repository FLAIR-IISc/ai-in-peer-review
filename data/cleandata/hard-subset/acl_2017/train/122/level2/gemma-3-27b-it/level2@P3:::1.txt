This paper introduces the Neural Belief Tracker (NBT), a framework for dialogue state tracking that aims to overcome the limitations of existing approaches reliant on large annotated datasets or hand-crafted lexicons. The core contribution is a method for coupling spoken language understanding and dialogue state tracking by reasoning over pre-trained word vectors, eliminating the need for explicit lexical matching. A secondary contribution is the demonstration that this approach matches state-of-the-art performance when lexicons are available and surpasses it when they are not. Finally, the work provides a thorough empirical evaluation on two datasets, DSTC2 and WOZ 2.0.

Strengths:

1. **Addressing a Significant Problem:** The paper tackles a critical challenge in dialogue systems: scaling belief tracking to more complex domains. The reliance on hand-crafted lexicons is indeed a significant bottleneck, and the NBT’s ability to mitigate this issue is a strong point. The paper clearly articulates this problem and positions the NBT as a viable solution.
2. **Strong Empirical Results:** The NBT models consistently outperform baseline delexicalization-based trackers, particularly on the WOZ 2.0 dataset where no lexicon is provided. The statistical significance of these improvements, as reported in Table 1, provides compelling evidence for the effectiveness of the proposed approach.  The comparison against a strong baseline (Henderson et al., 2014d and Wen et al., 2017) strengthens these findings.
3. **Demonstrated Benefit of Semantic Word Vectors:**  The ablation study in Table 2 convincingly shows the importance of utilizing semantically specialized word vectors (Paragram-SL999). This result highlights the value of incorporating semantic knowledge into the belief tracking process, which is a valuable insight for future research. The analysis of why GloVe vectors didn't show the same improvement is also insightful.
4. **Clear and Comprehensive Presentation:** The paper is well-structured and clearly explains the NBT architecture and its components. The diagrams (Figures 2, 3, and 4) effectively illustrate the flow of information within the models.  The background section provides sufficient context for understanding the problem and related work.

Weaknesses:

1. **Limited Analysis of Error Cases:** While the paper demonstrates strong overall performance, it lacks a detailed analysis of specific error cases. Understanding *where* the NBT models struggle (e.g., handling coreference, complex linguistic structures) would provide valuable insights for future improvements.  What types of utterances cause the NBT to fail, and how do those failures compare to the baseline?
2. **Context Modeling Simplification:** The context modeling approach, focusing solely on the previous system act, appears somewhat simplistic. Real-world dialogues often require considering a longer conversation history. While acknowledging the Markovian assumption, the paper doesn’t explore more sophisticated context modeling techniques, such as hierarchical recurrent encoders or attention mechanisms.
3. **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** The paper doesn't provide sufficient detail regarding hyperparameter tuning and sensitivity analysis. Were the hyperparameters optimized separately for each dataset? How sensitive is the performance to changes in key hyperparameters like learning rate, embedding dimension, or network architecture? Providing this information would increase the reproducibility and robustness of the results.
4. **Lack of Comparison with End-to-End Models:**  The paper compares against delexicalization-based models. A comparison with more recent end-to-end dialogue systems that directly map utterances to belief states (without explicit SLU and DST components) would provide a more comprehensive benchmark.

Questions to Authors:

1. Could the authors provide examples of error cases where the NBT models consistently fail, and discuss potential strategies for addressing these limitations?
2.  How did the authors determine the weighting factor (λ = 0.55) for combining turn-level and previous belief states? Was this value optimized on a development set, and if so, what was the size and characteristics of that set?
3. What is the computational cost associated with NBT models compared to the baseline models, especially regarding training time and inference speed? 
4. Have the authors experimented with different context modeling techniques beyond the single previous system act? If so, what were the results?

Additional Comments:

- The notation could be slightly improved for clarity (e.g., defining the dimensionality of vectors more consistently).
- It would be helpful to include a table summarizing the key hyperparameters used for each model variant.
- The authors should consider releasing the code and trained models to facilitate reproducibility and further research.
