## Review of "Neural Belief Tracker: Data-Driven Dialogue State Tracking"

This paper presents a novel Neural Belief Tracker (NBT) framework that aims to overcome the limitations of existing dialogue state tracking (DST) approaches, specifically their reliance on large annotated datasets for SLU or hand-crafted lexicons. The core idea is to leverage pre-trained word vectors to reason over user utterances and dialogue context, effectively learning semantic relationships without explicit linguistic resources. The authors claim that NBT models match the performance of state-of-the-art delexicalisation-based models and outperform them when lexicons are unavailable.

The primary contribution of this work is the demonstration of successful application of pre-trained word vector spaces to improve belief tracking performance. While the concept of using word embeddings isn’t novel, its successful integration into a coupled SLU/DST model, achieving competitive results *without* reliance on hand-crafted resources, is significant. A secondary contribution lies in the detailed architecture of the NBT model, with its context modelling component and careful design choices for interaction between utterance and candidate slot-value representations. The thorough experimental evaluation on two datasets (DSTC2 and WOZ 2.0) is also a valuable addition.

Strengths include the clear motivation and problem definition, the strong experimental results demonstrating performance parity with lexicon-based models, and the ablation study highlighting the importance of semantically specialized word vectors. The model's ability to generalize without explicit lexical knowledge is a key advantage for scalability. The paper is well-written and clearly explains the technical details.

However, the paper suffers from a few weaknesses. Firstly, the context modelling component, while conceptually sound, feels somewhat simplistic. The use of only the immediately preceding system act seems potentially limiting. A more comprehensive approach to dialogue history might yield further improvements. Secondly, the impact of different pre-trained word vector spaces, while demonstrated, could be explored in greater depth. A more rigorous comparison of different embedding types and training methodologies would be beneficial. Finally, while the paper acknowledges limitations, it lacks discussion on potential failure cases or biases inherent in the pre-trained word vectors.

Questions for Authors:

1.  Could you elaborate on the potential limitations of relying solely on the immediately preceding system act for context modelling? Have you explored more complex context representations?
2.  How sensitive is the model’s performance to the quality and domain-specificity of the pre-trained word vectors?
3.  What are some potential failure cases for the NBT model, and how might these be addressed in future work?
