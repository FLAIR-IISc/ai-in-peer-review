- Proposes Neural Belief Tracker (NBT) for dialogue state tracking that removes reliance on large SLU annotations or hand-crafted lexicons.  
- Core idea: leverage pre-trained word vectors to reason over user utterances and dialogue context, learning semantic relations without explicit linguistic resources.  
- Claim: NBT matches state-of-the-art delexicalisation models and outperforms them when lexicons are unavailable.  
- Primary contribution: demonstrates effective application of pre-trained embeddings within a coupled SLU/DST model to improve belief tracking.  
- Secondary contribution: detailed NBT architecture, including context modelling and interaction design between utterance and slot-value representations.  
- Provides thorough experimental evaluation on DSTC2 and WOZ 2.0 datasets.  
- Strength: clear motivation, strong experimental results, and ablation study showing importance of semantically specialized word vectors.  
- Strength: model generalizes without explicit lexical knowledge, improving scalability; paper is well-written and technically clear.  
- Weakness: context modelling is simplistic, using only the immediately preceding system act—may limit performance.  
- Weakness: limited exploration of the impact of different pre-trained word vector spaces; deeper comparison needed.  
- Weakness: lacks discussion of potential failure cases or biases inherent in pre-trained vectors.  
- Question: Could you elaborate on limitations of relying only on the immediately preceding system act and whether more complex context representations were explored?  
- Question: How sensitive is performance to the quality and domain-specificity of pre-trained word vectors?  
- Question: What are potential failure cases for NBT and how might they be addressed in future work?
