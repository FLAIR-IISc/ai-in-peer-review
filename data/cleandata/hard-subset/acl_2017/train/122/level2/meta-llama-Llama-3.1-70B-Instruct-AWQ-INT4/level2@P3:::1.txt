**Summary of the Paper**

This paper proposes a novel Neural Belief Tracker (NBT) framework for dialogue state tracking, which overcomes the limitations of current approaches by leveraging pre-trained word vectors and representation learning. The NBT model couples Spoken Language Understanding (SLU) and Dialogue State Tracking (DST), eliminating the need for hand-crafted semantic lexicons. The authors evaluate the NBT model on two datasets, DSTC2 and WOZ 2.0, and demonstrate its ability to match the performance of state-of-the-art models that rely on hand-crafted semantic lexicons and outperform them when such resources are not available.

**Main Contributions**

1. **Novel Neural Belief Tracker (NBT) framework**: The paper proposes a new framework that integrates SLU and DST, using pre-trained word vectors to handle lexical and morphological variation.
2. **Elimination of hand-crafted semantic lexicons**: The NBT model does not require hand-crafted semantic lexicons, making it more scalable and applicable to larger, more complex dialogue domains.
3. **Improved performance**: The authors demonstrate that the NBT model achieves state-of-the-art performance on two datasets, DSTC2 and WOZ 2.0, and outperforms baseline models that rely on hand-crafted semantic lexicons.

**Strengths**

1. **Innovative approach**: The paper proposes a novel approach to dialogue state tracking, leveraging pre-trained word vectors and representation learning to overcome the limitations of current approaches.
2. **Strong evaluation**: The authors evaluate the NBT model on two datasets, demonstrating its ability to achieve state-of-the-art performance and outperform baseline models.
3. **Improved scalability**: The NBT model eliminates the need for hand-crafted semantic lexicons, making it more scalable and applicable to larger, more complex dialogue domains.

**Weaknesses**

1. **Limited analysis of word vector spaces**: The paper primarily focuses on the use of semantically specialized Paragram-SL999 word vectors, but does not provide a comprehensive analysis of the impact of different word vector spaces on the NBT model's performance.
2. **Limited evaluation of ASR robustness**: The paper evaluates the NBT model on ASR N-best lists, but does not provide a comprehensive evaluation of its robustness to ASR errors.
3. **Limited discussion of future work**: The paper does not provide a detailed discussion of potential future work, such as applying the NBT model to multi-domain dialogue systems or languages other than English.

**Questions to Authors**

1. Can you provide a more comprehensive analysis of the impact of different word vector spaces on the NBT model's performance?
2. How does the NBT model handle ASR errors, and can you provide a more detailed evaluation of its robustness to ASR errors?
3. What are the potential applications of the NBT model to multi-domain dialogue systems, and how can it be adapted to handle complex morphological variation in languages other than English?
