**Summary of the Paper**

This paper proposes a novel Neural Belief Tracking (NBT) framework for dialogue state tracking, which overcomes the limitations of current approaches by leveraging pre-trained word vectors and representation learning. The NBT model couples Spoken Language Understanding (SLU) and Dialogue State Tracking (DST), eliminating the need for hand-crafted semantic lexicons. The authors evaluate the NBT model on two datasets, DSTC2 and WOZ 2.0, and show that it matches the performance of state-of-the-art models that rely on hand-crafted semantic lexicons and outperforms them when such lexicons are not available.

**Contributions**

1. **Novel Neural Belief Tracking Framework**: The paper proposes a new framework that combines SLU and DST, leveraging pre-trained word vectors and representation learning to overcome the limitations of current approaches.
2. **Improved Performance**: The NBT model achieves state-of-the-art performance on two datasets, DSTC2 and WOZ 2.0, without relying on hand-crafted semantic lexicons.
3. **Semantic Specialisation**: The paper demonstrates the importance of semantic specialisation in pre-trained word vectors for improving the performance of the NBT model.

**Strengths**

1. **Effective Use of Pre-trained Word Vectors**: The paper shows that pre-trained word vectors can be effectively used to improve the performance of the NBT model, especially when semantic specialisation is employed.
2. **Robustness to ASR Errors**: The NBT model demonstrates robustness to ASR errors, achieving high performance on the DSTC2 dataset, which contains noisy ASR output.
3. **Flexibility and Generalizability**: The paper demonstrates the flexibility and generalizability of the NBT model by evaluating it on two different datasets, DSTC2 and WOZ 2.0.

**Weaknesses**

1. **Dependence on High-Quality Pre-trained Word Vectors**: The performance of the NBT model relies heavily on the quality of the pre-trained word vectors, which may not always be available or may not capture the nuances of language.
2. **Limited Evaluation**: The paper evaluates the NBT model on only two datasets, which may not be representative of all possible dialogue state tracking tasks.
3. **Lack of Comparison to Other State-of-the-Art Models**: The paper does not compare the NBT model to other state-of-the-art models that may have been proposed since the publication of the baseline models used in the paper.

**Questions to Authors**

1. How do the authors plan to address the issue of dependence on high-quality pre-trained word vectors, especially in low-resource languages or domains?
2. Can the authors provide more details on the hyperparameter tuning process for the NBT model, especially for the semantic specialisation component?
3. How do the authors plan to extend the NBT model to handle more complex dialogue state tracking tasks, such as multi-domain dialogue systems or dialogue systems with complex morphological variation?
