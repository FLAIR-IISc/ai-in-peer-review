**Summary of the Paper**

This paper proposes a novel Neural Belief Tracking (NBT) framework for dialogue state tracking, which overcomes the limitations of current approaches by leveraging pre-trained word vectors and learning to compose them into distributed representations of user utterances and dialogue context. The NBT model couples Spoken Language Understanding (SLU) and Dialogue State Tracking (DST), efficiently learning to handle variation without requiring hand-crafted semantic lexicons. The authors evaluate the NBT model on two datasets, DSTC2 and WOZ 2.0, and demonstrate its ability to match the performance of state-of-the-art models that rely on hand-crafted semantic lexicons and outperform them when such resources are not provided.

**Main Contributions**

1. **Novel NBT Framework**: The authors propose a novel Neural Belief Tracking framework that leverages pre-trained word vectors and learns to compose them into distributed representations of user utterances and dialogue context.
2. **Efficient Learning**: The NBT model efficiently learns to handle variation without requiring hand-crafted semantic lexicons, making it more scalable to larger, more complex dialogue domains.
3. **State-of-the-Art Performance**: The authors demonstrate that the NBT model can match the performance of state-of-the-art models that rely on hand-crafted semantic lexicons and outperform them when such resources are not provided.

**Strengths**

1. **Effective Use of Pre-Trained Word Vectors**: The authors demonstrate the effectiveness of using pre-trained word vectors in the NBT model, which enables the model to handle semantic variation and produce high-quality intermediate representations.
2. **Scalability**: The NBT model is more scalable to larger, more complex dialogue domains, as it does not require hand-crafted semantic lexicons.
3. **State-of-the-Art Performance**: The authors demonstrate that the NBT model can achieve state-of-the-art performance on two datasets, DSTC2 and WOZ 2.0.

**Weaknesses**

1. **Dependence on Pre-Trained Word Vectors**: The NBT model relies heavily on pre-trained word vectors, which may not always be available or of high quality for certain languages or domains.
2. **Limited Robustness to ASR Errors**: The authors note that the NBT model's performance can be affected by ASR errors, which may limit its robustness in certain environments.
3. **Limited Evaluation**: The authors only evaluate the NBT model on two datasets, which may not be representative of all possible dialogue domains or scenarios.

**Questions to Authors**

1. How do the authors plan to address the dependence on pre-trained word vectors, and what alternatives can be used when such resources are not available?
2. Can the authors provide more details on how the NBT model can be adapted to handle ASR errors and improve its robustness in noisy environments?
3. How do the authors plan to extend the evaluation of the NBT model to other dialogue domains or scenarios, and what additional metrics or evaluation protocols can be used to assess its performance?
