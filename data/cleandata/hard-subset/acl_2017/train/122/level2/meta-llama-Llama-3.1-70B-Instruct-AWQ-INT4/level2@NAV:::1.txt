**Summary of the Paper**

This paper presents a novel neural belief tracking (NBT) framework for dialogue state tracking, which overcomes the limitations of traditional approaches by leveraging pre-trained word vectors to handle lexical and morphological variation. The NBT model couples Spoken Language Understanding (SLU) and Dialogue State Tracking (DST) without relying on hand-crafted semantic lexicons. The authors evaluate the NBT model on two datasets, DSTC2 and WOZ 2.0, and demonstrate that it matches the performance of state-of-the-art models that use hand-crafted semantic lexicons and outperforms them when such lexicons are not available.

**Main Contributions**

1. **Novel Neural Belief Tracking Framework**: The authors propose a new NBT framework that efficiently learns to handle variation in user language without requiring hand-crafted resources.
2. **Use of Pre-trained Word Vectors**: The NBT model leverages pre-trained word vectors to handle lexical and morphological variation, which is a significant improvement over traditional approaches.
3. **State-of-the-Art Performance**: The NBT model achieves state-of-the-art performance on two datasets, DSTC2 and WOZ 2.0, without relying on hand-crafted semantic lexicons.

**Strengths**

1. **Improved Handling of Variation**: The NBT model's use of pre-trained word vectors allows it to handle lexical and morphological variation more effectively than traditional approaches.
2. **State-of-the-Art Performance**: The NBT model's performance on two datasets demonstrates its ability to match or outperform state-of-the-art models that use hand-crafted semantic lexicons.
3. **Flexibility and Scalability**: The NBT model's ability to learn domain-specific paraphrasings and other kinds of variation makes it more flexible and scalable than traditional approaches.

**Weaknesses**

1. **Dependence on Pre-trained Word Vectors**: The NBT model's performance is dependent on the quality of the pre-trained word vectors used, which may not always be available or suitable for specific domains or tasks.
2. **Limited Evaluation**: The authors only evaluate the NBT model on two datasets, which may not be representative of all possible dialogue state tracking tasks or domains.
3. **Lack of Theoretical Analysis**: The authors do not provide a theoretical analysis of the NBT model's performance or its limitations, which may be useful for understanding its behavior and improving its design.

**Questions to Authors**

1. How do the authors plan to extend the NBT model to handle multi-domain dialogue systems or languages other than English?
2. Can the authors provide more details on the pre-trained word vectors used in the NBT model and how they were selected?
3. How do the authors plan to address the limitations of the NBT model, such as its dependence on pre-trained word vectors and limited evaluation?
