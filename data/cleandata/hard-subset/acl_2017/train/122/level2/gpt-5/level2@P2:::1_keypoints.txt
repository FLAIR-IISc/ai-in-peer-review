- Proposes the Neural Belief Tracker (NBT) family replacing delexicalisation and hand-crafted semantic dictionaries with learned representations from pre-trained word vectors.  
- Presents two NBT variants (NBT-DNN, NBT-CNN) that compose utterance vectors, use context gating with last system acts, and make per-slot/value binary decisions.  
- Evaluated on DSTC2 (ASR) and WOZ 2.0 (typed) with a rule-based temporal belief updater, showing consistent gains over delexicalised baselines.  
- Shows semantically specialised embeddings (Paragram-SL999) improve DST performance over random and GloVe embeddings.  
- Contribution: simple, effective SLU–DST coupling that avoids hand-crafted semantic dictionaries.  
- Contribution: empirical evidence that semantically specialised word vectors materially improve downstream DST (ablation: random/GloVe/Paragram).  
- Contribution: context-gated decision mechanism that uses last system acts to disambiguate short replies.  
- Strength: Clear motivation and practical significance in removing reliance on semantic dictionaries for scalability.  
- Strength: Solid empirical gains and statistical significance on DSTC2 and WOZ 2.0 for joint goal accuracy and request detection.  
- Strength: Thoughtful embedding analysis supporting the value of semantic specialisation and addressing distributional issues (e.g., antonymy).  
- Strength: Architecturally simple and efficient per-slot/value binary decisions with shared compositional parameters.  
- Strength: Context gating effectively handles confirm/request exchanges that lexical-overlap trackers struggle with.  
- Weakness: Experiments limited to a single domain with three informable slots, so scalability to larger/multi-domain ontologies is unverified.  
- Weakness: Context modelling limited to the immediately previous system act, risking missed cross-turn dependencies and lacking ablations on longer context.  
- Weakness: Training–test mismatch on DSTC2 (trained on transcriptions, evaluated on ASR) and no use of ASR confidences or lattices for robustness.  
- Weakness: Baselines may be unfairly weak—delexicalised baselines can’t use pre-trained embeddings, and automated-lexicon baselines (e.g., PPDB) were not tried.  
- Weakness: Incomplete analysis and missing ablations (error analysis, component contributions, fixed vs fine-tuned embeddings, OOV handling).  
- Question: How are OOV tokens handled at test time; are subword or character-level representations used?  
- Question: Were parameters shared across slots or slot-specific in experiments, and how does sharing affect performance?  
- Question: What is the marginal contribution of the context module; results with/without gating terms and with longer context?  
- Question: How sensitive are results to the belief-update λ; was λ learned or alternatives (trainable recurrent updater) tried?  
- Question: For DSTC2, was training on ASR N-best or using ASR confidences attempted to improve robustness?  
- Question: Can the authors report per-slot performance and an error breakdown (synonyms, antonyms, negation, multiword values)?  
- Suggestion: Compare to a stronger automated-lexicon baseline (e.g., PPDB or embedding-similarity expanded dictionaries).  
- Suggestion: Explore partial embedding fine-tuning, retrofitting/counter-fitted vectors, and subword embeddings for OOV and antonymy handling.  
- Suggestion: Evaluate on larger ontologies or multi-domain datasets and test generalisation to unseen slot values (zero/few-shot).  
- Suggestion: Provide more training details (regularisation, early stopping, parameter counts) and release code for reproducibility.  
- Minor: Fix formatting and citation/figure caption inconsistencies and correct typos.
