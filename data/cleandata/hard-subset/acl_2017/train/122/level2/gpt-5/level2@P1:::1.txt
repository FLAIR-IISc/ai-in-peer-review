Summary and contributions
This paper proposes the Neural Belief Tracker (NBT), a family of joint SLU/DST models that operate directly on pre-trained word embeddings rather than delexicalised features or hand-crafted semantic lexicons. Two instantiations (NBT-DNN, NBT-CNN) learn utterance and context representations and make binary decisions over candidate slot–value pairs, with simple context gating based on the last system act. Experiments on DSTC2 (ASR) and WOZ 2.0 (typed) show that NBT matches or outperforms strong delexicalisation-based baselines, especially when lexicons are unavailable, and that semantically specialised embeddings (Paragram-SL999) substantially boost performance.

Main contributions (as I see them)
1) A practical, lexicon-free belief tracking framework that leverages pre-trained, semantically specialised word vectors to capture paraphrastic variation and morphological/lexical diversity without manual dictionaries.
2) A simple, effective architecture combining semantic decoding (element-wise interaction between candidate and utterance representations) with lightweight context gating using the last system act, yielding competitive results across ASR and typed settings.
3) An empirical demonstration that semantically specialised embeddings materially improve downstream DST performance over random/GloVe, moving beyond intrinsic embedding evaluation.

Strengths
- Lexicon-free robustness to linguistic variation: The core idea—reasoning in a semantically specialised embedding space—directly addresses the scalability issue of hand-built semantic dictionaries. The model recognizes paraphrases like “inexpensive”→price=cheap without lexicons. Results on both datasets, and especially the ablation with Paragram vs GloVe/random, convincingly support this claim.

- Strong, fair empirical comparisons: The paper evaluates on two widely used, complementary datasets (ASR vs typed), compares to competitive delexicalisation-based models both with and without semantic dictionaries, and reports statistically significant gains on joint goals and requests. The request accuracy gains on WOZ 2.0 (91.2/91.6 vs 87.6) are substantial.

- Simple, modular architecture with clear modeling choices: The decomposition into representation learning, semantic decoding (r ⊗ c), and context gating (dr, dc) is easy to implement and reason about. The design choice to keep embeddings fixed and share parameters across values reduces data requirements and promotes generalisation to unseen lexical realisations.

- Insight into the role of embeddings: The embedding study isolates the impact of semantic specialisation, a useful takeaway for the community. The discussion around distributional relatedness vs similarity (e.g., antonyms) is well motivated and empirically backed.

- Practical belief state update over ASR N-best: The simple weighted combination with λ across turns and N-best hypotheses is transparent and reproducible, and yields solid performance under ASR noise.

Weaknesses
- Limited context modeling: The model assumes a Markovian context limited to the immediately preceding system acts and does not model longer dialogue history or user context (e.g., corrections, carryover, co-reference beyond the last turn). This may limit robustness in more complex dialogues, especially multi-turn coreference and revisions.

- Ontology dependence and scalability: The decision is made by iterating over all slot–value pairs, giving inference cost O(|S||V|) per turn. While manageable in the small Restaurant domain, this may become expensive with larger ontologies. Moreover, generalisation is to unseen wordings of known values, not to unseen values or slots.

- Incomplete ablations: While the embedding ablation is strong, there is no ablation teasing apart the contributions of semantic decoding vs context gating vs belief update λ. It would help to quantify how much each component contributes, and how sensitive performance is to keeping embeddings fixed vs (lightly) fine-tuning.

- Parameter sharing clarity: The text suggests both per-slot parameter sets (superscript s in representation learning) and the possibility of sharing across slots. This apparent inconsistency leaves unclear the degree of parameter tying in the reported models and its impact on data efficiency and scalability.

- Modest gains on DSTC2 goals vs lexicon baselines: On DSTC2, the improvement over a lexicon-enhanced baseline is small (73.4 vs 72.9 joint goals), though significant. The main gains seem to come from avoiding lexicon engineering rather than large absolute performance jumps; discussion of error types could clarify where headroom remains (e.g., ASR robustness).

Questions to authors
- Context and negation: How does the model explicitly disambiguate positive vs negative confirmations (e.g., “no, not Thai”)? Is there a dedicated feature or is it learned implicitly in the decision layer? Do you observe specific failure cases with negation/denial?

- Parameter sharing: In the reported experiments, are the representation and decoding parameters shared across slots, or is there a distinct parameter set per slot? Could you report performance and training stability under full sharing vs per-slot parameters?

- Scalability: What is the per-turn inference time as a function of |S||V|, and how would you adapt NBT for larger ontologies (e.g., candidate pruning, hierarchical value encoders, retrieval-first pipelines)?

- Embedding OOV and coverage: How often do you encounter OOV tokens with Paragram-SL999 in DSTC2/WOZ? Did you consider subword/character-level encoders (e.g., fastText) to mitigate OOVs?

- Belief update sensitivity: How sensitive are results to the λ hyperparameter and to the number/quality of ASR hypotheses? Did you try confusion networks rather than N-best lists?

Additional comments
- Reproducibility: Please clarify availability of code, the corrected DSTC2 transcripts, and the expanded WOZ 2.0 dataset. Reporting random seed variance would also help.

- Error analysis: A qualitative analysis of common errors (ASR-induced vs semantic vs context) would strengthen the paper and guide future work (e.g., handling of multi-constraint revisions).

- Future directions: Exploring lightweight fine-tuning of embeddings with similarity-preserving regularisation, plus subword models, may retain the benefits of semantic specialisation while adapting to domain-specific vocabulary.
