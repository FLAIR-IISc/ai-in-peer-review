Summary and contributions
This paper presents Neural Belief Tracker (NBT), a framework for dialogue state tracking that jointly performs SLU/DST without hand-crafted semantic lexicons. It operates over pre-trained, semantically specialized word vectors and learns to compose utterance, system-act context, and candidate slot–value representations to make binary decisions per slot–value at each turn. Two architectures are explored (NBT-DNN and NBT-CNN), and a simple rule-based belief update fuses ASR N-best lists with prior state. Experiments on DSTC2 (speech) and WOZ 2.0 (typed) show statistically significant gains over delexicalization-based baselines, with and without semantic dictionaries, and an ablation demonstrates the importance of semantically specialized embeddings.

As seen by this reviewer, the main contributions are:
1) A lexicon-free, embedding-driven SLU/DST framework that matches or exceeds delexicalization+dictionary systems (Table 1), demonstrating a path toward scalability beyond hand-crafted semantic resources.
2) An explicit, effective integration of dialogue context via gating with system Request/Confirm acts, improving handling of short confirmations and elliptical replies.
3) Empirical evidence that semantically specialized embeddings (Paragram-SL999) improve downstream DST, moving beyond intrinsic similarity evaluations (Table 2).

Strengths
- Strong empirical improvements with statistical significance on two benchmarks. On DSTC2, NBT-CNN improves joint goal accuracy to 73.4 versus 69.1 baseline and 72.9 with semantic dictionary; requests reach 96.5 versus 95.7. On WOZ 2.0, NBT-DNN reaches 84.4 joint goal vs 70.8 baseline and 83.7 with dictionary; requests improve to 91.6 vs 87.1–87.6. Significance (paired t-test, p < 0.05) is reported for key comparisons.
- Demonstrated robustness without hand-crafted lexicons. NBT matches or surpasses delexicalization systems even when those systems are given domain-specific dictionaries, and outperforms them when such lexicons are unavailable. This directly addresses scalability concerns raised for prior methods relying on manually curated rephrasings.
- Clear, modular architecture with principled context modeling. The gating mechanisms using the last system act (Equations 9–10) target common dialog phenomena (short “yes/no” confirmations and slot-specific follow-ups) and integrate seamlessly with the semantic decoder. This is a concrete, data-efficient alternative to end-to-end memory-heavy architectures.
- Useful analysis of representation quality. The ablation across random, GloVe, and Paragram-SL999 embeddings (Table 2) substantiates the claim that semantic specialization improves DST, particularly under ASR noise (DSTC2). This is a well-supported and broadly relevant finding.
- Practical considerations for noisy ASR and reproducibility. The simple N-best fusion with a tunable λ is transparent, and the paper states that cleaned DSTC2 transcriptions and the extended WOZ 2.0 dataset will be made available, aiding reproducibility.

Weaknesses
- Limited domain and ontology scale. Experiments are restricted to a single, small ontology (Cambridge restaurants with three informable slots). While parameter sharing and fixed embeddings are proposed as enablers of scalability, there is no empirical evidence on larger ontologies or multi-domain settings, nor any runtime/throughput analysis of iterating over all slot–value pairs per turn.
- Modest robustness under ASR noise relative to transcription. The paper notes that with gold transcriptions on DSTC2, goal accuracy rises to 0.96, suggesting that performance with realistic ASR remains constrained. Beyond N-best weighting and a single λ, there is no investigation of ASR-specific strategies (e.g., confusion networks, subword features) within NBT.
- Rule-based belief update and short-horizon context. The belief update is not learned and the context model is effectively Markovian with respect to the last system act. Longer-range dependencies (e.g., corrections, negations, or references spanning multiple turns) may be under-modeled; no ablation quantifies the contribution of the context gates vs. the semantic decoder.
- Baseline coverage and comparisons. While comparisons to Henderson et al. (2014) and Wen et al. (2017) are appropriate, there is no empirical comparison to recent memory/attention-based DST approaches (e.g., Perez and Liu, 2017) on the same datasets. This limits positioning against contemporary joint SLU/DST architectures available at the time.
- Limited analysis of failure modes and component contributions. Beyond the embedding choice, the paper lacks ablations for (a) context gating vs. no gating, (b) element-wise product vs. concatenation in the semantic decoder, and (c) slot-specific vs. shared parameters. A qualitative error analysis (e.g., negation, multi-valued slots, paraphrases outside embedding vocabularies) would clarify where NBT succeeds/fails.

Questions to authors (optional)
- How does NBT scale computationally with larger ontologies? Please provide per-turn inference cost and throughput for realistic numbers of slots/values.
- What is the class balance during training (positive vs. negative candidate pairs), and are any sampling or loss reweighting strategies used?
- Did sharing parameters across slots (rather than slot-specific matrices) degrade performance? Any results for partial sharing?
- How sensitive are results to λ and to the size/quality of N-best lists? Any experiments with confusion networks?
- How does NBT handle negation and corrections (e.g., “not Thai”, “actually, make it Chinese”) when not framed as a Confirm act by the system?
- What is the OOV coverage of Paragram-SL999 on DSTC2/WOZ vocabularies, and how are OOVs handled? Any benefit from subword features?

Additional comments
- The writing is clear and the architectural choices are well motivated. Including an ablation of the context module and a brief runtime/memory analysis would strengthen the paper materially.
- Releasing code in addition to the cleaned/extended datasets would further enhance impact.
- The contribution regarding semantically specialized embeddings influencing downstream DST is timely and valuable.
