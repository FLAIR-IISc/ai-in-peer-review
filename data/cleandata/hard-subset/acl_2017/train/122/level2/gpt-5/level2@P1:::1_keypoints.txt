- Proposes Neural Belief Tracker (NBT), a lexicon-free SLU/DST family using pre-trained word embeddings to score slot–value pairs with two instantiations (NBT-DNN, NBT-CNN) and simple context gating.

- Shows NBT matches or outperforms delexicalisation baselines on DSTC2 (ASR) and WOZ 2.0 (typed), with gains from semantically specialised embeddings (Paragram-SL999).

- Contribution: practical lexicon-free belief tracking leveraging semantically specialised vectors to handle paraphrase and morphological variation without manual dictionaries.

- Contribution: simple, modular architecture combining semantic decoding (element-wise candidate–utterance interaction) with lightweight last-act context gating.

- Contribution: empirical evidence that semantically specialised embeddings materially improve downstream DST performance over random/GloVe.

- Strength: robust to linguistic variation and paraphrase, demonstrated by experiments and embedding ablations.

- Strength: strong, fair empirical comparisons across ASR and typed datasets with statistically significant gains, notably request accuracy on WOZ 2.0.

- Strength: simple, interpretable modular design with fixed embeddings and parameter sharing that aids generalisation and implementation.

- Strength: insightful embedding study isolating the benefit of semantic specialisation and discussing distributional relatedness vs similarity.

- Strength: practical, reproducible belief state update over ASR N-best lists yielding solid ASR robustness.

- Weakness: limited context modeling—only immediate last system act considered, which may fail on longer histories, corrections, and coreference.

- Weakness: scalability concerns due to O(|S||V|) inference over slot–value pairs and limited generalisation to unseen values/slots.

- Weakness: incomplete ablations—no clear isolation of semantic decoding vs context gating vs belief update λ contributions or embedding fine-tuning sensitivity.

- Weakness: unclear parameter sharing: ambiguity about per-slot vs shared parameters and impact on data efficiency and scalability.

- Weakness: modest absolute gains on DSTC2 joint goals versus lexicon baselines; improvements mainly reduce lexicon engineering rather than large performance jumps.

- Question: how does the model handle negation/denial (e.g., “no, not Thai”); are such cases modeled explicitly or learned implicitly?

- Question: were representation and decoding parameters shared across slots or per-slot, and how does sharing affect performance and stability?

- Question: what is per-turn inference time as a function of |S||V|, and how could NBT be adapted for larger ontologies (pruning, hierarchical encoders, retrieval)?

- Question: what is OOV token frequency with Paragram-SL999 on DSTC2/WOZ, and were subword/character encoders (e.g., fastText) considered?

- Question: how sensitive are results to the λ belief-update hyperparameter and ASR N-best size/quality; were confusion networks tried?

- Additional: request clarifications on code availability, corrected DSTC2 transcripts, expanded WOZ dataset, and reporting of random-seed variance for reproducibility.

- Additional: recommend a qualitative error analysis separating ASR-induced, semantic, and context errors to guide improvements.

- Additional: suggest future work on lightweight embedding fine-tuning with similarity-preserving regularisation and subword models to retain specialisation while adapting to domain vocabulary.
