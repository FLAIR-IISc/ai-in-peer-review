Summary: The paper proposes Neural Belief Tracker (NBT), a joint SLU/DST framework that reasons directly over pre-trained word vectors rather than delexicalised features or hand-crafted semantic dictionaries. Two variants (NBT-DNN and NBT-CNN) compose utterance and context representations and interact with candidate slot-value representations via element-wise similarity and gated context mechanisms. Evaluations on DSTC2 (ASR) and WOZ 2.0 (typed) show that NBT matches lexicon-augmented delexicalisation-based trackers and outperforms them when no lexicons are provided; it also analyzes the impact of word vector quality, showing gains from semantically specialized embeddings.

Contributions (as I see them, strongest first):
- A practical joint SLU/DST architecture that eliminates reliance on semantic dictionaries by operating over fixed, semantically specialized word embeddings.
- A lightweight, effective context gating mechanism that leverages the last system act to disambiguate short user replies (request/confirm).
- An instructive analysis demonstrating that semantic specialization of word vectors (Paragram-SL999) significantly improves downstream DST performance.

Strengths:
- Practical impact: Removing hand-crafted lexicons directly addresses scalability and portability to larger domains; results on two datasets substantiate this claim.
- Solid empirical support: Statistically significant gains over delexicalised baselines on goals/requests; comparable to strong lexicon-equipped models, especially on WOZ 2.0, indicating improved handling of paraphrases.
- Clear, modular design: The representation learning, semantic decoding, and context gating modules are well-motivated and simple to implement; hyperparameters are mostly specified.
- Insightful analysis of embeddings: The comparison of random, GloVe, and Paragram vectors ties improvements to semantic similarity rather than mere distributional relatedness.

Weaknesses:
- Limited domain and context: Single-domain (restaurants) and a Markovian assumption using only the last system act; unclear generalization to multi-domain, longer-context dialogs.
- Belief update is rule-based: The tracker’s probabilistic turn-level predictions are combined with a hand-tuned λ; no learning-to-update or calibration analysis.
- Reproducibility gaps: Some training details (batch sizes, learning rates/schedules, early stopping, negative/positive sampling ratios) and computational costs are missing; code/data cleaning release is only promised.
- Embedding dependence: Fixing embeddings may hinder adaptation to domain-specific lexicon or new slots; no ablation on fine-tuning or OOV handling beyond pretrained coverage.

Questions to Authors:
- How sensitive is performance to λ in the belief update, and have you tried learning it or replacing the rule with a learned updater?
- Did you evaluate using longer dialogue context (e.g., multiple previous system/user turns) or alternative gating functions?
- What is the effect of fine-tuning the embeddings vs. keeping them fixed, particularly for rare values?
- How do errors break down across confirm vs. request acts and across slots/values (e.g., antonyms, negation)?
- Can you report training/inference time and parameter counts for NBT-DNN vs. NBT-CNN and release the DSTC2 “cleaned” transcriptions?
