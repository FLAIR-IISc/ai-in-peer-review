

Summary: This paper proposes Neural Belief Tracker (NBT), a novel framework for dialogue state tracking that leverages pre-trained word vectors to handle lexical variation without requiring hand-crafted semantic lexicons. The main contributions are: 1) a unified SLU-DST model that avoids dependency on domain-specific lexicons; 2) two model variants (NBT-DNN and NBT-CNN) that learn to compose word vectors into distributed representations; and 3) empirical demonstration that NBT matches lexicon-based models' performance while significantly outperforming them when lexicons are unavailable.

Strengths:
1. The paper effectively addresses a significant practical limitation in current dialogue systems: the scalability problem caused by dependency on hand-crafted semantic lexicons. The authors convincingly demonstrate that NBT achieves comparable performance to lexicon-based approaches without requiring these expensive resources, which is a major practical contribution.

2. The experimental evaluation on two datasets (DSTC2 and WOZ 2.0) is thorough and well-designed. The results show statistically significant improvements over baseline models, particularly on the WOZ 2.0 dataset with richer language. The authors also provide insightful analysis of how different word vector spaces impact performance, demonstrating that semantically specialized vectors (Paragram-SL999) significantly outperform generic GloVe and random initialization.

3. The NBT architecture is relatively simple and elegant, avoiding the complexity of many recent neural dialogue systems. By using pre-trained vectors and sharing parameters across slots, the approach minimizes the need for extensive domain-specific training data, which is a practical advantage for real-world deployment.

4. The paper makes a valuable contribution to understanding the relationship between word vector quality and downstream task performance. The demonstration that semantic specialisation improves belief tracking performance (rather than just using generic distributional vectors) is an important insight for the field.

Weaknesses:
1. The paper lacks sufficient comparison to more recent neural dialogue state tracking approaches published around the same time (2016-2017). For example, the authors cite Mrkšić et al. (2015, 2016) but don't compare against their later work (e.g., "Multidomain Dialog State Tracking using Recurrent Neural Networks" from ACL 2015) or other contemporary neural DST models that might have addressed similar challenges.

2. The evaluation is limited to a single domain (restaurant search) with a small number of slots (3 informable slots). The paper claims to address "larger, more complex dialogue domains," but provides no evidence of scalability beyond this simple domain. The authors should demonstrate how their approach would handle more complex domains with many more slots and values.

3. The paper doesn't adequately explain why the NBT approach works better than lexicon-based methods. While the results show superior performance, the authors don't provide sufficient analysis of which specific linguistic phenomena the NBT model handles better. For instance, they don't show examples where NBT correctly identifies slot values that lexicon-based models miss due to lack of lexical coverage.

4. The paper doesn't address critical challenges for real-world deployment: handling out-of-vocabulary words, adapting to new slot values without retraining, and performance degradation with noisy ASR. The authors mention ASR robustness briefly but don't provide any meaningful analysis of how NBT handles imperfect speech recognition.

5. The paper claims novelty by stating "to the best of our knowledge, NBT models are the first to successfully use pre-trained word vector spaces to improve the language understanding capability of belief tracking models." However, Rocktäschel et al. (2016) already used word vectors for semantic matching in dialogue state tracking, and the authors don't properly contextualize their work relative to this prior research.

Questions to Authors:
1. How would your approach scale to domains with 50+ slots and thousands of possible slot values, which are common in real-world applications?
2. Could you provide specific examples where NBT correctly identified slot values that lexicon-based models missed due to lack of lexical coverage?
3. How does your model handle slot values that weren't present in the training data (out-of-vocabulary values), and what is the impact on performance?
