- Proposes Neural Belief Tracker (NBT) using pre-trained word vectors to handle lexical variation without hand-crafted semantic lexicons.  
- Claims NBT matches lexicon-based models when lexicons exist and outperforms them otherwise.  
- Shows performance depends strongly on quality of underlying word vectors (Paragram-SL999 > GloVe > random).  
- Addresses scalability issue of lexicon dependency in dialogue state tracking.  
- Conceptually simple and leverages distributed representation advances.  
- Comprehensive evaluation on DSTC2 and WOZ 2.0 with statistically significant improvements in joint goal and request accuracy.  
- Demonstrates strong performance on WOZ 2.0, with up to 13.5% goal and 4.5% request improvements over baselines.  
- Lacks sufficient technical detail about the NBT architecture, especially context modeling.  
- Unclear how dialogue history beyond the last system act is incorporated and how it scales to longer dialogues.  
- Claim of being first to use pre-trained word vectors for belief tracking is insufficiently justified relative to related work.  
- Evaluation limited to single domain (restaurant) with only three informable slots, raising generalizability concerns.  
- Omits discussion of computational requirements and training data efficiency compared to baselines.  
- Questions about handling slot-values with no semantic relation to utterances (e.g., VIEW) and limitations in larger multi-slot domains.  
- Asks for comparisons to end-to-end systems that jointly learn state tracking and policies.  
- Recommends clearer relation to prior neural dialogue work, stronger literature justification, deployment discussions, and error analysis of word-vector benefits.
