- Contributions: proposes Neural Belief Tracker (NBT) that uses pre-trained word vectors to avoid hand-crafted semantic lexicons.  
- Contributions: presents two variants (NBT-DNN, NBT-CNN) that compose word vectors into distributed representations.  
- Contributions: empirically shows NBT matches lexicon-based performance and outperforms them when lexicons are unavailable.  

- Strength: addresses scalability by removing dependence on expensive hand-crafted lexicons, with convincing comparable performance.  
- Strength: thorough experiments on DSTC2 and WOZ 2.0, showing significant improvements and the benefit of semantically specialized vectors (Paragram-SL999).  
- Strength: simple, elegant architecture that shares parameters across slots and reduces need for domain-specific training data.  
- Strength: provides valuable insight that word vector semantic specialization improves downstream belief tracking performance.  

- Weakness: insufficient comparison to more recent/contemporary neural DST methods from 2015–2017.  
- Weakness: evaluation limited to a single (restaurant) domain with only three informable slots, so scalability claims are unsupported.  
- Weakness: lacks analysis explaining why NBT outperforms lexicon-based methods and missing concrete example cases.  
- Weakness: does not address real-world deployment challenges—out-of-vocabulary handling, adding new slot values without retraining, and robustness to noisy ASR.  
- Weakness: novelty claim is questionable given prior work (e.g., Rocktäschel et al. 2016) using word vectors for semantic matching.  

- Question: how does the approach scale to domains with 50+ slots and thousands of slot values?  
- Question: can the authors provide concrete examples where NBT finds slot values lexicon-based models miss?  
- Question: how does the model handle unseen/out-of-vocabulary slot values and what is the performance impact?
