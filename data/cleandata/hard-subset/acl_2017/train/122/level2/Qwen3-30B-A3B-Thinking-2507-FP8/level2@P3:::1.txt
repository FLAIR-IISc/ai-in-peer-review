

Summary: This paper proposes a Neural Belief Tracker (NBT) framework for dialogue state tracking that leverages pre-trained word vectors to handle lexical variation without requiring hand-crafted semantic lexicons. The main contributions are: 1) a novel approach to dialogue state tracking that uses distributed representations of user utterances and context; 2) demonstration that this approach matches lexicon-based models' performance when lexicons are available and outperforms them when they are not; 3) evidence that the quality of underlying word vectors significantly impacts performance.

Strengths: The work addresses a significant limitation in current dialogue state tracking systems, namely the dependency on hand-crafted semantic lexicons that hinders scalability. The approach is conceptually simple yet effective, leveraging advances in distributed representation learning. The evaluation is comprehensive, comparing against appropriate baselines on two distinct datasets (DSTC2 and WOZ 2.0) with statistically significant improvements shown for both joint goal and request accuracy metrics. The paper provides valuable insights into the importance of using semantically specialized word vectors, demonstrating that Paragram-SL999 vectors significantly outperform both GloVe and randomly initialized vectors, particularly on noisy data. The results on WOZ 2.0 show the model's ability to handle richer language without explicit lexicons, with improvements of up to 13.5% over baselines for goals and 4.5% for requests.

Weaknesses: The paper lacks sufficient technical detail about the NBT architecture, particularly the context modeling component. The description of how the model incorporates dialogue history (beyond the last system act) is superficial, and it's unclear how this would scale to longer dialogues. The claim that NBT is "the first to successfully use pre-trained word vector spaces to improve language understanding capability of belief tracking models" requires stronger justification; the paper doesn't sufficiently distinguish this work from related approaches like those in Rocktäschel et al. (2016) or Mrkšić et al. (2015). The evaluation is limited to a single domain (restaurant search) with only three informable slots, raising questions about generalizability to more complex domains with many more slots and values. The paper doesn't address computational requirements or training data efficiency compared to baselines, which is important for practical deployment.

Questions to Authors: How does the model handle slot-value pairs that have no semantic relationship to any words in the utterance (e.g., "I want a restaurant with a view" for a slot like "VIEW")? What are the specific limitations of the current NBT framework when applied to domains with many more slots and values than the restaurant domain? How does the NBT approach compare to recent end-to-end dialogue systems that jointly optimize dialogue state tracking and policy learning?

Additional Comments: The paper could benefit from a clearer explanation of the relationship between the NBT model and existing work on neural dialogue state tracking. The claim about being the first to use pre-trained word vectors for this task requires stronger evidence from the literature review. The paper would be strengthened by a more detailed discussion of the practical deployment challenges, including computational requirements and how the model handles rare slot-value pairs. The evaluation of the word vector spaces (Table 2) is valuable but would be more compelling with additional analysis of specific error cases where semantically specialized vectors provided advantages.
