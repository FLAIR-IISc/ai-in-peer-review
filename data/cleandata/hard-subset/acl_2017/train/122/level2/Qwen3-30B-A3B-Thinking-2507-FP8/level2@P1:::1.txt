

Summary: This paper proposes a Neural Belief Tracker (NBT) framework that addresses a key limitation in current dialogue state tracking systems: their reliance on hand-crafted semantic lexicons for handling lexical variation. The authors introduce two variants (NBT-DNN and NBT-CNN) that leverage pre-trained word vectors to compose distributed representations of user utterances and dialogue context, eliminating the need for manual lexicon construction. The main contributions are: 1) a novel data-driven approach to belief tracking that avoids hand-crafted resources; 2) demonstration that semantic word vectors effectively handle lexical variation; and 3) empirical evidence showing NBT matches or outperforms lexicon-based methods, especially when lexicons are unavailable.

Strengths:
1. The paper tackles a significant practical limitation in dialogue systems—scalability to complex domains due to lexicon dependency—which has been a persistent challenge in the field. The authors provide a compelling argument for why this limitation matters for real-world deployment.

2. The evaluation is well-designed and thorough, comparing NBT against appropriate baselines on two distinct datasets (DSTC2 and WOZ 2.0) with consistent, statistically significant improvements. The results show NBT-CNN achieving 73.4% goal accuracy on DSTC2 versus 72.9% for lexicon-enhanced baselines, with particularly strong performance on WOZ 2.0 (84.2% vs. 83.7%).

3. The ablation study on different word vector types (Table 2) provides strong evidence that semantic quality matters, showing Paragram-SL999 vectors significantly outperform GloVe and random initialization. This insight extends beyond dialogue state tracking to the broader NLP community.

4. The paper makes a clear contribution to understanding how to leverage pre-trained semantic representations in downstream tasks, moving beyond purely intrinsic evaluation to demonstrate practical benefits.

Weaknesses:
1. The paper lacks sufficient detail on how the model handles out-of-vocabulary words that are semantically unrelated to known slot values. While the authors mention that pre-trained vectors help with semantically related unseen words, they don't provide evidence or analysis of this capability in the paper.

2. The evaluation is limited to a single domain (restaurant search), making it difficult to assess the generalizability of the approach. It would be valuable to see results on more complex domains with larger ontologies to better understand scalability limits.

3. The computational cost of the NBT model, particularly the NBT-CNN variant with 300 convolutional filters, isn't addressed. For real-world deployment, understanding inference time and memory requirements relative to baseline methods would be crucial.

4. The paper doesn't discuss how the model would scale to very large ontologies (hundreds of slots with thousands of values), which is a key concern for practical deployment in complex dialogue systems.

Questions to Authors:
1. Could you provide more details on how the model handles out-of-vocabulary words that are semantically unrelated to known slot values, and whether there's evidence this is a significant limitation in practice?

2. Have you considered testing the model on a domain with a larger ontology to assess scalability beyond the restaurant domain used in the paper?

3. How would the computational requirements of the NBT model compare to traditional lexicon-based approaches, particularly for real-time dialogue systems?

Overall, this is a solid contribution that addresses a meaningful problem in dialogue systems. The approach is novel, well-evaluated, and has clear practical implications. The paper would be strengthened by addressing the limitations mentioned above, particularly regarding out-of-vocabulary handling, generalizability to other domains, and computational considerations. With these clarifications, this work could have significant impact on the field.
