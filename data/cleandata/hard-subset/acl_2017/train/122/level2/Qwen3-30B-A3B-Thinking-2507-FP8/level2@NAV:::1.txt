

Summary: The paper proposes Neural Belief Tracker (NBT), a framework for dialogue state tracking that eliminates the need for hand-crafted semantic lexicons by leveraging pre-trained word vectors. The main contributions are: (1) a novel approach to belief tracking using distributed representations of user utterances and dialogue context; (2) demonstrated performance matching state-of-the-art lexicon-based models and outperforming them when lexicons are unavailable; (3) analysis showing the critical importance of semantically-specialized word vectors for this task.

Strengths: The paper addresses a significant practical limitation in dialogue systems - the infeasibility of creating domain-specific semantic lexicons for large-scale applications. The evaluation on two datasets (DSTC2 and WOZ 2.0) provides strong empirical support for the claimed advantages, with statistically significant improvements (p<0.05) over baseline models. The ablation study on different word vector types (Paragram-SL999, GloVe, random initialization) convincingly demonstrates the importance of semantic content in word vectors for this task.

Weaknesses: The paper lacks sufficient discussion of scalability to large ontologies with thousands of slot-value pairs, which is a critical concern for real-world deployment. The ASR robustness issue is underdeveloped - the performance gap between clean text (WOZ 2.0) and ASR outputs (DSTC2) is substantial (91.6% vs. 73.4% goal accuracy), yet the paper only briefly mentions this as a limitation without proposing concrete solutions. The comparison to recent neural DST models using attention mechanisms or transformers is incomplete, which weakens the novelty claim.

The paper is well-written and makes a valuable contribution to the field by demonstrating a practical approach to scaling belief tracking. However, the authors should address the scalability concerns and provide more thorough analysis of ASR robustness. The work shows promise for deployment in real-world dialogue systems but requires further refinement to address these limitations before practical adoption.
