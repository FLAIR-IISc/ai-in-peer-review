## Review of "Neural Belief Tracker: Data-Driven Dialogue State Tracking"

This paper presents a novel Neural Belief Tracker (NBT) framework designed to overcome limitations of existing dialogue state tracking (DST) approaches, specifically their reliance on large annotated datasets for SLU or hand-crafted lexicons. The core idea is to leverage pre-trained word vectors to reason over user utterances and dialogue context, effectively learning semantic relationships without explicit lexical engineering. The authors claim that NBT matches the performance of state-of-the-art delexicalization-based models and outperforms them when lexicons are unavailable.

The primary contribution of this work is the demonstration that pre-trained word vectors can be effectively utilized within a belief tracking framework, achieving competitive performance without the need for hand-crafted resources. This is a significant step towards more scalable and adaptable DST systems. A secondary contribution lies in the exploration of different representation learning components (DNN and CNN) within the NBT architecture, providing insights into the benefits of convolutional approaches for capturing utterance context. Finally, the thorough experimental evaluation on two datasets (DSTC2 and WOZ 2.0) provides strong empirical support for the proposed approach.

The strengths of this paper are numerous. The problem addressed is important and well-motivated. The proposed approach is clearly explained and the architecture is well-illustrated. The experimental setup is comprehensive, including comparisons to strong baselines and ablation studies examining the impact of different word vector types. The results consistently demonstrate the effectiveness of NBT, particularly in low-resource scenarios. The authors also acknowledge the limitations of their approach, such as the reliance on high-quality pre-trained word vectors and the need for further work on ASR robustness.

However, the paper has some weaknesses. The context modelling component, while described, feels somewhat underdeveloped and lacks detailed justification for the chosen similarity metrics. The ablation study focusing on word vectors is insightful, but a more detailed analysis of *why* Paragram-SL999 vectors perform best would be valuable. Additionally, the belief state update mechanism is described as a simple rule-based approach, and exploring more sophisticated update strategies could potentially further improve performance.

**Questions to Authors:**

1.  Could you elaborate on the rationale behind using element-wise multiplication (⊗) in the context modelling stage, as opposed to a dot product?
2.  The paper highlights the importance of semantic specialization in word vectors. Could you discuss potential strategies for adapting or fine-tuning these vectors specifically for the DST task?
3.  What are the computational costs associated with the NBT models (DNN vs. CNN) compared to the baseline delexicalization-based models?
