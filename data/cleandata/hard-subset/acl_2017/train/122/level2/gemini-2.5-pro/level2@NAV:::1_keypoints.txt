Presents the Neural Belief Tracker (NBT): uses pre-trained word embeddings to relate user utterances to slot-value pairs, removing hand-crafted semantic dictionaries.

Contribution: Data-driven neural architecture replaces delexicalisation, handling linguistic variation without manual feature engineering.

Contribution: Empirical evaluation on DSTC2 and WOZ 2.0 shows NBT matches lexicon-dependent models and outperforms them when lexicons are unavailable.

Strength: High practical impact — removes need for domain-specific curated lexicons, improving scalability to new domains.

Strength: Convincing experimental validation — thorough tests on noisy and clean datasets, direct baselines comparison, and significance testing.

Strength: Well-motivated architecture — intuitive composition of utterance, context, and candidate representations; insightful analysis of word vector types.

Weakness: Simplified context modeling — only uses the last system turn, which may fail with long-range dialogue dependencies.

Weakness: Heuristic belief update — uses a rule-based interpolation with a single hyperparameter instead of a learned update mechanism.

Weakness: Scalability concerns — iterates over every slot-value pair, which could be computationally prohibitive for large ontologies and is not addressed.
