- Summary: Proposes the Neural Belief Tracker (NBT) that uses pre-trained word embeddings to perform dialogue state tracking without hand-crafted semantic dictionaries, evaluated on DSTC2 and WOZ 2.0 with strong results.

- Contribution: A data-driven neural DST architecture that reasons directly over word embeddings to eliminate the need for manual semantic dictionaries.

- Contribution: Strong empirical results showing NBT outperforms delexicalisation baselines without dictionaries and matches them when dictionaries are used.

- Contribution: Analysis demonstrating semantically specialized embeddings (Paragram-SL999) yield significant gains over GloVe and random vectors.

- Strength: High practical impact and improved scalability by removing costly, error-prone hand-crafted lexicons.

- Strength: Thorough and convincing empirical evaluation across two diverse datasets with appropriate baselines and significance testing.

- Strength: Well-motivated model architecture that cleanly separates utterance representation, semantic decoding, and context modeling, with two reasonable variants (NBT-DNN, NBT-CNN).

- Strength: Insightful ablation on embedding choice showing embedding quality directly affects downstream DST performance.

- Weakness: Scalability to large ontologies is untested; iterating over all slot-value pairs may be infeasible for domains with hundreds/thousands of values.

- Weakness: Limited context modeling that only uses the last system turn, ignoring longer dialogue history and long-range dependencies.

- Weakness: Architectural novelty is limited since components are standard neural building blocks composed for this task.

- Question: How will inference over all slot-value pairs scale to domains with very large value sets; have candidate retrieval or embedding nearest-neighbor strategies been considered?

- Question: Why was context limited to the last system turn instead of a recurrent turn-by-turn history model, and would richer context modeling improve performance?
