- Proposes the Neural Belief Tracker (NBT), a neural DST that classifies each slot-value pair using pre-trained word embeddings to replace hand-crafted lexicons.
- Contribution: introduces an end-to-end neural architecture operating on embeddings that removes brittle delexicalisation pipelines.
- Contribution: provides strong empirical evidence on DSTC2 and WOZ 2.0 that a data-driven NBT matches lexicon-augmented baselines and outperforms non-augmented ones.
- Contribution: analyzes the impact of embedding quality, showing semantically specialized vectors yield significant downstream gains.
- Strength: excellent motivation and high impact potential by addressing reliance on manual resources for task-oriented dialogue.
- Strength: thorough, convincing evaluation across datasets with fair comparisons to strong baselines.
- Strength: insightful representation analysis (random vs. GloVe vs. Paragram-SL999) that explains why the model works.
- Strength: clear writing, good presentation, helpful diagrams, and release of cleaned DSTC2 and WOZ 2.0 data.
- Weakness: inference requires iterating over all ontology slot-value pairs, raising scalability concerns for large/open value sets.
- Weakness: cross-turn belief update is a simple, non-learned linear interpolation rather than a learnable state-evolution model.
- Weakness: context modeling is limited to the immediately preceding system act and cannot handle long-range cross-turn references.
- Question: Have you considered alternatives to avoid iterating over all values, e.g., predicting a value embedding or framing as slot-tagging?
- Question: Can you provide more intuition for using dot-product multiplicative gates for context (especially the three-way dc term), and did you try concatenation or other context integrations?
- Question: Why do NBT-DNN and NBT-CNN perform similarly—any qualitative differences on longer or syntactically complex utterances?
