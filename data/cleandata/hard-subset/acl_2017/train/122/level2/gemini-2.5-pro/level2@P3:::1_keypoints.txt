- Summary: Proposes Neural Belief Tracker (NBT) for DST that uses pre-trained word embeddings to avoid supervised SLU or hand-crafted lexicons, with NBT-DNN and NBT-CNN variants evaluated on DSTC2 and WOZ 2.0.  
- Summary: NBT matches state-of-the-art when dictionaries are available and outperforms baselines when they are not.

- Contribution: Introduces a fully data-driven DST framework that replaces delexicalization using semantic information in pre-trained word vectors.  
- Contribution: Provides strong empirical validation on ASR-noisy (DSTC2) and cleaner (WOZ 2.0) datasets, comparing baselines with and without semantic dictionaries.  
- Contribution: Shows that pre-trained word vector quality (random vs. GloVe vs. Paragram-SL999) materially affects DST performance.

- Strength: Well-motivated, principled approach addressing scalability and fragility of hand-crafted resources by reasoning over general-purpose embeddings.  
- Strength: Convincing experimental design using two complementary datasets and fair comparisons isolating the benefit of the approach.  
- Strength: Clear writing, good diagrams, and commitment to release curated datasets to aid reproducibility.

- Weakness: Limited context modeling—Markovian context uses only the previous system turn and may miss longer-range dependencies.  
- Weakness: Scalability concerns—binary classification over all slot-value pairs could be computationally expensive for large ontologies or high-cardinality slots.  
- Weakness: Lacks qualitative error analysis to characterize remaining failure modes (e.g., negation, compositionality, OOVs).

- Question: Could the belief-state interpolation weight λ be made adaptive or conditioned on model confidence rather than fixed?  
- Question: Is there empirical or qualitative evidence that NBT-CNN handles longer utterances better than NBT-DNN, given their similar performance on the datasets?
