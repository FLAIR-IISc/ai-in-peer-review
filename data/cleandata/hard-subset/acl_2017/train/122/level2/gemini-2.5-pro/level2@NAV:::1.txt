**Summary and Contributions**
This paper presents the Neural Belief Tracker (NBT), a novel framework for dialogue state tracking that aims to improve scalability by removing the dependence on hand-crafted semantic dictionaries. The core idea is to leverage pre-trained word embeddings to directly model the semantic relationship between a user's utterance and candidate slot-value pairs from the domain ontology. The main contributions are: 1) A data-driven neural architecture that successfully replaces the traditional, brittle delexicalisation pipeline, significantly enhancing the model's ability to handle linguistic variation without manual feature engineering. 2) A strong empirical evaluation on two datasets (DSTC2 and WOZ 2.0) demonstrating that NBT matches the performance of lexicon-dependent models and significantly outperforms them when such lexicons are unavailable.

**Strengths**
1.  **High Practical Impact:** The work tackles a critical bottleneck in dialogue system development—the need for domain-specific, manually curated lexicons. By offering a robust, data-driven alternative, this paper presents a far more scalable approach for deploying dialogue systems in new and complex domains.
2.  **Convincing Experimental Validation:** The evaluation is thorough and well-designed. Using both noisy ASR output (DSTC2) and cleaner, more complex text (WOZ 2.0) effectively tests the model's robustness and semantic understanding. The direct comparison against baselines with and without lexicons provides compelling evidence for the paper's central claims, which are further supported by significance testing.
3.  **Well-Motivated Architecture:** The NBT model is an elegant and novel application of representation learning to this task. The architecture, which composes representations for the utterance, context, and candidate pair before modeling their interaction, is intuitive and shown to be highly effective. The analysis on the impact of different word vector types is also an insightful contribution.

**Weaknesses**
1.  **Simplified Context Modeling:** The model's context is limited to only the last system turn. This Markovian assumption may not hold in more complex dialogues with long-range dependencies, potentially limiting the model's applicability in scenarios where users refer to information from earlier in the conversation.
2.  **Heuristic Belief Update:** The belief state update mechanism is a simple, rule-based interpolation governed by a single hyperparameter. A learned update mechanism, perhaps using an RNN over dialogue turns, could potentially offer more robust and adaptive tracking performance across different dialogue flows.
3.  **Scalability Concerns for Large Ontologies:** The approach requires iterating over every possible slot-value pair to make a prediction. While feasible for the tested domains, this method's computational cost could become prohibitive in real-world scenarios with hundreds of slots or thousands of values per slot. The paper does not address how the approach might scale in such settings.
