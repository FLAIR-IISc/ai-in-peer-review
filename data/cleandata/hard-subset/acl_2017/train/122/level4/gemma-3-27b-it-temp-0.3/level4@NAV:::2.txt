This paper introduces a neural network framework for dialogue state tracking (DST).
The key contribution lies in the learning of representations for user utterances, system responses, and ontology entries, leveraging pre-trained word vectors.
The authors specifically evaluate two neural network architectures for utterance representation: NBT-DNN and NBT-CNN.
These learned representations are then integrated and utilized within a downstream network to perform binary classification for slot value pairs.
Experimental results demonstrate substantial performance gains over a delexicalized baseline.
Overall, this is a strong piece of work with a clear objective, a sound approach, and demonstrable improvements over existing methods.
However, the paper’s organization could be improved to enhance clarity, particularly for readers less familiar with the field.
Specifically, a more formal definition of DST is needed early in the paper.
The current presentation, especially when coupled with a discussion of spoken language understanding (SLU), lacks sufficient clarity.
I recommend presenting a general dialogue system architecture in Section 1, followed by a focused definition of the DST problem and its relationship to other components such as automatic speech recognition (ASR), SLU, and policy learning.
Furthermore, defining all notations used throughout the paper in an earlier section would significantly improve readability. 
Currently, several symbols (e.g., tq, ts, t_v) are introduced before their definitions.

Additional comments and questions include:
- Is it possible to perform SLU independently with this model? If not, the use of the term ‘joint’ may be misleading, as it implies the ability to handle both tasks separately.
- Could you provide statistics on the number of errors corrected in the original DSTC2 dataset?
If the number is not substantial, including comparisons to other published work, including DSTC2 submissions using the same dataset, would be valuable.
- Have you considered utilizing recurrent neural networks (RNNs) or long short-term memory networks (LSTMs) to capture sequential information in the utterance representation learning process?
Given the recent successes of these architectures in SLU, they may also prove effective for DST.
- Providing more detail regarding the semantic dictionary used in the baseline would help to illustrate the effort required for manual resource creation.
- Including example cases that were misclassified by the baseline but correctly predicted by your proposed models would be beneficial.
