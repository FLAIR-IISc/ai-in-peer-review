**Summary of the Paper and Main Contributions**

This paper proposes a novel Neural Belief Tracker (NBT) framework for dialogue state tracking, which leverages pre-trained word vectors to overcome the limitations of traditional approaches. The main contributions of this work are:

1. A new approach to dialogue state tracking that uses pre-trained word vectors to represent slot values and learns to compose them into distributed representations of user utterances and dialogue context.
2. A neural network architecture that integrates Spoken Language Understanding (SLU) and Dialogue State Tracking (DST) without relying on hand-crafted semantic lexicons.
3. Experimental results on two datasets that demonstrate significant improvements over baseline delexicalization-based approaches.

**Strengths**

This paper proposes a novel approach for dialogue state tracking that benefits from representing slot values with pre-trained embeddings and learns to compose them into distributed representations of user utterances and dialogue context. Experiments performed on two datasets show consistent and significant improvements over the baseline of previous delexicalization-based approaches. Alternative approaches (i.e., XAVIER, GloVe, Paragram-SL999) for pre-training word embeddings have been investigated, providing a comprehensive evaluation of the proposed method.

**Weaknesses**

Although one of the main motivations for using embeddings is to generalize to more complex dialogue domains where delexicalization may not scale, the datasets used seem limited. It is unclear how the approach would compare with and without a separate slot tagging component on more complex dialogues. For example, when computing similarity between the utterance and slot value pairs, one can actually limit the estimation to the span of the slot values, which should be applicable even when the values do not match.

Additionally, the examples in the introduction are misleading, as the dialogue state should also include "restaurant_name=The House". This raises another question: how does resolution of coreferences impact this task?

**General Discussion**

On the overall, the use of pre-trained word embeddings is a great idea, and the specific approach for using them is exciting. The paper provides a thorough evaluation of the proposed method and investigates alternative approaches for pre-training word embeddings. However, further research is needed to address the limitations of the current approach, including its performance on more complex dialogue domains and its handling of coreferences.

**Questions to Authors**

1. How do you plan to address the limitations of the current approach, including its performance on more complex dialogue domains?
2. Can you provide more details on how the resolution of coreferences impacts the task of dialogue state tracking?
3. Have you considered using other types of pre-trained embeddings, such as character-based or subword-based embeddings?
