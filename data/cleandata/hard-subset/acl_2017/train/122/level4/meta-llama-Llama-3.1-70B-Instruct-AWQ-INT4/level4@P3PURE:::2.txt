This paper presents a neural network-based framework for dialogue state tracking (DST). The main contribution of this work lies in learning representations of user utterances, system outputs, and ontology entries, all of which are based on pre-trained word vectors.

Particularly noteworthy is the comparison between two different neural network models for utterance representation: NBT-DNN and NBT-CNN. The learned representations are combined and used in the downstream network to make binary decisions for a given slot value pair. The experiment demonstrates that the proposed framework achieves significant performance improvements compared to the baseline with the delexicalized approach.

Overall, this is a quality work with a clear goal, reasonable idea, and improved results from previous studies. However, the paper could be better organized to effectively deliver its details, especially to readers unfamiliar with this area.

A more formal definition of DST should be provided at the beginning of the paper. The current explanation is not clear enough and may be confusing, especially when coupled with Spoken Language Understanding (SLU). I suggest providing a general architecture of a dialogue system in Section 1, followed by the problem definition of DST, focusing on its relationships to other components, including Automatic Speech Recognition (ASR), SLU, and policy learning. Additionally, defining all notations used throughout the paper in an earlier section would improve readability. Some symbols (e.g., tq, ts, t_v) are used much earlier than their descriptions.

Several questions and comments arise from the paper:

* Would it be possible to perform separate SLU with this model? If not, the term 'joint' could be misleading, suggesting that the model can handle both tasks.
* Could the authors provide statistics on the number of errors corrected from the original DSTC2 dataset? If the number is not substantial, the experiment could include comparisons with other published work, including DSTC2 entries, using the same dataset.
* What are the authors' thoughts on using Recurrent Neural Networks (RNNs) or Long Short-Term Memory (LSTMs) to learn sequential aspects in utterance representations? Considering the recent successes of these recurrent networks in SLU problems, they could be effective for DST as well.
* More details about the semantic dictionary used with the baseline would be helpful in understanding the cost of building such resources manually.
* Providing samples that were not correctly predicted by the baseline but were solved with the proposed models would be beneficial in illustrating the framework's effectiveness.

Addressing these points would strengthen the paper and provide a clearer understanding of the proposed framework's contributions and limitations.
