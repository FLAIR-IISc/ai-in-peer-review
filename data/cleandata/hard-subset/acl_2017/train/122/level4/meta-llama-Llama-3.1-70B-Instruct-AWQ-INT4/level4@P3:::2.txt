This paper presents a neural network-based framework for dialogue state tracking (DST). The main contribution of this work is in learning representations of user utterances, system outputs, and ontology entries, all based on pre-trained word vectors. Specifically, the authors compare two different neural network models, NBT-DNN and NBT-CNN, for utterance representation. The learned representations are combined and used in the downstream network to make binary decisions for given slot-value pairs. The experiments show that the proposed framework achieves significant performance improvements compared to the baseline with the delexicalized approach.

**Summary of Contributions**

1. A novel neural belief tracking framework that learns representations of user utterances, system outputs, and ontology entries using pre-trained word vectors.
2. Two neural network models, NBT-DNN and NBT-CNN, are proposed for utterance representation and compared in the experiments.
3. The framework achieves significant performance improvements compared to the baseline with the delexicalized approach.

**Strengths**

1. The paper presents a clear and reasonable idea for improving DST performance using pre-trained word vectors.
2. The experiments demonstrate significant performance improvements compared to the baseline.
3. The authors provide a thorough analysis of the results and discuss the implications of their findings.

**Weaknesses**

1. The paper could benefit from a more formal definition of DST at the beginning, especially for readers unfamiliar with the area.
2. The organization of the paper could be improved, with a clearer separation between the introduction, problem definition, and proposed solution.
3. Some notations are used before their descriptions, which may cause confusion.
4. The authors could provide more details about the semantic dictionary used with the baseline to highlight the cost of building such resources manually.
5. Including samples that were not correctly predicted by the baseline but solved with the proposed models would be helpful.

**Questions to Authors**

1. Would it be possible to perform separate SLU with this model? If not, the term 'joint' could be misleading.
2. Could you provide some statistics about the number of errors corrected from the original DSTC2 dataset?
3. What are your thoughts on using RNNs or LSTMs to learn sequential aspects in learning utterance representations?
4. Could you provide more details about the semantic dictionary used with the baseline?
5. Can you give some samples that were not correctly predicted by the baseline but solved with your proposed models?
