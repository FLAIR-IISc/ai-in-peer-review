**Strengths:**

This paper presents a novel approach to dialogue state tracking by leveraging pre-trained embeddings to represent slot values and learning to compose them into distributed representations of user utterances and dialogue context. The experimental results on two datasets demonstrate consistent and significant improvements over the baseline of previous delexicalization-based approaches. Furthermore, the authors investigate alternative methods for pre-training word embeddings, including XAVIER, GloVe, and Program-SL999.

**Weaknesses:**

One of the primary motivations for using embeddings is to generalize to more complex dialogue domains where delexicalization may not be scalable. However, the datasets used in this study appear limited, raising concerns about the approach's performance on more complex dialogues. It would be beneficial to compare the approach with and without a separate slot tagging component in such scenarios. Additionally, when computing similarity between the utterance and slot value pairs, the estimation could be limited to the span of the slot values, making it applicable even when the values do not match. The introduction's examples may be misleading, as the dialogue state should also include "restaurant_name=The House." This oversight raises questions about how coreference resolution impacts this task.

**General Discussion:**

Overall, the use of pre-trained word embeddings is a promising idea, and the specific approach presented in this paper is exciting. The authors demonstrate the potential of this method, and with further development and testing on more complex datasets, it could lead to significant advancements in dialogue state tracking.
