This paper presents a neural network-based framework for dialogue state tracking, which is a crucial component of modern spoken dialogue systems. The main contribution of this work lies in learning representations of user utterances, system outputs, and ontology entries, all of which are based on pre-trained word vectors. Specifically, the authors compare two different neural network models, NBT-DNN and NBT-CNN, for utterance representation. The learned representations are combined with each other and finally used in the downstream network to make binary decisions for a given slot-value pair.

The experiment shows that the proposed framework achieved significant performance improvements compared to the baseline with the delexicalized approach. It's generally a quality work with clear goals, reasonable ideas, and improved results from previous studies. However, the paper could be better organized to effectively deliver the details, especially to readers who are not familiar with this area.

Firstly, a more formal definition of dialogue state tracking (DST) needs to be provided at the beginning of this paper. It is not clear enough and could be more confusing after coupling with Spoken Language Understanding (SLU). A suggestion would be to provide a general architecture of a dialogue system described in Section 1, followed by the problem definition of DST focusing on its relationships to other components, including Automatic Speech Recognition (ASR), SLU, and policy learning. Additionally, it would help to improve readability if all the notations used throughout the paper were defined in an earlier section. Some symbols (e.g., tq, ts, tv) are used much earlier than their descriptions.

Here are some additional comments and questions:

* Would it be possible to perform separate SLU with this model? If not, the term 'joint' could be misleading, suggesting that this model is able to handle both tasks.
* Could you please provide some statistics about how many errors were corrected from the original DSTC2 dataset? If it is not very huge, the experiment could include comparisons with other published work, including DSTC2 entries using the same dataset.
* What do you think about using Recurrent Neural Networks (RNNs) or Long Short-Term Memory (LSTM) networks to learn the sequential aspects in learning utterance representations? Considering the recent successes of these recurrent networks in SLU problems, it could be effective for DST as well.
* Some more details about the semantic dictionary used with the baseline would help to imply the cost for building this kind of resource manually.
* It would be great if you could give some samples that were not correctly predicted by the baseline but solved with your proposed models.

Main Contributions:

1. The authors propose a novel neural belief tracking (NBT) framework that efficiently learns to handle variation without requiring hand-crafted resources.
2. The NBT models couple SLU and DST, leveraging semantic information from pre-trained word vectors to resolve lexical/morphological ambiguity.
3. The authors demonstrate the effectiveness of the NBT framework on two datasets, showing that it matches the performance of state-of-the-art models that rely on hand-crafted semantic lexicons and outperforms them when such resources are not provided.

Strengths:

1. The paper presents a clear and well-motivated idea for addressing the limitations of current belief tracking models.
2. The authors provide a thorough evaluation of their proposed framework on two datasets, demonstrating its effectiveness and robustness.
3. The use of pre-trained word vectors and the coupling of SLU and DST are innovative approaches that have the potential to improve the performance of dialogue state tracking models.

Weaknesses:

1. The paper could benefit from a clearer and more concise presentation of the proposed framework and its components.
2. Some of the notations and symbols used throughout the paper are not clearly defined, which may cause confusion for readers who are not familiar with the area.
3. The authors could provide more details about the semantic dictionary used with the baseline and the cost of building such resources manually.

Questions to Authors:

* Could you please provide more details about the semantic dictionary used with the baseline and the cost of building such resources manually?
* How do you think the proposed framework could be extended to handle more complex dialogue domains and larger ontologies?
* Are there any plans to explore the use of RNNs or LSTMs to learn the sequential aspects in learning utterance representations?
