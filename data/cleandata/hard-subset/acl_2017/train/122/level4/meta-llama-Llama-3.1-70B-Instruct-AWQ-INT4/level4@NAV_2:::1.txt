This paper proposes a novel approach for dialogue state tracking that benefits from representing slot values with pre-trained embeddings and learns to compose them into distributed representations of user utterances and dialogue context. The authors demonstrate the effectiveness of their approach through experiments on two datasets, showing consistent and significant improvements over the baseline of previous delexicalization-based approaches. Furthermore, they investigate alternative approaches for pre-training word embeddings, including XAVIER, GloVe, and Paragram-SL999.

The main contributions of this work are:
1. The proposal of a novel neural belief tracking (NBT) framework that leverages pre-trained word vectors to improve the language understanding capability of belief tracking models.
2. The demonstration of the effectiveness of the NBT approach through experiments on two datasets, showing significant improvements over the baseline of previous delexicalization-based approaches.
3. The investigation of alternative approaches for pre-training word embeddings, including XAVIER, GloVe, and Paragram-SL999, and the analysis of their impact on the performance of the NBT model.

Strengths:
1. The paper proposes a novel approach for dialogue state tracking that benefits from representing slot values with pre-trained embeddings and learns to compose them into distributed representations of user utterances and dialogue context.
2. The authors demonstrate the effectiveness of their approach through experiments on two datasets, showing consistent and significant improvements over the baseline of previous delexicalization-based approaches.
3. The investigation of alternative approaches for pre-training word embeddings provides valuable insights into the impact of different embedding methods on the performance of the NBT model.

Weaknesses:
1. The datasets used in the experiments seem limited, and it is unclear how the approach would perform on more complex dialogue domains.
2. The paper does not discuss the impact of coreference resolution on the task of dialogue state tracking, which could be an important consideration in more complex dialogue scenarios.
3. The examples in the introduction may be misleading, as they do not include the resolution of coreferences, which could impact the accuracy of the dialogue state tracking model.

Questions to Authors:
1. How do you plan to address the limitation of the datasets used in the experiments, and what steps will you take to evaluate the performance of the NBT model on more complex dialogue domains?
2. Can you provide more insight into how the NBT model handles coreference resolution, and what impact this has on the accuracy of the dialogue state tracking model?
3. How do you plan to extend the NBT model to handle more complex dialogue scenarios, such as those involving multiple intents or slot values?
