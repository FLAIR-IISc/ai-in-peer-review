This paper introduces a neural network-based framework for dialogue state tracking, primarily focusing on learning representations of user utterances, system outputs, and ontology entries, all rooted in pre-trained word vectors.

The core contribution of this work lies in its comparative analysis of two distinct neural network models, NBT-DNN and NBT-CNN, specifically for utterance representation. The learned representations are then combined and utilized in the downstream network to make binary decisions for a given slot value pair.

Experimental results demonstrate that the proposed framework achieves significant performance improvements over the baseline, which employs a delexicalized approach.

While this is a quality work with a clear objective, reasonable concept, and enhanced results compared to previous studies, the paper's organization could be improved to effectively convey its details, particularly to readers unfamiliar with this area.

To address this, a formal definition of Dialogue State Tracking (DST) should be provided at the beginning of the paper. The current explanation may be unclear and potentially confusing, especially when coupled with Spoken Language Understanding (SLU).

A suggested reorganization involves presenting a general architecture of a dialogue system in Section 1, followed by a problem definition of DST that highlights its relationships with other components, including Automatic Speech Recognition (ASR), SLU, and policy learning. Additionally, defining all notations used throughout the paper in an earlier section would enhance readability.

Some symbols, such as tq, ts, and t_v, are introduced before their descriptions, which may cause confusion.

Other comments and questions include:

- Is it possible to perform separate SLU with this model? If not, the term 'joint' might be misleading, suggesting that the model can handle both tasks.
- Could the authors provide statistics on the number of errors corrected from the original DSTC2 dataset? If the number is not substantial, the experiment could include comparisons with other published works using the same dataset.
- What are the authors' thoughts on using Recurrent Neural Networks (RNNs) or Long Short-Term Memory (LSTMs) to learn sequential aspects in utterance representation learning? Given the recent successes of these recurrent networks in SLU problems, they might be effective for DST as well.
- Providing more details about the semantic dictionary used with the baseline would help to imply the cost of manually building such resources.
- Including samples that were not correctly predicted by the baseline but were solved with the proposed models would be beneficial.
