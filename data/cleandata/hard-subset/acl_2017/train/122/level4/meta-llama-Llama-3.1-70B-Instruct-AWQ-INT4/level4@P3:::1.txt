**Summary of the Paper**

This paper proposes a novel Neural Belief Tracker (NBT) framework for dialogue state tracking, which leverages pre-trained word vectors to improve the language understanding capability of belief tracking models. The NBT model couples Spoken Language Understanding (SLU) and Dialogue State Tracking (DST), efficiently learning to handle variation without requiring hand-crafted resources. The authors evaluate the NBT model on two datasets, showing that it matches the performance of state-of-the-art models that rely on hand-crafted semantic lexicons and outperforms them when such lexicons are not provided.

**Main Contributions**

1. The NBT framework is the first to successfully use pre-trained word vector spaces to improve the language understanding capability of belief tracking models.
2. The authors propose two model variants, NBT-DNN and NBT-CNN, which differ in the method used to produce vector representations of user utterances.
3. The NBT model achieves state-of-the-art performance on two datasets, outperforming baseline models that rely on delexicalization-based approaches.

**Strengths**

* The paper proposes a novel approach for dialogue state tracking that benefits from representing slot values with pre-trained embeddings and learns to compose them into distributed representations of user utterances and dialogue context.
* Experiments performed on two datasets show consistent and significant improvements over the baseline of previous delexicalization-based approaches.
* Alternative approaches (i.e., XAVIER, GloVe, Paragram-SL999) for pre-training word embeddings have been investigated.

**Weaknesses**

* Although one of the main motivations for using embeddings is to generalize to more complex dialogue domains where delexicalization may not scale, the datasets used seem limited. It is unclear how the approach would compare with and without a separate slot tagging component on more complex dialogues.
* The paper does not address how the resolution of coreferences impacts the task of dialogue state tracking.
* The examples in the introduction may be misleading, as they do not include the "restaurant_name=The House" slot value pair in the dialogue state.

**Questions to Authors**

* How do you plan to extend the NBT framework to handle more complex dialogue domains, such as those with multiple intents or slot values?
* Can you provide more details on how the NBT model handles coreference resolution, and how it impacts the performance of the model?
* Have you considered using other pre-trained word embeddings, such as BERT or RoBERTa, and how do they compare to the Paragram-SL999 embeddings used in the paper?
