Summary: Introduces a Neural Belief Tracker (NBT) that uses pre-trained word vectors to jointly model SLU and DST, combining representations of user utterances, system outputs, and ontology entries to make slot-value decisions and achieves state-of-the-art results on DSTC2 and WOZ 2.0.

Contribution 1: Proposes two neural models (NBT-DNN and NBT-CNN) to learn representations of user utterances, system outputs, and ontology entries from pre-trained word vectors.

Contribution 2: Couples SLU and DST within a single framework to learn efficiently from data without hand-crafted resources.

Contribution 3: Demonstrates improved, state-of-the-art performance on DSTC2 and WOZ 2.0 compared to delexicalisation-based baselines.

Strength 1: Novel method for representing utterances, system outputs, and ontology entries using pre-trained word vectors.

Strength 2: Empirical improvement over baselines and strong performance on two benchmark datasets.

Strength 3: Reduces reliance on hand-crafted resources, enabling more efficient learning from data.

Weakness 1: Paper organization could be improved to present details more clearly for non-expert readers.

Weakness 2: Notation issues—some symbols are introduced before they are defined, causing confusion.

Weakness 3: Insufficient comparisons to other published work, including other DSTC2 entries using the same dataset.

Question 1: Can the model be used for separate SLU, or is the term "joint" misleading?

Question 2: How many errors from the original DSTC2 dataset were corrected and can you provide statistics?

Question 3: Have you considered using RNNs or LSTMs to capture sequential aspects of utterance representations?

Question 4: Please provide more details on the semantic dictionary used with the baseline and the manual cost of creating it.

Question 5: Can you show error analysis examples where the baseline failed but your models succeeded?
