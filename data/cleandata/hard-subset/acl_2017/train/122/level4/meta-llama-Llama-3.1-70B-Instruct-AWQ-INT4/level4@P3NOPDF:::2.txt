This paper presents a neural network-based framework for dialogue state tracking (DST). The main contribution of this work lies in learning representations of user utterances, system outputs, and ontology entries, all of which are based on pre-trained word vectors. Specifically, the authors compare two different neural network models, NBT-DNN and NBT-CNN, for utterance representation. The learned representations are combined and used in the downstream network to make binary decisions for a given slot value pair. The experiments demonstrate that the proposed framework achieves significant performance improvements compared to the baseline with the delexicalized approach.

Overall, this is a quality work with a clear goal, reasonable idea, and improved results from previous studies. However, the paper could benefit from better organization to effectively deliver its details, particularly for readers unfamiliar with this area.

Firstly, a more formal definition of DST should be provided at the beginning of the paper. The current explanation is unclear and may be confusing, especially when coupled with spoken language understanding (SLU). I suggest providing a general architecture of a dialogue system in Section 1, followed by the problem definition of DST, focusing on its relationships to other components, including automatic speech recognition (ASR), SLU, and policy learning. Additionally, defining all notations used throughout the paper in an earlier section would improve readability. Some symbols (e.g., tq, ts, t_v) are used much earlier than their descriptions.

I have several comments and questions:

1. Would it be possible to perform separate SLU with this model? If not, the term 'joint' could be misleading, suggesting that the model can handle both tasks.
2. Could you provide some statistics about the number of errors corrected from the original DSTC2 dataset? If the number is not substantial, the experiment could include comparisons with other published work, including DSTC2 entries using the same dataset.
3. What are your thoughts on using RNNs or LSTMs to learn sequential aspects in learning utterance representations? Considering the recent successes of these recurrent networks in SLU problems, they could be effective for DST as well.
4. Providing more details about the semantic dictionary used with the baseline would help to imply the cost of building this kind of resource manually.
5. It would be great if you could provide some samples that were not correctly predicted by the baseline but were solved with your proposed models.

By addressing these points, the paper can be improved in terms of clarity, organization, and overall impact.
