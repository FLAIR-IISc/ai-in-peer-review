- Proposes a neural-network framework for dialogue state tracking that learns representations of user utterances, system outputs, and ontology entries from pre-trained word vectors.
- Main contribution is a comparative analysis of two utterance representation models: NBT-DNN and NBT-CNN.
- Learned representations are combined and used downstream to make binary decisions for slot–value pairs.
- Experiments show significant improvement over a delexicalized baseline.
- Paper quality is good but paper organization and clarity need improvement for non-expert readers.
- Recommend adding a formal definition of Dialogue State Tracking (DST) at the start, and clarifying its distinction from SLU.
- Suggest reorganizing Section 1 to show a general dialogue system architecture, then define the DST problem and its relation to ASR, SLU, and policy learning.
- Advise defining all notations early to improve readability.
- Notes that some symbols (e.g., tq, ts, t_v) are introduced before being explained, causing confusion.
- Asks whether the model can perform separate SLU; warns that calling it “joint” may be misleading if it does not.
- Requests statistics on the number of errors corrected from the DSTC2 dataset and comparisons with other published works if corrections are few.
- Suggests exploring RNNs/LSTMs to capture sequential aspects of utterance representation given their success in SLU.
- Requests more details about the semantic dictionary used by the baseline to indicate manual resource cost.
- Recommends including example cases corrected by the proposed models that the baseline failed to solve.
