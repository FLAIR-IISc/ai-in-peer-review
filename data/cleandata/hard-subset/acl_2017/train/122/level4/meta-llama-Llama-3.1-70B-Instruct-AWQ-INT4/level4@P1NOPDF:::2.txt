This paper presents a neural network-based framework for dialogue state tracking (DST). The main contribution of this work lies in learning representations of user utterances, system outputs, and ontology entries, all of which are based on pre-trained word vectors.

Particularly noteworthy is the comparison of two different neural network models for utterance representation: NBT-DNN and NBT-CNN. The learned representations are combined and used in the downstream network to make binary decisions for a given slot value pair. The experiments demonstrate that the proposed framework achieves significant performance improvements compared to the baseline with the delexicalized approach.

Overall, this is a quality work with a clear goal, reasonable idea, and improved results compared to previous studies. However, the paper's organization could be improved to effectively convey the details, especially to readers unfamiliar with this area.

Firstly, a more formal definition of DST should be provided at the beginning of the paper. As it stands, the definition is unclear and may be confusing, especially when coupled with spoken language understanding (SLU). It would be beneficial to provide a general architecture of a dialogue system in Section 1, followed by the problem definition of DST, focusing on its relationships to other components, including automatic speech recognition (ASR), SLU, and policy learning. Additionally, defining all notations used throughout the paper in an earlier section would improve readability. Some symbols (e.g., tq, ts, t_v) are used before their descriptions, which may cause confusion.

Other comments and questions include:

- Would it be possible to perform separate SLU with this model? If not, the term 'joint' could be misleading, suggesting that the model can handle both tasks.
- Could the authors provide some statistics on the number of errors corrected from the original DSTC2 dataset? If the number is not substantial, it would be beneficial to include comparisons with other published work, including DSTC2 entries using the same dataset.
- What are the authors' thoughts on using RNNs or LSTMs to learn the sequential aspects of utterance representations? Given the recent successes of these recurrent networks in SLU problems, they may also be effective for DST.
- Providing more details about the semantic dictionary used with the baseline would help to illustrate the cost of building this type of resource manually.
- It would be great if the authors could provide some samples that were not correctly predicted by the baseline but were solved with the proposed models.
