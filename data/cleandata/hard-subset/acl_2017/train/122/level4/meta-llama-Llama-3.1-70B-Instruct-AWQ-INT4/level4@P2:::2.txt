This paper presents a neural network-based framework for dialogue state tracking, which is a crucial component in spoken dialogue systems. The authors propose a novel approach to learning representations of user utterances, system outputs, and ontology entries using pre-trained word vectors.

**Summary of the Paper**

The paper introduces a Neural Belief Tracker (NBT) framework that couples Spoken Language Understanding (SLU) and Dialogue State Tracking (DST) to efficiently learn from data without requiring hand-crafted resources. The NBT model uses pre-trained word vectors to reason about user utterances, system outputs, and ontology entries, and then combines these representations to make binary decisions about slot-value pairs. The authors evaluate their approach on two datasets, DSTC2 and WOZ 2.0, and show that their model outperforms baseline delexicalisation-based models and achieves state-of-the-art performance.

**Main Contributions**

1. **Learning representations of user utterances, system outputs, and ontology entries**: The authors propose two neural network models, NBT-DNN and NBT-CNN, to learn representations of user utterances, system outputs, and ontology entries using pre-trained word vectors.
2. **Coupling SLU and DST**: The NBT model combines SLU and DST to efficiently learn from data without requiring hand-crafted resources.
3. **Improved performance**: The authors demonstrate that their approach achieves state-of-the-art performance on two datasets, DSTC2 and WOZ 2.0.

**Strengths**

1. **Novel approach**: The authors propose a novel approach to learning representations of user utterances, system outputs, and ontology entries using pre-trained word vectors.
2. **Improved performance**: The authors demonstrate that their approach achieves state-of-the-art performance on two datasets.
3. **Efficient learning**: The NBT model efficiently learns from data without requiring hand-crafted resources.

**Weaknesses**

1. **Organization**: The paper could be better organized to effectively deliver the details, especially for readers who are not familiar with the area.
2. **Notation**: Some symbols are used before their descriptions, which can be confusing.
3. **Comparison to other work**: The authors could provide more comparisons to other published work, including DSTC2 entries using the same dataset.

**Questions to Authors**

1. **Separate SLU**: Would it be possible to perform separate SLU with this model? If not, the term 'joint' could be misleading.
2. **Error correction**: Could you please provide some statistics about how many errors were corrected from the original DSTC2 dataset?
3. **RNNs or LSTMs**: What do you think about using RNNs or LSTMs to learn the sequential aspects in learning utterance representations?
4. **Semantic dictionary**: Could you provide more details about the semantic dictionary used with the baseline to imply the cost for building this kind of resource manually?
5. **Error analysis**: Could you provide some samples that were not correctly predicted by the baseline but solved with your proposed models?
