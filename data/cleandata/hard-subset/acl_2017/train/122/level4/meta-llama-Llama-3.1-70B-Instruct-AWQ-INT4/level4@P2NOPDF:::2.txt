This paper presents a neural network-based framework for dialogue state tracking (DST). The primary contribution of this work lies in learning representations of user utterances, system outputs, and ontology entries, all of which are based on pre-trained word vectors. Specifically, the authors compare two different neural network models, NBT-DNN and NBT-CNN, for utterance representation. The learned representations are combined and used in the downstream network to make binary decisions for a given slot value pair. The experiments demonstrate that the proposed framework achieves significant performance improvements compared to the baseline with the delexicalized approach.

Overall, this is a quality work with a clear goal, reasonable idea, and improved results from previous studies. However, the paper could be better organized to effectively deliver the details, especially to readers unfamiliar with this area. 

Firstly, a more formal definition of DST should be provided at the beginning of the paper. The current explanation is not clear enough and may be confusing, especially when coupled with spoken language understanding (SLU). It would be beneficial to provide a general architecture of a dialogue system in Section 1, followed by the problem definition of DST, focusing on its relationships to other components, including automatic speech recognition (ASR), SLU, and policy learning. Additionally, defining all notations used throughout the paper in an earlier section would improve readability. Some symbols (e.g., tq, ts, t_v) are used much earlier than their descriptions.

Several comments and questions arise from the paper:

1. Would it be possible to perform separate SLU with this model? If not, the term 'joint' could be misleading, as it implies that the model can handle both tasks.

2. Could the authors provide some statistics about the number of errors corrected from the original DSTC2 dataset? If the number of corrections is not substantial, the experiment could include comparisons with other published work, including DSTC2 entries using the same dataset.

3. The authors may consider using RNNs or LSTMs to learn the sequential aspects in learning utterance representations. Given the recent successes of these recurrent networks in SLU problems, they could be effective for DST as well.

4. More details about the semantic dictionary used with the baseline would help to imply the cost of building this kind of resource manually.

5. Providing some samples that were not correctly predicted by the baseline but solved with the proposed models would be beneficial.

By addressing these points and reorganizing the paper, the authors can improve the clarity and readability of their work, making it more accessible to a broader audience.
