- Proposes a neural belief tracking (NBT) framework that learns representations of user utterances, system outputs, and ontology entries using pre-trained word vectors, comparing NBT-DNN and NBT-CNN for utterance representation.  
- Combines learned representations to make binary decisions for each slot–value pair in dialogue state tracking.  
- Achieves significant improvements over a delexicalized baseline and matches/outperforms state-of-the-art when hand-crafted semantic lexicons are absent.  
- Clear goals and reasonable ideas, with thorough evaluation on two datasets demonstrating effectiveness and robustness.  
- Paper organization and presentation could be improved for readers unfamiliar with DST.  
- Needs a formal definition of dialogue state tracking early on and a diagram of dialogue system architecture showing relations to ASR, SLU, and policy learning.  
- Notations and symbols (e.g., tq, ts, tv) are introduced before being defined, harming readability.  
- Clarify whether the model performs separate SLU—using the term "joint" may be misleading if it does not.  
- Provide statistics on how many errors were corrected in the DSTC2 dataset and compare with other published DSTC2 entries.  
- Consider using RNNs/LSTMs to capture sequential aspects of utterance representation, given their success in SLU.  
- Offer more details about the semantic dictionary used by the baseline and the manual cost of building such resources.  
- Supply sample utterances that the baseline mispredicted but the proposed models corrected to illustrate practical gains.  
- Main contributions summarized: novel NBT framework handling variation without hand-crafted resources; coupling SLU and DST via pre-trained vectors; empirical validation on two datasets.  
- Strengths summarized: well-motivated idea, thorough evaluation, innovative use of pre-trained vectors and SLU–DST coupling.  
- Weaknesses summarized: presentation clarity issues, undefined notations, and insufficient baseline semantic-dictionary details and cost analysis.  
- Questions for authors: details and cost of the semantic dictionary; scalability to complex domains and larger ontologies; plans to explore RNN/LSTM-based utterance representations.
