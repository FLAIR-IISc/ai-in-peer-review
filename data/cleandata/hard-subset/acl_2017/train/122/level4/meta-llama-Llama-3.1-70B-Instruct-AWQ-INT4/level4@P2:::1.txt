**Summary of the Paper**

This paper proposes a novel approach for dialogue state tracking, called Neural Belief Tracker (NBT), which leverages pre-trained word embeddings to represent slot values and learns to compose them into distributed representations of user utterances and dialogue context. The NBT model is designed to overcome the limitations of previous approaches, which rely on delexicalization or hand-crafted semantic lexicons. The authors evaluate their approach on two datasets, DSTC2 and WOZ 2.0, and demonstrate significant improvements over the baseline delexicalization-based approach.

**Main Contributions**

1. The paper proposes a novel approach for dialogue state tracking that leverages pre-trained word embeddings to represent slot values and learns to compose them into distributed representations of user utterances and dialogue context.
2. The authors evaluate their approach on two datasets, DSTC2 and WOZ 2.0, and demonstrate significant improvements over the baseline delexicalization-based approach.
3. The paper investigates alternative approaches for pre-training word embeddings, including XAVIER, GloVe, and Paragram-SL999, and shows that semantically specialized word embeddings lead to better performance.

**Strengths**

* The paper proposes a novel approach for dialogue state tracking that benefits from representing slot values with pre-trained embeddings and learns to compose them into distributed representations of user utterances and dialogue context.
* Experiments performed on two datasets show consistent and significant improvements over the baseline of previous delexicalization-based approach.
* The authors investigate alternative approaches for pre-training word embeddings and show that semantically specialized word embeddings lead to better performance.

**Weaknesses**

* The datasets used in the paper seem limited, and it is unclear how the approach would perform on more complex dialogues.
* The paper does not investigate the impact of coreference resolution on the task of dialogue state tracking.
* The examples in the introduction are misleading, and the dialogue state should include additional information, such as the restaurant name.

**Questions to Authors**

* How would the approach perform on more complex dialogues, such as those with multiple slots or more nuanced language?
* How does the approach handle coreference resolution, and what impact does it have on the task of dialogue state tracking?
* Can the authors provide more information on the limitations of the datasets used in the paper and how they plan to address these limitations in future work?
