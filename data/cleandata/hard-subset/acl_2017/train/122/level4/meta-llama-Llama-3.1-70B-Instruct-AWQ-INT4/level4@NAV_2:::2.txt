This paper presents a neural network-based framework for dialogue state tracking. The main contribution of this work is on learning representations of user utterances, system outputs, and also ontology entries, all of which are based on pre-trained word vectors. Particularly for the utterance representation, the authors compared two different neural network models: NBT-DNN and NBT-CNN. The learned representations are combined with each other and finally used in the downstream network to make binary decision for a given slot value pair. The experiment shows that the proposed framework achieved significant performance improvements compared to the baseline with the delexicalized approach.

Summary of the paper and main contributions:
The paper proposes a novel Neural Belief Tracking (NBT) framework designed to overcome current obstacles to deploying dialogue systems in real-world dialogue domains. The NBT models offer the known advantages of coupling Spoken Language Understanding and Dialogue State Tracking, without relying on hand-crafted semantic lexicons to achieve state-of-the-art performance. The main contributions of this work are:
1. Learning representations of user utterances, system outputs, and ontology entries based on pre-trained word vectors.
2. Proposing two different neural network models (NBT-DNN and NBT-CNN) for utterance representation.
3. Achieving significant performance improvements compared to the baseline with the delexicalized approach.

Strengths:
1. The paper presents a novel framework that overcomes the limitations of previous belief tracking models by leveraging semantic information from pre-trained word vectors.
2. The proposed models couple SLU and DST, efficiently learning to handle variation without requiring any hand-crafted resources.
3. The experiment shows that the proposed framework achieved significant performance improvements compared to the baseline with the delexicalized approach.
4. The paper provides a thorough evaluation of the proposed framework on two datasets, demonstrating its effectiveness in handling linguistic variation and robustness to ASR errors.

Weaknesses:
1. The paper could benefit from a clearer organization, particularly for readers who are not familiar with the area. A more formal definition of DST and its relationships to other components, including ASR, SLU, and policy learning, would be helpful.
2. The notation used throughout the paper could be defined in an earlier section to improve readability.
3. Some symbols (e.g., tq, ts, t_v) are used much earlier than their descriptions, which may cause confusion.
4. The paper could benefit from more details about the semantic dictionary used with the baseline, including the cost of building this kind of resource manually.
5. Providing samples that were not correctly predicted by the baseline but solved with the proposed models would be helpful in understanding the strengths of the proposed framework.

Questions to authors:
1. Would it be possible to perform separate SLU with this model? If not, the term 'joint' could be misleading that this model is able to handle both tasks.
2. Could you please provide some statistics about how many errors were corrected from the original DSTC2 dataset? If it is not very huge, the experiment could include comparisons also with other published work, including DSTC2 entries using the same dataset.
3. What do you think about using RNNs or LSTMs to learn the sequential aspects in learning utterance representations? Considering the recent successes of these recurrent networks in SLU problems, it could be effective to DST as well.
