This paper presents a neural network-based framework for dialogue state tracking (DST), with the primary contribution being the learning of representations for user utterances, system outputs, and ontology entries based on pre-trained word vectors.

The authors investigate two distinct neural network models, NBT-DNN and NBT-CNN, for utterance representation and combine the learned representations to make binary decisions for a given slot value pair in the downstream network. Experimental results demonstrate significant performance improvements over the baseline using a delexicalized approach.

Overall, this is a quality work with a clear goal, reasonable idea, and improved results compared to previous studies. However, the paper's organization could be improved to effectively convey the details, particularly for readers unfamiliar with this area.

A more formal definition of DST should be provided at the beginning of the paper, as the current explanation may be unclear and potentially confusing, especially when coupled with spoken language understanding (SLU). It would be beneficial to introduce a general architecture of a dialogue system in Section 1, followed by the problem definition of DST, highlighting its relationships to other components such as automatic speech recognition (ASR), SLU, and policy learning.

To enhance readability, all notations used throughout the paper should be defined in an earlier section. Some symbols (e.g., tq, ts, t_v) are introduced before their descriptions, which may cause confusion.

Additional comments and questions include:

- Is it possible to perform separate SLU with this model? If not, the term 'joint' might be misleading, suggesting that the model can handle both tasks.
- Could the authors provide statistics on the number of errors corrected from the original DSTC2 dataset? If the number is not substantial, it would be beneficial to include comparisons with other published work using the same dataset.
- What are the authors' thoughts on using RNNs or LSTMs to learn sequential aspects in utterance representations? Given the recent successes of these recurrent networks in SLU problems, they might also be effective for DST.
- More details about the semantic dictionary used with the baseline would help to convey the cost of building such resources manually.
- Providing samples that were not correctly predicted by the baseline but were solved with the proposed models would be beneficial.
