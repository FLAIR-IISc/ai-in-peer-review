This paper introduces a novel dialogue state tracking approach that leverages pre-trained embeddings to represent slot values and learns to combine these into distributed representations of user utterances and dialogue context. The experimental results on two datasets demonstrate consistent and significant improvements compared to a previous delexicalization-based baseline. The authors appropriately investigated the impact of different pre-training methods for word embeddings, including XAVIER, GloVe, and Program-SL999.

However, while the use of embeddings is motivated by improved generalization to more complex dialogue domains where delexicalization may struggle, the evaluation datasets appear limited in scope. It would be valuable to understand how the approach performs, with and without a dedicated slot tagging component, on more challenging dialogues. Specifically, restricting similarity calculations between utterances and slot value pairs to the span of the slot values – even in cases of mismatch – could prove beneficial. Furthermore, the examples provided in the introduction are potentially misleading; the dialogue state should arguably include "restaurant_name=The House". This raises a related question concerning the impact of coreference resolution on this task.

Overall, the incorporation of pre-trained word embeddings is a strong contribution, and the specific methodology for their application is promising.
