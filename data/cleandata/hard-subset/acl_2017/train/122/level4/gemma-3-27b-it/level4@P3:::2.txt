## Review of Neural Belief Tracker: Data-Driven Dialogue State Tracking

This paper presents a novel neural network-based framework for dialogue state tracking (DST). The core idea is to learn representations of user utterances, system outputs, and ontology entries using pre-trained word vectors, eliminating the need for hand-crafted lexicons or separate Spoken Language Understanding (SLU) modules. The authors explore two neural network architectures, NBT-DNN and NBT-CNN, for utterance representation and demonstrate improved performance over strong baselines on two standard datasets.

**Contributions:**

1. **A novel end-to-end neural framework for dialogue state tracking:** The paper introduces a compelling approach to DST that directly leverages pre-trained word embeddings to model semantic relationships and obviates the need for delexicalisation. This is a significant step toward scaling DST to more complex domains.
2. **Effective use of pre-trained word vectors for DST:** The work demonstrates the benefit of utilizing semantically-specialized word vectors (Paragram-SL999) for improving DST performance, particularly in scenarios where lexical variation is significant. This highlights the importance of semantic representation learning in dialogue systems.
3. **Strong empirical results:** The proposed NBT models consistently outperform strong baseline systems on both the DSTC2 and WOZ 2.0 datasets, demonstrating the practical effectiveness of the approach. The results are statistically significant and indicate a clear advantage over existing methods, especially when hand-crafted lexicons are unavailable.

**Strengths:**

1. **Addressing a critical challenge:** The paper tackles a key limitation of existing DST systems – their reliance on manually created resources like semantic lexicons or large-scale annotated data for SLU. This is a significant problem for deploying dialogue systems in new domains.
2. **Clear and well-motivated approach:** The proposed framework is logically sound and well-motivated. The authors clearly articulate the drawbacks of existing methods and present NBT as a viable solution.
3. **Comprehensive experimental evaluation:** The authors evaluate their models on two established datasets, using standard evaluation metrics. The comparison against strong baselines provides convincing evidence of the effectiveness of the proposed approach.
4. **Ablation study on word vectors:** The inclusion of results using different word vector types (random, GloVe, Paragram-SL999) effectively demonstrates the importance of semantic information in pre-trained embeddings for this task.
5. **Well-written and detailed model description:** While initially dense, the paper provides sufficient detail to understand the architecture and key components of the NBT models, including the representation learning and context modeling mechanisms.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Organization and accessibility:** The paper suffers from organizational issues that hinder readability, especially for those less familiar with DST. The initial presentation of DST concepts and related work feels disjointed.
2. **Lack of initial system overview:** A higher-level diagram illustrating the role of DST within a complete dialogue system architecture (ASR, SLU, DST, Policy Learning) would provide valuable context. Introducing DST after discussing the overall system would improve clarity.
3. **Notational clarity:** Several symbols are introduced before their definitions, creating confusion. A dedicated section defining all notations would significantly improve readability.
4. **The term "Joint SLU/DST" may be misleading:** While the models couple SLU and DST, it’s not clear if the architecture can perform standalone SLU. Clarifying this point is crucial.
5. **Limited error analysis:** While the results are compelling, providing specific examples of errors corrected by the NBT models (but missed by the baseline) would offer deeper insights into the strengths and weaknesses of the approach.

**Questions to Authors:**

1. Can the proposed model be used for separate SLU tasks, or is it inherently tied to the DST framework?
2. How many errors were corrected in the original DSTC2 dataset, and how might this impact comparisons to previous work on the original dataset?
3. Were alternative sequential modeling approaches (e.g., RNNs, LSTMs) explored for learning utterance representations? If so, what were the results? If not, could you comment on the potential benefits of such approaches?
4. Could you provide more details about the size and content of the semantic dictionary used by the baseline models to provide a clearer sense of the manual effort involved?

**Additional Comments:**

* It would be helpful to provide a more intuitive explanation of the element-wise multiplication operation (Equation 8) and its role in the model.
* Consider adding a table summarizing the key differences between NBT-DNN and NBT-CNN to highlight their respective strengths.
