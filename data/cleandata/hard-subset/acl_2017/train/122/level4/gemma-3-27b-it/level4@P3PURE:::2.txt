This paper presents a neural network-based framework for dialogue state tracking. The main contribution of this work lies in learning representations of user utterances, system outputs, and ontology entries, all based on pre-trained word vectors. Specifically, the authors compare two neural network models for utterance representation: NBT-DNN and NBT-CNN. These learned representations are combined and used in a downstream network to make binary decisions for each slot value pair. Experiments demonstrate that the proposed framework achieves significant performance improvements compared to a baseline utilizing a delexicalized approach. 

Overall, this is a quality work with a clear goal, a reasonable approach, and improved results compared to prior studies. However, the paper’s organization could be improved to more effectively convey the details, particularly for readers unfamiliar with the field. First, a more formal definition of dialogue state tracking (DST) should be provided at the beginning of the paper. The current presentation is insufficiently clear, and becomes more confusing when coupled with the discussion of spoken language understanding (SLU). I suggest presenting a general architecture of a dialogue system in Section 1, followed by a precise problem definition of DST, focusing on its relationships to other components such as automatic speech recognition (ASR), SLU, and policy learning. Furthermore, defining all notations used throughout the paper in an earlier section would greatly improve readability. Several symbols (e.g., tq, ts, t_v) are used before their descriptions appear. 

Below are additional comments and questions:

- Is it possible to perform separate SLU with this model? If not, the term ‘joint’ may be misleading, implying the model can handle both tasks independently.
- Could you provide statistics regarding the number of errors corrected from the original DSTC2 dataset? If this number is not substantial, the experiments should include comparisons with other published work, including DSTC2 entries using the same dataset.
- What are your thoughts on utilizing recurrent neural networks (RNNs) or long short-term memory networks (LSTMs) to learn the sequential aspects of utterance representations? Given the recent successes of these recurrent networks in SLU tasks, they may also be effective for DST.
- Providing more details about the semantic dictionary used with the baseline would help illustrate the cost of building such resources manually.
- It would be beneficial to include examples of instances incorrectly predicted by the baseline but correctly predicted by your proposed models.
