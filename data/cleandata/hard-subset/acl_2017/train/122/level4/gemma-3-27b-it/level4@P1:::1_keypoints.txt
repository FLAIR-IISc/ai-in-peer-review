- Proposes Neural Belief Tracker (NBT) using pre-trained word embeddings to represent slot values and compose utterance/context representations.  
- Experiments on two restaurant datasets show consistent, significant gains over delexicalization baselines.  
- Evaluates impact of different pre-trained embeddings (XAVIER, GloVe, Paragram-SL999).  
- Strength: successfully applies pre-trained vectors to improve belief tracking, reducing reliance on annotated data and hand-crafted lexicons.  
- Strength: NBT jointly models SLU and DST, handling lexical variation without manual resources and simplifying development.  
- Strength: Ablation study shows semantic specialization of embeddings matters for performance.  
- Weakness: Evaluation limited to constrained restaurant domains; generalization to larger, more complex dialogues is unclear.  
- Weakness: Unclear how model would perform with a separate slot-tagging component or when restricting similarity estimation to value spans.  
- Weakness: Example dialogue may be misleading—raises concerns about the model’s coreference resolution and handling of previously mentioned entities.  
- Weakness: Paper lacks analysis of computational cost, especially for the NBT-CNN variant (training/inference time vs. baselines).  
- Questions for authors: how performance scales with domain complexity (slots, values, turns); how coreference is handled; computational overhead of NBT-CNN vs NBT-DNN and baselines.  
- Additional comment: Writing is generally clear but some sentences are convoluted and repetitive; simplify phrasing and fix minor wording issues.
