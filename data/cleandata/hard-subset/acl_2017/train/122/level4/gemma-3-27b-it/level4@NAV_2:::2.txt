This paper presents a neural network-based framework for dialogue state tracking. The primary contribution of this work is a method for learning representations of user utterances, system outputs, and ontology entries based on pre-trained word vectors, thereby reducing reliance on hand-crafted lexical resources. Specifically, the authors explore two neural network architectures – NBT-DNN and NBT-CNN – for utterance representation, which are then integrated with candidate slot-value pair representations for downstream binary classification. Experimental results demonstrate significant performance improvements over a delexicalization-based baseline on both the DSTC2 and WOZ 2.0 datasets. 

**Strengths:**

1. **Elimination of Hand-Crafted Lexicons:** The most compelling strength of this work is its ability to achieve competitive performance *without* relying on manually constructed semantic lexicons. This is a significant advancement, as the creation and maintenance of such lexicons is a major bottleneck for scaling dialogue systems to new domains. The results clearly show the NBT models match lexicon-based performance and surpass it when lexicons are unavailable.
2. **Effective Use of Pre-trained Word Vectors:** The paper effectively leverages the semantic information encoded in pre-trained word vectors (specifically, Paragram-SL999) to handle lexical variation and improve understanding. The ablation study demonstrating the importance of semantic specialization in the word vectors is particularly insightful and strengthens this argument.
3. **Joint Modeling of SLU and DST:** The framework’s inherent coupling of spoken language understanding and dialogue state tracking is a strength, as prior work has shown the benefits of this approach. By directly reasoning over utterance representations, the model avoids the error propagation issues associated with a pipeline architecture.
4. **Solid Experimental Evaluation:** The evaluation on two distinct datasets—DSTC2 and WOZ 2.0—provides strong evidence for the generalizability of the proposed approach. The comparison against established baselines, including a state-of-the-art delexicalization model, is well-executed and highlights the advantages of the NBT framework.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Lack of Introductory Context:** The paper assumes a level of familiarity with dialogue state tracking that may not be shared by all readers. A more comprehensive introduction to DST, its challenges, and its place within a broader dialogue system architecture (including ASR, SLU, and policy learning) would significantly improve accessibility. Placing this foundational context earlier in the paper (Section 1) would be beneficial.
2. **Notational Clarity:** The paper introduces several symbols (e.g., tq, ts, tv) before they are formally defined, hindering readability. A dedicated section listing and defining all key notations would greatly enhance clarity.
3. **Limited Discussion of ASR Error Handling:** While the paper acknowledges the importance of robustness to ASR errors, the discussion is somewhat limited. The experiment showing improved performance with corrected transcriptions highlights this issue, but further analysis of the model’s behavior in the presence of noisy ASR data would be valuable.
4. **"Joint" Model Qualification:** The use of the term “joint SLU/DST” could be misleading. Clarification is needed regarding whether the model can function effectively *without* being trained end-to-end with SLU labels. If separate SLU capabilities are not inherent, a more precise description is warranted.

**Questions to Authors:**

1.  Could you quantify the number of errors corrected in the DSTC2 dataset and explain the potential impact of this correction on comparability with other published DSTC2 results?
2.  The paper describes the semantic dictionary used with the baseline. Could you provide more detail about the size of this dictionary and the effort required to create it, further illustrating the cost of this approach?
3.  Have you explored the use of recurrent neural networks (RNNs) or LSTMs for learning utterance representations? If so, what were the results? If not, what are the reasons for not pursuing this avenue, given the success of RNNs in related SLU tasks?
4.  Can you provide concrete examples of utterances where the NBT models outperform the baseline, and explain the likely reasons for this improvement? Showing the qualitative advantage would be beneficial.
