This paper presents a novel approach to dialogue state tracking that leverages pre-trained word embeddings to represent slot values and learns to compose these embeddings into distributed representations of user utterances and dialogue context. The core idea – utilizing embeddings to capture semantic similarity and improve generalization – is a strong contribution. Experimental results on two datasets demonstrate consistent and significant improvements over a strong delexicalization-based baseline, and a thorough investigation of different pre-training methods (XAVIER, GloVe, and Program-SL999) is presented.

However, the evaluation, while promising, raises some concerns regarding the generalizability of the proposed method. A key motivation for employing embeddings is to overcome the limitations of delexicalization in complex dialogue domains. The current experimental datasets, while well-established, appear relatively limited in this respect. Further evaluation on more challenging datasets with a wider range of slot types, dialogue acts, and contextual dependencies is crucial to validate the approach’s scalability. 

Specifically, I question how the performance would be affected by, or would benefit from, incorporating a dedicated slot tagging component, particularly when dealing with more complex dialogues. For instance, when calculating similarity between utterances and candidate slot value pairs, restricting the similarity estimation to the span of the slot values themselves could prove beneficial, even in cases where a direct value match is absent. This refinement could potentially enhance accuracy and robustness.

There are also points of clarification needed regarding the presented examples. The example provided in the introduction appears incomplete. If the user requests information about “The House” restaurant, the dialogue state should explicitly include “restaurant_name=The House” to be fully representative of the user’s intent. This raises a further critical question: how does the system handle coreference resolution, and what impact does it have on the accuracy of dialogue state tracking? The paper does not address this potentially significant aspect of real-world dialogue systems.

Despite these weaknesses, the proposed method exhibits a promising direction for dialogue state tracking. The use of pre-trained word embeddings is a valuable contribution, and the specific composition approach is well-motivated and effectively implemented. The reported gains over the baseline are substantial and demonstrate the potential of this technique. 

To strengthen the paper, I recommend the authors:

*   **Expand the evaluation:** Include experiments on more complex and diverse dialogue datasets.
*   **Investigate slot tagging:** Analyze the benefits of incorporating a separate slot tagging component.
*   **Address coreference resolution:** Discuss how the system handles coreference, or acknowledge it as a limitation.
*   **Clarify examples:** Ensure all examples accurately represent the full dialogue state.



Overall, this is a solid paper with a compelling idea. Addressing the concerns outlined above would significantly enhance its contribution and impact. I believe this work has the potential to advance the field of dialogue state tracking, and with further refinement, could serve as a valuable building block for more sophisticated dialogue systems.
