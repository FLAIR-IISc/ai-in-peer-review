- Proposes NBT: uses pre-trained embeddings to represent slot values and composes them into distributed representations of utterances and dialogue context.  
- Shows consistent, significant improvements over delexicalization-based baselines on DSTC2 and WOZ 2.0.  
- Thoroughly investigates impact of embedding collections (XAVIER, GloVe, Paragram-SL999).  
- Strength: effectively leverages pre-trained embeddings to reduce reliance on hand-crafted lexicons and large annotated data.  
- Strength: matches lexicon-based performance when lexicons exist and outperforms when they do not, indicating scalability to complex domains.  
- Strength: ablation study shows semantically-specialized vectors (Paragram-SL999) yield superior performance.  
- Strength: jointly models SLU and DST while avoiding explicit delexicalization.  
- Weakness: evaluation limited to DSTC2 and WOZ 2.0; needs testing on larger, more diverse and challenging dialogues (multi-turn, implicit confirmations).  
- Weakness: context modeling is limited (only previous system act); longer-range/contextual methods (e.g., hierarchical RNNs) could improve results.  
- Weakness: example dialogue contains an error suggesting potential issues with coreference resolution handling.  
- Question: how does NBT perform if a separate slot tagging component is used to restrict similarity estimation to predicted spans?  
- Question: how does the model address or mitigate coreference resolution in multi-turn dialogues?  
- Question: what is the computational cost of NBT versus delexicalization baselines (training time and inference speed)?  
- Additional: paper is well-written, comparisons are fair, metrics appropriate, and statistical significance testing is provided.
