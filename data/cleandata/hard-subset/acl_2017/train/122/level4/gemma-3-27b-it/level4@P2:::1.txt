This paper introduces a novel dialogue state tracking (DST) approach leveraging pre-trained word embeddings to represent slot values and compose them into distributed representations of user utterances and dialogue context. The core idea – moving away from exact matching and hand-crafted lexicons – is compelling, particularly for scaling to more complex dialogue domains. Evaluation on two datasets demonstrates consistent and statistically significant improvements over strong delexicalization-based baselines. The exploration of different pre-trained embedding methods (XAVIER, GloVe, Paragram-SL999) is a valuable contribution, highlighting the importance of semantic specialization in these embeddings.

**Contributions:**

1. **Novel Neural Belief Tracking (NBT) Framework:** The paper presents a novel end-to-end neural framework for DST that directly utilizes pre-trained word embeddings, eliminating the need for delexicalization and its associated limitations. This is the primary and strongest contribution.
2. **Effective Integration of Context:** The model incorporates dialogue context through a mechanism that considers the preceding system acts, improving tracking accuracy, especially in scenarios requiring clarification or confirmation.
3. **Empirical Validation & Embedding Analysis:**  The thorough experimental evaluation on two datasets, coupled with the analysis of different pre-trained embedding strategies, provides strong empirical support for the proposed approach and identifies the importance of semantically-specialized word vectors.

**Strengths:**

1. **Strong Performance Gains:** The NBT models consistently outperform competitive delexicalization-based baselines on both datasets. This demonstrates the effectiveness of the proposed approach and its ability to learn meaningful representations from pre-trained embeddings.
2. **Elimination of Hand-Crafted Lexicons:** By removing the reliance on manually constructed semantic dictionaries, the NBT framework offers a more scalable and adaptable solution for DST, particularly in complex domains where lexicon creation is a significant bottleneck.
3. **Insightful Embedding Analysis:** The comparison of different pre-trained embedding types (XAVIER, GloVe, Paragram-SL999) reveals the crucial role of semantic specialization in achieving optimal performance, providing valuable guidance for future research.
4. **Clear and Concise Presentation:** The paper is generally well-written and clearly explains the NBT framework, its components, and the experimental setup.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Dataset Complexity:** While the experimental results are promising, the datasets used (DSTC2 and WOZ 2.0) may not fully represent the complexity of real-world dialogue systems. Evaluating the model on more challenging datasets with longer, more ambiguous conversations is crucial.
2. **Potential for Slot Span Focus:** The current approach considers the entire utterance when comparing it to slot-value pairs. Exploring a mechanism to focus attention specifically on the span of potential slot values within the utterance could improve performance, particularly when values do not have exact matches.
3. **Oversimplified Dialogue State Example:** The introductory example of the dialogue state lacks completeness (e.g., omitting "restaurant_name=The House").  Furthermore, the model’s ability to handle coreference resolution—essential for real-world dialogues—is not discussed or evaluated.
4. **Lack of Ablation Studies:** While different embedding types were tested, more comprehensive ablation studies exploring the impact of individual components within the NBT architecture (e.g., context modeling, representation learning) would provide deeper insights into the model's behavior.



**Questions to Authors:**

1.  How would the NBT model perform on datasets with more complex and ambiguous dialogues, such as those involving implicit confirmations, elliptical references, or multi-turn dependencies?
2.  Have you considered incorporating a mechanism to focus attention specifically on the spans of potential slot values within the user utterance, and if so, what were the results?
3.  How does the NBT framework handle coreference resolution, and what impact does it have on tracking accuracy?
4. Can you provide more detailed ablation study results showing the contribution of each component of the NBT architecture (e.g., context modeling, the different representation learning modules)?

**Additional Comments:**

- The notation and descriptions of the models are clear.
- The choice of evaluation metrics (joint goal accuracy and request accuracy) is appropriate for this task.
- It would be helpful to discuss the computational cost of the NBT model compared to the baselines.
