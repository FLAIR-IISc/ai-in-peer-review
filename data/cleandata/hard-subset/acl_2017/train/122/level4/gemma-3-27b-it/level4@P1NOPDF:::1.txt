This paper proposes a novel approach to dialogue state tracking that leverages pre-trained embeddings to represent slot values and learns to compose these into distributed representations of user utterances and dialogue context. Experiments on two datasets demonstrate consistent and significant improvements over a previous delexicalization-based baseline. The authors investigated several alternative approaches for pre-training word embeddings, including XAVIER, GloVe, and Program-SL999.

Despite the promising results, the datasets used appear somewhat limited, particularly given the stated motivation of improving generalization to more complex dialogue domains where delexicalization struggles. I question how the approach would perform with and without a separate slot tagging component when applied to more intricate dialogues. For instance, when calculating the similarity between an utterance and slot value pairs, the estimation could be constrained to the span of the slot values themselves – a useful technique even when the values don’t directly match.

I also found the examples presented in the introduction to be slightly misleading. Shouldn’t the dialogue state include “restaurant_name=The House”? This raises a further question regarding how coreference resolution impacts performance on this task.

Overall, the use of pre-trained word embeddings is an excellent idea, and the specific method employed for their integration is compelling.
