This paper presents a neural network-based framework for dialogue state tracking (DST), a crucial component in goal-oriented dialogue systems. The primary contribution of this work lies in learning representations for user utterances, system responses, and ontology entries, all initialized with pre-trained word vectors. Specifically, the authors compare two neural network architectures – a Neural Bag-of-Tokens Deep Neural Network (NBT-DNN) and a Neural Bag-of-Tokens Convolutional Neural Network (NBT-CNN) – for utterance representation learning. These learned representations are then combined and fed into a downstream network to predict binary values for each slot-value pair. Experiments demonstrate that the proposed framework significantly outperforms a baseline system employing a delexicalized approach. 

Overall, this is a sound contribution with a clearly defined goal, a reasonable approach, and demonstrably improved results. However, the paper’s organization hinders its clarity, particularly for readers unfamiliar with the DST domain. 

My primary recommendation is a restructuring of the initial sections. Currently, the paper lacks a formal definition of DST at the outset. This is exacerbated by the coupling of DST with Slot Labeling Understanding (SLU), which creates confusion. I suggest beginning with a general overview of a goal-oriented dialogue system architecture in Section 1. This should be followed by a focused problem definition of DST, clearly delineating its relationship to other system components such as Automatic Speech Recognition (ASR), SLU, and policy learning. This would provide crucial context for the proposed work.

Furthermore, a comprehensive list of notations should be included in an early section, ideally following the problem definition. Several symbols (e.g., *t<sub>q</sub>*, *t<sub>s</sub>*, *t<sub>v</sub>*) are used before they are defined, diminishing readability. 

Below are additional comments and questions for the authors to consider:

* **Joint Modeling Clarification:** The paper frequently refers to a “joint” model. If the proposed model cannot perform separate SLU, the term ‘joint’ is misleading, as it implies a capability to handle both tasks independently. Please clarify the extent of integration between DST and SLU.
* **Error Analysis & Comparison to DSTC2:**  Providing statistics on the number of errors corrected in the original DSTC2 dataset would be valuable. If the number of corrections is not substantial, including comparisons to other published work from the DSTC2 challenge, using the same dataset, would strengthen the results and position this work within the broader research landscape.
* **Sequential Modeling:** The current approach utilizes bag-of-tokens models for utterance representation. Given the recent successes of Recurrent Neural Networks (RNNs) and Long Short-Term Memory (LSTMs) in SLU and related sequence modeling tasks, have the authors considered exploring these architectures to capture the sequential aspects of user utterances for DST? This could potentially further improve performance.
* **Baseline Resource Cost:** More detail regarding the semantic dictionary used in the baseline approach would be beneficial. Specifically, understanding the effort and cost associated with manually building such a resource would provide valuable context.
* **Qualitative Examples:** Including illustrative examples of instances that the baseline system incorrectly predicted but the proposed model successfully handled would significantly improve the paper's understandability and demonstrate the practical benefits of the approach. These examples would offer concrete evidence of the model’s strengths.



In conclusion, this paper presents a promising approach to dialogue state tracking. Addressing the organizational concerns and considering the suggestions outlined above would significantly enhance the clarity, impact, and overall quality of the work. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions.
