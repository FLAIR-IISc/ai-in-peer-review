This paper presents a novel neural network-based framework for dialogue state tracking (DST). The core contribution lies in learning distributed representations of user utterances, system outputs, and ontology entries using pre-trained word vectors, effectively bypassing the need for manually crafted lexicons or large-scale annotated data required by traditional approaches. The authors explore two neural network architectures for utterance representation: NBT-DNN and NBT-CNN. These representations are then integrated to make binary classification decisions for each slot-value pair. Experimental results demonstrate significant performance improvements compared to a strong delexicalization-based baseline, particularly in low-resource scenarios. 

This is a strong contribution, presenting a well-motivated approach with promising results. The paper addresses a key limitation of existing DST systems – their reliance on either extensive data or laborious manual effort for lexicon creation – and offers a scalable alternative. 

**Strengths:**

1. **Addresses a Significant Problem:** The paper tackles a critical challenge in DST: scaling to complex domains without requiring large annotated datasets or extensive manual lexicon construction. The reliance on pre-trained word vectors is a compelling approach to address this issue, offering a path towards more adaptable and deployable dialogue systems.
2. **Strong Empirical Results:** The NBT models consistently outperform the delexicalization baseline on both datasets, and achieve comparable performance to models *with* handcrafted lexicons. This is a substantial achievement, particularly in the low-resource setting where the benefits of avoiding manual lexicon creation are most pronounced. The statistical significance reported further supports these findings.
3. **Novel Architecture:** The proposed NBT architecture, combining representation learning with semantic decoding and context modeling, represents a thoughtful approach to the DST problem. The exploration of both DNN and CNN architectures for utterance representation provides valuable insight into effective methods for capturing linguistic information.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Organization and Clarity:** The paper's structure could be improved to enhance readability, especially for readers unfamiliar with the DST field. The initial sections lack a clear, concise definition of DST and its place within a broader dialogue system architecture.
2. **Notational Clarity:** The introduction of symbols (e.g., *t<sub>q</sub>*, *t<sub>s</sub>*, *t<sub>v</sub>*) before their definition creates confusion. A dedicated section outlining all notation would significantly improve clarity.
3. **Limited Discussion of Joint Modeling:** While the paper refers to "joint SLU/DST", it doesn't fully clarify the extent to which the model inherently performs SLU. A more explicit discussion of the model’s capabilities regarding separate SLU functionality would be beneficial.

**Questions to Authors:**

1. Can the model be readily adapted to perform separate SLU, or is its strength primarily in the joint modeling approach? If separate SLU is not possible, reconsider the use of the term “joint.”
2. What was the magnitude of corrections made to the original DSTC2 dataset? Including a comparison to existing DSTC2 results using the corrected dataset would provide valuable context.
3. Did you explore recurrent neural networks (RNNs) or LSTMs for learning utterance representations? Given their success in sequence modeling tasks, how do you justify the choice of DNN and CNN architectures?
4. Could you provide more detail regarding the size and construction process of the semantic dictionary used by the baseline model? This would help quantify the effort involved in creating such resources.
5. Can you provide specific examples of utterances or scenarios where the NBT models correctly predicted the dialogue state, while the baseline model failed? This would illustrate the practical benefits of the proposed approach.



**Additional Comments:**

* It would be helpful to briefly mention the computational cost associated with the NBT models compared to the baseline.
* Consider adding a visualization of the learned representations to provide further insight into how the model is capturing semantic information.
* The sentence "This is a quality work…" is inappropriate for a review and should be removed.
