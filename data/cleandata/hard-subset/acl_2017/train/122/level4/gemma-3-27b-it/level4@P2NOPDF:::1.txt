This paper presents a novel dialogue state tracking approach that leverages pre-trained word embeddings to represent slot values. The key idea is to compose these embeddings into distributed representations capturing both user utterances and the dialogue context. The experimental results on two datasets demonstrate consistent and significant improvements over a strong delexicalization-based baseline. The authors thoughtfully explore the impact of different pre-training methods for the word embeddings, including XAVIER, GloVe, and Program-SL999.

Despite the strengths of this work, some limitations should be addressed. While the motivation for employing embeddings – improved generalization to complex dialogue domains where delexicalization struggles – is sound, the evaluation datasets appear relatively constrained. It would be beneficial to see a comparison of the approach’s performance *with* and *without* a dedicated slot tagging component, particularly when applied to more challenging, complex dialogues. A potentially effective refinement involves focusing similarity computations between utterances and slot value pairs specifically on the span of those slot values, even in cases where direct value matches don’t occur. 

Furthermore, the example presented in the introduction is slightly unclear. Given the context, the dialogue state should arguably include “restaurant_name=The House.” This observation raises a related question regarding the impact of coreference resolution on this task. How does resolving references to entities within the dialogue affect the accuracy of state tracking using this approach? Addressing these considerations would strengthen the paper's contribution.

Overall, the utilization of pre-trained word embeddings is a compelling concept, and the specific methodology proposed by the authors is promising. The observed performance gains are encouraging and suggest a worthwhile direction for future research in dialogue state tracking.
