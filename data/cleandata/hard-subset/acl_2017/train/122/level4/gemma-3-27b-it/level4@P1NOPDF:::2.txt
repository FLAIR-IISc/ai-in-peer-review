This paper presents a neural network-based framework for dialogue state tracking. The main contribution of this work lies in learning representations of user utterances, system outputs, and ontology entries, all leveraging pre-trained word vectors. Specifically, the authors compare two neural network models for utterance representation: NBT-DNN and NBT-CNN. These learned representations are combined and fed into a downstream network, which makes a binary decision for each slot-value pair. Experiments demonstrate that the proposed framework achieves significant performance improvements over a delexicalized baseline. 

Overall, this is a solid contribution with a clear objective, a reasonable approach, and improved results compared to prior work. However, the paper’s organization could be improved to more effectively convey the details, particularly to readers less familiar with the field. 

First, a more formal definition of dialogue state tracking (DST) should be provided at the beginning of the paper. Currently, its definition is somewhat unclear and becomes more confusing when discussed in conjunction with spoken language understanding (SLU). I suggest presenting a general architecture of a dialogue system in Section 1, followed by a precise problem definition of DST, highlighting its relationship to other components such as automatic speech recognition (ASR), SLU, and policy learning. 

Furthermore, defining all notations used throughout the paper in an earlier section would significantly improve readability. Several symbols (e.g., *t<sub>q</sub>*, *t<sub>s</sub>*, *t<sub>v</sub>*) are used before they are formally introduced.

Below are several additional comments and questions:

- Is it possible to perform separate SLU using this model? If not, the term ‘joint’ may be misleading, implying the model can handle both tasks independently.
- Could you provide some statistics regarding the number of errors corrected from the original DSTC2 dataset? If the number is not substantial, the experiments could include comparisons with other published work, including DSTC2 submissions that used the same dataset.
- Have you considered using recurrent neural networks (RNNs) or LSTMs to capture sequential information when learning utterance representations? Given the recent successes of these recurrent networks in SLU, they may also prove effective for DST.
- Providing more details about the semantic dictionary used in the baseline would help illustrate the cost associated with building such resources manually.
- It would be beneficial to include example cases that were incorrectly predicted by the baseline but correctly predicted by your proposed models. This would offer valuable insight into the strengths of your approach.
