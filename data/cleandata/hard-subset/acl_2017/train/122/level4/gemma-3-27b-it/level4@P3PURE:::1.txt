This paper proposes a novel approach to dialogue state tracking that leverages pre-trained embeddings to represent slot values and learns to compose them into distributed representations of user utterances and dialogue context. Experiments on two datasets demonstrate consistent and significant improvements over a previous delexicalization-based baseline. The authors investigate several alternative approaches for pre-training word embeddings, including XAVIER, GloVe, and Program-SL999.

While a key motivation for using embeddings is to improve generalization to more complex dialogue domains where delexicalization may not scale effectively, the datasets used in this work appear limited in scope. It is unclear how the approach would perform with and without a separate slot tagging component when applied to more complex dialogues. For instance, when computing similarity between utterances and slot value pairs, the estimation could be constrained to the span of the potential slot values – a technique applicable even when direct value matches are absent. 

Additionally, the examples presented in the introduction are somewhat misleading. The provided dialogue state appears incomplete; for example, it should also include “restaurant_name=The House.” This raises a further question regarding how the resolution of coreferences impacts performance on this task.

Overall, the use of pre-trained word embeddings is a strong idea, and the specific approach employed is promising and exciting.
