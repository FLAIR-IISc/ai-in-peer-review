This paper proposes a novel approach for dialogue state tracking that leverages pre-trained embeddings to represent slot values and learns to compose them into distributed representations of user utterances and dialogue context. Experiments on two datasets demonstrate consistent and significant improvements over delexicalization-based baselines. The impact of different pre-trained word embedding collections (XAVIER, GloVe, Paragram-SL999) was also investigated.

**Strengths:**

The primary strength of this work is the successful application of pre-trained word vectors to improve the language understanding capability of belief tracking models. This is a significant contribution, as it addresses the limitations of both traditional approaches relying on large amounts of annotated data and those dependent on manually constructed lexicons. The results demonstrate that the proposed Neural Belief Tracker (NBT) matches the performance of lexicon-based models and substantially outperforms them when lexicons are unavailable. 

A further strength lies in the NBT’s ability to jointly model SLU and DST, learning to handle lexical variation without requiring hand-crafted resources. This streamlined approach simplifies the development process and enhances the scalability of the system.

The ablation study comparing different word vector collections highlights the importance of semantic specialization in word embeddings for this task, providing valuable insight into feature representation.

**Weaknesses:**

While the use of embeddings is motivated by the goal of scaling to more complex domains, the evaluation is currently limited to relatively constrained restaurant search datasets. It’s unclear how well the approach would generalize to dialogues with more diverse topics, longer turns, and greater linguistic complexity. I wonder how the approach would perform when augmented with a separate slot tagging component, particularly in more complex dialogues. Specifically, restricting similarity estimation between utterances and slot-value pairs to the span of those values – even when an exact match isn’t present – could be beneficial.

The example dialogue presented in the introduction is somewhat misleading. The belief state should arguably also include “restaurant_name=The House” after the third turn. This raises a broader question about how the model handles coreference resolution, a critical component in many real-world dialogues. It would be useful to understand the model's capabilities in resolving references to previously mentioned entities. 

The paper doesn't fully explore the computational cost of the proposed method, particularly the NBT-CNN variant. A discussion of training and inference time relative to baseline methods would be valuable.

**Questions to Authors:**

1.  How does the performance of the NBT model degrade as the complexity of the dialogue domain increases (e.g., number of slots, slot values, dialogue turns)?
2.  Could you elaborate on the model's ability to handle coreference resolution? Are there any specific strategies employed to address this challenge?
3.  What is the computational overhead of the NBT-CNN model compared to the NBT-DNN model and the baseline methods?

**Additional Comments:**

The writing is generally clear, but some sentences are slightly convoluted. Consider simplifying the phrasing for improved readability. (e.g., the sentences beginning "To best of our knowledge..." and "The primary motivation...") The use of "statis-tically significant" is slightly repetitive; consider using alternatives like "significant" or "significant improvement".
