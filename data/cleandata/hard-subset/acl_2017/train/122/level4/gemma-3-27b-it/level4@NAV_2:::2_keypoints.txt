1. Paper proposes neural-network DST using NBT-DNN and NBT-CNN with pre-trained word vectors to reduce hand-crafted lexical resources.  
2. Method learns representations for user utterances, system outputs, and ontology entries, integrated for binary slot-value classification.  
3. Shows significant improvements over a delexicalization baseline on DSTC2 and WOZ 2.0.  
4. Strength: achieves competitive performance without manually constructed semantic lexicons, aiding scalability.  
5. Strength: effectively leverages pre-trained Paragram-SL999 vectors and shows importance of semantic specialization.  
6. Strength: jointly models SLU and DST, reducing error propagation from pipeline architectures.  
7. Strength: solid evaluation on two datasets with comparisons to established baselines.  
8. Weakness: lacks sufficient introductory context on DST and its place in dialogue-system pipelines.  
9. Weakness: notational clarity issues—symbols introduced before formal definitions.  
10. Weakness: limited analysis of robustness to ASR errors and model behavior with noisy transcriptions.  
11. Weakness: ambiguous use of “joint SLU/DST”—unclear if end-to-end SLU labels are required.  
12. Question: how many errors were corrected in DSTC2 and how does that affect comparability to other results?  
13. Question: what is the size and creation effort of the semantic dictionary used with the baseline?  
14. Question: were RNNs/LSTMs explored for utterance representations, and if not, why?  
15. Question: can you provide example utterances where NBT outperforms the baseline and explain why?
