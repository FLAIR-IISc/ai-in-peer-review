This paper introduces a neural network framework for dialogue state tracking. 
The primary contribution lies in the learning of representations for user utterances, system responses, and ontology entries, leveraging pre-trained word vectors for each.
Specifically, the authors evaluate two neural network architectures for utterance representation: NBT-DNN and NBT-CNN.
These learned representations are then integrated and used within a downstream network to classify slot-value pairs as binary decisions.
Experimental results demonstrate substantial performance gains over a delexicalized baseline approach.
Overall, this is a solid piece of work with a clearly defined objective, a sound approach, and positive results building upon prior research.
However, the paper's organization could be improved to better convey the technical details, particularly for readers less familiar with the field.
A more formal definition of dialogue state tracking should be presented upfront. The current presentation, particularly when discussed alongside semantic language understanding (SLU), lacks clarity and could be confusing.
I recommend restructuring the paper to begin with a general dialogue system architecture in Section 1, followed by a dedicated problem definition for DST, explicitly outlining its relationship to components like automatic speech recognition (ASR), SLU, and policy learning.
Furthermore, defining all notations used throughout the paper in an earlier section would significantly enhance readability. Several symbols (e.g., *t<sub>q</sub>*, *t<sub>s</sub>*, *t<sub>v</sub>*) appear before their definitions.

Here are additional questions and suggestions:
- Is it possible to perform separate SLU using this model? If not, the use of the term 'joint' may be misleading, as it implies the model can handle both tasks independently.
- Could the authors provide statistics regarding the number of errors corrected in the original DSTC2 dataset? If this number is not substantial, including comparisons with other published work, including DSTC2 submissions utilizing the same dataset, would be beneficial.
- Have the authors considered using recurrent neural networks (RNNs) or long short-term memory networks (LSTMs) to capture sequential information when learning utterance representations? Given the success of these architectures in SLU, they may prove effective for DST as well.
- Providing additional details about the semantic dictionary used in the baseline would help illustrate the manual effort required to construct such resources.
- Including example cases where the baseline model failed but the proposed models succeeded would be highly valuable.
