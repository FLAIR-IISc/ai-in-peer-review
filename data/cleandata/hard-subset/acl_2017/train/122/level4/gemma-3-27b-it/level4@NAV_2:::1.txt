This paper proposes a novel approach for dialogue state tracking that leverages pre-trained embeddings to represent slot values and learns to compose them into distributed representations of user utterances and dialogue context. Experiments performed on two datasets demonstrate consistent and significant improvements over delexicalization-based baselines. The impact of different pre-trained word embedding collections (XAVIER, GloVe, Paragram-SL999) was also thoroughly investigated.

**Strengths:**
The primary strength of this work lies in its effective use of pre-trained word embeddings to overcome the limitations of traditional dialogue state tracking methods reliant on hand-crafted lexicons or large amounts of annotated data. The consistent performance gains across both datasets – matching delexicalization-based models when lexicons are available and significantly outperforming them when they are not – are compelling. This demonstrates the potential for scaling belief tracking models to more complex domains where lexicon creation is infeasible. A second notable strength is the ablation study exploring different word vector spaces; the clear demonstration that semantically-specialized vectors (Paragram-SL999) yield superior performance highlights the importance of semantic representation quality. Finally, the architecture’s ability to jointly model SLU and DST, while avoiding explicit delexicalization, is a significant contribution to the field.

**Weaknesses:**
Despite the promising results, the evaluation is somewhat limited by the datasets employed. While representing a good starting point, DSTC2 and WOZ 2.0 may not fully capture the complexity and variability of real-world dialogues. It would be valuable to assess the model’s performance on larger, more diverse datasets, and potentially on more challenging dialogue scenarios (e.g., multi-turn disambiguation, implicit confirmations). A potential area for improvement is the context modeling component. While considering the previous system act is a step in the right direction, exploring more sophisticated methods for capturing longer-range contextual dependencies (e.g., hierarchical recurrent networks) could further enhance performance. Finally, the example dialogue provided in the introduction is slightly inaccurate – the belief state after "Where is it?" should include "restaurant_name=The House" – and this raises a broader question about how the system handles coreference resolution, a critical aspect of real-world dialogue.

**Questions to Authors:**
1. How does the performance of the NBT model change when a separate slot tagging component is incorporated, specifically focusing on limiting similarity estimation to the spans of predicted slot values?
2. Can the authors elaborate on how the model addresses or mitigates challenges related to coreference resolution in multi-turn dialogues?
3. What is the computational cost of the NBT model compared to the baseline delexicalization-based models, particularly in terms of training time and inference speed?

**Additional Comments:**
The paper is well-written and clearly articulates the motivation, methodology, and results. The comparison to existing approaches is fair and thorough. The choice of metrics is appropriate, and the statistical significance tests provide valuable insights.
