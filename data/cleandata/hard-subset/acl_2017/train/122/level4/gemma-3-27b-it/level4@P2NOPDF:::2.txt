This paper introduces a neural network framework for dialogue state tracking (DST). The primary contribution lies in learning representations for user utterances, system responses, and ontology entries, leveraging pre-trained word vectors. The authors investigate two neural network architectures for utterance representation – a Neural Bag-of-Tokens with a Deep Neural Network (NBT-DNN) and a Neural Bag-of-Tokens with a Convolutional Neural Network (NBT-CNN). These learned representations are then combined and utilized in a downstream network to predict binary values for slot-value pairs. Experimental results demonstrate significant performance gains compared to a delexicalized baseline.

Overall, this is a sound piece of work with a clearly defined objective, a reasonable approach, and demonstrable improvements over existing methods. However, the paper’s organization could be improved to enhance clarity, particularly for readers less familiar with the field. 

Specifically, a more formal definition of DST is needed early in the paper – ideally in Section 1. The current presentation conflates DST with Semantic Language Understanding (SLU), which can be confusing. I recommend beginning with a general overview of a dialogue system architecture (currently described in Section 2), followed by a focused definition of DST and its relationship to other core components like Automatic Speech Recognition (ASR), SLU, and policy learning. This would establish a stronger foundation for understanding the paper’s contribution.

Furthermore, defining all notations used throughout the paper in an earlier section would significantly improve readability. Currently, several symbols (e.g., *t<sub>q</sub>*, *t<sub>s</sub>*, *t<sub>v</sub>*) are introduced before they are formally defined, which disrupts the flow of understanding.

I have the following additional questions and suggestions:

*   **Joint vs. Separate SLU:** Is it possible to perform SLU independently using this model? If not, the term "joint" may be misleading, as it implies the model can handle both tasks separately. Clarification on this point is important.
*   **Error Analysis & Comparisons:** Could the authors provide statistics on the number of errors corrected compared to the original DSTC2 dataset? If the number of corrections is relatively small, including comparisons to other published DSTC2 entries using the same dataset would strengthen the evaluation.
*   **Sequential Modeling:** Have the authors considered using Recurrent Neural Networks (RNNs) or Long Short-Term Memory networks (LSTMs) to capture the sequential information within user utterances? Given the recent success of these models in SLU, they may also prove effective for DST.
*   **Baseline Resource Cost:** Providing more details regarding the semantic dictionary used in the baseline would help illustrate the cost and effort involved in creating such resources manually. This context is valuable for understanding the advantages of the proposed approach.
*   **Qualitative Examples:** Including example dialogue turns that were incorrectly predicted by the baseline but correctly handled by the proposed models would provide valuable qualitative insight into the model’s improvements.
