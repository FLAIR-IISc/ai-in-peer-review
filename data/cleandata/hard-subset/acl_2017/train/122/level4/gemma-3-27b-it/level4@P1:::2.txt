This paper presents a neural network-based framework for dialogue state tracking. The main contribution of this work is learning representations of user utterances, system outputs, and ontology entries, all based on pre-trained word vectors. The authors compare two different neural network models for utterance representation: NBT-DNN and NBT-CNN. These learned representations are combined and used in a downstream network to make binary decisions for each slot-value pair. Experiments demonstrate that the proposed framework achieves significant performance improvements compared to a delexicalization-based baseline. This is a quality work with a clear goal, reasonable approach, and improved results compared to previous studies.

However, the paper’s organization could be improved to more effectively convey details, particularly to readers unfamiliar with the field. First, a more formal definition of dialogue state tracking (DST) should be provided earlier in the paper. The explanation is currently unclear and becomes more confusing when discussing the coupling with spoken language understanding (SLU). I suggest providing a general architecture of a dialogue system in Section 1, followed by a problem definition of DST, focusing on its relationships with other components including automatic speech recognition (ASR), SLU, and policy learning. Furthermore, readability would be enhanced by defining all notations used throughout the paper in an earlier section. Several symbols (e.g., *t<sub>q</sub>*, *t<sub>s</sub>*, *t<sub>v</sub>*) are used before their descriptions.

Below are further comments and questions:

*   Would it be possible to perform separate SLU with this model? If not, the term “joint” could be misleading, implying the model is capable of handling both tasks independently.
*   Could you provide statistics regarding the number of errors corrected in the original DSTC2 dataset? If the number is not substantial, the experiment could include comparisons with other published work, including DSTC2 entries, using the same cleaned dataset.
*   What are your thoughts on using recurrent neural networks (RNNs) or long short-term memory networks (LSTMs) to learn sequential aspects in utterance representations? Given the recent successes of these recurrent networks in SLU, they could potentially be effective for DST as well.
*   More details about the semantic dictionary used with the baseline would help illustrate the cost of manually building such resources.
*   Providing examples of predictions incorrectly made by the baseline but correctly handled by your proposed models would be insightful.
