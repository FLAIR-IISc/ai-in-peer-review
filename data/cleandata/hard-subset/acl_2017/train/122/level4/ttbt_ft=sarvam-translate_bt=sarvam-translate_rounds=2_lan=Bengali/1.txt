- **Strength:**
This paper proposes a new method for monitoring dialog state that benefits from presenting slot values through pre-trained embedding and learns to create distributed representations of user utterances and dialog context. Experiments conducted on two datasets show consistent and significant improvements from previous de-contextualization-based methods. Alternative methods of word embedding (e.g., XAVIER, GloVe, Program-SL999) have been studied.
- **Weakness:**
Although one of the main purposes of using embedding is to generalize to more complex dialog domains where de-contextualization may not extend, the datasets used seem limited. I am wondering how this method would work in more complex dialogs with and without a separate slot tagging component. For example, when calculating the similarity between a pair of utterances and slot values, one might actually limit the inference to the span of the slot values. This should apply even if the values do not match.
I think the examples in the introduction are misleading; shouldn't the dialog state include "restaurant_name=The House"? This raises another question: how does the resolution of coreference affect this work?

Overall, the use of pre-trained word embedding is a great idea, and the specific method for using them is exciting.
