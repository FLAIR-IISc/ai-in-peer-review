Review-
- Strengths:
This paper introduces a novel method for dialogue state tracking that leverages pre-trained embeddings to represent slot values and learns to compose them into distributed representations of user utterances and dialogue context. Experiments on two datasets show consistent, significant improvements over a prior delexicalization-based baseline. The study also explores alternative embedding pre-training methods (e.g., XAVIER, GloVe, Program-SL999).

- Weaknesses:
Although a primary motivation for using embeddings is to generalize to more complex dialogue domains where delexicalization may not scale, the chosen datasets seem limited. I am interested in how the approach compares with and without a separate slot-tagging component on more complex dialogues. For example, when computing similarity between an utterance and slot–value pairs, the estimation could be restricted to the span corresponding to the slot values, which should still apply even when surface forms do not match. The introductory example appears misleading—should the dialogue state not also include "restaurant_name=The House"? This also raises the question of how coreference resolution affects the task.

- General Discussion:
Overall, the use of pre-trained word embeddings is a strong idea, and the specific mechanism for exploiting them is compelling.
