This paper proposes a neural network-based framework for dialogue state tracking (DST) that learns distributed representations of user utterances, system outputs, and ontology entries from pre-trained word vectors. Two alternative utterance encoders are evaluated—NBT-DNN and NBT-CNN—and the resulting representations are combined and fed into a downstream classifier that makes a binary decision for each slot–value pair. On the DSTC2 dataset, the approach achieves substantial gains over a delexicalization-based baseline.

Strengths:
- Clear goal and motivation: reducing reliance on hand-engineered semantic dictionaries and leveraging pre-trained embeddings to improve generalization.
- Sensible modeling choices with a clean decomposition: separate encoders for utterances, system outputs, and ontology entries, followed by a straightforward binary decision layer for slot–value pairs.
- Empirical improvements over a strong delexicalized baseline on DSTC2, suggesting the method’s practical effectiveness.
- Comparison of two neural encoders (DNN vs. CNN) provides insight into representation learning for DST.

Major concerns and suggestions:
- Problem setup and organization: The paper needs an early, formal definition of DST and a clear description of how it relates to other dialogue system components. Present a high-level architecture in the introduction, followed by a precise DST problem definition (state variables, slots, values, belief representation, and update step) and a brief explanation of interfaces with ASR, SLU, and policy learning. Coupling the initial presentation of DST with SLU without this context is confusing for readers not already familiar with the area.
- Terminology: The term “joint” may be misleading if the model does not explicitly perform SLU alongside DST. If “joint” refers only to jointly modeling utterances, system outputs, and ontology entries within one network, please state this unambiguously early in the paper. If the framework cannot perform a separate SLU task, please avoid implying multi-task capability.
- Notation: Define all symbols in an early subsection and use them consistently. Several notations (e.g., tq, ts, tv) appear before their definitions, which hinders readability.
- Dataset corrections: Provide statistics on the number and types of corrections made to DSTC2 (e.g., annotation errors, ontology mismatches). If the corrections are modest, also report results on the original dataset and/or include comparisons with published DSTC2 entries to contextualize the gains. Clarify whether the corrected version will be publicly available for benchmarking.
- Baseline details: Describe the semantic dictionary used in the baseline (coverage, construction cost, examples) to highlight the manual effort your method avoids. This will help quantify the practical benefit of the proposed approach.
- Modeling alternatives: Given the strong performance of sequence models in SLU, discuss the use of RNNs/LSTMs for capturing sequential dependencies in utterances and system acts. If feasible, include an RNN/LSTM encoder as a comparison; otherwise, provide a rationale for preferring DNN/CNN encoders here (e.g., speed, data size, overfitting risks).
- Analysis and interpretability: Include qualitative examples where the baseline fails but your models succeed, and vice versa. Such examples would illuminate what the learned representations capture (e.g., paraphrase robustness, synonym handling, or resolution of lexical ambiguity).
- Experimental clarity and reproducibility: Specify key training details (source of pre-trained embeddings, whether embeddings are fine-tuned, optimization settings, regularization, early stopping). Make explicit how negatives are constructed for the binary decisions, how “none”/“don’t care” values are handled, and the set of slot–value pairs considered per turn.

Specific questions:
- Can the framework be adapted to perform SLU separately from DST? If not, please clarify the scope to avoid interpreting “joint” as SLU+DST.
- How many labels and/or transcripts were corrected in DSTC2, and in which categories? Are corrected files publicly available?
- What is the size/coverage of the semantic dictionary used in the delexicalized baseline, and what is the estimated manual effort to build it for a new domain?
- Have you tried RNN/LSTM encoders for utterances/system outputs? If yes, what were the outcomes? If not, what trade-offs led you to prefer DNN/CNN?
- Please provide several representative cases where your models corrected baseline errors.

Overall assessment:
This is a solid and timely contribution to neural dialogue state tracking. Framing DST as binary decisions over slot–value pairs while learning representations for utterances, system outputs, and ontology entries is a coherent, scalable approach that yields clear gains over delexicalized baselines. The main shortcomings lie in exposition and missing experimental details rather than in the core idea. Strengthening the problem definition and organization, clarifying terminology and notation, documenting dataset corrections, and adding analysis/examples would significantly improve readability, reproducibility, and the impact of the work.
