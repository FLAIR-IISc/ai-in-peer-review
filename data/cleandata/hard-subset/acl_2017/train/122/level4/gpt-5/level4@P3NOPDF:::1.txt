Summary
The paper presents a dialogue state tracking approach that represents slot values using pre-trained word embeddings and learns to compose these with representations of user utterances and dialogue context. The model estimates similarity between utterance/context encodings and slot–value representations to update the dialogue state. Experiments on two datasets show consistent and substantial improvements over a strong delexicalization-based baseline. The authors also compare multiple sources of pre-trained embeddings (e.g., XAVIER, GloVe, Program-SL999).

Strengths
- Clear and timely motivation: moving beyond delexicalization to leverage distributed semantics for better generalization, especially to paraphrases and lexical variation.
- Novelty in treating slot values as learned/initialized vectors and learning to compose utterance and context representations that align with these values.
- Empirical gains are consistent across two datasets and reported as significant relative to prior delexicalization-based methods, suggesting robustness.
- Systematic exploration of alternative pre-training choices for word embeddings, which is practically useful and adds depth to the study.
- Conceptually simple approach that could transfer across domains with minimal manual lexicon engineering.

Weaknesses and questions
- Limited evaluation scope: While the motivation emphasizes scaling to more complex domains where delexicalization struggles, the datasets used appear modest in size and complexity. It remains unclear how the method performs when the ontology is larger, slot values are highly compositional (e.g., multi-word or hierarchical values), and paraphrasing is more varied.
- Interaction with slot tagging: The paper positions the model as an alternative to delexicalization, but many production systems combine semantic similarity with slot tagging. A direct comparison with and without an explicit slot tagging component would clarify the trade-offs. In particular, when computing similarity for a slot–value pair, restricting attention to the utterance span hypothesized to express that slot (via a tagger or learned attention) could reduce noise and improve precision, even when surface forms do not match.
- Example clarity and state definition: The introductory example seems to omit “restaurant_name=The House” from the dialogue state. If the system aims to track entity names, this should be reflected. If not, clarifying the target ontology and which slots are tracked would help readers interpret results and examples consistently.
- Coreference and context modeling: How is coreference handled, if at all? In multi-turn dialogues, resolving pronouns and nominal references (e.g., “that place,” “the one you mentioned”) is crucial for accurate state tracking. Please discuss whether your context encoder is sufficient for such cases, whether an external coreference or entity resolution module could be integrated, and how that might affect performance.
- Robustness to unseen or rare values: How does the model handle out-of-vocabulary slot values or values unseen during training? Are value representations composed from constituent word embeddings (e.g., averaging) or learned as atomic vectors? Clarifying this would strengthen the claims about generalization.
- Details on embedding sources and fine-tuning: For the pre-trained embeddings (XAVIER, GloVe, Program-SL999), please specify training corpora, dimensionalities, whether they are fixed or fine-tuned, and how sensitive results are to these choices. A brief ablation (e.g., random vs. pre-trained, fixed vs. fine-tuned) would be informative.
- Evaluation depth: Beyond aggregate accuracy, a breakdown by slot, value frequency, and utterance length/complexity would help diagnose where the method shines or struggles. Error analysis focusing on near-miss semantic confusions (e.g., “cheap” vs. “inexpensive,” or similarly named restaurants) would add insight.

Suggestions for improvement
- Add a comparison that augments your model with a lightweight slot tagger or span predictor to restrict similarity computation to plausible spans, and report whether this improves performance or reduces spurious matches.
- Evaluate on (or at least simulate) a more complex domain with a larger ontology and richer paraphrasing. If new data collection is infeasible, consider constructing a composite or expanded test where multiple slots interact or values require compositional interpretation.
- Clarify ontology coverage and state definition in the examples; ensure that entity names (e.g., restaurant_name) are tracked if they are part of the intended state.
- Discuss or experiment with simple coreference strategies (e.g., pronoun resolution heuristics, entity memory) to show the model’s behavior in the presence of anaphora.
- Provide implementation details: value representation construction for multi-word values, handling of stopwords within values, OOV strategy, and whether the model can accommodate novel values at test time.
- Report statistical significance and include per-slot metrics and calibration details (e.g., thresholding or ranking procedures). If feasible, add qualitative nearest-neighbor analyses showing which utterance snippets most strongly activate each value.

Overall assessment
This is a well-motivated and promising approach to dialogue state tracking that advances beyond delexicalization by leveraging pre-trained embeddings and learned composition. The empirical gains are encouraging and the exploration of different pre-training sources is valuable. The primary limitation is the restricted evaluation scope relative to the paper’s stated motivation; stronger evidence on more complex domains and an analysis of interactions with slot tagging and coreference would substantially strengthen the contribution. With these clarifications and additional experiments (even partial), the paper would make a solid addition to the literature.
