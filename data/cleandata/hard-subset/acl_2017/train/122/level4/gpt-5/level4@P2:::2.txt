Summary and contributions:
This paper proposes the Neural Belief Tracker (NBT), a data-driven framework for dialogue state tracking that reasons over pre-trained word embeddings instead of hand-crafted semantic lexicons or separate SLU outputs. The model composes distributed representations of user utterances, system acts, and ontology items and makes a binary decision for each candidate slot–value at every turn. Two variants are compared: NBT-DNN (sum of n-gram embeddings with a shallow network) and NBT-CNN (convolutional filters over n-grams).

As I see them, the primary contributions are:
1) A joint SLU/DST framework that removes the need for delexicalization and semantic dictionaries by operating directly in a semantically specialized embedding space, with explicit mechanisms for semantic decoding and context gating.
2) An effective context model that conditions on preceding system acts (request/confirm) via learned similarity gates, improving handling of implicit confirmations and pronominal/elliptical responses.
3) Strong empirical validation on DSTC2 and WOZ 2.0, including statistically significant gains over delexicalized baselines and an informative analysis showing the importance of semantically specialized embeddings (Paragram-SL999) over GloVe or random initialization.

Strengths:
- Significant reduction of manual effort and improved scalability: By eliminating semantic dictionaries and relying on pre-trained, semantically specialized embeddings, NBT addresses a key bottleneck in scaling to larger domains. The results show parity with lexicon-enhanced models and clear gains when such lexicons are unavailable.
- Well-motivated architectural design: The decomposition into representation learning, semantic decoding (element-wise interaction between utterance and slot–value representations), and context modeling (gated by similarity to system acts) is coherent and directly addresses common DST challenges (lexical variation, implicit feedback).
- Solid empirical evidence across datasets: The model achieves statistically significant improvements in joint goal accuracy and request accuracy on both DSTC2 and WOZ 2.0, with larger gains on WOZ (richer language, no ASR noise). The ablation on word vector sources convincingly demonstrates that semantically specialized embeddings are beneficial, especially under ASR noise.
- Practical training choices and generalization: Keeping embeddings fixed and sharing parameters across values/slots aids generalization to unseen paraphrases (e.g., inexpensive vs. cheap) and reduces data requirements, an important consideration for real deployments.

Weaknesses:
- Organization and formalism: The paper would benefit from an earlier, formal definition of the DST task and its interface with ASR, SLU, and the dialogue manager. Introducing the overall SDS architecture in the Introduction (rather than Background) and consolidating notation up front would reduce reader confusion. Several symbols (e.g., tq, ts, tv) appear before they are defined.
- Ambiguity around “joint” SLU/DST: It is unclear whether the model can function as a standalone SLU component (i.e., outputting slot–value detections independent of belief updates) or only as an integrated DST module. The term “joint” could be misread as handling both tasks end-to-end versus tightly coupling SLU-like detection with DST decisions.
- Dataset corrections and comparability: The paper mentions manual correction of DSTC2 transcription errors but provides no statistics. Without quantifying the extent of corrections, it is difficult to assess comparability with published DSTC2 results or the impact of the cleaning on performance.
- Limited exploration of sequential modeling for utterances: Given the success of RNN/LSTM architectures in SLU, the choice to use only DNN/CNN encoders for utterance representations deserves deeper justification or comparison. Sequence models might better capture long-range dependencies and compositionality, especially in WOZ-like settings.
- Insufficient cost/benefit framing for semantic dictionaries and limited error analysis: While the paper argues against semantic dictionaries, it does not quantify their construction cost or coverage in the baselines. Moreover, the paper lacks qualitative error analysis or examples where NBT corrects specific baseline failures, which would clarify the performance gains and remaining failure modes.

Questions to authors:
- Can NBT be used to perform SLU as a separate module (i.e., outputting slot–value detections per turn without belief aggregation), or is it only intended as a joint SLU/DST component? If not, consider rephrasing “joint” to avoid confusion.
- Please report statistics on the DSTC2 transcription corrections (e.g., number and types of fixes). How sensitive are the results to these corrections? Can you provide comparisons with prior DSTC2 entries on the same corrected data or, alternatively, show performance on the uncorrected transcriptions for fair comparison?
- Why were RNN/LSTM encoders not explored for utterance representation? Do you expect them to help, especially for longer sentences in WOZ 2.0? Any preliminary experiments or constraints (data size, training stability) that discouraged their use?
- Provide more details about the semantic dictionary used by the baselines (e.g., size per slot, examples of paraphrases, construction effort). This would help quantify the manual cost your approach avoids.
- Include a small set of illustrative errors that the baselines make but NBT resolves (e.g., paraphrases, implicit confirmations, negations). Conversely, examples where NBT fails would be helpful to guide future work (e.g., ASR-induced confusions, rare paraphrases).
- Minor: Clarify the belief update coefficient λ selection process and sensitivity; summarize hyperparameters critical to reproduction (filter counts, hidden sizes, thresholding).
