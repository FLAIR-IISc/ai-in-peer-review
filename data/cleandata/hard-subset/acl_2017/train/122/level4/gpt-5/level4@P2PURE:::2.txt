This paper proposes a neural network–based framework for dialogue state tracking (DST). The main contribution is learning representations for user utterances, system outputs, and ontology entries, all derived from pre-trained word vectors. For utterance representation, the authors compare two neural models: NBT-DNN and NBT-CNN. These learned representations are combined and fed into a downstream network that makes a binary decision for each slot-value pair. Experiments show significant performance gains over a delexicalized baseline. Overall, this is solid work with a clear goal, a reasonable approach, and improvements over prior studies.

However, the paper’s organization makes it difficult to follow, especially for readers new to the area. First, a more formal definition of DST should appear at the beginning. The current presentation is unclear and becomes more confusing when intertwined with SLU. I recommend moving the general dialogue system architecture (currently in Section 2) to Section 1, followed by a precise problem definition of DST that clarifies its relationships to ASR, SLU, and policy learning. It would also improve readability to define all notation early; some symbols (e.g., t_q, t_s, t_v) appear before they are introduced.

Additional comments and questions:
- Can this model perform SLU as a separate component? If not, the term “joint” may be misleading, as it could imply the model handles both tasks.
- Please provide statistics on how many errors were corrected in the original DSTC2 dataset. If the number is not large, consider including comparisons with other published work, including DSTC2 entries using the same dataset.
- What is your view on using RNNs or LSTMs to model sequential aspects in utterance representations? Given their recent success in SLU, they might be effective for DST as well.
- More details about the semantic dictionary used in the baseline would help convey the manual effort required to build such resources.
- It would be helpful to include examples that the baseline misclassified but your proposed models correctly predicted.
