- Proposes Neural Belief Tracker (NBT) that reasons over pre-trained word embeddings to replace hand-crafted semantic lexicons and separate SLU modules.
- Shares parameters across slots/values and scores slot–value candidates via learned utterance–candidate interactions with context gating.
- Provides two model instantiations (DNN and CNN) and uses a simple context gating based on the last system act.
- Shows statistically significant improvements on DSTC2 and WOZ 2.0 over delexicalisation baselines, matching or exceeding lexicon-augmented models.
- Demonstrates that semantically specialized embeddings (Paragram-SL999) materially improve DST performance over GloVe and random initializations.
- Achieves strong empirical results without hand-crafted lexicons, improving joint goal and request accuracy.
- Uses fixed pre-trained vectors and parameter sharing to generalize to unseen rephrasings and reduce engineering effort.
- Employs an efficient element-wise utterance–candidate interaction and lightweight context gating.
- Provides model variants suited to different utterance lengths (CNN for longer, DNN simpler and competitive).
- Presents clear, reproducible architecture, training regimen, and belief update mechanism.
- Evaluation is limited to single-domain, small-ontology datasets, so scalability to large/multi-domain settings is untested.
- Context modeling is limited to the last system act, missing longer-range phenomena like cross-turn anaphora, corrections, and negation.
- Lacks comparison to strong span-level SLU/tagging pipelines, making it hard to isolate benefits over span-detection approaches.
- ASR handling relies on a rule-based belief update; robustness to ASR errors and training/testing mismatches is only partially explored.
- Assumes a closed ontology and fixed embeddings, leaving unclear handling of unseen multi-word values, OOVs, or zero-shot value generalization.
- Asks authors to report inference cost and runtime scaling for iterating over slot–value pairs in larger ontologies.
- Requests experiments encoding longer dialogue context (e.g., RNNs or memory networks) to test sensitivity to the Markov assumption.
- Suggests evaluating hybrid/span-aware variants that restrict NBT scoring to predicted spans to assess accuracy and speed trade-offs.
- Seeks zero-shot experiments for unseen values and clarification on handling negation/exclusion and corrections.
- Asks whether embeddings were fine-tuned (globally or selectively) and whether ASR features/confusion networks were integrated rather than using rule-based updates.
- Notes minor clarifications and reproducibility aids: clarify example about tracking entity names, fix references/typos, and release data/splits, code, and hyperparameters.
- Overall: solid, timely contribution with strong empirical support but limited evaluation scope relative to scalability claims; reviewer leans toward acceptance.
