This paper introduces a neural, data-driven approach to dialogue state tracking. Its primary contribution is a method for learning vector representations of user utterances, prior system outputs, and ontology items, all derived from pre-trained word embeddings. For utterance encoding, two architectures are compared: NBT-DNN and NBT-CNN. These representations are composed and passed to a downstream classifier that issues a binary decision for each candidate slot–value pair. Experiments show significant improvements over a delexicalisation-based baseline. Overall, this is solid work with a clear objective, sensible methodology, and better results than prior studies. However, the paper’s presentation could be better structured, particularly for readers not already familiar with the area.

First, a more formal and early definition of DST is needed. The current exposition is not sufficiently clear and becomes more confusing once it is coupled with SLU. I recommend introducing the general dialogue system architecture in Section 1 rather than Section 2, followed by a precise problem formulation of DST and its interfaces with ASR, SLU, and policy learning. It would also improve readability to define all notation earlier; several symbols (e.g., tq, ts, t_v) appear before they are explained.

Below are other comments or questions:
- Can this model support SLU as a stand-alone component? If not, the term “joint” may be misleading in implying it handles both tasks independently.
- Please report statistics on how many transcription errors were corrected in the original DSTC2 dataset. If the number is small, consider including comparisons with other published results (e.g., DSTC2 entries) on the same data.
- What is your view on using RNNs or LSTMs to capture sequential structure in utterance representation learning? Given their recent success in SLU, they might also benefit DST.
- More detail about the semantic dictionary used by the baseline would help quantify the manual effort required to build such resources.
- It would be helpful to show examples that the baseline misclassified but your models correctly handled.
