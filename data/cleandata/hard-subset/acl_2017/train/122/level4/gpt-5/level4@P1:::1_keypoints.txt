- Introduces Neural Belief Tracker (NBT): a lexicon-free DST operating directly on pre-trained word embeddings to decide slot–value presence.  
- Presents two NBT variants: NBT-DNN (n-gram aggregation via feed-forward net) and NBT-CNN (convolution + max-pooling over 1–3-grams).  
- Integrates context (system acts) via gating and uses a simple belief update over ASR N-best lists.  
- Main contribution: couples SLU and DST in embedding space, removing need for hand-crafted semantic lexicons.  
- Empirical claim: NBT matches or outperforms delexicalization-based baselines on DSTC2 and WOZ 2.0.  
- Shows embedding quality matters: Paragram-SL999 embeddings give stronger gains than GloVe or random init.  
- Strength: removes lexicon dependency, improving scalability to linguistic variation and capturing utterance–candidate interactions.  
- Strength: strong, statistically significant improvements in joint goal and request accuracy, rivaling lexicon-based systems.  
- Strength: sensible architecture choices and ablation (DNN vs CNN) clarify benefits of local n-grams and interaction mechanisms.  
- Strength: informative comparison of embedding types, highlighting benefits under noisy ASR.  
- Weakness: evaluation limited to small single-domain restaurant datasets; scalability to multi-domain/large ontologies not demonstrated.  
- Weakness: no comparison to span- or tag-based SLU approaches; unclear if explicit slot tagging would help reduce noise.  
- Weakness: unclear handling of entity names and coreference across turns; state definition and name handling need clarification.  
- Questions for authors: provide evidence or analysis of scalability to larger ontologies/multi-domain settings and computational/data requirements.  
- Questions for authors: compare to or combine with explicit slot-tagging/span-detection (e.g., BiLSTM-CRF) and report effects of constraining utterance–candidate interactions to predicted spans.  
- Questions for authors: clarify whether NBT includes proposed entity names in the belief state and how it handles coreference (e.g., “it”) across turns.  
- Overall assessment: well-executed, promising lexicon-free belief tracker with solid empirical gains, but claims about scalability and comparison to span-based SLU need stronger validation.
