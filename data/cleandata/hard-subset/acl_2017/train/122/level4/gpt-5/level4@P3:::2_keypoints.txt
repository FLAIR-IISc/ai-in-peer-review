- Introduces Neural Belief Tracker (NBT): a DST framework that reasons over pre-trained word vectors to avoid hand-crafted semantic lexicons and separate SLU components.  
- NBT variants: NBT-DNN (summed n-gram projections) and NBT-CNN (convolution + ReLU + max-pooling); context gating uses the last system act; rule-based belief update integrates ASR N-best.  
- Main contributions: data-driven DST leveraging semantically specialized embeddings, empirical gains over delexicalized baselines, and a simple context gating mechanism.  

Strengths:
- Removes reliance on brittle hand-built semantic lexicons while matching or improving accuracy.  
- Demonstrates clear value of semantically specialized embeddings (Paragram-SL999) with statistically significant gains.  
- Competitive performance on noisy (DSTC2) and clean (WOZ 2.0) datasets; effective handling of ASR N-best.  
- Modular and interpretable design with shared semantic-space parameterization enabling cross-slot sharing.  
- Reusable resources (cleaned DSTC2 transcriptions, expanded WOZ 2.0) and potential applicability to multi-domain and low-annotation settings.  

Weaknesses:
- Exposition and organization hinder accessibility: delayed formal setup, undefined notation, and vague system integration.  
- Limited exploration of sequence encoders: no RNN/LSTM or hybrid comparisons despite their prominence in SLU.  
- Narrow evaluation: single domain (restaurants), two datasets from same ontology, and incomplete baselines against top DSTC2 systems or ASR-aware models.  
- Sparse qualitative/error analysis: lacks examples of successes/failures to substantiate semantic composition claims.  
- Ambiguity about the meaning of “joint” SLU/DST and whether NBT can serve as a standalone SLU.  

Questions for authors:
- Can NBT produce standalone SLU outputs (per-utterance slot–value predictions or spans) independent of belief update?  
- How many DSTC2 transcription errors were corrected, of what types, and how do corrections affect benchmark comparisons?  
- What are the size, coverage, and construction cost of the semantic dictionaries used for baseline comparisons?  
- Were RNN/LSTM encoders or attention over n-grams tried, and how did they compare in accuracy and compute cost?  
- Please provide representative success cases (NBT wins) and typical failure cases (e.g., negation, ASR errors, multiword values).  

Additional suggestions:
- Reorganize introduction to include SDS pipeline overview, formal DST definition, and NBT’s interface with ASR/policy.  
- Define all symbols before use and consolidate notation in a short subsection.  
- Clarify context gating equations with intuition for element-wise gating vs. alternatives like concatenation.  
- Report sensitivity to belief update parameter λ and to the number of ASR hypotheses.  
- Discuss generalization to multi-domain settings, cross-slot sharing, and expected challenges (ontological conflicts, domain senses).  

Overall assessment:
- Solid, practically relevant contribution that advances data-driven DST by removing dictionary dependence; with clearer exposition, stronger baselines/encoder comparisons, and richer qualitative analysis, the paper merits acceptance.
