- Strengths:
This paper presents a new dialogue state tracking approach that leverages pre-trained word embeddings to represent slot values and learns to compose them into distributed representations of user utterances and dialogue context. Experiments on two datasets demonstrate consistent and statistically significant gains over prior delexicalization-based baselines. The study also examines alternative word vector initializations (Xavier/random), distributional embeddings (GloVe), and semantically specialized embeddings (Paragram-SL999).

- Weaknesses:
Although a key motivation is scaling to more complex domains where delexicalization is impractical, the evaluation domains are relatively limited. It would be useful to compare the method with and without a dedicated slot-tagging component on more complex dialogues. For example, when computing similarity between an utterance and slot–value pairs, the estimation could be constrained to spans likely to contain the value, which should remain applicable even without exact matches. The introductory example seems potentially misleading: shouldn’t the dialogue state also include “restaurant_name=The House”? This also raises the question of how coreference resolution influences performance on this task.

- General Discussion:
Overall, the use of pre-trained word embeddings is compelling, and the specific modeling approach for exploiting them is promising.
