This paper presents a neural network–based framework for dialogue state tracking (DST). The main contribution lies in learning representations of user utterances, system outputs, and ontology entries, all derived from pre-trained word vectors. For utterance representations in particular, the authors compare two neural models, NBT-DNN and NBT-CNN. The learned representations are combined and fed to a downstream network that makes a binary decision for each slot–value pair. Experiments show significant improvements over a delexicalized baseline approach. Overall, this is a solid piece of work with a clear goal, a reasonable idea, and improved results over prior studies.

However, the paper’s organization makes it difficult to follow, especially for readers not already familiar with the area. A more formal and earlier definition of the DST task is needed. The current presentation is not sufficiently clear and becomes more confusing when coupled with SLU. I suggest presenting a general dialogue system architecture in Section 1 (rather than Section 2), followed by a precise problem definition of DST that clarifies its relationship to ASR, SLU, and policy learning. It would also improve readability to define all notations early; several symbols (e.g., t_q, t_s, t_v) appear before they are introduced.

Comments and questions:
- Is it possible to perform SLU as a separate component with this model? If not, the term “joint” may be misleading, as it could suggest the model addresses both tasks.
- Please provide statistics on how many errors were corrected from the original DSTC2 dataset. If the number is not large, it would be helpful to include comparisons to other published work (including DSTC2 entries) on the same dataset.
- What is your view on using RNNs or LSTMs to capture sequential aspects for utterance representations? Given recent successes of recurrent models in SLU, they may also be effective for DST.
- More details about the semantic dictionary used in the baseline would help readers understand the manual effort required to construct such resources.
- Please include examples that the baseline mispredicts but your proposed models get right; such qualitative analysis would clarify where the gains come from.
