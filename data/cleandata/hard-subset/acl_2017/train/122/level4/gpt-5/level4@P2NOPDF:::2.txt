This paper presents a neural network framework for dialogue state tracking (DST) that learns representations of user utterances, system outputs, and ontology entries using pre-trained word embeddings. For utterance representation, the authors compare two variants: NBT-DNN and NBT-CNN. These learned representations are composed and fed into a downstream network that makes a binary decision for each slot-value pair. Experiments show significant gains over a delexicalization-based baseline. Overall, the paper has a clear objective, sound ideas, and improved results relative to prior work.

However, the paper’s organization makes it difficult to follow, especially for readers unfamiliar with DST. A more formal and self-contained definition of DST should appear at the beginning. The current exposition is not sufficiently clear and becomes more confusing when intertwined with SLU. I suggest moving the high-level dialogue system architecture to Section 1 (rather than Section 2), followed by a precise problem definition of DST and its interfaces with ASR, SLU, and policy learning. Readability would also benefit from consolidating notation early; several symbols (e.g., tq, ts, tv) are used before they are defined.

Additional comments and questions:
- Clarify the role of SLU. Is it possible to perform SLU separately with this model, or is SLU only implicitly handled within the DST framework? If separate SLU is not supported, the term “joint” may be misleading, as it could suggest the model addresses both tasks explicitly.
- Please report statistics on the number and types of corrections you made to the original DSTC2 dataset. If the volume of corrections is modest, consider including comparisons with other published work on the same (uncorrected) dataset, including DSTC2 leaderboard entries, to contextualize your improvements.
- What is your rationale for choosing DNN/CNN over recurrent models (RNNs/LSTMs) to capture sequential aspects of utterances? Given the recent success of recurrent architectures in SLU, they may also be effective for DST; a discussion or empirical comparison would strengthen the paper.
- Provide more details about the semantic dictionary used in the baseline (e.g., construction process, size, coverage). This would help readers assess the manual effort and domain expertise required to build such resources.
- Include qualitative examples where the baseline fails but the proposed models succeed. Such error analysis would illustrate what the learned representations capture and offer insight into remaining failure modes.
