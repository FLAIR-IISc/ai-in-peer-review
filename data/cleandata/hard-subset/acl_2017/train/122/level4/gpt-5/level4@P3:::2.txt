Summary and contributions
This paper introduces Neural Belief Tracker (NBT), a framework for dialogue state tracking that dispenses with hand-crafted semantic lexicons and separate SLU components by reasoning directly over pre-trained word vectors. The model composes utterance, context, and candidate slot–value representations, and makes a binary decision for each slot–value pair per turn. Two variants are proposed for utterance encoding: NBT-DNN (summed n-gram projections) and NBT-CNN (convolution + ReLU + max-pooling). Context is incorporated via simple gating based on the preceding system act (request/confirm). A rule-based belief update combines turn-level decisions with ASR N-best. Experiments on DSTC2 and WOZ 2.0 show significant gains over delexicalization-based baselines, including versions augmented with semantic dictionaries, and an analysis reveals that semantically specialized embeddings (Paragram-SL999) yield the best performance.

As I see them, the primary contributions are:
1) A data-driven DST framework that leverages semantically specialized pre-trained word vectors to jointly handle language understanding and belief tracking without hand-crafted semantic dictionaries.
2) Empirical evidence across two datasets that NBT matches or outperforms strong delexicalized baselines, with statistically significant gains when dictionaries are unavailable; and an informative ablation showing the importance of semantically specialized embeddings.
3) A simple, effective context gating mechanism that uses the last system act to resolve short, context-dependent user replies (e.g., yes/no to confirms, value mentions after requests).

Strengths
- Eliminates reliance on semantic lexicons while retaining or improving accuracy. The core practical advance is removing the brittle and costly requirement for hand-built rephrasings, a major barrier to scaling to larger ontologies. The NBT models equal dictionary-augmented baselines and outperform them when no dictionaries are provided.
- Clear demonstration of the value of semantically specialized embeddings. The comparison among random, GloVe, and Paragram-SL999 shows consistent, statistically significant gains for Paragram on both datasets (especially DSTC2), providing rare downstream validation that semantic specialization matters for DST.
- Competitive results on both noisy (DSTC2) and clean (WOZ 2.0) data. The model handles ASR N-best via a simple update rule and achieves strong joint goal and request accuracies. The larger gains on WOZ 2.0 support the claim that NBT better captures lexical variation beyond exact matching.
- Modular, interpretable design. The separation into utterance composition, semantic decoding (element-wise interaction between utterance and slot–value), and context gating is easy to follow and implement, and supports cross-slot parameter sharing by operating in a shared semantic space.
- Reusability and potential impact. Cleaning DSTC2 transcriptions and expanding WOZ 2.0 are valuable to the community. The approach is likely to be applicable to multi-domain systems and low-annotation regimes where lexicons are impractical.

Weaknesses
- Exposition and organization hinder accessibility. The paper delays the formal problem setup and uses notation (e.g., tq, ts, tv) before defining it. The coupling of SLU and DST is asserted early without a clear architectural overview of a typical dialogue system and how NBT interfaces with ASR and policy learning. This makes it harder for readers unfamiliar with DSTC to follow the contribution.
- Limited exploration of sequence modeling for utterances. Given strong recent results for RNN/LSTM encoders in SLU, it is surprising that only bagged n-gram projections and shallow CNNs are considered. While NBT-CNN is reasonable, a comparison to an RNN/LSTM encoder (or a hybrid CNN+RNN) would strengthen claims about representation learning.
- Narrow evaluation scope and incomplete baselines. Results are confined to a single domain (restaurants in Cambridge) and to two datasets derived from the same ontology. It would help to include comparisons to top DSTC2 systems evaluated on the same (corrected) transcripts or to more recent joint models that exploit ASR features, to better position NBT against the state of the art.
- Sparse qualitative/error analysis. The paper would benefit from examples where NBT succeeds while delexicalization fails (e.g., paraphrases, morphological variants, antonyms), and from failure cases (e.g., ASR-induced errors, unseen multiword values). This would substantiate the claim that semantic composition drives the gains.
- Ambiguity around “joint” SLU/DST. While NBT reasons directly over utterances and outputs belief updates, it is not clear whether it could serve as a standalone SLU component (e.g., outputting slot/value spans or distributions independent of the DST loop). The term “joint” could be misread as supporting both tasks explicitly.

Questions to authors
- Separate SLU: Can NBT be used to produce SLU outputs independently of DST (e.g., per-utterance slot–value predictions without belief update), or extract spans? If not, please clarify the intended meaning of “joint.”
- DSTC2 corrections: How many transcription errors were corrected, and what types? Please quantify their impact and, if feasible, compare to DSTC2 entries on the same corrected test set to ensure fair benchmarking.
- Baseline dictionaries: What is the size/coverage and construction cost of the semantic dictionaries used with baselines? Any statistics on average synonyms per value and examples?
- Encoder choices: Did you try RNN/LSTM encoders or attention over n-grams? If so, how did they compare in accuracy and compute cost? If not, what are the anticipated trade-offs?
- Error analysis: Please provide representative cases where NBT (especially with Paragram) succeeds while delex fails, and typical NBT failures (e.g., contradictory paraphrases, negation, multiword values).

Additional comments and suggestions
- Reorganize the introduction to include: (a) a concise overview of an SDS pipeline, (b) a formal DST problem definition with ontology and belief state notation, and (c) a clear description of how NBT replaces delexicalization and interfaces with ASR and policy.
- Define all symbols before use and consolidate notation in a short subsection.
- Clarify the context gating equations with a brief intuition (why element-wise gating with r is preferable to, e.g., concatenation).
- Report sensitivity to the belief update parameter λ, and (if possible) to the number of ASR hypotheses.
- Discuss generalization to multi-domain settings and cross-slot parameter sharing in more detail, including any preliminary results or expected challenges (e.g., ontological conflicts, domain-specific senses).

Overall assessment
A solid and practically relevant contribution that advances data-driven DST by leveraging semantically specialized word vectors to remove dictionary dependence. With improved exposition, stronger baselines or additional encoder comparisons, and richer qualitative analysis, this work would be a strong addition to ACL. I lean toward acceptance.
