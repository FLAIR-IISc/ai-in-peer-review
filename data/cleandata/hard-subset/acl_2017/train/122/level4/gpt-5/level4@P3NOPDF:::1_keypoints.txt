- Proposes dialogue state tracking by representing slot values as pre-trained word-embedding vectors and composing them with utterance/context encodings to estimate similarity for state updates.
- Shows consistent, substantial improvements over a strong delexicalization-based baseline on two datasets.
- Compares multiple pre-trained embedding sources (XAVIER, GloVe, Program-SL999), providing practical insight into embedding choices.
- Motivation is clear and timely: moving beyond delexicalization to handle paraphrases and lexical variation via distributed semantics.
- Novel contribution: treating slot values as learned/initialized vectors and learning composition to align utterances/contexts with values.
- Empirical results are robust across datasets and reported as significant relative to prior delexicalization methods.
- Approach is conceptually simple and potentially transferable across domains with less hand-engineered lexicon work.
- Evaluation scope is limited: datasets are modest, leaving open performance on larger ontologies, multi-word/hierarchical values, and richer paraphrasing.
- Lacks direct comparison or integration with slot tagging; unclear trade-offs when combining semantic similarity with explicit slot span prediction.
- Introductory example/state definition is unclear (omitted entity tracking like restaurant_name), needing clarification on target ontology and tracked slots.
- Unclear handling of coreference and multi-turn entity resolution; need discussion of whether the context encoder suffices or external modules are required.
- Ambiguity about robustness to unseen/rare values: whether values are atomic learned vectors or composed from constituent embeddings is not specified.
- Missing details on embedding sources and fine-tuning (training corpora, dimensionalities, fixed vs. fine-tuned) and sensitivity to these choices.
- Evaluation lacks depth: no breakdowns by slot, value frequency, utterance complexity, nor focused error analysis of semantic confusions.
- Suggestions: add experiments augmenting model with a slot tagger/span predictor, evaluate on more complex or composite domains, and clarify ontology coverage in examples.
- Suggestions: discuss/experiment with simple coreference strategies, provide implementation details for multi-word values and OOV handling, and report per-slot metrics, statistical significance, and qualitative nearest-neighbor analyses.
- Overall: promising, well-motivated approach with encouraging empirical gains, but limited evaluation and missing analyses weaken claims—additional experiments and clarifications would strengthen the paper.
