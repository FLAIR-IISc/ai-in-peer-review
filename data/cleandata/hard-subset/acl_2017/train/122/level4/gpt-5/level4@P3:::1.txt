Summary and contributions
This paper introduces Neural Belief Tracker (NBT), a data-driven framework for dialogue state tracking that reasons directly over pre-trained word embeddings rather than hand-crafted semantic lexicons or separate SLU modules. The model composes utterance and context representations and evaluates each slot–value candidate via a learned interaction between the utterance and candidate, augmented with context gating based on the previous system act. Experiments on DSTC2 (ASR) and WOZ 2.0 (typed) show statistically significant improvements over strong delexicalisation-based baselines.

Main contributions (as I see them):
1) A neural belief tracking framework that eliminates the need for hand-crafted semantic dictionaries by operating over semantically specialized word vectors and sharing parameters across slots/values.
2) A modular architecture with two effective instantiations (DNN and CNN) and a simple yet useful context gating mechanism that exploits the last system act (request/confirm) to disambiguate short user answers.
3) Empirical validation on two standard datasets, including analysis demonstrating that semantically specialized embeddings (Paragram-SL999) materially improve performance over GloVe and random initializations.

Strengths
1) Strong empirical results without hand-crafted lexicons. On DSTC2, NBT-CNN achieves 73.4 joint goal accuracy vs. 69.1 for the baseline, matching the dictionary-augmented baseline (72.9). On WOZ 2.0, NBT-DNN/CNN reach ~84.2–84.4 vs. 70.8 for the baseline and slightly outperform the lexicon-augmented model (83.7). Request accuracy also improves. The gains are statistically significant and substantiate the claim that NBT can replace delexicalization plus semantic dictionaries.
2) Clear demonstration of the value of semantically specialized embeddings. The comparison among Xavier-initialized, GloVe, and Paragram-SL999 embeddings shows consistent gains from semantic specialization, especially on noisy ASR data (DSTC2). This is a meaningful, well-executed analysis that links intrinsic embedding properties to downstream DST performance.
3) Simple, scalable modeling choices. The use of fixed pre-trained vectors and parameter sharing across values supports generalization to unseen rephrasings and reduces data/engineering requirements. The element-wise interaction between utterance and candidate, and the lightweight context gating via dot products with the previous system act, are elegant and efficient.
4) Model variants appropriate to different utterance lengths. The CNN instantiation is well-motivated for longer utterances and yields robust performance. The DNN variant is simpler and still competitive, which increases practical applicability.
5) Relevance and clarity. The paper addresses a well-known scalability bottleneck in DST, and the presentation of the architecture, training regimen, and belief update mechanism is clear enough to reproduce.

Weaknesses
1) Limited evaluation scope vs. scalability claims. Both datasets are single-domain (restaurants) with small ontologies (three informable slots). The paper’s central claim—scalability to larger, more complex domains—is not directly validated. It remains unclear how the approach scales in runtime and accuracy when iterating over thousands of slot–value candidates or when slot/value vocabularies are open-ended.
2) Context modeling is short-sighted by design. The Markovian assumption that only the last system act matters simplifies modeling but limits coverage of phenomena such as cross-turn anaphora, corrections, and implicit references that depend on longer dialogue history. The paper would benefit from analysis of failure cases involving longer-range context and negation (e.g., “not Thai” or “anything but expensive”).
3) Comparison set omits strong SLU tagging pipelines. A natural alternative is a sequence-labeling SLU module (e.g., CRF/RNN tagger) that detects slot values at the span level, feeding a tracker. The paper does not compare against such systems on the same data, nor does it evaluate a hybrid where NBT scoring is restricted to predicted spans. This makes it hard to isolate how much benefit arises from avoiding delexicalization vs. avoiding span detection.
4) ASR handling and training/testing mismatch. The belief update over N-best lists is rule-based and orthogonal to the core model. While the authors train on transcriptions and evaluate on ASR for DSTC2, the approach’s robustness to ASR errors is only partially explored. More detailed breakdowns (e.g., by WER, by slot) or comparisons to trackers leveraging lattices/confusion networks would strengthen the ASR story.
5) Ontology coverage and unknown values. The model presumes a closed ontology and relies on fixed embeddings. It is unclear how it handles unseen multi-word values, OOV tokens, or values absent from the embedding vocabulary. The ability to generalize to new values (zero-shot within a slot) is asserted but not directly tested.

Questions to authors
- Scalability: What is the computational cost of iterating over all slot–value pairs at inference time, and how does it scale to larger ontologies (e.g., multi-domain settings)? Have you profiled runtime per turn?
- Longer context: Did you try encoding more than the last system act (e.g., an RNN over dialogue acts or a memory network)? How sensitive is performance to the Markov assumption?
- Span-aware variants: Have you evaluated a hybrid that first detects candidate spans (via a tagger or attention) and restricts NBT scoring to those spans? How would this affect both accuracy and speed?
- Generalization to unseen values: How does NBT handle values not seen during training but present in the ontology at test time? Any zero-shot experiments across values or slots?
- Negation and exclusion: How does the model treat utterances like “not Thai,” “anything but expensive,” or corrections (“no, Italian”)? Are there explicit features or training examples for such cases?
- Embedding choices: You freeze embeddings; did you try fine-tuning with a small learning rate or retrofitting only a subset (e.g., value names) to improve domain adaptation?
- ASR robustness: Is λ in the belief update tuned per dataset/slot? Have you tested using confusion networks or ASR confidence features within NBT rather than at the update stage?

Additional comments
- The introductory example might suggest tracking entity names (e.g., “restaurant_name=The House”). If “name” is not an informable slot in the ontology, consider clarifying that distinction earlier to avoid confusion.
- Please standardize reference formatting and fix minor typos in the bibliography.
- Releasing the cleaned DSTC2 transcriptions and WOZ 2.0 split, along with code and hyperparameters, would greatly aid reproducibility.

Overall evaluation
A solid and timely contribution. The core idea—replacing hand-crafted lexicons with semantically specialized embeddings within a joint SLU/DST model—is well-motivated and empirically supported. The main limitation is the narrow evaluation scope relative to the scalability claim. With additional evidence on larger ontologies, more robust context modeling, and comparisons to span-based SLU pipelines, this work would be even stronger. I lean toward acceptance.
