Summary and contributions:
This paper introduces the Neural Belief Tracker (NBT), a framework for dialogue state tracking that dispenses with delexicalization and hand-crafted semantic lexicons by operating directly over pre-trained word embeddings. The model composes embeddings of user utterances and preceding system acts into distributed representations, and decides for each candidate slot–value pair whether it has been expressed. Two variants are presented: NBT-DNN, which aggregates n-gram representations via a feed-forward network, and NBT-CNN, which uses convolutional filters with max-pooling over 1–3-grams. A simple belief update rule integrates ASR N-best lists.

As I see them, the main contributions are:
1) A practical, lexicon-free DST framework that couples SLU and DST and reasons entirely in embedding space using learned interactions between utterance, candidate slot–value pairs, and context (system acts).
2) Empirical evidence on two datasets (DSTC2 and WOZ 2.0) that NBT matches or outperforms strong delexicalization-based baselines, including those augmented with semantic dictionaries.
3) An analysis showing that the semantic quality of word vectors matters: semantically specialized Paragram-SL999 embeddings yield the strongest gains over GloVe and randomly initialized (Xavier) vectors.

Strengths:
- Clear advance over delexicalization-based trackers: By removing the dependency on hand-crafted semantic dictionaries and operating in embedding space, the NBT addresses scalability and linguistic variation more naturally. The model design explicitly captures interactions between utterances and candidate slot–value pairs and integrates context via gating with system acts (request/confirm), which is well motivated for task-oriented dialogue.
- Strong and statistically significant empirical results: On both DSTC2 and WOZ 2.0, the NBT variants significantly improve joint goal accuracy over baselines; they also yield higher request accuracy. Importantly, the NBT matches or exceeds systems that rely on expert-built lexicons, strengthening the claim that lexicons are unnecessary when high-quality embeddings and an appropriate architecture are used.
- Sensible architectural choices and ablations: Presenting both DNN and CNN variants helps isolate the benefits of local n-gram modeling vs. global pooling. The semantic decoding via element-wise interaction between utterance and candidate representations is simple and effective, and the context gating aligns with how confirmations and requests occur in dialogue.
- Informative study of embedding quality: Comparing Xavier, GloVe, and Paragram-SL999 embeddings is valuable. The finding that specialized similarity-focused embeddings substantially improve goal tracking (especially with noisy ASR) is a useful takeaway for the community.

Weaknesses:
- Limited evaluation scope: A central motivation is scalability to larger, more complex domains where delexicalization does not scale. However, both datasets are single-domain restaurant search with small ontologies. The paper does not empirically validate scalability claims on richer, multi-domain, or large-ontology settings.
- Lack of comparison to span- or tag-based SLU: The model compares utterance-level representations to candidate slot–value pairs without explicit span identification. It would be informative to compare against (or augment with) a separate slot tagging component on more complex dialogues. For example, restricting the similarity computation to predicted spans for each candidate value could reduce noise and should apply even when surface forms do not match exactly.
- Clarification of state definition and coreference: The introductory example appears to omit constraints such as restaurant_name=The House when the system proposes an entity. This raises questions about how entity mentions and coreference (e.g., “it,” “that place”) affect belief tracking in this setup. The paper does not discuss coreference resolution or name handling, which could be important in realistic settings.

Questions to authors (optional):
- Can you provide evidence of scalability to larger ontologies or multi-domain settings (e.g., cross-slot parameter sharing across many domains, or experiments on a larger schema)? Absent new data, can you at least characterize the computational and data requirements as the ontology grows?
- How would NBT compare with a pipeline that includes an explicit slot tagging/span detection component (e.g., a BiLSTM-CRF), both in isolation and when combined with your semantic decoding? Have you tried constraining the utterance–candidate interaction to predicted spans, and does this help on longer, more complex utterances?
- In the introductory example, should the belief state include restaurant_name when the system proposes “The House”? More generally, how does NBT handle entity names and coreference across turns (e.g., users referring to a previously suggested venue as “it”)? Is any explicit mechanism needed, or does the context gating suffice?

Overall assessment:
The paper presents a timely and well-executed approach to belief tracking that avoids hand-crafted lexicons by leveraging pre-trained word embeddings, with clear gains over strong baselines and a useful analysis of embedding quality. The main limitation is the narrow evaluation scope; demonstrating performance on larger, more complex domains and comparing against span/tag-based SLU components would strengthen the claims about scalability and generalization. Nonetheless, the approach is sound and promising.
