This paper presents a neural network framework for dialogue state tracking (DST). The main contribution is learning distributed representations of user utterances, system outputs, and ontology entries, all initialized from pre-trained word vectors. For utterance representation, the authors compare two architectures: NBT-DNN and NBT-CNN. These learned representations are combined and fed into a downstream network that makes a binary decision for each slot-value pair. Experiments show significant improvements over a delexicalization-based baseline.

Overall, the work has a clear goal, a reasonable idea, and improved results over prior studies. However, the paper is not well organized to communicate details effectively to readers unfamiliar with the area. First, a more formal definition of DST should be provided at the outset. The current description is not sufficiently clear and becomes more confusing when coupled with SLU. I recommend presenting a general dialogue system architecture in Section 1 (rather than Section 2), followed by a precise DST problem definition that explains its relationships to ASR, SLU, and policy learning. Readability would also improve if all notations were defined early; some symbols (e.g., tq, ts, t_v) appear before they are explained.

Other comments and questions:
- Is it possible to perform SLU as a separate component with this model? If not, the term “joint” may be misleading, as it could suggest the model handles both SLU and DST.
- Please provide statistics on how many errors were corrected in the original DSTC2 dataset. If the number is not large, the experiments should also include comparisons with other published work, including DSTC2 submissions, using the same dataset.
- What about using RNNs or LSTMs to capture sequential information in utterance representations? Given their recent success in SLU, they could be effective for DST as well.
- Please provide more details about the semantic dictionary used in the baseline to clarify the manual effort required to build such resources.
- It would be helpful to include examples that the baseline predicts incorrectly but your proposed models handle correctly.
