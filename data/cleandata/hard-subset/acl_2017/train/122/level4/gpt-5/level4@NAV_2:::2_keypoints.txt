- Introduces a neural, data-driven DST that learns vector representations for user utterances, prior system outputs, and ontology items from pre-trained word embeddings.  
- Compares two utterance encoders (NBT-DNN and NBT-CNN) and uses composed representations in a classifier making binary slot–value decisions.  
- Reports significant improvements over a delexicalisation-based baseline; methodology and results are solid.  
- Paper presentation and structure need improvement for readers not already familiar with the area.  
- Recommend formally defining DST earlier and introducing the general dialogue system architecture in Section 1.  
- Clarify DST’s interfaces with ASR, SLU, and policy learning and provide a precise problem formulation.  
- Define notation earlier; symbols (e.g., tq, ts, t_v) appear before being explained.  
- Clarify whether the model can support SLU as a stand-alone component, since “joint” may be misleading.  
- Report how many transcription errors were corrected in the DSTC2 dataset and compare with other DSTC2 results if corrections are few.  
- Discuss the potential benefits of RNNs/LSTMs for capturing sequential structure in utterance representations.  
- Provide more detail on the semantic dictionary used by the baseline to quantify manual effort.  
- Include examples where the baseline misclassifies but the proposed models correctly handle the input.
