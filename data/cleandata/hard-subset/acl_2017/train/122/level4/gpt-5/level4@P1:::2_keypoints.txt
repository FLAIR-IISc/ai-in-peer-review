- Proposes the Neural Belief Tracker (NBT), a lexicon-free DST that reasons over pre-trained word vectors instead of delexicalization or hand-crafted dictionaries.
- NBT learns distributed representations of user utterances, preceding system acts, and slot–value pairs to decide if a candidate pair is expressed each turn.
- Compares two utterance encoders: NBT-DNN (transformed n-gram sums) and NBT-CNN (1–3-gram conv + ReLU + max-pool), with a simple gating context based on last system act.
- Shows statistically significant improvements on DSTC2 ASR and WOZ 2.0 text over delexicalization baselines, matching or exceeding dictionary-augmented systems.
- Ablation over embedding types (random, GloVe, Paragram-SL999) demonstrates semantically specialized embeddings (Paragram) substantially improve DST, especially under ASR noise.
- Clear motivation and empirical validation for a lexicon-free approach addressing the scalability bottleneck of hand-crafted dictionaries.
- Effective, simple context mechanism that handles system request/confirm interactions (e.g., yes/no) without complex dialogue history modeling.
- Solid ablation demonstrating the value of semantically specialized word vectors, with Paragram vectors consistently helping noisy ASR cases.
- Providing two sensible architectural variants (DNN and CNN) that share parameters across values, improving data efficiency and scalability.
- Paper organization and clarity need improvement: introduce formal DST problem definition, high-level dialogue-system architecture, and notation earlier.
- Potentially misleading use of “joint” terminology without clarifying whether NBT can perform standalone SLU.
- Lack of transparency about DSTC2 transcription corrections: no statistics on the scope or types of corrections, hindering comparability.
- Limited exploration of sequential encoders: no RNN/LSTM/GRU-based utterance encoder evaluated despite their SLU success.
- Insufficient detail on the baseline semantic dictionary (size, construction effort, coverage) and lack of qualitative error examples.
- Request clarification whether NBT can be adapted for standalone SLU (per-utterance slot–value tagging/detection) or what “joint” specifically implies.
- Ask for statistics on how many and what types of transcription errors were corrected in DSTC2, and impact on results versus published entries.
- Request assessment of using RNNs/LSTMs/GRUs for utterance representation and expected benefits over CNNs/n-gram summation.
- Ask for semantic dictionary statistics (entries per slot, human effort, sources) to quantify annotation burden NBT removes.
- Request error-analysis examples showing cases where baseline fails but NBT succeeds (paraphrases, morphological variants, context-dependent confirmations).
- Recommend moving system architecture overview and formal DST definition to the introduction and adding a concise notation section for readability.
