Summary and contributions
- The paper introduces the Neural Belief Tracker (NBT), a family of models that jointly perform SLU/DST by reasoning over pre-trained word embeddings rather than delexicalised features or hand-crafted semantic lexicons. Two variants are presented (NBT-DNN, NBT-CNN) that compose utterance, candidate slot–value, and context representations and make a binary decision per candidate.
- NBT explicitly models context via gating with the last system act (request/confirm) and uses an ASR-aware belief update rule over N-best lists. Word vectors are kept fixed to promote generalisation to unseen paraphrases; semantically specialised vectors (Paragram-SL999) are emphasised.
- On DSTC2 and WOZ 2.0, NBT matches or exceeds delexicalisation-based systems that use semantic dictionaries and significantly outperforms them when lexicons are unavailable. An embedding study shows specialised vectors outperform GloVe and random initialisation.

Strengths
- Removes reliance on hand-crafted semantic dictionaries while achieving state-of-the-art or better performance. This is a clear step toward scalable DST in richer domains, and the comparison with and without lexicons is compelling.
- Well-motivated architecture that separates (i) utterance/candidate semantic interaction and (ii) context gating based on system acts. The element-wise interaction (r⊗c) is sensible for learning feature-wise compatibilities, and the context module directly targets confirm/request phenomena that stymie delexicalisation.
- Comprehensive evaluation across speech (DSTC2) and text (WOZ 2.0) settings. The larger gains on WOZ support the claim that the method better captures linguistic variation, while the ASR experiments demonstrate robustness and practical utility.
- Careful study of word vector choices. Showing that semantically specialised vectors yield consistent improvements over GloVe/random strengthens the claim that NBT benefits from true semantic similarity rather than mere distributional relatedness.
- Clear and reproducible setup: fixed embeddings, two model variants (DNN/CNN), significance tests, and a simple, transparent belief update rule over ASR N-best hypotheses.

Weaknesses
- Limited empirical validation of scalability. All experiments are in a single, relatively small restaurant domain with three informable slots. Claims about improved scalability to larger or multi-domain ontologies remain untested; no experiments with substantially more slots/values are provided.
- Missing comparison to slot span tagging/sequence labeling pipelines. Since NBT decides over entire utterances per candidate pair, it may conflate mentions; span-restricted similarity or explicit slot-filling (e.g., CRF/LSTM) could be a strong baseline. A direct comparison would clarify the benefit of NBT’s candidate-pair approach.
- Handling of coreference and entity mentions is unclear. The introductory example appears to omit tracking of restaurant_name after a system suggestion. It is not obvious how NBT handles references like “that one” or confirmations tied to specific offered entities without explicit entity/coreference tracking.
- ASR robustness analysis is limited. Improvements on DSTC2 are modest compared to oracle transcripts. There is little breakdown by WER, n-best depth, or error types (substitutions vs deletions). The reliance on a tuned λ for belief updates may be dataset-specific.
- Computational considerations are underexplored. Iterating over all slot–value pairs each turn may become costly as ontologies grow. There is no complexity analysis, candidate pruning strategy, or discussion of batching/approximate search. Keeping embeddings fixed aids generalisation, but may hinder domain adaptation where semantics shift.

Questions to authors
- How would NBT compare to a pipeline or joint model with explicit slot span tagging (e.g., BiLSTM-CRF or RNN slot filling), possibly using span-restricted similarity/attention? Could this reduce false positives and improve interpretability?
- How does NBT handle unseen or open-vocabulary values and OOV words at test time? Would subword/character representations or fine-tuning embeddings help without sacrificing generalisation?
- What is the per-turn computational cost as ontology size increases? Can you incorporate candidate pruning, approximate nearest-neighbour search in embedding space, or learned proposal mechanisms?
- Did you run ablations on the context module (system-request vs system-confirm gates) and on the interaction function (element-wise product vs concatenation/dot product)? How critical are these choices?
- How sensitive are results to λ, ASR n-best depth, and the decision threshold? Do findings generalise across datasets with different ASR characteristics?
- Can the framework be extended to track suggested entities (e.g., restaurant_name) and to integrate coreference resolution, or would that require modifying the ontology and model inputs?

Additional comments (minor)
- Please clarify the introductory example and whether the belief state includes suggested entities and their attributes.
- An error analysis highlighting failures (e.g., antonyms, negation, long-distance dependencies, value revisions) would make the contribution more actionable.
- There are a few wording/typo issues; for instance, ensure “Paragram-SL999” is used consistently. Overall, the paper is clearly written.
