- Proposes a neural-network framework for DST that learns representations for user utterances, system outputs, and ontology entries from pre-trained word vectors.  
- Compares two utterance encoders: NBT-DNN and NBT-CNN.  
- Combines those representations and uses a downstream network to make binary decisions for each slot–value pair.  
- Reports significant performance gains over a delexicalized baseline.  
- Overall assessment: solid work with a clear goal, reasonable approach, and improvements over prior studies.  
- Organization critique: paper is hard to follow—needs a formal DST definition at the start and clearer separation from SLU.  
- Suggest moving the general dialogue system architecture to Section 1 and then precisely defining DST and its relation to ASR, SLU, and policy learning.  
- Notation issue: define all symbols early (e.g., t_q, t_s, t_v appear before introduction).  
- Clarify whether the model performs SLU as a separate component—calling it “joint” may be misleading if it doesn’t handle both tasks.  
- Provide statistics on how many errors were corrected in the original DSTC2 dataset and compare with other published DSTC2 entries using the same dataset.  
- Consider and discuss using RNNs/LSTMs to capture sequential aspects of utterance representations, given their SLU success.  
- Give more details about the semantic dictionary used in the baseline to indicate manual effort required.  
- Include example cases where the baseline misclassified but the proposed models made correct predictions.
