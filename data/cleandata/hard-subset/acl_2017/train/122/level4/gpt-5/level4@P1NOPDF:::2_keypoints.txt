- Paper proposes a neural DST framework that learns distributed representations of user utterances, system outputs, and ontology entries initialized from pre-trained word vectors.
- Compares two utterance encoders (NBT-DNN and NBT-CNN) whose representations are combined and used to make binary decisions for each slot–value pair.
- Experiments show significant improvement over a delexicalization-based baseline.
- Overall assessment: clear goal, reasonable idea, and improved results versus prior work.
- Paper organization and exposition are poor: lacks a formal DST definition up front and mixes DST with SLU, confusing readers unfamiliar with the area.
- Recommend presenting a general dialogue system architecture earlier and a precise DST problem definition explaining relations to ASR, SLU, and policy learning.
- Notations are introduced before definition (e.g., tq, ts, t_v); all symbols should be defined early.
- Clarify whether SLU can be performed as a separate component; the use of “joint” may be misleading if the model does not handle SLU.
- Provide statistics on how many errors were corrected in the DSTC2 dataset and, if few, include comparisons with other published DSTC2 submissions on the same dataset.
- Consider comparing to RNNs/LSTMs for utterance representation to capture sequential information, given their success in SLU.
- Provide more details about the semantic dictionary used in the baseline to clarify manual effort required.
- Include qualitative examples where the baseline fails but the proposed models succeed.
