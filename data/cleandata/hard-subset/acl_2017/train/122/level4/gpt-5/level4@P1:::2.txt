Summary and Contributions
This paper proposes the Neural Belief Tracker (NBT), a neural framework for dialogue state tracking that operates over pre-trained word vectors rather than delexicalized features or hand-crafted semantic dictionaries. The model learns distributed representations of user utterances, preceding system acts, and ontology slot–value pairs, and decides for each candidate pair whether it is expressed at the current turn. Two utterance encoders are compared: NBT-DNN (sum of transformed n-gram embeddings) and NBT-CNN (convolution, ReLU, max-pooling over 1–3-grams). A simple context mechanism gates utterance representations based on the last system act (request/confirm). Experiments on DSTC2 ASR data and WOZ 2.0 text show statistically significant improvements over delexicalization-based baselines, with or without semantic dictionaries. An ablation across embedding types (random, GloVe, Paragram-SL999) demonstrates the importance of semantically specialized word vectors.

As I see them, the main contributions are:
1) A practical, lexicon-free DST framework that couples SLU and DST by reasoning directly in a semantic embedding space, yielding significant gains over delexicalization-based trackers and matching dictionary-augmented systems.
2) Two alternative utterance encoders (DNN and CNN) that share parameters across ontology values and support scalable tracking without slot/value-specific classifiers.
3) Empirical evidence that semantically specialized embeddings (Paragram-SL999) substantially improve DST, especially under ASR noise, beyond what distributional vectors (GloVe) provide.

Strengths
- Clear motivation and empirical validation of a lexicon-free approach: The paper targets a real scalability bottleneck—dependence on hand-crafted semantic dictionaries—and shows that NBT attains statistically significant improvements on joint goal and request accuracy on both DSTC2 and WOZ 2.0. It matches dictionary-augmented baselines and outperforms them when dictionaries are unavailable.
- Effective use of context via system acts: The gating mechanism that conditions on the last system request/confirm act is simple and well-justified for task-oriented dialogue. It captures crucial interactions (e.g., handling “yes/no” confirmations) without complex dialogue history modeling.
- Solid ablation on embeddings: The comparison among random, GloVe, and Paragram-SL999 vectors cleanly isolates the role of semantic specialization. The finding that Paragram vectors consistently help (particularly for noisy ASR) is both credible and valuable for the community.
- Sensible architectural variants: Presenting both NBT-DNN and NBT-CNN is useful. The CNN variant is well-motivated for longer utterances; both models share parameters across values, improving data efficiency.

Weaknesses
- Organization and clarity issues: The paper would benefit from an earlier, formal problem definition of DST and a high-level architecture of a task-oriented dialogue system. Introducing this in the introduction (rather than Section 2) would help readers unfamiliar with the area, especially given the coupling with SLU. Notation should be defined upfront; symbols such as tq, ts, and tv appear before they are introduced.
- Potentially misleading “joint” terminology: It is unclear whether the model can be used for separate SLU. If not, describing the approach as “joint SLU/DST” without clarifying its standalone SLU capabilities risks confusion.
- Dataset correction transparency and comparability: The paper reports manual corrections to DSTC2 transcriptions but does not quantify the scope of corrections. Without statistics, it is hard to assess the impact on results or compare fairly with published DSTC2 entries using the uncorrected data.
- Limited exploration of sequential encoders: Given recent successes of RNN/LSTM models in SLU, it is surprising that no recurrent utterance encoder is evaluated. An LSTM/GRU-based encoder might better capture sequential dependencies than n-gram summation or CNNs for certain phenomena.
- Insufficient detail on baseline semantic dictionary and qualitative analysis: More information about the size, construction effort, and coverage of the semantic dictionary used with the baseline would clarify the practical cost of manual resources. Additionally, qualitative examples where the baseline fails but NBT succeeds would make the gains more concrete.

Questions to Authors
- Separate SLU: Can NBT be adapted to perform stand-alone SLU (e.g., per-utterance slot–value tagging or detection) without belief tracking? If not, please clarify the intended meaning of “joint” in the text.
- DSTC2 corrections: How many transcription errors were corrected in DSTC2, and of what types? Can you quantify the potential effect on performance and, if feasible, include comparisons against published DSTC2 results on the same (corrected) data?
- Recurrent encoders: What is your assessment of using RNNs/LSTMs (or GRUs) for utterance representation in this framework? Do you expect benefits over CNNs and n-gram summation, particularly for longer or more complex utterances?
- Semantic dictionary details: Please provide statistics on the baseline’s semantic dictionary (e.g., number of entries per slot, approximate human effort, sources). This would help readers gauge the annotation burden that NBT eliminates.
- Error analysis: Could you include examples that the baseline misclassifies but NBT handles correctly (e.g., paraphrases like “pricey” → expensive, morphological variants, context-dependent confirmations)?

Additional Comments
- Consider moving the system architecture overview and formal DST definition to the introduction, followed by a concise notation section to improve readability.
- Define all symbols at first use and ensure consistency across sections.
