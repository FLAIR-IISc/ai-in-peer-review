

This paper proposes a neural network framework for dialogue state tracking (DST), with its primary contribution being the learning of representations for user utterances, system outputs, and ontology entries using pre-trained word vectors. Specifically, the authors evaluate two neural network architectures—NBT-DNN and NBT-CNN—for utterance representation. These learned representations are integrated and subsequently employed in a downstream network to make binary decisions for slot-value pairs. Experimental results demonstrate significant performance gains over a delexicalized baseline. Overall, the work presents a clear objective, a reasonable approach, and improved results relative to prior studies.

However, the paper lacks effective organization for readers unfamiliar with DST, hindering the delivery of technical details. First, a formal definition of DST should be provided early in the paper (e.g., in Section 1), rather than later in Section 2. The current presentation is insufficiently clear and risks confusion when integrated with SLU (Spoken Language Understanding). We recommend restructuring: present a general dialogue system architecture in Section 1, followed by a DST problem definition that explicitly contextualizes its relationships with ASR, SLU, and policy learning. Additionally, all notations (e.g., \(t_q\), \(t_s\), \(t_v\)) should be defined in an early section, as their usage precedes their descriptions.

Specific concerns and suggestions:  
- The term "joint" may be misleading if the model cannot separately perform SLU. Clarify whether SLU can be run independently.  
- Provide statistics on error corrections from the original DSTC2 dataset. If corrections are minor, include comparisons with other DSTC2 entries using the same dataset.  
- Justify the choice of feedforward networks (NBT-DNN/CNN) over RNNs/LSTMs for capturing sequential aspects in utterances, especially given their proven success in SLU tasks.  
- Include details on the semantic dictionary used in the baseline to clarify manual resource costs.  
- Include concrete examples where the proposed model corrects errors made by the baseline.
