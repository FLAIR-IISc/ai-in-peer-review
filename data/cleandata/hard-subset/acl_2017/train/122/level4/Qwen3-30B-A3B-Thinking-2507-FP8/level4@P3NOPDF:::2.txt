

This paper proposes a neural network framework for dialogue state tracking (DST), leveraging pre-trained word vectors to learn representations for user utterances, system outputs, and ontology entries. The core innovation lies in combining these representations via a downstream binary decision network for slot-value prediction. The authors evaluate two neural architectures (NBT-DNN and NBT-CNN) for utterance encoding and demonstrate significant performance gains over a delexicalized baseline on DSTC2. The work presents a clear goal, reasonable methodology, and empirically validated improvements over prior approaches, representing a solid contribution to the field.

However, the paper’s organization and exposition significantly hinder its accessibility and impact. The introduction fails to establish a foundational understanding of DST within the broader dialogue system architecture. A formal definition of DST should precede technical details, explicitly clarifying its relationship with ASR, SLU, and policy learning. Currently, the problem definition is buried in Section 2, while Section 1 discusses the dialogue system architecture—a structural misplacement that confuses readers unfamiliar with the domain. The authors should reorganize: present a high-level dialogue system diagram in Section 1, followed by a precise DST problem statement contextualized within this framework. Additionally, inconsistent notation (e.g., symbols like *tq*, *ts*, *t_v* introduced before definition) disrupts readability. A dedicated notation section early in the paper would greatly improve clarity.

Beyond structure, several critical questions require addressing to validate the paper’s claims and significance:

1.  **Terminology Precision:** The paper describes a "joint" model, but it only addresses DST. If the framework *cannot* perform SLU jointly (as implied by the "joint" label), this is misleading. The authors must clarify whether SLU is handled separately or if the model’s scope is strictly DST. If the latter, the term "joint" should be revised to avoid confusion.

2.  **Baseline Contextualization:** The improvements over the delexicalized baseline are significant, but the paper lacks context on the *scale* of the original DSTC2 dataset corrections. If the dataset preprocessing (e.g., error corrections) was minimal, the authors should explicitly compare against published DSTC2 entries using the *same* dataset. This would better position the contribution against existing work.

3.  **Model Architecture Justification:** The choice to use DNN/CNN for utterance representation, rather than RNNs/LSTMs (which have proven effective in sequential tasks like SLU), warrants discussion. The authors should address why sequential modeling was deemed unnecessary or inferior here, especially given recent successes in SLU.

4.  **Resource Cost of Baseline:** The baseline employs a semantic dictionary. Providing statistics on its size, construction effort, or manual curation cost would help readers assess the practicality and scalability of the baseline approach versus the proposed model.

5.  **Error Analysis:** Including concrete examples where the baseline failed (e.g., misidentified slot values) but the proposed model succeeded would strengthen the empirical validation. This would illustrate *why* the representation learning approach resolves specific failure modes.

The paper’s core technical contribution is sound, but its current presentation obscures its value. The proposed framework’s advantages are not fully transparent due to organizational flaws and omitted contextual details. Addressing the structural issues (particularly the misplaced DST definition and notation) would make the work significantly more accessible. The technical questions raised—especially regarding the "joint" terminology, dataset context, and model choice—are not merely editorial but fundamental to the paper’s validity and positioning.

In summary, this is a well-executed study with promising results. With improved organization, clearer definitions, and responses to the specific points above, it would be a strong candidate for publication. The current draft, however, requires substantial revision to ensure its contributions are unambiguously communicated and contextualized. The authors should prioritize clarifying DST’s role within the dialogue system, resolving the "joint" terminology, and providing necessary baseline context before final submission.
