- Contribution: Proposes Neural Belief Tracker (NBT) that removes need for hand-crafted semantic lexicons while matching state-of-the-art DST performance.
- Contribution: Empirically shows semantically-specialized word vectors substantially improve dialogue state tracking.
- Contribution: Provides evidence that NBT generalizes better to domains without lexicons, addressing a scalability bottleneck.

- Strength: Eliminates lexicon dependency and matches/outperforms lexicon-based models, especially when lexicons are unavailable.
- Strength: Thorough comparison of embedding spaces (XAVIER, GloVe, Paragram-SL999) demonstrates semantic specialization yields significant gains (notably on noisy DSTC2).
- Strength: Evaluation on DSTC2 (ASR errors) and WOZ 2.0 (clean text) shows robust performance across noisy and clean settings and on richer vocabulary/longer sentences.

- Weakness: Evaluation limited to two datasets with simple ontologies (three informable slots), so scalability to larger, multi-domain settings is unproven.
- Weakness: Intro example omits restaurant_name slot, raising concerns about coreference resolution and whether slot-value pairs capture full dialogue state.
- Weakness: Paper does not discuss interaction with slot tagging components or handling of non-exact matches (e.g., pronouns referring to prior entities).

- Question: How does NBT perform on multi-domain systems with larger ontologies (e.g., 10+ informable slots)? Any preliminary results or analysis?
- Question: How does the model determine slot boundaries in utterances and assign parts of an utterance to specific slots, especially with multiple potential mentions?
- Question: How does NBT handle coreference (e.g., including restaurant_name when referenced indirectly like “it”)?
