

Strengths:  
This paper introduces a novel dialogue state tracking (DST) approach that leverages pre-trained word embeddings to represent slot values and composes them into distributed representations of user utterances and dialogue context. The method demonstrates consistent and statistically significant improvements over delexicalization-based baselines on two benchmark datasets (MultiWOZ and SGD). The authors rigorously evaluate alternative embedding strategies—including Xavier-initialized, GloVe, and Program-SL99 embeddings—to validate their choice of pre-trained embeddings, strengthening the technical contribution. The core idea of using distributed representations for slot values, rather than delexicalized tokens, is well-motivated and aligns with recent advances in contextual language modeling.  

Weaknesses:  
The primary limitation lies in the scope of empirical validation. While the authors position their work as addressing scalability challenges in complex dialogue domains (where delexicalization struggles), the datasets used (MultiWOZ and SGD) are relatively constrained in domain diversity and complexity compared to real-world applications. The paper would benefit from a more thorough analysis of performance on larger, more intricate datasets or a discussion of how the method handles out-of-domain slot values. Crucially, the authors do not investigate the impact of a separate slot tagging component—a common design in state-of-the-art DST systems. For instance, if slot boundaries are identified via a dedicated tagger, the similarity computation between utterances and slot values could be restricted to the span of the slot values (e.g., "The House" in "I want to book at The House"). This would improve robustness to value mismatches (e.g., "The House" vs. "House") and reduce noise from irrelevant context. A comparison of the model *with and without* such a component would clarify its necessity and generalizability.  

Additionally, the introductory example (Section 1) is misleading. It lists the dialogue state as `{"food": "Chinese", "area": "center"}` but omits the expected `restaurant_name=The House` entry, creating confusion about how the system handles explicit entity references. This raises a broader methodological question: how does the model resolve coreferences (e.g., "it" or "that place" referring to a previously mentioned restaurant)? Without addressing coreference resolution, the approach risks misinterpreting pronouns or ellipses in natural dialogue, which is critical for robust DST. The authors should either revise the example to include the full state or clarify how coreference is handled in their framework.  

General Discussion:  
The use of pre-trained embeddings for slot value representation is a well-motivated and promising direction for DST, and the authors’ specific implementation—focusing on compositional embedding of slot values within context—represents a meaningful advance over prior delexicalization methods. The technical execution is sound, and the results are compelling within the evaluated scope. However, to fully realize the potential of this approach for complex, real-world dialogues, the authors should address the gaps outlined above, particularly regarding slot tagging integration and coreference handling. The work would be significantly strengthened by: (1) expanding empirical validation to more diverse datasets, (2) benchmarking against systems with slot tagging components, and (3) clarifying how coreference is managed. These additions would better substantiate the claim that the method generalizes beyond current benchmarks.  

Overall, this is a strong contribution with clear potential. The paper is well-written and technically rigorous, but addressing the limitations above would elevate it from a promising baseline improvement to a more foundational advancement in DST. The reviewers recommend acceptance pending minor revisions to clarify the methodology and expand the evaluation scope.
