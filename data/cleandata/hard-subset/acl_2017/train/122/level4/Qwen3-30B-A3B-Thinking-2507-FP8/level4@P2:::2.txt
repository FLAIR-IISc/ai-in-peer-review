

Summary: This paper introduces the Neural Belief Tracker (NBT), a novel framework for dialogue state tracking that leverages pre-trained word vectors to handle lexical variation without requiring hand-crafted semantic dictionaries. The primary contribution is the demonstration that learning representations from pre-trained word vectors eliminates the need for domain-specific semantic lexicons while maintaining performance parity with delexicalization-based approaches. A secondary contribution is the development of two effective model variants (NBT-DNN and NBT-CNN) for learning utterance representations. The authors also demonstrate that semantic specialization of word vectors significantly improves performance.

Strengths:
1. The paper convincingly shows that NBT achieves performance matching delexicalization-based models (which rely on hand-crafted semantic dictionaries) while significantly outperforming them when such dictionaries are unavailable. This addresses a critical scalability challenge in dialogue systems.
2. The experimental results on two distinct datasets (DSTC2 and WOZ 2.0) demonstrate statistically significant improvements in both goal and request accuracy, with NBT-CNN achieving the strongest results. The paper effectively shows that semantic quality of word vectors directly impacts performance.
3. The authors provide a clear demonstration that the NBT framework can handle semantic variation without explicit lexicon construction, which is particularly valuable for complex domains where manual lexicon creation is infeasible. The comparison with different word vector types (XAVIER, GloVe, Paragram-SL999) is particularly insightful.

Weaknesses:
1. The paper's organization is problematic for readers unfamiliar with dialogue systems. Key concepts like dialogue state tracking (DST) should be formally defined at the beginning rather than in later sections. A general dialogue system architecture should be presented early to clarify DST's relationship with ASR, SLU, and policy learning.
2. Critical notation (e.g., tq, ts, tv) is used without proper definition early in the paper, creating confusion for readers. All symbols should be formally introduced in an early section before being used.
3. The paper's claim of being a "joint" model is misleading, as it does not handle separate SLU tasks. The authors should clarify whether the model could be used for standalone SLU or if the "joint" aspect is limited to the specific implementation described.
4. The paper lacks meaningful comparison with other published work using the same DSTC2 dataset. The authors should include results from other DSTC2 entries to strengthen the evaluation context.
5. The paper does not address why RNNs/LSTMs weren't used for sequential modeling, despite their demonstrated success in related SLU tasks. This omission is notable given the paper's focus on handling sequential dialogue context.

Questions to Authors:
1. Could the NBT framework be adapted to handle separate SLU tasks, or is the "joint" claim potentially misleading? If the model cannot be used as a standalone SLU system, please clarify this distinction.
2. How many errors were corrected in the DSTC2 dataset, and would it be possible to include comparisons with other published DSTC2 entries using the same dataset to better contextualize the results?
3. Given the success of RNNs/LSTMs in related SLU tasks, why weren't these architectures considered for learning sequential aspects of utterance representations? What specific limitations would prevent their effective use in this framework?
4. Could you provide details about the cost and effort involved in creating the semantic dictionary used with the baseline model? This would help readers understand the practical scalability challenge that NBT addresses.
5. Could you provide specific examples where the NBT model correctly identified slot-value pairs that the baseline model missed? This would help illustrate the model's strengths in handling lexical variation.
