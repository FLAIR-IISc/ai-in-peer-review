

Summary and Contributions:  
This paper introduces the Neural Belief Tracker (NBT), a novel framework for dialogue state tracking (DST) that eliminates reliance on hand-crafted semantic lexicons. The primary contribution is a representation learning approach using pre-trained word vectors to model user utterances, system context, and ontology entries—enabling robust handling of lexical variation without domain-specific resources. The secondary contribution is the empirical validation that NBT achieves performance parity with lexicon-enhanced baselines and significantly outperforms them when lexicons are unavailable. A tertiary contribution is demonstrating that semantic specialization of word vectors (e.g., Paragram-SL999) substantially boosts DST performance, particularly on noisy data.  

Strengths:  
1. **Novel representation learning for scalability**: NBT’s core innovation—using pre-trained word vectors to model semantic variation—directly addresses the critical scalability barrier in DST. Unlike prior lexicon-based methods, it avoids the labor-intensive manual construction of semantic dictionaries (e.g., Figure 1), which is infeasible for large domains. The paper compellingly shows NBT matches lexicon-enhanced baselines (DSTC2: 73.4% vs. 72.9%) while outperforming them when lexicons are absent (WOZ 2.0: 84.2% vs. 70.8% for baseline), proving its practical viability.  
2. **Strong empirical validation**: The evaluation on two distinct datasets (DSTC2 with ASR errors, WOZ 2.0 with rich language) robustly demonstrates NBT’s advantages. Table 1 confirms statistically significant gains (p < 0.05) in both joint goal and request accuracy. Crucially, the ablation in Table 2 proves semantic vector quality (Paragram-SL999) is pivotal: it outperforms GloVe and random initialization by 1.5–2.5% on DSTC2, where noise amplifies the need for semantic robustness.  
3. **Clear technical differentiation from prior work**: The paper explicitly contrasts NBT with two prior categories (separate SLU and joint SLU/DST) and positions itself as the first to use word vectors for DST without lexicons. The architecture (Figure 2) and ablation studies (e.g., NBT-CNN’s convolutional filters vs. NBT-DNN’s summation) provide transparent, actionable insights into why the approach works.  

Weaknesses:  
1. **Poor paper organization undermines accessibility**: The paper lacks a foundational DST definition early on, forcing readers to decipher technical terms (e.g., "delexicalisation," "slot-value pairs") in Section 1 without context. Section 2’s background on DSTC is misplaced; DST should be formally defined *before* discussing related work. This disrupts flow for non-specialists and risks misinterpretation (e.g., confusing "joint" SLU/DST with the model’s capabilities).  
2. **Ambiguous notation and terminology**: Key symbols (e.g., `tq`, `ts`, `tv` in Section 3.3) appear without definition, creating confusion about their roles in context modeling. The term "joint" is misleading: NBT integrates SLU/DST but *cannot* operate with a separate SLU module (as noted in the draft), contradicting the implication of flexibility. This requires explicit clarification.  
3. **Underdeveloped discussion of limitations**: The paper notes NBT’s stronger gains on WOZ 2.0 (clean text) vs. DSTC2 (noisy ASR), yet fails to propose concrete paths for ASR robustness—critical for real-world deployment. The claim that "future work should focus on better ASR compensation" is vague; a brief discussion of potential ASR integration strategies (e.g., incorporating ASR confidence scores) would strengthen the conclusion.  

Questions to Authors:  
1. The paper states NBT "couples SLU and DST" but does not clarify whether it *can* integrate with a separate SLU system (e.g., replacing the NBT’s semantic decoder with an external SLU). If not, the term "joint" is inaccurate and should be revised to avoid misleading readers.  
2. The DSTC2 dataset cleaning process (Section 5.1) mentions "manual spelling corrections" but omits statistics on error rates corrected. How many utterances were modified? Were these corrections consistent with DSTC2’s official guidelines? Including this would enable fairer comparison to prior DSTC2 entries.  
3. The paper uses n-gram-based representations (NBT-DNN/CNN) but does not address sequential modeling (e.g., RNNs/LSTMs). Given RNNs’ success in SLU (e.g., Mesnil et al., 2015), why were they excluded? Could they further improve performance on long utterances?
