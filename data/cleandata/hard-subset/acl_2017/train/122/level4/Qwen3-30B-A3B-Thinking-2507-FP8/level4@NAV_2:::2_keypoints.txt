- Proposes a Neural Belief Tracker (NBT) for dialogue state tracking that removes hand-crafted semantic lexicons by using pre-trained word vectors; introduces NBT-DNN and NBT-CNN variants and reports strong improvements over delexicalization baselines.
- Strength: Eliminates need for semantic lexicons, addressing scalability and matching performance of lexicon-based delexicalization, making it more suitable for complex real-world domains.
- Strength: Demonstrates consistent, statistically significant improvements on DSTC2 and WOZ 2.0, showing robustness to lexical variation.
- Strength: Provides evidence that semantically specialized word vectors (Paragram-SL999) outperform GloVe and random vectors, especially on noisy DSTC2 data.
- Weakness: Lacks a clear formal definition of dialogue state tracking and its relation to ASR, SLU, and policy learning, which would aid non-expert readers.
- Weakness: Uses key notations (e.g., tq, ts, tv) before defining them, causing avoidable confusion.
- Weakness: Describing the model as "joint" is potentially misleading; it's unclear whether it can be used with a separate SLU module.
- Question: Can the NBT framework be decoupled to work with a separate SLU module, or is it strictly a joint model?
- Question: Please provide statistics on the number of corrections made to the DSTC2 dataset and compare with other published DSTC2 results if corrections were minimal.
- Question: Why were sequential models like RNNs or LSTMs not explored for utterance representation given their success in SLU tasks?
- Question: Provide more details about the semantic dictionary used with the baseline to clarify the manual effort required.
- Question: Include example cases where the baseline failed but the NBT model correctly predicted slot-value pairs.
