- Introduces a novel DST method that leverages pre-trained word embeddings to represent slot values and compose distributed representations of utterances and dialogue context.  
- Demonstrates consistent, significant improvements over prior delexicalization-based baselines on two benchmark datasets.  
- Thoroughly evaluates alternative pre-training strategies (XAVIER, GloVe, Program-SL999), bolstering the validity of the embedding-based approach.  
- Evaluation is limited to relatively small-scale datasets, raising concerns about scalability to complex dialogue domains.  
- Unclear how the method performs on more complex, real-world dialogues—broader benchmarks are needed.  
- Questions remain about whether a separate slot tagging component is necessary for robustness; a comparison with and without it on challenging domains is missing.  
- Paper’s introductory example appears incorrect (omits restaurant_name=The House), highlighting potential issues in annotation or model assumptions.  
- Raises a critical unresolved question about coreference resolution—how the model handles indirect references to slot values across context.  
- The approach for computing similarity between utterances and slot values could be improved by focusing on spans containing slot mentions rather than entire utterances.  
- Core idea and embedding composition technique are well-motivated and innovative, aligning with advances in contextual representation learning.  
- Strong experimental results on standard benchmarks, but lack of complex-domain evaluation and unresolved coreference/slot-tagging issues limit claims of broad applicability; authors should address these in future work.
