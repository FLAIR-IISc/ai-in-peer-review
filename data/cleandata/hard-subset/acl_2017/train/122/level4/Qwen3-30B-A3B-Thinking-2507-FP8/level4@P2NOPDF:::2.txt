

This paper proposes a neural network framework for dialogue state tracking (DST), focusing on learning joint representations of user utterances, system outputs, and ontology entries using pre-trained word vectors. The core contribution lies in comparing two neural architectures—NBT-DNN and NBT-CNN—for utterance representation, fusing these representations into a downstream binary classifier for slot-value prediction. Experiments demonstrate significant performance gains over a delexicalized baseline, validating the approach’s efficacy. The work is well-motivated, technically sound, and delivers clear improvements over prior methods.  

However, the paper’s organization undermines its clarity, particularly for readers unfamiliar with DST. The current structure (Section 2) introduces DST without first establishing foundational context. We recommend restructuring:  
1. **Begin with a concise dialogue system architecture** (Section 1), explicitly linking DST to ASR, SLU, and policy learning. This clarifies DST’s role and avoids confusion when discussing SLU integration later.  
2. **Define all key notations upfront** (e.g., *tq*, *ts*, *t_v*). These symbols appear before definition, disrupting readability. A dedicated notation section early in the paper would resolve this.  

Additional concerns and suggestions:  
- **Terminology precision**: The term "joint" implies the model handles SLU and DST simultaneously. If SLU is not integrated (as suggested by the baseline’s delexicalized approach), this is misleading. Clarify whether the framework supports separate SLU or if "joint" refers only to slot-value prediction.  
- **Dataset validation**: The paper uses the DSTC2 dataset. Please provide statistics on how many errors were corrected in the dataset (e.g., via manual curation) and compare results against *all* published DSTC2 entries using the same dataset. A limited error correction scope may weaken the significance of improvements.  
- **Sequential modeling**: The paper uses CNN/DNN for utterance representation but does not explore RNNs/LSTMs, which have proven effective in SLU for capturing sequential dependencies. Given the success of these models in related tasks, discussing why they were not adopted (or testing them) would strengthen the methodology.  
- **Baseline transparency**: The semantic dictionary used in the baseline requires more detail. Specify its size, source, and manual effort involved to contextualize the resource cost for future work.  
- **Error analysis**: Include concrete examples where the proposed model corrects baseline failures (e.g., a slot-value pair mispredicted by the baseline but resolved by the new framework). This would better illustrate the model’s practical impact.  

The technical approach is promising, but the current presentation obscures key contributions. Addressing the structural issues—especially the lack of a clear DST definition and notation consistency—would significantly enhance the paper’s accessibility and scholarly value. The results are compelling, yet the narrative fails to fully convey *why* the method works better. A revised organization that foregrounds DST’s problem context and ties technical choices to established dialogue system components would elevate the work substantially.  

Overall, this is a strong technical contribution with clear empirical validation. With minor revisions to structure, notation, and contextual framing, it would be suitable for publication. The proposed framework demonstrates a meaningful step forward in DST, and the improvements over the baseline are well-supported. The remaining issues are primarily editorial and organizational, not methodological.
