

Summary: The paper introduces a Neural Belief Tracker (NBT) framework for dialogue state tracking that eliminates reliance on hand-crafted semantic lexicons by leveraging pre-trained word vectors to learn representations of user utterances, system outputs, and ontology entries. The authors propose two model variants, NBT-DNN and NBT-CNN, which differ in their representation learning approaches. The framework demonstrates significant performance improvements over delexicalization-based baselines without requiring semantic dictionaries, particularly when such resources are unavailable.

Strengths: 
1. The NBT framework successfully addresses the scalability limitations of current belief tracking approaches by eliminating the need for hand-crafted semantic lexicons while matching the performance of delexicalization-based models that rely on them, making it better suited for real-world deployment in complex dialogue domains.
2. The experimental results show consistent and statistically significant improvements across both DSTC2 and WOZ 2.0 datasets, with the NBT models matching or exceeding baseline performance without semantic lexicons, demonstrating the framework's robustness to lexical variation.
3. The authors provide compelling evidence for the importance of semantic-specialized word vectors, showing that Paragram-SL999 vectors significantly outperform both GloVe and randomly initialized vectors for dialogue state tracking, particularly on noisy DSTC2 data.

Weaknesses:
1. The paper lacks a clear formal definition of dialogue state tracking and its relationship to other dialogue system components (ASR, SLU, policy learning) at the beginning, which would help readers unfamiliar with the field understand the context better.
2. Key notations (e.g., tq, ts, tv) are used before their definitions, creating unnecessary confusion for readers who are not experts in dialogue systems.
3. The paper's description of the model as "joint" could be misleading, as it's not clear whether the framework could be adapted to work with a separate SLU module rather than being strictly coupled.

Questions to Authors:
1. Could the proposed NBT framework be used with a separate SLU module, or is it strictly a joint model? The current description suggests it's a joint approach, but the paper should clarify if it can be decoupled.
2. Please provide statistics on the number of errors corrected in the DSTC2 dataset. If the corrections are minimal, it would strengthen the paper to compare results with other published DSTC2 entries using the same dataset.
3. Why were RNNs or LSTMs not explored for learning sequential aspects in utterance representations, given their recent success in SLU tasks?
4. Could you provide more details about the semantic dictionary used with the baseline to better understand the manual effort required for such resources?
5. Please include examples of cases where the baseline failed but the NBT model successfully predicted the correct slot-value pairs.
