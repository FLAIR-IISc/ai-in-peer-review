

This paper proposes a neural network-based framework for dialogue state tracking (DST), with the primary contribution being a method to learn representations of user utterances, system outputs, and ontology entries using pre-trained word vectors. The authors compare two neural architectures (NBT-DNN and NBT-CNN) for utterance representation, combine these learned representations, and employ them in a downstream network for binary slot-value decision making. Experiments demonstrate significant performance gains over a delexicalized baseline, establishing a clear improvement over prior work.  

While the technical approach is well-motivated and results are compelling, the paper suffers from poor organization that hinders comprehension for readers unfamiliar with DST. Specifically:  
1. **Problem Definition**: DST must be formally defined at the outset (Section 1), not deferred to Section 2. A high-level dialogue system architecture should precede the DST problem statement, explicitly clarifying its relationships to ASR, SLU, and policy learning.  
2. **Notation Consistency**: Key symbols (e.g., \(t_q\), \(t_s\), \(t_v\)) are used before their definitions, creating confusion. All notations should be introduced in an early section (e.g., Section 2).  

Additional concerns:  
- **"Joint" Terminology**: The model handles DST only, not SLU. Using "joint" risks misleading readers into believing it integrates SLU/DST. Clarify whether separate SLU is feasible.  
- **DSTC2 Dataset Analysis**: Provide statistics on error corrections from the original DSTC2 dataset. If the correction rate is modest, include comparisons with other DSTC2 entries using the same dataset.  
- **Sequential Modeling**: Why not leverage RNNs/LSTMs for utterance representations, given their success in SLU and DST? This could better capture sequential dependencies.  
- **Baseline Resource Cost**: Elaborate on the semantic dictionary used in the baseline to contextualize the manual effort required.  
- **Error Analysis**: Include concrete examples where the proposed model corrects baseline errors (e.g., specific slot-value pairs).  

Overall, the work presents a valid contribution with strong empirical results. Addressing the organizational and explanatory gaps above would substantially strengthen the paper’s clarity and impact.
