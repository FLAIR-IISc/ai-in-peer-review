- Introduces Neural Belief Tracker (NBT) that uses pre-trained word embeddings to remove dependence on hand-crafted semantic lexicons, matching SOTA and outperforming when lexicons are absent.

- Contribution: scalable belief tracking that learns semantic variation without domain-specific lexicons.

- Contribution: empirical validation showing NBT matches lexicon-based models and outperforms them when lexicons are unavailable.

- Contribution: shows semantic quality of pre-trained vectors matters, with Paragram-SL999 giving the best results.

- Strength: addresses the scalability bottleneck of hand-crafted semantic dictionaries, improving adaptability to evolving domains.

- Strength: rigorous evaluation on DSTC2 and WOZ 2.0 with statistical validation and ablation studies demonstrating consistent improvements.

- Strength: provides insight that semantically specialized embeddings (Paragram-SL999) substantially improve downstream dialogue tracking performance.

- Weakness: evaluations are limited to simple restaurant domains, so scalability to larger, more complex ontologies is unclear.

- Weakness: lacks discussion/comparison with slot-tagging approaches or span-restricted matching, and how to handle cases where slot values aren’t explicitly mentioned.

- Weakness: example in Section 1 omits implicit reference handling (e.g., “Where is it?”), raising concerns about coreference resolution and belief state completeness.

- Question: How would NBT handle implicit slot mentions and coreference (e.g., “It’s a nice place” for restaurant name) and how would such techniques integrate?

- Question: How do the authors plan to evaluate NBT on more complex domains with larger ontologies, and what challenges are anticipated?

- Question: What is NBT’s computational complexity and scalability compared to delexicalization approaches as the number of slot-value pairs grows?
