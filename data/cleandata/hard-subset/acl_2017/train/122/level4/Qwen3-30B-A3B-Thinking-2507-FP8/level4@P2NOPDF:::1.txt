

Strengths:  
This paper introduces a novel dialogue state tracking (DST) approach that leverages pre-trained word embeddings to represent slot values and composes them into distributed representations of user utterances and dialogue context. The method demonstrates consistent and significant improvements over prior delexicalization-based baselines across two benchmark datasets. The authors thoroughly evaluate alternative pre-training strategies (XAVIER, GloVe, Program-SL999), strengthening the validity of their embedding-based approach.

Weaknesses:  
The primary limitation lies in the dataset scope. While the authors motivate their embedding-based approach for scaling to complex dialogue domains where delexicalization struggles, the evaluation relies on relatively small-scale datasets. It remains unclear how the method performs on more complex, real-world dialogues. Specifically, the authors should address whether a separate slot tagging component is necessary for robustness—comparing performance with and without it on challenging domains would clarify this. Additionally, the paper's introductory example appears misleading: the dialogue state should include "restaurant_name=The House" rather than omitting it. This raises a critical question about coreference resolution—how does the model handle cases where slot values (e.g., "The House") are referenced indirectly or require resolution from prior context? The current method’s ability to manage such cases is not addressed. Furthermore, the approach for computing similarity between utterances and slot values could be refined. Instead of matching entire utterances, the authors could restrict similarity estimation to the spans containing slot values (e.g., the phrase "The House" in "I want to book at The House"), which would remain effective even when exact value matches are absent.

General Discussion:  
The core idea of using pre-trained embeddings for DST is well-motivated and promising. The specific technique for composing embeddings to represent dialogue context is innovative and aligns with advances in contextual representation learning. The experimental results provide strong evidence for the approach’s efficacy on standard benchmarks. However, the lack of evaluation on more complex domains and unresolved questions about coreference handling and slot tagging integration represent significant gaps that must be addressed to establish broader applicability. The authors should prioritize these directions in future work to validate the method’s scalability beyond current datasets.
