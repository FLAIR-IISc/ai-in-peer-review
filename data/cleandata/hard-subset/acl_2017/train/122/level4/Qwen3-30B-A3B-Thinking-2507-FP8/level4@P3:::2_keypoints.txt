- Introduces Neural Belief Tracker (NBT), a data-driven DST framework that removes hand-crafted semantic lexicons by using pre-trained word vectors.  
- Proposes two model variants (NBT-DNN and NBT-CNN) to learn distributed representations of utterances, system outputs, and ontology entries.  
- Shows NBT matches lexicon-based models and outperforms them when lexicons are unavailable.  

- Contribution: removes dependency on hand-crafted semantic lexicons, improving scalability to complex domains.  
- Contribution: presents two neural architectures (NBT-DNN, NBT-CNN) leveraging pre-trained word vectors for dialogue representation.  
- Contribution: provides empirical evidence that semantically specialized pre-trained vectors (e.g., Paragram-SL999) improve DST performance.  

- Strength: addresses a key scalability limitation of DST by eliminating manual lexicon creation.  
- Strength: strong empirical validation on DSTC2 and WOZ 2.0 with statistically significant improvements (p < 0.05).  
- Strength: ablation shows semantic specialization of word vectors materially impacts results.  
- Strength: practical implementation details (ASR N-best belief update, λ tuning) support real-world deployment.  

- Weakness: poor organization and clarity; lacks an early formal definition of DST and system architecture.  
- Weakness: inconsistent notation usage (symbols used before definition) causing reader confusion.  
- Weakness: insufficient comparison with recent neural DST approaches beyond delexicalization baselines.  
- Weakness: lacks qualitative examples illustrating where NBT succeeds over baselines.  

- Question: Can the “joint” SLU/DST framework be adapted to work with separate SLU components, or is the term “joint” misleading?  
- Question: How many DSTC2 spelling errors were corrected and what was the impact on dataset size compared to prior work?  
- Question: What was the time/expertise/resource cost to construct the baseline semantic dictionaries and how would this scale?  
- Question: Please provide concrete examples where the baseline failed but NBT succeeded to illustrate lexical-variation handling.  

- Additional: Paper is overall well-written with clear model explanations and justified comparison of NBT variants.  
- Additional: Highlighting Paragram-SL999 as a key enabler is a valuable insight.  
- Additional: Recommend a more structured problem-definition at the start to aid reader comprehension.
