

Summary: The paper introduces the Neural Belief Tracker (NBT), a framework for dialogue state tracking that leverages pre-trained word vectors to handle lexical variation without requiring hand-crafted semantic lexicons. The authors propose two model variants (NBT-DNN and NBT-CNN) that compose user utterances and dialogue context into distributed representations for slot-value pair detection.

Main Contributions: 
1. A novel dialogue state tracking approach that uses pre-trained word vectors to handle semantic variation without manual lexicons, addressing a key limitation of previous methods.
2. Empirical demonstration that NBT models match the performance of lexicon-based methods when such resources exist and significantly outperform them when they do not.
3. Insight that semantic specialization of word vectors (Paragram-SL999) substantially improves performance compared to standard distributional vectors or random initialization.

Strengths:
1. The NBT framework effectively overcomes the primary limitation of current dialogue state trackers by eliminating dependency on hand-crafted semantic dictionaries, making it more scalable to complex domains.
2. The experimental results consistently show statistically significant improvements over baseline delexicalisation-based methods across two distinct datasets (DSTC2 and WOZ 2.0), with NBT-DNN and NBT-CNN both achieving superior joint goal and request accuracy.
3. The investigation of different word vector spaces (XAVIER, GloVe, Paragram-SL999) provides valuable evidence that semantic quality of word embeddings directly impacts downstream dialogue state tracking performance.

Weaknesses:
1. The evaluation datasets (DSTC2 and WOZ 2.0) are limited in scope and complexity, raising questions about how the approach would perform in more challenging, real-world dialogue domains with richer linguistic variation.
2. The paper does not address how the NBT approach would interact with a separate slot tagging component, which could be crucial for handling more complex dialogues where slot value spans need precise identification.
3. The example dialogue state in the introduction appears to omit the restaurant name ("The House"), suggesting potential issues with coreference resolution that could impact the belief tracking task.

Questions to Authors:
1. How would the NBT framework handle dialogues with multiple restaurant mentions requiring coreference resolution, and what modifications would be needed to address this?
2. Could the authors elaborate on how the model would perform if combined with a slot tagging component that identifies the specific spans of slot values in user utterances?
3. What specific challenges would the NBT approach face in more complex multi-domain dialogue scenarios beyond the restaurant domain explored in the paper?
