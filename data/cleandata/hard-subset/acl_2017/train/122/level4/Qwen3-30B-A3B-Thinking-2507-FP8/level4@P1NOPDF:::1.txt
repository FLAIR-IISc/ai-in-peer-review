

Strengths:  
This paper introduces a novel dialogue state tracking (DST) approach that leverages pre-trained embeddings to represent slot values, enabling the model to compose them into distributed representations of user utterances and dialogue context. Experiments on two datasets demonstrate consistent and significant improvements over prior delexicalization-based baselines. The authors also rigorously evaluate alternative pre-training methods (XAVIER, GloVe, Program-SL99) for word embeddings, strengthening the technical contribution.

Weaknesses:  
While the authors position pre-trained embeddings as a solution for generalizing to complex dialogue domains where delexicalization struggles, the evaluation relies on limited datasets. It would be valuable to compare the approach with and without a dedicated slot tagging component on more complex dialogues. Specifically, when computing similarity between utterances and slot value pairs, the model could restrict estimation to the spans containing slot values—a strategy applicable even when exact value matches are absent. Additionally, the introductory example appears misleading: the dialogue state should include "restaurant_name=The House" (assuming the user says "The House" in context). This raises a critical question: how does coreference resolution impact the task, particularly for ambiguous references like "it" or "that" in user utterances?

General Discussion:  
The use of pre-trained word embeddings is a well-motivated and promising direction for DST. The proposed method for composing these embeddings is particularly exciting and merits further exploration.
