

This paper introduces a neural network framework for dialogue state tracking (DST), with its primary contribution lying in learning unified representations for user utterances, system outputs, and ontology entries using pre-trained word vectors. Specifically, the authors evaluate two neural architectures—NBT-DNN and NBT-CNN—for utterance representation, integrate these learned representations, and employ them in a downstream network to generate binary decisions for slot-value pairs. Experimental results demonstrate significant performance gains over a delexicalized baseline. The work is well-motivated, logically structured, and yields improved results compared to prior studies.  

However, the paper’s organization could be enhanced to better convey technical details, particularly for readers unfamiliar with DST. A formal DST definition and a high-level dialogue system architecture should be introduced in Section 1 (not Section 2), explicitly clarifying DST’s relationship with ASR, SLU, and policy learning. Additionally, all notations (e.g., t_q, t_s, t_v) should be defined early to prevent confusion, as several symbols appear before their descriptions.  

Key questions for clarification:  
- Can the model independently handle SLU? If not, the term "joint" may mislead readers into believing it addresses both DST and SLU.  
- What is the error correction rate on the DSTC2 dataset? Including comparisons with other DSTC2 submissions using the same dataset would strengthen the evaluation.  
- Why were RNNs/LSTMs not explored for learning sequential aspects in utterance representations, given their success in SLU?  
- Please elaborate on the semantic dictionary used in the baseline, including manual resource costs.  
- Providing concrete examples where the baseline failed but the proposed model succeeded would further illustrate the approach’s efficacy.
