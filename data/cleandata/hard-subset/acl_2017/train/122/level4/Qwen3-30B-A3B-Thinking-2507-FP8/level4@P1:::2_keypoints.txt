- Introduces the Neural Belief Tracker (NBT) that uses pre-trained word vectors to perform dialogue state tracking without hand-crafted semantic lexicons.  
- Primary contribution: representation learning with pre-trained vectors modeling user utterances, system context, and ontology entries to handle lexical variation.  
- Secondary contribution: empirical validation showing NBT matches lexicon-enhanced baselines and outperforms them when lexicons are unavailable.  
- Tertiary contribution: demonstrates semantic specialization of word vectors (e.g., Paragram-SL999) substantially improves DST performance, especially on noisy data.  
- Strength: novel scalable approach that removes need for manual semantic dictionaries and shows practical viability across datasets.  
- Strength: strong empirical validation on DSTC2 and WOZ 2.0 with statistically significant gains and ablation showing vector quality matters.  
- Strength: clear technical differentiation from prior SLU/DST work, with architecture and ablation studies explaining model choices.  
- Weakness: poor paper organization—lack of an early formal DST definition and misplaced background disrupts accessibility for non-specialists.  
- Weakness: ambiguous notation and terminology (undefined symbols, misleading use of "joint") create confusion about model capabilities.  
- Weakness: limited discussion of limitations and lacking concrete suggestions for ASR robustness and real-world deployment.  
- Question: clarify whether NBT can integrate with a separate SLU system or if "joint" is a misleading term.  
- Question: provide statistics on the DSTC2 dataset cleaning (number of manual spelling corrections and consistency with official guidelines).  
- Question: explain why sequential models (RNNs/LSTMs) were excluded and whether they might improve performance on longer utterances.
