- Introduces the Neural Belief Tracker (NBT) that uses pre-trained word vectors to avoid hand-crafted semantic lexicons in dialogue state tracking.  
- NBT matches delexicalization-based methods when dictionaries exist and outperforms them when dictionaries are unavailable.  
- Demonstrates that embedding semantic quality matters: Paragram-SL999 yields the best results.

- Tackles scalability by eliminating reliance on large SLU annotations or manual lexicons, making the approach more domain-adaptable.  
- Robust evaluation on DSTC2 and WOZ 2.0 shows statistically significant gains in joint goal and request accuracy.  
- Comparison of embeddings (XAVIER, GloVe, Paragram-SL999) shows semantic-focused vectors improve downstream performance, especially on noisy/varied language.  
- Conceptually simple and implementable using standard neural components (DNN/CNN) with pre-trained vectors.

- Evaluation is restricted to two restaurant-domain datasets with small ontologies, so scalability to larger, richer domains is unclear.  
- Does not address coreference resolution across turns, risking missing references to previously mentioned entities.  
- No exploration of integrating a slot tagging component to pre-identify candidate spans for similarity computation.  
- Lacks error analysis, leaving failure modes (e.g., semantic confusions like “expensive” vs “cheap”) unexplored.

- How would the model perform on multi-domain datasets with significantly larger ontologies?  
- How does the model handle coreference (e.g., “it” referring to a previously mentioned restaurant)?  
- Could integrating a slot tagger to identify candidate spans before similarity computation improve accuracy?  
- What is the model’s performance on datasets with complex multi-word slot values (e.g., “highly-rated Thai restaurant”)?
