

This paper proposes a neural network framework for dialogue state tracking (DST), focusing on learning unified representations for user utterances, system outputs, and ontology entries using pre-trained word vectors. The core innovation lies in comparing two neural architectures—NBT-DNN and NBT-CNN—for utterance representation, followed by combining these representations in a downstream network to make slot-value decisions. Experiments demonstrate significant performance gains over a delexicalized baseline, yielding superior results compared to prior work. The paper presents a clear objective, sound methodology, and compelling empirical validation.

However, the paper’s organization hinders clarity, particularly for readers unfamiliar with DST. The foundational definition of DST should appear early—ideally in Section 1—as part of a high-level dialogue system architecture overview (including ASR, SLU, and policy components), rather than being deferred to Section 2. This would clarify DST’s role within the broader system and avoid confusion when discussing its relationship with SLU. Additionally, key notations (e.g., *t_q*, *t_s*, *t_v*) are used before their definitions, disrupting readability. A dedicated notation section early in the paper would greatly improve accessibility.

The following specific concerns require clarification:

1.  **Terminology precision:** The paper describes a "joint" model, but it is unclear whether this framework can independently handle SLU (e.g., intent detection, slot filling) without DST. If not, the term "joint" risks misrepresenting the model’s capabilities and should be revised or justified.
2.  **Dataset context:** The paper uses a modified DSTC2 dataset. Please provide statistics on the scale of corrections applied to the original DSTC2 data. If the modifications are minor, the evaluation should include comparisons against other published DSTC2 entries using the same dataset to better contextualize the gains.
3.  **Model architecture:** The authors opt for DNN/CNN for utterance representation. Given the sequential nature of dialogue and the recent success of RNNs/LSTMs in SLU tasks, could these architectures also be effective for DST? A brief discussion of this alternative would strengthen the methodology.
4.  **Baseline resource cost:** The baseline employs a semantic dictionary. Additional details on the manual effort required to build this resource (e.g., size, annotation complexity) would help readers assess the practicality of the approach.
5.  **Error analysis:** Including concrete examples of dialogue turns where the baseline failed but the proposed model succeeded would provide valuable insight into the model’s strengths and limitations.

Overall, the technical contribution is meaningful and well-supported by results. With improved organization, precise terminology, and the requested clarifications, this work would make a strong contribution to the field. The core idea is promising, but the current presentation requires refinement to maximize its impact.
