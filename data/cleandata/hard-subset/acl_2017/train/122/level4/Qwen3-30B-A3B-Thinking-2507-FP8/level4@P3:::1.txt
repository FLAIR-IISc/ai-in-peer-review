

**Summary:** This paper introduces Neural Belief Tracker (NBT), a novel framework for dialogue state tracking that overcomes limitations of existing approaches by leveraging pre-trained word vectors to learn distributed representations of user utterances and dialogue context. The authors claim three main contributions: (1) a framework that eliminates the need for hand-crafted semantic lexicons while matching state-of-the-art performance; (2) empirical demonstration that semantically-specialized word vectors significantly improve dialogue state tracking; and (3) evidence that the approach generalizes better to domains without lexicons, addressing a key scalability barrier.

**Strengths:**  
1. The NBT framework represents a significant methodological advance by removing the dependency on hand-crafted semantic lexicons, which has been a major bottleneck for scaling dialogue systems to complex domains. The authors convincingly demonstrate that NBT matches the performance of lexicon-based models (which have been the state-of-the-art) while significantly outperforming them when lexicons are unavailable. This addresses a critical practical limitation in the field.

2. The thorough investigation of different word vector spaces (XAVIER, GloVe, Paragram-SL999) provides compelling evidence for the importance of semantic content in word embeddings. The results clearly show that semantically-specialized vectors lead to substantial performance gains, particularly on noisy DSTC2 data where the gains over random initialization are statistically significant (p < 0.05). This demonstrates a valuable connection between embedding quality and downstream task performance.

3. The evaluation on two distinct datasets (DSTC2 with ASR errors and WOZ 2.0 with clean text) provides robust evidence of the framework's generalizability. The consistent improvements across both datasets strengthen the authors' claims, particularly for the WOZ 2.0 dataset where the model's ability to handle richer vocabulary and longer sentences is particularly evident.

**Weaknesses:**  
1. The evaluation is limited to two datasets with relatively simple ontologies (three informable slots), which doesn't fully validate the scalability claim. The authors should demonstrate performance on more complex, multi-domain datasets with larger ontologies (e.g., 10+ informable slots) to better establish the framework's practical utility for real-world deployment.

2. The introductory example (Figure 1) appears misleading as it omits the restaurant name slot, which should be part of the dialogue state. This raises concerns about how the model handles coreference resolution and whether the slot-value pair representation is sufficient to capture the complete dialogue state. The authors should clarify how the framework deals with entities that are not directly mentioned in the utterance but are referenced through pronouns or other means.

3. The paper doesn't address how the NBT framework would interact with a slot tagging component, which is common in many dialogue systems. Given that the NBT model computes similarity between utterances and slot-value pairs, it's unclear how the framework would handle cases where slot values don't match exactly (e.g., "it" referring to a previously mentioned restaurant). The authors should investigate whether the model would benefit from or be hindered by a separate slot tagging component.

**Questions to Authors:**  
1. How would the NBT framework perform on a multi-domain dialogue system with a larger ontology (e.g., 10+ informable slots) where the need for lexicons becomes more pronounced? Could you provide preliminary results or analysis on this?

2. The paper claims the framework handles lexical variation without lexicons, but it's unclear how the model identifies slot boundaries in the utterance. Could you clarify the mechanism for determining which parts of the utterance correspond to which slots, especially in cases with multiple potential slot mentions?

3. The example in Figure 1 shows "inform(price=cheap, food=Thai); request(address)" as the true state after the third user utterance. Shouldn't the dialogue state also include "restaurant_name=The House" based on the context? How does the NBT framework handle coreference resolution for such cases?
