- Strengths:
This paper proposes a new method for monitoring dialog state that benefits from presenting slot values through pre-trained embedding and learns to create distributed representations of user utterances and dialog context. Experiments conducted on two datasets show consistent and significant improvements from the previous de-contextualization-based method. Alternative methods for word embedding (e.g., XAVIER, GloVe, Program-SL999) have been studied.
- Weaknesses:
Although one of the main purposes of using embedding is to generalize to more complex dialog domains where de-contextualization may not extend, the datasets used seem limited. I wonder how this method would fare with and without a separate slot tagging component in more complex dialog. For example, when calculating similarity between utterances and slot value pairs, one might actually limit the inference to the span of slot values. This should apply even if the values do not match.
I think the examples in the introduction are misleading; shouldn't "restaurant_name=The House" be included in the dialog state? This brings up another question: how does the resolution of coreference affect this work?
- General Discussion:
Overall, the use of pre-trained word embedding is a great idea, and the specific method for using them is exciting.
