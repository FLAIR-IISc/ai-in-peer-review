This paper proposes a neural network framework for dialogue state tracking (DST). The core contribution lies in learning distributed representations for user utterances, system actions, and ontology entries, all initialized with pre-trained word vectors. The authors evaluate two architectures for encoding user utterances: a Deep Neural Network (NBT-DNN) and a Convolutional Neural Network (NBT-CNN). These learned representations are then combined to inform a final network that performs binary classification for each candidate slot-value pair. Experimental results on the DSTC2 dataset demonstrate that the proposed framework significantly outperforms a strong delexicalized baseline.

Overall, this is a quality paper with a clear objective, a well-motivated approach, and strong empirical results that advance the state-of-the-art on the task. However, the paper's presentation and organization could be substantially improved to make the work more accessible and impactful, especially for readers not already deeply familiar with this subfield.

**Major Comments:**

The primary weakness of the paper is its clarity and organization. While the technical work is sound, the current structure makes it difficult to fully grasp the context and details of the proposed method.

1.  **Problem Definition and Context:** A formal definition of Dialogue State Tracking is needed at the very beginning of the paper. The current introduction does not clearly delineate DST from related tasks like Spoken Language Understanding (SLU), which could create confusion. To provide better context, I suggest moving the general architecture of a dialogue system (currently described in Section 2) to Section 1. This would allow the authors to precisely situate DST in relation to other components like ASR, SLU, and dialogue policy, clarifying the inputs it receives and the outputs it is expected to produce.

2.  **Notation and Readability:** The paper's readability is hampered by the late introduction of key notations. Several symbols (e.g., tq, ts, t_v) are used in formulas and descriptions much earlier than their definitions appear. It would greatly improve the reader's experience if all mathematical notations were consolidated and defined in an early section before their first use.

**Minor Comments and Questions:**

1.  **"Joint" Modeling:** The term "joint" could be misleading. Does the model perform both SLU and DST as distinct, separable tasks, or does "joint" simply mean that SLU-like features are learned implicitly within the end-to-end DST framework? Clarifying the scope of the term would be helpful.

2.  **Dataset Modifications:** The paper mentions correcting errors in the DSTC2 dataset. Could you please provide some statistics on the number and type of corrections made? Using a modified dataset makes direct comparison with other published results on the standard DSTC2 benchmark difficult. If the number of corrections is not substantial, it would strengthen the paper significantly to also include comparisons with other DSTC2 entries on the original, uncorrected dataset.

3.  **Alternative Architectures:** The paper explores DNN and CNN models for utterance representation. Given the sequential nature of language, have the authors considered using Recurrent Neural Networks (RNNs) or LSTMs? These models have achieved considerable success on related SLU tasks and seem like a natural fit for learning representations from dialogue history. A brief discussion of why CNNs/DNNs were chosen over recurrent architectures would be valuable.

4.  **Baseline Details:** To better appreciate the benefits of the proposed approach, more details about the semantic dictionary used by the baseline model would be helpful. Information about the size of this dictionary and the manual effort required to create it would provide a clearer picture of the costs associated with traditional, resource-heavy systems that the proposed model aims to replace.

5.  **Qualitative Analysis:** The quantitative results are strong, but a qualitative error analysis would further improve the paper. I would encourage the authors to include a few concrete examples of dialogue turns that were incorrectly handled by the baseline but correctly classified by the proposed NBT models. This would provide valuable insight into the practical advantages of the learned representations.
