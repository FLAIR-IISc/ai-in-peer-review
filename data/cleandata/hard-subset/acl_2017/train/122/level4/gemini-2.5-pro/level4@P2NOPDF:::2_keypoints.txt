- Proposes a neural framework for DST that learns distributed representations for utterances, system actions, and ontology entries from pre-trained word vectors, avoiding hand-crafted lexical features.
- Compares two utterance encoding architectures: NBT-DNN and NBT-CNN, concatenating representations for binary classification of slot-value pairs.
- Reports strong empirical results on DSTC2, significantly outperforming a delexicalized baseline.
- Strength: well-motivated objective, sound methodology, and strong empirical evidence supporting the approach.
- Primary weakness: poor organization and presentation that may impede comprehension for non-experts in DST.
- Suggest moving general dialogue system architecture to the introduction and providing a formal DST problem definition clarifying its relation to ASR, SLU, and policy learning.
- Recommend consolidating and defining all mathematical notation (e.g., tq, ts, tv) in one place or upon first use to improve readability.
- Clarify use of the term “joint” — whether the model performs SLU as a separate task or if a different descriptor would be more accurate.
- Provide statistics and types of corrections made to the DSTC2 dataset, and include comparisons on the original unmodified DSTC2 to ensure fair benchmarking.
- Discuss why RNNs/LSTMs were not used for utterance representation and consider their potential benefits given utterance sequentiality.
- Give more details about the baseline’s semantic dictionary (size, coverage, manual effort) to contextualize the cost advantages of the proposed method.
- Include a qualitative error analysis with examples showing errors corrected by NBT-DNN/NBT-CNN but missed by the baseline to illustrate practical gains.
