- Introduces the Neural Belief Tracker (NBT), a dialogue state tracker that uses pre-trained word embeddings instead of delexicalization.
- NBT composes distributed representations of utterance and context and matches them against candidate slot-value pairs; two variants presented (NBT-DNN, NBT-CNN).
- Main contributions: a neural architecture that removes the need for hand-crafted lexicons, strong results on DSTC2 and WOZ 2.0, and analysis showing semantically specialized embeddings (Paragram-SL999) help most.
- Strength: principled, scalable-motivated approach addressing the delexicalization bottleneck and better handling paraphrase/lexical variation.
- Strength: thorough experiments showing NBT matches or outperforms delexicalization-based baselines, with larger gains when semantic dictionaries are unavailable and on richer language (WOZ 2.0).
- Strength: useful ablation study demonstrating that word-vector quality (semantic specialization) materially affects belief-tracking accuracy.
- Weakness: empirical evaluation is confined to two similar restaurant-domain datasets with a small ontology (three informable slots), limiting claims about scalability to larger, diverse domains.
- Weakness: model uses a single global utterance vector to match slot-values, which may dilute signals for multiple constraints and lacks comparison to local/span-based alternatives (e.g., slot tagging).
- Question to authors: does the belief state include tracking specific entity names/coreference (e.g., name="The House"), and if not, how would NBT incorporate such capabilities?
