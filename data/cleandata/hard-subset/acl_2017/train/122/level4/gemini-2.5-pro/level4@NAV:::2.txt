This paper introduces a neural network-based approach to dialogue state tracking (DST). The primary contribution is a method for learning distributed representations for user utterances, system responses, and ontology entries, all initialized with pre-trained word embeddings. For the specific task of representing user utterances, the authors evaluate and contrast two distinct neural architectures: an NBT-DNN and an NBT-CNN. These learned representations are subsequently integrated and passed to a downstream network, which performs a binary classification for each given slot-value pair. The experimental results demonstrate that the proposed framework achieves a significant improvement in performance over a delexicalized baseline approach.

Overall, this is a solid contribution with a well-defined objective, a sound methodology, and empirically superior results compared to prior work. However, the paper's organization hinders its clarity, making it difficult for readers less familiar with the domain to fully grasp the technical details. To begin with, a more formal definition of Dialogue State Tracking (DST) should be provided at the outset. The current explanation is ambiguous and becomes more confusing when discussed in conjunction with Spoken Language Understanding (SLU). I would recommend presenting the general dialogue system architecture in Section 1 rather than Section 2. This should be followed by a clear problem definition for DST, highlighting its interplay with other components like ASR, SLU, and policy learning. Furthermore, readability would be greatly enhanced by defining all mathematical notations in a dedicated, early section. For instance, symbols such as tq, ts, and t_v appear in the text well before their definitions are provided.

Below are additional comments and questions:

*   Can the proposed model be used to perform SLU as a standalone task? If not, the use of the term 'joint' may be misleading, as it implies the model is capable of addressing both tasks.
*   Could you provide statistics on the number of errors corrected in the DSTC2 dataset? If the number of corrections is not substantial, the experiments should also include comparisons with other published results on the original DSTC2 dataset, including official DSTC2 entries.
*   Have the authors considered using Recurrent Neural Networks (RNNs) or LSTMs to capture the sequential nature of user utterances when learning their representations? Given the recent success of these recurrent models in SLU, they might also prove effective for DST.
*   Providing more details about the semantic dictionary employed by the baseline model would be beneficial. This would help readers understand the manual effort and cost associated with creating such a resource.
*   The paper would be strengthened by including a qualitative analysis with examples that the baseline failed to predict correctly but the proposed models successfully resolved. This would offer valuable insight into the models' advantages.
