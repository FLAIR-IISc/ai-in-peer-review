- Introduces Neural Belief Tracker (NBT) that replaces delexicalization with learned semantic matching using pre-trained word vectors.  
- Proposes two NBT variants (NBT-DNN, NBT-CNN) that compose word vectors into utterance representations and make binary decisions for slot-value pairs.  
- Empirically, NBT matches state-of-the-art with semantic dictionaries and outperforms baselines without them; specialized word vectors (Paragram) yield strong gains.  
- Removes need for hand-crafted semantic lexicons, improving scalability and handling lexical/semantic variation via embeddings.  
- Evaluation is comprehensive: DSTC2 (ASR noise) and WOZ 2.0 (clean, diverse language) show consistent success across conditions.  
- Model description and figures are clear; architecture components and variant distinctions are well justified.  
- Paper lacks a formal problem definition and high-level system diagram; some notation is introduced before being defined.  
- No discussion or comparison with sequential models (RNN/LSTM/GRU); choice of DNN/CNN over recurrent architectures is unexplained.  
- Manual corrections to DSTC2 may affect comparability; paper should quantify edits and report results on uncorrected data.  
- Clarify how “joint” the model is and whether it can perform SLU standalone to extract slot-value pairs from single utterances.  
- Explain rationale for using DNN/CNN for utterance representation instead of sequential models like LSTMs, including trade-offs.  
- Provide statistics on the hand-crafted semantic dictionary used by the baseline (e.g., average lexical variants per ontology value).  
- Include qualitative error analysis with examples where NBT succeeds and delexicalization-based baselines fail (semantic cases).  
- Report details on DSTC2 corrections: number and types of errors fixed to support reproducibility.
