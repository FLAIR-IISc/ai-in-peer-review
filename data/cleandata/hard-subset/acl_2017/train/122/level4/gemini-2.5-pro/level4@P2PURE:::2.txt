This paper proposes a neural network framework for dialogue state tracking (DST). The primary contribution is a method for learning vector representations of user utterances, system outputs, and ontology entries, all initialized with pre-trained word vectors. For utterance representation, the authors evaluate two neural architectures: a Deep Neural Network (NBT-DNN) and a Convolutional Neural Network (NBT-CNN). These learned representations are combined and fed into a final network that performs binary classification for each candidate slot-value pair.

Experimental results demonstrate that the proposed framework significantly outperforms a delexicalized baseline. Overall, this is a quality paper with a well-defined goal, a sound methodology, and strong empirical results that advance the state of the art.

However, the paper's organization hinders its clarity, particularly for readers less familiar with the field. The paper would benefit from a more formal definition of Dialogue State Tracking (DST) in the introduction. The current description is somewhat ambiguous and becomes more confusing when discussed in relation to Spoken Language Understanding (SLU). I suggest moving the general dialogue system architecture from Section 2 to the introduction. This should be followed by a precise problem definition for DST that clarifies its relationship with other components like ASR, SLU, and policy learning. Furthermore, readability would be greatly enhanced if all notations were defined upon their first use; currently, several symbols (e.g., tq, ts, t_v) appear before their definitions.

Other comments and questions are listed below:

*   The term 'joint' could be misleading. Can the model perform SLU as a separate, standalone task? If not, the authors should clarify why this terminology is used, as it may imply the model can handle both DST and SLU.

*   The authors mention correcting errors in the DSTC2 dataset. Could you provide statistics on the number of corrections made? If the modifications are not extensive, it would strengthen the paper to also include experimental comparisons with other published work that used the original DSTC2 dataset.

*   Have the authors considered using Recurrent Neural Networks (RNNs) or LSTMs to model the sequential nature of user utterances? Given their recent success in SLU, these models seem like a natural fit for DST and could prove effective.

*   Providing more details about the semantic dictionary used for the baseline would be helpful. Information regarding the effort required to create it would better contextualize the cost of building such manual resources, thereby highlighting an advantage of the proposed approach.

*   To better illustrate the advantages of the proposed models, it would be beneficial to include a qualitative analysis with a few examples. Showing specific instances where the baseline fails but the proposed models succeed would make the improvements more concrete.
