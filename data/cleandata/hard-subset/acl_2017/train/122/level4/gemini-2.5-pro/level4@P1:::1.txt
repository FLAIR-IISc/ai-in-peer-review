### Summary of the Paper and Contributions
This paper introduces the Neural Belief Tracker (NBT), a novel framework for dialogue state tracking in task-oriented systems. The core idea is to move away from traditional delexicalization-based approaches, which rely on hand-crafted semantic dictionaries, and instead leverage pre-trained word embeddings to handle linguistic variation. The NBT models learn to compose distributed representations of the user utterance and dialogue context, and then match these against candidate slot-value pairs from the domain ontology to update the belief state. The authors present two variants of the model, NBT-DNN and NBT-CNN, and evaluate them on two restaurant-domain datasets.

The main contributions of this work are:
1.  A novel neural architecture for dialogue state tracking that operates directly on pre-trained word embeddings. This design effectively bypasses the need for delexicalization and manually created lexicons, which represents a significant step towards building more scalable dialogue systems.
2.  Strong empirical results on two datasets (DSTC2 and WOZ 2.0). The proposed NBT models are shown to match the performance of state-of-the-art systems that use hand-crafted lexicons, and significantly outperform them when such resources are unavailable.
3.  A valuable analysis of the impact of different word vector spaces on task performance. The experiments clearly show that semantically specialized embeddings (Paragram-SL999) provide a significant advantage over standard distributional embeddings (GloVe) and random initialization, offering useful insights for the community.

### Strengths
1.  The primary strength of this paper is its well-motivated and elegant approach to a key problem in dialogue systems. The reliance on hand-crafted lexicons for delexicalization is a well-known bottleneck that hinders scalability. By reasoning over pre-trained word vector spaces, the proposed NBT framework offers a principled and data-driven solution to handle paraphrasing and lexical variation, making it a much more promising direction for building trackers in complex, real-world domains.
2.  The experimental results are thorough and convincing. The authors demonstrate consistent and significant improvements over competitive delexicalization-based baselines across two distinct datasets. The fact that NBT performs on par with models augmented by semantic dictionaries—without requiring those dictionaries itself—is a powerful result that validates the effectiveness of the approach. The stronger gains on the WOZ 2.0 dataset, which contains richer and more natural language, further underscore the model's capacity for genuine semantic understanding.
3.  The ablation study comparing different word vector initializations is an excellent addition. It not only strengthens the paper's claims but also provides a clear and practical demonstration of the benefits of using semantically specialized word vectors for a downstream task. This finding—that vector space quality directly impacts belief tracking accuracy—is an important contribution in its own right.

### Weaknesses
1.  While a central motivation for the NBT is scalability, the empirical evaluation is confined to two datasets from the same, relatively simple, restaurant-finding domain. The domain ontology is small (three informable slots), which may not fully stress-test the model's capabilities. To substantiate the claims of scalability and generalization to more complex domains, an evaluation on a task with a significantly larger and more diverse ontology would be much more compelling.
2.  The model computes a single vector representation for the entire user utterance and compares it against candidate slot-value pairs. This "global" matching strategy may not be optimal, especially for longer utterances that might specify multiple constraints. For instance, in a more complex utterance, the signal from one slot-value pair could be diluted in the overall utterance representation. An alternative approach could involve a more "local" matching, for example, by first identifying potential value spans in the utterance (akin to slot tagging) and then computing similarity specifically over those spans. The paper does not discuss or compare against such alternatives, which could potentially be more robust in complex scenarios.

### Questions to Authors
1.  In the introduction, the example dialogue state after the system mentions "The House" is `inform(price=cheap, food=Thai); request(address)`. It seems that a complete belief state should also track the specific entity being discussed, i.e., `name="The House"`. Could you clarify the scope of the state tracking task addressed in this work? Specifically, does your model's definition of the belief state include tracking specific entity names or handling coreference, and if not, how do you envision integrating such capabilities into the NBT framework?
