- Introduces Neural Belief Tracker (NBT), an end-to-end DST that reasons over pre-trained word embeddings to avoid SLU modules and hand-crafted lexicons.  
- Proposes two NBT variants (NBT-DNN, NBT-CNN) that compare utterance/context representations to candidate slot-value pairs.  
- Demonstrates NBT matches state-of-the-art delexicalisation-based models and outperforms them when lexicons are unavailable.  
- Main contribution: a scalable, data-driven DST architecture replacing brittle lexicon matching by leveraging pre-trained embeddings.  
- Secondary contribution: strong empirical validation and analysis, showing embedding choice (random, GloVe, Paragram-SL999) significantly affects performance.  
- Strength: principled solution to reduce reliance on hand-crafted resources, improving portability to new domains.  
- Strength: convincing experiments on DSTC2 (ASR-noisy) and WOZ 2.0 (clean) that support claims, especially without lexicons.  
- Strength: insightful ablation showing semantically specialized embeddings substantially boost downstream DST performance.  
- Weakness: evaluation limited to a simple restaurant domain with only three informable slots, leaving scalability to large, complex domains untested.  
- Weakness: global utterance representation (summation/max-pooling) may dilute signals in longer turns with multiple constraints; slot-focused span detection might be better.  
- Q to authors: does NBT handle entity resolution (e.g., specific restaurant names) or is that out of scope?  
- Q to authors: would a hybrid approach (sequence tagging to locate value spans, then semantic comparison) improve robustness for complex multi-intent turns?
