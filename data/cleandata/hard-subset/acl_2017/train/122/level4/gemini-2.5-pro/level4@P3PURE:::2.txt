This paper proposes a neural network framework for dialogue state tracking (DST). The core contribution is a method for learning distributed representations of user utterances, system outputs, and ontology entries directly from pre-trained word vectors. For representing user utterances, the authors evaluate two architectures: a feed-forward neural network (NBT-DNN) and a convolutional neural network (NBT-CNN). These learned representations are then combined and fed into a final network that performs binary classification for each candidate slot-value pair. Experimental results on the DSTC2 dataset demonstrate that the proposed framework significantly outperforms a strong baseline that relies on a delexicalized approach.

Overall, this is a quality paper with a clear objective, a well-motivated approach, and strong empirical results. However, the paper's presentation and organization could be significantly improved to make the work more accessible, particularly for readers not deeply familiar with this specific subfield.

Specifically, the paper would benefit from a more formal definition of the DST task at the outset. The current description is not sufficiently clear and becomes potentially confusing when discussed in conjunction with Spoken Language Understanding (SLU). I suggest restructuring the introduction by first presenting the general architecture of a dialogue system (currently in Section 2) within Section 1. This should be followed by a precise problem definition for DST that clearly situates it relative to other components like ASR, SLU, and policy learning.

Furthermore, readability would be enhanced if all notations were consolidated and defined in an earlier section. Several symbols (e.g., t_q, t_s, t_v) are used before they are formally described.

Below are additional comments and questions:

*   The use of the term 'joint' could be misleading. Can this model perform SLU as a separate, distinct task? If not, please clarify what is meant by 'joint' modeling, as it might imply the model can handle both DST and SLU independently.
*   Could you provide statistics on the number of errors corrected in the DSTC2 dataset? If the number of corrections is not substantial, the experimental section would be strengthened by including comparisons with other published results on the original, uncorrected DSTC2 dataset.
*   Have the authors considered using Recurrent Neural Networks (RNNs) or LSTMs for modeling the user utterance? Given the sequential nature of language and the success of these architectures on related SLU tasks, they seem like a natural fit for this component and may offer further improvements.
*   To better highlight the advantages of the proposed approach, please provide more details about the semantic dictionary used by the baseline. This would help readers appreciate the significant manual effort and cost typically required to build such resources.
*   The analysis would be more compelling if it included a few qualitative examples. Please consider adding some samples that are incorrectly handled by the baseline but correctly predicted by the proposed NBT models to provide more intuition about their advantages.
