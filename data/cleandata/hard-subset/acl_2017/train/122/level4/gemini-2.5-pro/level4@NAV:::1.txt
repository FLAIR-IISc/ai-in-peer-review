- Strengths:
The primary contribution of this work is a new method for dialogue state tracking. The approach uniquely leverages pre-trained embeddings to represent slot values, which are then composed into distributed representations for both the user utterance and the broader dialogue context. Empirical evaluation on two benchmark datasets demonstrates that the proposed model consistently and significantly outperforms the delexicalization-based baseline. The authors also commendably explore the impact of different pre-trained word embeddings, including XAVIER, GloVe, and Program-SL999.

- Weaknesses:
While a primary motivation for employing embeddings is to generalize to complex domains where delexicalization is not scalable, the datasets used for evaluation are somewhat limited and may not fully demonstrate this advantage. It would be insightful to see a comparison of the proposed method against a pipeline that incorporates an explicit slot tagging component, particularly on more complex dialogue domains. For example, similarity computations could be constrained to specific spans identified as potential slot values, a strategy that should remain viable even when encountering out-of-vocabulary values.
The introductory example may be confusing, as the presented dialogue state appears to omit the "restaurant_name=The House" pair, which seems to be a necessary component. This observation raises a further question regarding the model's handling of coreference resolution and its impact on tracking accuracy.

- General Discussion:
Overall, the integration of pre-trained word embeddings for this task is a very promising direction, and the specific architectural choices for their composition are novel and compelling.
