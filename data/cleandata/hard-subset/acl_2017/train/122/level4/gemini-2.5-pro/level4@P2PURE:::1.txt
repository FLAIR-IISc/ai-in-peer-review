- Strengths:
This paper presents a novel approach to dialogue state tracking that represents slot values with pre-trained embeddings and composes them into distributed representations of user utterances and the dialogue context. Experiments on two datasets demonstrate consistent and significant improvements over a delexicalization-based baseline. The authors also conduct a valuable investigation into the effects of using different pre-trained word embeddings, including XAVIER, GloVe, and Program-SL999.

- Weaknesses:
A primary motivation for using embeddings is to generalize to complex dialogue domains where delexicalization does not scale. However, there is a mismatch between this motivation and the evaluation, as the datasets used for experiments appear to be of limited complexity. It would be insightful to understand how the approach would compare with and without its slot tagging component, particularly on more complex dialogues. For example, when computing similarity between the utterance and candidate slot-value pairs, the computation could be restricted to the text span where the slot value is likely to appear. This could be effective even for values not seen during training.

The introductory example appears to be misleading. The dialogue state shown seems incomplete; for instance, it omits the slot-value pair "restaurant_name=The House". This raises the question of how the model handles related challenges like coreference resolution, which is not discussed.

- General Discussion:
Overall, the use of pre-trained word embeddings for this task is well-motivated, and the specific method proposed for incorporating them is novel and promising.
