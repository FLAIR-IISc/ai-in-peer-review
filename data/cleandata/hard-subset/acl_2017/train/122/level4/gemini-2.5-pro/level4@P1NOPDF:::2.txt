This paper presents a neural network-based framework for dialogue state tracking (DST). The main contribution is a method for learning representations of user utterances, system outputs, and ontology entries, all derived from pre-trained word vectors. For utterance representation specifically, the authors compare two neural network models: NBT-DNN and NBT-CNN. These learned representations are then combined and fed into a downstream network that makes a binary decision for each slot-value pair. The experiments show that the proposed framework achieves significant performance improvements over a delexicalized baseline.

Overall, this is a high-quality paper with a clear objective, a well-motivated approach, and results that improve upon previous work. However, the paper's organization could be improved to more effectively convey the technical details, especially for readers who may not be experts in this specific area.

First, the paper would benefit from a more formal definition of DST in the introduction. The current description is not entirely clear and becomes more confusing when discussed in conjunction with Spoken Language Understanding (SLU). I suggest presenting the general architecture of a dialogue system in Section 1 (rather than Section 2) and then providing a precise problem definition for DST that clarifies its relationship with other components like ASR, SLU, and policy learning.

Furthermore, readability would be greatly improved if all mathematical notations were defined in an early, consolidated section. Several symbols (e.g., tq, ts, t_v) are used well before they are formally defined, which can hinder comprehension.

Below are some additional comments and questions:

*   The term 'joint' could be misleading. Is it possible to perform SLU as a standalone task with this model? If not, the term 'joint' might imply that the model can handle both tasks, when it seems more focused on using SLU-style features for the DST task.
*   Could you provide statistics on how many errors were corrected in the DSTC2 dataset? If the number of corrections is not substantial, the experiments would be strengthened by including comparisons with other published results on the original DSTC2 dataset, such as the official entries from the challenge.
*   Have the authors considered using Recurrent Neural Networks (RNNs) or LSTMs to capture sequential information when learning utterance representations? Given the recent success of these models in SLU, it seems plausible they would also be effective for DST.
*   More details about the semantic dictionary used for the baseline would be helpful. This would allow readers to better appreciate the manual effort and cost required to build such resources, further highlighting the benefits of the proposed approach.
*   The paper would be more compelling with a qualitative error analysis. Could you provide some examples of tracking errors made by the baseline that were correctly handled by the proposed models? This would provide valuable insight into the practical advantages of your framework.
