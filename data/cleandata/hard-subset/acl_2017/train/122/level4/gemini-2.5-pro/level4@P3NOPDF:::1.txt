### Summary and Major Points

This paper presents a novel and valuable approach to dialogue state tracking (DST) that moves beyond traditional delexicalization methods. The core idea of using pre-trained word embeddings to represent slot values and compose them into distributed representations of utterances and dialogue context is both well-motivated and promising. The experimental results on two datasets convincingly demonstrate significant improvements over delexicalization-based baselines.

While the technical approach is strong, the evaluation could be strengthened by using more complex dialogue domains to fully substantiate the claims of scalability and generalization. The paper would also benefit from a discussion of how the model handles more nuanced linguistic phenomena, such as coreference resolution. Overall, this is a solid paper with an exciting contribution to the field of dialogue systems.

### Strengths

*   **Novelty and Significance:** The paper’s primary strength lies in its novel application of pre-trained embeddings for DST. This method elegantly sidesteps the brittleness and scalability issues inherent in delexicalization, which often requires extensive domain-specific ontologies and cannot handle unseen slot values.
*   **Strong Empirical Results:** The authors conduct experiments on two datasets, demonstrating consistent and significant performance gains over a strong delexicalization-based baseline. This provides solid evidence for the efficacy of the proposed approach.
*   **Thorough Investigation:** The experimental setup is thorough and includes a valuable investigation into the impact of different pre-training methods for word embeddings (e.g., Xavier, GloVe, Program-SL999), adding depth to the empirical analysis.

### Weaknesses

*   **Limited Evaluation Datasets:** A primary motivation for this work is to develop a method that generalizes to complex dialogue domains where delexicalization struggles to scale. However, this strength is not fully demonstrated, as the experiments are conducted on datasets that appear to be of limited complexity. Evaluating the model on more challenging, multi-domain, or open-ended dialogue tasks would significantly strengthen the paper's claims about scalability.
*   **Interaction with Slot Tagging:** The paper could be improved by discussing the relationship between this end-to-end approach and a more modular one involving a separate slot tagging component, especially in the context of more complex dialogues. For instance, could performance be improved by first identifying a potential value's span in the utterance (using a slot tagger) and then computing the embedding similarity only within that constrained span? This hybrid approach might offer more precision, even for out-of-vocabulary or non-matching values, and warrants discussion.
*   **Clarity on Handling Complex Phenomena:** The introductory example may be slightly misleading. It omits "restaurant_name=The House" from the dialogue state, which seems to be a key piece of information provided by the user. This omission raises a broader question about how the proposed model handles phenomena like coreference resolution. If a user refers to an entity mentioned in a previous turn (e.g., "book a table there"), it is unclear how the current framework would resolve this reference and update the dialogue state accordingly. A discussion of these limitations or potential extensions would be welcome.
