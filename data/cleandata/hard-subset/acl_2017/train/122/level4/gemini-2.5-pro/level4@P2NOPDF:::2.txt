This paper proposes a neural network-based framework for Dialogue State Tracking (DST). The core contribution lies in learning distributed representations for user utterances, system actions, and ontology entries directly from pre-trained word vectors, thereby avoiding the need for hand-crafted lexical features. For utterance representation, the authors explore and compare two architectures: a Deep Neural Network (NBT-DNN) and a Convolutional Neural Network (NBT-CNN). These learned representations are then concatenated and fed into a final classification layer that makes a binary decision for each candidate slot-value pair. Experiments on the DSTC2 dataset demonstrate that the proposed framework significantly outperforms a strong baseline that relies on a delexicalized approach.

This is a well-motivated paper with a clear objective, a sound methodology, and strong empirical results. The idea of learning representations to move beyond delexicalization is timely and important for the field.

However, the paper's primary weakness lies in its organization and presentation, which may hinder comprehension, particularly for readers not deeply familiar with the dialogue state tracking literature. I offer the following suggestions to improve the clarity and impact of the work:

First, the paper would benefit from a more formal definition of Dialogue State Tracking (DST) in the introduction. The current description is brief, and its relationship with Spoken Language Understanding (SLU) is not clearly delineated, which can cause confusion. To address this, I suggest moving the general dialogue system architecture from Section 2 to the introduction. This should be followed by a precise problem definition for DST, clarifying its role and its interactions with other system components like ASR, SLU, and dialogue policy learning.

Second, the paper's readability would be greatly improved if all mathematical notations were defined in a dedicated section or upon their first use. Several symbols (e.g., tq, ts, tv) appear in the text well before their definitions are provided, forcing the reader to jump ahead to understand the current section. Consolidating these definitions would create a smoother reading experience.

Below are several other comments and questions for the authors:

- The term 'joint' is used to describe the model, but it is unclear if the model can perform SLU as a separate, distinct task. If not, this terminology could be misleading, as it typically implies a multi-task learning setup where both DST and SLU are optimized simultaneously. Could the authors clarify the choice of this term or consider a more precise descriptor?

- The authors mention correcting errors in the DSTC2 dataset. Could you provide statistics on the number and type of corrections made? If the modifications are minor, it would strengthen the paper to include comparisons with other published results on the original, unmodified DSTC2 dataset to ensure a fair and direct comparison with prior work.

- Given the sequential nature of user utterances, have the authors considered using Recurrent Neural Networks (RNNs) or LSTMs for learning utterance representations? These models have shown great success on related SLU tasks and might capture temporal dependencies within an utterance more effectively than the proposed DNN and CNN architectures. A discussion of this choice would be welcome.

- To better understand the advantages of the proposed neural approach, it would be helpful to have more details about the semantic dictionary used in the baseline system. Specifically, information about its size, coverage, and the manual effort required for its creation would better contextualize the cost of building such resources and highlight the benefits of avoiding them.

- The paper would be more compelling with a qualitative error analysis. Including a few examples of tracking errors made by the baseline that are correctly handled by the proposed models (NBT-DNN/NBT-CNN) would provide valuable insight into the practical advantages of the learned representations.
