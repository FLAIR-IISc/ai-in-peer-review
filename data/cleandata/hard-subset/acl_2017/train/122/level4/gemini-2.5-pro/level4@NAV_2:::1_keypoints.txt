- Summary: Proposes the Neural Belief Tracker (NBT), using pre-trained word embeddings to compose utterance/context vectors and score candidate slot–value pairs, avoiding delexicalization.

- Contribution 1: A data-driven joint SLU/DST architecture that uses pre-trained embeddings to remove dependence on hand-crafted semantic dictionaries.

- Contribution 2: Strong empirical results on DSTC2 and WOZ 2.0, outperforming delexicalization baselines and matching lexicon-augmented baselines.

- Contribution 3: Analysis showing semantically-specialized embeddings (Paragram-SL999) substantially improve belief-tracking performance over GloVe and random vectors.

- Strength 1: Effectively addresses reliance on manual lexicons by reasoning over dense vector representations, improving robustness and scalability to linguistic variation.

- Strength 2: Thorough evaluation across two datasets (one noisy, one cleaner) demonstrating consistent gains and robustness when lexicons are unavailable.

- Strength 3: Ablation/embedding study provides practical guidance that embedding quality, especially semantic specialization, is critical for performance.

- Weakness 1: Evaluation is limited to a simple restaurant domain with three slots, leaving scalability to large, complex ontologies (hundreds of slots/values) untested.

- Weakness 2: Global utterance-based similarity may struggle with turns containing multiple intents or extraneous information; localized/span-based methods are not explored or compared.

- Weakness 3: Paper’s example omits grounded entities, raising concerns about handling entity grounding and coreference resolution in multi-turn dialogues.

- Question 1: Would a hybrid approach (e.g., lightweight span detection followed by NBT similarity on spans) be more effective for complex domains/long utterances?

- Question 2: How does the framework handle entity grounding and coreference resolution (e.g., tracking named entities and resolving “it” across turns)?
