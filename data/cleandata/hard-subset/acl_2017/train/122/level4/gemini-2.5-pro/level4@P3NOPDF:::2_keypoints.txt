- Proposes a neural framework for DST that learns distributed representations for user utterances, system actions, and ontology entries, initialized with pre-trained word vectors.  
- Evaluates two utterance-encoding architectures (NBT-DNN and NBT-CNN) and combines representations to perform binary classification over candidate slot-value pairs.  
- Experimental results on DSTC2 show the approach significantly outperforms a strong delexicalized baseline.  
- Overall assessment: technically sound, well-motivated, and advances state-of-the-art, but presentation and organization need substantial improvement for broader accessibility.  
- Major weakness: paper clarity and organization hinder understanding of context and method details.  
- Suggestion: introduce a formal definition of Dialogue State Tracking up front and move the dialogue system architecture description to the introduction to clarify DST’s inputs/outputs and relation to ASR/SLU/policy.  
- Notation/readability issue: key symbols (e.g., tq, ts, t_v) are used before being defined; consolidate and define all mathematical notation early.  
- Clarify what “joint” modeling means—whether SLU and DST are separate tasks or SLU-like features are learned implicitly within the end-to-end DST model.  
- Dataset concern: report statistics on the DSTC2 corrections made, since modifying the dataset complicates comparisons with published results on the standard benchmark.  
- Architecture choice question: discuss why DNN/CNN were chosen over sequential models like RNNs/LSTMs, which are natural for dialogue history and SLU tasks.  
- Baseline details needed: provide information on the semantic dictionary (size, manual effort) used by the baseline to clarify the resource savings of the proposed method.  
- Suggestion for qualitative analysis: include example turns where the baseline fails but NBT succeeds to illustrate practical advantages of learned representations.
