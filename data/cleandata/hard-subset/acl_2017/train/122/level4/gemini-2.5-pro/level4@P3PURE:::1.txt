**Strengths:**

This paper proposes a novel approach to dialogue state tracking that represents slot values with pre-trained embeddings and learns to compose these into distributed representations of user utterances and the dialogue context. Experiments conducted on two datasets show consistent and significant improvements over a baseline using a delexicalization-based approach. The authors also commendably investigate several alternative methods for pre-training word embeddings (XAVIER, GloVe, Program-SL999).

**Weaknesses:**

One of the primary motivations for using embeddings is to generalize to complex dialogue domains where delexicalization may not scale well. However, the datasets used for evaluation appear somewhat limited in complexity and may not fully demonstrate this advantage.

The paper would be strengthened by a comparison of the proposed method against an approach that includes a separate slot tagging component, especially on more complex dialogues. For example, when computing the similarity between an utterance and a slot-value pair, the computation could be constrained to the text span identified by a slot tagger. Such a technique could be effective even for out-of-ontology values where the surface forms do not match exactly.

The example in the introduction may be misleading. It appears the dialogue state should also include "restaurant_name=The House". This omission raises a broader question about how the model handles coreference resolution, a factor that could significantly impact this task but is not addressed in the paper.

**General Discussion:**

Overall, the use of pre-trained word embeddings for dialogue state tracking is a well-motivated and promising direction. The specific method proposed for composing and utilizing these embeddings is novel and compelling, making this a solid contribution to the field.
