- Strengths:
This paper proposes a novel approach for dialogue state tracking that leverages pre-trained embeddings to represent slot values. The model learns to compose these embeddings into distributed representations of the user utterance and the dialogue context. Experiments on two datasets show consistent and significant improvements over a strong delexicalization-based baseline. The authors also thoroughly investigate several alternative methods for pre-training word embeddings, including XAVIER, GloVe, and Program-SL999.

- Weaknesses:
One of the main motivations for using embeddings is to generalize to more complex dialogue domains where delexicalization may not scale. However, the datasets used in the experiments seem limited in this regard. It would be interesting to see how the proposed approach would compare with and without a separate slot tagging component, especially on more complex dialogues.

For instance, when computing the similarity between the user utterance and candidate slot-value pairs, the model could restrict its estimation to specific spans in the utterance where slot values are likely to occur. This mechanism could be beneficial even when the user's phrasing does not contain an exact string match for a known value.

The introductory example may also be misleading. In the example provided, it seems the dialogue state should also include "restaurant_name=The House". This raises a broader question about how the model handles phenomena like coreference resolution, which are common in dialogue.

- General Discussion:
Overall, the use of pre-trained word embeddings for dialogue state tracking is a promising direction, and the specific approach for using them proposed in this paper is both novel and well-motivated.
