- Paper introduces the Neural Belief Tracker (NBT): end-to-end neural DST using pre-trained word embeddings, with NBT-DNN and NBT-CNN variants, avoiding hand-crafted lexicons and matching or beating baselines on DSTC2 and WOZ 2.0.  
- Contribution: a scalable, lexicon-free DST framework that learns from pretrained embeddings, reducing manual resource needs.  
- Contribution: strong empirical validation showing NBT outperforms delexicalization baselines and matches lexicon-augmented baselines.  
- Contribution: analysis demonstrating choice of pre-trained word vectors matters—Paragram-SL999 improves accuracy over GloVe or random vectors.  
- Strength: innovative, principled approach addressing lexical mismatch and scalability issues in DST.  
- Strength: convincing experimental results across noisy (DSTC2) and clean (WOZ 2.0) datasets.  
- Strength: overall high-quality work with clear objectives, sound methodology, and state-of-the-art advances.  
- Weakness: presentation may be hard for non-experts—needs formal DST definition, system diagram, and clearer notation.  
- Weakness: lacks qualitative/error analysis and illustrative examples showing where NBT improves over baselines.  
- Weakness: insufficient detail on baseline systems and on why DNN/CNN were chosen instead of recurrent encoders (e.g., LSTMs).  
- Question: Can NBT be adapted to perform SLU as a standalone task, or is "joint" only implicit within DST?  
- Question: Request statistics on the manual cleaning of DSTC2 and how those changes affect comparability with prior results.  
- Question: Have you considered RNN/LSTM utterance encoders for NBT, and what are the expected trade-offs versus DNN/CNN approaches?  
- Question: Please provide details on the semantic dictionary used for the baseline (size and effort required to create it).
