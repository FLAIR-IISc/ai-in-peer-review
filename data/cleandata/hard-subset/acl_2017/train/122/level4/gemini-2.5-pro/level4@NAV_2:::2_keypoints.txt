- Proposes the Neural Belief Tracker (NBT): a dialogue state tracker operating on pre-trained word embeddings with two variants (NBT-DNN, NBT-CNN) that make binary decisions for candidate slot-value pairs.  
- Contribution: a scalable, data-driven DST framework that reasons over embeddings and removes the need for hand-crafted semantic dictionaries.  
- Contribution: strong empirical results on DSTC2 and WOZ 2.0, outperforming delexicalization baselines and matching lexicon-augmented baselines.  
- Contribution: ablation showing embedding quality matters—Paragram-SL999 yields significant gains over random or standard distributional vectors.  
- Strength: well-motivated, elegant approach addressing scalability and linguistic variation by moving away from brittle delexicalization.  
- Strength: thorough experiments across two datasets with particularly strong performance on the linguistically richer WOZ 2.0.  
- Strength: valuable analysis linking pre-trained embedding quality to downstream DST performance.  
- Weakness: presentation could be clearer—needs a formal problem definition and better situating of DST within task-oriented dialogue systems.  
- Weakness: notation is inconsistent and some symbols are used before being defined, hurting readability.  
- Weakness: lacks qualitative/error analysis and concrete example utterances illustrating model advantages over baselines.  
- Question: why not evaluate or discuss recurrent architectures (LSTM/GRU) for utterance encoding given language’s sequential nature?  
- Question: clarify the “Joint SLU/DST” claim—can the model produce standalone turn-level SLU frames separate from the accumulated belief state?  
- Question: provide statistics on manual corrections to DSTC2 and compare results to the original uncorrected benchmark.  
- Question: give details on the baseline semantic dictionary (size, number of entries/rules, human effort) to quantify the practical benefit of avoiding manual resources.
