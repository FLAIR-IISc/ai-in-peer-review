### Summary of the paper
This paper introduces the Neural Belief Tracker (NBT), a novel framework for dialogue state tracking (DST) that aims to overcome the scalability limitations of previous methods. The core idea is to move away from systems that rely on hand-crafted semantic lexicons and delexicalization. Instead, NBT leverages pre-trained word embeddings to represent user utterances, system actions, and candidate slot-value pairs in a continuous vector space. The model learns to compose these representations and compute semantic similarity to determine which of the ontology-defined intents a user has expressed. The authors propose two variants, NBT-DNN and NBT-CNN, and evaluate them on two restaurant-finding datasets (DSTC2 and WOZ 2.0). The results show that NBT matches the performance of state-of-the-art models that use hand-crafted lexicons and significantly outperforms them when such resources are unavailable.

### Main Contributions
1.  **A novel, lexicon-free framework for Dialogue State Tracking:** The primary contribution is the NBT architecture itself. By reasoning over pre-trained word vectors instead of relying on exact string matching or delexicalization, the model provides a principled way to handle lexical and morphological variation in user utterances. This represents a significant and promising step towards building more scalable DST systems that can be more easily adapted to new and complex domains.
2.  **Strong empirical validation against competitive baselines:** The paper provides a thorough experimental evaluation showing that the proposed models achieve state-of-the-art performance on two different datasets. The models consistently outperform strong delexicalization-based baselines, with particularly notable gains on the WOZ 2.0 dataset, which contains richer and more varied language. This demonstrates the practical effectiveness of the proposed approach.
3.  **An insightful analysis of the role of word embedding quality:** The authors conduct an ablation study comparing the effects of using randomly initialized vectors, distributional vectors (GloVe), and semantically specialized vectors (Paragram-SL999). The results clearly show that higher-quality, semantically-tuned embeddings lead to significant performance gains, providing valuable evidence that connects intrinsic embedding quality to success in a downstream dialogue task.

### Strengths
1.  **Excellent motivation and novelty:** The paper effectively argues that reliance on hand-crafted resources like semantic dictionaries is a major bottleneck for scaling dialogue systems. The proposed solution—using learned distributed representations—is an elegant and modern approach to this long-standing problem. By sharing parameters across slots and values and leveraging semantic knowledge from word embeddings, the NBT framework is well-designed to learn efficiently from limited dialogue data.
2.  **Convincing and well-executed experiments:** The evaluation is comprehensive. The models are tested on two datasets with different characteristics (one with ASR noise, one with cleaner but more complex language), which helps to demonstrate the robustness of the approach. The choice of baselines is appropriate, representing the state-of-the-art in delexicalization-based tracking. The reported gains are statistically significant and compellingly support the paper's main claims.
3.  **Clear and well-written presentation:** The paper is very well-written, with clear explanations of the model architecture and motivations. The diagrams (Figures 2, 3, and 4) are effective in illustrating the information flow within the different NBT variants. The background section provides a useful categorization of prior work, effectively situating the NBT within the existing literature.

### Weaknesses
1.  **Limited evaluation domain challenges claims of scalability:** The central motivation of the paper is to create a model that scales to "larger, more complex dialogue domains" where creating lexicons is infeasible. However, the experiments are confined to two datasets from a single, relatively simple restaurant-finding domain with only three informable slots. The claims of scalability are not fully substantiated, as the model has not been tested on a domain with a large ontology (e.g., hundreds of slots and values), which is where delexicalization truly fails and where the computational cost of iterating over all slot-value pairs might become a serious issue.
2.  **Lack of comparison to span-based slot-tagging models:** The paper contrasts its joint SLU/DST approach with delexicalization-based models. However, it omits a comparison to another prominent line of work: pipeline systems where a separate neural slot-tagging model (e.g., a BiLSTM-CRF) first identifies relevant spans in the utterance. Such models can also leverage pre-trained embeddings and might be more precise, as they focus on local textual evidence rather than composing a representation of the entire utterance. It is unclear whether the NBT's approach is superior to a strong, modern slot-tagging pipeline.
3.  **Potential oversimplification of the dialogue state:** The introductory example shows the state as `inform(price=cheap, food=Thai); request(address)` after the user asks "Where is it?" about "The House." A complete dialogue state would also need to track the entity in focus (e.g., `restaurant="The House"`). It is unclear if the NBT framework is designed to handle this type of entity-centric tracking or coreference resolution (i.e., resolving "it" to "The House"), which are crucial aspects of DST in many real-world applications.

### Questions to Authors
1.  The proposed method iterates through every possible slot-value pair in the ontology for each turn. How do you see this approach scaling to domains with hundreds of informable slots or thousands of possible values (e.g., in a movie booking domain with thousands of movie titles)? Would the computational cost become prohibitive?
2.  Regarding the scope of dialogue state tracking: Does the NBT framework have a mechanism to track specific entities mentioned (e.g., "The House" in your example) or to handle coreference? Or is its scope limited to tracking constraints from the predefined slot-value ontology?
3.  Could you elaborate on why a pipeline approach with a state-of-the-art neural sequence tagger for slot filling was not considered as a baseline? Such a model could also leverage pre-trained word embeddings to handle lexical variation but would approach the problem by first identifying value spans rather than classifying the entire utterance.
