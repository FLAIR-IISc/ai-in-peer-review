### Summary and Contributions

This paper introduces the Neural Belief Tracker (NBT), a novel framework for dialogue state tracking (DST) that uses pre-trained word vectors to interpret user utterances. The core idea is to move away from traditional delexicalisation techniques, which rely on hand-crafted semantic dictionaries to handle linguistic variation, and instead learn distributed representations for user utterances, system acts, and ontology items. The model iterates through all possible slot-value pairs, making a binary decision for each one based on these learned representations. The authors propose two variants, NBT-DNN and NBT-CNN, which differ in how they compose the user utterance representation. The framework is evaluated on the DSTC2 and WOZ 2.0 datasets, demonstrating that it can match the performance of strong baselines that use hand-crafted lexicons and significantly outperform them when such resources are unavailable.

In my view, the main contributions of this work are:

1.  **A Novel, Scalable Framework for DST:** The primary contribution is the NBT framework itself, which is the first to successfully leverage pre-trained word embeddings for DST to handle lexical variation without relying on manually created semantic dictionaries. This data-driven approach directly addresses a major scalability bottleneck in building dialogue systems for new or complex domains.
2.  **Strong Empirical Validation:** The paper provides compelling experimental results on two datasets. The NBT models not only achieve state-of-the-art performance but do so without the hand-crafted resources required by competing models. The finding that NBT matches the performance of a lexicon-equipped baseline is a powerful demonstration of its effectiveness.
3.  **Analysis of Word Vector Impact:** The authors provide a valuable analysis showing that the quality of the pre-trained word vectors directly impacts downstream task performance. The demonstration that semantically specialized vectors (Paragram-SL999) yield significant gains over both randomly initialized and standard distributional vectors (GloVe) is an important finding for the broader NLP community.

### Strengths

1.  **Addresses a Key Limitation in Dialogue Systems:** The dependency on hand-crafted lexicons for delexicalisation is a well-known problem that hinders the scalability of dialogue systems. This paper presents a clean, principled, and effective neural solution to this problem. By reasoning over semantic vector spaces, the model can generalize to unseen paraphrases and synonyms, a critical capability for robust language understanding. This is a significant step forward for building more adaptable and low-resource dialogue systems.

2.  **Impressive and Well-Supported Results:** The experimental results are strong and convincing. As shown in Table 1, the NBT models achieve statistically significant improvements over strong, delexicalisation-based baselines on both the DSTC2 and WOZ 2.0 datasets. Crucially, the model matches the performance of the baseline *after* it has been augmented with a hand-crafted semantic dictionary. This result effectively proves that the proposed method can learn the necessary semantic relationships directly from data and word embeddings, validating the central hypothesis of the paper.

3.  **Thorough and Insightful Ablation Study:** The experiments in Section 6.2, which compare different types of word vectors, add significant value to the paper. By showing a clear performance hierarchy (Paragram > GloVe > Random), the authors provide concrete evidence that the semantic content of the embeddings is critical to the model's success. This analysis goes beyond simply presenting a new model and offers insights into *why* it works, strengthening the paper's conclusions and providing guidance for future research.

### Weaknesses

1.  **Clarity and Organization:** The paper's main weakness is its readability, particularly for readers not deeply familiar with the DST literature. The problem of Dialogue State Tracking is not formally defined until late in the paper, and a clear, high-level diagram of a typical dialogue system architecture would help contextualize the tracker's role. The paper jumps into model details in Section 3 without first providing a clear, formal definition of the inputs and outputs, which makes the subsequent architectural descriptions difficult to follow.

2.  **Lack of Clarity in Notations and Terminology:** Several key notations are used before they are fully defined. For example, the symbols `tq`, `ts`, and `tv` appear in the architecture diagram (Figure 2) and equations (9, 10) but are only explained through examples in the accompanying text. A more formal definition of these context variables is needed earlier. Additionally, the term "Joint SLU/DST" is used, but it is not clear if the model can produce structured SLU-style outputs. This terminology could be misleading if the model is only trained for the DST task.

3.  **Insufficient Experimental Detail and Context:** The authors mention that they manually corrected the DSTC2 dataset, but do not provide statistics on the number or type of corrections. This makes it difficult to compare their results to previously published work on the standard DSTC2 benchmark. If the corrections are substantial, it may be a different task; if they are minor, a comparison with other top DSTC2 systems would be valuable context. Furthermore, the paper's central claim is about replacing manual effort, but the cost of creating the baseline's semantic dictionary is not quantified, which would have made the advantage of the NBT approach more tangible.

### Questions to Authors

1.  Could you provide more detail on the semantic dictionary used with the baseline models? For instance, how many rules or entries does it contain? Quantifying the manual effort it represents would help to better frame the contribution of your work.
2.  You mention correcting the DSTC2 dataset. Could you provide statistics on the number of corrections made? Given that you re-ran the baseline, how do your baseline and NBT results compare to other top-performing systems from the original DSTC2 challenge on the original, uncorrected test set?
3.  Your model architecture relies on CNNs and feed-forward networks to represent the user utterance. Given the success of recurrent models (LSTMs/GRUs) for sequence modeling in many SLU tasks, have you considered exploring them for utterance representation within the NBT framework?
4.  To better illustrate the advantages of your model, could you provide a few concrete examples from the test set where the NBT model succeeded and the delexicalisation-based baseline failed? This would provide valuable qualitative insight into the types of linguistic variation your model handles effectively.
