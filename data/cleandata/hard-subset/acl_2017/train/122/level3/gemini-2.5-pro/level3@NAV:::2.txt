**Summary of the paper**
This paper presents the Neural Belief Tracker (NBT), a framework for dialogue state tracking that learns representations for user utterances, system acts, and ontology entries using pre-trained word vectors. The core idea is to move away from delexicalization and hand-crafted semantic dictionaries, instead leveraging the semantic information embedded in word vectors to handle linguistic variation. The authors propose and compare two models for representing user utterances, NBT-DNN and NBT-CNN. These learned representations are then combined to make binary classification decisions for each potential slot-value pair. The primary contribution is a scalable, data-driven DST framework that reduces reliance on domain-specific manual feature engineering. A secondary contribution is the empirical validation on two datasets, showing that the NBT models significantly outperform a strong baseline without a semantic dictionary and match its performance when one is provided.

**Strengths**
1.  **Improved Scalability:** The main strength of this work is its approach to tackling the scalability problem in DST. By using pre-trained word vectors to capture semantic similarity, the model avoids the need for extensive, hand-crafted semantic dictionaries that are a major bottleneck when developing systems for new or complex domains. This is a significant step towards more practical and adaptable dialogue systems.
2.  **Strong Empirical Performance:** The experimental results are convincing. The NBT models achieve significant improvements over a competitive delexicalization-based baseline on two different datasets (DSTC2 and WOZ 2.0). The fact that the model matches the performance of a baseline equipped with a semantic dictionary demonstrates its ability to learn to handle lexical variation effectively from data.
3.  **Novel and Sound Framework:** The paper introduces a clear and well-motivated neural architecture. The idea of learning separate representations for user input, system context, and candidate slot-value pairs before combining them for a final decision is logical and leads to strong results.

**Weaknesses**
1.  **Poor Readability and Organization:** The paper's greatest weakness is its presentation. It would benefit from a formal definition of DST and a clearer depiction of a general dialogue system architecture early on. Key notations (e.g., tq, ts, tv) are introduced in figures and text before they are formally defined, which confuses the reader. This lack of clarity makes the paper difficult to follow for readers not already expert in this specific subfield.
2.  **Lack of Model Exploration:** The paper focuses on DNN and CNN architectures for utterance encoding. However, it fails to explore or discuss Recurrent Neural Networks (RNNs/LSTMs), which have proven highly effective for sequential modeling in related SLU tasks. This omission feels like a missed opportunity to compare against another strong class of models.
3.  **Insufficient Qualitative Analysis:** The paper would be more impactful with a qualitative analysis. Providing a few concrete examples of dialogue turns where the delexicalized baseline fails but the NBT model succeeds would offer valuable insight into the practical advantages and error correction capabilities of the proposed method.

**Questions to Authors**
1.  You mention that your model performs "joint SLU/DST". Could you clarify this? The model appears to perform classification for pre-defined slot-value pairs. Can it also perform sequence labeling for value extraction like a traditional SLU model?
2.  Could you provide statistics on the corrections made to the DSTC2 dataset? If the corrections are minor, it would be helpful to include a comparison with other published results on the original DSTC2 test set for better context.
3.  To better frame the contribution, could you provide more details on the semantic dictionary used by the baseline (e.g., its size or the effort required to create it)? This would help quantify the manual cost your model successfully avoids.
