- NBT: neural DST using pre-trained word vectors to represent utterances, context, and slot-value pairs, making binary decisions per slot-value and avoiding delexicalization.  
- Two utterance encoders (NBT-DNN sums n-grams; NBT-CNN uses convolutions) evaluated on DSTC2 and WOZ 2.0; outperforms baseline and matches lexicon-augmented baseline.  
- Contribution: a scalable, data-driven DST architecture that replaces hand-crafted delexicalization with learned embeddings to handle lexical variation.  
- Contribution: strong empirical validation that vector-space representation learning is effective for DST, with performance tied to embedding quality.  
- Strength: well-motivated, elegant solution that achieves comparable/better results than systems using expert-crafted lexicons.  
- Strength: thorough experimental evaluation on two diverse datasets with appropriate baselines, showing substantial gains (notably on WOZ 2.0).  
- Strength: insightful ablation showing model success depends on high-quality semantic embeddings (Paragram vs GloVe vs random).  
- Weakness: poor organization and readability—key definitions and overview appear too late, making the paper hard for non-experts to follow.  
- Weakness: unclear notation and terminology (variables introduced before definition; "joint SLU/DST" ambiguous regarding open-vocabulary capability).  
- Weakness: limited discussion/justification of architectural choices (no RNN/LSTM exploration) and sparse details on the baseline semantic dictionary (size/creation effort).  
- Question: provide statistics on DSTC2 manual corrections and results on the original uncorrected test set for comparability.  
- Question: did you consider or experiment with RNN/LSTM-based utterance encoders?  
- Question: clarify whether "joint SLU/DST" means the model can extract open-vocabulary values or only classify ontology-defined slot-value pairs.  
- Question: please provide concrete dialogue examples where the delexicalized baseline fails but NBT correctly tracks the user's goal.
