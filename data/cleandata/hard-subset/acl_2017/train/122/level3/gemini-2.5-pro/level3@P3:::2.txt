**Summary of the Paper**

This paper presents the Neural Belief Tracker (NBT), a neural network framework for dialogue state tracking (DST) that operates directly on pre-trained word vectors. The core idea is to learn distributed representations for user utterances, preceding system acts, and candidate slot-value pairs from the domain ontology. By composing and comparing these learned vectors, the model makes a binary decision for each slot-value pair at every turn of the dialogue, thereby avoiding the need for hand-crafted semantic dictionaries and delexicalization common in previous state-of-the-art systems. The authors propose two variants for utterance representation, NBT-DNN (based on summing n-gram vectors) and NBT-CNN (based on convolutions), and evaluate them on the DSTC2 and WOZ 2.0 datasets. Experimental results show that the NBT models significantly outperform a competitive delexicalized baseline and match the performance of the same baseline when it is augmented with a hand-crafted semantic dictionary, demonstrating a promising path towards more scalable DST.

**Contributions**

1.  **A Scalable, Data-Driven DST Framework:** The primary contribution is a novel neural architecture for dialogue state tracking that successfully replaces the brittle, non-scalable delexicalization pipeline with learned representations derived from pre-trained word vectors. By modeling interactions between utterance, context, and ontology embeddings, the framework can handle lexical variation without domain-specific, hand-crafted resources (e.g., semantic dictionaries), which is a significant step toward building dialogue systems in more complex domains.
2.  **Strong Empirical Validation of Representation Learning for DST:** The paper provides a strong empirical demonstration that a vector-space approach to DST is not only viable but highly effective. The experiments on two distinct datasets (one with ASR noise, one with richer language) show that the NBT models achieve state-of-the-art results. Furthermore, the ablation study on different word vector sources (Table 2) provides valuable evidence that the model's performance is directly tied to the semantic quality of the underlying embeddings, confirming the central hypothesis of the work.

**Strengths**

1.  **Well-Motivated and Elegant Solution:** The paper addresses a clear and important limitation in existing DST systems: the reliance on manually curated resources for delexicalization. The proposed solution of using pre-trained word vectors to learn representations is elegant, well-motivated, and aligns with broader trends in NLP. The results strongly support the authors' claims, showing that their model can learn the necessary semantic relationships automatically, achieving comparable performance to a system using an expert-crafted lexicon (e.g., 73.4% vs. 72.9% joint goal accuracy on DSTC2).
2.  **Thorough Experimental Evaluation:** The evaluation is comprehensive and convincing. The use of two different datasets (DSTC2 and WOZ 2.0) demonstrates the model's robustness to both ASR noise and richer linguistic phenomena. The choice of baselines is appropriate, and the comparison against both the standard baseline and a lexicon-enhanced version effectively isolates and highlights the contribution of the proposed method. The gains, especially on the more complex WOZ 2.0 dataset (84.2% vs. 70.8% for NBT-CNN vs. baseline), are substantial.
3.  **Insightful Analysis of Word Vector Impact:** The experiment in Section 6.2, which compares randomly initialized vectors, GloVe, and semantically specialized Paragram vectors, is a key strength. It provides direct evidence that the model's success is not just due to the neural architecture but is critically dependent on leveraging the semantic knowledge encoded in high-quality pre-trained embeddings. This finding has implications beyond this specific paper for future work in dialogue systems.

**Weaknesses**

1.  **Poor Paper Organization and Readability:** The paper's structure makes it unnecessarily difficult for non-experts to follow. A formal definition of Dialogue State Tracking (DST) and a clear overview of a typical dialogue system architecture are needed much earlier to provide context. Figure 2, which illustrates the model, should be introduced in Section 1 to ground the reader from the outset. Key concepts are often explained too late, forcing the reader to piece together the problem setting and solution simultaneously.
2.  **Unclear Notation and Terminology:** Several key notations are used before they are defined. For instance, `tq`, `ts`, and `tv` appear in the model diagram (Figure 2) and the high-level description in Section 3 but are not formally defined until Section 3.3. This requires the reader to jump back and forth and hinders comprehension. Additionally, the term "joint SLU/DST" may be misleading. The model appears to perform classification over a closed set of ontology-defined slot-value pairs, and it is not clear if it can perform open-vocabulary slot-filling, a core task of SLU. This should be clarified.
3.  **Limited Discussion of Architectural Choices and Baselines:** The choice to explore only DNN and CNN models for utterance encoding is not fully justified. Given the sequential nature of utterances, Recurrent Neural Networks (RNNs/LSTMs) are a natural and powerful alternative that has seen widespread success in SLU. A discussion of why these were not considered is missing. Furthermore, the description of the "semantic dictionary" used by the baseline is too brief. Providing statistics on its size or the effort required to create it would more concretely quantify the practical advantage of the NBT approach.

**Questions to Authors**

1.  You mention that you manually corrected the DSTC2 dataset. Could you provide statistics on the extent of these corrections? To ensure comparability with prior work, it would be helpful to see results on the original, uncorrected test set, especially if the changes were minor.
2.  Your utterance representation models (NBT-DNN and NBT-CNN) are based on n-grams. Given their success in many sequence modeling tasks, including SLU, did you consider or experiment with RNN or LSTM-based encoders for the user utterance?
3.  Could you please clarify what "joint SLU/DST" means in the context of your model? Is the model capable of identifying values that are not explicitly defined in the ontology (i.e., performing open-vocabulary information extraction), or is it strictly a classifier for pre-defined slot-value pairs?
4.  The performance gains over the baseline are impressive. To provide more qualitative insight, could you present a few concrete examples where the delexicalization-based baseline fails but your NBT model correctly tracks the user's goal?
