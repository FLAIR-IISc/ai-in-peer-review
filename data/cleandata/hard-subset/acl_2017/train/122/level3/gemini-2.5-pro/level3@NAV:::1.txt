**Summary & Contributions**
This paper introduces the Neural Belief Tracker (NBT), a novel method for Dialogue State Tracking (DST) designed to overcome the limitations of delexicalization. Instead of relying on hand-crafted semantic lexicons to match slot values, the model represents utterances and candidate slot-value pairs using pre-trained word embeddings and learns a similarity function between them. The main contributions are:

1.  A novel and scalable DST framework that leverages pre-trained word embeddings to handle lexical variation, removing the dependency on manually curated dictionaries.
2.  Strong empirical results on two datasets (DSTC2 and WOZ 2.0), demonstrating that the proposed method consistently and significantly outperforms competitive delexicalization-based baselines, particularly when domain-specific lexicons are unavailable.
3.  An insightful analysis of different word embedding pre-training methods, showing that semantically specialized vectors (Paragram-SL999) yield the best performance.

**Strengths**
1.  The core idea of replacing brittle delexicalization with learned representations from pre-trained embeddings is a significant and valuable contribution. It directly addresses a major scalability bottleneck in building task-oriented dialogue systems for new, complex domains.
2.  The claims are well-supported by rigorous experiments. The NBT models not only outperform the standard baselines but also match the performance of baselines augmented with hand-crafted semantic dictionaries (Table 1), effectively learning the necessary semantic relationships from data and word vectors alone.
3.  The paper includes a valuable ablation study on the impact of different word vector spaces (Table 2). This analysis strengthens the paper’s conclusions by demonstrating that the quality of the pre-trained embeddings directly impacts downstream task performance, providing useful guidance for future work in this area.

**Weaknesses**
1.  The primary weakness is the limited scope of the evaluation datasets. The work is motivated by the need to scale to complex domains where creating lexicons is infeasible, yet the experiments are conducted on two relatively simple restaurant-finding datasets where delexicalization methods are still competitive. The paper's central claim about scalability would be much stronger if validated on a more complex, larger-ontology domain.
2.  The evaluation is missing a comparison to a potentially strong baseline architecture that combines slot-tagging with embedding similarity. The NBT model computes similarity over the entire utterance representation. An alternative approach would be to first identify candidate value spans (e.g., "Thai food") using a sequence tagger and then compute the similarity between the candidate span's embedding and the ontology value's embedding. This is a plausible alternative that might yield different results.
3.  The model's handling of dialogue context and coreference is unclear. The introduction's example ("Where is it?") implies the model can resolve the pronoun "it" to the previously mentioned restaurant. However, the context modeling component described in Section 3.3 appears insufficient for such reasoning, raising questions about how the model would perform in dialogues with multiple entities and complex references.

**Questions to Authors**
1.  The introduction example suggests the model handles coreference resolution (resolving "it" to the restaurant entity). Could the authors clarify how the proposed NBT architecture achieves this, or if this capability is beyond the current model's scope?
