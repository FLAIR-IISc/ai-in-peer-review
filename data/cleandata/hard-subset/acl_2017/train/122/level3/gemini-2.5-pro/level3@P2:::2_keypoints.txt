- Proposes the Neural Belief Tracker (NBT), a DST framework using pre-trained word vectors to interpret utterances instead of hand-crafted lexicons.  
- Iterates over all slot-value pairs and makes binary decisions using learned representations for utterances, system acts, and ontology items.  
- Introduces two variants, NBT-DNN and NBT-CNN, differing in how the user utterance is composed.  
- Evaluated on DSTC2 and WOZ 2.0, matching lexicon-equipped baselines and outperforming them when lexicons are unavailable.  
- Main contribution: a scalable, data-driven DST approach that handles lexical variation without manual semantic dictionaries.  
- Strong empirical validation showing state-of-the-art performance and parity with lexicon-augmented baselines.  
- Analysis showing embedding quality matters: Paragram-SL999 > GloVe > random initialization.  
- Strength: directly addresses the scalability bottleneck from delexicalisation by leveraging semantic vector spaces.  
- Strength: statistically significant experimental improvements and convincing comparisons to strong baselines.  
- Strength: insightful ablation study that clarifies why the model succeeds and guides future work on embeddings.  
- Weakness: paper clarity and organization are weak; DST is not formally defined early and model description is abrupt.  
- Weakness: notation and terminology are unclear—symbols (tq, ts, tv) and terms like “Joint SLU/DST” are introduced without formal definition.  
- Weakness: insufficient experimental detail—manual corrections to DSTC2 aren’t quantified and the effort to create baseline semantic dictionaries is not reported.  
- Questions for authors: please quantify the baseline semantic dictionary (size/effort), report statistics on DSTC2 corrections and comparisons to original benchmarks, discuss trying recurrent models (LSTM/GRU) for utterance representation, and provide qualitative examples where NBT succeeds but delexicalisation fails.
