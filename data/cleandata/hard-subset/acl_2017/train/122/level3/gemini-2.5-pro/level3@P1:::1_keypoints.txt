- Introduces Neural Belief Tracker (NBT), a lexicon-free DST that represents utterances, system acts, and slot-values in a shared pre-trained embedding space and scores semantic similarity.  
- Proposes two composition variants, NBT-DNN and NBT-CNN, and evaluates on DSTC2 and WOZ 2.0, matching or outperforming delexicalization baselines, especially without hand-crafted lexicons.  
- Main contribution: a principled, scalable alternative to brittle delexicalization that handles lexical variation via learned embeddings.  
- Main contribution: strong empirical validation showing embeddings can replace manual semantic dictionaries across two datasets.  
- Main contribution: an ablation showing embedding quality (random, GloVe, Paragram-SL999) materially affects DST performance, validating representation learning.  
- Strength: novel, scalable approach reducing need for domain-specific semantic dictionaries.  
- Strength: convincing, consistent empirical results on both spoken and typed datasets that isolate the benefit of learned paraphrases.  
- Strength: well-executed ablation demonstrating the importance of semantically specialized word vectors.  
- Weakness: evaluation limited to a simple restaurant domain with only three informable slots, leaving scalability to larger, more ambiguous domains untested.  
- Weakness: missing comparison to a span-based SLU baseline (e.g., BiLSTM-CRF) that would detect value spans before similarity scoring.  
- Weakness: context/co-reference modeling appears simplistic (gating on request/confirm acts) and may not handle pronoun resolution or complex multi-turn entity tracking.  
- Question: how does NBT resolve pronouns like “Where is it?”—does the model handle such co-reference implicitly or is this a limitation?  
- Question: did the authors consider or compare to an approach that first tags candidate value spans and then computes similarity between spans and canonical values?
