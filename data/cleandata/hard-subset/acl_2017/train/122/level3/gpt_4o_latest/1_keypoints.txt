- Introduces Neural Belief Tracking (NBT) models (NBT-DNN, NBT-CNN) that use pre-trained word embeddings for dialogue state tracking without hand-crafted semantic lexicons.  
- Demonstrates significant empirical improvements over delexicalization-based baselines on DSTC2 and WOZ 2.0.  
- Compares different pre-trained embeddings and highlights the benefit of semantically specialized vectors (e.g., Paragram-SL999).  
- Effectively leverages pre-trained embeddings to handle lexical and semantic variation, removing scalability limits of delexicalization.  
- Shows strong, statistically significant gains in joint goal accuracy and request accuracy, especially when semantic dictionaries are unavailable.  
- Provides a comprehensive evaluation of embedding choices, offering useful insights for downstream DST tasks.  
- Claims improved scalability and generalization by eliminating reliance on hand-crafted lexicons.  
- Evaluation is limited to two restaurant-domain datasets, raising concerns about generalization to other domains.  
- Does not explore the impact of separate slot tagging or span-restricted similarity estimation components.  
- Some introductory examples are misleading and the paper omits discussion of coreference resolution, a key DST challenge.  
- Robustness to noisy ASR outputs is not convincingly demonstrated; performance mainly shown on clean data.  
- Asks how the approach generalizes to multi-domain systems with larger ontologies and complex slot-value pairs.  
- Queries whether adding a separate slot tagging component or span-restricted similarity would improve NBT performance.  
- Asks how NBT models would handle coreference resolution in multi-turn dialogues.  
- Recommends acceptance, noting the paper’s scalable, lexicon-free contribution but urging future work on scalability and ASR robustness.
