Summary
This paper proposes the Neural Belief Tracker (NBT), a joint SLU/DST framework that reasons over pre-trained word embeddings to compose utterance and context representations and make binary decisions over candidate slot–value pairs. Evaluations on DSTC2 and WOZ 2.0 show consistent, statistically significant gains over delexicalization-based baselines, including when hand-crafted lexicons are absent. The paper also studies the impact of different embedding initializations (XAVIER, GloVe, Paragram-SL999).

Contributions (as I see them)
1) Empirical validation that semantically specialized pre-trained embeddings enable robust DST without semantic lexicons, yielding significant accuracy improvements on two public datasets.
2) A simple, modular joint SLU/DST architecture that composes utterance and last-system-act representations and uses element-wise interactions to score slot–value hypotheses.
3) A comparative analysis of embedding pre-training strategies showing clear benefits of semantic specialization (Paragram-SL999) over random/GloVe initializations.

Strengths
- Clear, consistent gains over strong delexicalization baselines across joint goal and request accuracy, with statistical significance reported. The improvements are especially notable on WOZ 2.0 (richer language), supporting the core claim that embeddings capture paraphrase variation.
- Sound architectural choices: fixed pre-trained vectors, parameter sharing across slots/values, and simple context gating for system requests/confirms yield a compact, data-efficient model.
- Informative ablations on word vectors. Showing Paragram-SL999 > GloVe/XAVIER strengthens the claim that semantic similarity (not mere relatedness) matters for DST.
- Practical belief update mechanism over ASR N-best lists, with explicit hyperparameter tuning, increases reproducibility and usefulness.

Weaknesses
- Limited domains: both datasets are single-domain Cambridge restaurants with small ontologies. Claims about scalability/generalization to complex or multi-domain DST remain untested.
- Missing comparison to a pipeline with a dedicated slot-tagging/sequence labeling component. A span-restricted similarity (computing scores only on predicted slot-value spans) could materially change the conclusions.
- Context modeling is Markovian and tied to last system acts; handling of coreference and anaphora is unclear. The introductory example suggests entity resolution (e.g., restaurant_name) that the current model may not address.
- No analysis of failure cases or robustness to unseen ontology values/long-tail paraphrases; unclear how well fixed embeddings transfer beyond the training vocabulary.

Questions to Authors (optional)
- Can you compare against a strong slot tagger (e.g., CRF/biLSTM) feeding a belief tracker, and/or report results with similarity restricted to predicted spans?
- How does NBT handle coreference beyond last-turn context (e.g., resolving “it/there” and restaurant names)?
- Any results on larger/multi-domain ontologies or cross-domain transfer?
- What are the main error modes, and how sensitive is performance to λ and embedding fine-tuning?
