- Introduces the Neural Belief Tracker (NBT) that replaces delexicalization and hand-crafted semantic dictionaries by reasoning over pre-trained word vectors.  
- NBT encodes user utterances, preceding system acts, and ontology slot-value pairs, and makes per-turn binary decisions for each slot-value pair.  
- Compares two utterance encoders: NBT-DNN (sum of n-gram vectors + nonlinearity) and NBT-CNN (convolution + ReLU + max-pool).  
- Incorporates context via a simple similarity-based gating conditioned on the last system request/confirm acts.  
- Uses a rule-based belief update that aggregates N-best ASR hypotheses and performs temporal smoothing.  
- Experiments on DSTC2 (ASR) and WOZ 2.0 (typed) show statistically significant improvements over delexicalized baselines, especially when no semantic dictionary is available.  
- Demonstrates that semantically specialized word vectors (Paragram-SL999) yield better tracking than GloVe or random initialization.

- Contribution: a lexicon-free DST architecture coupling SLU/DST by operating directly on pre-trained word vectors with shared parameters across ontology values.  
- Contribution: empirical evidence on DSTC2 and WOZ 2.0 that NBT matches or surpasses strong delexicalized baselines and isolates the benefit of semantically specialized vectors.  
- Contribution: a simple context-gating mechanism plus N-best ASR aggregation/update rule that improve robustness under noisy ASR.

- Strength: Strong, consistent empirical gains on both DSTC2 and WOZ 2.0 with statistically significant improvements across metrics and NBT variants.  
- Strength: Eliminates reliance on hand-crafted semantic dictionaries without sacrificing accuracy, aiding scalability.  
- Strength: Clear evidence that semantic vector quality (Paragram-SL999) materially improves DST performance, especially under ASR noise.  
- Strength: Simple, modular architecture with element-wise interactions and parameter sharing that is interpretable and efficient to train.  
- Strength: Promises data and reproducibility contributions via cleaned DSTC2 transcriptions and expanded WOZ 2.0 dataset with sufficient training/evaluation details.

- Weakness: Exposition and notation need tightening—symbols appear before definition and task/pipeline are not formally defined early.  
- Weakness: “Joint SLU/DST” terminology may be misleading since the model makes per-slot-value decisions conditioned only on the last system act.  
- Weakness: Limited comparisons and ablations—missing head-to-head with RNN/LSTM-based methods and ablations on gating, interaction types, parameter sharing, and embedding tuning.  
- Weakness: Scalability to large multi-domain ontologies not empirically validated; runtime/memory implications of iterating over all slot-value pairs are unreported.  
- Weakness: Baseline semantic dictionary details and manual annotation cost are under-specified, weakening the “no lexicon” claim.  
- Weakness: Limited qualitative/error analysis and few concrete examples showing where NBT succeeds or fails versus delexicalized baselines.

- Question: Provide a concise formal definition of the DST task and the specific state representation used, early in the paper.  
- Question: Can NBT be used as a standalone SLU detector, or is it tightly coupled to DST and belief updating?  
- Question: What are the statistics and nature of the DSTC2 transcription corrections, and do they affect test difficulty or comparisons to leaderboard entries?  
- Question: Please detail the semantic dictionaries used by baselines: authorship, entries per slot, example paraphrases, and estimated annotation effort.  
- Question: Provide runtime/throughput numbers and scaling behavior as ontology size grows (decisions per turn, ms per turn on GPU/CPU).  
- Question: Did you try fine-tuning or partially unfreezing word vectors, and what trade-offs arose vs using fixed specialized vectors?  
- Question: Why not model dialogue context or intra-utterance sequences with RNNs/LSTMs, and how would NBT handle longer contexts or multi-domain settings?  
- Question: Provide qualitative examples where NBT resolves paraphrases/ASR errors that delexicalized baselines miss, and examples of NBT failures.

- Additional: Define all symbols on first use, keep notation consistent, and include a compact figure showing the end-to-end SDS architecture placement.  
- Additional: Clarify whether parameters are fully shared across slots or partially shared and report performance differences.  
- Additional (minor): Improve exposition and presentation for non-expert readers.
