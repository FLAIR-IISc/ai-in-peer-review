Summary
This paper introduces the Neural Belief Tracker (NBT), a framework for dialogue state tracking that replaces delexicalization and hand-crafted semantic dictionaries with reasoning over pre-trained word vectors. The model learns vector representations for user utterances, preceding system acts, and ontology slot-value pairs, and then makes a binary decision for each slot-value pair per turn. Two utterance encoders are compared: NBT-DNN (sum of n-gram vectors with a nonlinearity) and NBT-CNN (convolution + ReLU + max-pool over n-grams). Context is incorporated via simple similarity-based gating that conditions on the last system request/confirm acts. A rule-based belief update aggregates N-best ASR hypotheses and performs temporal smoothing. Experiments on DSTC2 (ASR) and WOZ 2.0 (typed) show statistically significant improvements over delexicalized neural baselines, with especially strong gains when no semantic dictionary is available. The paper further shows that semantically specialized word vectors (Paragram-SL999) lead to better tracking than GloVe or random initialization.

Contributions (as I see them)
1) A lexicon-free DST architecture that couples SLU/DST by operating directly on pre-trained word vectors and sharing parameters across ontology values, thereby improving scalability and handling lexical/morphological variation without manual dictionaries.
2) Empirical evidence across two benchmarks (DSTC2, WOZ 2.0) that this approach matches or surpasses strong delexicalized baselines (including those aided by semantic dictionaries), plus an informative analysis isolating the benefit of semantically specialized vectors for DST.
3) A simple but effective context-gating mechanism for system requests/confirms and an N-best ASR aggregation/update rule that together yield robust performance in noisy ASR conditions.

Strengths
- Strong, consistent empirical gains where it matters most. On DSTC2, NBT-CNN improves joint goal accuracy over the baseline (69.1 → 73.4) and over the lexicon-aided baseline (72.9 → 73.4), and improves requests (95.7 → 96.5). On WOZ 2.0, gains are larger and more meaningful for semantic understanding: joint goals (70.8 → 84.2/84.4) and requests (87.1 → 91.2/91.6). Improvements are statistically significant and robust across both NBT variants.
- Removes reliance on hand-crafted semantic dictionaries without loss of accuracy. Matching or exceeding delexicalized models augmented with semantic dictionaries is a compelling practical contribution for scaling to larger domains and vocabularies.
- Clear demonstration that semantic vector quality matters for DST. The comparison of Paragram-SL999 vs GloVe vs random establishes that vectors specialized for semantic similarity (not mere relatedness) improve goal tracking, especially under ASR noise—an actionable takeaway for practitioners.
- Simple, modular architecture with parameter sharing. The element-wise interaction between utterance and candidate pair, combined with lightweight context gating, keeps the model interpretable and efficient to train, and allows sharing across values/slots to reduce data demands.
- Data and reproducibility contributions. The cleaned DSTC2 transcriptions and expanded WOZ 2.0 dataset (as stated to be released) are valuable for the community, and the paper reports sufficient training and evaluation details for reproduction.

Weaknesses
- Exposition and notation need tightening. Critical symbols (e.g., tq, ts, tv) appear before being defined, and a formal task definition of DST and the overall SDS pipeline is not presented early enough. This makes the paper harder to follow for non-experts.
- “Joint SLU/DST” terminology may be misleading. The model makes per-slot-value binary decisions given utterance and last system act; it is not obvious whether it can function as a standalone SLU module or whether it performs full joint inference beyond the Markov assumption over the last system turn. Clarification is needed.
- Limited comparisons and ablations. Beyond the chosen delexicalized baselines, there is no head-to-head with contemporary RNN/LSTM-based SLU/DST architectures that model sequential utterance structure or longer dialogue context. Ablations on the context-gating terms, element-wise vs concatenation interactions, per-slot vs fully shared parameters, and effect of fixed vs fine-tuned embeddings would strengthen the case.
- Scalability to large ontologies not empirically validated. The approach iterates over all slot-value pairs; while parameter sharing helps, runtime and memory implications for large multi-domain ontologies are not analyzed.
- Baseline semantic dictionary details and manual cost are under-specified. To substantiate the “no lexicon” advantage, the paper should quantify the size, construction effort, and coverage of the dictionaries used by baselines.
- Limited qualitative/error analysis. Few concrete examples are given showing how NBT succeeds where delexicalization fails (e.g., handling paraphrases like “inexpensive” → price=cheap, or short confirmations/negations conditioned on system offers).

Questions to Authors
- Please provide a concise, formal definition of the DST task and the specific state representation used (for both goals and requests), early in the paper, and a brief placement of the NBT within a standard SDS pipeline.
- Can NBT be used as a standalone SLU detector (i.e., predicting slot-value mentions without belief updating), or is it tightly coupled to DST? In what sense is it “joint” beyond leveraging the last system act?
- What are the statistics of your corrected DSTC2 transcriptions (number/types of fixes)? Do the corrections materially change test difficulty, and how do your results compare to top DSTC2 leaderboard entries under the same conditions?
- Please detail the semantic dictionaries used by the baselines: who authored them, approximate number of entries per slot, examples of paraphrases covered, and estimated annotation effort.
- Do you have runtime/throughput numbers and scaling behavior as ontology size grows (e.g., number of slot-value decisions per turn, milliseconds per turn on GPU/CPU)?
- Did you try fine-tuning word vectors or partially unfreezing them? Any trade-offs vs using fixed specialized vectors?
- Why not model dialogue context with an RNN/LSTM over multiple turns, or intra-utterance sequences with RNNs, given their success in SLU? How do you expect NBT to perform with longer contexts or multi-domain settings?
- Could you provide qualitative examples where NBT resolves paraphrases/ASR errors that the delexicalized baseline misses, and cases where NBT fails?

Additional Comments
- Define all symbols upon first use and keep notation consistent throughout.
- A compact figure summarizing the end-to-end SDS architecture and where NBT plugs in would help non-expert readers.
- Minor: clarify whether parameters are shared across slots or fully shared, and report any performance deltas between the two choices.
