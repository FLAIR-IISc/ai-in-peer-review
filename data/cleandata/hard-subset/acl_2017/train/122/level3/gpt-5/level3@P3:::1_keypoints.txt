- Proposal: Neural Belief Tracker (NBT) reasons over pre-trained word embeddings to perform dialogue state tracking without hand-built lexicons.  
- Architecture: Composes utterance and last-system-act embeddings and tests slot–value hypotheses via a learned similarity; two variants: NBT-DNN and NBT-CNN.  
- ASR handling: Uses a simple ASR N-best aggregation and belief-update rule.  
- Empirical result: Matches or exceeds delexicalization baselines on DSTC2 (ASR) and WOZ 2.0 (typed), with larger gains when semantic lexicons are unavailable.  
- Embedding study: Compares initialization methods (Xavier, GloVe, Paragram-SL999) and finds semantically specialized vectors (Paragram) perform best.  

Contributions:
- Empirical: Pre-trained semantically specialized embeddings substantially improve DST without hand-crafted lexicons.  
- Method: A simple, general DST architecture that tests slot–value hypotheses via embedding-space interactions (NBT-DNN/CNN).  
- Analysis: Demonstrates that semantic specialization of embeddings reliably boosts DST, especially under ASR noise.  

Strengths:
- Strong results without semantic lexicons, and competitive when lexicons are available.  
- Modular, data-efficient architecture with shared parameters and explicit last-turn gating.  
- CNN variant adds robustness for longer utterances; both variants show similar trends.  
- Practical ASR fusion and belief update that work effectively in experiments.  
- Meaningful embedding ablation highlighting issues like antonym conflation in distributional vectors.  
- Fixed embeddings and shared parameters improve generalization and reproducibility; datasets to be released.  

Weaknesses:
- Limited evaluation domains: single-domain, small-ontology restaurant dialogs; scalability to large/multi-domain ontologies untested.  
- Missing comparisons to slot-span taggers or tagger+similarity pipelines that could change conclusions.  
- Shallow, Markovian context modeling: only immediately preceding system acts considered, limiting coreference/anaphora handling.  
- Incomplete ablations: no detailed analysis isolating semantic decoder, context gates, or belief-update contributions.  
- Practical issues unaddressed: OOV handling, multiword value composition, and computational cost of enumerating all slot–value pairs.  

Questions for authors:
- How would performance change if similarity were computed over spans proposed by a slot tagger versus the whole utterance?  
- Please provide ablations for (a) removing request/confirm gating, (b) replacing element-wise multiplication with concatenation/bilinear forms, and (c) varying λ or alternative belief updates.  
- How are OOVs and multiword values handled; what is Paragram-SL999 coverage on these datasets?  
- What is per-turn computational complexity as ontology size grows; any pruning/batching recommendations?  
- Are there results on larger or multi-domain ontologies to support scalability claims?  

Additional comments:
- Clarify the introductory example about resolving restaurant_name and referential expressions like “it” under the Markovian context assumption.  
- Fix terminology typos (e.g., “Paragram-SL999”) and include a brief error analysis on antonyms/coreference/negation failures.  
- Consider emphasizing semantic-specialization benefits under ASR noise with a focused robustness study.
