Summary
This paper proposes Neural Belief Tracker (NBT), a data-driven framework for dialogue state tracking that avoids delexicalization and hand-crafted semantic dictionaries. NBT composes pre-trained word vectors into representations of the user utterance, the preceding system acts, and the candidate ontology item, and then makes a binary decision for each slot–value pair. Two utterance encoders are compared (a simple n-gram DNN and a CNN). Context is handled via lightweight gating using the last system request/confirm act. On DSTC2 (ASR) and WOZ 2.0 (typed) datasets, NBT significantly outperforms competitive delexicalized baselines, and matches or surpasses versions augmented with manual semantic lexicons. The paper also shows that semantically specialized embeddings (Paragram-SL999) improve tracking more than generic distributional vectors.

Contributions (as I see them)
1) Empirical demonstration that belief tracking can be performed without delexicalization or hand-crafted semantic dictionaries by reasoning directly over pre-trained, semantically specialized word vectors, with significant gains on two datasets.
2) A simple, effective architecture that combines (a) an utterance encoder (DNN or CNN), (b) a semantic decoder that aligns utterance and slot–value representations, and (c) a minimal context-gating mechanism informed by the last system act.
3) Analysis of the impact of word-vector choice, showing clear, statistically significant benefits of semantically specialized embeddings over random or generic distributional vectors.

Strengths
- Removes dependence on semantic lexicons while maintaining or improving SOTA performance. The NBT models outperform strong delexicalized baselines and match/surpass their lexicon-augmented counterparts (Table 1). This directly addresses a major scalability bottleneck in DST, especially for richer ontologies where dictionaries are infeasible.
- Clear, consistent gains across datasets and conditions. Improvements are significant for joint goal accuracy on both DSTC2 and WOZ 2.0; request accuracy also improves. The larger gains on WOZ 2.0 underscore the method’s ability to capture paraphrase and lexical variation.
- Convincing evidence for the value of semantic specialization. Table 2 shows Paragram-SL999 significantly outperforming GloVe and random initialization for goal tracking, especially under ASR noise. This is a useful, generalizable finding beyond this task.
- Simple, modular design with parameter sharing. Fixed embeddings and shared parameters across values/slots plausibly enable generalization to unseen yet semantically related forms (e.g., inexpensive vs. cheap) and offer a path toward scaling.
- Practicality and transparency. The belief update over ASR N-best lists is simple and easy to implement; the components (utterance encoder, semantic decoder, context gate) are described clearly enough to reproduce.

Weaknesses
- Clarity and organization issues impede accessibility. A formal DST problem statement and a brief dialogue system architecture overview should appear early. Important notation (e.g., tq, ts, tv) appears before being defined. The flow from Figure 2 to component details can be reorganized for non-experts.
- Ambiguity around “joint” SLU/DST claim. The model makes binary turn-level decisions and then uses a rule-based belief updater. It is not fully end-to-end joint with ASR or with a learned belief update; please clarify in what precise sense SLU and DST are “joint.”
- Limited context modeling and sequential reasoning. The Markov assumption (only last system acts) may miss longer-range dependencies and coreference. Given the success of RNN/LSTM/attention models in SLU/DST, it would be valuable to compare with or include a sequential context encoder (e.g., over dialogue turns and/or utterance tokens).
- Insufficient detail on baseline resources and manual cost. The paper compares against delexicalized baselines with and without semantic dictionaries, but does not quantify dictionary size, coverage, or construction effort. This information is needed to substantiate the claimed reduction in manual effort.
- Underdeveloped error analysis and qualitative evidence. The paper would benefit from concrete examples where NBT corrects baseline errors (e.g., paraphrases, morphology, confirm/request handling), and from ablations of the semantic decoder and context gating. This would sharpen understanding of where the gains come from.
- Dataset correction and comparability. The authors corrected DSTC2 transcriptions; the extent, statistics, and potential impact on comparability to prior DSTC2 results are not documented. Without this, it is hard to align with established challenge benchmarks.

Questions to Authors
- “Joint” characterization: Do you consider the model joint because it directly detects slot–value mentions without a separate SLU module, or is there any learned belief update component? How would you position NBT relative to truly end-to-end trackers that consume ASR lattices/confusion networks directly?
- DSTC2 corrections: What fraction of tokens/turns were corrected? Can you report performance using uncorrected transcripts, and discuss comparability to official challenge entries?
- Baseline semantic dictionaries: What are their sizes (per slot), construction procedures, and time/cost? Any automatic expansions? How sensitive are baselines to dictionary quality?
- Context and polarity: How does the model handle negative confirmations (e.g., “no, not Turkish”)? Is there an explicit polarity detector, or is this learned implicitly? Any statistics on confirm/request subsets?
- Embeddings and OOVs: Are Paragram vectors kept fixed throughout (text says yes)? How are OOV tokens handled at test time? Any impact of subword models?
- Ablations: What is performance without the context-gating terms (dr, dc), without the element-wise product in the semantic decoder, or using dot-product similarity? How much does each component contribute?
- RNN/CNN alternatives: Did you try a biLSTM over tokens, or a simple GRU over turns for context? Any reason to prefer the current CNN/DNN encoders empirically?

Additional Comments
- Improve exposition: define DST formally early; give a compact figure of the overall SDS pipeline and where NBT sits; introduce all symbols before use; ensure Figure 2 is referenced where each component is described.
- Provide qualitative examples where specialized embeddings help (e.g., inexpensive→cheap, downtown→centre), including cases where generic embeddings fail.
- Report parameter counts, training times, and hyperparameters (filters, hidden sizes, thresholds) for reproducibility.
- Consider comparing to a recent RNN-based DST baseline using token-level sequential modeling, or include it as a third NBT variant.
- Releasing the cleaned DSTC2 and WOZ 2.0 splits (as indicated) will strengthen the paper’s impact.
