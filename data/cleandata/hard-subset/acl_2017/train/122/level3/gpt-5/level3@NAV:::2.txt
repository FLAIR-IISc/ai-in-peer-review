Summary: The paper proposes Neural Belief Tracker (NBT), a joint SLU/DST framework that reasons over pre-trained, semantically specialized word vectors to avoid hand-crafted semantic lexicons. It learns vector representations for user utterances, ontology slot/value entries, and prior system acts, and combines them to make binary decisions per slot–value. Two utterance encoders are compared (NBT-DNN and NBT-CNN), and a simple ASR N-best belief update is introduced. Experiments on DSTC2 and WOZ 2.0 show consistent, statistically significant gains over a strong delexicalized baseline and match or exceed performance when a semantic dictionary is withheld.

Contributions (as I see them):
- Empirical demonstration that semantically specialized pre-trained embeddings enable lexicon-free DST with significant accuracy gains across noisy (DSTC2) and clean (WOZ) settings.
- A simple, modular joint SLU/DST architecture that combines learned utterance, ontology, and system-act representations via context gating and element-wise interactions.
- Comparative assessment of sentence encoders (DNN vs CNN) and evidence that fixed, specialized embeddings are critical for performance.

Strengths:
- Strong, statistically significant improvements over competitive delexicalized baselines; robustness demonstrated across two datasets with different noise profiles.
- Removes reliance on hand-crafted lexicons while maintaining or improving accuracy, supporting scalability to larger ontologies.
- Clear modeling of system context (request/confirm) via gating, capturing pragmatic phenomena (yes/no confirmations) effectively.
- Sensible parameter sharing and fixed embeddings promote generalization to unseen paraphrases and reduce data/engineering burden.

Weaknesses:
- Organization/readability issues: key notions (e.g., formal DST definition, place within standard SDS pipeline) appear late; non-experts will struggle.
- Notation is introduced before being defined (e.g., tq, ts, tv), hindering comprehension; symbols should be declared upfront.
- The term “joint” may be misleading; it is unclear whether the model can be used as a standalone SLU or only within the DST loop.
- Limited exploration of sequential utterance modeling; RNN/LSTM encoders have been effective for SLU and merit comparison.
- Insufficient detail on the corrected DSTC2 set (extent of corrections) and on the baseline’s semantic dictionary (size, construction cost), and no positioning versus DSTC2 leaderboard if corrections are minor; qualitative error analyses/examples are sparse.

Questions to Authors:
- Please provide statistics on the DSTC2 corrections and report performance versus prior DSTC2 entries to calibrate gains.
- Can NBT operate as a separate SLU module, or is it exclusively a joint tracker? Clarify the “joint” claim.
- What are the sizes and creation costs of the semantic dictionaries used by the baselines?
- Why were RNN/LSTM utterance encoders not explored; do you expect gains on longer utterances?
- Include concrete examples where NBT corrects delexicalization errors, and report sensitivity to λ and N-best depth.
