- Summary: Proposes Neural Belief Tracker (NBT) that uses pre-trained word vectors to track dialogue state without delexicalization or hand-crafted lexicons, comparing DNN and CNN utterance encoders and lightweight context gating, and showing strong results on DSTC2 and WOZ 2.0.  
- Contribution 1: Empirically shows belief tracking can be done without delexicalization/manual dictionaries using semantically specialized embeddings.  
- Contribution 2: Introduces a simple architecture combining an utterance encoder, a semantic decoder aligning utterance and slot–value vectors, and a minimal context gate.  
- Contribution 3: Demonstrates semantically specialized embeddings (Paragram-SL999) outperform generic or random vectors for tracking.  
- Strength: Eliminates reliance on semantic lexicons while matching or improving SOTA, addressing a major scalability bottleneck.  
- Strength: Consistent, significant gains on joint goal and request accuracy across DSTC2 and WOZ 2.0 datasets.  
- Strength: Convincing evidence that semantic specialization of embeddings meaningfully improves robustness, especially under ASR noise.  
- Strength: Simple, modular design with shared parameters and fixed embeddings that plausibly generalize to unseen lexical variants.  
- Strength: Practical and transparent implementation (simple belief update over ASR N-best lists) with reproducible components.  
- Weakness: Paper clarity and organization need improvement—formal DST statement, earlier architecture overview, and definitions of notation are missing.  
- Weakness: Claim of “joint” SLU/DST is ambiguous since the model makes binary turn-level decisions and uses a rule-based belief updater rather than a learned end-to-end update.  
- Weakness: Context modeling is limited to last system act (Markov assumption), risking missed long-range dependencies and coreference.  
- Weakness: Insufficient detail on baseline semantic dictionaries—no quantification of size, coverage, construction effort, or sensitivity analysis.  
- Weakness: Lack of error analysis and qualitative examples showing where NBT corrects baseline failures and which components drive gains.  
- Weakness: DSTC2 transcript corrections are undocumented—extent and impact on comparability to prior results are unclear.  
- Question: Clarify “joint” characterization—does NBT learn belief updates or only detect slot–value mentions, and how does it compare to end-to-end trackers consuming ASR lattices?  
- Question: How many DSTC2 tokens/turns were corrected, can results on uncorrected transcripts be reported, and how comparable are corrected results to official benchmarks?  
- Question: Provide sizes, construction procedures, time/cost, and any automatic expansions for baseline semantic dictionaries, and sensitivity to dictionary quality.  
- Question: How does the model handle negative confirmations (polarity) — explicit detector or implicit learning — and any stats for confirm/request subsets?  
- Question: Are Paragram embeddings kept fixed at training, how are OOV tokens handled at test time, and would subword models help?  
- Question: Request ablations: performance without context-gating terms, without element-wise product in decoder, and using dot-product similarity.  
- Question: Were RNN/biLSTM or GRU-based encoders or turn-level sequential models tried, and why prefer CNN/DNN empirically?  
- Additional comment: Improve exposition by formally defining DST early, showing overall SDS pipeline, and introducing symbols before use.  
- Additional comment: Provide qualitative examples where specialized embeddings help (paraphrases/morphology) and failure cases.  
- Additional comment: Report parameter counts, training times, and hyperparameters for reproducibility.  
- Additional comment: Compare to or include an RNN-based DST baseline as an alternative encoder variant.  
- Additional comment: Release the cleaned DSTC2 and WOZ 2.0 splits to strengthen impact and reproducibility.
