Summary
The paper proposes Neural Belief Tracker (NBT), a dialogue state tracking (DST) framework that reasons over pre-trained word embeddings rather than delexicalized features or hand-built semantic lexicons. NBT composes word vectors into distributed representations of user utterances and the immediately preceding system acts, and tests each ontology slot–value hypothesis by learning a similarity function between the utterance/context representation and the slot–value embedding. Two instantiations are presented (a lightweight DNN over cumulative n-grams and a CNN over 1–3-gram windows). A simple ASR N-best aggregation and belief-update rule is used. On DSTC2 (ASR) and WOZ 2.0 (typed), NBT matches or exceeds state-of-the-art delexicalization-based baselines, and the gains grow when semantic lexicons are unavailable. An additional study compares different embedding initializations (XAVIER, GloVe, Paragram-SL999), finding that semantically specialized vectors yield the best results.

Contributions (as I see them, ordered by strength)
1) Empirical finding: Pre-trained, semantically specialized word embeddings substantially improve DST without hand-crafted semantic lexicons, yielding consistent, significant gains over strong delexicalization-based baselines on DSTC2 and WOZ 2.0.

2) Method: A simple, general DST architecture that composes utterance and last-turn system-act representations and tests slot–value hypotheses via a learned interaction in embedding space (with two practical variants, NBT-DNN and NBT-CNN) plus a lightweight ASR N-best belief update.

3) Analysis of pre-training: Comparative evaluation of embedding sources (random/Xavier, GloVe, Paragram-SL999) showing that semantic specialization (Paragram-SL999) reliably boosts DST, especially under ASR noise.

Strengths
- Clear, consistent improvements without semantic lexicons: On both DSTC2 and WOZ 2.0, NBT-DNN/CNN significantly outperform strong delexicalization-based trackers. Notably, when baselines lack hand-crafted lexicons, NBT’s gains are larger; when lexicons are present, NBT matches or exceeds them, supporting the claim that embeddings can replace brittle lexicons.

- Sound, modular architecture with strong inductive bias: Iterating over slot–value hypotheses with shared parameters, interaction via element-wise multiplication, and explicit gating from last-turn system acts is simple, data-efficient, and empirically effective. The CNN variant provides robustness for longer utterances; both variants demonstrate similar performance trends.

- Practicality under ASR and noisy inputs: The ASR N-best fusion and belief update are simple but effective. While the model shines most on clean WOZ data, it still improves DSTC2 request/goal accuracy under ASR, and dramatically improves with transcripts—suggesting the core semantic matching is solid and bottlenecked by recognition noise.

- Meaningful embedding ablation: The comparison among Xavier, GloVe, and Paragram-SL999 isolates the role of semantic specialization. The finding that distributional vectors can conflate antonyms (e.g., expensive/inexpensive) and hurt performance, while Paragram helps, is insightful and actionable for practitioners.

- Reproducibility/engineering choices: Fixed embeddings reduce overfitting and allow generalization to unseen synonyms; parameters are largely shared across values/slots, mitigating data requirements. The datasets (cleaned DSTC2, extended WOZ 2.0) are promised for release.

Weaknesses
- Limited domains and ontologies; untested scalability: Both datasets are single-domain, small-ontology restaurant dialogs for Cambridge. The method still enumerates all slot–value pairs per turn; no experiments examine very large ontologies, multi-domain settings, or dynamic ontologies. Claims about avoiding delexicalization bottlenecks are promising but remain to be demonstrated in more complex domains where delexicalization truly fails.

- Missing comparison to slot-span taggers: The evaluation omits models with a separate span tagging/slot filling component (e.g., CRF/RNN taggers or joint intent/slot models). Constraining similarity to detected spans could change results and more fairly test whether learned similarity over the entire utterance is the key driver of gains.

- Context modeling is shallow and Markovian: Only the immediately preceding system acts are considered. This limits handling of discourse phenomena (e.g., longer-range anaphora and coreference). The introduction’s example risks confusion (e.g., whether restaurant_name should be resolved); the model has no explicit mechanism for resolving “it” beyond last-act gating, raising questions about robustness to referential language beyond confirmations/requests.

- Limited ablation on model components: While embedding ablations are provided, there is no detailed ablation of semantic decoding vs. context gates vs. the belief update rule (e.g., removing element-wise multiplication, removing confirm/request gates, varying λ). Such analysis would clarify where gains originate and guide future extensions.

- Practical concerns not addressed: OOV handling beyond fixed embeddings (subword), multiword value composition details, and computational cost of iterating over all slot–value pairs are not analyzed. For large ontologies, per-turn inference cost and memory may become nontrivial.

Questions to Authors
- How would NBT perform if similarity were computed only over spans proposed by a separate slot tagger (or a learned span extractor), versus over the whole utterance? Could you report such a comparison?

- Can you provide ablations on: (a) removing request/confirm gating, (b) replacing element-wise multiplication with concatenation or a bilinear form, and (c) varying λ or alternative belief update rules?

- How do you handle OOVs and multiword values in practice (e.g., subword embeddings, character CNNs)? What is the coverage of Paragram-SL999 on the datasets?

- What is the computational complexity per turn as ontology size grows? Any batching/caching strategies or approximate candidate pruning you recommend?

- Do you have results beyond Cambridge restaurants (e.g., larger, multi-domain ontologies) to support the scalability claim?

Additional Comments
- Please clarify the introductory example’s state tracking for restaurant_name and how referential expressions like “it” are resolved under your Markovian context assumption.

- Minor: ensure consistent terminology (“Paragram-SL999,” not “Program-SL999”); a brief error analysis would strengthen the paper, especially on failures involving antonyms/coreference/negation.

- The finding that semantic specialization is particularly helpful under ASR noise is compelling; consider highlighting this with a targeted ASR-noise robustness study.
