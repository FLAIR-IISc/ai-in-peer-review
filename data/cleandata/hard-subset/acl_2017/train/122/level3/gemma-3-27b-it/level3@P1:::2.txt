This paper presents a novel Neural Belief Tracker (NBT) framework aimed at improving dialogue state tracking, a core component of spoken dialogue systems. The NBT leverages pre-trained word vectors to overcome limitations of existing approaches that rely heavily on large annotated datasets for Spoken Language Understanding (SLU) or hand-crafted lexicons for handling lexical variation. The core contribution lies in learning distributed representations of user utterances, system context, and ontology entries to determine the probability of specific slot-value pairs being expressed in a dialogue turn. Two model variants, NBT-DNN and NBT-CNN, are proposed for composing these representations. Experimental results on two restaurant search datasets (DSTC2 and WOZ 2.0) demonstrate that the NBT models achieve performance comparable to delexicalization-based models while significantly outperforming them when hand-crafted lexicons are unavailable.

The primary contribution of this work is the successful application of pre-trained word vector spaces to improve language understanding within a belief tracking model. This is significant as it reduces the dependence on costly and domain-specific resources like manually created lexicons and large labeled datasets. The ability to generalize to unseen linguistic variations through semantic similarity encoded in the word vectors is a key strength. A secondary contribution lies in the proposed framework for composing representations of utterance, context, and ontology items, which facilitates joint reasoning over these elements. Finally, the comparative evaluation against established delexicalization techniques provides a clear demonstration of the NBT's effectiveness.

**Strengths:**

1. **Addresses a critical limitation in DST:** The paper directly tackles the scalability issue of existing DST approaches by reducing reliance on annotated data and hand-crafted resources. This is particularly important for real-world applications in complex domains where creating such resources is impractical.
2. **Strong empirical results:** The NBT models consistently outperform the delexicalization baseline when no lexicon is provided, and match its performance when one is available. This demonstrates the effectiveness of the proposed approach and its ability to leverage pre-trained semantic information. The results on both DSTC2 and WOZ 2.0 datasets bolster the claim of generalizability.
3. **Novel use of pre-trained word vectors:** The application of pre-trained word vectors, specifically semantically specialized ones, to the DST task is a novel and effective idea. This allows the model to capture semantic similarity beyond exact lexical matches, enhancing its robustness to variations in user language.
4. **Clear problem statement and motivation:** The paper clearly articulates the challenges faced by traditional DST methods and provides a compelling motivation for the proposed approach. The connection to the limitations of SLU-based and delexicalization-based systems is well-established.
5. **Comprehensive experimentation:** The paper includes a thorough experimental evaluation, comparing two NBT variants with a strong baseline and analyzing the impact of different word vector types.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Lack of contextual clarity:** While the paper presents a complete system, it lacks sufficient context for readers unfamiliar with the specifics of dialogue state tracking. A more formal definition of DST and a broader overview of a typical dialogue system architecture early in the paper would improve accessibility.
2. **Notational inconsistencies & unclear definitions:** Symbols like *tq, ts, tv* are introduced without prior definition, hindering comprehension. A glossary of notations or earlier definitions would improve readability.
3. **"Joint" modeling ambiguity:** The term “joint SLU/DST” may be misleading. The paper does not explicitly demonstrate the ability to perform SLU as a separate task. Clarification on whether the model outputs slot-value pairs directly, or if a separate SLU component could be built on top of it, is needed.
4. **Limited analysis of DSTC2 dataset corrections:** The paper mentions correcting spelling errors in the DSTC2 dataset. Providing statistics on the number and type of corrections made, and a comparison of results *with* and *without* the corrections, would help assess their impact and further validate the model's robustness.  Comparing performance against other DSTC2 systems would also provide useful context.
5. **Potential for sequential modeling improvement:** While the NBT effectively leverages contextual information, the use of solely the preceding system act as context may be limiting. Exploring recurrent neural networks (RNNs) or Long Short-Term Memory (LSTM) networks to model the entire dialogue history could potentially further improve performance, given their success in SLU tasks.



**Questions to Authors:**

1. Could you clarify whether the NBT model can be readily adapted to perform SLU as a separate task, or is it solely a belief tracker?
2. What was the scale of the corrections made to the DSTC2 dataset, and how did these corrections affect the experimental results?
3.  What is the size and composition of the semantic dictionary used in the baseline model? Providing an estimate of the manual effort required to create this dictionary would highlight the advantage of the proposed approach.
4. Could you provide specific examples where the NBT model correctly identified user intents that the baseline model failed to capture, illustrating the benefit of using pre-trained word vectors?

**Additional Comments:**

- The paper would benefit from more illustrative examples of how the NBT models function with actual dialogue turns.
- A discussion of potential limitations of the approach, such as its sensitivity to the quality of pre-trained word vectors, would strengthen the analysis.
