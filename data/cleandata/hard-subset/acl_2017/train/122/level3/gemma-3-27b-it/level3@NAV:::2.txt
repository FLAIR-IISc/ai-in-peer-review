## Review of “Neural Belief Tracker: Data-Driven Dialogue State Tracking”

This paper presents a novel neural network framework, the Neural Belief Tracker (NBT), for dialogue state tracking (DST) that leverages pre-trained word vectors to learn representations for user utterances, system outputs, and ontology entries. The primary contribution lies in this learned representation approach, aiming to overcome limitations of traditional methods reliant on extensive annotated data or manually crafted lexicons. The authors compare two utterance representation models – NBT-DNN and NBT-CNN – and combine these representations to make binary decisions for slot-value pairs. Experimental results on DSTC2 and WOZ 2.0 datasets demonstrate significant improvements over a delexicalized baseline, particularly in scenarios lacking hand-crafted resources.

The core strength of this work is its demonstrated ability to achieve competitive DST performance *without* relying on extensive manual effort in lexicon creation. The results are compelling, showing that learned representations from pre-trained word vectors can effectively capture semantic nuances and handle lexical variations. The consistent performance improvement over the baseline across both datasets, particularly the stronger gains on the more complex WOZ 2.0 dataset, lends further support to the effectiveness of the approach. The ablation study regarding the use of semantically specialized word vectors is also valuable, highlighting the importance of semantic information in these representations. Finally, the explicit focus on scaling DST to more complex domains is a practical and welcome contribution.

However, the paper suffers from several weaknesses hindering its clarity and impact. The organization is somewhat disjointed, making it difficult for readers unfamiliar with the DST field to follow the logic. The paper lacks a formal definition of DST early on, and a clearer placement of the general dialogue system architecture would improve understanding. Notations (e.g., tq, ts, t_v) are introduced before being defined, disrupting the flow. While the authors claim a “joint” model, it’s unclear whether the model truly performs separate SLU and DST stages or an integrated process.

Furthermore, while improvements over the baseline are reported, the paper doesn’t provide statistics on the degree of correction to the DSTC2 dataset, making it hard to judge the significance of the baseline corrections. Comparisons with other recent DSTC2 entries beyond the baseline would also provide useful context. The authors should also explore the use of RNNs/LSTMs for sequential utterance modeling, given their established success in SLU tasks. Finally, more detail on the semantic dictionary used in the baseline is needed to understand the manual effort involved and better assess the cost savings of the NBT approach. Providing concrete example cases where the NBT model correctly identifies states the baseline misses would further strengthen the paper.



**Questions to Authors:**

1. Can you provide more detail on the specific corrections made to the DSTC2 dataset, and quantify the impact of these corrections on baseline performance?
2.  Is the model performing separate SLU and DST stages, or is it a truly joint model? If separate, please clarify the SLU component.
3.  Could you provide examples where the NBT model successfully corrects errors made by the baseline system? 
4.  Why were RNNs/LSTMs not considered for sequential utterance modeling?
