- Proposes Neural Belief Tracker (NBT) that uses pre-trained word vectors to avoid reliance on SLU annotations or hand-crafted lexicons.  
- Composes word vectors into representations of utterances and context, enabling semantic similarity reasoning without explicit matching.  
- Introduces two NBT variants (NBT-DNN, NBT-CNN) for different composition complexities.  
- Demonstrates competitive or superior performance to delexicalization-based models on two restaurant search datasets.  
- Shows that NBT matches lexicon-based model performance without needing hand-crafted lexicons.  
- Provides statistically significant empirical improvements, especially on WOZ 2.0.  
- Ablation study compares Random, GloVe, and Paragram-SL999 embeddings, highlighting the benefit of semantically specialized vectors.  
- Well-motivated architectural choices (e.g., element-wise multiplication, context modeling) with clear rationale.  
- Clear, concise presentation with helpful diagrams and equations.  
- Experiments limited to restaurant search domain, leaving generalizability to other domains untested.  
- Missing comparison to strong slot-tagging / sequence tagging baselines that predict slot-value spans directly.  
- Uses a relatively simple, rule-based belief state update that may miss benefits from richer update mechanisms.  
- Introductory example may be ambiguous about entity resolution and coreference handling.  
- Question: How does NBT handle coreference and tracking entities across turns?  
- Question: What are training and inference computational costs relative to delexicalization baselines?  
- Question: How sensitive is performance to the choice of pre-trained vectors and why Paragram-SL999 helps?  
- Question: Were alternative combination methods besides element-wise multiplication tried and with what outcome?  
- Suggestion: Release cleaned DSTC2 dataset with code to improve reproducibility.  
- Suggestion: Add discussion of failure cases and future directions beyond domain adaptation.
