- Proposes a novel DST model that composes utterance and context representations using pre-trained word embeddings to move beyond delexicalization.  
- Leverages pre-trained embeddings to directly model semantic similarity, enabling handling of linguistic variation without hand-crafted lexicons.  
- Empirically shows consistent, significant improvements over delexicalization-based baselines on DSTC2 and WOZ 2.0.  
- Thoroughly evaluates different embedding pre-training methods and highlights the benefit of semantically specialized embeddings (Paragram-SL999).  
- Strength: convincing experimental results and an insightful ablation study; model and experiments are clearly presented for reproducibility.  
- Weakness: datasets used (DSTC2, WOZ 2.0) are limited, raising concerns about generalization to more complex or open-domain dialogues.  
- Weakness: no comparison to a separate slot-tagging component; restricting similarity calculations to slot-value spans may overstate performance.  
- Introductory example may be misleading about resolved entities and lacks discussion of coreference resolution.  
- Modeling context with a Markovian assumption is potentially restrictive; richer dialogue history representations could improve results.  
- Question: how does the model handle coreference across multiple turns?  
- Question: what is the computational cost of using pre-trained embeddings during training and inference?  
- Question: how do different window sizes or aggregation methods for n-grams in NBT-DNN affect performance?  
- Question: how sensitive is performance to the choice of similarity metric in the semantic decoding module?  
- Additional suggestion: include discussion of potential failure cases and limitations.  
- Future direction: explore integration of external knowledge sources (e.g., knowledge graphs).
