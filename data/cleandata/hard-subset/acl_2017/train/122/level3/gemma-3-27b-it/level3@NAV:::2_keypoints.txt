- Proposes the Neural Belief Tracker (NBT), a neural DST framework that uses pre-trained word vectors to represent user utterances, system outputs, and ontology entries.  
- Compares two utterance encoders (NBT-DNN and NBT-CNN) and combines their representations to make binary slot-value decisions.  
- Shows significant improvements over a delexicalized baseline on DSTC2 and WOZ 2.0, especially when hand-crafted resources are unavailable.  
- Main strength: achieves competitive DST without extensive manual lexicons, leveraging semantic word vectors to handle lexical variation.  
- Ablation study highlights the importance of semantically specialized word vectors for performance.  
- Emphasizes practical goal of scaling DST to more complex domains without heavy manual effort.  
- Paper organization is disjointed and hard to follow for readers new to DST.  
- Lacks an early formal definition of DST and clearer placement of the general dialogue system architecture.  
- Notations (e.g., tq, ts, t_v) are introduced before they are defined, disrupting readability.  
- Unclear whether the model truly performs joint SLU+DST or separates SLU and DST stages; joint claim is not well supported.  
- No statistics or quantification of the corrections made to the DSTC2 dataset, making baseline comparisons hard to judge.  
- Missing comparisons to other recent DSTC2 entries beyond the delexicalized baseline for broader context.  
- Authors should consider RNNs/LSTMs for sequential utterance modeling given their SLU success.  
- Insufficient detail on the semantic dictionary used in the baseline, hindering assessment of manual effort saved by NBT.  
- Lacks concrete example cases showing where NBT corrects baseline errors to illustrate practical gains.  
- Questions for authors: (1) detail and quantify DSTC2 corrections and their impact on baseline; (2) clarify whether SLU and DST are separate or jointly modeled; (3) provide examples where NBT fixes baseline errors; (4) explain why RNNs/LSTMs were not considered.
