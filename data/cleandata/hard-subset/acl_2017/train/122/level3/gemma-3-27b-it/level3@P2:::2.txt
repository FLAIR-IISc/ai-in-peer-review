## Review for ACL 2017 - Neural Belief Tracker: Data-Driven Dialogue State Tracking

This paper presents a novel neural belief tracking (NBT) framework that leverages pre-trained word vectors to address limitations of existing dialogue state tracking (DST) approaches, specifically their reliance on large annotated datasets for spoken language understanding (SLU) or hand-crafted semantic lexicons. The authors propose models that learn representations for user utterances, system outputs, and ontology entries, and combine these to make binary decisions about the presence of slot-value pairs in the dialogue state. The experimental results demonstrate significant improvements over a delexicalization-based baseline on two standard datasets.

**Contributions:**

The primary contribution of this work is a novel approach to dialogue state tracking that effectively utilizes pre-trained word vectors to learn representations of dialogue elements, reducing the need for extensive manual annotation or large-scale lexicons. This is a significant step towards building more scalable and adaptable DST systems. 

A secondary contribution lies in the comparative analysis of two different utterance representation models – NBT-DNN and NBT-CNN – demonstrating the benefits of convolutional neural networks in capturing contextual information within utterances. 

Finally, the comprehensive experimental evaluation on both the DSTC2 and WOZ 2.0 datasets provides strong empirical evidence supporting the effectiveness of the proposed framework and its robustness to noisy ASR data, particularly when compared to a strong delexicalization baseline.

**Strengths:**

1. **Strong Empirical Results:** The NBT models consistently outperform the delexicalization-based baseline across both datasets, achieving statistically significant improvements in both goal and request accuracy. This demonstrates the practical effectiveness of the proposed approach. The gains on the WOZ 2.0 dataset, where the language is more complex and free of ASR errors, particularly highlight the model’s ability to learn semantic variations.

2. **Addresses a Key Challenge:** The paper tackles a crucial bottleneck in DST – the need for either large annotated datasets or extensive hand-crafted lexicons. By effectively leveraging pre-trained word vectors, the NBT framework offers a compelling solution for building DST systems that can scale to more complex domains without requiring substantial manual effort.

3. **Clear Motivation and Problem Definition:** The introduction effectively articulates the limitations of existing DST approaches and clearly motivates the development of the NBT framework. The authors provide a concise overview of the DST task and its importance within the broader context of spoken dialogue systems.

4. **Thorough Experimental Design:** The authors conduct a thorough experimental evaluation, comparing the NBT models to a strong baseline and analyzing the impact of different word vector embeddings.  The use of two distinct datasets (DSTC2 and WOZ 2.0) increases the generalizability of the findings.

5. **Analysis of Word Vector Quality:**  The insightful analysis demonstrating the importance of *semantically* specialized word vectors (Paragram-SL999) over distributional (GloVe) or randomly initialized vectors is a strong contribution.  This underscores the importance of semantic content for this task.



**Weaknesses:**

1. **Paper Organization and Readability:** The paper suffers from organizational issues and a lack of clarity in presentation. The foundational concepts of DST are not formally defined early on, and the general architecture of a dialogue system is not clearly situated before introducing the NBT framework. This makes the paper less accessible to readers unfamiliar with the field.

2. **Notational Clarity:** The introduction of symbols (e.g., *tq*, *ts*, *tv*) before their definitions creates confusion.  A more structured presentation with clear definitions of all variables would significantly improve readability.

3. **“Joint” Model Clarification:** The term "joint SLU/DST" is used, but it is unclear whether the model truly performs independent SLU. Clarification is needed regarding whether the model is capable of performing SLU as a separate component or if it inherently integrates SLU and DST into a single process.

4. **Dataset Correction Details:**  The authors mention correcting spelling errors in the DSTC2 dataset. Providing statistics on the number of corrections made and comparing performance with other DSTC2 entries on the original dataset would provide valuable context.

5. **Limited Exploration of Sequential Modeling:** While the NBT framework demonstrates strong performance, the authors do not explore the potential benefits of using recurrent neural networks (RNNs) or LSTMs for sequential utterance modeling. Given the success of RNNs in SLU tasks, exploring their application within the NBT framework could potentially further improve performance.



**Questions to Authors:**

1. Could you provide a more formal definition of the Dialogue State Tracking (DST) task and situate the NBT framework within the broader architecture of a typical spoken dialogue system?

2.  Can you elaborate on the extent of corrections made to the DSTC2 dataset? Specifically, how many errors were corrected, and how does the model’s performance compare on the original, uncorrected dataset to existing DSTC2 entries?

3.  The paper mentions that the NBT models learn to handle lexical variation. Can you provide specific examples where the proposed model correctly identifies intents that the baseline model misses due to lexical variation?

4.  Regarding the semantic dictionary used in the baseline model, can you quantify the manual effort required to create and maintain it (e.g., number of entries, person-hours)?

5.  Have you considered incorporating RNNs or LSTMs into the NBT framework to leverage the benefits of sequential modeling for utterance representations? If not, what were the reasons for excluding these architectures?



**Additional Comments:**

The paper demonstrates a promising approach to dialogue state tracking and offers valuable insights into the effective use of pre-trained word vectors. Addressing the weaknesses outlined above, particularly regarding clarity of presentation and exploration of alternative architectures, would further strengthen the contribution and impact of this work.
