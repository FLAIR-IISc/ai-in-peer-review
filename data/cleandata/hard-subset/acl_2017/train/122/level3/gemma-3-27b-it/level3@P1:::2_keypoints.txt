- Proposes Neural Belief Tracker (NBT) using pre-trained word vectors to improve dialogue state tracking and reduce reliance on large annotated SLU datasets or hand-crafted lexicons.  
- Learns distributed representations of user utterances, system context, and ontology entries to predict slot-value probabilities.  
- Introduces two composition variants: NBT-DNN and NBT-CNN.  
- Evaluated on DSTC2 and WOZ 2.0, matching delexicalization with lexicons and outperforming it when lexicons are absent.  
- Primary contribution: successful application of semantically specialized pre-trained word vectors to belief tracking, enabling generalization to unseen lexical variations.  
- Secondary contribution: a framework for jointly composing utterance, context, and ontology representations for joint reasoning.  
- Strength: reduces DST scalability issues by lowering dependence on annotated data and manual lexicons.  
- Strength: strong empirical results showing robustness across datasets and conditions.  
- Strength: novel and effective use of pre-trained word vectors to capture semantic similarity beyond exact matches.  
- Strength: clear problem statement and motivation linking limitations of SLU/delexicalization approaches to the proposed method.  
- Strength: comprehensive experiments comparing two NBT variants, baselines, and different word vector types.  
- Weakness: lacks sufficient introductory context and a formal DST definition for readers unfamiliar with DST specifics.  
- Weakness: notational inconsistencies (e.g., undefined symbols tq, ts, tv) hinder readability.  
- Weakness: ambiguous use of “joint SLU/DST” without demonstrating separate SLU capability or clarifying output granularity.  
- Weakness: insufficient analysis of DSTC2 spelling corrections—no statistics or results with/without corrections and limited comparison to other DSTC2 systems.  
- Weakness: context modeling may be limited by using only the preceding system act; sequential models like RNNs/LSTMs could be explored.
