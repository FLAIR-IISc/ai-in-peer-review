## Review for ACL 2017 - Neural Belief Tracker: Data-Driven Dialogue State Tracking

This paper proposes a novel Neural Belief Tracking (NBT) framework that leverages pre-trained word vectors to overcome limitations of traditional dialogue state tracking (DST) approaches, specifically their reliance on large annotated datasets for Spoken Language Understanding (SLU) or hand-crafted semantic lexicons for delexicalization. The core idea is to compose pre-trained word vectors into distributed representations of user utterances and dialogue context, allowing the model to reason directly over semantic similarity without explicit matching. The authors demonstrate competitive performance on two restaurant search datasets, matching or surpassing delexicalization-based models, particularly in scenarios where semantic lexicons are unavailable.

**Contributions:**

The primary contribution of this work is the demonstration that pre-trained word vector spaces can be effectively utilized to improve the language understanding capabilities of belief tracking models. This is a significant step towards more scalable DST systems, reducing the dependence on expensive and domain-specific resources like hand-crafted lexicons.  A second contribution is the NBT framework itself, offering two distinct model variants (NBT-DNN and NBT-CNN) for composing these word vector representations, allowing for different levels of complexity in capturing utterance semantics. Finally, the empirical evaluation, showcasing consistent improvements over strong baselines on two datasets, validates the proposed approach and provides a strong foundation for future research.

**Strengths:**

1. **Addresses a Key Challenge in DST:** The paper directly tackles the scalability issue of DST, a critical bottleneck for deploying dialogue systems in complex, real-world domains. The reliance on hand-crafted lexicons, as acknowledged in the paper, becomes unsustainable as domains grow in size and linguistic diversity. The NBT framework offers a promising alternative.
2. **Strong Empirical Results:** The reported performance gains over established delexicalization-based baselines, particularly on the WOZ 2.0 dataset, are compelling. Matching the performance of lexicon-based models *without* the need for those lexicons is a significant achievement.  The statistical significance reported further strengthens these findings.
3. **Thorough Ablation Study with Word Embeddings:** The comparison of different pre-trained word vector approaches (Random, GloVe, Paragram-SL999) provides valuable insights into the importance of semantic specialization within the embeddings.  The clear demonstration that semantically specialized vectors outperform distributional ones highlights a critical design choice.
4. **Well-Motivated Design Choices:** The paper provides clear rationale for the architectural choices, such as the use of element-wise multiplication for combining utterance and candidate slot-value representations, and the inclusion of context modeling components. This aids in understanding the underlying principles of the NBT framework.
5. **Clear and Concise Presentation:** The paper is well-written and clearly explains the core concepts and implementation details. The diagrams and equations are helpful for understanding the model architecture.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Dataset Scope:** The experiments are currently limited to the restaurant search domain. While the results are promising, the generalizability of the NBT framework to more complex and diverse dialogue domains remains an open question.  The authors acknowledge this in the conclusion, but it's a significant limitation.
2. **Lack of Comparison to a Strong Slot-Tagging Baseline:** The paper lacks a comparison to a dedicated slot-tagging component. Evaluating performance against a system that directly predicts slot-value spans, rather than iterating over all possible pairs, would provide a more comprehensive assessment of the NBT’s effectiveness.  A comparison with a state-of-the-art sequence tagging model would be particularly valuable.
3. **Simplified Belief State Update Mechanism:** The rule-based belief state update mechanism, while functional, appears relatively simplistic.  More sophisticated update mechanisms, potentially leveraging the NBT’s internal representations, could further enhance performance.
4. **Potential Ambiguity in the Introductory Example:** The introductory example, while illustrating the task, could be misleading regarding the expected output. The dialogue state representation seems to omit resolution of entities like the restaurant name.  It is unclear how the NBT handles coreference resolution, which is critical in more complex dialogues.

**Questions to Authors:**

1.  How does the NBT framework handle coreference resolution? Specifically, how does it track entities mentioned across multiple turns, as in the example provided in the introduction?
2.  Could you elaborate on the computational cost of the NBT models compared to the delexicalization-based baselines? Are there any significant differences in training or inference time?
3.  How sensitive is the performance of the NBT models to the choice of pre-trained word vectors? Are there specific properties of the Paragram-SL999 vectors that contribute to their superior performance?
4.  Have you explored alternative methods for combining the utterance and candidate slot-value representations beyond element-wise multiplication? If so, what were the results?



**Additional Comments:**

The authors should consider making the cleaned DSTC2 dataset available alongside the code, as this would significantly enhance the reproducibility of the results.  A brief discussion of potential failure cases and future research directions beyond domain adaptation would also be valuable.
