## Review for Neural Belief Tracker: Data-Driven Dialogue State Tracking (ACL 2017)

This paper presents a neural network framework for dialogue state tracking (DST) that leverages pre-trained word vectors to learn representations for user utterances, system outputs, and ontology entries. The core contribution lies in moving away from exact matching and hand-crafted lexicons, allowing the model to reason directly over distributed semantic representations. The authors compare two utterance representation models, NBT-DNN and NBT-CNN, and combine learned representations to make binary decisions for slot-value pairs. Experimental results demonstrate significant improvement over a delexicalized baseline, particularly in a setting where lexicons are unavailable. Overall, the paper addresses a crucial challenge in DST – scalability – with a reasonable and well-motivated approach.

**Contributions:**

1. **Data-Driven DST via Semantic Representations:** The primary contribution is the demonstration that DST can be effectively performed by learning distributed representations from pre-trained word vectors, eliminating the need for manually crafted lexicons. This is significant because it addresses a major bottleneck for scaling DST systems to more complex domains.
2. **NBT Framework with Representation Learning:** The proposed NBT framework, encompassing both DNN and CNN variants for utterance representation, provides a concrete implementation for this data-driven approach. The architecture, combining utterance, context, and slot-value representations, is well-structured and allows for learning nuanced interactions.
3. **Empirical Validation & Baseline Comparison:** The thorough experimental evaluation on two datasets (DSTC2 and WOZ 2.0), and the clear comparison against both delexicalized and lexicon-enhanced baselines, provide strong evidence for the effectiveness of the proposed approach.



**Strengths:**

1. **Addressing a Key Problem:** The paper directly tackles a significant problem in DST – the reliance on labor-intensive lexicon creation. The proposed solution offers a promising path toward more scalable and adaptable DST systems.
2. **Sound Methodology:** The use of pre-trained word vectors is a sensible choice, and the exploration of both DNN and CNN architectures for utterance representation is well-justified. The context modelling component, though relatively simple, adds a crucial dimension to the model.
3. **Strong Empirical Results:** The reported results consistently outperform the baseline models, demonstrating the practical effectiveness of the NBT framework. The fact that the NBT models match lexicon-enhanced baselines *without* using lexicons is particularly noteworthy.
4. **Clear Problem Definition:** The introduction clearly articulates the challenges of DST and motivates the need for a new approach. The paper effectively situates the work within the broader context of dialogue system research.
5. **Availability of Data:** The authors commit to releasing the corrected DSTC2 dataset, which is a valuable contribution to the research community.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Organization & Readability:**  The paper suffers from weaknesses in organization and readability, particularly for readers unfamiliar with the DST field. Key concepts are introduced before being fully defined (e.g., tq, ts, t_v). The high-level architecture of a dialogue system, and the precise role of DST within it, isn't sufficiently established upfront.
2. **"Joint" SLU/DST Clarification:** The paper claims to “couple SLU and DST.”  It needs to explicitly clarify whether the model performs separate SLU *and* DST, or a truly *joint* modeling process. If the model still iterates through slot-value pairs, performing a binary classification for each, it is not a full joint model, and this distinction should be made clear.
3. **DSTC2 Dataset Correction Details:** The authors mention correcting the DSTC2 dataset, but do not provide any statistics on the nature or extent of these corrections. It is crucial to understand how significant these corrections are; if they are minor, comparisons to existing DSTC2 entries should be presented to contextualize the gains.
4. **Limited Exploration of Sequential Modeling:** While the CNN variant offers some sequential modeling capability, the paper doesn't explore more powerful sequential models like RNNs or LSTMs for utterance representation. These models have proven successful in SLU tasks and could potentially further improve performance.
5. **Baseline Semantic Dictionary Details:**  The authors mention the semantic dictionary used in the baseline model. Providing more details regarding its size, creation process (manual effort involved), and coverage would help to better appreciate the advantages of the proposed approach.
6. **Illustrative Examples:**  The paper would benefit from including example cases where the NBT model correctly identifies a slot-value pair that the baseline model misses, and vice-versa. This would provide concrete insights into the strengths and weaknesses of the approach.




**Questions to Authors:**

1. Can you clarify whether the NBT model performs separate SLU and DST or a truly joint modelling process?
2. What is the nature and extent of the corrections made to the DSTC2 dataset? Can you provide statistics on the changes?
3. Why were RNNs/LSTMs not considered for utterance representation? Did you perform any preliminary experiments?
4. Can you provide more details about the semantic dictionary used in the baseline model, including its size, creation process, and coverage?
5. Could you provide a few specific examples where the NBT model correctly identifies a slot-value pair that the baseline model fails to detect, and vice-versa?

**Additional Comments:**

* The notation could be improved for clarity. For example, defining all symbols before they are used would be helpful.
* A visual diagram showing the overall dialogue system architecture, with DST highlighted, would aid understanding.
