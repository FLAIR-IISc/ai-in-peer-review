- Data-driven DST using pre-trained word vectors removes need for manual lexicons, improving scalability.
- NBT framework (DNN and CNN variants) combines utterance, context, and slot-value representations to decide slot-value pairs.
- Empirical validation on DSTC2 and WOZ 2.0 shows strong gains over delexicalized baselines and matches lexicon-enhanced baselines without lexicons.

- Addresses the key DST bottleneck of labor-intensive lexicon creation with a promising solution.
- Methodology is sound: use of pre-trained embeddings and exploration of DNN/CNN utterance encoders with context modeling.
- Strong empirical results demonstrate practical effectiveness of the NBT approach.
- Clear problem definition and good situating within dialogue system research.
- Commitment to release corrected DSTC2 dataset is a valuable contribution.

- Paper organization and readability are weak; several concepts/symbols are introduced before being defined.
- Unclear whether the model performs truly joint SLU/DST or per slot-value binary classification.
- No statistics or details provided about the corrections made to the DSTC2 dataset.
- Limited exploration of sequential models (RNNs/LSTMs) for utterance representation.
- Insufficient detail on the baseline semantic dictionary (size, manual effort, coverage).
- Lacks illustrative examples showing concrete cases where NBT outperforms or fails compared to the baseline.

- Is NBT performing separate SLU and DST or true joint modeling?
- What exactly was changed in the DSTC2 corrections and how extensive are those changes (statistics)?
- Why were RNNs/LSTMs not considered or were any preliminary experiments conducted?
- Please provide details about the baseline semantic dictionary: size, creation process, and coverage.
- Can you provide example dialogues where NBT detects slot-values missed by the baseline and vice-versa?
