This paper proposes a novel Neural Belief Tracker (NBT) framework for dialogue state tracking (DST) that moves away from traditional delexicalization techniques and instead leverages pre-trained word embeddings to represent user utterances and dialogue context. The core idea is to compose these embeddings to create distributed representations, allowing the model to reason about semantic similarity without relying on hand-crafted lexicons. The authors demonstrate that NBT models match the performance of state-of-the-art delexicalization-based approaches and significantly outperform them when such lexicons are unavailable, making it a promising approach for scaling DST to more complex domains.

The primary contribution of this work lies in the successful application of pre-trained word vector spaces to improve the language understanding capabilities of belief tracking models. While pre-trained embeddings have been used in related NLP tasks, this work demonstrates their efficacy in directly addressing the limitations of traditional DST methods dependent on lexical matching or manual lexicon creation.  A second, significant contribution is the demonstration that NBT models can achieve competitive performance *without* relying on extensive hand-crafted resources, a major bottleneck for deploying DST systems in new domains. The empirical evaluation on two datasets clearly supports this claim, showing strong performance even in the absence of semantic dictionaries.  Finally, the insightful analysis of different pre-trained embedding strategies (XAVIER, GloVe, and Paragram-SL999) highlights the importance of semantic specialization in these vector spaces for optimal DST performance.

**Strengths:**

1. **Novel Approach:** The proposed NBT framework offers a fresh perspective on DST, moving beyond traditional methods and exploiting the power of representation learning. The use of pre-trained word embeddings to capture semantic similarity is a compelling and well-motivated idea.
2. **Strong Empirical Results:** The consistently significant performance gains over strong baselines on both DSTC2 and WOZ 2.0 datasets demonstrate the effectiveness of the NBT models. Matching or exceeding the performance of delexicalization-based models without the need for hand-crafted resources is a major achievement.
3. **Ablative Analysis of Embeddings:** The comprehensive comparison of different pre-trained embedding strategies (XAVIER, GloVe, Paragram-SL999) provides valuable insights into the importance of semantic specialization for DST. The clear demonstration that Paragram-SL999 vectors yield the best results justifies the focus on semantically-aware representations.
4. **Addresses a Core Limitation:** The work directly addresses the scalability problem of traditional DST approaches that rely on manual lexicon creation, making it more practical for real-world applications in complex domains.
5. **Clear and Well-Structured Presentation:** The paper is generally well-written and clearly explains the motivation, methodology, and results.  The diagrams illustrating the model architecture are helpful in understanding the flow of information.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Dataset Scope:** While the results are promising, the evaluation is limited to restaurant search domains. It’s unclear how well the NBT framework would generalize to more complex dialogue domains with larger ontologies and more nuanced user interactions. The authors acknowledge this limitation in the conclusion.
2. **Lack of Comparison with Slot-Tagging Component:** The paper does not compare the NBT model to a standard sequence-labeling or slot-tagging approach.  Restricting the similarity computation to slot-value spans within the utterance, rather than considering the entire utterance, could potentially impact the results and warrants investigation.
3. **Potentially Misleading Introduction Example:** The introductory example of a dialogue turn demonstrating dialogue state tracking seems to overlook coreference resolution. The example shows “inform(price=cheap, food=Thai)” after “Where is it?”, yet “it” refers to the restaurant and a restaurant name (or ID) should be resolved, not simply updated with the existing inform slots. This raises questions about the model’s handling of implicit references and coreference resolution.
4. **Context Modelling Simplicity:** The context modelling component relies solely on the last system utterance. More sophisticated approaches that consider a longer dialogue history or incorporate a memory network might further improve performance.
5. **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** While the paper reports the optimal lambda value for the belief state update, a more detailed analysis of hyperparameter sensitivity across the different model components would be beneficial.

**Questions to Authors:**

1. How does the NBT framework perform on more complex dialogue domains beyond restaurant search? Are there any plans to evaluate the model on datasets with larger ontologies or more diverse user interactions?
2. Have you considered incorporating a slot-tagging component alongside the NBT model? How would this influence the overall performance and potentially address the limitation of only considering slot-value spans?
3. Can you elaborate on how the NBT framework handles coreference resolution and implicit references within the dialogue context, as illustrated in the introductory example?
4. What is the computational cost of the NBT model compared to traditional delexicalization-based approaches? Could the computational complexity be a barrier to deployment in real-time applications?
5. What is the impact of different pre-trained word embedding sizes on the performance of the NBT models? Is there a trade-off between embedding dimensionality and computational efficiency?

**Additional Comments:**

The paper would benefit from a discussion of potential error analysis. Identifying common failure cases could provide valuable insights into the limitations of the current framework and guide future research. Furthermore, the code and datasets used in the experiments should be made publicly available to facilitate reproducibility and further investigation. Overall, this is a strong paper presenting a valuable contribution to the field of dialogue state tracking. The proposed NBT framework shows significant promise and has the potential to address a key challenge in deploying robust and scalable dialogue systems.
