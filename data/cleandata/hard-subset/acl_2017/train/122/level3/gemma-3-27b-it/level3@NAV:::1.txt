## Review of "Neural Belief Tracker: Data-Driven Dialogue State Tracking"

This paper presents a novel approach to dialogue state tracking (DST) using pre-trained word embeddings to compose distributed representations of utterances and dialogue context. The core idea – leveraging pre-trained embeddings to move beyond delexicalization – is strong and addresses a key limitation of current DST methods. The authors demonstrate consistent, significant improvements over delexicalization-based baselines on two datasets, and a thorough evaluation of different embedding pre-training approaches (XAVIER, GloVe, Program-SL999) adds to the work’s strength.

The primary contribution of this work, in my view, lies in the successful application of pre-trained word embeddings to directly model semantic similarity within the DST framework.  The authors effectively show that incorporating semantic information from these embeddings allows the model to handle linguistic variation without relying on hand-crafted lexicons, a significant step towards scalability. A secondary contribution is the empirical demonstration of the importance of *semantically specialized* word embeddings (Paragram-SL999) for this task, highlighting the benefit of moving beyond general-purpose distributional representations. 

The strengths of this submission are clear. The experimental results are convincing, showing the NBT models match or exceed the performance of lexicon-based systems and significantly outperform them when lexicons are unavailable. The ablation study with different embedding types is insightful, and the clear presentation of the model architecture and experimental setup facilitates reproducibility. 

However, the paper has weaknesses. The datasets (DSTC2 and WOZ 2.0) are relatively limited in scope and complexity. This raises concerns about the generalization ability of the proposed method to more realistic, open-domain dialogue scenarios where delexicalization strategies truly struggle. Furthermore, the evaluation doesn’t include a comparison against a separate slot-tagging component. Restricting similarity calculations to slot-value spans might inflate performance and obscure potential issues with broader contextual understanding. 

The introductory example is also potentially misleading. The stated dialogue state after "Where is it?" (inform(price=cheap, food=Thai); request(address)) suggests a resolved restaurant name, while a coreference resolution component isn't discussed. This prompts questions about how the model handles such scenarios. Finally, the reliance on a Markovian assumption for context modeling seems restrictive; a more comprehensive dialogue history representation could further improve performance.

**Questions to Authors:**

1.  How does the model handle coreference resolution, specifically when resolving entities mentioned across multiple turns?
2.  Could you elaborate on the computational cost of using the pre-trained embeddings, particularly during training and inference?
3.  What is the impact of using different window sizes or aggregation methods for the n-grams in the NBT-DNN model?
4.  How sensitive is the model to the choice of the similarity metric used in the semantic decoding module (e.g., dot product vs. element-wise multiplication)?

**Additional Comments:**

The paper would benefit from a discussion of potential failure cases and limitations of the proposed approach. Exploring the integration of external knowledge sources (e.g., knowledge graphs) could be a promising avenue for future work.
