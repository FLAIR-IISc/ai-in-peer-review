**Summary**

This paper proposes a novel Neural Belief Tracking (NBT) framework for dialogue state tracking, which overcomes the limitations of traditional approaches by leveraging pre-trained word vectors to learn representations for user utterances, system outputs, and ontology entries. The authors present two variants of the NBT model, NBT-DNN and NBT-CNN, and evaluate their performance on two datasets, DSTC2 and WOZ 2.0. The results show that the NBT models outperform the baseline delexicalisation-based models in terms of both joint goal and request accuracies, and that the use of semantically specialised word vectors leads to considerable performance gains.

**Main Contributions**

1. The authors propose a novel NBT framework that couples Spoken Language Understanding and Dialogue State Tracking, without relying on hand-crafted semantic lexicons.
2. The NBT models learn to compose pre-trained word vectors into distributed representations of user utterances and dialogue context, allowing for efficient handling of lexical and morphological variation.
3. The authors evaluate the performance of the NBT models on two datasets, DSTC2 and WOZ 2.0, and demonstrate their ability to outperform baseline delexicalisation-based models.

**Strengths**

1. The paper presents a novel and effective approach to dialogue state tracking, which addresses the limitations of traditional methods.
2. The authors provide a thorough evaluation of the NBT models on two datasets, demonstrating their ability to outperform baseline models.
3. The use of semantically specialised word vectors is shown to lead to considerable performance gains, highlighting the importance of semantic information in dialogue state tracking.
4. The paper is well-motivated, and the authors provide a clear explanation of the limitations of traditional approaches and the benefits of the proposed NBT framework.

**Weaknesses**

1. The paper could benefit from a clearer explanation of the NBT framework and its components, particularly for non-experts in the field.
2. The notation used in the paper is not always clear, and some symbols are introduced before being defined.
3. The authors could provide more details on the semantic dictionary used in the baseline model, and how it is constructed.
4. The paper could benefit from more example cases where the proposed model fixes errors made by the baseline model.

**Questions to Authors**

1. Can you provide more details on how the NBT models handle out-of-vocabulary words and unseen slot-value pairs?
2. How do the NBT models perform when faced with noisy or ambiguous user input?
3. Can you provide more information on the computational resources required to train and deploy the NBT models?
4. Have you considered using other types of word embeddings, such as BERT or RoBERTa, in the NBT framework?
5. Can you provide more details on the task-specific semantic dictionary used in the baseline model, and how it is constructed?
