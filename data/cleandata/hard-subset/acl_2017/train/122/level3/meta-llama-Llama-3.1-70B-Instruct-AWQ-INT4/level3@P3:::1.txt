**Summary of the Paper**

The paper proposes a novel neural belief tracking (NBT) framework for dialogue state tracking, which overcomes the limitations of previous approaches by leveraging pre-trained word vectors to handle lexical and morphological ambiguity. The NBT model couples Spoken Language Understanding (SLU) and Dialogue State Tracking (DST), eliminating the need for hand-crafted semantic lexicons. The authors evaluate their approach on two datasets, demonstrating significant improvements over delexicalization-based baselines.

**Main Contributions**

1. **Novel DST method**: The paper proposes a new DST method that represents slot values with pre-trained embeddings and composes them into distributed utterance and context representations.
2. **Improved performance**: The NBT models show consistent, significant improvements over previous delexicalization-based baselines on two datasets.
3. **Evaluation of alternative embedding pre-training approaches**: The authors evaluate the performance of different word vector collections, including XAVIER, GloVe, and Paragram-SL999, and demonstrate the importance of semantic specialisation in improving performance.

**Strengths**

1. **Strong and exciting idea**: The use of pre-trained word embeddings is a novel and promising approach to DST, which has the potential to improve the language understanding capability of belief tracking models.
2. **Significant performance improvements**: The NBT models demonstrate significant improvements over previous approaches on two datasets, indicating the effectiveness of the proposed method.
3. **Robust evaluation**: The authors evaluate their approach on two datasets and compare it to several baselines, providing a comprehensive understanding of the strengths and weaknesses of the proposed method.

**Weaknesses**

1. **Limited datasets**: The authors only evaluate their approach on two datasets, which may not be representative of more complex dialogue domains where delexicalization fails.
2. **Lack of comparison with separate slot-tagging component**: The authors do not compare their approach to a separate slot-tagging component, which could provide a more comprehensive understanding of the strengths and weaknesses of the proposed method.
3. **Intro example may be misleading**: The intro example may be misleading about dialogue state, as it does not resolve the reference to "restaurant_name", which could raise questions about coreference resolution handling.

**Questions to Authors**

1. How do you plan to address the issue of coreference resolution in your approach, particularly in cases where the dialogue state involves references to entities mentioned earlier in the conversation?
2. Can you provide more details on how you plan to extend your approach to more complex dialogue domains, where delexicalization may not be effective?
3. Have you considered evaluating your approach on other datasets or domains, such as multi-domain dialogue systems or languages other than English?

**Additional Comments**

The paper provides a comprehensive evaluation of the proposed approach, but could benefit from a more detailed discussion of the limitations and potential extensions of the method. Additionally, the authors could provide more details on the implementation of the NBT models, including the hyperparameter settings and the training procedure.
