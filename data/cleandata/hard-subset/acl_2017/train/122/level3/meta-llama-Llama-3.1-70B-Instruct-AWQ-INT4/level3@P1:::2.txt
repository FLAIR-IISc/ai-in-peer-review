**Summary of the Paper**

The paper proposes a novel neural belief tracking (NBT) framework for dialogue state tracking, which is a crucial component of modern spoken dialogue systems. The NBT framework leverages pre-trained word vectors to learn representations for user utterances, system outputs, and ontology entries, and combines these representations to make binary decisions for slot-value pairs. The authors evaluate two utterance representation models, NBT-DNN and NBT-CNN, and demonstrate significant improvement over a delexicalized baseline on two datasets, DSTC2 and WOZ 2.0.

**Contributions of the Work**

1. **Novel neural belief tracking framework**: The paper proposes a new framework for dialogue state tracking that leverages pre-trained word vectors to learn representations for user utterances, system outputs, and ontology entries.
2. **Improved performance over delexicalized baseline**: The authors demonstrate significant improvement over a delexicalized baseline on two datasets, DSTC2 and WOZ 2.0, using the proposed NBT framework.
3. **Comparison of utterance representation models**: The paper compares two utterance representation models, NBT-DNN and NBT-CNN, and evaluates their performance on the two datasets.

**Strengths of the Paper**

1. **Effective use of pre-trained word vectors**: The paper demonstrates the effectiveness of using pre-trained word vectors to learn representations for user utterances, system outputs, and ontology entries.
2. **Improved performance on DSTC2 and WOZ 2.0 datasets**: The authors demonstrate significant improvement over a delexicalized baseline on two datasets, DSTC2 and WOZ 2.0, using the proposed NBT framework.
3. **Clear goals and reasonable ideas**: The paper has clear goals and reasonable ideas that lead to improved results.

**Weaknesses of the Paper**

1. **Weak paper organization and readability**: The paper organization and readability are weak, making it difficult for non-experts to understand the details of the proposed framework.
2. **Lack of formal definition of DST**: The paper does not provide a formal definition of dialogue state tracking (DST) early on, making it difficult for non-experts to understand the context of the work.
3. **Unclear notation**: The paper introduces notations (e.g., tq, ts, t_v) before defining them, which can be confusing for readers.
4. **Lack of clarity on separate SLU**: The paper does not clearly indicate whether the model can perform separate spoken language understanding (SLU) or if the term "joint" is misleading.

**Questions to Authors**

1. Can you provide a formal definition of dialogue state tracking (DST) early in the paper to help non-experts understand the context of the work?
2. Can you clarify whether the model can perform separate spoken language understanding (SLU) or if the term "joint" is misleading?
3. Can you provide statistics on the corrected DSTC2 dataset and comparisons with other DSTC2 entries if the corrections are small?
4. Can you explore the use of recurrent neural networks (RNNs) or long short-term memory (LSTMs) for sequential utterance modeling, given their success in SLU?
5. Can you provide more details on the semantic dictionary used in the baseline to indicate the manual cost of creating such a dictionary?
6. Can you provide example cases where the proposed model fixes errors made by the baseline model?
