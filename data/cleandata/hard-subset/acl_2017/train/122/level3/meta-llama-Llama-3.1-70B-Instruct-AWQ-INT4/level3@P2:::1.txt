**Summary**

This paper proposes a novel Neural Belief Tracker (NBT) framework for dialogue state tracking (DST) that leverages pre-trained word embeddings to represent slot values and composes them into distributed utterance and context representations. The authors evaluate their approach on two datasets, demonstrating consistent and significant improvements over previous delexicalization-based baselines. The paper also explores the impact of different embedding pre-training approaches on the model's performance.

**Contributions**

1. **Novel DST method using pre-trained embeddings**: The paper introduces a new approach to DST that uses pre-trained word embeddings to represent slot values and composes them into distributed utterance and context representations. This method is shown to be effective in improving the performance of DST models.
2. **Evaluation on two datasets**: The authors evaluate their approach on two datasets, demonstrating its effectiveness in different scenarios.
3. **Comparison of embedding pre-training approaches**: The paper compares the performance of different embedding pre-training approaches, including XAVIER, GloVe, and Paragram-SL999, providing insights into the impact of these approaches on the model's performance.

**Strengths**

1. **Improved performance**: The paper demonstrates consistent and significant improvements over previous delexicalization-based baselines on two datasets.
2. **Novel approach**: The use of pre-trained word embeddings to represent slot values and compose them into distributed utterance and context representations is a new and exciting idea.
3. **Thorough evaluation**: The authors provide a thorough evaluation of their approach, including a comparison of different embedding pre-training approaches.

**Weaknesses**

1. **Limited datasets**: The datasets used in the paper appear limited, raising concerns about the generalization of the approach to more complex dialogue domains where delexicalization fails.
2. **Lack of comparison with separate slot-tagging component**: The paper does not compare the performance of the NBT model with a separate slot-tagging component, which could provide additional insights into the effectiveness of the approach.
3. **Handling of coreference resolution**: The paper does not provide clear information on how the NBT model handles coreference resolution, which could be an important aspect of DST.

**Questions to Authors**

1. How do you plan to address the limitation of the datasets used in the paper and evaluate the generalization of the approach to more complex dialogue domains?
2. Can you provide a comparison of the performance of the NBT model with a separate slot-tagging component?
3. How does the NBT model handle coreference resolution, and what are the implications of this for DST?

**Additional Comments**

The paper provides a thorough evaluation of the NBT model, but some additional information would be helpful to fully understand the approach. For example, it would be useful to know more about the hyperparameter tuning process and how the model was optimized for the different datasets. Additionally, some visualizations of the learned representations would be helpful to illustrate the effectiveness of the approach.
