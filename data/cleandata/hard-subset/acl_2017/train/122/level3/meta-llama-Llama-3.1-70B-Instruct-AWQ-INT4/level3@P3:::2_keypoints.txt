- Proposes a Neural Belief Tracking (NBT) framework that leverages pre-trained word vectors to jointly handle SLU and dialogue state tracking without hand-crafted semantic lexicons.  
- Introduces two NBT variants (NBT-DNN and NBT-CNN) and evaluates them on DSTC2 and WOZ 2.0.  
- NBT models outperform delexicalisation-based baselines on joint goal and request accuracies.  
- Use of semantically specialised word vectors yields considerable performance gains.  
- NBT composes pre-trained word vectors into distributed representations for user utterances, system outputs, and ontology entries.  
- Framework enables efficient handling of lexical and morphological variation.  
- Thorough evaluation on two datasets demonstrates empirical improvements over baselines.  
- Paper is well-motivated and clearly explains limitations of traditional approaches and benefits of NBT.  
- Exposition could be clearer: the NBT framework and its components need more accessible explanation for non-experts.  
- Notation is sometimes unclear, with symbols introduced before definition.  
- More details required on the semantic dictionary used by the baseline and its construction.  
- Would benefit from additional example cases showing when NBT corrects baseline errors.  
- Question: How do NBT models handle out-of-vocabulary words and unseen slot-value pairs?  
- Question: How robust are NBT models to noisy or ambiguous user input?  
- Question: What computational resources are needed to train and deploy the NBT models?  
- Question: Have you considered using contextual embeddings (e.g., BERT, RoBERTa) in the NBT framework?  
- Question: Please provide more details on the task-specific semantic dictionary used in the baseline and its construction.
