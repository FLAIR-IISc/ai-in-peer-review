**Summary of the Paper**

The paper proposes a novel Neural Belief Tracking (NBT) framework for dialogue state tracking, which overcomes the limitations of previous approaches by leveraging pre-trained word vectors to handle lexical and morphological variation. The NBT model couples Spoken Language Understanding (SLU) and Dialogue State Tracking (DST), eliminating the need for hand-crafted semantic lexicons. The framework is evaluated on two datasets, DSTC2 and WOZ 2.0, and shows significant improvement over a delexicalized baseline.

**Main Contributions**

1. **Learning representations for user utterances, system outputs, and ontology entries**: The NBT model learns to compose pre-trained word vectors into distributed representations of user utterances and dialogue context, enabling it to handle variation without requiring hand-crafted resources.
2. **Combining learned representations to make binary decisions for slot-value pairs**: The model uses the learned representations to make binary decisions regarding the current candidate pair, taking into account the preceding system acts and the user utterance.
3. **Significant improvement over a delexicalized baseline**: The NBT model outperforms the baseline model in terms of both joint goal and request accuracies, demonstrating its effectiveness in handling semantic variation.

**Strengths**

1. **Effective use of pre-trained word vectors**: The NBT model leverages the semantic relations embedded in pre-trained word vectors to handle semantic variation, resulting in significant performance gains.
2. **Coupling SLU and DST**: The model eliminates the need for hand-crafted semantic lexicons, making it more scalable and efficient.
3. **Improved performance on noisy data**: The NBT model shows robust performance on the DSTC2 dataset, which contains noisy ASR predictions.

**Weaknesses**

1. **Paper organization and readability**: The paper's organization and readability are weak, making it difficult for non-experts to follow.
2. **Lack of formal definition of DST**: The paper does not provide a formal definition of DST, which may lead to confusion.
3. **Notations and definitions**: The paper introduces notations before defining them, which may cause confusion.

**Questions to Authors**

2. Can you provide example cases where the proposed model fixes baseline errors?
3. How does the model handle out-of-vocabulary words or unseen slot-value pairs?
4. Can you provide more information on the hyperparameter tuning process for the NBT model?
