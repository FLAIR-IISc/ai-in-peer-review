**Summary of the Paper**

The paper proposes a novel Neural Belief Tracking (NBT) framework for Dialogue State Tracking (DST) in spoken dialogue systems. The NBT model uses pre-trained word embeddings to represent slot values and composes them into distributed utterance and context representations. The model is designed to overcome the limitations of traditional DST methods, which rely on hand-crafted semantic lexicons or separate Spoken Language Understanding (SLU) modules. The authors evaluate the NBT model on two datasets, DSTC2 and WOZ 2.0, and show consistent and significant improvements over previous delexicalization-based baselines.

**Main Contributions**

1. **Novel DST method using pre-trained word embeddings**: The paper proposes a new approach to DST that leverages pre-trained word embeddings to represent slot values and composes them into distributed utterance and context representations.
2. **Consistent and significant improvements over baselines**: The authors demonstrate that the NBT model outperforms previous delexicalization-based baselines on two datasets, DSTC2 and WOZ 2.0.
3. **Evaluation of alternative embedding pre-training approaches**: The paper evaluates the performance of the NBT model using different embedding pre-training approaches, including XAVIER, GloVe, and Paragram-SL999.

**Strengths**

1. **Effective use of pre-trained word embeddings**: The paper demonstrates the effectiveness of using pre-trained word embeddings in a DST model, which can capture semantic relationships between words and improve the model's performance.
2. **Consistent and significant improvements over baselines**: The authors show that the NBT model outperforms previous delexicalization-based baselines on two datasets, which suggests that the model is robust and effective.
3. **Novel approach to DST**: The paper proposes a new approach to DST that leverages pre-trained word embeddings, which can potentially lead to new insights and improvements in the field.

**Weaknesses**

1. **Limited datasets**: The paper uses only two datasets, DSTC2 and WOZ 2.0, which may not be representative of more complex dialogue domains where delexicalization fails.
2. **Lack of comparison with separate slot-tagging component**: The paper does not compare the NBT model with a separate slot-tagging component, which could potentially affect the results.
3. **Intro example may be misleading**: The intro example may be misleading about dialogue state, as it does not resolve the restaurant_name, which could prompt questions about coreference resolution handling.

**Questions to Authors**

1. How do you plan to extend the NBT model to more complex dialogue domains where delexicalization fails?
2. Can you provide more details on how the NBT model handles coreference resolution, particularly in cases where the dialogue state is not explicitly mentioned?
3. How do you plan to compare the NBT model with separate slot-tagging components, and what are the potential implications of such a comparison?

**Additional Comments**

The paper presents an exciting and novel approach to DST using pre-trained word embeddings. The results are promising, and the authors provide a thorough evaluation of the NBT model. However, there are some limitations and potential areas for improvement, such as the use of more diverse datasets and the comparison with separate slot-tagging components. Overall, the paper contributes to the advancement of DST research and has the potential to lead to new insights and improvements in the field.
