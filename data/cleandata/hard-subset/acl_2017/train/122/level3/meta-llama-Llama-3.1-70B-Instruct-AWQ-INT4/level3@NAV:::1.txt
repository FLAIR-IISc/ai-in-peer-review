**Summary of the Paper**

This paper proposes a novel Neural Belief Tracker (NBT) framework for dialogue state tracking, which overcomes the limitations of previous approaches by leveraging pre-trained word vectors to handle lexical and morphological ambiguity. The NBT model couples Spoken Language Understanding (SLU) and Dialogue State Tracking (DST) without relying on hand-crafted semantic lexicons. The authors evaluate their approach on two datasets, DSTC2 and WOZ 2.0, and show that it outperforms delexicalisation-based models with and without semantic dictionaries.

**Main Contributions**

1. **Novel DST method**: The paper proposes a new DST method that represents slot values with pre-trained embeddings and composes them into distributed utterance and context representations.
2. **Improved performance**: The authors demonstrate that their approach consistently outperforms previous delexicalisation-based models on two datasets.
3. **Evaluation of alternative embedding pre-training approaches**: The paper evaluates the performance of different word vector collections, including XAVIER, GloVe, and Paragram-SL999.

**Strengths**

1. **Strong and exciting idea**: Using pre-trained word embeddings is a promising approach for improving the language understanding capability of belief tracking models.
2. **Consistent improvements**: The NBT model shows consistent and significant improvements over previous delexicalisation-based models on two datasets.
3. **Robust evaluation**: The authors evaluate their approach on two datasets with different characteristics, demonstrating its robustness and generalizability.

**Weaknesses**

1. **Limited datasets**: The paper only evaluates the NBT model on two datasets, which may not be representative of more complex dialogue domains.
2. **Lack of comparison with separate slot-tagging component**: The authors do not compare their approach with a separate slot-tagging component, which could be a useful baseline.
3. **Intro example may be misleading**: The example in the introduction may be misleading about dialogue state, prompting questions about coreference resolution handling.

**Questions to Authors**

1. How do you plan to address the limitation of the NBT model to more complex dialogue domains?
2. Can you provide more details on how the NBT model handles coreference resolution?
3. How do you plan to evaluate the NBT model on more diverse datasets in the future?
