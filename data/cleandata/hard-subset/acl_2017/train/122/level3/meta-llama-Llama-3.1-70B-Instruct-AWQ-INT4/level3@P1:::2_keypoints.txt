- Summary: Proposes a neural belief tracking (NBT) framework using pre-trained word vectors to represent utterances, system outputs, and ontology entries; evaluates NBT-DNN and NBT-CNN and shows gains over a delexicalized baseline on DSTC2 and WOZ 2.0.

- Contribution: Introduces a novel NBT framework for dialogue state tracking leveraging pre-trained embeddings.

- Contribution: Demonstrates significant performance improvement over a delexicalized baseline on DSTC2 and WOZ 2.0.

- Contribution: Compares two utterance representation models (NBT-DNN vs NBT-CNN) and reports their performance.

- Strength: Effectively leverages pre-trained word vectors for representation learning across utterances, system outputs, and ontology entries.

- Strength: Shows clear empirical improvements on standard DST datasets (DSTC2 and WOZ 2.0).

- Strength: Paper has clear goals and reasonable ideas that yield improved results.

- Weakness: Paper organization and readability are weak, making details hard to follow for non-experts.

- Weakness: No early formal definition of dialogue state tracking (DST), hindering accessibility for non-experts.

- Weakness: Notation is introduced (e.g., tq, ts, t_v) before being defined, causing confusion.

- Weakness: Unclear whether the model supports separate SLU or if “joint” is misleading.

- Question: Provide a formal definition of DST early in the paper for non-expert readers.

- Question: Clarify whether the model performs separate SLU or genuinely joint SLU/DST.

- Question: Provide statistics on the corrected DSTC2 dataset and comparisons with other DSTC2 entries if corrections are minor.

- Question: Discuss or evaluate RNNs/LSTMs for sequential utterance modeling given their success in SLU.

- Question: Provide more details on the semantic dictionary used in the baseline to indicate the manual cost of creating it.

- Question: Include example cases where the proposed model corrects errors made by the baseline.
