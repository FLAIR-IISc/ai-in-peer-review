- Proposes Neural Belief Tracker (NBT) framework using pre-trained word vectors for dialogue state tracking with two variants (NBT-DNN, NBT-CNN).
- Models make binary slot-value decisions by combining learned representations of user utterances, system outputs, and ontology entries without exact matching.
- Evaluated on DSTC2 and WOZ 2.0, NBT outperforms delexicalized baselines and scales better when semantic dictionaries are unavailable.
- Contribution: removes dependency on hand-crafted semantic lexicons by learning distributed representations from pretrained embeddings.
- Contribution: novel method to combine utterance, system, and ontology representations to predict slot-value pairs.
- Contribution: empirically shows that semantically specialized word vectors (Paragram-SL999) substantially improve DST performance.
- Strength: addresses practical scaling limitation of DST by eliminating manual lexicons and matching or surpassing lexicon-based performance.
- Strength: thorough experimental evaluation across two datasets (noisy ASR and clean text) with strong baselines and analysis.
- Strength: important insight that word vector semantic quality directly impacts downstream DST performance.
- Weakness: paper organization and clarity are poor, with confusing notation and missing early formal definition of DST and system context.
- Weakness: “joint” SLU/DST claim is potentially misleading—unclear if the model can serve as standalone SLU.
- Weakness: insufficient detail on manual corrections to the DSTC2 dataset (no statistics or contextual comparisons).
- Weakness: lacks qualitative error analysis or examples showing where NBT corrects baseline mistakes.
- Weakness: missed opportunity to explore sequential models (RNNs/LSTMs) for utterance modeling despite acknowledging them as future work.
- Questions for authors: clarify whether the model can perform standalone SLU or is strictly joint SLU/DST.
- Questions for authors: provide statistics on DSTC2 corrections (percent corrected, errors fixed) and comparisons with other DSTC2 entries.
- Questions for authors: explain why RNNs/LSTMs were not explored for sequential utterance modeling.
- Questions for authors: provide concrete examples where NBT correctly identifies slot-value pairs that baselines missed, especially with semantic variation.
