

# Review for ACL 2017

## Summary and Main Contributions
The paper introduces a Neural Belief Tracker (NBT) framework that addresses limitations in current dialogue state tracking (DST) approaches by using pre-trained word vectors to handle lexical variation without requiring hand-crafted semantic lexicons. The main contributions, as I see them, are:

1. A novel DST framework that eliminates the need for hand-crafted semantic lexicons by using pre-trained word vectors to handle lexical variation, enabling scalable deployment in complex domains.
2. Demonstrated performance that matches delexicalization-based models (which require semantic lexicons) and outperforms them when such lexicons are unavailable, with statistically significant gains across two datasets.
3. Insight into the importance of semantic content in word vectors for DST performance, showing that semantically-specialized vectors (Paragram-SL999) significantly outperform general distributional vectors (GloVe) and random initialization.

## Strengths
1. The paper makes a compelling case for the practical advantage of eliminating hand-crafted semantic lexicons, which is a significant barrier to scaling dialogue systems to larger domains. The NBT framework allows for data-driven handling of lexical variation without requiring domain-specific linguistic expertise, addressing a key limitation in current approaches.

2. The evaluation demonstrates consistent, statistically significant improvements over baseline delexicalization-based models across two datasets (DSTC2 and WOZ 2.0), with gains of up to 3.4% in joint goal accuracy on DSTC2 and 10.5% on WOZ 2.0. This robust performance across different data characteristics (noisy ASR vs. clean text) strongly supports the paper's claims.

3. The ablation study on different word vector types provides valuable empirical evidence that semantic content in word vectors significantly impacts DST performance. This is an important contribution to understanding how to best leverage pre-trained representations for dialogue systems, moving beyond intrinsic evaluation to demonstrate downstream benefits.

## Weaknesses
1. The datasets used (DSTC2 and WOZ 2.0) are limited to a single restaurant domain with only three informable slots (FOOD TYPE, AREA, PRICE). This raises concerns about the generalizability of the approach to more complex dialogue domains with numerous slots and values, where current delexicalization-based methods might fail but the NBT approach's scalability is unproven.

2. The paper lacks comparison with a separate slot-tagging component, which is a common approach in modern dialogue systems. Without this comparison, it's difficult to assess whether the NBT's improvements are due to the joint modeling approach or the use of pre-trained embeddings. The authors' claim that NBT "efficiently learns to handle variation without requiring any hand-crafted resources" is not fully substantiated without this comparison.

3. The introductory example in Section 1 appears misleading about dialogue state tracking, particularly regarding how the system handles restaurant names (e.g., "The House" mentioned in the example). The paper doesn't address how the system resolves coreference (e.g., when a user says "it" referring to a restaurant mentioned earlier), which is a critical challenge in dialogue state tracking.

4. The paper doesn't adequately address how the NBT model handles cases where slot values are mentioned in a way that requires coreference resolution (e.g., "it" referring to a restaurant mentioned earlier in the conversation). This omission is significant given the example provided in the introduction.

## Questions to Authors
1. How would the NBT model handle cases where slot values require coreference resolution (e.g., "it" referring to a restaurant mentioned earlier in the conversation)?

2. Could you provide more details on the domain ontology used for the DSTC2 and WOZ 2.0 datasets, particularly regarding the number of slots and values, and how this compares to more complex domains?

3. How does the NBT model scale to domains with many more slots and values, where the current approach of iterating over all slot-value pairs might become computationally expensive?

Overall, the paper presents a strong and exciting idea that uses pre-trained word embeddings to address a significant limitation in current DST approaches. The core contribution of eliminating the need for hand-crafted semantic lexicons is valuable and well-supported by the results. However, the limited scope of the evaluation and the lack of comparison with standard slot-tagging approaches are significant weaknesses that need to be addressed before the work can be fully appreciated as a major contribution to the field.
