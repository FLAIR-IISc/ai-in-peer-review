

This paper proposes Neural Belief Tracker (NBT), a novel dialogue state tracking method that represents slot values using pre-trained embeddings and composes them into distributed utterance and context representations. The main contributions are: 1) a novel DST approach using pre-trained embeddings instead of delexicalization; 2) consistent, significant improvements over delexicalization-based baselines on two datasets; and 3) demonstration of the importance of semantic-specialized word vectors for DST.

Strengths: The core idea of using pre-trained embeddings to handle lexical variation is strong and well-motivated. The paper convincingly demonstrates that semantic-specialized Paragram-SL999 vectors significantly outperform distributional GloVe and random initializations, providing valuable insights into the importance of semantic quality in word vectors. The experimental results are robust, showing consistent, statistically significant improvements over baselines across both datasets.

Weaknesses: The primary concern is the limited dataset scope (both restaurant domains), raising serious questions about generalization to more complex dialogue domains where delexicalization fails. The paper lacks comparison with a separate slot-tagging component, which is a standard alternative approach; the restricted similarity computation to slot-value spans could significantly affect results. The introductory example (restaurant_name) appears misleading as it doesn't properly handle coreference resolution, suggesting potential issues with the model's ability to manage such cases.

The paper makes an interesting contribution to the field by demonstrating that pre-trained word embeddings can effectively replace hand-crafted semantic lexicons, but the limited evaluation scope and lack of comparison with alternative architectures weaken the claims of general applicability. The authors should address whether the approach can handle coreference resolution and whether the improvements would persist with more complex dialogue domains.

Overall, the embedding approach is promising, but the paper's scope and evaluation limitations prevent it from making a strong contribution to the field. The authors should provide more comprehensive evaluation on diverse datasets and compare with slot-tagging approaches to strengthen their claims.
