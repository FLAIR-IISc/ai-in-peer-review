

This paper introduces the Neural Belief Tracker (NBT) framework for dialogue state tracking, leveraging pre-trained word vectors to learn representations of user utterances, system outputs, and ontology entries. The main contribution is a data-driven approach that eliminates reliance on hand-crafted semantic dictionaries, which is a significant limitation in current dialogue systems. The authors compare two variants (NBT-DNN and NBT-CNN) that combine learned representations to make binary decisions about slot-value pairs.

Strengths: The work addresses a meaningful problem in dialogue systems by demonstrating that semantic quality in pre-trained word vectors significantly impacts performance. The experimental results show clear improvements over delexicalized baselines (73.4% vs. 72.9% on DSTC2 goals), particularly when semantic dictionaries aren't available. The analysis of different word vector spaces provides valuable insights into the importance of semantic content for downstream tasks.

Weaknesses: The paper suffers from poor organization and readability. Key terms like "DST" are introduced without formal definition, and notations (tq, ts, tv) appear before being defined. The use of "joint" SLU/DST is misleading since the model doesn't perform separate SLU. The authors fail to provide concrete examples where NBT corrects baseline errors, making it difficult to assess practical benefits. Additionally, the paper lacks details on the semantic dictionary used in the baseline and statistics about corrected DSTC2 dataset entries.

Questions to Authors: Please clarify whether the model performs separate SLU or truly joint SLU/DST. Provide specific examples where NBT fixes baseline errors. Include statistics on the corrected DSTC2 dataset and comparisons with other DSTC2 entries. Explain why RNNs/LSTMs aren't explored for sequential utterance modeling given their success in SLU.
