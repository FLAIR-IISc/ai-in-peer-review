1. Proposes Neural Belief Tracker (NBT) that uses pre-trained word embeddings to replace delexicalization and handle lexical variation without hand-crafted dictionaries.  
2. Shows NBT matches delexicalization models with dictionaries and outperforms them when dictionaries are unavailable across two datasets.  
3. Demonstrates semantically specialized embeddings (Paragram-SL999) outperform distributional (GloVe) and random (XAVIER) vectors.  
4. Novel idea addressing scalability in dialogue state tracking by removing reliance on hand-crafted semantic resources.  
5. Strong empirical validation on DSTC2 and WOZ 2.0 with statistically significant improvements and embedding comparisons.  
6. Well-engineered, reproducible implementation with two variants (NBT-DNN, NBT-CNN) and clear architectural detail.  
7. Evaluation is limited to a single restaurant domain with a very simple ontology (three slots), raising generalizability concerns to complex/multi-domain settings.  
8. Lacks comparison to a slot-tagging + value-matching pipeline and does not justify restricting similarity computation to slot-value spans rather than full utterance context.  
9. Introductory example mentions "restaurant_name" despite the ontology containing only three informable slots, creating a misleading or unclear presentation.  
10. Question: How does the model handle slot values (e.g., restaurant_name) not defined in the ontology and what's their relation to the experimental ontology?  
11. Question: How would NBT compare to a pipeline approach using a slot-tagger followed by a value-matcher—would NBT still hold advantages?  
12. Question: How would NBT scale and perform on more complex, multi-domain dialogue systems with larger ontologies and more slots?
