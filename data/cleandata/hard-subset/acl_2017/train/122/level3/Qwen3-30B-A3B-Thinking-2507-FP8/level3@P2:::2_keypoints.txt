- Introduces the Neural Belief Tracker (NBT) that uses pre-trained word vectors to avoid hand-crafted semantic dictionaries and handle lexical variation.  
- Proposes two model variants (NBT-DNN and NBT-CNN) that learn distributed representations of user utterances, dialogue context, and ontology entries.  
- Shows NBT matches or exceeds dictionary-based methods without requiring semantic dictionaries.  

- Addresses a major DST limitation by removing dependence on domain-specific semantic dictionaries, improving scalability.  
- Comprehensive experiments on DSTC2 and WOZ 2.0 demonstrate consistent improvements and superiority when lexicons are unavailable.  
- Highlights the importance of semantically specialized word vectors (Paragram-SL999) which outperform GloVe and random initializations.  

- Poor paper organization and readability; key terms and notations are introduced without proper definitions.  
- Unclear whether the model truly implements joint SLU/DST or still relies on a separate SLU component.  
- Insufficient details about the baseline semantic dictionary (size, creation effort) to assess claimed advantages.  
- Omits discussion of sequential modeling (e.g., RNNs/LSTMs) for dialogue context despite its relevance.  
- Lacks concrete examples showing where NBT corrects baseline errors to illustrate practical impact.  

- Request a formal definition of DST early in the paper for non-expert readers.  
- Clarify how this approach differs from previous joint SLU/DST methods and whether it performs joint modeling.  
- Provide statistics on the corrected DSTC2 dataset (number of spelling corrections and impact on results).  
- Give details on the baseline semantic dictionary, including size and manual effort to compile it.  
- Supply specific examples where NBT succeeds while the baseline fails.
