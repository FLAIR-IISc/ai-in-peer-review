

Summary: The paper proposes Neural Belief Tracker (NBT), a novel dialogue state tracking framework that replaces hand-crafted semantic lexicons with pre-trained word vectors to handle lexical variation. The main contribution is the demonstration that NBT matches delexicalization-based models' performance while eliminating the need for domain-specific semantic dictionaries. A secondary contribution is showing that semantic-specialized word vectors (Paragram-SL999) significantly improve performance over generic embeddings. A third contribution is establishing a data-driven approach to dialogue state tracking that scales better to complex domains.

Strengths: First, NBT provides a fundamentally different approach to dialogue state tracking that avoids the scalability limitations of existing methods. By leveraging pre-trained word vectors instead of hand-crafted lexicons, the authors demonstrate a solution that can potentially handle the linguistic variation in real-world dialogue systems without requiring domain-specific resources. Second, the paper provides strong empirical evidence of NBT's superiority, with statistically significant gains over baseline delexicalization-based models on both DSTC2 and WOZ 2.0 datasets, particularly when semantic dictionaries are unavailable. Third, the evaluation of different embedding approaches (XAVIER, GloVe, Paragram-SL999) convincingly demonstrates the importance of semantic content in word vectors for dialogue state tracking, providing valuable insights for future work in this area.

Weaknesses: First, the evaluation is limited to two restaurant domain datasets, which are relatively simple and small. This raises concerns about generalizability to more complex dialogue domains where delexicalization-based approaches fail. The paper would benefit from testing on larger, more diverse datasets to demonstrate true scalability. Second, the paper lacks comparison with a separate slot-tagging component, which might be more effective for identifying slot values. The authors restrict similarity computation to slot-value spans, which could be a significant limitation compared to more comprehensive approaches. Third, the introductory example contains a misleading representation of the dialogue state (e.g., "restaurant_name" is not resolved), which could confuse readers about how the model handles coreference and entity resolution. Fourth, the paper does not address how NBT would handle morphological variation in non-English languages or complex domains with many slot-value combinations.

Questions to Authors: How would the NBT framework handle cases where the same slot value appears in multiple forms across different dialogue domains (e.g., "cheap" vs. "inexpensive" for price)? Could you provide more details about the semantic dictionary used for the baseline comparison, particularly regarding how it was constructed and its coverage of linguistic variation? How does the model handle cases where the same entity is referred to with different names in the dialogue (e.g., "The House" vs. "it" in the example), and what would be the impact on performance if coreference resolution were not handled?
