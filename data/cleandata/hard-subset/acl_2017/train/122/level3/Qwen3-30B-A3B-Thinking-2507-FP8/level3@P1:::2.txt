

Summary and Main Contributions: This paper introduces Neural Belief Tracker (NBT), a novel framework for dialogue state tracking that overcomes limitations of previous approaches by using pre-trained word vectors instead of hand-crafted semantic lexicons. The main contributions are: 1) A neural framework that couples spoken language understanding and dialogue state tracking without requiring domain-specific semantic dictionaries; 2) Effective representation learning for user utterances, system outputs, and ontology entries using pre-trained word vectors; and 3) Two model variants (NBT-DNN and NBT-CNN) that demonstrate significant improvements over delexicalization-based baselines.

Strengths: The paper makes a compelling case for the scalability of NBT to larger domains, showing that it matches the performance of delexicalization-based models that rely on hand-crafted semantic dictionaries while significantly outperforming them when such resources are unavailable. This addresses a critical limitation in current dialogue systems, as creating semantic dictionaries for complex domains is labor-intensive and often infeasible. The experimental results on both DSTC2 and WOZ 2.0 datasets provide strong evidence for the approach, with statistically significant improvements across both joint goal and request accuracy metrics. The paper's demonstration that semantic-specialized word vectors (Paragram-SL999) yield better performance than generic GloVe or randomly initialized vectors is particularly valuable, showing that semantic quality matters for downstream NLP tasks.

The paper's contribution to understanding the relationship between word vector semantics and dialogue state tracking performance is novel and significant, moving beyond previous intrinsic evaluations of word vectors. The authors provide clear evidence that NBT models can learn linguistic variation without explicit semantic dictionaries, which is a major advantage over existing approaches. The evaluation framework is well-designed, comparing against both baseline delexicalization models and lexicon-enhanced versions, which provides a comprehensive picture of NBT's strengths.

Weaknesses: The paper's organization and presentation are problematic, making it difficult for readers to follow the technical contributions. Key terms like DST (Dialogue State Tracking) are not formally defined early in the paper, and notation such as tq, ts, tv is introduced before being defined, creating confusion. The claim of "joint" SLU/DST is misleading as it doesn't clearly distinguish the approach from previous joint models, which also operate on the same principle. The paper would benefit from a clearer architecture diagram showing the relationship between the NBT model and the broader dialogue system.

The paper lacks sufficient detail about the baseline semantic dictionary used, making it difficult to assess the manual effort required to create such resources. Without this information, it's hard to quantify the practical advantage of NBT. The authors also don't provide concrete examples of utterances where NBT successfully identifies slot-value pairs that the baseline missed, which would strengthen the argument for the model's advantages.

The paper's failure to address why RNNs/LSTMs weren't used for sequential modeling of utterances is notable, given their success in related SLU tasks. The authors should explain why they chose convolutional approaches over recurrent ones for this particular problem.

Questions to Authors: 
1. Could you provide statistics on the number of corrections made to the DSTC2 dataset? How significant are these corrections compared to the original dataset?
2. Please clarify the manual effort required to create the semantic dictionary used in the baseline. How many human hours would be needed to create such a dictionary for a domain with 100 slot-value pairs?
3. Could you provide specific examples where NBT correctly identified slot-value pairs that the baseline missed, particularly in cases where the user used non-standard phrasing?
4. Why didn't you explore RNN/LSTM architectures for sequential modeling of utterances, given their demonstrated success in SLU tasks? How would these architectures compare to your proposed models?
