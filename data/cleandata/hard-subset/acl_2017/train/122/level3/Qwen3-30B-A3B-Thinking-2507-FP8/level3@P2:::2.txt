

# Review for ACL 2017

## Summary and Main Contributions
This paper introduces the Neural Belief Tracker (NBT), a novel framework for dialogue state tracking that overcomes current limitations by leveraging pre-trained word vectors instead of requiring hand-crafted semantic dictionaries. The primary contribution is a scalable approach that efficiently handles lexical variation without domain-specific resources. The secondary contribution is two model variants (NBT-DNN and NBT-CNN) that learn distributed representations of user utterances, dialogue context, and ontology entries. The tertiary contribution is demonstrating that this approach matches or exceeds the performance of dictionary-based methods without requiring semantic dictionaries.

## Strengths
1. The paper addresses a significant limitation in current dialogue state tracking approaches: the dependency on hand-crafted semantic dictionaries, which are not scalable to complex domains. By using pre-trained word vectors to handle lexical variation, the NBT framework provides a more scalable solution that doesn't require domain-specific knowledge. This is a strong contribution with clear practical implications.

2. The experimental evaluation is comprehensive, showing consistent improvements over baseline delexicalization-based models on both DSTC2 and WOZ 2.0 datasets. The results demonstrate that NBT matches the performance of lexicon-enhanced baselines while significantly outperforming them when lexicons are unavailable, which directly supports the paper's main claim.

3. The paper provides valuable insights into the importance of semantic quality in word vectors, showing that semantically specialized Paragram-SL999 vectors substantially outperform both random initializations and distributional GloVe vectors. This finding has broader implications for natural language processing research beyond dialogue state tracking.

## Weaknesses
1. The paper's organization and readability are poor, making it difficult to follow for non-experts. Key terms like "DST" are not formally defined early in the paper, and notations (e.g., tq, ts, tv) are introduced before being defined. This significantly hinders comprehension of the model architecture.

2. It's unclear whether the model truly performs joint SLU/DST or if it's still relying on a separate SLU component. The paper claims to "couple SLU and DST" but doesn't clarify how this differs from previous joint approaches. The term "joint" is potentially misleading without this clarification.

3. The paper doesn't provide sufficient details about the semantic dictionary used in the baseline, making it difficult to assess the manual effort required to create it. The authors should clarify the size of the dictionary, the effort involved, and how it was compiled to properly contextualize the claimed advantages.

4. The paper omits discussion of sequential modeling for dialogue context, despite the success of RNNs/LSTMs in SLU tasks. Given the sequential nature of dialogue, this is an important omission that limits the model's potential.

5. The paper lacks specific examples where the proposed model fixes errors made by the baseline, which would help clarify the practical impact of the approach. Providing concrete examples would strengthen the paper's contribution.

## Questions to Authors
1. Could you provide a formal definition of DST early in the paper to help readers unfamiliar with the field understand the problem context?

2. How does the proposed model differ from previous joint SLU/DST approaches? Is it truly performing joint modeling or is it still relying on a separate SLU component?

3. What are the statistics on the corrected DSTC2 dataset? How many spelling errors were corrected, and how did this impact the results?

4. Could you provide more details about the semantic dictionary used in the baseline, including its size and the manual effort required to create it?

5. Could you provide specific examples where the NBT model successfully handles cases that the baseline model fails at?
