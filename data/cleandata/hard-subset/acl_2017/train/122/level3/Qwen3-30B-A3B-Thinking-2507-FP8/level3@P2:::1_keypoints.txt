- Proposes Neural Belief Tracker (NBT) that uses pre-trained word vectors to handle lexical variation and remove hand-crafted semantic lexicons for dialogue state tracking.  
- Reports NBT matches delexicalization-based models and outperforms them when lexicons are unavailable, with significant gains on DSTC2 and WOZ 2.0.  
- Shows semantically-specialized vectors (Paragram-SL999) substantially outperform general vectors (GloVe) and random initialization for DST.  
- Eliminating hand-crafted lexicons is a practical advantage for scaling dialogue systems to larger domains.  
- Empirical results show consistent, statistically significant improvements (up to 3.4% on DSTC2, 10.5% on WOZ 2.0) across noisy and clean datasets.  
- Ablation study provides evidence that semantic content in pre-trained embeddings yields downstream DST benefits.  
- Evaluation is limited to a single restaurant domain with only three informable slots, raising doubts about generalizability to complex domains.  
- No comparison with slot-tagging approaches, leaving uncertainty whether gains stem from embeddings or the joint modeling approach.  
- Introductory example is misleading about handling restaurant names and does not address coreference resolution.  
- Paper omits how NBT handles slot values requiring coreference (e.g., pronouns referring to previously mentioned entities).  
- Scalability concerns: iterating over all slot–value pairs may be computationally expensive in domains with many slots/values.  
- Questions requested: how NBT handles coreference, details on DSTC2/WOZ2.0 ontologies (slots/values) and how NBT scales to much larger domains.  
- Overall: promising idea that removes need for semantic lexicons and is empirically supported, but limited evaluation and missing comparisons reduce its impact.
