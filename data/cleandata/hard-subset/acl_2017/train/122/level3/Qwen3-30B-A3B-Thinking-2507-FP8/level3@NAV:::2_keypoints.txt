- Introduces Neural Belief Tracker (NBT) using pre-trained word vectors to represent user utterances, system outputs, and ontology entries.  
- Main contribution: data-driven DST that removes reliance on hand-crafted semantic dictionaries.  
- Compares two NBT variants (NBT-DNN, NBT-CNN) that make binary slot-value decisions.  
- Strength: tackles a meaningful DST problem and shows semantic quality of pre-trained vectors strongly affects performance.  
- Strength: empirical improvements over delexicalized baselines (73.4% vs. 72.9% on DSTC2 goals), especially when semantic dictionaries are unavailable.  
- Strength: analysis of different word vector spaces gives useful insights into semantic content for downstream tasks.  
- Weakness: paper organization and readability are poor; terms (e.g., "DST") and notations (tq, ts, tv) are introduced without definition.  
- Weakness: "joint SLU/DST" label is misleading since the model does not perform a separate SLU component.  
- Weakness: no concrete examples showing where NBT corrects baseline errors, hindering assessment of practical benefits.  
- Weakness: missing details on the semantic dictionary used by the baseline and missing statistics about corrected DSTC2 dataset entries.  
- Question: clarify whether the model performs separate SLU or truly joint SLU/DST.  
- Question: provide specific examples where NBT fixes baseline errors.  
- Question: include statistics on the corrected DSTC2 dataset and comparisons with other DSTC2 entries.  
- Question: explain why RNNs/LSTMs for sequential utterance modeling were not explored, given their success in SLU.
