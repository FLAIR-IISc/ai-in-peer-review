## Review of "Neural Belief Tracker: Data-Driven Dialogue State Tracking"

This paper presents a neural network framework, the Neural Belief Tracker (NBT), for dialogue state tracking (DST) that leverages pre-trained word vectors to learn representations of user utterances, system outputs, and ontology entries. The primary contribution lies in learning these representations directly from data, circumventing the need for extensive manual annotation or hand-crafted lexicons, a common bottleneck in traditional DST systems. The authors compare two utterance representation models – NBT-DNN and NBT-CNN – and combine these learned representations to make binary decisions regarding slot-value pair presence. Experimental results on DSTC2 and WOZ 2.0 datasets demonstrate significant improvements over a delexicalized baseline, particularly in scenarios lacking hand-crafted resources.

The strongest aspect of this work is the demonstrated ability to achieve competitive DST performance *without* relying on manually curated lexicons. This is a significant step towards scaling DST to more complex domains where lexicon creation is impractical. The thorough experimental evaluation, including comparisons to strong baselines and analysis of the impact of different word vector types, further strengthens this claim. The clear demonstration that semantically specialized word vectors are crucial for performance is also a valuable contribution. Finally, the paper addresses a practical problem in dialogue systems and offers a promising data-driven solution.

However, the paper suffers from weaknesses in presentation and clarity. The organization is somewhat disjointed, and the introduction of notations (e.g., *tq*, *ts*, *t_v*) precedes their definition, hindering understanding for readers unfamiliar with the DST literature. While the authors claim a “joint” model, it’s unclear whether the system performs separate SLU and DST components or truly integrates them; this needs clarification. Furthermore, the correction applied to the DSTC2 dataset should be quantified – if the corrections are minimal, comparisons to other DSTC2 entries become more meaningful. Exploring the use of recurrent neural networks (RNNs) or LSTMs for sequential utterance modeling, given their success in SLU, would be a logical extension. Finally, providing example cases where the NBT model correctly identifies states missed by the baseline would concretely illustrate its benefits.

**Questions to Authors:**

1.  Can you provide a formal definition of Dialogue State Tracking early in the paper, perhaps in the introduction?
2.  Please clarify the architecture of the system – does it perform separate SLU and DST, or is it a truly joint model?
3.  What was the nature and extent of the corrections made to the DSTC2 dataset?
4.  Could you provide specific examples where the NBT model successfully corrects errors made by the baseline system?
5.  What was the semantic dictionary used in the baseline, and what was the estimated manual cost (in person-hours) to create it?

**Additional Comments:**

A diagram illustrating the overall dialogue system architecture, placing the NBT within the broader context, would be beneficial. Consider adding ablation studies to assess the contribution of each component of the NBT model.
