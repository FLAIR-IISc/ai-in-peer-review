## Review of "Neural Belief Tracker: Data-Driven Dialogue State Tracking"

This paper presents a novel approach to dialogue state tracking (DST) called the Neural Belief Tracker (NBT), which leverages pre-trained word embeddings to represent slot values and compose them into distributed representations of utterances and context. The core idea of moving away from exact matching and delexicalization towards semantic representation is a strong contribution. The authors demonstrate consistent and significant improvements over delexicalization-based baselines on two restaurant search datasets, showcasing the effectiveness of their approach. The thorough evaluation of different embedding pre-training methods (XAVIER, GloVe, Program-SL999) further strengthens the work.

The primary contribution of this work, in my view, lies in the successful application of pre-trained word embeddings to the DST task, effectively bypassing the need for hand-crafted lexicons. This is particularly valuable as it addresses a key scalability issue in traditional DST systems. A secondary contribution is the demonstration that semantically specialized word vectors (Paragram-SL999) significantly outperform generic distributional embeddings, highlighting the importance of semantic content in the embedding space. Finally, the architecture, while not groundbreaking, provides a solid framework for integrating these embeddings into a belief tracking system.

However, the paper has some weaknesses. The datasets used (DSTC2 and WOZ 2.0) are relatively limited in scope and complexity. This raises concerns about the generalization ability of the NBT model to more complex dialogue domains where delexicalization struggles, and where a wider range of linguistic phenomena are present. The evaluation lacks a comparison with a separate slot-tagging component. Computing similarity solely within slot-value spans might inflate performance; a more comprehensive evaluation would include identifying slot mentions independently. The introductory example, while illustrative, is potentially misleading. The restaurant name should be resolved, suggesting a need for coreference resolution handling, which isn’t discussed. 

**Strengths:**

*   **Novelty of Approach:** Utilizing pre-trained word embeddings for DST is a promising direction, offering a potential solution to the lexicon scalability problem.
*   **Strong Empirical Results:** Consistent and significant improvements over strong baselines on two datasets demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed method.
*   **Ablation Study:** The evaluation of different embedding pre-training approaches provides valuable insights into the importance of semantic information.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Limited Dataset Scope:** The datasets may not adequately represent the complexity of real-world dialogue scenarios.
*   **Missing Comparison:** Lack of comparison to a slot-tagging component limits understanding of the model’s true capabilities.
*   **Potentially Misleading Example:** The introductory example raises questions about coreference resolution handling.



**Questions to Authors:**

1.  How does the NBT model handle coreference resolution, particularly in scenarios where slot values are referred to using pronouns or other referring expressions?
2.  Could you elaborate on the computational cost of using pre-trained embeddings compared to traditional delexicalization methods, especially for large-scale ontologies?
3.  Do you foresee any challenges in adapting the NBT model to multi-domain dialogue systems, and if so, what strategies might be employed to address them?
