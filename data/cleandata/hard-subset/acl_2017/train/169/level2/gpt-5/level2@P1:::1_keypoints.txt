- Presents a pipeline to automatically annotate GEC outputs with explicit edits and error types for fine-grained evaluation.
- Combines a linguistically-enhanced edit alignment algorithm and a dataset-independent rule-based error-type classifier using automatic POS/lemmas/parses and dictionary checks.
- Small manual study reports high appropriateness of predicted categories and the tool is used for the first detailed error-type analysis of CoNLL-2014 systems; tool to be released.

- Addresses a real evaluation gap by enabling diagnostic, reproducible error-type evaluation of system outputs.
- Dataset independence and transparency: deterministic rules on automatic linguistic markup make decisions interpretable and corpus-agnostic.
- Empirical validation: expert evaluation (5 raters, 200 edits) shows ≥95% Good/Acceptable judgments despite noisy edit boundaries.
- Sensible handling of reference–hypothesis mismatch by reprocessing references for exact edit alignment; bootstrap test shows no significant difference vs gold.
- Novel, actionable CoNLL-2014 analyses highlighting per-type leaders, operation-tier performance, multi-token edit difficulties, and detection vs correction differences.

- Limited intrinsic evaluation: 200-edit study doesn’t provide per-type precision/recall, confusion matrices, or breakdown of alignment/tagging vs classification errors.
- Dependence on automatic tagging/parsing and resources may flip labels; no sensitivity analysis across taggers or ablation tests; GB-centric dictionary may bias SPELL classifications.
- Design choices in error taxonomy (e.g., treating modals as tense) may collapse meaningful distinctions; no quantitative mapping to existing schemes.
- Exact-span matching is simple but potentially brittle vs flexible multi-annotator boundaries; claim that M2 overestimates needs controlled experiments.
- Scope not fully demonstrated: only English learner corpora evaluated; no runtime/scalability details or concrete release/maturity information.

- What is the rule ordering and conflict resolution strategy; can you provide per-rule/type coverage and common confusion pairs?
- Have you tested robustness across alternative taggers/parsers or n-best tagging; can you quantify POS-driven “Bad” judgments and mitigation heuristics?
- How do you handle SPELL/ORTH with US/UK variants, domain terms, and potential conflation with tokenization/punctuation errors?
- For multi-reference settings, how does exact-match scoring aggregate across references and what are effect sizes (ΔF0.5) when using auto vs gold references?
- Can you design controlled tests to quantify differences between M2’s flexible boundaries and your exact-match approach by edit type and length?
- Will you expand human evaluation to report per-type Good/Acceptable/Bad rates and inter-rater agreement, especially for low-frequency types?
- What are the plans for tool release (code, formats, license) and portability to other languages or tokenization conventions?

- Overall: timely and useful contribution that would benefit from stronger intrinsic classifier evaluation, clearer taxonomy trade-offs, and more rigorous scoring analyses to aid adoption.
