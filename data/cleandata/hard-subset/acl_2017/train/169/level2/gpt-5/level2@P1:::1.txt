Summary and Contributions
This paper presents a pipeline to automatically annotate unannotated GEC output with explicit edits and error types, enabling fine-grained error-type evaluation beyond overall scores. The approach combines (i) a linguistically-enhanced edit alignment algorithm to extract edits from original–corrected sentence pairs and (ii) a dataset-independent, rule-based error type classifier that relies only on automatic linguistic markup (POS, lemmas, dependency parses, and dictionary checks). A small manual study reports high appropriateness judgments for the predicted categories, and the annotated outputs are used to deliver the first detailed error-type performance analysis of CoNLL-2014 systems. The tool is to be released.

As I see them, the main contributions are:
1) A dataset-agnostic, deterministic rule-based error typing framework and classifier (25 base types with compositional operation/type granularity, 55 valid combinations) based solely on automatic analyses.
2) A practical pipeline that standardizes alignment and labeling for both hypotheses and references, making simple exact-edit comparison feasible and enabling detailed evaluations (operation tier, type tier, multi-token edits, detection vs correction).
3) A comprehensive error-type analysis of CoNLL-2014 systems, yielding actionable insights into complementary strengths across systems and categories.

Strengths
- Addresses a real evaluation gap with a practical, reproducible solution. The inability to measure error-type performance on system outputs has hindered diagnostic analysis. The proposed pipeline directly enables this and should be broadly useful to the community.
- Dataset independence and transparency. By relying on automatic POS/lemma/parse and deterministic rules, the method avoids dependence on corpus-specific annotation schemes and training data. Decisions are interpretable and traceable—highly valuable for error analysis and for reproducible evaluation.
- Empirical validation of acceptability. The expert evaluation (5 raters, 200 edits) shows that ≥95% of predicted types are judged Good/Acceptable by each rater, despite noisy automatic edit boundaries. This supports the practical reliability of the classification for evaluation purposes.
- Careful treatment of reference–hypothesis mismatch. Reprocessing gold references with the same pipeline to align boundaries/types is a sensible design choice for exact edit matching. The bootstrap test showing no significant difference (auto vs gold reference) across teams strengthens confidence that the automatic annotations are fit for purpose.
- Novel and useful CoNLL-2014 analyses. The paper delivers the first detailed error-type breakdown across systems, including operation-tier performance, per-type leaders, multi-token edit performance (highlighting MT’s relative advantage and the general difficulty), and detection vs correction. These insights are actionable for system design and combination.

Weaknesses
- Limited intrinsic evaluation of the classifier. The 200-edit human study assesses perceived appropriateness, but does not quantify precision/recall per error type or confusion patterns. Rare categories may be underrepresented. The evaluation conflates errors from alignment and tagging with classification; a breakdown would clarify where errors originate. The claim of ≥95% Good/Acceptable would benefit from per-type reliability and more instances.
- Dependence on automatic tagging/parsing and resources. The approach is sensitive to POS/parse errors (acknowledged), which can flip labels (e.g., NOUN:NUM vs VERB:SVA). There is no sensitivity analysis across taggers/parsers, nor an ablation showing which annotations are essential. The use of a GB-centric dictionary may bias SPELL classifications and US/UK variant handling; policy for variant tolerance is not discussed.
- Error scheme design choices and mapping. Some categories (e.g., treating modals as tense) collapse distinctions that are meaningful to many researchers. There is no quantitative mapping to existing schemes to show interoperability or to assess coverage differences. While the design trade-off is reasonable, clearer justification and guidance for cross-walking would help adoption.
- Exact-span matching vs flexible evaluation. The proposed scorer uses exact span+correction matches; this is simple and transparent but may be brittle with multi-annotator references and alternative valid segmentations. The critique of M2’s dynamic boundaries is reasonable, but the claim that M2 “overestimates” would be stronger with controlled experiments (e.g., synthetic boundary variants) and an analysis of impact by error type and edit length.
- Scope and generality. The method is presented as dataset-independent, but only English learner corpora are considered; language portability is not demonstrated. Runtime/scalability and tool maturity (e.g., release details, formats, licensing) are not discussed.

Questions to Authors
- Classification details: What is the rule ordering and conflict resolution strategy? Could you provide coverage statistics per rule and per type, and typical confusion pairs (e.g., DET vs PRON, NOUN:NUM vs VERB:SVA)? An ablation (without lemmas, without parses) would clarify which annotations are most critical.
- Robustness to tagging/parsing errors: Have you tested alternative taggers/parsers or n-best tagging to reduce brittleness? Can you quantify how often mis-POS drives “Bad” judgments in the study, and whether simple heuristics (e.g., agreement checks) could mitigate this?
- SPELL and ORTH policies: How do you handle US/UK variants and domain-specific terms? Do you whitelist variants or score them separately? Could ORTH (e.g., case/whitespace) be conflated with punctuation/tokenization errors?
- Reference processing: For multi-reference settings, how does exact-match scoring aggregate across references (union, best match)? Could you report effect sizes (ΔF0.5) per team when using auto vs gold references?
- M2 comparison: Can you design a controlled test set with known edit boundaries to quantify the inflation from M2’s boundary choices vs your exact-match approach, including by edit length and type?
- Error-type reliability: Can you expand the human evaluation to report per-type Good/Acceptable/Bad rates and inter-rater agreement, especially for low-frequency types (e.g., WO, VERB:INFL)?
- Generality and release: What are the plans for tool release (code, models, license) and supported formats? How portable is the approach to other languages or to corpora with different tokenization conventions?

Overall, this is a timely and useful contribution. Strengthening the intrinsic evaluation of the classifier, clarifying design trade-offs in the error scheme, and providing more rigorous analysis of the scoring behavior would further improve the paper and facilitate adoption.
