Summary: The paper proposes a two-step pipeline to automatically annotate system outputs for grammatical error correction (GEC) with edit spans and error types: (1) linguistically enhanced alignment for edit extraction, and (2) a dataset-independent, rule-based error type classifier driven by automatic linguistic markup (POS, lemmas, dependencies). A small human study reports that predicted error types are judged “Good” or “Acceptable” in at least 95% of cases per rater. The authors then re-annotate CoNLL-2014 system outputs and references to enable per-error-type precision/recall analysis, demonstrating new insights into systems’ strengths, detection vs. correction performance, and multi-token edits.

Contributions (as I see them):
- A practical, dataset-agnostic rule-based error typing framework with an operational taxonomy enabling granular analysis across operation tiers (Missing/Unnecessary/Replacement) and POS/morphology categories.
- An evaluation methodology that decouples edit extraction from scoring and shows that auto-annotated references yield scores statistically indistinguishable from gold under bootstrap tests, enabling scalable, consistent error-type evaluation.
- A comprehensive error-type analysis of CoNLL-2014 systems, revealing complementary strengths and actionable targets (e.g., multi-token, Unnecessary edits).

Strengths:
- Usefulness/impact: The tool fills a real evaluation gap by enabling per-type precision/recall and detection analyses; practitioners can diagnose systems and target improvements. Evidence: detailed tables for 24+ categories, operation-tier breakdowns, and multi-token diagnostics.
- Methodological clarity and reproducibility intent: The pipeline depends on standard NLP tooling; the rule-based design is deterministic and explainable; code release is promised.
- Empirical validation that auto-annotated references preserve system rankings and scores (no significant differences across teams; 1,000-sample bootstrap), supporting the practicality of fully automatic evaluation at scale.
- Human validation, albeit small, indicates high appropriateness of labels (86.3% Good, 10% Acceptable; κ_free=0.724), and transparent error attribution (mainly POS tagging mistakes).

Weaknesses:
- Limited novelty: The alignment step builds on prior work; the main novelty is a rule-based labeling scheme. A deeper comparison to learned classifiers on the same scheme is missing.
- Small, English-centric validation: 200 edits, 5 raters; heavy reliance on spaCy/English resources (Hunspell). Cross-dataset/language generalizability is untested.
- Sensitivity and transparency: The rule inventory and decision logic are not fully specified; no ablations (e.g., POS vs. dependencies) or robustness to different taggers/parsers.
- Potential circularity: Reprocessing gold references aligns them to the hypothesis extraction scheme; although significance tests help, a complementary evaluation against untouched gold could better quantify biases, especially for multi-token boundaries.

Questions to authors:
- How many rules are in the classifier, and what is their coverage by category? Can you release the full rule set and ablations?
- How does performance vary with different taggers/parsers or on other GEC corpora/languages?
- Can you provide a larger human validation and an error analysis quantifying failure modes (e.g., boundary vs. POS errors)?
- Beyond CoNLL-2014, do your conclusions about M2’s overestimation and system-specific strengths generalize?
