Summary and claimed contributions
This paper proposes a method to automatically annotate grammatical error correction (GEC) system outputs with edit spans and error-type labels, enabling precision/recall/F measures by error type rather than recall-only analyses. The approach consists of (i) a linguistically enhanced alignment to extract edits from source–hypothesis/reference pairs and (ii) a dataset-agnostic, rule-based classifier that assigns one of 55 error types using only automatic linguistic markup (POS, lemmas, dependencies, stemming, dictionary). A small human study rates predicted error types “Good/Acceptable” in ≥95% of cases. The tool is used to re-annotate CoNLL-2014 system outputs and references to provide the first detailed, cross-system error-type analysis (e.g., by operation tier, POS-based categories, multi-token edits, detection vs. correction).

Contributions (as I see them; strongest first)
1) A practical, deterministic, dataset-agnostic pipeline (alignment + rule-based classification) to annotate edits and error types in unannotated GEC outputs, enabling type-level precision/recall/F evaluation and analysis.
2) A comprehensive empirical analysis of CoNLL-2014 systems by error type, operation, and edit size, yielding actionable comparative insights into system strengths/weaknesses.
3) An empirical examination of evaluation choices (auto vs. gold reference processing; M2 vs. fixed-span matching), highlighting that reprocessing references with the same automatic pipeline preserves overall rankings while simplifying evaluation.

Strengths
- Clear practical value and community impact: The lack of error-type precision/recall has long impeded diagnostic evaluation in GEC. The proposed pipeline, if released as stated, fills this gap and supports method development (e.g., identifying which systems are best at Missing vs. Replacement vs. Unnecessary errors, multi-token edits, and specific POS categories).
- Sensible design for dataset-agnostic classification: The rule-based classifier relies only on automatic features and well-motivated linguistic cues, avoiding dependence on corpus-specific label sets or trained models. Determinism improves transparency and debuggability.
- Human assessment of label appropriateness: Although small-scale, the ≥95% Good/Acceptable ratings by five researchers (with decent agreement) substantiate that the rule set produces mostly reasonable types despite noisy automatic edit boundaries.
- Insightful analysis of evaluation artifacts: The discussion of how M2’s dynamic boundary matching can inflate scores, and the demonstration that auto-processing references does not significantly change results, is useful and well-argued, with bootstrap testing to support claims.
- Rich empirical insights: The breakdowns (operation tier, detailed error types, multi-token edits, detection vs. correction) produce novel and actionable observations (e.g., weak performance on Unnecessary edits for many systems; classifiers vs. MT differing by categories; difficulty with multi-token edits).

Weaknesses
- Limited validation of the classifier and over-interpretation: The main quality evidence is a 200-edit human study on two corpora. This is small and subjective; “qualitatively as good as human annotations” when reprocessing references is not directly supported (no direct type label agreement vs. gold type annotations is shown). There exist corpora with error-type annotations (e.g., FCE, NUCLE) from which a mapping to your scheme could be attempted to quantify accuracy at scale.
- Heavy dependence on automatic tagging/parsing without robustness analysis: The paper acknowledges POS/parse errors drive some mislabels, but there is no sensitivity/ablation analysis (e.g., different taggers/parsers, dictionary variants, or removing dependency features). It is unclear how stable the classifier is under different NLP pipelines and across domains.
- Rule coverage and maintainability not evaluated: The rule set is central, yet there is no detail on the number/complexity of rules, coverage statistics, conflicts, or ambiguous cases. No error analysis categorizes “Bad” judgments or common confusions (e.g., NOUN:NUM vs. VERB:SVA) to guide future rule refinement.
- Limited external validity of analysis: All diagnostic analyses are on CoNLL-2014 systems. It would strengthen claims to include other datasets/systems (e.g., JFLEG for fluency-oriented edits, FCE test) to show generality, particularly for multi-token and WO errors.
- Claims about evaluation simplification risk masking boundary errors: Reprocessing references to match the hypothesis pipeline solves comparability but may also entrench systematic boundary mismatches. There is no study of how often auto boundaries diverge from human boundaries per type or whether conclusions change under stricter span criteria.

Questions to authors
- Can you provide a quantitative evaluation of type assignment against an existing human-typed corpus via a mapping (e.g., FCE/NUCLE), at least for the overlapping categories? Even partial mapping would better quantify precision/recall per type.
- How many rules are in the classifier? What is the conflict resolution strategy, and how are ties or ambiguous cases handled?
- How sensitive are results to the choice of tagger/parser/dictionary? Have you tried more than one pipeline or ablations (e.g., POS-only vs. POS+lemma vs. +dependencies)?
- For multi-token edits, what proportion of “Bad” labels stem from span errors vs. classification per se? Can you report boundary agreement vs. gold spans for a sample?
- Will the released tool include scripts to reprocess references, and a stable interface to ensure reproducibility across environments?

Additional comments and suggestions
- Provide an error analysis taxonomy for misclassifications (with examples) to guide rule refinement; report Good vs. Acceptable by type to identify weaker categories.
- Consider adding confidence signals (e.g., soft labels or “ambiguous” flags) when tagger evidence conflicts, rather than forcing a single deterministic label.
- Clarify how UNK is produced and used in evaluation; does it conflate detection failures and boundary errors?
- Include runtime and throughput statistics to help practitioners adopt the tool at scale.
- The paper claims applicability to “any dataset,” but the current implementation is English-specific (spaCy English models, Hunspell GB). Please clarify language scope and what would be required for multilingual support.

Overall assessment
A timely, practically useful contribution that enables much-needed type-level evaluation and yields new insights into GEC systems. The methodology is sound and well-motivated, and the tool would likely be adopted by the community. The main limitations are the modest validation of the classifier and the lack of robustness/ablation analyses. Strengthening empirical validation (mapped evaluation against human-typed corpora, robustness studies, and richer error analysis) would considerably improve the paper.
