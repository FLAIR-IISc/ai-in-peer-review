## Review of "Automatic Annotation and Evaluation of Error Types for Grammatical Error Correction"

This paper addresses a significant challenge in Grammatical Error Correction (GEC): the lack of readily available, annotated data for evaluating system performance at the error-type level. The authors propose a method to automatically annotate system outputs with error types, leveraging linguistically-enhanced alignment and a novel rule-based classifier. The core contribution is a dataset-independent approach to error type classification, bypassing the limitations of training data bias inherent in machine learning methods. A secondary contribution lies in applying this method to re-evaluate the CoNLL-2014 shared task submissions, providing a detailed error-type analysis previously unavailable.

The strengths of this work are considerable. The high agreement rate (over 95%) between the automated annotations and human judgment is compelling evidence of the method’s effectiveness. The rule-based approach is well-motivated, offering transparency and avoiding the need for labeled training data – a major advantage in a field where annotation is expensive and standards vary. The re-analysis of the CoNLL-2014 data is valuable, revealing nuanced performance differences between systems and error types. The authors also rightly point out the limitations of existing evaluation metrics like M2, highlighting potential overestimation of performance. 

However, the paper has weaknesses. While the 95% agreement rate is impressive, the evaluation is on a relatively small scale (200 edits). A more extensive evaluation with a wider range of error types and text sources would strengthen the claims. The paper lacks a thorough comparison to existing error type prediction methods, even acknowledging that direct comparison is difficult due to differing evaluation schemes. A discussion of the computational cost of the pipeline would also be beneficial. Furthermore, the analysis of the CoNLL-2014 results, while insightful, feels somewhat descriptive. Deeper analysis of *why* certain systems excel at specific error types would be more impactful.

**Questions to Authors:**

1.  Could you provide more details on the computational resources required to run the annotation pipeline (e.g., processing time for a given corpus size)?
2.  While acknowledging the difficulty, could you discuss how your approach compares qualitatively to existing machine learning-based error type classifiers, even without a direct quantitative comparison?
3.  The paper mentions the potential for adding more detailed error categories. What are the trade-offs between granularity and practicality, and how did you determine the optimal level of detail for your framework?
