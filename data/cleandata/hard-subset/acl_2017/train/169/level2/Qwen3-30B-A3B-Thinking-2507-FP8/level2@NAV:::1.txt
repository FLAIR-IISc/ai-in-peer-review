

This paper addresses a significant gap in Grammatical Error Correction (GEC) evaluation by proposing an automatic method to annotate error types in system outputs. The main contribution is a dataset-independent approach that uses linguistically-enhanced alignment to extract edits between original and corrected sentences, followed by a rule-based classifier to assign error types based solely on automatically obtained linguistic features (POS tags, lemmas, etc.). The authors apply this method to the CoNLL-2014 shared task data to conduct the first detailed error type analysis of GEC systems.

Strengths: The paper tackles a genuinely important problem in GEC evaluation—error type performance analysis has been impossible due to lack of annotation in system outputs. The human evaluation of the classifier (95% "Good" or "Acceptable" ratings) provides strong evidence of the method's quality. The application to CoNLL-2014 demonstrates practical value, revealing nuanced system strengths and weaknesses across 25 error types that would have remained hidden with standard metrics. The dataset independence of the approach is a significant advantage over previous ML-based methods requiring dataset-specific training.

Weaknesses: The paper insufficiently demonstrates how this approach improves upon prior work. While they mention Swanson and Yamangil (2012) and Xue and Hwa (2014), they don't provide clear comparative metrics showing superior error type annotation quality. The claim of "dataset independence" isn't sufficiently validated, as the method is only tested on CoNLL-2014 and FCE-test without broader evaluation. The paper also underaddresses limitations—particularly how POS tagging errors propagate to error type misclassification, and whether the method handles complex multi-token errors well (despite showing poor overall performance on multi-token edits).

The authors should strengthen their case by: (1) providing direct comparisons with previous error type annotation methods, (2) expanding the validation across multiple diverse GEC datasets, and (3) more thoroughly analyzing the limitations of the POS-dependent approach. Despite these weaknesses, the work enables valuable new insights into GEC system performance and represents a significant contribution to evaluation methodology in the field.
