- Proposes a method to automatically annotate GEC system outputs with error type information, filling a major gap in GEC evaluation.

- Main contributions: linguistically-enhanced alignment for edit extraction, a dataset-independent rule-based error-typing framework using automatic linguistic properties, and the first detailed error-type analysis on CoNLL-2014.

- Addresses an important problem by enabling nuanced error-type evaluation beyond overall GEC performance.

- Rule-based typing is well-motivated and executed, relying only on automatic POS/lemma features and avoiding labeled training data.

- Small-scale human evaluation (5 raters, 200 examples) shows >95% "Good" or "Acceptable" ratings, supporting classifier quality.

- Application to CoNLL-2014 yields useful insights into system-specific strengths (e.g., CAMB for missing tokens, CUUI for replacements).

- Demonstrates that automatic reference processing produces results comparable to gold references and offers a transparent alternative to the M2 scorer.

- Insufficient evidence that edit extraction errors don't materially affect downstream error-type analysis; propagation of extraction mistakes is not thoroughly analyzed.

- The "dataset-independent" claim is under-supported because evaluation is limited to CoNLL-2014 and FCE-test and may not cover new error types or annotation schemes.

- Practical limitations are under-addressed, including impact of POS tagger or other automatic linguistic errors on typing accuracy without quantification or mitigation strategies.

- Analysis tables are valuable but the paper lacks deeper discussion of implications (e.g., why some teams failed to correct unnecessary token errors and what design choices explain differences).

- Question: How would the framework handle error types outside the current 25-category scheme or errors emerging in new datasets/domains?

- Question: How does the edit extraction handle cases where human annotators split multi-token edits differently, and how does that affect error-type analysis?

- Question: Why did many teams omit spell checkers despite common spelling errors in learner text, and how does that omission affect the generalizability of the error-type analysis?
