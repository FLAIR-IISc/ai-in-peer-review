**Summary of the Paper**

The paper presents a method for automatic annotation and evaluation of error types for Grammatical Error Correction (GEC) systems. The authors propose a linguistically-enhanced alignment algorithm to extract edits between parallel original and corrected sentences, and a new rule-based classifier to classify these edits into error types. The classifier is dataset-independent and relies only on automatically obtained information such as POS tags and lemmas. The authors evaluate their approach on the CoNLL-2014 shared task data and demonstrate its effectiveness in providing a detailed error type analysis of GEC systems.

**Main Contributions**

1. **Automatic annotation of error types**: The authors propose a method for automatic annotation of error types in GEC systems, which can be used to standardize existing error correction corpora or facilitate a detailed error type evaluation.
2. **Dataset-independent classifier**: The authors develop a rule-based classifier that is dataset-independent and relies only on automatically obtained information such as POS tags and lemmas.
3. **Detailed error type analysis**: The authors demonstrate the effectiveness of their approach in providing a detailed error type analysis of GEC systems on the CoNLL-2014 shared task data.

**Strengths**

1. **Effective annotation of error types**: The authors' approach is effective in annotating error types in GEC systems, with high agreement between human raters and the automatic annotation tool.
2. **Detailed error type analysis**: The authors' approach provides a detailed error type analysis of GEC systems, which can be used to identify strengths and weaknesses of different systems.
3. **Dataset-independent classifier**: The authors' classifier is dataset-independent, which makes it a valuable resource for the GEC community.

**Weaknesses**

1. **Limited evaluation**: The authors' evaluation is limited to the CoNLL-2014 shared task data, and it is unclear how well the approach will generalize to other datasets.
2. **Rule-based classifier**: The authors' classifier is rule-based, which may limit its ability to capture complex error types or nuances in language.
3. **No comparison to state-of-the-art**: The authors do not compare their approach to state-of-the-art GEC systems or error type annotation tools.

**Questions to Authors**

1. How do you plan to extend your approach to handle more complex error types or nuances in language?
2. Can you provide more details on the rule-based classifier and how it was developed?
3. How do you plan to evaluate the effectiveness of your approach on other datasets or in different contexts?

**Additional Comments**

The authors' approach is a valuable contribution to the GEC community, as it provides a method for automatic annotation and evaluation of error types. However, further evaluation and comparison to state-of-the-art approaches are needed to fully assess its effectiveness.
