This paper presents a novel approach to automatic annotation and evaluation of error types for Grammatical Error Correction (GEC) systems. The authors propose a linguistically-enhanced alignment algorithm to extract edits between parallel original and corrected sentences, followed by a rule-based classifier to assign error types. The classifier is dataset-independent and relies solely on automatically obtained information such as POS tags and lemmas.

**Main Contributions:**

1. **Automatic Annotation Tool**: The authors develop an automatic annotation tool that can standardize existing error correction corpora and facilitate detailed error type evaluation.
2. **Rule-based Classifier**: The proposed rule-based classifier is dataset-independent and achieves high accuracy in assigning error types, with human evaluators rating the predicted error types as "Good" or "Acceptable" in at least 95% of cases.
3. **Detailed Error Type Evaluation**: The authors demonstrate the value of their approach by carrying out a detailed evaluation of system error type performance for the first time for all teams in the CoNLL-2014 shared task on GEC.

**Strengths:**

1. **Novel Approach**: The authors propose a novel approach to automatic annotation and evaluation of error types for GEC systems, which is a significant contribution to the field.
2. **High Accuracy**: The proposed rule-based classifier achieves high accuracy in assigning error types, with human evaluators rating the predicted error types as "Good" or "Acceptable" in at least 95% of cases.
3. **Detailed Evaluation**: The authors provide a detailed evaluation of system error type performance for the first time for all teams in the CoNLL-2014 shared task on GEC, which is a significant contribution to the field.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Evaluation**: The authors only evaluate their approach on a small-scale manual evaluation, which may not be representative of the entire dataset.
2. **Lack of Comparison**: The authors do not compare their approach with other existing methods, which makes it difficult to assess the effectiveness of their approach.
3. **Error Type Limitations**: The authors acknowledge that their error type framework may not be exhaustive, and some error types may be difficult to classify.

**Questions to Authors:**

1. How do you plan to address the limitations of your error type framework, and what additional error types do you think should be included?
2. Can you provide a more detailed comparison of your approach with other existing methods, such as machine learning-based approaches?
3. How do you plan to evaluate the effectiveness of your approach on a larger scale, and what metrics do you think are most relevant for evaluating GEC systems?

**Additional Comments:**

1. The authors should consider providing more details on the implementation of their rule-based classifier, such as the specific rules used and how they were developed.
2. The authors should also consider providing more details on the evaluation metrics used, such as the F-score and its calculation.
3. The paper could benefit from a more detailed discussion of the implications of the results, such as how the findings can be used to improve GEC systems.
