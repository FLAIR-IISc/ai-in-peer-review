**Summary of the Paper**

The paper presents a novel approach to automatic annotation and evaluation of error types for Grammatical Error Correction (GEC) systems. The authors propose a linguistically-enhanced alignment algorithm to extract edits between parallel original and corrected sentences, and a new rule-based classifier to classify these edits into error types. The classifier is dataset-independent and relies on automatically obtained information such as POS tags and lemmas. The authors evaluate their approach on the CoNLL-2014 shared task data and demonstrate its effectiveness in providing a detailed error type analysis of GEC systems.

**Main Contributions**

1. A novel approach to automatic annotation and evaluation of error types for GEC systems, which overcomes the limitation of previous methods that relied on manual annotation or were biased towards specific datasets.
2. A new rule-based classifier that is dataset-independent and relies on automatically obtained information, making it more transparent and consistent than machine learning approaches.
3. A detailed error type analysis of GEC systems, which provides insights into the strengths and weaknesses of different approaches and can inform the development of more effective GEC systems.

**Strengths**

1. The paper presents a well-motivated and well-executed approach to addressing a significant problem in GEC.
2. The authors provide a thorough evaluation of their approach, including a small-scale manual evaluation and a large-scale evaluation on the CoNLL-2014 shared task data.
3. The paper includes a detailed analysis of the results, highlighting the strengths and weaknesses of different GEC systems and providing insights into the error types that are most challenging to correct.

**Weaknesses**

1. The paper assumes that the edit extraction algorithm is accurate, but does not provide a thorough evaluation of its performance.
2. The classifier is evaluated on a small set of error types, and it is unclear how well it would perform on a larger set of error types.
3. The paper does not provide a direct comparison with other approaches to error type evaluation, making it difficult to assess the relative strengths and weaknesses of the proposed approach.

**Questions to Authors**

1. How does the performance of the edit extraction algorithm affect the overall performance of the approach?
2. Can the classifier be extended to handle a larger set of error types, and if so, how would its performance be affected?
3. How does the proposed approach compare to other approaches to error type evaluation, such as machine learning-based approaches?
