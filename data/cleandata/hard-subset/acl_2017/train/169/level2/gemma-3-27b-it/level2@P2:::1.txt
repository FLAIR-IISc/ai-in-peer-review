This paper presents a method for automatically annotating grammatical errors in corrected text, enabling detailed error type analysis of GEC systems. The core idea is to leverage linguistically-enhanced alignment to extract edits and then classify them using a rule-based classifier. The authors demonstrate the approach by re-analyzing the CoNLL-2014 shared task data.

**Contributions:**

1. **Automated Error Type Annotation:** The most significant contribution is a pipeline for automatically annotating error types in GEC system outputs. This addresses a crucial gap in GEC evaluation, allowing for analysis beyond overall scores.
2. **Dataset-Independent Rule-Based Classifier:** The proposed rule-based classifier, relying only on automatically obtained features (POS tags, lemmas, etc.), is a valuable contribution. This avoids the dataset bias inherent in machine learning approaches and allows for consistent evaluation across datasets.
3. **Detailed CoNLL-2014 Analysis:** The application of this method to the CoNLL-2014 data provides a detailed analysis of system performance at the error-type level, revealing strengths and weaknesses of different systems.

**Strengths:**

1. **Addresses a Critical Need:** The lack of detailed error type analysis in GEC is a well-known issue, and this paper directly addresses it with a practical solution. The ability to analyze system performance on specific error types is highly valuable.
2. **Robustness of the Classifier:** The high "Good" or "Acceptable" rating (>=95%) from GEC researchers validates the effectiveness of the rule-based classifier.  This demonstrates that the automated annotations are reasonably accurate and reliable.
3. **Dataset Independence:** The dataset-independent nature of the classifier is a major strength. It avoids the need for expensive and potentially inconsistent manual annotation for each dataset. This promotes comparability of GEC systems across different corpora.
4. **Detailed Analysis & Insights:**  The analysis of the CoNLL-2014 data reveals interesting insights into system performance, such as the struggles with specific error types (e.g., unnecessary token errors) and the complementary strengths of different approaches.
5. **Tool Release:** The authors plan to release the tool, which facilitates reproducibility and allows other researchers to benefit from their work.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Scope of Error Types:** While the 25 error types are a good start, they may not capture the full complexity of grammatical errors.  The authors themselves acknowledge the trade-off between informativeness and practicality, and the current categorization might miss nuanced errors.
2. **Dependence on Alignment Quality:** The accuracy of the entire pipeline relies heavily on the quality of the initial alignment step. While the linguistically-enhanced alignment is a good choice, alignment errors will inevitably propagate to the error type classification. The paper doesn't sufficiently discuss the impact of alignment errors on the final results.
3. **Comparison to Gold Standards:**  The decision to re-annotate the reference data with the automatic pipeline, rather than comparing against the original gold annotations, is questionable. While the authors justify this with concerns about consistency, it effectively removes a direct comparison to human judgments and potentially masks inaccuracies in the automated system. The bootstrap significance testing doesn’t fully address this concern.
4. **Lack of Error Analysis of Classifier Mistakes:** The paper reports high agreement rates from raters, but does not delve into the *types* of errors the classifier makes. A detailed error analysis (e.g., which error types are most frequently misclassified, what linguistic features cause confusion) would strengthen the paper.
5. **Limited Evaluation beyond CoNLL-2014:** The evaluation is focused solely on the CoNLL-2014 dataset.  Demonstrating the generalizability of the approach on other GEC datasets would increase confidence in its robustness.

**Questions to Authors:**

1.  What is the estimated error rate of the alignment step, and how does this error rate affect the overall accuracy of the error type annotation?
2.  Why was the decision made to re-annotate the reference data automatically instead of directly comparing to the original gold annotations? Could you provide a more detailed justification for this choice?
3.  Can you provide a breakdown of the types of errors made by the rule-based classifier? What are the most frequently misclassified error types, and what linguistic features contribute to these errors?
4.  Have you considered extending the error type scheme to include more fine-grained categories, and if so, what challenges did you encounter?
5.  What are the computational costs associated with running the annotation pipeline, and how scalable is it to large datasets?
