## Review of "Automatic Annotation and Evaluation of Error Types for Grammatical Error Correction"

This paper addresses a significant problem in Grammatical Error Correction (GEC): the lack of detailed error type analysis due to the absence of annotated system outputs. The authors propose a method to automatically annotate unannotated correction data, leveraging linguistically-enhanced alignment and a novel rule-based classifier, enabling detailed error type performance evaluation. The core contribution is a dataset-independent error typing framework with impressive human evaluation results (>95% “Good” or “Acceptable” ratings) and its application to analyze the CoNLL-2014 GEC shared task submissions. A secondary, but valuable contribution, is the release of the tool used for this annotation, which will be beneficial to the GEC community. 

The strengths of this work lie in its practical applicability and clear methodology. The rule-based approach to error typing is particularly compelling, offering robustness against dataset bias, a common issue with machine learning methods. The thorough evaluation, including comparison to gold standards and a detailed breakdown of error types for CoNLL-2014 teams, provides valuable insights into system performance. The authors correctly identify limitations of existing evaluation metrics (like M2) and highlight the potential benefits of their approach. 

However, the paper has several weaknesses. While the human evaluation results are strong, the sample size (200 edits) feels relatively small. A larger scale evaluation would strengthen these findings. The analysis of CoNLL-2014 results, while detailed, primarily presents descriptive statistics. It would be more insightful to discuss potential reasons for observed differences in performance across teams, going beyond simply stating they "had different strengths and weaknesses". Further, the discussion of multi-token errors feels somewhat underdeveloped, and the paper doesn't fully explore the implications of systems struggling with these edits. Lastly, the paper lacks a discussion of computational cost and scalability of the proposed approach.

**Questions to Authors:**

1.  What is the computational cost of the annotation pipeline (alignment + classification) in terms of time per sentence/document?
2.  How well does the proposed framework generalize to different types of learner errors (e.g., errors from different L1 backgrounds)?
3.  Could you elaborate on potential strategies to improve the handling of multi-token errors?
