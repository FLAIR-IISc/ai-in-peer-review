This paper presents a method for automatically annotating grammatical errors in corrected text, enabling detailed evaluation of Grammatical Error Correction (GEC) systems beyond simple recall. The core idea is to leverage a linguistically-enhanced alignment algorithm to extract edits and then classify them using a dataset-independent, rule-based classifier. The authors demonstrate their approach by analyzing the CoNLL-2014 shared task data.

**Contributions:**

1. **Automated Error Type Annotation:** The paper’s primary contribution is a practical method for automatically annotating GEC system outputs with error type information. This addresses a significant limitation in GEC evaluation, where detailed error analysis has been hampered by the lack of annotated outputs.
2. **Dataset-Independent Rule-Based Classifier:** The development of a rule-based classifier for error typing, designed to be independent of specific training datasets, is a notable contribution. This avoids the issues of bias and incompatibility that plague machine learning approaches trained on differently annotated corpora.
3. **Detailed CoNLL-2014 Analysis:** The application of the method to the CoNLL-2014 data and the resulting detailed error type analysis provide valuable insights into the strengths and weaknesses of participating systems. This granular analysis is only possible due to the automated annotation capability.

**Strengths:**

1. **Addressing a Critical Gap:** The paper directly tackles a long-standing problem in GEC evaluation.  The ability to automatically annotate system outputs is a significant step forward, enabling more nuanced and informative performance analysis.
2. **Strong Validation of Classifier:** The human evaluation demonstrating >95% agreement with the rule-based classifier’s predictions is compelling evidence of its accuracy and reliability.  This is a crucial validation point, given the rule-based nature of the approach.
3. **Practicality and Replicability:** The authors explicitly state the tool will be released, promoting reproducibility and broader use within the GEC community. This is a highly positive aspect.
4. **Detailed Analysis:** The breakdown of results by error type and operation (missing, unnecessary, replacement) provides valuable insights beyond overall scores, demonstrating the utility of the automated annotation system. The discussion of individual system strengths is insightful.
5. **Comparison to M2 Scorer:**  The thoughtful discussion and validation regarding the differences between the proposed evaluation approach and the widely used M2 scorer are valuable, highlighting potential biases in existing evaluation methodologies.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Scope of Evaluation:** While the CoNLL-2014 analysis is informative, it is limited to a single dataset and a specific set of systems.  Evaluating the generalizability of the approach to other GEC datasets and contemporary systems would strengthen the paper.
2. **Dependency on POS Tagger Accuracy:** The rule-based classifier heavily relies on the accuracy of POS tagging, stemming, and lemmatization. While acknowledged, the impact of POS tagging errors on the final error type classification could be more thoroughly investigated. The example provided (ring -> rings) is a good start, but a more systematic analysis of tagging-induced errors would be beneficial.
3. **Granularity of Error Types:** The 25 error types, while comprehensive, might be too granular for some analyses. The discussion of the trade-off between informativeness and practicality is good, but further exploration of collapsing or aggregating error types to different levels of granularity could be beneficial.
4. **Handling of Complex Errors:** The paper primarily focuses on relatively straightforward errors. The ability of the system to accurately classify more complex errors, involving multiple interacting grammatical issues, is not fully explored.
5. **Lack of Comparison to Existing Automated Approaches:** While the paper references some previous work on automated error typing, a direct comparative evaluation against those methods (e.g., Felice et al., 2016) would provide a clearer picture of the advantages of the proposed approach.



**Questions to Authors:**

1.  How sensitive is the classifier’s performance to errors in the POS tagging, stemming, or lemmatization stages? Could you provide an ablation study demonstrating the impact of using different POS taggers or stemming algorithms?
2.  Have you considered alternative methods for resolving ambiguities when multiple error types are plausible for a given edit? For instance, could a probabilistic model or a more sophisticated rule-based system be used to select the most likely error type?
3.  What are the computational costs (time and resources) associated with running the automatic annotation tool on a large corpus of GEC data?
4.  You mention the potential for collapsing error types to different levels of granularity. Have you experimented with this, and if so, what impact did it have on the analysis of CoNLL-2014 data?
