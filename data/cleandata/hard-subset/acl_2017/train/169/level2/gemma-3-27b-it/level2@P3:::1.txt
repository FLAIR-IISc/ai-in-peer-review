This paper presents a method for automatically annotating grammatical errors in corrected text, enabling detailed error type analysis of GEC systems. The core contribution lies in a linguistically-enhanced alignment algorithm coupled with a dataset-independent, rule-based error type classifier. A secondary contribution is the application of this tool to re-evaluate the CoNLL-2014 shared task systems, providing novel insights into system performance across different error types. Finally, the release of the annotation tool itself is a valuable addition to the GEC community.

**Strengths:**

1. **Addresses a Significant Gap:** The paper directly tackles a crucial limitation in GEC evaluation – the lack of readily available error type annotations for system outputs. This hinders detailed analysis and targeted improvement of GEC systems. Providing a method to automatically generate these annotations is a valuable contribution.
2. **Strong Validation of the Classifier:** The manual evaluation, with raters assessing >95% of classifications as “Good” or “Acceptable”, provides strong evidence of the classifier’s accuracy and reliability. This is particularly impressive given the inherent noise introduced by automatic edit extraction. The use of inter-annotator agreement (κfree = 0.724) further supports the validity of the approach.
3. **Detailed CoNLL-2014 Analysis:** The re-evaluation of the CoNLL-2014 systems reveals nuanced performance differences between teams, going beyond overall scores.  The observations regarding strengths and weaknesses across error types (e.g., CUUI for unnecessary errors, CAMB for missing errors) are insightful and offer directions for future research. The comparison of detection vs. correction performance is a novel and potentially useful perspective. 
4. **Dataset Independence:** The rule-based classifier’s reliance on automatic markup (POS tags, lemmas) rather than labelled training data is a significant advantage. This avoids the issues of domain adaptation and annotation scheme inconsistencies that plague machine learning approaches.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Novelty in Alignment:** The paper relies on a previously published alignment algorithm (Felice et al., 2016). While acknowledged, the novelty of the overall approach rests heavily on the classifier. A more substantial contribution in the alignment stage would strengthen the paper.
2. **Simplified Evaluation of Classifier:** While the manual evaluation is strong, it’s limited to 200 edits.  A more extensive evaluation, perhaps on multiple datasets, would further bolster confidence in the classifier’s generalizability.
3. **Potential for Error Propagation:** The pipeline involves multiple steps (alignment, POS tagging, rule application). Errors in earlier stages (e.g., inaccurate alignment or POS tagging) can propagate and affect the final error type classification.  The paper acknowledges POS tagging errors but doesn't fully quantify their impact.
4. **Presentation of Results:** The presentation of the CoNLL-2014 results (Table 6) is dense and difficult to parse.  Visualizations or a more concise summary of key findings would improve readability. The table also includes a large amount of data with many dashes, making it hard to extract meaningful insights at a glance.



**Questions to Authors:**

1.  The paper states that the error scheme is a compromise between informativeness and practicality. Could the authors elaborate on the specific design choices that led to this compromise, and provide examples of error distinctions that were considered but ultimately excluded?
2. How sensitive is the performance of the classifier to the quality of the POS tagger and other automatic markup tools (lemmatizer, stemmer)? Have the authors explored using different POS taggers or incorporating confidence scores from the POS tagger into the rule-based system?
3.  The CoNLL-2014 analysis reveals that several teams struggled with correcting unnecessary token errors. Do the authors have any hypotheses as to why this is the case? Is it a limitation of the systems themselves, or a challenge inherent in identifying and removing unnecessary tokens?
4. The paper mentions that the tool will be released. Will this include pre-trained POS taggers and other necessary components, or will users need to provide their own?



**Additional Comments:**

*   Table 9 (complete list of error codes) could be improved by providing brief examples of each error type.
*   The reference list is incomplete, with some citations missing publication details (e.g., "Human evaluation of gram, Edward Gillian…").
*   The phrasing "dataset-independent" regarding the classifier might be too strong. While it doesn't require *labeled* training data, it still relies on the performance of *automatic* tools (POS tagger, lemmatizer) that may be dataset-specific. A more nuanced phrasing might be "training-data-free".
