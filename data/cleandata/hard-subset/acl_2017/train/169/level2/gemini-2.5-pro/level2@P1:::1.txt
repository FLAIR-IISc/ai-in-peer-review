**Summary**

This paper presents a method and a tool for automatically annotating grammatical error correction (GEC) data with edit spans and error types. The primary motivation is to overcome a significant limitation in GEC evaluation, where the lack of annotated system output prevents the calculation of detailed error-type precision and F-scores. The proposed pipeline first uses a linguistically-enhanced alignment algorithm to extract edits between original and corrected sentences. It then classifies these edits using a novel, dataset-independent, rule-based classifier that relies on automatic linguistic annotations (e.g., POS tags, lemmas). The authors validate their classifier through a manual study where human experts found over 95% of the assigned error types to be appropriate. The main contribution of the paper is a demonstration of the tool's utility through a comprehensive, fine-grained error analysis of the systems from the CoNLL-2014 shared task, providing novel insights into the strengths and weaknesses of different GEC approaches. The authors also state their intention to release the tool to the community.

**Strengths**

1.  **Addresses a Clear and Important Problem:** The work tackles a well-recognized and important gap in GEC evaluation. The inability to compute precision for specific error types has long hindered a deep understanding of system performance. This paper provides a practical and effective solution that enables a much richer and more insightful analysis, as powerfully demonstrated by the detailed breakdown of the CoNLL-2014 systems.

2.  **Novel and Well-Justified Error Typing Method:** The proposed rule-based error classifier is a key contribution. The authors correctly identify the dataset-bias limitations of supervised classifiers and instead propose a transparent, deterministic, and dataset-agnostic alternative. The analogy to "automating the annotation guidelines" is apt and represents a sound approach. The high quality of the classifier is convincingly demonstrated by the manual evaluation, where experts rated its output as "Good" or "Acceptable" in over 95% of cases.

3.  **Comprehensive and Insightful Experimental Analysis:** The application of the tool to the CoNLL-2014 shared task data is a significant contribution in its own right. The paper presents a wealth of new information about the performance of various GEC systems that was previously inaccessible. The analyses by operation type (Missing/Unnecessary/Replacement), general error type (Table 6), multi-token edits, and the detection vs. correction comparison are all highly informative and provide the community with valuable insights into what works for which errors.

4.  **Contribution of a Reusable Community Resource:** The commitment to release the annotation tool is a major strength. This will allow other researchers to replicate the analyses, apply the tool to new systems and datasets, and work towards a more standardized and fine-grained evaluation paradigm in GEC. This significantly increases the potential impact of the work.

**Weaknesses**

1.  **Discarding the Gold Standard Annotation:** A primary methodological concern is the decision to re-process the gold standard reference annotations with the proposed automatic tool (Section 4.1). While the authors argue this is necessary for a fair comparison and show that it doesn't significantly alter overall system rankings, it means the evaluation is no longer performed against a human-annotated ground truth. This approach evaluates systems based on the edits discoverable by the automatic aligner, which may systematically differ from human judgments. For instance, if the aligner favors single, complex edits where humans prefer multiple, simpler ones, it could inadvertently penalize systems that make smaller, more targeted corrections. The claim that the "auto annotations are qualitatively as good as human annotations" based on stable rankings is a strong one that may not be fully supported by the evidence provided.

2.  **Limited Discussion of Classifier Limitations:** The manual evaluation of the classifier is positive, but the paper could benefit from a more in-depth qualitative analysis of its failure cases. The authors state that most "Bad" ratings were due to POS tagging errors, but providing concrete examples and a more systematic breakdown would be valuable. It is unclear how sensitive the classifier is to the performance of the upstream NLP tools (tagger, parser, etc.) or if there are specific linguistic constructions or error types where the rule-based approach is known to be brittle. A deeper discussion of these limitations would provide a more complete picture of the tool's reliability.

3.  **Clarity of Comparison with the M2 Scorer:** In Section 4.1, the paper critiques the M2 scorer for potentially inflating scores due to its dynamic edit boundary matching. While these are known characteristics of the M2 scorer, the argument is presented somewhat briefly. A few concrete, side-by-side examples illustrating how the proposed method and the M2 scorer would evaluate the same set of hypothesis and reference edits differently would make this point much stronger and clearer for the reader.

**Questions to Authors**

1.  Regarding the re-annotation of the gold reference: Could you elaborate on why a direct comparison against the original human-annotated edits was not pursued? For example, would it be possible to use an evaluation strategy that allows for partial credit or fuzzy matching if the automatically identified hypothesis edits do not perfectly align with the gold standard ones? While your method ensures consistency, moving the evaluation away from the human ground truth seems like a significant trade-off.

2.  The error typology is comprehensive. Could you briefly discuss the development process for these 25 categories and their corresponding classification rules? Furthermore, how extensible is the framework? If a researcher wanted to add a new, more fine-grained error category (e.g., separating modal verb errors from the general VERB:TENSE category), what would that process entail?

3.  The analysis in Section 4.2 reveals the interesting finding that five teams' systems failed to correct any "Unnecessary" errors. The paper notes there is "no obvious explanation." Could your tool be used to perform a more qualitative analysis to diagnose *why* these systems fail on what seems to be a relatively simple error type? For example, do their outputs suggest they never propose deletions at all?
