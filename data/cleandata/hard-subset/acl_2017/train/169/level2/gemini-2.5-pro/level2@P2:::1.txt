**Summary of the paper and main contributions**

This paper introduces a method for automatically annotating grammatical error correction (GEC) data with error types, enabling a more detailed evaluation of GEC systems beyond a single aggregate score. The method first uses a linguistically-enhanced alignment algorithm to extract edits between original and corrected sentences. It then classifies these edits using a novel, dataset-independent, rule-based classifier that leverages automatic linguistic annotations like POS tags and lemmas.

The main contributions of this work are:
1.  **A practical tool and methodology for fine-grained GEC evaluation.** By annotating both system hypotheses and the reference text in a consistent manner, the paper enables the calculation of precision, recall, and F-scores for specific error categories, a long-standing need in the GEC community. The authors plan to release their tool, which will be a significant asset for researchers.
2.  **The first detailed error-type analysis of the CoNLL-2014 shared task.** The authors apply their tool to the outputs of all participating systems, providing a comprehensive and insightful breakdown of system performance across 25 error types. This analysis reveals the specific strengths and weaknesses of different GEC architectures (e.g., MT vs. classifier-based), offering valuable lessons for future system development.
3.  **A novel, rule-based error classification framework.** The proposed classifier is dataset-agnostic, requires no training data, and is demonstrated to be highly effective via a human evaluation where experts rated 95% of its outputs as "Good" or "Acceptable".

**Strengths**

1.  **High practical impact and clear motivation.** The paper addresses a critical gap in GEC evaluation. The inability to analyze system performance on specific error types has been a major limitation, making it difficult to understand system behavior and diagnose failures. This work provides a direct and effective solution. The detailed analysis of the CoNLL-2014 systems is a powerful demonstration of the tool's utility and produces genuinely new insights into the performance of established GEC systems.

2.  **Thorough and insightful experimental analysis.** The paper goes far beyond simply presenting a tool. The authors conduct a multi-faceted analysis of the CoNLL-2014 results, breaking down performance by operation type (Missing/Replacement/Unnecessary), 25 granular error types, multi-token edits, and detection vs. correction. This comprehensive analysis (e.g., Tables 5, 6, 8) is the core strength of the paper, revealing interesting patterns such as the failure of several systems to handle "Unnecessary" errors and the superiority of MT-based systems for multi-token edits.

3.  **Sound and well-justified methodology.** The authors' decision to create an automatic reference by re-processing the gold data is a key methodological strength. This ensures a fair comparison where both reference and hypothesis are subject to the same potential biases of the automatic annotation pipeline. The validation in Table 4, showing that system rankings are not significantly affected by this change, effectively preempts criticism and builds confidence in the subsequent analysis.

4.  **Novelty and transparency of the rule-based classifier.** The choice to develop a dataset-independent, rule-based classifier is well-motivated. It avoids the significant problem of annotation scheme incompatibility across different GEC corpora, making the tool broadly applicable. The authors rightly point out that such a system is more transparent and deterministic than ML-based approaches, which is a valuable property for an evaluation tool. The manual evaluation of the classifier's output confirms its high quality.

**Weaknesses**

1.  **Insufficient detail on the rule-based classifier.** The paper's primary methodological contribution is the rule-based classifier, yet its inner workings are almost completely opaque. The authors state that it uses POS tags, lemmas, and dependency parses, but provide no examples of the actual rules. How does the system differentiate `VERB:TENSE` from `VERB:SVA`? What constitutes a `MORPH` error? Without this information, the work is not reproducible from the paper alone, and the claim of transparency over ML models is weakened, as the classifier remains a black box to the reader. Including a few representative rules or a link to a technical appendix would significantly improve the paper.

2.  **The criticism of the M2 scorer feels overstated.** In Section 4.1, the paper argues that the M2 scorer "tends to overestimate performance" by being flexible with edit boundaries to maximize TPs and minimize FPs. While this is a known characteristic, it was designed to handle valid disagreements in human annotation. The proposed method, which relies on a single, fixed annotation, is cleaner but simply shifts the complexity and potential for error to the automatic alignment stage. For instance, an aligner producing one multi-token edit will never match a gold reference that splits it into two. While the authors show overall scores are stable, this simplification and the strong claim against M2 could be presented more neutrally.

3.  **Unsubstantiated comparison to machine learning classifiers.** The paper dismisses ML-based error classifiers by stating they are corpus-biased and citing F1 scores of 50-70 from other work. However, the authors' own evaluation uses a different metric (human "Good/Acceptable/Bad" ratings), making a direct comparison of performance impossible. To make a stronger claim of superiority, the authors could have evaluated their classifier against a gold standard from a public corpus and reported comparable metrics like F1-score, or at least acknowledged that the evaluation methods are not directly comparable.

**Questions to Authors**

1.  Could you please provide a few examples of the rules used by your classifier to distinguish between related error types, such as the different verb error categories (`VERB:FORM`, `VERB:SVA`, `VERB:TENSE`)? This would greatly increase the clarity and reproducibility of your work.

2.  Table 4 shows that overall system scores are stable when moving from the gold reference to your automatic reference. Have you investigated if this stability holds at the individual error category level? Is it possible that the automatic annotator systematically disagrees with human annotators on certain error types (e.g., `OTHER`), which could skew the fine-grained analysis in Table 6?
