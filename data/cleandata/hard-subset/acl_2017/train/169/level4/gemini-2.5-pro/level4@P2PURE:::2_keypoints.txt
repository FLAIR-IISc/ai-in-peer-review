- Introduces a novel GEC evaluation method that measures performance by error type including precision, not just recall.  
- Key advantages: computes precision by error type, requires no manual error annotations, and handles multi-token errors.  
- Human evaluation suggests automatically assigned error tags for pre-computed spans are largely appropriate.  

- The rules used to derive error types are not described, a major omission given their centrality.  
- The basis for chosen error categories is unclear (not linked to prior work or rationale).  
- Classification rules likely depend on alignment/edits despite the authors' independence claim, but this is not discussed.  
- Classifier evaluation lacks a thorough error analysis and therefore gives no clear directions for improving the classifier.  
- Human validation may be biased: judges saw proposed tags, had a binary choice, and evaluated noisy spans, inflating "appropriate" rates.  
- The authors should validate classifier output on manually corrected, noise-free edit boundaries.  

- Method appears to assume continuous error spans (like M2), raising concerns about handling discontinuous edits (e.g., German separable verbs).  
- Portability to other languages is not discussed; edit detection and classification rules are likely language-dependent and unspecified in number/complexity.  

- The significance test details are unclear (on what data it was run), and using F0.5 for significance is questionable due to weak discriminative power.  
- Comparison of gold vs auto references seems to focus on edit boundaries and not on edit classification.  

- Error-type analysis lacks interpretation and system-context: poor scores may reflect systems not designed to target certain error types.  
- Specific surprising results (e.g., zero corrections for "unnecessary token") are explainable by system design (POS-based rule systems) but not discussed.  
- Some reported failures (e.g., CUUI word order) suggest possible misclassification by the evaluation tool, which is not investigated.  
- Authors should report raw TP/FP/FN counts (appendix) to clarify underlying instance counts per error type.  
- The claim that error-tagging system outputs was "previously impossible" should be qualified; modular systems can sometimes be tagged, but this method unifies comparison.  
- Multi-token error analysis is valuable but needs more interpretation, including that many systems were not trained on appropriate parallel learner-fluent corpora.  

- Reference section has formatting and metadata errors (capitalization, URL wrapping, missing pages).  
- Overall: core idea is strong but the paper is not ready for publication until rules, classifier error analysis, language-generalization, and data/reporting issues are addressed.
