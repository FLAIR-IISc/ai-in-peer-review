- Proposes a two-stage framework (linguistically-enhanced alignment + rule-based classifier) for automatic annotation and type-based precision/recall evaluation of GEC outputs, applied to CoNLL-2014.

- Contribution: a novel end-to-end methodology enabling fine-grained GEC evaluation and per-error-type precision, with the tool publicly released.

- Contribution: a comprehensive, system-level analysis of all CoNLL-2014 submissions revealing architecture-specific strengths and weaknesses.

- Strength: Enables much deeper analysis by breaking down performance by error type, edit operation, edit length, and separating detection vs. correction.

- Strength: Rule-based, dataset-agnostic classifier is transparent in principle, deterministic, requires no training data, and is easily reusable across outputs.

- Strength: Human evaluation showed ≥95% of automatic error-type assignments rated "Good" or "Acceptable," supporting classifier validity.

- Weakness: Core classification rules are not described, preventing assessment of linguistic validity, bias, and reproducibility.

- Weakness: Generalizability to other languages is unclear; preprocessing and rule sets likely English-specific and may fail on phenomena like discontinuous edits.

- Weakness: Classifier evaluation is superficial—little analysis of which error types suffer from upstream POS/tagging errors or other failure modes, and potential rater bias in evaluation.

- Weakness: Reference comparison claim (auto ≈ human) is overstated; using F0.5 significance tests can mask precision/recall differences and it’s unclear what aspects were compared.

- Weakness: Interpretation of CoNLL-2014 results is limited—evaluation may conflate systems not attempting an error type with systems failing at it, making zero scores misleading.

- Question: Please provide representative rules, a high-level description of the rule formalism/structure, and the approximate number of rules.

- Question: How much effort is required to adapt the framework to languages with different morphology/syntax (e.g., German, Russian), which components are language-dependent, and how would rules handle discontinuous edits?

- Question: Can the framework distinguish between a system that didn’t attempt an error type and one that attempted but had zero true positives?
