**Strengths:**
*   The paper addresses a useful application for language teachers and learners.
*   The proposed method could support more fine-grained comparisons of GEC systems.

**Weaknesses:**
*   The description of the core system is highly superficial.
*   The evaluation methodology has several significant shortcomings.

**General Discussion:**

The paper presents a method for automatically annotating the output of Grammatical Error Correction (GEC) systems with error types. This is a valuable application, as this information can benefit both language teachers and learners. Since many GEC systems only provide a corrected sentence without specifying the error type, this approach could enable finer-grained comparisons between systems, evaluating not just overall performance but also recall and precision on specific error categories.

Unfortunately, the paper's execution is lacking, with a superficial system description and an unconvincing evaluation.

The core of the system relies on a set of rules, which are presumably hand-crafted, but the paper provides almost no details about them. To properly understand the contribution, the authors should provide examples of these rules, specify the total number of rules, and describe their complexity and interaction (e.g., are they ordered, and could one rule prevent another from being applied?). Instead of providing these crucial details, a significant portion of the paper is devoted to re-evaluating the systems from the CoNLL-2014 shared task. Much of this space, including the full-page Table 6, simply presents results, and the accompanying text largely repeats figures already available in the table.

The evaluation of the proposed error annotation system itself is not convincing and suffers from several methodological flaws.

First, the evaluation relies on human raters judging the system's output rather than having them independently annotate a gold standard. For a small test set of 200 sentences, creating a gold standard with a fixed set of tags should have been feasible. The chosen approach of rating pre-annotated data is known to inflate agreement scores and can yield a more favorable impression of system performance than a comparison against a true gold standard would (cf. Marcus et al., 1993).

Second, the reporting of rater agreement is misleading. The paper states that "all 5 raters individually considered at least 95% of our rule-based error types to be either 'Good' or 'Acceptable'". It is not standard practice to average individual scores this way, as this method obscures the distribution of disagreements. For instance, if the 5% of "bad" scores from each of the five raters were assigned to distinct edits, then as many as 18.5% of the edits could have been considered "bad" by at least one annotator. This is a significantly more concerning figure than the reported 3.7% average.

Third, the paper provides no information about the test data itself, such as the distribution of error categories it contains or which of these categories were covered by the annotations rated as "good".

Fourth, the paper mentions that "edit boundaries might be unusual" but fails to explain what this means. A more precise description with examples is needed, along with a discussion of whether this could be problematic for the system's intended applications.

The authors claim their system is less domain-dependent than machine learning approaches that require training data. I am not convinced this is the case. Rule-based systems have their own dependencies; for example, the Hunspell dictionary is unlikely to have uniform coverage across all domains, and manually-crafted rules can also encode domain-specific assumptions. Furthermore, such rules are entirely language-dependent, which is a clear drawback compared to data-driven approaches. The claim of domain independence is further undermined by the fact that all test data used (FCE-test, CoNLL-2014) comes from a single, narrow domain: student essays.

The motivation for designing a new set of error categories is also unclear. The paper's only justification is to allow for easy searching of general categories (e.g., "NOUN"). However, other existing tagsets, such as the one in Nicholls (2003), already support this functionality. It is also unclear why the CoNLL-2014 tagset was not used, as this would have enabled a direct evaluation against the CoNLL gold standard.

In summary, the main contribution of the paper is difficult to identify. Is it a new system? If so, the core technical details are missing. Is it a new set of error categories? If so, its motivation and advantages are not discussed. Is it a new evaluation of the CoNLL-2014 systems? If so, the analysis is superficial. The paper touches on several interesting topics but fails to make a clear, well-supported contribution in any of them.
