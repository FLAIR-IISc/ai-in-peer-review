This paper presents a system for automatically annotating the output of Grammatical Error Correction (GEC) systems with specific error types. The goal is valuable, as such information can provide useful feedback to language learners and enable more fine-grained, diagnostic evaluations of GEC systems. However, the paper in its current form has major weaknesses, including a superficial description of the core system and a seriously flawed evaluation methodology. These issues prevent a clear understanding of the paper's contribution and undermine the validity of its conclusions.

### Strengths

1.  **Useful Application:** The proposed tool addresses a clear need in the GEC community. Both language learners and teachers can benefit from explicit feedback on the types of errors being made, which most GEC systems do not provide.
2.  **Enables Finer-Grained Analysis:** By tagging edits with error types, the system allows for a more detailed comparison of GEC systems beyond aggregate metrics like F1-score. This facilitates analysis of system strengths and weaknesses with respect to specific grammatical phenomena.

### Weaknesses

The paper suffers from significant weaknesses in three main areas: the description of the system, the evaluation methodology, and the overall motivation and focus.

**1. Superficial System Description**

The core of the proposed system is a set of rules, but the paper provides almost no detail about them. For the work to be understood and reproducible, this section needs to be substantially expanded. The authors should address the following:

*   **Rule Creation:** Were the rules created manually or semi-automatically? What resources or linguistic expertise informed their development?
*   **Rule Examples:** The paper should include several concrete examples of rules to illustrate their format, complexity, and linguistic basis.
*   **Rule Set Details:** What is the total number of rules? How are they structured? How are they ordered or prioritized, and could the application of one rule block another?
*   **Paper Organization:** Instead of providing these crucial details, the paper dedicates a significant amount of space (including the full-page Table 6) to re-evaluating the CoNLL-2014 shared task systems. Much of the text in this section merely repeats figures from the table, space that would be better used to describe the authors' own system.

**2. Flawed Evaluation Methodology**

The evaluation of the proposed error annotation system is not satisfying and has several methodological flaws that call the results into question.

*   **Lack of an Independent Gold Standard:** The annotators were asked to rate the system's output as "Good," "Acceptable," or "Bad" rather than independently annotating the test data from scratch. It is well-established that reviewing pre-annotated data leads to significantly higher agreement and approval rates compared to independent annotation (e.g., Marcus et al., 1993). A more rigorous evaluation would require creating a gold standard for the 200 test sentences and comparing the system's output against it.
*   **Incorrect Reporting of Rater Agreement:** The paper states that "all 5 raters individually considered at least 95% of our rule-based error types to be either 'Good' or 'Acceptable'," and then averages the percentage of "Bad" ratings (3.7%). This is statistically unsound and not standard practice for reporting inter-annotator agreement. It obscures the actual level of disagreement. For example, if each of the five annotators assigned their ~4% "Bad" ratings to entirely different edits, up to 20% of the edits could have been considered "Bad" by at least one annotator. Standard metrics like Fleiss' Kappa should be used to properly measure inter-annotator agreement.
*   **Insufficient Detail on Test Data:** The paper provides no information about the distribution of error categories within the 200-sentence test set. It is unclear which error types were actually tested and how comprehensive the evaluation was.
*   **Vague Terminology:** The paper mentions that some "edit boundaries might be unusual" but fails to define this term precisely or provide examples. It is unclear whether this is a minor issue or a systematic problem that could affect the system's utility.

**3. Unclear Motivation and Questionable Claims**

*   **Motivation for a New Tagset:** The paper does not adequately justify the need for a new set of error categories. Why were existing tagsets, such as the one from Nicholls (2003) or the one used in the CoNLL-2014 shared task, not sufficient? Using the CoNLL-2014 tagset, for example, would have enabled a direct and automatic evaluation against the widely-used CoNLL gold standard, making the evaluation much stronger.
*   **Domain Dependence Claim:** The authors claim their system is less domain-dependent than machine learning-based systems because it does not require training data. This claim is questionable. The system relies on tools like Hunspell, whose dictionary coverage can be domain-specific. Furthermore, manually-crafted rules are often implicitly tuned to the data the developer has in mind and are, by definition, language-dependent, a clear drawback compared to data-driven approaches. The evaluation is also confined to a single domain (learner essays), which does not support the broad claim of domain independence.

### Conclusion

In its current state, the paper's primary contribution is unclear. If the contribution is the new system, it is too poorly described to be of value. If it is the new error tagset, it is not sufficiently motivated or discussed. If it is the re-evaluation of the CoNLL-2014 systems, the analysis is superficial. While the problem the paper addresses is important, the execution is lacking in rigor and detail. I recommend rejecting the paper in its current form but would encourage the authors to significantly revise it by focusing on a thorough description and a methodologically sound evaluation of their system.
