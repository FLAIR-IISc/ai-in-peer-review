### **Summary**

This paper presents a method for automatically annotating the output of Grammatical Error Correction (GEC) systems with error type information. The proposed two-step approach first uses a linguistically-enhanced alignment algorithm to extract edits between original and corrected sentences. It then classifies these edits using a new, dataset-independent, rule-based classifier. The authors evaluate their classifier via a small-scale manual rating study and demonstrate its utility by applying it to the system outputs from the CoNLL-2014 shared task, providing the first detailed, error-type-specific analysis of system performance.

### **Main Contributions**

1.  **A Tool for Fine-Grained GEC Evaluation:** The primary contribution is a method and tool for automatically annotating GEC system output with error types. This enables a more detailed analysis of system strengths and weaknesses beyond a single aggregate score.
2.  **A Detailed Analysis of CoNLL-2014 Systems:** The paper provides an extensive, error-by-error breakdown of the performance of all systems that participated in the CoNLL-2014 shared task, offering new insights into the capabilities of different GEC architectures.

### **Strengths**

1.  **High Practical Utility:** The proposed tool addresses a clear need in the GEC community. Automatically identifying error types in system output is valuable for both system developers, who can perform more fine-grained error analysis, and for end-users such as language learners and teachers, who can benefit from explicit feedback on the types of errors being made and corrected.
2.  **Enables Deeper System Comparison:** The work successfully demonstrates that an aggregate F-score can hide important differences between systems. By providing per-category precision and recall, the authors facilitate a much richer comparison, revealing, for example, that one system may excel at correcting determiner errors while another is better at verb tense. This level of detail is crucial for understanding the current state of GEC and for designing better hybrid systems.

### **Weaknesses**

1.  **Lack of System Details:** The description of the proposed system is highly superficial. The core of the method is a rule-based classifier, yet the paper provides almost no details about these rules. Are they manually created? The authors should provide examples of the rules, state how many rules there are, discuss their complexity, and explain how they are ordered or prioritized (e.g., could an early rule prevent the application of a later one?). Instead of presenting these crucial details, the paper devotes a significant amount of space (including an entire page for Table 6) to the evaluation of the CoNLL-2014 systems, repeating many facts that are already present in the table.
2.  **Unconvincing Evaluation Methodology:** The evaluation of the rule-based classifier is unconvincing for several reasons:
    *   **Rating vs. Independent Annotation:** Instead of having human experts rate the system's output, a stronger evaluation would have involved asking them to independently annotate a gold standard for the 200 test edits. The chosen methodology of rating pre-annotated data is known to produce inflated agreement scores compared to annotating from scratch (cf. Marcus et al., 1993). Creating a gold standard for 200 items with a fixed tagset should be feasible and would provide a much more reliable benchmark.
    *   **Misleading Presentation of Agreement:** The presentation of the human evaluation results is potentially misleading. Reporting that "all 5 raters individually considered at least 95% of our rule-based error types to be either 'Good' or 'Acceptable'" obscures the actual level of disagreement. A worst-case scenario, consistent with the reported data, is that 18.5% of the edits (5 raters × ~3.7% bad ratings) were considered "Bad" by at least one annotator. This paints a much less favorable picture than the reported average of 3.7%.
    *   **Insufficient Detail on Test Data:** The paper provides no information about the test set used for the human evaluation. For instance, what is the distribution of error categories in the 200-item sample? Without this, it is impossible to know which error types were actually covered in the evaluation.
3.  **Unsupported Claims of Dataset Independence:** The authors claim their system is "entirely dataset independent," but this claim is questionable. The system relies on several components, such as a Hunspell dictionary, whose vocabulary coverage may vary across domains. Furthermore, manually created rules are often implicitly tuned to the data the developer has in mind and are completely language-dependent, which is a significant drawback compared to machine learning approaches that can be retrained for new languages. The evaluation is also conducted only on student essays (FCE and CoNLL-2014), a single domain.
4.  **Unclear Motivation for New Error Schema:** The paper introduces a new set of error categories without sufficient justification. Why not use an existing, well-established schema, such as the one from the CoNLL-2014 shared task itself? Using the CoNLL-2014 tagset would have allowed for a direct comparison against the official gold standard annotations, which would be a much more powerful evaluation. The paper's overall focus is also muddled: it is not clear whether the main contribution is the new system (which is poorly described), the new error schema (which is poorly motivated), or the analysis of the CoNLL systems (which is extensive but superficial).

### **Questions to Authors**

1.  Could you provide more detail on your rule-based classifier? Specifically, could you show some examples of the rules, state approximately how many rules there are, and explain how conflicts between rules are resolved?
2.  What was the motivation for designing a new error tagset instead of using the existing CoNLL-2014 tagset? Using the latter would have enabled a direct evaluation against the gold-standard reference, avoiding the need to re-process the reference file and conduct a separate, small-scale human rating study.
3.  You mention that "edit boundaries had been determined automatically, and hence might be unusual." Could you please elaborate on what makes them "unusual," provide some examples, and discuss whether this could be problematic for the practical application of your system?
