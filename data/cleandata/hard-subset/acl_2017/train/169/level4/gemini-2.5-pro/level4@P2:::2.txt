### **Summary and Contributions**

This paper introduces a novel, two-stage framework for the automatic annotation and evaluation of Grammatical Error Correction (GEC) systems. The method first uses a linguistically-enhanced alignment algorithm to extract edits between original and corrected sentences. It then classifies these edits using a new, dataset-independent, rule-based classifier. This allows for the first time a detailed, type-based evaluation of GEC system outputs in terms of both precision and recall, which was not previously possible as system outputs are typically unannotated. The utility of the framework is demonstrated through a comprehensive analysis of the systems submitted to the CoNLL-2014 shared task.

In my view, the main contributions are:

1.  **A Novel Framework for Fine-Grained GEC Evaluation:** The primary contribution is the complete, end-to-end methodology for automatically annotating GEC system outputs. By making it possible to calculate precision scores for specific error types, this work provides the GEC community with a powerful tool for moving beyond monolithic F-scores and toward a deeper, more nuanced understanding of system behavior. The public release of the tool is a significant asset.

2.  **A Comprehensive Analysis of CoNLL-2014 Systems:** The paper applies the proposed framework to conduct a detailed breakdown of the performance of all systems from the CoNLL-2014 shared task. This empirical analysis is a valuable contribution in its own right, revealing specific strengths and weaknesses of different GEC architectures (e.g., MT-based, classifier-based) on a wide range of error types.

### **Strengths**

1.  **Enables Deeper System Analysis:** The proposed evaluation paradigm is a crucial step forward for GEC research. It allows for a much more insightful analysis of system performance than was previously possible. By breaking down performance by error type (e.g., Determiners, Verb Tense), edit operation (Missing, Replacement, Unnecessary), and even edit length (multi-token errors), the framework equips researchers to identify specific system failures, diagnose problems, and guide future development. The ability to distinguish between detection and correction performance is another valuable feature.

2.  **Methodological Soundness and Reusability:** The rule-based, dataset-agnostic nature of the error type classifier is a significant advantage. Unlike machine learning approaches, it does not require labeled training data and is not biased towards a particular annotation scheme. This makes the approach highly transparent (in principle), deterministic, and easily applicable to new system outputs without retraining.

3.  **Positive Human Evaluation Results:** The manual evaluation of the error classifier demonstrates its effectiveness. The finding that human experts rated at least 95% of the automatically assigned error types as "Good" or "Acceptable" lends strong support to the validity of the rule-based approach, even when operating on automatically extracted (and thus potentially noisy) edit boundaries.

### **Weaknesses**

1.  **Lack of Transparency in the Classification Rules:** The paper's central component is the rule-based classifier, yet the rules themselves are not described. The paper provides the error categories but offers no insight into the logic used to assign an edit to a category. Without examples or a high-level description of the rules' complexity and structure, it is impossible for the reader to assess their linguistic validity, understand the classifier's inherent biases or limitations, or replicate the work. This opacity undermines a key claimed advantage of the rule-based approach over "black box" machine learning models.

2.  **Unclear Generalizability and Portability to Other Languages:** The evaluation is conducted exclusively on English. The paper does not discuss the effort that would be required to adapt the framework to other languages. The pre-processing pipeline (POS tagging, parsing) is clearly language-dependent, but more importantly, the classification rules are likely tailored to English syntax and morphology. It is unclear how the system would handle phenomena not present in English, such as the discontinuous edits common in German (e.g., with separable verb prefixes). A discussion of the language-specific components and potential porting challenges is necessary to assess the broader applicability of this work.

3.  **Insufficient Analysis in Classifier and Reference Evaluations:**
    *   **Classifier Evaluation:** The error analysis for the classifier is superficial. The paper states that "Bad" ratings were often due to POS tagging errors but fails to explore this further. Which error types are most susceptible to such upstream errors? What other failure modes exist? This analysis is crucial for understanding the classifier's limitations and outlining paths for future improvement. Furthermore, the human evaluation setup may have introduced bias; asking raters to judge categories for noisy, pre-determined spans could make them more lenient.
    *   **Reference Comparison:** The claim that the "auto annotations are qualitatively as good as human annotations" seems overstated. The significance testing was performed on F0.5 scores, which can obscure significant differences in precision and recall. It is also unclear whether this comparison only evaluated edit boundaries or if it also accounted for the different error classification schemes between the auto and gold references.

4.  **Limited Interpretation of CoNLL-2014 Results:** The analysis of the CoNLL-2014 systems, while extensive, could be more insightful. The paper often presents results without connecting them to the known design of the systems. For instance, it expresses surprise that five teams failed to correct unnecessary token errors, when for some of these systems, this is an expected outcome of their architecture. Similarly, reporting a zero score for a system on an error type (e.g., CUUI on Word Order) is misleading if that system was never designed to address it. This points to a potential limitation in the evaluation: the inability to distinguish between a system *failing* at a task versus *not attempting* it.

### **Questions to Authors**

1.  Could you please provide a few representative examples of the rules used by your classifier? A high-level description of the rule formalism, their general structure, and their approximate number would greatly improve the paper's transparency and replicability.

2.  What is your assessment of the effort required to adapt this framework to another language with different morphological or syntactic properties (e.g., German, Russian)? Which components are language-dependent, and how might the rules need to be changed to accommodate phenomena like discontinuous edits?

3.  In your analysis of the CoNLL-2014 systems (Table 6), when a system scores zero on a particular error type, can your framework distinguish between a system that was not designed to handle that error and one that attempted corrections but failed completely (i.e., had 0 TPs)? This distinction seems critical for a fair interpretation of system capabilities.
