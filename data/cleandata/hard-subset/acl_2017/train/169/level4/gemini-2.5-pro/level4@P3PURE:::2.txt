The paper presents a novel approach for evaluating grammatical error correction (GEC) systems. The proposed method makes it possible to assess system performance by error type in terms of both precision and recall. This is a significant advance, as computing precision by error type was previously not feasible for most systems, which do not produce output annotated with error categories.

**Strengths:**
- The proposed evaluation framework is an important contribution towards a more fine-grained analysis of GEC system behavior.
- The paper provides a broad evaluation across a variety of existing GEC systems.
- The approach has several advantages over previous work:
  - It enables the computation of precision by error type.
  - It is independent of manual error annotation in system outputs.
  - It can assess performance on multi-token errors.
- A human evaluation shows that the automatically assigned error tags for pre-computed edit spans are largely considered appropriate by experts.

**Weaknesses:**
- A key component of the method—the rules used to derive error types—is not described in the paper.
- The classifier evaluation lacks a thorough error analysis. Consequently, the paper offers limited direction for future work on improving the classifier.
- The evaluation is confined to English, and the paper does not discuss the potential difficulty of adapting the approach to other languages.

### Classifier and Classifier Evaluation

The basis for the chosen error categories is unclear; the authors should clarify if this taxonomy is based on previous work.

While the authors claim the overall approach is independent of the alignment algorithm, the classification rules themselves are likely dependent on the aligner's output. The paper provides no details on this dependency. Given that the error classification rules are a central contribution, the reader should be given at least some examples of what these rules look like.

Unfortunately, the proposed evaluation is only applied to English. The paper does not elaborate on what changes would be necessary to apply the classifier to other languages. It is reasonable to assume that the rules for determining both edit boundaries and error tags are language-dependent and would require adaptation, as might the set of error categories itself. Without any details on the rules (e.g., how many exist per error type, their complexity), it is impossible for the reader to estimate the effort required to adapt this approach to another language.

The error spans computed in the pre-processing step appear to be inherently continuous, similar to the M2 scorer. This is potentially problematic for errors that involve discontinuous constituents. For example, in German, verbs with separable prefixes can create such edits (e.g., `[1st constituent] [verb] [other constituents] [verb prefix]`). It is unclear whether the proposed classifier can handle such discontinuous edit spans.

The authors state that human judges rated at least 95% of the automatically assigned tags as appropriate, "despite the degree of noise introduced by automatic edit extraction." I would interpret this result with more caution. Raters might be more forgiving when judging tags for edits with noisy boundaries. Furthermore, since they were shown the system's proposed tag rather than asked to select one from scratch, they may have been biased towards accepting the system's choice. The binary rating scale ('Bad' vs. 'Appropriate') may also have pushed raters to select 'Appropriate' in ambiguous cases. To make this evaluation more sound, the authors should also report on human judgments of the classifier's output on manually created gold-standard boundaries, which would isolate the classifier's performance from the noise introduced by the automatic edit extraction.

The paper lacks a thorough error analysis of the classifier itself. Beyond mentioning that "Bad" ratings often trace back to incorrect POS tags, the paper does not analyze the conditions under which the classifier fails. An analysis of when raters chose "Bad" versus "Appropriate" could provide valuable insights and suggest concrete directions for improving the classifier.

### Gold Reference vs. Auto Reference

It is unclear on what data the significance tests were performed. If they were performed on F0.5 scores, this may be problematic, as F0.5 is a derived measure with potentially weak discriminative power (i.e., systems with very different precision and recall profiles can achieve the same F0.5 score). Additionally, the beginning of Section 4.1 mentions a mismatch between the automatic and gold references in terms of both alignment and classification. However, the subsequent comparison appears to focus only on edit boundaries, not on the classification labels themselves.

### Error Type Evaluation

The paper expresses surprise that five teams failed to correct any unnecessary token errors. However, for at least two of these systems, this is expected behavior. The UFC system, for example, is a rule-based approach operating on POS tags (Ng et al., 2014), which makes it difficult to identify superfluous words. Similarly, Rozovskaya & Roth (2016) explain why the AMU system performs poorly on this error type.

The analysis of the results in Table 6 does not consider whether the evaluated systems were designed to handle each specific error type. For some errors, like word order, the mapping between the gold standard and the automatic evaluation is straightforward. However, it is unclear whether some systems failed on certain error types or simply were not designed to correct them. For instance, the CUUI system is reported with 0.0 precision and recall for word order errors, but this system does not target word order corrections. This case (and likely others) suggests potential classification errors that are neither analyzed nor discussed.

For Table 6, please report the raw counts for TP, FP, and FN in an appendix. This would facilitate comparison using other metrics and would also clarify the number of instances on which the results for some system/error-type pairs are based, which may be very small.

Regarding the statement that "All but 2 teams (IITB and IPN) achieved the best score in at least 1 category, which suggests that different approaches to GEC complement different error types," it would be beneficial to note that this finding is in line with previous research.

The multi-token error analysis is useful, but the results require more interpretation. The paper should acknowledge that some systems may be inherently unable to correct such errors, and that none of the systems were trained on parallel corpora specifically designed to handle the types of non-fluent edits described by Sakaguchi et al. (2016).

### Other

- The authors should acknowledge that for some GEC systems, particularly those with a modular architecture, it was already possible to provide error-type annotations by tracking which submodule was responsible for a given change. The primary advantage of the proposed approach is that it provides a unified framework for comparing all types of GEC systems, including those for which error-tagged output is not otherwise available.
- References: Several titles are missing capitalization. The URL for Sakaguchi et al. (2016) needs to be properly wrapped. Page information is missing for Efron and Tibshirani (1993).

### Author Response

I agree that the proposed approach is not "fatally flawed," and this review highlights its many positive aspects. The core idea is strong, but the paper in its current form is not ready for publication.

The error classification rules are the foundation of this work, and their omission is a major weakness. This is not a matter of simply adding a few examples; for the work to be replicable and adaptable, the rules themselves must be described in sufficient detail.

The potential for generalization to other languages cannot be an afterthought. It would be a serious limitation if the approach, by design, were restricted to English. Even without performing a full adaptation, the paper must be transparent enough to allow others to estimate the effort and potential success of porting it to a new language. Simply stating that most GEC research targets English does not excuse this omission; it reinforces the problem.

You suggest that the error types a system can handle are "usually obvious from the tables," but I disagree. As noted with the CUUI and unnecessary token examples, this is not always the case. For the five systems that fail to correct unnecessary tokens (Table 5), it is likely they were not designed to do so. This contradicts the paper's claim that "There is also no obvious explanation as to why these teams had difficulty with this error type."
