### **Summary and Contributions**

This paper introduces a novel, two-stage methodology for the automatic annotation and fine-grained evaluation of Grammatical Error Correction (GEC) systems. The proposed approach first uses a linguistically-enhanced alignment algorithm to extract edits between original and corrected sentences. It then classifies these edits using a new, rule-based framework that operates on automatic linguistic annotations (e.g., POS tags, lemmas). The primary value of this work is that it enables, for the first time, the calculation of precision and F-scores for specific error types across any GEC system, as system output does not need to be manually annotated. To demonstrate its utility, the authors apply their tool to re-evaluate the outputs of all systems from the CoNLL-2014 shared task, providing a detailed breakdown of system performance by error category.

In my view, the main contributions are:

1.  **A novel, dataset-agnostic framework for automatic error type annotation.** The key contribution is the rule-based classifier that enables detailed error analysis (including precision) for any GEC system's output. Its independence from manually annotated training data is a significant practical advantage over potential machine learning-based alternatives.
2.  **A detailed, fine-grained re-analysis of the CoNLL-2014 shared task.** This analysis provides valuable new insights into the comparative strengths and weaknesses of different GEC architectures (e.g., MT-based, classifier-based), revealing performance patterns that were previously obscured by a single aggregate score. This serves as a significant resource for the GEC community.

### **Strengths**

1.  **Enables Deeper, More Meaningful GEC System Analysis.** The primary strength of this work is its potential to move GEC evaluation beyond a single F-score. By providing a method to calculate precision, recall, and F-score on a per-error-type basis, it allows researchers to conduct much more granular diagnostics. This is crucial for understanding *why* a system performs well or poorly and for guiding future research (e.g., by combining systems with complementary strengths, as the authors suggest for CAMB and CUUI). The analysis of multi-token errors and the separation of detection versus correction performance further underscore the diagnostic power of this approach.

2.  **A Practical and High-Quality Annotation Approach.** The proposed rule-based classifier is dataset-independent and relies only on standard automatic preprocessing tools (POS tagging, parsing, etc.). This makes the framework highly practical and broadly applicable without requiring labeled training data for new annotation schemes or domains. The human evaluation provides strong evidence for the quality of the method, with experts rating over 95% of the automatically assigned error types as either "Good" or "Acceptable." This level of quality is impressive and validates the approach's utility.

3.  **Comprehensive and Insightful Empirical Demonstration.** The paper does an excellent job of demonstrating its value by applying the method to the CoNLL-2014 shared task. The results presented in Tables 5, 6, and 8 are insightful and provide the community with a new lens through which to view the performance of these widely-studied systems. For example, the finding that many systems completely fail to correct "Unnecessary" token errors, or that MT-based systems are superior at handling multi-token edits, are concrete and valuable takeaways.

### **Weaknesses**

1.  **The Core Method is Not Described.** The paper's central technical contribution—the rule-based classifier for error typing—is treated as a black box. The paper states that rules were written based on POS tags and other linguistic features, but provides no examples, no discussion of their complexity or logic, and no information about the rule set's size or development process. This lack of transparency is a major weakness, as it makes the work impossible to reproduce, critique, or extend. Without understanding the rules, a reader cannot gauge the method's limitations or the effort required to adapt it.

2.  **The Scope is Limited and Generalizability is Unclear.** The entire framework is developed and evaluated only for English. The paper does not discuss the challenges or requirements for porting the approach to other languages. This is a significant omission, as the reliance on a specific preprocessing pipeline (spaCy, NLTK) and a Penn Treebank-based tagset suggests that adaptation would be non-trivial. Furthermore, the underlying edit extraction method appears to assume continuous error spans. It is unclear how the system would handle discontinuous edits (e.g., separable verbs in German: "kam ... an"), which are common in many languages. This raises questions about the fundamental applicability of the approach beyond English.

3.  **Superficial Analysis of Both the Classifier and the CoNLL-2014 Results.**
    *   **Classifier Evaluation:** The human evaluation, while showing high agreement, lacks a deep error analysis. The paper cursorily attributes most "Bad" ratings to POS tagging errors but does not investigate further. What are the most difficult cases for the classifier? What are the sources of "Acceptable" vs. "Good" ratings? This analysis is needed to understand the current limitations and outline paths for future improvement.
    *   **CoNLL-2014 Analysis:** The discussion of the CoNLL-2014 results sometimes lacks depth. For instance, the authors express surprise that five teams failed to correct unnecessary token errors, but a deeper look at the original system papers would provide clear explanations (e.g., some systems were simply not designed to perform deletions). Similarly, presenting a zero score for a system on an error type it was not designed to correct (e.g., CUUI on word order) should prompt a discussion of whether the system failed or the tool potentially misclassified an edit, rather than just reporting the number. The analysis would be stronger if it integrated knowledge of the systems' designs.

### **Questions to Authors**

1.  Could you please provide a description or at least a few representative examples of the rules used for error classification? For instance, what logic distinguishes a `R:VERB:FORM` error from a `R:VERB:TENSE` error? This would address the major weakness regarding the method's transparency.

2.  What do you foresee as the main challenges in adapting this framework to another language with different morphological or syntactic properties, such as Russian or German? Specifically, can your edit extraction and classification approach handle discontinuous constituents?

3.  In Table 6, the CUUI system is reported with 0.0 for both precision and recall on Word Order (WO) errors. The original paper on CUUI states it does not target reordering errors. Does this zero-score simply reflect the system's design, or could it point to cases where your tool classified a complex, non-WO edit made by CUUI as a WO error, which then failed to match the reference?

4.  Regarding the significance testing in Section 4.1, could you clarify on what data the bootstrap resampling was performed? Was it on the final system-level F0.5 scores, or on a distribution of sentence-level or document-level scores?
