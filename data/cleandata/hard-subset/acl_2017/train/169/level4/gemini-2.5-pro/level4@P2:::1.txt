### Summary

This paper presents a method for automatically annotating the output of Grammatical Error Correction (GEC) systems with error types. The proposed approach is a two-stage process: first, it uses a linguistically-enhanced alignment algorithm to extract edits between original and corrected sentences. Second, it employs a novel rule-based classifier to assign an error type to each extracted edit. The authors demonstrate the utility of their tool by applying it to the system outputs from the CoNLL-2014 shared task, enabling a fine-grained, error-type-specific evaluation of GEC systems for the first time, including precision and recall scores for each error category.

### Contributions

1.  **A Detailed, Fine-Grained Analysis of GEC Systems:** The primary contribution is the application of the proposed tool to conduct a novel and insightful analysis of the CoNLL-2014 shared task systems. This analysis moves beyond a single aggregate score to reveal the specific strengths and weaknesses of different GEC architectures across a wide range of error types, which was not previously possible.

2.  **A Tool and Framework for Automatic Error Annotation:** The paper introduces a complete framework and a publicly available tool for annotating unannotated GEC output. This is a valuable resource for the community, facilitating more nuanced system comparisons and potentially providing more specific feedback for language learners.

### Strengths

1.  **Addresses a Clear Need in GEC Evaluation:** The work addresses a significant limitation in standard GEC evaluation, where error-type performance has traditionally been limited to recall. By enabling the calculation of precision and F-scores for specific error types, the proposed method allows for a much more thorough and informative comparison of GEC systems. The analysis in Section 4, which highlights how different systems excel at different categories (e.g., classifier-based systems for verb form errors vs. MT-based systems for multi-token edits), effectively demonstrates the value of this approach.

2.  **Provides Actionable Insights for Future GEC Research:** The detailed breakdown of system performance offers valuable insights that can guide future research. For example, the finding that many high-performing systems completely failed to address "Unnecessary" token errors, or that multi-token edits are handled poorly by most systems except those using MT, points to specific, actionable areas for improvement and suggests the potential benefits of hybrid systems.

3.  **Well-Motivated Approach to Standardizing Evaluation:** The paper correctly identifies that for a fair comparison, both the reference and hypothesis files must be processed in the same way. The decision to re-process the gold standard reference annotations using the same automatic tool (Section 4.1) is a methodologically sound way to ensure that the comparison is consistent, and the authors convincingly show that this does not significantly alter the overall system rankings.

### Weaknesses

1.  **Insufficient Detail on the Core Technical Method:** The paper's main technical novelty, the rule-based error classifier, is described too superficially. The core of the system is presented as a black box. The paper would be substantially stronger if it provided details on these rules. For instance: How many rules are there? How were they developed (e.g., manually crafted based on linguistic expertise or semi-automatically derived)? What is their general format or complexity? How is rule ordering or potential conflict handled? Much of the paper is dedicated to reporting the CoNLL-2014 results (with significant portions of the text simply restating figures from the large tables), and this space could have been better used to describe and justify the core methodology. Without these details, the work is difficult to assess and reproduce.

2.  **Methodological Concerns with the Classifier Evaluation:** The human evaluation of the classifier is not fully convincing.
    *   Raters were asked to judge the system's output ("Good", "Acceptable", "Bad") rather than performing independent annotation of the 200 test edits. This evaluation design is known to be susceptible to confirmation bias and tends to produce higher agreement scores than when annotators create a gold standard from scratch.
    *   The reporting of the results may be misleading. The paper states that "all 5 raters individually considered at least 95% of our rule-based error types to be either 'Good' or 'Acceptable'". However, if the edits rated as "Bad" by different annotators were not the same, the total percentage of edits considered "Bad" by *at least one* annotator could be as high as 18.5% (5 raters * ~3.7% avg bad score). The average "Bad" score of 3.7% obscures this potentially high level of disagreement. A more transparent metric would be the percentage of items that received no "Bad" ratings from any annotator.

3.  **Lack of Justification for a New Error Typology:** The paper introduces a new set of 25 error categories but provides little motivation for this choice over existing, well-established schemes. Specifically, it is unclear why the CoNLL-2014 error tagset was not used. Adopting the CoNLL-2014 tagset would have enabled a direct, quantitative evaluation of the classifier against the shared task's gold-standard annotations. Such an evaluation would be much more rigorous and persuasive than the small-scale human rating study presented.

4.  **Overstated Claim of "Dataset Independence":** The authors claim their system is "entirely dataset independent" as a key advantage over machine learning approaches. This claim is questionable. The system relies on several components—a POS tagger, lemmatizer, dependency parser, and a Hunspell dictionary—which are themselves trained on specific datasets and are known to have domain dependencies. Furthermore, manually created rules are often implicitly tuned to the phenomena observed in the data used for their development (in this case, likely learner English essays), and they are certainly language-dependent.

### Questions to Authors

1.  Could you please provide more detail about the rule-based classifier? Specifically, could you share some examples of the rules, state the approximate number of rules in the system, and explain the mechanism for resolving conflicts or defining precedence between rules?

2.  In your human evaluation, what percentage of the 200 edits were rated as "Bad" by at least one of the five raters?

3.  What was the rationale for creating a new error typology instead of adopting the existing CoNLL-2014 tagset? Using the CoNLL-2014 tagset would have allowed for a direct comparison against the gold-standard annotations, providing a more robust evaluation of your classifier's accuracy.

4.  The paper mentions that raters were told "edit boundaries had been determined automatically, and hence might be unusual." Could you please elaborate on what constitutes an "unusual" boundary and provide an example? How might such boundaries affect the utility and interpretation of the error analysis?

-   l. 319: The phrase "50-70 F1" is ambiguous. Please clarify if this means an F1-score between 0.50 and 0.70.
