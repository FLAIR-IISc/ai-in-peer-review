The paper presents a novel approach for evaluating Grammatical Error Correction (GEC) systems. The proposed method enables performance assessment by error type, calculating both precision and recall. This is a significant contribution, as calculating precision by error type was not previously possible for most systems, which do not produce outputs annotated with error categories.

**Strengths:**
- The proposed evaluation method is an important contribution to the analysis of GEC system behavior.
- The paper includes an evaluation of a variety of GEC systems.
- The approach has several advantages over previous work:
  - It enables the computation of precision by error type.
  - It is independent of manual error annotation on system outputs.
  - It can assess performance on multi-token errors.
- Human evaluation shows a high level of agreement with the automatically assigned error tags, even for automatically detected error spans.

**Weaknesses:**
- A key component of the method—the rules used to derive error types—is not described.
- The classifier evaluation lacks a thorough error analysis. Consequently, the paper offers little direction for future work on improving the classifier.
- The evaluation was performed only on English, and it is unclear how portable the approach is to other languages.

### Classifier and Classifier Evaluation

The basis for the chosen error categories is unclear. Are they based on previous work?

While the authors claim the overall approach is independent of the alignment algorithm, the classification rules themselves are likely not. The paper provides no details on this dependency. Given that the error classification rules are a central contribution, the reader should be given at least a glimpse of their structure and complexity.

Furthermore, the paper does not apply the proposed evaluation to languages other than English, nor does it elaborate on the changes required to do so. I assume that both the edit boundary detection and error tagging rules are language-dependent and would require adaptation, as would the error categories themselves. Without any details on the rules (e.g., their overall number, complexity, or structure), it is impossible to estimate the effort required to port this approach to another language.

The error spans appear to be inherently continuous, similar to the M2 scorer. This is problematic for errors that are discontinuous. For example, in German main clauses, verbs with separable prefixes are split (e.g., `[1st constituent] [verb] [other constituents] [verb prefix]`). It is unclear if the proposed classifier can handle such discontinuous edits.

The authors state that human judges rated at least 95% of the automatically assigned error tags as "Appropriate" "despite the degree of noise introduced by automatic edit extraction." I would be more cautious in interpreting this result. Raters may have been more forgiving due to the noisy edit boundaries. Furthermore, since they were shown the system's proposed tag rather than being asked to select one from scratch, they may have been biased toward accepting the system's output. The binary choice between "Bad (Not Appropriate)" and "Appropriate" may also have pushed raters to select "Appropriate" in borderline cases. To make this evaluation more sound, the authors should also report human judgments on classifier output for manually-created, gold-standard boundaries, thereby removing the noise from automatic edit detection.

The classifier evaluation is missing a thorough error analysis. The paper only mentions that "Bad" ratings often trace back to incorrect POS tags. A deeper analysis is needed: What are the common characteristics of edits rated "Bad" versus "Appropriate"? Does this analysis suggest specific avenues for improving the classifier?

### Gold Reference vs. Auto Reference

It is unclear what data the significance test was performed on. If the test was conducted on F0.5 scores, I would caution against this, as F0.5 is a derived metric with weak discriminative power (two systems can have very different precision and recall profiles but the same F0.5 score). Additionally, while the authors refer to the mismatch between the automatic and reference evaluations in terms of alignment and classification, the comparison between the gold and automatic evaluation setups appears to be based only on edit boundaries, not on the resulting error classifications.

### Error Type Evaluation

The finding that five teams failed to correct any unnecessary token errors is not surprising. For at least two systems, there is a straightforward architectural explanation. The UFC system's rule-based approach operates on POS tags, from which it is not possible to identify superfluous words. Similarly, Rozovskaya & Roth (2016) explain why the AMU system performs poorly on this error type.

The analysis of Table 6 fails to consider whether systems were designed to handle specific error types. For some errors, such as word order, there is a direct mapping between the gold and automatic evaluation. However, it remains unclear whether systems failed on certain error types or were simply not designed to correct them. For example, CUUI is reported with a precision and recall of 0.0 on word order errors, but this system does not target such errors. The CUUI case (and likely others) also suggests a potential classification error, which is neither analyzed nor discussed.

I recommend that the authors report the raw TP, FP, and FN counts in an appendix for Table 6. This would allow for comparison using other metrics and would also clarify the number of instances underlying the results for each system and error type, which appears to be very small in some cases.

You write, "All but 2 teams (IITB and IPN) achieved the best score in at least 1 category, which suggests that different approaches to GEC complement different error types." It would strengthen the paper to note that this finding is in line with previous research.

The multi-token error analysis is helpful, but the results require more interpretation. Some systems are likely architecturally unable to correct such errors, and none of the systems were trained on a parallel corpus of learner data with fluent corrections (in the sense of Sakaguchi et al., 2016).

### Other
- The authors should acknowledge that for certain GEC systems (e.g., modular systems with components for specific error types), it was already possible to provide error-annotated output, albeit with some adaptation. The primary benefit of the proposed approach is that it provides a unified framework for comparing all types of GEC systems, including those for which producing error-tagged output is not straightforward.
- References: Some titles lack capitalization. The URL for Sakaguchi et al. (2016) needs to be wrapped. Page information is missing for Efron and Tibshirani (1993).

### Author Response

I agree that the approach is not "fatally flawed," and my review highlights several positive aspects. The core idea is promising, but the paper in its current form is not ready for publication.

The error classification rules are the foundation of this work, and their absence from the paper is a major omission. This is not merely a matter of adding more examples. For the work to be replicable and adaptable, the rules must be described in sufficient detail.

Generalization to other languages cannot be an afterthought. If the approach is designed to work only for English, this is a serious limitation that must be stated. The paper should be transparent enough for a reader to estimate the effort required for adaptation. Simply noting that most GEC research targets English learners does not excuse this omission; it reinforces the problem.

You state that the error types a system can tackle are "usually obvious from the tables." I disagree that it is this simple. As mentioned in my review, five systems show zero performance on unnecessary token errors (Table 5). In the paper, you write, "There is also no obvious explanation as to why these teams had difficulty with this error type." However, for several of these systems, the reason is clear from their architecture: they were not designed to correct this error type. This highlights a gap in your analysis.
