This paper introduces an innovative methodology for the evaluation of Grammatical Error Correction (GEC) systems. The proposed method enables a fine-grained performance assessment, breaking down results by specific error types. Crucially, it facilitates the calculation of both precision and recall for each category—a significant advancement, as measuring precision by error type has been a long-standing challenge due to the lack of error annotations in system outputs.

Strengths:
 - The proposed evaluation framework is a significant contribution towards a more nuanced analysis of GEC system behavior.
 - The methodology is demonstrated on a diverse set of GEC systems.
 - The approach offers distinct benefits compared to existing evaluation techniques:
   - It enables the computation of precision on a per-error-type basis.
   - It operates without reliance on manually annotated error corpora.
   - It is capable of evaluating performance on multi-token errors.
 - Human evaluation confirms the high quality of the automatically assigned error categories, with experts largely validating the system's output.

Weaknesses:
 - A critical component of the methodology—the rule set used to classify error types—is not detailed in the paper.
 - The classifier evaluation is superficial; it is missing a comprehensive error analysis and, consequently, does not suggest clear avenues for future improvements to the classifier.
 - The evaluation is confined to English, and the paper fails to discuss the feasibility or complexity of adapting the approach to other languages.

### Classifier and Classifier Evaluation
====================================
The origins and theoretical underpinnings of the chosen error categories are not specified. It is unclear if they are derived from previous work in the field.

While the overall approach is presented as alignment-agnostic, the classification rules themselves are likely dependent on the specific aligner used, a detail the authors do not address. The error categories and the rules to assign them are a cornerstone of this work, and the reader should be provided with at least an example of what a classification rule looks like.

The lack of application to languages other than English is a notable limitation. The paper does not elaborate on the modifications required to apply the classifier to new languages. It is plausible that both the edit boundary detection rules and the error classification rules possess language-specific dependencies that would need adaptation. The error categories themselves might also require revision. Without any detail on the rules (e.g., their total number, complexity, or structure), it is impossible for a reader to gauge the effort required for such an adaptation.

The pre-computed error spans appear to be constrained to continuous sequences of tokens, similar to the M2 scorer. This is a potential issue, as some errors can only be accurately categorized with discontinuous spans. For instance, in German main clauses, verbs with separable prefixes are split (e.g., `[constituent] [verb] ... [prefix]`). It is uncertain whether the classifier can handle such discontinuous edits.

The authors state that human judges found at least 95% of the automated tags appropriate, "despite the degree of noise introduced by automatic edit extraction." This conclusion should be treated with caution. Raters might have been more lenient given the noisy boundaries. Furthermore, since they were presented with the system-generated tag rather than being asked to select one independently, they may have been biased towards accepting the system's suggestion, especially in ambiguous cases. The binary rating scale of "Bad (Not Appropriate)" versus "Appropriate" could also have influenced raters to choose "Appropriate" when a more nuanced option was not available. To strengthen this evaluation, the authors should also assess human ratings of the classifier's output on manually created, noise-free boundaries.

The classifier evaluation is missing a thorough error analysis. The paper only briefly mentions that "Bad" ratings often stem from incorrect POS tags. A deeper investigation is needed: What specific scenarios lead to "Bad" ratings versus "Appropriate" ones? Do these patterns reveal clear opportunities for improving the classifier?

### Gold Reference vs. Auto Reference
=================================
The data used for the significance test is not clearly specified. If the test was performed on F0.5 scores, this choice is questionable, as F0.5 is a derived metric with weak discriminative power (two systems can have identical F0.5 scores with vastly different precision and recall profiles). Furthermore, while the beginning of Section 4.1 discusses mismatches in both alignment and classification, the subsequent comparison between the gold and automatic references appears to focus solely on edit boundaries, not on classification accuracy.

### Error Type Evaluation
=====================
The finding that five teams failed to correct any unnecessary token errors is presented as surprising, but it is not. For several of these systems, this behavior is expected given their design. For example, the UFC system's rule-based approach operates on POS tags, which are insufficient for reliably identifying superfluous words. Similarly, Rozovskaya & Roth (2016) have previously explained why the AMU system performs poorly on this error type.

The paper does not analyze the results in Table 6 in the context of whether the systems were designed to handle specific error types. For certain categories, like word order, the mapping between the gold and auto-reference seems straightforward. However, it is unclear if systems performed poorly on certain error types or simply were not designed to address them. For instance, the CUUI system is reported with a precision and recall of 0.0 for word order errors, which it does not target. This particular case (and likely others) may also point to an error in the proposed classification system, which is neither analyzed nor discussed.

To facilitate more transparent and flexible comparisons, the appendix should include raw counts for True Positives, False Positives, True Negatives, and False Negatives for the results in Table 6. This would allow for comparison using alternative metrics and would also clarify the statistical power of the results, as some may be based on a very small number of instances.

When stating, "All but 2 teams (IITB and IPN) achieved the best score in at least 1 category, which suggests that different approaches to GEC complement different error types," it would be beneficial to note that this observation aligns with previous research findings.

The analysis of multi-token errors is valuable for future work, but the results require deeper interpretation. The paper should acknowledge that some systems may be inherently unable to correct such errors, and that none of the evaluated systems were trained on parallel corpora specifically designed with fluent corrections of learner errors (in the sense of Sakaguchi et al., 2016).

### Other
=====
- The paper should acknowledge that for certain GEC architectures, such as those with dedicated submodules for specific error types, providing error annotations was already possible. While this might require system-specific adaptations, the key contribution of the proposed approach is that it provides a unified framework for comparing all GEC systems, regardless of whether generating tagged output is straightforward for them.
- References: Several titles are missing proper capitalization. The URL for Sakaguchi et al. (2016) requires wrapping. Page numbers are missing for Efron and Tibshirani (1993).
