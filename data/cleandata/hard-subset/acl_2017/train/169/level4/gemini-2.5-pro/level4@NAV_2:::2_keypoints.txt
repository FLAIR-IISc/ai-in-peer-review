- Paper proposes a two-stage method: linguistically-enhanced alignment to extract edits, then a dataset-independent rule-based classifier to label error types for fine-grained GEC evaluation, demonstrated on CoNLL-2014 systems.

Contributions:
- Detailed re-evaluation of CoNLL-2014 systems revealing strengths and weaknesses across many error types.
- A portable, rule-based error-type annotation framework that avoids labeled training data and corpus-specific biases.
- A methodology enabling precision (and recall) calculation per error type by annotating system hypotheses.

Strengths:
- Moves beyond aggregate scores to provide a nuanced diagnostic tool for GEC system behavior.
- Dataset-independence and reliance on standard NLP preprocessing make the approach portable.
- Manual evaluation shows high annotation quality (95% rated Good or Acceptable).
- Enables novel analyses (precision per error type, multi-token edit evaluation) not possible before.

Weaknesses:
- The core error-classification rules are not described at all, preventing understanding or replication.
- No qualitative error analysis of classifier mistakes; limited discussion of "Bad" or "Acceptable" ratings and systematic weaknesses.
- Lack of discussion on adapting the framework to languages other than English and which components are language-dependent.

Questions for authors:
- Please provide representative examples of classification rules (e.g., for VERB:SVA, DET, WO) to illustrate logic and complexity.
- Have you evaluated the classifier on gold-standard edit boundaries to isolate alignment noise and measure an upper bound on rule accuracy?
- Clarify the bootstrap significance test: was it on F0.5 scores, and how should we interpret no difference between gold vs. automatic references given F-score limitations?
- Can you reframe analyses considering system architectures (e.g., systems that don't attempt certain corrections), and does the classifier distinguish between failing to correct vs. not attempting an error type?
