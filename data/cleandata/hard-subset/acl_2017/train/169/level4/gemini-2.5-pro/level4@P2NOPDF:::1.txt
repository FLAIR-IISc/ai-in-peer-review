**Summary**

This paper introduces a system for automatically annotating the output of Grammatical Error Correction (GEC) systems with specific error types. The proposed application is highly valuable, as it can provide useful, explicit feedback for language learners and educators, and also enables a more fine-grained, diagnostic comparison of different GEC systems. However, the paper suffers from two major shortcomings: a critically underspecified description of the system's core methodology and an evaluation that is insufficient and methodologically flawed. The paper presents a promising idea but lacks the technical depth and rigorous evaluation necessary for a scientific publication.

**Strengths**

*   **Useful Application:** The goal of providing explicit error types for GEC output is commendable. This information is valuable for pedagogical purposes (for both teachers and learners) and is a feature often missing in GEC systems that only provide a corrected sentence.
*   **Fine-Grained GEC Comparison:** The system facilitates a more nuanced comparison of GEC systems beyond simple precision and recall, allowing for performance analysis on specific error categories.

**Weaknesses and Detailed Comments**

The weaknesses of this paper are significant and concern the core scientific contributions.

1.  **Superficial System Description:** The paper's primary weakness is the almost complete lack of detail about the proposed system. The core of the system is described as a set of rules, but the reader is left with no concrete understanding of how it works. The authors should provide answers to fundamental questions:
    *   Were the rules created manually? If so, what was the development process?
    *   How many rules are in the system? What is their general complexity?
    *   Could the authors provide several illustrative examples of these rules?
    *   How are the rules ordered or prioritized? Is it possible for an early rule to incorrectly fire and block the application of a more appropriate, later rule?

    Instead of providing these essential details, the paper dedicates a disproportionate amount of space (including the full-page Table 6) to re-evaluating the CoNLL-2014 shared task systems. While this analysis is an application of the tool, it comes at the expense of describing the tool itself, which should be the central contribution of the paper.

2.  **Unsatisfying Evaluation Methodology:** The evaluation of the proposed system is not convincing for several reasons:
    *   **Lack of a True Gold Standard:** The annotators were asked to rate the system's pre-annotated output as "Good," "Acceptable," or "Bad." A much more rigorous approach would have been to have annotators independently annotate a gold standard for the 200 test sentences from scratch. The chosen methodology is known to significantly inflate agreement scores compared to annotation from a blank slate (cf. Marcus et al., 1993). Given the small test set, creating a proper gold standard would have been feasible and would have provided a far more reliable benchmark.
    *   **Misleading Reporting of Agreement:** The paper reports that "all 5 raters individually considered at least 95% of our rule-based error types to be either 'Good' or 'Acceptable'." Reporting averaged individual scores is not standard practice and can mask significant disagreement. A worst-case scenario is that the 5% of "Bad" ratings from each of the five annotators all applied to different edits. This would mean that up to 25% of edits were considered "Bad" by at least one annotator—a much less favorable result than the average of 3.7% "Bad" ratings implies. Standard inter-annotator agreement metrics like Fleiss' Kappa should have been calculated and reported.
    *   **No Information on Test Data:** The paper provides no characterization of the 200-sentence test set. What is the distribution of error categories within this data? Without this information, it is impossible to know which error types the system was actually tested on or to assess its coverage.
    *   **Vague Terminology:** The paper mentions that "edit boundaries might be unusual" but fails to explain what this means. Concrete examples are needed, along with a discussion of whether this phenomenon could be problematic for the system's intended applications.

3.  **Unsupported Claims and Unclear Motivation:**
    *   **Domain Dependence:** The authors claim their system is less domain-dependent than trained systems. This assertion is questionable. A system relying on resources like Hunspell's dictionary and manually-created rules can be highly dependent on both domain and language. Moreover, this claim is not supported by the evaluation, which uses data from only one domain: student essays (FCE and CoNLL-2014).
    *   **Justification for New Error Tagset:** The motivation for designing a new set of error categories is not adequately explained. The paper mentions the ability to search for underspecified categories (e.g., "NOUN"), but it is unclear why existing, well-established tagsets like that of Nicholls (2003) would be insufficient. A more obvious choice would have been to use the CoNLL-2014 tagset, which would have enabled a direct and automatic evaluation against the CoNLL gold standard data.

**Conclusion**

In its current form, the paper's central contribution is unclear. If the contribution is the new system, it is not described in sufficient detail. If it is the new set of error categories, it is not motivated or compared to existing standards. If it is the evaluation of the CoNLL-2014 systems, the analysis remains superficial. While the idea is promising, the paper fails to deliver a substantial, well-described, and rigorously evaluated contribution.
