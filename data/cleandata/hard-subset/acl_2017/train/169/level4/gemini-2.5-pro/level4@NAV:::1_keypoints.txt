- Strength: The application has clear practical utility for language instructors and learners and enables finer-grained, error-specific comparison of GEC systems.  
- Weakness: The system description is critically underspecified; key rule details (examples, count, complexity, ordering, interactions) are missing.  
- The paper devotes excessive space to re-evaluating CoNLL-2014 systems, repeating table information rather than explaining the method.  
- Evaluation protocol is flawed: annotators rated system output instead of creating an independent gold standard, which can bias results.  
- Rater-agreement reporting is unconventional and misleading—averaging individual rater scores can hide substantial disagreement.  
- No information is provided about test data composition (error-category distribution or which categories the 200 sentences cover).  
- The issue of “unusual edit boundaries” is mentioned but not explained or exemplified, and its impact is not analyzed.  
- The claim that the rule-based system is less domain-dependent than trained systems is unsubstantiated; rules and resources (e.g., Hunspell) can be domain- and language-dependent.  
- Evaluation is limited to a single domain (student essays), so domain-independence claims are unsupported.  
- The motivation for a new error-category set is weak; existing hierarchical tagsets (e.g., Nicholls 2003) or CoNLL-2014 tags would already support useful grouping and gold-standard comparison.  
- The paper’s primary contribution is unclear: system lacks technical detail, categories are not well motivated, and the analysis of CoNLL systems is superficial.  
- Minor but numerous typos and bibliography capitalization errors need correction (e.g., “c.f.” → “cf.”, M2 → M², clarify “50–70 F1”, capitalization of ESL/Fleiss).
