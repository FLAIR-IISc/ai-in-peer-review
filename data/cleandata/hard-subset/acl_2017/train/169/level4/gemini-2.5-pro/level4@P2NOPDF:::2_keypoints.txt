- Proposes a new GEC evaluation framework that enables error-type–level analysis including precision as well as recall.
- Demonstrates the method on diverse GEC systems from a shared task, showing practical utility.
- Framework is independent of manual annotation in system outputs and handles multi-token errors.
- Human evaluation indicates a high approval rate for automatically assigned error tags.

- The rules used to classify errors are not described, undermining reproducibility and extensibility.
- The classifier evaluation lacks detailed error analysis, offering limited guidance for improvements.
- The method is only evaluated on English and lacks discussion of adaptation effort for other languages.

- Paper does not justify choice of error categories or relate them to established taxonomies.
- Complete omission of classification rules is a major concern, since they likely depend on the aligner.
- No examples of rule-based assignments are provided, preventing replication and understanding.
- Language-specific dependencies (POS tagsets, edit extraction) are not addressed, leaving cross-lingual effort unclear.
- Reliance on continuous error spans may fail on discontinuous edits (e.g., separable-prefix verbs in German).
- The 95% "appropriate" tag claim may be inflated by noisy edit boundaries and a binary rating scheme.
- Human evaluation should use manually corrected boundaries to isolate classifier performance from alignment noise.
- Missing in-depth error analysis (e.g., types of errors causing "Bad" ratings and actionable fixes).

- Unclear which data/metric were used in significance tests; using F0.5 can mask precision/recall differences.
- Comparison of gold vs. automatic references appears focused on edit boundaries and omits label-level analysis.

- Surprising zero corrections on some error types may reflect system design (e.g., POS-based rule systems) rather than failure.
- Some reported failures (e.g., CUUI on word order) may indicate classification errors, not system incapacity.
- Recommend reporting raw TP/FP/FN counts for per-error-type tables to show underlying instance counts and robustness.
- Connect findings about system complementarity to prior work showing similar complementary strengths.
- Multi-token error analysis is useful but should note evaluated systems were not trained on corpora designed for long-range corrections.

- Claim that precision-by-error-type was previously impossible should be qualified; rule-based modular systems can report this.
- Paper needs reference formatting fixes (title capitalization, Sakaguchi URL wrapping, missing page info for Efron & Tibshirani).
