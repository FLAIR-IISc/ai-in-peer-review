- Strengths: The proposed application has clear practical utility for language instructors and learners, and it facilitates a more detailed, error-specific comparison of GEC systems.
- Weaknesses: The system description lacks sufficient detail, and the evaluation methodology has several significant shortcomings.
- General Discussion:
The paper introduces a method for automatically annotating the output of Grammatical Error Correction (GEC) systems with specific error categories. The motivation for this work is strong, as such annotations have clear practical value for language learners and instructors (since many GEC systems only provide a correction without error classification). This approach also enables finer-grained, error-specific analyses of GEC system performance, moving beyond global precision and recall metrics.

However, the description of the system itself is critically underspecified. The core of the method is a set of rules, which are presumably manually created, but the paper offers no insight into their nature. The authors should provide crucial details, such as examples of rules, the total number of rules, their general complexity, and their interaction (e.g., is there a specific order of application, and can one rule prevent another from firing?). Instead of providing these essential technical details, a significant portion of the paper is allocated to re-evaluating the systems from the CoNLL-2014 shared task. This includes a full-page table (Table 6) and accompanying text that largely repeats information readily available in the table.

The evaluation of the proposed system is unconvincing for several reasons.
First, the evaluation protocol is flawed. Instead of having annotators create an independent gold standard for the 200 test sentences, they were asked to rate the system's output. For a dataset of this size with a predefined tagset, creating a gold standard is feasible and would have been a more rigorous approach. The chosen method is known to inflate agreement scores by biasing annotators with pre-existing information (see Marcus et al., 1993).

Second, the method of reporting rater agreement is unconventional and potentially misleading. The paper states that "all 5 raters individually considered at least 95% of our rule-based error types to be either 'Good' or 'Acceptable'." Averaging individual ratings in this manner is not standard practice and can obscure significant disagreement. For instance, if the 5% of "bad" scores from each rater were assigned to different edits, it could imply that up to 18.5% of edits were considered "bad" by at least one annotator—a far less favorable result than the reported average of 3.7%.

Third, the paper provides no information about the test data itself, such as the distribution of error categories or which categories were actually covered by the 200 sentences that were evaluated.

Fourth, the mention of "unusual edit boundaries" is not explained. This point requires a more precise description with examples, along with a discussion of whether this could be problematic for the system's application.

The authors' claim that their rule-based system is less domain-dependent than trained systems is not well-supported. Manually-created rules can themselves be domain-specific, and components like Hunspell's dictionary may not have uniform coverage across different domains. Furthermore, rule-based systems are inherently language-dependent, which is a clear disadvantage compared to machine learning approaches. The evaluation itself is confined to a single domain (student essays), which does not substantiate the claim of domain independence.

The justification for creating a new set of error categories is weak. The stated reason—to allow for easy searching of general categories (e.g., "NOUN")—is a feature already supported by hierarchical tagsets like the one in Nicholls (2003). An alternative, such as using the CoNLL-2014 tagset, would have allowed for direct evaluation using the CoNLL gold standard.

In conclusion, the paper's primary contribution is unclear. If it is the system, key details are missing. If it is the new error categories, the motivation and design are not adequately discussed. If it is the evaluation of CoNLL-2014 systems, the analysis is superficial. The paper touches on all these aspects without offering sufficient depth in any of them.
