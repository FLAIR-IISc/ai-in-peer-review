This paper introduces a novel, automated method for evaluating Grammatical Error Correction (GEC) systems. The primary contribution is a framework that enables the assessment of GEC system performance by specific error types, calculating not only recall but also precision. This is a significant step forward, as previous methods generally could not compute precision by error type because system outputs are not typically annotated with such information.

**Strengths**

The proposed evaluation framework is a valuable contribution to the GEC field and represents an important step toward more fine-grained analysis of system behavior. The key advantages of this approach are threefold: it enables the calculation of precision by error type, it operates without requiring manual error annotations on system outputs, and it can handle multi-token errors. The authors demonstrate the utility of their method by applying it to a diverse set of GEC systems. Furthermore, the human evaluation, which found that experts approved of the vast majority of automatically assigned error tags, lends credibility to the core classification component.

**Weaknesses**

Despite its strengths, the paper suffers from several major weaknesses that hinder its impact and replicability. The most critical issue is the complete lack of description for the rules used to derive error types. Additionally, the evaluation of the classifier lacks a thorough error analysis, and the paper fails to discuss the method's applicability to languages other than English. Finally, the analysis of different GEC systems' performance by error type could be more nuanced.

**Detailed Comments**

**1. Lack of Detail on Error Classification Rules**

A critical omission is the lack of detail regarding the rule-based system for assigning error categories. The paper does not specify the basis on which the error categories were devised (e.g., are they based on previous work?) or provide any examples of the rules themselves. This is a major component of the paper's contribution, and its absence makes the work difficult to replicate or adapt. While the overall approach is presented as independent of the alignment algorithm, the rules are likely not. The paper should at least provide a glimpse into the structure of these rules, their complexity, and their potential dependence on the specific alignment algorithm used. Without this information, it is impossible for readers to assess the effort required to apply this method to new datasets or languages.

**2. Generalization to Other Languages**

The evaluation is confined to English, and the paper does not discuss the challenges or requirements for adapting the approach to other languages. It is likely that the rules for determining both edit boundaries and error tags are language-dependent and would require significant adaptation. For instance, the assumption of continuous error spans is problematic for languages like German with separable verb prefixes, which require discontinuous edits. The authors should provide enough detail about the current rule set for a reader to estimate the effort involved in such a port. Simply stating that most GEC research targets English does not excuse the lack of discussion on generalizability.

**3. Classifier Evaluation and Error Analysis**

The evaluation of the classifier could be more rigorous. The human validation of the automatically assigned tags is a good step, but its design may introduce bias. Raters might have been more lenient with noisy edit boundaries, and the binary choice between "Appropriate" and "Bad" could push borderline cases into the "Appropriate" category. A more robust evaluation would involve having judges assess the classifier's output on manually corrected boundaries to isolate classifier performance from noise introduced by automatic edit extraction.

Furthermore, the paper lacks a thorough error analysis of the classifier itself. Beyond mentioning that "Bad" ratings often stem from incorrect POS tags, the paper does not investigate the specific conditions under which the classifier fails. A deeper analysis could provide valuable insights into the classifier's limitations and suggest concrete directions for future improvement.

**4. Analysis of GEC System Performance**

The analysis of the GEC systems' performance by error type needs more depth and nuance. Firstly, the details of the significance testing are unclear. The paper should specify on which metric (e.g., F0.5 scores, precision, recall) the test was performed, as a derived measure like F0.5 can obscure important differences in precision and recall.

More importantly, the discussion of the results in Table 6 is sometimes superficial. For instance, the paper expresses surprise that several systems fail to correct any unnecessary token errors. However, for some of these systems, this is an expected outcome based on their design (e.g., the UFC system's reliance on POS tags, as noted by Ng et al., 2014). The analysis should account for the known capabilities and limitations of the systems being evaluated. The case of the CUUI system, which is reported with zero precision and recall for word order errors it was not designed to correct, points to a potential classification error in the proposed method itself, which is neither analyzed nor discussed.

**Suggestions for Improvement**

To improve the paper, the following points should also be addressed:

*   **Reporting:** It would be beneficial to report the raw counts (TP, FP, FN) for the results in Table 6, perhaps in an appendix. This would allow for more transparent comparison and re-calculation of metrics, and it would clarify the number of instances on which some results are based.
*   **Contextualizing Findings:** The paper correctly notes that different GEC approaches seem to complement each other, and it would strengthen the discussion to connect this finding to previous research that has suggested the same.
*   **Positioning vs. Prior Work:** The introduction should acknowledge that for some modular, rule-based systems, it was previously possible to get error-tagged outputs, even if not in a standardized, comparable way. This would help to more precisely position the contribution of providing a unified evaluation framework.
*   **Discontinuous Edits:** The paper should discuss how the proposed method handles (or could be extended to handle) discontinuous edits, which are common in many languages.
*   **References:** Several references require formatting corrections (e.g., missing capitalizations, page information, and proper URL wrapping).
