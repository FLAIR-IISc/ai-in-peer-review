**Summary of the paper**

This paper introduces a two-stage method for automatically annotating Grammatical Error Correction (GEC) system outputs with error type information, a task which has previously been hindered by the lack of annotations in system hypotheses. The proposed approach first employs a linguistically-enhanced alignment algorithm to extract edits between source and corrected sentences. Subsequently, a new dataset-independent, rule-based classifier assigns an error type to each extracted edit. To demonstrate the utility of this method, the authors apply their tool to the system outputs from the CoNLL-2014 shared task, conducting the first comprehensive, error-type-specific evaluation of GEC systems at this level of detail.

**Contributions**

1.  The primary contribution is the detailed, fine-grained analysis of the systems participating in the CoNLL-2014 GEC shared task. By automatically annotating system hypotheses, the authors provide a novel and insightful breakdown of performance across 25 distinct error categories, revealing specific strengths and weaknesses of different GEC architectures that are obscured by aggregate metrics.
2.  A secondary contribution is the development and release of the tool itself. This tool provides a practical solution for automatically annotating GEC data, which is valuable not only for researchers wishing to perform more detailed system comparisons but also for educators and language learners who can benefit from explicit feedback on error types.

**Strengths**

1.  **Addresses a Practical and Important Need:** The paper tackles a significant limitation in GEC evaluation. By enabling the automatic annotation of system output, it provides a means for end-users, such as language teachers and learners, to gain a clearer understanding of the errors being corrected. This is a highly useful application, as most GEC systems simply output corrected text without explanation.
2.  **Enables Fine-Grained System Comparison:** The proposed framework moves beyond single F-scores and allows for a more nuanced evaluation of GEC systems. The detailed breakdown of performance by error type (e.g., Tables 5, 6, and 7) offers valuable insights into the complementary strengths of various approaches (e.g., MT-based vs. classifier-based), which can guide future work in developing more effective hybrid systems.

**Weaknesses**

1.  **Superficial Description of the Core Method:** The paper's core technical contribution, the rule-based error classifier, is severely underspecified. The authors provide no concrete details about the rules, such as their quantity, complexity, structure, or the logic for their ordering and application. Without examples or a more thorough description, the classifier remains a black box, making the work difficult to scrutinize or reproduce. A significant portion of the paper is instead devoted to presenting evaluation results (e.g., the full-page Table 6), much of which restates figures from the table without providing deeper analysis.
2.  **Unconvincing Evaluation of the Classifier:** The manual evaluation of the proposed classifier has several methodological flaws that undermine its conclusions.
    *   The evaluation protocol, where raters judged the system's pre-annotated output rather than annotating a gold standard from scratch, is known to produce inflated agreement scores and is not standard practice. A more rigorous evaluation would compare the system's output to an independently created gold standard.
    *   The method of reporting rater agreement is misleading. Stating that each of the 5 raters found over 95% of annotations "Good" or "Acceptable" obscures the overall disagreement. If the "Bad" ratings were distributed across different items, it is possible that up to 18.5% of the edits (5 * 3.7%) were deemed "Bad" by at least one expert, which paints a much less favorable picture of the classifier's quality.
    *   Key details about the 200-item test set are missing, such as the distribution of error categories it contains. This makes it impossible to know whether the evaluation covers a representative range of phenomena.
3.  **Ambiguous Motivation and Contribution:** The paper's central goal is unclear.
    *   If the contribution is the new rule-based system, it is not described in sufficient detail.
    *   If the contribution is the new set of error categories, its creation is not motivated. The paper fails to explain why existing, well-established tagsets (like the one from the CoNLL-2014 shared task itself) were not used. Adopting the official CoNLL-2014 tagset would have enabled a direct, quantitative evaluation against the task's gold-standard data.
    *   If the contribution is the re-evaluation of CoNLL-2014 systems, the analysis is largely a superficial presentation of results rather than a deep investigation into the underlying reasons for system performance differences.
4.  **Questionable Claim of Dataset Independence:** The authors claim the system is dataset-independent, which is debatable. The method relies on tools like Hunspell, whose dictionary coverage can be domain-specific. Furthermore, manually created rules are often implicitly tuned for the domain they were developed on (here, student essays) and are inherently language-dependent, which is a notable drawback compared to machine learning approaches that can be more easily adapted to new languages and domains with appropriate training data.

**Questions to Authors**

1.  Could you provide more technical details about the rule-based classifier? Specifically, could you show some representative examples of rules, state the total number of rules, and explain how their application is ordered to handle complex cases or resolve conflicts?
2.  The term "unusual" edit boundaries is mentioned in the evaluation section. Could you please provide examples of what constitutes an "unusual" boundary and discuss whether this phenomenon could negatively impact the utility of your tool for GEC evaluation?
3.  What was the rationale for creating a new error typology instead of adopting an existing scheme, particularly the one used in the CoNLL-2014 shared task? Using the official tagset would seem to offer a direct way to evaluate your classifier's performance against the gold standard annotations.
4.  Regarding the manual evaluation, why did you opt for having raters judge system output instead of the more standard practice of having them create an independent gold standard for comparison? This would have provided a more objective and convincing measure of your classifier's accuracy.
