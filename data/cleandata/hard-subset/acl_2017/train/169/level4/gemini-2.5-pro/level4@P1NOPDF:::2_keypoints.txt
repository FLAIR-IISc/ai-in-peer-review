- Proposes a novel method for evaluating GEC systems by error type, computing both precision and recall.  
- Enables computation of precision by error type even for systems that don't output error-category annotations.  
- Evaluation covers a variety of GEC systems.  
- Method is independent of manual error annotation on system outputs.  
- Method can assess multi-token errors.  
- Human evaluation shows high agreement with automatically assigned error tags.  

- The error-type derivation rules (central component) are not described.  
- The classifier evaluation lacks a thorough error analysis, offering little guidance for improvements.  
- Evaluation is performed only on English; portability to other languages is unclear.  
- Basis for chosen error categories is unclear and possibly not grounded in prior work.  
- The classification rules’ dependence on the alignment algorithm is not explained.  
- No details on rule count, complexity, or structure, making adaptation effort estimation impossible.  
- Potential inability to handle discontinuous edits (e.g., separable verb prefixes in German) is not addressed.  
- Human evaluation may be biased by showing raters system tags and using a binary “Appropriate/Bad” choice; gold-standard boundary judgments are needed.  
- Insufficient analysis of characteristics distinguishing edits rated “Bad” versus “Appropriate”; POS-tag errors are noted but not explored.  
- Unclear what data the statistical significance tests used and caution urged about relying on F0.5.  
- Comparison between gold and automatic evaluations seems limited to edit boundaries, not resulting classifications.  
- Paper fails to consider that some systems are architecturally unable or not designed to correct certain error types (e.g., unnecessary token, word order).  
- Possible classification errors (e.g., CUUI on word order) are neither analyzed nor discussed.  
- Recommend reporting raw TP/FP/FN counts (e.g., for Table 6) to enable alternative metrics and clarify instance counts.  
- The claim that different systems complement different error types should be linked to prior research.  
- Multi-token error analysis needs more interpretation; some systems may be incapable of correcting them and training data limitations exist.  
- Acknowledge that some modular systems can already produce error-annotated outputs; the main contribution is a unified evaluation framework.  
- Several reference and formatting issues need fixing (capitalization, URL wrapping, missing page info).  
- Reviewer concurs the idea is promising but the paper is not yet ready for publication.  
- The absence of detailed error classification rules is a major omission preventing replicability.  
- Generalization to non-English languages must be explicitly addressed or stated as a limitation.  
- The authors’ claim that error-targeting is “obvious from the tables” is disputed; architectural explanations account for some zero scores.
