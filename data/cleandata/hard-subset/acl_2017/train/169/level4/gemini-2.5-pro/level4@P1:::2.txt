This paper presents a novel, rule-based approach for the automatic annotation and evaluation of Grammatical Error Correction (GEC) systems. This enables a fine-grained evaluation of system performance by error type, calculating both precision and recall, which was not previously possible as system outputs are typically unannotated.

### Strengths
- The proposed evaluation method is a significant step forward for analyzing the behavior of GEC systems in detail.
- The paper provides a comprehensive analysis of various systems from the CoNLL-2014 shared task, demonstrating the utility of the approach.
- The approach offers several key advantages over previous work: it enables the calculation of precision by error type, it is independent of manual error annotations for system outputs, and it allows for a distinct evaluation of performance on multi-token errors.
- A manual evaluation shows that the automatically assigned error categories are judged as appropriate by human experts in the vast majority of cases.

### Weaknesses
- A major weakness is the lack of description for the rule-based classifier. The rules for assigning error types are a core component of the contribution, but they are not described in the paper.
- The evaluation of the classifier is superficial. It lacks a detailed error analysis, and consequently, the paper offers no discussion on potential avenues for improving the classifier.
- The approach is only evaluated on English, and the paper does not discuss the challenges or requirements for adapting it to other languages.

### Classifier and Classifier Evaluation

**Rules and Error Categories:** The paper does not state the basis on which the error categories were devised or whether they are based on established taxonomies from previous work. While the authors claim the overall approach is independent of the alignment algorithm, the classification rules themselves are likely coupled to the output of the alignment step. Since the error classification framework is a central contribution, the paper should provide a much clearer description of these rules, including examples, to allow the reader to understand their logic and complexity.

**Applicability to Other Languages:** The evaluation is confined to English. The paper should discuss what changes would be necessary to adapt the classifier to other languages. I assume the rules for both edit boundary detection and error tagging are language-dependent and would require adaptation. Without any detail on the number or complexity of the rules, it is impossible for the reader to estimate the effort required for such an adaptation.

**Discontinuous Edits:** The alignment method appears to produce only continuous edit spans, similar to the M2 scorer. This is a limitation, as some grammatical errors are inherently discontinuous. For example, in German, verbs with separable prefixes can create discontinuous dependencies. It is unclear if the proposed classifier can handle such cases.

**Manual Evaluation:** I am skeptical of the conclusion drawn from the manual evaluation. Raters were told that boundaries were noisy, which may have made them more forgiving in their judgments. Furthermore, the evaluation setup, where raters see the system's proposed tag, could introduce confirmation bias. To isolate the performance of the classifier from the alignment step, the authors should conduct a second evaluation where raters assess the classifier's output on gold-standard, manually created edit boundaries.

**Error Analysis:** The paper lacks a qualitative error analysis of the classifier's performance. The authors mention that "Bad" ratings often stem from POS tagging errors, but provide no further detail. What kinds of errors lead to "Acceptable" vs. "Good" ratings? A deeper analysis of these cases is needed to identify specific weaknesses and guide future improvements to the classifier.

### Gold Reference vs. Auto Reference

The description of the significance test is vague. On what metric were the tests performed? If they were run on the final F0.5 scores, this is not ideal, as F-scores can obscure significant differences in precision and recall. Furthermore, the comparison in this section seems to focus only on the effect of automatic versus manual edit boundaries; it's unclear if the effect of different classification schemes (manual vs. automatic) was also considered.

### Error Type Evaluation

**Analysis of System Failures:** The paper expresses surprise that five teams failed to correct any unnecessary token errors. This finding is less surprising when considering the architecture of these systems. For instance, the UFC system is rule-based and operates on POS tags, making it difficult to identify superfluous words. Similarly, as explained by Rozovskaya & Roth (2016), the AMU system's SMT architecture is not well-suited for this error type.

The analysis of Table 6 is missing a crucial dimension: whether systems were explicitly designed to handle certain error types. For many error types where a system scores 0, it is unclear if the system failed to correct them or was simply not designed to address them. For example, the CUUI system scores 0.0 on word order errors, which is expected as it does not target them. This also points to a potential misclassification by the proposed tool, as the CUUI system should not be generating word order corrections (and thus should have no FPs), yet the table shows P=0 and R=0. This discrepancy is not analyzed or discussed.

**Reporting and Interpretation:** For better transparency and to facilitate further analysis, the raw TP, FP, and FN counts for Table 6 should be provided in an appendix. This would also clarify the number of instances on which some of the scores are based, which appears to be very small in some cases.

The conclusion that "different approaches to GEC complement different error types" should acknowledge that this finding is consistent with previous research in the field. The multi-token error analysis is a valuable contribution, but the interpretation of the results could be deeper. The poor performance is likely due to inherent architectural limitations and the fact that these systems were not trained on corpora designed to promote fluency edits of the kind discussed by Sakaguchi et al. (2016).

### Other Comments
- The paper claims that detailed error analysis was previously impossible. This should be qualified. For modular GEC systems with specific components for different error types, such analysis is often possible. A key strength of the proposed approach, which should be emphasized, is that it provides a *unified framework* for comparing all types of GEC systems, including monolithic ones where such analysis is not straightforward.
- The bibliography requires formatting corrections (e.g., inconsistent capitalization in titles, missing page numbers for Efron and Tibshirani (1993)).
