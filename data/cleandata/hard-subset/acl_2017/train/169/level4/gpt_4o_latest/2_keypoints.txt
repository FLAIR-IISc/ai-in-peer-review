- Introduces an innovative framework to evaluate GEC systems by error type, measuring both precision and recall.  
- Framework can compute precision by error type, works without manual error annotations, and handles multi-token errors.  
- Evaluates a diverse set of GEC systems.  
- Human experts rated ~95% of automatic error tags as appropriate, but this result may be biased by noisy boundaries and lack of a middle rating option.  
- Critical rules for deriving error types are not described in sufficient detail, harming replicability.  
- Unclear whether error categories are derived from prior work or how they were chosen.  
- Classification rules may depend on alignment algorithm, but the paper gives insufficient detail or examples of these rules.  
- Classifier evaluation lacks detailed error analysis and actionable directions for improving the classifier.  
- Raters were not asked to tag errors independently of system outputs; evaluation should include manual boundaries to isolate classifier performance.  
- Paper does not analyze when or why raters chose "Bad" vs "Appropriate" or how expert feedback could guide improvements.  
- Evaluation is restricted to English and does not discuss adaptations needed for other languages or the complexity/number of rules required.  
- The method may struggle with discontinuous error spans (e.g., German separable verbs); classifier handling of such cases is unclear.  
- Significance testing procedure is unclear; relying on F0.5 is problematic due to its limited discriminative power.  
- Gold vs. auto-reference comparison appears to focus on boundaries only, not on classification differences.  
- Paper lacks analysis of whether systems were designed to handle specific error types (e.g., CUUI’s 0.0 for word order may reflect design, not failure).  
- Raw TP/FP/TN/FN counts should be provided (e.g., in an appendix) to allow alternative metric comparisons and reveal small-sample issues.  
- The finding that different systems excel at different error types aligns with prior research and should be noted.  
- Multi-token error analysis is useful but needs interpretation, since some systems may be incapable of correcting such errors or lack appropriate training data.  
- Authors should acknowledge that some systems could produce error annotations pre-existingly (via submodules), while the proposed approach enables unified comparison.  
- Minor issues: reference formatting/capitalization and missing page numbers/URL wrapping need correction.  
- Overall judgment: promising approach but not ready for publication due to insufficient rule detail and lack of clarity on cross-lingual generalizability and classifier error analysis.
