- Introduces a method to automatically annotate GEC outputs with error types, enabling more granular evaluation than overall scores.
- Core approach: linguistically-enhanced alignment to extract edits followed by a new rule-based error-type classifier.
- Demonstrated on CoNLL-2014 data and released the accompanying tool.
- Strength: dataset-independence via automatic POS tagging and linguistic features, avoiding labeled training data.
- Human evaluation reports high agreement (over 95% “Good” or “Acceptable”) with system error-type assignments.
- Weakness: description of the rule-based system is superficial—missing examples, rule count, complexity, ordering, and interaction details.
- Much of the paper redundantly restates CoNLL-2014 results instead of deeper methodological discussion.
- Evaluation methodology is questionable: uses human ratings of the system’s own output rather than an independently created gold standard.
- Averaging rater scores is problematic; reporting any-rater “Bad” rates (~18.5%) gives a starker view of errors than the reported averages.
- Lacks reporting on test data characteristics (e.g., distribution of error categories).
- The phrase “edit boundaries might be unusual” is unclear and its impact is not analyzed.
- Claim of reduced domain dependence is debatable due to reliance on resources like Hunspell and potentially domain-specific manual rules.
- Limited evaluation domain (student essays) raises concerns about generalizability.
- Motivation for a new error category scheme is weak; abandoning established tagsets (e.g., CoNLL-2014) hinders direct comparison to gold standards.
- Overall impact is reduced by superficial system description, questionable evaluation, and weak justification for the new tagset.
- Q1: Provide detailed examples of rules, number of rules, ordering, and rule interactions.
- Q2: Explain why evaluation used human ratings of system output instead of an independent gold-standard annotation.
- Q3: Provide a detailed analysis of cases rated “Bad” and how to address them.
- Q4: Report system performance across domains beyond student essays.
- Q5: Clarify limitations of the Hunspell dictionary for different error types and domains.
- Additional: Correct typos (e.g., “c.f.” → “cf.”, “M2” → “M^2”, clarify “50-70 F1”, fix casing like “ESL”).
- Additional: Add a brief explanation of Table 9 in the main text.
- Additional: Include an ablation study to quantify contributions of different linguistic features to classifier performance.
