The paper introduces a novel evaluation methodology for grammatical error correction (GEC) systems, enabling performance assessment by error type with both precision and recall – a capability previously limited by the lack of error category annotations in system outputs.

**Strengths:**

*   The proposed evaluation represents a significant advancement for analyzing GEC system behavior.
*   The paper provides a comprehensive evaluation across a variety of GEC systems.
*   The approach offers several advantages over existing methods, including its ability to compute precision by error type, its independence from manual error annotation, and its capacity to assess performance on multi-token errors.
*   Human experts largely concur with the automatically assigned error tags for pre-computed error spans.

**Weaknesses:**

*   The rules used to derive error types are not detailed within the paper.
*   The classifier evaluation lacks a thorough error analysis and consequently does not offer clear directions for future classifier improvements.
*   The evaluation is limited to English, and the challenges of adapting the approach to other languages are unclear.

**Classifier and Classifier Evaluation**

The rationale behind the error category design is not specified; it is unclear if these categories build upon prior work. While the approach is generally independent of the alignment algorithm, the rules likely are not, yet the authors omit crucial details regarding these rules. Given the centrality of error categories to the paper, a demonstration of a rule example is essential for reader comprehension.

The paper does not extend the proposed evaluation to languages beyond English, nor does it discuss the necessary modifications for applying the classifier to other languages.  Adaptations to edit boundary determination and error tag assignment, potentially dependent on language-specific features and pre-processing pipelines, are likely required.  Furthermore, the error categories themselves may necessitate revision.  The absence of detail regarding the complexity and number of rules used for error category assignment hinders estimation of the effort required for cross-lingual adaptation.

The inherent continuity of the pre-processing step’s error spans, mirroring the M2 scorer, presents a limitation. This is because accurately tagging errors sometimes requires discontinuous spans.  For example, in German, separable verb prefixes are separated; the ability of the classifier to handle such discontinuous edits is unknown.

The authors state that human judges deemed at least 95% of automatically assigned error tags as appropriate "despite the degree of noise introduced by automatic edit extraction." This claim warrants caution, as raters might exhibit leniency in the presence of noisy boundaries and may be biased by system output when assessing tag appropriateness. The lack of a rating option between "Bad" and "Appropriate" could also influence responses. A more robust evaluation would involve assessing human judgments on classifier output with manually created boundaries. 

The classifier evaluation lacks a comprehensive error analysis. While it is noted that “Bad” ratings often correlate with incorrect POS tags, further investigation is needed.  Questions to address include: under what circumstances did raters select “Bad” versus “Appropriate”? Do expert ratings provide insights for classifier improvement?

**Gold Reference vs. Auto Reference**

The specific data used for the significance test is unclear. Testing on F0.5 scores is questionable due to their limited discriminatory power as derived measures; recall and precision performance can vary significantly while yielding the same F0.5 score. The paper highlights misalignment between automatic and reference outputs in terms of alignment and classification, but the comparison between gold and reference standards appears limited to boundary discrepancies, not classification.

**Error Type Evaluation**

The observation that five teams failed to correct unnecessary token errors is unsurprising. Two of those systems (UFC and AMU) have known limitations that prevent them from addressing superfluous words; UFC operates solely on POS tags, while Rozovskaya & Roth (2016) explain AMU's poor performance in this area. 

The authors do not analyze or interpret the results in Table 6 with respect to system design capabilities.  For certain error types, such as word order errors, a clear correspondence exists between gold standard and auto reference. However, it remains unclear whether systems completely failed to address specific error types or were simply not designed to do so. The CUUI system’s precision and recall of 0.0, despite not targeting word order errors, suggests a classification error, which is not discussed.

Please include raw values for True Positives (TP), False Positives (FP), True Negatives (TN), and False Negatives (FN) in the appendix for Table 6 to facilitate comparison between systems using alternative metrics. Furthermore, indicate in the results which error types and systems are based on a limited number of instances.

The statement that “All but 2 teams (IITB and IPN) achieved the best score in at least 1 category, which suggests that different approaches to GEC complement different error types” aligns with existing research and should be acknowledged as such. 

The analysis of multi-token errors is valuable for future work, but requires further interpretation. Some systems may be inherently unable to correct these errors, particularly given that none were trained on parallel corpora of learner data and fluent corrections (Sakaguchi et al., 2016).

**Other**

*   The paper should acknowledge that error annotations are not impossible for all GEC approaches, particularly those with dedicated submodules for specific error types. Adapting such systems to output error-tagged results is feasible, and the proposed approach offers a unified framework for comparing systems with varying output capabilities.
*   References: Some titles are missing capitalization. The URL for Sakaguchi et al. (2016) requires formatting. Page information is absent for Efron and Tibshirani (1993).

**Author response**

I agree that your approach is not “fatally flawed” and I think this review actually points out quite some positive aspects. The approach is good, but the paper is not ready.

The basis for the paper are the rules for classifying errors and the lack of description is a major factor. This is not just a matter about additional examples. If the rules are not seen as a one-off implementation, they need to be described to be replicable or to adapt them.

Generalization to other languages should not be an afterthought. It would be serious limitation if the approach only worked on one language by design. Even if you don't perform an adaption for other languages, your approach should be transparent enough for others to estimate how much work such an adaptation would be and how well it could reasonably work. Just stating that most research is targeted at ESL only reinforces the problem.

You write that the error types certain systems tackle would be “usually obvious from the tables”. I don't think it is as simple as that -- see the CUUI example mentioned above as well as the unnecessary token errors. There are five systems that don't correct them (Table 5) and it should therefore be obvious that they did not try to tackle them. However, in the paper you write that “There is also no obvious explanation as to why these teams had difficulty with this error type”.
