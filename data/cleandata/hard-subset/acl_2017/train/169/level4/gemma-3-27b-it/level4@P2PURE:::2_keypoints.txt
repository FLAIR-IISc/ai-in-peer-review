- Introduces a novel GEC evaluation that measures performance by error type, capturing both recall and precision without manual error annotations.
- Evaluation is a significant advancement for analyzing GEC system behavior.
- Paper evaluates a diverse set of GEC systems.
- Method advantages: computes precision per error type, is independent of manual annotation, and can evaluate multi-token errors.
- Human experts largely agreed with automatically assigned error tags for pre-computed spans.
- The rules used to derive error types are not described, preventing replication or understanding.
- The rationale and literature grounding for the error categories are unclear.
- Categorization rules likely depend on alignment/preprocessing but these dependencies aren’t detailed.
- No illustrative rule or description of number/complexity of categories to estimate adaptation effort.
- Classifier evaluation lacks a thorough error analysis and guidance for improvement.
- Human rater agreement may be biased by noisy automatic boundaries and knowledge of system outputs; no neutral rating option was provided.
- Preprocessing uses continuous edit spans, which may fail for discontinuous edits (e.g., separable verb prefixes in German).
- Unclear whether the significance test used appropriate statistics (possible misuse of F0.5) and whether comparisons covered classification as well as boundaries.
- Paper doesn’t analyze whether systems were designed to address specific error types, confounding interpretation of failures (e.g., unnecessary token and CUUI cases).
- Results in Table 6 lack raw TP/FP/TN/FN counts and may be based on small sample sizes—these should be reported.
- Observation that different systems excel on different error types aligns with prior research and should be acknowledged.
- Multi-token error analysis is useful but requires interpretation since some systems cannot handle such errors and none were trained on learner→fluent corpora.
- Paper should acknowledge prior GEC approaches with explicit error-tagging modules and clarify advantages of the proposed unified comparison.
- Minor issues: reference formatting and missing page info for cited works.
- Author concurs approach is sound but emphasizes the need to fully specify classification rules and cross-language generalizability; disputes that error-targeting by systems is always obvious.
