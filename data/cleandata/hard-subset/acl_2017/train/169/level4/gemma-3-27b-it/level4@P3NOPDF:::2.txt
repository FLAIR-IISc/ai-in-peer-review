The paper presents a novel approach for evaluating grammatical error correction (GEC) systems. This approach enables the assessment of GEC system performance by error type, considering both recall and precision – a capability previously limited due to the typical lack of error category annotations in system outputs.

**Strengths:**

*   The proposed evaluation represents an important step forward for analyzing GEC system behavior.
*   The paper includes a comprehensive evaluation across a variety of GEC systems.
*   The approach offers several advantages over previous work: it computes precision by error type, is independent of manual error annotation, and can assess performance on multi-token errors.
*   Human experts largely validate the automatically selected error tags for pre-computed error spans.

**Weaknesses:**

*   A crucial component – the rules used to derive error types – are not described. This lack of transparency hinders replicability and adaptation of the approach.
*   The classifier evaluation lacks a thorough error analysis and consequently, lacks concrete directions for future classifier improvements.
*   The evaluation is currently limited to English, and it remains unclear how easily the approach could be extended to other languages. The effort required for adaptation is uncertain.

---

**Classifier and Classifier Evaluation**

The basis for devising the error categories is unclear. Are they grounded in prior work? While the approach is generally independent of the alignment algorithm, the rules likely are not, yet the authors provide no details about them. The error categories are central to the paper; readers deserve at least a representative example of a rule used to assign an error type.

The paper does not extend the proposed evaluation to languages beyond English, nor does it discuss the necessary modifications for applying the classifier to other languages.  The rules determining edit boundaries and error tags likely depend on the target language and pre-processing pipeline, necessitating adaptation. Error categories themselves may also require adjustment. Without details regarding the complexity and number of rules used for error category assignment, estimating the effort required for cross-lingual application remains difficult.

The pre-processing step yields inherently continuous error spans, similar to the M2 scorer. This is problematic because accurately tagging some errors requires discontinuous spans. For example, in German, verbs with separable prefixes are often separated in main clauses, presenting a challenge for classifiers expecting continuous edits.  Can the classifier handle such discontinuous spans?

The authors state that human judges rated at least 95% of automatically assigned error tags as appropriate “despite the degree of noise introduced by automatic edit extraction” (p. 295). This claim warrants caution. Raters might have been more lenient given noisy boundaries and were potentially biased toward the system output by knowing it during the tagging process.  The absence of a rating option between "Bad (Not Appropriate)" and "Appropriate" could also have influenced the ratings.  A more robust evaluation would involve assessing human judgments on classifier output with manually created (noise-free) boundaries.

The classifier evaluation lacks a detailed error analysis. While a correlation with POS tagging errors is noted, further investigation is needed. When did raters select "Bad" versus "Appropriate"? Do expert ratings suggest specific avenues for improving the classifier?

---

**Gold Reference vs. Auto Reference**

The data underlying the significance test is unclear. Was the test performed on F0.5 scores? If so, this is questionable, as F0.5 is a derived metric with limited discriminative power; different recall and precision values can yield the same F0.5 score. Furthermore, while the introduction to Section 4.1 highlights misalignment issues in both alignment and classification, the comparison between gold and reference appears limited to boundaries, not classification.

---

**Error Type Evaluation**

The finding that five teams failed to correct unnecessary token errors is not surprising.  At least two systems – UFC and AMU – have known limitations in this area. UFC’s rule-based approach relies on POS tags, making it inherently incapable of identifying superfluous words. Rozovskaya & Roth (2016) explain AMU’s poor performance on superfluous words. 

The authors do not analyze or interpret the results in Table 6 concerning whether the evaluated systems were *designed* to handle each error type. For certain errors, like word order, there’s a clear correspondence between the gold standard and auto-reference. However, it’s unclear whether systems failed due to inherent limitations or simply lack of targeted design (e.g., CUUI achieving precision+recall=0.0 despite not explicitly targeting word order errors). The CUUI case, and others like it, suggests potential errors in classification that are neither analyzed nor discussed. 

Please include raw values (TP, FP, TN, FN) in the appendix for Table 6 to facilitate easier system comparison using alternative metrics. Also, note when results in Table 6 are based on a small number of instances.

The statement that “All but 2 teams (IITB and IPN) achieved the best score in at least 1 category, which suggests that different approaches to GEC complement different error types” (p. 606) would be strengthened by acknowledging its alignment with previous research.

The multi-token error analysis is valuable, but requires more interpretation. Some systems may be fundamentally unable to correct these errors, particularly given that none were trained on parallel corpora of learner data and fluent corrections (Sakaguchi et al., 2016).

---

**Other**

*   The authors should acknowledge that error annotations were potentially achievable for some GEC approaches, especially those with dedicated submodules for specific error types. Adapting such systems to produce tagged outputs might be possible, and the proposed approach provides a unified framework for comparing systems with varying annotation capabilities.
*   **References:** Several titles are missing capitalization. The URL for Sakaguchi et al. (2016) should be enclosed in angle brackets. Page information is missing for Efron and Tibshirani (1993).

**Author Response**

I agree that your approach is not “fatally flawed” and I think this review actually points out quite some positive aspects. The approach is good, but the paper is not ready. The basis for the paper are the rules for classifying errors and the lack of description is a major factor. This is not just a matter about additional examples. If the rules are not seen as a one-off implementation, they need to be described to be replicable or to adapt them.

Generalization to other languages should not be an afterthought. It would be a serious limitation if the approach only worked on one language by design. Even if you don't perform an adaption for other languages, your approach should be transparent enough for others to estimate how much work such an adaptation would be and how well it could reasonably work. Just stating that most research is targeted at ESL only reinforces the problem.

You write that the error types certain systems tackle would be “usually obvious from the tables”. I don't think it is as simple as that -- see the CUUI example mentioned above as well as the unnecessary token errors. There are five systems that don't correct them (Table 5) and it should therefore be obvious that they did not try to tackle them. However, in the paper you write that “There is also no obvious explanation as to why these teams had difficulty with this error type”.
