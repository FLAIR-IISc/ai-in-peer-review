The paper presents a novel approach for evaluating grammatical error correction (GEC) systems. This approach enables performance assessment of GEC systems by error type, considering both recall and precision – a capability previously limited due to the lack of error category annotations in system outputs.

**Strengths:**

*   The proposed evaluation method represents a significant advancement for analyzing GEC system behavior.
*   The paper includes a comprehensive evaluation across a diverse set of GEC systems.
*   The approach offers several advantages over existing methods: it computes precision by error type, operates independently of manual error annotation, and can assess performance on multi-token errors.
*   Human experts largely validate the automatically assigned error tags for pre-computed error spans.

**Weaknesses:**

*   A crucial component – the rules used to derive error types – are not detailed within the paper.
*   The classifier evaluation lacks a comprehensive error analysis, hindering the identification of future directions for classifier improvement.
*   The evaluation is limited to English, and the effort required to adapt the approach to other languages remains unclear.

**Classifier and Classifier Evaluation**

The foundation for the error categories is unclear; are they informed by prior research? While the approach generally avoids dependency on alignment algorithms, the rules likely are dependent, yet the authors provide no such details. The error categories are central to the paper, and readers deserve at least an illustrative example of a rule used for error type assignment.

The paper does not extend the proposed evaluation to languages beyond English, nor does it elaborate on the modifications needed to run the classifier on other languages. Adaptations to edit boundary determination, tagging rules, and potentially the error categories themselves are anticipated, given their dependence on language-specific characteristics and pre-processing pipelines. The authors do not provide sufficient detail (e.g., the total number of error categories, their complexity) to estimate the effort required for cross-lingual adaptation.

The pre-processing step generates inherently continuous error spans, which may be problematic for errors requiring discontinuous tagging, such as those found in languages like German (e.g., separable verb prefixes). It is unclear whether the classifier can accurately tag such discontinuous edits.

The authors report that human judges rated at least 95% of automatically assigned error tags as appropriate, despite the noise introduced by automatic edit extraction. This claim warrants caution, as raters may have exhibited leniency in the presence of noisy boundaries, and the evaluation setup allowed for bias towards the system output. The absence of a middle ground rating option ("Neither Bad nor Appropriate") could also have influenced responses. To strengthen the evaluation, the authors should assess human ratings of classifier output with manually created boundaries, eliminating the influence of automatic extraction noise.

The classifier evaluation lacks a detailed error analysis. While it's noted that “Bad” ratings are often linked to incorrect POS tags, further investigation is needed. Specifically, the authors should address questions like: What characteristics of instances lead to “Bad” versus “Appropriate” ratings? Do expert ratings suggest concrete improvements to the classifier?

**Gold Reference vs. Auto Reference**

It is unclear which data was used for the significance test. Testing on F0.5 scores is potentially problematic, as this is a derived measure with limited discriminatory power; differing recall and precision values can yield the same F0.5 score. Furthermore, the authors mention misalignment between automatic and reference outputs in Section 4.1 concerning alignment and classification, but the comparison between gold and reference data appears to focus solely on boundary agreement, excluding classification discrepancies.

**Error Type Evaluation**

The failure of five teams to correct unnecessary token errors is not surprising. Straightforward explanations exist for at least two systems: UFC’s rule-based approach relies on POS tags, making superfluous word identification impossible, and Rozovskaya & Roth (2016) explain AMU's performance limitations on superfluous words.

The authors do not analyze or contextualize the results in Table 6 regarding the design goals of each system. For some error types (e.g., word order), a clear mapping exists between gold standard and automatic reference errors. However, it remains unclear whether systems failed entirely on specific error types or were simply not designed to address them. The CUUI example (precision+recall=0.0 despite not targeting word order errors) suggests a potential misclassification that is neither analyzed nor discussed.

The authors should include raw values (TP, FP, TN, FN) for Table 6 in the appendix to facilitate comparisons between systems using various metrics. Furthermore, the number of instances underlying the results in Table 6 appears small for some error types and systems; this should be clarified when reporting the raw values.

The statement that “All but 2 teams (IITB and IPN) achieved the best score in at least 1 category, which suggests that different approaches to GEC complement different error types” (606) would benefit from acknowledgment of related findings in prior research.

The analysis of multi-token errors is valuable for future work, but requires more interpretation. Certain systems are likely inherently incapable of correcting such errors. However, none of the evaluated systems were trained on parallel corpora of learner data with fluent corrections (as defined by Sakaguchi et al., 2016).

**Other**

*   The authors should acknowledge that prior GEC approaches sometimes included error-specific submodules, enabling error annotations. Adapting these systems to produce tagged outputs would be necessary for comparison, but the proposed approach allows for a unified comparison of GEC systems, even those for which producing error-tagged outputs is not straightforward.
*   References: Some titles lack capitalization. The URL for Sakaguchi et al. (2016) requires wrapping. Page information is missing for Efron and Tibshirani (1993).

**Author Response**

I agree that your approach is not “fatally flawed” and I think this review actually points out quite some positive aspects. The approach is good, but the paper is not ready.

The basis for the paper are the rules for classifying errors and the lack of description is a major factor. This is not just a matter about additional examples. If the rules are not seen as a one-off implementation, they need to be described to be replicable or to adapt them.

Generalization to other languages should not be an afterthought. It would be a serious limitation if the approach only worked on one language by design. Even if you don't perform an adaptation for other languages, your approach should be transparent enough for others to estimate how much work such an adaptation would be and how well it could reasonably work. Just stating that most research is targeted at ESL only reinforces the problem.

You write that the error types certain systems tackle would be “usually obvious from the tables”. I don't think it is as simple as that -- see the CUUI example mentioned above as well as the unnecessary token errors. There are five systems that don't correct them (Table 5) and it should therefore be obvious that they did not try to tackle them. However, in the paper you write that “There is also no obvious explanation as to why these teams had difficulty with this error type”.
