This paper presents an approach for automatically enriching the output of grammatical error correction (GEC) systems with error type annotations. This is a potentially valuable contribution, as providing learners and educators with explicit error type information, beyond just a corrected sentence, can be highly beneficial. Furthermore, the ability to analyze GEC system performance at a fine-grained level – considering precision, recall, and error type-specific metrics – is a clear strength of this work.

However, the paper suffers from a significant lack of detail in its system description and a less-than-convincing evaluation. The core of the system appears to rely on a set of manually crafted rules, yet the paper provides almost no specifics regarding these rules. Crucially, the authors should include examples illustrating rule structure, quantify the total number of rules, and discuss their complexity and potential interactions (e.g., ordering and potential blocking). The extended presentation of CoNLL-2014 system results in Table 6 and the surrounding text largely reiterate information readily available within the table itself, detracting from a deeper analysis.

The evaluation methodology raises several concerns. First, rather than relying on annotator ratings of system output, a gold standard should have been established through independent annotation of the 200 test sentences. Creating a gold standard, given the relatively small test set and a fixed tagset, is feasible and would provide a more reliable basis for comparison, avoiding potential biases inherent in evaluating pre-annotated data (Marcus et al., 1993). Second, the method of averaging individual rater scores is questionable. Reporting that 3.7% of edits were deemed “bad” obscures the fact that 18.5% of edits received a “bad” rating from *at least one* annotator, a considerably more critical figure. Third, the paper lacks essential information about the test data, including the number of error categories present and the specific categories covered by the annotators’ “good” ratings. Finally, the meaning of “unusual edit boundaries” requires clarification and illustration; its potential impact on system applicability should be addressed. 

The authors claim reduced domain dependence compared to data-driven approaches. However, this assertion is not entirely convincing. Components like Hunspell’s vocabulary are inherently domain-specific, and manually created rules are susceptible to the same limitations, and are entirely language dependent. This is a key drawback compared to machine learning methods. Furthermore, the evaluation data (FCE and CoNLL-2014) is limited to the domain of student essays, restricting the generalizability of these claims. 

The rationale behind the newly designed error category set is unclear. While the authors cite the need to facilitate searching for underspecified categories, existing tagsets like those in Nicholls (2003) appear to offer similar functionality. Leveraging the CoNLL-2014 tagset would have allowed for direct evaluation against the existing gold standard. 

In summary, the core motivation of this paper feels somewhat diffuse. It is unclear whether the primary contribution lies in a new system, a novel error categorization scheme, or an evaluation of existing GEC systems. The lack of detail regarding the system implementation and the shortcomings of the evaluation hinder a clear assessment of its significance.
