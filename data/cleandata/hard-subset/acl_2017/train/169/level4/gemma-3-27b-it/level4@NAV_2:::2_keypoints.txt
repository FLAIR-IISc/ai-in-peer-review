- Novel evaluation enables measuring precision by error type for GEC without requiring manual error-category annotation.  
- Important contribution for analyzing GEC system behavior; evaluated on a variety of systems.  
- Advantages highlighted: computes precision by error type, independent of manual annotation, and handles multi-token errors.  
- Automatically assigned error tags were largely judged appropriate by human experts.  
- The rules used to derive error types are not described.  
- Classifier evaluation lacks thorough error analysis and concrete directions for improvement.  
- Unclear how easily the approach generalizes to other languages; rules and categories likely require adaptation.  
- Unclear dependence of the tagging rules on the alignment algorithm; no details provided.  
- No examples or breakdown of the error-type assignment rules (count, complexity, per-type rules).  
- Pre-processing creates continuous error spans, which is problematic for discontinuous edits (e.g., separable verb prefixes).  
- Human rater approval may be biased by noisy boundaries and lack of intermediate rating options; blind/manual-boundary evaluation was not done.  
- Classifier analysis is shallow (e.g., “Bad” blamed on wrong POS only); missing when/why raters labeled tags “Bad” vs “Appropriate.”  
- Unclear what data/statistic the significance tests used; testing on F0.5 is questionable.  
- Gold vs. auto-reference comparison appears limited to boundaries and not to classification differences.  
- Some systems’ zero scores on certain error types may reflect system design limitations rather than true failures; paper lacks such system-design mapping.  
- Table 6 lacks raw TP/FP/TN/FN counts, making alternative comparisons and small-sample issues hard to assess.  
- The statement that teams excel in different categories aligns with prior work but is not referenced.  
- Multi-token error analysis is promising but needs deeper interpretation: some systems inherently cannot correct such errors and training data differences matter.  
- Paper should note that some GEC systems can produce error annotations via submodules; the proposed approach mainly unifies comparisons across systems.  
- Minor issues in references: capitalization inconsistencies, missing page information, and URL formatting.
