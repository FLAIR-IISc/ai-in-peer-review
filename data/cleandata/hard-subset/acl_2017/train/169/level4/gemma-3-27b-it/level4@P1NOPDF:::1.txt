- Strengths: Useful application for teachers and learners; supports fine-grained comparison of GEC systems.
- Weaknesses: Highly superficial system description; evaluation is not convincing.

- General Discussion:
The paper presents an approach for automatically enriching the output of GEC systems with error type information. This is a valuable application, as both teachers and learners can benefit from knowing the specific errors made (many GEC systems only provide a corrected version). It also enables more nuanced comparisons of GEC systems, offering precision, recall, and precision figures for individual error types.

Unfortunately, the system description is highly superficial. The core of the system appears to be a set of manually created rules, but the paper lacks crucial details. The authors should, for example, provide sample rules, specify the total number of rules, describe their complexity, and explain their ordering (could earlier rules prevent later ones from being applied?). Instead of detailing the system, several pages are dedicated to an evaluation of systems participating in CoNLL-2014. Table 6, which spans an entire page, lists results for all systems, and the accompanying text largely repeats information readily available in the table.

The evaluation of the proposed system is also unconvincing in several respects. First, annotators should have independently annotated a gold standard for the 200 test sentences, rather than simply rating the system’s output. Given the limited size of the test set and a fixed tagset, creating a gold standard should be feasible. The current approach likely yields more favorable ratings than comparison against a genuine gold standard (see Marcus et al. (1993) for a discussion of agreement when reviewing pre-annotated data versus annotating from scratch).

Second, the statement that “all 5 raters individually considered at least 95% of our rule-based error types to be either ‘Good’ or ‘Acceptable’” is misleading. Averaging individual rater scores is not standard practice. If each “bad” score corresponded to a different edit, 18.5% of the edits were deemed “bad” by at least one annotator – a significantly worse result than the reported average of 3.7%. 

Third, the paper provides no information about the test data, such as the number of error categories present or which categories are covered by the annotations deemed “good.”

Fourth, the meaning of “edit boundaries might be unusual” is unclear. A more precise explanation, with examples, is needed. Could this pose a problem for the system’s application?

The authors claim their system is less domain-dependent than systems requiring training data. This assertion is questionable. For example, Hunspell’s vocabulary likely has varying coverage across domains, and manually created rules can be domain-dependent – and are inherently language-dependent, a clear disadvantage compared to machine learning approaches. Furthermore, the test data (FCE test, CoNLL-2014) is limited to one domain: student essays.

The rationale for designing a new set of error categories is also unclear. One stated reason is to facilitate searching for underspecified categories like “NOUN.” However, the tagset presented in Nicholls (2003) appears to support similar searches, or why not utilize the CoNLL-2014 tagset, allowing for evaluation against the existing CoNLL gold standard?

In summary, the paper’s core motivation remains somewhat ambiguous. Is it presenting a new system? But critical details are omitted. Is it introducing a new error category set? But insufficient justification is provided. Or is it evaluating the CoNLL-2014 systems? But the presentation of results feels superficial.
