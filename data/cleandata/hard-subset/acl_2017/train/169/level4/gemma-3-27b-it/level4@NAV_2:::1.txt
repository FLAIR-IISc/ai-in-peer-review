The paper presents an approach for automatically enriching the output of Grammatical Error Correction (GEC) systems with error type information. This has potential value for both pedagogical applications and for enabling more fine-grained comparisons of GEC system performance. It allows for analysis of precision in general, and error type-specific figures for recall and precision. 

The primary contribution of this work is the development of a dataset-independent, rule-based framework for classifying edits made by GEC systems. This framework avoids the limitations of training data bias inherent in machine learning approaches. A secondary contribution is the application of this framework to analyze the performance of systems participating in the CoNLL-2014 shared task, providing a novel error-type-specific evaluation.

**Strengths:**

1. **Novelty of Dataset-Independent Approach:** The rule-based approach to error type classification, relying solely on automatic linguistic markup (POS tags, lemmas, etc.), is a significant strength. This avoids the need for labeled training data, a common bottleneck in GEC research, and improves portability across datasets. The reported high agreement rates (over 95% “Good” or “Acceptable” from raters) suggest the approach is reasonably effective.
2. **Detailed CoNLL-2014 Analysis:** Applying the framework to the CoNLL-2014 data provides a valuable and previously unavailable detailed analysis of system performance. This allows for a more nuanced understanding of the strengths and weaknesses of different GEC approaches than was previously possible with overall scores alone. The granularity of error type analysis is a significant contribution.
3. **Potential for Practical Applications:** The ability to automatically annotate GEC output with error types has clear practical applications for language learners and educators. Providing learners with feedback on specific error types can facilitate more targeted learning.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Insufficient System Description:** The description of the rule-based framework remains superficial. The paper lacks crucial details regarding the rules themselves – examples, total number, complexity, ordering logic, and potential rule interactions are not provided. This hinders assessment of the system’s design and potential limitations.
2. **Questionable Evaluation Methodology:** The evaluation relies on expert ratings of system-generated annotations rather than comparison against a gold standard annotation of the test data. While expert ratings provide some validation, they are susceptible to biases inherent in evaluating pre-annotated data. The averaging of individual rater assessments obscures the distribution of “bad” ratings.
3. **Limited Scope of Error Analysis:** The presentation of results for the CoNLL-2014 systems is overly descriptive and lacks deeper analysis. Table 6, while comprehensive, is not effectively synthesized to draw meaningful conclusions beyond those already apparent from the system descriptions. The discussion of multi-token edits and error detection is similarly descriptive rather than analytical.

**Questions to Authors:**

1. Could the authors provide several representative examples of the rules used in their error type classification framework, demonstrating their complexity and interaction?
2. What was the distribution of “bad” ratings across the 200 test sentences used for evaluation?
3. How representative are the FCE-test and CoNLL-2014 datasets of other domains of learner writing? What steps were taken to ensure the generalizability of the framework?
4. The paper mentions that edit boundaries “might be unusual” due to the automatic extraction process. Can the authors quantify the impact of these potentially incorrect boundaries on the overall evaluation?
