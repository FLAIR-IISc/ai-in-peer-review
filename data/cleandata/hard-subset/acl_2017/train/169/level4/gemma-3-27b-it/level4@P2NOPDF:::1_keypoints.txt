- Positive: Method enriches GEC outputs with error-type information, enabling targeted feedback and error-specific evaluation metrics.  
- Major omission: System description lacks detail about the manually crafted rules (count, complexity, examples, interactions, ordering/blocking).  
- Paper content imbalance: Large sections repeat CoNLL-2014 results instead of explaining the proposed system.  
- Evaluation flaw: No independent gold-standard annotations; relying on ratings of system output risks inflated scores.  
- Contradictory scoring: Reported average “bad” edit rate 3.7% understates that 18.5% of edits had at least one “bad” rating.  
- Missing test-data details: No info on number of error categories present or per-category distribution of “good” ratings.  
- Unclear terminology: “Unusual edit boundaries” is not defined or exemplified, so its impact is unknown.  
- Domain-dependence claim questionable: Reliance on Hunspell and manual rules imposes domain/language limits; evaluation only on student essays limits generalizability.  
- Unjustified taxonomy: Motivation for the new error category set is unclear; existing tagsets (e.g., Nicholls, CoNLL-2014) could suffice and allow direct comparison.  
- Ambiguous contribution: Unclear whether paper proposes a novel system, a new categorization scheme, or a superficial evaluation of existing systems.  
- Minor issues: Several typographical/formatting errors and inconsistent reference capitalization (e.g., “c.f.”→“cf.”, “M2”→“M^2”, clarify “50-70 F1”, “esl”→“ESL”, “fleiss”→“Fleiss”).
