- Valuable application: enriching GEC outputs with error-type information benefits teachers/learners and enables finer-grained system comparison.
- System description superficial: core rule-based approach lacks sufficient detail and illustrative examples.
- Missing rule specifics: number, complexity, ordering, and whether earlier rules block later ones are not described.
- Paper overemphasizes CoNLL-2014 system results and repeats information already in Table 6.
- Evaluation should use an independently annotated gold standard for the 200 test sentences, not ratings of pre-annotated output.
- Rater-score reporting is misleading: averaging hides that up to 18.5% of edits could be rated “bad” by at least one annotator.
- Insufficient test-data information: no details on number or coverage of error categories present in the test set.
- Ambiguous phrase “edit boundaries might be unusual” needs precise definition and examples to assess impact.
- Questionable domain-independence claim: tools like Hunspell and manual rules can be domain- and language-dependent; test data is limited to student essays.
- Unclear motivation for a new error category set and lack of justification for not using existing sets (e.g., Nicholls 2003 or CoNLL-2014).
- Overall ambiguity: unclear whether the paper’s contribution is a new system, a new tagset, or an evaluation of CoNLL-2014 systems, with crucial details omitted.
- Typos and minor issues: multiple formatting/capitalization and reference errors (cf., M^2, percent sign in “50-70 F1”, ESL casing, Fleiss/Kappa, reference punctuation).
