This paper presents a method for automatically annotating grammatical error correction (GEC) system output with error types, a valuable contribution for both educators and researchers seeking more granular system evaluations. The ability to move beyond recall-based performance metrics and analyze systems by error type is a significant step forward. However, despite a promising premise, the paper suffers from a superficial system description, a flawed evaluation methodology, and a lack of clear motivation for key design choices.

**Summary of the Paper & Contributions:**

The authors address the challenge of evaluating GEC systems on a per-error-type basis, which was previously hindered by the lack of annotated system outputs. Their approach utilizes a linguistically-enhanced alignment algorithm to extract edits between original and corrected sentences, followed by a rule-based classifier to assign error types. The core contribution is the development of this dataset-independent, rule-based error typing framework and its application to the CoNLL-2014 shared task data. A secondary contribution is the provision of the tool implementing this framework, which is stated to be released with the paper. A further, though less central, contribution is the detailed analysis of CoNLL-2014 systems, enabled by the automated annotation.

**Strengths:**

1. **Practical Utility:** The core idea of automatically annotating GEC system output is genuinely useful. Providing detailed error type information is beneficial for learners, teachers, and developers aiming to understand and improve GEC systems. This addresses a clear gap in the field.
2. **Dataset Independence:** The rule-based approach to error typing, aiming for dataset agnosticism, is a laudable goal. The authors correctly identify the issues with classifier bias towards specific training corpora.
3. **Detailed CoNLL-2014 Analysis:** The application of the tool to the CoNLL-2014 data provides a valuable (though arguably overly detailed) resource for the GEC community. The identification of strengths and weaknesses of different systems across error types offers potential avenues for future research.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Superficial System Description:** The paper provides insufficient detail regarding the rule-based classifier. The description is vague; crucial information is missing. For example, the authors should provide example rules, the total number of rules, their complexity, and the rule application order. Without this, assessing the system's design choices is impossible.
2. **Unsatisfactory Evaluation:** The evaluation methodology is fundamentally flawed. Using human ratings of the *system's output* instead of creating a gold standard annotation for a test set is problematic. Agreement metrics in such scenarios are known to be inflated compared to annotating from scratch (Marcus et al., 1993). Reporting individual rater statistics is misleading; a more appropriate approach would be to calculate inter-annotator agreement.  The reported "95% Good/Acceptable" rating is also potentially misleading without understanding the distribution of "Bad" ratings.
3. **Lack of Motivation & Justification:** The rationale for creating a new error category scheme is weak. The stated benefit – enabling searches for broader categories – is already achievable with existing tagsets (e.g., Nicholls, 2003). The decision *not* to leverage the CoNLL-2014 tagset, preventing a direct comparison with the gold standard, is particularly puzzling.
4. **Domain Dependence Concerns:** While the authors claim dataset independence, aspects of the system (Hunspell’s vocabulary, manually created rules) are inherently domain-dependent. The test data (FCE-test, CoNLL-2014) is limited to student essays, potentially limiting generalizability.

**Questions to Authors:**

1. Can you provide a representative sample of the rules used in your error type classifier, illustrating their complexity and interaction?
2. Why was a gold standard annotation not created for a subset of the test data, as opposed to relying on ratings of the system’s output?
3. Why was the CoNLL-2014 error type tagset not used, and how does your proposed scheme offer a significant advantage?
4. How does your system handle cases where the automatic edit boundaries are inaccurate ("unusual" boundaries)? Could this impact the error type classification?
5. Can you elaborate on the potential domain dependence of the Hunspell dictionary and the manually created rules?
