The paper presents a novel approach for evaluating grammatical error correction (GEC) systems. This approach enables the assessment of GEC system performance by error type, considering both recall and precision, which was previously challenging since system outputs are typically not annotated with error categories.

**Strengths:**

*   The proposed evaluation is an important step forward for analyzing GEC system behavior.
*   The paper includes evaluations for a variety of systems.
*   The approach offers several advantages over previous work:
    *   It computes precision by error type.
    *   It is independent of manual error annotation.
    *   It can assess performance on multi-token errors.
*   The automatically selected error tags for pre-computed error spans are largely confirmed by human experts.

**Weaknesses:**

*   A crucial component – the rules used to derive error types – are not described.
*   The classifier evaluation lacks thorough error analysis and, consequently, does not offer concrete directions for improving the classifier.
*   The evaluation was conducted solely on English, and it’s unclear how readily the approach could be applied to other languages.

**Classifier and Classifier Evaluation**

The basis for defining the error categories is unclear. Are they grounded in prior work?

While the approach is generally independent of the alignment algorithm, the rules likely are not, yet the authors do not provide sufficient detail. Error categories are central to the paper, and readers should at least see an example of a rule used to assign an error type.

Unfortunately, the paper does not evaluate the proposed evaluation method on languages other than English. It also lacks discussion of the changes needed to adapt the classifier for other languages. I expect that the rules determining edit boundaries and error tags would depend on the language and pre-processing pipeline, requiring adaptation. Furthermore, the error categories themselves might need to be revised. The authors provide no detail regarding the complexity or number of rules used for assigning error categories, making it difficult to estimate the effort required to apply the approach to another language.

The error spans identified in the pre-processing step are inherently continuous (similar to the M2 scorer). This is a limitation, as some errors require discontinuous error spans for accurate tagging. For instance, in German, verbs with separable prefixes are often separated: \[first constituent] \[verb] \[other constituents] \[verb prefix]. Would the classifier be able to identify these discontinuous edit spans?

The authors state that human judges rated at least 95% of the automatically assigned error tags as appropriate “despite the degree of noise introduced by automatic edit extraction” (p. 295). I believe this claim warrants caution, as raters may have been more lenient given noisy boundaries. Additionally, they were not asked to select a tag without system output context, potentially introducing bias. The lack of a rating option between “Bad (Not Appropriate)” and “Appropriate” could also have influenced raters toward the latter. To strengthen the evaluation, the authors should assess how human judges rate classifier output with manually created boundaries, eliminating the noise from faulty boundaries.

The classifier evaluation lacks a thorough error analysis. It is only noted that “Bad” ratings are often linked to incorrect POS tags. I would like to see answers to the following questions: When did raters select “Bad” versus “Appropriate”? Does expert evaluation highlight opportunities to improve the classifier?

**Gold Reference vs. Auto Reference**

It is unclear on what data the significance test was performed. Was it performed on the F0.5 scores? If so, this may not be ideal, as it’s a derived measure with limited discriminatory power (performance in terms of recall and precision can differ substantially while yielding the same F0.5 score). Additionally, in the beginning of Section 4.1, the authors mention mismatches between automatic and reference outputs in terms of alignment and classification, but the comparison between gold and reference appears to be limited to boundaries, not classification.

**Error Type Evaluation**

It is unsurprising that five teams (~line 473) failed to correct any unnecessary token errors. At least two of these systems have known limitations in handling superfluous words. UFC, for example, relies on a rule-based approach using POS tags (Ng et al., 2014), which is insufficient to identify superfluous words. Rozovskaya & Roth (2016) explain AMU's poor performance on superfluous words.

The authors do not analyze or comment on the results in Table 6 concerning whether the systems were designed to handle the respective error types. For some error types, there is a direct correspondence between the gold standard and the auto reference, such as word order errors. It remains unclear whether the systems completely failed on specific error types or were simply not designed to correct them (CUUI, for example, achieves precision+recall=0.0 despite not targeting word order errors). The CUUI case—and likely others—suggests an error in classification, which is neither analyzed nor discussed.

Please provide raw values for TP, FP, TN, and FN in the appendix for Table 6. This will facilitate comparison of the systems using other metrics. Additionally, it appears that the results in Table 6 for some error types and systems are based on a limited number of instances, which should be clarified when presenting the raw values.

Your statement, “All but 2 teams (IITB and IPN) achieved the best score in at least 1 category, which suggests that different approaches to GEC complement different error types” (line 606) would be strengthened by acknowledging its consistency with previous research.

Multi-token error analysis is valuable for future work, but the results require further interpretation. Some systems may be inherently unable to correct such errors, but none were trained on a parallel corpus of learner data and fluent corrections (in the sense of Sakaguchi et al., 2016).

**Other**

*   The authors should note that, for some GEC approaches, providing error annotations wasn't previously impossible (e.g., systems with submodules for specific error types). Admittedly, adaptation would be needed to include the submodule responsible for a change in the system output. Nevertheless, the proposed approach offers a unified way to compare GEC systems, including those for which producing error-tagged output is not straightforward.
*   **References:** Some titles are missing capitalization. The URL for Sakaguchi et al. (2016) needs to be properly formatted. Page information is missing for Efron and Tibshirani (1993).

**Author Response**

I agree that your approach is not “fatally flawed,” and I appreciate that the review highlights several positive aspects. The approach is sound, but the paper is not yet ready for publication.

The rules for classifying errors are the foundation of this work, and their lack of description is a major deficiency. This is not simply a matter of providing additional examples; the rules must be described to ensure replicability and adaptability if they are to be seen as more than a one-off implementation.

Generalization to other languages should not be an afterthought. A serious limitation would be if the approach only functions for English by design. Even without adapting the method for other languages, the approach should be transparent enough for others to estimate the effort and potential success of such adaptation. Simply stating that most research focuses on ESL only exacerbates this concern.

You argue that whether systems tackle certain error types is “usually obvious from the tables.” I disagree—consider the CUUI example, as well as the unnecessary token errors. Five systems fail to correct them (Table 5), suggesting they did not attempt to do so. However, the paper states, “There is also no obvious explanation as to why these teams had difficulty with this error type.”
