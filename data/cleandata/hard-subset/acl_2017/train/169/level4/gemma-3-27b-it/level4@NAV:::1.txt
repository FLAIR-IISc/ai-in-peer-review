## Review

**Strengths:** The work presents a potentially useful application for both educators and learners, enabling more informative feedback on grammatical errors. Furthermore, it facilitates a finer-grained comparison of GEC systems, moving beyond overall precision to include error type-specific recall and precision metrics.

**Weaknesses:** The system description is notably superficial. While the core relies on a set of (presumably) manually created rules, the paper lacks essential details regarding these rules – examples, total number, complexity, ordering logic (and potential blocking effects), are all absent.  Instead of detailed system explanation, a substantial portion of the paper is dedicated to restating results from the CoNLL-2014 evaluation, largely mirroring information readily available in Table 6. The evaluation of the proposed system itself is also unconvincing.

**General Discussion:**

This paper introduces a method for automatically enriching the output of GEC systems with error type information. This is a valuable contribution, as many GEC systems currently only provide corrected text without specifying the nature of the errors.  The ability to identify error types benefits both teachers and learners and allows for a more nuanced evaluation of GEC system performance. 

However, the description of the system remains significantly underdeveloped. The foundational rules driving the error tagging are not explained.  Providing illustrative examples, quantifying the rule set’s size and complexity, and clarifying the rule application order (and potential for rule conflicts) are crucial.  The paper's extensive recounting of CoNLL-2014 system results, largely repeating data already present in Table 6, detracts from a deeper system analysis.

The evaluation methodology is also problematic.  Rather than establishing a gold standard through independent annotation of the test sentences, the evaluation relies on ratings of the system's output. This approach introduces potential bias, as demonstrated by research (e.g., Marcus et al., 1993) showing differing agreement rates when reviewing pre-annotated data versus annotating from scratch.  Furthermore, the averaging of individual rater scores is unconventional; calculating the proportion of edits flagged as "bad" by *any* rater (approximately 18.5%) presents a less favorable picture than the reported average of 3.7%.  Critical details regarding the test data are also missing, including the number of error categories represented and the coverage of categories considered "good" by the annotators. The phrase "edit boundaries might be unusual" requires greater clarity and illustrative examples to assess its potential impact. 

The authors claim reduced domain dependence compared to data-driven systems.  However, this assertion is questionable. Resources like Hunspell’s vocabulary are inherently domain-specific, and manually crafted rules are equally susceptible to domain bias and, crucially, are entirely language-dependent – a limitation relative to machine learning approaches.  Moreover, the evaluation data (FCE-test, CoNLL-2014) is confined to the domain of student essays.

The rationale for designing a novel set of error categories is unclear. While the authors cite searchability for broad categories as a benefit, existing tagsets (e.g., Nicholls, 2003) appear to offer similar functionality.  Utilizing the CoNLL-2014 tagset would have facilitated direct comparison with their existing gold standard. 

In summary, the paper’s core motivation is ambiguous. It is not sufficiently focused on a new system (given the lack of detail), a novel error categorization (due to limited justification), or a comprehensive evaluation of existing GEC systems (given the superficial presentation of results).
