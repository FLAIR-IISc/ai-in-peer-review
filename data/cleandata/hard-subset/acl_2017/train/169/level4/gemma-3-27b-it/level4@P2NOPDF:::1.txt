This paper proposes a method for automatically enriching the output of grammatical error correction (GEC) systems with error type information, a potentially valuable tool for both educators and learners. The ability to identify specific error types, beyond simply providing a corrected sentence, allows for more targeted feedback and a more nuanced comparison of GEC systems – moving beyond overall precision to error-specific recall and precision figures.

However, the paper suffers from a significant lack of detail in its system description and a largely unconvincing evaluation. The core of the system appears to rely on a set of manually crafted rules, yet the paper provides almost no information about these rules. Crucial details are missing: how many rules exist, their complexity, examples illustrating their application, and the potential for rule interaction (e.g., rule ordering and blocking). Instead of delving into the system's mechanics, a substantial portion of the paper is dedicated to presenting and reiterating results from the CoNLL-2014 shared task, largely duplicating information already present in Table 6. 

The evaluation methodology is also problematic. Rather than establishing a gold standard annotation of the 200 test sentences by independent annotators, the evaluation focuses on ratings of the system’s output. This approach is prone to inflated scores due to annotators evaluating pre-annotated data (as highlighted by Marcus et al., 1993). The paper reports an average “bad” edit rate of 3.7%, but a more disaggregated view reveals that 18.5% of edits received a “bad” rating from *at least one* annotator – a considerably more concerning figure. Furthermore, the paper lacks essential information about the test data itself, such as the number of error categories present and the distribution of “good” ratings across those categories. The meaning of “unusual edit boundaries” is also left vague, without explanation or illustrative examples, and its potential impact on system performance is unclear.

The authors claim their system is less domain-dependent than systems requiring training data. This claim is questionable. The reliance on resources like Hunspell’s vocabulary introduces domain limitations, and manually crafted rules are inherently domain and language specific – a disadvantage compared to machine learning approaches. The exclusive use of student essays (FCE-test, CoNLL-2014) further restricts the generalizability of the findings. 

The motivation behind the newly designed error category set is also unclear. While the authors suggest the new tags facilitate searching for underspecified categories, existing tagsets (e.g., Nicholls, 2003, or the CoNLL-2014 tagset) appear to offer similar functionality. Utilizing the CoNLL-2014 tagset would have enabled a more direct evaluation against the existing gold standard.

In summary, the paper's central contribution remains ambiguous. It’s unclear whether the focus is on a novel system (whose details are largely absent), a new error categorization scheme (without sufficient justification), or an evaluation of existing CoNLL-2014 systems (presented superficially).
