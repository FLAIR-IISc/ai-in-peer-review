The paper presents a novel approach for evaluating grammatical error correction (GEC) systems. This approach enables the assessment of GEC system performance by error type, including precision, which was previously largely inaccessible since system output is typically not annotated with error categories.

**Strengths:**

*   The proposed evaluation method represents an important advancement for analyzing GEC system behavior.
*   The paper includes a comprehensive evaluation across a variety of systems.
*   The approach offers several advantages over prior work: it computes precision by error type, is independent of manual error annotation, and can assess performance on multi-token errors.
*   Human experts largely validated the automatically assigned error tags for pre-computed error spans.

**Weaknesses:**

*   The core of the method – the rules for deriving error types – are not sufficiently detailed.
*   The classifier evaluation lacks a thorough error analysis, hindering the identification of avenues for improvement.
*   The evaluation is limited to English, and the challenges of adapting the approach to other languages are unclear.

**Classifier and Classifier Evaluation**

The foundations of the error categories used are not fully explained. It is unclear if they build upon existing frameworks. While the approach is generally independent of the alignment algorithm, the relationship between the rules and the alignment process isn’t clear. The error categories are central to the paper, and readers should be provided with a representative example of a rule used to assign an error type.

The paper lacks discussion of the effort required to adapt the approach to other languages.  The rules for edit boundary determination and error tag assignment likely depend on language-specific processing pipelines. The paper doesn’t address how these rules might need to be modified or if the error categories themselves would require adjustment. 

The use of continuous edit spans is a potential limitation, as it may struggle with errors that require discontinuous spans (such as separable verbs in German). The authors should acknowledge this limitation and discuss potential solutions.

The authors’ interpretation of the human evaluation results ("despite the degree of noise introduced by automatic edit extraction") is overly optimistic. Raters may have been more lenient due to noisy boundaries.  Furthermore, the lack of a "neutral" rating option could have biased responses towards "Acceptable." A more rigorous evaluation would involve assessing classifier output with manually created boundaries.

The classifier evaluation lacks detailed error analysis. Beyond noting that “Bad” ratings often correlate with POS tagging errors, the authors should analyze rating patterns to identify specific areas for classifier improvement.

**Gold Reference vs. Auto Reference**

The precise data used for the significance test is unclear. Testing on F0.5 scores, a derived metric, may not be the most sensitive approach.  Furthermore, the comparison between gold and automatic references appears to focus only on boundaries, not on classification.

**Error Type Evaluation**

The lack of surprise regarding some systems’ inability to correct specific error types (e.g., unnecessary tokens) is insufficient. The paper should analyze results in relation to each system’s design – whether the system was intended to address particular error types. Some errors have a straightforward mapping between gold and auto references, and this should be considered in the analysis.  The authors should also present raw TP, FP, TN, and FN values in the appendix to facilitate further comparison.  The limited instances for certain error types and systems should also be noted.

The claim that different approaches complement different error types is supported but would benefit from referencing relevant prior work.

**Multi-token Error Analysis**

The multi-token error analysis is valuable. However, the results should be further interpreted, acknowledging that some systems may be inherently unsuited to correcting these errors and that current GEC systems are generally not trained on parallel corpora of learner data and fluent corrections.

**Other**

*   The authors should acknowledge that some GEC approaches already allow for error annotations, even if they require adaptation. The proposed approach provides a unified framework for comparing systems with varying levels of annotation capability.
*   References contain formatting inconsistencies. URLs should be properly formatted, and page numbers should be included where applicable.
