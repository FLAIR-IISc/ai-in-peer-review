- Novel evaluation method enables per-error-type assessment of GEC systems, including precision, without manual annotation.  
- Paper presents a comprehensive evaluation across a variety of systems; human experts largely validated automatic error tags.  
- Method handles multi-token errors and is independent of manual error annotation.  
- Rules for deriving error types are insufficiently detailed and lack representative examples.  
- Relationship between the error-typing rules and the alignment algorithm is unclear.  
- Classifier evaluation lacks thorough error analysis, limiting identification of improvement avenues.  
- Paper does not discuss the effort or modifications required to adapt the approach to other languages.  
- Use of continuous edit spans may fail for discontinuous errors (e.g., separable verbs) and this limitation is not addressed.  
- Interpretation of human evaluation is overly optimistic; noisy boundaries and no “neutral” option may have biased ratings.  
- Classifier validation should include assessment against manually created boundaries rather than only automatic spans.  
- Significance-test data are unclear; using F0.5 may be insensitive, and the gold vs. auto comparison focuses on boundaries, not classification.  
- Analysis does not link system design to performance on specific error types; some system failures are unsurprising and need contextualization.  
- Authors should publish raw TP/FP/TN/FN counts and note sparse instances for certain error-type/system combinations.  
- Claim that different approaches complement different error types is plausible but needs citations to prior work.  
- Multi-token error analysis is useful but should acknowledge many systems are inherently unsuited and not trained on learner-fluent parallel data.  
- Paper should acknowledge some existing GEC methods already support error annotations and position the proposed method as a unifying framework.  
- References contain formatting inconsistencies (URLs, page numbers) that should be corrected.
