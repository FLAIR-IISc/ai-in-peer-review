This paper introduces a method for automatically annotating grammatical error correction (GEC) system outputs with error types, addressing a gap in evaluating GEC systems beyond overall scores. This is a valuable contribution as it could benefit both educators and learners by providing more granular feedback, and allows for a more nuanced comparison of GEC systems.

The core idea involves linguistically-enhanced alignment to extract edits between original and corrected sentences, followed by classification using a new rule-based framework. The authors demonstrate the approach on the CoNLL-2014 shared task data. A key strength lies in the framework's dataset independence, relying on automatic POS tagging and linguistic features rather than requiring labeled training data. Human evaluation indicates high agreement (over 95% rated as “Good” or “Acceptable”) with the system's error type assignments. The release of the tool accompanying the paper is also a positive aspect.

However, the paper suffers from several weaknesses. The description of the rule-based system is surprisingly superficial. While the authors mention a set of manually created rules, they provide limited detail – no examples, the number of rules, their complexity, or how they interact (e.g., rule ordering). Much of the paper is dedicated to presenting results from the CoNLL-2014 shared task, often simply restating information already apparent in tables, detracting from a deeper discussion of the proposed method.

The evaluation methodology is also questionable. Instead of creating a gold standard annotation of the test set *independently* for comparison, the evaluation relies on human ratings of the *system's* output. This introduces potential bias, as assessing pre-annotated data tends to yield higher agreement than annotating from scratch. Averaging individual rater scores is also problematic; reporting the percentage of edits receiving a “bad” rating from *any* annotator (around 18.5%) provides a more accurate picture of the system’s limitations than the reported average of 3.7%. Furthermore, the paper lacks information about the characteristics of the test data (e.g., the distribution of error categories). The meaning of “edit boundaries might be unusual” is unclear and requires elaboration – and an assessment of its potential impact.

The claim of reduced domain dependence is debatable. The reliance on resources like Hunspell, with its inherent vocabulary limitations, and the potential domain-specificity of manually crafted rules, challenges this assertion. The exclusive use of student essays (FCE-test and CoNLL-2014) as test data raises concerns about generalizability. 

The motivation for introducing a new error category scheme is weak. The stated benefit of easily searching for broader categories doesn’t justify abandoning existing, well-established tagsets like those used in CoNLL-2014. Utilizing the CoNLL-2014 tagset would have enabled a direct comparison against the existing gold standard.

In summary, while the paper presents a potentially useful application, its impact is diminished by a superficial system description, questionable evaluation methodology, and a lack of compelling justification for the proposed error categorization scheme.

**Questions to Authors:**

1.  Could you provide more detailed examples of the rules used in your error type classification framework? Specifically, how many rules are there, how are they ordered, and how do they interact with each other?
2.  Why did you choose to evaluate your system by assessing its agreement with human ratings of its *own* output, rather than creating a gold standard annotation of the test data?
3.  Can you provide a more detailed analysis of the cases where raters assigned a "Bad" rating, and how these errors might be addressed?
4.  How does the performance of your system vary across different domains beyond student essays?
5.  What are the limitations of the Hunspell dictionary in relation to different error types and domains?
