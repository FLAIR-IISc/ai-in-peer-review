The paper presents a novel approach for evaluating grammatical error correction (GEC) systems. This approach enables the assessment of GEC system performance by error type, considering both recall and precision, which was previously difficult to achieve due to the typical lack of error category annotations in system outputs.

Strengths:
 - The proposed evaluation represents a significant advancement for analyzing GEC system behavior.
 - The paper includes evaluation results for a diverse set of systems.
 - The approach offers several advantages over prior work:
   - It computes precision specifically by error type.
   - It operates independently of manual error annotation.
   - It facilitates the evaluation of performance on multi-token errors.
 - Human experts largely concur with the automatically assigned error tags for pre-computed error spans.

Weaknesses:
 - A crucial component – the rules used to derive error types – are not detailed.
 - The classifier evaluation lacks a comprehensive error analysis, and consequently, lacks guidance for future improvements to the classifier.
 - The evaluation was limited to English, and the difficulty of applying the approach to other languages remains unclear.

Classifier and Classifier Evaluation
====================================
The rationale behind the development of the error categories is unclear. Are they grounded in existing literature?
While the approach is generally independent of the alignment algorithm, the categorization rules likely are not, yet the authors do not provide these details. The error categories are central to the paper, and the reader deserves at least an illustrative example of a rule for assigning an error type.
Unfortunately, the proposed evaluation was not applied to languages other than English. The paper also does not discuss the necessary modifications to deploy the classifier in other languages. I anticipate that the rules for determining edit boundaries and error tags will be language- and pre-processing pipeline-dependent, requiring adaptation. Furthermore, the error categories themselves might need revision. The authors do not provide sufficient detail regarding the error category assignment rules (total number of categories, complexity of individual rules) to estimate the effort needed for adaptation to another language.
The error spans identified in the pre-processing stage appear to be inherently continuous (similar to the M2 scorer), which is a limitation as some errors require discontinuous spans for accurate tagging. For instance, in German, verbs with separable prefixes are often separated in the main clause: [1st constituent] [verb] [other constituents] [verb prefix]. Can the classifier identify such discontinuous edit spans?
The authors state that human judges rated at least 95% of automatically assigned error tags as appropriate “despite the degree of noise introduced by automatic edit extraction” (p. 295). I am cautious about this interpretation, as raters may have been more lenient given noisy boundaries. Moreover, they were not asked to select a tag without knowledge of the system output, potentially introducing bias. The absence of a rating option between “Bad (Not Appropriate)” and “Appropriate” may have further encouraged raters to choose “Appropriate”. To improve the evaluation’s robustness, the authors should also assess how human judges rate the classifier’s output with manually created boundaries, eliminating the noise from faulty boundaries.
The classifier evaluation lacks a thorough error analysis. It is only mentioned that “Bad” ratings are often linked to incorrect POS tags. I would like to see answers to the following questions: Under what circumstances did raters select “Bad” versus “Appropriate”? Does expert feedback suggest avenues for improving the classifier?

Gold Reference vs. Auto Reference
=================================
The specific data used for the significance test is unclear. Was the test performed on the F0.5 scores? If so, this may not be appropriate, as F0.5 is a derived measure with limited discriminative power (performance in terms of recall and precision can differ significantly while yielding the same F0.5 score). Additionally, the authors mention a mismatch between automatic and reference outputs regarding alignment and classification at the beginning of Section 4.1, but the comparison between gold and reference standards appears to be limited to boundaries, not classification.

Error Type Evaluation
=====================
It is unsurprising that five teams (~line 473) failed to correct any unnecessary token errors. For at least two of the systems, a clear explanation exists: UFC’s rule-based approach relies on POS tags (Ng et al., 2014), making it impossible to identify superfluous words based on POS alone. Rozovskaya & Roth (2016) explain AMU’s poor performance on superfluous words.
The authors do not analyze or comment on the results in Table 6 with respect to whether the systems were designed to address the specific error types. A straightforward mapping exists between error types in the gold standard and the automatic reference, such as for word order errors. It remains unclear whether the systems completely failed on specific error types or were simply not designed to correct them (e.g., CUUI achieves precision+recall=0.0, despite not targeting word order errors). The CUUI case (and likely others) suggests an error in classification, which is neither analyzed nor discussed.
Please also report raw values for TP, FP, TN, and FN in the appendix for Table 6. This will facilitate comparisons between systems using different metrics. Additionally, the results in Table 6 for some error types and systems appear to be based on a limited number of instances. This should be clarified when reporting raw values.
You write, “All but 2 teams (IITB and IPN) achieved the best score in at least 1 category, which suggests that different approaches to GEC complement different error types” (p. 606). It would be beneficial to acknowledge that this observation aligns with previous research.
The multi-token error analysis is valuable for future work, but the results require further interpretation: some systems are inherently incapable of correcting such errors, and none of the systems were trained on a parallel corpus of learner data and fluent corrections (in the sense of Sakaguchi et al., 2016).

Other
=====
- The authors should acknowledge that error annotations were previously possible for some GEC approaches, such as systems with dedicated submodules for each error type. While such systems would require adaptation to include submodule output in system outputs, the proposed approach allows for a unified comparison of GEC systems, including those for which generating tagged outputs is not straightforward.
- References: Some titles lack capitalization. The URL for Sakaguchi et al. (2016) should be enclosed in angle brackets. Page information is missing for Efron and Tibshirani (1993).

Author response
===============
I concur that this approach is not “fatally flawed” and appreciate that the review highlights several positive aspects. The approach is sound, but the paper requires further development.
The foundation of the paper lies in the rules for classifying errors, and the lack of detailed description is a significant drawback. This is not simply a matter of providing additional examples. If the rules are intended as a generalizable implementation, they must be described to enable replication or adaptation.
Generalization to other languages should not be an afterthought. A serious limitation would be if the approach is inherently restricted to a single language. Even without adapting the approach to other languages, it should be transparent enough for others to estimate the effort required for adaptation and its potential success. Simply stating that most research focuses on ESL only exacerbates this issue.
You suggest that the error types addressed by certain systems are “usually obvious from the tables”. I disagree; the CUUI example and the unnecessary token errors demonstrate this. Five systems do not correct unnecessary token errors (Table 5), indicating they did not attempt to address them. However, the paper states, “There is also no obvious explanation as to why these teams had difficulty with this error type.”
