- Proposed evaluation method advances GEC analysis by measuring precision and recall by error type without needing manual error-category annotations.
- Paper includes a comprehensive evaluation across diverse GEC systems.
- Method supports precision by error type, is independent of manual annotation, and can assess multi-token errors.
- Human experts largely validated the automatically assigned error tags for pre-computed error spans.

- The rules used to derive error types are not described in the paper.
- Classifier evaluation lacks a comprehensive error analysis, limiting insight into improvement directions.
- Evaluation is limited to English and the effort required for cross-lingual adaptation is unclear.

- The basis and provenance of the error categories are unclear and likely depend on undocumented rules and/or alignments; illustrative rule examples are needed.
- No details on adapting the classifier for other languages, including edit boundary determination, tagging rules, and possible category changes.
- Continuous pre-processing error spans may fail on discontinuous edits (e.g., German separable verbs); classifier suitability is unclear.
- Human validation (≥95% appropriate) may be biased by noisy automatic boundaries and the rating setup; manual-boundary validation is recommended.
- The classifier evaluation should analyze what leads to “Bad” vs “Appropriate” ratings and whether expert feedback suggests concrete fixes.

- It is unclear which data were used for the significance tests; using F0.5 is problematic because differing precision/recall can yield the same score.
- Comparisons between gold and auto references focus on boundary agreement and omit classification discrepancy analysis.

- Failure of several systems to correct unnecessary tokens is unsurprising and may be explained by system design (e.g., rule-based POS reliance).
- Results (e.g., Table 6) are not contextualized by each system’s design goals, leaving unclear whether failures reflect capability or intent.
- Raw contingency counts (TP/FP/TN/FN) and underlying instance counts are missing and should be provided for clarity and alternative metrics.
- The claim that different approaches complement different error types should acknowledge prior related findings.
- Multi-token error analysis is promising but needs more interpretation; some systems may be inherently incapable and none were trained on fluent-corrected learner corpora.

- Paper should acknowledge prior GEC systems with error-specific submodules and discuss adapting them for comparison; the proposed approach enables unified comparison across systems.
- Reference formatting and bibliographic details need correction (capitalization, URLs, missing page info).

- Author agrees approach is promising but paper isn’t ready; rules must be described for replicability and adaptation.
- Generalization to other languages must be addressed explicitly; transparency is needed so others can estimate adaptation effort.
- Tables do not make system design or failure causes obvious (e.g., CUUI example); deeper analysis is required.
