- Summary: Proposes automatic annotation of GEC outputs with error types using linguistically-enhanced alignment and a rule-based typing framework to enable detailed error-type analysis beyond recall.

- Contribution 1: Novel method to automatically annotate system outputs with error-type information for detailed GEC analysis.

- Contribution 2: Dataset-independent, deterministic rule-based error typing framework that requires no labeled training data.

- Contribution 3: Comprehensive error-type analysis of all systems in CoNLL-2014, showing different approaches excel on different error categories.

- Strength 1: Addresses a genuine gap in GEC evaluation by enabling fine-grained error-type analysis rather than only recall.

- Strength 2: Rule-based framework is simple and practical, avoiding labeled data and dataset-specific classifiers.

- Strength 3: Application to CoNLL-2014 yields useful insights about complementary strengths of different GEC approaches (e.g., MT vs. classifiers).

- Weakness 1: Description of the rule-based system is superficial—no examples, rule count, ordering, or interactions are given to assess novelty or limitations.

- Weakness 2: Evaluation methodology is flawed—raters judged system outputs rather than creating an independent gold standard, and averaging ratings without proper inter-annotator analysis may misrepresent quality.

- Weakness 3: Motivation for a new error taxonomy is unclear; the paper doesn’t justify superiority over existing taxonomies or the claim of improved “searchability.”

- Weakness 4: Claim of dataset-independence is doubtful since the approach relies on Hunspell and manual rules and was tested only on student essays, raising domain/language generalization concerns.

- Weakness 5: Presentation of CoNLL-2014 results is repetitive and superficial, offering little meaningful analysis beyond tabulated data.

- Question 1: Provide concrete examples of the rule-based error typing rules, their number, ordering, and prioritization.

- Question 2: Why create a new error taxonomy instead of using existing ones, and how is it superior for GEC evaluation?

- Question 3: What evidence supports the approach’s applicability across different domains or languages beyond student essays?

- Question 4: How were “bad” ratings distributed across edits, and how would multiple “bad” ratings for a single edit affect the claimed 95% “Good”/“Acceptable” figure?
