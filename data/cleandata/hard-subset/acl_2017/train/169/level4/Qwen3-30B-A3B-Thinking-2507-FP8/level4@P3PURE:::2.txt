

The paper presents a novel approach for evaluating grammatical error correction (GEC) systems, enabling assessment by error type in terms of both recall and precision—previously unattainable since system outputs are typically unannotated with error categories.

**Strengths:**
- The proposed evaluation represents an important step toward analyzing GEC system behavior.
- The paper includes evaluations across a diverse set of systems.
- The approach offers several advantages over prior work:
  - Computation of precision by error type.
  - Independence from manual error annotation.
  - Capability to assess performance on multi-token errors.
- Automatically selected error tags for pre-computed error spans are largely approved by human experts.

**Weaknesses:**
- The rules for deriving error types are not described, which is a critical omission.
- The classifier evaluation lacks thorough error analysis and does not provide directions for future improvements.
- The evaluation is limited to English, and the paper fails to address the effort required to adapt the approach to other languages.

**Classifier and Classifier Evaluation:**
The basis for the error categories is unclear—were they derived from prior work? While the approach claims independence from alignment algorithms, the rules likely depend on them, yet the authors provide no details. Error categories are central to the paper; readers deserve examples of rule structures (e.g., number of rules, complexity, error type distribution) to understand implementation and adaptation effort.  

The pre-processing step computes inherently continuous error spans (like the M2 scorer), which is problematic for discontinuous errors (e.g., German separable verbs: *ab* + *gehen* → *ab* [verb] [constituents] *gehen*). The classifier’s ability to tag such spans remains unaddressed.  

The claim that human judges rated ≥95% of automatically assigned tags as appropriate "despite noise from automatic edit extraction" (p. 295) is questionable. Raters may have been biased toward system output when boundaries were noisy and lacked nuanced rating options (e.g., no intermediate "Partially Appropriate" between "Bad" and "Appropriate"). To strengthen validity, the authors should evaluate human ratings on manually created boundaries (without noise).  

The classifier evaluation lacks error analysis. For instance, the note that "Bad" ratings typically stem from incorrect POS tags requires deeper investigation: *When* did raters select "Bad" vs. "Appropriate"? Do expert ratings suggest concrete classifier improvements?

**Gold Reference vs. Auto Reference:**
The significance test’s exact basis is unclear—was it performed on F₀.₅ scores? Using F₀.₅ is problematic, as it is a derived metric with weak discriminative power (different recall/precision profiles can yield identical F₀.₅ scores). Additionally, Section 4.1 references alignment/classification mismatches between automatic and reference, but the gold vs. auto comparison only addresses boundary alignment, not classification.

**Error Type Evaluation:**
It is unsurprising that ~5 teams (p. 473) achieved zero recall on unnecessary token errors. For instance, UFC’s rule-based approach (Ng et al., 2014) operates on POS tags alone, making superfluous word detection impossible. Rozovskaya & Roth (2016) explain AMU’s poor performance on such errors. The paper fails to analyze whether system failures on specific error types (e.g., CUUI, precision+recall=0.0) reflect design limitations (e.g., CUUI not targeting word order errors) or classification errors. This ambiguity requires explicit discussion.  

Raw TP/FP/TN/FN values for Table 6 should be included in the appendix to enable alternative metric comparisons. The paper also does not clarify if results for certain error types/systems are based on minimal instances.

The statement that "all but 2 teams (IITB and IPN) achieved the best score in at least 1 category" (p. 606) aligns with prior work on complementary error-type handling—this should be explicitly noted.

Multi-token error analysis is valuable but needs deeper interpretation. None of the evaluated systems were trained on parallel learner data with fluent corrections (Sakaguchi et al., 2016), so system inability to handle such errors may not indicate a flaw but a training limitation.

**Other:**
- The paper should acknowledge that error annotations were feasible for some GEC systems (e.g., those with error-type-specific submodules), though adapting them to produce error-tagged outputs would require modification. The proposed approach uniquely enables unified comparison for systems where such annotation is non-trivial.
- References: Capitalize titles appropriately; wrap Sakaguchi et al. (2016) URL; add page numbers for Efron & Tibshirani (1993).

**Author Response:**
I agree the approach is not "fatally flawed" and your review highlights key strengths. However, the paper is not ready for publication. The lack of description for the error classification rules is a major flaw—it is not merely a matter of providing examples. If the rules are not documented for replication or adaptation, the approach lacks reproducibility. Generalization to other languages should not be an afterthought; the paper must transparently estimate adaptation effort. Stating that most research targets ESL only exacerbates this limitation.  

Your critique of the CUUI example and unnecessary token errors is valid. The paper claims "no obvious explanation" for teams struggling with unnecessary tokens (Table 5), yet five systems failed to correct them—this clearly indicates they were not designed to handle such errors. The paper’s assertion that this was "not obvious from the tables" contradicts the data.
