- Two-step approach: linguistically-enhanced alignment to extract edits, then a rule-based classifier using automatic linguistic features to label error types in GEC outputs.

- Primary contribution: enables detailed error-type analysis (precision and recall) for GEC systems.

- Secondary contribution: dataset-independent, rule-based classifier that needs no labeled training data.

- Tertiary contribution: comprehensive error-type analysis of all CoNLL-2014 shared task systems.

- Strength: addresses an important gap in GEC evaluation by allowing per-error-type analysis.

- Strength: rule-based method is transparent and more broadly applicable than dataset-dependent ML approaches.

- Strength: human evaluation shows 95%+ of predicted error types rated "Good" or "Acceptable," supporting reliability.

- Weakness: insufficient detail about the 25 error-type rules, hindering replication and assessment.

- Weakness: classifier evaluation lacks deep error-analysis (which error types fail and why) and concrete improvement suggestions.

- Weakness: evaluation limited to English with no discussion of generalization to other languages or language-specific phenomena.

- Weakness: human evaluation may be biased because raters knew boundaries were automatic; no comparison with manual boundaries.

- Question: provide specific rule examples (e.g., why "could → should" is labeled tense vs. modal and which rule decides this).

- Question: how would the approach handle discontinuous edit spans (e.g., German separable verbs) and what rule changes are needed?

- Question: was there a separate evaluation with manually created boundaries to assess error-type classification independent of boundary quality?

- Question: clarify which CoNLL-2014 systems were designed to handle unnecessary tokens and why five teams failed on those errors.

- Question: estimate the effort and which system components would need modification to adapt the approach to languages with different grammatical structures.
