

The paper proposes enriching GEC system outputs with explicit error types, offering a potentially valuable tool for educators and learners by enabling fine-grained system comparison. However, significant weaknesses in system description, evaluation methodology, and motivation undermine the contribution.

**Strengths:** The core application is compelling. Explicit error typing addresses a clear gap in current GEC outputs (which typically provide only corrected text), directly benefiting pedagogical use and nuanced system benchmarking via error-type-specific precision/recall metrics.

**Critical Weaknesses:**

1.  **Superficial System Description:** The paper’s core mechanism—a rule-based error type classifier—is described only at a surface level. Crucially, it omits essential details: the number of rules, their complexity, ordering logic (and potential conflicts between rules), and concrete examples. Instead of clarifying the system, the paper devotes excessive space to listing CoNLL-2014 results (Table 6, one full page) with redundant textual repetition.

2.  **Flawed Evaluation Methodology:**
    *   **Lack of Gold Standard:** Annotators rated *system outputs* rather than independently creating a gold standard for the 200 test sentences. Given the fixed tagset and small test size, a true gold standard is feasible. Relying on pre-annotated outputs (as noted by Marcus et al., 1993) likely inflates agreement scores compared to annotating from scratch.
    *   **Misreported Inter-Annotator Agreement:** The claim that "all 5 raters individually considered at least 95% of error types as 'Good' or 'Acceptable'" is misleading. Reporting the *average* 3.7% "bad" rate obscures that 18.5% of edits were deemed "bad" by *at least one* rater (the paper provides no distribution of these disagreements). This discrepancy significantly weakens the evaluation's credibility.
    *   **Insufficient Test Data Specification:** The paper fails to describe the test data's error category distribution, the specific categories covered, or the proportion of categories rated "good" by annotators. This omission prevents understanding the scope and representativeness of the evaluation.
    *   **Unclear Ambiguity:** The phrase "edit boundaries might be unusual" lacks definition and examples. The paper does not explain *why* this is problematic or how it impacts system utility.

3.  **Unsubstantiated Claims & Motivational Ambiguity:**
    *   The assertion that the system is "less domain dependent" than training-data approaches is unsupported. Manually crafted rules are inherently domain-dependent (e.g., Hunspell's vocabulary coverage varies by domain), and the evaluation uses only student essay data (FCE test, CoNLL-2014), providing no evidence of cross-domain robustness.
    *   The rationale for designing a new error tagset is weak. The stated goal (enabling searches for underspecified categories like "NOUN") is achievable with existing sets (e.g., Nicholls, 2003) or the CoNLL-2014 tagset itself (which would have allowed direct use of the CoNLL gold standard for evaluation). The paper offers no justification for this deviation.

**Conclusion:** The paper’s primary contribution remains ambiguous. It is unclear whether the focus is on a novel system (whose critical details are omitted), a novel tagset (with insufficient motivation), or an evaluation of CoNLL-2014 systems (presented superficially). The evaluation methodology contains fundamental flaws that invalidate key claims, while the system description lacks the necessary detail for reproducibility or technical assessment. The potential utility of error typing is overshadowed by these execution shortcomings.
