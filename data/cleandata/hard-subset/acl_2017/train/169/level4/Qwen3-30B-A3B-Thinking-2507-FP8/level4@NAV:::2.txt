

The paper introduces a novel methodology for evaluating grammatical error correction (GEC) systems, enabling performance assessment by error type—not only for recall but also for precision—previously unattainable as system outputs typically lack error category annotations.  

Strengths:  
- The evaluation framework constitutes a significant advancement for analyzing GEC system behavior.  
- The paper evaluates diverse GEC systems comprehensively.  
- Key advantages over prior work include:  
  - Precision computation per error type  
  - Independence from manual error annotation  
  - Capability to assess multi-token errors  
- Automatically assigned error tags for pre-computed error spans received strong approval from human experts.  

Weaknesses:  
- The rules for deriving error types are inadequately described.  
- The classifier evaluation lacks thorough error analysis and actionable future work directions.  
- Evaluation is limited to English; the feasibility of adapting the approach to other languages is unaddressed.  

Classifier and Classifier Evaluation  
====================================  
The basis for error category design remains unclear—whether derived from prior work or otherwise. While the approach claims independence from alignment algorithms, the rules likely depend on them; this dependency is unexplained. Error categories are central to the paper, yet no examples of rule implementation (e.g., total categories, complexity per type) are provided, hindering replication or adaptation estimates.  

The pre-processing step generates inherently continuous error spans (like the M2 scorer), which cannot accurately tag discontinuous errors (e.g., German separable verb prefixes: [1st constituent] [verb] [other constituents] [verb prefix]). The classifier’s ability to handle such cases is unverified.  

The claim that human judges rated ≥95% of automatically assigned tags as appropriate (p. 295) requires caution. Raters may have been biased toward system outputs due to noisy boundaries, and the absence of a middle rating option ("Bad" vs. "Appropriate") likely inflated approval rates. A more robust evaluation would compare human ratings on manually created boundaries.  

The classifier evaluation omits error analysis: it states "Bad" ratings stem from incorrect POS tags but fails to clarify *when* raters selected "Bad" or "Appropriate," or how these insights could improve the classifier.  

Gold Reference vs. Auto Reference  
=================================  
The significance test’s exact data (e.g., F₀.₅ scores) is unspecified. Using F₀.₅ for significance testing is suboptimal due to its weak discriminative power (differing recall/precision pairs may yield identical scores). Additionally, the gold-reference vs. auto-reference comparison focuses solely on boundary alignment, not classification, contradicting earlier claims about classification mismatches.  

Error Type Evaluation  
===================  
The observation that ~5 teams failed to correct unnecessary token errors (p. 473) is unsurprising. UFC’s rule-based approach (Ng et al., 2014) inherently cannot handle superfluous words via POS tags alone; Rozovskaya & Roth (2016) explain AMU’s poor performance on this error type. The paper fails to analyze whether systems were *designed* to address specific error types (e.g., CUUI’s precision+recall=0.0 despite not targeting word order errors), suggesting potential classification errors unaddressed in the analysis.  

Raw TP/FP/TN/FN values for Table 6 should be provided in an appendix for transparency and cross-measure comparison. Some error-type results appear based on minimal instances, which should be explicitly noted. The claim that "all but 2 teams achieved best scores in ≥1 category" (p. 606) aligns with prior research—this should be acknowledged.  

Multi-token error analysis requires deeper interpretation: no systems were trained on learner-data parallel corpora (Sakaguchi et al., 2016), so inherent limitations may not reflect model capability.  

Other  
=====  
- The claim that error annotation was "impossible" for some systems (e.g., those with error-type-specific submodules) is overstated. While adaptation would be needed, the proposed approach enables unified comparison across systems with and without error-tagged outputs.  
- References require correction: inconsistent capitalization, un-wrapped URL for Sakaguchi et al. (2016), missing page numbers for Efron & Tibshirani (1993).  

Author response  
===============  
I concur the approach is not "fatally flawed" and highlights positive aspects, but the paper is incomplete. The absence of error-type rule descriptions is a critical flaw, not merely a matter of additional examples—without this, replication or adaptation is impossible.  

Generalization to other languages must be addressed proactively, not as an afterthought. Stating that most research targets ESL only exacerbates the limitation. The assertion that error-type system capabilities are "usually obvious from the tables" is inaccurate (e.g., CUUI’s failure on word order errors vs. unnecessary tokens). Five systems omitted unnecessary token correction (Table 5), yet the paper claims "no obvious explanation" for their difficulty—this contradiction requires resolution.
