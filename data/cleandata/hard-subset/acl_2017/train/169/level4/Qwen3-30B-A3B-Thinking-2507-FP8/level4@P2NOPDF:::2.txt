

The paper introduces a novel method for evaluating grammatical error correction (GEC) systems by error type, enabling precision-based assessment alongside recall—a capability previously unattainable due to the lack of error-category annotations in system outputs.  

**Strengths:**  
- This evaluation framework significantly advances the analysis of GEC system behavior.  
- It evaluates diverse systems, demonstrating broad applicability.  
- Key advantages over prior work include:  
  (i) precision computation per error type,  
  (ii) independence from manual error annotation,  
  (iii) capability to assess multi-token errors.  
- Human experts largely approved automatically assigned error tags, validating the approach’s reliability.  

**Weaknesses:**  
1. **Insufficient detail on error categorization rules:** The paper omits critical descriptions of how error types are derived (e.g., number of rules, complexity, or basis in prior work). Without this, replication or adaptation is infeasible.  
2. **Inadequate classifier evaluation:** The error analysis lacks depth, failing to identify why "Bad" ratings occur (e.g., tied to POS tag errors) or suggest concrete improvements.  
3. **Limited language scalability:** The evaluation is restricted to English, with no discussion of necessary adaptations for other languages. Rules for error tagging and edit boundaries likely depend on linguistic features or preprocessing pipelines (e.g., German separable verbs like "anrufen" split as "an rufen"), yet the paper provides no guidance on estimating adaptation effort.  
4. **Rater bias concerns:** Human evaluations of automatically tagged errors (with noisy boundaries) may have been lenient due to rater familiarity with system outputs. The absence of a "neutral" rating option and lack of manual-boundary validation weaken the reliability of the 95% approval rate claim.  
5. **Gold vs. auto-reference ambiguity:** Significance tests appear to use F0.5 scores (which lack discriminative power), and the comparison between gold and auto references focuses only on boundaries, not classification alignment.  
6. **Misinterpretation of system failures:** Table 6 results for error types like "unnecessary token" (e.g., 5 teams failing to correct them) are not contextualized against system design. For instance, UFC’s POS-based approach inherently cannot handle superfluous words, yet the paper wrongly claims no "obvious explanation" exists. The CUUI case (precision+recall=0.0 for non-word-order errors) suggests classification errors but receives no analysis.  
7. **Missing technical details:** Raw TP/FP/TN/FN values for Table 6 are absent, hindering alternative metric comparisons. Some error-type results appear based on minimal instances, which should be flagged.  

**Specific Recommendations:**  
- **Error categorization:** Provide concrete examples of error-type rules (e.g., "Rule 3: Tag 'Word Order' if subject-verb inversion occurs in main clauses").  
- **Classifier evaluation:** Include error analysis by rating ("Bad" vs. "Appropriate") and validate against manually tagged boundaries.  
- **Language generalization:** Explicitly address linguistic dependencies (e.g., "German requires X adaptation due to Y") and discuss how error categories might need redefinition.  
- **Table 6 interpretation:** Clarify whether system failures reflect design limitations (e.g., "AMU’s poor superfluous-word handling aligns with Rozovskaya & Roth, 2016") or classification flaws. Report raw counts in the appendix.  
- **Other:** Correct reference capitalization (e.g., "Sakaguchi et al., 2016") and include page numbers for Efron & Tibshirani (1993).  

**Additional Note:** While the approach is valuable, the paper understates its limitations. The claim that "all but 2 teams achieved best scores in at least one category" (line 606) should cite prior work confirming this complementarity. The method’s utility for systems with error-specific submodules (e.g., POS-based GEC) is acknowledged but not contrasted with existing capabilities.  

**Overall:** The core idea is promising but requires substantial revisions to clarify methodology, contextualize results, and address generalizability. Without these, the contribution lacks sufficient rigor for publication.
