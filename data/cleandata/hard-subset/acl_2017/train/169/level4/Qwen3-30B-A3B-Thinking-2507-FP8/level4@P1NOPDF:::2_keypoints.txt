- Introduces a novel method to evaluate GEC systems by error type, enabling precision and recall assessment without manual error-category annotations.  
- Evaluation framework is a meaningful advance and is tested on multiple GEC systems.  
- Key technical advantages: computes error-type-specific precision, requires no manual annotation, and handles multi-token errors.  
- Automatically assigned error tags largely agree with human expert judgments.  
- Core rules for deriving error categories are not described (no examples, counts, or distributions), making the method non-replicable and lacking transparency.  
- Classifier evaluation is insufficient: missing detailed error analysis, unclear rater criteria for "Bad" vs "Appropriate," and reliance on noisy auto-extracted boundaries may bias human judgments; authors should compare against manually corrected boundaries.  
- Scope is limited to English with no discussion of adapting rules to other languages; language-specific phenomena likely require rule changes.  
- Ambiguity in gold vs. auto-reference comparisons: using F0.5 conflates precision/recall trade-offs and the analysis focuses on boundary alignment rather than classification consistency.  
- Error-type analysis is incomplete: fails to link system design to poor performance on specific errors, ignores apparent classification errors (e.g., CUUI = 0.0), and omits raw TP/FP/TN/FN counts and sample-size clarity for reported results.  
- The claim that error-type annotation was previously impossible is overstated—some prior systems produce error-type outputs; the method’s contribution is unifying evaluation for systems without such outputs.  
- Presentation issues: reference formatting errors and a misleading claim that error types are obvious from tables despite counterexamples (e.g., unnecessary-token failures).  
- Overall judgment: method is valuable but paper is not ready for publication until error-categorization rules, multilingual adaptation, and deeper error/system analyses are addressed.
