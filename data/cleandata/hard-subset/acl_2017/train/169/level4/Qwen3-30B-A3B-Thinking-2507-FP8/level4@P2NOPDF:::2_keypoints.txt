- Introduces a novel method to evaluate GEC systems by error type, enabling per-type precision as well as recall.  
- Evaluation framework advances GEC analysis and applies across diverse systems.  
- Key advantages: computes precision per error type, avoids manual error annotations, and handles multi-token errors.  
- Human experts largely approved the automatic error tags, supporting reliability.

- Insufficient detail on error-categorization rules (number, complexity, provenance), preventing replication or adaptation.  
- Inadequate classifier evaluation: no analysis of why items get "Bad" ratings or concrete suggestions for improvement.  
- Limited language scalability: evaluation restricted to English with no guidance on adapting rules or boundaries to other languages or preprocessing differences.  
- Rater bias concerns: possible leniency from rater familiarity, no "neutral" option, and lack of manual-boundary validation weaken the 95% approval claim.  
- Ambiguity in gold vs. auto-reference comparisons: use of F0.5 for significance testing and focus on boundaries rather than classification alignment is problematic.  
- Misinterpretation of system failures: failures (e.g., inability to remove superfluous tokens) are not contextualized with system design limitations or classification errors.  
- Missing technical details: raw TP/FP/TN/FN counts for Table 6 are absent and some error-type results appear based on very few instances.

- Recommendation: provide concrete error-type rules and examples to enable replication.  
- Recommendation: expand classifier evaluation by analyzing "Bad" vs. "Appropriate" cases and validating against manual boundaries.  
- Recommendation: explicitly address language-generalization issues and required adaptations (e.g., German separable verbs).  
- Recommendation: clarify Table 6 interpretations (design limitation vs. classifier error) and report raw counts in the appendix.  
- Recommendation: correct minor reference and formatting errors (e.g., capitalization, page numbers).

- Additional note: approach is valuable but understates limitations; claims about team complementarity need citations and comparison to systems with error-specific submodules.  
- Overall: promising contribution but requires substantial methodological clarification, contextualized analysis, and generalizability work before publication.
