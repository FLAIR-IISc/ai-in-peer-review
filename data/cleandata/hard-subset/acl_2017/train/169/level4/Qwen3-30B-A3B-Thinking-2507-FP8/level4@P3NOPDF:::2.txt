

The paper introduces a novel method for granular evaluation of grammatical error correction (GEC) systems by error type, enabling precision-based assessment alongside recall—a significant advancement over prior approaches that lacked error-type granularity. Strengths include its potential to advance GEC system analysis, thorough evaluation across diverse systems, and key advantages: error-type precision computation, independence from manual error annotation, and support for multi-token errors. Human experts largely validated the automatically assigned error tags, enhancing credibility.

However, the paper requires major revisions to address critical methodological gaps. The most urgent issue is the **absence of detailed error-type rules**, which constitute the core innovation. The paper states error categories were devised but provides no examples, rules, or justification for their design (e.g., derivation from prior work, category count, complexity). Without this, the approach is irreproducible and its adaptability to other languages cannot be assessed. The claim that "most research targets ESL" fails to address the fundamental need for transparent, language-agnostic rule specifications.  

The **classifier evaluation lacks depth**. The paper notes "Bad" ratings typically stem from POS errors but omits: (1) when raters selected "Bad" vs. "Appropriate," (2) whether the classifier’s weaknesses (e.g., handling discontinuous errors like German separable verbs) could be systematically improved, and (3) how human judgments might be biased by noisy automatic boundaries (raters saw system output, potentially inflating "Appropriate" ratings). A robust evaluation would compare manual vs. automatic boundary results.  

**Generalization to other languages is inadequately addressed**. The evaluation is confined to English, yet the rules for error tagging and edit boundaries likely depend on language-specific features (e.g., morphology, pre-processing). The paper does not discuss adaptation effort, required rule modifications, or whether error categories need redefinition. The claim that "the approach is language-agnostic" is unsubstantiated without concrete analysis of these dependencies.  

**Analysis of system performance is flawed**. The paper attributes zero scores for unnecessary token errors (e.g., CUUI in Table 6) to "no obvious explanation," yet Table 5 shows five systems failed to correct them. For UFC, the rule-based approach inherently cannot handle superfluous words (Ng et al., 2014), and AMU’s limitations are documented (Rozovskaya & Roth, 2016). The paper should explicitly link system design to error-type performance rather than treating failures as unexplained. Similarly, the claim that "all but 2 teams achieved best scores in at least one category" (line 606) should cite prior work showing this complementarity is expected.  

**Methodological inconsistencies undermine results**. The significance test uses F0.5 scores (Section 4.1), which lack discriminative power—systems with divergent precision/recall can share identical F0.5 values. The comparison between gold and auto references focuses solely on alignment boundaries, ignoring classification mismatches (e.g., error type assignment). Raw counts (TP/FP/TN/FN) for Table 6 should be provided in the appendix to enable alternative metric comparisons, especially given sparse data for some error types/systems.  

**Minor issues require attention**:  
- References: Capitalization errors in titles; Sakaguchi et al. (2016) URL needs wrapping; missing page numbers for Efron & Tibshirani (1993).  
- The paper incorrectly states that error-type annotation was "previously impossible" for systems without error-specific submodules (e.g., rule-based systems with targeted submodules could generate such annotations). The proposed method’s value lies in unified evaluation, not novelty of error tagging per se.  

**Recommendation**: Major revision. The core innovation—error-type precision—holds promise but is undermined by insufficient methodological detail. The authors must:  
1. Provide explicit, reproducible rules for error categorization (with examples).  
2. Conduct a thorough classifier error analysis (e.g., error type by error category, not just POS).  
3. Demonstrate language-adaptation feasibility (e.g., via a brief German case study or detailed adaptation roadmap).  
4. Correct flawed system-performance analysis (CUUI, unnecessary tokens) and clarify F0.5 usage.  
Without these, the paper’s contribution cannot be validated or extended. The current draft presents an incomplete framework, not a ready-to-use method.
