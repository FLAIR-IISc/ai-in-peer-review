- Introduces a novel method to evaluate GEC systems by error type, enabling precision and recall per category without requiring system outputs to be pre-annotated.

- Evaluation across a diverse set of GEC systems demonstrates the method’s applicability.

- Key advantages include computing precision per error type, avoiding manual error annotation, and handling multi-token errors.

- Automatically selected error tags for pre-computed error spans were judged appropriate by human experts.

- The rules for deriving error types are inadequately described, harming reproducibility.

- Likely dependence on alignment/preprocessing rules is not documented, contradicting claims of independence from alignment algorithms.

- No example rule is provided for assigning error types, leaving readers unable to assess rule complexity or scope.

- Classifier evaluation lacks detailed error analysis and concrete recommendations for improvement.

- Human rating methodology is flawed: lack of an intermediate rating option and possible bias toward system output may have inflated “Appropriate” ratings.

- Human evaluation should be repeated using manually created edit boundaries to validate classifier-tag appropriateness.

- Evaluation is limited to English and omits discussion of cross-lingual adaptation challenges and required preprocessing/rule changes.

- The approach may struggle with discontinuous edits (e.g., German separable verbs), but this is not explored.

- The claim of 95% appropriate human ratings needs caution due to potential rater bias and noisy boundary effects.

- Comparison between gold and auto references appears limited to boundaries; classification mismatches are not thoroughly compared.

- Use of F0.5 in significance testing is problematic due to weak discriminative power and unclear reporting of exact data/metrics.

- Error-type system analyses are shallow: the paper fails to analyze why systems succeeded or failed on specific error types (e.g., word order, unnecessary tokens).

- Some reported per-system/per-type results are based on few instances; raw TP/FP/TN/FN counts should be provided (e.g., in an appendix).

- The observation that different teams excel in different categories aligns with prior work and should be acknowledged.

- Multi-token error performance may reflect lack of appropriate training data rather than system or classifier failure; this nuance is not discussed.

- The authors should acknowledge that some systems could feasibly output error-type annotations (e.g., via submodules), and position their method relative to such systems.

- Presentation issues: inconsistent reference capitalization, a problematic URL formatting, and incomplete bibliographic details (Efron & Tibshirani).
