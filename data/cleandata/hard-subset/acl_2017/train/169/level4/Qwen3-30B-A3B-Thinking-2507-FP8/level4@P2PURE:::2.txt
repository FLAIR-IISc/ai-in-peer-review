

The paper introduces a novel method for evaluating grammatical error correction (GEC) systems by error type, enabling assessment of both precision and recall per error category—a capability previously unattainable since system outputs are typically unannotated with error types.

Strengths:
- The proposed evaluation represents a significant advancement for analyzing GEC system behavior.
- The paper evaluates a diverse range of GEC systems.
- Key advantages over prior work include:
  - Computation of precision per error type
  - Independence from manual error annotation
  - Capability to assess performance on multi-token errors
- Automatically selected error tags for pre-computed error spans received strong approval from human experts.

Weaknesses:
- The rules for deriving error types are inadequately described.
- The classifier evaluation lacks thorough error analysis and fails to propose concrete directions for classifier improvement.
- Evaluation is limited to English, with no discussion of challenges for adapting the approach to other languages.

Classifier and Classifier Evaluation
====================================
It is unclear how error categories were derived—whether based on prior work. Although the approach claims independence from alignment algorithms, the rules likely depend on them, yet details are omitted. Error categories are central to the paper; readers require at least one example rule for assigning error types.

The paper evaluates only English, omitting discussion of adaptations needed for other languages. Rules for edit boundary determination and error tagging likely depend on language-specific preprocessing, requiring adaptation. Error categories may also need modification. The authors provide no details on the number, complexity, or scope of error assignment rules, hindering estimation of cross-lingual adaptation effort.

Pre-computed error spans appear inherently continuous (like the M2 scorer), which is problematic for discontinuous errors. For instance, German separable verbs split across clauses (e.g., [first constituent] [verb] [other constituents] [verb prefix])—can the classifier accurately tag such spans?

The claim that "all human judges rated at least 95% of automatically assigned error tags as appropriate despite noise from automatic edit extraction" (p. 295) requires caution. Raters may have been biased toward system output when boundaries were noisy, as they evaluated tags without knowing the system output. The absence of an intermediate rating option between "Bad" and "Appropriate" likely skewed results toward "Appropriate." To strengthen validity, the authors should evaluate human ratings of classifier output with manually created boundaries (no noise).

The classifier evaluation lacks error analysis. It notes "Bad" ratings typically stem from incorrect POS tagging but does not address: When did raters select "Bad" versus "Appropriate"? Do expert ratings reveal specific classifier improvements?

Gold Reference vs. Auto Reference
=================================
The significance test's exact data and metrics (e.g., F0.5 scores) are unclear. Using F0.5 is problematic due to its weak discriminative power (differing recall/precision pairs may yield identical scores). Section 4.1 mentions alignment/classification mismatches between gold and reference, but the comparison appears limited to boundaries, not classification.

Error Type Evaluation
=====================
It is unsurprising that ~5 teams (line 473) failed to correct unnecessary token errors. UFC’s rule-based approach (Ng et al., 2014) cannot identify superfluous words from POS tags alone. Rozovskaya & Roth (2016) explain AMU’s poor performance on such errors. The authors fail to analyze why systems succeeded or failed per error type. For example, word order errors have clear gold-to-auto mappings, but CUUI’s 0.0 precision+recall (Table 6) likely reflects its lack of word order correction capability—not a system failure or classification error. This omission highlights a classification flaw needing analysis.

Raw TP/FP/TN/FN values for Table 6 should be provided in an appendix for metric comparisons. Some error type/system results in Table 6 appear based on few instances—this should be clarified with raw data.

The statement that "all but 2 teams (IITB and IPN) achieved the best score in at least 1 category, suggesting different GEC approaches complement different error types" (line 606) aligns with prior research and should be noted.

Multi-token error analysis requires deeper interpretation: None of the systems were trained on parallel learner data with fluent corrections (Sakaguchi et al., 2016), so poor performance may reflect inherent limitations rather than model shortcomings.

Other
=====
- The authors should acknowledge that error annotation was feasible for some GEC systems (e.g., those with error-type-specific submodules), though adapting them to include relevant submodules would be needed. The proposed method enables unified comparison for systems where error-tagged output is non-trivial.
- References: Capitalization is inconsistent; Sakaguchi et al. (2016) URL requires wrapping; Efron & Tibshirani (1993) lacks page numbers.

Author Response
===============
I agree the approach is not "fatally flawed" and the review rightly highlights positive aspects. The approach is sound, but the paper is not ready. The core issue is the inadequate description of error classification rules—this is not merely a matter of adding examples. If rules are not documented for replication or adaptation, the contribution lacks reproducibility. Generalization to other languages cannot be an afterthought; if the approach is inherently English-specific, this is a serious limitation. Even without cross-lingual experiments, the method must transparently enable estimation of adaptation effort. Stating "most research targets ESL" reinforces this problem.  

You note that error type handling by systems is "usually obvious from the tables," but this is not straightforward. The CUUI example (Table 5) shows five systems failed unnecessary token correction—this should be obvious as evidence they did not target such errors. Yet the paper claims "there is no obvious explanation for these teams' difficulty," which is incorrect.
