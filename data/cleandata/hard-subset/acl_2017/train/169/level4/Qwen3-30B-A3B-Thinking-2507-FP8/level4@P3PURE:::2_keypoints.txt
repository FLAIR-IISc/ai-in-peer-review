- Novel evaluation enables assessment of GEC systems by error type in both recall and precision without requiring system outputs to be pre-annotated.
- Paper evaluates a diverse set of GEC systems.
- Approach advantages: computes precision by error type.
- Approach advantages: independent from manual error annotation.
- Approach advantages: can assess multi-token errors.
- Automatically selected error tags for pre-computed spans were largely approved by human experts.
- Critical omission: rules for deriving error types are not described, harming reproducibility and adaptability.
- Classifier evaluation lacks thorough error analysis and guidance for improvement.
- Evaluation is limited to English and fails to estimate the effort required to adapt the method to other languages.
- Unclear basis for error categories and lack of detail on rule structure, complexity, and dependence on alignment algorithms.
- Pre-processing that computes continuous spans is problematic for discontinuous error types (e.g., German separable verbs); classifier handling of these is unaddressed.
- Human rating claim (≥95% appropriate) is questionable due to potential rater bias, noisy boundaries, and coarse rating options; recommend testing on manually created boundaries.
- Classifier error analysis is shallow—examples like POS-tag-related "Bad" ratings need investigation into when and why errors occur and what fixes are suggested.
- Significance testing is unclear (was it on F0.5?), and F0.5 is a weak discriminative metric for comparing differing precision/recall profiles.
- Gold vs. auto comparison addresses boundary alignment but neglects classification mismatches between automatic and reference annotations.
- Error-type evaluation fails to determine whether low performance on certain types (e.g., unnecessary tokens, CUUI) is due to system design/training limitations or classifier errors.
- Raw TP/FP/TN/FN counts (e.g., for Table 6) should be provided to enable alternative metric analyses.
- Observation that most teams excelled in at least one category aligns with prior findings on complementary error-type strengths and should be explicitly noted.
- Multi-token error results need deeper interpretation considering many systems were not trained on parallel learner→fluent corrections.
- Paper should acknowledge some systems could feasibly produce error-tagged outputs with modification; the proposed approach uniquely enables unified comparison where that is non-trivial.
- Minor fixes: capitalize reference titles, wrap URLs, and add missing page numbers for cited works.
- Author response: agrees paper isn’t fatally flawed but is not ready—lack of documented rules makes approach irreproducible and cross-lingual generalization must be addressed.
- Author response: data show multiple systems failed on unnecessary-token errors due to design/training limits, contradicting authors’ claim of “no obvious explanation.”
