

Strengths: The paper presents a valuable method for automatically annotating error types in Grammatical Error Correction (GEC) system outputs, which has clear utility for both educational applications (teachers and learners) and for conducting more nuanced comparisons between GEC systems through error-type-specific performance metrics.

Weaknesses: The description of the core system remains highly superficial, particularly regarding the rule-based classifier. The paper fails to provide essential details about the rules themselves, including examples, the total number of rules, their complexity, and their ordering (which could affect rule application). Instead of addressing these critical technical aspects, the paper dedicates extensive space to presenting results from the CoNLL-2014 analysis (Table 6, which occupies an entire page) without adding meaningful interpretation beyond what is evident from the table.

The evaluation methodology has significant shortcomings. First, the authors should have established a gold standard by having annotators independently label the 200 test sentences rather than rating the system's output. As noted in Marcus et al. (1993), agreement rates differ substantially when reviewing pre-annotated data versus creating annotations from scratch. Second, the presentation of the rater results (95% "Good" or "Acceptable" per rater) is misleading; the paper averages individual rater scores rather than reporting inter-annotator agreement. If the "bad" ratings were distributed across different edits (which is not specified), 18.5% of edits were considered "bad" by at least one annotator, which is substantially higher than the reported 3.7% average. Third, the paper provides no details about the test data's characteristics, such as the number of error categories covered or which specific categories were evaluated. Fourth, the statement that "edit boundaries might be unusual" requires clarification with specific examples to assess potential application issues. Fifth, the claim that the system is less domain-dependent than machine learning approaches is questionable; Hunspell's vocabulary coverage varies across domains, and manually created rules remain highly language-dependent, especially given that the test data (FCE-test and CoNLL-2014) comes exclusively from student essays. Finally, the motivation for creating a new error category set is inadequately explained; the authors fail to justify why this new taxonomy is superior to existing ones like Nicholls (2003) or the CoNLL-2014 tagset, which could have enabled direct evaluation against the shared task's gold standard.

Overall, the paper's main contribution remains unclear: it is not sufficiently about a novel system (due to the missing rule details), not adequately about a new error taxonomy (lacking justification), and not substantially about the CoNLL-2014 evaluation (which is presented superficially).
