- Introduces a novel GEC evaluation method that measures performance by error type for both recall and precision.  
- Method enables error-type evaluation without requiring manual error-category annotations.  
- Framework can assess multi-token errors, an improvement over prior work.  
- Authors evaluated a diverse set of GEC systems comprehensively.  
- Automatically assigned error tags were rated appropriate by human experts (≥95% claimed).  

- Rules for deriving error types are inadequately described, hindering replication.  
- Dependency on alignment algorithms (despite claim of independence) is unclear and unexplained.  
- No examples or counts of implemented rules/categories are provided, preventing adaptation estimates.  
- Preprocessing creates continuous error spans and cannot accurately handle discontinuous errors (e.g., separable verb prefixes).  
- Classifier’s ability to handle discontinuous and complex cases is unverified.  

- Human rating claim (≥95% appropriate) may be biased due to noisy boundaries and lack of a middle/bad-option, inflating approval.  
- Robustness of human evaluation is questionable; comparison to manual boundaries was not performed.  
- Classifier evaluation lacks detailed error analysis and actionable guidance for improvement.  
- It is not reported when raters selected "Bad" vs. "Appropriate" or how error sources (e.g., POS tagging) affect decisions.  

- Significance testing details (e.g., F0.5 scores used) are unspecified and F0.5 may be a poor discriminative metric.  
- Gold-reference vs. auto-reference comparison focuses on boundary alignment only, contradicting claims about classification mismatches.  

- Paper does not analyze whether systems were designed to address specific error types, leading to misleading interpretations (e.g., CUUI and word order errors).  
- Some error-type results are based on very few instances and should be flagged as low-resource observations.  
- Raw TP/FP/TN/FN counts (e.g., for Table 6) are missing and should be provided for transparency and cross-measure comparisons.  
- The observation that some teams failed to correct unnecessary tokens is unsurprising and linked to known limitations of certain systems (e.g., rule-based UFC).  

- Multi-token error analysis lacks depth; system limitations may stem from absence of learner-parallel training data rather than model capability.  
- Claim that error annotation was "impossible" for some systems is overstated; adaptation is needed but unified comparison is feasible.  

- References contain formatting errors and missing details (capitalization, unwrapped URL, missing page numbers).  
- Author response concedes the approach is viable but the paper is incomplete without detailed error-type rules.  
- Generalization to languages beyond English is not addressed and must be proactively discussed.  
- The paper contains contradictions (e.g., claiming "no obvious explanation" for system failures while evidence suggests design limitations).
