

The paper introduces a novel method for evaluating grammatical error correction (GEC) systems by error type, enabling assessment of both precision and recall—previously unattainable since system outputs typically lack error-category annotations.  

**Strengths:**  
- The evaluation framework is a significant step toward analyzing GEC system behavior.  
- The study evaluates multiple GEC systems.  
- Key advantages over prior work include:  
  • Error-type-specific precision computation.  
  • Independence from manual error annotation.  
  • Capability to assess multi-token errors.  
- Automatically assigned error tags are largely approved by human experts.  

**Weaknesses:**  
1. **Incomplete description of error categorization rules:** The core mechanism—rules for deriving error types—is not specified. The paper fails to provide even a single example of how an error type is assigned (e.g., number of rules, complexity, or error-type distribution), making replication or adaptation impossible. Without this, the approach lacks transparency.  
2. **Insufficient classifier evaluation:** The classifier’s performance lacks detailed error analysis. For instance, the paper notes "Bad" ratings often stem from incorrect POS tags but does not clarify *when* raters selected "Bad" versus "Appropriate," nor does it propose concrete improvements. Crucially, human evaluation relied on automatically extracted boundaries (with "noise"), potentially biasing raters toward system output. To strengthen validity, the authors should compare ratings using manually corrected boundaries.  
3. **Limited multilingual scope:** The evaluation is restricted to English, with no discussion of adaptation challenges for other languages. The rules for determining edit boundaries and error tags likely depend on language-specific features (e.g., German separable verbs: *[first constituent] [verb] [other constituents] [verb prefix]*), yet the paper offers no insight into how these would need modification. The claim that "most research targets ESL only" does not address the feasibility of multilingual application.  
4. **Gold vs. auto-reference ambiguity:** Significance testing appears to use F₀.₅ scores, which conflates recall and precision (different recall-precision trade-offs may yield identical F₀.₅ values). Additionally, the comparison between gold and auto-reference focuses solely on boundary alignment, not classification consistency.  
5. **Error-type analysis gaps:**  
   - Table 6 reports systems failing to correct unnecessary tokens (e.g., 5 teams, including UFC and AMU). The paper claims "no obvious explanation" for this, but UFC’s POS-based approach inherently cannot handle superfluous words (Ng et al., 2014), and Rozovskaya & Roth (2016) explain AMU’s limitations. The authors should explicitly link system design to error-type performance.  
   - CUUI’s precision+recall=0.0 (despite not targeting word-order errors) suggests a classification error, yet this is neither analyzed nor discussed.  
   - Raw TP/FP/TN/FN values for Table 6 should be provided in an appendix to enable alternative metric comparisons. Some error-type results (e.g., for specific systems) appear based on minimal instances—this should be clarified.  
   - The claim that "all but 2 teams achieved best scores in at least one category" aligns with prior findings (e.g., Soricut et al., 2015), but this should be explicitly cited.  
6. **Mischaracterization of prior work:** The paper states error-type annotation was "previously impossible," but systems with error-type-specific submodules (e.g., for tense or word order) *could* provide such annotations. The proposed method’s value lies in enabling unified comparison for systems *not* designed for error-tagged outputs, not in novelty for all approaches.  

**Other Notes:**  
- Reference formatting: Some titles lack standard capitalization; Sakaguchi et al. (2016) needs a wrapped URL; Efron & Tibshirani (1993) is missing page numbers.  
- The assertion that "error types tackled by systems are usually obvious from tables" is misleading—see CUUI and unnecessary-token examples. Five teams failed to correct unnecessary tokens (Table 5), yet the paper claims "no obvious explanation" for this failure.  

**Author Response Consideration:**  
The approach is valuable but the paper is not ready for publication. The absence of error-categorization rules is not merely a minor omission—it renders the method non-replicable. Multilingual adaptation must be addressed proactively, not as an afterthought. The critique of CUUI and unnecessary-token errors remains valid: the paper’s lack of analysis for these cases undermines its claims. The reviewer’s core argument—that the rules’ absence is a critical flaw—must be addressed before acceptance.
