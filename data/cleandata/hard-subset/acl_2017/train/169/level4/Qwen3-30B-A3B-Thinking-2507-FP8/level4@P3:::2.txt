

**Summary and Main Contributions**

This paper addresses a critical gap in Grammatical Error Correction (GEC) evaluation: the inability to assess system performance by error type due to lack of annotation in system outputs. The authors propose a two-step approach to automatically annotate GEC system outputs with error type information. First, they use linguistically-enhanced alignment to extract edits between original and corrected sentences. Second, they classify these edits using a rule-based framework that relies solely on automatically obtained linguistic features (POS tags, lemmas, etc.). The primary contribution is a method enabling detailed error type analysis for GEC systems for the first time. The secondary contribution is a dataset-independent classifier that avoids the need for labeled training data. The tertiary contribution is a comprehensive error type analysis of all systems from the CoNLL-2014 shared task.

**Strengths**

1. The paper solves a genuinely important problem in GEC evaluation. Previous work could only measure overall system performance or error type recall, but not precision by error type. This method enables detailed error type analysis, which is crucial for understanding system strengths and weaknesses.

2. The rule-based classifier is a significant technical contribution. By relying only on automatically obtained linguistic features (POS tags, lemmas), it avoids the dataset dependency that plagues machine learning approaches. This makes the approach more broadly applicable and transparent than previous methods.

3. The human evaluation of the classifier is convincing. With 95%+ of predicted error types rated as "Good" or "Acceptable" by experts, the authors provide strong evidence that their automatic annotation is reliable despite the noise from automatic edit extraction. This validation is particularly important given the lack of gold standard labels for error types.

**Weaknesses**

1. The paper lacks sufficient detail about the error categorization rules, making the approach difficult to replicate or adapt. The authors mention they have "25 error types" and "rules" but provide no examples of these rules or how they operate. Without this information, it's impossible to assess whether the categorization is comprehensive or how it would work on other languages or error types.

2. The classifier evaluation lacks depth. The authors note that "Bad" ratings were usually due to POS tagging errors, but they don't provide a thorough error analysis. For instance, they don't identify which specific error types were most problematic or suggest concrete improvements for the classifier. This omission is particularly problematic given the importance of the classifier to the overall approach.

3. The evaluation is limited to English, and the paper doesn't discuss how the approach would generalize to other languages. The authors mention that "most research is targeted at ESL only," but this doesn't address the technical challenges of adapting the approach to other languages. For example, the rules for error categorization likely depend on language-specific phenomena (like German separable verbs), but the paper doesn't explain how these would be handled.

4. The human evaluation methodology has potential biases. Raters were aware that edit boundaries were automatically determined, which might have made them more forgiving of boundary errors. The authors should have compared the classifier's performance on automatically extracted boundaries versus manually created boundaries to establish a more rigorous evaluation.

**Questions to Authors**

1. Can you provide specific examples of your error categorization rules? For instance, how would you classify "could → should" as a tense error rather than a modal error, and what rule determines this classification?

2. How would you adapt your approach to handle discontinuous edit spans (e.g., German separable verbs)? What specific changes to your rules would be necessary?

3. The paper claims that "all human judges rated at least 95% of our rule-based error types as either 'Good' or 'Acceptable'." Did you conduct a separate evaluation where boundaries were manually created (not automatically extracted) to validate that the error type classification itself is robust, independent of boundary quality?

4. For the five teams that failed to correct unnecessary token errors (Table 5), the paper states "there is also no obvious explanation as to why these teams had difficulty." However, for some systems (e.g., UFC with its rule-based approach), it should be obvious why they couldn't handle unnecessary tokens. Can you clarify which teams' systems were designed to handle unnecessary tokens and which weren't?

5. How would you estimate the effort required to adapt this approach to another language? What specific components of your system would need modification for languages with different grammatical structures?
