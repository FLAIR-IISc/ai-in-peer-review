

Strengths: The paper addresses a valuable application for both teachers and learners by automatically enriching GEC system outputs with error types—a useful complement to systems that only provide corrected text. This also enables finer-grained comparison of GEC systems across error types, including precision and recall metrics.

Weaknesses: The system description is overly superficial, and the evaluation lacks rigor.

General Discussion:  
The core contribution—automatically tagging GEC outputs with error types—is well-motivated and practically useful. However, the system’s technical details are insufficiently described. The approach relies on manually created rules, yet the paper omits critical specifics: examples of rules, their total count, complexity, ordering logic (e.g., whether earlier rules might block later ones), and design rationale. Instead, the paper dedicates excessive space to rehashing CoNLL-2014 system results (Table 6 spans a full page, with repetitive textual descriptions of tabulated data).  

The evaluation suffers from multiple flaws. First, the authors used system outputs (not a gold-standard annotation) as the basis for annotator ratings. For 200 test sentences, a gold standard could have been established directly by annotators, avoiding the inflated agreement rates likely caused by pre-annotated data (as shown in Marcus et al., 1993). Second, the claim that "all 5 raters individually considered at least 95% of error types as 'Good' or 'Acceptable'" misrepresents the data. Averaging individual ratings is not standard practice; if the 18.5% "bad" ratings (per the paper) reflect distinct edits (which the paper fails to specify), this distribution suggests far more severe issues than the reported 3.7% average. Third, the test data’s error categories are undefined—no details are given on how many categories exist, which are covered, or why certain categories were omitted. Fourth, the phrase "edit boundaries might be unusual" lacks clarity; examples and explanation are needed to assess potential application impacts.  

The assertion that the system is "less domain-dependent than data-driven approaches" is questionable. While Hunspell’s vocabulary may not cover all domains equally, manually crafted rules are inherently domain-specific and language-dependent—a significant drawback versus machine learning methods. The evaluation further compounds this by testing exclusively on student essays (FCE-test, CoNLL-2014), limiting generalizability.  

The rationale for designing a new error category set is unclear. The authors cite "easy search for underspecified categories" (e.g., avoiding overly broad tags like "NOUN"), but Nicholls (2003) already offers a tagset supporting this functionality. Alternatively, adopting the CoNLL-2014 tagset would have enabled direct use of its gold standard for evaluation.  

Ultimately, the paper’s primary contribution remains ambiguous. Is it presenting a novel system? If so, its core mechanics are omitted. Is it proposing new error categories? If so, motivation and comparison are lacking. Is it evaluating CoNLL-2014 systems? The results are presented superficially.
