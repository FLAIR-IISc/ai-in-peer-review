- Introduces a novel, granular error-type evaluation for GEC that enables precision-based assessment alongside recall.  
- Strength: potential to advance GEC analysis, evaluated across diverse systems, supports error-type precision, needs no manual error annotation, handles multi-token errors, and automated tags were largely validated by experts.  
- Major flaw: no detailed error-type rules, examples, or justification are provided, making the method irreproducible and its language-agnostic claim unsupported.  
- Classifier evaluation is shallow: missing when raters chose "Bad" vs "Appropriate," lack of analysis on systematic classifier weaknesses (e.g., discontinuous errors), and potential rater bias from noisy automatic boundaries.  
- Cross-lingual generalization is inadequately addressed; no discussion of adaptation effort, rule modification, or language-specific dependencies (morphology, preprocessing).  
- System-performance analysis is flawed: failures (e.g., unnecessary token errors/CUUI) are treated as unexplained instead of linked to known system design limitations.  
- Methodological inconsistencies: reliance on F0.5 for significance testing is problematic, comparisons focus only on boundary alignment ignoring classification mismatches, and raw TP/FP/TN/FN counts are missing.  
- Minor issues: reference formatting errors, incorrect claim that error-type annotation was previously impossible, and overstated novelty versus value in unified evaluation.  
- Required revisions: provide explicit reproducible error-category rules with examples; perform thorough classifier error analysis by error category; demonstrate language-adaptation feasibility (e.g., German case study or roadmap); and fix system-performance and statistical analysis issues (including raw counts and F0.5 clarification).
