Summary
The paper presents a method for automatically enriching the outputs of grammatical error correction (GEC) systems with error-type labels. This is a useful contribution for both teachers and learners, since most GEC systems only provide a corrected version without making error types explicit. The approach also enables finer-grained comparisons of GEC systems, including overall precision and error-type-specific precision and recall.

Strengths
- Practical and potentially impactful application for teachers and learners.
- Facilitates fine-grained evaluation and comparison of GEC systems, including error-type-specific metrics.

Weaknesses
- The system description is highly superficial; crucial implementation details are missing.
- The evaluation methodology is not satisfying and leaves important questions unanswered.

Detailed comments

System description
- The core of the system appears to be a set of (manually?) created rules, but the paper provides no substantive details about them. The authors should:
  - Show representative examples of rules.
  - State the total number of rules.
  - Describe their complexity.
  - Explain how rules are ordered and whether early rules can block later ones.
- Instead of providing these key details, several pages are devoted to evaluating the systems that participated in CoNLL-2014. Table 6 (which occupies an entire page) lists results for all systems, and the text reiterates many facts and figures that are already evident from the table.

Evaluation concerns
1) Gold standard vs. rating system output
- The annotators should have independently created a gold standard for the 200 test sentences, rather than only rating the system’s output. Given a fixed tagset, producing a gold standard for such a small test set should be feasible.
- The approach taken is likely to yield more favorable ratings than a comparison with a genuine gold standard (see, e.g., Marcus et al., 1993, for differences in agreement when reviewing pre-annotated data vs. annotating from scratch).

2) Aggregation of rater judgments
- The paper states that “all 5 raters individually considered at least 95% of our rule-based error types to be either ‘Good’ or ‘Acceptable’.” Treating raters individually and averaging in this way is not standard practice.
- If each “bad” score were assigned to different edits (the distribution is not reported), then 18.5% of the edits were considered “bad” by at least one annotator—this is substantially worse than the reported average of 3.7%.

3) Test data characterization
- The paper provides no information about the test data: for example, how many error categories are present or which categories are covered (based on those rated “good” by the annotators).

4) Edit boundaries
- The claim that “edit boundaries might be unusual” needs a precise definition and concrete examples. Could this characteristic cause problems for downstream use of the system?

Domain dependence
- The paper asserts that the system is less domain dependent than approaches requiring training data. This is not convincing. For example, Hunspell’s vocabulary may provide uneven domain coverage, and manually created rules can themselves be domain dependent. Moreover, the rules are entirely language dependent, which is a clear drawback relative to machine learning approaches.
- The evaluation data (FCE-test, CoNLL-2014) come from a single domain: student essays. This further limits claims about domain robustness.

Error category design
- It is unclear why a new set of error categories was introduced. The stated motivation—enabling searches for underspecified categories (e.g., “NOUN”)—appears to be supported by the tagset in Nicholls (2003) as well.
- Alternatively, using the CoNLL-2014 tagset would have allowed direct evaluation against the CoNLL gold standard.

Overall assessment
The paper’s main motivation remains unclear. If the contribution is a new system, then critical technical details are missing. If it is a new set of error categories, there is little motivation or discussion. If it is primarily an evaluation of CoNLL-2014 systems, the presentation of results is superficial and largely duplicates information in an oversized table.
