Summary
This paper proposes a novel framework for evaluating grammatical error correction (GEC) systems by error type, reporting both precision and recall without requiring manual error-tagged system outputs. The authors apply the method to multiple systems and claim support from human judgments for the automatically assigned tags. The direction is timely and valuable for analyzing system behavior beyond aggregate scores.

Strengths
- The method enables precision-by-type evaluation, which was previously impractical without manual categorization.
- It is independent of manual error annotation and can assess multi-token errors.
- The evaluation covers a range of systems, providing informative cross-system comparisons.
- Human experts largely approve the automatically selected tags for pre-computed error spans.

Weaknesses
- The core rules for deriving error types are not described, limiting reproducibility and transparency.
- The classifier evaluation lacks a detailed error analysis and does not outline concrete avenues for improvement.
- The approach is evaluated only on English; applicability to other languages is not demonstrated or clearly scoped.
- The treatment of edit spans appears restricted to continuous spans, which may be inadequate for languages with discontinuous phenomena.
- Some analyses conflate evaluation artifacts with system capabilities and do not account for systems’ design targets.

Detailed comments

Classifier and error categories
- The paper does not explain how error categories were devised or whether they are grounded in prior taxonomies. This is central to interpretability and comparability.
- Although the overall framework is alignment-agnostic, the rules used to assign categories likely depend on the alignment and preprocessing pipeline; the paper should clarify this dependency.
- Readers need at least several representative rules per major category, including their reliance on POS tags, morphology, dependency relations, or other features, and an estimate of rule coverage and complexity (counts per category, typical patterns, exceptions).

Generalization beyond English
- The paper does not discuss what would be required to port the method to other languages. Given likely dependencies on tokenization, morphology, POS tagging, and syntactic patterns, the effort and feasibility should be estimated.
- Some languages require discontinuous spans to capture errors accurately (e.g., German separable verb prefixes). The current reliance on continuous spans (as in M2) may prevent accurate tagging and should be explicitly addressed. Can the classifier handle discontinuous edits, and if not, what extensions are needed?
- If error categories are English-specific, specify what would change for morphologically rich or free word order languages.

Human judgment study
- The high “Appropriate” rate may be inflated by noisy boundaries: raters might be more forgiving when spans are imperfect, and knowing system outputs could bias choices.
- The rating scale (no middle option between “Bad” and “Appropriate”) likely skewed responses toward “Appropriate.”
- To strengthen validity, include an evaluation using manually curated edit boundaries and a richer rating scale (e.g., Bad/Partially appropriate/Appropriate), ideally with adjudication. Report inter-annotator agreement.

Classifier error analysis and improvement paths
- The paper notes that “Bad” tags are often due to POS errors, but a deeper analysis is needed: which categories are most error-prone, what are recurring confusion patterns, and where do boundary vs. feature errors dominate?
- Use this analysis to propose concrete improvements (e.g., more robust POS tagging, dependency features, lexicon cues, or training a supervised classifier to complement rules).

Gold vs. automatic reference comparison
- It is unclear what metric and units were used for significance testing. If significance was tested on F0.5, this is problematic because it conflates precision and recall and can mask divergent behaviors.
- Prefer significance testing on per-type precision and recall (e.g., bootstrap over sentences/edits) and report confidence intervals. Clarify whether differences in alignment and classification were both assessed, not just boundary quality.

Error type evaluation and system design
- Some conclusions appear to overlook whether systems were designed to target certain error types. For instance, it is unsurprising that several teams fail on unnecessary token errors; certain approaches (e.g., POS-rule-based systems like UFC) cannot reliably detect superfluous tokens, and prior work explains specific weaknesses (e.g., AMU).
- Results such as CUUI’s zero precision/recall on word order should be interpreted in light of system scope; discrepancies may also indicate classification errors, which should be analyzed and discussed.
- Please include raw TP/FP/TN/FN counts by system and error type (in an appendix). This enables re-computation of alternative measures and reveals when results are based on small sample sizes.
- The observation that most teams achieve the best score in at least one category aligns with prior findings that different approaches complement different error types; citing this connection would strengthen the narrative.

Multi-token errors
- The multi-token analysis is valuable but needs more interpretation. Some systems are likely inherently limited on multi-token edits, and none were trained on parallel learner–fluent corpora that target such phenomena. Discuss how training data and model classes affect these outcomes.

Positioning and prior capability
- Note that some modular GEC systems can, in principle, output error-tagged corrections if submodule provenance is preserved. The contribution here is a unified, system-agnostic way to compare systems by error type, which remains highly valuable.

Presentation and references
- Provide clearer descriptions and examples of rules for error categorization and edit boundary determination, including dependencies on preprocessing.
- Ensure reference formatting: fix capitalization in titles, wrap URLs (e.g., Sakaguchi et al., 2016), and add missing page information (e.g., Efron and Tibshirani, 1993).

Overall assessment and recommendation
The paper addresses an important gap in GEC evaluation and offers a promising, broadly applicable framework. However, the current version lacks essential details about the rule set, provides limited error analysis, and does not adequately address generalization beyond English or discontinuous edits. With substantial revisions—especially full documentation of the categorization rules, a stronger human evaluation, clearer significance testing, raw counts, and an explicit path to multilingual applicability—the contribution would be strong. As it stands, the approach is sound in principle, but the paper is not yet ready.
