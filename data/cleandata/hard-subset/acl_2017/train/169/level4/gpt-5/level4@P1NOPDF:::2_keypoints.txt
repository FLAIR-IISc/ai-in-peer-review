- Paper introduces a novel evaluation framework for GEC that enables per–error-type precision and recall without manual error annotations.
- Strength: evaluation framework is a valuable advance for analyzing GEC system behavior.
- Strength: paper evaluates a diverse set of GEC systems.
- Strength/Advantage: computes precision by error type, unlike prior work.
- Strength/Advantage: does not rely on manual error annotation.
- Strength/Advantage: handles multi-token errors.
- Strength: human experts largely approved automatically assigned error tags for pre-computed spans.
- Weakness: rules used to derive error types are not described.
- Weakness: classifier evaluation lacks thorough error analysis and concrete improvement directions.
- Weakness: evaluation is English-only and unclear how to port to other languages.
- Classifier: basis for error categories is unclear and may not be grounded in prior work.
- Classifier: rules likely depend on alignment/preprocessing but no examples or details are provided.
- Classifier: without rule details (count/complexity), effort to adapt to other languages is impossible to estimate.
- Classifier: preprocessing yields continuous spans, problematic for discontinuous edits (e.g., separable German verbs); classifier’s handling of discontinuous edits is unclear.
- Human evaluation: reported ≥95% appropriateness may be optimistic due to boundary-noise bias and lack of intermediate rating options; need evaluations with manual boundaries.
- Classifier evaluation: lack of substantive error analysis—need conditions causing “Bad” vs “Appropriate” and expert-guided improvements.
- Gold vs auto reference: unclear which data/significance tests target; using F0.5 for significance is questionable due to weak discriminative power.
- Gold vs auto: apparent comparison may only concern boundaries, not classification mismatches.
- Error-type results: failures to correct unnecessary-token errors are unsurprising for some systems and may reflect system design rather than evaluation failure.
- Error-type analysis: paper does not analyze whether systems were designed to target each error type, leading to ambiguous interpretations (e.g., CUUI on word order).
- Request: report raw TP/FP/TN/FN counts (e.g., in appendix) to clarify sparse-instance effects.
- Observation: finding that different teams excel in different categories aligns with prior research and should be noted.
- Multi-token errors: analysis is useful but needs more interpretation—some systems inherently unable to correct these and training data differences matter.
- Other: note that some GEC systems can output error annotations and the approach enables unified comparison even when annotations are not exposed.
- References: citation formatting issues and missing page/URL details need correction.
- Author response summary: reviewer finds approach promising but incomplete; absence of rule descriptions is a major replication/adaptation issue and multilingual generalization must be addressed.
