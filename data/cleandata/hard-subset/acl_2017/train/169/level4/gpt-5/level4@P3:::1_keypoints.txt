- Proposes a deterministic, dataset-agnostic pipeline to extract edit spans and assign rule-based error types using automatic linguistic markup and spelling checks.

- Combines linguistically enhanced alignment for edit extraction with a rule-driven error-typing scheme (POS, lemmas, morphology, dependencies, stemming, Hunspell).

- Validates predicted types via expert ratings (>95% Good/Acceptable per rater) and applies the tool to detailed error-type analyses of CoNLL-2014 systems.

- Contribution: a practical, transparent, rule-based tool to annotate edits and error types without labeled training data.

- Contribution: enables comprehensive, type-level analyses of CoNLL-2014 systems, revealing system-specific strengths/weaknesses and multi-token edit challenges.

- Contribution: proposes an evaluation protocol that reprocesses gold references with the same pipeline to allow direct edit matching, with limited impact on team rankings versus M2.

- Strength: practical tooling likely to aid researchers, teachers, and learners; deterministic rules make it auditable and reproducible.

- Strength: dataset-independent, transparent design using automatic linguistic features avoids reliance on corpus-specific tags or opaque learned classifiers.

- Strength: insightful, fine-grained results (operation-tier breakdowns, multi-token stats) that reveal useful system diagnostics.

- Strength: separates alignment and scoring to simplify edit-level TP/FP/FN assessment; bootstrap suggests small differences between gold and auto-processed refs.

- Strength: high human acceptability of predicted types despite automation noise (κfree ≈ 0.724).

- Weakness: paper omits key details about the rule set (number, coverage, examples, precedence, conflict resolution, maintenance).

- Weakness: evaluation asks raters to score system outputs instead of creating an independent gold standard with the proposed tagset, likely inflating acceptability.

- Weakness: sampled evaluation edits lack characterization by category or difficulty, so representativeness is unclear.

- Weakness: ambiguous treatment and quantification of edit boundary errors; needs examples and impact analysis.

- Weakness: claims of domain/language independence are overstated given reliance on spaCy, UD mapping, and Hunspell; portability not evaluated.

- Weakness: insufficient motivation for a new tagset versus existing ones (e.g., Nicholls, CoNLL-2014); trade-offs not fully justified.

- Question: How many rules exist, with representative examples, ordering/precedence, conflict resolution, and coverage per rule family?

- Question: Provide concrete examples and frequency of “unusual” edit boundaries and analyze their effect on types and scoring.

- Question: Can you create a small gold set (300–500 edits) independently annotated to report IAA and per-type precision/recall?

- Question: What fraction of edits received at least one “Bad” rating, majority-label acceptability, and confusion patterns between categories?

- Question: How sensitive is the classifier to POS/parsing errors, different taggers/parsers, and dictionary coverage; what’s required to port to other languages?

- Question: Why not reuse or extend existing tagsets (CoNLL/Nicholls), and which analyses uniquely require your new tagset?

- Additional: move exhaustive per-type tables to appendix, report “50–70 F1” as percentages with clear protocol, fix stylistic/typo issues and notation consistency.

- Overall: a useful, timely, and practical tool with insightful analyses but requiring much more detail on rules and a stronger gold-standard evaluation to meet conference standards; positive if addressed.
