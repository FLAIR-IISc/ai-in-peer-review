- Strengths: Practical application for teachers and learners; enables fine-grained comparison of GEC systems.
- Weaknesses: Very shallow system description; evaluation is unsatisfactory.

- General Discussion:
The paper proposes automatically augmenting GEC outputs with error types. This is a valuable contribution, as both teachers and learners can benefit from explicit error-type information (many GEC systems provide only corrected text without labeling the error). It also supports more granular comparisons of GEC systems, including overall precision and error-type-specific precision and recall.
However, the system description is highly superficial. The core appears to be a set of (manually?) crafted rules, yet the paper provides no concrete details. The authors should include examples of rules, the number of rules, their complexity, the ordering and control strategy (e.g., could an earlier rule prevent a later one from firing?), and related implementation specifics. Instead of these crucial details, several pages focus on evaluating systems from CoNLL-2014. Table 6 occupies an entire page, and much of the text reiterates information that is directly visible in the table.
The evaluation of the proposed system is unsatisfying in multiple ways.
First, rather than merely rating the system’s output, annotators should have independently created a gold standard for the 200 test sentences. With a fixed tagset, producing a gold standard for such a small dataset is feasible. The chosen protocol likely inflates quality estimates relative to comparison against a true gold standard (see, e.g., Marcus et al. (1993) on higher agreement when reviewing pre-annotated data vs. annotating from scratch).
Second, the claim that “all 5 raters individually considered at least 95% of our rule-based error types to be either ‘Good’ or ‘Acceptable’” is methodologically problematic. It is not standard practice to consider multiple raters individually and then average their proportions. If each “bad” judgment falls on different edits (the paper does not report the distribution), then 18.5% of edits were labeled “bad” by at least one annotator—substantially worse than the 3.7% average reported.
Third, the test data are insufficiently described: we are not told how many error categories they include or which categories are covered (e.g., those rated “good” by annotators).
Fourth, the statement that “edit boundaries might be unusual” needs a precise definition and illustrative examples. Could such boundary issues impede system usage?
The authors claim the system is less domain dependent than data-driven approaches. This is doubtful. For instance, Hunspell’s lexicon likely varies in coverage across domains, and hand-written rules can themselves be domain dependent; they are also entirely language dependent, which is a clear disadvantage relative to many machine learning approaches. Moreover, the evaluation datasets (FCE-test, CoNLL-2014) come from a single domain: student essays.
It is also unclear why a new error category set was introduced. The stated motivation—facilitating searches for underspecified categories (e.g., a general “NOUN”)—seems already supported by the Nicholls (2003) tagset. Alternatively, using the CoNLL-2014 tagset would have enabled direct evaluation against the CoNLL gold standard.
In sum, the paper’s main motivation is unclear. Is the contribution a new system? If so, essential details are missing. Is it a new error taxonomy? Then motivation and discussion are minimal. Is it an evaluation of CoNLL-2014 systems? The presentation is superficial.
