- Proposes a novel evaluation method enabling per–error-type precision and recall for GEC without manual error-category annotation.
- Important contribution for analyzing GEC system behavior.
- Evaluates a diverse set of GEC systems.
- Method advantages: estimates precision by error type, avoids manual annotation, and handles multi-token errors.
- Human experts largely endorsed automatically assigned error labels for pre-computed error spans.
- Critical component—the rules used to derive error types—is not described, undermining reproducibility.
- Classifier evaluation lacks substantive error analysis and concrete directions for improvement.
- Evaluation is limited to English and the effort to port to other languages is unclear.
- Unclear whether the error taxonomy is derived from prior literature or how rules were constructed.
- Rules for assigning error types likely depend on alignment/preprocessing but are not detailed or exemplified.
- No discussion of necessary adaptations (rules/categories/preprocessing) for other languages.
- Preprocessing produces continuous error spans, which may fail on discontinuous-span phenomena (e.g., separable verbs in German).
- Human rater results may be biased by noisy automatic boundaries and lack of a neutral rating option; need rating with manual boundaries.
- Classifier “Bad” ratings are attributed to POS errors but lack deeper analysis of when and why raters judged tags inappropriate.
- Significance testing is unclear; using F0.5 for tests is problematic because it can mask differing precision/recall tradeoffs.
- Gold vs. auto comparisons appear to address only boundary differences, not classification mismatches.
- Some systems failed on certain error types because they were not designed to handle them; paper does not analyze system design vs. performance.
- Table 6 needs raw TP/FP/TN/FN counts in an appendix to enable alternative metrics and reveal sparse-data issues.
- Observation that different systems excel on different error types aligns with prior work and should be noted.
- Multi-token error findings need more interpretation; some systems may inherently be unable to correct them and training data limitations are relevant.
- Acknowledge that some GEC systems can already provide error annotations (e.g., modular systems), and the paper should contrast its unified approach with those.
- Minor issues: reference capitalization, missing page numbers, and a misformatted URL.
- Author response concedes approach is solid but reiterates that lack of rule-description and cross-lingual transparency are major shortcomings.
