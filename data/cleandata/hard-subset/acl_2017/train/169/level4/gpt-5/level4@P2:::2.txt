Summary and contributions (as I see them)
- The paper proposes a pipeline to automatically annotate untagged GEC system outputs with error types: (i) linguistically informed alignment to extract edits, and (ii) a dataset-agnostic, rule-based classifier to assign error categories. This enables per–error-type precision and recall, overcoming the prior limitation of recall-only analyses.
- The authors apply the method to CoNLL-2014 system outputs and provide the first broad, per-type analysis across systems, including operation tier (Missing/Replacement/Unnecessary), multi-token edits, and detection vs. correction.
- A small human study suggests predicted error types are acceptable in ≥95% of cases.

Strengths
- Important capability: The work enables per–error-type precision, allowing more diagnostic evaluation than overall scores or recall-only analyses. This fills a clear methodological gap in GEC evaluation.
- Broad empirical application: The analysis spans many diverse systems from CoNLL-2014, yielding actionable insights (e.g., system-specific strengths by error type, difficulty of multi-token errors).
- Practical advantages: The framework is training-free, dataset-independent, and based on automatic markup, making it straightforward to apply across corpora. It also fixes edit spans, enabling analyses of multi-token edits and detection vs. correction.
- Human validation: Expert ratings indicate that the automatically assigned error categories are generally appropriate, despite noise from automatic alignment.
- Transparent evaluation design: Decoupling edit extraction from scoring (simple edit matching) clarifies where differences arise compared to M2 and motivates discussion of inflated M2 scores.

Weaknesses
- Insufficient detail on the classifier rules: The paper does not describe the core rule set (coverage, structure, examples, decision hierarchy, and dependencies on POS/lemmas/parses). Without at least a representative sample of rules with concrete examples and failure modes, the contribution is hard to reproduce, adapt, or critique.
- Limited and under-analyzed classifier evaluation: The human study lacks detailed error analysis. What types most often receive “Bad”? How often do errors stem from POS vs. parsing vs. rule design? What improvements follow from these findings? Also, because raters saw noisy, automatically segmented edits and had only Good/Acceptable/Bad options, ratings may overestimate perceived appropriateness. A complementary evaluation with manual edit boundaries would make the assessment stronger.
- Language generalization is unclear: Although the classifier is designed to be dataset-independent, portability to other languages is under-specified. What adaptations are needed for different tag sets, morphology, tokenization, and syntax? Are error categories language-agnostic, or would they require redesign? Discontinuous phenomena (e.g., German separable verb prefixes) raise questions about assumptions of contiguous spans.
- Mismatch analyses and statistics: In the gold vs. auto reference comparison, it is unclear exactly what was tested for significance (e.g., F0.5 only). Because F-scores can mask divergent precision/recall, a test on paired P/R (or counts) would be more informative. The discussion frames the mismatch as both alignment and classification, but the experiment appears to primarily test boundary effects, not label consistency.
- Interpretation of per-type results needs more context: Several systems do not target certain error types; zero scores may reflect design rather than failure. The paper should distinguish “not attempted” from “attempted but failed,” and discuss how this interacts with mapping between gold and auto categories. Raw counts (TP/FP/FN) per type would support alternative metrics and reveal data sparsity issues.

Questions to authors
- Rule set: How many rules are there overall and per category? What is the rule structure (priority/order, back-off, conflict resolution)? Please include examples that show how rules map POS/lemma/parse patterns to categories, and typical edge cases.
- Dependencies: To what extent are the rules tied to specific tag sets, lemmatizers, dependency schemes, or Hunspell? How hard would it be to port the system to a different pipeline?
- Error scheme design: Which categories derive from prior annotation schemes? Which are new, and why? How did you balance granularity vs. practicality (e.g., modals vs. tense)?
- Discontinuous edits: Can your alignment and classifier handle non-contiguous spans? If not, how would you extend the method for languages that require them?
- Human study: Could you report results broken down by category and by source of failure (POS/parse/rule)? Can you replicate the study with gold boundaries to quantify the effect of alignment noise? Did raters see the system correction, and might that bias judgments?

Additional comments
- It is not surprising that some systems show zero performance on Unnecessary edits; several approaches (e.g., POS-rule-only pipelines, some SMT setups) are not designed to delete tokens or to detect superfluous words. Please reflect design intent in the analysis and cite known explanations (e.g., Rozovskaya & Roth, 2016 for AMU).
- For Table 6, please provide TP/FP/FN counts per team/type (appendix). This will expose low-support categories and allow re-analysis with alternative metrics.
- The multi-token analysis would benefit from clarifying which architectures are inherently limited on multi-token edits and from noting that few systems were trained for fluency-oriented rewrites (per Sakaguchi et al., 2016).
- Some systems could, in principle, emit error tags via modular subcomponents; your approach’s value is in enabling cross-system, unified tagging without system modifications—worth stating explicitly.
- References need cleanup (capitalization, URLs, missing page ranges).

Post-rebuttal comments
- The core value of the paper rests on the error-typing rules. To make the work replicable and extensible, the paper should include a substantive description of the rule set (structure, representative rules, and examples), not just a few illustrative cases.
- Language generalization should be addressed explicitly: what parts are language- or pipeline-dependent, what redesign is needed for categories, and what effort is required to support languages with discontinuous constructions.
- Statements about which error types systems “obviously” addressed are not always clear from the tables (e.g., CUUI and WO, Unnecessary). Please qualify claims and separate “not attempted” from “attempted but failed,” using system descriptions and your annotations.
