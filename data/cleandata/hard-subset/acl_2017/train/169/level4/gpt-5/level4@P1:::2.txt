Summary and contributions
- This paper proposes a method to automatically annotate parallel original/corrected sentences with explicit edit spans and error types, enabling per-error-type evaluation of GEC system outputs in terms of both precision and recall. The approach (i) extracts edits using a linguistically enhanced alignment, and (ii) assigns error types with a dataset-independent, rule-based classifier driven by automatic markup.
- The authors release a tool and apply it to all CoNLL-2014 system outputs, providing the first detailed cross-system analysis by error type, including operation tier (Missing/Unnecessary/Replacement), multi-token edits, and detection vs. correction.
- A small-scale human study reports that ≥95% of automatically assigned error types were judged “Good” or “Acceptable.”

Strengths
- Useful evaluation capability: The work fills a long-standing gap by enabling per-type precision (not only recall) for unannotated hypotheses, supporting more diagnostic evaluation of GEC systems.
- Broad application: The tool is applied to many systems from CoNLL-2014, yielding informative cross-system comparisons (e.g., operation tier, error-type breakdowns, multi-token edits).
- Methodological advantages: 
  - Dataset independence (no labeled training needed), relying only on automatic tags/lemmas/parses.
  - Transparent, deterministic labeling rules.
  - Separation of edit extraction from scoring simplifies evaluation and supports multi-token analyses.
- Human validation: Experts judged most tags appropriate despite noisy edit boundaries, suggesting the rules are reasonable in practice.

Weaknesses
- Limited transparency/replicability of the rule set: The core contribution—the rules mapping edits to error types—is not described. Readers cannot assess design rationale (e.g., category definitions beyond the table), coverage, dependence on specific POS/lemma/parse conventions, or complexity (how many rules overall/per category, example rules, exceptions). Without these, reproducibility and adaptation are unclear.
- Classifier evaluation design and analysis are underdeveloped:
  - The human study may be confounded by noisy, automatically derived edit spans. Raters might have been lenient; the rating scale lacked a gradation between “Appropriate” and “Bad,” potentially biasing results. A complementary evaluation using manually curated edit spans would be informative.
  - The paper only notes that “Bad” cases often trace to POS errors. A deeper error analysis (when “Bad” vs. “Acceptable,” systematic confusions across types, rule failures) is needed to guide improvements.
- Generalization beyond English is not addressed: The approach depends on POS tags, lemmas, stems, dependency parses, and a spellchecker lexicon. It is unclear how much retuning is needed for other languages (tagset mapping, morphology, word order phenomena, lexical resources). Error categories may need adaptation. The effort and feasibility should be estimated.
- Edit span limitations: The alignment yields contiguous spans. Some languages and phenomena require discontinuous edits (e.g., German separable verb prefixes). The paper does not discuss whether the framework can represent/tag discontinuous edits or how it would be extended.
- Gold vs. auto reference analysis is underspecified:
  - It is unclear exactly what was bootstrapped (sentence-level F0.5? per-type scores?) and whether testing on a derived measure like F0.5 is appropriate given differing precision/recall trade-offs that can yield the same F0.5.
  - The discussion of the mismatch (alignment and classification) reads as though only boundary differences are reconciled; please clarify whether error-type mappings are also normalized.
- Evaluation reporting and interpretation:
  - Table 5 “unnecessary” results: It is not surprising that some teams score 0. For example, UFC’s POS-based rules are ill-suited to detect superfluous words, and Rozovskaya & Roth (2016) discuss why AMU struggles here. The analysis should acknowledge whether systems targeted a given type at all versus “failing” on it.
  - Table 6: For some systems/types, reported precision/recall are zero despite the system not targeting that type (e.g., CUUI and WO). This suggests either a mismatch in goals or possible misclassification; the paper should disentangle “not attempted” from “attempted but failed.”
  - Please provide raw TP/FP/FN per team and type in an appendix to enable alternative metrics and to show when results are based on very few instances.
  - Multi-token errors: The finding that only MT-based systems exceed 10 F0.5 is valuable, but needs more context (e.g., whether systems are inherently unable to propose longer edits; lack of fluency-oriented training data).

Questions to authors
- Error scheme and rules:
  - On what prior work or guidelines are your categories based? What principles guided your consolidation (e.g., modals folded into VERB:TENSE)?
  - How many rules do you use overall and per category? How complex are they (feature sets, fallbacks)? Please include several concrete rule examples.
  - How dependent are the rules on a particular tagger/parser/lemma/stemmer and their tagsets? How do you handle tagging/parsing errors?
- Cross-lingual applicability:
  - What changes would be needed to port to another language (e.g., UD tag mapping, morphology modules, lexicons, word-order rules)? Roughly how much effort do you anticipate?
  - Can your framework handle discontinuous edits? If not, what extensions would be required?
- Evaluation details:
  - What statistic was bootstrapped in Section 4.1 (sentence-level F0.5, macro-averaged per-type, micro-averaged)? Why is that choice appropriate?
  - Will you release raw TP/FP/FN counts per type to support alternative analyses?
  - In the human study, can you report when raters chose “Acceptable” vs. “Good,” and common “Bad” patterns beyond POS tagging issues? Would you consider a follow-up with manual edit spans?

Additional comments
- It has been possible for some modular GEC systems to produce error-tagged outputs (e.g., classifier-per-type architectures), but your approach is valuable because it enables uniform comparison across heterogeneous systems without modifying them.
- References: Please fix capitalization, wrap long URLs (e.g., Sakaguchi et al., 2016), and add missing page info (e.g., Efron and Tibshirani, 1993).

Post-rebuttal comments
I appreciate the authors’ clarifications. The approach is promising, but the paper still lacks essential detail on the rule set—the foundation of the contribution. If the rules are intended to be more than a one-off implementation, they must be documented to enable replication and adaptation. Generalization beyond English should not be an afterthought; even without experiments in other languages, the paper should be transparent enough for readers to estimate the adaptation effort and likely performance. Finally, inferring which error types a system targets is not “usually obvious from the tables.” The CUUI and unnecessary-token examples show why explicit discussion is needed.
