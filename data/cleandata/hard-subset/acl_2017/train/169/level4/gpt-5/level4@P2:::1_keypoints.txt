- Proposes a two-step method: linguistically enhanced alignment to extract edits, then a rule-based, dataset-agnostic classifier to assign error types.
- Applies the method to CoNLL-2014 outputs to provide the first detailed per-type analysis of GEC systems and promises a public tool for fine-grained evaluation.

- Practical impact: automatically labeling system outputs with error types is useful for teachers, learners, and developers diagnosing systems.
- Enables finer-grained system comparison via per-type precision/recall and operation-level analyses (Missing/Unnecessary/Replacement).
- Potential portability: relies on automatic linguistic markup and deterministic rules rather than dataset-specific training, suggesting cross-corpus applicability.

- Core method underdescribed: the rule-based classifier lacks concrete rule examples, counts, coverage, ordering/priority, and conflict-resolution details, harming reproducibility and bias assessment.
- Human evaluation design is weak: using pre-annotated Good/Acceptable/Bad judgments instead of building a small gold standard can inflate agreement and is suboptimal.
- Rater aggregation is unclear: reporting per-rater ≥95% Good/Acceptable hides per-edit distributions and the proportion of edits judged Bad by any rater.
- Missing test-data details: no breakdown of which error categories occurred in the 200-sample, their frequencies, or per-category quality rates.
- “Unusual edit boundaries” are mentioned but undocumented: no examples, frequency estimates, or analysis of impact on typing and interpretability.
- Domain-independence claims are questionable: method depends on external resources (spaCy, Hunspell) and handwritten rules, with all experiments on learner essays only.
- Taxonomy rationale is underdeveloped: introduces a new tagset without clear justification over existing schemes (e.g., Nicholls 2003, CoNLL-2014) or mappings to them.

- Request details on rules/classifier: number of rules, organization/priority, exclusivity, concrete examples, conflict handling, default/UNK behavior, and coverage by feature type.
- Request analysis of alignment/edit boundaries: examples of unusual boundaries, frequency, impact on typing, and any evaluation against human spans.
- Request improved human-eval reporting: per-edit aggregated judgments (e.g., majority Bad), category-wise counts and Good/Acceptable/Bad rates for the 200 edits, and explanation for not creating a small gold standard with adjudication.
- Ask about domain/resource dependence: effect of Hunspell coverage on SPELL across domains, any out-of-domain tests, and what porting to another language would require.
- Ask about taxonomy choice: why a new tagset vs. existing ones and whether tool could output both custom tags and mapped existing categories.

- Suggest trimming long per-team/per-type descriptive text to make room for detailed rule descriptions, evaluation design, and boundary/error analyses.
- If claiming auto re-annotation of references is “as good as” gold, report diagnostics on segmentation divergences and effect sizes across scoring schemes.

- Typo/style fixes: use “cf.” not “c.f.”; consistently style “M^2”; clarify “50–70 F1” as percent; fix reference/capitalization issues (e.g., ESL, Fleiss, Kappa).

- Overall: promising and practical work, but needs substantially more detail on the rule-based classifier, stronger/clearer evaluation (small gold standard and better aggregation), justification for the tagset, and clarification of boundary issues and domain dependence.
