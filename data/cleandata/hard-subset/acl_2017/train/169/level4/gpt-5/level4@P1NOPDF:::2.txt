The paper presents a novel approach for evaluating grammatical error correction (GEC) systems. It enables per–error-type assessment of both recall and precision—previously infeasible in general because system outputs are not typically annotated with error categories.

Strengths:
- The evaluation framework is a valuable step toward analyzing GEC system behavior.
- The paper evaluates a diverse set of systems.
- Advantages over prior work:
  - Computes precision by error type.
  - Does not rely on manual error annotation.
  - Handles multi-token errors.
- Human experts largely approved the automatically selected error tags for pre-computed error spans.

Weaknesses:
- A key component—the rules used to derive error types—is not described.
- The classifier evaluation lacks a thorough error analysis and, consequently, concrete directions for improving the classifier.
- The evaluation is English-only; it is unclear how difficult it would be to apply the approach to other languages.

Classifier and classifier evaluation:
- The basis for the error categories is unclear. Are they grounded in prior work?
- While the overall approach is alignment-agnostic, the rules likely are not; yet no details are provided. Given that the error categories are central, readers should at least see examples of rules assigning error types.
- The method is not tested beyond English, and the paper does not detail what would be required to port it to other languages. Rules for edit boundary detection and error tagging plausibly depend on the language and pre-processing pipeline and may need adaptation; the error categories themselves might also need revision. Without details on the tagging rules (how many per type? how complex?), it is impossible to estimate the effort to apply the approach to other languages.
- The pre-processing step yields inherently continuous error spans (as with the M2 scorer). This is problematic for errors that require discontinuous spans. For example, in German main clauses, verbs with separable prefixes appear discontinuously: [1st constituent] [verb] [other constituents] [verb prefix]. Can the classifier handle discontinuous edits?
- The paper reports that all human judges rated at least 95% of the automatically assigned tags as appropriate “despite the degree of noise introduced by automatic edit extraction” (p. 295). This may be optimistic: raters might have been lenient when boundaries were noisy; they were not asked to tag without seeing the system output and thus may have been biased toward it in boundary-noise cases; and there was no rating option between “Bad (Not Appropriate)” and “Appropriate,” which could favor “Appropriate.” To strengthen the evaluation, also assess ratings when edit boundaries are manually created (i.e., without boundary noise).
- The classifier evaluation lacks a substantive error analysis. It is only noted that “Bad” ratings are usually due to POS tagging errors. Please analyze: under what conditions did raters choose “Bad” vs. “Appropriate”? Do expert ratings suggest concrete ways to improve the classifier?

Gold reference vs. auto reference:
- It is unclear what data the significance tests target. Were they run on F0.5 scores? If so, this is questionable: F0.5 is a derived measure with weak discriminative power—systems can have very different precision/recall profiles yet identical F0.5. Also, at the start of Section 4.1 the authors mention alignment and classification mismatches between automatic and reference, but the gold vs. reference comparison appears to concern only boundaries, not classification.

Error type evaluation:
- It is not surprising that five teams (~line 473) failed to correct any unnecessary-token errors. For at least two systems, the reasons are straightforward. Most obviously, UFC’s rule-based approach operates on POS tags (Ng et al., 2014), which are insufficient to detect superfluous words. Rozovskaya & Roth (2016) also explain AMU’s poor performance on superfluous words.
- The paper does not analyze or comment on whether systems were designed to handle each error type. For some types, there is a straightforward mapping between the gold standard and auto reference (e.g., word order). It remains unclear whether systems failed on certain types or simply were not designed to tackle them. For instance, CUUI is reported with precision+recall=0.0 on word order, though it does not target that error type. This likely indicates classification errors, which are neither analyzed nor discussed.
- Please report raw TP, FP, TN, and FN for Table 6 (e.g., in an appendix) to facilitate alternative comparisons. Some results seem to be based on very few instances; raw counts would make this explicit.
- You write: “All but 2 teams (IITB and IPN) achieved the best score in at least 1 category, which suggests that different approaches to GEC complement different error types” (p. 606). It would be helpful to note that this aligns with prior research.
- The multi-token error analysis is useful for future work, but the results need more interpretation. Some systems are likely inherently unable to correct such errors, and none of the systems were trained on parallel learner–fluent corpora (in the sense of Sakaguchi et al., 2016).

Other:
- The paper should note that some GEC systems can, in principle, provide error annotations (e.g., modular systems with dedicated components per error type). While such systems might need modification to expose which module triggered an output change, your approach enables unified comparison of systems for which error-tagged output is not straightforward.
- References: Some titles lack capitalization; the URL for Sakaguchi et al. (2016) needs wrapping; page information is missing for Efron and Tibshirani (1993).

Author response:
I agree the approach is not “fatally flawed,” and my review highlights several positives. The approach is promising, but the paper is not yet ready. The rules for error classification are foundational, and their absence is a major issue. This is not just about adding a few examples: if the rules are not a one-off implementation, they must be described to enable replication and adaptation.

Generalization to other languages should not be an afterthought. It would be a serious limitation if the approach were, by design, English-only. Even without conducting multilingual experiments, the paper should be transparent enough for others to estimate the effort and feasibility of adapting the approach. Simply noting that most research targets ESL reinforces the problem.

You write that the error types targeted by certain systems are “usually obvious from the tables.” I disagree—see the CUUI example and the unnecessary-token errors. Five systems do not correct unnecessary tokens (Table 5), so it should be evident they did not target them; yet the paper states, “There is also no obvious explanation as to why these teams had difficulty with this error type.”
