Summary
This paper proposes an automatic pipeline to annotate grammatical error correction (GEC) outputs with explicit edit spans and error types. The approach combines linguistically enhanced alignment for edit extraction with a dataset-agnostic, rule-based error typing scheme driven by automatically obtained linguistic markup (POS, lemmas, morphology, dependencies, stemming, and Hunspell-based spelling). The authors evaluate the plausibility of the predicted types via expert ratings (>95% Good/Acceptable per rater) and use the tool to conduct a detailed error-type analysis of CoNLL-2014 systems, including operation-tier breakdowns, multi-token edits, and detection vs. correction.

Contributions (as I see them)
1) A practical, deterministic, rule-based framework and tool to automatically annotate edits and error types in unannotated GEC outputs, designed to be dataset-agnostic and transparent.
2) A comprehensive, type-level analysis of CoNLL-2014 systems enabled by the tool, revealing system-specific strengths/weaknesses across operations and categories, and surfacing challenges in multi-token edits.
3) A simple evaluation protocol that reprocesses gold references with the same automatic pipeline to enable direct edit matching; empirical evidence suggests limited impact on team rankings relative to M2.

Strengths
- Practical impact and tooling: The proposed pipeline addresses a pressing gap for researchers, teachers, and learners: turning “just corrections” into interpretable error-type feedback. Its deterministic rules and reliance on automatic markup make it simple to deploy, auditable, and explainable. Releasing the tool increases reproducibility and likely community adoption.
- Transparent, dataset-independent design: By operating on automatic linguistic features rather than corpus-specific tags, the method avoids dependence on labeled training data and provides clear, rule-level justifications for classification decisions. This transparency is valuable compared with opaque learned classifiers and facilitates targeted error analysis and improvement.
- Insightful, fine-grained evaluation of CoNLL-2014 systems: The error-type breakdowns, operation-tier results, and multi-token statistics generate useful findings (e.g., many systems struggle with unnecessary deletions; CUUI excels at VERB:FORM; hybrid MT systems handle missing tokens comparatively better; multi-token edits remain difficult across the board). These analyses can inform system design and combination strategies.
- Clean separation of alignment and scoring: Treating edit extraction and evaluation as separate components simplifies comparisons and allows for direct TP/FP/FN computations at the edit level. The bootstrap analysis suggesting negligible differences between gold vs. auto-processed references strengthens the case for the proposed protocol in practice.
- Human acceptability of predicted types: Despite automation noise, expert ratings (>95% Good/Acceptable per rater; κfree ≈ 0.724) indicate that the rule-based scheme is largely reasonable in context, and error cases often stem from upstream tagging mistakes.

Weaknesses
- Insufficient system detail: The core classifier hinges on rules, yet the paper omits key specifics: number and coverage of rules, examples, rule precedence/ordering, conflict resolution, complexity, and maintenance strategy (e.g., how updates are managed when adding categories). Without these details, it is difficult to assess the true scope, scalability, and reproducibility of the approach, or to replicate/extend it.
- Evaluation design is limited: The user study asks raters to score system outputs rather than creating a small gold standard with independent annotations using the proposed tagset. This design likely yields higher acceptability than a true gold standard comparison. The aggregation is also potentially misleading: reporting per-rater acceptability hides the per-edit distribution; if “Bad” ratings do not overlap across raters, the fraction of edits deemed “Bad” by at least one annotator could be substantially higher than 3.7%. The paper should report per-edit majority labels, proportion of edits with any “Bad” judgment, and agreement on type labels (not just κfree on ternary quality labels).
- Incomplete data characterization: The 200 edits sampled from FCE-test and CoNLL-2014 are not characterized by category, difficulty, or distribution. Without this, it is unclear how representative the sample is, which types dominate, or whether the classifier’s strengths align with frequent vs. rare categories.
- Ambiguity around edit boundaries: The claim that “edit boundaries might be unusual” needs concrete examples. Boundary errors can directly affect both type assignment and downstream evaluation (e.g., splitting vs. merging edits, especially for multi-token phenomena). The paper should quantify boundary deviations from gold and analyze their impact on type assignment and scoring.
- Questionable claims about domain independence: While the method is dataset-independent, it is not domain- or language-independent. It relies on spaCy models, UD mapping quality, and Hunspell coverage. Performance likely varies by domain (e.g., specialized lexicons) and language; the current evaluation is confined to learner essays. Some discussion of portability and anticipated failure modes would be helpful.
- Motivation for a new tagset is underdeveloped: The paper argues for flexible querying across levels of granularity, but it does not clearly justify why existing schemes (e.g., Nicholls 2003, CoNLL-2014) cannot support these needs. Using the CoNLL tagset would have enabled direct gold comparisons. The design trade-offs (informativeness vs. practicality) deserve more explicit discussion and empirical validation.

Questions to Authors
- Rules and precedence: How many rules are there? Provide representative examples (including edge cases like modal vs. tense changes). How are rules ordered? How are conflicts resolved? What is the coverage of each rule family?
- Boundary behavior: Show concrete instances of “unusual” boundaries, quantify how often they occur, and analyze how they affect type assignment and scoring (e.g., merges/splits).
- Gold-standard evaluation: Can you annotate a small gold set (e.g., 300–500 edits) with your tagset, independently by two annotators, and report inter-annotator agreement and precision/recall per type?
- Aggregation of ratings: What fraction of edits received at least one “Bad”? What are majority-label acceptability rates? Can you report confusion patterns between closely related categories?
- Domain and language transfer: How sensitive is the classifier to POS/parsing errors? Any ablations with different taggers/parsers or dictionary coverage? What would be required to port the scheme to another language?
- Tagset rationale: Why not reuse or extend CoNLL/Nicholls? Which analyses are enabled by your tagset that are impractical with existing ones?

Additional comments and typos
- Overlong result tables: Consider moving exhaustive per-type tables to an appendix and focusing the main text on key insights and error analyses.
- Clarify “50–70 F1”: please report as percentages and specify the evaluation protocol for comparability.
- Style: use “cf.” not “c.f.”; case consistency in references (e.g., ESL); check names and capitalization (e.g., Fleiss, Kappa). “M2” is standard nomenclature; if typographically rendering M^2, ensure consistency.
- Minor: fix “cateogories” → “categories”; ensure clean footnote formatting for Damerau–Levenshtein and remove stray markers.

Overall recommendation
A useful and timely tool with clear practical value and insightful analyses, but the paper needs substantially more detail on the rule-based system and a stronger, more rigorous evaluation (including a small gold standard and clearer aggregation/reporting) to meet ACL standards. I would be positive if these concerns are addressed.
