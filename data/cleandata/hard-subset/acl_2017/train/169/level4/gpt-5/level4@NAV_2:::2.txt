The paper introduces a new method for evaluating grammatical error correction (GEC) systems, enabling per-error-type assessment in terms of both recall and precision. This addresses the prior limitation that system outputs generally lack error-type annotations, which restricted analyses to recall only.

Strengths:
- The proposed evaluation framework is an impactful step toward fine-grained analysis of GEC system behavior.
- The paper evaluates a diverse set of systems.
- The approach offers several advantages over prior work:
  - it reports precision by error type
  - it does not depend on manual error-type annotation
  - it supports assessing performance on multi-token edits
- Human experts largely endorsed the automatically assigned error tags for the extracted edit spans.

Weaknesses:
- A central component—the rules used to assign error types—is not described.
- The classifier evaluation lacks a detailed error analysis and, consequently, actionable directions for improving the classifier.
- The evaluation covers only English, and the effort required to apply the approach to other languages is unclear.

Classifier and Classifier Evaluation
====================================
The rationale for the error taxonomy is not explained. Is it derived from existing schemes?

While the overall evaluation pipeline is nominally alignment-agnostic, the rule set likely depends on the specifics of the alignment/merging algorithm, but the paper provides no detail. Given that the taxonomy and rules are fundamental to the contribution, the reader should at least see representative rules and how they map features to categories.

The paper evaluates only English and does not discuss what would be needed to port the approach to other languages. It seems likely that both the edit-boundary rules and the tagging rules depend on the language and preprocessing pipeline, implying adaptation would be required. The taxonomy might also need adjustment. No information is given about the number of rules, their complexity, or coverage per category, making it difficult to estimate the effort to adapt to other languages.

Edit spans appear to be restricted to contiguous ranges (as in the M2 scorer). Some error types are naturally discontinuous. For instance, in German main clauses, separable verb prefixes occur in non-contiguous positions: [1st constituent] [verb] [other constituents] [verb prefix]. Can the classifier represent and tag discontinuous edits?

The authors state that all judges rated at least 95% of automatically assigned tags as “Good” or “Acceptable” “despite the degree of noise introduced by automatic edit extraction” (295). This conclusion may be optimistic: raters might have been lenient when faced with noisy boundaries, and seeing the system’s correction could bias choices. Moreover, the rating scale offered no middle option between “Bad (Not Appropriate)” and “Appropriate,” potentially pushing raters toward “Appropriate.” A stronger evaluation would include ratings on edits with manually curated boundaries (i.e., without boundary noise) to isolate the classifier’s labeling quality.

The classifier evaluation lacks a substantive error analysis. Beyond noting that “Bad” cases often stem from POS tagging errors, it would be useful to report when raters selected “Bad” versus “Appropriate,” and what these cases suggest about rule gaps or systematic confusions that could guide improvements.

Gold Reference vs. Auto Reference
=================================
It is unclear exactly what statistic the significance test targeted. Was the test run on F0.5? If so, that is not ideal: F0.5 is a derived metric with limited discriminative power (different precision/recall pairs can yield the same F0.5). Additionally, Section 4.1 introduces the mismatch between automatic and gold references in both alignment and classification, but the gold-vs-auto comparison appears to focus on boundary consistency rather than error-type agreement.

Error Type Evaluation
=====================
It is not surprising that five teams (~line 473) failed to correct any unnecessary token errors. For at least two systems there are straightforward reasons. For UFC, the rule-based approach operates on POS tags (Ng et al., 2014), which is insufficient for reliably detecting superfluous tokens. Rozovskaya & Roth (2016) also discuss why AMU underperforms on superfluous words.

The analysis around Table 6 does not account for whether systems were designed to address specific error types. For some categories (e.g., word order), the mapping between gold and auto reference is direct; however, the paper does not distinguish between failure to correct because the system could not handle the type versus because it was not intended to. For instance, CUUI is reported with precision=recall=0.0 on word order, although that system does not target this error type; this may indicate a classification artifact that is neither analyzed nor discussed.

Please include raw TP, FP, TN, FN counts in an appendix for Table 6. This facilitates alternative comparisons and makes clear where some type–system pairs are based on very few instances.

The statement “All but 2 teams (IITB and IPN) achieved the best score in at least 1 category, which suggests that different approaches to GEC complement different error types.” (606) could note that this aligns with prior findings.

The multi-token edit analysis is valuable, but the discussion needs more context. Some systems are inherently unable to perform such edits, and none were trained on learner–fluent parallel data explicitly targeting fluency-oriented rewrites (in the sense of Sakaguchi et al., 2016). This should inform the interpretation of the results.

Other
=====
- The paper should acknowledge that some GEC systems could, in principle, output error annotations (e.g., architectures with per-type modules), though this often requires non-trivial engineering to trace edits to responsible modules. The proposed method enables a unified comparison across systems that do not naturally emit error-type labels.
- References: Several titles have capitalization issues; the Sakaguchi et al. (2016) URL needs wrapping; page numbers are missing for Efron and Tibshirani (1993).

Author response
===============
I do not regard the approach as “fatally flawed,” and this review highlights several positives. The approach is promising, but the paper is not yet ready.

The rules for error-type classification are the backbone of the work, and the lack of description is a major shortcoming. This goes beyond adding a few examples: if the rules are intended as more than a one-off implementation, they must be documented sufficiently for replication and adaptation.

Generalization beyond English should not be an afterthought. If the method is language-specific by design, that is a serious limitation. Even without conducting multilingual experiments, the paper should be transparent enough for readers to estimate the effort and feasibility of porting the method.

You state that the error types a system targets are “usually obvious from the tables,” but this is not always the case—the CUUI example and the unnecessary-token results illustrate this. In Table 5, five systems do not correct unnecessary tokens at all; this strongly suggests they did not attempt that category. Yet the paper says “There is also no obvious explanation as to why these teams had difficulty with this error type,” which overlooks design constraints and known explanations in the literature.
