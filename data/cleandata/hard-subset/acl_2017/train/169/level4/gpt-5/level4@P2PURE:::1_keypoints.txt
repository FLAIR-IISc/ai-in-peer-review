- Strength: Practical application for teachers and learners by augmenting GEC outputs with explicit error types.  
- Strength: Enables fine-grained comparison of GEC systems (overall and error-type-specific precision/recall).  
- Weakness: System description is very superficial; core appears to be handcrafted rules but lacks concrete details.  
- Missing details requested: examples of rules, number and complexity of rules, rule ordering/control strategy, and implementation specifics.  
- Paper spends excessive space reiterating CoNLL-2014 evaluation results (large table and redundant text) instead of describing the system.  
- Evaluation is flawed: annotators should have created an independent gold standard for the 200 test sentences.  
- Rater-aggregation is methodologically problematic—reporting per-rater “good/acceptable” rates can mask that many edits were judged “bad” by at least one annotator.  
- Test data are insufficiently described: unclear how many and which error categories are included and which categories annotators rated “good.”  
- Ambiguous claim about “unusual edit boundaries” needs a precise definition and examples to assess impact on usability.  
- Domain-independence claim is doubtful: Hunspell lexicon and handcrafted rules likely vary by domain and are language-dependent; evaluation datasets are limited to student essays.  
- Unclear motivation for introducing a new error category set—existing tagsets (e.g., Nicholls 2003 or CoNLL-2014) could serve or enable direct comparisons.  
- Main contribution is unclear: is it a new system, a new taxonomy, or an evaluation of CoNLL-2014 systems? Each requires different, missing elements.  
- Typos/style issues to fix: "c.f." -> "cf.", "M2" -> "M^2", clarify "50–70 F1" as percent, and correct reference capitalization (e.g., "ESL", "Fleiss, Kappa").
