- Proposes a novel evaluation method for GEC that enables per-error-type recall and precision without manual error-category annotations.  
- Strength: important advance for deeper analysis of GEC system behavior.  
- Strength: evaluation includes a diverse set of systems.  
- Strength: unlike prior work, the approach computes precision by error type, avoids manual annotation, and handles multi-token errors.  
- Strength: human experts largely approved automatically selected error tags for pre-computed spans.  
- Weakness: the rules mapping edits to error types are not described.  
- Weakness: classifier evaluation lacks a thorough error analysis and concrete improvement directions.  
- Weakness: evaluation is limited to English and unclear how to adapt to other languages.  
- Classifier: unclear how error categories were devised and whether they follow prior work.  
- Classifier: rules likely depend on alignment and preprocessing but are undocumented; examples and complexity counts are missing.  
- Classifier: continuous edit spans may fail for discontinuous phenomena (e.g., German separable-prefix verbs); portability concerns unaddressed.  
- Human evaluation: raters may have been biased by noisy boundaries and the presented system tags; need evaluation with manually created boundaries.  
- Classifier evaluation: missing detailed analysis of when raters chose “Bad” vs “Appropriate” and concrete fixes beyond POS-tag errors.  
- Gold vs auto reference: unclear which data and metrics were used for significance testing; using F0.5 may be inappropriate and comparison seems to address boundaries only, not classification.  
- Error-type results: paper fails to analyze whether systems were designed to handle specific error types; some low scores likely reflect system design rather than failures.  
- Error-type results: examples (e.g., unnecessary tokens, CUUI) suggest classification errors or system limitations and lack discussion.  
- Request: report raw TP/FP/TN/FN for Table 6 to aid interpretation and avoid small-count misinterpretation.  
- Observation: finding that different teams excel at different types aligns with prior research and should be noted.  
- Multi-token error analysis: useful but needs interpretation given many systems may inherently be unable to correct such errors or lack appropriate training data.  
- Other: note that some GEC systems can produce error annotations via submodules; references contain formatting and bibliographic errors.  
- Author response: authors agree method is sound but paper not ready; rules must be documented for replication and cross-lingual generalization must be addressed.
