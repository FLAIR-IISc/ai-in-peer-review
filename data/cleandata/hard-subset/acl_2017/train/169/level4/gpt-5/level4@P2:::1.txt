Summary and contributions (as I see them)
- The paper proposes a two-step method to automatically annotate GEC outputs with error types: (i) extract edits using a linguistically enhanced alignment and (ii) classify edits with a new, rule-based, dataset-agnostic scheme.
- It applies the method to CoNLL-2014 system outputs to provide the first detailed, by-type analysis of GEC systems.
- A tool is promised for public release, enabling fine-grained evaluation (e.g., by operation type and POS-like categories).

Strengths
- Practical impact: Automatically attaching error types to system outputs is valuable for teachers and learners, who often see only corrected text without explanations. It can also help developers diagnose system behavior by error category.
- Enables finer-grained comparison of systems: The framework supports per-type precision/recall and operation-level analyses (Missing/Unnecessary/Replacement), facilitating nuanced comparisons beyond a single overall score.
- Potentially portable approach: By relying on automatic linguistic markup and deterministic rules rather than dataset-specific training, the method could be applied across corpora and systems. This is an attractive claim, even if some caveats remain (see Weaknesses).

Weaknesses
- Insufficient system description: The core of the method is a rule-based classifier, yet the paper gives almost no detail about the rules. Readers need concrete examples, rule counts, coverage, complexity, ordering/priority (e.g., whether early rules preempt later ones), and how conflicts are resolved. Without these, it is hard to assess reproducibility, extensibility, or biases.
- Evaluation design falls short:
  - Human study design: Instead of building a small gold standard (feasible for 200 edits with a fixed tagset), raters judged the system’s labels as Good/Acceptable/Bad. Reviewing pre-annotated output is known to inflate agreement compared with annotating from scratch (cf. Marcus et al., 1993).
  - Aggregation of rater judgments: Reporting that each rater individually judged ≥95% as Good/Acceptable obscures distribution across edits. If “Bad” judgments are spread across different edits, a substantial portion of edits may be problematic for at least one rater. A per-edit aggregation (e.g., majority vote “Bad”) and clearer statistics are needed.
  - Missing details on test data: The paper does not report which error categories occurred in the 200-sample evaluation, their frequencies, or which categories were judged Good/Acceptable. This prevents assessing coverage and where the rules succeed/fail.
- Ambiguity around “unusual edit boundaries”: The paper notes that boundaries from automatic alignment may be “unusual” but gives no examples or analysis of how often this occurs, how severe the deviations are, or whether it undermines interpretability or downstream use.
- Questionable claims of domain independence: Although the approach does not require labeled training data, it still depends on resources (e.g., spaCy POS/lemmas, dependencies; Hunspell lexicon) and hand-written rules that can be domain- and language-specific. Moreover, all experiments use learner essays (FCE-test, CoNLL-2014), so cross-domain robustness remains unproven.
- Rationale for a new error taxonomy is underdeveloped: The paper motivates its tagset primarily by flexible querying (e.g., NOUN vs. R:NOUN), but existing schemes (e.g., Nicholls, 2003) and the CoNLL-2014 categories can also support such aggregation. Using an established scheme would enable direct evaluation against existing gold labels.

Questions to authors (to be addressed in the rebuttal)
- Rules and classifier:
  - How many rules are there? How are they organized (priority/ordering)? Are they mutually exclusive? Provide several concrete rule examples (inputs, features used, outputs).
  - How are conflicts handled when multiple rules apply? Is there backoff? What is the default class and when is UNK used?
  - What is the coverage of POS-only rules vs. rules that use morphology, spelling, or dependency information?
- Alignment and edit boundaries:
  - Please provide examples of “unusual” edit boundaries and quantify their frequency and impact on error typing. How sensitive are your rules to boundary errors?
  - Did you evaluate boundary accuracy separately (e.g., vs. human edit spans)?
- Human evaluation:
  - Provide per-edit aggregation of rater judgments (e.g., proportion of edits that at least one rater labeled Bad; majority-vote outcomes).
  - Report category-wise results for the 200-edit sample: which types appear, with counts, and Good/Acceptable/Bad rates per type.
  - Why not construct a small gold standard (two independent annotators + adjudication) for these 200 edits? What prevents that?
- Domain and resource dependence:
  - How does Hunspell coverage affect SPELL classification across domains? Any OOD tests (e.g., non-essay texts, specialized vocabulary)?
  - To what extent are the rules language-specific? What would be required to port to another language?
- Taxonomy choice:
  - Why introduce a new tagset instead of using Nicholls (2003) or CoNLL-2014 categories? Could your tool output both (your tags + mapped tags) to enable direct comparison with existing gold labels?

Additional comments
- The long per-team/per-type tables and descriptive text largely restate numbers that readers can read off the tables. Consider trimming and using the space to detail the rules, evaluation design, and error analyses (including boundary examples).
- If claiming that auto re-annotation of references is “as good as” gold for scoring, please report more diagnostics (e.g., how often auto and gold segmentations diverge; effect sizes) and clarify implications for different scoring schemes.

Typos and style
- Use “cf.” not “c.f.” (e.g., l129 and elsewhere).
- M^2 scorer: use a superscript 2 (or consistently styled “M^2”) instead of plain “M2” (e.g., l366 and elsewhere).
- “50–70 F1” (l319): clarify whether this means 50–70% F1.
- References: fix casing and names (e.g., “ESL”; “Fleiss,” “Kappa”). For consistency, ensure proper capitalization throughout (e.g., l878/79, l908).

Overall recommendation
The problem is important and the idea is promising and practical, but the paper needs substantially more detail on the rule-based classifier, a stronger and clearer evaluation (ideally with a small gold standard and better rater aggregation), justification for the tagset, and clarification of boundary issues and domain dependence.
