- Strengths: Useful application for teachers and learners; supports fine-grained comparison of GEC systems.

- Weaknesses: Superficial system description; unsatisfactory evaluation.

- General Discussion:
The paper proposes automatically enriching GEC system outputs with error types. This is a valuable contribution: both teachers and learners can benefit from explicit error labels (many GEC systems provide only corrected text), and it enables finer-grained comparisons across systems, including overall precision and error-type–specific precision and recall.

However, the system description is highly superficial. The core appears to be a set of (manually?) created rules, yet the paper provides no substantive details. The authors should include representative rule examples, the total number of rules, their complexity, how they are prioritized or ordered (e.g., can an earlier rule block a later one?), and any mechanisms for conflict resolution. Instead of these crucial details, several pages focus on evaluating CoNLL-2014 systems. Table 6 alone occupies an entire page, and the text largely reiterates numbers that are already apparent from the table.

The evaluation of the proposed system is unsatisfactory in several respects:
1) Gold standard vs. rating system output: The annotators should have independently created a gold standard for the 200 test sentences rather than simply rating the system’s output. With a fixed tag set, it should be feasible to produce a gold standard for such a small test set. The chosen approach likely inflates perceived quality compared to evaluation against a true gold standard (cf. Marcus et al., 1993, on agreement when reviewing pre-annotated data vs. annotating from scratch).
2) Aggregation across raters: The paper states that “all 5 raters individually considered at least 95% of our rule-based error types to be either ‘Good’ or ‘Acceptable.’” It is not standard practice to consider multiple raters individually and then average in this way. If each “bad” judgment fell on different edits (the paper does not report the distribution), then 18.5% of edits would have been judged “bad” by at least one annotator—substantially worse than the reported 3.7% average.
3) Missing details about test data: The paper does not report how many error categories are present in the test set, which categories are covered, or which categories were deemed “good” by the annotators. This makes it impossible to assess coverage or potential bias toward certain categories.
4) “Unusual” edit boundaries: The claim that “edit boundaries might be unusual” is vague. Please define precisely what constitutes an unusual boundary and provide examples. Could this affect system applicability or lead to misclassification?

The paper also claims reduced domain dependence relative to systems requiring training data. I am not convinced. For example, Hunspell’s lexicon is unlikely to cover all domains uniformly, manually constructed rules can themselves be domain-specific, and they are entirely language-dependent—an obvious drawback relative to many machine learning approaches. Moreover, the evaluation data (FCE-test, CoNLL-2014) come from a single domain: student essays.

The motivation for introducing a new error-category set is unclear. One stated reason is to facilitate searches for underspecified categories (e.g., “NOUN”). However, the Nicholls (2003) tag set appears to support such queries as well. Alternatively, why not adopt the CoNLL-2014 tag set? Doing so would also have enabled direct evaluation against the CoNLL gold standard.

In sum, the paper’s primary focus is unclear. If it introduces a new system, the essential implementation details are missing. If it proposes a new tag set, the rationale and discussion are minimal. If it aims to evaluate CoNLL-2014 systems, the analysis remains superficial.
