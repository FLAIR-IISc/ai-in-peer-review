- Strengths: The paper targets a practical and valuable use case: attaching error-type labels to GEC outputs, which can benefit teachers and learners who typically see only corrected text without explicit error categories. It also enables more granular benchmarking of GEC systems, providing overall precision as well as type-specific precision and recall.

- Weaknesses: The system description is overly high-level; key implementation details are missing. The evaluation setup is not sufficiently convincing.

- General Discussion:
The work proposes to automatically enrich GEC hypotheses with error-type annotations. This is a useful contribution because many systems do not expose error categories, and such labels are informative for pedagogy and for fine-grained system comparison. The approach relies on linguistically informed alignment to extract edits and a rule-based, dataset-agnostic classifier to assign error types, followed by an analysis of CoNLL-2014 system outputs.

However, the technical exposition of the core method is too shallow. The rules appear to be hand-crafted, yet the paper omits essential information about them: examples of rules, their total number, typical complexity, ordering/priority (and whether early rules preempt later ones), and how conflicts are resolved. Instead of providing these crucial details, substantial space is devoted to reporting CoNLL-2014 team results; Table 6 alone spans a full page, and much of the accompanying text restates what is already evident from the table.

The evaluation has several shortcomings. First, rather than asking annotators to independently create a gold standard for the 200 sampled edits, the study asks them to rate the system’s outputs. With a fixed tagset, producing a gold reference for a set this small is feasible, and rating pre-annotated output is known to inflate apparent quality compared with de novo annotation (cf. Marcus et al., 1993). Second, the claim that “all 5 raters individually considered at least 95% of predicted error types to be ‘Good’ or ‘Acceptable’” aggregates per-rater proportions; this is not standard practice. If “Bad” judgments are distributed across different items, up to 18.5% of edits could have been judged “Bad” by at least one annotator—materially worse than the reported 3.7% average “Bad” rate. Third, the paper does not characterize the sampled data in terms of which error categories are present or their distribution, nor which categories were actually covered by “Good” ratings. Fourth, the note that “edit boundaries might be unusual” needs concrete explanation and examples, along with a discussion of whether such boundary artifacts hinder downstream use.

The claim of reduced domain dependence relative to learned classifiers is also questionable. Hunspell coverage varies by domain, hand-written rules can encode domain biases, and the method is inherently language-specific, which is a disadvantage compared to some ML-based approaches. Furthermore, the experiments draw on learner essay data (FCE-test, CoNLL-2014), i.e., a single domain.

It is also unclear why a new error taxonomy is needed. One stated benefit is the ability to query underspecified categories (e.g., “NOUN”). The Nicholls (2003) scheme appears to support such hierarchical queries, and adopting the CoNLL-2014 tagset would have enabled direct evaluation against its gold labels. The paper would benefit from a stronger motivation for the new scheme and a discussion of trade-offs versus existing schemes.

In sum, the primary goal of the paper is ambiguous. If the contribution is a new system, then its most important technical details are missing. If it is a new error taxonomy, the motivation and analysis are underdeveloped. If the aim is an evaluation of CoNLL-2014 systems, the presentation largely recapitulates table entries and offers limited deeper insight.
