- Novel evaluation enables per-error-type precision (not just recall) and handles multi-token errors without manual error annotation.
- Evaluation covers a variety of GEC systems.
- Automatically assigned error tags are largely endorsed by human judges (~95% rated appropriate).
- Major omission: rules used to derive/assign error types are not described or exemplified.
- Unclear whether error categories are based on prior work or how they were devised.
- Likely dependence of error-tagging rules on alignment and preprocessing, but paper gives no details on rule count/complexity.
- No discussion of how to adapt rules/classifier to other languages; generalizability is not addressed.
- Preprocessing produces continuous spans, which may fail for discontinuous edits (e.g., German separable verbs); classifier’s ability to tag discontinuous spans is not evaluated.
- Human evaluation methodology is problematic: raters saw system output (possible bias), no intermediate rating option, and no comparison against manually created boundaries.
- Classifier evaluation lacks detailed error analysis and actionable insights for improvement; “Bad” ratings are only loosely attributed to POS errors.
- Significance-testing details are unclear; using F0.5 for tests is questionable due to weak discriminative power.
- Comparison between gold and auto references appears limited to boundaries and not to classification, which is not clearly explained.
- Error-type results are not analyzed with respect to whether systems were designed to handle those error types.
- Some system failures (e.g., zero scores) may reflect design limitations rather than classification errors, but this is not discussed.
- Request to report raw TP/FP/TN/FN counts (e.g., in appendix) to enable alternative comparisons and reveal sparse-data issues.
- Multi-token error analysis is useful but needs interpretation—systems/training data may be inherently unable to correct such errors.
- Paper should acknowledge that some GEC systems can already produce error annotations (e.g., submodule-based systems), while noting the proposed method’s advantage for unified comparison.
- Minor issues: reference formatting and missing page/URL wrapping details.
- Author-response alignment: reviewer agrees approach is promising but stresses that missing rule descriptions and lack of cross-language generalization are major flaws that must be addressed.
