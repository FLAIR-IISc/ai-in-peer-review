- Paper proposes a method to evaluate GEC systems per error type, reporting both recall and precision to overcome lack of error-type annotations.

- Strength: framework enables fine-grained analysis of GEC behavior.

- Strength: evaluation includes a diverse set of systems.

- Strength: reports precision by error type, requires no manual error-type annotation, and supports multi-token edits.

- Strength: human experts largely endorsed the automatically assigned error tags.

- Weakness: the rules used to assign error types are not described.

- Weakness: the classifier evaluation lacks detailed error analysis and actionable improvement directions.

- Weakness: evaluation is limited to English and portability to other languages is unclear.

- Taxonomy rationale is not explained or tied to existing schemes.

- Rule set likely depends on alignment/merging specifics, but no representative rules or feature-to-category mappings are provided.

- No information on number, complexity, or per-category coverage of rules, making adaptation effort unclear.

- Edit spans appear restricted to contiguous ranges, raising concerns about handling discontinuous edits (e.g., separable verb prefixes).

- Rater judgments may be biased by noisy automatic boundaries and the rating scale lacked a neutral option; curated-boundary evaluation is needed to isolate classifier quality.

- Classifier evaluation reports POS errors as a cause for “Bad” cases but lacks systematic breakdown of “Bad” vs “Appropriate” cases to identify rule gaps or confusions.

- Significance-testing target is unclear (was F0.5 used?), and F0.5 may be a poor choice due to limited discriminative power.

- Gold-vs-auto comparison focuses on boundary consistency but does not clearly assess error-type agreement.

- Some systems’ failures (e.g., on unnecessary tokens) may reflect system design limitations rather than classification errors; analysis does not account for which systems target which error types.

- Request to include raw TP/FP/TN/FN counts (e.g., for Table 6) to enable clearer, alternative comparisons.

- Observation that different teams excel in different categories aligns with prior findings and should be noted.

- Multi-token edit analysis is valuable but needs context: many systems cannot perform such edits and were not trained on fluency-oriented rewrites.

- Paper should acknowledge that some systems could output error annotations and position the method as enabling unified comparison across such systems.

- Editorial issues: reference capitalization problems, a URL wrapping issue, and missing page numbers for Efron & Tibshirani (1993).

- Reviewer: approach is promising but not yet ready; lack of documented rules is a major shortcoming preventing replication and adaptation.

- Generalization beyond English must be addressed or acknowledged as a serious limitation.

- Authors’ claim that targeted error types are “usually obvious” is challenged by examples (e.g., CUUI, unnecessary-token) showing the need to consider system design constraints.
