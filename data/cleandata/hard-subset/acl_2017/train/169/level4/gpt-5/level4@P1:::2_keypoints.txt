- Proposes an automatic method to annotate original/corrected sentence pairs with explicit edit spans and error types for per-type precision and recall evaluation.
- Method extracts edits via linguistically enhanced alignment and assigns types with a dataset-independent, rule-based classifier driven by automatic markup.
- Releases a tool and applies it to all CoNLL-2014 system outputs, producing the first detailed cross-system error-type analysis (operation tier, multi-token edits, detection vs. correction).
- Small human study reports ≥95% of auto-assigned error types judged “Good” or “Acceptable.”

- Strength: Fills a gap by enabling per-error-type precision (not only recall) for unannotated hypotheses, improving diagnostic evaluation.
- Strength: Broad application to many CoNLL-2014 systems yields informative cross-system comparisons.
- Strength: Dataset independence — no labeled training required; uses automatic tags/lemmas/parses.
- Strength: Transparent, deterministic labeling rules (claimed).
- Strength: Separation of edit extraction from scoring simplifies evaluation and supports multi-token analyses.
- Strength: Human validation suggests rules are reasonable despite noisy boundaries.

- Weakness: Core rules mapping edits to error types are not described, harming transparency, reproducibility, and assessment of coverage/design rationale.
- Weakness: Human study confounded by noisy auto-derived edit spans and an insufficient rating scale; manual-span evaluation needed.
- Weakness: Classifier error analysis is shallow — lacks breakdown of “Bad” vs. “Acceptable,” systematic confusions, and rule failure cases.
- Weakness: Cross-lingual generalization unaddressed — unclear retuning effort for tagsets, morphology, lexicons, and possible category adaptation.
- Weakness: Alignment yields only contiguous spans; discontinuous edits (e.g., separable verb prefixes) are not handled or discussed.
- Weakness: Unclear what statistic was bootstrapped (sentence F0.5 vs. per-type averages) and whether error-type mappings were normalized between gold and auto.
- Weakness: Evaluation interpretation issues — some systems scoring zero for types they may not target; paper should distinguish “not attempted” from “attempted but failed.”
- Weakness: Reports lack raw TP/FP/FN per team/type, which is needed for alternative metrics and to reveal results based on few instances.
- Weakness: Multi-token error findings need context about whether systems can propose longer edits or lack relevant training data.

- Question: On what prior work/guidelines are the error categories based, and what principles guided consolidation?
- Question: How many rules are used overall and per category, and what is their complexity? Please provide concrete rule examples.
- Question: How dependent are rules on specific tagger/parser/lemmatizer conventions, and how are tagging/parsing errors handled?
- Question: What changes and effort are required to port the framework to another language (UD mapping, morphology, lexicons, word-order rules)?
- Question: Can the framework handle discontinuous edits; if not, what extensions are needed?
- Question: Which statistic was bootstrapped in Section 4.1 (sentence-level F0.5, macro/micro per-type), and why is it appropriate?
- Question: Will raw TP/FP/FN counts per type be released to support alternative analyses?
- Question: In the human study, how often were items rated “Good” vs. “Acceptable,” and what common “Bad” error patterns beyond POS issues occurred?

- Additional: The approach is valuable because it enables uniform comparison across heterogeneous systems without requiring system modification.
- Additional: Fix minor reference/formatting issues (capitalization, long URLs, missing page info).

- Post-rebuttal: Paper remains insufficiently detailed about the rule set; documentation is required for replication and adaptation.
- Post-rebuttal: Generalization beyond English should be addressed explicitly so readers can estimate adaptation effort and likely performance.
- Post-rebuttal: The paper must more clearly infer or report which error types systems actually target rather than assuming it is obvious from tables.
