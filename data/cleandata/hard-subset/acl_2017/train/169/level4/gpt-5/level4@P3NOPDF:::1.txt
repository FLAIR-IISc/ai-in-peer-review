Summary
The paper proposes a rule-based method for automatically assigning error-type labels to the outputs of grammatical error correction (GEC) systems. This is a timely and useful goal: explicit error types can support teachers and learners and enable finer-grained system analysis beyond overall precision/recall or F scores.

Strengths
- Clear and practically relevant motivation: labeled error types benefit pedagogy and diagnostic evaluation of GEC systems.
- Potential to enable fine-grained comparisons across systems (overall precision/recall and per-type breakdowns).
- Focus on post hoc labeling could be broadly useful across existing GEC outputs.

Weaknesses
- System description is far too superficial to assess validity or reproduce results.
- Evaluation design is not convincing: no independently created gold standard, limited reporting on inter-annotator agreement, and unclear aggregation of ratings.
- Domain and language independence claims are not substantiated.
- Rationale for introducing a new tagset is underdeveloped; alignment with existing tagsets is missing.
- Extensive space given to CoNLL-2014 system results with little analysis; core method details are omitted.

Detailed comments and suggestions

1) Method description and reproducibility
- The paper should provide concrete details about the rule set: how many rules; how they were created (handwritten? semi-automatic?); representative examples; rule complexity (patterns, features used); and coverage by error type.
- Describe rule ordering and conflict resolution. Do earlier rules block later ones? Is there priority or scoring? How are overlapping matches handled?
- Specify all external resources and preprocessors (e.g., tokenizer, POS tagger, parser, Hunspell), versions, and configurations. Clarify whether lexicon/domain coverage affects performance.
- Discuss ambiguity handling (multiple possible labels), tie-breaking, and default behaviors.
- Without these details, it is difficult to judge generalizability or to replicate the approach. Consider releasing rules, mappings, and scripts.

2) Evaluation design
- Gold standard vs. rating system output: Instead of rating the system’s assigned labels, create an independently annotated gold standard on the 200 sentences (feasible at this scale), then evaluate precision/recall per error type and overall. Prior work (e.g., Marcus et al., 1993) shows that reviewing pre-annotated output inflates agreement compared to independent annotation from scratch.
- Inter-annotator agreement: Report agreement over both label types and edit boundaries (e.g., Fleiss’ kappa for multiple raters, or Krippendorff’s alpha), with confidence intervals. Agreement on boundaries is particularly important if “edit boundaries might be unusual.”
- Aggregation of ratings: Averaging “Good/Acceptable/Bad” per rater obscures the distribution across edits. Report, for each edit, how many raters judged it “Bad,” and summarize the proportion of edits with at least one “Bad” judgment. Provide per-type breakdowns and a confusion matrix.
- Test data characterization: Specify the distribution of error categories in the test set, the number of edits per type, and which types are present/absent. Report coverage (what proportion of edits received a type) and how long-tail categories were treated.
- Baselines and ablations: Include baselines (e.g., a simple labeler based on alignment heuristics or a frequency-based default) and ablations (e.g., without Hunspell; without specific rule families) to demonstrate the contribution of each component.
- Error analysis: Provide qualitative examples of correct and incorrect label assignments, with discussion of typical failure modes.

3) “Unusual” edit boundaries
- Clarify what is meant by “edit boundaries might be unusual” and show concrete examples. Are boundaries misaligned with minimal edits, token boundaries, or M^2 conventions? Explain how such boundaries impact labeling accuracy and downstream uses (teacher feedback, per-type metrics). If boundary conventions differ from M^2, justify and quantify the effect.

4) Domain and language dependence
- The claim of reduced domain dependence relative to learned models is not supported. Lexica (e.g., Hunspell) vary in domain coverage, and handcrafted rules can encode domain assumptions. Provide cross-domain evaluation (beyond student essays in FCE-test and CoNLL-2014) or qualify the claim.
- Language dependence is strong for rule-based systems; discuss portability to other languages, the effort required to port rules, and any language-agnostic design choices.

5) Tagset design and alignment
- The motivation for introducing a new tagset is unclear. If the goal is searchability or hierarchical grouping (e.g., general “NOUN” vs. specific subtypes), explain why existing tagsets (e.g., FCE/Nicholls 2003 or CoNLL-2014) are insufficient and what concrete benefits your design brings.
- Provide an explicit mapping between your tags and existing schemes, ideally with examples. If you adopt or align with CoNLL-2014 categories, you could leverage its gold standard for evaluation.
- Discuss granularity trade-offs, inter-annotator reliability, and expected utility for teachers/learners.

6) Use of space and analysis of CoNLL-2014 systems
- The long results table for all CoNLL-2014 systems adds little without analysis. Move exhaustive tables to an appendix and focus on substantive insights enabled by your labeling (e.g., which systems excel at specific error types, where they differ, and how conclusions change versus aggregate F scores). Avoid repeating numbers that are obvious from the table.

7) Clarity, claims, and contributions
- Clearly articulate the paper’s primary contribution: a labeling system, a tagset, and/or an evaluative study of GEC outputs. Each claimed contribution should be supported with sufficient methodological detail, a solid evaluation, and clear takeaways.

Minor issues and typos
- l129 (and elsewhere): “c.f.” → “cf.”
- l366 (and elsewhere): “M2” → typeset as M^2 (M with superscript 2).
- l319: “50–70 F1” → clarify (e.g., “50–70% F1”).
- Check references for case: e.g., l908: “esl” → “ESL”; l878/79: capitalize “Fleiss” and “Kappa.”
- Proofread for consistency in capitalization of error types, tag names, and resource names.

Overall recommendation
Promising and useful idea, but the paper in its current form lacks essential methodological detail and a rigorous, transparent evaluation. I encourage the authors to (i) fully document the rule set and processing pipeline, (ii) conduct an evaluation against an independently annotated gold standard with proper agreement metrics and clearer aggregation, (iii) justify and align the tagset with prior work, and (iv) provide focused analyses demonstrating the added value of error-type labeling.
