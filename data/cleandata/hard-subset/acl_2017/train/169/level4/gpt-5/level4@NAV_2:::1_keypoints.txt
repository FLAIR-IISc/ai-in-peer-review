- Targets a practical use case: adding error-type labels to GEC outputs benefits teachers/learners and enables fine-grained benchmarking.  
- System description is overly high-level and omits key implementation details.  
- Evaluation setup is unconvincing and inadequately described.  
- Method: uses linguistically informed alignment to extract edits and a rule-based, dataset-agnostic classifier, applied to CoNLL-2014 outputs.  
- Missing crucial information about the hand-crafted rules: examples, total number, complexity, rule ordering/priority, and conflict resolution.  
- Paper devotes excessive space to restating CoNLL-2014 team results (e.g., Table 6) instead of method details or insight.  
- Evaluation used annotators to rate system outputs rather than produce an independent gold standard, which can inflate quality estimates.  
- Aggregating per-rater proportions misrepresents disagreement; distributed “Bad” judgments could mean up to ~18.5% problematic edits despite a low average.  
- No breakdown of sampled data by error category or which categories received “Good” ratings.  
- “Unusual” edit boundary artifacts are noted but not exemplified or analyzed for downstream impact.  
- Claim of reduced domain dependence is questionable given Hunspell limits, potential domain biases in hand-written rules, language specificity, and single-domain experiments.  
- Need for a new error taxonomy is unclear; lacks motivation and comparison to existing schemes (e.g., Nicholls, CoNLL-2014).  
- Primary contribution is ambiguous: missing technical detail if the contribution is a system, or weak motivation/analysis if it’s a taxonomy or evaluation.  
- Typos and formatting issues noted (use “cf.”, typeset “M^2”, clarify “50–70% F1”, standardize reference capitalization and names).
