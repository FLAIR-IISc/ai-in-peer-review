The paper introduces a novel evaluation method for Grammatical Error Correction (GEC) systems, enabling performance assessment by error type based on both precision and recall – a capability previously limited by the lack of error category annotations in system outputs.

**Strengths:**

*   The proposed evaluation represents a significant advancement for analyzing GEC system behavior.
*   The paper provides a comprehensive evaluation across a diverse set of GEC systems.
*   The approach offers several advantages over existing methods: it calculates precision by error type, operates independently of manual error annotation, and can assess performance on multi-token errors.
*   Human experts largely validate the automatically assigned error tags for pre-computed error spans.

**Weaknesses:**

*   The core component – the rules used to derive error types – are not detailed within the paper.
*   The classifier evaluation lacks a thorough error analysis, hindering the identification of potential improvements for the classifier.
*   The evaluation is currently limited to English, and the challenges of adapting the approach to other languages remain unclear.

**Classifier and Classifier Evaluation**

The rationale behind the error category design is not explicitly stated. It is unclear whether these categories are grounded in prior research. While the approach generally avoids reliance on alignment algorithms, the underlying rules likely do depend on them, but this dependency is not discussed. The error categories are central to the paper, and readers require at least a representative example of a rule used for error type assignment.

The paper does not extend the proposed evaluation to languages beyond English, nor does it discuss the necessary modifications for applying the classifier to other languages. The rules governing edit boundary determination and error tag assignment likely depend on the specific language and pre-processing pipeline, necessitating adaptation. Furthermore, the error categories themselves may require revision. The authors do not provide sufficient detail regarding the complexity and number of rules used for error category assignment to allow for an estimation of the effort required for cross-lingual adaptation.

The pre-processing step yields inherently continuous error spans, mirroring the behavior of the M2 scorer. This is problematic as some errors require discontinuous spans for accurate tagging. For example, in German, separable verb prefixes are often separated in main clauses. It is unclear whether the classifier can handle such discontinuous edit spans.

The authors state that human judges rated at least 95% of automatically assigned error tags as appropriate, despite the noise introduced by automatic edit extraction. This claim warrants caution, as raters may have been more lenient due to boundary inaccuracies. The evaluation design, where raters were aware of the system output, may have introduced bias. The absence of a rating option between "Bad" and "Appropriate" could also have influenced responses. A more robust evaluation would involve assessing human ratings of classifier output with manually created, noise-free boundaries.

The classifier evaluation lacks a detailed error analysis. While the authors note that "Bad" ratings are often linked to incorrect POS tags, further investigation is needed. Specifically, the paper should address when raters selected "Bad" versus "Appropriate," and whether expert ratings suggest avenues for classifier improvement.

**Gold Reference vs. Auto Reference**

The specific data used for the significance test is not clearly defined. If the test was performed on F0.5 scores, this may be inappropriate given their limited discriminative power, as differing recall and precision values can yield the same F0.5 score. Additionally, the authors highlight a mismatch between automatic and reference outputs regarding alignment and classification in Section 4.1, but the comparison between gold and reference data appears to be limited to boundary alignment, not classification.

**Error Type Evaluation**

The failure of five teams to correct unnecessary token errors is not surprising. At least two systems, such as UFC, are inherently unable to handle superfluous words due to their reliance on POS tags. Rozovskaya & Roth (2016) also explain AMU’s poor performance on this error type.

The authors do not analyze or comment on the results in Table 6 in relation to the systems’ intended capabilities. For certain error types, like word order errors, a direct mapping exists between the gold standard and auto reference. However, it remains unclear whether systems genuinely failed to correct specific error types or were simply not designed to do so. The CUUI case, with precision+recall=0.0 despite not targeting word order errors, suggests a potential misclassification that is neither analyzed nor discussed.

Please include raw values for TP, FP, TN, and FN in the appendix for Table 6 to facilitate easier comparison between systems using alternative metrics. Furthermore, the results in Table 6 for some error types and systems are based on a limited number of instances, which should be clarified when reporting the raw values.

The statement that “All but 2 teams (IITB and IPN) achieved the best score in at least 1 category, which suggests that different approaches to GEC complement different error types” (606) would benefit from acknowledging prior research supporting this observation.

The multi-token error analysis is valuable for future work, but requires further interpretation. Some systems may be fundamentally incapable of correcting such errors, and none of the evaluated systems were trained on a parallel corpus of learner data and fluent corrections (as defined by Sakaguchi et al., 2016).

**Other**

*   The authors should acknowledge that error annotations were previously possible for some GEC approaches, particularly those with dedicated submodules for specific error types. Adapting these systems to include the submodule responsible for a change in the system output would allow for comparison with the proposed approach in a unified manner.
*   References: Some titles are missing capitalization. The URL for Sakaguchi et al. (2016) should be properly formatted. Page information is missing for Efron and Tibshirani (1993).
