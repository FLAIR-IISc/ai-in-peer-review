## Review

**Strengths:** The approach offers a valuable application for both educators and learners, facilitating fine-grained comparison of Grammatical Error Correction (GEC) systems through precision, recall, and error type-specific metrics.

**Weaknesses:** The system description is notably superficial, and the evaluation presented is unconvincing.

**General Discussion:**

This paper introduces a method for automatically annotating GEC system outputs with error types – a beneficial addition, as many GEC systems only provide corrected text without explicit error identification. This capability enables more nuanced system comparisons, moving beyond overall precision to error-specific performance analysis. 

However, the core of the system, a set of rules, is described with insufficient detail. The paper lacks crucial information such as examples of these rules, their total number, complexity, and ordering logic (e.g., potential blocking relationships between rules). Instead of detailing the system’s mechanics, a significant portion of the paper is dedicated to presenting results from the CoNLL-2014 shared task, largely reiterating information readily available in Table 6.

The evaluation of the proposed system is problematic on several fronts. First, rather than having annotators independently create a gold standard for the test set, they were asked to rate the system’s output. Establishing a gold standard for the relatively small test set would have been feasible and would provide a more robust evaluation baseline, avoiding the potential inflation of results observed when reviewing pre-annotated data (Marcus et al., 1993). 

Second, the averaging of individual rater scores is questionable. Reporting that 18.5% of edits received a “bad” rating from at least one annotator presents a more concerning picture than the reported average of 3.7%. Third, the paper lacks essential information about the test data itself, including the number and coverage of error categories considered “good” by the annotators. Finally, the phrase “edit boundaries might be unusual” requires clarification and illustrative examples; its potential impact on system application should be addressed.

The authors claim reduced domain dependence compared to data-driven systems. This assertion is debatable. The reliance on resources like Hunspell’s vocabulary introduces domain-specific limitations, and manually crafted rules are inherently language and potentially domain-dependent – a disadvantage relative to machine learning approaches. Furthermore, the evaluation data (FCE and CoNLL-2014) are limited to student essays, representing a single domain.

The rationale behind the new error category design remains unclear. While the authors cite the need for searching underspecified categories, existing tagsets (e.g., Nicholls, 2003) appear to offer similar functionality. Utilizing the CoNLL-2014 tagset would have allowed for direct evaluation against the existing gold standard.

In summary, the paper’s central motivation is ambiguous. It is unclear whether the primary contribution lies in the system itself (given the lack of detail), the proposed error categories (with limited justification), or the evaluation of CoNLL-2014 systems (presented superficially).
