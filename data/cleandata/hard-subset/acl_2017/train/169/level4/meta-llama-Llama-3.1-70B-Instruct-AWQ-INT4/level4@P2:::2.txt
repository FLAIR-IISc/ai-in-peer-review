**Summary of the Paper**

This paper presents a novel approach for evaluating grammatical error correction (GEC) systems. The authors propose a method to automatically annotate parallel error correction data with explicit edit spans and error type information, enabling the assessment of GEC system performance by error type in terms of both recall and precision. The approach uses a linguistically-enhanced alignment algorithm to extract edits between parallel original and corrected sentences and a new rule-based classifier to assign error types.

**Contributions of the Paper**

1. **Automatic annotation of error types**: The authors propose a method to automatically annotate error correction data with error type information, enabling the evaluation of GEC systems by error type.
2. **Dataset-independent error type evaluation**: The approach is independent of manual error annotation and can assess the performance of GEC systems on multi-token errors.
3. **Detailed evaluation of system error type performance**: The authors demonstrate the value of their approach by carrying out a detailed evaluation of system error type performance for the first time for all teams in the CoNLL-2014 shared task on Grammatical Error Correction.

**Strengths of the Paper**

1. The proposed evaluation is an important stepping stone for analyzing GEC system behavior.
2. The paper includes evaluation for a variety of systems.
3. The approach has several advantages over previous work, including computing precision by error type, independence of manual error annotation, and assessing performance on multi-token errors.
4. The automatically selected error tags for pre-computed error spans are mostly approved of by human experts.

**Weaknesses of the Paper**

1. **Lack of description of error type classification rules**: The authors do not provide details on the rules used to assign error types, making it difficult to understand the approach and its limitations.
2. **Limited evaluation of the classifier**: The classifier evaluation lacks a thorough error analysis, and the authors do not provide directions for future work on how to improve the classifier.
3. **Unclear generalizability to other languages**: The authors do not provide information on how to adapt the approach to other languages, which could be a significant limitation.
4. **Lack of transparency in error type evaluation**: The authors do not provide raw values for TP, FP, TN, and FN in the appendix, making it difficult to compare the systems using other measures.

**Questions to Authors**

1. Can you provide more details on the rules used to assign error types and how they were developed?
2. How do you plan to improve the classifier, and what are the potential limitations of the current approach?
3. Can you provide more information on how to adapt the approach to other languages, and what are the potential challenges?
4. Can you provide raw values for TP, FP, TN, and FN in the appendix to enable a more detailed comparison of the systems?
