The paper presents a novel approach for evaluating grammatical error correction (GEC) systems, allowing for the assessment of their performance by error type in terms of both recall and precision. This is a significant improvement over previous methods, which typically only evaluated GEC systems in terms of recall, and often relied on manual error annotation.

Strengths of the paper include the importance of the proposed evaluation approach for analyzing GEC system behavior, the inclusion of evaluations for a variety of systems, and the advantages of the approach over previous work. Specifically, the approach computes precision by error type, is independent of manual error annotation, and can assess performance on multi-token errors. Additionally, the automatically selected error tags for pre-computed error spans are mostly approved by human experts.

However, there are several weaknesses to the paper. A key aspect of the approach, the rules for deriving error types, are not described in sufficient detail. The classifier evaluation also lacks a thorough error analysis, and does not provide clear directions for future work on improving the classifier. Furthermore, the evaluation was only performed for English, and it is unclear how difficult it would be to adapt the approach to other languages.

One major concern is the lack of detail provided on the rules for assigning error categories. The reader is not given a clear understanding of how these rules work, or how they were developed. This makes it difficult to evaluate the effectiveness of the approach, and to consider how it might be adapted to other languages. Additionally, the error categories themselves may need to be changed or expanded when applying the approach to other languages.

The paper also does not address the potential issue of discontinuous error spans. In some languages, such as German, errors may span multiple tokens in a sentence, and the approach may not be able to accurately identify these errors. The authors should consider how to modify the approach to handle such cases.

The evaluation of the classifier also raises some concerns. While the authors report that human judges rated at least 95% of the automatically assigned error tags as appropriate, this judgment may be overly optimistic. The raters may have been more forgiving of noisy boundaries, and the lack of a rating option between "Bad (Not Appropriate)" and "Appropriate" may have led raters to select "Appropriate" over "Bad". To make the evaluation more sound, the authors should also evaluate how human judges rate the classifier output when the boundaries are manually created.

The comparison between gold and auto references is also unclear. The authors refer to the mismatch between automatic and reference in terms of alignment and classification, but the comparison is only made in terms of boundaries, not classification. The authors should clarify this point and provide more detail on the comparison.

The error type evaluation also raises some questions. The authors do not analyze or comment on the results in Table 6 with respect to whether the systems were designed to handle the error type. For some error types, there is a straightforward mapping between error type in the gold standard and in the auto reference, but the authors do not discuss this. Additionally, the authors should report raw values for TP, FP, TN, and FN in the appendix for Table 6, to make it easier to compare the systems using other measures.

Finally, the authors should mention that the finding that different approaches to GEC complement different error types is in line with previous research. The multi-token error analysis is helpful for future work, but the results need more interpretation. Some systems may be inherently unable to correct such errors, but none of the systems were trained on a parallel corpus of learner data and fluent corrections.

In terms of minor points, the authors should mention that for some GEC approaches, it was not impossible to provide error annotations before, e.g., systems with submodules for one error type each. The references also need to be checked for formatting errors, such as missing capitalizations and page information.

Overall, while the approach presented in the paper has many strengths, the paper itself requires more work to be considered ready for publication. The authors need to provide more detail on the rules for deriving error types, and to address the concerns raised above.
