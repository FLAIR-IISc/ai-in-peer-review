Strengths: The proposed system offers a valuable application for both educators and learners, enabling a more nuanced comparison of Grammar Error Correction (GEC) systems.

Weaknesses: The paper's description of the system is overly superficial, and the evaluation methodology is not convincing.

General Discussion:

The paper presents a novel approach to automatically enriching the output of GEC systems with error types, providing a useful application for teachers and learners. This enhancement facilitates a finer-grained comparison of GEC systems in terms of precision, recall, and error type-specific figures. However, the description of the system remains lacking in detail, particularly regarding the set of manually created rules that comprise its core. The authors should provide examples of these rules, specify their number, complexity, and ordering, to enable a deeper understanding of the system.

Instead of delving into the system's details, the paper dedicates several pages to evaluating the systems that participated in CoNLL-2014. The presentation of results in Table 6 is repetitive and could be improved. Moreover, the evaluation of the proposed system is unsatisfying in several aspects. Firstly, the annotators should have independently annotated a gold standard for the 200 test sentences, rather than simply rating the system's output. This approach may have yielded more accurate results, as seen in Marcus et al. (1993).

Secondly, the paper's evaluation methodology is flawed, as it considers multiple raters individually and averages their ratings. This approach may mask significant discrepancies in ratings, as evidenced by the 18.5% of edits considered "bad" by some annotators. Furthermore, the paper lacks information about the test data, including the number of error categories and their distribution.

Thirdly, the statement that "edit boundaries might be unusual" requires clarification and examples to understand its implications for the system's application. Additionally, the authors' claim that their system is less domain-dependent than systems requiring training data is questionable, as manually created rules can be domain-dependent, and the test data used is from a single domain (student essays).

The introduction of a new set of error categories is unclear, and the motivation behind this decision is not adequately discussed. The tagset presented in Nicholls (2003) seems to support similar searches, and using the CoNLL-2014 tagset could have enabled the use of the CoNLL gold standard for evaluation.

In conclusion, the paper's main motivation remains unclear, and the presentation of results is superficial. The authors should provide more details about the system, clarify the evaluation methodology, and discuss the implications of their approach.
