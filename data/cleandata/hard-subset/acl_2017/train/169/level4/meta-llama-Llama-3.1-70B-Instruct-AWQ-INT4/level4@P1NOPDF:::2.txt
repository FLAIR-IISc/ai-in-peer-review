The paper presents a novel approach for evaluating grammatical error correction (GEC) systems, enabling the assessment of their performance by error type in terms of both recall and precision. This approach is particularly significant, as it addresses a limitation of previous methods, which often relied on manual error annotation.

Strengths of the paper include:

- The proposed evaluation method is an essential step forward in analyzing GEC system behavior.
- The paper includes a comprehensive evaluation of various systems.
- The approach has several advantages over previous work, including the ability to compute precision by error type, independence from manual error annotation, and the capacity to assess performance on multi-token errors.
- The automatically selected error tags for pre-computed error spans are largely approved by human experts.

However, there are several weaknesses that need to be addressed:

- A crucial aspect of the paper, the rules for deriving error types, is not described in sufficient detail.
- The classifier evaluation lacks a thorough error analysis, which would provide valuable insights into directions for future work on improving the classifier.
- The evaluation was only performed for English, and it is unclear how challenging it would be to adapt the approach to another language.

Classifier and Classifier Evaluation
====================================

The basis for the error categories is unclear, and it is uncertain whether they are based on previous work. Although the approach is generally independent of the alignment algorithm, the rules for assigning error types are likely dependent on the algorithm, but the authors do not provide sufficient details. The error categories are a vital component of the paper, and the reader should be given a clear understanding of how the rules for assigning error types are defined.

Unfortunately, the paper does not apply the proposed evaluation to languages other than English, nor does it elaborate on the necessary changes to adapt the classifier to other languages. It is likely that the rules for determining edit boundaries and error tags are language-dependent and would require modifications. Additionally, the error categories might need to be revised. The authors should provide more information on the rules for assigning error categories, including their complexity and the number of rules per error type, to facilitate an estimation of the effort required to adapt the approach to another language.

The error spans computed in the pre-processing step appear to be inherently continuous, which is problematic, as there are errors that can only be accurately tagged with discontinuous error spans. For instance, in German, verbs with separable prefixes are separated from each other in the main clause. It is unclear whether the classifier can handle discontinuous edit spans.

The authors state that all human judges rated at least 95% of the automatically assigned error tags as appropriate, despite the noise introduced by automatic edit extraction. However, this judgment should be treated with caution, as the raters might have been more forgiving when the boundaries were noisy. Furthermore, the raters were not asked to select a tag without knowing the system output, which could have biased them towards the system output. Additionally, there was no rating option between "Bad (Not Appropriate)" and "Appropriate," which might have led raters to select "Appropriate" over "Bad." To strengthen the evaluation, the authors should also assess how human judges rate the classifier output when the boundaries are manually created, i.e., without the noise introduced by faulty boundaries.

The classifier evaluation lacks a thorough error analysis. It is only mentioned that "Bad" ratings are usually due to incorrect POS tags. Further analysis is necessary to address questions such as when raters select "Bad" versus "Appropriate" and whether the ratings by experts point to possibilities for improving the classifier.

Gold Reference vs. Auto Reference
=================================

It is unclear what data the significance test was performed on. If the test was conducted on the F0.5 scores, this might not be the best approach, as F0.5 is a derived measure with limited discriminative power. The performance in terms of recall and precision can be vastly different but still result in the same F0.5 score. At the beginning of Section 4.1, the authors refer to the mismatch between automatic and reference in terms of alignment and classification, but the comparison between gold and reference is only in terms of boundaries, not classification.

Error Type Evaluation
=====================

It is not surprising that five teams failed to correct any unnecessary token errors. For at least two of the systems, there is a straightforward explanation for this failure. The most obvious is UFC, which uses a rule-based approach that operates on POS tags and is unable to determine superfluous words based on POS alone. Rozovskaya & Roth (2016) provide an explanation for AMU's poor performance on superfluous words.

The authors do not analyze or comment on the results in Table 6 with respect to whether the systems were designed to handle the error type. For some error types, there is a straightforward mapping between the error type in the gold standard and the auto reference, such as word order errors. However, it remains unclear whether the systems failed completely on specific error types or were simply not designed to correct them. For instance, CUUI is reported with precision+recall=0.0, although it does not target word order errors. This also points to an error in the classification, which is neither analyzed nor discussed.

The authors should report raw values for TP, FP, TN, and FN in the appendix for Table 6, as this would facilitate comparison of the systems using other measures. Additionally, it appears that for some error types and systems, the results in Table 6 are based on only a few instances. Reporting raw values would also clarify this.

The authors state that "All but 2 teams (IITB and IPN) achieved the best score in at least 1 category, which suggests that different approaches to GEC complement different error types." It would be beneficial to mention that this is in line with previous research.

The multi-token error analysis is helpful for future work, but the results require more interpretation. Some systems are probably inherently unable to correct such errors, but none of the systems were trained on a parallel corpus of learner data and fluent corrections.

Other
=====

- The authors should acknowledge that for some GEC approaches, it was not impossible to provide error annotations before, such as systems with submodules for specific error types. Although the system would need to be adapted to include the submodule responsible for a change in the system output, the proposed approach enables the comparison of GEC systems for which producing an error-tagged output is not straightforward to other systems in a unified way.
- References: Some titles lack capitalization. The URL for Sakaguchi et al. (2016) needs to be wrapped, and page information is missing for Efron and Tibshirani (1993).

Author response
===============

I agree that the approach is not "fatally flawed," and I believe this review highlights several positive aspects. However, the paper is not yet ready for publication.

The basis for the paper is the rules for classifying errors, and the lack of description is a significant limitation. This is not just a matter of providing additional examples; the rules need to be described to be replicable or adaptable.

Generalization to other languages should not be an afterthought. It would be a serious limitation if the approach only worked on one language by design. Even if the authors do not perform an adaptation for other languages, their approach should be transparent enough for others to estimate the effort required for such an adaptation and how well it could reasonably work. Simply stating that most research is targeted at ESL only reinforces the problem.

The authors write that the error types certain systems tackle would be "usually obvious from the tables." However, this is not as simple as it seems. For instance, the CUUI example and the unnecessary token errors demonstrate that there are cases where it is not clear whether the systems failed completely on specific error types or were simply not designed to correct them. There are five systems that do not correct unnecessary token errors (Table 5), and it should be obvious that they did not attempt to tackle them. However, in the paper, the authors state that "There is also no obvious explanation as to why these teams had difficulty with this error type."
