- Proposes a novel GEC evaluation method that measures recall and precision by error type, improving over prior methods that lacked error-category annotation.  
- Evaluates a variety of systems, offering a comprehensive comparison.  
- Method advantages: computes precision by error type, avoids manual error annotation, and handles multi-token errors.  
- Automatically selected error tags are largely confirmed by human experts.  
- Major weakness: rules for deriving error types are not described, making error categories opaque and hard to adapt.  
- Classifier evaluation is limited, lacking thorough error analysis and concrete directions for improvement.  
- Evaluation was performed only for English; adaptability to other languages is unclear.  
- Need to detail how error-category rules were developed and how they can be adapted to other languages.  
- Investigate why human judges labeled some tags “Bad” and report findings to guide classifier improvements.  
- Unclear on which data the significance test was run; clarify and ensure thorough gold vs. auto reference comparison.  
- Insufficient analysis of results (Table 6); authors should interpret whether systems were designed for specific error types and provide raw TP/FP/TN/FN in an appendix.  
- Acknowledge that some prior GEC approaches could produce error annotations, but emphasize the value of a unified comparison enabled by this method.  
- References need correct formatting (capitalization and page info).  
- Authors concede the paper isn’t ready: main limitations are the missing error-classification rules and the need for transparent discussion of multilingual generalization.
