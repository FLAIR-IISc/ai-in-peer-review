The paper presents a novel approach for evaluating grammatical error correction (GEC) systems, enabling the assessment of their performance by error type in terms of both recall and precision. This is a significant improvement over previous methods, as it does not require manual error annotation of system output.

Strengths:
- The proposed evaluation approach is an important step towards analyzing GEC system behavior.
- The paper includes a comprehensive evaluation of various GEC systems.
- The approach has several advantages over previous work, including the computation of precision by error type, independence from manual error annotation, and the ability to assess performance on multi-token errors.
- The automatically selected error tags for pre-computed error spans are largely approved by human experts.

Weaknesses:
- A crucial aspect of the approach, the rules for deriving error types, is not described in detail.
- The classifier evaluation lacks a thorough error analysis, which is necessary for identifying directions for future work on improving the classifier.
- The evaluation is limited to English, and it is unclear how challenging it would be to adapt the approach to other languages.

Classifier and Classifier Evaluation
====================================
The basis for the error categories is unclear, and it is uncertain whether they are based on previous work. Although the approach is generally independent of the alignment algorithm, the rules used are likely not, but the authors do not provide details on this. The error categories are a critical component of the paper, and the reader should be given insight into how a rule for assigning an error type is constructed.

Unfortunately, the paper does not apply the proposed evaluation to languages other than English, nor does it elaborate on the necessary changes to adapt the classifier to other languages. It is likely that the rules used for determining edit boundaries and error tags depend on the language and pre-processing pipeline, requiring adaptation. Additionally, the error categories might need to be modified. The authors do not provide information on the rules for assigning error categories, making it difficult to estimate the effort required to adapt the approach to another language.

The error spans computed in the pre-processing step appear to be inherently continuous, which is problematic for errors that can only be accurately tagged with discontinuous error spans. For example, in German, verbs with separable prefixes are separated from each other in the main clause. It is unclear whether the classifier can handle discontinuous edit spans.

The authors report that human judges rated at least 95% of the automatically assigned error tags as appropriate, despite the noise introduced by automatic edit extraction. However, this judgment may be overly optimistic, as raters may have been more forgiving when boundaries were noisy. Moreover, raters were not asked to select a tag without knowing the system output, which may have biased them towards the system output. Furthermore, there was no rating option between "Bad (Not Appropriate)" and "Appropriate," which may have led raters to select "Appropriate" over "Bad." To strengthen the evaluation, the authors should also assess how human judges rate the classifier output when boundaries are manually created, without the noise introduced by faulty boundaries.

The classifier evaluation lacks a thorough error analysis. It is only mentioned that "Bad" ratings are usually due to incorrect POS tags. Questions that need to be addressed include: When did raters select "Bad," and when did they select "Appropriate"? Does the rating by experts point to possibilities for improving the classifier?

Gold Reference vs. Auto Reference
=================================
It is unclear on what data the significance test was performed. If it was performed on F0.5 scores, this may not be a good idea, as F0.5 is a derived measure with weak discriminative power. The comparison between gold and auto references is only in terms of boundaries, not classification.

Error Type Evaluation
=====================
It is not surprising that five teams failed to correct unnecessary token errors, as some systems are not designed to handle such errors. For example, UFC's rule-based approach works on POS tags, making it impossible to determine superfluous words based on POS alone. The authors do not analyze or comment on the results in Table 6 with respect to whether the systems were designed to handle specific error types. For some error types, there is a straightforward mapping between error types in the gold standard and auto reference, but it remains unclear whether systems failed completely on specific error types or were not designed to correct them.

Please report raw values for TP, FP, TN, and FN in the appendix for Table 6, making it easier to compare systems using other measures. Additionally, it appears that for some error types and systems, the results in Table 6 are based on only a few instances, which should be made clear when reporting raw values.

The statement "All but 2 teams (IITB and IPN) achieved the best score in at least 1 category, which suggests that different approaches to GEC complement different error types" would benefit from a mention that this is in line with previous research.

The multi-token error analysis is helpful for future work, but the results require more interpretation. Some systems are probably inherently unable to correct such errors, and none of the systems were trained on a parallel corpus of learner data and fluent corrections.

Other
=====
- The authors should acknowledge that for some GEC approaches, it was not impossible to provide error annotations before, such as systems with submodules for specific error types.
- References: Some titles lack capitalization, the URL for Sakaguchi et al. (2016) needs to be wrapped, and page information is missing for Efron and Tibshirani (1993).

Author response
===============
I agree that the approach is not "fatally flawed," and this review highlights several positive aspects. However, the paper is not yet ready for publication.

The basis for the paper, the rules for classifying errors, is not described in detail, which is a major concern. This lack of description is not just a matter of providing additional examples but is essential for making the rules replicable or adaptable.

Generalization to other languages should not be an afterthought. It would be a serious limitation if the approach only worked on one language by design. Even if the authors do not perform an adaptation for other languages, the approach should be transparent enough for others to estimate the effort required for such an adaptation and how well it could reasonably work. Simply stating that most research is targeted at ESL only reinforces the problem.

The authors' statement that the error types certain systems tackle are "usually obvious from the tables" oversimplifies the issue. As seen in the CUUI example and unnecessary token errors, there are cases where it is not obvious whether systems failed to correct specific error types or were not designed to correct them.
