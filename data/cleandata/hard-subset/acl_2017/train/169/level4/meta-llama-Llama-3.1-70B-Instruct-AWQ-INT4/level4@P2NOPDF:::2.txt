The paper presents a novel approach for evaluating grammatical error correction (GEC) systems, enabling the assessment of their performance by error type in terms of both recall and precision. This approach is particularly significant, as it does not require manual error annotation, which is typically not available for system output.

Strengths of the paper include the importance of the proposed evaluation for analyzing GEC system behavior, the inclusion of evaluations for various systems, and the advantages of the approach over previous work. Specifically, the approach computes precision by error type, is independent of manual error annotation, and can assess performance on multi-token errors. Additionally, the automatically selected error tags for pre-computed error spans are mostly approved by human experts.

However, the paper also has some weaknesses. A key aspect of the approach, the rules for deriving error types, is not described. The classifier evaluation lacks a thorough error analysis and does not provide directions for future work on improving the classifier. Furthermore, the evaluation was only performed for English, and it is unclear how difficult it would be to adapt the approach to another language.

The classifier and classifier evaluation are significant components of the paper, but the basis for the error categories is unclear. The authors do not provide details on how the rules for assigning error types were devised, nor do they explain how the error categories were determined. This lack of transparency makes it difficult to understand the approach and estimate the effort required to adapt it to another language.

The error spans computed in the pre-processing step seem to be inherently continuous, which can be problematic for errors that require discontinuous error spans. For example, in German, verbs with separable prefixes are separated from each other in the main clause. It is unclear whether the classifier can handle such cases.

The evaluation of the classifier is also problematic. The authors report that human judges rated at least 95% of the automatically assigned error tags as appropriate, despite the noise introduced by automatic edit extraction. However, this judgment may be biased, as the raters were not asked to select a tag without knowing the system output. Additionally, there was no rating option between "Bad (Not Appropriate)" and "Appropriate", which may have led raters to select "Appropriate" over "Bad". To make the evaluation more sound, the authors should also evaluate how human judges rate the classifier output if the boundaries were manually created, without the noise introduced by faulty boundaries.

The classifier evaluation lacks a thorough error analysis. The authors only mention that "Bad" is usually traced back to a wrong POS tag, but do not provide further insights into the errors. Questions that need to be addressed include when raters select "Bad" versus "Appropriate" and whether the rating by experts points to possibilities for improving the classifier.

The comparison between gold and auto reference is also unclear. The authors refer to the mismatch between automatic and reference in terms of alignment and classification, but the comparison is only in terms of boundaries, not classification. The significance test was performed on an unclear dataset, and it is unclear whether the authors tested on the F0.5 scores, which may not be a good idea due to their weak discriminative power.

The error type evaluation is also problematic. The authors do not analyze or comment on the results in Table 6 with respect to whether the systems were designed to handle the error type. For some error types, there is a straightforward mapping between error type in the gold standard and in the auto reference, but it remains unclear whether the systems failed completely on specific error types or were just not designed to correct them.

To improve the paper, the authors should provide more details on the rules for assigning error types, perform a thorough error analysis, and evaluate the classifier on manually created boundaries. Additionally, the authors should report raw values for TP, FP, TN, and FN in the appendix for Table 6 to enable easier comparison of the systems using other measures.

Overall, while the approach presented in the paper is significant, the paper is not yet ready for publication. The authors need to address the weaknesses mentioned above to make the paper more robust and transparent.
