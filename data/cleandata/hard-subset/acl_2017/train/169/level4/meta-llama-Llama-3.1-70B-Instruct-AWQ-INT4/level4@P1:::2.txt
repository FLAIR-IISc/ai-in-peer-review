The paper presents a novel approach for evaluating grammatical error correction (GEC) systems, enabling the assessment of their performance by error type in terms of both precision and recall. This approach is an important stepping stone for analyzing GEC system behavior.

Main Contributions:

1. A novel evaluation method for GEC systems that assesses their performance by error type in terms of both precision and recall.
2. A dataset-independent rule-based framework for classifying edits, which relies only on automatically obtained information such as POS tags and lemmas.
3. A detailed evaluation of system error type performance for all teams in the CoNLL-2014 shared task on Grammatical Error Correction.

Strengths:

1. The proposed evaluation is an important stepping stone for analyzing GEC system behavior.
2. The paper includes evaluation for a variety of systems, demonstrating the approach's applicability.
3. The approach has several advantages over previous work, including computing precision by error type, independence from manual error annotation, and assessing performance on multi-token errors.
4. The automatically selected error tags for pre-computed error spans are mostly approved by human experts.

Weaknesses:

1. A key part – the rules to derive error types – are not described, making it difficult to understand the classifier's decision-making process.
2. The classifier evaluation lacks a thorough error analysis and directions for future work on how to improve the classifier.
3. The evaluation was only performed for English, and it is unclear how difficult it would be to adapt the approach to another language.

Questions to Authors:

1. Can you provide a detailed description of the rules used to derive error types and how they were developed?
2. How do you plan to improve the classifier, and what are the potential limitations of the current approach?
3. Have you considered adapting the approach to other languages, and if so, what changes would be necessary?
4. Can you provide raw values for TP, FP, TN, and FN in the appendix for Table 6 to facilitate comparison of systems using other measures?

Additional Comments:

1. The authors should mention that for some GEC approaches, it was not impossible to provide error annotations before, but the proposed approach enables a unified comparison of GEC systems.
2. References should be formatted correctly, with proper capitalization and page information.
3. The URL for Sakaguchi et al. (2016) needs to be wrapped.
