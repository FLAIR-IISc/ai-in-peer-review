- Proposes a novel GEC evaluation that measures recall and precision by error type without requiring manual annotation.  
- Important step toward analyzing GEC system behavior.  
- Paper includes a comprehensive evaluation of multiple GEC systems.  
- Advantages: computes precision by error type, avoids manual annotation, and handles multi-token errors.  
- Automatically selected error tags are reportedly largely approved by human experts.  
- Crucial rules for deriving error types are not described in sufficient detail.  
- Classifier evaluation lacks a thorough error analysis to guide improvements.  
- Evaluation is limited to English; adaptability to other languages is unclear.  
- Basis and provenance of the error categories are unclear and possibly not tied to prior work.  
- Alignment/rule dependencies likely affect error-type assignment but are not explained.  
- Readers are not shown how to construct or replicate rules for assigning error types.  
- No discussion of necessary adaptations for other languages or language-specific preprocessing dependencies.  
- Pre-computed error spans are continuous, which may fail for discontinuous edits (e.g., German separable verbs).  
- Human approval of auto tags may be optimistic due to noisy boundaries, lack of blind tagging, and coarse rating options.  
- Recommend evaluating human judgments on classifier output with manually created boundaries.  
- Classifier error analysis is too shallow—need to know when raters chose “Bad” vs “Appropriate” and actionable error types.  
- Significance-test data is unclear; using F0.5 for significance may be inappropriate; gold vs auto comparison focuses on boundaries only.  
- Some systems’ failures may reflect design limits (not being intended to fix certain error types); authors don’t analyze system intent vs results.  
- Request to include raw TP/FP/TN/FN counts (appendix) and note small-sample cells in Table 6.  
- Observation that different systems excel on different categories matches prior findings and should be noted.  
- Multi-token error analysis is promising but needs deeper interpretation; many systems weren’t trained on learner-to-fluent parallel data.  
- Authors should acknowledge that some GEC systems could previously provide error annotations (e.g., via submodules).  
- Reference and formatting issues: inconsistent capitalization, wrapping/URL fixes, and missing page info.  
- Author response concedes the paper isn’t ready: lack of replicable rule detail, insufficient treatment of cross-lingual generalization, and oversimplified claims about systems’ error-type coverage.
