**Strengths:** The proposed system has a useful application for both teachers and learners, providing fine-grained comparison of Grammar Error Correction (GEC) systems. By automatically enriching the output of GEC systems with error types, it enables users to benefit from explicit error information, which is often lacking in standard GEC outputs.

**Weaknesses:** The paper's description of the system is highly superficial, lacking essential details about the core components. The evaluation of the system is also unsatisfying, with several methodological concerns.

**General Discussion:**

The paper presents an approach to automatically enriching GEC system outputs with error types, a useful application for both teachers and learners. This information allows for finer-grained comparison of GEC systems, including precision and error-type specific recall and precision. However, the system's description remains superficial, failing to provide crucial details about the manually created rules that form its core. The authors should provide examples of these rules, specify their number, complexity, and ordering, to enable a deeper understanding of the system.

Instead of presenting relevant system details, the paper devotes several pages to evaluating the systems that participated in CoNLL-2014. The evaluation is not satisfying, with several concerns. Firstly, the annotators should have independently annotated a gold standard for the 200 test sentences, rather than simply rating the system's output. This would have provided a more reliable evaluation. Secondly, the paper's method of averaging individual ratings from multiple annotators is unconventional and may lead to inaccurate results. A more common practice would be to consider the distribution of ratings, which could reveal a more significant percentage of "bad" scores. Thirdly, the paper lacks information about the test data, including the number and types of error categories it contains. Finally, the statement about "edit boundaries might be unusual" requires a more precise description and examples to understand its implications.

The authors claim that their system is less domain-dependent compared to systems requiring training data. However, this assertion is questionable, as manually created rules can be domain-dependent, and the test data used (FCE-test, CoNLL-2014) are from a single domain (student essays). The design of a new set of error categories also remains unclear, with insufficient motivation or discussion provided. The use of existing tagsets, such as the CoNLL-2014 tagset, could have facilitated evaluation and comparison.

In conclusion, the paper's main motivation remains unclear, and the most important details of the system are left out. The evaluation and presentation of results are also superficial, making it difficult to assess the system's effectiveness.
