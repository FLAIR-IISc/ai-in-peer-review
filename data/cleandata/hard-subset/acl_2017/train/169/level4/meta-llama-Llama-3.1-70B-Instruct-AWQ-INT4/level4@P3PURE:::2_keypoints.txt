- Proposes a novel evaluation allowing per-error-type precision and recall for GEC without needing system outputs manually annotated.  
- Important contribution for analyzing GEC system behavior and tested across a variety of systems.  
- Advantages claimed: computes precision by error type, independent of manual annotation, and handles multi-token errors.  
- Automatically selected error tags were largely approved by human experts.  

- Rules for deriving error types are not described, undermining reproducibility and transparency.  
- The classifier evaluation lacks in-depth error analysis and actionable directions to improve the classifier.  
- Evaluation is conducted only on English; portability and adaptation effort for other languages are unclear.  

- Unclear provenance and rationale for chosen error categories and whether they depend on alignment rules.  
- The paper should show examples and counts/complexity of rules used to assign error types.  
- Preprocessing produces continuous edit spans, which may fail for discontinuous errors (e.g., German separable verbs).  
- Human judge approval of tags may be biased by noisy boundaries and limited rating options; need evaluation with manual boundaries.  
- Classifier error analysis is superficial—more detail needed on when raters labeled outputs "Bad" vs "Appropriate" and implications for improvement.  

- Unclear what data/statistics were used for significance testing; using F0.5 may be inappropriate due to weak discriminative power.  
- Comparison between gold and auto references appears focused on boundaries, not on classification differences.  

- Some systems’ failure on specific error types (e.g., unnecessary token) may be due to system design, not classifier errors; authors do not analyze this.  
- Table 6 results lack raw TP/FP/TN/FN counts—these should be provided to enable alternative comparisons and reveal low-sample issues.  
- paper does not analyze whether systems were designed to handle the reported error types, leading to possible misinterpretation of results.  
- Multi-token error analysis is useful but needs more interpretation given system training differences and inherent system limitations.  
- The claim that different approaches complement each other should be linked to prior research.  

- Should acknowledge that some GEC systems could already produce error annotations via submodules, while noting the proposed approach unifies comparison across systems.  
- Several reference formatting issues and missing bibliographic details need fixing.  

- Reviewer agrees approach is promising but the paper is not ready due to missing rule descriptions and insufficient generalization discussion.  
- Rules must be documented for replication and to estimate adaptation effort for other languages; generalization cannot be an afterthought.  
- The authors’ assertion that there is “no obvious explanation” for some system failures is disputed and requires deeper analysis.
