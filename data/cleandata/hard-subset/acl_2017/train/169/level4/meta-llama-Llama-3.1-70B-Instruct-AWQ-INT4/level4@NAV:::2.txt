This paper proposes a novel approach for evaluating grammatical error correction (GEC) systems, allowing for the assessment of system performance by error type in terms of both recall and precision. This is a significant improvement over previous methods, which generally did not annotate system output with error categories.

Strengths of the paper include:

* The proposed evaluation approach is a crucial step in analyzing GEC system behavior.
* The paper evaluates a variety of systems, providing a comprehensive comparison.
* The approach offers several advantages over previous work, including the computation of precision by error type, independence from manual error annotation, and the ability to assess performance on multi-token errors.
* The automatically selected error tags for pre-computed error spans are largely approved by human experts.

However, the paper also has several weaknesses:

* A key component of the approach – the rules for deriving error types – is not described, making it difficult to understand the basis for the error categories.
* The classifier evaluation lacks a thorough error analysis and does not provide directions for future work on improving the classifier.
* The evaluation was only performed for English, and it is unclear how difficult it would be to adapt the approach to another language.

Classifier and Classifier Evaluation
====================================

The paper does not provide sufficient information on the rules used to derive error categories, making it difficult to understand the basis for the error types. The authors should provide more details on the rules, including how they were developed and how they can be adapted to other languages.

The evaluation of the classifier is also limited, as it does not provide a thorough error analysis. The authors should investigate why human judges rated certain error tags as "Bad" and how the classifier can be improved.

Gold Reference vs. Auto Reference
=================================

The paper does not clearly state on what data the significance test was performed. The authors should clarify this and ensure that the comparison between gold and auto references is thorough.

Error Type Evaluation
=====================

The paper does not provide sufficient analysis of the results in Table 6, particularly with respect to whether the systems were designed to handle specific error types. The authors should provide more interpretation of the results and report raw values for TP, FP, TN, and FN in the appendix.

Other
=====

* The authors should acknowledge that some GEC approaches may have been able to provide error annotations before, but the proposed approach enables a unified comparison of systems.
* The references should be formatted correctly, with proper capitalization and page information.

Author Response
===============

The author acknowledges that the approach is not "fatally flawed" but agrees that the paper is not ready for publication. The lack of description of the rules for classifying errors is a major limitation, and the paper should provide more details on the rules and their adaptability to other languages. The author also agrees that generalization to other languages should not be an afterthought and that the approach should be transparent enough for others to estimate the effort required for adaptation.
