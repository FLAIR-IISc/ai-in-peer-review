This paper presents a new approach for evaluating grammatical error correction (GEC) systems. This approach makes it possible to assess the performance of GEC systems based on the error type, not only in terms of recall but also in terms of precision, which was not generally possible before because system output was not usually annotated with error categories.

**Strengths:**
- The proposed evaluation is an important step in analyzing the behavior of GEC systems.
- The paper includes evaluations for various systems.
- This approach has several advantages over previous work:
    - It calculates precision based on the error type.
    - It is independent of manual error annotation.
    - It can assess performance on multi-token errors.
    - The error tags automatically selected for the pre-computed error span are mostly approved by human experts.

**Weaknesses:**
- A crucial part – the rules for deriving error types – is not described.
- The classifier evaluation lacks a complete error analysis and lacks guidelines for future work to improve the classifier based on it.
- The evaluation was only done for English, and it is not clear how difficult it would be to use this approach on any other language.

**Classifier and Classifier Evaluation**
====================================
It is not clear on what basis the error categories were prepared. Are they based on previous work?

Although this approach is generally independent of alignment algorithms, the rules are probably not, but the authors have not provided details on this. Error categories are a major part of the paper, and the reader should at least get a glimpse of what the rule for assigning an error type looks like.

Unfortunately, the paper does not apply the proposed evaluation to languages other than English. It also does not elaborate on what changes would be required to run the classifier on other languages. I assume that the rules used to determine the error tag, as well as the editing limits, depend to a certain extent on the language/pre-processing pipeline and therefore need to be adapted. Furthermore, the error categories may need to be changed. The authors have not provided any details on the rules for assigning error categories (how many in total/per error type? How complex are they?) so that an estimate of the effort required to use the approach on any other language can be made.

The error spans computed in the pre-processing step appear inherently continuous (which is also the case with the M2 scorer), which is problematic because there are errors that can only be accurately tagged when the error span is discontinuous. For example, in German, verbs with separable prefixes are separated from each other in the main clause: [first component] [verb] [other component] [verb prefix]. Would the classifier be able to tag discontinuous editing spans?

The authors write that all human judges automatically considered at least 95% of the assigned error tags appropriate "despite the degree of noise introduced by the automated editing extraction" (295). I would be more cautious with this judgment because raters may also have to be more forgiving when noise occurs. Furthermore, they were not asked to select the tag without knowing the system output, but may be more biased towards the system output in the case of noise limits. In addition, there was no rating option between "bad (not suitable)" and "suitable", which could lead raters to select "suitable" over "bad". To make the evaluation more concrete, the authors should also evaluate how human judges rate the classifier output if the limits were manually created, i.e., without the noise introduced by the faulty limits.

The classifier evaluation lacks a complete error analysis. It only mentions that "bad" is usually found back as a false POS tag. The questions I want to see addressed: when did the raters choose "bad", when did they choose "suitable"? Does the rating by experts point to the possibilities of improving the classifier?

**Gold Reference vs. Auto Reference**
=================================
It is not clear on what data the significance test was actually performed on. Did you test at an F0.5 score? If so, I don't think it's a good idea because it is a derivative measure with weak differential power (performance in terms of recall and precision may be completely different but their F0.5 score may be the same). Furthermore, at the beginning of section 4.1, the authors mention the mismatch between the automatic and reference in terms of alignment and classification, but as far as I can tell, the comparison between gold and reference is only in terms of limits and not in terms of classification.

**Error Type Evaluation**
=====================
I am not surprised that 5 teams (~line 473) failed to correct any unnecessary token errors. There is at least a direct explanation for why they cannot handle unnecessary words. The most obvious is UFC: their rule-based approach works on POS tags (Ng et al., 2014) and it is not possible to determine unnecessary words based solely on POS. Rozovskaya & Roth (2016) provide an explanation for why AMU performs poorly on unnecessary words.

The authors do not analyze or comment on the results in Table 6 as to whether the systems were designed to handle the error type. For some error types, there is a straightforward mapping between the error type in gold standard and auto reference, for example for word order error. It is still unclear whether the systems completely failed on specific error types or were not designed to correct them (for example CUUI is reported with precision+recall=0.0, although it does not target word order errors). In the CUUI case (and perhaps similar cases), it also points to an error in classification that has neither been analyzed nor discussed.

Please also report the raw values for TP, FP, TN, FN in the appendix to Table 6. This makes it easier to compare the systems using other measures. Furthermore, it seems that the results in Table 6 for some error types and systems are based only on a few examples. This will also be clarified when reporting the raw values.

You write "All but 2 teams (IITB and IPN) achieved the best score in at least 1 category, which suggests that the different approaches of GEC are complementary to different error types." (606) It would be good to mention here that this is consistent with previous research.

Multi-token error analysis is helpful for future work but the result needs further interpretation: some systems are probably inherently unable to correct such errors but no system was trained on learner data and a corpus parallel to the fluent (in the sense of Sakaguchi et al., 2016) corrections.

**Other**
=====
- The authors should mention that for some GEC approaches, providing error annotation was not previously impossible, for example systems with submodules for each error type.

Acceptably, the system would need to be adapted to include the submodules responsible for changes in system output. Still, the proposed approach enables comparison of GEC systems for which the production of error tag output is not directly straightforward for other systems.
- Reference: There is a lack of capitalization in some titles. The URL for Sakaguchi et al. (2016) needs to be wrapped. Page information for Efron and Tibshirani (1993) is missing.

**Author Response**
===============
I agree that your approach is not "deathly flawed" and I think this review actually points out some positive aspects. The approach is good, but the paper is not ready.

The basis of the paper is the rules for classifying errors and the lack of detail is a major factor. This is not just a matter of additional examples. If the rules are not seen as an implementation once, they need to be repeatable or described to be adapted.

Generalization in other languages should not be a later consideration. It would be a serious limitation if the approach only works on one language by design. Even if you do not adapt for other languages, your approach should be so transparent that other people can estimate how much work such adaptation will take and how well it may work. Just saying that most research is only targeted on ESL makes the problem even more serious.

You write that the error type faced by some systems will be "usually clear from the tables". I don't think it's that simple - look at the CUUI example mentioned above as well as the unnecessary token errors. There are five systems that do not correct them (Table 5) and therefore it should be clear that they did not try to deal with them. However, in the paper you write that "there is also no clear explanation as to why these teams had difficulty with this error type".
