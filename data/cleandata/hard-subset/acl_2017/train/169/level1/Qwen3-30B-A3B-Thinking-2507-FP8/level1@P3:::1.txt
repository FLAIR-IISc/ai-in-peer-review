

# Peer Review for ACL 2017

## Summary and Main Contributions

This paper addresses a critical gap in Grammatical Error Correction (GEC) evaluation: the inability to assess system performance by specific error types, as system outputs lack error type annotations while reference corrections have them. The authors propose an automatic annotation method that enables detailed error type analysis for the first time. The primary contribution is a linguistically-enhanced alignment algorithm to extract edits between original and corrected sentences, combined with a rule-based classifier that categorizes edits into error types without requiring labeled training data. The secondary contribution is the detailed error type analysis of CoNLL-2014 systems, revealing which systems excel at specific error types (e.g., CAMB excelled at 10 error categories, while 5 teams failed to correct unnecessary token errors). The tertiary contribution is the dataset-independent nature of their approach, which avoids the limitations of previous machine learning-based error typing methods.

## Strengths

1. **Solves a genuine and significant problem in GEC evaluation**: Prior to this work, researchers could only measure overall GEC performance (e.g., precision, recall), hiding important information about which error types systems handle well or poorly. This is a fundamental limitation in the field, as understanding error type performance is crucial for targeted system improvements. The authors demonstrate this problem's significance by showing that different systems have different strengths across error types, which was previously impossible to assess.

2. **Innovative dataset-independent rule-based classifier**: Most previous error typing approaches relied on machine learning classifiers trained on specific datasets, which are not transferable across different corpora. The authors' rule-based approach, which depends only on automatically obtained linguistic features (POS tags, lemmas, etc.), avoids this limitation and creates a truly generalizable solution. The manual evaluation showing >95% "Good" or "Acceptable" ratings from 5 expert raters (86.3% "Good" overall) provides strong validation of their approach's effectiveness.

3. **Actionable insights from CoNLL-2014 analysis**: The paper's application of their method to the CoNLL-2014 shared task data yields valuable insights for the GEC community. For example, they identify that "5 teams failed to correctly resolve any unnecessary token errors at all" despite these being theoretically easier to correct (requiring only deletion, not substitution). This finding challenges assumptions about error correction difficulty and suggests new research directions. Their analysis also reveals that different approaches complement different error types (e.g., MT approaches excel at NOUN errors while classifiers are better for VERB:FORM errors), providing concrete guidance for future system design.

## Weaknesses

1. **Insufficient analysis of alignment error propagation**: The paper acknowledges that "whenever raters judged an edit as 'Bad', this could usually be traced back to a mistake made by the POS tagger," yet fails to systematically analyze how alignment errors (from the linguistically-enhanced alignment algorithm) affect error type classification. The authors cite Felice et al. (2016) as achieving 80-85 F1 on alignment, but don't provide evidence that this level of alignment accuracy is sufficient for accurate error typing. Without this analysis, the paper overstates the reliability of their approach.

2. **Overstated dataset independence**: The paper claims their classifier is "entirely dataset independent," but they only test it on two datasets (FCE-test and CoNLL-2014). They don't demonstrate how it would perform on other GEC datasets with different error types or annotation schemes (e.g., BEA-2019). For instance, the CoNLL-2014 dataset has different error types than FCE, and it's unclear whether the same rules would apply. The authors would need to test their approach on multiple datasets to validate their dataset-independence claim.

3. **Lack of direct comparison with previous approaches**: The authors mention that previous approaches had "50-70 F1" but don't provide a direct comparison with their rule-based approach on the same dataset using the same evaluation metrics. Without this comparison, it's impossible to determine whether their approach is actually better or just different. The paper could have conducted a more rigorous evaluation by applying their classifier to existing datasets used in previous work.

4. **Unaddressed limitations of rule-based approach**: The paper doesn't discuss the limitations of their rule-based approach for error types that don't map cleanly to POS tags (e.g., complex combinations of errors like tense and subject-verb agreement). They acknowledge this in their error scheme design ("we currently label [could → should] a tense error, when it might otherwise be considered a modal error"), but don't propose a solution for handling such cases. This represents a significant gap in their methodology.

## Questions to Authors

1. The paper states "all 5 raters individually considered at least 95% of our rule-based error types to be either 'Good' or 'Acceptable'." However, Rater 3's data shows 83.0% "Good" and 13.0% "Acceptable," totaling 96%. Could you clarify this discrepancy?

2. How would your approach handle error types that are combinations of multiple error types (e.g., a tense error that also involves a subject-verb agreement error)? The paper mentions this as a limitation in their error scheme design but doesn't propose a solution.

3. The paper claims that "our approach is analogous to automating the annotation guidelines given to human annotators." How would you handle situations where human annotators might disagree on error types (e.g., a case could be classified as either NOUN:NUM or VERB:SVA), which is common in GEC annotation?
