Proposes automatic error-type annotation for GEC outputs using linguistically-enhanced edit extraction plus a rule-based classifier.  
Offers a dataset-independent rule-based error type classifier that requires no labeled training data.  
Presents the first detailed error-type analysis of CoNLL-2014 GEC systems, showing which systems excel by error type.  
Addresses a critical gap in GEC evaluation by enabling error-type specific performance measurement.  
Demonstrates practical effectiveness with high human-evaluation ratings (95%+ "Good" or "Acceptable") and immediate applicability across datasets.  
Empirical analysis yields actionable insights (e.g., CAMB best on unnecessary-token, CUUI best on VERB:FORM) suggesting benefit from hybrid approaches.  
Limited evaluation of the edit extraction/alignment component—no comparison to alternatives or quantified error rates.  
Insufficient discussion of the rule-based classifier’s limitations and how ambiguous cases affect analysis validity.  
Findings are not fully contextualized into concrete guidance for future system design or research directions.  
Requests examples of error-type distinctions that are difficult to capture and their potential impact on analysis.  
Asks how the error-typing framework relates to fluency evaluation rather than just grammaticality.  
Requests examples of multi-token edits successfully handled by top systems (AMU, CAMB, UMC) and why they succeeded.  
Overall assessment: valuable, practical, well-evaluated contribution whose released tool will benefit the community despite evaluative and contextual limitations.
