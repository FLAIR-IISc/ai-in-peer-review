

# Peer Review for ACL 2017

## Summary and Contributions

This paper addresses a fundamental gap in Grammatical Error Correction (GEC) evaluation: the inability to assess system performance by error type because system outputs lacked error type annotations. The authors propose a method to automatically annotate unannotated GEC system outputs with error type information through two main components: (1) linguistically-enhanced edit extraction using an alignment algorithm, and (2) a rule-based classifier for error type assignment. The paper makes three key contributions as I see them:

1. **A practical solution for automatic error type annotation** - The most significant contribution, enabling detailed error type analysis that was previously impossible due to the lack of annotation on system outputs.

2. **A dataset-independent error type classification framework** - The rule-based classifier requires no labeled training data and works across different datasets, unlike machine learning approaches that require retraining for each dataset.

3. **First detailed error type analysis of GEC systems** - The authors applied their method to CoNLL-2014 data, revealing which systems excelled at which error types, providing valuable insights for future research.

## Strengths

1. **Solves a critical gap in GEC evaluation** - The paper identifies and addresses a fundamental problem that has limited the field for years. Prior to this work, researchers could only measure overall GEC performance, not how systems performed on specific error types. This is a major contribution to the field, as understanding system strengths and weaknesses by error type is essential for targeted improvement.

2. **Practical and effective methodology** - The authors' rule-based classifier achieved impressive results in human evaluation (95%+ "Good" or "Acceptable" ratings across 5 raters), demonstrating its practical utility. The dataset independence of their approach is particularly valuable, as it eliminates the need for retraining on different datasets, making it immediately applicable to existing and future GEC work.

3. **Valuable empirical analysis** - The CoNLL-2014 analysis reveals important insights about system performance across error types. For example, the paper shows that CAMB was most successful at correcting unnecessary token errors, while CUUI excelled at VERB:FORM errors. This detailed breakdown provides concrete guidance for future system design, suggesting that combining approaches (e.g., CAMB's hybrid MT with CUUI's classifier) could improve overall performance.

## Weaknesses

1. **Limited evaluation of the edit extraction component** - While the authors evaluate their error typing component thoroughly, they provide minimal analysis of the edit extraction step. The paper mentions using Felice et al.'s (2016) linguistically-enhanced alignment, but doesn't evaluate how well this alignment works compared to alternatives or quantify its error rate. Since error typing depends on accurate edit extraction, this is a significant oversight.

2. **Insufficient discussion of the rule-based classifier's limitations** - The paper acknowledges that some error types are difficult to categorize (e.g., "could → should" as tense vs. modal error), but doesn't explore how these ambiguities affect the overall analysis. The authors state their framework is a "compromise between informativeness and practicality," but don't demonstrate how this compromise impacts the validity of their findings.

3. **Limited contextualization of findings** - The paper presents detailed error type analysis but doesn't clearly articulate how these findings could directly inform future research. For instance, it mentions that "different systems had different strengths and weaknesses," but doesn't provide specific guidance on how researchers could leverage these insights to build better systems. The connection between the findings and future work could be strengthened.

## Questions to Authors

1. The paper acknowledges that "it is always possible to add new categories for increasingly detailed error types" but states "it is impractical to create new categories and rules to differentiate between such narrow distinctions." Could you provide specific examples of error type distinctions that would be difficult to capture with your framework, and how these might affect the analysis?

2. The paper states that "only three teams achieved scores greater than 10 F0.5 for multi-token edits," and notes this is significant because "the main goal of GEC should be to produce fluent-sounding, rather than just grammatical, sentences." How does your error type framework relate to fluency evaluation, which is increasingly recognized as important in GEC?

3. In Table 8, the authors show that multi-token edit correction performance is generally low. Could you provide examples of multi-token errors that were successfully handled by the top-performing systems (AMU, CAMB, UMC), and explain why these were successful?

## Additional Comments

The paper makes a valuable contribution to the GEC field by enabling detailed error type analysis. The methodology is practical and well-evaluated, and the CoNLL-2014 analysis provides novel insights. The release of the tool with the paper will be a significant resource for the community. While the paper has some limitations in evaluation and contextualization, these could be addressed in the author response, making this a strong candidate for acceptance at ACL 2017.
