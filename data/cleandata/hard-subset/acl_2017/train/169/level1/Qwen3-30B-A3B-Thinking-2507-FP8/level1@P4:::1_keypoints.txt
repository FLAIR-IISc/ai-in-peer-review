- Method to automatically annotate GEC system outputs with error types using linguistically-enhanced alignment and a rule-based classifier.  
- Dataset-independent framework relying only on automatically obtained linguistic properties (POS, lemmas) without labeled training data.  
- Detailed error-type analysis applied to CoNLL-2014 systems, revealing system-specific strengths and weaknesses.  

Strengths:
- Fills a clear gap in GEC evaluation by enabling detailed error-type analysis of previously unannotated system outputs.  
- Rule-based, dataset-independent classifier validated by human evaluation (>95% rated Good/Acceptable), showing practical effectiveness.  
- Application to CoNLL-2014 yields concrete insights (e.g., teams failing to correct unnecessary token errors), suggesting complementary system strengths.  
- Technical approach and edit extraction/error typing are clearly explained and reproducible; offers a 25-type taxonomy with human validation.  
- Automatic reference processing compares favorably to gold references (no significant difference) and is positioned as an improvement over M2-style metrics.  

Weaknesses:
- Novelty is unclear: edit extraction and typing rely heavily on prior work (Felice et al. 2016; Swanson & Yamangil 2012).  
- The 25-error-type classification appears arbitrary and lacks justification or evidence of optimality.  
- Human evaluation covers only 200 edits, raising concerns about robustness and sampling bias across error types.  
- Paper doesn’t convincingly show that its detailed error-type analysis yields insights unobtainable by other evaluation methods.  
- System-level findings may not demonstrate meaningful differences beyond overall scores and could have alternate explanations.  

Questions to authors:
- How does your 25-type scheme improve on or address limitations of prior taxonomies (FCE, CoNLL-2014), and why is it preferable?  
- How do you mitigate POS-tagging errors propagating into error-type classification, especially for POS-sensitive types?  
- Can you provide statistical significance analyses for team differences across error types, particularly where differences are small but potentially meaningful?
