- Addresses a major gap: proposes an automatic method to annotate grammatical errors in corrected text, enabling fine-grained error-type evaluation beyond simple recall.  
- Strong validation: the rule-based classifier was manually evaluated and experts rated >95% of predicted error types as “Good” or “Acceptable,” supporting annotation quality despite alignment noise.  
- Valuable application: applied the tool to CoNLL-2014 submissions to provide per-system, per-error-type breakdowns that reveal complementary strengths and common weaknesses (e.g., ADV, CONJ).  
- Dataset-independent: rule-based approach uses automatic features (POS, lemmas) and requires no labeled training data, making it broadly applicable.  

- Limited novelty: the linguistically-enhanced alignment and rule-based classification borrow from prior work, with the main contribution being implementation and application rather than new algorithms.  
- POS tagging dependence: classifier accuracy hinges on POS tagger quality, and the effect of tagging errors is not fully quantified or mitigated.  
- Edit extraction evaluation shallow: the paper reuses an existing edit extraction method without thoroughly assessing how extraction errors propagate into error typing.  
- Limited human comparison: validation is on a small sample; a larger comparison to gold-standard human annotations is lacking.  

- What is the quantified impact of POS tagging errors on classification, and were mitigation strategies tested?  
- Can you hypothesize why specific approaches (e.g., MT vs. other methods) perform better on particular error types based on algorithmic characteristics?  
- What specific discrepancies arose when reprocessing references with automatic alignment, and can you provide examples where automatic processing changed results versus gold annotations?
