## Review of "Automatic Annotation and Evaluation of Error Types for Grammatical Error Correction"

This paper addresses a significant limitation in Grammatical Error Correction (GEC) research: the lack of system output annotation for detailed error type analysis. The authors propose a pipeline to automatically annotate GEC system outputs with error types, enabling granular evaluation beyond overall scores. This is achieved through linguistically-enhanced alignment to extract edits and a novel, dataset-independent rule-based classifier. The work then demonstrates its utility by applying it to the CoNLL-2014 shared task data.

The primary contribution is the development and validation of a dataset-independent, rule-based error type classifier. Existing approaches rely on training data annotated according to specific schemes, hindering cross-dataset comparison. The authors successfully create a classifier based solely on automatic linguistic features (POS tags, lemmas) which achieves high agreement with human raters (>95% “Good” or “Acceptable” ratings). A second, important contribution is the application of this tool to re-evaluate the CoNLL-2014 systems, providing detailed error type breakdowns unavailable previously. The resulting insights into the strengths and weaknesses of different systems (MT vs. classifiers) offer valuable direction for future GEC research.

**Strengths:**

*   **Novelty of Approach:** The dataset-independent rule-based classifier is a genuinely novel contribution, addressing a crucial bottleneck in GEC evaluation.
*   **Strong Validation:** The high agreement rate with human raters convincingly demonstrates the classifier’s effectiveness and reliability.
*   **Practical Application & Insights:** Applying the tool to the CoNLL-2014 data provides immediately useful, detailed analysis of existing systems, revealing complementary strengths of different approaches. 
*   **Open-Source Tool:** Releasing the annotation tool enhances the reproducibility of the work and broadens its impact on the GEC community.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Limited Scope of Analysis:** While the CoNLL-2014 analysis is valuable, it focuses on a single dataset. Evaluating the tool’s generalizability on more diverse GEC datasets would strengthen the claims.
*   **Rule-Based Limitations:** While dataset-independence is a strength, rule-based systems can be brittle and may struggle with nuanced or unusual errors not explicitly covered by the rules.  The authors acknowledge this limitation regarding increasingly detailed error categorization.
*   **Dependence on POS Tagger Accuracy:** The classifier’s performance is heavily reliant on the accuracy of the underlying POS tagger, as highlighted by the error analysis. Errors in POS tagging directly impact the classification results.

**Questions to Authors:**

1.  Could you elaborate on the potential for extending the rule-based classifier to incorporate more complex linguistic features or machine learning components to address the limitations of the current system?
2.  How sensitive is the performance of the error type classifier to different POS taggers? Have you experimented with other taggers and observed significant variations in results?
3.  You mention that the M2 scorer tends to overestimate performance. Could you further quantify this overestimation and discuss the implications for interpreting previous GEC results?
