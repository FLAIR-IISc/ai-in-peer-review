This paper presents a method for automatically annotating grammatical errors in corrected text, enabling detailed error type analysis of GEC systems. The core idea is to use a linguistically-enhanced alignment algorithm to extract edits between original and corrected sentences, then classify those edits using a rule-based classifier. The authors demonstrate this approach by re-analyzing the CoNLL-2014 shared task data.

**Main Contributions:**

1. **Automated Error Type Annotation Pipeline:** The primary contribution is a functional pipeline for automatically annotating GEC outputs with error types. This addresses a significant limitation in the field, where evaluating system performance beyond overall metrics was previously impossible due to a lack of annotated system outputs.
2. **Dataset-Independent Rule-Based Classifier:** The authors introduce a novel rule-based error type classifier that, critically, avoids reliance on training data specific to any particular error annotation scheme. This dataset independence is a valuable feature, allowing for consistent evaluation across different corpora.
3. **Detailed Analysis of CoNLL-2014 Systems:** The application of the developed pipeline to the CoNLL-2014 data provides a comprehensive error type analysis of the participating systems, revealing strengths and weaknesses of different approaches. This detailed analysis, enabled by the automatic annotation, is a substantial contribution.

**Strengths:**

1. **Addresses a Critical Gap:** The paper directly tackles a major obstacle in GEC research – the inability to perform detailed error type analysis of system outputs. This limitation has hindered progress in understanding and improving GEC systems, and this work provides a clear solution.
2. **Strong Validation of Classifier:** The manual evaluation, demonstrating >95% agreement with human raters on the appropriateness of error type assignments, is extremely compelling. This high level of accuracy provides confidence in the reliability of the automated annotation.
3. **Practical and Reproducible:** The authors clearly state their intention to release the tool, increasing the potential impact of this work. The methodology is well-explained, making it relatively easy to reproduce and adapt.
4. **Insightful CoNLL-2014 Analysis:** The detailed analysis of the CoNLL-2014 systems uncovers interesting patterns regarding the strengths and weaknesses of different GEC approaches concerning specific error types. This provides valuable insights for future research.
5. **Clear and Well-Structured Presentation:** The paper is clearly written and logically structured, making it easy to understand the methodology and results.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Novelty of Core Components:** While the *combination* of linguistically-enhanced alignment and a rule-based classifier is valuable, both components individually rely on existing work (Felice et al., 2016; Swanson & Yamangil, 2012). The novelty lies primarily in the integration and application to the specific problem of automated error type annotation.
2. **Dependence on POS Tagging Accuracy:** The classifier's reliance on POS tags makes it vulnerable to errors in the POS tagger. The paper acknowledges this as a source of errors in the evaluation, but further investigation into the impact of POS tagging errors would be beneficial.
3. **Simplistic Comparison with Gold References:** While the authors justify re-processing the gold references, the approach feels somewhat contrived. The potential impact of this re-processing on the results could be further investigated and discussed. The rationale that the M2 scorer *overestimates* performance feels a little assertive and could be bolstered with more evidence beyond referencing previous work.
4. **Limited Scope of Error Categories:** The 25 error types, while comprehensive, might not fully capture the nuances of all grammatical errors. Exploring the possibility of more fine-grained error categorization could be beneficial.

**Questions to Authors:**

1. How sensitive is the performance of the classifier to errors in the POS tagger? Did you investigate the frequency of misclassifications attributable to POS tagging errors?
2. Could you elaborate on the rationale behind re-processing the gold reference data? What specific concerns about comparability motivated this decision, and how might this re-processing affect the reported results?
3. What are the computational costs associated with running the annotation pipeline? This information would be useful for assessing the practical feasibility of applying the tool to large datasets.
4. What are the limitations of the rule-based approach? Are there error types for which the rule-based system consistently performs poorly, and how might these be addressed in future work?

**Additional Comments:**

*  Table 9 (complete list of error types) would benefit from a clearer explanation of the ‘M:’, ‘U:’, and ‘R:’ prefixes.
*  The phrase "dataset-agnostic" is used frequently. While technically accurate, it might be helpful to clarify that this independence comes at the cost of potentially missing nuances specific to individual datasets.
* It's unclear what the 'UNK' error category signifies. A clearer definition in the text would be helpful.
