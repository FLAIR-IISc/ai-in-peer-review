This paper presents a method for automatically annotating grammatical errors in corrected text, enabling detailed error type analysis of GEC systems. The core contribution is a rule-based error typing classifier, operating on linguistically-enhanced aligned edits, designed to be dataset-agnostic. A secondary, but significant contribution is the application of this tool to re-evaluate the CoNLL-2014 shared task systems, providing a more granular breakdown of performance than previously available. Finally, the release of the annotation tool itself is a valuable contribution to the GEC community.

**Strengths:**

1. **Novelty and Importance of the Problem:** The problem addressed – lack of error type annotations for GEC system outputs – is a genuine bottleneck in GEC research. Existing evaluation metrics rely heavily on recall or require manual annotation, making detailed analysis difficult. Providing a method to automatically annotate outputs is a significant step towards more nuanced system evaluation and targeted improvement.
2. **Dataset-Agnostic Approach:** The rule-based classifier, relying solely on automatic linguistic features (POS tags, lemmas, etc.), is a strong point. This avoids the issues of bias and incompatibility inherent in machine learning classifiers trained on different datasets with potentially differing annotation schemes. The 95%+ agreement with human raters demonstrates the effectiveness of this approach.
3. **Detailed CoNLL-2014 Analysis:**  The re-evaluation of CoNLL-2014 systems using the developed tool offers valuable insights. The breakdown of performance by error type and operation (missing, unnecessary, replacement) reveals complementary strengths of different systems, suggesting avenues for future research combining approaches. The detection vs correction analysis adds another layer of insight.
4. **Practical Tool Release:** Making the annotation tool publicly available is a commendable decision. This will facilitate broader adoption and further research in the field.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Novelty of Individual Components:** While the *combination* of linguistically-enhanced alignment and a rule-based classifier is effective, neither component is entirely novel. Linguistically-informed alignment algorithms (Felice et al., 2016) and rule-based error typing (Swanson & Yamangil, 2012) have been explored previously. The main novelty lies in their integration and the resulting dataset-agnostic property.
2. **Dependency on Alignment Quality:** The entire pipeline’s accuracy is fundamentally dependent on the quality of the initial alignment step. While Felice et al. (2016) achieves good alignment F1 scores, misalignments will inevitably propagate errors through the system. The paper doesn’t provide a detailed error analysis of alignment failures and their impact on the final error type classifications.
3. **Granularity of Error Types:** The 25 error types, while comprehensive, may still be too broad for certain applications. The discussion regarding modal vs. tense errors (Section 3.2) highlights this challenge. A more fine-grained classification could potentially reveal more specific system weaknesses, but would also increase the complexity of the rule-based system.
4. **Lack of Comparison to Other Automatic Annotation Methods:** The paper doesn’t directly compare the performance of this approach to other existing automatic annotation techniques, if any. A comparative evaluation would strengthen the claim of superiority.

**Questions to Authors:**

1.  What is the impact of alignment errors on the accuracy of the error type classification? Can you provide an analysis of errors stemming from misalignments?
2.  Have you considered incorporating more sophisticated linguistic features beyond POS tags and lemmas into the rule-based classifier? (e.g., dependency relations, semantic information)
3.  The paper mentions that the annotation tool will be released. Will the rule-based classifier also be released as code, allowing for customization and extension?
4.  Are there plans to apply this tool to more recent GEC datasets or systems beyond CoNLL-2014?
