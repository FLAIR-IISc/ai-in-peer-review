This paper presents a valuable contribution to the field of Grammatical Error Correction (GEC) by addressing the critical issue of evaluating system performance on a granular, error-type level. The authors successfully develop a method to automatically annotate system outputs with error types, enabling detailed analysis previously hampered by the lack of annotated system hypotheses. The core contribution lies in the combination of linguistically-enhanced alignment (Felice et al., 2016) with a novel, dataset-independent rule-based error classification framework. While the individual components aren’t entirely novel, their integration and application to a large-scale evaluation of the CoNLL-2014 systems is significant. A secondary contribution is the released tool, which will undoubtedly benefit the GEC research community. Finally, the detailed analysis of the CoNLL-2014 systems, showcasing the differing strengths of various approaches across error types, provides valuable insights for future research.

**Strengths:**

1. **Addresses a Critical Gap:** The paper directly tackles a major limitation in GEC evaluation – the lack of annotated system outputs. This prevents detailed error analysis and hinders progress towards more targeted system improvements. Providing a method for automated annotation is a significant step forward.
2. **Robust Rule-Based Classifier:** The dataset-independent nature of the error classification framework is a major strength. Unlike machine learning approaches which require labelled data and are susceptible to domain bias, the rule-based system leverages automatically obtainable features (POS tags, lemmas). The high agreement with human raters ( >95% “Good” or “Acceptable”) demonstrates the effectiveness and reliability of this approach. This suggests that at least for error *typing* – once the edit boundaries are determined – a simple, linguistically motivated rule-based approach is surprisingly effective.
3. **Comprehensive CoNLL-2014 Analysis:** Applying the annotation tool to the CoNLL-2014 systems and presenting a detailed breakdown of performance by error type is a substantial contribution. The results reveal nuanced strengths and weaknesses of different systems, offering valuable guidance for future development (e.g., the benefit of combining MT and classifier approaches). The identification of error types where all systems struggle (ADV, CONJ) also highlights areas requiring further research.
4. **Practical Impact & Tool Release:** The authors commit to releasing the annotation tool, which will empower other researchers to conduct similar detailed analyses on their own systems and datasets, furthering the field. This promotes reproducibility and facilitates collaborative research.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Reliance on Edit Extraction Quality:** The entire pipeline hinges on the accuracy of the initial edit extraction step (Felice et al., 2016). While the authors acknowledge this, the impact of imperfect edit boundaries on the subsequent error type classification isn't fully explored. Errors in edit extraction will directly propagate to misclassifications, potentially inflating or deflating reported performance. A more thorough error analysis of the edit extraction phase itself would strengthen the paper.
2. **Limited Evaluation of the Classifier:** The evaluation of the classifier relies on a relatively small-scale manual evaluation (200 edits). While the high agreement rate is encouraging, a more extensive evaluation with a larger, diverse dataset, and ideally multiple raters beyond the initial five, would provide greater confidence in the generalizability of the results. The Kappa score of 0.724 is reasonable but could be improved with more raters and clearer guidelines.
3. **Comparison with M2 Scorer is Underdeveloped:** The authors correctly point out that the M2 scorer can overestimate performance, but the discussion remains somewhat superficial. A deeper dive into *why* M2 inflates scores, and a comparison of the types of errors each scorer identifies (and misidentifies) would provide a more compelling argument for the advantages of their approach. The explanation regarding merging edits feels incomplete.
4. **Lack of Error Analysis in Specific Cases:** While the authors present aggregate results for each error type, a more in-depth qualitative error analysis illustrating specific instances where the classifier succeeds and fails would be beneficial. Examples of misclassified errors and the reasoning behind them would provide valuable insights.

**Questions to Authors:**

1.  What is the sensitivity of the error type classification to errors in the edit extraction phase? Have you conducted any experiments to quantify the impact of imperfect edit boundaries on the final results?
2.  Could you provide a more detailed explanation of the discrepancies between the M2 scorer and your approach, including examples of edits where they diverge?
3.  How does the performance of the rule-based classifier compare to other state-of-the-art error type classification methods (e.g., those based on machine learning) on publicly available datasets, even acknowledging the domain adaptation issues?
4.  Are there any plans to extend the rule-based classifier to handle more complex error types or to incorporate additional linguistic features beyond POS tags and lemmas?



**Additional Comments:**

*   Table 2 (Error Categories) could benefit from more detailed descriptions of each error type, especially for less common ones.
*   The citation style is inconsistent (e.g., "Sakaguchi et al., 2016" vs. "Felice et al., 2016"). Standardize the citation format.
*   Consider discussing the limitations of the rule-based approach for handling nuanced or context-dependent errors.
