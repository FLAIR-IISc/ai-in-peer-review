This paper presents a method for automatically annotating grammatical errors in corrected text, enabling detailed evaluation of GEC systems beyond simple recall. The core contribution is a linguistically-enhanced alignment combined with a dataset-independent, rule-based error type classifier. A secondary, yet valuable contribution, is the application of this method to analyze the CoNLL-2014 shared task submissions, providing a detailed error type breakdown for each system—something previously unavailable. Finally, the authors provide a publicly available tool implementing their approach.

**Strengths:**

1. **Addresses a significant gap in GEC evaluation:**  Prior to this work, detailed error-type based analysis of GEC systems was severely limited by the lack of annotated system outputs. This paper directly addresses this limitation, providing a method to automatically generate such annotations. The ability to move beyond overall scores to specific error type performance is a major step forward for the field.
2. **Strong validation of the classifier:** The authors convincingly demonstrate the reliability of their rule-based classifier through manual evaluation. The fact that experts rated >95% of the predicted error types as “Good” or “Acceptable” is a strong indicator of its quality, especially given that the alignment process itself introduces some noise. This provides confidence in the usability of the annotations for downstream analysis.
3. **Detailed analysis of CoNLL-2014 systems:** The application of the tool to the CoNLL-2014 data is a valuable contribution.  The breakdown of performance by error type for each participating system is insightful and reveals complementary strengths and weaknesses between different approaches. The observations about the difficulty of correcting certain error types (ADV, CONJ) and the success of different approaches (MT for multi-token edits) are useful for future research. 
4. **Dataset independence:** The rule-based nature of the classifier, relying on automatically obtained features (POS tags, lemmas), avoids the need for labeled training data and makes the approach applicable to a wide range of datasets, reducing the reliance on domain-specific annotations.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited novelty in individual components:** The linguistically-enhanced alignment and rule-based classification are not entirely novel. Previous work (cited by the authors) exists on both, and the combination doesn't represent a fundamentally new algorithmic breakthrough. The primary innovation lies in the specific implementation and application to the problem of GEC evaluation, rather than groundbreaking new techniques.
2. **Dependence on POS tagging accuracy:** The classifier’s performance is heavily reliant on the accuracy of the POS tagger. While the authors acknowledge this (and provide some evaluation data in Table 3), the impact of POS tagging errors on the overall annotation quality is not fully quantified or mitigated. Erroneous POS tags can lead to misclassification, potentially affecting the accuracy of the subsequent analysis.
3. **Simplistic evaluation of edit extraction:** The paper utilizes an existing edit extraction method (Felice et al., 2016) without significant modification or evaluation of its performance in the context of their error typing framework. A more thorough assessment of the impact of edit extraction errors on the final results would strengthen the analysis. The inherent limitation of edit extraction, particularly when dealing with complex sentences, could introduce errors that cascade through the subsequent steps.
4. **Lack of comparison to human annotations beyond the initial validation:** While the authors show high agreement with human raters on a small sample, it would be beneficial to have a more comprehensive comparison between the automatic annotations and gold-standard human annotations on a larger dataset to fully assess the tool’s accuracy and limitations.

**Questions to Authors:**

1.  Could you provide more detail on the impact of POS tagging errors on the error type classification? Did you investigate any strategies to mitigate the effects of inaccurate POS tags?
2.  The analysis of CoNLL-2014 results highlights differences in performance between systems.  Beyond the observations already made, can you offer any hypotheses about *why* certain approaches excel at specific error types, based on the characteristics of the algorithms themselves?
3.  The paper states that reprocessing the reference file with the automatic alignment doesn't significantly impact the overall scores. Could you elaborate on the specific discrepancies observed, and provide examples of where the automatic processing *did* lead to differences in results compared to using the gold standard annotations?
