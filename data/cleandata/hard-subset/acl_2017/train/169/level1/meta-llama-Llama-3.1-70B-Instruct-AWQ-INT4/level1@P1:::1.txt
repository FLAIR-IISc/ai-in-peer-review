**Summary of the Paper**

This paper presents a novel approach to automatic annotation and evaluation of error types for Grammatical Error Correction (GEC) systems. The authors propose a linguistically-enhanced alignment algorithm to extract edits between parallel original and corrected sentences, and a new rule-based classifier to assign error types to these edits. The classifier is dataset-independent and relies only on automatically obtained information such as POS tags and lemmas. The authors evaluate their approach on the CoNLL-2014 shared task dataset and demonstrate its effectiveness in providing a detailed analysis of system error type performance.

**Main Contributions**

1. **Novel approach to automatic annotation and evaluation of error types**: The authors propose a new method for automatic annotation and evaluation of error types for GEC systems, which is more detailed and accurate than previous approaches.
2. **Linguistically-enhanced alignment algorithm**: The authors use a linguistically-enhanced alignment algorithm to extract edits between parallel original and corrected sentences, which is more effective than previous approaches.
3. **Dataset-independent rule-based classifier**: The authors propose a new rule-based classifier that is dataset-independent and relies only on automatically obtained information such as POS tags and lemmas, which makes it more practical and efficient.

**Strengths**

1. **Effective evaluation of error types**: The authors' approach provides a detailed analysis of system error type performance, which is essential for improving GEC systems.
2. **Dataset-independent classifier**: The authors' classifier is dataset-independent, which makes it more practical and efficient for evaluating GEC systems.
3. **High accuracy**: The authors' approach achieves high accuracy in evaluating error types, with an overall F-score of 86.3% and a high inter-annotator agreement of 0.724 κfree.

**Weaknesses**

1. **Limited evaluation**: The authors' evaluation is limited to the CoNLL-2014 shared task dataset, which may not be representative of all GEC systems.
2. **Dependence on POS tags and lemmas**: The authors' classifier relies on POS tags and lemmas, which may not always be accurate or available.
3. **Limited handling of complex errors**: The authors' approach may not be able to handle complex errors that involve multiple tokens or sentences.

**Questions to Authors**

1. How do the authors plan to extend their approach to handle complex errors that involve multiple tokens or sentences?
2. Can the authors provide more details on how their classifier handles errors that are not well-characterized by POS tags and lemmas?
3. How do the authors plan to evaluate their approach on other datasets and GEC systems?

**Additional Comments**

The authors' approach is a significant contribution to the field of GEC, and their evaluation results are impressive. However, the authors should consider extending their approach to handle complex errors and evaluating their approach on other datasets and GEC systems.
