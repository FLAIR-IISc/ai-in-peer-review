**Summary of the Paper**

This paper proposes a method for automatically annotating parallel error correction data with explicit edit spans and error type information. The authors use a linguistically-enhanced alignment algorithm to extract edits between original and corrected sentences and then classify them using a new dataset-independent rule-based classifier. They evaluate their approach by applying it to the system output produced in the CoNLL-2014 shared task and carrying out a detailed analysis of system error type performance.

**Main Contributions**

1. **Automatic Annotation and Evaluation of Error Types**: The authors propose a method for automatically annotating parallel error correction data with explicit edit spans and error type information, enabling a more detailed evaluation of system performance.
2. **Dataset-Independent Rule-Based Classifier**: The authors introduce a new rule-based classifier that is entirely dataset-independent, relying only on automatically obtained information such as POS tags and lemmas.
3. **Detailed Error Type Analysis**: The authors demonstrate the value of their approach by carrying out a detailed evaluation of system error type performance for the first time for all teams in the CoNLL-2014 shared task on Grammatical Error Correction.

**Strengths**

1. **Novel Approach to Error Type Evaluation**: The authors' method enables a more detailed evaluation of system performance by automatically annotating parallel error correction data with explicit edit spans and error type information.
2. **Dataset-Independent Classifier**: The authors' rule-based classifier is entirely dataset-independent, making it a valuable contribution to the field of Grammatical Error Correction.
3. **Comprehensive Evaluation**: The authors' evaluation of their approach using the CoNLL-2014 shared task data provides a comprehensive analysis of system error type performance.

**Weaknesses**

1. **Limited Evaluation**: The authors' evaluation is limited to a small-scale manual evaluation of their classifier, which may not be representative of the classifier's performance on larger datasets.
2. **Comparison to Other Metrics**: The authors' comparison of their approach to other metrics, such as the M2 scorer, is limited and may not provide a complete picture of the strengths and weaknesses of their approach.
3. **Error Type Classification**: The authors' rule-based classifier may not be able to capture the nuances of human error type classification, potentially leading to inconsistencies in the evaluation results.

**Questions to Authors**

1. How do the authors plan to address the limitations of their evaluation, such as the small-scale manual evaluation of their classifier?
2. Can the authors provide a more detailed comparison of their approach to other metrics, such as the M2 scorer, to better understand the strengths and weaknesses of their approach?
3. How do the authors plan to refine their rule-based classifier to better capture the nuances of human error type classification?
