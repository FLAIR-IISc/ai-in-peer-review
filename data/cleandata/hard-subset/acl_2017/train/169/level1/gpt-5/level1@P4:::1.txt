Summary and contributions
The paper addresses the lack of error-type evaluation for grammatical error correction (GEC) system outputs, which historically precluded precision-oriented analysis because hypotheses were unannotated. The authors propose a two-stage pipeline: (i) linguistically enhanced alignment to extract edits between source and corrected sentences; (ii) a dataset-agnostic, deterministic rule-based classifier that assigns fine-grained error types using only automatic linguistic markup (POS, lemmas, stems, dependency parses, dictionary lookup). A small human study judges predicted error types as “Good” or “Acceptable” in ≥95% of cases per rater. The tool is then used to annotate CoNLL-2014 system outputs and references (the latter reprocessed automatically), enabling the first detailed, per-type analysis of system performance, including operation tiers (Missing/Replacement/Unnecessary), detailed categories, multi-token edits, and detection vs. correction.

Contributions (as assessed), in order of strength:
1) A dataset-agnostic, deterministic error-typing framework and tool that enables per-error-type precision/recall evaluation of unannotated GEC outputs, with high human judged appropriateness and transparent decision traces.
2) A simple edit-comparison evaluation protocol that, after reprocessing references automatically, allows direct overlap-based scoring and per-type breakdowns; the authors show no significant difference vs. gold references for overall F0.5 and analyze differences with the M2 scorer.
3) A comprehensive, first-of-its-kind error-type analysis of CoNLL-2014 systems, revealing complementary strengths across approaches (e.g., classifiers vs. MT), weaknesses on multi-token edits, and gaps in handling Unnecessary errors.

Strengths
- Clear, practically important problem and actionable solution: The work fills a recognized gap by enabling precision/recall per error type on system outputs. This allows targeted diagnostics and is immediately useful to the community.
- Deterministic, interpretable design: The classifier depends only on automatic markup and fixed rules, making decisions traceable and reproducible across datasets without retraining. Separating alignment from evaluation simplifies analysis and avoids metric-specific alignment heuristics.
- Human validation supports utility: Five GEC researchers rated 200 edits; overall 86.3% “Good” and 10.0% “Acceptable”; each rater ≥95% Good+Acceptable. Agreement is substantial (κfree=0.724). The paper pinpoints tagging errors as a common cause of “Bad” labels, indicating where improvements would most help.
- Empirical evidence that automatic reprocessing preserves overall rankings: Using bootstrap tests on CoNLL-2014, auto-processed references yield no significant difference in overall F0.5 compared to gold under both M2 and the proposed overlap scorer. This supports using the auto pipeline for consistent hypothesis–reference comparison.
- Insightful analyses that expose system behavior: The operation-tier results show many systems ignore Unnecessary errors; per-type breakdowns identify which approaches work best for DET, ORTH, VERB:FORM, etc.; multi-token analyses show current limits (only MT systems exceed 10 F0.5); detection vs. correction comparisons reveal systems that detect many errors but correct few. These findings are grounded in the presented tables and are valuable for future research directions.
- Tool release and standardized scheme: The promise to release the tool and a coherent set of 25 base types (55 valid combinations) facilitates adoption, comparability, and future benchmarks.

Weaknesses
- Limited intrinsic evaluation of the classifier: The human study is small (200 edits) and reports acceptability rather than accuracy against gold error types; there is no per-category validation, confusion analysis, or larger-scale assessment. Comparisons to learned classifiers are not directly comparable due to differing schemes and metrics.
- Dependence on automatic linguistic preprocessing without robustness quantification: The method relies on POS/lemmas/parses/stemming and notes mis-tagging as a failure source, but there is no ablation study (e.g., gold POS on a subset) to quantify sensitivity, nor an analysis of how such errors propagate to per-type scores.
- Alignment and boundary effects: The approach hinges on automatic edit spans (Felice et al., 2016; 80–85 F1). While raters were instructed to ignore boundary oddities, downstream evaluation uses exact span matching. Reprocessing references automatically introduces a distributional shift; the claim that auto references are “as good as gold” is supported only for overall F0.5, not per-type or per-operation tiers. Detailed p-values are not reported.
- Rule set and scheme design under-specified: The paper motivates pragmatic choices (e.g., folding modals into tense) but does not fully describe the rule inventory, priorities, or conflict resolution, limiting reproducibility and extension. The implications of the scheme’s granularity on interpretability and cross-corpus comparability are discussed qualitatively but not empirically.
- Claims about M2 overestimation are not quantified: The qualitative argument that M2 inflates scores via dynamic boundary optimization is plausible and consistent with prior work, but the paper does not provide controlled experiments (e.g., varying max unchanged words, fixing boundaries) to measure the inflation or its impact per error type.

Questions to authors
- Can you provide per-type human validation (or at least per-tier) and an error analysis of the 3.7% “Bad” predictions to identify systematic misclassifications?
- How sensitive is the classifier to tagging/parsing errors? Could you report an ablation with gold POS on a small subset, or quantify changes when disabling components (e.g., dependency features, stemming, dictionary)?
- How are overlapping/nested edits handled in alignment and typing? For word-order errors (transpositions), what are the precise rules and limitations?
- How does the tool handle multiple references? Do you merge edits/types across annotators or score each reference separately? What is the impact on per-type precision/recall?
- Could you release or append the full rule inventory with examples and precedence, to facilitate community verification and extension?
- Can you report per-type comparisons between auto- and gold-processed references, and between detection vs. correction, to ensure the “no significant difference” finding holds beyond overall F0.5?
- For the M2 discussion, can you include a controlled experiment that fixes edit spans (or varies max unchanged words) to quantify the alleged inflation and its per-type effects?
- What are the runtime and memory characteristics of the pipeline on the CoNLL-2014 test set and larger corpora?
