Summary and contributions
This paper proposes a practical pipeline to automatically annotate parallel original/corrected sentences with explicit edit spans and error types, enabling type-wise evaluation of grammatical error correction (GEC) systems, including on unannotated hypothesis outputs. The approach combines (i) linguistically-enhanced alignment (Damerau-Levenshtein with POS/lemma costs) to extract edits and (ii) a deterministic, dataset-agnostic rule-based classifier that maps edits to a compact, POS-centered error taxonomy with operation prefixes (Missing, Unnecessary, Replacement). A small human study rates the predicted types as Good/Acceptable ≥95% per rater; the tool is then used to analyze CoNLL-2014 system outputs by error type.

As I see it, the main contributions (in order of strength) are:
1) A usable, dataset-agnostic, deterministic error-type annotation pipeline for GEC hypotheses that enables detailed, comparable error-type evaluation (including operation tier, multi-token, and detection vs. correction).
2) An error-type framework and rule-based classifier that relies only on automatic linguistic markup, with encouraging manual validation.
3) A comprehensive, novel analysis of CoNLL-2014 systems by error category, revealing complementary strengths across approaches (MT vs. classifiers vs. rules), and highlighting performance gaps (e.g., multi-token edits and Unnecessary errors).

Strengths
- High practical impact and fill an evaluation gap. The community has lacked a way to score system outputs by error type without manual annotation. By aligning and typing system hypotheses and reprocessing references consistently, the authors enable straightforward edit-level comparison and produce actionable per-type diagnostics (Tables 5–6), operation-tier breakdowns, multi-token edit performance (Table 8), and detection vs. correction. The public release (promised) could rapidly become a staple for GEC evaluation.

- Simple, transparent, and dataset-agnostic method. The fully rule-based classifier depends only on automatic POS/lemmas/UD mapping and basic lexical resources (Hunspell). This avoids training bias and inter-corpus incompatibilities that affect supervised approaches. Determinism also supports interpretability and debugging—valuable in evaluation tooling.

- Empirical validation, albeit small-scale, is encouraging. Despite automatic edit extraction noise, 5 expert raters judged 86.3% Good and 10.0% Acceptable, totaling 96.3% Good/Acceptable, with good agreement (κfree = 0.724). This is compelling for an evaluation tool, especially when compared against prior ML classifiers’ 50–70 F1 on error typing.

- Insightful analysis that yields concrete takeaways. The per-type results surface clear patterns: classifiers excel on VERB:FORM and VERB:SVA; hybrid MT leads for Missing and many token categories; several teams surprisingly failed to handle Unnecessary deletions; MT and some LMs handle NOUN:NUM well; spellchecking is underexploited; multi-token edits remain weak across systems; and detection often outpaces correction. These are practically useful for system design.

- Thoughtful discussion of reference processing and metric behavior. Showing no significant difference between gold and auto references under both M2 and their simpler edit comparison helps justify consistent auto processing for fair comparison. The discussion of M2’s tendency to inflate scores via dynamic boundary selection is useful context.

Weaknesses
- Limited, subjective validation of the classifier. The only quantitative evidence is a 200-edit, two-corpus human rating of appropriateness (3-way scale). There is no gold standard label set, confusion analysis, or per-category accuracy. The ratings conflate classifier quality with POS/dependency errors, and raters were asked to ignore boundary issues. The study’s small size and focus on CoNLL/FCE limit claims of robustness and generalizability.

- Error scheme design and rule set are under-specified. While the taxonomy balances practicality and informativeness, several broad categories (e.g., VERB:TENSE includes modals, passives) can mask systematic distinctions; conversely, some categories rely heavily on POS and morphology and will be brittle when tagging is wrong (learner text is challenging). Without an explicit rule list or ablations (e.g., effect of POS errors), it is hard to gauge stability. “OTHER” and “UNK” categories risk becoming sinks, potentially biasing per-type comparisons.

- Dependence on automatic alignment and exact-span matching may bias evaluation. The approach requires identical spans/corrections to match, which penalizes segmentation mismatches and favors the chosen alignment heuristic. While the auto-vs-gold reference comparison shows small overall differences on CoNLL-2014, there is no analysis of per-type sensitivity, nor of alternative alignment strategies. The critique of M2 is plausible but not accompanied by a controlled error analysis across categories.

- Reproducibility and scope limitations. The paper notes the tool “will be released,” but at review time the rules, thresholds, and resources (e.g., the precise UD mapping, handling of contractions/orthography) are not enumerated. There is no evaluation of robustness to different taggers/parsers, domain shifts, or spelling lexicons (e.g., US vs. UK). The method is English-only as presented (Hunspell GB, Penn→UD mapping), yet the pitch is dataset-agnostic; cross-lingual portability is untested.

- Statistical rigor on downstream analysis is light. While bootstrap tests are reported for overall scores in Table 4, the per-category comparisons (Tables 5–6, Table 8) lack confidence intervals or significance testing, despite many comparisons. This limits the strength of some claims regarding which approach “outperforms” others per type.

Questions to authors
- Can you provide the full rule inventory and decision procedure (including precedence, fallback behavior, and examples), along with an error-type confusion analysis from the human study? Which categories contribute most to “Bad” ratings?

- How sensitive are type assignments to tagging/parsing errors? Have you tried alternative taggers/parsers or UD mappings, and can you report variance? Do you have a mitigation (e.g., backoff rules, ensemble tagging)?

- How portable is the framework to other languages? What components are language-specific (e.g., Hunspell dictionary, POS set), and what is needed to adapt?

- Can you report per-type reliability of the classifier (e.g., manual spot-checks per category) and an ablation showing the impact of the linguistically-enhanced alignment vs. simpler alignment on match rates and per-type scores?

- Will you release the scorer with fixed versions of dependencies and scripts to reprocess CoNLL-2014 hypotheses and references, to ensure exact reproducibility?

Overall, this is a timely, useful evaluation paper with a straightforward, implementable approach and compelling application to a prominent benchmark. Despite limitations in validation depth and reproducibility details, the practical utility and insights argue for acceptance after addressing clarifications and releasing the tool and rules.
