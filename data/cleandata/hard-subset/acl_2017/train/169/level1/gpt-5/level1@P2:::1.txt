Summary
The paper proposes an automatic pipeline to annotate unlabelled grammatical error correction (GEC) outputs with edit spans and error types, enabling detailed, per-error-type evaluation of systems. The pipeline combines (i) a linguistically informed alignment-based edit extractor (Felice et al., 2016) and (ii) a dataset-agnostic, rule-based error type classifier driven by automatic POS/lemma/parse information. The authors validate the classifier via expert judgments (>95% Good/Acceptable over 200 edits; κfree=0.724) and apply the tool to the CoNLL-2014 shared task outputs to produce the first comprehensive error-type performance analysis across teams, including operation-tier (Missing/Unnecessary/Replacement), per-type breakdowns, multi-token edits, and detection vs. correction. They also argue that reprocessing the gold references through the same automatic pipeline creates a consistent evaluation setting and yields similar aggregate F0.5 scores (bootstrap test, p>.05) to using gold references.

Contributions (as I see them)
1) A practical, deterministic, dataset-agnostic error typing framework and tool for GEC outputs, enabling per-type evaluation without labelled training data.
2) A large-scale, insightful error-type analysis of CoNLL-2014 systems, revealing complementary strengths/weaknesses across approaches, the difficulty of multi-token edits, and a clearer picture of detection vs. correction capabilities.
3) A methodological evaluation protocol that fixes edit spans in both hypothesis and reference (via automatic reprocessing), making edit-level comparison straightforward and reproducible; with evidence of comparable aggregate scores to the standard M2 scorer.

Strengths
1) Addresses a clear gap in GEC evaluation practice. Existing metrics obscure per-type performance and focus on recall or aggregate scores. The proposed tool provides actionable diagnostics (e.g., CUUI’s strong VERB:FORM, CAMB’s DET and WO, widespread neglect of Unnecessary edits), which can guide system design. The CoNLL-2014 reanalysis is substantive and demonstrates utility beyond a toy setting.
2) Deterministic, interpretable, and dataset-agnostic classification. The rule-based classifier relies on automatic POS/lemma/parse and simple lexical resources, avoiding training data and corpus-specific schemas. This increases transparency (traceable rules for decisions), consistency (no annotator or classifier drift), and portability across datasets. Expert judgments (>95% Good/Acceptable; 200 edits; 5 raters; κfree=0.724) support that the rules largely align with human expectations despite noisy automatic markup.
3) Clean separation of edit extraction and evaluation with consistent processing of both hypothesis and reference. By reprocessing the reference, the comparison reduces to exact span+correction matching, avoiding dynamic boundary effects. The authors show no significant differences between gold vs. auto references across teams (Table 4; bootstrap), bolstering confidence in the approach for aggregate scoring.
4) Rich, empirically grounded insights likely to impact future work. Findings such as the general weakness on multi-token edits (only ~10% of edits but low performance; only MT systems exceed 10 F0.5), the detection–correction gap, and the variability across operation tiers and error types are valuable for prioritizing research (e.g., fluency-oriented, structural edits; deletion modeling; type-specific classifiers).
5) Tool release. Assuming the tool is indeed released as promised, it will facilitate reproducibility and community uptake.

Weaknesses
1) Limited and coarse validation of the classifier. The 200-edit, three-way Good/Acceptable/Bad evaluation, while encouraging, lacks per-type precision/recall or confusion analyses and does not quantify performance against a gold error-type standard. Given the reliance on automatic POS/parsing, more rigorous evaluation (e.g., larger sample, stratified by type, errorful vs. fluent contexts) is needed to substantiate 95% acceptability and to identify systematic failure modes (e.g., POS/lemma ambiguity causing NOUN:NUM vs. VERB:SVA confusions).
2) Sensitivity to upstream annotation quality and English-specific resources. Although “dataset-agnostic,” the method depends on English POS tagging, UD mapping, dependency parsing, and Hunspell. The authors acknowledge POS errors can flip categories. Robustness across domains (e.g., very noisy learner text, different L1 populations) and language portability are not evaluated. Claims of generality would be stronger with out-of-domain tests or partial error analyses across alternative taggers/parsers.
3) Potential methodological bias from using the same alignment/merging rules for both hypothesis and reprocessed reference. While aggregate scores match gold/M2, category-level distributions and multi-token span boundaries may be sensitive to the cost function and merging heuristics. A sensitivity analysis (e.g., ablations or alternative alignment settings) would increase confidence that the reported type-wise conclusions are not artifacts of the alignment.
4) Claims about M2 “overestimation” need firmer grounding. The examples are persuasive, and prior work is cited, but the paper does not quantify the magnitude or conditions under which M2’s dynamic boundary optimization inflates scores versus the proposed fixed-span protocol. A controlled study on synthetic cases or human-aligned micro-analyses would clarify trade-offs.
5) Rule set and taxonomy design choices are somewhat under-specified. The paper defers many details (e.g., full rule list, precedence, tie-breaking) and acknowledges compromises (e.g., modal vs. tense). For reproducibility and community extension, the exact rules and their interactions should be documented exhaustively; otherwise, replicating or extending the scheme is difficult.

Questions to Authors
- Will you release the complete rule set (with precedence and tie-breaking) and alignment cost/merging parameters, plus scripts to reproduce all CoNLL-2014 analyses?
- How does classifier performance vary by error type and under POS/parsing errors? Can you report a per-type manual validation on a larger, stratified sample?
- How robust are the results to using different POS taggers/parsers or to removing the dependency parse? Any ablation or sensitivity results?
- Can the approach generalize to other English domains or to other languages with appropriate taggers/dictionaries? Any preliminary evidence?
- For the M2 critique, can you quantify average inflation and show cases where your protocol under- or over-estimates relative to human-aligned spans?

Additional Comments
- Please fix reference formatting (several entries have placeholders/question marks) and minor typographical issues.
- Specify spaCy model version and configuration, the UD mapping used, Hunspell version (given), and any non-default parameters in the alignment algorithm.
- Consider releasing per-type detection breakdowns and multi-token length distributions to complement the presented aggregates.

Overall evaluation
A practical, impactful contribution that fills a real evaluation gap in GEC with a well-motivated, interpretable method and useful analysis. While the validation could be stronger and some methodological choices warrant deeper scrutiny, I lean toward acceptance given the tool’s utility and the quality of insights provided.
