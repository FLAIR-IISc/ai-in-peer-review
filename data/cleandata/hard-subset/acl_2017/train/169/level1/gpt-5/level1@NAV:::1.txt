Summary
This paper presents an automatic pipeline to annotate untyped GEC hypotheses with explicit edit spans and error types. It first extracts edits via a linguistically-enhanced alignment (Damerau-Levenshtein with POS/lemma costs), then assigns types using a deterministic, dataset-agnostic rule-based classifier driven by automatic POS/lemmas/dependencies. A small manual study finds >95% of predicted types rated Good/Acceptable by each of five raters (overall 86.3% Good, 10% Acceptable; κfree=0.724). The authors apply the tool to CoNLL-2014 outputs to deliver the first large-scale error-type analysis across systems, including operation-level, type-level, multi-token, and detection vs correction breakdowns. They also show that auto-processed references yield comparable scores to gold via bootstrap testing, and discuss why M2 may inflate scores.

Contributions (as I see them)
1) A practical, deterministic, dataset-agnostic rule-based error type classifier coupled with linguistically-informed edit extraction that enables error-type evaluation of unannotated GEC outputs.
2) A comprehensive, empirical error-type analysis of CoNLL-2014 systems, surfacing actionable strengths/weaknesses (e.g., poor handling of unnecessary tokens, multi-token edits).
3) An evaluation protocol demonstrating auto-referenced scoring is statistically indistinguishable from gold and highlighting limitations of the M2 scorer.

Strengths
- High practical utility and reproducibility: The deterministic classifier (no training data) plus released tool addresses a long-standing gap—typing system hypotheses—making nuanced evaluation broadly accessible.
- Empirical validation: Human ratings (>95% Good/Acceptable per rater; κfree=0.724) support the appropriateness of type assignments despite automatic boundaries, and errors are traceable to POS mistakes, underscoring transparency.
- Comprehensive analysis with clear insights: The CoNLL-2014 breakdown reveals meaningful patterns (e.g., classifier strengths on VERB:FORM, MT advantages on multi-token edits), offering concrete guidance for system design.
- Sound evaluation methodology: Separating alignment from scoring, reprocessing references for comparability, and bootstrap significance testing strengthen the credibility of conclusions; the critique of M2 boundary dynamics is well-argued with examples.

Weaknesses
- Limited scale of classifier validation: Only 200 edits and an appropriateness metric; no per-type precision/recall against a harmonized gold reduces confidence in fine-grained accuracy.
- Pipeline brittleness and language dependence: Reliance on specific English taggers/parsers/stemmers and Hunspell may limit robustness and portability; POS/tagging errors propagate to type errors.
- Scheme design choices and ambiguity: Collapsing modals into tense and other compromises may obscure important distinctions; guidance on downstream impact is limited.
- Boundary sensitivity: Using automatically derived edit spans can misalign with human edits, potentially affecting type counts and F-scores; more ablation on alignment choices would help.
- Generalization beyond CoNLL/FCE not demonstrated; cross-dataset or non-English evidence is absent.

Questions to Authors (Optional)
- Can you provide ablations on features (POS vs lemma vs dependencies) and on alignment parameters to quantify sensitivity?
- Is there a coarse mapping to shared schemes that would allow per-type precision/recall against gold-typed corpora?
- How portable is the framework to other languages given UD tags; any preliminary results?
- Will the tool include diagnostics to flag low-confidence types (e.g., when POS disagreement is detected)?
