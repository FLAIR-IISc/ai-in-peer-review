Summary and Contributions
This paper proposes a pipeline to automatically annotate GEC outputs with edit spans and error types, enabling per-error-type precision/recall instead of recall-only analyses. The method (i) uses a linguistically-informed Damerau–Levenshtein alignment (Felice et al., 2016) to extract edits, and (ii) a deterministic, dataset-independent rule-based classifier that assigns error types based on automatically derived properties (POS/lemma/stem/UD tags, dependency parses, Hunspell). A small-scale human study rates the predicted types as Good/Acceptable in ≥95% of cases. The authors then re-annotate both references and system outputs for CoNLL-2014 to produce the first comprehensive error-type breakdown across systems, including analysis by operation (Missing/Unnecessary/Replacement), by fine-grained type, and by multi-token edits and detection vs. correction.

As I see them, primary contributions are:
1) A practical, dataset-agnostic rule-based error-type classification framework and accompanying tool that can annotate both hypotheses and references consistently, enabling fair type-wise evaluation.
2) A detailed empirical analysis of error-type performance across CoNLL-2014 systems, revealing complementary strengths/weaknesses (e.g., MT vs. classifiers, operation-tier differences, multi-token edits).
3) An evaluation showing that replacing gold references with automatically re-processed references yields similar overall scores (bootstrap p>0.05) and a critique that M2 may overestimate via dynamic boundary choices.

Strengths
- Clear, actionable contribution to evaluation practice: The tool and schema allow the community to move beyond aggregate F0.5 and recall-only type analyses. By ensuring identical processing for hypotheses and references, the evaluation becomes transparent (span+correction match) and directly interpretable by error type and operation. This has immediate utility for system diagnostics and benchmarking.

- Careful, linguistically motivated engineering: Building on Felice et al. (2016)’s alignment, the classifier exploits robust signals (POS/lemma/UD/dependencies) and avoids dataset-specific training, making it widely applicable. The taxonomy is thoughtfully balanced between granularity and practicality, including operation-tier, POS-tier, and morphology-tier types with well-motivated exceptions (e.g., modals folded into TENSE).

- Informative large-scale analysis with novel insights: The CoNLL-2014 error-type breakdown surfaces non-obvious findings (e.g., many systems fail to handle Unnecessary tokens; CUUI excels on VERB:FORM and ORTH; MT excels on some NOUN/NOUN:NUM; hybrid MT best on DET and PREP). The multi-token analysis is particularly valuable, highlighting a community-wide weakness relevant to fluency-oriented correction.

- Human validation of type assignment: Although limited in size, the expert ratings (86% Good, 10% Acceptable, κfree=0.724) indicate the rules align with expert intuition, and the observed errors are largely attributable to tagging mistakes, supporting the approach’s reliability.

- Useful perspective on M2 and reference processing: The demonstration that automatic reprocessing of references produces similar scores to gold across teams, and the analysis of how M2’s dynamic boundaries can inflate scores, are valuable contributions to ongoing metric discussions.

Weaknesses
- Limited scale and depth of classifier evaluation: The 200-edit human study (5 raters) measures perceived appropriateness rather than accuracy against a gold standard labeled in the proposed schema. There is no per-type error analysis, no robustness study to tagging/parsing noise, and no comparison to ML classifiers on a common schema. This weakens claims of general reliability and dataset-agnosticism.

- Potential brittleness to NLP preprocessing and dialectal bias: The rules hinge on spaCy v1.6 POS/UD parses and Hunspell GB-large. Tagging/parsing errors directly propagate to type errors; the paper acknowledges this but does not quantify sensitivity. The reliance on a British dictionary conflates spelling with dialect (e.g., color→colour), which could penalize systems targeting American English.

- Schema choices and transparency: Some categories (MORPH, OTHER) are broad and may absorb heterogeneous phenomena; modals are subsumed under TENSE, which some users may find unintuitive. The paper does not fully document rule ordering/conflict resolution or provide an ablation of rule components, making it hard to assess how easily the schema could be adapted or extended.

- Equivalence of auto vs. gold reference is under-evidenced: The bootstrap tests show no significant differences in aggregate F0.5, but equivalence is not established (lack of power, no per-type or per-operation analyses, no boundary-level agreement). Moreover, “no difference” in aggregate does not guarantee that type distributions are preserved; subtle mismatches could affect downstream diagnostic conclusions.

- Evaluation protocol trade-offs vs. M2 not fully settled: While the critique that M2 can inflate via boundary choices is fair, exact span+correction matching can be overly brittle (e.g., semantically equivalent but differently segmented edits). The paper does not explore tolerant matching at the type level (e.g., allowing aligned sub-edits within an edit span) or report how often span mismatches drive disagreements.

Questions to Authors
- Can you provide more detail on the rule engine: rule ordering, conflict resolution, and the features used by each major rule family (e.g., SVA, VERB:FORM, NOUN:NUM)? Is the rulebase configurable for alternative type taxonomies (e.g., separating modals)?

- How sensitive is the classifier to tagging/parsing errors? Could you report per-type accuracy changes when using gold POS/UD (where available) or when degrading tagger accuracy? Any ablation of POS vs. lemma vs. dependencies?

- Dialect handling: How are American/British variants treated? Can the spelling detector be switched to different lexicons, and does this materially change type distributions and system rankings?

- Equivalence study: Beyond aggregate F0.5, can you report per-type and per-operation comparisons between gold and auto references, and boundary-level agreement (e.g., edit span IoU)? This would strengthen the claim that auto annotations are “qualitatively as good as” human annotations.

- Generality: Have you tried the tool on other corpora with different annotation guidelines (e.g., JFLEG for fluency edits)? How does the classifier handle paraphrastic/fluency edits, and what fraction end up as OTHER/MORPH?

Overall recommendation: This is a solid, practical contribution that enables much-needed type-level evaluation in GEC and offers valuable empirical insights. With stronger validation of the classifier and more transparent rule documentation, it would be a highly impactful resource paper. I lean toward acceptance.
