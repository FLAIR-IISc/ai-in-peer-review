## Review of "Automatic Annotation and Evaluation of Error Types for Grammatical Error Correction"

This paper addresses a significant challenge in Grammatical Error Correction (GEC): the lack of readily available, annotated data for evaluating system performance at the error-type level. The authors propose a method to automatically annotate system outputs with error types, leveraging linguistically-enhanced alignment and a novel rule-based classifier. The core contribution is a dataset-independent error typing approach, circumventing the need for labeled training data, which is a major advantage given the variability in annotation schemes across datasets. A secondary, but valuable, contribution is the application of this method to re-evaluate the CoNLL-2014 shared task systems, providing a detailed error-type analysis previously unavailable. 

The strengths of this work lie in its practical utility and sound methodology. The high agreement rate (over 95%) between the automated annotations and human judgment demonstrates the effectiveness of the rule-based classifier.  The authors convincingly argue for the benefits of their approach over machine learning methods, highlighting the issues of dataset bias and lack of transparency.  Re-analyzing the CoNLL-2014 data provides concrete evidence of the method’s applicability and reveals interesting insights into the strengths and weaknesses of different GEC systems.  The authors’ commitment to releasing the annotation tool further enhances the reproducibility and impact of their work.

However, the paper has some weaknesses. The rule-based approach, while effective, feels somewhat limited in its expressiveness. While the authors acknowledge this, a discussion of potential extensions or limitations in handling complex error interactions would be beneficial. The analysis of the CoNLL-2014 data, while insightful, is largely descriptive.  A more in-depth statistical analysis, perhaps correlating error type performance with system architecture or training data, would strengthen the conclusions. Finally, the paper lacks a comparative analysis against existing, albeit limited, error type annotation methods. 

**Questions to Authors:**

1.  Could you elaborate on the limitations of the rule-based approach and potential avenues for extending it to handle more complex error interactions?
2.  What is the computational cost of the annotation pipeline, and how does it scale with the size of the input corpus?
3.  How does the performance of your rule-based classifier compare to existing supervised methods when trained on a common dataset (even if it requires adapting those methods to your error scheme)?
