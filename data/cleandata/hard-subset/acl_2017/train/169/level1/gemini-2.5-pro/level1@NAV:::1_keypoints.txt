- Introduces a dataset-independent, rule-based method that aligns source and hypothesis sentences and classifies GEC edits by error type using automatic linguistic features.
- Validates the classifier with human evaluation (over 95% outputs rated "Good" or "Acceptable") and applies it to all CoNLL-2014 systems for fine-grained error analysis.
- Releases a practical annotation tool that requires no labeled training data, enabling detailed, reproducible GEC evaluations.
- Provides high-impact, fine-grained analysis revealing system-specific strengths (e.g., CUUI strong on replacements, CAMB on missing tokens), motivating hybrid systems.
- Re-annotates gold references automatically and shows system rankings remain stable, supporting methodological soundness and fairness.
- The tool’s dataset-independence and no-training-data requirement make it broadly useful and reproducible for future GEC research.
- Classifier accuracy is limited by upstream POS tagger/parser performance, causing most "Bad" ratings and risking degraded performance across domains or proficiencies.
- The rule-based classifier may be brittle and hard to extend; the paper lacks discussion on engineering effort needed to adapt to new error schemas or languages.
- Main technical novelty is the rule-based classifier; core edit extraction relies on prior work (Felice et al., 2016), so novelty is concentrated rather than across the full pipeline.
