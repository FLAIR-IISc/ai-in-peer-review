### ACL 2017 Review

**Summary of the Paper**
This paper addresses a significant limitation in the evaluation of Grammatical Error Correction (GEC) systems: the inability to measure performance on specific error types beyond simple recall, as system outputs are not annotated. The authors propose a two-step, fully automatic pipeline to solve this. First, they use an existing linguistically-enhanced alignment algorithm to extract edits between original and corrected sentences. Second, and this is the core of their contribution, they develop a novel, dataset-independent, rule-based classifier to assign error types to these edits. This classifier relies solely on automatic linguistic features (POS tags, lemmas, etc.). The authors validate their classifier through a manual evaluation where GEC researchers rated over 95% of its outputs as "Good" or "Acceptable". Finally, they apply this tool to the CoNLL-2014 shared task submissions, providing the first-ever detailed, F-score-based analysis of system performance across a wide range of error types, revealing novel insights into the strengths and weaknesses of different GEC architectures.

**Main Contributions**
1.  **A Novel Rule-Based Error Classifier and Annotation Tool:** The primary contribution is the development of a transparent, deterministic, and dataset-independent method for classifying grammatical errors. Unlike machine learning approaches, it requires no training data and its decisions are fully interpretable. The high agreement from human experts validates its effectiveness. The authors' plan to release the tool represents a significant service to the GEC community, enabling standardized and detailed evaluation for future research.
2.  **In-depth Analysis of CoNLL-2014 Systems:** The paper provides a comprehensive and insightful analysis of the performance of the CoNLL-2014 shared task systems. This analysis, which was not possible before, breaks down performance by error operation (Missing, Unnecessary, Replacement), general error type, multi-token edits, and the distinction between error detection and correction. This empirical result is a substantial contribution in its own right, highlighting systemic strengths and weaknesses across different GEC approaches (e.g., MT-based vs. classifier-based systems).

**Strengths**
1.  **Addresses a Clear and Important Problem:** The GEC community has long been hampered by evaluation metrics that consolidate performance into a single score. This work directly tackles this issue by providing a means for fine-grained analysis. Understanding which error types a system struggles with is crucial for targeted improvement and for building effective hybrid systems. The utility of the proposed method is high and immediately apparent.
2.  **Methodologically Sound and Well-Justified Approach:** The decision to use a rule-based classifier is a key strength. It circumvents the need for labeled training data, which is often a bottleneck and can introduce dataset-specific biases. This makes the tool generalizable. Furthermore, the authors make a clever and crucial methodological choice to re-process the gold-standard reference data with their own pipeline. This ensures a fair, apples-to-apples comparison and they empirically show that this does not significantly alter the overall system rankings, thereby validating the approach.
3.  **Thorough and Illuminating Experimental Results:** The analysis presented in Section 4 is exemplary. It is not merely a proof-of-concept but a deep dive that yields valuable insights for the field. For example, the finding that five teams failed to correct any "Unnecessary" token errors is surprising and actionable. Similarly, the breakdown showing that different systems excel at different categories (e.g., CAMB's MT system on MT-friendly errors like determiners and prepositions, CUUI's classifier on orthography and verb forms) provides strong evidence for the potential of hybrid models. The analyses of multi-token edits and detection vs. correction further enrich the paper's contribution.
4.  **High-Quality Evaluation of the Proposed Classifier:** In the absence of a gold standard for their new error schema, the authors perform a manual evaluation with domain experts. Achieving a >95% "Good" or "Acceptable" rating from five independent researchers is a very strong result that builds significant confidence in the quality of the automatic annotations. The reported inter-annotator agreement is also solid.

**Weaknesses**
1.  **Dependence on Upstream NLP Tools:** The classifier's performance is fundamentally capped by the accuracy of the tools used for preprocessing (spaCy for POS tagging/parsing, NLTK for stemming). The authors rightly note that most "Bad" classifications in their manual evaluation were due to POS tagging errors. This dependency could be a significant issue when applying the tool to text from different domains (e.g., social media, legal text) where taggers are known to perform less well, or on sentences with highly ungrammatical structures that confuse parsers. The paper does not explore the robustness of the system to this kind of noise.
2.  **Limited Scope and Details of the Human Evaluation:** While the results of the human evaluation are strong, the scale is relatively small (200 edits). It is also unclear if the 5 "GEC researchers" were authors of the paper or otherwise affiliated with the project, which could introduce bias. Furthermore, the distribution of error types within this 200-item sample is not described. If the sample was dominated by frequent and easy-to-classify errors, the high performance might not fully represent the classifier's ability to handle more complex or rare error types.
3.  **Potential Brittleness and Lack of Adaptability:** A common critique of rule-based systems is their potential brittleness and the engineering effort required to extend them. The authors mention forgoing a "modal error" category for practical reasons. This raises the question of how easily the framework could be adapted or extended by other researchers. A short discussion on the modularity of the rule set and the process for adding a new rule or modifying an existing one would strengthen the paper's claims of practicality.

**Questions to Authors**
1.  Could you provide more detail on the human evaluation? Specifically, were the five raters independent of this work? Was the random sample of 200 edits stratified in any way to ensure it contained a representative distribution of error types, including less common ones?
2.  Your system's accuracy is closely tied to the performance of the upstream spaCy tagger/parser. Have you performed any analysis on how performance degrades on out-of-domain data or on sentences with a higher error density, where these tools are likely to make more mistakes?
3.  You mention that your framework is a "compromise between informativeness and practicality." Could you briefly elaborate on the process and expected difficulty of extending your rule-based system? For instance, what would be involved in adding the `R:MODAL` category you discussed, and how would you ensure it doesn't conflict with the existing `R:VERB:TENSE` category?
