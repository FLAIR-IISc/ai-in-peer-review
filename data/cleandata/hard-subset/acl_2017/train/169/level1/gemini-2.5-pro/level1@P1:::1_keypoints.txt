- Introduces a two-step method (linguistically-enhanced alignment + rule-based classifier) for automatic, fine-grained annotation of GEC system outputs.
- Contribution: provides a practical, publicly-released pipeline enabling detailed error-type precision/F-score evaluation across many error categories.
- Contribution: presents the first comprehensive, fine-grained error-type analysis of all CoNLL-2014 shared-task systems, revealing system-specific strengths and weaknesses.
- Strength: High practical impact—fills a long-standing gap in GEC evaluation and offers a valuable community tool for diagnostic analysis.
- Strength: Thorough experimental analysis uncovers actionable findings (e.g., weaknesses on multi-token edits, system-specific strengths like orthography or prepositions).
- Strength: Methodological choices are sound—builds on state-of-the-art edit extraction and uses a transparent, dataset-independent rule-based classifier validated by human ratings.
- Strength: Careful evaluation design—re-annotating the reference with the same tool ensures fair comparison and preserves overall system rankings.
- Weakness: Insufficient implementation detail on the rule-based classifier—no example rules, priority/hierarchy, or ambiguity-resolution strategy provided.
- Weakness: Classifier evaluation is limited and potentially biased—small sample (200 edits) and unclear independence of the 5 human raters.
- Weakness: Claim of "dataset independence" is overstated because performance depends on upstream NLP tools (spaCy, Hunspell) that vary by domain.
- Weakness: The proposed strict matching alternative to M2 may be overly punitive and could unfairly penalize valid corrections aligned or categorized differently.
- Question: Please provide detailed logic/decision process and priority ordering for key classifier rules and conflict resolution.
- Question: Clarify the relationship of the 5 GEC researcher raters to the authors/project and how annotators handled disagreements with automatic edit boundaries.
- Question: Have you compared the distribution of error types between manual reference annotations and your automatic reference annotations to check for systematic biases?
