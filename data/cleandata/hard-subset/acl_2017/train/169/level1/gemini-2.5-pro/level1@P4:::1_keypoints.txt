- Introduces a two-stage pipeline for automatic GEC error-type annotation: linguistically-enhanced alignment followed by a rule-based 25-way classifier.
- Tool is dataset-independent, uses automatic linguistic features (POS, lemmas), and will be publicly released.
- Validated classifier via manual expert evaluation with >95% predictions judged "Good" or "Acceptable" and κ=0.724 inter-annotator agreement.
- Enables full precision, recall, and F-score breakdowns by error type and is applied to re-evaluate CoNLL-2014 systems.
- Contribution: a complete methodology and tool for fine-grained automatic GEC evaluation addressing lack of per-error-type precision/recall.
- Contribution: comprehensive, novel analysis of CoNLL-2014 systems revealing system-specific strengths/weaknesses previously hidden by aggregate scores.
- Contribution: a transparent, reusable rule-based error classification framework that does not require annotated training data.
- Strength: high impact — fills a long-standing gap by allowing nuanced, diagnostic evaluation of GEC systems beyond recall-only reporting.
- Strength: sound methodology — builds on state-of-the-art edit extraction and re-processes references for consistent comparison, with experiments showing rankings are stable.
- Strength: insightful empirical findings (e.g., some systems miss "Unnecessary" token errors; MT systems handle multi-token edits better; classifier-based systems excel at VERB:FORM).
- Strength: thorough classifier validation with domain experts, high agreement with human judgments supporting credibility of results.
- Weakness: poor transparency — rules of the classifier are not described, making replication and bias assessment difficult.
- Weakness: unquantified dependency on upstream NLP tools (spaCy, NLTK), with no analysis of how tagging/parsing errors on learner data affect classifications.
- Weakness: potential bias from choice of Felice et al. (2016) aligner — its edit granularity influences evaluation and may favor systems with similar edit styles; comparison to M2 scorer could be more balanced.
- Question: please detail the classifier logic and decision process for ambiguous cases (e.g., R:MORPH vs R:NOUN/R:VERB vs R:VERB:FORM).
- Question: have you analyzed how spaCy/NLTK tagging/parser errors on learner English propagate to error-type assignments and which categories are most sensitive?
- Question: explain trade-offs of requiring exact match on edit span and correction string versus the more flexible M2 scorer, and whether this penalizes semantically equivalent lexical variants.
