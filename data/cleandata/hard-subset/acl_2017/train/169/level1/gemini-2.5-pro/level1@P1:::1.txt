**Summary of the Paper and Main Contributions**

This paper presents a novel method for the automatic annotation and fine-grained evaluation of Grammatical Error Correction (GEC) systems. The core problem addressed is that GEC system outputs are unannotated, making it impossible to calculate precision or F-scores for specific error types, and limiting analysis to recall only. The proposed solution is a two-step process: first, it uses a pre-existing linguistically-enhanced alignment algorithm to extract edits between original and corrected sentences. Second, it uses a new, dataset-independent, rule-based classifier to assign an error type to each extracted edit. The authors demonstrate the value of their tool by applying it to the system outputs of the CoNLL-2014 shared task, providing the first-ever comprehensive, error-type-level analysis of all participating systems. They will release the tool to the public.

The main contributions of this work are:

1.  **A practical tool and methodology for detailed, automatic GEC evaluation.** The paper provides a complete pipeline to move from unannotated system outputs to fine-grained performance scores across dozens of error types. The promised release of this tool represents a significant contribution to the GEC community, enabling more insightful diagnostics of system performance.
2.  **The first comprehensive, fine-grained analysis of the CoNLL-2014 shared task systems.** The application of the tool yields a rich analysis that uncovers specific strengths and weaknesses of different GEC architectures (e.g., MT-based vs. classifier-based), which were previously obscured by a single overall score. This analysis is valuable in its own right and serves as a powerful proof-of-concept for the proposed methodology.

**Strengths**

1.  **High Impact and Practical Utility:** This work addresses a critical and long-standing gap in GEC evaluation. The ability to automatically generate detailed error-type reports for any GEC system allows for much deeper system analysis, facilitating targeted improvements and better understanding of different GEC approaches. The release of the accompanying tool will be a highly valuable resource for the community, likely becoming a standard for GEC analysis.
2.  **Thorough and Insightful Experimental Analysis:** The analysis of the CoNLL-2014 systems is exemplary and provides significant value beyond simply demonstrating the tool. The authors uncover numerous interesting findings, such as the surprising inability of several systems to handle "Unnecessary" token errors, the complementary strengths of different systems on specific error types (e.g., CUUI on orthography, CAMB on prepositions), and the general weakness of all systems on multi-token edits. These insights are actionable for future GEC research.
3.  **Sound Methodological Choices:** The overall approach is well-reasoned. The authors build upon the state-of-the-art for edit extraction and develop a novel rule-based classifier for error typing. The choice of a rule-based system is well-justified by its transparency and independence from training data and specific annotation schemes. Furthermore, the human evaluation of the classifier, while small, shows very strong results (>95% "Good" or "Acceptable" ratings), validating the quality of the automatic annotations.
4.  **Careful Evaluation Methodology:** A crucial insight is the decision to re-annotate the gold-standard reference with the same automatic tool used for the system hypotheses. This ensures a fair and direct comparison. The authors rightly show that this re-annotation does not significantly alter the overall system rankings, which strongly supports the validity of their subsequent fine-grained analysis.

**Weaknesses**

1.  **Insufficient Detail on the Rule-Based Classifier:** The primary technical novelty of the paper is the rule-based error classifier, yet its implementation is described in very high-level terms. The paper does not provide examples of the rules, explain their hierarchy or priority, or describe how ambiguities are resolved (e.g., for an edit that could be classified as `R:MORPH` or `R:VERB:FORM`). This lack of detail makes the core component of the work difficult to scrutinize and reproduce from the paper alone, even if the tool will be released.
2.  **Limited and Potentially Biased Classifier Evaluation:** The human evaluation of the classifier, while showing positive results, is based on a small sample of 200 edits. Furthermore, the paper states the raters were "5 GEC researchers" but does not specify if they were independent of the project. If the raters were the authors or close colleagues, this could introduce bias. Finally, asking raters to judge an error category for an edit whose boundaries were also determined automatically can be difficult; a poor edit span could confound the judgment of the error type's appropriateness.
3.  **The Claim of "Dataset Independence" is Overstated:** The paper claims the classifier is "entirely dataset independent." However, its performance is fundamentally tied to the accuracy of upstream NLP tools (spaCy for POS-tagging/parsing, Hunspell for spelling). These tools are known to have domain-specific performance variations. The quality of the automatic annotation will therefore degrade on text that is out-of-domain for these prerequisite tools. This nuance is not discussed, and the claim of complete independence is thus too strong.
4.  **Potentially Unbalanced Criticism of the M2 Scorer:** The paper points out valid flaws in the M2 scorer, such as its tendency to merge false positives and maximize true positives. However, the proposed alternative—strict matching of automatically annotated spans—may be overly punitive. The flexibility of M2 was intended to handle legitimate annotation variations. The authors' method may penalize systems for producing valid corrections that happen to be aligned or categorized differently by their automatic process, an issue that is not fully explored.

**Questions to Authors**

1.  Could you provide more detail on the logic of the rule-based classifier? For example, could you describe the decision process for a few key error types or explain the priority order if an edit matches the criteria for multiple rules?
2.  Regarding the human evaluation of the classifier: Could you clarify the relationship of the 5 "GEC researchers" to the authors/project? Also, can you comment on how annotators were instructed to handle cases where they disagreed with the automatically generated edit boundaries when rating the appropriateness of the assigned error type?
3.  Table 4 shows that re-annotating the reference doesn't significantly change overall F-scores. Have you analyzed how the *distribution* of error types changes between the original manual annotation and your automatic annotation of the reference set? This would help understand if your tool introduces any systematic biases in classification compared to human annotators.
