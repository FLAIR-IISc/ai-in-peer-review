**Summary of the paper and main contributions**

This paper introduces a method for automatically annotating Grammatical Error Correction (GEC) system outputs with error types, enabling a more detailed evaluation than was previously possible. The authors present a two-stage pipeline: first, they use a pre-existing linguistically-enhanced alignment algorithm to extract edits between original and corrected sentences. Second, they introduce a novel, rule-based classifier to assign one of 25 error types to each extracted edit. This classifier is designed to be dataset-independent, relying only on automatic linguistic features like POS tags and lemmas. The authors validate their classifier through a manual expert evaluation, where over 95% of its predictions were rated as "Good" or "Acceptable". The primary application of this method is a detailed re-evaluation of the systems from the CoNLL-2014 shared task, providing the first full precision, recall, and F-score breakdown by error type for these systems. The tool developed for this process is to be publicly released.

The main contributions of this work are:
1.  A complete methodology and an accompanying tool for the fine-grained, automatic evaluation of GEC systems. This addresses a significant limitation in the field by enabling the calculation of precision, recall, and F-scores for specific error categories.
2.  A comprehensive and novel analysis of the CoNLL-2014 shared task systems. This analysis provides valuable insights into the specific strengths and weaknesses of different GEC architectures (e.g., MT-based, classifier-based) across a wide range of error types, which were previously obscured by aggregate scores.
3.  A dataset-independent, rule-based error classification framework. Its transparency and lack of dependence on annotated training data make it a portable and reusable resource for the GEC community.

**Strengths**

1.  **High Impact on GEC Evaluation:** The paper addresses a clear and long-standing gap in GEC evaluation. The standard practice of only reporting recall for error types provides an incomplete picture of system performance. By enabling the calculation of full precision/recall/F-score metrics, this work allows for a much more nuanced and diagnostically useful analysis of GEC systems. This can help researchers better understand system behavior and focus efforts on specific areas for improvement.

2.  **Sound and Rigorous Methodology:** The proposed pipeline is logical and well-reasoned. The choice to build upon a state-of-the-art edit extraction method is practical. Critically, the authors recognize the need for a consistent comparison by re-processing the gold-standard reference annotations using their own automatic pipeline. The experiment shown in Table 4, which demonstrates that this re-processing does not significantly alter the overall system rankings, provides strong evidence for the validity of this approach.

3.  **Insightful Empirical Analysis:** The application of the tool to the CoNLL-2014 shared task data is not merely a proof-of-concept but a substantial contribution in its own right. The paper presents a wealth of new information in Tables 5, 6, 7, and 8. The findings are insightful, such as identifying that several systems failed to correct any "Unnecessary" token errors, that MT-based systems were superior at handling multi-token edits, and that classifier-based approaches excelled at specific categories like `VERB:FORM`. These detailed results are of high value to the GEC research community.

4.  **Thorough Validation of the Classifier:** In the absence of a gold-standard dataset for their specific error scheme, the authors perform a manual evaluation of their rule-based classifier with five domain experts. The high agreement with human judgments (>95% "Good" or "Acceptable") and solid inter-annotator agreement (κ=0.724) lend significant credibility to the classifier's output, and by extension, to the validity of the overall evaluation framework.

**Weaknesses**

1.  **Lack of Transparency in the Rule-Based Classifier:** The paper describes the classifier as a key component of its contribution, but provides almost no detail about the rules themselves. It is stated that classification is based on POS tags, lemmas, and other automatic features, but the logic for distinguishing between related error types (e.g., `VERB:TENSE` vs. `VERB:FORM` vs. `VERB:SVA`) is not explained. This makes the classifier a "black box," which hinders the replicability of the work and makes it difficult for readers to assess its potential biases or limitations.

2.  **Unexamined Dependency on Upstream NLP Tools:** The entire annotation pipeline is contingent on the performance of external tools (spaCy, NLTK) for POS tagging, lemmatization, and parsing. It is well-known that such tools can suffer performance degradation on non-native (learner) text. The paper acknowledges that tagging errors can lead to incorrect classifications but does not quantify this impact or discuss how it might systematically affect the analysis. A more thorough discussion of the reliability of these prerequisite tools on learner data and the potential downstream effects on the final evaluation would strengthen the paper's claims.

3.  **Potential Bias from the Choice of Aligner:** The evaluation hinges on the initial edit extraction step, for which the Felice et al. (2016) aligner is used. While this aligner is reported to perform well, its specific characteristics (e.g., its heuristics for merging adjacent edits) will fundamentally shape the resulting analysis. For instance, its preference for creating a single multi-token edit like `[have eating -> has eaten]` versus two single-token edits will influence which systems are rewarded. While re-annotating the reference ensures consistency, it bakes the aligner's specific view of edits into the evaluation, potentially favoring systems that produce edits with similar granularity. The discussion comparing this approach to the M2 scorer could be more balanced, acknowledging that different alignment strategies represent different, potentially equally valid, evaluation philosophies.

**Questions to Authors**

1.  Could you provide more detail about the logic of the rule-based classifier? For instance, could you outline the decision process for a few potentially ambiguous cases, such as how the system distinguishes between a morphological error (`R:MORPH`), a part-of-speech error (`R:NOUN` vs. `R:VERB`), and a verb form error (`R:VERB:FORM`)?

2.  The reliability of the analysis depends on the accuracy of the automatic linguistic markup (POS tags, etc.) on learner English. Have you conducted any analysis on how errors from the spaCy tagger/parser propagate and affect the final error type classifications? Are certain error categories more sensitive to tagging errors than others?

3.  Your evaluation metric requires an exact match of both the edit span and the correction string. In contrast, the M2 scorer offers more flexibility. Could you elaborate on the trade-offs of this stricter matching criterion? For example, does your approach risk unduly penalizing systems that produce corrections that are semantically correct but lexically different from the reference (e.g., correcting "big error" to "large error" when the reference has "major error")?
