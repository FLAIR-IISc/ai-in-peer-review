- Paper addresses inability to compute precision/F-scores per GEC error type by proposing a two-stage automatic pipeline: linguistically-enhanced alignment + rule-based, dataset-independent error-type classifier.  
- Classifier uses linguistic features (POS, lemmas, etc.) and requires no labeled training data; experts rated >95% of its predictions "Good" or "Acceptable."  
- Authors re-annotate CoNLL-2014 system outputs and gold references to provide the first fine-grained comparative analysis across 25 error types.  

- Contribution: a novel automatic methodology and tool for annotating GEC outputs with error types, enabling interpretable, actionable evaluation beyond a single F-score.  
- Contribution: the first comprehensive, fine-grained error analysis of CoNLL-2014 systems, revealing system-specific strengths and weaknesses.  

- Strength: solves a critical, long-standing evaluation problem in GEC, enabling targeted error analysis and system improvement.  
- Strength: sound, transparent methodological choice—a rule-based, dataset-independent classifier that avoids labeled training data and is interpretable.  
- Strength: insightful empirical findings (e.g., MT systems handle multi-token edits better; CAMB/CUUI complementary strengths) with practical research implications.  
- Strength: high practical impact and reproducibility potential through planned tool release.  

- Weakness: final annotations depend heavily on upstream components (alignment tool and spaCy); propagation of upstream errors is not fully analyzed.  
- Weakness: classifier evaluation is limited (only 200 edits); claims of human-level quality and stable rankings may mask systematic biases.  
- Weakness: re-processing the human gold reference may discard valuable human judgments for complex edits; discrepancies are not analyzed.  

- Question: how sensitive is the error classifier to the choice/performance of underlying linguistic tools (e.g., swapping spaCy for another tagger/parser)?  
- Question: can you provide an error analysis of edits where automatic annotations diverge most from original human annotations, highlighting complex failure cases?  
- Question: can the framework be used to build automatic ensemble/routing systems that assign sentences to the best-performing GEC system per error profile?
