### Summary of the Paper
This paper addresses a significant limitation in the evaluation of Grammatical Error Correction (GEC) systems: the inability to compute precision and F-scores for specific error types, as system outputs are not annotated. The authors propose a two-stage, fully automatic pipeline to solve this. First, they use a linguistically-enhanced alignment algorithm to extract edit operations between original and corrected sentences. Second, they introduce a novel, rule-based classifier that assigns an error type to each extracted edit based on automatically derived linguistic features (POS tags, lemmas, etc.). The key advantage of this classifier is its dataset-independence, requiring no labeled training data. The authors validate their classifier via a manual study where experts rated over 95% of its predictions as "Good" or "Acceptable".

As a major application, the authors apply their tool to the system outputs of the CoNLL-2014 shared task. By re-annotating both the system hypotheses and the gold reference files with their tool to ensure consistency, they provide the first-ever detailed, comparative analysis of system performance across a fine-grained set of 25 error types, revealing specific strengths and weaknesses of different GEC approaches.

### Contributions
1.  **A novel, automatic methodology and accompanying tool for annotating GEC system outputs with error types.** This is the primary contribution. The combination of a linguistically-informed alignment and a dataset-independent, rule-based classifier provides a practical solution to a long-standing problem in GEC evaluation. This work moves the field beyond a single, opaque F-score towards a more interpretable and actionable evaluation paradigm.

2.  **The first comprehensive, fine-grained error analysis of the CoNLL-2014 GEC shared task systems.** This is a significant empirical contribution that demonstrates the utility of the proposed tool. The detailed breakdown of system performance provides valuable insights into the capabilities of different GEC architectures (e.g., MT-based vs. classifier-based), highlighting systemic weaknesses and complementary strengths that were previously obscured by aggregate scores.

### Strengths
1.  **Addresses a Critical and Unsolved Problem in GEC Evaluation:** For years, the community has relied on recall-only metrics for error types or a single overall F-score (M2). This has severely limited our understanding of GEC system behavior. This paper provides a well-motivated and effective solution, enabling researchers to finally ask questions like "Is my system good at correcting verb tense but bad at prepositions?" This level of detailed analysis is crucial for targeted system improvement, error analysis, and building effective hybrid or ensemble systems.

2.  **Sound and Well-Justified Methodological Choices:** The authors' choice of a rule-based, dataset-independent classifier is a major strength. Unlike machine learning approaches (e.g., Swanson & Yamangil, 2012), this method is not tied to a specific annotation scheme or training corpus, making it broadly applicable and comparable across different datasets. This approach increases transparency—one can always trace why a certain label was assigned—and avoids the need for annotated training data, which is a major bottleneck. The validation via expert human judgment, showing >95% "Good" or "Acceptable" ratings, lends strong credibility to the classifier's output.

3.  **Powerful and Insightful Empirical Analysis:** The application of the tool to the CoNLL-2014 shared task is far more than a simple proof-of-concept; it is a substantial piece of analysis in its own right. The paper uncovers numerous non-obvious findings: for example, that five teams failed to correct any "Unnecessary" token errors, that MT-based systems are superior at handling multi-token edits, and the complementary strengths of the CAMB (best at missing words) and CUUI (best at replacements) systems. These insights are directly useful for future GEC research.

4.  **Practical Impact and Reproducibility:** The authors state they will release their tool. This is a significant contribution that allows the entire community to adopt this more nuanced evaluation method. It enables other researchers to replicate the CoNLL-2014 analysis, evaluate new systems in the same framework, and potentially extend the tool itself. This makes the work not just an academic exercise but a practical asset for the field.

### Weaknesses
1.  **High Dependency on Upstream Tool Performance:** The quality of the final annotations is entirely dependent on the performance of the components in the pipeline: the alignment tool (Felice et al., 2016) and the linguistic feature extractor (spaCy for POS tagging, parsing, etc.). The paper notes that POS tagging errors can lead to misclassifications, but the extent of this problem is not fully explored. Given that the alignment tool itself has an F1 of 80-85%, a significant portion of edits may be incorrectly identified *before* the classification step even begins. An analysis of how these upstream errors propagate and affect the final distribution of error types would strengthen the paper's claims of reliability.

2.  **Limited Scale of Classifier Evaluation:** While the manual evaluation is a good start, it is conducted on only 200 edits. Claiming that the automatic annotations are "qualitatively as good as human annotations" based on this and the stability of overall system rankings (Table 4) is a strong claim. The stability in rankings could simply mean the automatic method introduces a consistent bias across all systems. A more in-depth evaluation, perhaps on a larger sample or a qualitative analysis of the 3.7% "Bad" cases, could provide a more nuanced understanding of the classifier's failure modes.

3.  **Potential Loss of Information when Reprocessing the Gold Reference:** The decision to re-process the manually annotated gold reference with the automatic tool is pragmatic and necessary for a fair comparison. However, this discards potentially valuable and correct human judgments, especially for complex or ambiguous edits that the automatic alignment might struggle with. The paper demonstrates this has no significant impact on the final *scores*, but it does not discuss what kind of information is lost. A brief analysis of the discrepancies between the original gold edits and the re-processed automatic edits would be very informative, highlighting the current limitations of the automatic approach.

### Questions to Authors
1.  Could you elaborate on the sensitivity of your error classifier to the performance of the underlying linguistic tools? For example, if you were to swap spaCy for another POS tagger/parser known to have different performance characteristics on learner text, how much would the resulting error type distributions for a given GEC system change?

2.  Regarding the decision to re-process the gold reference: Could you provide a brief error analysis of the edits where your automatic annotation process differs most significantly from the original human annotation? This would help clarify the specific types of complex edits (e.g., phrasal, non-contiguous) where the current method might be less reliable.

3.  Your analysis clearly shows that different systems excel at different error types (e.g., CAMB on Missing, CUUI on Replacement/Orthography). Does your framework open up possibilities for automatically constructing powerful ensemble systems? For example, could the output of your tool on a development set be used to train a router that sends different sentences to the most appropriate GEC system?
