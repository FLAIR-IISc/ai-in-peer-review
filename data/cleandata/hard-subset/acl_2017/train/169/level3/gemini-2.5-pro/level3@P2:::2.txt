### Summary and Contributions

This paper presents a novel, fully automatic pipeline for annotating and evaluating grammatical error correction (GEC) systems at the level of specific error types. The primary limitation of previous GEC evaluations was the inability to calculate precision for different error types, as system outputs are not annotated. This work addresses that gap by first extracting edits between original and corrected sentences using a linguistically-informed alignment algorithm, and then classifying these edits using a new rule-based, dataset-independent error typology. The authors validate their automatic error classification through a human evaluation study, where experts rated over 95% of the classifications as "Good" or "Acceptable". Finally, they apply their tool to re-evaluate the outputs of the CoNLL-2014 shared task, providing the first detailed analysis of system performance (precision, recall, and F-score) across a wide range of error types.

In my view, the main contributions of this work are:

1.  **A novel framework and open-source tool for fine-grained GEC evaluation.** The ability to automatically annotate system output enables the calculation of precision, recall, and F-scores for specific error categories. This provides a much more nuanced view of system performance than a single overall score and is a significant step forward for GEC evaluation methodology.

2.  **A comprehensive and insightful re-analysis of the CoNLL-2014 shared task systems.** By applying their framework, the authors uncover specific strengths and weaknesses of various GEC approaches that were previously obscured. For example, they show that some systems completely fail to handle "unnecessary" token errors, while others excel at specific categories like morphology or verb forms. This analysis is a valuable resource for the community and can guide future work in hybrid GEC systems.

### Strengths

1.  **Enables a more powerful and diagnostic form of GEC evaluation.** The core strength of this work is that it moves GEC evaluation beyond recall-only metrics for error types. By providing precision and F-scores for 25 distinct categories (Tables 5 and 6), the method allows researchers to diagnose system failures with high granularity. This is crucial for targeted system improvement, as it highlights whether a system is failing to detect certain errors (low recall) or is over-generating incorrect "fixes" (low precision).

2.  **The proposed method is dataset-independent and validated by human experts.** The rule-based classifier does not require manually annotated training data, making it flexible and applicable to any parallel corpus. This is a significant advantage over machine learning-based classifiers, which are often tied to specific annotation schemes and datasets. The validity of the approach is supported by a human evaluation where five experts found the automatic classifications to be highly appropriate (86.3% "Good", 10.0% "Acceptable"), demonstrating the robustness of the rule-based method despite noise from automatic alignment.

3.  **The detailed analysis of CoNLL-2014 provides concrete insights for the field.** The paper serves as an excellent demonstration of the tool's utility. The findings are compelling and actionable; for instance, the observation that classifier-based approaches (CUUI) and MT-based approaches (CAMB) have complementary strengths in correcting different error types (Table 7) provides strong evidence for pursuing hybrid models. The analyses of multi-token edits (Table 8) and detection vs. correction performance (Figure 1) further enrich our understanding of the capabilities of modern GEC systems.

### Weaknesses

1.  **The classification rules are not described, which makes the work impossible to reproduce or extend.** This is the most significant weakness of the paper. While the authors state that their classifier is rule-based and uses features like POS tags and lemmas, they provide absolutely no detail on what these rules are or how they were developed. For a scientific contribution to be valuable, its methodology must be transparent. While releasing the tool is commendable, the core logic should be described in the paper itself so that others can understand, verify, and build upon the method. Without this information, the classifier is a black box.

2.  **The evaluation of the classifier lacks depth and a clear analysis of failure cases.** The human evaluation is a good first step, but it is superficial. The authors cursorily attribute "Bad" ratings to POS tagging errors but provide no systematic analysis of why the classifier fails. For instance, it is unclear if the failures are concentrated in specific error types or stem from other upstream issues like lemmatization or parsing. Furthermore, since the human rating was done on automatically determined edit boundaries, it is difficult to disentangle alignment errors from classification errors. A controlled experiment where experts rate classifications on gold-standard boundaries would provide a clearer assessment of the classifier's accuracy.

3.  **The work is presented as English-only, with no discussion of its generalizability to other languages.** The entire pipeline relies on tools and linguistic resources specific to English (spaCy, Penn Treebank tags, Hunspell). The paper fails to discuss the potential challenges of adapting this framework to other languages, particularly those with richer morphology or different syntactic structures (e.g., handling discontinuous edits like separable verb prefixes in German). This omission limits the perceived scope and impact of the contribution.

### Questions to Authors

1.  **Reproducibility of Rules:** Could you please provide a description of the rules used by your classifier? For example, how does a rule differentiate between a `VERB:TENSE` error and a `VERB:SVA` error? Even a few illustrative examples would greatly improve the paper's transparency.

2.  **Error Typology:** How did you arrive at the 25 error categories in your framework? Do they align with or extend existing error taxonomies from the GEC or Second Language Acquisition (SLA) literature? A brief justification for the typology would be helpful.

3.  **Evaluation Rigor:** The comparison between gold and auto references in Section 4.1 concludes that they are qualitatively equivalent. Was the significance testing performed directly on the F0.5 scores? Given that F0.5 can be insensitive, did you also consider testing for significant differences in precision and recall separately?

4.  **Raw Counts:** Could you consider adding the raw TP, FP, and FN counts to tables like Table 6? This would allow readers to better assess the statistical significance of the results, especially for rare error types where performance might be based on only a handful of instances.
