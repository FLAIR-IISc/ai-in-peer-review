**Summary of the paper**
This paper presents a novel, two-stage method for automatically annotating the output of Grammatical Error Correction (GEC) systems with error types. The method first uses a linguistically-enhanced alignment algorithm to extract edits between original and corrected sentences. Then, a new rule-based, dataset-independent classifier assigns an error type to each extracted edit. A key benefit of this approach is that it enables, for the first time, the calculation of precision, recall, and F-score for GEC systems on a per-error-type basis, whereas previous evaluations were limited to recall. The authors validate their classifier via a human study where experts rated over 95% of the automatically assigned tags as "Good" or "Acceptable". They demonstrate the utility of their method by applying it to the CoNLL-2014 shared task outputs, providing a detailed and insightful analysis of the strengths and weaknesses of various GEC systems across 25 error categories.

**Contributions**
1.  **A novel and comprehensive evaluation methodology for GEC:** The primary contribution is a complete pipeline for evaluating GEC systems by error type, including precision, not just recall. This moves the field beyond monolithic F-scores and provides a much-needed tool for fine-grained system analysis.
2.  **A dataset-independent, rule-based error classifier:** The paper introduces a classifier that relies on automatic linguistic markup (POS tags, lemmas, etc.) rather than labeled training data. This makes the approach highly portable across different datasets without requiring retraining and ensures transparency in classification decisions.
3.  **An in-depth analysis of CoNLL-2014 systems:** The application of the proposed methodology to the CoNLL-2014 shared task provides a valuable and detailed breakdown of system performance. This analysis reveals complementary strengths of different GEC architectures (e.g., MT-based vs. classifier-based) and uncovers systemic weaknesses, offering concrete insights for future research.

**Strengths**
1.  **Significant Advance in GEC Evaluation:** The ability to measure both precision and recall for specific error types is a major step forward for the field. It allows researchers to move beyond a single F-score and diagnose system failures more precisely. For example, the analysis in Table 7 showing that CUUI’s classifier outperforms CAMB’s MT system on replacement determiner errors, despite having a lower overall score, is an insight that would be invisible with previous metrics. This finer-grained analysis is crucial for targeted system improvement.
2.  **Broad Applicability and Practicality:** The proposed method is shown to work across a dozen different systems from the CoNLL-2014 shared task, which employ a wide variety of techniques (MT, classifiers, rules, language models). Its independence from manually annotated training data for error classification makes it practical and easy to apply to new systems and datasets. The fact that it can handle multi-token edits is also a significant advantage over simpler alignment methods.
3.  **High-Quality Automatic Annotation:** The authors validate their rule-based classifier with a human evaluation study, which is essential given the absence of gold-standard labels. The result that human experts found 96.3% of the assigned error categories to be "Good" or "Acceptable" (Table 3) lends strong credibility to the core component of their methodology and supports the validity of the subsequent CoNLL-2014 analysis.

**Weaknesses**
1.  **Lack of Reproducibility and Transparency in the Method:** The paper's most critical flaw is the complete omission of the rules used by the error type classifier. The authors state that the classifier is based on "automatic POS tags" and other features, but provide no examples or descriptions of these rules. This makes the work impossible to replicate, verify, or build upon. Furthermore, it is unclear how the 25 error categories were devised or if they are based on prior work. The rules likely have a strong dependency on the upstream alignment and POS tagging tools, but the nature and sensitivity of this dependency are not discussed, threatening the robustness of the approach.
2.  **Limited Scope and Unaddressed Generalization:** The entire methodology is developed and tested only on English. The paper does not discuss the significant challenges of adapting this rule-based approach to other languages. For instance, languages with rich morphology or syntactic phenomena like separable verb prefixes in German (resulting in discontinuous edits) could pose serious problems for both the alignment and classification stages. The lack of discussion on this front significantly narrows the perceived impact and generality of the proposed framework.
3.  **Insufficient Analysis and Vague Experimental Details:** While the human evaluation of the classifier is a strength, the analysis of its failures is superficial. The "Bad" labels are cursorily attributed to POS tagging errors without a systematic analysis of other potential causes. The rating task itself may be biased, as raters judged labels on potentially noisy automatic boundaries; testing the classifier's output on manual boundaries would have helped isolate its performance. Additionally, several details of the main evaluation are unclear: the significance testing setup is not fully described (e.g., was the F0.5 score itself tested?), and the raw TP/FP/FN counts are not provided, preventing readers from assessing sample sizes or computing alternative metrics. Finally, the analysis of system performance (e.g., on multi-token errors) could be more deeply interpreted by considering the architectural limitations of the systems being evaluated.

**Questions to Authors**
1.  Could you please provide a description of the rules used by your classifier, or at least a representative subset for a few error categories (e.g., VERB:TENSE, DET, MORPH)? Without this information, the core contribution of the paper is not reproducible.
2.  Your human evaluation showed 3.7% of tags were rated "Bad," which you attribute mainly to POS tagging errors. Did you perform a more systematic error analysis? Have you considered evaluating the classifier's accuracy on gold-standard edit boundaries to disentangle classification quality from alignment quality?
3.  Could you discuss the main challenges and a potential roadmap for adapting your rule-based approach to a morphologically richer language or a language with more flexible word order than English? This would help clarify the generalizability of your method.
