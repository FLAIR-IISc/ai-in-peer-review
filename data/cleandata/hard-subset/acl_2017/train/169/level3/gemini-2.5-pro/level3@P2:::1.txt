### Summary
This paper presents a method to automatically annotate grammatical error correction (GEC) data with error types, which enables a more fine-grained evaluation of GEC systems beyond a single aggregate score. The proposed approach consists of two main steps: 1) using a linguistically-enhanced alignment algorithm to extract edits between original and corrected sentences, and 2) classifying these edits using a new, rule-based framework that relies on automatic linguistic markup (e.g., POS tags, lemmas). The authors demonstrate the utility of their tool by applying it to the system outputs of the CoNLL-2014 shared task, providing the first detailed, error-type-level performance analysis (Precision, Recall, F0.5) for all participating systems.

### Contributions
1.  **A Tool for Fine-Grained GEC Evaluation:** The primary contribution is the development and release of a tool that automatically annotates edits in GEC system output with specific error types. This tool fills a significant gap in GEC evaluation, allowing researchers to move beyond single performance scores and analyze system strengths and weaknesses across a detailed typology of grammatical errors.

2.  **A Comprehensive Error Analysis of CoNLL-2014 Systems:** The paper provides a valuable and detailed re-evaluation of the CoNLL-2014 shared task systems. This analysis uncovers novel insights, such as which architectural approaches (e.g., MT-based vs. classifier-based) excel at correcting particular error categories (e.g., determiners, verb forms) and highlights common weaknesses across all systems, such as the difficulty of handling multi-token edits.

### Strengths
1.  **Addresses a Clear Need in the GEC Community:** The work is well-motivated and addresses a long-standing limitation in GEC evaluation. The ability to automatically generate performance metrics for specific error types is highly valuable for diagnosing system failures, conducting informative comparisons between models, and guiding future research toward areas of greatest need. This type of analysis has the potential to accelerate progress in the field.

2.  **Strong Empirical Demonstration of Utility:** The paper's core claims are substantiated through a large-scale analysis of the CoNLL-2014 shared task. The results presented in Tables 5, 6, and 8 are insightful and provide a concrete demonstration of the tool's value. For example, the finding that five teams failed to correct any "Unnecessary" token errors, or that MT-based systems were superior at multi-token edits, offers actionable information for GEC practitioners.

3.  **Positive Human Evaluation of the Classifier:** The proposed rule-based error classifier was evaluated by five human experts who rated its output on 200 edits. The result that over 95% of the classifications were deemed "Good" or "Acceptable" by each rater provides strong evidence that the automatic annotation process is reliable and produces meaningful error labels, despite potential noise from the upstream edit extraction step.

### Weaknesses
1.  **Insufficient Detail on the Core Methodology:** The paper's main technical contribution is a rule-based classifier, yet its description is highly superficial. The authors state that the classifier is based on POS tags and other automatic features but provide no concrete examples of the rules, their complexity, their number, or how rule precedence is handled. This lack of detail makes the work difficult to reproduce, scrutinize, or build upon. The central component of the paper is effectively a black box.

2.  **Flawed Evaluation Methodology for the Classifier:** The manual evaluation of the error classifier is methodologically weak. Instead of creating an independent gold standard for the 200 test edits and measuring the classifier's accuracy against it, the authors asked raters to judge the *appropriateness* of the system's prediction. This approach is susceptible to confirmation bias. Furthermore, the aggregation of results in Table 3—averaging the percentage scores of individual raters—is unconventional and potentially misleading. A more standard report would show the distribution of ratings across all adjudicated items.

3.  **Unjustified Design Choices and Unsubstantiated Claims:**
    *   The paper introduces a new 25-category error tagset without justifying why existing, well-established schemes (such as the one used in the CoNLL-2014 task itself) were inadequate. A comparison or mapping to existing tagsets would have strengthened the work.
    *   The claim that the classifier is "entirely dataset independent" is overstated. The system relies on tools like spaCy and Hunspell, whose performance is known to vary across domains and text genres. The evaluation is also conducted exclusively on learner English essays, so its generalizability remains unproven.

4.  **Imbalanced Focus on Results over Method:** A disproportionate amount of space is dedicated to presenting the CoNLL-2014 results (e.g., the very large Table 6), with much of the accompanying text simply restating observations that are directly apparent from the table. This space would have been better utilized to provide the missing details about the rule-based classifier (Weakness 1) and to better justify the methodological choices (Weakness 3).

### Questions to Authors
1.  Could you please provide more detail on the rule-based classifier? For example, could you share a few representative rules for distinguishing between closely related error types (e.g., `VERB:TENSE` vs. `VERB:SVA` vs. `VERB:INFL`) and explain the logic for rule ordering or conflict resolution?

2.  What was the rationale for designing a new error tagset rather than using or extending an existing one, such as the official CoNLL-2014 tagset? How does your scheme relate to or improve upon prior taxonomies?

3.  Given that the classifier's performance is dependent on upstream tools (POS tagger, parser, dictionary) that can be domain-sensitive, could you elaborate on the claim that your approach is "entirely dataset independent"? Have you tested its robustness on out-of-domain text?

4.  In your evaluation, raters judged the system's output rather than creating an independent gold standard. While the agreement results are encouraging, this setup makes it difficult to assess the classifier's absolute accuracy. Do you have plans to create a manually annotated test set to compute standard metrics (precision/recall) for the error typing task itself?
