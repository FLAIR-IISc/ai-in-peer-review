**Summary of the Paper**
This paper presents a two-stage method for automatically annotating parallel original/corrected sentences with grammatical error types. The method first uses a linguistically-enhanced alignment algorithm to extract edits and then classifies these edits using a new, dataset-independent, rule-based classifier. The authors evaluate their classifier via a small-scale manual rating study, where they report that over 95% of the classifications were deemed "Good" or "Acceptable" by human experts. The main application of this work is a detailed, fine-grained analysis of the system outputs from the CoNLL-2014 shared task on grammatical error correction (GEC), providing the first comprehensive breakdown of system performance across 25 distinct error categories. The annotation tool is promised to be released publicly.

**Contributions of this work**
1.  **A Novel and Insightful Fine-Grained Analysis of GEC Systems:** The paper's most significant contribution is the detailed breakdown of the CoNLL-2014 systems' performance. This analysis moves beyond a single aggregate score to reveal specific strengths and weaknesses of different GEC architectures (e.g., MT-based vs. classifier-based) on particular error types (e.g., prepositions, verb tense, multi-token edits). This provides valuable insights for the GEC community.
2.  **A Publicly Available Tool for Automatic Error Annotation:** The release of the described tool is a practical contribution that enables other researchers to perform similar detailed evaluations. This could help standardize fine-grained analysis in the GEC field and facilitate more targeted system development.

**Strengths**
1.  **High Potential Impact and Utility:** The proposed method for automatic error-type annotation has significant practical value. For GEC researchers, it enables a much more nuanced diagnosis of system performance, highlighting specific areas for improvement. For the field of computer-assisted language learning (CALL), such a tool could be adapted to provide learners with more specific and actionable feedback on their writing.
2.  **Thorough and Illuminating Experimental Analysis:** The application of the tool to the CoNLL-2014 shared task data is comprehensive and well-executed. The authors analyze performance from multiple angles: by broad operation type (Missing, Replacement, Unnecessary), by specific linguistic error categories (Table 6), by edit size (multi-token edits), and by task (detection vs. correction). This multi-faceted analysis yields interesting and novel findings, such as the widespread difficulty systems have with multi-token edits and the complementary nature of different systems across error types.
3.  **Sound Approach to Ensuring Fair Evaluation:** The decision to reprocess the gold-standard reference annotations using the same automatic tool as the system hypotheses is a clever and important step. This ensures that the comparison is fair by removing the mismatch between manual and automatic annotation styles. The authors provide strong evidence in Table 4 that this re-processing does not significantly alter overall system rankings, which validates the methodology.

**Weaknesses**
1.  **Superficial Description of the Core Method:** The paper's primary technical contribution—the rule-based classifier—is described in insufficient detail, rendering the work irreproducible. The authors state that the classifier is based on rules over POS tags and other linguistic features but provide no concrete examples of these rules, their complexity, their precedence, or how conflicts are resolved. This lack of transparency makes it impossible to assess the novelty and sophistication of the classifier itself.
2.  **Flawed and Potentially Misleading Human Evaluation:** The evaluation of the classifier's accuracy is problematic. Rather than comparing the system's output against an independently created gold standard, the authors asked raters to directly judge the system's labels, which is susceptible to confirmation bias. The reporting of results is also questionable; the paper reports an overall score by averaging the percentage scores of individual raters, which is not a statistically sound way to aggregate categorical ratings. The raw distribution of ratings across all judgments should have been reported instead. Furthermore, no information is given about the composition of the 200-item test set, leaving the reader to wonder which of the 25 error types were actually covered in the evaluation.
3.  **Unsubstantiated Claim of "Dataset Independence":** The claim that the classifier is "entirely dataset independent" is overstated. The system relies on a stack of standard NLP tools (POS tagger, parser, lemmatizer, dictionary) which are themselves dependent on the language, domain, and annotation style of the corpora they were trained on. The rules were also manually developed, which likely introduces implicit biases based on the data the authors observed. True independence would require demonstrating robustness across diverse domains beyond English learner essays.
4.  **Lack of Justification for a New Error Tagset:** The paper introduces a new 25-type error schema without adequately justifying why existing, widely-used tagsets (such as the one from the CoNLL-2014 task itself) were insufficient. While the new schema appears well-structured, introducing another standard without a clear comparative motivation risks further fragmenting annotation practices in the field.

**Questions to Authors**
1.  Could you provide several concrete examples of the rules in your classifier? For example, what is the specific rule logic that distinguishes a `VERB:TENSE` error from a `VERB:SVA` error, and how is the application order of such rules determined?
2.  Regarding the human evaluation in Table 3, could you provide the raw distribution of "Good," "Acceptable," and "Bad" ratings across the total 1000 judgments (200 edits × 5 raters)? What was the distribution of error categories in the 200-edit evaluation set?
3.  You mention that automatically determined "edit boundaries might be unusual." Could you provide examples of such boundaries and discuss how these potentially incorrect spans might negatively impact the accuracy of the subsequent error classification stage?
4.  What was the rationale for developing a new error annotation schema instead of adapting an existing one, like the official CoNLL-2014 schema? A direct mapping would have allowed for a more direct analysis of how systems performed on the originally defined error types.

**Minor Comments**
-   There are several typos and formatting issues, e.g., "c.f." should be "cf.", "M2 scorer" should probably be "M² scorer", and the formatting of references is inconsistent (e.g., capitalization, author formatting).
