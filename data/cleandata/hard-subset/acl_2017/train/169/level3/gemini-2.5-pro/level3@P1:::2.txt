**Summary of the Paper**
This paper addresses a key limitation in the evaluation of Grammatical Error Correction (GEC) systems: the inability to measure performance for specific error types beyond recall. The authors propose a two-step, fully automatic method to overcome this. First, they use a linguistically-enhanced alignment algorithm to extract edits between original and corrected sentences. Second, they introduce a novel, rule-based classifier that assigns error types to these edits using automatically derived linguistic features (e.g., POS tags, lemmas), making it independent of any specific annotated dataset. The authors validate their classifier via a manual study where human experts rated over 95% of the automatically assigned types as "Good" or "Acceptable". As a demonstration of their method's utility, they apply it to the system outputs of the CoNLL-2014 shared task, providing the first comprehensive, fine-grained analysis of system performance (including precision, recall, and F-score) across a wide range of error types.

**Contributions**
1.  **A Novel GEC Evaluation Methodology:** The primary contribution is a complete pipeline for the fine-grained evaluation of GEC systems. By automatically annotating both system hypotheses and reference corrections in a consistent format, this method enables the calculation of precision, recall, and F-scores for 25 distinct error types. This provides a much deeper insight into system performance than was previously possible with metrics like the M2 Scorer, which only provided recall for error types. The detailed re-evaluation of the CoNLL-2014 systems is a strong demonstration of the value of this contribution.

2.  **A Dataset-Independent Error Type Classifier:** A significant secondary contribution is the rule-based error type classifier itself. Unlike machine learning models that require training on specific, manually annotated corpora, this classifier relies only on the output of standard NLP preprocessing tools (POS tagger, lemmatizer, etc.). This makes the approach highly portable and applicable to any parallel text without needing dedicated training data. The strong results from the human evaluation lend credibility to the quality of its output.

**Strengths**
1.  **Enables Deeper GEC System Analysis:** The paper's strongest argument for acceptance is that its method enables a far more insightful analysis of GEC system capabilities than existing metrics. The breakdown of CoNLL-2014 results in Tables 5 and 6 is a clear demonstration. It reveals system-specific strengths and weaknesses that are completely hidden by an overall F0.5 score. For example, the finding that five teams failed to correct any "Unnecessary" token errors, or that CUUI's classifier excelled at replacement determiner errors while CAMB's MT system was better overall, are valuable insights for the field that can guide future hybrid system development.

2.  **Strong Empirical Validation of the Classifier:** The authors provide solid evidence for the validity of their automatic error type classification. In a manual evaluation with five GEC researchers, the assigned error types were judged to be "Good" or "Acceptable" in at least 95% of cases by every rater. This is a crucial result, as it provides confidence that the subsequent detailed analysis of the CoNLL-2014 systems is based on meaningful and accurate annotations, not just noisy automatic labels.

3.  **Broad Applicability and Public Release:** The proposed method is shown to be applicable to a wide variety of GEC systems, including those based on machine translation, classifiers, rules, and hybrid approaches, as demonstrated by its application to all 12 distinct systems from the CoNLL-2014 shared task. The authors' commitment to releasing their tool will allow other researchers to easily replicate their analysis, apply it to new systems and datasets, and potentially build upon the framework.

**Weaknesses**
1.  **Lack of Detail on the Classification Rules:** The most significant weakness of this paper is the complete omission of the rules used by the error type classifier. The classifier is a central component of the contribution, yet its logic is treated as a black box. The paper mentions that rules are based on "automatic POS tags," lemmas, stems, and dependencies, but provides no examples or detailed description of the rule set. This severely hinders reproducibility. While the tool's release is welcome, a scientific paper must be self-contained and allow readers to understand and critically evaluate the method itself. Without the rules, this is impossible.

2.  **Superficial Analysis of Classifier Errors:** While the human evaluation provides high-level validation, it lacks a detailed error analysis. The paper cursorily attributes most "Bad" judgments (3.7% of the total) to POS tagging errors but provides no systematic breakdown. Which error types are most prone to misclassification? What are other sources of error? For instance, how are ambiguous cases like `[could -> should]` (which the authors note could be TENSE or MODAL) handled by the rules, and do these cases correlate with lower human ratings? A more thorough analysis of the classifier's failure modes is needed to understand its limitations.

3.  **Limited Scope and Unaddressed Generalizability:** The entire methodology is developed and tested only on English. The pipeline relies on a suite of English-specific tools and resources (spaCy's English models, Penn Treebank tags, Hunspell GB dictionary). The paper makes no attempt to discuss the challenges of adapting this framework to other languages, particularly those with rich morphology or different syntactic structures (e.g., separable verbs) that might challenge the underlying alignment and classification assumptions. This limits the perceived contribution to the GEC community as a whole.

**Questions to Authors**
1.  The paper's core contribution, the rule-based classifier, is not described. Could you provide a detailed description or at least a representative subset of the rules used to assign the 25 error categories? How do these rules interact with the output of the edit extraction stage? For instance, how does an error in alignment (e.g., incorrect span) propagate to the classification stage?

2.  Could you elaborate on the development of the 25 error categories? Are they based on or mapped from existing GEC annotation schemes (e.g., the CoNLL-2014 scheme), or were they developed from scratch?

3.  The human evaluation asked raters to judge the error category irrespective of the automatically determined edit boundary. This could potentially bias their ratings, as an awkward boundary might make any category seem less appropriate. Have you considered evaluating the classifier's performance on edits with gold-standard boundaries to isolate its effectiveness from noise introduced by the alignment step?

4.  The current method appears to assume contiguous edits. How would the proposed framework handle discontinuous edits, such as separable verb prefixes in German (e.g., "er ruft an" -> "er anruft" is a single verb error)? Does the underlying alignment algorithm support this?

5.  Regarding Table 4, could you clarify the setup for the bootstrap significance test? Was the test performed on the F0.5 scores directly? Given that F0.5 can be less discriminative than other measures, have you considered other tests? The claim that "auto annotations are qualitatively as good as human annotations" seems to be based on the lack of a significant difference in final scores, but this primarily validates the alignment. How do you validate that the automatic error *type classification* is qualitatively as good as the manual classification used in the original CoNLL-2014 reference?
