**Summary of the paper:**
This paper introduces a novel, rule-based method to automatically annotate the outputs of Grammatical Error Correction (GEC) systems with error types. By applying these annotations to both system hypotheses and a re-processed reference, the authors enable a fine-grained evaluation that, for the first time, measures precision, recall, and F-score for specific error categories. The method is demonstrated through a detailed re-evaluation of all systems from the CoNLL-2014 shared task.

**Main Contributions:**
1.  The primary contribution is a new evaluation framework that allows for measuring both precision and recall by error type for GEC systems. This moves beyond the limitations of existing recall-only metrics and provides a much richer analysis of system behavior.
2.  A secondary contribution is the comprehensive error analysis of the CoNLL-2014 systems, which yields new insights into the comparative strengths of different GEC architectures and serves as a valuable resource for the community.

**Strengths:**
The proposed method addresses a significant gap in GEC evaluation. By enabling the calculation of precision for error types, it allows researchers to distinguish between systems that are cautious and accurate versus those that are aggressive and noisy, a distinction lost in recall-only analyses. The approach is laudably dataset-independent, relying on standard NLP toolkit outputs rather than manually annotated training data, which ensures broad applicability. The authors validate their automatic labels through a human study, where experts found over 95% of the classifications to be "Good" or "Acceptable," lending strong credibility to the framework's output.

**Weaknesses:**
Despite its promise, the paper suffers from several major weaknesses. The most critical is the complete omission of the classification rules. Without these details, the core method is a black box, rendering the work entirely irreproducible. It is also unclear how the 25 error categories were designed or if they align with established annotation schemes.

Secondly, the evaluation is limited to English, and the paper fails to discuss how this language-dependent, rule-based system could be adapted to other languages, particularly those with features like discontinuous edits that the current alignment approach may not handle.

Finally, the analysis lacks depth. The discussion of "Bad" ratings from human experts is superficial, failing to provide a systematic breakdown of error sources beyond blaming the POS tagger. Furthermore, the paper does not report raw TP/FP/FN counts, making it impossible to assess the statistical significance of results for rare error types, and fails to analyze whether system performance on certain categories (e.g., Unnecessary tokens) stems from architectural limitations rather than simple correction failures.

While the approach is promising, the paper is not ready for publication. For the work to be a meaningful contribution, the rules must be described in full to ensure reproducibility, and the challenge of generalizing to other languages must be addressed.
