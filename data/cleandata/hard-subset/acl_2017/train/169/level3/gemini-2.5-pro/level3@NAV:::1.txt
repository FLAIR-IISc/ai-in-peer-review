**Summary of the paper**

This paper presents a rule-based method for automatically annotating edits in parallel original/corrected sentences with error types. The primary motivation is to enable a fine-grained evaluation of Grammatical Error Correction (GEC) systems beyond recall-only metrics for specific error types. The authors develop a new error tagset and a classifier that uses linguistic features (POS tags, lemmas, etc.) to assign these tags. They evaluate their classifier via human rating and then apply it to re-evaluate the system outputs from the CoNLL-2014 shared task, providing a detailed breakdown of performance by error type for each participating system.

**Contributions**

1.  The key contribution is an applied methodology for fine-grained comparative analysis of GEC systems. By annotating system outputs, the work provides a valuable lens for understanding the specific strengths and weaknesses of different GEC architectures (e.g., MT-based vs. classifier-based) on categories like determiner errors or verb tense.
2.  The paper provides a detailed and extensive analysis of the CoNLL-2014 systems, which serves as a useful case study and resource for the GEC community.

**Strengths**

1.  **High Utility for the GEC Community:** The paper’s main strength lies in its practical application. The ability to perform a detailed, multi-faceted comparison of GEC systems is a significant step forward from relying on a single aggregate score. This can help researchers diagnose model deficiencies and guide the development of hybrid systems that combine the strengths of different approaches.
2.  **Enables Deeper Analysis:** The proposed tool facilitates novel analyses, such as comparing system performance on detection versus correction or evaluating performance on multi-token edits. This provides insights that are otherwise obscured by standard evaluation metrics.

**Weaknesses**

1.  **Lack of Methodological Detail and Reproducibility:** The paper's core technical contribution—the rule-based classifier—is described in a highly superficial manner. The rule set is a black box; the paper omits crucial details such as rule examples, the number of rules, their complexity, their ordering, and how conflicts are resolved. This makes the work difficult to scrutinize, reproduce, or build upon.
2.  **Insufficient Justification and Unclear Motivation:** The overall motivation is muddled. It is unclear whether the main contribution is the new system, the new tagset, or the evaluation. The authors design a new error tagset without justifying why existing, well-established tagsets (e.g., Nicholls, CoNLL-2014) were insufficient.
3.  **Flawed Classifier Evaluation:** The evaluation of the error classifier is weak. Using raters to judge system output is not a substitute for creating an independent gold standard for the 200 test sentences. Furthermore, the aggregation of rater scores by averaging individual percentages is misleading, and the paper fails to report the distribution of “Bad” ratings across error types, which could reveal systematic failures.
4.  **Unsubstantiated Claims:** The claim that the method is "dataset-independent" is unsubstantiated. The system relies on tools like POS taggers, parsers, and Hunspell dictionaries, which are known to be domain- and language-dependent. The evaluation is performed exclusively on student essays, which does not support a broad claim of domain independence.
5.  **Poor Presentation:** The paper overemphasizes the CoNLL-2014 results with an excessively large table (Table 6) and then uses significant text space to repeat facts that are already visible in it. This space could have been better used to describe the core methodology. The paper also suffers from minor but frequent typos and formatting errors (e.g., "c.f.", M2).
