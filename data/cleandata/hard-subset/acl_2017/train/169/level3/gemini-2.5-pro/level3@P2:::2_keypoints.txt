- Presents a novel, fully automatic pipeline that extracts edits with a linguistically-informed aligner and classifies them using a rule-based, dataset-independent error typology.  
- Validates automatic classifications via human evaluation (≈96% rated Good/Acceptable) and applies the tool to re-analyze CoNLL-2014 outputs with per-error-type precision, recall, and F-scores.  
- Contribution: open-source framework enabling fine-grained GEC evaluation (precision/recall/F1) across error categories.  
- Contribution: comprehensive re-analysis of CoNLL-2014 revealing system-specific strengths/weaknesses and guiding hybrid GEC approaches.  
- Strength: Enables diagnostic evaluation with 25 error categories, revealing whether systems fail by low recall or low precision.  
- Strength: Rule-based classifier is dataset-independent and validated by experts (86.3% Good, 10.0% Acceptable), avoiding need for annotated training data.  
- Strength: Detailed analyses (system comparisons, multi-token edits, detection vs. correction) provide actionable insights for the field.  
- Weakness: Classification rules are not described in the paper, making the method irreproducible and effectively a black box despite released code.  
- Weakness: Human evaluation lacks depth and systematic failure analysis; alignment vs. classification errors are not disentangled and gold-boundary tests are missing.  
- Weakness: English-only implementation with no discussion of generalizability to other languages or language-specific challenges.  
- Q1: Request for description/examples of classification rules (e.g., how VERB:TENSE vs VERB:SVA are distinguished).  
- Q2: Ask how the 25 error categories were chosen and whether they align with existing GEC/SLA taxonomies.  
- Q3: Request clarification on statistical testing — whether significance was tested on F0.5 and whether precision/recall were tested separately.  
- Q4: Request adding raw TP/FP/FN counts (e.g., in Table 6) to assess statistical significance, especially for rare error types.
