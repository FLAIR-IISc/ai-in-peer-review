### **Summary**

This paper presents a method for automatically annotating the output of Grammatical Error Correction (GEC) systems with error types. The approach first uses a linguistically-enhanced alignment algorithm to extract edits between original and corrected sentences. It then classifies these edits using a new, rule-based classifier that relies on automatic linguistic markup (POS tags, lemmas, etc.). The authors evaluate the classifier's output via a small-scale human rating study, where they report high "Good" or "Acceptable" ratings. Finally, they apply their tool to the system outputs from the CoNLL-2014 shared task, providing the first detailed, per-error-type analysis of system performance, including breakdowns by operation type, error category, edit length, and detection vs. correction.

### **Contributions**

1.  **A Tool and Methodology for Fine-Grained GEC Evaluation:** The primary contribution is a method that enables a detailed, error-type-level analysis of GEC system outputs. By automatically annotating system hypotheses, this work moves beyond single, monolithic performance scores (like M^2), allowing researchers to identify the specific strengths and weaknesses of different GEC approaches. This is a valuable contribution to GEC diagnostics and error analysis.

2.  **A Detailed Comparative Analysis of CoNLL-2014 Systems:** The paper provides the first comprehensive breakdown of performance across a wide range of error types for all systems participating in the CoNLL-2014 shared task. This analysis itself serves as a useful resource, highlighting which architectural choices (e.g., MT-based, classifier-based) are better suited for correcting specific types of errors (e.g., determiners, verb forms, spelling).

### **Strengths**

1.  **High Potential Impact for GEC Research and Pedagogy:** The ability to perform automatic, fine-grained error analysis is a significant step forward for the GEC field. It allows system developers to diagnose performance bottlenecks and potentially build hybrid systems that leverage the complementary strengths of different models (as suggested in the analysis of CAMB and CUUI systems). Beyond research, such a tool could be invaluable for language teachers and learners by providing specific feedback on recurring error patterns.

2.  **Enables Novel and Insightful Analyses:** The paper successfully demonstrates the utility of its approach by conducting several analyses that are not possible with standard GEC metrics. The breakdowns by operation type (Table 5), multi-token edits (Table 8), and detection vs. correction (Figure 1) provide a much richer picture of system behavior than an overall F-score. For instance, the finding that many systems struggle with multi-token edits is an important insight for future research focused on fluency.

### **Weaknesses**

1.  **The Core Method is Underspecified and Not Reproducible:** The paper's primary technical contribution—the rule-based error classifier—is treated as a black box. The description in Section 3 is highly superficial, stating that rules are based on POS tags and other automatic markup but providing zero examples of the rules themselves. Critical details are missing: How many rules are there? What is their complexity? In what order are they applied? How are conflicts resolved? Without this information, the work is not reproducible, and the classifier cannot be properly evaluated or understood by the reader.

2.  **Evaluation of the Classifier is Flawed and Potentially Misleading:** The manual evaluation in Section 3.3 has several methodological issues.
    *   **Lack of Gold Standard:** The authors opted for human rating of the system's output rather than creating an independent, gold-standard annotation for the 200 test edits. This makes the evaluation subjective and less rigorous.
    *   **Misleading Aggregation:** The "OVERALL" scores in Table 3 are an average of the five raters' individual percentage scores. This is a non-standard way to report agreement and can mask significant disagreement. A more transparent report would show the distribution of ratings per item (e.g., what percentage of items received at least one "Bad" rating?). The claim that ">95% of ... types [are] either 'Good' or 'Acceptable'" is based on each rater's individual performance, not on collective agreement, which is a much weaker claim.
    *   **Lack of Test Data Details:** The paper provides no information about the composition of the 200 edits used for evaluation. It is unknown how many of the 25 error categories were represented or their distribution. If the set was dominated by easy-to-classify errors (e.g., spelling), the high agreement scores would not be representative of the classifier's general performance.

3.  **Lack of Motivation for Key Design Choices:** The paper fails to justify several central decisions. Most notably, it introduces a new 25-type error schema without explaining why existing, widely-used tagsets (e.g., Nicholls, the CoNLL-2014 tagset) were insufficient. Using an established tagset, or mapping to one, would have made the results more comparable to previous work. This, combined with the lack of detail about the system, makes the overall motivation unclear: is the main contribution the system, the tagset, or the evaluation? The paper attempts all three but provides insufficient depth for the first two.

4.  **Presentation Obscures Insight and Contains Unsubstantiated Claims:**
    *   The extensive CoNLL-2014 results (Table 6) occupy a large portion of the paper, and the accompanying text often just reiterates observations directly visible in the table rather than providing deeper analysis or synthesis. This space would have been better used to describe the classification method.
    *   The claim that the rule-based approach has lower domain dependence is unsubstantiated. The rules and the resources they depend on (e.g., POS taggers, Hunspell dictionaries) can be highly sensitive to domain and language variety. The claim is not tested, as all experiments are on student essays.
    *   The paper contains several minor but distracting presentation issues, including typos ("c.f." instead of "cf."), inconsistent formatting ("M2" vs. M²), and poorly formatted references.

### **Questions to Authors**

1.  Could you please provide concrete examples of the rules used in your classifier? To ensure reproducibility, it would be helpful to understand their structure, quantity, and the logic for their application (e.g., ordering).

2.  Regarding the rater evaluation in Table 3, how were the "OVERALL" percentages calculated? Can you provide the distribution of ratings across items? For example, what percentage of the 200 items received at least one "Bad" rating from any of the five raters?

3.  What was the distribution of error types in the 200-edit set used for the manual evaluation? How does this distribution compare to that of the full CoNLL-2014 test set, and how might this affect the interpretation of the high agreement scores?

4.  What was the rationale for developing a new error tagset instead of using or extending an existing one, such as the official CoNLL-2014 tagset? Using a standard tagset would seem to facilitate more direct comparison and evaluation.
