- Introduces a two-stage automatic annotation method (linguistically-enhanced edit extraction + rule-based error-type classification using POS/lemmas) validated by human evaluation and applied to CoNLL-2014.

- Contribution: enables measurement of GEC performance by error type including precision (not just recall).

- Contribution: method does not require manual error annotations and can handle multi-token errors.

- Contribution: demonstrates broad applicability by evaluating multiple GEC systems on a major shared task.

- Strength: substantial methodological advance allowing targeted diagnosis of system strengths and weaknesses by error type.

- Strength: human validation (5 raters, 200 samples) shows high reliability (95%+ “Good” or “Acceptable”).

- Strength: practical utility shown via meaningful system-level insights on CoNLL-2014 outputs.

- Weakness: lacks reproducibility because the specific rule set for error-type classification is not provided.

- Weakness: incomplete classifier evaluation—no systematic analysis or examples of “Bad” labels and their causes.

- Weakness: evaluated only on English with no discussion of generalization to languages with different morphology or discontinuous edits.

- Weakness: insufficient statistical reporting (no raw TP/FP/TN/FN counts), obscuring significance and cases with very few instances.

- Weakness: no justification or provenance for the chosen error-type taxonomy and its relation to prior frameworks.

- Q to authors: please provide the exact classification rules with examples and edge-case handling.

- Q to authors: how does the method handle discontinuous edit spans (e.g., separable verb prefixes in other languages)?

- Q to authors: provide concrete evidence/examples supporting the claim that the M2 scorer overestimates performance.

- Q to authors: why did five teams fail to correct any unnecessary-token errors—system design issue or evaluation artifact?

- Recommendation: promising but not ready for acceptance—requires full rule disclosure and discussion of cross-linguistic generalization to be a strong contribution.
