

# Review for ACL 2017

## Summary and Main Contributions
This paper addresses a critical gap in Grammatical Error Correction (GEC) evaluation by introducing a method to automatically annotate error types in system outputs. Prior work could only measure GEC performance in terms of recall because system outputs lacked error type annotations. The authors present a two-step approach: (1) linguistically-enhanced alignment to extract edits between original and corrected sentences, and (2) a rule-based error classification framework that's dataset-independent and relies solely on automatically obtained linguistic properties (POS tags, lemmas, etc.). The main contributions, ordered by strength, are:

1. A novel method for automatically annotating error types in GEC outputs, enabling detailed error type analysis and precision-based evaluation (not just recall).
2. A dataset-independent rule-based error classification framework that doesn't require labeled training data and is fully deterministic.
3. The first comprehensive error type analysis of multiple GEC systems across the CoNLL-2014 shared task, revealing nuanced system strengths and weaknesses.

## Strengths

1. **Fundamental advancement in GEC evaluation**: This work solves a core problem in the field by enabling precision-based evaluation of error types. Previous metrics could only report recall (how many errors were corrected), but this paper allows researchers to determine which specific error types systems are good or bad at correcting. The authors demonstrate this by conducting the first detailed error type analysis for all teams in the CoNLL-2014 shared task, revealing that different systems have complementary strengths (e.g., CAMB excelled at Missing errors while CUUI was better at Replacement errors).

2. **Strong validation of automatic error typing**: The authors conducted a small-scale human evaluation where 5 GEC experts rated the appropriateness of predicted error types. All raters considered at least 95% of the predictions as "Good" or "Acceptable," with the highest agreement (86.3%) for "Good" labels. This validation is crucial for establishing credibility, especially given the noise introduced by automatic edit extraction.

3. **Practical utility and immediate applicability**: The authors released their tool with the paper, making it immediately useful for the GEC community. Their analysis demonstrates how different approaches to GEC complement different error types, providing valuable insights for future system design. The paper also shows that their automatic annotations produce results comparable to gold-standard references, with no statistically significant difference (p > .05) between auto and gold references.

## Weaknesses

1. **Lack of implementation details for reproducibility**: The paper fails to provide the actual rules used for error classification, which is essential for reproducibility. Without this, readers cannot replicate or extend the work. The authors claim their approach is "dataset-independent" and "rule-based," but the absence of the rule set severely limits the paper's scientific contribution.

2. **Limited language scope and generalizability**: The evaluation is restricted to English, and the paper doesn't address how the approach would generalize to other languages. Given that GEC is inherently a multilingual task, this limitation is significant. The authors should have discussed how their method might handle discontinuous edits (e.g., separable verb prefixes in German) or morphological differences in other languages.

3. **Insufficient statistical reporting**: The paper doesn't provide raw counts (TP/FP/TN/FN) for the error type analysis (Table 6), making it difficult to assess the reliability of results for categories with few instances (e.g., CONJ, PRON). Some error types have very low counts (e.g., CONJ has 0.00 precision for several teams), but the paper doesn't clarify whether these results are statistically meaningful.

## Questions to Authors

1. Could you provide the complete rule set for your error classification framework? Without this, it's impossible to replicate or extend your work.

2. How would your approach handle discontinuous edits (e.g., separable verb phrases in German), which are common in some languages? Have you tested this on any non-English data?

3. Could you provide the raw counts (TP/FP/TN/FN) for your error type analysis? This would allow readers to assess the reliability of results for categories with few instances.

## Additional Comments
The paper's claim that their approach "bypasses bias effects altogether" is somewhat overstated, as the human evaluation could still be influenced by noisy edit boundaries. The authors should acknowledge this limitation. Additionally, the paper doesn't address how some GEC systems can already produce error-tagged outputs via submodules, which slightly diminishes the novelty of their contribution. The reference formatting also contains inconsistencies that should be addressed before publication.
