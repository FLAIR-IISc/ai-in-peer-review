- Summary: Proposes a dataset-independent rule-based framework that labels GEC system edits with error types using POS/lemmas, validated by humans and applied to CoNLL-2014 for comprehensive error-type analysis.

- Strength: Enables precision-based error-type analysis previously impossible, revealing detailed system strengths and weaknesses.

- Strength: Rule-based classifier is dataset-independent and uses only automatically obtained linguistic information, requiring no retraining or manual annotation.

- Strength: Human evaluation reports 95% of predicted error types as "Good" or "Acceptable", supporting annotation quality despite noisy edit extraction.

- Strength: Analysis of CoNLL-2014 reveals complementary system behaviors (e.g., MT excels at noun errors; classifiers handle verb form errors).

- Weakness: Paper lacks sufficient detail on the rule-based classification rules, hindering reproducibility and adoption.

- Weakness: No systematic analysis of "Bad" human labels or investigation into underlying causes beyond POS errors.

- Weakness: Evaluation is English-only with no evidence or discussion of cross-lingual applicability or morphological variation.

- Weakness: Raw counts (TP/FP/TN/FN) for error-type analysis are missing, preventing assessment of statistical significance and reliability for low-count types.

- Weakness: No discussion of handling discontinuous edit spans (e.g., separable verb prefixes), limiting applicability to non-English languages.

- Question: Provide the complete set of rules used for error-type classification to enable reproducibility.

- Question: What evidence supports the method's applicability to languages with different morphological structures than English?

- Question: Can you provide raw TP/FP/TN/FN counts for error types with low F0.5 scores to assess reliability?

- Question: How does the approach handle discontinuous edits like separable verb constructions in German or Dutch?
