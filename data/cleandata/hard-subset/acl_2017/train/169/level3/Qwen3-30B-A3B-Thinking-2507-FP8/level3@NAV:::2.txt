

This paper proposes a novel method for automatically evaluating Grammatical Error Correction (GEC) systems by error type, including precision metrics rather than just recall. The authors introduce a linguistically-enhanced alignment approach to extract edits between original and corrected sentences, followed by a rule-based classifier that categorizes errors without requiring manual annotation. The method demonstrates broad applicability across multiple GEC systems and achieves high human agreement (95% "Good" or "Acceptable" ratings) on automatically assigned error types.

The primary contribution is the development of a dataset-independent evaluation framework that enables detailed error type analysis for GEC systems. The secondary contribution is the demonstration of this framework's utility through a comprehensive analysis of CoNLL-2014 systems, revealing nuanced system strengths and weaknesses across 24 error categories.

Strengths include the method's ability to handle multi-token errors without manual annotation, its independence from labeled training data, and the high human agreement with automatically assigned error types. The evaluation across multiple systems demonstrates the approach's practical utility for GEC research.

However, several critical weaknesses prevent acceptance. The rules for error type derivation remain unspecified, making replication impossible. The error categories lack justification regarding their relationship to prior work or linguistic validity. The evaluation is limited to English without addressing adaptation to other languages, despite the method's claimed generalizability. The classifier evaluation lacks detailed error analysis and fails to address why certain error types (e.g., "Bad" ratings) occur. The paper does not report raw TP/FP/TN/FN counts for statistical analysis, and the significance testing setup is unclear. Additionally, the paper fails to distinguish between system design limitations and evaluation failures, particularly regarding multi-token errors and discontinuous edit spans.

The authors acknowledge these issues in their response, but the paper requires substantial revisions to address reproducibility concerns and language generalization before publication. While promising, the current work is not ready for acceptance due to these fundamental methodological gaps.
