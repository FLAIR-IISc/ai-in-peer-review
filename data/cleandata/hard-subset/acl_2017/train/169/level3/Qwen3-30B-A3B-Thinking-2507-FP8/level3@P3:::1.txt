

# Review of "Automatic Annotation and Evaluation of Error Types for Grammatical Error Correction" (ACL 2017)

## Summary
This paper proposes a method to automatically annotate error types in Grammatical Error Correction (GEC) systems by first extracting edits between original and corrected sentences using linguistically-enhanced alignment, then classifying these edits with a rule-based framework. The authors claim this enables fine-grained analysis of system performance across error types, which was previously impossible due to the lack of error-type annotations in system output. They apply their method to CoNLL-2014 shared task data to conduct the first detailed error-type analysis of GEC systems.

## Main Contributions
The paper claims three main contributions: (1) a method to automatically annotate error types in GEC systems, (2) a dataset-independent rule-based classifier for error typing, and (3) a detailed error-type analysis of CoNLL-2014 systems. However, the primary contribution appears to be the practical utility of enabling fine-grained error analysis for GEC systems, rather than the specific rule-based classifier or the CoNLL-2014 analysis.

## Strengths
1. **Practical Utility for GEC Evaluation**: The paper's most significant contribution is enabling fine-grained error-type analysis of GEC systems, which provides valuable insights for both researchers and educators. This addresses a critical gap in GEC evaluation where overall scores conceal system strengths and weaknesses across different error types, making it highly useful for teachers and learners to understand system capabilities.

2. **Dataset Independence**: The rule-based classifier's reliance solely on automatically obtained linguistic features (POS tags, lemmas, etc.) makes it inherently dataset-independent, avoiding the compatibility issues that plague machine learning approaches that require different classifiers for different datasets. This is a practical advantage for standardizing GEC evaluation.

3. **Validation of Approach**: The human evaluation showing 95% of error types were rated "Good" or "Acceptable" by experts provides reasonable validation for the approach, demonstrating that the automatic error typing is sufficiently reliable for practical use.

## Weaknesses
1. **Superficial System Description**: The core rule-based classifier is described with minimal detail. The paper lacks concrete examples of how rules are applied, the complexity of the rule set, how rules are ordered or prioritized, and the counts of rules per error type. This makes it impossible to assess the robustness or completeness of the system.

2. **Flawed Human Evaluation Methodology**: The evaluation of the classifier used raters to judge predicted error types rather than creating an independent gold standard. The aggregation of rater scores by averaging percentages is misleading, and the distribution of "bad" ratings (which were 3.7% overall) is not reported. The paper claims raters were told to ignore unusual edit boundaries, but this undermines the evaluation's validity.

3. **Unclear Motivation and Justification**: The paper fails to clearly articulate its primary contribution (new system, new tagset, or evaluation method). The design of the new error tagset is particularly problematic—there's no justification for why existing tagsets (like Nicholls or CoNLL-2014) couldn't be used, and the paper doesn't explain how this new tagset improves upon them.

4. **Overemphasis on CoNLL-2014 Results**: The paper devotes significant space to CoNLL-2014 results, including large tables that repeat facts visible in the tables themselves. The test data composition (error category distribution) is not reported, making it impossible to assess the representativeness of the analysis.

## Questions to Authors
1. Why was a new error tagset designed instead of adapting an existing one (e.g., CoNLL-2014 or Nicholls)? What specific limitations of existing tagsets necessitated a new framework?

2. The paper claims lower domain dependence, but manual rules and Hunspell vocabularies can be domain-dependent. What evidence supports the claim of domain independence when the evaluation was limited to student essays?

3. The paper states "edit boundaries might be unusual" but provides no precise definition or examples of what constitutes an unusual boundary. How does this affect error classification, and what is the impact on the analysis?

4. The rule-based classifier is described as "dataset-independent" but the evaluation was limited to only two datasets (FCE-test and CoNLL-2014). How would the classifier perform on other GEC datasets with different error distributions or linguistic characteristics?

The paper presents a potentially useful approach for GEC evaluation, but its weaknesses significantly undermine its contribution. The superficial description of the core method, flawed evaluation methodology, and unclear motivation prevent it from making a strong case for its novelty or practical utility beyond the specific CoNLL-2014 analysis. The paper would benefit from a clearer articulation of its contribution, more thorough description of the rule-based system, and a more robust evaluation methodology.
