

# Review for ACL 2017

## Summary and Main Contributions

This paper addresses a significant gap in Grammatical Error Correction (GEC) evaluation by introducing a method to automatically annotate system outputs with error type information. The main contributions are:

1. A novel rule-based error typing framework that operates without labeled training data, making it dataset-independent and transparent.
2. A method to automatically annotate GEC system outputs with error types, enabling detailed error type analysis previously impossible due to lack of annotated system outputs.
3. A comprehensive error type analysis of all systems in the CoNLL-2014 shared task, revealing system-specific strengths and weaknesses across 25 error categories.

## Strengths

1. **Solves a critical problem in GEC evaluation**: The paper addresses a genuine limitation in the field - the inability to perform detailed error type analysis of GEC system outputs without manual annotation. This is a significant contribution as it enables researchers to understand not just overall system performance, but where specific systems excel or fail. The authors successfully demonstrate this value by providing the first detailed error type analysis of CoNLL-2014 systems.

2. **Transparent and dataset-independent approach**: The rule-based classifier offers advantages over machine learning approaches that require labeled data for each dataset. The authors' claim that their method is "analogous to automating the annotation guidelines" is compelling, as it provides a consistent, interpretable error typing framework. The validation showing 95% of predicted error types were rated "Good" or "Acceptable" by human experts provides strong evidence of the method's effectiveness.

3. **Actionable insights for the GEC community**: The detailed analysis reveals that different systems have complementary strengths (e.g., CAMB excelled at missing errors while CUUI performed better at replacement determiner errors). This insight could guide future system development by suggesting how to combine approaches to address specific error types, as noted in the paper's conclusion.

## Weaknesses

1. **Superficial description of the rule set**: The core contribution - the rule-based classifier - is described in a highly superficial manner. The paper lacks concrete examples of the rules, their counts, complexity, and ordering details. Without this information, it's impossible to assess the rule set's sophistication or potential limitations, making it difficult to replicate or build upon the work.

2. **Flawed classifier evaluation methodology**: The paper evaluates the classifier by having experts rate the predicted error types in context, but this approach has critical flaws. The evaluation was conducted on system output (not on a gold-standard dataset), and the authors didn't create an independent gold standard for the 200 test sentences. The aggregation of rater scores by simply averaging percentages is misleading, as it obscures the distribution of "bad" ratings (which were reported as 2-4% per rater but not aggregated).

3. **Unsubstantiated claim of dataset independence**: The paper claims their approach is "dataset independent" and "does not require labelled training data," but this is unsubstantiated. The authors use Hunspell dictionaries and manual rules that can be domain- and language-dependent. The test corpora are limited to student essays, so the claim of general applicability is questionable without broader testing.

## Questions to Authors

1. Could you provide specific examples of the rules used in your classifier, including their complexity, ordering, and how they handle edge cases? This would help assess the practicality of your approach.

2. Given that your classifier evaluation was conducted on system output rather than creating an independent gold standard, how do you address the potential bias in the evaluation (e.g., if the system output already had errors in the edit boundaries)?

3. The paper states that "all 5 raters individually considered at least 95% of our rule-based error types to be either 'Good' or 'Acceptable'." Could you provide the full distribution of ratings (not just the aggregate) to help assess the consistency of the raters?

4. The paper claims the error typing framework is "dataset independent," but your test data only includes student essays. How would your approach perform on other text types or domains?
