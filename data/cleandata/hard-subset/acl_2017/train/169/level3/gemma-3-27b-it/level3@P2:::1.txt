This paper presents a method for automatic annotation of grammatical errors in corrected text, aiming to enable more fine-grained evaluation of Grammatical Error Correction (GEC) systems. The core idea is to leverage linguistically-enhanced alignment to extract edits between original and corrected sentences, and then classify these edits using a rule-based system. The authors demonstrate this approach by re-evaluating the systems participating in the CoNLL-2014 shared task.

The primary contribution of this work lies in providing a pipeline for automatically annotating GEC system outputs with error type information, overcoming a significant limitation in previous evaluations. This has the potential to be a valuable resource for both researchers and educators, enabling detailed analysis of system performance and providing insights for targeted improvement. A secondary, though less substantial, contribution is the new error type tagset proposed by the authors, designed to be dataset-independent. Finally, the detailed re-evaluation of the CoNLL-2014 systems using this new pipeline offers a compelling, if somewhat repetitive, illustration of the approach’s capabilities.

**Strengths:**

1. **Practical Utility:** The developed tool addresses a genuine need in the GEC community. The ability to automatically annotate system outputs with error types significantly enhances the possibilities for detailed analysis and comparison of different approaches. This is particularly useful for identifying specific weaknesses of individual systems and guiding future research.
2. **Clear Motivation and Problem Definition:** The paper clearly articulates the problem of lacking error type annotations for GEC systems and provides a well-defined motivation for developing a solution. The limitations of relying solely on recall scores are effectively highlighted.
3. **Human Evaluation Validation:** The manual evaluation with GEC researchers, demonstrating >95% agreement with the automatically assigned error types, provides a reasonable degree of confidence in the accuracy and reliability of the rule-based classifier. This lends credibility to the approach despite the lack of a traditional gold standard dataset.
4. **Detailed CoNLL-2014 Re-evaluation:** The extensive analysis of the CoNLL-2014 systems, presented in multiple tables, provides a comprehensive view of system performance across different error types. This detailed breakdown offers valuable insights into the strengths and weaknesses of various GEC approaches.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Superficial System Description:** The description of the core rule-based classification system is surprisingly shallow. The paper lacks concrete details about the rules themselves – no examples are given, the number of rules is not stated, and their complexity and ordering are not discussed. This makes it difficult to assess the ingenuity or robustness of the approach.
2. **Overemphasis on CoNLL-2014 Results:** A significant portion of the paper is dedicated to presenting and reiterating results from the CoNLL-2014 re-evaluation. While this demonstrates the application of the method, the discussion feels repetitive and often simply restates observations directly apparent from the tables. 
3. **Evaluation Concerns:** The evaluation methodology, relying on human ratings of system outputs rather than constructing a dedicated gold standard for a test set, raises concerns. The aggregation of rater scores by averaging individual percentages is potentially misleading and obscures the distribution of "bad" ratings. Reporting this distribution would provide a more nuanced understanding of the classifier's performance. Furthermore, the paper fails to provide details regarding the test data composition – the number of error categories represented and the coverage of different error types are not addressed.
4. **Vague Statement Regarding Edit Boundaries:** The authors acknowledge that the automatically extracted edit boundaries "might be unusual" but provide no specific examples or discussion of the potential impact this has on the evaluation. This lack of clarity undermines confidence in the fairness of the comparison between systems.
5. **Limited Domain Independence Claim:** The claim of dataset independence is unsubstantiated. The reliance on rule-based systems and external resources like Hunspell dictionaries inherently introduces domain and language dependencies. The limited scope of the test corpora (primarily student essays) further restricts the generalizability of the approach.
6. **Unclear Motivation & Contribution:** The overarching contribution of this paper remains somewhat unclear. Is it the system itself, the novel error tagset, or the evaluation methodology? The lack of a clear focus hinders the assessment of the work’s overall impact.

**Questions to Authors:**

1.  Could you provide a more detailed description of the rule-based classifier, including examples of the rules, the total number of rules, and the logic for their ordering?
2.  What is the distribution of "bad" ratings received during the human evaluation? Could you provide a breakdown of disagreements between raters?
3.  What is the composition of the test data used for evaluation? Specifically, what proportion of examples cover each error category in your tagset?
4.  Can you elaborate on the nature of “unusual” edit boundaries identified by the automatic alignment process and discuss the potential impact of these boundaries on the evaluation results?
5.  Why was a new error tagset developed instead of adopting or extending existing tagsets like those used in CoNLL-2014?

**Additional Comments:**

The paper suffers from several minor formatting issues, including inconsistent citation styles (e.g., "c.f." vs. "cf.") and typos. A thorough proofread is recommended. The use of "M^2" instead of "M2" is also noted.
