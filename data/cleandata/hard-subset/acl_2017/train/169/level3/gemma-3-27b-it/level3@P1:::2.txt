This paper introduces a novel method for automatically annotating grammatical errors in system output for Grammatical Error Correction (GEC), enabling detailed error type performance analysis – previously limited to recall. The core contribution lies in a linguistically-enhanced alignment algorithm combined with a dataset-independent, rule-based classifier. This allows for the generation of error type annotations without relying on manually annotated data, a significant advantage. Further contributing to the value of the work is the application of this method to analyze the CoNLL-2014 shared task systems, providing the first comprehensive error type breakdown of these systems. The high degree of agreement between automatically predicted error types and human expert ratings (over 95% rated “Good” or “Acceptable”) validates the efficacy of the approach.

The strengths of this submission are considerable. First, the ability to move beyond simple recall-based evaluation to precision and F-score-based evaluation *by error type* is a substantial advancement for GEC research. This granular analysis offers crucial insights into system strengths and weaknesses, guiding future development. Second, the independence from manually annotated data drastically lowers the barrier to evaluating GEC systems, as annotation is a costly and time-consuming process. This is particularly valuable for rapidly prototyping and comparing new approaches. Finally, the comprehensive evaluation of the CoNLL-2014 systems demonstrates the practical applicability and scalability of the proposed method. The detailed analysis presented, uncovering differences in performance across teams and error types, provides a valuable resource for the GEC community. The fact that the tool will be released with the paper further enhances the contribution, promoting reproducibility and wider adoption.

Despite these strengths, the submission has notable weaknesses. The most significant is the lack of detailed description of the rules used by the error type classifier. This opaqueness hinders reproducibility and makes it difficult to assess the validity and generalizability of the approach. Without understanding the precise criteria used to assign error tags, it’s impossible to determine if the classifier is making sensible decisions or relying on spurious correlations. A second weakness lies in the limited error analysis of the classifier's performance. The evaluation relies heavily on aggregate ratings and doesn't provide a systematic breakdown of *why* the classifier makes mistakes, particularly for the “Bad” ratings, beyond a cursory mention of POS tagging errors.  Finally, the evaluation is exclusively conducted on English, raising concerns about the cross-linguistic applicability of the method. The rules are likely English-specific, and the authors do not discuss potential adaptations for other languages with different grammatical structures.

Several questions remain for the authors to address. First, a clear and exhaustive description of the rule-based classifier is essential. Please provide the complete set of rules used for error type assignment. Second, a more detailed analysis of classifier errors is needed. What specific types of errors consistently lead to “Bad” ratings? Are there systematic patterns in the mistakes made by the classifier? Third, could the authors discuss the potential challenges of adapting this approach to languages other than English? What modifications would be necessary to handle different grammatical structures and linguistic features? Fourth, how sensitive are the results to the choice of linguistic tools (e.g., spaCy)? A brief ablation study would be helpful to understand the impact of different components.

Additional comments include concerns regarding the significance testing setup and the limited comparison between gold and auto references. The paper should also acknowledge existing systems with error-tagging capabilities and address the novelty in relation to these. Finally, the reference formatting and completeness of citation details should be checked. The paper presents a promising approach, but addressing these weaknesses and clarifying the outlined points is crucial before publication.
