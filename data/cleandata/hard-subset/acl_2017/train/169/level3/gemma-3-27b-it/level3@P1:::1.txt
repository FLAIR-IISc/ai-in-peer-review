This paper presents a method for automatic annotation of grammatical errors in corrected text, aiming to enable more detailed evaluation of Grammatical Error Correction (GEC) systems. The core idea is to use linguistically-enhanced alignment to extract edits between original and corrected sentences and then classify these edits using a new, rule-based error typing framework. The authors demonstrate the application of this framework to the CoNLL-2014 shared task data, providing a detailed analysis of system error type performance—something previously hindered by the lack of annotated system outputs. The primary contribution is the creation of a pipeline that allows for automatic, dataset-independent error type annotation, and the subsequent application of this pipeline to a large GEC dataset. A secondary contribution lies in the detailed error analysis of the CoNLL-2014 systems, revealing differences in performance across various error types. 

**Strengths:**

1. **Practical Utility & Novel Application:** The ability to automatically annotate system outputs with error types is highly valuable. This has direct implications for both GEC research and educational applications, allowing for a more nuanced understanding of system strengths and weaknesses, and providing teachers and learners with specific feedback. This automated evaluation approach is a significant step beyond simple recall-based metrics.
2. **Dataset-Independent Approach:**  The rule-based classifier, designed to be independent of specific training corpora, is a commendable goal.  Addressing the issue of dataset bias in error type evaluation is a critical problem, and this approach attempts to mitigate that challenge. The reliance on automatic markup avoids the need for costly and potentially inconsistent human annotation for each new dataset.
3. **Detailed CoNLL-2014 Analysis:** The application of the system to the CoNLL-2014 shared task data offers a substantial analysis, revealing differences between participating systems.  The detailed breakdown by error type provides concrete insights into the performance of different approaches, going beyond overall F0.5 scores. This level of granular analysis was previously unavailable and provides a valuable resource for the GEC community. 

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Insufficient System Description:** The description of the core rule-based classifier is remarkably superficial. The paper lacks crucial details about the rules themselves – examples, the number of rules, their complexity, the ordering of rule application, and the handling of conflicts. This makes it difficult to assess the robustness and generalizability of the approach.  Without this information, the contribution feels incomplete.
2. **Overemphasis on CoNLL-2014 Results:** The paper dedicates a significant portion of its space to presenting the CoNLL-2014 results in tabular form. Much of the information presented in these tables is directly apparent upon inspection and does not require extensive textual explanation. The paper would benefit from a more focused discussion of key findings and their implications, rather than simply reiterating the table contents.
3. **Questionable Evaluation Methodology:** The evaluation relies on human raters assessing the appropriateness of predicted error types *after* the system has processed the data. This introduces a potential bias as raters are evaluating the output of the automatic pipeline, not a truly independent gold standard.  The lack of an independent gold standard for the 200 test sentences compromises the validity of the “95% Good/Acceptable” claim. Furthermore, the paper lacks a report of the distribution of "Bad" ratings; knowing how many edits were incorrectly categorized would provide a more complete picture of performance.
4. **Limited Data Characterization:** There’s a lack of information regarding the composition of the test data. The paper doesn’t specify the distribution of error categories within the data or indicate whether all error types are adequately represented. This is important because performance on rarer error types may be less reliable and could skew overall results.
5. **Vague Claims & Unsubstantiated Arguments:** The claim that “edit boundaries might be unusual” lacks precision and supporting evidence. Examples of these “unusual” boundaries and a discussion of their potential impact on the evaluation are necessary. The claim of lower domain dependence is also unsubstantiated. Rule-based systems and vocabularies like Hunspell are inherently domain- and language-dependent, and the test corpora consist of student essays, limiting the generalizability of the findings.
6. **Unclear Motivation & Contributions:** The paper’s overall motivation is unclear. Is the main contribution a novel GEC system, a new error tagset, or an evaluation framework? Important details are omitted, leading to confusion about the paper's core message.

**Questions to Authors:**

1.  Could you provide a more detailed description of the rule-based classifier, including examples of the rules, their complexity, and how they are ordered and resolved in cases of conflict?
2.  What is the distribution of “Bad” ratings from the human evaluation?  Knowing how many edits were misclassified will help assess the robustness of the system.
3.  What is the distribution of error types within the CoNLL-2014 dataset used for evaluation?
4.  Can you provide examples of the “unusual” edit boundaries identified by the automatic alignment process, and discuss their potential impact on the evaluation results?
5.  Why was a new error tagset developed instead of utilizing existing, established tagsets like those used in the CoNLL-2014 shared task or the work of Nicholls? What specific limitations of these existing tagsets motivated the creation of a new one?

**Additional Comments:**

The paper contains several typos and inconsistencies in reference formatting (e.g., "c.f." should be "cf.", M2 should be represented as M^2).  Careful proofreading and consistent formatting of references are needed.
