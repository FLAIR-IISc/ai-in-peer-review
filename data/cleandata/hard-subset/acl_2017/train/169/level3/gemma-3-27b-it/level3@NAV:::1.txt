## Review of "Automatic Annotation and Evaluation of Error Types for Grammatical Error Correction"

This paper tackles the challenging problem of evaluating Grammatical Error Correction (GEC) systems at a granular, error-type level. The authors propose a method to automatically annotate system outputs, enabling detailed analysis previously hampered by the lack of annotated GEC system outputs. The primary contribution lies in this automated annotation pipeline, which combines linguistically-enhanced alignment with a novel rule-based error type classifier. The subsequent application of this pipeline to the CoNLL-2014 shared task data provides a valuable, detailed analysis of system performance across various error categories, representing a secondary contribution.

The strength of this work lies in its practical utility. Providing teachers and learners with fine-grained comparisons of GEC systems – identifying strengths and weaknesses in addressing specific error types – is genuinely valuable. The created tool, promised for release, has the potential to be widely adopted within the GEC research community. Furthermore, the authors highlight inconsistencies in existing evaluation metrics (like M2), prompting a necessary discussion on evaluation methodology.

However, the paper suffers from several weaknesses. The description of the core rule-based classifier is superficial. Crucially, the paper lacks concrete examples of the rules, statistical counts relating to rule application, a discussion of the rule order, or an explanation of how conflicts are resolved. This makes it difficult to assess the robustness and generalizability of the approach. The paper overemphasizes the CoNLL-2014 results, presenting a large table with repetitive observations readily apparent from the data itself. A more concise presentation and insightful interpretation are needed. 

A significant methodological concern is the evaluation. Relying on human raters to judge system outputs *without* creating an independent gold standard for the 200 test sentences introduces subjectivity and potential bias. The aggregation of rater scores via simple averaging is also misleading; the distribution of "bad" ratings is not reported, obscuring potential areas of disagreement. Furthermore, the lack of information regarding the test data’s composition – the number of error categories represented and their distribution – limits the interpretability of the results. The claim that “edit boundaries might be unusual” needs specific examples and a discussion of the impact on evaluation accuracy. The authors' claim of lower domain dependence is unsubstantiated given the reliance on manual rules and Hunspell vocabularies, and the limitation of test data to student essays. 

Finally, the motivation feels unclear. Is the main contribution the system, the tagset, or the evaluation methodology? The paper lacks a clear framing statement articulating the key novelty and significance of the work. Numerous typos and formatting errors in the references further detract from the professionalism of the submission.



**Questions to Authors:**

1.  Could you provide at least 5-10 concrete examples of the rules used in the error-type classifier, including how they are applied and resolved in cases of conflict?
2.  What is the distribution of the different error types within the test set (CoNLL-2014 output)? 
3.  Please provide a detailed breakdown of the "bad" ratings received from the human raters – which error types were most frequently flagged as incorrect, and what was the nature of the disagreement?
4.  What steps were taken to address potential bias introduced by the raters in the evaluation process?
5.  Can you elaborate on the limitations of your approach in handling errors outside of the student essay domain?
