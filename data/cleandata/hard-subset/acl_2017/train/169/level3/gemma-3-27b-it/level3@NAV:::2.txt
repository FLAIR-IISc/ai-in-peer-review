## Review of "Automatic Annotation and Evaluation of Error Types for Grammatical Error Correction"

This paper presents a method for automatically annotating grammatical errors in text and evaluating GEC systems by error type, moving beyond simple recall-based metrics. The core idea – leveraging linguistically-enhanced alignment and a rule-based classifier – is promising, and the authors demonstrate its application to the CoNLL-2014 shared task data, providing a novel and detailed analysis of system performance. The authors’ successful validation of the automatic annotations (over 95% agreement with experts) is a key strength, showcasing the potential for large-scale, automated GEC evaluation. The release of the associated tool is also a valuable contribution.

However, the paper suffers from several significant weaknesses hindering its impact and reproducibility. The most critical flaw is the lack of a detailed description of the rules used to derive the error types. Without this information, replicating the results and applying the method to new datasets is impossible. Furthermore, the evaluation of the classifier feels superficial; while human ratings are encouraging, a more detailed error analysis (e.g., confusion matrix) is needed to identify specific weaknesses and directions for improvement. 

The study's limited scope – focusing solely on English – is also a concern. The authors do not address the challenges of adapting this approach to morphologically richer languages or languages with different grammatical structures. The derivation of error categories is also unexplained; it’s unclear whether these categories are established in prior work or are ad-hoc. The potential for the method to struggle with discontinuous edits is not addressed. Finally, the significance testing setup is unclear and the use of F0.5 as a discriminatory measure feels weak.

**Strengths:**

*   **Novel Evaluation Method:** Provides a granular, precision-focused approach to GEC evaluation, going beyond recall.
*   **Dataset Independence:** The rule-based classifier doesn't rely on labelled training data.
*   **Broad Applicability:** Demonstrated successfully on multiple CoNLL-2014 systems.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Reproducibility:** Undefined rules for deriving error types prevent replication.
*   **Classifier Evaluation:** Lacks detailed error analysis and concrete improvement directions.
*   **Language Specificity:** Evaluation limited to English; no discussion of generalization.



**Questions to Authors:**

1.  Please provide a comprehensive description of the rules used to classify errors. This is crucial for reproducibility.
2.  Can you provide a confusion matrix or other detailed error analysis for the classifier?
3.  How would you anticipate the proposed method performing on languages with discontinuous morphology (e.g., German)?
4.  How do the error categories relate to existing taxonomies of grammatical errors?
5.  What were the specific parameters used for the significance tests? Was F0.5 the only metric tested?



**Additional Comments:**

*   Reference formatting inconsistencies exist.
*   Missing page numbers/URLs in some citations.
*   The paper should acknowledge existing GEC systems with built-in error tagging capabilities.
