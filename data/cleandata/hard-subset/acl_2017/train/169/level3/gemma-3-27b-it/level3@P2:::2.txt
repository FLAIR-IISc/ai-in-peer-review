## Review for ACL 2017 - Automatic Annotation and Evaluation of Error Types for Grammatical Error Correction

This paper presents a novel method for automatically annotating and evaluating grammatical error correction (GEC) systems by error type. The key contribution is a pipeline enabling precision-based error type evaluation – previously limited to recall – using linguistically-enhanced alignment and a new, dataset-independent rule-based classifier. A second significant contribution is the application of this method to analyze the CoNLL-2014 shared task submissions, providing detailed error type performance insights. Finally, the authors contribute a publicly released tool implementing this annotation and evaluation framework.

**Strengths:**

The most compelling aspect of this work is the ability to move beyond recall-based evaluation of GEC systems and measure precision at the error type level. This addresses a critical gap in the field, allowing for more nuanced analysis of system strengths and weaknesses. The authors convincingly argue that understanding *what* types of errors a system corrects well (or poorly) is crucial for driving future development. The large-scale application to the CoNLL-2014 data further strengthens this contribution, offering a valuable resource for the GEC community. 

The dataset-independent nature of the rule-based classifier is another substantial strength. By relying solely on automatic markup (POS tags, lemmas, etc.), the approach avoids the biases inherent in classifiers trained on specific, potentially inconsistently annotated, corpora. This promotes generalizability and reduces the effort required to apply the method to new datasets.

The high level of agreement between the automatic error type assignments and human raters (over 95% rated as "Good" or "Acceptable") lends credibility to the approach. This suggests that the rules effectively capture the essential characteristics of different error types, even given the noise introduced by automatic edit extraction. 

Finally, the public release of the tool facilitates reproducibility and encourages further research in this area.



**Weaknesses:**

Despite the potential of this work, several weaknesses hinder its overall impact. The most significant is the lack of detailed explanation regarding the rules used for error type classification. While the paper states the framework is rule-based, the actual rules are not explicitly outlined, making replication difficult and hindering a thorough understanding of the method’s limitations. A clear, comprehensive description of these rules is essential. 

The evaluation of the classifier itself is limited. While the human evaluation provides positive feedback, it lacks a detailed error analysis. Specifically, the paper does not explore *why* the classifier occasionally makes “Bad” predictions beyond a cursory mention of POS tagging errors. A more systematic investigation into these failures would reveal areas for improvement. 

The scope of the evaluation is currently limited to English. The paper does not address the challenges of adapting the approach to other languages with different linguistic structures or POS tagsets. Addressing this limitation, even through a discussion of potential hurdles and necessary modifications, would increase the work’s broader relevance.

**Questions to Authors:**

1.  Could you provide a detailed description of the rules used for assigning error types? Specifically, how are the rules structured and how do they handle different linguistic contexts?
2.  Beyond POS tagging errors, what other factors contribute to the classifier making “Bad” predictions, according to the human raters? Can you provide examples?
3.  What challenges would need to be addressed to adapt this approach to languages other than English, and what modifications would be necessary?
4.  The significance test you report seems to focus on whether the auto-reference is as good as the gold. Did you test statistical significance of the F0.5 scores themselves across different systems? If so, can you report these?

**Additional Comments:**

*   The paper states a comparison is made between gold and auto references "despite claims otherwise," but the nature of that comparison is unclear. Clarify whether the comparison extends beyond edit boundaries and encompasses error *type* classifications.
*   Consider including raw TP/FP/TN/FN counts for Table 6 to provide a more complete picture of system performance and assess the statistical significance of the observed differences.
*   Acknowledge existing GEC systems that incorporate error tagging as a submodule. This provides context for the novelty of the proposed approach.
*   Review reference formatting and ensure completeness (e.g., missing page numbers or URLs).



In conclusion, this paper presents a promising approach to a long-standing problem in GEC evaluation. However, the lack of transparency regarding the classification rules and the limited evaluation hinder its overall impact. Addressing these weaknesses, particularly by providing a detailed description of the rules, would significantly strengthen the paper and enhance its contribution to the field. While the current submission is not ready for acceptance, it has the potential to be a valuable contribution with revisions.
