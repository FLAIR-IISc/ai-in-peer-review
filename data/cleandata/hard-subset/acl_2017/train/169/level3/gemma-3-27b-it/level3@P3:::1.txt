## Review of “Automatic Annotation and Evaluation of Error Types for Grammatical Error Correction”

This paper presents a method for automatically annotating grammatical errors in corrected text and applying this to analyze systems participating in the CoNLL-2014 shared task. The core idea – leveraging linguistically-enhanced alignment and a rule-based classifier – offers a potentially valuable contribution to the field by enabling more fine-grained evaluation of GEC systems than previously possible. However, the paper suffers from significant shortcomings in terms of methodological rigor, presentation, and clarity of contribution.

**Main Contributions (as perceived by this reviewer):**

1. **Automated Error Annotation Pipeline:** The primary contribution is the development of a pipeline for automatically extracting edits and classifying error types from parallel corrected text. This pipeline, building on previous work in alignment (Felice et al., 2016), provides a tool for analyzing GEC systems without manual annotation.
2. **Detailed CoNLL-2014 System Analysis:**  The application of this pipeline to the CoNLL-2014 data, and the resulting detailed performance breakdown of individual systems, is a valuable empirical contribution. The analysis reveals interesting system-specific strengths and weaknesses across various error categories.
3. **Potential for Standardized Evaluation:** The proposed approach hints at the possibility of a more standardized and automated evaluation framework for GEC, moving beyond reliance on potentially biased or inconsistent human annotation.

**Strengths:**

* **Practical Application:** The automated annotation tool is demonstrably useful. It enables a level of granularity in system evaluation that was previously inaccessible, opening avenues for more targeted system development. The stated intent to release the tool further enhances this benefit.
* **Fine-Grained Error Analysis:** The CoNLL-2014 analysis provides a valuable snapshot of system performance across a range of error types. Identifying specific weaknesses of each system is a significant step towards improving overall GEC performance. The breakdown by operation tier (Missing, Unnecessary, Replacement) is particularly insightful.
* **Novelty in Automation:** The effort to move beyond recall-based evaluation by automating the annotation process is a worthwhile endeavor. The rule-based approach, while having limitations (discussed below), offers an alternative to machine learning methods prone to dataset bias.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Superficial System Description:** The description of the core rule-based classifier is frustratingly superficial. The paper states that rules are based on POS tags and lemmas, but provides only a handful of examples (Table 2). Crucially, it lacks any information about the *number* of rules, their complexity, the ordering in which they are applied, or how conflicts are resolved. This makes it difficult to assess the robustness or generalizability of the approach.
* **Overemphasis on CoNLL-2014 Results:** The paper dedicates a disproportionate amount of space (a large table and extensive discussion) to the CoNLL-2014 analysis. Much of the discussion simply reiterates facts that are readily apparent from the table, adding little analytical insight.
* **Flawed Evaluation Methodology:**  The evaluation relies on asking raters to judge the appropriateness of the *automatically generated* error type assignments, rather than creating an independent gold standard for the 200 test sentences. This introduces circularity – the evaluation is effectively validating the tool itself, rather than assessing its accuracy against ground truth.
* **Misleading Aggregation of Rater Scores:** Averaging individual rater percentages (as presented in Table 3) obscures potentially important disagreements. The distribution of “Bad” ratings is not reported, making it difficult to assess the reliability of the classification. A simple average masks the degree of consensus or dissent among raters.
* **Lack of Data Composition Information:** The paper provides no information about the composition of the test data. Knowing the distribution of error categories within the dataset is crucial for interpreting the results. Are certain error types systematically underrepresented? Are the errors representative of typical learner language?
* **Vague Claims & Unjustified Assertions:** The claim that “edit boundaries might be unusual” is poorly defined and lacks concrete examples. The potential impact of inaccurate edit boundaries on the error type classification is not adequately addressed.  The claim of lower domain dependence is unsubstantiated. Rule-based systems relying on Hunspell vocabularies *are* domain- and language-dependent, and the test data is limited to student essays.
* **Unnecessary New Tagset:** The motivation for designing a new error tagset is unclear. The paper does not adequately justify why existing, established tagsets (Nicholls, CoNLL-2014) were not used or adapted.
* **Unclear Overall Motivation:** It is difficult to discern the primary contribution of this work. Is it a new GEC system? A novel error tagset? Or simply an evaluation methodology? Important details, like the scope of application and potential limitations, are omitted, making it hard to assess the significance of the work.
* **Presentation & Technical Issues:** Numerous typos and inconsistencies in the references detract from the paper’s credibility (e.g., “c.f.” → “cf.”, “M2” → “M^2”, inconsistent casing).

**Questions to Authors:**

1.  Can you provide a more detailed description of the rule-based classifier, including the number of rules, their complexity, the ordering of rules, and the conflict resolution strategy?
2.  Why was a new error tagset created instead of utilizing existing, established tagsets? What specific limitations of those tagsets motivated this decision?
3.  What steps were taken to ensure the reliability of the rater judgments, and why was the distribution of "Bad" ratings not reported?
4.  What is the composition of the test data in terms of error types?
5.  Can you elaborate on the potential impact of inaccurate edit boundaries (as acknowledged in the paper) on the accuracy of the error type classification?
