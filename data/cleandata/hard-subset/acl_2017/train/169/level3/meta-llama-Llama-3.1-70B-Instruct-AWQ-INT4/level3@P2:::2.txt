**Summary of the Paper**

The paper proposes a novel method for automatically annotating and evaluating error types in Grammatical Error Correction (GEC) systems. The approach uses a linguistically-enhanced alignment algorithm to extract edits between parallel original and corrected sentences and a new rule-based classifier to assign error types. The method is independent of manual error annotation and can handle multi-token errors. The authors apply their approach to the system output produced in the CoNLL-2014 shared task and provide a detailed analysis of system error type performance.

**Main Contributions**

1. **Novel evaluation method**: The paper proposes a new method for evaluating GEC performance by error type, including precision, not just recall.
2. **Automated error annotation**: The approach provides a way to automatically annotate error correction data with explicit edit spans and error type information.
3. **Detailed error analysis**: The method allows for a detailed analysis of system error type performance, including precision, recall, and F-score.

**Strengths**

1. **Broad applicability**: The evaluation covers a variety of systems, showing the broad applicability of the approach.
2. **High human approval rate**: Automatically selected error tags are largely approved by human experts, indicating the effectiveness of the approach.
3. **Improved evaluation**: The method provides a more detailed evaluation of GEC systems, enabling researchers to identify strengths and weaknesses.

**Weaknesses**

1. **Lack of rule description**: The rules used to derive error types are not described, threatening reproducibility.
2. **Limited classifier evaluation**: The classifier evaluation lacks detailed error analysis and concrete directions for improvement.
3. **Language limitation**: The evaluation is performed only on English, and the paper does not address adapting the approach to other languages.

**Questions to Authors**

1. Can you provide a detailed description of the rules used to derive error types and explain how they were developed?
2. How do you plan to address the limitation of the approach to English and adapt it to other languages?
3. Can you provide a more detailed analysis of the classifier's performance, including error analysis and concrete directions for improvement?

**Additional Comments**

The paper has some minor issues with reference formatting and missing page/URL details in citations. Additionally, the author-response summary indicates that the reviewer agrees the approach is promising but the paper is not ready, and rules must be described for replication and generalization to other languages must be addressed.
