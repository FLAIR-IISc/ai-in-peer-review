**Summary of the Paper**

The paper proposes a method for automatic annotation and evaluation of error types for Grammatical Error Correction (GEC) systems. The authors develop a linguistically-enhanced alignment algorithm to extract edits between parallel original and corrected sentences, and a new rule-based classifier to assign error types to these edits. The classifier is dataset-independent and relies on automatic markup information such as POS tags and lemmas. The authors evaluate their approach on a small-scale manual evaluation and demonstrate its value by analyzing the performance of GEC systems in the CoNLL-2014 shared task.

**Main Contributions**

1. **Automatic Annotation and Evaluation of Error Types**: The authors propose a method for automatic annotation and evaluation of error types for GEC systems, which enables fine-grained comparison of system performance.
2. **Linguistically-Enhanced Alignment Algorithm**: The authors develop a linguistically-enhanced alignment algorithm to extract edits between parallel original and corrected sentences.
3. **Dataset-Independent Classifier**: The authors propose a new rule-based classifier that is dataset-independent and relies on automatic markup information such as POS tags and lemmas.

**Strengths**

1. **Useful Application**: The proposed method has a useful application for teachers and learners, enabling fine-grained comparison of GEC systems.
2. **Detailed Evaluation**: The authors provide a detailed evaluation of the performance of GEC systems in the CoNLL-2014 shared task.

**Weaknesses**

1. **Superficial System Description**: The system description is highly superficial, lacking examples, counts, complexity, and ordering details of the core rule set.
2. **Overemphasis on CoNLL-2014 Results**: The paper overemphasizes the CoNLL-2014 system results, repeating facts that are visible from the table.
3. **Evaluation Methodology**: The evaluation used raters to judge system output rather than creating an independent gold standard for the 200 test sentences.
4. **Aggregation of Rater Scores**: The aggregation of rater scores is misleading, averaging individual rater percentages without reporting the distribution of "bad" ratings.
5. **Lack of Information about Test Data**: There is no information about the composition of the test data, including the number of error categories or which categories are covered.
6. **Vague Claims**: The claim that "edit boundaries might be unusual" needs precise definition and examples, and the potential impact on the results should be discussed.
7. **Unclear Motivation**: The overall motivation of the paper is unclear, and important details are omitted.

**Questions to Authors**

1. Can you provide more details about the core rule set, including examples, counts, complexity, and ordering details?
2. How do you justify the use of a new error-tagset, and why did you not use existing tagsets such as Nicholls or CoNLL-2014?
3. Can you provide more information about the test data composition, including the number of error categories and which categories are covered?
4. How do you address the potential impact of unusual edit boundaries on the results, and can you provide precise definition and examples?
5. Can you clarify the overall motivation of the paper, and what is the main contribution: a new system, a new tagset, or an evaluation?
