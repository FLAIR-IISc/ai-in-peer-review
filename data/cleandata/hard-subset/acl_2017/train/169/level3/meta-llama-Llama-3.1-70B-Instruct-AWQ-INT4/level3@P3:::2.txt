The paper "Automatic Annotation and Evaluation of Error Types for Grammatical Error Correction" presents a novel approach to automatically annotate and evaluate error types in grammatical error correction (GEC) systems. The authors propose a two-stage approach, first using a linguistically-enhanced alignment algorithm to extract edits between parallel original and corrected sentences, and then classifying them using a new rule-based framework.

The main contributions of this paper are:

1. A novel evaluation method that enables measuring GEC performance by error type, including precision, not just recall.
2. An approach that is independent of manual error annotation and can handle multi-token errors.
3. A comprehensive evaluation of various GEC systems, demonstrating the broad applicability of the approach.

The strengths of this paper include:

1. The proposed approach is entirely dataset-independent and relies only on automatically obtained information such as POS tags and lemmas.
2. The authors demonstrate the value of their approach by carrying out a detailed evaluation of system error type performance for the first time for all teams in the CoNLL-2014 shared task on GEC.
3. The paper provides a comprehensive analysis of error types, including a breakdown of Determiner errors for two teams using different approaches.

However, there are also some weaknesses and limitations in this paper:

1. The rules used to derive error types are not described, threatening reproducibility.
2. The classifier evaluation lacks detailed error analysis and concrete directions for improvement.
3. The evaluation is performed only on English, and the paper does not address adapting the approach to other languages.
4. The significance test setup is unclear, and the use of F0.5 as a basis for discrimination may be weak.
5. The comparison between gold and auto references appears limited to boundaries and not classification, despite claims otherwise.
6. Some systems' failure on certain error types may reflect system design limits rather than evaluation failure, but the paper does not analyze this.
7. The paper does not report raw TP/FP/TN/FN counts, which are needed to assess sample sizes and compute other measures.
8. Results for some systems/error types may be based on very few instances, which is not clarified.

To address these limitations, the authors should:

1. Describe the rules used to derive error types in detail to ensure reproducibility.
2. Provide a more comprehensive error analysis and concrete directions for improvement.
3. Investigate adapting the approach to other languages.
4. Clarify the significance test setup and consider using alternative metrics.
5. Provide a more detailed comparison between gold and auto references.
6. Analyze the relationship between system design and evaluation failure.
7. Report raw TP/FP/TN/FN counts to facilitate further analysis.
8. Clarify the number of instances used to compute results for each system/error type.

In conclusion, this paper presents a promising approach to automatically annotating and evaluating error types in GEC systems. However, to fully realize its potential, the authors should address the limitations and weaknesses identified above.

Questions to Authors:

1. Can you provide more details on the rules used to derive error types?
2. How do you plan to adapt the approach to other languages?
3. Can you clarify the significance test setup and consider using alternative metrics?
4. How do you plan to address the limitations of the F0.5 metric?
5. Can you provide more details on the comparison between gold and auto references?
6. How do you plan to analyze the relationship between system design and evaluation failure?
7. Can you report raw TP/FP/TN/FN counts for each system/error type?
8. How do you plan to address the issue of results being based on very few instances for some systems/error types?

Additional Comments:

* The paper would benefit from a more detailed description of the linguistically-enhanced alignment algorithm used to extract edits.
* The authors should consider providing more examples of error types and their corresponding classifications.
* The paper could benefit from a more comprehensive discussion of the implications of the results for GEC system design and evaluation.
