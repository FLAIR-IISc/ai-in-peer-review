This paper presents a method to automatically annotate parallel error correction data with explicit edit spans and error type information. The authors' approach consists of two main steps: automatic edit extraction using a linguistically-enhanced alignment algorithm, and classification of edits according to a new rule-based framework. The tool used to perform this task will be released with the paper.

The main contributions of this work are the development of a dataset-independent rule-based classifier for error type evaluation and the application of this approach to the system output produced in the CoNLL-2014 shared task. The authors claim that their approach enables a more detailed evaluation of system error type performance, which can be useful for teachers and learners.

The authors' approach has several strengths. Firstly, it is dataset-independent, meaning that it does not require labelled training data. Secondly, it is fully deterministic, which bypasses bias effects and makes it more consistent. The authors also provide a detailed evaluation of their approach, including a small-scale manual evaluation and an analysis of system error type performance for the CoNLL-2014 shared task.

However, the paper also has several weaknesses. Firstly, the system description is highly superficial, lacking examples, counts, complexity, and ordering details. Secondly, the paper overemphasizes CoNLL-2014 system results, repeating facts that are visible from the table. The evaluation used raters to judge system output rather than creating an independent gold standard for the 200 test sentences. The aggregation of rater scores is also misleading, as it averages individual rater percentages without reporting the distribution of "bad" ratings. Additionally, there is no information about the test data composition, including the number of error categories or which categories are covered.

The authors also make several unsubstantiated claims, including the claim that their approach has lower domain dependence. They also fail to provide a clear definition of "edit boundaries might be unusual" and do not discuss the potential impact of this on their results. Furthermore, the authors do not justify the need for a new error-tagset, as existing tagsets (Nicholls, CoNLL-2014) could have been used.

Overall, the paper's motivation is unclear, and important details are omitted. The authors also fail to provide a clear explanation of their contributions, which makes it difficult to evaluate the significance of their work.

The paper's strengths are:

1. The authors' approach is useful for teachers and learners, enabling fine-grained comparison of GEC systems.
2. The approach is dataset-independent, meaning that it does not require labelled training data.
3. The approach is fully deterministic, which bypasses bias effects and makes it more consistent.

The paper's weaknesses are:

1. The system description is highly superficial, lacking examples, counts, complexity, and ordering details.
2. The paper overemphasizes CoNLL-2014 system results, repeating facts that are visible from the table.
3. The evaluation used raters to judge system output rather than creating an independent gold standard for the 200 test sentences.
4. The aggregation of rater scores is misleading, as it averages individual rater percentages without reporting the distribution of "bad" ratings.
5. There is no information about the test data composition, including the number of error categories or which categories are covered.

Questions to authors:

1. Can you provide more details about the system description, including examples, counts, complexity, and ordering details?
2. How do you justify the need for a new error-tagset, and why did you not use existing tagsets (Nicholls, CoNLL-2014)?
3. Can you provide a clear definition of "edit boundaries might be unusual" and discuss the potential impact of this on your results?
4. How do you plan to address the limitations of your evaluation, including the use of raters to judge system output and the aggregation of rater scores?
5. Can you provide more information about the test data composition, including the number of error categories or which categories are covered?
