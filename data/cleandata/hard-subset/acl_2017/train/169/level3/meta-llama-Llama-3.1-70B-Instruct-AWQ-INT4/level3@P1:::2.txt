**Summary of the Paper**

The paper proposes a novel method for automatic annotation and evaluation of error types for Grammatical Error Correction (GEC) systems. The approach uses a linguistically-enhanced alignment algorithm to extract edits between parallel original and corrected sentences, and a new dataset-independent rule-based classifier to assign error types to these edits. The authors evaluate their approach by applying it to the system output produced in the CoNLL-2014 shared task and demonstrate its value in providing a detailed analysis of system error type performance.

**Main Contributions**

1. A novel evaluation method that enables measuring GEC performance by error type, including precision, not just recall.
2. An approach that is independent of manual error annotation and can handle multi-token errors.
3. A comprehensive evaluation of the approach covering a variety of systems, showing its broad applicability.

**Strengths**

1. The approach is promising and has the potential to provide a more detailed and accurate evaluation of GEC systems.
2. The use of a linguistically-enhanced alignment algorithm and a rule-based classifier allows for a more nuanced analysis of error types.
3. The evaluation is comprehensive and covers a variety of systems, demonstrating the approach's broad applicability.

**Weaknesses**

1. The rules used to derive error types are not described, threatening reproducibility.
2. The classifier evaluation lacks detailed error analysis and concrete directions for improvement.
3. The evaluation is performed only on English, and the paper does not address adapting the approach to other languages.
4. The significance test setup is unclear, and the comparison between gold and auto references appears limited to boundaries and not classification.
5. Some results may be based on very few instances, and the paper does not provide raw TP/FP/TN/FN counts, which are needed to assess sample sizes and compute other measures.

**Questions to Authors**

1. Can you provide a detailed description of the rules used to derive error types, and how they were developed?
2. How do you plan to address the issue of adapting the approach to other languages?
3. Can you provide more information on the significance test setup and the comparison between gold and auto references?
4. How do you plan to address the issue of noisy boundaries and the lack of intermediate rating options in the human rating of automatic tags?
5. Can you provide raw TP/FP/TN/FN counts for the results in Table 6, and clarify the sample sizes for each system and error type?
