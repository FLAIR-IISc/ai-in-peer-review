Summary and contributions
- The paper proposes an automatic pipeline to annotate system hypotheses in GEC with explicit edit spans and error types, combining a linguistically enhanced alignment for edit extraction with a deterministic, dataset-agnostic rule-based classifier. This enables measuring precision and recall by error type, not just recall, and allows analysis of multi-token edits and detection vs. correction.
- The authors validate the classifier with a small human study (≥95% Good/Acceptable), and demonstrate the approach on all CoNLL-2014 systems, providing the first broad, per-type performance analysis across systems. They also show that using automatically reprocessed references yields comparable overall scores to gold references.
- A practical tool is promised for release, which could standardize error-type evaluation and facilitate more granular analysis across datasets and systems.

Strengths
- Enabling precision-aware, per-type evaluation: Prior GEC work largely lacked per-type precision because system outputs were untyped; this work closes that gap. The ability to slice performance along operation tier (Missing/Unnecessary/Replacement) and POS/morph tiers is highly valuable for diagnosing systems (e.g., CUUI vs. CAMB differences for DET and VERB:FORM).
- Dataset-agnostic, transparent classification: The rules rely only on automatic markup (POS/lemma/parse/stemming/Hunspell), avoiding corpus-specific training. Determinism improves interpretability and consistency, addressing annotator bias inherent to learned classifiers. The human study (86.3% Good, +10% Acceptable, 3.7% Bad overall) supports practical usability.
- Broad applicability demonstrated: Applying the method to all CoNLL-2014 outputs shows scalability and reveals actionable insights (e.g., widespread difficulty with Unnecessary edits, strengths of classifier-based vs. MT approaches per type, multi-token weakness across systems).
- Separation of alignment and evaluation: Fixing edit spans prior to scoring simplifies evaluation and avoids some pathologies of dynamic-boundary metrics such as M2, enabling clearer attribution of TP/FP/FN and facilitating new analyses (multi-token size, detection vs. correction).
- Useful analyses beyond headline scores: The operation-tier breakdown, detailed per-type tables, and multi-token/detection analyses expose complementary strengths and gaps that overall F0.5 obscures, and suggest directions for hybrid systems.

Weaknesses
- Reproducibility: rules and dependence on preprocessing not specified: The rule inventory, priority/exception handling, and decision logic are not described in enough detail to reproduce results or extend the scheme. The approach is sensitive to POS/lemma/parse quality (authors attribute most Bad cases to POS errors), but there is no systematic accounting of how preprocessing errors propagate or how robust the rules are to different taggers/parsers.
- Error scheme design and relation to prior work unclear: It is not explained how the 25 categories (and 55 operation/type combinations) were chosen, how they map to existing schemes (e.g., M2 categories, ERRANT, FCE), or what trade-offs were made (e.g., conflating modal/tense). Without this, it is hard to assess coverage, comparability, and the generality of conclusions.
- Limited evaluation depth: The human study is small (200 edits) and conflates boundary errors with type assignment. There is no detailed analysis of Good vs. Acceptable vs. Bad judgments (e.g., confusion matrix by type, correlation with tagger errors, impact of dependency features). No ablations (e.g., removing dependency features, changing taggers) quantify where the rules help or fail.
- Language scope and discontinuous edits: The method is only demonstrated for English. Sensitivity to language-specific morphology/syntax (e.g., separable verb particles, rich inflection, clitics, non-space-delimited scripts) and discontinuous edits is not addressed. The alignment/edit model assumes contiguous spans and may struggle for languages like German or for word-order changes spanning long distances.
- Statistical testing and reference reprocessing details: The bootstrap significance tests are insufficiently specified (unit of resampling, stratification, metric tested, per-team vs. pooled). The “gold vs. auto reference” comparison appears focused on span boundaries and overall F0.5; it does not demonstrate that error-type classifications remain consistent, which is critical for the paper’s core claim.
- Reporting/interpretability gaps: Per-type tables omit raw TP/FP/FN counts, making it hard to assess reliability for sparse categories. Some “best” results are likely based on very few instances. Multi-token findings are reported but not disentangled from system design/training factors (e.g., MT vs. classifier, phrase lengths). The SPELL category relies on a specific GB Hunspell dictionary (potential bias) without discussion.

Questions to authors
- Rules and implementation: Please provide the full set of rules, precedence ordering, and examples of ambiguous cases. How are conflicts resolved? What features from the dependency parse are used?
- Preprocessing sensitivity: Which exact spaCy model(s) and versions were used? How does performance change with alternative taggers/parsers (e.g., UD models), or without dependency features? Can you quantify how often POS/lemma/parse errors lead to misclassification per type?
- Error scheme: How were categories selected and mapped to existing annotation schemes? Can you provide a mapping table to common schemes (e.g., M2/FCE/ERRANT) and discuss known mismatches (e.g., modal vs. tense)?
- Reference reprocessing: When reprocessing references, how often does the automatic classifier’s type agree with the human label by type? Please report agreement rates by category.
- Statistical testing: What was the resampling unit (sentence vs. edit), number of samples per run, and metric (F0.5 micro)? Were tests per team or pooled? Please include confidence intervals for key comparisons.
- Data reporting: Can you add TP/FP/FN counts per team/type and note categories with <20 gold instances? Provide detection-vs-correction breakdown by type.
- Language generalization: What is required to port the approach to other languages? How would you handle non-contiguous edits (e.g., separable verbs), clitics, or script differences? Any preliminary non-English results?
- Boundaries vs. labels in human study: Can you rerun a subset with manual boundaries fixed to test whether judgments of “Bad” persist? Provide a confusion analysis of Good vs. Acceptable vs. Bad by type.

Additional comments
- Some GEC pipelines can expose internal error labels (e.g., classifier-based subsystems). A brief discussion would strengthen the “novelty” framing.
- Please report tool availability, versioning, and configuration files to ensure replicability (including dictionary versions for SPELL).
- Minor: fix citation formatting (author lists, venues, years, URLs/page ranges).
