Summary and claimed contributions
The paper proposes a pipeline for automatically annotating GEC hypotheses with edit spans and error types via (i) a linguistically enhanced alignment and (ii) a deterministic, dataset-agnostic, rule-based error-type classifier. The tool is used to produce fine-grained analyses of CoNLL-2014 system outputs, including operation tiers, detailed error categories, multi-token edits, and detection vs. correction. As I see it, the main contributions are: (1) a practical evaluation workflow/tool enabling fine-grained comparison of GEC systems; (2) a transparent, rule-based classifier intended to be dataset-independent; (3) an illustrative analysis of CoNLL-2014 systems by error type.

Strengths
- Practical utility for teachers and researchers. The ability to break down performance by operation (Missing/Unnecessary/Replacement), POS-like types, and multi-token spans offers actionable diagnostics that current GEC metrics lack. The detection vs. correction view is also informative.
- Deterministic, interpretable labeling. Rules grounded in POS/lemmas and Hunspell allow tracing why a type was assigned, which can aid system development and pedagogy.
- Empirical usability on a standard benchmark. Applying the tool to the full CoNLL-2014 outputs shows how different systems excel in different categories, and the bootstrap analysis suggests auto- vs. gold-reference processing preserves rankings.

Weaknesses
- Insufficient method detail. The core rule set is described superficially: there are no examples of rules, counts, coverage per type, conflict resolution, ordering/priority, or complexity analysis. This undermines reproducibility and external validation.
- Evaluation design is weak. Instead of building an independent gold for the 200 edits, the paper uses 5 raters to judge predicted labels, then averages per-rater percentages. The aggregation is misleading, the distribution of “Bad” ratings and category-wise errors are not reported, and the “unusual edit boundaries” caveat is vague and unanalyzed.
- Overemphasis on CoNLL-2014 tables with limited insight. Large tables mostly restate visible facts; deeper analyses (e.g., error-category prevalence, confidence intervals per type, ablations of alignment vs. classification) are missing.
- Unsubstantiated claims of domain independence. Rules, POS/dependency tags, and Hunspell lexicons can be domain- and language-dependent; tests are confined to learner essays. No cross-domain or cross-register evidence is provided.
- Tagset motivation unclear. A new tagset is introduced without justifying why Nicholls or CoNLL-2014 tagsets are inadequate, nor mapping between schemes.
- Presentation issues: typos and references (“c.f.” → “cf.”, “M2” → “M^2”), inconsistent casing, and a malformed bibliography.

Questions to authors
- Provide a full rule inventory: number of rules, examples, precedence/ordering, conflict resolution, and per-type coverage/precision.
- Define “unusual edit boundaries” precisely, quantify their frequency, and analyze their impact on rater judgments and scoring.
- Justify the new tagset versus existing ones; provide mappings and discuss implications for comparability.
- Report test set composition: counts per error category and which categories are covered in the 200-edit sample.
- Clarify rater protocol and aggregation: per-rater confusion matrices, distribution of “Bad” ratings by type, and reliability per category.
- Substantiate domain independence with cross-domain experiments or discuss limitations.
