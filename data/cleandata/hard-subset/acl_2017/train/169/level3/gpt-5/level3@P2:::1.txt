Summary and claimed contributions
This paper introduces a deterministic pipeline for automatically annotating grammatical error correction (GEC) outputs with edit spans and error types. The pipeline aligns original/corrected sentences with a linguistically-informed Damerau–Levenshtein alignment and assigns error types via a rule-based, dataset-agnostic classifier that relies on automatic linguistic markup (POS, lemmas, dependency parses) and Hunspell. The authors validate the classifier with a small human appropriateness study and then use the tool to analyze CoNLL-2014 shared task systems by error type, operation (Missing/Replacement/Unnecessary), edit size, and detection vs. correction.

Contributions as I see them (in order of strength):
1) A practical, deterministic tool that enables fine-grained, consistent error-type analysis of unannotated GEC outputs, facilitating system comparison beyond aggregate metrics.
2) A comprehensive error-type breakdown of CoNLL-2014 systems (including operation tier and multi-token edits), yielding actionable insights about complementary system strengths.
3) An evaluation approach based on fixed edit spans (auto-aligning both hypotheses and references) and a discussion contrasting it with M2, suggesting M2 inflates scores.

Strengths
- Utility for the community: The tool directly addresses a persistent gap—lack of type-level analysis on system hypotheses—useful for both researchers and educators. The operation-tier and multi-token analyses are especially helpful to diagnose system behavior.
- Deterministic and interpretable classification: Rule-based typing grounded in automatic markup improves transparency and reproducibility. The modular tagset design allows flexible slicing (e.g., R:NOUN vs. all noun errors).
- Broad empirical application: Applying the annotator across all CoNLL-2014 systems demonstrates scalability and provides a valuable benchmark of system strengths/weaknesses by type, operation, and edit size.
- Separation of extraction and scoring: Fixing spans on both hypothesis and reference simplifies evaluation and exposes issues in dynamic-boundary metrics (e.g., M2), a useful perspective for metric design.
- Initial quality indication: The small human study (κfree = 0.724; per-rater ≥95% Good/Acceptable) suggests the rules often yield plausible categories, despite noisy automatic alignment.

Weaknesses
- Insufficient technical detail about the core rule set: The description of the classifier is too superficial. The paper lacks a complete rule inventory, examples per category, counts of rules, precedence/ordering, conflict resolution, and complexity/coverage analysis. Without these, it is hard to assess novelty, reproducibility, and where errors arise.
- Inadequate validation design and reporting: The human evaluation judges “appropriateness” of predicted types on automatically extracted edits rather than establishing an independent gold standard with human-typed edits and carefully verified boundaries. The aggregation of rater scores appears to average per-rater percentages rather than report pooled counts with confidence intervals; the distribution of “Bad” ratings across categories is not shown. Claims like “qualitatively as good as human annotations” based on non-significant differences in system scores are too strong (absence of significance ≠ equivalence).
- Missing dataset composition details: The 200-edit sample’s category distribution, sentence sources, and overlap with specific error types are not reported. Without coverage information, it’s unclear which categories were actually validated and where the rules may underperform.
- Unclear or unsupported claims about domain (in)dependence: The approach depends on English-specific resources (Penn-to-UD mapping, spaCy models, Lancaster stemmer) and a GB Hunspell dictionary; generalization beyond student essays or across domains/variants (e.g., US/GB spelling) is not demonstrated. The “dataset-agnostic” claim needs empirical support (e.g., Lang-8, JFLEG).
- Tagset motivation and mapping: The rationale for proposing a new error tagset (vs. adapting Nicholls, CoNLL-2014, or other established schemes) is not well justified. No mapping between the new tags and existing schemes is provided, hindering comparability.
- Emphasis on large tables without deeper analysis: Several sections reiterate observations readily visible from the tables. Space could be used to probe error patterns (e.g., confusion analyses, ablations, rule failure modes) and to provide concrete guidance for system builders.
- Editing and reference issues: Multiple typos and inconsistent reference formatting (e.g., “c.f.” vs. “cf.”, “M2” vs. M^2, inconsistent capitalization and bibliographic fields) detract from clarity.

Questions to authors (to be addressed in the response)
- Rule set specification: How many rules per category? What is the rule formalism, precedence, and conflict resolution? Please provide concrete rule examples for at least 10 representative categories and a flow diagram of decision logic.
- Coverage and errors: What proportion of edits fall into UNK or OTHER? Provide a confusion matrix of predicted vs. human-typed categories on a human-annotated subset, with per-category precision/recall.
- Evaluation design: Can you create a small gold set with manually verified boundaries and error types independent of your system, and report classifier performance (per-category and overall)? Also report pooled rater counts with CIs and the distribution of “Bad” judgments by category.
- Edit boundaries: Provide explicit examples of “unusual” boundaries, quantify their frequency, and analyze their impact on typing accuracy. How sensitive are results to POS/dependency errors? Any post-hoc correction rules?
- Domain and variant robustness: How does the classifier perform on alternative corpora (e.g., JFLEG, Lang-8)? What is the impact of GB vs. US spelling (Hunspell GB-large) on SPELL and ORTH categories?
- Tagset rationale and mapping: Why not reuse or extend an established tagset? Can you provide a mapping to CoNLL/Nicholls categories to support comparability?
- Reproducibility: Specify exact versions of spaCy models, stemmers, Hunspell dictionaries, and all parameters (e.g., alignment costs, merging rules). Will you release the full rule set and scripts?

Additional comments
- Consider an ablation study (alignment variants; with/without dependency features; with/without Hunspell).
- Report time/space complexity and throughput on CoNLL-2014 to guide adoption.
- Editorial: fix typos, normalize references (authors/titles/venues), use “cf.” not “c.f.”, and consistent notation for M^2.

Overall, the work is promising and potentially very useful to the GEC community, but the paper needs substantially more detail on the classifier, stronger validation with an independent gold standard, clearer justification of the tagset, and tighter analysis.
