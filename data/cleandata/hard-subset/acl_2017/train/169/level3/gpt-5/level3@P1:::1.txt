Summary
The paper proposes a two-step method to automatically annotate grammatical error correction (GEC) outputs with explicit edits and error types: (1) linguistically-enhanced alignment for edit extraction; (2) a dataset-independent, rule-based classifier that assigns one of 55 error categories using only automatic markup (POS, lemmas, dependencies, Hunspell). A small human study rates predicted types as “Good/Acceptable” in ≥95% of cases. The authors then re-annotate CoNLL-2014 system outputs and perform the first detailed, per-error-type evaluation across systems, including analyses by operation (Missing/Unnecessary/Replacement), fine-grained categories, multi-token edits, and detection vs. correction. They also argue that simple explicit edit comparison can avoid some inflation effects of M2.

Contributions (as I see them)
1) A practical, release-ready automatic annotation tool that aligns edits and assigns interpretable, rule-based error types to unannotated system outputs, enabling fine-grained evaluation and facilitating reproducible comparisons.
2) A comprehensive per-error-type analysis of CoNLL-2014 system performance (including operation tier, multi-token edits, and detection vs. correction), yielding actionable insights about which approaches target which error families.
3) An evaluation protocol that compares system hypotheses and references at the edit level (with consistent reprocessing of references), together with evidence that M2 can overestimate performance.

Strengths
- Clear practical utility and immediate impact: The tool fills a longstanding gap—annotating unlabelled system outputs—allowing educators and researchers to diagnose strengths/weaknesses by error type rather than a single overall score. This supports targeted pedagogical feedback and focused system improvements.
- Deterministic and interpretable classification: Rule-based categories grounded in automatic markup make decisions traceable, reproducible, and explainable, addressing opacity issues of learned classifiers. The approach is dataset-agnostic by design and avoids training-data mismatches across corpora.
- First systematic, cross-system error-type analysis: The paper surfaces nontrivial findings (e.g., widespread neglect of unnecessary-token errors; different methods excelling on different categories; MT strength on multi-token edits but overall difficulty; classifier advantage on VERB:FORM; detection vs. correction gaps). These insights can guide ensemble designs and research priorities.
- Sensible evaluation framing and empirical care: Re-annotating references to match hypothesis processing reduces alignment mismatches; bootstrap tests show no significant differences between gold and auto references for overall scores. The critique of M2’s dynamic-boundary behavior is useful for the community.
- Tool release and compatibility with existing pipelines: Using spaCy, NLTK stemming, and Hunspell makes the approach easy to adopt; the error scheme’s modularity supports analysis at multiple granularities (operation, POS, morphology).

Weaknesses
- Overly superficial system description: The rule set is a central contribution but lacks the necessary detail for scrutiny or reproduction. The paper omits the number of rules, their prioritization/ordering, coverage, representative patterns, conflict resolution, and complexity. Without examples for challenging categories (e.g., MORPH vs. VERB:FORM vs. VERB:TENSE; ORTH vs. SPELL; WO), it is hard to assess design quality and edge cases.
- Evaluation design and reporting are limited: The 200-edit study uses raters to judge the system’s own predictions rather than creating an independent gold standard for edits and types. Results are reported as averaged rater percentages, which can mislead; the distribution of “Bad” ratings per instance, majority-vote accuracy, and calibration across raters are not reported. A confusion matrix of predicted vs. human type, category-wise breakdown, and error-attribution to tagging/parsing would strengthen claims.
- Data composition and domain scope are unclear: The sample’s category distribution is not provided (how many per type/operation), making it difficult to interpret the ≥95% Good/Acceptable figure. Claims of low domain dependence are not substantiated: rules and Hunspell lexicon may be domain- and variety-dependent (e.g., GB spellings), and experiments are confined to learner-essay corpora (FCE, CoNLL-2014). No out-of-domain test (news/wiki) or ablations (different taggers/dictionaries) are reported.
- Unclear motivation and tagset rationale: It is not clear whether the main contribution is the tool, the error-type scheme, or the evaluation. The paper introduces a new tagset but does not justify why existing schemes (e.g., Nicholls/FCE, CoNLL/NUS) were insufficient, nor does it provide mappings. Without such justification or mappings, comparability to prior work is diminished.
- Presentation issues: The paper dedicates excessive space to large tables and restating values visible in those tables rather than analyzing trends and implications. The statement that “edit boundaries might be unusual” needs definition, examples, and an analysis of their impact on evaluation. Minor but noticeable typos and reference-format inconsistencies remain.

Questions to Authors
- Rule inventory and design: How many rules are there? How are they ordered and disambiguated? What are representative rules for ambiguous cases (e.g., distinguishing MORPH vs. VERB:FORM, ORTH vs. SPELL, WO vs. PREP)? Please include a rule taxonomy, coverage statistics, and examples for “hard” edits.
- Error-boundary behavior: What constitutes “unusual” boundaries? Provide qualitative examples and quantify how often boundaries diverge from human segmentations. How sensitive are per-type scores to boundary choices?
- Rater study methodology: Report per-edit majority vote, distribution of “Bad” across edits (e.g., ≥1 rater vs. unanimous), category-wise results, and a confusion matrix against human-assigned types. Why not create a small gold set with adjudication to serve as an anchor for future work?
- Domain and resources: How does performance change with different taggers/parsers or dictionaries (e.g., US vs. GB Hunspell)? Any results on non-learner text? What is required to adapt to other languages or genres?
- Tagset justification and mapping: Why introduce a new scheme? Can you provide mapping tables to FCE/CoNLL categories to support backward compatibility? What criteria guided the granularity (e.g., collapsing modal vs. tense)?
- Scoring and M2: Can you quantify how often M2’s dynamic merging/splitting changes outcomes on this dataset? Are there cases where your fixed-span comparison underestimates sensible partial matches that differ only in boundaries? Consider reporting both.

Overall, the paper addresses an important gap with a practical tool and yields useful insights, but it needs substantially more detail on the rule system, stronger and more transparent evaluation, clearer motivation for the new tagset, and tighter analysis beyond large tables.
