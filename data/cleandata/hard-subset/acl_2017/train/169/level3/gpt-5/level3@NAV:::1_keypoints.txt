- Proposes a pipeline to auto-annotate GEC hypotheses with edit spans and error types using linguistically enhanced alignment plus a deterministic rule-based classifier.
- Claims contributions: a practical fine-grained GEC evaluation tool, a transparent dataset-agnostic rule-based classifier, and an illustrative error-type analysis of CoNLL-2014 systems.

Strengths:
- Offers practical, actionable diagnostics (operation tiers, POS-like types, multi-token spans, detection vs. correction) useful for teachers and researchers.
- Deterministic and interpretable labeling grounded in POS/lemmas and Hunspell enables traceability and pedagogy/system development.
- Demonstrates empirical usability on CoNLL-2014 and shows system ranking largely preserved between auto- and gold-reference processing.

Weaknesses:
- Core rule set description is superficial: lacks rule examples, counts, per-type coverage, conflict resolution, ordering, and complexity analysis.
- Evaluation design is weak and potentially misleading: uses 5 raters on predicted labels without constructing an independent gold, lacks distributional reporting of “Bad” ratings and other statistics.
- Analysis overemphasizes large CoNLL-2014 tables without deeper insights like per-type prevalence, CIs per type, or alignment vs. classification ablations.
- Claims of domain independence are unsubstantiated; rules, POS/dependency, and lexicons may be domain- and language-dependent and were only tested on learner essays.
- New tagset motivation and justification are unclear; no comparison or mapping to existing Nicholls or CoNLL-2014 tagsets.
- Presentation problems: typos, inconsistent casing, notation errors, and a malformed bibliography.

Questions for authors:
- Provide a full rule inventory: number of rules, examples, precedence/order, conflict resolution, and per-type coverage/precision.
- Precisely define “unusual edit boundaries,” quantify their frequency, and analyze their impact on rater judgments and scoring.
- Justify the new tagset, provide mappings to existing schemes, and discuss implications for comparability.
- Report test set composition: counts per error category and which categories appear in the 200-edit sample.
- Clarify rater protocol and aggregation: per-rater confusion matrices, distribution of “Bad” ratings by type, and reliability per category.
- Substantiate domain independence with cross-domain experiments or explicitly discuss the limitations.
