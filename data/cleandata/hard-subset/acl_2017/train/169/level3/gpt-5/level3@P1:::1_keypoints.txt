- Summary: Proposes a two-step method—linguistically-enhanced alignment and a rule-based 55-category classifier using POS/lemmas/deps/Hunspell—to annotate GEC outputs and analyze system performance per error type.
- Summary: Re-annotates CoNLL-2014 system outputs and conducts detailed analyses by operation, fine-grained categories, multi-token edits, and detection vs. correction; reports a small human study with ≥95% Good/Acceptable ratings.

- Contribution: Provides a practical, release-ready automatic annotation tool for aligning edits and assigning interpretable error types to unlabelled system outputs.
- Contribution: Presents the first comprehensive per-error-type analysis of CoNLL-2014 systems, including operation tiers, multi-token edits, and detection vs. correction breakdowns.
- Contribution: Proposes an evaluation protocol comparing hypotheses and references at the edit level and argues that explicit edit comparison can mitigate M2 inflation effects.

- Strength: Fills a practical gap by enabling fine-grained, interpretable evaluation useful for educators and researchers.
- Strength: Uses deterministic, rule-based classification grounded in automatic markup for reproducibility and explainability, avoiding learned-classifier opacity.
- Strength: Delivers novel cross-system findings (e.g., neglect of unnecessary-token errors, MT strengths on multi-token edits, classifier advantage on VERB:FORM).
- Strength: Shows empirical care—re-annotating references, bootstrapping tests, and critiquing M2’s dynamic-boundary behavior.
- Strength: Tool release and use of common NLP tools (spaCy, Hunspell) make adoption and modular analysis straightforward.

- Weakness: Rule set description is too superficial—missing number of rules, ordering, coverage, conflict resolution, and representative hard-case examples.
- Weakness: Evaluation reporting is limited—raters judged system predictions without an independent gold, lacking majority-vote, per-instance “Bad” distributions, confusion matrices, and category breakdowns.
- Weakness: Data/domain scope unclear—no category distribution, potential domain/variety dependence (Hunspell, GB vs US), and no out-of-domain or ablation tests.
- Weakness: Unclear motivation and tagset rationale—insufficient justification for a new scheme or mappings to existing tagsets, reducing comparability.
- Weakness: Presentation issues—excessive table space over analysis, undefined “unusual” edit boundaries, and minor typos/reference inconsistencies.

- Question: How many rules exist, how are they ordered/disambiguated, and what are representative rules for ambiguous cases (e.g., MORPH vs VERB:FORM)?
- Question: What defines “unusual” edit boundaries, how often do they diverge from human segmentation, and how sensitive are per-type scores to boundary choices?
- Question: Can you report rater-study details—per-edit majority vote, distribution of “Bad” ratings, category-wise results, and a confusion matrix versus human types; why not create an adjudicated gold set?
- Question: How does performance vary with different taggers/parsers or dictionaries (US vs GB Hunspell), on non-learner text, and what is needed to adapt to other languages/genres?
- Question: Why introduce a new tagset—provide mappings to FCE/CoNLL and criteria for chosen granularity; and how often does M2’s dynamic merging/splitting alter evaluation outcomes?
