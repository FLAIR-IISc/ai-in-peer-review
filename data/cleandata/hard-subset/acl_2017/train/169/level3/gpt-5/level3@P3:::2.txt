Summary
This paper proposes an automatic method to annotate grammatical error correction (GEC) edits with error-type labels using a linguistically enhanced alignment to extract edits and a dataset-independent rule-based classifier to assign types. The key novelty is enabling per-error-type evaluation that includes precision (not just recall), applicable to unannotated system outputs. A small human study rates predicted types as Good/Acceptable in ≥95% of cases. The method is used to re-annotate CoNLL-2014 system outputs, yielding the first broad per-type analysis across systems, including multi-token edits and detection vs. correction.

Main contributions (as I see them)
1) A practical, dataset-agnostic pipeline to automatically extract and type edits in system outputs, enabling per-type precision/recall evaluation and analysis across systems and edit sizes.
2) An empirical study showing the approach’s applicability to many systems and error types, with human validation of tag quality.
3) A simple evaluation protocol that separates edit extraction from scoring, facilitating comparisons and new analyses (e.g., multi-token, detection vs. correction).

Strengths
- Moves the field beyond recall-only per-type reports: By auto-annotating hypotheses, the method supports per-type precision, recall, and F0.5, revealing complementary strengths among systems (e.g., CUUI vs. CAMB) and quantifying multi-token performance.
- Dataset independence and no need for manually tagged outputs: The rule-based classifier depends only on automatic markup (POS/lemma/deps/stemming/Hunspell), avoiding training-data biases and enabling broad application to legacy outputs (e.g., CoNLL-2014).
- Handles multi-token edits and fixes spans up front: This makes size-based analysis possible and clearer than metrics that implicitly modify boundaries at scoring time. The demonstration that MT systems fare relatively better on multi-token edits is informative.
- Broad coverage across systems and types: The study covers many systems from CoNLL-2014 and a wide set of error types, showing the method’s versatility and surfacing interesting findings (e.g., widespread difficulty with unnecessary tokens and ADV/CONJ).
- Human validation of tags: Experts rate ≥95% of tags Good/Acceptable, lending credibility to the rule-based approach; inter-rater agreement is reasonable.

Weaknesses
- Reproducibility and transparency: The rules that map edits to types are not described in sufficient detail (nor is the full ruleset released), including how POS/lemma/dependency/UD mappings, alignment costs, and merging rules interact. This threatens replication and hinders extension to new domains and languages. The paper also does not clarify how error categories were devised or how they relate to prior schemes.
- Evaluation design and analysis gaps: The classifier evaluation lacks detailed error analysis (e.g., confusion by type, boundary vs. typing errors), ablations (e.g., removing deps/lemmas), or concrete guidance for improvement. “Bad” ratings are ascribed to POS errors without systematic evidence; there is no analysis of rater choices across Good/Acceptable/Bad, nor tests with manual boundaries to quantify boundary-noise bias or the effect of having only three rating options.
- Over-reliance on English-specific preprocessing; no multilingual path: The approach is only demonstrated for English. It likely depends on language-specific POS tags, tokenization, morphology, and alignment behavior. Challenges such as discontinuous edits (e.g., separable verb prefixes in German) and clitics are not addressed, limiting generalization claims.
- Statistical testing and comparisons are unclear: The bootstrap setup is underspecified (what statistic was tested—F0.5 overall, per-type? sentence- vs. corpus-level resampling?). F0.5 may be a weak discriminator for some claims; per-type significance or multiple-comparison control is not reported. The gold vs. auto reference comparison seems to focus on boundary alignment, not classification quality, despite broader claims.
- Reporting and interpretability issues: Table 6 lacks raw TP/FP/TN/FN counts, making it hard to assess sample sizes and confidence. Some per-type results are likely based on very few instances, yet this is not flagged. Multi-token results are not contextualized by systems’ design/training (e.g., whether modules can propose/align multi-span edits). Some observed “failures” (e.g., unnecessary tokens) may reflect system design choices rather than evaluation failure. The paper also overlooks that certain GEC pipelines can output internal error tags via submodules. Minor: inconsistent reference formatting and missing pages/URLs.

Questions to Authors (for rebuttal)
- Rules and dependencies:
  - Will you release the complete ruleset, with exact decision order, features used (POS tag sets, UD mapping), thresholds, and all alignment/merging parameters to ensure replication?
  - How sensitive is typing to alignment parameters and preprocessing choices (tokenizer, POS model, lemmatizer, parser)? Any ablations or robustness checks?
  - How were error categories designed? Do they align with existing schemes (e.g., FCE, CoNLL annotations), and what motivated divergences?

- Evaluation methodology:
  - In the significance tests comparing gold vs. auto references, what exact metric(s) were tested (overall F0.5, per-type F0.5)? What was the unit of resampling and was multiple testing controlled?
  - Can you report per-type TP/FP/FN counts (and ideally TN) for key tables (e.g., Table 6) to assess data sparsity and enable recomputation of alternative measures/confidence intervals?
  - For the human study, can you re-run a subset with manually curated boundaries to quantify the bias from automatic boundaries, and provide a finer rating scale or pairwise comparisons to reduce central tendency?

- Scope and generalization:
  - How would you adapt the method to languages with discontinuous or non-projective edits (e.g., German separable prefixes, clitics, templatic morphology)? What changes to alignment and rules are required?
  - Can you demonstrate the pipeline on at least one additional language (or provide a concrete plan and anticipated challenges)?

- Analysis depth:
  - Can you provide a systematic analysis of when raters chose Good vs. Acceptable vs. Bad per error type, and detailed confusion for the “Bad” subset (beyond POS tagging errors)?
  - For multi-token edits, can you disaggregate by common patterns (e.g., phrasal insertions/deletions, reordering) and relate outcomes to system architectures/training corpora?
  - For categories where many systems scored zero (e.g., unnecessary tokens), can you verify whether systems simply did not attempt those operations (e.g., by design) versus the evaluation missing certain edit forms?

Additional comments
- Please clarify whether your gold vs. auto comparison also evaluates consistency of error-type classification, not just boundaries.
- Consider acknowledging that some systems can expose internal error tags (e.g., classifier components), even if not directly in final outputs.
- Please fix reference formatting and add missing page numbers/URLs.
