Summary and contributions
This paper proposes a practical pipeline for automatically annotating GEC system outputs with explicit edit spans and error-type labels, enabling per-type evaluation that includes precision, recall, and F0.5 rather than recall-only analyses. The method combines linguistically enhanced edit extraction (Damerau–Levenshtein with POS/lemma features) with a deterministic, dataset-agnostic rule-based classifier that maps edits to a 55-code inventory. The authors apply the tool to CoNLL-2014 system outputs to produce the first broad, per-error-type comparison across systems, including breakdowns by operation (Missing/Unnecessary/Replacement), multi-token edits, and detection vs. correction.

As I see them, the main contributions are:
1) An evaluation protocol and tool that standardizes error-type scoring on unannotated hypotheses, delivering per-type precision/recall and supporting multi-token and detection-vs-correction analyses.
2) A large-scale, comparative error-type analysis of CoNLL-2014 systems that reveals complementary strengths/weaknesses and highlights persistent challenges (e.g., multi-token edits).
3) A deterministic, dataset-independent error-type classifier with high expert acceptability (>95% Good/Acceptable on a 200-edit sample).

Strengths
- Enabling per-type precision/recall on hypotheses: Prior analyses often measured recall against gold annotations, leaving precision by type inaccessible. By aligning and typing both hypotheses and references consistently, the paper permits direct edit matching and per-type F0.5. This yields operational insights (e.g., CUUI vs. CAMB strengths on DET and VERB:FORM; system weaknesses on unnecessary token deletions) that were not visible in aggregate scores.
- Dataset-agnostic and transparent classification: The rule-based classifier relies on automatic markup (POS, lemma, UD tags, dependency, stemming, speller) and is fully deterministic. This increases interpretability and avoids dataset-specific training biases that complicate cross-corpus comparisons. The human rating study shows high acceptability despite noisy automatic boundaries.
- Broad applicability and empirical coverage: The approach is applied to all CoNLL-2014 submissions, illustrating robustness across diverse architectures (MT, classifiers, rules, LM). The paper further analyzes operation tier, detailed type-by-operation breakdowns (e.g., DET), multi-token edits, and detection vs. correction, offering actionable diagnostics for system builders.
- Simple, modular scoring: The explicit edit comparison (fixed spans) decouples extraction from evaluation. The demonstration that auto-processed references yield results comparable to gold (bootstrap p > .05) supports practicality; the discussion of M2’s tendency to inflate scores clarifies metric behavior.

Weaknesses
- Reproducibility: The paper does not describe the rule set used to map automatic markup to the 55 error codes (conditions, precedence/priority, conflict resolution, dependence on UD vs. PTB tags, handling of multiword expressions). Without a rule inventory or pseudo-code, replication, auditing, and extension are hindered—even if the tool is released.
- Methodological clarity and sensitivity: The classifier is acknowledged to be sensitive to POS tagging errors, but there is no systematic error analysis or ablation (e.g., gold POS vs. automatic, alternative taggers/parsers, effect of Hunspell dictionary choice). The pipeline’s dependence on alignment quality, parser errors, and preprocessing decisions is not quantified.
- Human evaluation limitations: The small English-only sample (200 edits) is rated with 3 labels and uses automatically derived boundaries that raters were instructed to ignore—yet boundary errors can bias perceived type appropriateness. There is no per-type breakdown, confusion analysis, or stratification by edit size; “Bad” judgments are attributed to POS errors without evidence beyond examples.
- Scope and language generality: The approach is evaluated only on English. The paper does not discuss adapting the inventory and rules to morphologically rich or non-configurational languages, nor how to handle discontinuous edits (e.g., separable verb particles) or movement. The current span model appears contiguous and may not capture such phenomena.
- Statistical reporting and interpretability: The bootstrap test setup is under-specified (unit of resampling, statistic—F0.5?, per-sentence vs. per-edit). The comparison of gold vs. auto references seems focused on span segmentation; it is unclear whether agreement on error-type labels was assessed. Tables omit raw TP/FP/FN counts and per-type support sizes; several high/low F-scores could be based on few instances, limiting interpretability. Findings about system failures (e.g., unnecessary token deletions) are not contextualized by system design constraints (e.g., MT phrase tables, decoding limits).

Questions to authors
- Rules and implementation: Can you provide the full rule inventory (or pseudo-code), including precedence, tie-breaking, and dependencies on spaCy/UD/lemmatizer/stemmer/speller? How would a reader re-implement the classifier from the paper?
- Sensitivity analysis: How does per-type performance change with different taggers/parsers or with gold POS? What fraction of “Bad” ratings are traceable to tagging vs. rule errors vs. boundary errors?
- Discontinuous edits: Can your alignment and typing represent non-contiguous edits (e.g., verb-particle separations, reordering)? If not, how would you extend the method?
- Statistical testing: What was the resampling unit and statistic for the bootstrap tests in Table 4? Did you test both boundary-only and boundary+type exact matches?
- Reporting: For Tables 5–6 and 8, can you release TP/FP/FN and support sizes per system and category, and mark categories with very small counts? Did you filter low-support categories?
- Generalization: What are concrete steps to adapt the inventory and rules to other languages (tagset mappings, morphology layer, lexicons)? Any preliminary non-English experiments?

Additional comments
- Please acknowledge that some GEC systems can output internal error tags; your contribution is to standardize evaluation across systems without requiring such outputs.
- Provide the tool URL, license, and environment details; encapsulate dependencies (e.g., spaCy v1.6 models) to ensure reproducibility.
- Improve references (venues, pages, URLs) and fix formatting inconsistencies.

Overall assessment
A timely and practically useful evaluation contribution that could become a community tool for per-type GEC analysis. However, the paper in its current form lacks crucial details for reproducibility and broader applicability, and several methodological aspects (sensitivity, statistics, human evaluation design) need tightening. I view the work as promising but not yet ready; providing the rule specification, clearer statistical/reporting practices, and a plan for cross-lingual generalization would substantially strengthen the submission.
