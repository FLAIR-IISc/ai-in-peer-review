Summary and Contributions
This paper proposes an automatic framework to annotate and evaluate GEC system outputs by error type without manual labeling. It uses linguistically enhanced alignment to extract edits and a rule-based, dataset-agnostic classifier to assign error tags, enabling precision and recall per type (and multi-token analysis). Applied to CoNLL-2014 systems, the approach reveals type-specific strengths/weaknesses. As I see it, the main contributions are:
1) A practical, annotation-free evaluation protocol that yields precision- and recall-by-error-type, addressing a long-standing gap in GEC evaluation.
2) A broad empirical analysis across many systems, including operation-tier and multi-token performance, demonstrating applicability.
3) A rule-based error tagger with high expert acceptability (≈95% Good/Acceptable).

Strengths
- Enables precision by error type, not just recall: This directly supports diagnostic evaluation and model development, and is actionable (e.g., DET vs VERB:FORM vs multi-token).
- Annotation independence and dataset-agnostic design: Rules depend on automatic markup, avoiding domain-specific training and facilitating reuse across corpora and systems.
- Multi-token edit handling and clear separation of alignment vs evaluation: Fixing spans and comparing edits directly is conceptually clean and exposes weaknesses in dynamic-boundary scorers.
- Broad applicability validated on CoNLL-2014 outputs: Demonstrates utility across diverse system families (MT, classifiers, rules) and reveals complementary strengths.
- Human validation of tags: High Good/Acceptable rates support the practical reliability of the rule-based labels.

Weaknesses
- Reproducibility: The rules used to derive error types are not described in sufficient detail, and dependencies on alignment/preprocessing (tagger, lemmatizer, parser, merging) are under-specified, threatening replication and extension.
- Evaluation depth: Classifier error analysis is cursory (e.g., “Bad” attributed to POS errors), potential rating bias from noisy boundaries and coarse options is untested, and comparison of gold vs auto references seems limited to boundary matching rather than full classification.
- Generalization: Evaluation is only on English; challenges such as discontinuous spans (e.g., separable verbs in German) and adaptation to morphologically rich languages are not addressed.
- Statistical and reporting gaps: The significance testing setup is unclear (what metric tested; was F0.5 used?), raw TP/FP/TN/FN counts per type/system are missing (hindering sample-size assessment and alternative metrics), and very small per-type instance counts are not flagged.
- Interpretation limits: Some system failures (e.g., unnecessary tokens) may reflect design choices rather than evaluation shortcomings; multi-token results need more analysis relative to training/data and known system constraints. The paper should also acknowledge that some GEC systems can output error tags via submodules.

Questions to Authors
- Will you release the full rule set, code, and exact alignment/markup configurations? How stable are results to these choices?
- Can you provide per-type TP/FP/TN/FN counts and confidence intervals, and clarify the significance testing target (F0.5?)?
- How will you handle discontinuous edits and adapt the method to languages beyond English?
- Can you re-run the human study with manual boundaries and finer-grained rating scales?
- Can you compare automatic vs gold classification (not just boundaries) to substantiate equivalence claims?
