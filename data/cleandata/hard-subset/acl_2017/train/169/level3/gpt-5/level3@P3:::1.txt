Summary
The paper presents a pipeline that (a) extracts edits via a linguistically-enhanced alignment and (b) assigns error types via a deterministic, rule-based classifier built on automatic POS/lemma/parse information and Hunspell. The aim is to make system output comparable to references at the error-type level. A small human rating study (200 edits, 5 raters) is used to assess the classifier’s appropriateness. The tool is then used to re-annotate CoNLL-2014 system outputs and references to enable per-type evaluation, operation-tier analysis (Missing/Unnecessary/Replacement), multi-token analysis, and detection vs. correction.

Contributions (as I see them; ordered by strength)
1) A practical evaluation tool that makes fine-grained, per-error-type assessment of GEC systems feasible without training data, enabling richer analysis for researchers, teachers, and learners.
2) A methodology that separates alignment from scoring and normalizes hypothesis and reference annotations, supporting straightforward, transparent edit matching and detailed analyses (operation tier, multi-token, detection vs. correction).
3) A large-scale, per-type comparative analysis of CoNLL-2014 system outputs illustrating how different approaches specialize on different error categories.

Strengths
1) Clear practical utility and fine-grained comparison. The pipeline enables per-type, per-operation, and multi-token analyses that existing metrics cannot, giving actionable insights (e.g., CUUI’s strength on R:DET vs. CAMB’s advantage on M:DET; multi-token edits are uniformly hard). This is directly useful to system builders and to educators wanting to diagnose learner difficulties or tool weaknesses.
2) Transparent, deterministic classification. Rules based on POS/lemmas and surface cues provide traceable, debuggable decisions, avoiding training bias and black-box behavior. This is valuable for error analysis and pedagogical feedback.
3) Separation of alignment and evaluation. Reprocessing references with the same alignment/classification used for hypotheses yields comparable objects for scoring, simplifying evaluation and avoiding hidden boundary heuristics in scorers. The bootstrap finding of no significant difference between gold vs. auto references (on F0.5) supports the practicality of this normalization.
4) Broader evaluation perspectives. The work usefully highlights detection vs. correction and multi-token performance—two dimensions often under-reported in GEC—thereby refining our understanding of what current systems actually do well.
5) Potential for community impact. If released, the tool can standardize per-type reporting, promote reproducibility, and accelerate research on targeted error handling.

Weaknesses
1) Insufficient technical detail on the rule-based classifier. The core contribution is under-specified: no inventory of rules (counts, coverage, complexity), no precedence/ordering or conflict-resolution policy, no concrete rule examples per category, and no statistics on fallback/UNK routing. Without this, it is hard to assess reproducibility, complexity, and where errors arise.
2) Evaluation design does not establish validity against an independent gold. The 200-edit rating study uses acceptability judgments on system-predicted types, with the caveat that “edit boundaries might be unusual,” but there is no gold standard with fixed boundaries and gold error labels for the same sentences. This conflates boundary and type errors and cannot yield per-type precision/recall vs. gold. The “>95% Good/Acceptable per rater” claim is also based on averaging rater percentages rather than a micro-average over items; no CIs/variance are reported; and the distribution of “Bad” ratings by type/operation is missing.
3) Overemphasis on CoNLL-2014 results and table narration. The paper allocates substantial space to listing per-type numbers already visible in large tables, repeating observations with limited synthesis. This crowds out crucial method details (rules, mappings, error scheme rationale) and reduces the paper’s signal-to-noise ratio.
4) Unsubstantiated claims about dataset/domain independence. The classifier relies on English POS/lemma/UD mappings, GB Hunspell wordlists, and hand-written rules; results are shown only on learner-essay domains (FCE, CoNLL). Claims of lower domain dependence need cross-domain (news/wiki/spoken) and cross-language validation or a more modest, clearly scoped claim. Manual rules and dictionaries are, in practice, domain- and language-dependent.
5) Tagset motivation and compatibility unclear. The paper introduces a new tagset without explaining why existing schemes (e.g., Nicholls, CoNLL-2014) were inadequate or providing mappings/conversion. The design principles, intended granularity, and known limitations (e.g., conflating modal vs. tense) should be explicitly justified, with discussion of compatibility for benchmarks and pedagogy.
6) Ambiguity in overall motivation. It is unclear whether the main contribution is a new system/pipeline, a new error tagset, or an evaluation protocol. Important details are omitted (rules, ordering, category coverage and distributions in test data), making the positioning fuzzy.
7) Edit boundary caveat is vague. “Boundaries might be unusual” needs definition, examples, and analysis of impact on type labeling and scoring; quantify how often auto reprocessing changes reference boundaries and the effect sizes on P/R/F.

Questions to Authors
- Rule set: How many rules are there per category? What is the exact matching/precedence scheme, conflict resolution, and default behavior? Please include representative rule templates and examples.
- Coverage: What fraction of edits fall into UNK or OTHER by corpus? Which categories are most error-prone according to the rater study?
- Rater aggregation: Did you average per-rater percentages or micro-average over all items? Please report CIs/SEs and the distribution of “Bad” by category and operation.
- Gold validation: Can you release a small gold standard (same 200 sentences) with fixed boundaries and gold labels to report per-type P/R and boundary error rates?
- Domain dependence: What happens if you swap Hunspell-GB for a US or learner-specific lexicon? Have you tried a non-essay domain or a different English variety?
- Tagset rationale: Why a new tagset? Can you provide mappings to Nicholls/CoNLL categories and discuss trade-offs?
- Edit boundaries: Please define “unusual,” quantify the mismatch rates between gold vs. auto boundaries, and show the impact on scores.

Additional Comments
- Exposition: Move detailed method (alignment parameters, rule inventory, tagset rationale) earlier; condense table narration; keep large per-type tables in the appendix.
- Data composition: Report the number of edits per category in the 200-sentence sample and in CoNLL-2014; indicate which categories are covered.
- Notation/style: Use “cf.” (not “c.f.”); typeset M^2 consistently; fix inconsistent capitalization and incomplete/broken references; ensure ACL-style formatting.
- Reproducibility: Provide code, rule files, tagset definition, and mapping scripts; specify spaCy/UD versions and Hunspell dictionary; state license.
