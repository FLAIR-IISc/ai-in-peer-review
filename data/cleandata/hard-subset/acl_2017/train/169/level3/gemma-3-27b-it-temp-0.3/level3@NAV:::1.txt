## Review of "Automatic Annotation and Evaluation of Error Types for Grammatical Error Correction"

This paper presents a method for automatically annotating grammatical errors in corrected text, enabling detailed error type analysis of GEC systems. The core idea – leveraging linguistically-enhanced alignment and a rule-based classifier – is potentially valuable, particularly for facilitating more granular evaluation than currently possible with existing metrics. The authors demonstrate this by re-evaluating the CoNLL-2014 shared task systems, providing a detailed breakdown of performance across various error types.

The primary contribution of this work, as I see it, lies in the creation of a tool and methodology for *post-hoc* error type annotation of GEC system outputs. This allows for a level of analysis previously unavailable and provides a useful resource for the GEC community. A secondary contribution is the detailed analysis of the CoNLL-2014 systems using this new methodology, revealing interesting insights into the strengths and weaknesses of different approaches.

However, the paper suffers from several significant weaknesses. The system description is remarkably superficial. The rule-based classifier is presented as a “framework,” but lacks concrete details. There are no examples of the rules, no discussion of their complexity, or explanation of the ordering in which they are applied. This makes it difficult to assess the robustness and generalizability of the approach. The paper overemphasizes the CoNLL-2014 results, presenting a large table with repetitive observations directly evident from the data. 

Furthermore, the evaluation methodology is questionable. Relying on human raters to judge the *appropriateness* of error type assignments, rather than creating a gold standard for the 200 test sentences, introduces subjectivity and limits the reliability of the results. The aggregation of rater scores (averaging percentages) is misleading, and the distribution of “bad” ratings is not reported. The lack of information about the test data composition (error category distribution) also hinders interpretability. 

Finally, the claim of lower domain dependence is unsubstantiated. The manual rules and Hunspell vocabularies are inherently domain and language-dependent, and the test data is limited to student essays. The motivation for designing a new error tagset, rather than utilizing existing ones, is also unclear. Numerous minor issues, including typos and inconsistent reference formatting, detract from the paper’s overall polish.



**Questions to Authors:**

1.  Could you provide concrete examples of the rules used in your error type classifier, including details about their complexity and order of application?
2.  Why was a new error tagset designed instead of leveraging existing, well-established tagsets like those used in CoNLL-2014 or Nicholls?
3.  Can you provide a breakdown of the distribution of “bad” ratings from the human evaluation, and discuss how this might influence your conclusions?
4.  What is a precise definition of “unusual” edit boundaries, and how might these affect the performance of your system?
