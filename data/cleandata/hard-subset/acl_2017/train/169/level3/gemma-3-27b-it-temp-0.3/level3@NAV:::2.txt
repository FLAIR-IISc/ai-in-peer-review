## Review of "Automatic Annotation and Evaluation of Error Types for Grammatical Error Correction"

This paper presents a novel approach to evaluating Grammatical Error Correction (GEC) systems by automatically annotating system outputs with error types. The core idea – leveraging linguistically-enhanced alignment and a rule-based classifier – is promising, as it addresses the long-standing limitation of evaluating GEC beyond simple recall. The authors demonstrate the applicability of their method by analyzing outputs from the CoNLL-2014 shared task, providing a detailed error type breakdown for multiple systems. The high agreement rate (over 95%) between automatic tags and human expert judgment is a significant strength, suggesting the rule-based classifier is reasonably effective. 

The primary contribution of this work, in my view, is the creation of a methodology for detailed, automated error type analysis in GEC, rather than a particularly novel classifier itself. The ability to move beyond recall-based evaluation and provide precision-level insights into system performance is valuable for the GEC research community. A secondary contribution is the dataset generated by applying this method to the CoNLL-2014 systems, offering a rich resource for future research.

However, the paper suffers from several weaknesses that currently preclude acceptance. The most critical issue is the lack of transparency regarding the rules used to derive error types. Without a clear description of these rules, reproducibility is impossible, and the generalizability of the approach is questionable. The classifier evaluation is superficial; a more in-depth error analysis is needed to understand the classifier’s limitations and identify areas for improvement. Furthermore, the evaluation is limited to English, and the authors do not discuss the challenges of adapting the approach to other languages with different linguistic structures.

Several other concerns detract from the paper’s overall quality. The origin and rationale behind the chosen error categories are unclear, and their relationship to existing error taxonomies is not discussed. The dependence of error tag assignment on alignment and preprocessing is acknowledged but not detailed. The method’s potential struggles with discontinuous edit spans are not addressed. The significance testing setup is unclear, and the use of F0.5 as a discriminatory metric is debatable. Finally, the paper lacks raw counts for true positives, false positives, etc., hindering a thorough assessment of the results.

**Questions to Authors:**

1.  Please provide a detailed description of the rules used to classify edits into different error types.
2.  What steps would be necessary to adapt this approach to languages other than English, and what challenges might be encountered?
3.  Can you provide a more detailed error analysis of the classifier, including examples of misclassifications and potential causes?
4.  How do the chosen error categories relate to existing error taxonomies in the GEC literature?
