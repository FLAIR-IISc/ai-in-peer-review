Summary
This paper proposes a deterministic, dataset-agnostic pipeline to automatically annotate grammatical error corrections with explicit edit spans and error types, enabling fine-grained evaluation across systems and categories. It combines a linguistically-enhanced edit alignment (Damerau-Levenshtein with POS/lemma costs) with a new rule-based error type framework that depends only on automatic markup (POS/UD tags, lemmas, stems, deps, Hunspell). A small manual study finds high appropriateness of predicted types (>95% Good or Acceptable), and the authors use the tool to re-annotate CoNLL-2014 hypotheses and references, producing the first comprehensive error-type analysis across teams. They further argue that using automatically annotated references yields results comparable to gold under both M2 and their exact-edit comparison, and highlight limitations of M2’s dynamic alignment.

Main contributions (as I see them)
1) A new, deterministic, rule-based error typing framework and tool that is dataset- and scheme-agnostic and operates solely on automatic linguistic markup; this enables transparent, reproducible, inter-operable error-type evaluation.
2) A simple edit-level evaluation protocol that separates alignment from scoring and supports exact, error-type-aware comparisons across hypotheses and references.
3) A comprehensive, first-of-its-kind error-type analysis of CoNLL-2014 system outputs (including operation tiers, detailed category breakdowns, multi-token edits, and detection vs. correction), yielding actionable insights into system strengths/weaknesses.

Strengths
- Novel, practical infrastructure contribution: The rule-based, dataset-agnostic error typing fills a major gap not addressed by popular GEC metrics (e.g., M2, GLEU/I-measure variants) which do not provide per-type scores. This can reshape how the community diagnoses and reports GEC results.
- Transparency and determinism: Rules tied to automatic markup make decisions explainable, reproducible, and consistent, avoiding annotator and model bias. This is valuable for shared tasks, benchmarking, and error profiling.
- Empirical utility demonstrated at scale: Applying the tool to all CoNLL-2014 outputs reveals substantive findings (e.g., widespread difficulty with unnecessary token errors; classifier advantages for VERB:FORM; MT advantages on multi-token edits; distinct strengths across teams), which are hard to obtain without this framework.
- Sensible separation of concerns: Treating alignment and evaluation as separate tasks simplifies scoring semantics and exposes M2 behavior that can inflate scores via dynamic boundary choices. The exact-edit comparison is intuitive and auditable.
- Early but positive validation: The expert rating study, despite being small, shows strong perceived appropriateness of categories under realistic, noisy conditions, suggesting the rule base has good face validity.

Weaknesses
- Limited validation depth and scope: The manual evaluation is small (200 edits, 5 raters) with coarse labels (Good/Acceptable/Bad). There is no error-type confusion analysis, no breakdown by operation tier or edit length, and no cross-domain validation. The claim that “auto annotations are as good as human” in evaluation is bold given modest evidence (non-significant differences don’t imply equivalence).
- Heavy reliance on automatic tagging and parsing with unknown robustness: The framework’s accuracy hinges on POS/lemma/UD/deps quality. The paper shows tagger-induced misclassifications (e.g., NOUN:NUM vs VERB:SVA) but provides no sensitivity analysis across taggers, versions, or domains, nor ablations quantifying the impact of each markup source.
- Design choices and coverage not fully justified: The category inventory and precedence rules (e.g., modals folded into tense; MORPH as a catch-all) are pragmatic but somewhat ad hoc. It’s unclear how overlapping rules are resolved, how conflicts are handled, or how often the UNK/OTHER categories are used. Multi-token and paraphrastic edits remain hard to type; guidance on extensibility is limited.
- Evaluation protocol strictness: Exact span-and-correction matching penalizes near-misses and synonymous corrections, potentially underestimating systems oriented toward fluency. While this is acknowledged, the paper doesn’t explore approximate matching variants (e.g., relaxed span boundaries, paraphrase-aware comparison) or quantify trade-offs.
- Reproducibility details: The tool is “to be released,” but no link/commit/version is provided. The rule set itself, error precedence, and configuration (e.g., UD mapping table, Hunspell version) should be documented to ensure faithful replication. Reference formatting and some citations are also incomplete.

Questions to authors
- Can you release the full rule set, precedence order, and decision logic (including fallbacks to OTHER/UNK), along with a stable tool link and versioned configuration?
- How sensitive is the classifier to the choice of POS/dependency tagger? Can you report results with at least one alternative tagger or a small gold POS subset to estimate upper bounds?
- Do you have a confusion analysis from the manual evaluation (which categories are most often mis-typed)? What proportion of errors fall into OTHER/UNK?
- Can you quantify the impact of each automatic markup source (POS, lemma, deps, stemming, Hunspell) via ablations?
- For evaluation, could you report results with a relaxed comparison (e.g., merged spans, synonym lists, or token-level overlap) to show robustness relative to M2 and to your exact matching?
- How often do your automatic references split/merge gold edits, and what is the empirical effect on per-type scores?

Recommendation
Weak accept. The paper offers a useful, novel, and bold infrastructure contribution that enables a new level of diagnostic GEC evaluation. Despite limitations in validation depth and dependencies on automatic markup, the approach is impactful and timely, and releasing the tool/rules would likely benefit the community.
