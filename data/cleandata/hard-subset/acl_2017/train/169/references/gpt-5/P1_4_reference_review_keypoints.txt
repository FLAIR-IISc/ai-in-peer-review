- Proposes an automatic pipeline that annotates GEC edits with explicit spans and error types using POS/lemma-aware Damerau–Levenshtein and deterministic rule-based classification.  
- Uses automatic linguistic markup (POS, lemmas, stems, UD, dependencies, Hunspell) to drive a deterministic error-type classifier.  
- Manual study (5 experts, 200 edits) reports 86.3% Good, 10.0% Acceptable, 3.7% Bad type assignments; κfree = 0.724.  
- Re-annotates CoNLL-2014 outputs and references, providing per-type and per-operation evaluations and diagnostics (multi-token, detection vs. correction).  
- Finds system-level F0.5 against auto-reprocessed references similar to gold (no significant difference) and argues M2 inflates scores via dynamic boundary selection.  

Main contributions:
- A dataset-agnostic, rule-based error-typing framework and tool for consistent, interpretable error-type evaluation.  
- An evaluation protocol that separates edit extraction from scoring and supports direct span comparison and rich diagnostics.  
- A comprehensive re-analysis of CoNLL-2014 producing actionable per-type system insights.  

Strengths:
- Practical impact and standardization: enables detailed, comparable analyses across systems without retraining.  
- Interpretability and transparency: deterministic POS/UD-grounded rules make decisions explainable and auditable.  
- Reasonable classifier quality: 96.3% Good/Acceptable judgments with substantial agreement; bad cases mostly due to upstream POS errors.  
- Methodological clarity: separating extraction from evaluation and critiquing M2’s dynamic boundaries reduces confounds.  
- Rich empirical insights: identifies specific system strengths/weaknesses (e.g., ORTH, VERB:FORM, DET) and multi-token/edit challenges.  

Weaknesses:
- Limited validation: small manual sample, coarse rubric, no per-type agreement or comparison to fully human-typed corpora.  
- Missing rule specification: full rule set, precedence, tie-breaking, and coverage analysis are not provided in the paper.  
- Equating auto and gold references: system-level F0.5 similarity doesn’t prove per-edit boundary/type equivalence.  
- Dependence on upstream NLP: no robustness analysis across different taggers/parsers; errors propagate to typing.  
- Unproven generality beyond English: experiments only on English and uses GB spellings, so portability claims are untested.  
- Rigid segmentation: strict span-and-correction matching may penalize valid alternative segmentations or paraphrases; no partial-credit explored.  

Questions for authors:
- Will you release the full rule set with precedence, tie-breaking, and a formal decision description?  
- How sensitive are results to choice/quality of POS tagger and parser; do different taggers change outcomes?  
- Can you provide per-edit boundary and type agreement between gold and auto references?  
- Do you have mappings from your taxonomy to human schemes (e.g., FCE/NUCLE) for validation?  
- What fraction of edits are labeled OTHER or UNK, and what are the dominant phenomena within them?  
- How language-portable is the tool under UD; which components need language-specific adaptation?  
- Will you provide scripts to convert between M2 and your format, reproduce CoNLL-2014 analyses, and what is the tool’s license?
