Summary and claimed contributions
- The paper proposes a pipeline to automatically annotate parallel GEC data with explicit edit spans and error types. It combines (i) linguistically-enhanced edit extraction (Felice et al., 2016) and (ii) a deterministic, rule-based error typing framework driven by automatic POS/lemma/parse features. The tool is used to annotate both hypotheses and references to enable error-type–level evaluation and analyses.
- A small-scale human study (5 raters, 200 edits) rates 86.3% of predicted types as Good and 10.0% as Acceptable. The authors then analyze CoNLL-2014 system outputs to produce per-type performance breakdowns (including by operation tier, multi-token edits, and detection vs. correction) and argue that reprocessing gold references with the same automatic pipeline yields similar overall scores to using human references.

Main contributions (as I see them)
1) A practical, transparent rule-based framework and tool for error-type annotation of GEC edits that aims to be dataset-agnostic and reproducible.
2) A detailed error-type analysis of CoNLL-2014 system outputs enabled by the tool, offering insights into system strengths/weaknesses (e.g., operation-tier and multi-token performance).
3) An empirical observation that reprocessing references automatically yields similar overall system rankings/scores to those with gold references, and a critique of M2’s dynamic boundary behavior.

Strengths
- Practical utility and transparency: The rule-based scheme is deterministic, interpretable, and does not require labeled training data. Given persistent confusion around error schemes and incompatibility across corpora, a clear and dataset-agnostic tool can help standardize error-type reporting and facilitate analysis across systems.
- Leverages established components: The edit extraction builds on Felice et al. (2016), which has been shown to approximate human edits reasonably well. The dependence on standard NLP tools (spaCy, UD mapping, Hunspell) lowers adoption barriers.
- Error-type analyses at multiple granularities: The paper demonstrates concrete, potentially actionable analyses (operation tier; specific POS/morph categories; multi-token edits; detection vs. correction). These results can guide system developers toward targeted improvements and combination strategies.
- Annotating both hypothesis and reference with a unified pipeline: This addresses edit-boundary mismatches and simplifies comparison. The bootstrap test showing no significant difference between auto and gold references (at least for CoNLL-2014) is encouraging for using auto-processed references in analysis workflows.
- Tool release: Releasing the tool (assuming it is robustly packaged) is valuable for the community and reproducibility.

Weaknesses
- Limited validation of error-type classifier accuracy: The main evidence is a small human rating study (200 edits, Good/Acceptable/Bad). There is no quantitative evaluation against gold error-type labels (even via a mapped scheme), no per-category accuracy, and no comparison to strong learned baselines under a compatible inventory. The claim that results validate the rule-based approach is under-supported for a new taxonomy intended for evaluation.
- Overclaim regarding “auto as good as human” references: Failing to find a significant difference in system scores (p > .05) does not establish equivalence. No equivalence testing (e.g., TOST), correlation per sentence/system, or agreement on edit sets/types is reported. Moreover, the conclusion rests on one dataset (CoNLL-2014) and one scoring setting. This weakens the methodological rigor of the central premise that automatic reprocessing of references is harmless.
- Sensitivity to upstream NLP errors not quantified: The approach hinges on automatic POS/lemma/parse in learner text, where tagging/parsing is error-prone. The paper notes mis-typing due to POS errors but provides no systematic analysis, no upper bounds (e.g., with gold POS on a small subset), and no sensitivity/ablation to different taggers/parsers. This is a critical risk for the claimed dataset-agnostic property and for downstream conclusions drawn from the analyses.
- Taxonomy design and mapping to existing schemes: The proposed inventory blends POS-based categories with bespoke morphological classes (e.g., VERB:TENSE also covering modals/passive). The design choices are only heuristically motivated, with no mapping experiments to widely used schemes (e.g., NUCLE/M2 categories) to assess compatibility. Without such mapping/validation, cross-paper comparability and adoption may be limited.
- Evaluation scope and external validity: Analyses focus largely on CoNLL-2014 (plus the 200-edit human check drawn from FCE and CoNLL). There is no evaluation on other corpora or languages, and no test of robustness to domain variation. The paper also critiques M2 but does not comprehensively compare against other metrics (e.g., GLEU, newer scorers) or quantify rank correlation.

Questions to authors
- Can you provide a quantitative evaluation of type assignment against a human-typed corpus under a mapped inventory? Even a partial mapping to NUCLE/M2 or a newly annotated subset with your scheme would strengthen claims. Please report per-category precision/recall.
- How sensitive are your type assignments to POS/lemma/parse errors? Can you estimate an upper bound using gold POS on a subset, and/or compare across taggers/parsers?
- For the “auto vs. gold reference” claim, can you report: (a) rank correlation across systems, (b) per-sentence agreement in TPs/FPs/FNs, and (c) an equivalence test with a predefined tolerance? Do results hold for other datasets?
- Could you clarify the disambiguation rules among VERB:TENSE, VERB:FORM, MORPH, and WO in borderline cases, and provide error analyses for frequent confusions?
- Will the released tool include the full rule set, configuration to swap taggers/parsers, and scripts to reproduce all tables? What is the license?

Additional comments
- The empirical insights (e.g., multi-token edit performance, operation-tier breakdowns) are useful, but their reliability depends on the correctness of automatic spans and types. Strengthening validation is essential for this work to serve as a community standard.
- The references contain formatting issues; please fix for camera-ready.

Overall, the paper addresses a real need and offers a pragmatic solution, but the validation of the core methodological claims is not yet robust. Strengthening the evaluation as suggested would significantly improve the case for acceptance.
