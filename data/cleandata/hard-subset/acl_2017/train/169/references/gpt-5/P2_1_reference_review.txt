Summary and claimed contributions
- This paper proposes an automatic pipeline to annotate grammatical error correction (GEC) outputs with explicit edit spans and error types, enabling fine-grained error-type evaluation for systems that do not provide error labels. The pipeline: (i) extracts edits via a linguistically-informed alignment (Damerau-Levenshtein with POS/lemma costs per Felice et al., 2016), and (ii) assigns error types using a deterministic, rule-based classifier driven by automatically obtained annotations (POS, lemma, stems, dependency, Hunspell).
- The authors introduce a 25-type scheme (55 operation-type combinations) designed for evaluation, with modularity to aggregate by operation (Missing/Replacement/Unnecessary), POS-like type, or more detailed morphology/tense categories.
- They validate the classifier with a small expert study (5 raters, 200 edits): 86.3% Good, 10% Acceptable, 3.7% Bad; κfree=0.724. They then re-annotate CoNLL-2014 system outputs and references, show that replacing gold references with automatic ones does not significantly alter overall F0.5 scores (bootstrap p>.05), and provide the first broad error-type analysis across teams, including operation-tier performance, detailed category breakdowns, multi-token edits, and detection vs correction.

My view of main contributions (in order)
1) A practical, deterministic, dataset-agnostic error typing framework and tool for GEC that enables system-level error-type evaluation without labeled training data.
2) A comprehensive error-type analysis of CoNLL-2014 systems (operation tier, per-category, multi-token, detection vs correction), offering actionable insights into complementary strengths of different approaches.
3) Methodological clarification separating edit extraction from evaluation, with evidence that auto-annotated references can substitute for gold references without materially affecting overall scores.

Strengths
- Addresses a clear gap with tangible impact: The inability to compute error-type scores for unannotated system outputs has hindered diagnosis and progress in GEC. Providing a tool and a scheme that work “out of the box” is practically useful for the community and can standardize analyses across datasets and systems.
- Deterministic, interpretable classification: Rules grounded in POS/lemma/parse make decisions explainable and replicable. This contrasts favorably with prior learned classifiers that are dataset-specific and opaque; it also reduces annotator bias and variance. The expert study (96.3% Good/Acceptable) supports appropriateness of category assignments.
- Sensible decomposition of the pipeline and evaluation: Treating edit extraction and evaluation as separate tasks is methodologically clean. The experiment showing no significant difference between gold and automatically reprocessed references (with both M2 and their matching approach) increases confidence that automatic re-annotation does not distort overall performance.
- Insightful empirical analysis: The CoNLL-2014 breakdowns reveal non-obvious findings (e.g., many systems fail on Unnecessary edits; classifiers excel at VERB:FORM; MT systems better at multi-token edits; detection substantially exceeds correction for some teams). These actionable insights can guide system design and hybridization.
- Reproducibility and resource release: The paper commits to releasing the tool and uses standard off-the-shelf components (spaCy, NLTK, Hunspell), enhancing reproducibility.

Weaknesses
- Limited validation of typing accuracy and generality: The manual study (200 edits, English only, two corpora) is small for a 55-category space, and judgments measure perceived appropriateness rather than accuracy against a gold error scheme. There is no per-category agreement or error analysis by type. The “dataset-agnostic” claim is qualified by strong dependence on English-specific resources (PTB/UD mapping, Hunspell GB) and may not transfer to other languages or domains without retooling.
- Pipeline sensitivity and error propagation: The classifier hinges on automatic POS/lemma/parse quality; the authors note that “Bad” judgments often stem from POS errors. There is no systematic robustness analysis (e.g., ablations with different taggers/parsers, error rates by tagging quality), nor calibration of category decisions under noisy parses, which could bias type distributions.
- Evaluation framing and claims: The assertion that M2 “overestimates” performance is plausible and supported by examples, but the evidence is largely qualitative. A systematic, controlled comparison (e.g., on synthetic edits where true boundaries are known) would strengthen the claim. Similarly, the conclusion that auto annotations are “as good as” human annotations is based on overall F0.5 stability; it does not ensure that type-level distributions or multi-token spans match gold analyses.
- Scope of comparisons and related work: The paper positions itself against learned classifiers and M2, but broader discussion of alternative alignment/typing frameworks or subsequent GEC metrics is limited. It would help to map the proposed categories to existing annotation schemes and quantify coverage/consistency across corpora.
- Ambiguity and granularity in the scheme: Some categories (MORPH vs VERB:FORM/TENSE; OTHER) may conflate heterogeneous phenomena. The paper acknowledges trade-offs between informativeness and practicality, but clearer operational definitions and tie-breaking rules, plus illustrative edge cases (e.g., modal vs tense interactions), would aid adoption.

Questions to authors
- Category definitions: Could you provide the full rule set and decision order, with disambiguation logic for overlapping cases (e.g., MORPH vs VERB:FORM; modal vs tense; ADJ:FORM vs MORPH)? How are multiword expressions and phrasal verb particles handled?
- Robustness: How sensitive are type assignments to the choice/version of tagger/parser and to domain shifts (e.g., essays vs news)? Can you report per-category reliability when swapping spaCy for another tagger, or under injected tagging noise?
- Generality: What would be required to port the framework to other languages (dependencies on tagsets, lexicons, morphological analyzers)? Have you tried other English dictionaries or American vs British variants given Hunspell GB?
- Agreement analysis: Can you break down the human evaluation by error type and by error source (edit extraction vs typing), and provide examples of systematic failure modes?
- Reference reprocessing: Beyond overall F0.5, how do auto vs gold references compare at the type level (e.g., distributional shifts, multi-token edit counts)? Any cases where automatic re-annotation notably changes conclusions?

Overall assessment against reviewing criteria
- Originality: Moderate. The alignment builds on prior work; the main novelty is the deterministic, evaluation-oriented error typing and its application at scale.
- Soundness/correctness: Reasonably sound, but would benefit from deeper validation of typing accuracy and robustness, and more systematic evidence for metric claims.
- Clarity: Generally clear, with useful examples; more precise category definitions would help.
- Significance: High practical value for the GEC community due to the released tool and the comprehensive analysis; likely to influence evaluation practice.
- Resources/reproducibility: Strong, pending public release of the tool and rules.

I lean toward a weak accept, contingent on clarifications and expanded validation.
