Summary and claimed contributions
This paper presents a deterministic, dataset-agnostic pipeline to automatically annotate grammatical error correction (GEC) outputs with explicit edit spans and error types. The pipeline first extracts edits using a linguistically-informed alignment (Felice et al., 2016), then assigns error types via a rule-based classifier that relies only on automatically produced annotations (POS, lemma, morphology, dependency) and dictionaries. The authors apply the tool to CoNLL-2014 system outputs, enabling per-error-type evaluation, operation-tier analysis (Missing/Unnecessary/Replacement), detection vs. correction analysis, and multi-token edit evaluation. A small human study rates the appropriateness of predicted error types as largely Good/Acceptable. The tool is promised for release.

Contributions (as I see them), in order of strength:
1) A practical, deterministic, dataset-agnostic error typing framework and tool that assigns transparent, rule-based error categories from automatic markup, enabling standardized per-type evaluation of system outputs.
2) A simple edit-level evaluation protocol that compares hypothesis and (reprocessed) reference edits by exact span+correction match, making edit extraction and evaluation separable and allowing per-type precision/recall/Fβ and detection-only metrics.
3) An empirical analysis of CoNLL-2014 systems by error type and operation, uncovering complementary system strengths, weaknesses on multi-token edits, and limitations of the M2 scorer’s dynamic boundary behavior.

Strengths
- Practicality and transparency of the rule-based scheme: The classifier depends only on automatic annotations and explicit rules, making decisions inspectable and reproducible. This is valuable for error analysis and avoids domain-specific trained classifiers. The modular design (operation tier, POS/morph tiers) affords flexible granularity (e.g., R:NOUN vs. R:VERB:TENSE).
- Evidence that auto-typed edits are acceptable in practice: In a blind rating of 200 edits by five experts, 86.3% were “Good” and 10.0% “Acceptable,” despite noise from automatic alignment. Agreement (κfree=0.724) is reasonable. The paper also shows that reprocessing gold references with the same pipeline yields scores not significantly different from gold (bootstrap p>.05) across teams.
- Clear separation of alignment and scoring: By fixing edits and then scoring via exact matches, the approach makes evaluation simpler and more interpretable than metrics that embed alignment (e.g., M2). The critique that M2 can inflate scores via dynamic boundary choices is well-argued with concrete examples.
- Insightful system-level analyses: The per-type and operation-tier breakdowns produce actionable insights (e.g., widespread difficulty with Unnecessary edits; classifier strengths on VERB:FORM and ORTH; hybrid MT strengths on Missing/DET; general weakness on multi-token edits and fluency-oriented changes). These analyses are not obtainable with standard aggregate metrics.
- Resource contribution: The promised release of an annotation/evaluation tool that standardizes error typing across corpora and systems would be a valuable community asset.

Weaknesses
- Limited novelty: The alignment component is adopted from prior work, and rule-based error typing by POS/morph categories has precedent. The main novelty lies in engineering a deterministic, unified scheme and applying it broadly. For a top-tier venue, the conceptual delta may be viewed as modest.
- Evaluation scope is small and English-only: The human study covers 200 edits, not stratified by type/operation/length, and shows that many “Bad” labels stem from tagging errors. There is no quantitative comparison to alternative classifiers built on UD tags/lemmas or to other rule-based schemes. It is unclear how well the rules generalize across domains, corpora (e.g., Lang-8, NUCLE), or languages.
- Dependence on automatic markup quality without sensitivity analysis: The approach inherits errors from POS tagging, lemmatization, parsing, spelling detection, and tokenization. The paper acknowledges POS-induced mislabeling (e.g., NOUN:NUM vs. VERB:SVA) but does not quantify robustness. No ablation (e.g., different taggers/parsers, UD vs. PTB tags) or error propagation analysis is presented.
- Under-specification of the rules and categories: The paper lists categories and provides examples, but the classification rules, precedence, and disambiguation logic are not fully described, hindering scrutiny and independent reimplementation. The boundaries between MORPH vs. VERB:FORM/TENSE and OTHER vs. semantic paraphrase are somewhat ad hoc and may encode English-specific assumptions.
- Exact-span matching may be too strict for fair scoring: The evaluation requires identical spans and corrections, penalizing partial overlaps or equivalent decompositions, and excludes soft equivalences (e.g., paraphrastic yet acceptable fixes). While simplicity is a virtue, some discussion of trade-offs versus span-tolerant matching or equivalence classes would strengthen claims. Also, the exclusive use of F0.5 biases precision; it is unclear whether conclusions hold for other β or detection-only metrics at scale.
- Presentation and referencing issues: Some references are incomplete/duplicated; certain implementation details (tool versions, parameters like max-unchanged-words, tokenization settings) are scattered or missing.

Questions to authors
- Rules and implementation: Will you release the full rule set, precedence order, and mapping from spaCy/PTB to UD used by the classifier? How do you resolve conflicts when multiple rules could apply?
- Robustness: How sensitive are per-type scores to the choice/version of POS tagger, lemmatizer, parser, and dictionary? Can you report results with an alternative tagger or with UD-only tags?
- Evaluation: Can you provide a larger, stratified human evaluation (e.g., by type, operation, and edit length), a confusion matrix across categories, and error analysis for the main failure modes?
- Scoring choices: How do results change with F1 or F2 instead of F0.5? Can you report overlap-based matching (ignoring exact spans) as a supplementary view? For detection-only, please specify precisely how overlap is computed.
- Generalization: Have you applied the tool to other corpora (e.g., Lang-8, NUCLE) or to modern neural GEC outputs? Any preliminary non-English experiments?

Additional comments and suggestions
- Include an ablation swapping the alignment algorithm or adjusting its parameters to show stability of downstream per-type analyses.
- Provide per-type frequency normalization and macro-averaged scores to mitigate imbalance.
- Clarify category design decisions (e.g., MODAL vs. TENSE) and consider adding explicit guidance for ambiguous edits or an UNK-with-reasons facility.
- Improve citation formatting and ensure the promised code, configs, and annotated CoNLL-2014 outputs are publicly available for reproduction.

Overall: A solid, useful evaluation/analysis contribution with clear practical impact, albeit with limited methodological novelty and a relatively narrow evaluation. Strengthening robustness analyses and releasing full rules/code would significantly improve the paper.
