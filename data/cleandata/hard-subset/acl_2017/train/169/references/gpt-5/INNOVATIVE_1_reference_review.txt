Summary and main contributions
This paper proposes a deterministic framework to automatically annotate parallel GEC data with explicit edit spans and error types and then uses it to deliver fine-grained evaluation. It combines a linguistically-informed edit aligner with a rule-based error type classifier that relies only on automatically derived linguistic markup.

Contributions (as I see them), in order of importance:
1) A dataset-agnostic, transparent, rule-based error typing framework and tool that operates on automatic POS/lemma/parse to assign consistent error categories to edits, enabling per-type evaluation without labeled training data.
2) A practical evaluation protocol that re-annotates both hypotheses and references uniformly and evaluates by exact edit matches, decoupling edit extraction from scoring and exposing per-type precision/recall as well as operation-tier (Missing/Unnecessary/Replacement), multi-token, and detection vs. correction analyses.
3) A comprehensive re-analysis of CoNLL-2014 systems with these new lenses, surfacing actionable insights about complementary system strengths and categories that remain challenging.

Strengths
- Conceptual novelty with field-wide utility: Turning untyped system outputs into error-typed annotations in a deterministic, resource-light way is a valuable shift in GEC evaluation. The approach removes the training-corpus dependency of prior classifiers and meaningfully advances our ability to do per-type system diagnostics and comparisons.
- Transparency and reproducibility: The rules are deterministic and based on standard automatic annotations (POS, lemmas, dependency parse, stemming, spell dictionary). This yields interpretable decisions and facilitates debugging and extension. Tool release further increases impact.
- Sound evaluation design choice: Reprocessing the reference with the same automatic pipeline reduces hypothesis–reference mismatch and yields stable overall scores (no significant difference vs. gold with M2 and the proposed scorer). This is a pragmatic and defensible design given the goal of per-type comparability.
- Rich, actionable analyses: The paper goes beyond overall scores to expose operation-tier performance, detailed per-type precision/recall, multi-token edit performance, and detection vs. correction. These analyses reveal complementary strengths between MT and classifier-based systems and highlight neglected capabilities (e.g., unnecessary deletions, spell checking, multi-token edits).
- Empirical validation of the classifier’s appropriateness: Although limited in size, the 5-expert evaluation shows high rates of Good/Acceptable labels (>95% per rater) and decent agreement (κfree=0.724), lending credibility to the rules despite the noisy automatic boundaries.

Weaknesses
- Limited validation scope and metric: The manual evaluation covers only 200 edits and measures subjective appropriateness rather than accuracy against a gold-typed corpus. There is no large-scale, per-type agreement study versus human-typed categories or any cross-corpus validation. This makes it hard to quantify biases introduced by automatic POS/parse errors or to estimate true per-type precision/recall of the classifier.
- Language generality is under-substantiated: While called dataset-agnostic, the framework is English-centric (spaCy English models, Penn-to-UD mapping, GB Hunspell). Portability to other languages with different morphology, scripts, or error phenomena is not evaluated, and requirements for language adaptation are not detailed.
- Taxonomy design and mapping: The chosen 25 base types (55 combinations) are reasonable, but the rationale and trade-offs versus existing schemes are only qualitatively discussed. There is no mapping to prevalent annotation standards across corpora, nor evidence that the categories capture pedagogically or application-relevant distinctions beyond convenience for rule writing.
- Auto re-annotation of references trades gold signal for uniformity without deeper analysis: The equivalence to gold is demonstrated only on aggregate F0.5, not on per-type agreement or span agreement. It remains unclear how much category drift and span fragmentation/merging the auto step introduces and how that affects downstream per-type conclusions.
- Missing ablations and robustness checks: There is no analysis of the contribution of individual rule groups, the impact of specific taggers/parsers, or sensitivity to tagging/parsing errors. Given the reliance on automatic markup, such a study would strengthen confidence in conclusions, especially for categories like VERB:SVA or NOUN:NUM.

Questions to authors
- Portability: What exact resources and language-specific components are required to port the framework to another language (e.g., POS/lemmatizer quality thresholds, morphological analyzers, dictionaries)? Do you have preliminary results on any non-English data?
- Robustness: Can you quantify how often POS/lemma/parse errors change the assigned error type (e.g., NOUN:NUM vs. VERB:SVA) and how overall per-type scores vary if you swap taggers/parsers or degrade tagging accuracy?
- Taxonomy alignment: Can you provide a mapping between your categories and those used in major corpora (e.g., NUCLE/CoNLL annotations) and discuss where mismatches occur? Could the tool output multiple granularities or configurable taxonomies?
- Reference re-annotation: Beyond overall F0.5, what is the span-level agreement between gold and auto references (e.g., overlap/Jaccard) and the per-type label agreement? How stable are team rankings per type under gold vs. auto references?
- Rule transparency and coverage: Will you release the full rule set, precedence/ordering, and coverage statistics per rule and per category? Are there known ambiguous cases where multiple rules compete?
- Multi-token edits: How often does the aligner merge or split multi-token spans compared to human segmentation? Can you report per-type performance specifically for multi-token edits to diagnose where systems fail?

Overall, while methodologically modest, the paper takes a bold, practical step toward standardized, fine-grained GEC evaluation and offers a tool that the community can immediately use. With stronger validation and clearer portability guidance, this could become the default for error-type-aware GEC evaluation. I am inclined to support acceptance given the high potential impact on how we analyze and compare GEC systems.
