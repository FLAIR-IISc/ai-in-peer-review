- Proposes a pipeline to auto-annotate GEC outputs with explicit edit spans and error types using a POS/lemma-biased aligner and deterministic rule-based classifier.
- Applies the tool to re-annotate CoNLL-2014 references and system hypotheses, producing 25 base categories (55 with operation/type combos) for per-type evaluation.
- Small human study reports >95% of automatic category assignments rated Good or Acceptable.
- Claims the framework is dataset-agnostic, transparent, and decouples alignment from scoring.

Contributions:
- Presents a practical, deterministic, dataset-agnostic error typing tool for annotating unlabelled GEC outputs.
- Provides a comprehensive per-type analysis of CoNLL-2014 systems, revealing strengths, weaknesses, and metric inconsistencies.
- Proposes an evaluation methodology that reprocesses references to remove boundary mismatches and yields similar overall scores to gold references.

Strengths:
- Addresses a practical GEC evaluation need and offers a reusable, reproducible tool likely to benefit the community.
- Sensible modular pipeline separating alignment, typing, and evaluation for interpretability and explainability.
- Empirical evidence that automatic re-annotation preserves overall evaluation scores across teams.
- Informative per-type analyses uncover novel insights (e.g., difficulty with Unnecessary edits, MT strengths on multi-token edits).
- Deterministic, rule-based approach makes decisions auditable and suitable for diagnostic evaluation.

Weaknesses:
- Limited validation: human evaluation is small (200 edits), coarse-grained, and lacks per-type accuracy or confusion analysis.
- Heavy dependence on automatic markup (POS/lemmas/dependencies/Hunspell) with no robustness study across taggers, parsers, or domains.
- Incomplete methodological transparency: full rule inventory, precedence, and conflict-resolution strategies are not provided.
- Strict edit-level scoring may underestimate partial matches and lacks correlation with human judgments to justify superiority over M2.
- Limited generalization evidence: no experiments across other corpora, guidelines, or languages to support dataset-agnostic claims.
- Analysis depth is limited: some findings are reported but not deeply probed or statistically tested.

Questions for authors:
- Will the full rule set, precedence order, and code be released, and how are conflicting rules resolved or UNK fallbacks handled?
- How sensitive are type assignments to the choice/version of POS tagger and dependency parser?
- How are dialectal variants, proper names, and domain-specific terms handled in the SPELL checks?
- How are dual references in CoNLL-2014 handled during edit comparison and does auto-processing of both refs change conclusions?
- Can you provide per-category accuracy/confusion on a manually typed subset and error analysis for commonly confused categories?
- Have you tried the framework on other datasets or languages and what would porting require?

Additional suggestions:
- Provide an ablation of signals (POS types, lemmas, dependencies, stems) and their contribution to typing accuracy and alignment quality.
- Report correlations with alternate metrics or human judgments to validate strict edit scoring.
- Include a confusion matrix and illustrative failure cases to guide future rule refinement.
- Clarify WO detection criteria and interaction with multi-token spans.
- Fix minor reference formatting issues for camera-ready.
