Summary and contributions
This paper proposes an automatic pipeline to (a) extract edits between original and corrected sentences via a linguistically-enhanced alignment algorithm, and (b) assign dataset-agnostic error types to edits via a deterministic, rule-based classifier that relies on automatic linguistic markup (POS, lemmas, stems, dependencies, Hunspell). The authors then re-annotate both hypotheses and references to enable simple exact edit matching for evaluation and present an extensive error-type analysis of CoNLL-2014 system outputs. A small manual study (200 edits, 5 raters) rates the predicted error types as Good/Acceptable in ~96% of cases.

Contributions (as I see them)
1) A deterministic, rule-based error typing framework tied to automatic linguistic markup, enabling dataset-agnostic error-type evaluation of GEC outputs.
2) A practical evaluation protocol that reprocesses references to align with system hypotheses, allowing direct edit-by-edit comparison and fine-grained analysis (operation tier and error categories, including multi-token handling).
3) A comprehensive, error-type-level analysis of CoNLL-2014 systems, surfacing strengths/weaknesses by operation and fine-grained types.

Strengths
- Practical impact and tooling: The general idea of automatically assigning error types to unannotated GEC outputs, and reprocessing references to standardize evaluation, is valuable for the community. The framework can facilitate detailed diagnostics across systems and error categories, something previously hard to obtain.
- Dataset-agnostic design: The decision to base typing solely on automatic markup (POS, lemmata, UD mapping, dictionary) is principled and avoids dependence on corpus-specific schemes or supervised labelers, improving portability and transparency.
- Transparency and determinism: The rule-based approach makes classification decisions inspectable, addressing opacity/bias issues in supervised classifiers and enabling precise debugging of error-type assignments.
- Large-scale analysis with actionable insights: The CoNLL-2014 breakdowns (operation-tier, category-level, multi-token, detection vs. correction) provide useful comparative observations (e.g., differing strengths of MT vs. classifier systems; multi-token edits remain difficult).
- Separation of alignment and scoring: Conceptually decoupling edit extraction from scoring is clean and avoids some scoring-time boundary optimization issues (they correctly note that scorers like M2 can inflate scores by dynamic boundary choices).

Weaknesses
- Limited and indirect validation of the error typing: The only evaluation is a 200-edit subjective rating with coarse labels (Good/Acceptable/Bad). There is no type-level accuracy against a gold error-typed corpus (even a small one), no confusion analysis, and no ablations quantifying the contribution of each rule source (POS vs. lemma vs. dependency vs. Hunspell). The strong claim of dataset-agnostic reliability is under-supported.
- Heavy dependence on noisy automatic markup without robustness analysis: The authors note POS errors often drive “Bad” labels but do not quantify sensitivity (e.g., swapping taggers/parsers, oracle tags on a subset). Using spaCy v1.6 and Lancaster stemmer may be suboptimal; no experiments show that better taggers materially improve typing accuracy or that the framework is stable across tools.
- Reprocessing human references and claiming “qualitatively as good as human” based on non-significant difference is weakly justified: The bootstrap protocol is under-specified (resampling unit, per-system stratification, reporting of confidence intervals), and relying on F0.5 similarity alone is not sufficient evidence that automatic references capture the same phenomena as human annotations. Rank correlations across systems, and pairwise significance of rankings, would be more convincing.
- Inadequate statistical rigor in per-type comparisons: The many per-type F0.5 numbers lack counts (support per type) and significance testing. Several categories likely have very few instances, making the highlighted “best” scores potentially unstable. Without per-type instance counts, standard errors, or multiple-comparison control, conclusions are tentative at best.
- Insufficient specification and reproducibility of the rule set: The paper does not provide the full rule inventory, precedence/decision logic, or formal definitions for tricky cases (e.g., differentiating MORPH vs. ADJ:FORM vs. ADV; SPELL vs. ORTH vs. VARIANT; modal vs. tense decisions). The promise to “release the tool” is useful, but the paper should stand on its own with enough detail to reimplement or at least audit decisions. Additionally, language generality claims are untested; the framework appears English-specific (Hunspell GB, English POS/UD assumptions).
- Evaluation choices under-discussed: The use of F0.5 for all granular evaluations may not be appropriate across categories with different precision/recall trade-offs. Detection is defined via span overlap only, but this inherits all alignment errors; alternatives (token-level or BIO-level detection) are not considered. Multi-token evaluation is based on a small subset (~220 edits), yet broad conclusions are drawn without uncertainty estimates.
- Presentation and formatting issues: Numerous formatting artifacts (broken hyphenations; footnotes in body; dangling/garbled references; missing figure/table context; inconsistent notation; typos) hinder readability and reproducibility. References are incomplete/inconsistent and sometimes malformed.

Questions to authors
1) Rule specification: Please provide the complete rule set, conflict resolution order, and features used per rule (POS/lemma/dependency/stem/Hunspell), along with examples for ambiguous cases (e.g., SPELL vs. ORTH; MORPH vs. POS-based categories; VERB:TENSE vs. VERB:FORM vs. MODAL-like replacements).
2) Robustness to markup: How sensitive is error typing to the choice of tagger/parser/dictionary? Can you report results with a second tagger/parser and an oracle POS subset to quantify error propagation?
3) Gold for typing: Can you construct or map a small gold set (e.g., align a portion of an existing error-typed corpus to your scheme) to report type-level precision/recall and a confusion matrix?
4) Reference reprocessing: Please detail the bootstrap procedure (resampling unit; per-system vs. pooled; confidence intervals) and report rank correlations (Kendall tau/Spearman) between gold-reference M2, auto-reference M2, and your scorer. Do pairwise system comparisons preserve significance?
5) Per-type support and significance: For Tables 5–6, please include counts (TP/FP/FN) per team/type and perform significance testing or at least report confidence intervals. How many instances exist per type, and how stable are the “best” scores?
6) Language generalization: What would be required to port the framework to other languages (tagset mappings, morphology tiers, dictionaries)? Are any rules English-specific?
7) Tooling and reproducibility: Will you release code, models, and an annotation schema/spec? Can the tool preserve original segmentation/case and provide machine-readable outputs with rationales for each decision?

Overall, the paper tackles a meaningful and practical problem and offers a plausible solution with promising utility. However, the methodological validation is not rigorous enough, the rule system is under-specified, and the statistical analysis is thin, while numerous presentation issues detract from clarity. Strengthening empirical validation, detailing the rules, adding robustness analyses, and cleaning up the presentation would substantially improve the work. At present, I lean toward rejection despite the potential impact of the tooling.
