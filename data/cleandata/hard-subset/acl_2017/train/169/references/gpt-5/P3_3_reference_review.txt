Summary and main contributions
- A rule-based, dataset-agnostic framework to automatically type grammatical error correction (GEC) edits, driven only by automatic linguistic markup (POS, lemmas, stems, dependencies, spelling lexicon). This enables per-error-type evaluation without requiring corpus-specific training.
- A unified pipeline that (i) extracts edits via a linguistically-enhanced alignment (Damerau–Levenshtein with POS/lemma costs) and (ii) classifies edits into a modular scheme (55 possible types via M/ U/ R × 25 base categories). The same pipeline is applied to both hypotheses and references, permitting simple exact-match edit comparison.
- An empirical analysis of CoNLL-2014 system outputs with detailed per-type results, operation-tier analysis (Missing/Unnecessary/Replacement), multi-token performance, and detection vs. correction. A small human study suggests high appropriateness of the rule-based types.

Strengths
- Addresses a real evaluation gap: Per-type performance is crucial to diagnose GEC systems, yet standard scorers (e.g., M2) do not provide robust error-type breakdowns for hypotheses. The proposed pipeline is practical, transparent, and fills a widely felt need in the community.
- Dataset-agnostic, deterministic classifier: By relying on automatic markup rather than corpus-specific training, the method avoids domain-mismatch issues and classifier bias across annotation schemes. Determinism improves interpretability and reproducibility; rules can be inspected and debated, which is valuable for community adoption.
- Separation of edit extraction and evaluation: Fixing spans and using exact-match edit comparison makes the evaluation step simple and auditable. The demonstration that reprocessing references with the same pipeline yields system-level scores similar to gold (via bootstrap) is useful evidence that the automatic reference is serviceable for comparative evaluation.
- Insightful analysis of CoNLL-2014: The per-type breakdowns reveal complementary strengths across systems (e.g., CUUI on VERB:FORM and ORTH vs. CAMB on DET and PREP), the widespread difficulty with Unnecessary deletions and multi-token edits, and detection–correction gaps. These findings can concretely guide future system design and hybridization.
- Tool release: If delivered, the tool can standardize reporting and enable consistent, fine-grained evaluations across future work, with likely high impact.

Weaknesses
- Limited validation of the classifier: The manual evaluation covers 200 edits with five raters and measures perceived appropriateness, not accuracy against a gold error-type scheme. There is no per-category analysis of rater agreement, no comparison to human-typed labels on an existing corpus (e.g., a mapped subset), nor ablations to quantify sensitivity to tagging/lemmatization/parsing errors. The strong claim that auto annotations are “qualitatively as good as human” is not fully substantiated, especially per-type.
- Under-specified rule set and decision procedure: The paper does not detail rule ordering, conflict resolution, or tie-breaking when multiple categories plausibly apply (e.g., spelling vs morphology, modals vs tense). Word Order and Morphology detection criteria are only briefly mentioned. Without more specifics or examples, it is hard to assess coverage, robustness, and maintainability.
- Heavy dependence on upstream NLP quality: The approach is brittle to POS/lemma/dependency errors (the authors acknowledge this), but there is no sensitivity analysis across taggers/parsers, nor confidence-aware handling. Given that learner text is noisy, error propagation could systematically skew certain categories (e.g., PRON vs DET, NOUN vs VERB).
- Evaluation focus and evidence: The equivalence between “gold” and “auto” references is established at the overall F0.5 system level on a single dataset. Missing are per-type correlations, rank correlations across teams per type, and confidence intervals for per-type scores, which would strengthen the validity of the proposed evaluation pipeline. The conclusion that M2 “overestimates” may conflate different design goals (dynamic minimal edit sets vs fixed spans); demonstrating correlation with human judgments at the system level would be more convincing.
- Generality and scope: The framework is described as dataset-independent, but it is English-centric (Penn-to-UD mapping, Hunspell GB, spaCy v1.6 models). Claims should be scoped accordingly, or preliminary results on another English GEC dataset and/or other taggers should be provided. Rare categories (e.g., NOUN:POSS, WO) suffer from data sparsity; the stability of F0.5 under class imbalance is not discussed.

Questions to authors
1) Rule specification: Can you provide a precise description (or release) of the rule set, including precedence, conflict resolution, and fallback behavior? How do you disambiguate categories when multiple could apply?
2) Sensitivity analysis: How sensitive are type assignments to the choice of tagger/lemmatizer/parser? Have you tested alternatives (e.g., different spaCy models or taggers) and measured stability (e.g., Cohen’s kappa between outputs)?
3) Validation depth: Can you report per-category human ratings and IAA, and/or compare to a mapped subset of a human-typed corpus to estimate accuracy per type? Correlations of per-type system rankings between gold and auto references would be particularly informative.
4) Span strictness: Your evaluation requires exact span and correction match. Have you experimented with relaxed matching (e.g., partial span overlap, character-level overlap) for multi-token edits, and does it change system-level or per-type conclusions?
5) Language and domain scope: Do you envision language portability beyond English with modest adaptations (tagset mapping, lexicons)? What would be needed for such transfer?

Additional comments
- Please include confidence intervals for per-type scores and for multi-token results; many cells are sparse and unstable.
- Clarify the bootstrap procedure (sampling unit, paired vs unpaired, number of references considered).
- The references section has formatting inconsistencies; please fix for camera-ready.
- Releasing the tool with comprehensive documentation, rule files, and scripts to reproduce all tables will substantially enhance impact.

Overall: This is a useful and timely contribution that can standardize fine-grained GEC evaluation. The methodological novelty is moderate, but the engineering, analysis, and potential for community benefit are strong. I recommend acceptance after addressing validation depth and specification clarity.
