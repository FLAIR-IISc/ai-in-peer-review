Summary and main contributions
This paper proposes a pipeline to automatically annotate grammatical error correction (GEC) outputs with explicit edit spans and error types, enabling per-type evaluation of system performance. The approach comprises: (i) linguistically informed edit extraction via a Damerau-Levenshtein aligner with costs biased by POS/lemma (Felice et al., 2016), and (ii) a deterministic, rule-based error type classifier driven by automatically obtained markup (POS, UD tags, lemmas, stems, dependencies, Hunspell-based nonword detection). The authors apply the tool to re-annotate CoNLL-2014 references and all system hypotheses, and analyze system performance across 25 base categories (55 with operation/type combinations), including operation-level (Missing/Unnecessary/Replacement), POS-level, and morphology/other categories. A small human study rates category assignments as Good/Acceptable in >95% of cases. The authors argue their framework is dataset-agnostic, transparent, and decouples alignment from scoring.

Contributions as I see them:
1) A practical, deterministic, dataset-agnostic error typing framework and tool that can annotate unlabelled GEC hypotheses and references for fine-grained evaluation.
2) A comprehensive per-type analysis of CoNLL-2014 systems, revealing complementary strengths, weaknesses on multi-token edits, and inconsistencies in how current metrics (M2) handle edit boundaries.
3) An evaluation methodology that reprocesses references to remove boundary mismatches and yields similar overall scores to gold references, enabling consistent, straightforward edit-level scoring.

Strengths
- Clear problem framing and practical impact: The paper tackles a real pain point in GEC—lack of error-type accountability for system outputs—and proposes a usable, reproducible solution. Releasing a tool that can annotate legacy outputs and future submissions will likely have immediate community benefit and standardize per-type reporting.
- Sensible, modular pipeline: Separating alignment from error typing and from evaluation is methodologically clean. Leveraging a linguistically enhanced aligner and deterministic rules leads to explainable decisions and avoids dataset dependence inherent in supervised classifiers.
- Evidence that automatic re-annotation preserves overall evaluation: The comparison of gold vs automatic references with bootstrap testing (no significant differences across teams) supports the practicality of reprocessing references for consistent edit boundaries. The discussion contrasting the proposed strict edit matching with M2’s tendency to inflate TPs is insightful and well-argued.
- Informative error-type analyses: Operation-level and fine-grained type results provide novel insights (e.g., widespread difficulty with Unnecessary edits, classifier success on VERB:FORM, MT strength on multi-token edits and NOUN/DET/PRON). The detection vs correction analysis is also useful and underreported in the literature.
- Determinism and interpretability: Rules grounded in POS/lemma/dependency and dictionary checks make decisions auditable, a property valued for diagnostic evaluation and tool-driven error analysis.

Weaknesses
- Limited validation of the classifier and pipeline: The human evaluation covers 200 edits across two corpora, with coarse labels (Good/Acceptable/Bad) and kappa reported only as κfree. While encouraging, this is small and does not quantify per-type accuracy, confusion patterns, or end-to-end agreement with expert taxonomy on a larger gold-typed set. The conclusion that auto annotations are “qualitatively as good as human annotations” is too strong based solely on unchanged overall F0.5 and a small appropriateness study.
- Dependence on automatic markup quality: The rules rely critically on POS/lemma/dependencies and Hunspell; mis-tags directly affect type assignment (acknowledged). There is no robustness study across taggers/parsers or domains, nor an ablation of which signals matter most. Using a specific tagger (spaCy v1.6) and a GB-centric dictionary raises questions about generalization (e.g., US variants, noisy learner text, other genres).
- Incomplete methodological transparency: The paper does not present the full rule inventory, decision order, tie-breaking strategies, or how overlapping conditions are handled. This limits reproducibility and makes it hard to assess coverage and corner cases (e.g., mapping “could→should” to tense, interactions between VERB:* subtypes and MODALs, or WO detection criteria).
- Evaluation scope and claims: The edit-level scoring is strict (exact span+correction), which likely underestimates partial matches. While the paper argues M2 overestimates, it does not provide correlation to human judgments or show that its stricter matching aligns better with perceived quality. Also, the claim of dataset-agnosticism would be stronger with experiments on multiple corpora with different guidelines (e.g., FCE, NUCLE) and demonstrating stable per-type conclusions.
- Analysis depth: Some intriguing findings (e.g., many systems failing on Unnecessary edits; multi-token edits being uniformly hard) are reported but not deeply probed. For example, deletion precision/recall trade-offs, system design choices, or the impact of edit boundary policies could explain these results. Statistical testing on per-type differences is also absent.

Questions to Authors
- Rules and reproducibility: Will you release the full rule set, precedence order, and code? How do you resolve conflicts when multiple rules apply? Are there fallbacks to UNK and under what conditions?
- Tagger/parser sensitivity: How sensitive are your type assignments to the choice/version of POS tagger and dependency parser? Have you tried alternatives or more recent models? Any quantitative robustness check?
- Dictionary and variant handling: How do you treat dialectal variants (color/colour), proper names, and domain-specific terms in SPELL? Would a different dictionary materially change results?
- Boundary effects and multi-refs: CoNLL-2014 has dual references. How do you handle multiple gold annotations in your edit comparison? Do conclusions hold if both references are auto-processed and a best match policy is adopted?
- Per-type validation: Can you provide per-category accuracy/confusion for the classifier on a manually typed subset, beyond “appropriateness” ratings? Any error analysis for the most confused categories (e.g., NOUN vs VERB:SVA)?
- Generalization: Have you tried your framework on other datasets or languages? What would be required to port the rules? Are there language-specific assumptions (e.g., SVA, determiners) hard-coded?

Additional comments and suggestions
- Provide an ablation showing the contribution of different signals (UD POS vs PTB POS vs lemmas vs dependencies vs stems) to typing accuracy, and of merging rules to alignment quality.
- Consider reporting correlations with alternate metrics (e.g., GLEU/chrF/COMET-style) or human judgments to situate your strict edit scoring.
- Include a confusion matrix and illustrative failure cases to guide future rule refinement.
- Clarify how WO is detected and how it interacts with multi-token spans.
- Minor: Some reference entries are malformed; please fix for camera-ready.

Overall, this is a timely, useful tool and analysis paper. With stronger validation and fuller methodological disclosure, it would make a solid contribution to the GEC evaluation toolbox.
