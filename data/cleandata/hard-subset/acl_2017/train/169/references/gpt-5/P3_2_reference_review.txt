Summary and contributions
This paper proposes an automatic pipeline to annotate grammatical edits with error types and to evaluate GEC systems by error category. The method has two stages: (i) edit extraction via a linguistically enhanced Damerau–Levenshtein alignment (Felice et al., 2016), and (ii) deterministic, rule-based error typing using only automatic mark-up (POS, lemmas, stems, dependency parses, Hunspell), mapped to a compact, dataset-agnostic error scheme (≈25 base types with Missing/Replacement/Unnecessary prefixes and morphological subtypes; 55 valid combinations). The authors validate the classifier via a small expert judgment study (200 edits; 86% “Good”, 10% “Acceptable”) and then analyze the CoNLL-2014 system outputs by type, operation tier, multi-token edits, and detection vs. correction. They argue that reprocessing references with the same automatic pipeline removes hypothesis–reference mismatches without materially changing overall scores, and release a tool.

Main contributions (as I see them)
1) A deterministic, interpretable, dataset-agnostic error typing framework and tool that labels system outputs using only automatic linguistic mark-up, enabling per-type evaluation at multiple granularities.
2) A comprehensive, type-level analysis of CoNLL-2014 systems, including operation-tier breakdown, multi-token edits, and detection vs. correction, surfacing complementary strengths of different approaches.
3) A practical evaluation protocol that separates edit extraction from scoring via direct edit matching and shows that using auto-processed references yields comparable overall scores to gold references on CoNLL-2014.

Strengths
- Practical and impactful tooling: The authors address a long-standing gap—lack of standardized error-type annotations for system outputs—by delivering a deterministic annotator and evaluation protocol. The ability to compute per-type, per-operation, and detection/correction metrics is highly valuable for system development, ablation, and error analysis.

- Dataset-agnostic scheme and interpretability: By tying rules to UD/POS/lemma features and avoiding training on any specific corpus, the framework avoids cross-corpus label incompatibilities and classifier domain bias. Deterministic rules afford traceability about why each label was assigned, a real advantage over opaque ML classifiers.

- Empirical utility demonstrated on a major benchmark: The CoNLL-2014 analysis is extensive, yielding actionable insights: e.g., widespread weakness on unnecessary-token deletions, multi-token edits, and a consistent detection–correction gap; complementary strengths (e.g., CAMB vs. CUUI) that motivate hybridization. The operation-tier and fine-grained type breakdowns are particularly informative.

- Sensible decoupling of extraction and evaluation: Treating alignment and scoring as separate tasks and scoring via exact edit matching is simple, transparent, and avoids the conflation often present in metrics like M2 that optimize edit boundaries internally. The discussion about M2’s tendency to inflate scores is timely and useful.

- Initial human validation: The expert judgment study, despite its scale, indicates that the rules produce largely appropriate labels even with noisy automatic extraction; inter-rater agreement (κfree=0.724) is encouraging.

Weaknesses
- Limited validation of the classifier and scheme: The small-scale, holistic “appropriateness” rating on 200 edits is insufficient to assess per-type accuracy, error sources, or robustness. There is no quantitative evaluation against a gold error-typing standard (even on a subset with mapped labels), nor an ablation showing the contribution of POS vs. lemma vs. dependency vs. dictionary. The claim that “auto annotations are qualitatively as good as human” is too strong given the evidence.

- Heavy dependence on noisy automatic analyses without sensitivity analysis: The rules hinge on POS/UD tags, lemmas, stems, and dependency parses—known to degrade on learner English. While the authors note POS errors, they neither quantify their impact nor test alternative taggers or settings. A sensitivity/robustness study (e.g., swap taggers, remove dependencies) would strengthen confidence in the approach.

- Evidence for “auto reference ≈ gold reference” is narrow: The bootstrap analysis focuses on overall F0.5 on a single dataset and does not probe per-type effects. It is not shown whether rankings or significance change at the category level, nor whether the conclusion holds with multiple references or other datasets. Additionally, the critique of M2 is qualitative; a controlled experiment varying the max unchanged words parameter or comparing to human judgments (cf. Napoles et al., 2015) would substantiate the inflation claim.

- Ambiguity and coverage in the error scheme: Some categories collapse linguistically distinct phenomena (e.g., modality within VERB:TENSE, MORPH vs. VERB:FORM), while OTHER and UNK catch-alls remain large. The paper argues for practicality, but without reporting per-type distribution, ambiguity rates, or common confusions, it is hard to assess how informative the scheme is across corpora.

- Scope and generalization: All analyses are on CoNLL-2014; no experiments on other GEC corpora (e.g., FCE, JFLEG) for per-type evaluation are reported, despite mentioning broader applicability. The English-specific resources (spaCy English, GB Hunspell) mean “dataset-agnostic” should be qualified as “corpus-agnostic for English,” which should be stated clearly.

Questions to authors
1) Can you provide per-type confusion diagnostics from the expert study (or a larger one), indicating which categories are most often misassigned and the main sources (tagging vs. rule precedence vs. extraction errors)?

2) How sensitive are the labels to the choice of tagger/parser/dictionary? Could you report label agreement and downstream per-type F0.5 when swapping spaCy for another pipeline, or when removing dependency features?

3) For the “auto vs. gold reference” comparison, do team rankings and pairwise significance remain stable per error type and operation tier? Can you share Kendall’s τ/ρ correlations across teams under gold vs. auto references?

4) How do you handle multiple-reference evaluation (two annotators) under the exact-edit-matching scheme? Do you take a union-of-edits reference, and if so, how do you resolve conflicts?

5) Can you share more details on rule precedence and coverage (e.g., when a change can be both NOUN:NUM and VERB:SVA under ambiguous tagging)? Is there a back-off or tie-breaking strategy?

6) For spelling, how do you handle dialectal variants (color/colour) and capitalization/ORTH interactions? Does GB Hunspell bias labels relative to US-trained systems?

Additional comments
- The release of code/rules is critical for reproducibility; please specify license, versions (spaCy model, UD mapping), and whether you will release the CoNLL-2014 re-annotated outputs (subject to licensing).

- Some references appear malformed; please clean citations.

- Consider reporting per-type frequency distributions and the proportion of edits labeled OTHER/UNK to assess coverage.

- A small ablation on multi-token edits (e.g., how often do your alignments split/merge multiword changes vs. gold) would contextualize the low system F0.5 on multi-token phenomena.

Overall, this is a useful, tool-oriented paper with clear practical value and insightful analyses. Strengthening the validation and robustness claims would substantially improve its impact.
