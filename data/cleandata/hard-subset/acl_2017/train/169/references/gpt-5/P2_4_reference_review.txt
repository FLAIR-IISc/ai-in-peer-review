Summary and main contributions
This paper proposes a deterministic, dataset-agnostic framework and tool to automatically annotate grammatical error correction (GEC) outputs with edit spans and error types, enabling fine-grained evaluation beyond overall scores. The approach (i) uses a linguistically enhanced alignment (Felice et al., 2016) to extract edits between source and corrected sentences and (ii) classifies edits with a rule-based error-typing scheme driven by automatically obtained linguistic markup (POS, lemma, morphology, dependency), yielding 55 possible error codes across operation (missing/replacement/unnecessary) and linguistic categories. The authors validate the classifier via small-scale human judgments and demonstrate the tool by re-annotating and evaluating all CoNLL-2014 system outputs, providing analyses by operation tier, error type, multi-token edits, and detection vs. correction.

In my view, the primary contributions are:
1) A practical, deterministic error typing and evaluation tool for GEC that produces comparable, type-level metrics for any system output without labeled training data.
2) A detailed empirical analysis of CoNLL-2014 systems by error type and operation, made possible by the tool, surfacing complementary strengths and weaknesses across systems.
3) An evaluation protocol separating edit extraction from scoring, along with evidence that auto-reprocessing references yields similar system rankings to gold references, and a critique of M2’s boundary-optimization bias.

Strengths
1) Practical impact and reproducibility: The community lacks a consistent, open tool for type-wise GEC evaluation. A deterministic, rules-based annotator that depends only on standard NLP pipelines and will be released is valuable for benchmarking, error analysis, and model development. Its transparency (traceable rule decisions) is a pragmatic advantage over opaque learned classifiers.

2) Dataset-agnostic by design: By relying on automatic markup rather than trained typers tied to specific annotation schemes, the approach sidesteps inconsistency across corpora and allows comparable cross-system analyses. This meets a tangible need given heterogeneous type taxonomies in prior GEC datasets.

3) Empirical insights enabled by the tool: The paper presents granular findings (e.g., which teams handled missing vs. unnecessary vs. replacement edits; per-type winners; difficulty of multi-token edits; detection vs. correction gaps). These insights are non-trivial, actionable, and hard to obtain without standardized error typing.

4) Sensible evaluation methodology choices: Separating edit extraction from scoring reduces confounds present in metrics that optimize boundaries on-the-fly. The bootstrap test comparing gold vs. auto references (no significant differences in F0.5) supports that the auto-reference pipeline is not degrading evaluation quality in this setting.

Weaknesses
1) Limited validation of error typing accuracy: The human evaluation covers only 200 edits across two datasets, with coarse Good/Acceptable/Bad labels and free-marginal kappa only. There is no per-type accuracy, confusion analysis, or stratification by operation tier or edit length. Given the framework’s centrality, a more rigorous study (larger sample, type-conditional results, inter-annotator agreement with fixed-marginal kappa, impact of boundary errors) is needed to establish reliability.

2) Heavy dependence on upstream NLP quality without robustness analysis: POS/lemma/parse errors on learner text can mislabel types (the authors note this), yet there is no quantified sensitivity analysis (e.g., swapping taggers/parsers, measuring degradation, or leveraging tagger confidence). This undermines the claim of dataset-agnosticity and raises concerns for generalization across domains and proficiency levels.

3) Language and corpus scope: Despite using UD tags, the rules, lexicons (Hunspell GB-large), and categories are English-centric. There’s no demonstration on non-CoNLL corpora (e.g., FCE, JFLEG) beyond small sampling in the manual study, nor on contemporary neural GEC outputs. This limits evidence that the tool generalizes across annotation styles, corpora, and time.

4) Methodological confounds between alignment and typing: The final labels depend on both the alignment algorithm and the rule set. While the authors reprocess references to align with hypotheses, this choice also discards human boundaries that sometimes carry meaningful structure. No ablation is provided to quantify how alternative alignment settings or merging rules affect type distribution and scores.

5) Comparison to learned typers is incomplete: Prior ML-based typers are discussed, but there is no head-to-head evaluation using a common scheme and metric (even on a subset mapped to shared categories). Without such a baseline, it’s hard to judge the trade-off between transparency and potential accuracy.

Questions to authors
1) Can you provide per-type human evaluation results (accuracy and agreement by category and operation tier), and a breakdown of where “Bad” judgments cluster (e.g., POS-sensitive categories like NOUN:NUM vs. VERB:SVA)?

2) How sensitive are type assignments to the choice of tagger/parser/lemmatizer? Please report results with an alternative pipeline (e.g., Stanza) and quantify variance in type distributions and scores.

3) How large is OTHER and UNK in practice across datasets, and what are the most frequent error patterns falling into OTHER? Could the rules be extended to reduce OTHER without sacrificing determinism?

4) Do you plan to support non-English languages? What changes (lexicons, morph rules) would be required, and how much of the current rule framework is language-specific vs. UD-generic?

5) Can you quantify the impact of the alignment component on final type labels and scores (e.g., varying merge rules/penalties, or comparing to a simpler Levenshtein baseline)? This would help isolate where errors originate.

6) For the M2 critique, can you provide concrete numeric examples (TP/FP deltas) and effect sizes across teams to substantiate “inflation,” and clarify whether rankings change materially?

Overall assessment
The paper addresses an important practical gap with a usable, transparent tool and demonstrates its utility through non-trivial analyses. However, the validation of the core error typing component is thin, and robustness/generalization evidence is limited. With stronger empirical validation (per-type human evaluation, robustness analyses, broader corpora), this work would be a solid contribution as an evaluation resource. As is, it meets the criteria of usefulness and clarity, with moderate novelty and limited methodological rigor.
