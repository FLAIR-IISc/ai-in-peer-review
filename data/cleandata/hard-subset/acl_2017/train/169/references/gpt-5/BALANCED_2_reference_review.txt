Summary
This paper proposes a practical pipeline for automatically annotating grammatical error correction (GEC) outputs with explicit edit spans and error types, enabling detailed error-type evaluation of systems that do not themselves output such labels. The pipeline has two steps: (i) edit extraction via a linguistically-enhanced alignment algorithm (Felice et al., 2016), and (ii) a deterministic, rule-based error-type classifier that depends only on automatic linguistic markup (POS, lemmas, stems, dependency parses, and a spell dictionary). A small expert study (5 raters, 200 edits) judged 96.3% of predicted error types as Good or Acceptable. The authors then re-annotate both CoNLL-2014 references and system hypotheses with the same pipeline and use simple edit comparison to compute scores and fine-grained analyses by error type, operation (Missing/Unnecessary/Replacement), and edit size. They argue that automatic re-annotation of references yields scores comparable to gold references and that their evaluation avoids issues in the M2 scorer stemming from dynamic boundary optimization.

Contributions (as I see them)
1) A dataset-agnostic, deterministic tool for edit extraction and error-type labeling that standardizes hypothesis and reference annotations and enables fine-grained, interpretable evaluation. The tool’s transparency and lack of training data requirements are compelling for the community.
2) A comprehensive re-analysis of CoNLL-2014 systems by error operation tier, detailed error types, multi-token edits, and detection vs. correction, yielding actionable insights into system strengths/weaknesses beyond overall scores.
3) Evidence that automatically re-annotating references can be used for evaluation without materially changing rankings, plus a critical discussion of how the M2 scorer can inflate scores by optimizing edit boundaries.

Strengths
- High utility and impact: The community has long lacked a standardized, automatic way to compare systems by error type. This work fills that gap in a way that is simple to adopt (no training), interpretable (rule-based), and reproducible. The promise to release the tool is important for uptake.
- Transparent, deterministic design: Rules grounded in POS/lemma/parse features make each classification decision explainable. This is valuable for error analyses and for diagnosing system behavior, in contrast to opaque classifiers trained on heterogeneous schemes.
- Solid expert validation for a hard-to-gold-label task: Although small, the study shows that experts consider >95% of categories Good/Acceptable despite noisy, automatically extracted edits; inter-rater agreement (κ_free=0.724) is respectable given the task.
- Clear empirical value demonstrated: The CoNLL-2014 re-analysis yields insights (e.g., widespread neglect of Unnecessary errors; MT systems better at multi-token edits; classifier strengths for VERB:FORM; detection vs. correction gaps) that can inform future system design and hybrid combinations.
- Clean separation of alignment and evaluation: Reprocessing both hypotheses and references with a common pipeline simplifies matching and avoids mixing alignment decisions into the metric, addressing a known pain point in GEC evaluation.

Weaknesses
- Limited and coarse evaluation of the classifier: The manual study is small (200 edits), uses coarse Good/Acceptable/Bad judgments, and lacks per-type or per-phenomenon breakdowns. Without a stronger intrinsic evaluation (e.g., on a subset mapped to coarse-compatible human labels) or ablations, it is hard to gauge performance by type or the contribution of each signal (POS vs. lemma vs. deps vs. Hunspell). The paper acknowledges POS tagging errors drive some failures; sensitivity analyses (e.g., with better taggers or gold tags) would strengthen claims.
- “Dataset-agnostic” and “bias-free” claims are overstated: While the method avoids training data, the rule set encodes design choices specific to English (e.g., reliance on Penn/UD tags, English spell dictionary, treatment of modal/tense). The taxonomy does not map to all existing schemes, potentially hindering comparability across corpora. A mapping study to other prevalent schemes or at least to coarse categories would help.
- Automatic re-annotation of references: The conclusion that auto annotations are “qualitatively as good as human annotations” is based on global F0.5 similarity and bootstrap tests across teams. However, the analysis does not show how span resegmentation changes the distribution of edit sizes/types, nor whether minority categories are affected. Per-type comparisons, span-difference statistics, and error bars would anchor the claim more firmly.
- Discussion of M2 inflation is suggestive but under-evidenced: The examples are plausible and align with prior critiques, but a controlled experiment varying the max-unchanged-words parameter and quantifying TP/FP changes would make the point more rigorous.
- Generalization and dependencies: The approach relies on spaCy v1.6, specific tag mappings, and Hunspell GB-large. There is little discussion of robustness to different taggers/parsers, learner domains, or other languages. Since the contribution is pitched as dataset-agnostic, cross-corpus (e.g., FCE/NUCLE/JFLEG/Lang-8) and cross-preprocessor experiments would be valuable.

Questions to Authors
- How many explicit rules are in the classifier, and what is their coverage by type? Could you provide a confusion snapshot (e.g., when an edit is misassigned, what are the top confusions)?
- What fraction of edits are labeled UNK, and how does that vary by corpus/type?
- Can you report a per-type breakdown of the manual evaluation (Good/Acceptable/Bad by category) and a sensitivity analysis using a stronger tagger or gold POS/lemmas for a subset?
- How often does automatic re-annotation split/merge gold edits (distribution of span differences), and which categories are most affected?
- Did you evaluate detection-by-type (not just overall) to confirm whether the observed detection vs. correction gaps are uniform across categories?
- Can you share ablations for the classifier (without deps, without Hunspell, etc.) and for the alignment step (e.g., token-level Levenshtein vs. linguistically enhanced) to quantify contributions?
- What is the plan for releasing the tool (license, versioned dependencies, and a stable taxonomy)? Is there a conversion table to other error schemes to aid adoption?

Additional Comments
- The taxonomy trade-off (informativeness vs. practicality) is well-motivated; still, users would benefit from guidance on ambiguous cases (e.g., modals vs. tense; morphology vs. spelling vs. orthography) and from a documented decision rationale for each rule.
- Reference formatting contains numerous glitches and incomplete citations; please correct these.

Overall recommendation
Weak accept. The work offers a practical, needed tool and demonstrates clear empirical value through a comprehensive error-type analysis of CoNLL-2014 outputs. Strengthening the validation (per-type manual evaluation, ablations, span-difference analysis, and a more rigorous study of M2 behavior) would substantially improve the paper, but even in its current form the contribution is likely to be useful and influential for the GEC community.
