Summary and main contributions
- A deterministic, rule-based framework to assign error types to edits in GEC output using only automatic linguistic markup (POS, lemmas, stems, dependencies, Hunspell), intended to be dataset-agnostic and interpretable.
- A pipeline that (i) extracts edits via a linguistically-enhanced Damerau–Levenshtein alignment (Felice et al., 2016) and (ii) classifies them into an error taxonomy with operation prefixes (Missing/Replacement/Unnecessary), enabling per-type evaluation of unannotated system outputs.
- An empirical analysis of CoNLL-2014 systems at multiple granularities (operation tier, error-type tier, fine-grained DET breakdown, multi-token edits, detection vs. correction) and a claim that auto-reprocessed references yield rankings similar to gold.

Strengths
- Clear motivation and practical utility: The mismatch between gold error-typed references and untyped system outputs is real; a tool to standardize edit spans and label error types fills a practical gap for error-type evaluation and system diagnostics beyond global F-scores.
- Deterministic, interpretable design: Rules based only on automatic annotations make the classification transparent and debuggable, avoiding opaque ML models trained on idiosyncratic schemes. The operation-tier prefixing (M/R/U) offers flexible slicing.
- Separation of alignment and evaluation: Treating edit extraction and scoring as decoupled steps simplifies evaluation and makes error-type analysis straightforward once both hypothesis and reference are normalized into the same representation.
- Informative case study on CoNLL-2014: The per-type breakdowns surface complementary strengths (e.g., CUUI on VERB:FORM/ORTH, CAMB on DET/PREP/Missing), highlight systematic weaknesses (e.g., Unnecessary deletions, multi-token edits), and reveal detection-vs-correction gaps. These analyses are actionable for future system design.
- Feasibility evidence: Despite noisy automatic boundaries, the small manual assessment reports 86.3% “Good” and 10.0% “Acceptable” labels, with decent IAA (κ_free = 0.724), suggesting the rules are often reasonable in context.

Weaknesses
- Validation is too limited and conflates components. The manual evaluation is only 200 edits across two corpora, with no per-type, per-operation or per-length breakdowns, no confusion matrix, and no comparison to a strong baseline (e.g., an ML classifier trained on the same markup). Raters were told to ignore potentially incorrect boundaries, yet category judgments can depend on boundary choices; thus alignment and typing effects are entangled but not disentangled. The claim that the approach is “dataset-agnostic” is not rigorously demonstrated (no cross-corpus stratification, no domain/language variant tests).
- Heavy dependence on automatic NLP and English-specific resources undermines the “dataset-agnostic” claim. The approach hinges on POS/lemma/dep accuracy and Hunspell GB-large (British) dictionary. CoNLL-2014 data are not uniformly British, and “color→colour” being labeled “SPELL” presupposes a normative variety. No analysis of how tagger/parser/dictionary errors propagate into error-type assignment (beyond anecdotal mentions). No ablation (e.g., without dependencies or stems), and no robustness to different taggers/dictionaries.
- Reference reprocessing and significance testing are under-specified. The key claim that auto-reprocessed references are “as good as” gold is supported by bootstrap tests with p > .05, but details are missing: unit of resampling (sentences vs. edits), multiple-comparison corrections across teams, confidence intervals, and power analysis. Moreover, replacing gold spans/types with auto-derived ones could systematically bias which edits match; the study does not quantify mismatches or analyze when auto spans diverge from gold (especially on multi-token edits).
- Taxonomy and rule set are insufficiently specified for reproduction and scrutiny. The paper mentions 25 types earlier, then shows 55 possible combinations with M/R/U; the mapping rules, precedence/priority among overlapping rules, handling of ambiguous cases (e.g., VERB:TENSE vs modal changes), and details of UD/Penn tag conversions are not fully documented. Without the explicit rule list and mapping tables, claims of interpretability/reproducibility are weakened. The promise to release the tool lacks a concrete link, versioning, or license.
- Methodological and reporting issues: 
  - The comparison to prior ML approaches reports incompatible metrics (F1 vs Good/Acceptable), which does not substantiate superiority. 
  - The claim that M2 “overestimates” is asserted with qualitative examples; there is no systematic quantification (e.g., controlled experiments varying max unchanged words). 
  - The detection metric is vaguely described; no precise definition or scores are reported, and Figure 1 is not reproducible from the text.
  - Some interpretations seem overstated (e.g., “team did not attempt” equated with TP+FP=0; they might have attempted but produced no matches).
- Numerous presentation and formatting problems:
  - References are severely malformed (e.g., “Gillian.,? \Q2015\E”, truncated venues/years, duplicate author tokens), which impedes verification. 
  - Footnotes and links are inconsistently placed; “Damerau-Levenshtein1” with misplaced note; “c.f.” used instead of “cf.” 
  - Tables are unwieldy/hard to read; numbering and cross-references (e.g., “Table 2” vs in-text code table) are inconsistent. 
  - Typos/orthographic issues and hyphenation artifacts abound. For ACL standards, this draft is below acceptable polish.

Questions to authors
- Reproducibility: Will you release the full rule set, precedence ordering, UD/Penn mapping table, stemming/lemmatization normalization steps, and the exact spaCy model versions used? Please provide a permanent URL and license.
- Robustness: How sensitive are error-type assignments to the choice of tagger/parser/dictionary? Provide ablations (e.g., swapping spaCy for another tagger, removing dependencies, US vs GB dictionary) and report per-type deltas.
- Validation: Can you provide a confusion matrix of predicted vs. human-judged error types; per-type Good/Acceptable/Bad rates; and an expanded sample size (>1k edits) across multiple corpora? Also, can you quantify the effect of automatic alignment errors on type assignments (e.g., by re-labeling with gold spans where available)?
- Reference reprocessing: What is the resampling unit in your bootstrap test? Did you correct for multiple comparisons across teams? Please provide CIs and an analysis of cases where auto vs gold references disagree.
- M2 analysis: Can you quantify the alleged inflation with controlled experiments (varying max unchanged words, or fixing boundaries) and report magnitude of changes across teams/types?
- Taxonomy design: Are categories mutually exclusive, and how do you resolve ties (e.g., VERB:TENSE vs VERB:FORM; modal vs tense)? How do you handle standard-variant spellings (US vs UK) so as not to penalize valid variants?

Overall evaluation
The idea and utility are valuable, but the current submission lacks methodological rigor, thorough validation, detailed specification of rules/resources, and adequate presentation quality. In its present form, I do not recommend acceptance. A substantially revised version with stronger empirical validation, clear reproducibility artifacts, and polished writing could be impactful as a resource/methods paper for GEC error-type evaluation.
