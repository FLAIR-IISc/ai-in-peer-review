Summary and contributions
This paper proposes a pipeline to (i) automatically extract edits between learner and corrected sentences using a linguistically-enhanced Damerau–Levenshtein aligner, and (ii) assign error types via a deterministic, rule-based classifier that relies only on automatic linguistic markup (POS, lemmas, dependencies, stemming, dictionary lookup). The authors then re-annotate CoNLL-2014 system outputs and references with this pipeline to enable per-type evaluation, and present aggregate analyses (operation tier, error categories, multi-token edits, detection vs. correction).

Contributions as I see them:
1) A practical, rule-based error typing framework and tool that is dataset-agnostic in design and produces interpretable error-type annotations without training data.
2) A large-scale re-annotation and analysis of CoNLL-2014 system outputs that surfaces per-type strengths/weaknesses across systems and edit operations.
3) An evaluation protocol that compares hypothesis vs. reference by exact edit span/correction match, and a comparison against M2 highlighting potential inflation from dynamic boundary matching.

Strengths
- Practical utility and potential impact: The tool fills a gap in GEC evaluation by enabling consistent, per-type analysis across systems without requiring gold type labels. If released and adopted, this could standardize reporting beyond single overall scores and support targeted system development.
- Transparent, interpretable design: The rule-based classifier is deterministic and auditable. This is a welcome contrast to opaque classifiers and facilitates error analysis and reproducibility.
- CoNLL-2014 analysis at scale: The paper adds value by systematically breaking down system performance by operation (Missing/Replacement/Unnecessary), error categories, and multi-token edits, revealing complementary strengths of MT vs. classifiers and highlighting general weaknesses (e.g., multi-token edits).
- Separation of extraction and scoring: Treating alignment/extraction and scoring separately is conceptually cleaner than metrics that conflate them; the paper illustrates how dynamic boundary choices can inflate scores (with references to prior work).
- Initial human validation: The small manual assessment shows high “Good/Acceptable” rates and reasonable agreement (κfree=0.724), suggesting the rules are often sensible despite automatic markup noise.

Weaknesses
- Limited and indirect validation of error typing accuracy: The core claim—reliable, dataset-agnostic error typing—rests on 200 edits judged for appropriateness (Good/Acceptable/Bad) rather than an evaluation against gold error-type labels with per-type confusion analysis. No per-type precision/recall for the classifier is provided. This makes it hard to trust the fine-grained per-type scores that drive the conclusions.
- Dependence on automatic markup without robustness analysis: The pipeline critically relies on POS, lemmas, dependencies, stemming, and dictionary lookup; errors are acknowledged to propagate (e.g., POS ambiguity ring→rings), but there is no ablation, sensitivity analysis, or comparison across taggers/parsers/dictionaries. The dataset-agnostic claim is untested on other corpora or domains, and English-only assumptions (Hunspell GB dict, specific tagsets) are not discussed.
- Reference reprocessing and evaluation design: The decision to discard gold reference spans and types and replace them with automatic ones is consequential. The bootstrap result (no significant differences) is reported without details (unit of resampling, per-sentence vs. per-edit, variance across systems), and without rank correlation analyses. The claim that auto annotations are “qualitatively as good as human annotations” is too strong given the evidence. Moreover, the ensuing per-type evaluations reflect the tool’s own classification errors on both hypothesis and reference, compounding bias.
- Novelty and positioning: The edit extraction reuses prior work (Felice et al., 2016), and the classifier is a set of POS- and markup-driven rules. While the resulting framework may be useful, the methodological novelty is modest; stronger contributions would require rigorous validation and cross-dataset generality.
- Presentation and bibliographic issues: Several references are malformed and incomplete. Important implementation details are sparse (full rule inventory, conflict resolution between overlapping rules, handling of edge cases like multi-edit interactions). The release status is “will be released,” which limits reproducibility at review time.

Questions to authors
- Error typing validation: Can you provide a gold-labeled subset with authoritative error types (e.g., mapped from a widely used scheme) and report per-type precision/recall and confusion matrices for your classifier? How often do type misclassifications change system-level rankings for that type?
- Robustness to markup: How sensitive are per-type results to the choice of tagger/parser/dictionary? Please report an ablation (e.g., POS only vs. POS+lemma+dep), and compare spaCy v1.6 to at least one alternative. What fraction of “Bad” judgments is attributable to each component?
- Reference reprocessing: Please provide rank correlations (Spearman/Kendall) between gold-reference M2, auto-reference M2, and your scorer across systems. For the bootstrap, what was the resampling unit and statistic? Did you control for multiple comparisons?
- Rule framework details: Could you release or at least summarize the full rule set and precedence/backup logic? How are conflicts resolved when multiple rules apply? How are multi-token, cross-POS edits typed?
- Generality and scope: Does the approach generalize to other corpora (e.g., FCE, NUCLE beyond CoNLL test, JFLEG) without re-tuning? Any preliminary results? What are the assumptions for extending to other languages?
- Multi-token edits: To what extent do your alignment choices bias the identification of multi-token edits? Have you compared multi-token spans against a gold segmentation?

Additional comments
- The observation about M2’s dynamic boundary inflation is interesting; correlating both metrics with human judgments at the system level would strengthen the claim.
- Please fix the references and ensure the tool and rules are released with sufficient documentation.

Recommendation
While the tool is potentially useful and the analysis is informative, the empirical validation of the error typing (and the evaluation design around auto-reprocessing references) is too weak for acceptance at this time. A more rigorous, per-type validation against gold labels, robustness studies, and clearer evidence that rankings and conclusions are stable would substantially strengthen the paper.
