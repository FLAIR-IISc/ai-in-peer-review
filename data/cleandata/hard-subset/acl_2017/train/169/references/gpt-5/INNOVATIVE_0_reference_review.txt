Summary and contributions
This paper proposes a deterministic, dataset-agnostic framework to automatically annotate GEC edits with error types and use these annotations to perform per-error-type evaluation of system outputs. The pipeline first extracts edits via a linguistically-informed alignment, then classifies them with a new rule-based taxonomy based solely on automatic linguistic markup (POS, lemmas, stems, dependencies). The authors apply the tool to all CoNLL-2014 submissions, providing detailed analyses (operation tier, fine-grained types, multi-token edits, detection vs. correction), and argue that reprocessing both hypotheses and references with the same automatic alignment/typing enables simpler, more transparent scoring.

Main contributions (as I see them)
- A dataset-agnostic, rule-based error typing framework that requires no labeled training data and yields interpretable, deterministic rationales for edit categories.
- An evaluation protocol that aligns and types both reference and hypothesis with the same automatic pipeline, enabling direct exact-span edit comparison and error-type scores, plus evidence and discussion showing how M2 can inflate scores.
- A comprehensive, per-error-type analysis of CoNLL-2014 system outputs revealing complementary system strengths, weaknesses on multi-token edits, and a detection vs. correction gap; accompanied by a planned tool release.

Strengths
- Novel enabling capability: The paper fills a long-standing gap in GEC evaluation—consistent, per-error-type scoring of system outputs without requiring system-side annotation. This is a meaningful shift that can steer research toward targeted improvements and system combination.
- Deterministic, interpretable labeling: The rule-based design is transparent and dataset-agnostic, avoiding the incompatibilities of trained classifiers across corpora. The manual assessment (5 raters, 200 edits) shows high acceptability (≈96% good/acceptable), supporting practical utility despite inevitable noise from automatic markup.
- Clean separation of alignment and scoring: Reprocessing references and hypotheses through the same aligner/typer yields a straightforward, reproducible evaluation based on exact edit matches. The analysis contrasting this with M2 is insightful and practically relevant; it highlights how dynamic boundary selection can inflate TP and suppress FP.
- Rich, actionable system insights: The analyses (operation tier, fine-grained categories, multi-token performance, detection vs. correction) are detailed and informative. Findings like (i) different approaches excelling on different types, (ii) widespread weakness on unnecessary edits and multi-token edits, and (iii) detection-correction gaps, are valuable signals for future model design and hybridization.
- Tool release and flexible taxonomy: The taxonomy allows evaluation at multiple granularities (e.g., by operation, POS, or specific subtypes), which broadens its applicability across research questions and facilitates standardized reporting.

Weaknesses
- Limited validation depth: The classifier evaluation is small-scale (200 edits, two datasets) and uses coarse Good/Acceptable/Bad ratings. There is no quantitative comparison to a gold, human-typed corpus, and no per-type reliability analysis. The paper acknowledges tagger-induced errors but does not quantify the impact or provide a sensitivity analysis (e.g., with corrected tags).
- Scope and generality claims: While “dataset-agnostic,” the method is tailored to English and specific tools (spaCy v1.6, PTB→UD mapping, Hunspell). The paper does not demonstrate cross-linguistic applicability or discuss requirements for morphologically rich languages; thus “dataset-agnostic” should not be conflated with “language-agnostic.”
- Taxonomy design trade-offs: Several design choices (e.g., folding modals into tense) are pragmatic but somewhat ad hoc, and their downstream implications for pedagogy or system diagnostics are not explored. There is no mapping to common human annotation schemes, raising questions about comparability and adoption in broader contexts.
- Claims about auto vs. gold references: The conclusion that auto annotations are “as good as human” is supported only by lack of significant difference in overall F0.5 across systems. It does not address per-type divergence, edit-boundary fidelity, or per-edit agreement; statistical testing on finer-grained metrics would strengthen the claim.
- Dependency on alignment and markup quality: The entire evaluation hinges on the Felice et al. aligner and automatic tagging/parsing. There is no ablation or error analysis to quantify how alignment or tagging errors propagate to type distribution and scores, nor guidance on how robust results are to alternative tools/versions.

Questions to authors (for rebuttal)
- Sensitivity to tagging/parsing errors: Can you quantify how often incorrect POS/parse assignments change the final error category? Do results materially change if you use a stronger tagger/parser or gold POS for a subset?
- Per-type reliability: Can you provide per-category Good/Acceptable/Bad distributions and a confusion analysis to identify which categories are most error-prone?
- Generalization beyond English: What changes (rules, resources) are needed to port the framework to other languages, especially morphologically rich or non-configurational ones? Any preliminary experiments?
- Taxonomy alignment: How does your scheme map to existing error taxonomies used in NUCLE/CoNLL and educational assessment? Could you provide a mapping table to facilitate adoption?
- Auto vs. gold references: Beyond overall F0.5, what is the per-error-type agreement between auto-processed and gold references? Any differences in multi-token edits or operation-tier distributions?
- Release details: Will you release the annotated CoNLL-2014 outputs, the alignment+typing tool, and the scoring script under a permissive license? Is the taxonomy configurable for future extensions?

Overall, I view this as a valuable, infrastructure-style contribution that enables a new level of diagnostic evaluation in GEC. While some validations are limited and several design choices are pragmatic, the conceptual advance—automatic, consistent, per-type evaluation of arbitrary system outputs—is significant and timely. I lean toward acceptance.
