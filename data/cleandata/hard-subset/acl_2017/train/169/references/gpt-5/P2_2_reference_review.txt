Summary and claimed contributions
- The paper presents an automatic pipeline to annotate grammatical error correction (GEC) outputs with explicit edit spans and error types. It leverages a linguistically-enhanced Damerau–Levenshtein aligner from prior work to extract edits and introduces a new rule-based, dataset-agnostic error typing scheme using automatically derived mark-up (POS, lemmas, morphology, dependency parses, spell dictionary).
- Using the same pipeline to reprocess references and hypotheses, the authors evaluate systems by direct edit comparison and provide detailed breakdowns by operation (Missing/Unnecessary/Replacement), fine-grained error type, multi-token edits, and detection vs. correction on CoNLL-2014 system outputs. They show that auto-reprocessing gold references yields near-identical overall scores to using original gold references with M2, and argue M2 tends to overestimate performance.
- A small manual evaluation (200 edits, 5 raters) suggests the rule-based types are judged Good or Acceptable in ~96% of cases. The authors plan to release the tool.

My view of the contributions (in order of strength)
1) A practical, deterministic, dataset-agnostic error typing framework and tool that enables per-type evaluation of any GEC hypothesis without supervised training.
2) A comprehensive, new analysis of CoNLL-2014 systems by error type, operation tier, multi-token edits, and detection vs. correction that reveals complementary strengths across approaches.
3) A simple, transparent evaluation protocol that separates edit extraction from scoring and shows that auto-reprocessing references does not materially change overall system scores.

Strengths
- Addresses a clear unmet need: The lack of error-type-specific evaluation has long hindered diagnosis of GEC systems. The proposed rule-based scheme provides fine-grained, interpretable categories that can be aggregated flexibly (by POS, operation, or specific subtypes). This is likely to be immediately useful for researchers and practitioners.
- Deterministic, transparent, and dataset-agnostic: All decisions are grounded in automatic linguistic mark-up and hand-written rules, avoiding incompatibilities between differently trained classifiers across corpora. This makes the method reproducible, explainable (you can trace why a type was assigned), and robust to domain shifts relative to ML-based typers.
- Careful, multi-view evaluation and actionable insights: The paper goes beyond a single scalar score. Operation-tier results highlight widespread neglect of Unnecessary edits; type-level breakdowns show where MT vs. classifier vs. rules work best (e.g., DET, NOUN:NUM, VERB:FORM, ORTH); multi-token analysis shows general weakness on complex fluency edits; detection vs. correction reveals sizable gaps for some systems. These insights can guide system combination and targeted improvements.
- Sensible scoring setup and discussion of M2: By aligning and typing both hypotheses and references with the same pipeline, the evaluation becomes a straightforward edit comparison with clear TP/FP/FN semantics, and avoids hidden optimizations in dynamic edit boundary selection. The observation that M2 can inflate scores is consistent with prior critiques; the proposed alternative is simpler to reason about.
- Resource contribution: Committing to release a tool that standardizes annotation and evaluation is valuable for the community.

Weaknesses
- Limited validation of error typing accuracy: The core claim (high-quality error typing) rests on a small, subjective study (200 edits, 5 raters) with coarse labels (Good/Acceptable/Bad). There is no evaluation against a gold, human-typed schema (e.g., where human annotators assign types under a consistent guideline), nor a quantitative comparison to existing automatic typers. This makes it hard to assess reliability of per-type scores, especially for rarer categories.
- Heavy dependence on automatic mark-up quality without robustness analysis: The authors note that mis-typed POS leads to wrong categories, but do not quantify sensitivity to tagging/parsing errors, nor test alternate taggers/parsers. Since the method is built on automatic analyses, error propagation could systematically distort certain categories (e.g., NOUN vs. VERB confusions).
- Claim about auto vs. gold references may be overstated: The conclusion that “auto annotations are qualitatively as good as human annotations” is inferred from non-significant differences in overall F0.5 when reprocessing gold references. This does not validate edit boundaries or error types per se; it only shows that overall system scores don’t shift significantly on this dataset. A more direct assessment of alignment quality and type fidelity against human references is needed.
- Insufficient methodological detail for full reproducibility of the rule set: While the taxonomy is listed, the specific rules, precedence, and disambiguation strategies are not fully specified in the paper. Without the released code, replication or critique of the rule base is difficult. An ablation study (e.g., removing morphology, removing dependency features) is also missing.
- Scope limited to CoNLL-2014 system outputs: The analyses are compelling but restricted to one testbed. Cross-dataset validation (e.g., FCE, JFLEG) would better establish generality, especially given differences in annotation guidelines and text domains.

Questions to authors
- Can you quantify the sensitivity of your error typing to the choice of tagger/parser? For example, how much do per-type F-scores change if you switch to another POS/dependency model or add simple domain adaptation?
- Do you have any evaluation against a human-typed error scheme (even on a small subset) to estimate precision/recall per category rather than Good/Acceptable/Bad judgments? Which categories are most/least reliable?
- Could you provide more detail on the rule engine: rule ordering/priority, tie-breaking, and handling of multi-label ambiguities (e.g., NOUN:NUM vs. VERB:SVA in ring→rings)? Is there a confidence score?
- How often do your auto edit boundaries disagree with human boundaries, and how does that affect per-type evaluation? Any statistics on single-token vs. multi-token boundary mismatches?
- Beyond CoNLL-2014, have you applied the tool to other corpora (FCE, JFLEG) and observed similar stability in overall scores and similar per-type patterns?

Overall assessment
- Originality: Moderate. The aligner is from prior work; the main novelty is a deterministic, dataset-agnostic error typing framework and its use for detailed evaluation.
- Soundness: Reasonable for a systems paper, but empirical validation of the typing accuracy is limited and relies on subjective judgments.
- Significance: High practical value. The tool and analyses fill a real gap and can influence how the field evaluates GEC systems.
- Clarity: Generally clear, with useful tables and analyses; however, the rule details and some references/presentation need polishing.

I lean toward acceptance as a practical, impactful contribution conditional on releasing the tool and clarifying validation and rule details; the methodological limitations should be acknowledged more explicitly.
