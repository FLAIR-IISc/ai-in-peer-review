Summary
This paper proposes an automatic framework to annotate grammatical error correction (GEC) outputs with error types and to evaluate systems by type. The pipeline (i) extracts edits via a linguistically-enhanced Damerau–Levenshtein aligner (Felice et al., 2016) and (ii) assigns error types using a deterministic rule-based classifier that relies solely on automatic linguistic markup (POS, lemmas, dependencies, stemming). The authors validate the classifier with a small human study and then re-annotate the CoNLL-2014 system outputs and references to analyze performance by error type, error operation (Missing/Unnecessary/Replacement), and edit span.

Contributions (as I see them)
1) A deterministic, dataset-agnostic rule-based error-type annotator for GEC that operates on automatic linguistic markup and exposes an explicit, modular error type taxonomy (with operation and POS/morph layers).
2) A thorough, type-wise re-analysis of CoNLL-2014 systems enabled by consistent auto-annotations (including operation tier and multi-token edits), offering diagnostic insights beyond overall scores.
3) A simple span-and-correction matching evaluation that decouples edit extraction from scoring, with empirical comparison to M2 and bootstrap tests showing no significant differences in overall F0.5.

Strengths
- Practicality and transparency: The rules are deterministic and interpretable, enabling exact post hoc reasoning for type assignments, which is valuable for error analysis and system development. The modular taxonomy (operation × type) is flexible and practical for diagnostics.
- Dataset-agnostic design: By relying only on automatic markup (POS/lemma/dep/spelling), the approach avoids dependence on corpus-specific guidelines and training data. This is a tangible advantage over learned type classifiers that do not transfer well across schemes.
- Useful system-level insights: The CoNLL-2014 analysis surfaces actionable findings (e.g., unusual weakness on Unnecessary edits for many systems; classifier advantage on VERB:FORM; MT advantage on multi-token edits), which could inform system combinations and future research directions (e.g., fluency/multi-token).
- Decoupled evaluation: The proposed exact-span edit comparison clarifies evaluation semantics, reducing the confounding between evaluation and alignment present in metrics that predict edit boundaries dynamically. The M2 discussion is useful context for the community.
- Human appropriateness ratings: Despite noise from auto alignment, five GEC researchers rated 95% of types as Good/Acceptable, and inter-rater agreement (κ_free = 0.724) suggests reasonable consistency.

Weaknesses
- Limited methodological novelty and missing rule details: The alignment component is prior work; the new contribution is the rule-based classifier, but the paper does not provide sufficient detail on the rules, precedence, tie-breaking, or coverage to enable independent verification. Without a full rule specification and ablations (e.g., which features matter, robustness to tagging/parsing errors), the technical contribution is hard to assess and reproduce.
- Evaluation of error typing is small-scale and indirect: The 200-edit human study uses an appropriateness rating rather than a direct comparison to gold type labels. Given existing corpora with type annotations (e.g., FCE, NUCLE), some mapping experiment to a common superset/subset would be feasible and more informative (per-type precision/recall, confusion). The current study is underpowered and potentially biased by automatic alignment errors that raters were asked to ignore. Also, κ_free may not be the most informative for a 3-level ordinal scale; a weighted agreement or more detailed inter-rater analysis would help.
- “Dataset-agnostic” and “bias-free” claims overstate reality: The approach encodes design choices (tagsets, UD mapping, dictionary, UK vs. US spelling conventions) and inherits biases from automatic taggers/parsers trained on native text. The paper acknowledges POS errors but does not quantify their impact or provide robustness checks (e.g., different taggers, different varieties of English).
- Reprocessing the gold reference and statistical testing: The claim that automatic references are “qualitatively as good as human annotations” is too strong. The bootstrap setup lacks details (unit of resampling, per-team vs. pooled, multiple comparisons correction). The similarity in F0.5 to M2 may be due to ceiling effects rather than equivalence. Moreover, exact-span matching after auto-realignment changes the target task; it may hide legitimate human edit segmentations and reduce comparability to prior work.
- Sparse multilingual/generalization evidence: Although framed as dataset-agnostic, experiments are limited to English CoNLL-2014 (and a small sample from FCE). No evidence is provided for applicability to other languages, domains, or even different English varieties. The reliance on spaCy v1.6, UD mapping, and Hunspell GB-large suggests potential brittleness across domains and varieties (e.g., American spelling).
- Presentation and bibliographic quality: Numerous formatting/citation issues reduce professionalism and hamper verification: truncated references (“Proceedings of the Eighteenth Confer-”), stray punctuation and escape characters, repeated author names, missing years, and incomplete venues. Footnotes are intrusive and misnumbered in running text. The list of valid error combinations appears in an appendix-like table but cross-referencing is inconsistent. These are fixable but currently substantial.

Questions to Authors
1) Rules and reproducibility: Can you release the full rule set (ordered, with tie-breaking), including the exact POS/lemma/dep patterns, normalization steps, and the mapping from PTB to UD tags? How many rules per type? Any learned thresholds?
2) Robustness: How sensitive is type assignment to the choice of tagger/parser and to tagging/parsing errors? Please provide ablations with alternative taggers or with gold POS (where available), and quantify the impact per type.
3) Gold mapping: Can you provide an evaluation mapping your types to a subset of FCE/NUCLE categories and report per-type precision/recall against human labels? Even a partial mapping would strengthen validity claims.
4) Spelling and variety: How do you handle UK vs. US spellings (e.g., colour/color) and domain-specific lexicons? Does using GB-large inflate SPELL detection or misclassify acceptable variants?
5) Bootstrap details: What was the resampling unit (sentence, document, edit), how were teams handled (per-team or pooled), and did you correct for multiple hypotheses? Please report confidence intervals.
6) Release details: Will code, models, and exact dependencies (versions) be released with a permanent URL and license? Is the tool deterministic across platforms?

Overall, the work is practical and potentially impactful as infrastructure for GEC diagnostics, but the methodological exposition and evaluation rigor need strengthening. I lean toward rejection in its current form due to the accumulation of methodological and presentation weaknesses, though I encourage resubmission with fuller rule disclosure, stronger validation against human type labels, clearer statistical methodology, and improved presentation.
