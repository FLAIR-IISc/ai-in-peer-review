Summary and claimed contributions
- The paper proposes an automatic pipeline to annotate grammatical error correction (GEC) outputs with edit spans and error types. It uses a linguistically-enhanced alignment (Damerau-Levenshtein with POS/lemma costs) to extract edits and a new rule-based classifier that assigns one of 55 error types based solely on automatic markup (POS, lemmas, stems, dependency parses, Hunspell). The authors release the tool.
- A small manual study (5 raters, 200 edits) rates predicted categories as Good/Acceptable/Bad, with 96.3% Good/Acceptable overall. The method is then used to re-annotate CoNLL-2014 system outputs and references to enable simple span+correction matching, and to provide detailed per-type analyses (including operation tier, multi-token edits, detection vs. correction).
- The paper argues its approach is dataset-agnostic, transparent, and more straightforward than metrics that integrate alignment into scoring (e.g., M2), and shows that reprocessing references automatically has no significant impact on overall F0.5 scores.

Contributions as I see them (in order of strength)
1) A practical, released tool/pipeline that standardizes error-type annotation and enables per-type evaluation across systems and edits, together with a unified, reasonably comprehensive error taxonomy.
2) A broad empirical analysis of CoNLL-2014 systems by error type and operation, surfacing complementary strengths that are not visible in aggregate metrics.
3) A simple evaluation protocol that fixes edit spans for both hypotheses and references and yields similar overall rankings to M2 on CoNLL-2014.

Strengths
- Community utility and transparency: Providing a released tool that deterministically assigns error types addresses a real gap in GEC evaluation and facilitates diagnostics. The deterministic rules make the rationale traceable, which is appealing for error analysis and system development.
- Sensible use of established components: The alignment stage leverages prior state-of-the-art (Felice et al., 2016). The reliance on widely used NLP tools (spaCy, UD mapping, Hunspell) lowers adoption barriers.
- CoNLL-2014-wide analysis: Applying the tool to all shared-task systems and reporting per-type, operation-tier, multi-token, and detection vs. correction analyses is valuable. The findings (e.g., differing strengths across teams, multi-token edits being challenging) are informative and likely reproducible.
- Simpler scoring with comparable outcomes: The demonstration that reprocessing references and using exact span+correction matching produces overall F0.5 scores similar to M2 (with bootstrap showing no significant differences) supports the practicality of the proposed evaluation pathway.
- Error taxonomy design: The tiered taxonomy (operation, POS/morphology/other) balances granularity and practicality, and the ability to slice results (e.g., R:NOUN vs. M:DET) is useful for targeted evaluation.

Weaknesses
- Limited validation of the classifier’s correctness: The only evaluation is a small subjective rating study (200 edits) where raters judged “appropriateness.” There is no quantitative per-type precision/recall against a gold error-type corpus (e.g., FCE/NUCLE) or even a carefully annotated subset mapped to the proposed scheme. Without such validation, the correctness of fine-grained per-type counts—and thus the downstream analyses—remains uncertain.
- Dependence on noisy automatic markup vs. “dataset-agnostic” claim: The approach crucially depends on English-specific resources (Penn→UD mapping, Hunspell GB, spaCy v1.6) and tagger/parser accuracy. The paper itself notes that many “Bad” labels were due to POS errors. There is no sensitivity analysis to tagger/parser choice or noise levels, nor any evidence of cross-domain or cross-lingual robustness. The dataset-agnostic claim is therefore overstated.
- Circularity and potential bias in reprocessing references: Re-annotating references with the same automatic alignment and typing as hypotheses ensures consistency but also removes human segmentation/type decisions, potentially masking errors introduced by the pipeline. While overall F0.5 similarity to M2 is shown, the paper does not examine whether per-type distributions remain faithful to human annotations, or whether the pipeline systematically shifts edit boundaries and types.
- Insufficient methodological detail and ablations: The rules underlying the 55 error types are not fully specified or evaluated (e.g., decision order, conflict resolution, reliance on dependency relations). There is no ablation to quantify the contribution of individual signals (POS vs. lemma vs. dependency vs. Hunspell) or of the enhanced alignment vs. plain Levenshtein. This limits reproducibility and makes it hard to assess where errors arise.
- Comparisons to alternative approaches are inconclusive: The paper dismisses ML-based type classifiers as “incomparable” due to differing schemes but could still provide a mapping-based comparison on a subset to establish relative accuracy. Similarly, the critique that M2 overestimates performance is known, but the proposed alternative is only validated on one dataset, with no analysis under multiple references or different unchanged-words settings.

Questions to authors (to be addressed in rebuttal)
- Can you provide a quantitative validation on a subset with human-typed labels mapped to your scheme (e.g., on FCE/NUCLE), reporting per-type precision/recall and macro/micro scores? Even a modest, well-annotated subset would substantiate per-type correctness.
- How sensitive are your type assignments to the choice of tagger/parser and to tagging errors? Please report a sensitivity analysis (e.g., spaCy vs. another tagger; artificially noised tags) and quantify the impact on type accuracy.
- What is the full rule set and decision procedure? In what order are rules applied; how are conflicts resolved; how is dependency information used; what are the fallbacks? Will you release these specifications with the tool?
- In the bootstrap test comparing gold vs. auto references: what was the resampling unit (sentence-level), and what are the p-values or confidence intervals per team? Did you also test per-type differences, not only overall F0.5?
- Can you show an analysis of boundary differences between human vs. auto references (e.g., distribution of edit-length changes, merge/split rates) and how these affect per-type counts?
- How portable is the framework beyond English? What modifications are needed (lexicons, tagset mappings), and have you run any preliminary tests in another language?

Additional comments
- The references contain many formatting inconsistencies and apparent placeholders; these should be corrected.
- It would help to include a compact mapping from common existing schemes to your taxonomy, and example-based guidelines to aid interpretability.
- If space allows, add a small error analysis highlighting the most common misclassifications and their root causes (tagging, rule gaps, alignment).

Overall recommendation
Borderline reject. The paper addresses a real need and offers a useful tool with promising qualitative results and insightful analyses. However, the core technical claim—a reliable, dataset-agnostic, rule-based error-type annotator—lacks rigorous validation and sensitivity analysis. Strengthening the empirical evaluation as suggested would substantially improve confidence in the per-type findings and the broader claims.
