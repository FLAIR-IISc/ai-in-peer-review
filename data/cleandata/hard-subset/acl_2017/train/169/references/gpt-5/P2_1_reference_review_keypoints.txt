- Proposes an automatic pipeline to annotate GEC outputs with explicit edit spans and error types using linguistically-informed alignment and a deterministic rule-based classifier.
- Alignment uses Damerau-Levenshtein with POS/lemma costs; typing uses rules driven by POS, lemma, stems, dependency, and Hunspell.
- Introduces a 25-type scheme (55 operation-type combos) that can be aggregated by operation, POS-like type, or morphological/tense detail.
- Validates classifier with a small expert study (5 raters, 200 edits): 86.3% Good, 10% Acceptable, 3.7% Bad; κfree=0.724.
- Re-annotates CoNLL-2014 outputs and references and finds no significant difference in overall F0.5 when replacing gold with automatic references (bootstrap p>.05).
- Provides a broad error-type analysis across CoNLL-2014 teams, covering operation-tier, per-category, multi-token edits, and detection vs correction.
- Main contribution 1: A practical, deterministic, dataset-agnostic error-typing tool enabling system-level error-type evaluation without training data.
- Main contribution 2: A comprehensive error-type analysis of CoNLL-2014 systems offering actionable insights into complementary system strengths.
- Main contribution 3: Methodological clarification separating edit extraction from evaluation and evidence that auto-annotated references can substitute gold references for overall scores.
- Strength: Fills a practical gap by enabling error-type scoring for unannotated outputs and could standardize analyses across systems and datasets.
- Strength: Deterministic, interpretable rules make classifications explainable and replicable, reducing annotator bias compared to learned classifiers.
- Strength: Methodologically clean decomposition of extraction vs evaluation, with experiments supporting non-distortion of overall performance by auto re-annotation.
- Strength: Empirical analysis yields non-obvious, actionable findings (e.g., weaknesses on Unnecessary edits; MT strength on multi-token edits).
- Strength: Commits to releasing resources and uses standard, off-the-shelf components, improving reproducibility.
- Weakness: Limited validation—200 edits is small for a 55-category space and measures perceived appropriateness rather than gold-standard accuracy.
- Weakness: Strong dependence on English-specific resources (PTB/UD mapping, Hunspell GB) challenges cross-lingual/domain generality.
- Weakness: Pipeline is sensitive to POS/lemma/parse quality; no systematic robustness analysis or ablations with alternative taggers/parsers.
- Weakness: Evidence that M2 “overestimates” performance is largely qualitative; lacks controlled experiments (e.g., synthetic edits) to support the claim.
- Weakness: Scope of comparisons is narrow; limited mapping to existing annotation schemes and coverage quantification across corpora.
- Weakness: Ambiguity and granularity in the scheme (e.g., MORPH vs VERB:FORM/TENSE; OTHER) need clearer operational definitions and edge-case rules.
- Questions: Request full rule set and decision order, including disambiguation for overlapping categories and handling of multiword expressions/phrasal particles.
- Questions: Ask for robustness tests swapping taggers/parsers or injecting tagging noise and per-category reliability under such changes.
- Questions: Request details on porting to other languages and sensitivity to alternative dictionaries (American vs British Hunspell).
- Questions: Ask for breakdown of human evaluation by error type and by error source, with examples of systematic failure modes.
- Questions: Request comparison of auto vs gold references at the type level, including distributional shifts and multi-token edit differences.
- Overall: Moderately original and practically significant, reasonably sound but needs deeper validation and clearer category definitions; reviewer leans weak accept contingent on clarifications and expanded validation.
