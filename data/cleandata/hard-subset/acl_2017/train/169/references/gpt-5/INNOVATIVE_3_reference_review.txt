Summary and contributions
- A unified, rule-based framework to automatically extract edits and assign dataset-agnostic error types to grammatical error correction (GEC) outputs, relying only on automatic markup (POS, lemmas, stemming, dependencies). This enables per-error-type evaluation without needing corpus-specific training.
- A simple, transparent evaluation protocol that separates alignment from scoring and compares hypothesis vs. reference by exact span+correction matching. The authors show that reprocessing references with the same automatic pipeline yields no statistically significant difference from gold references for overall scores.
- A comprehensive re-analysis of all CoNLL-2014 systems with fine-grained error-type breakdowns (including operation tier, multi-token edits, and detection vs. correction), yielding actionable insights. The tool is to be released.

Strengths
- Clear, useful, and novel evaluation infrastructure for the field. The community has lacked a dataset-agnostic way to obtain per-type scores; this framework operationalizes widely used linguistic categories into deterministic rules, making error-type evaluation feasible across systems and corpora. This feels like a meaningful new direction rather than an incremental tweak.
- Transparency and interpretability. Because typing is rule-based and depends only on automatic markup, one can explain why a label was assigned, avoid annotator bias, and maintain consistency. The manual evaluation (5 experts on 200 edits) indicates 96.3% Good/Acceptable despite noisy automatic boundaries, suggesting practical reliability.
- Compelling demonstration on CoNLL-2014. The per-type analyses reveal non-obvious insights (e.g., many systems fail to fix unnecessary tokens; multi-token edits remain a major weakness; complementary strengths between MT and classifier-based systems). These findings can concretely guide future system design and benchmarking.
- Separation of alignment and evaluation, and examination of M2 effects. The argument that M2 can inflate scores via dynamic boundary choices is important. Showing that automatic reprocessing of references does not significantly alter team rankings lends credence to using automatically standardized references for fair comparisons and error typing.
- Potential for broad community impact. A public tool that standardizes error-type annotation and supports detection vs. correction breakdowns can become shared infrastructure for GEC research, reproducible diagnostics, and future shared tasks.

Weaknesses
- Limited validation of the classifier and rules. The manual study covers only 200 edits; there is no per-type accuracy, confusion matrix, or sensitivity analysis. Since many errors stem from POS/parse mistakes, robustness to different taggers/parsers is crucial but untested. An ablation (e.g., no dependencies, different taggers) and per-type reliability would strengthen claims of dataset independence.
- Design choices and category granularity can feel ad hoc. The scheme collapses modal vs. tense and other nuanced distinctions for practicality, which is reasonable, but the paper lacks principled justification for the final set of 25 types and the 55 combinations. Without clear guidelines and empirical checks across corpora, the claim of dataset agnosticism may be overstated.
- Metric strictness and handling of multiple annotations. Exact match on span+correction can under-credit valid alternative segmentations or paraphrastic corrections (which the authors admit via OTHER). CoNLL has dual annotations; while the authors reprocess references automatically, the paper does not fully discuss how multiple references are reconciled under their scorer, or quantify how often strict matching penalizes reasonable edits relative to M2.
- Evidence for the M2 critique is largely qualitative. While the boundary-inflation argument is plausible, the paper would benefit from quantitative comparisons (e.g., correlation with M2, effect sizes per category, examples at scale where M2 merges FPs) to substantiate the claim and guide when to prefer one scorer over the other.
- Scope and generality. All experiments are on English CoNLL-2014 (with a small manual check on FCE). The portability to other datasets (e.g., JFLEG) and especially other languages is asserted via UD mapping but not demonstrated. Given the reliance on specific tools (spaCy v1.6, Lancaster stemmer, Hunspell), reproducibility and long-term maintainability require clearer commitments.

Questions to authors (optional)
- Tool release: Please provide a link, license, and an exact snapshot of the rules, tag mappings, and configuration (spaCy model versions, parser, stemmer, dictionary). Will you maintain compatibility with newer NLP toolchains?
- Robustness: How sensitive are type assignments to the choice of tagger/parser and to tagging errors? Can you report per-type agreement and a confusion matrix, and an ablation replacing spaCy with another tagger?
- Rule set: How many rules are there, how are conflicts resolved (priority/ordering), and what fraction of edits fall into OTHER/UNK? Could you include a concise set of annotation guidelines mirroring your deterministic policy?
- Multiple references: How does your scorer handle dual annotations (e.g., CoNLL)? Do you merge or score against each and take the max? Could you report correlation with M2 across teams and the frequency of boundary mismatches that your method penalizes but M2 credits?
- Generalization: Have you tried JFLEG or other corpora, and non-English GEC? Even a small pilot would strengthen the dataset-agnostic claim.

Overall, this paper makes a valuable and fairly novel contribution as evaluation infrastructure, with convincing qualitative insights and a useful tool. The methodological imperfections and limited validation are acceptable given the potential impact and the clarity of the idea.
