Summary and contributions
This paper proposes a deterministic, rule-based pipeline to automatically annotate grammatical error corrections with edit spans and error types, aiming to enable fine-grained evaluation of GEC systems without requiring human-annotated hypotheses. The pipeline (i) extracts edits via a linguistically-enhanced alignment (Felice et al., 2016) and (ii) assigns error types using rules over automatic linguistic markup (POS, lemmas, stems, UD tags, dependencies, dictionary lookups). The authors validate the classifier via a small expert rating study and demonstrate the utility by re-annotating CoNLL-2014 outputs and producing detailed per-type analyses. They also argue that reprocessing references automatically yields scores similar to gold references under both M2 and their exact-edit comparison.

Contributions as I see them:
1) Practical evaluation framework: a deterministic, dataset-agnostic rule set for error-type labeling of edits, enabling granular, interpretable system analysis.
2) Empirical analysis: a comprehensive per-type, per-operation breakdown of CoNLL-2014 systems, including multi-token and detection-vs-correction views.
3) Evaluation insight: evidence that automatic reprocessing of references produces scores statistically indistinguishable from gold in this setting, and a critique that M2 can inflate scores via boundary choices.

Strengths
- Practical significance and interpretability: A rule-based error typing scheme tied to explicit edit spans addresses a persistent gap in GEC evaluation. Deterministic labeling is transparent, allows precise error-type slicing (e.g., R:NOUN vs M:DET), and offers actionable diagnostics for system developers.
- Separation of alignment and scoring: Treating edit extraction separately from evaluation clarifies what is being measured and simplifies comparisons, in contrast to metrics that conflate alignment with scoring.
- Useful CoNLL-2014 reanalysis: The per-type and per-operation results highlight complementary strengths across systems (e.g., CAMB on missing tokens, CUUI on replacements, VERB:FORM), plausibly guiding method combinations. The multi-token and detection-vs-correction analyses are particularly informative and underreported in prior work.
- Initial human validation: Despite limitations, five expert judgments over 200 edits yield high Good/Acceptable rates (>95% per rater), suggesting the rule set is often reasonable even with noisy automatic boundaries/tags.
- Tool promise: The intention to release a tool is valuable to the community and could standardize error-type evaluation.

Weaknesses
- Overstated “dataset-agnostic” and “as good as human” claims: The pipeline depends on English-specific resources (spaCy English models, UD mapping, Lancaster stemmer, GB Hunspell dictionary) and error categories tailored to English. “Dataset-agnostic” is not “language-agnostic” and even across English datasets, reliance on a specific tagger/parser/dictionary introduces bias. The claim that automatic references are “as good as” human annotations is based on non-significant differences (p > .05) rather than equivalence tests; lack of significance does not imply equivalence. A TOST or confidence-interval-based equivalence analysis is needed, ideally across multiple datasets.
- Limited and noisy classifier validation: The human study covers only 200 edits across two corpora and 25+ categories, so per-category coverage is sparse. Raters were asked to ignore edit-boundary quality, yet category assignment often depends on those boundaries; this instruction can artificially inflate perceived appropriateness. The attribution of “Bad” labels to POS errors is anecdotal; no quantitative breakdown of error sources (tagger vs rule vs alignment) is provided.
- Reproducibility gaps: The paper does not include the full rule set, priority/ordering, or disambiguation details, nor alignment cost parameters and merging rules used from Felice et al. (2016). Without an anonymized link to code/resources and complete rules, the work is difficult to verify or extend. Version locking (spaCy v1.6) suggests brittleness; no analysis of robustness across taggers/parsers/dictionaries is provided.
- Metric design and analysis issues: The proposed exact span+correction matching is strict and may undercount valid corrections that differ only in span segmentation; there is no sensitivity analysis to boundary perturbations, nor a correlation study with human adequacy/fluency judgments or alternative metrics (e.g., GLEU/CHR-F variants). The argument that M2 “overestimates” is plausible but supported by isolated examples and aggregate score differences without ground-truth-based validation. The bootstrap significance testing lacks details: resampling unit (sentence vs document), handling of multiple references, confidence intervals, and adjustments for multiple comparisons are unspecified.
- Scope and datedness: The evaluation is limited to CoNLL-2014 system outputs. No results on more recent benchmarks (e.g., JFLEG, BEA-2019) are shown, limiting evidence of generality. The error inventory design choices (e.g., conflating modal/tense, REPL-only categories) are reasonable compromises, but the absence of confusion matrices, per-type frequency-normalized scores (macro vs micro), and an error propagation analysis undermines the methodological completeness.
- Presentation and formatting issues: Numerous typographical issues, inconsistent hyphenation and footnotes (e.g., “how- ever”, “in- dependent”, “Damerau-Levenshtein1”, “spaCy2”, “NLTK.3”), incomplete/garbled references, and missing/unclear artifacts (Table 2 referred to but not shown; Table 7 and Figure 1 referenced but absent) detract from clarity and rigor.

Questions to authors
1) Rules and reproducibility: Can you provide the full set of classification rules (including ordering, tie-breaking), the exact UD/Penn mapping, and alignment parameters/merging heuristics? Will you release an anonymized code archive for review?
2) Robustness: How sensitive are error-type assignments to the choice of tagger/parser/dictionary? Please report results with at least one alternative tagger/parser and dictionary, and quantify category shifts.
3) Equivalence vs. non-significance: Please redo the auto-vs-gold reference comparison using equivalence testing (e.g., TOST) with CIs, across multiple datasets, and report system-rank correlation (Spearman) and absolute differences.
4) Boundary sensitivity: How do your scores change if you relax span matching (e.g., allowing overlap or minimal-span tolerance), or compare head-aligned spans? Provide a sensitivity analysis.
5) Validation scale: Can you extend the human validation to more edits and report per-category accuracy and a confusion matrix? Also quantify the proportion of “Bad” cases due to POS/parse errors vs. rule errors vs. alignment errors.
6) Generality: Please include experiments on at least one additional benchmark (e.g., JFLEG, BEA-2019) and discuss adaptation beyond English.
7) Metric correlation: Do per-type and overall scores correlate with human judgments of system quality across systems? Provide correlation analyses against human rankings if available.

Overall, while the paper addresses a real need and offers a potentially useful tool, the combination of overstated claims, limited validation, reproducibility gaps, and presentation issues currently undermines its suitability. Strengthening evaluation rigor, releasing the rules/code, and improving presentation would substantially improve the contribution.
