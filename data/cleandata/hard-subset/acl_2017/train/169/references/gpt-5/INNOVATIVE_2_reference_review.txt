Summary and main contributions
This paper proposes a deterministic pipeline to automatically annotate grammatical error correction (GEC) outputs with explicit edit spans and error types, enabling per-error-type evaluation without requiring systems to produce type labels. The approach first extracts edits via a linguistically informed alignment (Felice et al., 2016), then assigns error types using a new rule-based framework that depends only on automatic linguistic markup (POS, lemma, stems, dependency parsing). A small human study rates the assigned types as Good/Acceptable for ~96% of sampled edits. The authors re-annotate CoNLL-2014 references to match their automatic scheme and perform a comprehensive error-type analysis across systems, including operation-tier breakdowns (Missing/Unnecessary/Replacement), multi-token edits, and detection vs. correction.

As I see them, the key contributions are:
1) A dataset-agnostic, deterministic error-typing framework and tool for GEC that enables automatic per-type evaluation of unannotated system outputs, with interpretable, rule-based decisions and a flexible taxonomy.
2) An evaluation protocol that standardizes both hypotheses and references via automatic alignment and typing, allowing simple exact-match scoring, per-type F-scores, and additional analyses (detection vs correction, multi-token edits); evidence that auto-processed references do not significantly change overall results vs. gold, and a useful diagnosis of M2’s optimistic bias.
3) A large-scale diagnostic analysis of CoNLL-2014 systems across many error categories, yielding actionable insights on which approaches handle which error types.

Strengths
- Novel diagnostic capability and practical impact: The main novelty is enabling per-error-type evaluation from untyped system outputs via a deterministic, dataset-agnostic annotator. This fills a longstanding gap in GEC evaluation and will likely influence methodology, benchmarking, and system development. The approach is simple to adopt and promises immediate utility to the community.
- Interpretability and consistency: The rule-based classifier is transparent: one can trace why a type was assigned. This is valuable in error analysis and avoids the domain biases of trained classifiers. The 5-rater evaluation reports 86.3% Good and 10% Acceptable, with decent agreement (κ_free=0.724), supporting practical reliability despite automatic preprocessing noise.
- Sound treatment of reference mismatch and metric effects: Reprocessing references to align with the hypothesis processing pipeline is pragmatic; bootstrap tests show no significant differences vs. gold. The critique of M2’s dynamic edit merging (inflating TPs and compressing nearby FPs) is well-argued and practically relevant; the simple exact-match scorer is a useful baseline.
- Breadth and depth of analyses: The operation-tier breakdown, general and detailed type analyses, multi-token edit performance, and detection vs. correction comparison yield concrete, system-level insights (e.g., frequent neglect of unnecessary deletions; classifiers excelling on VERB:FORM/SVA; MT better on multi-token edits). These findings are actionable and inform hybrid designs.
- Tool release promise: If delivered, the tool will likely become a standard resource for error-type evaluation, amplifying the paper’s impact.

Weaknesses
- Limited validation scope: The human evaluation covers only 200 edits across two datasets; there is no system-level correlation to human judgments nor cross-genre validation. The study asks raters to ignore potential misaligned boundaries; yet the evaluation relies on exact span matches, so the effect of alignment errors on per-type scores remains under-explored.
- Dependence on specific English tooling: The framework relies on spaCy v1.6, Lancaster stemming, and Hunspell; robustness to different taggers/parsers, newer models, or noisy tagging is not quantified. Despite “dataset-agnostic” claims, language-agnosticism is not demonstrated. The susceptibility to POS mislabeling (acknowledged) is not systematically measured.
- Taxonomy and rule design choices: The scheme conflates some phenomena (e.g., tense vs modality), and design trade-offs are acknowledged but remain ad hoc. There is no ablation or coverage analysis to show which rule groups drive performance, nor a mapping to existing annotation schemes to facilitate interoperability.
- Alignment reliance and boundary sensitivity: The pipeline inherits boundary errors from Felice et al. (2016), yet the paper does not quantify how boundary inaccuracies skew per-type metrics or multi-token analyses. While the authors argue M2 overestimates, it would help to complement with a sensitivity analysis showing how varying merge parameters or alternative aligners change conclusions.
- Presentation and references: The references section appears inconsistent/partial, which detracts from polish. Some claims (e.g., generalizability) would benefit from additional empirical support or clearer caveats.

Questions to authors
- Release details: Will you release the full code, rule set, and the auto-annotated CoNLL-2014 hypotheses/references? Under what license, and with documentation to ensure reproducibility?
- Robustness to NLP preprocessing: How sensitive are type assignments to the choice/version of POS tagger and parser? Can you report per-type stability and overall F0.5 when swapping in another tagger or a newer spaCy version?
- Impact of alignment errors: Can you quantify how alignment boundary errors affect per-type scores and multi-token analyses, e.g., by comparing against a subset with human-aligned spans?
- Portability: What is needed to port the framework to other languages (e.g., different tagsets, morphological analyzers)? Are there preliminary results beyond English?
- Interoperability: Can you provide a mapping between your types and popular existing schemes used in GEC corpora, to ease adoption and comparison?
- Detection vs. correction breakdown: Can you provide per-type detection scores (not just overall) to separate detection from correction capability for common categories (DET, PREP, VERB:FORM)?

Overall, I view this as a valuable, conceptually novel evaluation contribution with clear practical impact. While some validations are limited, the idea and tool are strong enough to merit publication, especially if the release is timely and the robustness analyses can be augmented.
