Summary and contributions
- A deterministic, rule-based framework for assigning error types to edits in grammatical error correction (GEC), operating on automatically aligned edits and shallow linguistic markup (POS, lemmas, dependencies). The scheme defines 25 base categories, with operation prefixes, enabling granular analyses.
- A practical pipeline/tool that (i) extracts edits via a linguistically enhanced alignment (Damerau–Levenshtein with POS/lemma costs) and (ii) annotates both hypotheses and references with error types, allowing simple edit-overlap scoring and breakdowns by type, operation, and edit span.
- An empirical re-analysis of CoNLL-2014 system outputs at the error-type level, including detection vs correction and multi-token edits, and a claim that automatically reprocessed references yield similar overall F0.5 to gold references.

Strengths
- Addresses a real gap in GEC evaluation: Most post-2014 metrics do not provide error-type breakdowns. A deterministic, transparent framework is useful for diagnostics and system development. The tool release promises practical impact.
- Methodological transparency and interpretability: Rule-based typing depending only on automatic markup is easy to audit and reason about, unlike opaque learned classifiers trained on idiosyncratic schemes. The authors’ emphasis on reproducibility and deterministic decisions is welcome.
- Pragmatic insights from large-scale analysis: The CoNLL-2014 per-type breakdowns expose complementary strengths across MT, classifier, and hybrid approaches (e.g., CUUI on VERB:FORM and ORTH; CAMB on DET and PREP; general weakness on multi-token and WO). These are actionable for system combination and future model design.
- Separation of alignment and scoring: The simple “exact span+correction match” evaluation clarifies what is being credited and avoids the boundary-optimizing behavior of M2 documented by others. The bootstrap test suggests that auto reprocessing of references does not materially change overall rankings.
- Lightweight, dataset-agnostic operationalization: Depending only on automatic tags and dictionaries lowers the barrier to running type-level evaluations across corpora and new systems without re-annotation.

Weaknesses
- Limited validation of the error typing: The only evaluation is a small (n=200) manual appropriateness rating with 5 raters; no comparison to any gold error-type annotations or mapped schemes. “Good/Acceptable/Bad” is a coarse, subjective measure, and the sample is too small to establish per-type reliability. The κfree=0.724 is encouraging but does not speak to accuracy against a gold scheme.
- Sensitivity to upstream NLP errors is acknowledged but not quantified. Many categories (e.g., VERB:SVA vs NOUN:NUM) hinge on POS and dependencies; the paper shows anecdotal mis-typings but no robustness analysis across taggers/parsers, domains, or errorful learner text. Claims of “dataset independence” are overstated given heavy reliance on English-specific tagsets, lemmatizers, and Hunspell.
- The decision to “auto-process” the gold reference to match the hypothesis pipeline is risky. While the bootstrap shows similar overall F0.5, the potential loss of human edit boundaries/types could bias per-type findings. The evidence presented is only for overall scores, not for type-level stability, which is what the tool is meant to enable.
- The critique of M2 and the superiority of the proposed scoring are argued via examples rather than a systematic comparison (e.g., correlation with human judgments, or controlled experiments varying the max unchanged words parameter). Without such validation, it is hard to endorse replacing the de facto standard for system ranking, even if the proposed scorer is valuable diagnostically.
- Insufficient detail on the rule set: The paper does not present the rules, precedence/tying strategies, or coverage statistics (e.g., fraction of edits falling into OTHER/UNK, per-type distribution before/after auto-reference processing). Reproducibility hinges on these; “will be released” is not a substitute for documentation within the paper.

Questions to authors
- Can you provide a quantitative validation against any corpus with human error-type labels (even after mapping to your scheme), reporting per-type precision/recall and an overall confusion matrix? If mapping is needed, please define it and analyze mapping-induced ambiguity.
- How robust is the classifier to tagging/parsing errors? Please report sensitivity analyses: swap in different taggers/parsers, vary tagging error rates synthetically, and measure changes in type assignments and per-type metrics.
- What is the full set of rules and their priority/decision logic? How do you resolve conflicts when multiple rules fire, and what proportion of edits fall back to OTHER/UNK?
- How stable are the per-type results when using gold vs auto references? Please report per-type deltas and significance, not only overall F0.5.
- Can you provide any evidence of correlation between your simple edit-overlap scores and human judgments (system-level or sentence-level), relative to M2 or other established metrics?

Additional comments
- The framework appears English-specific (POS set, Hunspell GB-large). Please clarify intended scope and any steps needed to port to other languages.
- The sample for manual evaluation (200 edits) is small and not stratified by type; a stratified design would better assess problematic categories (e.g., ADV, CONJ, WO).
- Some references are incomplete/garbled; please fix citations and ensure datasets/versions are specified (e.g., spaCy v1.6 is dated; note implications).
- The modal vs tense categorization is acknowledged as a compromise; consider documenting known gray areas and how often they occur.

Overall recommendation
A useful diagnostic tool with clear practical value, but the empirical validation of the error typing and the stability of per-type evaluations are not yet rigorous enough for strong claims. I encourage acceptance if the authors can substantially strengthen the validation (gold-type comparisons, robustness analyses, per-type stability with auto/gold references) and document the rules; otherwise, this is borderline.
