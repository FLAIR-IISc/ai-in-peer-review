Summary
The paper presents an automatic pipeline to annotate grammatical error corrections with explicit edit spans and error types, enabling per-type evaluation of GEC systems. It (i) extracts edits via a linguistically-informed alignment (Damerau–Levenshtein with POS/lemma-aware costs and rules), then (ii) assigns error types using a deterministic, rule-based classifier that relies only on automatically derived features (POS, lemmas, stems, dependency parses, Hunspell). The authors propose a dataset-agnostic, hierarchical error taxonomy (operation tier: Missing/Unnecessary/Replacement; token/morphology tiers; 55 valid combinations). A small manual study (200 edits, 5 raters) rates 96.3% of predicted types as Good/Acceptable with κ_free=0.724. Applying the tool to all CoNLL-2014 outputs, they re-annotate both system hypotheses and references, show negligible differences between gold vs auto references under both M2 and a simple exact-match scorer (bootstrap p>.05), and provide the first detailed per-type, per-operation, and multi-token/detection analyses across systems. The tool will be released.

Main contributions (as I see them)
1) Practical evaluation resource: A deterministic, dataset-agnostic tool to automatically align and type edits, enabling per-error-type evaluation and analysis of GEC systems at multiple granularities.
2) New, modular error taxonomy engineered for automatic assignment and evaluation (operation, token, morphology tiers).
3) Empirical insights: A comprehensive, type-level analysis of CoNLL-2014 systems (including operation-tier, multi-token, and detection vs correction), revealing complementary strengths and persistent weaknesses.

Strengths
- Addresses a real evaluation gap with a usable, reproducible tool. Prior GEC evaluations could not compare per-type behavior of hypothesis outputs; this pipeline standardizes both hypotheses and references into the same annotation space and allows straightforward, exact-match scoring. This materially advances analysis beyond aggregate F0.5.
- Dataset-agnostic, explainable error typing. The deterministic classifier uses only automatic annotations (POS/lemma/stem/deps/spelling), avoiding training on corpus-specific schemes. This solves the incompatibility issues of learned classifiers trained on heterogeneous annotation standards, and enables transparent, auditable decisions.
- Evidence that auto-annotated references suffice for evaluation. Reprocessing gold reference edits with the same automatic pipeline yields non-significant differences versus original gold under both M2 and their exact-match scorer across all teams (1,000 bootstrap iterations, p>.05). This supports using an automatic reference to remove alignment-format mismatches and to simplify scoring.
- Informative, actionable system-level insights. The per-type analysis reveals, e.g., widespread failure to handle Unnecessary tokens (five teams scored zero), complementary strengths (e.g., CAMB on Missing, CUUI on Replacement and ORTH), and that only MT-based systems surpass 10 F0.5 on multi-token edits. The detection vs correction view clarifies where systems primarily fail (generation vs identification).
- Sensible validation of the rule-based classifier. In a blinded, context-aware rating of 200 edits, 86.3% Good and 10.0% Acceptable judgments (only 3.7% Bad), with κ_free=0.724, is strong for an automatic, dataset-agnostic approach given noisy automatic edit boundaries and POS errors.

Weaknesses
- Limited intrinsic evaluation of the classifier. The 200-edit human study is small, uses coarse Good/Acceptable/Bad labels, and does not report per-type accuracy or confidence intervals. Many error types are rare; utility for long-tail categories is uncertain. A deeper intrinsic evaluation (e.g., per-type confusion, sensitivity to boundary noise) would strengthen the claims.
- Heavy dependence on upstream NLP quality and English-only scope. The approach relies on spaCy v1.6 POS/lemma/deps and Hunspell; the paper notes POS-driven mislabels (e.g., NOUN:NUM vs VERB:SVA). No ablation is provided to quantify sensitivity to tagging/parsing errors, nor evidence of portability to other languages/domains or taggers. Claims of dataset-agnosticism are limited to English corpora.
- Taxonomy design and coverage not empirically validated. The 25 base types (55 combinations) are motivated pragmatically but include broad catch-alls (MORPH, OTHER) and gray areas (e.g., modals folded into TENSE). There is no study of annotator consistency with the scheme itself, mapping to existing standards (e.g., FCE, NUCLE), or impact of alternative granularity choices on evaluation conclusions.
- Reference auto-processing risks masking mismatches. While scores are statistically similar, reprocessing gold references to match the hypothesis pipeline may institutionalize alignment/type biases, potentially obscuring genuine system improvements that would have matched the original human segmentation. A more granular analysis of how auto vs gold references differ (e.g., boundary length distributions, TP/FP split shifts by type) would clarify this risk.
- Limited comparison to learning-based type classifiers or alternative alignment strategies. The paper cites prior F1 ranges (50–70) but does not provide a controlled head-to-head comparison under the same taxonomy/metric, nor an ablation of the alignment/merging rules’ contribution to downstream type assignment accuracy.

Questions to Authors
- Can you provide per-type confusion statistics and confidence intervals for the human study? Which categories most frequently receive “Bad,” and are these attributable to specific POS/dependency errors?
- How sensitive is the classifier to the choice/set of upstream tools? Have you tried newer taggers/parsers (or UD vs PTB tags) and quantified impact on type assignment and evaluation conclusions?
- Could you release a mapping between your taxonomy and existing annotation schemes (e.g., FCE/NUCLE categories) and report agreement on a subset with human type labels?
- What fraction of edits are assigned to MORPH or OTHER, and how do conclusions change if these are split further or excluded?

Additional comments
- The claim that M2 overestimates performance is plausible and supported by qualitative examples; a small quantitative experiment (e.g., controlled synthetic edits varying max unchanged words) would make this more convincing.
- Releasing the tool, scripts, and the auto-annotated CoNLL-2014 outputs will maximize impact and reproducibility.

Recommendation: This is a practical, impactful evaluation paper with a useful tool and strong analysis. With strengthened intrinsic evaluation and sensitivity analyses, it would be a solid accept.
