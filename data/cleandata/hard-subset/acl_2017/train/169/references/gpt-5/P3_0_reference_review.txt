Summary and main contributions
This paper proposes a fully automatic pipeline to annotate grammatical error correction (GEC) outputs with explicit edit spans and error types, enabling type-level evaluation that has been largely missing since CoNLL-2014. The method has two steps: (i) edit extraction via a linguistically enhanced Damerau–Levenshtein alignment (Felice et al., 2016); (ii) rule-based error type classification that relies only on automatic mark-up (POS/UD tags, lemmas, stems, dependency parses, Hunspell). The authors release a tool and demonstrate its use by re-annotating all CoNLL-2014 system outputs to provide detailed, per-type analyses (including operation tier, multi-token edits, and detection vs. correction).

Contributions as I see them:
1) A deterministic, dataset-agnostic rule-based framework for error type classification that is transparent and does not require labeled training data.
2) A practical evaluation methodology that reprocesses both references and hypotheses with the same alignment and typing, yielding a simple exact-match edit comparison and enabling fine-grained error-type scoring.
3) A comprehensive post hoc analysis of CoNLL-2014 systems by error type, surfacing complementary strengths (e.g., CUUI on Unnecessary/Replacement vs. CAMB on Missing) and highlighting weak spots (e.g., multi-token edits).

Strengths
- Practical impact and tooling: The paper addresses a long-standing pain point—lack of error-type evaluation—by releasing a tool with immediately actionable outputs. The deterministic rules improve interpretability and facilitate debugging of GEC systems.
- Methodological clarity and transparency: By decoupling edit extraction from evaluation and using a simple exact-match comparison, the evaluation becomes easier to reason about than dynamic-boundary metrics (e.g., M2). The critique of M2’s tendency to inflate TPs is apt and illustrated with concrete cases.
- Dataset independence (within English) and no training data: The rule-based typing depends only on automatic mark-up, reducing domain mismatch issues inherent in learned classifiers trained on specific schemes. This also enables uniform processing of legacy system outputs.
- Informative empirical analysis: The CoNLL-2014 re-analysis yields novel insights (e.g., widespread failure to address Unnecessary edits, classifier strength on VERB:FORM, MT advantage on multi-token edits). The detection vs. correction plot is valuable and rarely reported.
- Human validation of the classifier: A small but informative expert evaluation suggests the rule-based types are judged Good/Acceptable in ~96% of cases, with inter-rater agreement (κfree=0.724) indicating reasonable consistency.

Weaknesses
- Limited validation of the classifier: The human study is small (200 edits) and uses subjective Good/Acceptable/Bad labels rather than a gold standard of error types. Many “Bad” cases are attributed to POS tagging errors, but there is no quantitative analysis of how tagging/parsing inaccuracies propagate to type errors. An ablation/sensitivity study (e.g., swapping taggers, using gold POS on a subset) would strengthen claims.
- Potential circularity and brittleness in evaluation: Reprocessing both hypothesis and reference with the same alignment and rules ensures consistency but may mask mismatches with human intent (e.g., span choices, operation type). Exact span match can be overly strict and undercount legitimate TPs. The paper contrasts with M2 but does not quantify the degree of under/overestimation or provide alternative tolerant matching for robustness.
- Generality claims: The approach is “dataset-agnostic” but depends on English resources (spaCy PTB tags, UD mapping, Hunspell GB). It is unclear how readily the framework transfers to other languages or even substantially different English domains (e.g., social media, code-mixing). Some categories (e.g., MORPH vs POS-based types) may not map cleanly across languages.
- Ambiguity and rule specification: The paper does not fully specify the rule ordering, precedence, and conflict resolution among overlapping categories (e.g., MORPH vs. VERB:FORM vs. NOUN:NUM; token-level vs. phrase-level WO). Without a detailed rulebook and decision logs, reproducibility and interpretability claims are weakened.
- Statistical rigor and stability of per-type analyses: Many category scores are based on small counts (e.g., NOUN:POSS, VERB:INFL), leading to high variance and occasional perfect scores. No confidence intervals or multiple-comparison controls are provided; readers may over-interpret differences. Similarly, the “no significant difference” result for auto vs. gold references is shown only at the global score level; stability at the per-type level is not assessed.

Questions to authors
1) Rule specification: Could you provide the complete ordered rule list, including precedence and tie-breaking, and quantify how often multiple rules fire? How are multi-token edits typed when tokens span heterogeneous POS?
2) Sensitivity to automatic mark-up: How robust are types to tagging/parsing errors? Please report results using an alternative tagger/parser and, if possible, a small gold POS subset to estimate upper bounds.
3) Operation-tier validity: How often do alignment/merging decisions flip an edit between Missing/Unnecessary/Replacement? Can you validate the operation-tier labels against human annotations on a subset?
4) Evaluation robustness: Have you tried a relaxed matching protocol (e.g., boundary tolerance, head-aligned spans) to quantify how much exact matching undercounts TPs relative to M2? Can you provide effect sizes rather than qualitative claims about M2 inflation?
5) Category distributions: For each error type (and operation-tier combination), can you provide counts to contextualize F-scores and, ideally, confidence intervals? This would help assess the reliability of per-type rankings.
6) Generalization: What is required to port the framework to other languages? Which parts are language-specific (e.g., POS inventory, morphology, dictionaries), and do you foresee categories that will not generalize?
7) UNK handling: When and how is UNK assigned in automatic annotation? Is it used for hypotheses (e.g., detection-only edits), and how does it interact with detection vs. correction scoring?

Overall, this is a useful, well-motivated contribution that fills a practical gap in GEC evaluation. Strengthening the empirical validation (robustness to mark-up errors, clearer rulebook, per-type stability) and clarifying generalization limits would significantly improve the paper. I view the release of a reliable, interpretable tool for error-type evaluation as the key contribution.
