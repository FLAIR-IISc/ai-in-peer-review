Summary and main contributions
This paper proposes an automatic, dataset-agnostic pipeline to annotate grammatical error correction (GEC) outputs with explicit edit boundaries and error types, enabling fine-grained evaluation beyond overall F-score. The method has two steps: (i) linguistically-enhanced edit extraction using Damerau-Levenshtein with POS/lemma-aware costs and merging rules; (ii) a deterministic, rule-based error-type classifier that assigns one of 25 base categories (55 operation-type combinations) using only automatic markup (POS, UD tags, lemmas, stems, dependencies, Hunspell). The authors validate the classifier via a small human study and demonstrate the utility of the annotations by re-evaluating all CoNLL-2014 systems with type-specific metrics, analyses by operation (Missing/Replacement/Unnecessary), multi-token edits, and detection vs. correction.

As I see it, the primary contributions are:
1) A practical, deterministic, dataset-agnostic framework and tool for automatically typing edits in parallel GEC data (both references and hypotheses), enabling detailed and consistent error-type evaluation.
2) An empirical analysis of CoNLL-2014 systems by error type, edit operation, multi-token scope, and detection vs. correction, yielding actionable insights about system strengths/weaknesses across categories.
3) A simple evaluation protocol that compares edits via span+correction equivalence, decoupling alignment from scoring and facilitating transparent, type-aware metrics.

Strengths
- Addresses a clear gap and provides a usable tool: Prior to this work, error-type evaluation of GEC systems was ad hoc or limited to recall on human-typed references. By automatically typing hypothesis edits and reprocessing references to match, the paper enables like-for-like, type-aware evaluation with minimal plumbing. This is genuinely useful for the community and has immediate practical value for analysis and system development.

- Deterministic, dataset-agnostic design with transparent decisions: Basing classification solely on automatic markup (POS/UD/lemmas/etc.) and explicit rules avoids dependence on corpus-specific training and opaque model biases. The design makes decisions auditable and reproducible, and supports cross-corpus comparability—highly desirable properties for evaluation tooling.

- Empirical utility demonstrated on a major benchmark: The extensive CoNLL-2014 analysis shows how the tool surfaces meaningful differences across systems (e.g., CUUI strong on Replacement and ORTH, CAMB strong on Missing and PREP; multi-token edits favor MT; detection vs. correction gaps). These observations are difficult to obtain with standard overall metrics and can guide targeted system improvements or ensembling.

- Sensible separation of alignment and scoring: Fixing edit spans first and then performing exact-match scoring is conceptually clean, reduces metric confounds, and reveals how M2’s dynamic boundary choices can inflate scores. This clarity is valuable in ongoing discussions about GEC metrics.

- Initial validation of classifier appropriateness: Despite being small, the human study reports high Good/Acceptable rates (>95% per rater, ~86% Good overall) and reasonable agreement (κ_free=0.724), supporting the basic adequacy of the rule-based scheme for evaluation purposes.

Weaknesses
- Limited validation rigor and scope: The classification evaluation is small (200 edits), subjective (Good/Acceptable/Bad), and not benchmarked against a gold-typed corpus. No quantitative per-type precision/recall for the classifier is provided, and there is limited error analysis beyond noting POS tagging errors. An ablation study (e.g., POS-only vs. POS+lemma vs. +dependencies) and sensitivity to different taggers/parsers would better establish robustness.

- Heavy dependence on upstream automatic markup quality: The approach inherits errors from POS/lemma/parse/stemming and dictionary resources (spaCy v1.6, UD mapping, Hunspell GB). The paper acknowledges this but does not quantify the impact. Without robustness analysis (e.g., tagger accuracy perturbations, alternative tagger backends), confidence in cross-domain reliability is constrained.

- Error scheme design choices and coverage lack deeper justification: The scheme conflates modal vs. tense and restricts some categories to Replacement only (e.g., ADJ:FORM), with “OTHER” and “UNK” as catch-alls. While the practicality trade-off is acknowledged, there is no quantitative distribution of types (e.g., how often “OTHER” is used), nor a mapping to existing schemes to ease interpretability. This limits comparability and may obscure nuanced phenomena (e.g., modality, multiword constructions, semantic paraphrase).

- Evaluation metric trade-offs not fully explored: Exact span+correction match is transparent but strict; it can penalize legitimate alternative corrections and paraphrases (especially for fluency and multi-token edits). The critique that M2 “overestimates” performance is plausible but not validated against human judgments or alternative metrics (GLEU, I-measure, ERRANT-compatible variants). Correlation with human adequacy/fluency ratings (e.g., on JFLEG) would strengthen claims.

- Reprocessing “gold” references with the automatic pipeline: While bootstrap tests suggest no significant difference in overall scores, the implications for type-level analyses are not quantified. If type labels or boundaries shift under auto processing, type-specific conclusions could be affected; more granular comparisons (e.g., per-type agreement between gold vs. auto) would increase confidence.

Questions to authors
1) Rule base and reproducibility: Will you release the full, executable rule set and alignment parameters (including merging rules), with versioned dependencies and random seeds, to ensure replicability? Is the tool robust to updates in spaCy/UD/taggers?

2) Robustness and ablations: How sensitive is classification to POS/lemma/parse errors? Can you report an ablation (e.g., POS-only vs. POS+lemma+dep) and a tagger swap experiment? What fraction of “Bad” labels is removed with improved tagging?

3) Type scheme mapping and interpretability: Can you provide a mapping to commonly used error taxonomies (e.g., FCE/NUCLE categories) to facilitate adoption? What are the empirical frequencies of OTHER/UNK across datasets, and typical causes?

4) Metric validation: Can you report correlations between your exact-match type-aware scores and human judgments (at least overall), and contrast with M2/GLEU/I-measure? For multi-reference settings, how do you aggregate matches across references?

5) Generalization: Have you applied the tool to other corpora (FCE, JFLEG) and domains? Any preliminary results on non-English languages if UD/POS resources are available?

6) Multi-token edits: Can you quantify how often boundary disagreements (merge/split) between systems and references drive failures on multi-token edits, and whether alternative matching (e.g., minimal edit set) changes conclusions?

Overall, this is a timely, practical contribution that can materially improve GEC evaluation and analysis. Strengthening the validation (larger study, ablations, metric correlations) and clarifying scheme/metric trade-offs would further solidify the impact. I lean positive given the tool’s utility and the informative analyses.
