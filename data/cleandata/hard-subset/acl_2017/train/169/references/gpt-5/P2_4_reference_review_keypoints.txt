- Proposes a deterministic, dataset-agnostic tool to auto-annotate GEC outputs with edit spans and 55 rule-based error types using linguistically enhanced alignment and automatic linguistic markup.  
- Validates the typer with small-scale human judgments and demonstrates utility by re-annotating and analyzing all CoNLL-2014 system outputs.

Primary contributions:
- Provides a practical, deterministic error-typing/evaluation tool that needs no labeled training data.  
- Delivers a detailed empirical analysis of CoNLL-2014 systems by error type and operation.  
- Proposes an evaluation protocol separating edit extraction from scoring and critiques M2’s boundary-optimization bias.

Strengths:
- High practical impact and reproducibility via a transparent, rules-based annotator relying on standard NLP pipelines.  
- Dataset-agnostic design through automatic linguistic markup avoids corpus-specific trained typers.  
- Enables granular, actionable empirical insights (operation tiers, per-type winners, multi-token difficulty, detection vs. correction gaps).  
- Sensible methodology separating extraction from scoring and bootstrap evidence that auto-reprocessing references preserves rankings.

Weaknesses:
- Limited validation: only 200 edits judged, coarse labels, no per-type accuracy, confusion analysis, or stratified reliability study.  
- Heavy dependence on upstream POS/lemma/parse quality without quantified robustness or sensitivity analyses.  
- English-centric resources and evaluations; limited evidence of generalization to other corpora, languages, or contemporary neural outputs.  
- Confound between alignment and typing decisions with no ablation showing alignment’s impact on types and scores.  
- Incomplete comparison to learned typers—no head-to-head evaluation on a shared mapped scheme to quantify trade-offs.

Key questions for authors:
- Provide per-type human evaluation results (accuracy, agreement by category and operation) and where “Bad” judgments cluster.  
- Report sensitivity of type assignments to different tagger/parser/lemmatizer choices (e.g., Stanza) and variance in type distributions.  
- Quantify size and common patterns of OTHER/UNK and whether rules can reduce OTHER without losing determinism.  
- Clarify plans and requirements for non-English support and which parts of the framework are UD-generic versus English-specific.  
- Quantify alignment’s impact on type labels and scores (vary merge rules/penalties or compare to Levenshtein baseline).  
- Provide numeric examples and effect sizes showing M2’s purported inflation (TP/FP deltas) and whether team rankings change materially.

Overall assessment:
- Useful, clear, and practical contribution with moderate novelty; tool and analyses are valuable but the core typing validation and robustness/generalization evidence are currently insufficient.
