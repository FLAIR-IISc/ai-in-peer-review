- Paper introduces an automatic pipeline to annotate GEC outputs with explicit edit spans and error types using prior alignment and rule-based linguistic markup.
- Pipeline has two components: linguistically informed edit extraction (Felice et al., 2016) and a deterministic rule-based error-type classifier using POS/lemmas/UD/morphology/dependencies.
- Proposes a modular, dataset-agnostic error scheme (25 base types, operation prefixes M/R/U, ~55 valid combinations) designed for evaluation and flexible slicing.
- Presents an empirical study reprocessing CoNLL-2014 outputs, comparing to M2, and performing per-error-type, multi-token, and detection-vs-correction analyses; tool release is promised.
- Strength: Fills a practical evaluation gap by enabling system-level error-type analysis on unannotated hypotheses without supervised training.
- Strength: Classification is interpretable and auditable, relying on automatic markup and deterministic rules, supporting nuanced evaluation slices.
- Strength: Validation shows automatic reprocessing of references yields no statistically significant difference versus gold in their tests, supporting viability of automated reference processing.
- Strength: Empirical findings provide actionable insights (e.g., weaknesses on unnecessary-token edits, multi-token edits, and different system strengths by category).
- Strength: Small expert judgment study reports high perceived classifier quality (86% Good, κfree=0.724), with most errors tied to POS tagging issues.
- Weakness: Methodological novelty is limited; the work mainly composes existing alignment methods with straightforward rule-based classification.
- Weakness: Classifier evaluation is small-scale and indirect (200 edits, subjective ratings) rather than accuracy against mapped gold labels.
- Weakness: Approach depends on specific preprocessors (spaCy v1.6, Hunspell, UD mapping) and appears English-only, with no robustness checks across tools or domains.
- Weakness: Claims about M2 overestimation and equivalence to gold need stronger statistical framing and controlled experiments or equivalence testing.
- Weakness: Exact-span matching remains brittle and may penalize valid alternative segmentations; impact of alignment choices on final metrics is not quantified.
- Weakness: Reproducibility concerns—no provided URL or full rule descriptions; code, rule inventory, and configurations are not yet available.
- Question: Will you release full code, rule engine, alignment configs, tagger/lemmatizer versions, mappings, license, and timeline?
- Question: How sensitive are error-type assignments to different POS/taggers/parsers and to domain shifts (e.g., scientific vs learner essays)?
- Question: Can you provide mappings and agreement analyses between your error types and existing schemes like NUCLE/CoNLL-2014?
- Question: Why fold modal changes into VERB:TENSE and how often would finer-grained modal categories affect diagnostics?
- Question: Can you estimate how alignment errors change error-type labels or per-category scores?
- Question: Can the tool report per-error-type detection vs correction breakdowns, and do those patterns differ meaningfully by category?
- Overall assessment: Useful, interpretable, and practical evaluation resource with significant empirical insights, but would benefit from broader validation, stronger statistical claims, mapping to prior schemes, and public artifact release.
