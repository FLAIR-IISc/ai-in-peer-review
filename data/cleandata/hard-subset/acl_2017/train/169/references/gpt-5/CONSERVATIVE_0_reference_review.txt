Summary and claimed contributions
This paper proposes a two-stage, rule-based framework to automatically annotate grammatical error correction (GEC) outputs with explicit edit boundaries and error types. The pipeline: (i) extract edits with a linguistically-informed alignment (Felice et al., 2016), and (ii) assign error types using deterministic rules based on automatic linguistic markup (POS, lemmas, stems, dependencies, hunspell). The authors use this to re-annotate CoNLL-2014 system outputs and provide per-type analyses, including operation-tier (Missing/Replacement/Unnecessary), detailed POS/morph categories, multi-token edits, and detection vs. correction breakdowns. They also argue that reprocessing references automatically (to match system-side automatic segmentation) yields similar overall scores to gold references and enables straightforward edit-level F0.5 computation.

Contributions, as I see them (in order of strength)
1) A practical, dataset- and system-agnostic tool/pipeline to automatically assign error types to GEC edits, enabling per-type evaluation without requiring supervised training on specific taxonomies.
2) A comprehensive per-type analysis of CoNLL-2014 systems, illustrating differences across approaches and highlighting gaps (e.g., poor handling of multi-token edits, limited treatment of Unnecessary errors).
3) A simple evaluation protocol based on edit-span matching (after auto-processing references) and an empirical observation that using auto vs. gold references does not significantly change overall F0.5 for CoNLL-2014 systems.

Strengths
- Addresses a real and long-standing gap in GEC evaluation: lack of standardized, automatic error-type breakdowns. The proposed rule-based typing offers immediate utility to the community, especially for ablating system components and for directing research toward weak error families.
- Methodological simplicity and transparency: the rules are deterministic and based on standard automatic annotations. This makes decisions interpretable and avoids dependence on training corpora with incompatible label schemes.
- Useful large-scale application: applying the framework to all CoNLL-2014 systems yields concrete, actionable observations (e.g., classifier strengths on VERB:FORM and ORTH; MT advantages for multi-token edits; widespread neglect of spell checking).
- Separation of alignment and scoring: the paper clarifies how edit-span mismatches impact scoring, and provides an alternative that fixes spans beforehand. This makes the implications of different granularity choices explicit to users.
- Tool release (promised) increases reproducibility and potential impact; the framework could become a de facto standard for error-type analysis, improving comparability across future work.

Weaknesses
- Limited validation of the error-type classifier: The primary evaluation is a small, subjective rating of 200 edits with coarse Good/Acceptable/Bad labels. There is no per-type accuracy, confusion analysis, or comparison against alternative automatic typers on a shared labeling scheme. Claims of dataset-agnostic classification and “bias-free” decisions are not substantiated empirically beyond this small study, and the evaluation blends noise from automatic edit extraction with typing decisions.
- Heavy dependence on upstream automatic tools without sensitivity analysis: POS/lemma/parse/stemming/hunspell errors are acknowledged as sources of misclassification, but the paper does not quantify robustness. There is no ablation across taggers/parsers, nor analysis of how tagging quality affects per-type accuracy and system rankings. The dependency on particular English resources undermines the “dataset-agnostic” claim and raises questions about portability to other genres/languages.
- Reprocessing gold references: The conclusion that auto references are “qualitatively as good” as human annotations is based on overall F0.5 (with bootstrap) but does not examine per-type impacts or edit-size distributions. It remains unclear how often auto segmentation contradicts human boundaries and whether that introduces systematic biases in per-type evaluation or in multi-token edit assessment.
- Taxonomy design choices insufficiently justified: The scheme collapses distinctions (e.g., modals under tense) and introduces categories that do not map one-to-one with established guidelines used in legacy corpora. The paper lacks a principled mapping between existing annotation standards and the new inventory, making it hard to interpret longitudinal results or reuse legacy annotations.
- Claims about the M2 scorer “overestimating” performance are asserted with anecdotal examples but without a systematic study. The extent of inflation, dependence on parameters (e.g., max unchanged words), and practical implications for ranking systems are not quantified.
- Reproducibility/details: The rule set itself is not specified in enough detail to allow independent reimplementation or scrutiny. It’s unclear how ties/conflicts are resolved and how word-order or morphology rules interact with POS-based defaults.

Questions to authors
1) Can you provide a quantitative evaluation of error typing beyond the 200-edit subjective study? For example, annotate a larger sample with gold error types under your scheme (with multiple annotators), report per-type precision/recall, confusion matrices, and agreement with your automatic labels.
2) How sensitive are results to the choice of tagger/parser and dictionary? Please report per-type performance deltas when swapping spaCy with another tagger or using gold POS on a subset, and analyze the impact on system rankings.
3) How often does your automatic edit extraction disagree with human edit boundaries in CoNLL-2014, and how does that affect per-type scores and multi-token evaluation? A quantitative analysis of boundary errors would strengthen the decision to auto-process references.
4) Please release or document the full rule set and decision order. How do you handle ambiguous cases (e.g., NOUN vs VERB) when POS is unreliable? Are there back-off strategies?
5) Can you provide a principled mapping from widely used error taxonomies to your categories, and a discussion of how differences affect interpretability of prior results?
6) For the claim that M2 overestimates: can you measure, across the test set, the frequency and magnitude of TP inflation/FP merging relative to fixed-span scoring, and assess whether system rankings change?

Additional comments
- The reference list contains formatting issues and inconsistencies; please correct for camera-ready.
- The tool’s broader applicability beyond English learner data remains to be demonstrated; if claimed as dataset-agnostic, cross-domain/corpus evidence would be helpful.
