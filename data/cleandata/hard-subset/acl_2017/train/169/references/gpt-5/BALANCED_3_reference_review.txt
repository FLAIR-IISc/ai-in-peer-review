Summary
This paper proposes a two-stage pipeline to automatically annotate grammatical error correction (GEC) outputs with explicit edit spans and error types. The first stage uses a linguistically informed alignment algorithm to extract edits between original and corrected sentences; the second is a deterministic, rule-based classifier that assigns error types using automatically derived information (POS, lemmas, stems, dependencies, Hunspell). The authors argue this dataset-agnostic approach enables consistent per-error-type evaluation across systems, which prior work could not provide. They manually assess the classifier’s appropriateness (200 edits, 5 raters) and apply the tool to re-annotate hypotheses and references for all CoNLL-2014 systems, reporting a rich set of analyses at multiple granularities (operation tier: Missing/Unnecessary/Replacement; error category; multi-token edits; detection vs correction). They also show that evaluating with automatically processed references produces scores statistically indistinguishable from using gold references, and discuss how M2’s dynamic boundary selection can inflate scores.

Main contributions (as I see them)
1) A practical, deterministic, dataset-agnostic rule-based framework and tool for annotating GEC edits with error types, enabling consistent per-type evaluation across systems and corpora.
2) A comprehensive per-type analysis of CoNLL-2014 system outputs (including operation tier, multi-token edits, and detection vs correction), yielding actionable insights about system strengths/weaknesses not visible in aggregate metrics.
3) Evidence that automatic re-annotation of references yields overall scores comparable to gold references, plus a simpler exact-match scoring protocol that separates edit extraction from evaluation.

Strengths
- High practical value and community impact: The tool fills a clear gap: prior GEC evaluations lacked consistent, system-level error-type breakdowns. The ability to produce per-type, per-operation, and multi-token analyses is immediately useful for diagnosis, ablation, and system combination.
- Deterministic, interpretable, and dataset-independent design: By relying only on automatic markup and explicit rules, the classifier is transparent and consistent, avoiding training-data biases and facilitating error forensics. The modular taxonomy supports flexible aggregation (e.g., R:NOUN vs VERB:TENSE).
- Empirical validation and insights: The manual study (5 experts, κfree=0.724) finds 86% Good and 10% Acceptable labels despite noisy automatic alignments. Applying the tool to CoNLL-2014 surfaces non-obvious findings (e.g., widespread failure on Unnecessary edits; classifier strength on VERB:FORM; MT’s relative advantage on multi-token edits; closer competition in detection vs correction).
- Sound evaluation protocol decisions: Re-annotating references to match hypothesis processing and then validating via bootstrap tests is methodologically clean and practically important. Separating edit extraction and evaluation clarifies where errors arise and makes the scoring assumptions explicit.
- Clear presentation and breadth of analysis: The paper covers operation tier, general and detailed categories, multi-token edits, and detection vs correction, demonstrating the framework’s flexibility and analytical power.

Weaknesses
- Limited scale and granularity of classifier validation: The manual evaluation (200 edits) is small relative to the taxonomy’s breadth and offers only coarse “appropriateness” ratings. There is no per-category accuracy, confusion analysis, or breakdown by edit size, nor ablations isolating the impact of POS/lemma/stemming/parse. The reported errors often stem from tagging, but the contribution of tagging vs rule mistakes is not quantified.
- Potential brittleness and English specificity: While claimed dataset-agnostic, the approach depends on English POS/UD mapping, spaCy models, and Hunspell; it is unclear how well it generalizes to other languages, domains, or even different English taggers/parsers. The taxonomy also encodes English-specific categories (e.g., SVA), limiting cross-lingual applicability.
- Exact-match evaluation sensitivity: The proposed scoring requires exact span and correction matches. This design, while simple, may be brittle to alignment errors, tokenization differences, or reasonable alternate spans/corrections, potentially distorting per-type scores. The paper shows overall comparability to gold/M2 but does not quantify robustness of per-type results to alignment noise.
- Transparency and reproducibility gaps: The paper lists categories and valid combinations, but the concrete classification rules and their ordering/priority are not detailed. Without the full rule set and configuration (e.g., spaCy version, UD mapping specifics), reproduction and scrutiny are difficult. The promised tool release is essential; at submission time this limits verifiability.
- Claims about M2 inflation are suggestive but under-quantified: The critique is plausible and grounded in examples, but there is no systematic quantification of the inflation across categories or conditions, nor controlled experiments varying max unchanged words to substantiate the claim.

Questions to Authors
- Rules and prioritization: Can you release the full set of classification rules, their order of application, and examples per rule? How do you resolve conflicts when multiple rules match?
- Tagger/parser dependence: How sensitive are results to the choice of POS tagger and dependency parser? Have you tried alternative taggers or spaCy model versions, and can you report variance in per-type scores?
- Error analysis granularity: Can you provide a confusion matrix of predicted vs expert-judged categories, and a breakdown of “Bad” judgments by source (alignment error vs tagging error vs rule misclassification)?
- Per-type validation: Do you have any subset with manual gold error types (e.g., parts of FCE/CoNLL) to assess per-category precision/recall of your classifier directly, beyond appropriateness ratings?
- Robustness to span variation: How often do your automatically extracted spans diverge from human spans for multi-token edits, and how does that affect per-type evaluation? Could a lenient matching heuristic (e.g., overlap with compatible type) mitigate brittleness without reintroducing M2-like inflation?
- Generalization: Have you applied the tool to other GEC corpora or to different English varieties (e.g., American vs British spelling) to test the Hunspell/orthography rules? Any plans for cross-lingual extensions or language-agnostic subsets of the taxonomy?

Additional comments and suggestions
- Expand the validation with larger, stratified samples across categories and edit sizes; add per-type precision/recall, confusion, and ablations (e.g., remove dependencies, remove stems, alter UD mapping).
- Quantify the contribution of tagging/parsing errors to misclassification, and consider lightweight post-hoc heuristics to hedge common POS ambiguities (e.g., ring as NOUN vs VERB).
- Provide a mapping between your taxonomy and existing annotation schemes used in FCE/CoNLL to facilitate comparability and adoption.
- Consider reporting correlations between per-type system scores and human judgments of overall quality or fluency to argue for the evaluation’s external validity.
- Include efficiency/runtime stats and memory footprint to inform practical deployment.

Overall, this is a timely and valuable engineering contribution that enables analyses the community currently lacks. Strengthening the empirical validation (scale, per-type accuracy, robustness) and releasing the full rules/code would make the case more compelling.
