- Summary: Proposes an automatic, rule-based error-type annotation method combining linguistic edit extraction, applied to CoNLL-2014 outputs, with a tool to be released.

- Contribution 1: Introduces a practical, dataset-independent rule-based method for automatic error type annotation that avoids costly labeled data and retraining.

- Contribution 2: Provides detailed, error-type-specific analysis of CoNLL-2014 systems, revealing system strengths and weaknesses.

- Contribution 3: Shows that automatically annotated references yield evaluation results comparable to reprocessed gold-standard annotations.

- Strength 1: The fully automated, dataset-agnostic approach is novel and practical compared to supervised classifiers prone to dataset bias.

- Strength 2: Evaluation is thorough, including manual assessment, comparisons to M2, and bootstrap testing for auto vs. gold comparisons.

- Strength 3: Paper is clearly written, well-structured, and provides sufficient replication detail and good tables/figures.

- Strength 4: Tool release promises community benefit and wider adoption.

- Strength 5: Analysis yields insightful findings (e.g., many systems struggle with unnecessary token errors; MT vs classifier differences).

- Weakness 1: The 25 error-type taxonomy may be too coarse-grained for some uses, with "Other" as a catch-all needing refinement.

- Weakness 2: Reliance on POS tagging and lemmatization makes the classifier sensitive to tagger errors, causing misclassifications.

- Weakness 3: Cascading errors from imperfect edit extraction/alignment (80–85 F1) can propagate and affect error-type assignment accuracy.

- Weakness 4: Lacks direct comparison to alternative rule-based or recent neural automatic annotation/evaluation methods.

- Question 1: How can the 25 error types be expanded or refined to provide more granular categories?

- Question 2: How sensitive is classifier performance to the choice of POS tagger, and were alternative taggers or tag correction methods explored?

- Question 3: What is the estimated impact of edit-extraction/alignment errors on final error-type misclassification rates?

- Question 4: How well does the method generalize across domains and learner levels, and how might degradation be mitigated?

- Additional comment 1: Table 2 listing the complete error types is very useful.

- Additional comment 2: The discussion highlighting biases in the M2 scorer is valuable.

- Additional comment 3: It would be interesting to correlate error-type difficulty with linguistic complexity.

- Overall assessment: Strong, practical paper with minor weaknesses; recommend acceptance with minor revisions.
