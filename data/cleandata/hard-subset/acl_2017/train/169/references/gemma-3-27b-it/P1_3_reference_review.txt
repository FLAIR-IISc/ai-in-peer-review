## Peer Review: Automatic Annotation and Evaluation of Error Types for Grammatical Error Correction

**Summary:**

This paper presents a method for automatically annotating grammatical error correction (GEC) system outputs with error types. The core idea is to leverage linguistically-enhanced edit alignment (Felice et al., 2016) to identify edits and then apply a rule-based system, grounded in POS tags and edit operations (insertion, deletion, substitution), to categorize those edits. The authors argue this enables more granular evaluation of GEC systems than currently possible, moving beyond overall scores to error-type specific performance.  They demonstrate their approach by re-evaluating the CoNLL-2014 shared task systems, showcasing the ability to break down performance by error type.

**Main Contributions (as I see them):**

1. **A practical and reproducible method for automatic error type annotation:** The presented rule-based system appears robust and, crucially, *dataset-agnostic*.  This is a significant advantage over purely machine learning approaches that require training data specific to each annotation scheme. The authors' intention to release the tool is a major plus.
2. **A detailed re-evaluation of the CoNLL-2014 shared task:**  This analysis provides valuable insights into the strengths and weaknesses of different GEC systems with respect to specific error types, contributing to a better understanding of the landscape of GEC research.
3. **Highlighting the mismatch between error detection and correction:** The analysis of the CoNLL-2014 systems reveals that many systems are better at *detecting* errors than actually *correcting* them, a subtle but important point for future research.

**Strengths:**

1. **Novelty and Practicality of the Approach:** The rule-based approach for error typing is a clever solution to the problem of adapting to different annotation schemes. The reliance on readily available (automatic) linguistic features (POS tags, lemmas) makes it highly practical and scalable.  This contrasts favorably with reliance on potentially scarce and expensive annotated data.
2. **Comprehensive Evaluation:** The authors provide a thorough evaluation of their system, including a manual validation step and a large-scale application to the CoNLL-2014 data.  The inclusion of bootstrap significance testing and comparison with the standard M2 scorer strengthen the findings. The detailed breakdown of performance across error types and edit operations is a key strength.
3. **Clear Presentation and Motivation:** The paper is well-written and clearly articulates the problem being addressed and the motivations behind the proposed solution.  The authors effectively contextualize their work within the GEC literature and highlight the limitations of existing evaluation metrics.
4. **Release of the Tool:** The commitment to releasing the developed tool is a significant contribution to the research community. It allows for easy replication and extension of the work.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Scope of Manual Evaluation:** While the manual evaluation is helpful, a sample size of 200 edits (from just two datasets) feels relatively small given the potential for nuance in error typing. The reliance on 5 raters and the somewhat subjective "Good," "Acceptable," "Bad" scale could benefit from a more detailed inter-annotator agreement analysis beyond the Kappa score.
2. **Potential for Error Propagation from Edit Extraction:** The accuracy of the entire pipeline is fundamentally tied to the quality of the initial edit extraction step (Felice et al., 2016). Errors in this stage will inevitably propagate to the error typing stage. While the authors acknowledge this, the impact of these errors on the overall results is not fully quantified.  Reporting the F1 score of the edit extraction on the CoNLL-2014 test set would be valuable.
3. **Simplification of Error Categories:**  While the dataset-agnostic nature of the rule-based system is a strength, it also leads to a level of simplification in error categories. Some error types (e.g., modal errors vs. tense errors, as discussed in section 3.2) are inevitably lumped together. This is a reasonable trade-off, but it’s important to acknowledge the inherent limitations.  



**Questions to Authors:**

1. What was the F1 score achieved by the edit extraction algorithm (Felice et al., 2016) specifically on the CoNLL-2014 test set used in your analysis?  This will help readers understand the potential impact of errors in this first step.
2. Could you provide more details about the distribution of error types in the CoNLL-2014 dataset *after* applying your automated annotation scheme? This would give a better sense of the prevalence of different error types and how your tool is distributing the classifications.
3.  You mention that the M2 scorer tends to overestimate performance.  Could you elaborate on this point and provide examples of how this overestimation manifests itself in the CoNLL-2014 data?
4. You mention the possibility of building specialist classifiers.  Have you experimented with combining your rule-based system with a small number of ML classifiers focused on a small subset of errors that are difficult for your rule based system to handle?



**Additional Comments:**

*   Table 9 (complete list of error codes) is useful but could be formatted for easier readability.
*   The discussion of multi-token errors (section 4.6) is insightful.
*   The paper would benefit from a discussion of the limitations of the rule-based approach, such as its inability to capture complex stylistic errors or subtle semantic nuances.
* The paper would benefit from an appendix providing the code for the rule-based classifier.



**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper that addresses a significant challenge in GEC evaluation. The proposed approach is novel, practical, and well-evaluated. While there are some minor weaknesses, the contributions of this work are substantial and warrant acceptance. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions, addressing the questions and suggestions outlined above.
