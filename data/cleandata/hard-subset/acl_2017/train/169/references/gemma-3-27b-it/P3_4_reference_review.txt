## Review for ACL - Automatic Annotation and Evaluation of Error Types for Grammatical Error Correction

**Summary:**

This paper addresses the critical issue of detailed error analysis in Grammatical Error Correction (GEC) systems, specifically stemming from the lack of error type annotations in system outputs following the CoNLL-2014 shared task. The authors propose a pipeline for automatically annotating GEC system hypotheses with error types, enabling more granular evaluation. The pipeline consists of linguistically-enhanced edit extraction (leveraging Felice et al., 2016) followed by a rule-based error typing scheme. The authors release a tool implementing this pipeline and demonstrate its effectiveness through analysis of the CoNLL-2014 data.

**Main Contributions (as I see them):**

1. **A Practical Tool for Error Type Annotation:** The most significant contribution is the readily available tool that automates the process of annotating GEC system outputs with error types. This lowers the barrier to more in-depth analysis and comparison of GEC systems.
2. **A Dataset-Independent Rule-Based Error Typing Scheme:** The authors demonstrate a viable alternative to machine learning-based error typing, which suffers from dataset bias. Their rule-based scheme, grounded in linguistic features, offers a more consistent and transparent approach.
3. **Detailed Analysis of CoNLL-2014 Systems:** The application of the tool to the CoNLL-2014 data provides valuable insights into the strengths and weaknesses of different systems with respect to specific error types.

**Strengths:**

1. **Addresses a Real and Important Problem:**  The lack of detailed error analysis has been a long-standing limitation in GEC research. This paper directly tackles this problem with a practical solution. The motivation is well-articulated and the problem is clearly defined.
2. **Sound Methodology:** Leveraging a state-of-the-art edit extraction method (Felice et al., 2016) provides a strong foundation for the pipeline. The choice of a rule-based approach for error typing, with its inherent advantages in terms of dataset independence and interpretability, is well-justified.
3. **Strong Evaluation and Validation:** The manual evaluation with GEC researchers, demonstrating >95% agreement with predicted error types, is compelling. The comparison of results using "auto" vs. "gold" references is insightful and validates the quality of the automated annotation. The exploration of different granularity levels (operation tier, detailed error types) showcases the flexibility of the framework.
4. **Clear Presentation & Reproducibility:** The paper is well-written and easy to follow. The authors’ commitment to releasing the tool is a significant positive aspect, promoting reproducibility and further research.  The inclusion of a complete list of error codes (Table 9) is helpful.
5. **Insightful Analysis:** The analysis of CoNLL-2014 systems reveals valuable patterns and suggests potential areas for improvement. The observation regarding the difficulty of correcting unnecessary token errors, and the complementary strengths of different systems, are particularly noteworthy.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Novelty of Core Techniques:**  While the *combination* of existing techniques is valuable, neither the edit extraction nor the rule-based classification is fundamentally novel. The paper’s contribution lies more in the integration and application of these techniques to the specific problem of GEC error type evaluation.  This is not necessarily a fatal flaw, but should be acknowledged.
2. **Dependency on POS Tagging Accuracy:** The rule-based error typing is heavily reliant on the accuracy of POS tags, as acknowledged by the authors. Errors in POS tagging can propagate and lead to misclassification. While the authors use a strong POS tagger (spaCy), this remains a potential bottleneck.  A more thorough error analysis examining the impact of POS tagging errors would be beneficial.
3. **Simplified Evaluation Metric:**  The direct comparison of edit spans, while simplifying evaluation, might be overly strict. It doesn't account for equivalent corrections that are expressed differently.  While the authors acknowledge the potential inflation of M2 scores, a more nuanced comparison beyond simply using "auto" references would be valuable.
4. **Limited Scope of CoNLL-2014 Analysis:** While the CoNLL-2014 analysis is insightful, it is limited to that specific dataset.  Evaluating the generalizability of the tool and framework on other GEC datasets (with different characteristics) would strengthen the findings.



**Questions to Authors:**

1. **Error Analysis of Rule-Based Classifier:** Can you provide a more detailed error analysis of the rule-based classifier? Specifically, what types of errors does it most frequently misclassify, and what are the common causes of these errors (e.g., POS tagging errors, ambiguous rules)?
2. **Impact of POS Tagging Errors:** Have you quantified the impact of POS tagging errors on the overall accuracy of the error typing? Perhaps through a small-scale experiment where you artificially introduce errors in the POS tags and measure the resulting change in classification accuracy?
3. **Extension to More Complex Errors:**  How easily could the framework be extended to handle more complex and nuanced error types that are not easily categorized by POS tags and basic linguistic features?
4. **Evaluation with Human Annotations:**  While the rater evaluation is valuable, do you have plans to compare the automatically annotated data with a set of manually annotated data on a larger scale to quantify the precision and recall of the system against human gold standard?

**Additional Comments:**

*   The paper would benefit from a discussion of the limitations of the rule-based approach.
*   A brief mention of related work on error typology in GEC (beyond the cited papers) would be helpful.



**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong and valuable contribution to the field of GEC. The authors have addressed a critical need for more detailed error analysis and have provided a practical tool that will be useful to the research community. While the core techniques are not entirely novel, the integration and application to this specific problem are well-executed and the results are compelling. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions addressing the weaknesses outlined above.
