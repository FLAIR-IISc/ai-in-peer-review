This paper presents a method for automatically annotating grammatical error correction (GEC) system outputs with error types, aiming to address the limitations of the CoNLL-2014 shared task evaluation. The core contribution is a rule-based error typing framework built upon linguistically-informed edit extraction. The authors claim to provide a dataset-agnostic and transparent method for detailed GEC evaluation, releasing a tool to facilitate this process. A secondary contribution is a re-analysis of the CoNLL-2014 shared task data using this new annotation scheme, revealing insights into the strengths and weaknesses of participating systems.

**Strengths:**

1. **Addressing a Clear Need:** The paper tackles a valid problem—the lack of detailed error type analysis in GEC evaluation. The inability to dissect system performance by error category hinders progress and understanding of GEC systems. Providing a method to address this is potentially useful.
2. **Methodological Soundness (in principle):** The choice of a rule-based approach, while potentially less flexible than machine learning, offers transparency and avoids the dataset bias inherent in trained classifiers. The reliance on automatically obtainable features (POS tags, lemmas) is a strength, facilitating broader applicability. The utilization of a well-established edit extraction method (Felice et al., 2016) provides a solid foundation.
3. **Comprehensive Error Type Scheme:** The 25-category error scheme, with the flexibility to evaluate at varying levels of granularity, appears reasonably comprehensive and thoughtfully designed. The attention to distinguishing between missing, unnecessary, and replacement errors is positive.
4. **Initial Validation:** The manual evaluation, while small-scale, provides some initial evidence of the rule-based classifier’s reasonableness, with raters finding >95% of predictions acceptable.  The reported inter-annotator agreement is also reasonable.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Lack of Rigorous Evaluation:** The core weakness lies in the limited and indirect evaluation of the error typing framework. Relying on human evaluation of appropriateness *after* automatic edit extraction introduces confounding factors; errors in the alignment process are not separated from errors in the typing process. A gold standard evaluation dataset *specifically annotated for error type* is crucial, and its absence severely limits confidence in the method’s accuracy. The comparison to previous classifier-based approaches is also problematic, as the error schemes are different.
2. **Overreliance on Existing Work:** The paper heavily relies on the work of Felice et al. (2016) for edit extraction. While this is a sensible choice, the paper does not sufficiently demonstrate the benefit of the *combined* approach—i.e., how the proposed error typing framework specifically enhances the value of the extracted edits. The paper essentially builds *on top of* existing work without making a sufficiently independent contribution in terms of core methodology.
3. **Re-analysis Limitations:** The re-analysis of CoNLL-2014 data, while interesting, is undermined by the need to re-process the gold standard references using the same automatic pipeline. While the authors claim this doesn’t significantly affect results, it’s a non-standard practice that casts doubt on the validity of the comparisons.  The observations regarding system performance are largely descriptive and lack deeper analysis. For example, simply stating that some teams struggled with unnecessary token errors doesn’t provide actionable insights.
4. **Limited Novelty:** While the combination of linguistically informed edit extraction with rule-based error typing is not entirely novel, the paper doesn’t present a significant advancement beyond existing techniques. The approach feels like a well-engineered application of existing methods rather than a breakthrough innovation.

**Questions to Authors:**

1. Can you provide any results on the precision, recall, and F1-score of your error typing framework when compared to a gold-standard dataset of annotated edits? If such a dataset is not available, what steps are being taken to create one?
2. How robust is the framework to errors in the edit extraction phase?  What is the impact of incorrect edit boundaries on the accuracy of error type assignment?
3.  The paper claims dataset independence.  How would the performance of the framework change if applied to a dataset with different annotation guidelines, such as a dataset focused on a different L1 background?
4. What are the limitations of the rule-based approach? Are there specific error types that consistently pose challenges for the classifier?



**Additional Comments:**

* Table 9, listing the complete error types, is helpful but could be formatted for better readability.
* The discussion of the M2 scorer and its potential biases is insightful.
* The paper is well-written and clearly structured. However, the lack of strong empirical validation significantly weakens the overall contribution.

Overall, this paper presents a reasonably sound approach to a relevant problem, but the lack of rigorous evaluation and limited novelty prevent a strong recommendation for acceptance. The tool release is a positive aspect, but its value is diminished without stronger evidence of its accuracy and reliability. I recommend rejection in its current form, with encouragement to the authors to prioritize a robust evaluation using gold-standard data in future work.
