## Review for ACL: Automatic Annotation and Evaluation of Error Types for Grammatical Error Correction

**Summary:**

This paper addresses the challenge of evaluating Grammatical Error Correction (GEC) systems at a granular, error-type level. The authors present a pipeline for automatically annotating system outputs with error types, enabling more detailed analysis than previously possible. The pipeline consists of linguistically-enhanced edit extraction (leveraging Felice et al., 2016) followed by rule-based error type classification based on POS tags and edit operations. The authors demonstrate the utility of their approach by re-evaluating the CoNLL-2014 shared task submissions, providing insights into the strengths and weaknesses of different systems.

**Main Contributions (as I see them):**

1. **A Practical and Reproducible Tool for Error Type Annotation:** The most significant contribution is the development and (promised) release of a tool for automatically annotating GEC outputs. This directly addresses a known bottleneck in GEC evaluation, allowing for deeper analysis of system performance beyond overall scores.
2. **A Dataset-Independent Rule-Based Error Typing Framework:** The authors’ decision to create a rule-based system, rather than relying on supervised learning, is a smart one.  It avoids the issues of domain dependence and data scarcity inherent in training classifiers for different error annotation schemes.
3. **Detailed Re-evaluation of CoNLL-2014:** The application of their tool to the CoNLL-2014 data provides a valuable case study, revealing interesting patterns in system behavior that weren’t apparent from the original evaluation. 



**Strengths:**

1. **Addresses a Critical Gap in GEC Evaluation:** The paper tackles a long-standing problem in the field - the lack of fine-grained error type evaluation. The ability to automatically annotate system outputs is a significant step forward.
2. **Strong Methodological Foundation:**  Building on the work of Felice et al. (2016) for edit extraction is sensible, leveraging a well-established and accurate approach.  The rule-based error typing is clearly explained and justified, with a strong rationale for avoiding machine learning approaches.
3. **Thorough Evaluation & Insightful Analysis:** The detailed re-evaluation of CoNLL-2014, combined with the analysis of different error types and operation tiers, provides valuable insights into the performance of various GEC systems. The finding regarding the difficulty of correcting unnecessary errors is particularly interesting.
4. **Clear and Well-Written:** The paper is generally well-written and easy to follow, with a clear explanation of the methodology and results. The inclusion of a comprehensive error type list (Table 2) is helpful.
5. **Commitment to Reproducibility:** The promise to release the tool is essential for the community to benefit from this work.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Evaluation of the Annotation Accuracy:** While the manual evaluation with 5 researchers is a good start, the sample size (200 edits) is relatively small. A larger-scale evaluation, perhaps using crowd-sourcing, would provide more confidence in the accuracy of the automatic annotation process.  The focus on “Good”, “Acceptable”, and “Bad” is also somewhat vague; inter-annotator agreement on *specific* error type assignments would be more informative.
2. **Potential Bias in the Rule-Based System:** While avoiding training data dependence is a strength, the rules themselves inherently reflect the authors’ biases regarding error categorization. While the raters generally agreed with the categorization, it's unclear whether they would have made different choices if they were defining the error types themselves.
3. **Over-Reliance on POS Tagging:** The dependence on POS tagging introduces a point of failure. The results show errors stemming from POS tagging inaccuracies, highlighting a vulnerability.  Exploring ways to make the system more robust to POS tagging errors (e.g., incorporating contextual information) would be beneficial.
4. **Limited Discussion of Error Boundaries:** The paper acknowledges the potential for misalignment of edit boundaries between the automatic and gold references but doesn't fully explore the implications of these discrepancies. More analysis of boundary errors would be useful.
5. **The M2 Scorer comparison feels somewhat defensive:** The justification for using auto-references instead of gold references, while reasonable, feels slightly apologetic. Acknowledging the limitations of *both* reference types and focusing on the relative insights gained from their approach would be more convincing.



**Questions to Authors:**

1.  Could you provide more detail on the types of errors observed when raters judged edits as “Bad”? Was there a pattern in these errors beyond POS tagging inaccuracies?
2.  Given the reliance on POS tagging, have you experimented with different POS taggers or considered incorporating multiple taggers and a consensus mechanism?
3.  You mention the possibility of adding new categories for more detailed error types. What are some examples of such categories, and what would be the trade-offs of increasing the granularity of the error scheme?
4.  How robust is your approach to handling noisy or ungrammatical input? Does the performance degrade significantly when applied to text with more severe errors?
5.  Regarding the observation that systems struggle with unnecessary token errors, do you have any hypotheses as to why this is the case? Is it a detection problem, a correction problem, or both?




**Additional Comments:**

*   The table formatting is a bit dense and could be improved for readability.  
*   The phrase "dataset-agnostic" is used frequently. While technically accurate, it might be better to emphasize “domain-independent” to avoid confusion.
*   It would be interesting to see a comparison of the performance of your tool on different GEC datasets (e.g., FCE, BEA).



**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper that addresses an important challenge in the field of GEC. The authors’ approach is well-motivated, clearly explained, and supported by compelling results. While there are some weaknesses, they are relatively minor and do not detract significantly from the overall value of the work. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions to address the concerns raised in the review.
