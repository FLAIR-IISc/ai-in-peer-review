## Review for ACL: Automatic Annotation and Evaluation of Error Types for Grammatical Error Correction

**Summary:**

This paper presents a method for automatically annotating grammatical error correction (GEC) system outputs with error types. The approach consists of linguistically-enhanced edit extraction followed by rule-based error type classification, relying solely on automatic linguistic analysis (POS tags, lemmas, stemming) to avoid dependence on labeled training data. The authors demonstrate the utility of their tool by re-analyzing the CoNLL-2014 shared task data, providing a detailed breakdown of system performance across different error types and operations.

**Main Contributions:**

1. **A Dataset-Independent Error Type Annotation Framework:** The core contribution is a rule-based system for assigning error types that doesn't require task-specific or domain-specific training data. This addresses a significant limitation in GEC evaluation, where comparing systems across different datasets is difficult due to annotation scheme variations.
2. **Detailed Analysis of CoNLL-2014 Systems:** The authors provide a much richer analysis of the CoNLL-2014 GEC systems than previously possible, going beyond overall scores to reveal strengths and weaknesses in handling specific error types.
3. **A Publicly Available Tool:**  The authors state the tool will be released alongside the paper, which promotes reproducibility and enables further research in this area.

**Strengths:**

1. **Addresses a Crucial Gap in GEC Evaluation:** The paper tackles a well-recognized problem – the lack of detailed, standardized error type analysis in GEC.  Existing metrics often treat corrections as binary (correct/incorrect) without revealing *what* errors systems are good or bad at fixing. This work directly addresses this limitation.
2. **Strong Justification for Rule-Based Approach:** The authors convincingly argue for a rule-based approach, highlighting the drawbacks of machine learning methods tied to specific training data and potential biases. The emphasis on transparency and deterministic behavior is a strong point.
3. **Robust Evaluation & Validation:** The manual evaluation, despite being small-scale, provides a reasonable level of confidence in the accuracy of the automatic annotation.  The bootstrap significance testing confirming equivalence between gold and auto-annotated references is also valuable. The analysis of the CoNLL-2014 data is comprehensive and insightful, revealing differences between systems.
4. **Practical Impact:**  The publicly available tool will be highly useful to the GEC community, enabling researchers to more effectively analyze and compare their systems. The detailed error type breakdowns provided in the paper offer concrete directions for future research.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Scope of Error Types:** While 25 error types are a good start, they might not be granular enough to capture the full complexity of grammatical errors. The authors acknowledge this and the trade-off with practicality, but a more extensive typology might be valuable.
2. **Dependence on POS Tagging Accuracy:** The system’s performance is intrinsically linked to the accuracy of the POS tagger and other NLP tools used (lemmatization, stemming). Errors in these tools will directly impact the accuracy of the error type classification, as demonstrated by the raters' feedback.  The authors acknowledge this but don't provide detailed error analysis relating to specific NLP tool failures.
3. **Simplified Edit Comparison:** While the authors convincingly argue against the biases of the M2 scorer, their proposed edit comparison method feels somewhat simplistic.  Ignoring partial matches or subtle variations in corrections could lead to underestimation of system performance. A more nuanced comparison might be warranted.
4. **Limited Discussion of Error Interactions:** The analysis largely focuses on individual error types. However, real-world errors often involve interactions between multiple error types. Exploring these interactions could provide deeper insights.



**Questions to Authors:**

1. **Error Analysis & NLP Tooling:** Could you provide a more detailed analysis of errors made by your system that can be traced back to inaccuracies in the POS tagger or other NLP tools? What steps could be taken to mitigate the impact of these errors?
2. **Edit Comparison:** Could you elaborate on the rationale behind choosing a strict edit comparison approach? Have you explored alternative comparison methods (e.g., allowing for partial matches or fuzzy matching) and if so, what were the results?
3. **Future Work - Error Type Granularity:** Do you have plans to expand the error type typology in the future? If so, what are some of the additional error types you are considering and how would you address the trade-off between informativeness and practicality?
4. **Error Interaction:** Do you plan to explore error interactions, such as the relationships between different error types, in future work?




**Additional Comments:**

* Table 9, listing the error code combinations, is very helpful for understanding the granularity of the framework.
* The clear presentation of results, along with the tables and explanations, makes the paper easy to follow.




**Overall Assessment:**

This is a valuable contribution to the field of GEC.  The proposed method for automatic error type annotation is well-motivated, robustly evaluated, and has the potential to significantly improve the way GEC systems are analyzed and compared. While there are some minor weaknesses, the strengths of the work outweigh them.  I recommend acceptance with minor revisions addressing the questions above.
