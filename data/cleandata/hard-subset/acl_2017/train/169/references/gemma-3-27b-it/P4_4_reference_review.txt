This paper addresses the challenge of automatically annotating grammatical error correction (GEC) system outputs with error type information, a missing component in previous shared tasks like CoNLL-2014. The authors propose a two-step approach: linguistically-enhanced edit extraction followed by rule-based error type classification. The primary contributions are: (1) a rule-based error typing framework independent of labeled training data, relying instead on automatic linguistic markup; (2) a publicly released tool implementing this annotation pipeline; and (3) a detailed re-evaluation of the CoNLL-2014 shared task results using the proposed annotation scheme, providing novel insights into system strengths and weaknesses.

**Strengths:**

1. **Novelty of the Rule-Based Approach:** The development of a dataset-agnostic error typing framework is a significant contribution. Existing methods relying on machine learning are often tied to specific datasets and annotation schemes, limiting comparability. The rule-based approach, leveraging automatically obtained linguistic features, provides a more standardized and portable solution. This is particularly valuable for analyzing systems trained and tested on diverse datasets.

2. **Comprehensive CoNLL-2014 Re-Analysis:**  Applying the proposed tool to the CoNLL-2014 data and performing a detailed error type analysis offers valuable insights into the performance of different systems. This re-evaluation goes beyond overall scores, highlighting strengths and weaknesses related to specific error types (e.g., identifying that CUUI excels at unnecessary token errors while CAMB performs best on missing token errors). This level of granularity is a key advance.

3. **Practical Tool Release:** The authors' commitment to releasing the annotation tool increases the impact of this work. This allows other researchers to readily apply the framework to their own GEC systems and datasets, fostering reproducibility and further research in this area. The ability to obtain standardized error type annotations from system outputs is a valuable resource for the GEC community.

4. **Clear Justification and Evaluation:** The paper clearly articulates the motivation behind the work – the limitations of existing GEC evaluation methods – and provides a thorough evaluation of the rule-based classifier. While not relying on a gold standard for direct accuracy measurement, the manual evaluation by GEC researchers demonstrates the reasonableness and acceptability of the predicted error categories, with high agreement rates.




**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Scope of Error Type Granularity:** While the 25 error types are a good starting point, the framework might be too coarse-grained for some applications. Many error types encompass a wide range of issues, and finer-grained distinctions could be valuable for more targeted system improvements. The authors acknowledge this limitation, but a discussion of potential avenues for expansion would be beneficial.

2. **Dependence on POS Tagging Accuracy:** The rule-based approach heavily relies on the accuracy of POS tagging, lemmatization, and stemming. The authors recognize this and mention inter-annotator disagreement can often be traced back to tagging errors. Errors in these automatic processes will inevitably propagate to the error type classification, potentially limiting the reliability of the annotations.  A quantitative analysis of the impact of POS tagging accuracy on annotation quality would strengthen the paper.

3. **Oversimplification in Reference Comparison:** The decision to re-process the reference data using the automatic alignment and classification pipeline, while addressing a mismatch, potentially discards valuable information from the original human annotations. While the results indicate minimal impact on overall scores, a more nuanced discussion of the trade-offs involved in this simplification is warranted.  Specifically, exploring the discrepancies between the original and re-processed reference annotations could provide insights into the limitations of the automated pipeline.




**Questions to Authors:**

1.  Could you provide a more detailed analysis of the types of errors that frequently lead to disagreement between the automated classification and the human raters in the evaluation, specifically relating this back to the POS tagging accuracy?
2.  What are your plans for extending the error type framework to incorporate more fine-grained distinctions, and how do you envision maintaining the dataset independence of the approach as the number of rules increases?
3.  Could you elaborate on the specific reasons for the poor performance on multi-token edits? Is it due to the alignment algorithm, the error typing rules, or other factors?
