- Proposes an automatic, rule-based pipeline that annotates GEC system outputs with error types using linguistically-enhanced alignment and automatic linguistic features.
- Releases a practical, reproducible tool and methodology for automatic error-type annotation adaptable to new datasets.
- Applies the tool to CoNLL-2014 systems, producing detailed error-type analyses that reveal system strengths and weaknesses beyond overall scores.
- Shows that a rule-based classifier can achieve high agreement with human annotators, offering a viable alternative to ML-based typers especially for dataset-independent use.
- Addresses a significant evaluation gap in GEC by enabling detailed error-type analysis rather than relying solely on aggregate scores.
- Uses a strong methodology combining linguistically-informed alignment and carefully designed rule-based classification, justified by dataset independence goals.
- Provides a thorough evaluation including human assessment of classification accuracy, comparisons to M2 scoring, and analysis of edit size effects.
- Enhances reproducibility and community utility by releasing the annotation tool.
- Presents clear, well-organized writing that makes the approach and results easy to follow.
- Human evaluation is limited in scale (200 edits, 5 raters) and could benefit from larger samples and more inter-annotator agreement context.
- Error scheme granularity choices are potentially problematic (e.g., modal vs. tense distinctions) and sensitivity to category definitions is not fully explored.
- Lacks a detailed error analysis of the annotation tool itself—what misclassifications occur and why beyond noting POS tagging issues.
- Does not compare directly to existing ML-based automatic typers on the same dataset to benchmark relative performance.
- Requests clarification on criteria for defining error-category granularity and when to merge vs. split categories.
- Asks for a breakdown of the most frequent misclassifications made by the automatic annotator.
- Inquires whether different POS taggers or stemming algorithms were tried and how much they affected classifier performance.
- Requests release of code/scripts for the detailed system analysis (e.g., tables) to facilitate reproduction and extension.
- Asks for a deeper explanation of why the M2 scorer overestimates performance and how that affects Table 4 interpretations.
- Suggests adding a brief explanation of why Damerau-Levenshtein is referenced for readers unfamiliar with edit distance variants.
- Recommends improving Table 2 formatting for readability.
- Advises adding an explicit limitations and future work section addressing noted weaknesses.
- Overall recommendation: strong, practical contribution with minor revisions requested; recommend acceptance with minor revisions.
