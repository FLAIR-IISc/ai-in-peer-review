## Peer Review: Automatic Annotation and Evaluation of Error Types for Grammatical Error Correction

**Summary:**

This paper presents a method for automatically annotating grammatical error correction (GEC) system outputs with error types. The approach consists of two main stages: linguistically-enhanced edit extraction and rule-based error type classification. The authors argue that this automated annotation allows for a more detailed evaluation of GEC systems than previously possible, overcoming the limitations of existing evaluation metrics. They demonstrate their approach by re-evaluating the CoNLL-2014 shared task data and providing a detailed breakdown of error type performance for each participating team.

**Main Contributions:**

1. **Automated Error Type Annotation Pipeline:** The development and implementation of a complete pipeline for automatically annotating GEC corrections with error types, addressing a significant gap in the field. This pipeline relies on linguistic features and a rule-based system, making it dataset-independent.
2. **Detailed Analysis of CoNLL-2014 Systems:**  A comprehensive re-evaluation of the CoNLL-2014 shared task systems, providing detailed error type performance data for each system. This analysis uncovers interesting insights into the strengths and weaknesses of different GEC approaches.
3. **Dataset-Independent Error Typing:**  The development of a rule-based error typing framework that avoids the biases inherent in machine learning approaches trained on specific datasets.  This promotes comparability across different GEC corpora.



**Strengths:**

1. **Addresses a Critical Need:** The lack of detailed error type evaluation has been a longstanding problem in GEC. This paper directly tackles this issue with a practical and well-defined solution. The ability to automatically annotate corrections is highly valuable for researchers and developers.
2. **Strong Technical Approach:**  Leveraging linguistically-informed edit extraction (Felice et al., 2016) provides a robust foundation for the annotation process. The rule-based error typing, while potentially less flexible than machine learning, offers the crucial advantage of dataset independence and transparency. The modularity of the error type scheme (prefixing with M, R, or U) adds further granularity.
3. **Comprehensive Evaluation:**  Applying the tool to the CoNLL-2014 data is a convincing demonstration of its utility. The detailed analysis provided, including breakdowns by operation tier and specific error types, reveals valuable insights that were previously inaccessible.  The comparison with the M2 scorer, and the explanation of its potential biases, is a significant contribution.
4. **Reproducibility & Accessibility:**  The authors’ commitment to releasing the tool with the paper ensures reproducibility and encourages further research. This is a crucial aspect of making the work impactful.
5. **Rigorous Evaluation of the Classifier:** The manual evaluation of the rule-based classifier, demonstrating >95% “Good” or “Acceptable” ratings, provides confidence in the quality of the automatic annotation.




**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Scope of Error Types:** While 25 error types is a good start, the framework appears somewhat limited in its granularity. The authors acknowledge this in the discussion of modal errors vs. tense errors (Section 3.2).  A more extensive taxonomy might be needed to capture the full range of GEC errors.
2. **Dependency on POS Tagging Accuracy:** The entire system relies heavily on the accuracy of POS tagging. As demonstrated by the raters' feedback (Section 3.3), errors in POS tagging can directly translate to incorrect error type assignments.  The impact of POS tagging errors isn’t fully quantified.
3. **Over-reliance on Rule-Based Approach:** While dataset independence is a strength, the rigidness of the rule-based system may limit its ability to handle complex or nuanced errors that don't neatly fit into predefined categories. The lack of adaptation mechanisms is a potential drawback.
4. **Missing Error Detection Analysis:** While the paper touches upon detection vs. correction, the analysis feels somewhat superficial.  A more in-depth study of the systems’ ability to *identify* errors, separate from their ability to *correct* them, would be valuable. 
5. **Limited Discussion of Inter-Annotator Agreement:** Although kappa is reported, the study only involved 5 raters. While acceptable, increasing the number of raters could further solidify the evaluation.

**Questions to Authors:**

1.  You mention the potential for expanding the error scheme with more granular categories (Section 3.2). What are the biggest challenges in expanding the scheme, and what types of errors would you prioritize adding?
2.  How sensitive is the performance of the error type classification to errors in the POS tagging stage? Could you provide a quantitative analysis of the impact of POS tagging errors on the final error type assignments?  
3.  Do you foresee any possibilities of incorporating a machine learning component into the framework to address the limitations of the rule-based approach, while still maintaining dataset independence (e.g., through transfer learning or meta-learning)?
4.  The paper notes that multi-token errors were particularly difficult for the systems to correct. Could you delve deeper into *why* this is the case? Are there specific types of multi-token errors that pose the biggest challenges?




**Additional Comments:**

*   Table 2 is difficult to read due to its length and formatting. Consider breaking it down into smaller tables or using a different presentation style.
*   The reference list is missing some formatting details (e.g., consistent capitalization).




**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper that makes a significant contribution to the field of Grammatical Error Correction. The proposed method addresses a crucial need for detailed error type analysis, and the thorough evaluation on the CoNLL-2014 data provides valuable insights. While there are some limitations, the strengths of the work clearly outweigh the weaknesses. I recommend **acceptance** with minor revisions to address the questions and suggestions outlined above.
