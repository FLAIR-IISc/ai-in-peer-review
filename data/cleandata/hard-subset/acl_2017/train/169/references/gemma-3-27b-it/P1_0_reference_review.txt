## Peer Review: Automatic Annotation and Evaluation of Error Types for Grammatical Error Correction

**Summary:**

This paper addresses the problem of evaluating Grammatical Error Correction (GEC) systems beyond overall scores, by proposing a method for automatically annotating system outputs with error types. The approach leverages linguistically-enhanced edit alignment followed by a rule-based error type classification system. The authors demonstrate the utility of their tool by re-evaluating the CoNLL-2014 shared task data, providing a more granular analysis of team performance.  A key claim is that their rule-based system achieves high agreement with human annotators (over 95% “Good” or “Acceptable” ratings) and provides a dataset-agnostic evaluation framework.

**Main Contributions (as I see them):**

1. **A practical, automated pipeline for error type annotation:** The authors provide a readily usable tool (to be released) that addresses a significant bottleneck in GEC evaluation - the lack of detailed, error-type specific feedback for systems.
2. **A dataset-agnostic error typing framework:** By relying on automatic linguistic features (POS tags, lemmas, etc.), the system avoids the biases inherent in training classifiers on specific annotated datasets. This is a valuable contribution for broader GEC research.
3. **A re-analysis of CoNLL-2014 results:** The application of the tool to the CoNLL-2014 dataset provides a valuable new perspective on the strengths and weaknesses of different GEC systems, revealing nuanced performance differences.

**Strengths:**

1. **Addressing a clear need:** The lack of fine-grained error analysis in GEC has long been a limitation. This paper directly tackles that issue with a well-motivated and practical solution.
2. **Strong Validation:** The manual evaluation, while small-scale, provides compelling evidence for the accuracy and reliability of the rule-based classifier.  The >95% “Good”/“Acceptable” rating is a significant result.
3. **Comprehensive Error Type Scheme:** The 25 error types, combined with the ability to evaluate at different levels of granularity (Missing, Replacement, Unnecessary prefixes), offer a rich and flexible framework for analysis.  The clear table outlining the scheme (Table 2) is helpful.
4. **Practical Focus:**  The emphasis on using readily available tools (spaCy, NLTK, Hunspell) and avoiding reliance on labeled training data makes the approach highly accessible and scalable.
5. **Insightful CoNLL-2014 Analysis:** The analysis of the CoNLL-2014 data reveals interesting patterns in team performance, highlighting the strengths of different approaches for specific error types.  This provides concrete avenues for future research.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Novelty in Core Techniques:** The use of linguistically-enhanced alignment (Felice et al., 2016) and rule-based classification are not entirely novel.  The main contribution lies in the *combination* of these techniques and their application to the specific problem of error type evaluation. This should be more explicitly acknowledged.
2. **Dependence on POS Tagging Accuracy:** The classifier's performance is clearly tied to the accuracy of the POS tagger. While the authors acknowledge this in the error analysis (Table 3, example with "ring"), the sensitivity of the system to POS tagging errors warrants further investigation and potential mitigation strategies.  A more thorough error analysis, quantifying the impact of POS tagging inaccuracies on the overall results, would be beneficial.
3. **Lack of Comparison to Existing Supervised Approaches:** While the authors rightly point out the limitations of supervised methods, a brief comparison (even if qualitative) to the performance of previously published classifiers on the same data would strengthen the argument for the rule-based approach.  Demonstrating how the proposed approach avoids the pitfalls of those methods would be persuasive.
4. **Evaluation Scope:** The manual evaluation involved only 200 edits. While this provides a valuable initial assessment, a larger-scale evaluation, ideally on multiple datasets, would further bolster confidence in the classifier’s generalizability.




**Questions to Authors:**

1.  How sensitive is the overall performance of the system to the accuracy of the POS tagger? Can you provide more quantitative data on the impact of POS tagging errors on error type classification?
2.  While you highlight the dataset-agnostic nature of your approach, have you tested it on datasets significantly different from the CoNLL-2014 dataset (e.g., different learner L1 backgrounds)? If so, what were the results?
3.  Have you considered incorporating additional features beyond POS tags and lemmas to improve the classifier’s accuracy (e.g., dependency parse information, word embeddings)?
4.  How does your system handle cases where the automatically extracted edit boundaries differ significantly from human-annotated boundaries?  Does the evaluation methodology account for this discrepancy?
5. Could you elaborate on the specific motivations behind the design choices within your error type scheme (e.g., why differentiate between certain types of errors and not others)?



**Additional Comments:**

*   The paper is well-written and clearly organized.
*   The inclusion of a complete list of error types (Table 9) in the appendix is helpful.
*   The decision to release the tool with the paper is commendable and will greatly benefit the GEC community.
*   Consider adding a brief discussion of the limitations of the rule-based approach, such as its inability to capture nuanced or context-dependent errors.




Overall, this is a strong paper that addresses an important problem in GEC evaluation. The proposed approach is practical, well-validated, and has the potential to significantly advance the field.  Addressing the weaknesses outlined above would further strengthen the contribution and make this a highly valuable addition to the ACL literature. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions.
