This paper tackles the important problem of evaluating Grammatical Error Correction (GEC) systems beyond overall scores, by automatically annotating system outputs with error types. The core contribution is a rule-based error typing framework that operates solely on automatically obtained linguistic features (POS tags, lemmas, etc.), avoiding the need for labeled training data and the associated dataset-dependency issues. A secondary, but valuable, contribution is the release of the annotation tool alongside the paper, facilitating reproducibility and further research. Finally, the comprehensive analysis of the CoNLL-2014 shared task data using this tool provides a useful baseline and identifies areas where existing GEC systems struggle.

**Strengths:**

1. **Novelty of the Approach:** The dataset-agnostic, rule-based error typing is a genuinely novel idea. Most existing approaches rely on supervised learning, making them difficult to adapt to new datasets or error taxonomies. This approach sidesteps that issue entirely, offering a more flexible and potentially more robust solution. The focus on automating annotation guidelines, rather than replicating human annotation *performance*, is a smart and under-explored direction.
2. **Practicality & Accessibility:** Releasing the annotation tool is crucial. It immediately lowers the barrier to entry for researchers wanting to perform detailed GEC system evaluations. The straightforward rule-based nature of the error typing makes it easily inspectable and modifiable, fostering community contribution and refinement.
3. **Insightful Analysis of CoNLL-2014:** The application of the tool to the CoNLL-2014 data yields valuable insights into the strengths and weaknesses of different systems. Highlighting the struggles with unnecessary token errors and multi-token edits, for example, points to specific areas for improvement in GEC research. The finding that different systems excel at different error *types* reinforces the need for this granular level of analysis.
4. **Strong Validation, Given the Task:** While full gold-standard evaluation is impossible, the manual evaluation by GEC researchers, demonstrating >95% agreement with the rule-based classifications, is a strong indicator of the framework's effectiveness. The comparison to M2 scores and the argument for its potential inflation are also insightful.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Error Typing Granularity:** While the 25 error types are a good starting point, they feel somewhat coarse-grained. The authors acknowledge this, but a more nuanced scheme could unlock even deeper insights. The trade-off between informativeness and practicality is valid, but the current framework leans heavily towards practicality.  A consideration of dependency parse information beyond just POS tags might yield finer distinctions.
2. **Reliance on POS Tagging Accuracy:** The entire framework hinges on accurate POS tagging. The paper acknowledges this, and the manual evaluation points to POS tagging errors as a source of misclassification, but doesn’t fully explore the impact of POS tagger errors on downstream performance. An error analysis specifically focusing on POS tagging-induced errors would be valuable.
3. **Evaluation Focus:** The evaluation primarily focuses on agreement with human ratings and comparisons to M2 scores. A more direct evaluation of the *usefulness* of the error type annotations – for example, in guiding system development or error analysis – would strengthen the paper. Demonstrating how these annotations could be used to improve a GEC system, even in a small-scale experiment, would be compelling.
4. **Minor: Presentation of Results:** Table 6 is extremely dense and difficult to parse. Presenting the results in a more visually appealing manner (e.g., using heatmaps or grouped bar charts) would improve readability.

**Questions to Authors:**

1.  Could you elaborate on the limitations of the current error typing scheme and discuss potential avenues for expanding it to include more nuanced error categories?
2.  What is the estimated impact of POS tagging errors on the overall accuracy of the error type classification? Have you considered incorporating more robust POS taggers or error correction mechanisms to mitigate this issue?
3.  Can you provide a concrete example of how the error type annotations generated by your tool could be used to guide the development or improvement of a specific GEC system?
4.  Regarding the finding that systems struggle with unnecessary token errors, do you have any hypotheses as to *why* this is the case? Could this be related to the difficulty of reliably detecting superfluous words?



**Additional Comments:**

The paper is well-written and clearly explains the methodology. The related work section is comprehensive. The inclusion of the complete list of error types in the appendix is helpful.  The bootstrapping methodology used to validate the "auto vs. gold" results is appropriate. Overall, this is a promising and innovative contribution to the field of GEC, and I believe it warrants acceptance despite the minor weaknesses. The novelty of the approach and the potential impact of the released tool outweigh the methodological imperfections.
