## Review of "Automatic Annotation and Evaluation of Error Types for Grammatical Error Correction"

**Summary:** This paper addresses the problem of evaluating Grammatical Error Correction (GEC) systems beyond overall scores by proposing a method for automatically annotating system outputs with error types. The core idea is to combine linguistically-enhanced edit extraction (leveraging Felice et al., 2016) with a rule-based error typing framework. The authors demonstrate the effectiveness of their approach through an analysis of the CoNLL-2014 shared task data, showing it allows for detailed error-type specific evaluations and highlighting strengths/weaknesses of individual systems.  The authors will release the tool developed with this paper.

**Main Contributions (as I see them):**

1. **A practical and accessible method for automatic error type annotation:** The rule-based approach, relying on automatically obtained linguistic features, is a significant contribution.  It avoids the need for expensive and potentially biased labeled data for each new dataset or system, making error type analysis more readily applicable.
2. **Detailed analysis of CoNLL-2014 systems:** Applying the method to the CoNLL-2014 data provides valuable insights into the performance of different systems on specific error types, something previously impossible due to the lack of standardized error annotation.  This analysis itself is a substantial contribution to the GEC community.
3. **Demonstration of the equivalence of auto vs. gold reference annotations for evaluation:**  The finding that using automatically annotated references for evaluation doesn't significantly impact results (compared to gold-standard annotations after reprocessing) is an important methodological observation.

**Strengths:**

1. **Novelty and Practicality:** While the idea of error type analysis isn't new, the fully automated, dataset-independent approach is highly practical and fills a crucial gap in the field. Existing methods relying on classifiers require significant retraining and are prone to dataset bias. This work sidesteps that problem elegantly.
2. **Thorough Evaluation:** The paper provides a comprehensive evaluation, including a manual assessment of the rule-based classifier’s accuracy, comparison with existing metrics (M2 scorer), and a detailed breakdown of system performance on different error types.  The use of bootstrap significance testing to validate the auto vs. gold reference comparison is commendable.
3. **Clear and Well-Structured Presentation:** The paper is well-written, clearly explains the methodology, and provides sufficient detail for replication.  The organization is logical, and the tables/figures are well-designed.
4. **Availability of Tool:** The promise to release the tool is a significant benefit to the research community, enabling wider adoption and further research.
5. **Insightful Findings:**  The analysis of the CoNLL-2014 systems reveals intriguing patterns, such as the struggle of many systems with unnecessary token errors and the differing strengths of MT vs. classifier approaches.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Scope of Error Categories:** While the 25 error types are a good starting point, they might be too coarse-grained for some applications. The authors acknowledge this limitation, but it's worth emphasizing that a more granular scheme could reveal even more nuanced performance differences.  Specifically, the categorization of "Other" feels like a catch-all and could benefit from further refinement.
2. **Reliance on POS Tagging Accuracy:** The performance of the rule-based classifier is heavily dependent on the accuracy of the POS tagger and lemmatizer. The paper acknowledges this, pointing to examples where tagging errors lead to misclassification.  A sensitivity analysis exploring the impact of different POS tagging systems or error correction techniques for tags would strengthen the work.
3. **Potential for Cascading Errors:** The method relies on accurate edit extraction *before* error typing.  Errors in the initial alignment step could propagate and lead to incorrect error type assignments.  The paper mentions the alignment accuracy of Felice et al. (2016) (80-85 F1), but it would be helpful to quantify the impact of alignment errors on the final error type annotation accuracy.  
4. **Lack of Comparison to Alternative Automatic Annotation Approaches:** While the authors position their approach against supervised learning methods, a more direct comparison to other rule-based systems, or recent advancements in neural-based automatic post-editing evaluation metrics, would provide further context and demonstrate the relative advantages of their method.



**Questions to Authors:**

1.  Could you elaborate on the limitations of the current 25 error types and discuss potential avenues for expanding the framework to include more granular categories?
2.  How sensitive is the performance of the error type classifier to the specific POS tagger used?  Have you explored using alternative taggers or error correction techniques for POS tags to improve accuracy?
3.  Can you provide an estimate of the impact of errors in the edit extraction phase on the accuracy of the error type annotation?  For example, what percentage of misclassified errors are directly attributable to inaccurate edit boundaries?
4.  How does this method perform on GEC systems trained with different data domains or different target learner levels? Does the performance degrade significantly in these cases, and if so, are there potential strategies to mitigate these effects?




**Additional Comments:**

* The table detailing the complete list of error types (Table 2) is incredibly useful.
* The discussion of the M2 scorer's biases is valuable and highlights the importance of careful metric selection.
* It might be interesting to explore the correlation between the difficulty of correcting certain error types (as revealed by the CoNLL-2014 analysis) and the linguistic complexity of those errors.



**Overall Assessment:**  This is a strong paper with significant contributions to the field of GEC. The proposed method is practical, well-evaluated, and promises to facilitate more detailed and insightful analysis of GEC systems. The weaknesses are relatively minor and do not detract significantly from the overall value of the work. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions.
