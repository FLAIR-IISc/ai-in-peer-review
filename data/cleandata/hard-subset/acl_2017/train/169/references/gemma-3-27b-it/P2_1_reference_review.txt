## Review for ACL

**Summary:**

This paper presents a method for automatically annotating grammatical error correction (GEC) system outputs with error types. The core idea is to leverage linguistically-enhanced edit alignment followed by a rule-based classification scheme, designed to be dataset-agnostic. The authors demonstrate the tool's utility by re-analyzing the CoNLL-2014 shared task data, offering a more granular evaluation of system performance than previously possible.

**Main Contributions:**

1. **Automated Error Type Annotation:** The most significant contribution is a practical method for automatically assigning error types to edits made by GEC systems. This fills a gap in the GEC evaluation landscape, which traditionally lacked fine-grained error analysis of system outputs.
2. **Dataset-Agnostic Rule-Based Classifier:** The rule-based approach to error type classification is a key feature. It avoids the need for labeled training data, which is often specific to particular datasets and annotation schemes, making it broadly applicable.
3. **Re-evaluation of CoNLL-2014:** The application of the tool to the CoNLL-2014 data provides a valuable case study and highlights the different strengths of various systems in correcting specific error types.

**Strengths:**

1. **Addresses a critical need:** The paper tackles a genuinely important problem in GEC – the lack of detailed error analysis.  Without knowing *what* errors a system corrects well (or poorly), it's difficult to make informed improvements.  The tool directly addresses this.
2. **Strong technical approach:** The combination of linguistically-informed edit alignment (building on Felice et al., 2016) and a rule-based classifier appears well-motivated and effective. The dataset-agnostic nature of the classifier is particularly appealing given the variability in annotation schemes.
3. **Rigorous evaluation and insightful analysis:** The authors go beyond simply presenting the tool and apply it to a substantial dataset (CoNLL-2014). The analysis of team performance based on error types is revealing and offers practical suggestions for future research (e.g., combining the strengths of different approaches). The comparison to M2 scorer and discussion of its limitations is excellent.
4. **Tool Release:** The authors commit to releasing the tool, which is crucial for the broader GEC community to benefit from their work.  This significantly increases the impact of the research.
5. **Good human evaluation:** The small-scale human evaluation, while not extensive, provides convincing evidence of the classifier's accuracy and reliability, particularly given the inherent noise in automatic edit extraction.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Scope of Human Evaluation:** The human evaluation is relatively small (200 edits) and focuses solely on the *appropriateness* of error categories. It doesn't assess the accuracy of the edit *boundaries* identified by the alignment algorithm, which is a critical component of the system. Boundary errors could significantly impact the overall evaluation.
2. **Rule-Based System Complexity:** While the dataset-agnostic approach is a strength, the rule-based system is inherently complex. Maintaining and extending this system as new error types are identified could become challenging.  The authors acknowledge this, but it remains a potential limitation.
3. **Dependence on POS Tagger:**  The performance of the system is heavily reliant on the accuracy of the POS tagger. The authors acknowledge this, and the evaluation indicates that tagging errors are a frequent source of misclassification. Investigating methods to make the system more robust to tagging errors would be beneficial.
4. **Lack of Comparison to Existing Error Type Prediction Methods:**  While the authors mention existing work (Swanson & Yamangil, Xue & Hwa), there isn't a direct comparison of their rule-based system against these approaches, particularly in terms of accuracy and efficiency.




**Questions to Authors:**

1.  What is the computational cost of the annotation process (time to annotate a sentence, etc.)?  Is it scalable to very large datasets?
2.  The paper highlights the importance of detecting multi-token errors.  Have you considered any specific techniques to improve the performance of edit alignment on these types of errors?
3.  Could you provide more details about the specific types of tagging errors that most frequently lead to misclassifications? And what strategies, if any, are you considering to mitigate these errors?
4.  What is the error distribution over the different error types for human annotations? Does the distribution align with the automatic annotations?



**Additional Comments:**

The paper is well-written and clearly presented. Table 9 is helpful in understanding the complete set of error codes. The choice of CoNLL-2014 as a testbed is sensible.



**Overall Assessment:**

I recommend **acceptance** with minor revisions. This paper presents a valuable contribution to the field of GEC. The proposed tool addresses a significant need, the approach is technically sound, and the evaluation is thorough and insightful. Addressing the weaknesses identified above, particularly expanding the human evaluation to include boundary accuracy and providing a more direct comparison to existing methods, would further strengthen the paper. The release of the tool is a major benefit to the community.
