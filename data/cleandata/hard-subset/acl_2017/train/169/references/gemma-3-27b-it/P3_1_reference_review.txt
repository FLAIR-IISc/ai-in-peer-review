## Review for ACL: Automatic Annotation and Evaluation of Error Types for Grammatical Error Correction

**Summary:**

This paper addresses the problem of evaluating Grammatical Error Correction (GEC) systems beyond overall scores, by proposing a method for automatically annotating system outputs with error types. The core idea is to leverage linguistically-enhanced alignment to extract edits and then apply a rule-based classification scheme based on readily available automatic linguistic features (POS tags, lemmas, etc.). The authors demonstrate their approach on the CoNLL-2014 shared task data, comparing their auto-annotations to original (human) annotations and conducting a detailed error type analysis of the participating systems. They release the tool developed as part of this work.

**Main Contributions (as I see them):**

1. **A Practical and Reproducible Automatic Error Type Annotation Pipeline:** The paper provides a readily usable tool and detailed methodology for automatically annotating GEC outputs with error types. This is valuable for the community as it overcomes a significant bottleneck in detailed GEC system evaluation. The reliance on automatic features makes it easily adaptable to new datasets.
2. **Detailed Analysis of CoNLL-2014 Systems:** The application of the tool to the CoNLL-2014 data provides a rich and insightful error type analysis of the participating systems, revealing strengths and weaknesses of different approaches that were previously obscured by overall scores.
3. **Demonstration of the Viability of Rule-Based Error Typing:** The work successfully demonstrates that a rule-based approach can achieve surprisingly high agreement with human annotators, challenging the dominance of machine learning-based approaches in this area, especially when dealing with potentially noisy or differently annotated datasets.

**Strengths:**

1. **Addresses a Significant Problem:**  The lack of detailed error type analysis in GEC evaluation has been a longstanding issue. This paper directly tackles this limitation, offering a practical solution.
2. **Strong Methodology:** The combination of linguistically-informed alignment with a carefully designed rule-based classification framework is well-justified. The authors clearly articulate the rationale behind their design choices, particularly the focus on dataset independence.
3. **Thorough Evaluation:** The evaluation is comprehensive.  The authors not only assess the accuracy of the error type classification (through human evaluation), but also demonstrate the utility of the tool by applying it to a large, established dataset (CoNLL-2014) and performing a detailed system analysis.  The comparison to the M2 scorer and investigation of edit size are particularly insightful.
4. **Reproducibility & Tool Release:**  The release of the tool is a major strength, fostering reproducibility and facilitating further research in the field.
5. **Clear Writing and Organization:** The paper is well-written and logically structured, making it easy to follow the methodology and understand the results.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Scope of Human Evaluation:** The human evaluation, while positive, is relatively small (200 edits, 5 raters). A larger-scale evaluation, perhaps with inter-annotator agreement measures beyond Kappa, would further bolster confidence in the accuracy of the rule-based system. The reported Kappa of 0.724 is good, but context is missing – is this good *for this task*?
2. **Error Scheme Granularity:** While the flexibility of the error scheme is a strength, the authors acknowledge a trade-off between informativeness and practicality. The provided example of ‘could → should’ being classified as a tense error, rather than a modal error, illustrates the limitations. More discussion on the criteria used to resolve such ambiguities and the potential impact on the analysis would be helpful.  It's not clear how sensitive the results are to the specific choices made in defining the error categories.
3. **Missing Error Analysis of the Automatic Annotator:**  The paper focuses on evaluating the *system outputs* using the tool.  It would be valuable to include an analysis of the errors made *by the annotation tool itself* - what types of errors does it misclassify, and why?  Understanding the limitations of the annotation process is crucial for interpreting the results of the system analysis.  While the authors mention POS tagging errors as a source of misclassification, a more detailed analysis would be useful.
4. **Lack of Comparison to Existing Automatic Typers:** The authors frame their work as a solution to the limitations of ML-based approaches (training data dependency). However, a brief comparison to the performance of those approaches *on the same dataset* would provide a more concrete benchmark and demonstrate the benefits of the proposed rule-based method.



**Questions to Authors:**

1.  Could you elaborate on the specific criteria used to define the granularity of the error categories?  Specifically, how did you decide when to create a new category versus lumping errors together?
2.  What types of errors does the automatic annotation tool most frequently misclassify? Could you provide a breakdown of these errors by type?
3.  Have you experimented with different automatic POS taggers or stemming algorithms?  If so, did these changes significantly impact the performance of the rule-based classifier?
4.  Would you consider releasing the code for the detailed system analysis (e.g., the scripts used to generate Tables 5 & 6), to allow others to easily reproduce and extend your findings?
5.  The paper mentions that the M2 scorer tends to overestimate performance.  Can you provide a more detailed explanation of *why* this occurs, and how it might affect the interpretation of the results presented in Table 4?




**Additional Comments:**

*   The reference to "Damerau-Levenshtein" is a bit abrupt. A sentence explaining why this is relevant would be helpful for readers less familiar with edit distance algorithms.
*   Table 2 is a bit difficult to read due to its formatting.  Consider using a more visually clear table structure.
*   Consider adding a section on limitations and future work, explicitly addressing the weaknesses outlined above.



**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper that makes a valuable contribution to the field of GEC evaluation. The proposed method is practical, well-justified, and thoroughly evaluated. While there are some minor weaknesses, the strengths of the work significantly outweigh them. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions addressing the questions and comments above.
