## Review of "Automatic Annotation and Evaluation of Error Types for Grammatical Error Correction"

**Summary:**

This paper addresses the problem of evaluating Grammatical Error Correction (GEC) systems beyond overall scores by proposing a method for automatically annotating system outputs with error types. The approach combines a linguistically-enhanced alignment algorithm with a rule-based error typing framework.  The authors demonstrate the utility of their tool by re-evaluating the CoNLL-2014 shared task systems, providing a more granular analysis of performance than previously possible.

**Main Contributions (as I see them):**

1. **A practical tool for automatic error type annotation:** The developed tool fills a significant gap in the GEC evaluation landscape. It allows for more detailed analysis of system performance, moving beyond a single F0.5 score.  The release of the tool is a valuable contribution to the community.
2. **A novel rule-based error typing framework:**  The framework, based on automatically obtained linguistic features (POS tags, lemmas), offers a dataset-independent alternative to machine learning approaches for error typing.  This is a strength, as it avoids the need for labeled training data for each new dataset.
3. **Detailed Re-analysis of CoNLL-2014:**  The application of the tool to the CoNLL-2014 data provides a concrete demonstration of its utility and offers new insights into the strengths and weaknesses of the participating systems.


**Strengths:**

1. **Addresses a critical need in GEC evaluation:** The lack of detailed error type analysis has long been a limitation in the field. This work directly addresses this limitation, providing a mechanism to understand *how* systems are failing, not just *that* they are failing.
2. **Strong technical approach:** The combination of a robust alignment algorithm (Felice et al., 2016) and a carefully designed rule-based system seems well-reasoned. The dataset-independent nature of the error typing is a key advantage.
3. **Thorough evaluation:** The authors conduct a comprehensive evaluation, including a manual validation of the error typing accuracy, a re-evaluation of the CoNLL-2014 systems, and a detailed breakdown of performance across different error types and edit operations. The bootstrap significance testing is a good practice.
4. **Clear and well-written:** The paper is generally well-written and easy to follow. The explanation of the error typing framework and the rationale behind the design choices are clear.
5. **Practical impact:** The tool’s release will immediately benefit researchers in the field, enabling more insightful and actionable evaluations of GEC systems.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited scope of manual evaluation:**  While the manual evaluation demonstrates reasonable accuracy ( >95% "Good" or "Acceptable"), a sample size of 200 edits is relatively small. The evaluation relies heavily on the quality of the POS tagger, and errors in tagging directly impact the accuracy of the error typing.  A more detailed error analysis of the misclassifications identified by the raters would be helpful.
2. **Reliance on external tools:** The entire pipeline relies on accurate POS tagging, lemmatization, stemming, and dependency parsing.  Errors in these tools will propagate through the system, impacting the quality of the annotation. The paper acknowledges this, but doesn't fully explore the sensitivity of the system to different tool configurations.
3. **Comparison to existing methods:**  The authors state that comparing to previous classifier-based approaches is difficult due to different error schemes. However, a more direct comparison, perhaps on a shared subset of errors, would strengthen the paper. Quantifying the improvement (or lack thereof) in error type classification accuracy would be valuable.
4. **Potential for overly granular error categories:**  The 25 error types, with prefixes for operation type (M, U, R), could lead to a very fragmented view of performance.  It is not always clear if this level of granularity is truly meaningful or simply adds complexity.




**Questions to Authors:**

1.  How sensitive is the overall system performance to the accuracy of the underlying NLP tools (POS tagger, lemmatizer, etc.)?  Have you explored different tool configurations?
2.  Could you provide more details on the errors identified during the manual evaluation? What types of errors were most frequently misclassified, and why?  Was it typically due to POS tagging errors or limitations in the rule-based framework?
3.  While acknowledging the challenges in comparing to prior work due to different error schemes, could you provide any quantitative comparison, even on a limited subset of error types, to demonstrate the performance of your rule-based approach relative to state-of-the-art classifiers?
4.  Do you have plans to extend the tool to support other languages or GEC datasets?  Are there any anticipated challenges in adapting the rule-based framework to different linguistic structures?




**Additional Comments:**

*   Table 9 (Complete list of valid error code combinations) is helpful for understanding the granularity of the error typing.
*   The discussion of the M2 scorer's bias is insightful.



**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper that addresses an important challenge in the GEC field.  The proposed tool and framework are well-designed, and the evaluation is thorough. While the manual evaluation could be more comprehensive, and a more direct comparison to existing methods would be beneficial, the paper makes a significant contribution to the field.  I recommend **acceptance**.
