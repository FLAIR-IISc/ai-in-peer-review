This paper tackles the important problem of evaluating Grammatical Error Correction (GEC) systems beyond overall scores, by proposing a method for automatic annotation of error types. The core contribution is a rule-based system for classifying edits, designed to be dataset-agnostic and transparent. A secondary contribution is the application of this tool to re-evaluate the CoNLL-2014 shared task results, providing a more granular analysis. Finally, releasing the tool itself is a valuable contribution to the community.

**Strengths:**

1. **Novelty of Approach:** The explicit focus on *automatic* error type annotation is a genuinely novel and valuable direction. While error type analysis is crucial for GEC improvement, it's historically been hampered by the cost and inconsistency of manual annotation. This work offers a promising path towards automated, scalable, and reproducible evaluation. The rule-based approach, intentionally eschewing data-hungry machine learning for dataset independence, is a bold and refreshing choice.
2. **Practicality & Transparency:** The rule-based system’s reliance on readily available automatic linguistic features (POS tags, lemmas) is a significant strength. It avoids the need for large, annotated corpora, making it readily applicable to new datasets. The deterministic nature of the rules enhances transparency; one can clearly understand *why* an edit was classified as a particular error type.
3. **Re-evaluation of CoNLL-2014:** Applying the tool to the CoNLL-2014 data is a smart move. It demonstrates the practicality of the approach and provides potentially insightful findings regarding the strengths and weaknesses of different GEC systems, specifically highlighting that different systems excel at different error types. The observation about MT being strong at multi-token edits is noteworthy.
4. **Good Evaluation (Despite Limitations):** The manual evaluation with GEC researchers, while small-scale, provides reasonable evidence of the classifier’s accuracy. The fact that raters found >95% of classifications "Good" or "Acceptable" is encouraging, given the inherent noise from automatic edit extraction. 

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Scope of Evaluation:** The manual evaluation is small (200 edits) and relies on subjective ratings ("Good," "Acceptable," "Bad"). While the kappa score is decent, a more rigorous quantitative evaluation against a gold standard (even if limited) would be beneficial. The reliance on raters to evaluate edits *after* automatic boundary detection is a potential source of bias; the quality of the edit extraction directly impacts assessment.
2. **Simplistic Error Scheme:** While the 25 error types are a good starting point, the scheme feels somewhat coarse-grained. The discussion in Section 3.2 acknowledges this, but a more nuanced categorization might capture subtle error patterns more effectively.  The discussion around modal vs. tense errors feels unresolved.
3. **Lack of Comparison to Existing Methods:** The paper mentions existing methods for error type classification (Swanson & Yamangil, Xue & Hwa), but offers limited direct comparison. While the authors emphasize the dataset-agnostic nature of their approach, a brief comparison of performance on a common dataset would strengthen the argument. Just stating that previous classifier-based methods are "biased" isn't a sufficient justification.
4. **Dependence on External Tools:** The system’s performance is reliant on the accuracy of spaCy, Hunspell, and the Lancaster stemmer. Errors in these tools directly propagate to the error classification. The paper briefly acknowledges this, but doesn't fully address the potential impact.

**Questions to Authors:**

1.  Could you provide examples of edits where your system consistently misclassifies the error type, and discuss potential improvements to the rule-based system to address these cases?
2.  You mention the potential for a more nuanced error categorization. What specific refinements to the error scheme are you considering, and what would be the trade-offs (complexity vs. expressiveness)?
3.  Have you explored any methods for automatically evaluating the performance of your error typing system beyond the manual evaluation presented in the paper? For example, could you use a small, manually annotated dataset as a "held-out" test set?
4. How robust is the system to differences in POS tagger performance across languages? Would the system require significant adaptation for non-English GEC?



**Additional Comments:**

*   Table 9 (complete list of error codes) is helpful for understanding the granularity of the system.
*   The discussion of the M2 scorer in Section 4.1 is insightful.
*   The paper is generally well-written and clearly explains the methodology.



Overall, despite some weaknesses, I believe this paper presents a significant contribution to the field of GEC. The proposed approach is novel, practical, and transparent, and the re-evaluation of the CoNLL-2014 data provides valuable insights. The potential impact of this work is considerable, and I recommend **acceptance**, acknowledging that further refinement and evaluation would be beneficial. I am willing to overlook the limited evaluation and the somewhat coarse-grained error scheme given the novelty and promise of the approach.
