This paper addresses a critical gap in Grammatical Error Correction (GEC) evaluation: the lack of detailed error type annotation for system outputs. The authors propose a method to automatically annotate corrections with error types, enabling more granular performance analysis than previously possible. The core contribution is a rule-based error typing framework leveraging automatically obtained linguistic features (POS tags, lemmas, etc.), making it dataset-agnostic and transparent. A secondary contribution is the application of this framework to re-evaluate the CoNLL-2014 shared task systems, providing a novel perspective on their performance. Finally, the authors generously offer to release their tool, which is valuable to the GEC community.

**Strengths:**

1. **Novelty and Impact:** The primary strength of this work is its novelty. While GEC has advanced, detailed error-specific evaluation has remained surprisingly elusive. This paper directly tackles this issue, offering a practical solution.  The potential impact on GEC research is significant; researchers can now gain deeper insights into system weaknesses and strengths, facilitating targeted improvements.
2. **Dataset Agnosticism:** The rule-based approach is a key advantage.  Machine learning-based error typing is often brittle and requires retraining for each dataset. The authors' method, relying on automatically obtainable features, avoids this limitation and promises greater portability. This is a conceptually strong design choice.
3. **Transparency & Determinism:** The rule-based nature also affords transparency. Unlike black-box machine learning models, the authors' system provides clear explanations for its classifications, aiding in debugging and understanding. The deterministic nature ensures consistency.
4. **CoNLL-2014 Re-evaluation:** Applying the tool to the CoNLL-2014 data provides a compelling demonstration of its utility and generates genuinely new findings. The insights gleaned from this re-evaluation, such as the struggles with unnecessary token errors across many systems, are valuable.
5. **Tool Release:** The commitment to releasing the tool is commendable and will undoubtedly benefit the GEC community.  Accessibility is crucial for adoption and further research.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Validation Beyond Expert Rating:** While the expert rating provides some validation, it’s relatively small-scale (200 edits) and lacks a direct comparison against a gold standard.  The authors acknowledge this but the lack of a more comprehensive evaluation leaves some uncertainty about the framework’s overall accuracy. A comparison with human annotations on a larger corpus, even if costly, would substantially strengthen the work.
2. **Error Scheme Complexity and Potential Overlap:** The 25 error types, with prefixes indicating operation type, are potentially complex. While the flexibility is desirable, it also introduces the possibility of subjective interpretation and ambiguity.  The authors acknowledge this difficulty, and the discussion on modal vs. tense errors is insightful, but a more rigorous analysis of inter-annotator agreement *within* the framework, rather than just “good/acceptable/bad”, would be helpful.
3. **Reliance on POS Tagger Accuracy:** The performance is highly dependent on the accuracy of the POS tagger. The authors rightly point this out, but the impact is potentially substantial.  Errors in POS tagging directly translate into incorrect error type assignments. Investigating the sensitivity of the system to POS tagging errors (e.g., through controlled experiments with noisy POS tags) would be beneficial.
4. **Simplistic Multi-Token Error Analysis:** The discussion on multi-token errors feels somewhat underdeveloped.  The statement that “only three teams achieved scores greater than 10 F0.5” is striking but lacks sufficient context or analysis. Is this inherently difficult for all GEC systems, or is it specific to the CoNLL-2014 data?

**Questions to Authors:**

1.  You mention potential ambiguity in the error scheme (e.g. modal vs. tense). Could you elaborate on other areas where the error type categorization might be subjective, and how the rules are designed to resolve such ambiguities?
2.  Could you provide more detail on the error analysis for multi-token edits? Were there specific types of multi-token edits that systems consistently struggled with?
3.  What steps could be taken to mitigate the impact of POS tagging errors on the accuracy of the framework? Have you considered using a more robust (but potentially slower) POS tagger?
4.  Regarding the comparison with the M2 scorer, you state that it "overestimates performance". Could you quantify this overestimation on the CoNLL-2014 data, and provide some specific examples where the two scores diverge significantly?



**Additional Comments:**

* The table formatting is a little awkward and difficult to parse. Consider simplifying the table structure or using a different visualization.
* The discussion of bootstrap significance testing is concise but sufficient.
* The reference list is well-formatted and complete.



Overall, this is a strong and impactful paper. The proposed method addresses a significant limitation in GEC evaluation and offers a promising path forward. Despite some minor weaknesses, the novelty and potential benefit to the community outweigh the concerns. I recommend **acceptance**.
