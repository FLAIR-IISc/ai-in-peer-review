This paper addresses the important problem of evaluating Grammatical Error Correction (GEC) systems beyond overall scores, by proposing a method for automatically annotating system outputs with error types. The core idea – leveraging linguistically-enhanced alignment followed by rule-based error typing – is sound and tackles a significant gap in the field, particularly stemming from the CoNLL-2014 shared task. The authors clearly articulate the motivation, and the paper is well-written and easy to follow.

**Main Contributions:**

1. **Automated Error Type Annotation Pipeline:** The most significant contribution is the development of a complete pipeline for automatically annotating GEC system outputs with error types. This enables more granular evaluation than previously possible, moving beyond simple F0.5 scores.
2. **Rule-Based Error Typing Framework:** The authors propose a novel, dataset-agnostic rule-based framework for error typing. This is a substantial improvement over previous machine learning approaches which suffer from domain dependence and lack transparency. The framework’s design, leveraging automatic POS tagging and linguistic features, is practical and effective.
3. **Detailed CoNLL-2014 Analysis:** Applying the developed tool to the CoNLL-2014 dataset provides valuable insights into the strengths and weaknesses of different GEC systems, showcasing the benefits of fine-grained error type analysis and providing a rich empirical evaluation.

**Strengths:**

1. **Addresses a Critical Gap:** The paper directly addresses a long-standing issue in GEC evaluation – the lack of detailed error type analysis. The ability to automatically annotate system outputs unlocks more informative and actionable evaluation metrics.
2. **Strong Technical Approach:** The combination of linguistically-enhanced alignment (building on existing work by Felice et al.) and a well-designed rule-based classification system is a compelling technical solution. The choice to avoid machine learning for error typing is justified and leads to a more robust and transparent system.
3. **Thorough Evaluation:** The extensive analysis of the CoNLL-2014 shared task results is a major strength. The authors go beyond simply presenting scores, delving into team-specific performance on different error types and exploring the relationship between detection and correction accuracy.
4. **Practical Tool Release:** The commitment to releasing the developed tool is a significant benefit to the research community. This will facilitate further research in GEC evaluation and system development.
5. **Clear and Concise Writing:** The paper is well-structured and clearly written, making it easy to understand the proposed method and its evaluation.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Novelty in Alignment:** While the overall pipeline is novel, the edit extraction stage relies heavily on existing work (Felice et al., 2016). The paper does not present any substantial improvements to the alignment algorithm itself.  Acknowledging this more explicitly would be beneficial.
2. **Evaluation Methodology Caveats:** While the decision to re-annotate the reference data using the automatic pipeline is justified, the paper could more thoroughly discuss the potential impact of this decision on the results. The fact that the auto-annotations aren’t *gold* standard could affect the conclusions drawn.
3. **Manual Evaluation Scale:** The manual evaluation of the rule-based classifier, while valuable, is based on a relatively small sample size (200 edits). A larger-scale evaluation, potentially with more raters, would increase confidence in the reported results.  The kappa score is good, but a larger dataset would be more convincing.
4. **Incomplete Error Type Coverage:** While the 25 error types are a good starting point, the authors acknowledge that the framework could be extended to include more detailed distinctions. Exploring the trade-offs between granularity and practicality further would be useful. The table in the appendix is difficult to read and could be improved.
5. **Limited Discussion of Error Propagation:** Errors in POS tagging or stemming can clearly impact the accuracy of the rule-based classifier. The paper mentions this briefly, but a more detailed analysis of error propagation and its potential effects on the results would be valuable.

**Questions to Authors:**

1.  The paper states the rule-based classifier is "entirely dataset independent." While this is a significant advantage, are there any specific linguistic phenomena or error types where the performance of the classifier is demonstrably weaker, perhaps due to limitations in the underlying NLP tools (POS tagger, lemmatizer)?
2.  Could you elaborate on the design choices made when defining the 25 error types? What criteria were used to determine the level of granularity, and how did you balance informativeness with practicality?
3.  You mention the possibility of merging edits in the alignment stage. How does this merging process potentially affect the accuracy of the error type assignment, particularly for complex errors involving multiple edits?
4.  The comparison between M2 scores and your approach suggests M2 overestimates performance. Could you provide more specific examples of scenarios where this occurs, and discuss the implications for interpreting GEC evaluation results?



**Additional Comments:**

*   Table 2 could benefit from a brief description of the motivation behind each error category.
*   Consider adding a discussion of potential applications for the developed tool beyond GEC evaluation, such as error analysis in learner corpora or automated feedback generation.
*   The reference list is well-formatted, but could be checked for completeness and consistency.



Overall, this is a strong paper that makes a valuable contribution to the field of Grammatical Error Correction. While there are some minor weaknesses, the strengths of the work – particularly the development of a practical and transparent error type annotation pipeline and the thorough analysis of the CoNLL-2014 dataset – outweigh these concerns. I recommend acceptance.
