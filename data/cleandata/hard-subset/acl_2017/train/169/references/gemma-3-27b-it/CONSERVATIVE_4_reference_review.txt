This paper proposes a method for automatically annotating grammatical error correction (GEC) system outputs with error types, aiming to address the lack of detailed error analysis in previous evaluations like CoNLL-2014. The core approach involves linguistically-enhanced edit extraction followed by rule-based error type classification. The authors release a tool implementing this method.

The primary contribution is a practical, automated system for error type annotation of GEC outputs, facilitating more granular evaluation. A secondary contribution is the proposed rule-based error typing framework, which prioritizes dataset independence. Finally, the re-analysis of the CoNLL-2014 shared task data using this new annotation scheme offers some interesting, though not groundbreaking, observations about system performance.

**Strengths:**

1. **Practicality and Accessibility:** The availability of a tool for automatic error annotation is valuable to the GEC community. Manually annotating error types is expensive and time-consuming; an automated solution, even if imperfect, lowers the barrier to detailed analysis.
2. **Dataset Independence:** The rule-based approach, relying on automatic linguistic features (POS tags, lemmas, etc.), is a reasonable design choice, avoiding the need for large, labeled datasets specific to each evaluation setting. This is particularly attractive given the variability in annotation schemes.
3. **Detailed CoNLL-2014 Analysis:**  The application to the CoNLL-2014 data provides a tangible demonstration of the tool’s utility and reveals some specific strengths and weaknesses of different systems, offering insights for future research. The comparison of M2 scoring with their approach, and the justification for using auto-annotated references, are thoughtful considerations.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Lack of Rigorous Evaluation of the Annotation Scheme Itself:** The primary weakness is the limited evaluation of the rule-based error typing. A manual evaluation with only 200 edits and 5 raters is insufficient to establish confidence in the accuracy and consistency of the annotation scheme. While the reported agreement is encouraging, a more comprehensive evaluation, potentially including comparison with human-annotated gold standards on a larger dataset (even if different from CoNLL-2014), is crucial. Claiming >95% "Good" or "Acceptable" ratings is insufficient; a breakdown of disagreement and specific error cases is needed.
2. **Limited Novelty:** The individual components – edit extraction using linguistic alignment and rule-based classification – are not novel. The authors build on established methods (Felice et al., 2016; Swanson and Yamangil, 2012) rather than presenting a truly innovative approach. The primary advance is the *combination* of these techniques into a complete system specifically designed for error type annotation, but the degree of novelty here is incremental.
3. **Over-Reliance on Existing Tools and Potential Error Propagation:** The system’s accuracy is heavily dependent on the performance of the underlying NLP tools (spaCy, Hunspell, Lancaster Stemmer). Errors in POS tagging, lemmatization, or stemming will inevitably propagate to the error type classification, potentially leading to inaccurate annotations.  The authors acknowledge this in the evaluation but do not adequately quantify its impact.
4. **Limited Scope of Error Types:** While the 25 error types are a reasonable starting point, the framework may be too simplistic to capture the full complexity of real-world grammatical errors. The authors acknowledge this in Section 3.2 but do not discuss potential extensions or limitations.

**Questions to Authors:**

1.  Could you provide a more detailed error analysis of the disagreements observed during the manual evaluation? Specifically, what types of errors were most frequently misclassified, and what were the common sources of disagreement among the raters?
2.  What steps have you taken to mitigate the impact of errors in the underlying NLP tools (spaCy, Hunspell, etc.) on the accuracy of the error type classification?
3.  How does the performance of your tool compare to the performance of human annotators on the same data, in terms of agreement (e.g., Cohen’s Kappa)? While obtaining fully annotated gold standards is challenging, even a smaller-scale comparison would be valuable.
4.  Could you provide examples of error cases where your rule-based approach is demonstrably superior to a machine-learning-based approach, considering the potential biases of ML classifiers towards specific training corpora?



**Additional Comments:**

The table listing error types (Table 2) is helpful. The discussion of the M2 scorer and its tendency to inflate scores is also valuable. Consider expanding the discussion of the limitations of the framework in the conclusion, and highlighting future directions for improvement (e.g., incorporating more sophisticated NLP techniques, expanding the error type taxonomy).
