This paper presents a method for automatically annotating grammatical error correction (GEC) system outputs with error types, aiming to address a limitation of the CoNLL-2014 shared task where system-level error type performance was unassessable. The core contributions are a linguistically-enhanced edit extraction method (leveraging prior work) and a novel, rule-based error type classification framework designed for dataset independence. A secondary contribution is the application of this framework to re-analyze the CoNLL-2014 data and provide more granular performance insights.

**Strengths:**

1. **Addresses a Valid Problem:** The authors correctly identify a weakness in prior GEC evaluation – the lack of detailed error type analysis for system outputs. Providing a tool for this purpose is a practical and potentially valuable contribution.
2. **Clear and Methodologically Sound Approach:** The method itself is well-defined. Building on established edit extraction techniques (Felice et al., 2016) and employing a rule-based, rather than machine learning-based, approach to error typing is a sensible choice, aiming for portability and interpretability.
3. **Rigorous Evaluation (within constraints):** The authors acknowledge the difficulty of evaluating a rule-based classifier without gold standard labels and employ a reasonable workaround – manual evaluation by GEC experts to assess the appropriateness of the assigned error types. The reported agreement is promising.
4. **Detailed Re-analysis of CoNLL-2014:** The application of their tool to the CoNLL-2014 dataset provides concrete examples of its utility and reveals interesting, potentially actionable, insights into the strengths and weaknesses of different systems. The comparison between M2 scoring and their approach is valuable.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Novelty:** While the *combination* of existing edit extraction with a new rule-based classifier is reasonable, the individual components lack substantial novelty. The reliance on established methods diminishes the overall impact of the work. The rule-based classifier, while practical, feels somewhat ad-hoc and lacks theoretical grounding.
2. **Insufficient Comparison to Existing Error Typing Methods:** The paper mentions existing machine learning-based error typing methods (Swanson & Yamangil, 2012) but dismisses them quickly, primarily due to dataset bias. A more thorough comparison, even acknowledging the bias issue, is necessary. Demonstrating a clear advantage over these methods – perhaps through cross-dataset performance or robustness to domain shifts – would significantly strengthen the paper. The claim of dataset independence needs stronger justification beyond simply stating it.
3. **Overstated Claims of Accuracy:** While the expert evaluation shows good agreement, it’s a small-scale evaluation (200 edits) and susceptible to bias. The statement that raters considered >95% of error types as “Good” or “Acceptable” seems overly optimistic given the inherent ambiguity in error classification. More quantitative details of the evaluation (e.g., kappa statistics per error type) would be helpful.
4. **Lack of Error Analysis of Classifier Failures:** The paper briefly mentions that errors in POS tagging lead to incorrect error type assignments. A more in-depth analysis of the types of errors the classifier *consistently* gets wrong, and the reasons behind those errors, would provide valuable insights and directions for future improvement.

**Questions to Authors:**

1. Could you provide a more detailed comparison with existing machine learning-based error typing methods, even if it involves acknowledging the challenges of cross-dataset evaluation? Specifically, what performance differences would you expect if you were to train and test a machine learning classifier on the CoNLL-2014 data?
2. Can you provide a breakdown of the expert evaluation results by error type? Which error types were most consistently rated as “Good” versus “Bad”?
3. What specific steps could be taken to improve the robustness of the rule-based classifier to errors in POS tagging and other automatic markup components?
4. The paper highlights that multi-token edits are difficult to correct. Is the error extraction process itself reliably identifying multi-token edits, or is this a source of error that impacts the evaluation?

**Additional Comments:**

*   Table 9 (complete list of error codes) is helpful and well-organized.
*   The discussion of the M2 scorer’s bias is valuable and demonstrates a nuanced understanding of GEC evaluation.
*   The motivation for choosing a rule-based approach is clear and defensible, but the authors should acknowledge the limitations of this approach in terms of adaptability and expressiveness.
