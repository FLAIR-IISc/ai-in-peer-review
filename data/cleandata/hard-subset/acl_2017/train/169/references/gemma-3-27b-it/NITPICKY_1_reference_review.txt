This paper proposes a method for automatically annotating grammatical error correction (GEC) system outputs with error types, addressing a gap identified in the CoNLL-2014 shared task where system-level error type performance was difficult to assess. The core idea involves linguistically-enhanced edit extraction followed by rule-based error type classification, aiming for dataset independence. The authors release a tool implementing this approach.

The primary contribution is a rule-based framework for error type annotation that avoids reliance on labelled training data, a potential advantage over machine learning approaches susceptible to domain bias. A secondary contribution is the application of this framework to re-evaluate the CoNLL-2014 shared task systems, offering a more granular analysis of their performance. A tertiary contribution is the release of a potentially useful tool for GEC evaluation.

**Strengths:**

1. **Addresses a Practical Problem:** The paper directly tackles a real issue in GEC evaluation – the lack of readily available, detailed error type annotations for system outputs. This is valuable for researchers wanting to understand *where* their systems succeed and fail.
2. **Dataset Independence is Appealing:** The rule-based approach, aiming for dataset independence, is a strength. The authors convincingly argue that training data biases can be problematic, particularly when evaluating across different corpora.
3. **Comprehensive Error Type Scheme:** The 25-category error type scheme, and its flexibility for varying granularity (M:, R:, U: prefixes), appears well-considered and potentially useful for nuanced analysis. The tabular presentation of the scheme is clear.
4. **Good Rater Agreement:** The manual evaluation demonstrating >95% “Good” or “Acceptable” ratings from GEC researchers is a positive signal, despite the small scale. The acknowledgement of noise from automatic edit extraction is also commendable.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Overstated Novelty:** While avoiding labelled data is a valuable design choice, the core techniques – linguistically-enhanced alignment (Felice et al., 2016) and rule-based classification – are not fundamentally novel. The innovation lies in the *combination* and application to error type annotation, but the paper sometimes implies greater originality.
2. **Insufficient Detail on Rule Creation:** The paper states that rules were written based on POS tags, but provides very little detail on *how* these rules were formulated, especially for more complex error types. This makes reproducibility and further refinement difficult. A more extensive appendix detailing the rules is crucial.
3. **Reliance on Automatic Tools & Error Propagation:** The entire pipeline relies heavily on the accuracy of automatic POS tagging, lemmatization, stemming, and dependency parsing. The paper acknowledges POS tagger errors as a source of misclassification in the manual evaluation (Table 3), but doesn’t sufficiently address the potential for systematic errors to propagate throughout the annotation process and impact the CoNLL-2014 re-evaluation. What is the error rate of spaCy on the CoNLL-2014 data, and how might this skew the results?
4. **M2 Scorer Caveats Minimised:** While the authors correctly identify the M2 scorer’s tendency to inflate scores, they downplay this issue somewhat. The comparison in Table 4 feels less convincing given the known limitations of M2. A more cautious interpretation of the results is needed.
5. **Inconsistent Presentation & Minor Errors:** Numerous small inconsistencies detract from the paper’s polish. For example, the “Damerau-Levenshtein” footnote appears mid-sentence. The references section is missing some punctuation and appears to be an incomplete listing. Several minor typos exist throughout (e.g. “To ke” in Table 9). These suggest a lack of meticulous attention to detail.

**Questions to Authors:**

1. Can you provide a more detailed description of the rules used for error type classification, ideally in an appendix? Include examples of rules for less straightforward error types like “Word Order” or “Other”.
2. What is the POS tagging accuracy of spaCy v1.6 on the CoNLL-2014 dataset? How might POS tagging errors systematically bias the error type annotation?
3. How sensitive is the overall performance of your system to different POS taggers or lemmatizers? Did you experiment with alternative tools?
4. Why was Hunspell GB-large chosen specifically? Are there reasons to believe this dictionary is better suited for GEC error detection than other options?
5. Can you clarify the rationale behind the decision to discard gold reference information in favour of automatically annotated references? What steps were taken to validate the accuracy of the automated reference annotations independently of the manual evaluation?



**Additional Comments:**

* Table 7 is referenced in the text but does not appear in the submission. This is a critical omission.
* The formatting of the tables could be improved for readability.
* The paper relies heavily on citations to Felice et al. (2016). While this work is clearly relevant, a more balanced presentation of related work might be beneficial.
* The phrasing in several sections is verbose and could be more concise.

Overall, this paper presents a potentially useful contribution to GEC evaluation. However, the lack of detail in certain areas, the reliance on error-prone automatic tools, the overstated claims of novelty, and the numerous minor flaws prevent me from recommending acceptance at this time. A major revision addressing the weaknesses outlined above is necessary.
