This paper addresses the important problem of automatically annotating grammatical error correction (GEC) data with error types, a capability lacking in previous evaluations like CoNLL-2014. The authors propose a pipeline consisting of linguistically-enhanced edit extraction followed by rule-based error typing. The primary contribution is a dataset-agnostic, rule-based error typing framework, which is a significant step towards standardized and detailed GEC evaluation. A secondary contribution is the application of this framework to re-analyze the CoNLL-2014 shared task data, providing novel insights into the strengths and weaknesses of different systems. Finally, the authors provide a publicly released tool implementing their approach.

**Strengths:**

1. **Addressing a Critical Gap:** The paper directly tackles a long-standing issue in GEC – the lack of detailed error type analysis. The CoNLL-2014 task highlighted this limitation, and this work provides a valuable solution. The ability to automatically annotate corrections is essential for more nuanced evaluation and system development.
2. **Novel Rule-Based Framework:** The proposed rule-based error typing approach is a strong point.  Its dataset independence is a key advantage over machine learning approaches, which suffer from domain dependence. The transparency and determinism of the rules are also desirable features, allowing for easier understanding and debugging. The 25 error type granularity (and combinability) feels well-considered.
3. **CoNLL-2014 Re-analysis:** Applying the framework to the CoNLL-2014 data yields interesting observations. The detailed breakdown of performance by error type, and operation type (missing, unnecessary, replacement) provides actionable insights for GEC researchers. The comparison of different systems' strengths and weaknesses is a valuable contribution.
4. **Tool Release:** Releasing the annotation tool is a positive step toward reproducibility and wider adoption of the proposed methodology. This encourages further research and builds upon the work presented.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Evaluation of the Typing Framework:** While the manual evaluation with GEC researchers is a good start, it feels somewhat limited in scope (200 edits, 5 raters).  The “Good”, “Acceptable”, “Bad” scale is somewhat coarse-grained, and a more detailed assessment of the framework’s accuracy against human annotations (even on a small dataset) would be beneficial. The Kappa score of 0.724, while good, doesn't offer complete confidence.
2. **Dependence on POS Tagging Accuracy:**  The framework’s reliance on POS tags is a potential bottleneck. The authors acknowledge this in the evaluation, noting that errors in POS tagging can lead to misclassification. A more thorough error analysis specifically focused on the impact of POS tagging errors on the overall accuracy of the framework would strengthen the paper. How robust is the system to variations in POS tagger performance?
3. **Oversimplification of Error Categories:** The paper acknowledges the challenge of defining error categories. The discussion of the trade-off between informativeness and practicality is good, but some error types (e.g., "Other") feel too broad. A more detailed justification for the chosen categorization scheme, and a discussion of its limitations, would be helpful.
4. **M2 Scorer Comparison Concerns:** The justification for reprocessing the reference data to align with the automatic annotations is reasonable, but the discussion regarding the M2 scorer’s potential for inflated scores feels a little dismissive. While the authors argue it overestimates performance, the M2 scorer *is* the standard metric, and a more nuanced discussion of its strengths and weaknesses is warranted.

**Questions to Authors:**

1.  Could you provide a more detailed error analysis focusing on the impact of POS tagging errors on the error type classification accuracy? What percentage of misclassifications can be directly attributed to incorrect POS tags?
2.  Regarding the “Other” error category, what types of errors are typically classified as such, and what are the reasons for not creating more specific categories for them?
3.  You mention that the framework is "analogous to automating the annotation guidelines." Could you elaborate on the similarities and differences between your rules and traditional human annotation guidelines? What principles informed the design of your rules in relation to these guidelines?
4.  What are the computational resources required to run the annotation tool on a large dataset (e.g., CoNLL-2014)?



**Additional Comments:**

*   Table 9 listing the error code combinations is excellent.
*   The paper is well-written and clearly organized.
*   The reference list is comprehensive.
*   It would be useful to briefly mention the limitations of the rule-based approach; are there error types it struggles with consistently?



Overall, this is a solid and valuable contribution to the field of GEC. The proposed framework and its application to the CoNLL-2014 data provide important insights and a useful tool for future research. While the evaluation could be strengthened with a more detailed analysis, the work is well-motivated, well-executed, and addresses a significant need in the community. I recommend acceptance, with minor revisions addressing the weaknesses outlined above.
