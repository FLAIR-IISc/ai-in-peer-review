This paper proposes a method for automatically annotating grammatical error correction (GEC) system outputs with error types. The approach involves linguistically-enhanced edit extraction followed by rule-based error type classification. The authors claim to address a gap in existing GEC evaluation methodologies by enabling detailed error type analysis. The primary contribution, as I see it, is a *system* for automatically annotating GEC outputs, rather than a fundamentally novel algorithmic advancement. A secondary contribution is the rule-based error typing scheme itself, designed for dataset-agnostic evaluation. A tertiary contribution is the re-analysis of the CoNLL-2014 shared task data using their system, providing some initial observations about system performance at a granular level.

**Strengths:**

1. **Practicality and Reproducibility:** The stated goal of enabling detailed error type analysis for GEC systems is valuable. The authors explicitly state they will release the tool, which fosters reproducibility and allows the community to build upon their work.
2. **Dataset-Agnostic Approach:**  The rule-based nature of the error typing is a clear strength.  Avoiding reliance on labeled training data mitigates the portability issues associated with machine learning approaches trained on specific corpora with varying annotation guidelines. This is a well-articulated and desirable property.
3. **Detailed CoNLL-2014 Re-analysis:** The re-evaluation of the CoNLL-2014 systems, even with the caveats discussed in the paper (using auto-annotated references), provides potentially useful insights into system strengths and weaknesses across different error types. The observation regarding the underperformance on unnecessary token errors is intriguing.
4. **Clear and Well-Organized Presentation:** The paper is generally well-written and structured. The methodology is explained clearly, and the experimental setup is straightforward to understand.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Lack of Novelty in Core Methodology:** The individual components – linguistically-enhanced edit extraction (Felice et al., 2016) and rule-based classification – are not novel contributions *in this paper*. The primary novelty lies in the *combination* and application to this specific task, which feels incremental. The paper relies heavily on citing prior work for the crucial algorithmic pieces.
2. **Overstated Classifier Performance:** The manual evaluation of the rule-based classifier, while positive, feels somewhat weak given the small sample size (200 edits) and the potential for confirmation bias. The statement that “all 5 raters individually considered at least 95% of our rule-based error types to be either ‘Good’ or ‘Acceptable’” is a high bar that warrants more scrutiny. The provided table shows significant variation among raters. The fact that errors are frequently traced back to POS tagging errors *highlights a fragility* in the entire pipeline, rather than bolstering confidence in the classifier.
3. **Justification for Auto vs. Gold Reference:** The decision to reprocess the reference data using the automatic annotation pipeline is presented as necessary due to inconsistencies, but the justification is not entirely convincing.  While acknowledged, downplaying the use of gold standard annotations in evaluation seems questionable. The lack of a comparison *with* gold standard evaluation, even as a supplementary result, is a significant oversight.
4. **Insufficient Error Analysis:**  While the paper presents results at a granular level (Table 6), the analysis is largely descriptive. There's a lack of in-depth investigation into *why* certain systems struggle with particular error types. The conclusions drawn are somewhat superficial. For example, stating that MT approaches are effective at correcting noun errors without further analysis is not particularly insightful.
5. **Formatting and Presentation Issues:** Table 2 (Error Categories) is difficult to read due to its length and formatting. The complete list of 25 error types is visually overwhelming. Table 9 in the appendix seems redundant given Table 2.

**Questions to Authors:**

1.  Given the reliance on POS tagging, what specific strategies were employed to mitigate the impact of POS tagging errors on the overall accuracy of the error typing?  Can you provide examples where incorrect POS tags led to misclassification, and how the system handles such cases?
2.  You argue that a dataset-agnostic approach is desirable. However, the rules were developed and evaluated on specific datasets (FCE-test and CoNLL-2014).  How confident are you that the rules will generalize effectively to entirely different domains and writing styles?  Have you conducted any preliminary experiments on unseen data?
3.  The paper mentions a trade-off between informativeness and practicality in the error scheme design.  Can you elaborate on specific design choices where you prioritized practicality over potentially more nuanced error categorization?
4.   Regarding the evaluation, what is the inter-annotator agreement for the *manual evaluation* process described in Section 3.3, excluding the raters’ evaluation of the system output? This would give a baseline to understand the inherent difficulty of assessing the appropriateness of error categories.



**Additional Comments:**

* The citation style is inconsistent. Some references have full publication details, while others do not.
* The reference to "Human evaluation of gram, Edward Gillian, Gillian.?" is incomplete and appears erroneous.
* A more rigorous ablation study, evaluating the impact of individual components (e.g., the linguistic features in the edit extraction) would strengthen the paper.




Overall, this paper presents a useful tool and a reasonable, though not groundbreaking, approach to GEC evaluation. However, the lack of significant novelty, the somewhat overstated claims regarding classifier performance, and the insufficient error analysis lead me to recommend **rejection**.  While the work has potential, it doesn’t meet the bar for acceptance at a highly selective conference like ACL. The paper could benefit from a more critical self-assessment of its contributions and a more thorough investigation of the limitations of its approach.
