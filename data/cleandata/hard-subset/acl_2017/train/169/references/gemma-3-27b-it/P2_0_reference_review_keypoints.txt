- Proposes an automatic error-type annotation method for GEC by combining a linguistically-enhanced alignment algorithm with a rule-based error typing framework and applies it to CoNLL-2014.
- Presents a practical, released tool that enables more detailed GEC evaluation beyond a single F0.5 score.
- Introduces a dataset-independent, rule-based error-typing framework using POS tags and lemmas, avoiding labeled training data.
- Re-analyzes CoNLL-2014 systems to demonstrate the tool’s utility and provide granular insights into system strengths and weaknesses.
- Addresses a critical need in GEC evaluation by enabling analysis of how systems fail, not just overall scores.
- Uses a robust technical approach combining prior alignment methods and carefully designed rules, emphasizing dataset independence.
- Conducts a thorough evaluation including manual validation, system re-evaluation, per-error-type breakdowns, and bootstrap significance testing.
- Paper is clear and well-written, with rationale and framework explanations easy to follow.
- Tool release has immediate practical impact for researchers seeking actionable GEC evaluations.
- Manual evaluation sample is small (200 edits) and depends heavily on POS tagging quality, limiting validation strength.
- Pipeline relies on external NLP tools (POS tagger, lemmatizer, parser), with no sensitivity analysis of different tool configurations.
- Lacks a direct quantitative comparison to classifier-based error-typing methods, making relative performance unclear.
- The 25 error types (with operation prefixes) risk producing overly granular, fragmented analyses that may not be meaningful.
- Asks about sensitivity to underlying NLP tool accuracy and whether different tool configurations were tested.
- Requests details on frequent misclassification types and whether errors stem from POS tagging or rule limitations.
- Suggests providing any quantitative comparison to prior classifier approaches, even on a shared subset of error types.
- Inquires about plans and challenges for adapting the tool and rules to other languages or GEC datasets.
- Notes Table 9 is helpful for understanding error-code granularity and highlights an insightful discussion of M2 scorer bias.
- Overall assessment: strong, well-designed contribution with practical impact; recommend acceptance despite the need for broader manual validation and clearer comparisons to prior methods.
