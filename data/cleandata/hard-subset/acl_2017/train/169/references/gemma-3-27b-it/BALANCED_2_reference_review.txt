## Review of "Automatic Annotation and Evaluation of Error Types for Grammatical Error Correction"

This paper addresses the important problem of automatically annotating grammatical errors in text, a crucial step for detailed evaluation of Grammatical Error Correction (GEC) systems. The authors propose a two-stage pipeline: linguistically-enhanced edit extraction followed by rule-based error typing. The work is motivated by the limitations of previous evaluations, particularly the CoNLL-2014 shared task, which lacked detailed error type analysis. The authors also promise to release the tool they developed with the paper.

**Main Contributions:**

1. **A robust and practical pipeline for automatic error type annotation:** This is the core contribution. The authors successfully combine existing linguistically-informed edit extraction with a novel rule-based error typing approach, achieving high agreement with human annotators ( >95% rated as “Good” or “Acceptable”). The dataset-agnostic nature of the rule-based system is a particularly strong point.
2. **Detailed re-evaluation of the CoNLL-2014 shared task:** The paper presents a comprehensive error type analysis of the CoNLL-2014 systems, revealing interesting insights into their strengths and weaknesses. This analysis itself is a valuable contribution, providing a more nuanced understanding of GEC system performance than previously possible.
3. **Demonstration of the utility of automatic annotation for fine-grained evaluation:** The paper showcases how the automatic annotation facilitates a more detailed evaluation, moving beyond overall scores to assess performance on specific error types and edit sizes. This helps identify areas where individual systems excel or struggle, paving the way for targeted improvements.

**Strengths:**

1. **Strong Motivation and Clear Problem Definition:** The paper clearly articulates the need for detailed error type analysis in GEC and positions the work as a direct response to the limitations of previous evaluations.
2. **Sound Methodology:** The combination of linguistically-enhanced edit extraction and rule-based error typing is well-justified. The choice of utilizing existing, well-performing edit extraction methods (Felice et al., 2016) is sensible. The rule-based approach to error typing, while seemingly simple, proves surprisingly effective and avoids the limitations of training data dependence.
3. **Rigorous Evaluation:** The authors employ a rigorous evaluation strategy, including manual evaluation of the error type classifier and a thorough re-evaluation of the CoNLL-2014 shared task results. The bootstrap significance testing to validate the use of auto-references is commendable.
4. **Actionable Insights:** The detailed analysis of the CoNLL-2014 systems reveals valuable insights into the strengths and weaknesses of different approaches, such as the effectiveness of MT for multi-token errors and the struggles with certain error types like contractions. These insights can guide future research.
5. **Tool Release:** The commitment to release the developed tool is a significant positive aspect, promoting reproducibility and facilitating further research in the field.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Comparison with Existing Error Typing Methods:** While the paper acknowledges existing work on error typing (Swanson & Yamangil, 2012; Xue & Hwa, 2014), it doesn’t provide a direct comparison of the proposed rule-based system with these methods. A more thorough comparison, even if limited by differences in datasets, would strengthen the argument for the novelty and effectiveness of the proposed approach.
2. **Dependence on POS Tagging Accuracy:** The rule-based error typing heavily relies on the accuracy of POS tagging. The paper acknowledges this, but a more detailed discussion of the potential impact of POS tagging errors on the overall performance would be beneficial. Reporting POS tagging accuracy on the datasets used would also be valuable.
3. **Simplistic Evaluation Metric:** The use of precision, recall, and F0.5, while standard, might not fully capture the nuances of GEC evaluation. Exploring other metrics that better reflect the complexity of error correction, such as GLEU or ChrF, could provide a more comprehensive assessment.
4. **Lack of Error Analysis of Classifier Mistakes:** While the paper reports overall accuracy of the error type classifier, a deeper dive into the types of errors it makes would be helpful. Identifying common misclassifications could reveal areas for improvement in the rule-based system.
5. **Limited Discussion of Generalizability:** While the authors state the approach is dataset-agnostic, a discussion of its performance on datasets significantly different from CoNLL-2014 (e.g., learner corpora from different L1 backgrounds) would be beneficial to assess its true generalizability.

**Questions to Authors:**

1. Could you provide a more direct comparison of your rule-based error typing approach with existing machine learning-based methods (e.g., Swanson & Yamangil, 2012; Xue & Hwa, 2014), even if limited by dataset differences?
2. What is the POS tagging accuracy of the spaCy tagger on the datasets used in your evaluation?
3. Have you considered exploring other evaluation metrics beyond precision, recall, and F0.5, such as GLEU or ChrF, to provide a more comprehensive assessment of performance?
4. Can you provide examples of common misclassifications made by your rule-based error type classifier, and what factors contribute to these errors?
5. Have you tested the performance of your system on GEC datasets significantly different from CoNLL-2014, and what were the results?



**Additional Comments:**

* Table 2 could benefit from a brief explanation of how the different prefixes (M:, R:, U:) impact the evaluation granularity.
* The discussion of the M2 scorer’s tendency to overestimate performance is insightful.
* The reference to the HOO shared tasks (Dale & Kilgarriff, 2011) is a useful connection.



**Overall:**

This is a strong paper that makes a significant contribution to the field of GEC. The proposed approach for automatic error type annotation is robust, practical, and provides valuable insights into the performance of existing GEC systems. While there are a few minor weaknesses, they do not detract significantly from the overall quality of the work. I recommend **acceptance** with minor revisions addressing the points raised in the "Weaknesses" section and responding to the questions posed.
