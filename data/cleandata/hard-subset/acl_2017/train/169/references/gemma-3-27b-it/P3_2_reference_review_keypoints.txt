- Proposes a pipeline for automatic, error-type level annotation of GEC outputs using enhanced edit extraction and rule-based classification, applied to CoNLL-2014.
- Contribution: Releases a practical, reproducible tool for automatic GEC error-type annotation.
- Contribution: Introduces a dataset-independent, rule-based error typing framework to avoid domain/data dependence.
- Contribution: Provides a detailed re-evaluation of CoNLL-2014 revealing system-level error-type patterns.
- Strength: Addresses the gap in fine-grained GEC evaluation by enabling automatic error-type analysis.
- Strength: Builds on established edit-extraction methods (Felice et al., 2016) with a well-justified rule-based design.
- Strength: Offers thorough analysis and insightful findings (e.g., difficulty with unnecessary token errors).
- Strength: Clear, well-written presentation with a comprehensive error-type list.
- Strength: Commits to releasing the tool, supporting reproducibility.
- Weakness: Annotation accuracy evaluation is limited (only 200 edits, vague quality categories); larger-scale and agreement-based evaluation needed.
- Weakness: Rule-based system may reflect author biases in categorization despite rater agreement.
- Weakness: Heavy reliance on POS tagging introduces vulnerabilities from tagging errors.
- Weakness: Limited analysis of edit boundary misalignments between automatic and gold annotations.
- Weakness: Defensive justification for using auto-references over gold references; should better acknowledge both approaches' limitations.
- Question: What specific error patterns caused edits to be judged “Bad” beyond POS tagging mistakes?
- Question: Have alternative POS taggers or ensemble/tagger-consensus approaches been tested to improve robustness?
- Question: What new, more granular error categories might be added and what trade-offs would they introduce?
- Question: How robust is the method on noisier or more ungrammatical input; how does performance degrade?
- Question: Why do systems struggle with unnecessary token errors—detection, correction, or both?
- Comment: Table formatting is dense and could be improved for readability.
- Comment: Prefer “domain-independent” over repeated use of “dataset-agnostic” for clarity.
- Comment: Would be informative to compare tool performance across other GEC datasets (e.g., FCE, BEA).
- Overall: Strong, well-motivated paper that fills an important gap in GEC evaluation; recommend acceptance with minor revisions.
