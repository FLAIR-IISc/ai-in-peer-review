### **Summary and Contributions**

This paper presents a method and a tool for the automatic annotation of grammatical error corrections with specific error types. The primary motivation is to enable a more fine-grained evaluation of Grammatical Error Correction (GEC) systems beyond a single aggregate score, which was a limitation of past shared tasks like CoNLL-2014. The proposed method is a two-stage pipeline: first, it uses a pre-existing linguistically-enhanced alignment algorithm to extract edits between original and corrected sentences. Second, it classifies these edits using a novel, rule-based framework that relies on automatic linguistic markup (POS tags, lemmas, etc.), making it dataset-agnostic. The authors demonstrate the utility of their tool by performing the first detailed error-type analysis of all systems from the CoNLL-2014 shared task, revealing specific strengths and weaknesses of different GEC approaches.

From my perspective, the main contributions are:

1.  **A practical tool and methodology for detailed GEC evaluation:** The primary contribution is the development and release of a tool that fills a significant gap in GEC evaluation. By enabling the calculation of precision, recall, and F-score for specific error categories, it allows researchers to move beyond monolithic scores and gain deeper insights into system performance. The proposed workflow, including the crucial step of re-annotating the gold reference for a fair comparison, is a valuable contribution to the community.
2.  **A novel, comprehensive analysis of the CoNLL-2014 shared task systems:** The paper provides a valuable and insightful analysis that was previously impossible. The breakdown of system performance by error type (Tables 5, 6, 7), edit size (Table 8), and the distinction between detection and correction (Figure 1) offers new knowledge about the capabilities of various well-known GEC systems. This analysis serves as an excellent demonstration of the tool's power and is a significant empirical contribution in its own right.
3.  **A transparent and dataset-independent error classification framework:** The authors propose a new rule-based error typology and classifier. Its main advantage is that it does not require labeled training data and is completely deterministic and transparent, unlike machine learning-based approaches. This makes it highly reusable and adaptable across different datasets.

### **Strengths**

1.  **Addresses a well-defined and important problem:** The paper tackles a clear and recognized limitation in GEC evaluation. The inability to analyze *what kind* of errors a system is good or bad at correcting has hindered progress. This work provides a concrete and immediately useful solution to this problem, which will be of high value to anyone working on GEC.
2.  **Sound and well-motivated methodology:** The overall approach is logical and well-justified. The choice to build on an existing high-performing edit extractor (Felice et al., 2016) is sensible. The decision to use a rule-based classifier is a key strength, as it ensures transparency, reproducibility, and dataset independence, avoiding the common pitfalls of ML models which can be brittle across domains. The justification for creating an "auto reference" to ensure a fair comparison is clever, and the empirical evidence in Table 4 convincingly shows that this does not significantly alter system rankings.
3.  **Thorough and insightful experimental analysis:** The application of the tool to the CoNLL-2014 shared task data is extensive and provides a wealth of new information. The analysis goes beyond simple error types, investigating edit operations (Missing, Replacement, Unnecessary), multi-token edits, and the detection-vs-correction trade-off. The findings, such as the surprising inability of several systems to handle "Unnecessary" errors or the general weakness on multi-token edits, are genuinely interesting and actionable for future research.
4.  **High potential for community impact:** The authors' commitment to releasing the tool is a major strength. This will allow other researchers to easily adopt this more detailed evaluation paradigm for their own work, fostering better analysis and comparison of GEC systems. This moves the field towards a more nuanced understanding of GEC performance.

### **Weaknesses**

1.  **Over-reliance on the quality of upstream NLP tools:** The entire pipeline's accuracy is fundamentally capped by the performance of its components: the edit aligner, POS tagger, lemmatizer, and parser. The paper acknowledges that POS tagging errors can lead to misclassifications but doesn't fully discuss the implications of this dependency. Learner text is notoriously difficult for standard NLP tools, and systematic errors from these tools could lead to systematic biases in the error analysis. For instance, if a tagger consistently mislabels gerunds in learner English, the analysis of `VERB:FORM` errors could be skewed for all systems.
2.  **The evaluation of the classifier is somewhat indirect:** The authors conduct a small-scale manual evaluation where experts rate the "appropriateness" of the predicted labels. While the results are strong (>95% "Good" or "Acceptable"), this is an evaluation of plausibility rather than correctness against a gold standard. The authors argue that no gold standard exists for their scheme, which is true, but they could have strengthened their evaluation by mapping their categories to an existing, manually annotated corpus (like FCE) to show correlations and measure agreement on overlapping categories. The comparison to the 50-70 F1 of previous work is explicitly noted as "incomparable" and thus adds little weight to their claim.
3.  **Limited discussion on the extensibility of the error framework:** The authors correctly state that any error scheme is a compromise between informativeness and practicality. They give an example of `could -> should` being classified as `VERB:TENSE` rather than a more specific `MODAL` error. While this is a reasonable choice, the paper would be stronger if it briefly discussed how a researcher might extend the rule-based framework. How easy is it to add new rules or refine existing ones? A short discussion on the modularity and user-configurability of the classifier would enhance its value as a community tool.

### **Questions to Authors**

1.  Your classifier's performance is highly dependent on the accuracy of the spaCy POS tagger on learner text. Have you performed any analysis on how systematic tagging errors might influence the final error distribution? For example, if the tagger struggles with certain verb-noun ambiguities common in learner writing, could this disproportionately affect the scores for `VERB:SVA` vs. `NOUN:NUM` errors and potentially bias the interpretation of a system's strengths?
2.  The decision to re-process the reference annotations with your automatic aligner is a key part of your methodology for ensuring a fair comparison. However, different aligners can have different intrinsic biases (e.g., preferring one long edit over two short ones, or vice versa). Could you comment on whether you observed any such bias in the Felice et al. (2016) aligner and how that might shape the resulting analysis, particularly in the breakdown of multi-token vs. single-token edits?
