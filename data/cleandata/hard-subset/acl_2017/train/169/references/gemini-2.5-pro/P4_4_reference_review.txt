### Summary of the Paper
This paper introduces a novel, fully automatic two-step method for annotating grammatical error correction (GEC) data with explicit edit spans and error types. The primary goal is to enable a more fine-grained evaluation of GEC systems beyond a single aggregate score. The first step uses a linguistically-enhanced alignment algorithm to extract edits between original and corrected sentences. The second step classifies these edits using a new, rule-based framework that relies exclusively on automatic linguistic markup (e.g., POS tags, lemmas), making the process dataset-independent and transparent. The authors demonstrate the utility of their method by applying it to the system outputs from the CoNLL-2014 shared task, providing the first comprehensive, per-error-type analysis for all participating teams. The paper concludes that this detailed analysis reveals system-specific strengths and weaknesses that were previously obscured, and the authors intend to release their tool to the community.

### Contributions
1.  **A novel, fully automatic, and dataset-independent framework for GEC error type annotation.** The core contribution is the rule-based classifier that assigns error types based on automatic linguistic features. This approach contrasts with prior machine learning-based methods by being transparent, deterministic, and not requiring labeled training data, making it highly suitable for a standardized evaluation tool.
2.  **The first comprehensive, fine-grained performance analysis of all systems from the CoNLL-2014 shared task.** By applying their tool, the authors generate a detailed breakdown of each system's performance across 25 error categories. This analysis provides valuable and previously unavailable insights into the comparative strengths and weaknesses of different GEC architectures (e.g., MT-based, classifier-based) on specific grammatical phenomena.
3.  **A methodology for standardized GEC evaluation that decouples edit extraction from scoring.** By pre-annotating both system hypotheses and the reference with edits, evaluation simplifies to a direct comparison of annotations. The authors demonstrate that re-processing the gold reference with their automatic pipeline does not significantly alter system rankings, validating this as a viable method for creating a consistent evaluation setting.

### Strengths
1.  **High Practical Impact and Utility:** The work addresses a significant and long-standing limitation in GEC evaluation. A single F-score is insufficient for understanding system behavior. This tool enables researchers to diagnose model failures, identify complementary system strengths for ensemble methods, and track progress on specific error types. The detailed analysis of the CoNLL-2014 systems is a powerful demonstration of the tool's value, revealing, for example, that several systems completely failed to handle "Unnecessary" token errors, an insight impossible to gain from the original shared task results.
2.  **Sound and Well-Motivated Technical Approach:** The methodology is principled and well-justified. It builds upon a state-of-the-art method for edit extraction and introduces a rule-based classifier for error typing. The choice of a rule-based system over a machine learning one is a key strength for this application, as it ensures transparency, perfect consistency (determinism), and portability across different datasets without retraining. This makes the tool ideal for a standardized benchmark.
3.  **Thorough and Insightful Experimental Analysis:** The paper provides a compelling and multi-faceted demonstration of the proposed framework.
    *   The validation experiment in Section 4.1, which shows that using an automatically processed reference yields system rankings consistent with those from the gold reference, is crucial for establishing the credibility of the proposed evaluation pipeline.
    *   The results presented in Tables 5, 6, and 8 are novel and highly informative, offering detailed breakdowns by operation type, general error category, and multi-token edits. These analyses support the central claim that different GEC approaches have distinct and often complementary performance profiles.
    *   The distinction between error detection and correction performance (Figure 1) is another valuable analysis enabled by the tool, highlighting that a system's ability to identify an error does not always correlate with its ability to fix it.
4.  **Clarity of Presentation:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and easy to follow. The motivation is clearly articulated, the methodology is described logically, and the extensive results are presented in a structured and digestible manner. The tables are comprehensive and directly support the paper's conclusions.

### Weaknesses
1.  **Limited Evaluation of the Rule-Based Classifier:** The primary technical contribution is the rule-based classifier, but its evaluation is based on a small-scale manual study of "appropriateness" on 200 edits. While the agreement and ratings are high, this evaluation does not measure correctness against a gold standard for the proposed error schema. More importantly, the paper attributes most "Bad" ratings to upstream POS tagging errors without quantifying this claim or discussing the impact of potential flaws in the classification rules themselves. The robustness of the rules on more diverse datasets or more complex error types remains an open question.
2.  **Lack of Transparency in Classification Rules:** The rule-based nature of the classifier is presented as a key advantage for transparency, yet the paper does not provide any examples of the rules. The logic for distinguishing between closely related categories (e.g., `R:MORPH` vs. `R:ADJ:FORM` vs. general POS changes like `R:ADJ`) is not explained. Without insight into the rule logic, it is difficult for the reader to assess the linguistic soundness or potential limitations of the error typology and the classifier. A brief description of the logic for a few key or ambiguous cases would significantly strengthen the paper.
3.  **Unexplored Sensitivity to Upstream NLP Tools:** The entire annotation pipeline is dependent on the output of external tools (spaCy, NLTK). The performance of these tools (e.g., POS tagger, lemmatizer) directly impacts the final error classifications. The paper acknowledges this dependency when discussing sources of error but does not analyze its sensitivity. It is unclear how much the error distributions and resulting system rankings would change if a different tagger or a more recent version of spaCy were used. This dependency introduces a potential source of variability that could affect the goal of a perfectly standardized evaluation.

### Questions to Authors
1.  The manual evaluation in Section 3.3 is a key piece of evidence for your classifier's quality. Could you provide more detail on the 3.7% of edits rated as "Bad"? You suggest most are due to POS tagging errors, but could you confirm if any were attributed by raters to flawed or inappropriate classification rules?
2.  The rules for classification are central to your contribution. Could you provide 2-3 examples of the actual rules to illustrate how the classification logic works? For instance, what features and logic are used to distinguish a `R:MORPH` edit from a standard POS-change edit (e.g., `quick` -> `quickly`), or to classify an edit as `VERB:TENSE`?
3.  Your framework is designed to be a standard for evaluation, but it relies on a specific version of spaCy (v1.6) from 2017. How sensitive are your final annotations and the resulting CoNLL-2014 system analysis to the choice and version of the underlying NLP pipeline? For instance, would using a modern version of spaCy or a different toolkit significantly alter the distribution of error types or the relative performance of the systems?
