### Summary and Contributions

This paper presents a tool for the automatic annotation of grammatical error correction (GEC) data. The process involves two stages: first, extracting edits between original and corrected sentences using a linguistically-enhanced alignment algorithm from prior work, and second, classifying these edits into one of 25 error types using a novel, rule-based framework. The authors demonstrate the utility of their tool by performing a detailed re-evaluation of the systems from the CoNLL-2014 shared task, offering fine-grained insights into system performance across different error types, edit operations, and edit lengths.

As I see them, the main contributions are:
1.  **A practical tool and methodology for fine-grained GEC evaluation.** The most significant contribution is the tool itself (to be released), which allows researchers to move beyond a single overall score and analyze GEC system outputs in a detailed, standardized manner. The proposed methodology of re-annotating the gold reference with the same automatic pipeline to ensure a fair comparison is a key part of this contribution.
2.  **A novel, in-depth analysis of the CoNLL-2014 shared task systems.** The paper provides the first comprehensive breakdown of system performance by error type for this widely-cited benchmark. This analysis yields valuable new insights, such as identifying specific error types that are poorly handled by most systems (e.g., unnecessary token errors) and highlighting the complementary strengths of different GEC approaches.

### Strengths

1.  **Addresses a Clear Community Need:** The GEC field has long been hampered by evaluation metrics that condense system performance into a single number, obscuring important details. This work directly addresses this limitation by providing a framework for standardized, multi-faceted evaluation. Releasing the tool will be a valuable service to the community, facilitating more nuanced comparisons and reproducible research.
2.  **Valuable and Insightful Re-analysis:** The detailed breakdown of the CoNLL-2014 results in Section 4 is excellent. The analysis at the operation tier (Table 5), general error type tier (Table 6), and even for multi-token edits (Table 8) provides a wealth of information that was previously unavailable. For instance, the finding that five teams failed to correct any "Unnecessary" errors is a surprising and important observation. These results can directly inform future GEC research by highlighting specific areas for improvement.
3.  **Sound Evaluation Setup:** The decision to reprocess the gold reference data using the same automatic alignment and classification pipeline as the system hypotheses (Section 4.1) is a methodologically sound and crucial step. This ensures that the comparison is fair, as it evaluates systems against a reference that is subject to the same potential automatic annotation errors. This is a subtle but very important point that strengthens the validity of the subsequent analysis.

### Weaknesses

My main concerns with this paper relate to the evaluation of the proposed error classifier and several instances of overstatement and imprecision.

1.  **Insufficient and Subjective Evaluation of the Classifier:** The core technical contribution—the rule-based classifier—is not rigorously evaluated. The manual evaluation in Section 3.3 is anecdotal at best.
    *   The rating scale ("Good", "Acceptable", "Bad") is highly subjective and its definition is vague. The distinction between "Good" and "Acceptable" is particularly unclear.
    *   The evaluation conflates errors from the edit extraction stage with errors from the classification stage. Raters were asked to judge the category's appropriateness for an automatically determined span, which may itself be flawed. A proper evaluation would have assessed the classifier's performance on gold-standard edit spans first to isolate its effectiveness.
    *   The comparison to previous classifiers (50-70 F1) is dismissed as "incomparable," yet it is the only quantitative comparison offered. Without a direct comparison on a common task, the claim that the high agreement ratings "validate the efficacy of our rule-based approach" is not well-supported.
    *   The claim that "bad" ratings could "usually be traced back to a mistake made by the POS tagger" is an unsubstantiated anecdote. A quantitative error analysis of the "Bad" cases is needed to support this.

2.  **Overstated Claims and Unsubstantiated Justifications:** The paper makes several strong claims that are not fully supported.
    *   The assertion that the rule-based approach "bypasses bias effects altogether and should hence be more consistent" (Section 3) is a significant overstatement. The system is deterministic, but it is heavily biased by the authors' hand-crafted rules and the systematic errors of its dependencies (spaCy, NLTK). This is a different kind of bias, not an absence of it.
    *   In Section 4.1, the authors conclude from the lack of a statistically significant difference in scores that "our auto annotations are qualitatively as good as human annotations." This is a logical fallacy. Failure to reject the null hypothesis does not prove it. A more accurate interpretation is that the annotation noise affects all systems similarly, thus preserving their relative rankings, which is a much weaker claim than qualitative equivalence.

3.  **Lack of Professionalism in Presentation:** The paper suffers from numerous minor but distracting formatting and consistency issues that undermine its quality.
    *   **Bibliography:** The reference list is in a state of disarray. Several entries are malformed (e.g., the first reference to "Gillian", "Napoles et al\.,?"), contain extraneous characters, and are inconsistently formatted. This level of sloppiness is unacceptable for a conference submission.
    *   **Tables:** In Table 6, the highlighting of the best F-score is inconsistent. For `VERB:INFL`, three teams achieve a perfect score of 100.00, yet only RAC is highlighted. It is also unclear what a dash (-) signifies across the tables; it seems to mean TP+FP=0, but this should be explicitly and consistently defined in the captions.
    *   **Footnotes:** The URLs in footnotes 2, 3, and 5 are provided without access dates, and the URL in footnote 4 is broken across a line.

### Questions to Authors

1.  The evaluation of your core classifier in Section 3.3 is quite weak. Could you perform a more rigorous, quantitative evaluation? For example, by applying your classifier to the gold-standard edits from the CoNLL-2014 reference data (ignoring your automatic edit extraction) and reporting precision/recall/F1 against the original 28 CoNLL-2014 error types (mapping where possible)? This would provide a more direct and less subjective measure of your classifier's quality.
2.  Your claim that a rule-based system "bypasses bias effects altogether" seems overly strong. Could you please clarify this statement? How do you account for the inherent biases introduced by your choice of tools (e.g., spaCy's tagset and error patterns) and the specific rules you have designed?
3.  In Section 4.1, you argue that since system rankings do not change significantly between gold and auto references, the auto annotations are "qualitatively as good as human annotations." Do you not agree this is a logical overreach? Isn't it more likely that the noise from the automatic annotation process simply affects all systems in a similar way, thus failing to alter their relative performance?
