### Summary and Contributions

This paper introduces a methodology and a tool for the automatic annotation of grammatical edits with fine-grained error types. The process involves two steps: first, extracting edits between original and corrected sentences using a linguistically-enhanced alignment algorithm, and second, classifying these edits using a novel, rule-based framework. This framework is designed to be dataset-independent, relying on standard linguistic features (e.g., POS tags, lemmas) rather than corpus-specific training. The authors demonstrate the utility of their system by conducting the first-ever detailed, error-type-level analysis of all system submissions from the CoNLL-2014 GEC shared task. This analysis provides new insights into the relative strengths and weaknesses of different GEC architectures on specific error categories.

In my view, the main contributions of this work are:

1.  **A comprehensive, comparative error analysis of the CoNLL-2014 shared task systems.** This analysis is a significant contribution to the GEC community. By revealing which system architectures excel at correcting specific error types, the paper provides a valuable resource that can guide future research, particularly in the development of hybrid and ensemble systems.
2.  **The development and release of an open-source tool and a transparent, rule-based framework for GEC error analysis.** While the underlying concepts are not entirely new, their integration into a robust, dataset-agnostic tool that facilitates detailed evaluation is a solid engineering achievement that addresses a clear need in the field.

### Strengths

1.  **High Practical Utility and Impact:** The paper addresses a critical and long-standing gap in GEC evaluation. Standard metrics provide a single score, obscuring system performance on different linguistic phenomena. This work provides a tool that allows for automatic, fine-grained error analysis of any GEC system output, enabling researchers to perform more nuanced system comparisons and diagnostics. The detailed re-evaluation of the CoNLL-2014 task is a powerful demonstration of the tool's value and produces findings of immediate interest to the community.
2.  **Sound and Transparent Methodology:** The choice of a rule-based classifier that is independent of any specific annotated dataset is well-justified and a key strength. It ensures the tool's generalizability across different corpora and annotation schemes. Furthermore, the transparency of the rules is a significant advantage over machine learning-based classifiers; as the authors note, it is always possible to determine why an edit was assigned a particular category. This makes the system's outputs interpretable and trustworthy.
3.  **Thorough and Insightful Analysis:** The application of the tool to the CoNLL-2014 data is exemplary. The authors go beyond a simple proof-of-concept and deliver a substantial analysis that uncovers several important patterns. For example, the discovery that five teams' systems made no corrections for "Unnecessary" errors, the confirmation of complementary strengths between MT and classifier-based systems, and the quantification of poor performance on multi-token edits are all valuable, actionable insights for GEC practitioners. The additional analyses of detection vs. correction and multi-token edits further underscore the tool's versatility.
4.  **Appropriate Evaluation:** Lacking a gold standard for their new typology, the authors conduct a manual evaluation with domain experts. The finding that raters judged over 95% of the automatic classifications as "Good" or "Acceptable" provides strong evidence for the quality and reliability of the rule-based framework. The decision to re-process the gold reference data using the same automatic pipeline is also methodologically sound, ensuring that the evaluation is a fair comparison between identically-processed files.

### Weaknesses

1.  **Dependency on Upstream NLP Tools:** The framework's accuracy is fundamentally capped by the performance of the underlying NLP pipeline (POS tagger, parser, etc.). The paper acknowledges that classification errors can often be traced back to these tools, but the potential impact of this is not fully explored. Standard NLP tools are known to perform less reliably on learner text, which often contains ungrammatical and idiosyncratic structures. A more detailed discussion of how these upstream errors cascade and potentially skew the distribution of classified error types would strengthen the paper. For instance, an analysis of the tagger's accuracy on learner data and its correlation with "Bad" classifications would provide important context.
2.  **Limited Discussion of Ambiguity and Rule Precedence:** The paper states that its error typology is a "compromise between informativeness and practicality," which is a reasonable design choice. However, it does not detail how the rule-based system handles complex or ambiguous edits that could plausibly be classified in multiple ways (e.g., an edit involving both tense and subject-verb agreement). Providing more insight into the system's decision-making process, such as the precedence order of the rules or the mechanism for resolving conflicts, would improve the clarity and reproducibility of the method.
3.  **Justification of the Error Typology:** The paper presents its 25 error categories but provides little rationale for why this specific set was chosen, or how it improves upon or relates to previous error annotation schemes used in GEC and ESL research (e.g., the original CoNLL-2014 error types). Situating the proposed typology within the broader literature on error analysis would help readers understand its design principles and potential limitations.
4.  **Incremental Methodological Novelty:** The paper effectively combines and builds upon existing work—using an alignment method from Felice et al. (2016) and a POS-based classification idea inspired by Swanson and Yamangil (2012). While the synthesis, the creation of a comprehensive rule set, and the application are novel and valuable, the core technical components are not groundbreaking. The primary contribution is thus more in the application and analysis than in fundamental methodology.

### Questions to Authors

1.  Your framework's reliability hinges on the performance of tools like spaCy on learner English. Could you comment on the potential for systematic biases in your error analysis that might arise from the known difficulties these tools face with non-native text? For example, are certain error types more prone to misclassification due to common POS tagging mistakes on ungrammatical sentences?
2.  Could you elaborate on the internal logic of your classifier when an edit could match the rules for multiple error categories? Is there an explicit hierarchy or precedence order for the rules that resolves these ambiguities?
3.  The detailed re-analysis of the CoNLL-2014 task is very illuminating. Have you considered applying your tool to the outputs of more recent, state-of-the-art GEC systems (e.g., Transformer-based models)? Such an analysis would be highly valuable to see if the error patterns you identified in 2014-era systems still hold true today.
