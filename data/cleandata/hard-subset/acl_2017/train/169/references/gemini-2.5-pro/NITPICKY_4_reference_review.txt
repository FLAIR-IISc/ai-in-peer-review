### Summary and Contributions

This paper presents a tool and methodology for the automatic annotation and fine-grained evaluation of grammatical error correction (GEC) systems. The method first uses an existing alignment algorithm to extract edits between source and target sentences and then applies a novel, rule-based classifier to assign error types from a newly proposed 25-category scheme. The primary contribution of this work is an extensive and detailed re-analysis of the system outputs from the CoNLL-2014 shared task, offering new insights into the specific strengths and weaknesses of each participating system. A secondary contribution is the public release of the tool itself, which could facilitate more standardized and detailed GEC evaluation in future work.

### Strengths

1.  **Addresses a Well-Defined Need in GEC Evaluation:** The paper correctly identifies a significant limitation in standard GEC evaluation—that an overall F-score obscures system performance on different error types. The proposed methodology provides a concrete solution to this problem, enabling a much richer analysis than is typically performed.

2.  **Thorough and Insightful Empirical Analysis:** The core strength of the paper lies in its detailed re-evaluation of the CoNLL-2014 shared task (Section 4). The analysis is multifaceted, breaking down performance by operation type (Missing, Replacement, Unnecessary), general error category, multi-token edits, and the distinction between error detection and correction. The findings are valuable to the community; for instance, the observation that several systems completely fail to address unnecessary token errors or that MT-based systems are superior at multi-token edits provides actionable insights for future GEC research.

3.  **Practical Utility and Reproducibility:** The authors promise to release their tool, which represents a valuable practical contribution to the field. Furthermore, the rule-based approach, while simple, is transparent and deterministic. The use of standard, publicly available NLP tools (spaCy, NLTK) for preprocessing enhances the potential for reproducibility.

### Weaknesses

Despite the paper's laudable goal and useful analysis, it is marred by a significant number of methodological and presentational flaws that, in aggregate, undermine confidence in the results and conclusions.

1.  **Insufficient and Flawed Evaluation of the Error Classifier:** The validation of the core component of this work—the rule-based error classifier—is critically insufficient. The evaluation described in Section 3.3 has several issues:
    *   **Protocol:** Raters were instructed to "focus on the appropriateness of the error category regardless of whether they agreed with the boundary or not." This is a deeply flawed protocol. An error category is inextricably linked to its edit span. An incorrect span can make an error type seem appropriate when the underlying edit extraction is wrong, or vice versa. This instruction renders the evaluation of the classification step largely meaningless, as it is not evaluated on the actual task it performs (classifying automatically determined edits).
    *   **Scale and Subjectivity:** An evaluation on only 200 random edits with a subjective "Good/Acceptable/Bad" rating scale is too small and too vague to robustly validate a tool intended for large-scale, automatic evaluation. The distinction between "Good" and "Acceptable" ("appropriate, but probably not optimum") suggests the rule-based system often settles for suboptimal classifications.
    *   **Misleading Comparison:** The authors contrast their 95% "Good/Acceptable" score with a "50-70 F1" score from previous work, while immediately admitting the metrics are "incomparable". This comparison should be removed, as it serves only to create a false impression of superiority over a more rigorous evaluation metric.

2.  **Unjustified Conclusion about Auto-Reference Quality:** In Section 4.1, the authors reprocess the gold reference data with their automatic tool. Based on the observation that system rankings do not change significantly, they conclude that their "auto annotations are qualitatively as good as human annotations." This is a significant overstatement. The stability of system rankings under F0.5 does not prove the quality of the annotations. It may simply indicate that the annotation noise introduced by the tool affects all systems in a similar way or that the metric is insensitive to the introduced changes. To make such a strong claim, a direct, fine-grained comparison between the automatic reference edits and the original gold edits is necessary, which is not provided.

3.  **Numerous and Severe Presentational Flaws:** The paper exhibits a lack of meticulousness that is unbecoming of a scientific publication.
    *   **Missing Figures and Tables:** The text makes explicit reference to "Table 7" (Section 4.4) and "Figure 1" (Section 4.6), neither of which are included in the paper. This is a major oversight that makes it impossible to fully evaluate the claims made in those sections.
    *   **Incoherent Reference List:** The bibliography is riddled with formatting errors, extraneous characters (`?`, `\Q2015\E`, `\.,?`), and repeated author names. This suggests a failure to properly compile or check the final document and reflects a significant lack of care.
    *   **Lack of Clarity:** The explanation for classifying `[could → should]` as a VERB:TENSE error is unconvincing and seems arbitrary. Furthermore, the distinction between a dash (`-`) and `0.00` in the results tables is not explicitly defined, leaving the reader to guess its meaning. The density of Table 6 makes it very difficult to parse, and key trends highlighted in the text are not always easy to verify.

### Questions to Authors

1.  Could you please provide the missing Table 7 and Figure 1?
2.  Can you justify the evaluation protocol for your classifier where raters were asked to ignore the correctness of the edit boundaries? Given that the error type is dependent on the boundary, how can this evaluation be considered valid?
3.  Your claim that the "auto annotations are qualitatively as good as human annotations" is very strong. Beyond showing that system rankings are stable, what direct evidence can you provide to support this? For instance, what is the F1-score between your automatically generated reference annotations and the original human-created gold annotations?
