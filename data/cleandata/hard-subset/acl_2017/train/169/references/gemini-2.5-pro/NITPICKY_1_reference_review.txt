**Summary of the Contribution**

This paper presents a method and a tool for automatically annotating grammatical error corrections with fine-grained error types, enabling a more detailed analysis of GEC system performance than what is possible with overall metrics like M2. The authors propose a two-step process: first, extracting edits using an existing linguistically-enhanced alignment algorithm, and second, classifying these edits with a novel, dataset-independent, rule-based framework. The primary contribution is a comprehensive and insightful re-evaluation of the CoNLL-2014 shared task systems, providing the first public breakdown of system performance across 25 distinct error categories. A secondary contribution is the rule-based annotation framework itself and the accompanying tool, which promises to facilitate more nuanced and standardized GEC evaluation in future work.

**Strengths**

1.  **Addresses a significant need in GEC evaluation:** The paper correctly identifies a major limitation of current GEC evaluation practices. An overall F-score conceals system-specific strengths and weaknesses, hindering progress. By providing a method for detailed error-type analysis, this work offers the community a valuable diagnostic tool. The re-analysis of the CoNLL-2014 shared task is a powerful demonstration of the tool's utility, revealing, for example, that several systems completely failed to handle unnecessary token errors and that MT-based systems were superior at correcting complex multi-token edits.

2.  **Pragmatic and well-justified methodological choices:** The decision to use a rule-based system for error classification is well-motivated by the goals of dataset independence and transparency. Unlike ML-based classifiers which are tied to specific annotation schemes and training data, this approach provides a stable and universally applicable framework, analogous to automating a single set of annotation guidelines. Releasing the tool is a significant plus, maximizing the work's potential impact on future GEC research.

3.  **Extensive and valuable empirical analysis:** The paper provides a wealth of new information about the performance of various GEC architectures. The analyses at the operation level (Table 5), the general error type level (Table 6), and for specific phenomena like multi-token edits (Table 8) and error detection (Figure 1) are thorough and yield actionable insights. For example, the discovery that CUUI’s classifier excelled at replacement determiner errors despite CAMB’s MT system being better on determiners overall (Section 4.4) highlights the potential for creating powerful hybrid systems.

**Weaknesses**

1.  **The evaluation of the core contribution (the classifier) is insufficient and potentially misleading.** The classifier's quality is central to the validity of the entire analysis, yet its evaluation in Section 3.3 is unconvincing.
    *   The authors use a subjective manual rating of "Good/Acceptable/Bad" and explicitly state that a direct comparison to previous F1-scores is "incomparable", yet in the next clause they use the high ratings to "validate the efficacy of our rule-based approach" relative to those scores. This is a logically flawed argument.
    *   The choice to not evaluate against an existing gold-standard dataset is poorly justified. While annotation schemes may differ, it would be highly informative to measure how well the proposed ruleset reproduces the labels in, for example, the CoNLL-2014 gold reference for overlapping categories. This would provide a much more objective measure of quality than a small-scale, subjective human rating.
    *   The use of κ-free (Randolph, 2005) for inter-annotator agreement is non-standard. The authors should justify why this was chosen over more common metrics like Fleiss' Kappa and provide context for interpreting the 0.724 score.

2.  **The rule-based classification framework is entirely opaque.** The paper describes *what* the classifier does (assigns error types based on automatic markup) but provides absolutely no detail on *how* it does it. This is a critical omission for the core technical contribution of the paper. What are the actual rules? How does a rule differentiate `VERB:TENSE` from `VERB:FORM`? How are ambiguities and rule conflicts resolved? Without at least a few illustrative examples of the rules, the work is not fully reproducible from the text alone, and the claim of being "transparent" is unsubstantiated.

3.  **The paper makes an unsubstantiated leap in logic regarding annotation quality.** In Section 4.1, the authors find no statistically significant difference in system rankings when using their automatically-annotated reference versus the original gold reference. From this, they conclude that their "auto annotations are qualitatively as good as human annotations." This conclusion is not supported by the evidence. The finding only demonstrates that the errors introduced by the automatic annotation process are systematic and do not significantly alter the *relative* performance of the systems. It is entirely possible that the automatic process is consistently wrong (e.g., mislabeling an entire class of errors), which would preserve rankings but produce a flawed analysis. The claim of being "qualitatively as good" requires a direct comparison of annotation quality, not a secondary analysis of system rankings.

4.  **Numerous formatting and presentation errors undermine the paper's professionalism.** The submission is marred by a lack of attention to detail.
    *   The reference list is highly inconsistent and contains egregious errors (e.g., random question marks, backslashes, inconsistent author formatting, and a garbled entry for "Human evaluation of gram..."). This reflects a very rushed preparation process.
    *   The caption for Table 6 states that "The highest F-score for each type is highlighted," but no highlighting is present in the table, making it difficult for the reader to verify the claims made in Section 4.3.
    *   The use of dashes in Tables 5 and 6 for systems that made no corrections of a certain type is ambiguous. It would be clearer to report scores of 0 where applicable.
    *   Footnotes are used for basic definitions ("Damerau-Levenshtein...") and URLs, which deviates from standard ACL formatting practices.

**Questions to Authors**

1.  The core technical contribution, the rule-based classifier, is treated as a black box. Could you please provide a few concrete examples of the rules used (e.g., for `VERB:SVA`, `ORTH`, and `NOUN:NUM`) and explain your mechanism for resolving cases where an edit might match more than one rule?
2.  Your claim that the auto-annotations are "qualitatively as good as human annotations" is based on the stability of system rankings. Could you please address the alternative possibility that this stability is simply due to systematic errors in your annotation process, and comment on why a more direct comparison of annotation quality was not performed?
3.  Could you justify the use of the κ-free metric for inter-annotator agreement over more standard alternatives like Fleiss' Kappa?
