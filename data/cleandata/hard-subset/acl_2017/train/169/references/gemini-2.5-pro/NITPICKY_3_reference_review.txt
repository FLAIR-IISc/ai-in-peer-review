### Summary of the Paper
This paper introduces a methodology and an accompanying tool for the automatic annotation of grammatical error types in parallel text. The process involves two stages: first, extracting edits using the linguistically-enhanced alignment method from Felice et al. (2016), and second, classifying these edits using a newly proposed rule-based framework that relies on automatic linguistic markup (e.g., POS tags, lemmas). The authors use this tool to conduct a detailed re-evaluation of the system outputs from the CoNLL-2014 shared task, providing a fine-grained analysis of each team's performance across 25 error categories, as well as by operation type (Missing, Unnecessary, Replacement), edit size, and error detection vs. correction.

### Main Contributions
1.  **A Detailed Re-analysis of CoNLL-2014 Systems:** The most significant contribution is the extensive and insightful performance breakdown of the GEC systems from the CoNLL-2014 shared task. This analysis uncovers specific strengths and weaknesses of different GEC approaches that were previously obscured by a single aggregate score.
2.  **An Open-Source Tool for Fine-Grained GEC Evaluation:** The paper provides a practical contribution to the community by releasing a tool that automates the process of error-type annotation. This facilitates standardized, detailed, and reproducible evaluation for GEC research.

### Strengths
1.  **Valuable Community Resource and Analysis:** The in-depth analysis of the CoNLL-2014 systems is a genuine service to the GEC community. The findings—such as the widespread failure to correct unnecessary token errors, the specific proficiencies of different systems (e.g., CAMB on missing tokens, CUUI on replacements), and the universal difficulty with multi-token edits—provide concrete, actionable insights for future GEC research.
2.  **Focus on Reproducibility:** The authors' commitment to releasing their tool is highly commendable. An open-source, standardized tool for error analysis has the potential to significantly benefit the field by allowing researchers to consistently diagnose their own systems' performance.
3.  **Multi-faceted Evaluation:** The paper presents a laudably comprehensive evaluation, looking beyond simple error categories to analyze performance on different edit operations, multi-token phenomena, and the important distinction between error detection and correction. This multi-faceted approach encourages a more nuanced understanding of system performance.

### Weaknesses
1.  **Insufficient Evaluation and Overclaiming:** The evaluation of the core technical contribution—the rule-based classifier—is superficial and leads to a significant overclaim.
    *   The authors state that their results are "incomparable with previous approaches" but still juxtapose their >95% human rating against a "50-70 F1" score for ML classifiers, which is misleading. A rigorous comparison would require training an ML classifier on their new error scheme.
    *   The human evaluation itself is small (200 edits) and relies on a subjective 3-point scale ("Good", "Acceptable", "Bad"). The distinction between "Good" and "Acceptable" is not clearly defined.
    *   The central conclusion in Section 4.1 that "our auto annotations are qualitatively as good as human annotations" is a major logical flaw. The evidence provided is that final system rankings do not change significantly when using the auto-annotated reference. This does not mean the annotations themselves are qualitatively equivalent; it only means the errors introduced by the automatic process did not systematically alter the final F-scores on this specific dataset. A direct qualitative comparison of the annotations is required to support such a strong claim.

2.  **Lack of Methodological Detail:** The paper provides very little information about the "new rule-based framework" itself. The description is high-level, stating that rules are based on POS tags and other features, but no actual rules or logic are presented. For example, how does the system differentiate between `VERB:TENSE`, `VERB:FORM`, and `VERB:SVA`? How are rule application conflicts resolved? Without these details, the core method remains a black box, hindering understanding and reproducibility. The justification for certain classification decisions (e.g., `[could -> should]` as `VERB:TENSE`) as a "compromise between informativeness and practicality" is vague and unconvincing.

3.  **Poor Technical and Formatting Polish:** The paper suffers from a notable number of formatting and presentation errors that suggest a lack of careful proofreading.
    *   **The reference section is completely broken.** Many entries are incomplete, contain BibTeX parsing artifacts (e.g., `et al\.,?`, `\Q2015\E`), and are inconsistently formatted. This is a critical flaw for a scientific publication.
    *   **A referenced table is missing.** Section 4.4 discusses the contents of "Table 7" in detail, but this table does not appear in the paper, making the entire section impossible to verify.
    *   The paper states that the highest F-score in each category in Table 6 is highlighted, but no highlighting is present.
    *   The system dependencies are outdated (spaCy v1.6). While perhaps a minor point, the performance of all upstream NLP components has improved substantially in newer versions, which could impact the validity and reproducibility of the results.

### Questions to Authors
1.  Could you please provide more concrete details about the rule-based classifier? For example, can you share the decision logic or a few key rules for disambiguating complex cases, particularly among the different `VERB:` subtypes?
2.  The claim that your auto-annotations are "qualitatively as good as human annotations" is based on the indirect evidence of stable system-level F-scores. Can you provide a more direct analysis to substantiate this? For example, what is the F1-score of your automatic annotation process against the original gold CoNLL-2014 annotations, even if the error schemes differ? Or, could you provide an analysis of the types of disagreements between your tool and the human annotators?
3.  Section 4.4 relies entirely on Table 7, which is missing from the manuscript. Could you please provide this table and ensure all tables and figures referenced in the text are included?
