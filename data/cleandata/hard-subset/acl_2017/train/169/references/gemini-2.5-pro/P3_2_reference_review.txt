### Summary and Contributions

This paper presents a method and a tool for the automatic annotation of grammatical error corrections with error types. The primary goal is to enable detailed, per-error-type evaluation of GEC systems, which is not possible with standard metrics like the M2 scorer. The proposed pipeline first uses a linguistically-enhanced alignment algorithm (Felice et al., 2016) to extract edits between original and corrected sentences. It then classifies these edits using a novel, rule-based framework that relies on automatic linguistic markup (POS tags, lemmas, etc.). The authors demonstrate the utility of their approach by performing an in-depth re-analysis of the system outputs from the CoNLL-2014 shared task, providing the first comprehensive breakdown of system performance by error type.

The main contributions of this work, as I see them, are:

1.  **A practical, dataset-agnostic tool for fine-grained GEC evaluation.** The key contribution is the methodology and accompanying software for automatically annotating system outputs. By creating a transparent, rule-based system, the authors provide a valuable resource that allows researchers to move beyond a single F-score and diagnose the specific strengths and weaknesses of their models.
2.  **An insightful and extensive re-analysis of the CoNLL-2014 shared task.** The paper applies its tool to a major GEC benchmark, yielding a wealth of new insights. The analysis across different tiers (operation, general type, multi-token edits, detection vs. correction) reveals patterns that were previously obscured, such as the surprising inability of many systems to handle "Unnecessary" token errors and the complementary strengths of different architectures (e.g., MT vs. classifier-based).

### Strengths

1.  **Addresses a significant and persistent gap in GEC evaluation.** The paper correctly identifies a major limitation of standard GEC evaluation: a single aggregate score hides crucial details about system performance. Providing a method to automatically generate per-error-type scores is a substantial service to the community, enabling more nuanced system comparison and targeted model improvement.
2.  **Well-justified and pragmatic methodological choices.** The decision to use a rule-based classifier instead of a machine-learning one is well-defended. The arguments for dataset independence, transparency, and consistency are compelling. In a field where evaluation standards can be murky, a deterministic and interpretable system is a significant advantage. The small-scale human evaluation, showing that experts find over 95% of the classifications "Good" or "Acceptable," provides strong support for the quality of the rules.
3.  **Thorough and illuminating experimental analysis.** The re-evaluation of the CoNLL-2014 systems is exemplary. It is not merely a proof-of-concept but a significant empirical contribution in its own right. The paper uncovers valuable findings, such as the general weakness of systems on multi-token edits (a key area for future fluency-focused research) and the clear distinction between error detection and correction performance. Table 6, while dense, is a treasure trove of information for GEC researchers.
4.  **Careful handling of evaluation mismatch.** The authors astutely recognize that comparing automatically annotated hypotheses against a manually annotated reference is problematic. Their solution—to re-process the gold reference with their own automatic tool to create a consistent "auto reference"—is clever and well-reasoned. The validation in Table 4, showing that system rankings are not significantly affected by this change, effectively preempts a major potential criticism and strengthens the validity of their subsequent analysis.

### Weaknesses

1.  **The evaluation of the error classifier is indirect and potentially optimistic.** The human evaluation asked raters to judge the "appropriateness" of a predicted label for an automatically extracted edit. This is not the same as measuring the classifier's accuracy against a gold standard. While the authors argue that different annotation schemes make direct comparison difficult, a more rigorous evaluation would involve mapping their 25 categories to the original CoNLL-2014 categories (where possible) and reporting agreement statistics. The claim that their auto-annotations are "qualitatively as good as human annotations" based on the stability of overall system rankings (Section 4.1) is a logical leap; the rankings could remain stable even if the underlying error attributions are substantially different from human judgments.
2.  **The system's performance is highly dependent on upstream components, whose errors are not fully analyzed.** The entire pipeline's accuracy hinges on the initial edit extraction and the linguistic markup (POS tagging, parsing). The paper acknowledges that tagging errors can lead to classification mistakes (e.g., the "ring/rings" example), but the cascading effect of these errors is not systematically evaluated. For instance, an error in the alignment stage (e.g., incorrectly segmenting one complex edit into two simple ones) would fundamentally alter the input to the classifier, leading to misleading error statistics that are not the fault of the classification rules themselves.
3.  **The error taxonomy, while functional, has inherent limitations.** The authors acknowledge that their framework is a "compromise between informativeness and practicality," which is a reasonable design choice. However, this compromise means that certain complex phenomena are either conflated or ignored. For example, modals are grouped under VERB:TENSE, and complex paraphrases are relegated to the "OTHER" category. This limits the depth of analysis for more semantic or stylistic corrections, which are increasingly a focus of modern GEC research aiming for fluency over strict grammaticality.

### Questions to Authors

1.  Regarding the evaluation of the classifier: Could you provide a more direct comparison against the original, manually annotated CoNLL-2014 reference? Even with differing error schemes, it would be insightful to see a confusion matrix or an agreement score for the subset of categories that can be reasonably mapped between the two schemes. This would provide a more concrete measure of your classifier's accuracy beyond the subjective human ratings.
2.  The "OTHER" category serves as a catch-all for edits that don't fit the rule-based system. What percentage of edits in the CoNLL-2014 test set were classified as "OTHER"? A high frequency could indicate a significant blind spot in the taxonomy, particularly for the complex, multi-token edits that you identify as a major challenge for GEC systems.
3.  The analysis is based on pre-neural and early hybrid systems from 2014. Have you performed any preliminary analysis applying your tool to the outputs of modern, large-scale Transformer-based GEC models? It would be fascinating to know if the distribution of corrected error types has shifted and whether modern systems exhibit different strengths and weaknesses (e.g., are they better at multi-token edits but perhaps worse at specific morphological phenomena?).
