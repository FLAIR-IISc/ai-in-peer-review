### Summary and Contributions

This paper introduces a method and a publicly available tool for automatically annotating Grammatical Error Correction (GEC) data with edit spans and fine-grained error types. The process involves two main stages: first, extracting edits between original and corrected sentences using a linguistically-enhanced alignment algorithm, and second, classifying these edits using a novel, dataset-agnostic, rule-based framework. The primary utility of this work is demonstrated through a comprehensive re-evaluation of the CoNLL-2014 shared task, providing the first detailed breakdown of each participating system's performance across 25 distinct error categories.

From my perspective, the main contributions of this work are, in order of importance:

1.  **A comprehensive, novel analysis of the CoNLL-2014 GEC systems.** This is the paper's strongest contribution. By providing the first detailed, comparative analysis of system performance by error type, the authors offer invaluable insights into the specific strengths and weaknesses of different GEC architectures (e.g., MT-based, classifier-based). This analysis helps explain performance differences that are obscured by a single aggregate score and can guide future research in hybrid systems.
2.  **A reusable tool and a transparent, rule-based error classification framework.** The authors' decision to release their tool is a significant service to the GEC community. The framework's dataset-agnostic and rule-based nature makes it highly practical, as it does not require retraining and its classifications are transparent and deterministic. This can help standardize detailed evaluation across different GEC studies.
3.  **A methodology for achieving a more consistent GEC evaluation.** The paper argues for separating the annotation pipeline (edit extraction and typing) from the evaluation step (a simple comparison of fixed edits). By applying their automatic annotation tool to both the system hypotheses and the reference text, they eliminate annotation mismatches, creating a more controlled and fair comparison.

### Strengths

1.  **High Practical Impact and Community Value:** This work directly addresses a long-standing need in the GEC community for more fine-grained evaluation. An overall F-score reveals little about a system's behavior. The provided tool enables researchers to diagnose their systems' specific failures and successes, fostering more targeted and efficient model development. The release of the tool significantly magnifies the paper's impact.
2.  **Thorough and Insightful Experimental Analysis:** The re-analysis of the CoNLL-2014 shared task is exemplary. The authors slice the data in multiple revealing ways: by operation type (Missing, Unnecessary, Replacement), by detailed error category, by edit length (multi-token edits), and by task (detection vs. correction). The findings are concrete and actionable—for example, that MT-based systems excel at multi-token edits, or that several top systems surprisingly failed to correct any unnecessary token errors. This analysis is a major contribution in its own right.
3.  **Sound and Well-Justified Methodological Choices:** The proposed pipeline is logical and pragmatic. Using a rule-based classifier is a smart choice for an evaluation tool, as its transparency, consistency, and independence from training data are critical for standardization. Furthermore, the approach of re-annotating the gold reference to ensure a fair comparison with automatically annotated hypotheses is a clever solution to the "apples and oranges" problem in evaluation, and the authors validate it by showing that system rankings remain stable.
4.  **Credible Validation of the Error Classifier:** Lacking a gold standard for their new error schema, the authors perform a manual evaluation with 5 GEC experts. The results—with over 95% of classifications rated as "Good" or "Acceptable"—provide strong evidence for the quality and reliability of the rule-based classifier, which is the technical core of the proposed framework.

### Weaknesses

1.  **Lack of Transparency in the Rule-Based Classifier:** While the paper's key technical contribution is the rule-based classifier, its inner workings are completely opaque. The authors state it is "analogous to automating annotation guidelines," but provide no examples of these rules. How does the system use POS tags, lemmas, and dependency parses to distinguish, for example, a `VERB:TENSE` error from a `VERB:FORM` or a `MORPH` error? Without at least a few illustrative rules or a high-level description of the logic, the classifier remains a black box. This makes it difficult to fully assess its linguistic soundness, potential biases, or limitations, and hampers the full reproducibility of the *method*, even if the tool is released.
2.  **Overstated Claim on Annotation Quality:** The authors claim that because automatic re-annotation of the reference text does not significantly change system rankings, their "auto annotations are qualitatively as good as human annotations." This conclusion is not fully supported by the evidence. The stability of rankings could simply mean that the errors and biases of the automatic annotator affect all systems in a uniform way. The primary evidence for annotation quality is the small-scale manual evaluation from Section 3.3. The claim should be softened to state that using an automatic reference provides a *consistent* and *reliable* basis for comparison, which is sufficient justification for the method.
3.  **Potential for Cascading Errors is Under-discussed:** The entire annotation pipeline relies on a chain of NLP tools (POS tagger, lemmatizer, parser, aligner). The paper briefly notes that a POS tagging error can cause a misclassification, but the potential impact of these upstream errors on the final analysis is not explored. How robust is the system to noise from these components? For instance, would using a different POS tagger or parser lead to substantially different conclusions about the CoNLL systems? A sensitivity analysis or more detailed discussion of this issue would strengthen the paper.

### Questions to Authors

1.  Could you please provide one or two examples of the rules in your classifier? For instance, what features and logic does a rule use to identify a `R:NOUN:NUM` (Noun Number) error versus a `R:SPELL` (Spelling) error, especially in a case like `cat -> cats` vs. `catt -> cat`? This would greatly improve the transparency of your method.
2.  Your manual evaluation asked raters to judge the error category for a *given* automatically extracted edit span. However, the edit extraction itself can be a source of disagreement. For example, correcting `...have eating...` to `...has eaten...` could be seen as one multi-token edit or two single-token edits (`have->has`, `eating->eaten`). How does your aligner typically handle such cases, and how does this choice of granularity affect the resulting error analysis and the interpretation of system capabilities?
3.  The development of an error taxonomy involves many design choices. Could you briefly elaborate on the process that led to your 25 categories? For example, what was the rationale for subsuming modal verb errors under the `VERB:TENSE` category, rather than creating a separate `MODAL` category?
