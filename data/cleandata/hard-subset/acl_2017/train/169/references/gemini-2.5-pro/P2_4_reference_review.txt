### Summary and Contributions

This paper introduces a methodology and a tool for the automatic annotation and fine-grained evaluation of Grammatical Error Correction (GEC) systems. The core problem addressed is that global metrics (like the M2 Scorer) conceal system-specific strengths and weaknesses, making detailed error analysis difficult. The proposed solution is a two-stage process: first, it uses a pre-existing linguistically-enhanced alignment algorithm to extract edits between original and corrected sentences. Second, it classifies these edits using a novel, rule-based framework that relies on automatic linguistic features (POS tags, lemmas, etc.). The authors demonstrate the utility of their tool by performing a comprehensive re-evaluation of the systems from the CoNLL-2014 shared task, providing unprecedented insight into their performance across 25 distinct error types.

In my view, the main contributions are:

1.  **A novel, open-source tool and a sound methodology for fine-grained GEC evaluation.** This is a significant practical contribution that fills a clear gap in the field. It enables researchers to move beyond single-score leaderboards and conduct detailed, reproducible error analyses to better understand and improve their models.
2.  **An insightful and extensive re-analysis of the CoNLL-2014 shared task.** This analysis is not merely a demonstration but a valuable contribution in its own right. It uncovers important findings, such as the surprising inability of several systems to handle "unnecessary" token errors, the specific error types best handled by MT vs. classifier-based approaches, and the general poor performance on multi-token edits.
3.  **A proposal for a more transparent and simplified GEC evaluation paradigm.** By pre-annotating both system outputs and reference files using the same tool, the evaluation step is reduced to a direct comparison of edits. The authors compellingly argue and show that this approach is as reliable as using the original gold-standard annotations while being more straightforward.

### Strengths

1.  **High Practical Impact:** The paper addresses a long-standing and critical need in the GEC community. The lack of standardized, automatic tools for error-type analysis has been a major bottleneck for progress. By providing an open-source tool, this work has the potential to become a standard for GEC evaluation, enabling richer analysis and more targeted system development.
2.  **Sound and Well-Justified Methodology:** The authors make excellent design choices. The use of a rule-based classifier is a key strength, as it is deterministic, transparent, and dataset-agnostic, avoiding the black-box nature and training-data biases of machine learning classifiers. Furthermore, the decision to re-process the gold reference with the same automatic pipeline to ensure a fair comparison is clever and empirically justified (Table 4), demonstrating a robust evaluation setup.
3.  **Thorough and Illuminating Experimental Analysis:** The CoNLL-2014 case study is exemplary. The authors slice the data in multiple ways—by operation (Missing/Unnecessary/Replacement), by 25 general error types, by multi-token edits, and by comparing detection versus correction. This multi-faceted analysis provides a wealth of new information about the systems and showcases the power of the proposed tool. The findings are concrete and actionable for GEC researchers.
4.  **Convincing Evaluation of the Classifier:** In the absence of a gold standard for their new error schema, the manual evaluation by five GEC researchers is a strong and appropriate alternative. The result that over 96% of the automatically assigned labels were deemed "Good" or "Acceptable" by experts, coupled with good inter-annotator agreement, provides high confidence in the quality of the rule-based error typer.

### Weaknesses

1.  **Lack of Detail on the Rule-Based Classifier:** While the concept of a rule-based classifier is a strength, the paper is surprisingly light on the details of the rules themselves. The primary technical contribution is this new classification framework, yet the reader is given little insight into its internal logic. For example, how are complex cases disambiguated (e.g., `VERB:TENSE` vs. `VERB:FORM`)? What is the hierarchy or precedence of the rules? Including a few rule examples or a more detailed description in an appendix would have greatly improved the paper's clarity and reproducibility.
2.  **Potential Brittleness due to Upstream Errors:** The entire pipeline's performance is contingent on the quality of the upstream linguistic analysis tools (POS tagger, parser, etc.). The authors acknowledge that most "Bad" classifications in their manual evaluation stemmed from POS tagging errors. This is a critical point, as such tools are often less accurate on ungrammatical learner text than on standard corpora. The paper would be stronger if it discussed this limitation more directly, perhaps by reporting the accuracy of the POS tagger on the original CoNLL sentences to give a sense of the scale of this issue.
3.  **Analysis of the Edit Extraction Component is Limited:** The paper adopts the alignment algorithm from Felice et al. (2016) and cites its high performance. However, errors from this crucial first step (e.g., incorrect edit boundaries) inevitably propagate to the classification stage and can skew the entire analysis. While the manual evaluation provides some assurance, a sensitivity analysis would have been beneficial. For instance, how much do the final error-type distributions and system rankings change if a simpler alignment method (like the one by Swanson and Yamangil) were used instead? This would help isolate the contribution of the classifier from that of the aligner.

### Questions to Authors

1.  The evaluation in Table 4 shows that re-annotating the gold reference does not significantly impact the overall F0.5 scores. However, does this process significantly alter the *distribution* of error types in the reference? For example, does your aligner tend to merge edits that were annotated as separate in the gold standard, potentially shifting the counts from simple error types towards more complex ones?
2.  Could you elaborate on the logic used to handle ambiguous edits? For instance, an edit like `quick` -> `quickly` could be seen as a simple part-of-speech replacement (`R:ADJ` to `R:ADV`) or a more specific `R:MORPH` error. How does your rule-based system prioritize or decide between such overlapping categories?
