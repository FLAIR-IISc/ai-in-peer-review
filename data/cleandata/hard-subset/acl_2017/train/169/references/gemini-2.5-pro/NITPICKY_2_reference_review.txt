### Summary and Contributions

This paper introduces a method for automatically annotating grammatical error correction data with edit spans and error types. The process involves two stages: first, extracting edits using an existing linguistically-enhanced alignment algorithm, and second, classifying these edits using a novel, rule-based framework that relies on automatic linguistic markup (e.g., POS tags, lemmas). The authors conduct a small-scale manual evaluation to assess the appropriateness of their rule-based classifier. The primary application and contribution of the work is a detailed re-evaluation of the system outputs from the CoNLL-2014 shared task, providing a granular breakdown of system performance by error type, which has not been previously possible.

As I see them, the contributions of this work are:
1.  **A detailed empirical analysis of the CoNLL-2014 GEC shared task systems.** This analysis provides valuable and novel insights into the comparative strengths and weaknesses of different GEC architectures (e.g., MT-based, classifier-based) across a wide range of error types.
2.  **A reusable tool and methodology for fine-grained GEC evaluation.** By proposing a standardized pipeline for annotation and creating an "auto reference" for fair comparison, the work offers a practical path forward for more insightful GEC evaluation, which the authors promise to release.

### Strengths

1.  **Addresses a significant gap in GEC evaluation.** The paper correctly identifies that single, aggregate performance metrics (like F0.5) conceal important details about a system's behavior. By enabling a fine-grained analysis, this work allows for a much deeper understanding of GEC systems, which is crucial for targeted model improvement.
2.  **The CoNLL-2014 re-analysis yields compelling insights.** The paper's core strength is its application. The analysis in Section 4 is thorough and reveals interesting patterns. For instance, the observation that some systems completely fail on "Unnecessary" errors, or that MT-based systems are superior for multi-token edits, provides concrete, actionable knowledge for the GEC community. The detection-vs-correction analysis is also a valuable distinction.
3.  **Pragmatic approach to ensuring a fair evaluation.** The decision to re-process the gold reference with the same automatic tools to create an "auto reference" (Section 4.1) is a methodologically sound and clever way to circumvent the mismatch between manual gold annotations and automatic hypothesis annotations. The authors provide strong evidence (Table 4, significance testing) that this does not unduly alter system rankings, lending credibility to their subsequent analysis.

### Weaknesses

My recommendation is primarily based on a substantial number of methodological and presentational flaws which, in aggregate, undermine the submission's quality.

1.  **Methodological choices are questionable and outdated.** The entire rule-based framework is critically dependent on the quality of its underlying linguistic analysis tools. The authors use spaCy v1.6, a version that is several years and major architectural revisions out of date, as well as a Hunspell dictionary from 2016. Modern NLP pipelines offer significantly improved tagging, parsing, and lemmatization accuracy, which would directly impact the quality of the proposed error classification. Basing a new framework and a benchmark re-evaluation on outdated components is a significant methodological weakness.
2.  **Key claims are overstated and insufficiently supported.**
    *   The claim of a "dataset-independent" classifier is inaccurate. The system is entirely dependent on the performance of its upstream tools. As the authors themselves note, classification failures are often traceable to POS tagging errors. This makes the system "tagger-dependent," and the performance of taggers is known to vary across domains.
    *   The conclusion in Section 4.1 that "our auto annotations are qualitatively as good as human annotations" is a major overclaim. The evidence shows only that *system rankings* are stable, which is not the same as the annotations themselves having equivalent quality. This leap in logic misrepresents the experimental findings.
3.  **Evaluation of the classifier is superficial.** The manual evaluation in Section 3.3 is based on a very small sample of 200 edits. For a framework with 55 distinct error types, this is insufficient to draw robust conclusions about its accuracy and coverage. Furthermore, the authors' comparison to previous classifier F1-scores is immediately dismissed as "incomparable," which makes its inclusion confusing and self-serving.
4.  **Severe presentational and formatting flaws.** The manuscript appears to be incomplete and was not carefully proofread.
    *   The paper makes explicit references to **Table 7** (Section 4.4) and **Figure 1** (Section 4.6), neither of which are included in the PDF. This is a critical omission that prevents the reader from fully evaluating the results.
    *   The **References section is catastrophically malformed**. It contains numerous BibTeX parsing errors (e.g., "? \Q2015\E", "Junczys.Dowmunt and Grundkiewicz.,?"), which is unacceptable for a formal submission and suggests extreme carelessness.
    *   The formatting of **Table 9** in the appendix is corrupted (e.g., "Pa rt O fS pe ec h").
    *   **Table 6** is excessively large and dense, making it very difficult to read and extract information from. A more effective visualization or a summary table focusing on key findings would have been preferable.

### Questions to Authors

1.  Could you please provide the missing Table 7 and Figure 1 and briefly summarize the insights they were intended to convey?
2.  Can you justify the decision to build this framework upon significantly outdated tools (spaCy v1.6)? Have you quantified how much the automatic annotations change when using a modern, state-of-the-art NLP pipeline?
3.  The statement that your auto-annotations are "qualitatively as good as human annotations" is a very strong claim based on indirect evidence (stable system rankings). Can you provide a more direct measure of quality by, for instance, calculating an F-score between your automatic reference annotations and the original CoNLL-2014 gold annotations? This would provide a clearer picture of the information loss incurred by your process.
