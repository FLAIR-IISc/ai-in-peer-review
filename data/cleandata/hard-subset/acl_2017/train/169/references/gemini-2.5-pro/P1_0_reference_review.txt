### Summary and Contributions

This paper introduces a tool and methodology for the automatic annotation of grammatical error correction (GEC) data with detailed error types. The process involves two main stages: first, extracting edits between original and corrected sentences using an existing linguistically-enhanced alignment algorithm, and second, classifying these edits using a novel, rule-based framework. This framework is intentionally dataset-agnostic, relying only on automatically derived linguistic features (POS tags, lemmas, etc.). The primary value of this work is demonstrated through an extensive, fine-grained re-evaluation of the system outputs from the CoNLL-2014 shared task, providing the first detailed comparison of system performance across a wide range of specific error types.

The main contributions of this work, as I see them, are:

1.  **A comprehensive, fine-grained empirical analysis of the CoNLL-2014 shared task systems.** This is the paper's strongest contribution. The detailed breakdown of performance by error type (Tables 5, 6, 7), operation, multi-token edits, and detection vs. correction provides a wealth of novel insights into the specific strengths and weaknesses of the GEC systems of that era. This analysis is a significant resource for the community.
2.  **A publicly available tool and a transparent, rule-based framework for GEC evaluation.** By creating a deterministic, dataset-independent system, the authors provide a standardized method for detailed error analysis that does not require labeled training data. This is a valuable contribution towards more reproducible and insightful GEC evaluation.
3.  **A sound methodological proposal for ensuring fair comparison in automatic evaluation.** The authors' decision to re-process the gold-standard reference data using the same automatic pipeline as the system hypotheses (creating an "auto reference") is a crucial and well-justified step that addresses a key mismatch in evaluation.

### Strengths

1.  **High Community Value and Novel Insights:** The paper's most significant strength is the detailed analysis of the CoNLL-2014 systems. This moves beyond a single F-score and reveals actionable insights. For example, the finding that several systems completely failed to address unnecessary token errors (Table 5) or that MT-based systems were uniquely (though still poorly) equipped to handle multi-token edits (Table 8) is highly informative. This analysis provides a valuable historical baseline and can inform the development of hybrid systems by highlighting which architectures are best suited for specific error categories.
2.  **Well-Justified Methodological Choices:** The decision to use a rule-based classifier is pragmatic and effectively defended. For an evaluation tool, transparency, determinism, and independence from training data are highly desirable properties, and the rule-based approach delivers on these. Similarly, the creation of an "auto reference" to ensure a like-for-like comparison is a methodologically sound and important step that strengthens the validity of the results.
3.  **Pragmatic and Reasonable Evaluation:** Lacking a gold standard for their novel error scheme, the authors performed a small-scale manual evaluation with GEC researchers. The results, showing that over 96% of classifications were deemed "Good" or "Acceptable," provide solid evidence for the classifier's efficacy. This is a sensible and convincing way to validate their approach in the absence of a direct benchmark.
4.  **Clarity and Presentation:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and easy to follow. The motivation is clearly stated, the methodology is described logically, and the results are presented in clear, detailed tables that support the paper's claims effectively. The release of the tool further increases the impact of the work.

### Weaknesses

1.  **Limited Scope of Classifier Evaluation:** While the manual evaluation is a good starting point, its small scale (200 edits) limits the conclusions that can be drawn. The authors state that most "Bad" ratings were due to upstream POS tagging errors, but this is not quantified. The robustness of the entire pipeline to errors from its dependencies (spaCy, NLTK) is a critical aspect that is not explored in depth. An error analysis of the classifier itself—identifying which rules or error types are most brittle—would significantly strengthen the paper.
2.  **Lack of Comparison to Other Error Taxonomies:** The paper introduces a new error classification scheme with 25 types but does not situate it with respect to previously established taxonomies (e.g., the original CoNLL-2014 scheme, or those used for annotating the FCE or NUCLE corpora). A discussion comparing the coverage, granularity, and design principles of their scheme against others would help readers understand its relative merits and limitations and assess its broader applicability beyond this specific study.
3.  **Analysis is Potentially Dated:** The analysis is focused exclusively on systems from 2014. While this is a foundational benchmark, the GEC field has since been transformed by large-scale pretrained language models and neural sequence-to-sequence approaches. The insights derived from analyzing rule-based, SMT, and classifier-based systems may not be fully generalizable to the current state-of-the-art. The paper would be strengthened by acknowledging this limitation and briefly discussing how the tool could be applied to analyze and understand the behavior of modern neural GEC systems.

### Questions to Authors

1.  Regarding the classifier's failures, you mention they can often be traced back to POS tagging mistakes. Could you provide a more quantitative breakdown of the causes for the 3.7% of "Bad" ratings? For example, what percentage is attributable to POS errors vs. lemmatization errors vs. flaws in the rule logic itself?
2.  The "auto reference" approach is well-motivated, but it inherently discards human annotation, which may capture complex edits that an automatic aligner struggles with (e.g., paraphrases). Have you analyzed the types of edits in the gold reference that are most frequently "lost" or altered during this automatic reprocessing? Is there a risk that this process systematically penalizes systems that can make more complex, human-like corrections?
3.  The analysis of multi-token edits is very interesting. Could your framework be used to distinguish between different types of multi-token edits? For instance, splitting a compound word (`Bestfriend` -> `best friend`) is structurally different from a phrasal correction (`have eating` -> `has eaten`). Does your tool support this level of analysis, and if so, could it reveal further insights into system capabilities?
