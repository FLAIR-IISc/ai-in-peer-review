### Summary and Contributions

This paper presents a method and a corresponding tool to automatically annotate Grammatical Error Correction (GEC) system outputs with edit spans and error types. The primary goal is to enable detailed, fine-grained evaluation of GEC systems beyond a single aggregate score. The contributions of this work, as I see them, are:

1.  **A Complete, Open-Source Pipeline for Fine-Grained GEC Evaluation:** The main contribution is a practical and fully-realized system for detailed GEC analysis. It combines an existing state-of-the-art alignment tool with a novel, rule-based error type classifier. By making this tool available, the authors provide a valuable resource that allows researchers to standardize evaluation and better understand the strengths and weaknesses of different GEC models.

2.  **A Comprehensive, First-of-its-Kind Analysis of the CoNLL-2014 Shared Task Systems:** The paper uses the proposed tool to conduct an extensive and insightful re-evaluation of the CoNLL-2014 shared task submissions. This analysis provides the community with the first direct comparison of these systems at the error-type level, revealing important patterns (e.g., the superiority of MT systems for multi-token edits, the failure of several systems to handle unnecessary words) that were previously obscured by aggregate scores. This analysis itself is a significant contribution to the GEC literature.

### Strengths

1.  **High Practical Utility and Community Value:** The work addresses a clear and persistent need in the GEC field. The lack of a standardized tool for detailed error analysis has made it difficult to compare systems meaningfully and guide future research. By providing an end-to-end, open-source tool, this paper makes a direct and impactful contribution that can benefit the entire community.

2.  **Sound and Well-Justified Methodological Choices:** The authors' approach is pragmatic and robust.
    *   The choice of a rule-based classifier over a machine learning one is well-defended. It makes the system dataset-agnostic, transparent (one can always see *why* a type was assigned), and free from the need for labeled training data, which is often a bottleneck.
    *   The decision to re-process the gold reference with the same automatic pipeline ("auto reference") is a methodologically sound way to ensure a fair and direct comparison, and the authors validate that this does not significantly alter the overall system rankings.

3.  **Thorough and Insightful Experimental Analysis:** The application of the tool to the CoNLL-2014 data is not a mere proof-of-concept; it is a substantial piece of analysis. The authors slice the results in multiple informative ways: by high-level operation (Missing, Replacement, Unnecessary), by specific error type (Table 6), by multi-token edits (Table 8), and by comparing detection versus correction performance (Figure 1). This multifaceted analysis yields numerous valuable insights and sets a new standard for GEC system evaluation papers.

4.  **Strong Evaluation of the Proposed Classifier:** While a gold standard for their new error schema doesn't exist, the authors perform a sensible manual evaluation with 5 domain experts. The results, showing that >95% of classifications were deemed "Good" or "Acceptable" with good inter-annotator agreement, provide strong evidence for the quality and reliability of the rule-based classifier.

### Weaknesses

1.  **Lack of Detail on the Rule-Based Classifier:** While the paper justifies the *choice* of a rule-based system, it provides very little detail about the rules themselves. The classifier is the core novel component of the proposed pipeline, yet its internal logic is treated as a black box. The paper states that classification is based on POS tags, lemmas, etc., but does not describe the rule hierarchy or how conflicts are resolved. For example, for an edit like `informations -> information`, how is it classified as `NOUN:INFL` and not `NOUN:NUM`? For `quick (adj) -> quickly (adv)`, how is the decision made to label it `MORPH`? More detail, perhaps in an appendix, on the decision logic would significantly improve the clarity and reproducibility of the work.

2.  **Limited Contextualization of the Error Schema:** The paper introduces a new error schema with 25 types but does not discuss how it relates to or improves upon existing GEC error typologies, such as the one used in the original CoNLL-2014 annotations. A brief comparison justifying the design choices (e.g., why certain categories were merged, split, or created) would help readers understand the linguistic and practical motivations behind the new framework and assess its advantages over previous schemas.

3.  **Potential Overstatement Regarding M2 Scorer:** The paper claims the M2 scorer "tends to overestimate performance" and "exploits its dynamic edit boundary prediction" to maximize TPs and minimize FPs. While previous work is cited, this is a strong claim. The M2 scorer's flexibility in matching is designed to be lenient towards equally valid but differently tokenized annotations (e.g., matching a system's `have eating -> has eaten` against a reference's `have -> has` + `eating -> eaten`). Framing this design feature as "overestimation" or an "exploit" might be overly critical. The authors' simpler exact-match approach is cleaner but also potentially harsher and less flexible to annotation variations. A more nuanced discussion of the trade-offs between the two evaluation approaches would be more balanced.

### Questions to Authors

1.  Could you elaborate on the decision logic of the rule-based classifier? Specifically, how does the system prioritize rules when an edit could plausibly be classified into multiple categories? For example, for an edit involving a change in both POS and morphology, how is the final category determined?

2.  Regarding the manual evaluation of the classifier, could you provide a brief qualitative analysis of the 3.7% of edits rated as "Bad"? The paper notes that POS tagging errors are a primary cause, but were there any cases where the POS tags were correct but the rule itself was deemed inappropriate or insufficient by the human raters? This could shed light on the inherent limitations of the rule-based approach.

3.  The analysis of multi-token edits is very interesting. Does your framework distinguish between contiguous multi-token edits (e.g., `in order to -> to`) and non-contiguous ones (e.g., word order shifts)? If so, did you observe any performance differences between these sub-types across the systems?
