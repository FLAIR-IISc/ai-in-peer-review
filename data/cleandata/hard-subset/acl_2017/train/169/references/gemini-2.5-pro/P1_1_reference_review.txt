### Summary and Main Contributions

This paper presents a framework and a publicly available tool for automatically annotating grammatical error correction (GEC) data with detailed error types. The process involves two main steps: first, extracting edits between an original and corrected sentence pair using a pre-existing linguistically-enhanced alignment algorithm; second, classifying these edits into one of 25 error types using a novel, rule-based framework that relies on automatic linguistic markup (POS tags, lemmas, etc.). The authors demonstrate the utility of their tool by performing an in-depth, fine-grained analysis of the system outputs from the CoNLL-2014 shared task, revealing specific strengths and weaknesses of different GEC approaches that were previously obscured by a single overall score.

As I see them, the main contributions of this work are:

1.  **An insightful and comprehensive re-evaluation of the CoNLL-2014 shared task.** This analysis is the most significant contribution, providing novel and valuable insights into the performance of various GEC architectures on specific error types. It is a service to the GEC community, offering a much richer understanding of the state-of-the-art from that period and highlighting areas for improvement (e.g., multi-token edits, unnecessary word errors).

2.  **The development and public release of a dataset-agnostic tool for fine-grained GEC evaluation.** By creating a transparent, rule-based system that does not require training on a specific annotated corpus, the authors provide a reusable resource that can standardize error analysis across different datasets and systems. The tool's release significantly enhances the paper's impact.

### Strengths

1.  **High practical value and potential for community impact.** The paper addresses a well-known limitation in GEC evaluation—the lack of standardized, fine-grained analysis. The ability to automatically annotate system output allows for a deeper diagnosis of model performance beyond a single F-score. The release of the tool is a major strength, empowering other researchers to conduct similar analyses on their own systems, which could guide future research directions.

2.  **Sound and careful evaluation methodology.** The authors demonstrate a strong understanding of the nuances of GEC evaluation. The decision to re-process the gold-standard reference data with their own automatic annotation pipeline (Section 4.1) to create a fair "auto reference" is crucial and well-justified. It ensures that systems are not penalized for alignment or classification decisions that differ from the manual gold standard but are consistent with the tool's logic. This shows commendable scientific rigor.

3.  **The analysis is thorough and reveals genuinely interesting findings.** The breakdown of system performance is not superficial. The paper examines results from multiple perspectives: operation type (Missing/Unnecessary/Replacement), specific error categories (Table 6), detailed sub-categories (Table 7), multi-token edits (Table 8), and the distinction between detection and correction (Figure 1). For example, the finding that five teams failed to correct any "Unnecessary" errors, or that MT-based systems were superior at multi-token edits, provides concrete evidence about the capabilities of different GEC paradigms.

4.  **The rule-based approach is well-motivated.** While a machine learning classifier might seem like a more modern approach, the authors' choice of a rule-based system is well-argued. The benefits of dataset-independence, transparency (it's always clear *why* an error was classified a certain way), and consistency are significant advantages for a tool intended for standardized evaluation.

### Weaknesses

1.  **The evaluation of the core error classifier is limited.** The primary technical novelty of the paper is the rule-based error classifier, yet its evaluation is based on a small-scale manual study where 5 researchers rated the appropriateness of 200 classifications. While the >95% "Good" or "Acceptable" rating is positive, this evaluation is subjective and lacks a direct, quantitative comparison to established benchmarks. The authors argue that ML approaches are dataset-dependent and thus incomparable, but it would have been possible to train and test an ML classifier on a single corpus (e.g., FCE) and compare its F1-score on a held-out set against the performance of this rule-based system on the same data (after mapping the error types). This would have provided a more concrete grounding for the classifier's performance.

2.  **The novelty of the technical method is incremental.** The work heavily relies on pre-existing components. The edit extraction is taken directly from Felice et al. (2016), and the classification rules are based on outputs from standard NLP toolkits (spaCy, NLTK). While the integration and the design of the rule set are new, the paper's contribution lies more in the engineering of a useful system and its application to a novel analysis, rather than in proposing a fundamentally new NLP technique.

3.  **Lack of analysis on the impact of cascading errors from the underlying NLP toolkit.** The entire pipeline's accuracy is contingent on the performance of the POS tagger, lemmatizer, and parser. The paper acknowledges this, noting that POS tagging mistakes can lead to misclassifications (e.g., `ring -> rings` as NOUN:NUM vs. VERB:SVA). However, there is no sensitivity analysis to quantify this problem. How would the overall CoNLL-2014 analysis change if a different, perhaps more accurate, set of underlying tools were used? This is a key question for a deterministic, rule-based system, and its absence is a weakness.

### Questions to Authors

1.  Regarding the evaluation of the classifier: Could you elaborate on why a more direct, quantitative comparison was not performed? For example, one could take the manually annotated FCE or CoNLL-2014 test set, create a mapping from the original error tags to your new 25-type scheme, and then calculate precision, recall, and F1 for your classifier against this mapped gold standard. While the mapping might be imperfect, it would provide a more direct point of comparison than the qualitative human judgments.

2.  The paper makes a compelling argument for re-annotating the gold reference to create an "auto reference" for fair comparison. However, the official M² scorer was designed to handle some annotation ambiguity by matching against multiple annotators and allowing for phrasal equivalence. Does your stricter matching criterion (exact span and correction string) risk being too punitive for corrections that are semantically equivalent but phrased differently (e.g., "a reason for this is" -> "this is because")?

3.  Could you provide more detail on the rule-based classifier itself? The paper describes the concept and inputs, but gives no examples of the actual rules. For instance, how does the system differentiate a `VERB:TENSE` error from a `VERB:FORM` error, especially in complex cases? A few examples in an appendix would greatly improve the paper's clarity and reproducibility.
