### Summary and Contributions

This paper presents a method for automatically annotating grammatical error correction (GEC) data with explicit error spans and types. The proposed pipeline first uses an existing alignment algorithm (Felice et al., 2016) to extract edits between original and corrected sentences. It then classifies these edits using a novel, rule-based framework that relies on automatic linguistic markup (POS tags, lemmas, etc.). The authors use this tool to perform a detailed, error-type-specific re-evaluation of the systems from the CoNLL-2014 shared task, providing insights that are not visible from overall F-scores.

From my perspective, the main contributions of this work are:

1.  **A detailed and insightful analysis of system performance in the CoNLL-2014 shared task.** The paper provides the first comprehensive breakdown of how different GEC architectures perform on a wide range of specific error types, which is a valuable resource for the community.
2.  **A publicly available tool for standardized GEC evaluation.** By releasing their tool, the authors provide a means for researchers to conduct more fine-grained and reproducible analyses of their own systems.

### Strengths

1.  **Addresses a well-defined and important limitation in GEC evaluation.** Current standard metrics, like the M2 scorer, provide a single score that obscures system-specific strengths and weaknesses. This work directly tackles this issue by enabling evaluation at the error-type level, which is crucial for advancing the field and understanding the capabilities of different GEC models.
2.  **The analysis of the CoNLL-2014 systems is thorough and valuable.** The breakdown of performance by operation type (Missing/Unnecessary/Replacement), general error categories (Table 6), multi-token edits, and detection vs. correction provides a multi-faceted view of system behavior. These findings, such as the poor performance of many systems on "Unnecessary" errors or the general weakness in handling multi-token edits, are insightful and can guide future research.
3.  **The proposed framework demonstrates clear practical utility.** The release of a tool that can be applied to any parallel GEC data to produce standardized, fine-grained annotations is a welcome contribution. This encourages standardized evaluation practices and allows researchers to benchmark their systems in a more detailed manner than was previously possible.

### Weaknesses

1.  **The core methodological contribution—the rule-based classifier—is not rigorously validated against established methods.** The authors motivate their choice of a rule-based system by criticizing machine learning classifiers as being "biased" and "dataset-dependent." However, they fail to substantiate this criticism with evidence or provide a direct, quantitative comparison. The evaluation provided (manual rating by 5 experts on 200 examples) is small-scale, subjective, and insufficient to establish the superiority or even adequacy of the proposed method. A standard evaluation would involve comparing the rule-based classifier against a conventional classifier (e.g., Maximum Entropy, as in prior work) on a held-out, manually annotated test set. Without such a baseline, the claims of the classifier's efficacy are unproven.
2.  **The evaluation of the error classifier itself is methodologically questionable.** The manual evaluation protocol, which asked raters to assess the "appropriateness of the predicted error category" regardless of the automatically determined edit boundary, is a significant confound. An error annotation consists of both a span and a category; judging one without the other is problematic. A well-chosen category for a badly-spanned edit is still an incorrect annotation. This protocol likely inflates the reported agreement and "Good/Acceptable" scores. Furthermore, the details of the rule-based framework are not provided, making it difficult for the reader to assess its logic, potential biases, or limitations.
3.  **The decision to discard the gold-standard reference annotations in favor of automatically generated ones is not well-justified.** The authors re-process the gold reference data using their automatic pipeline to ensure a "fair comparison." They justify this by showing that overall F0.5 scores do not change significantly (Table 4). However, this finding at a macro level does not guarantee that the distribution of error types remains consistent. Discarding high-quality human annotations introduces an unquantified amount of noise into the reference standard. The claim that this makes the automatic annotations "qualitatively as good as human annotations" is a strong overstatement not supported by the evidence provided. A more convincing approach would be to measure the discrepancy between the tool's output and the original gold annotations to characterize the tool's error profile.

### Questions to Authors

1.  Could you provide a direct, quantitative comparison of your rule-based error classifier against a standard machine learning baseline (e.g., a MaxEnt classifier as used in Swanson & Yamangil, 2012) on an established, manually annotated dataset (e.g., FCE or the original CoNLL data)? This would provide a much-needed empirical grounding for the quality of your classification method.
2.  In your justification for replacing the gold reference with an automatically annotated version, you show that overall F-scores are stable. Did you also analyze whether the distribution of error types differs significantly between the original gold reference and your auto-generated reference? A shift in this distribution could impact the validity of the fine-grained analysis that forms the core of your results.
3.  Could you elaborate on the inter-annotator agreement study for the classifier evaluation? Specifically, what were the primary sources of disagreement that led to "Acceptable" or "Bad" ratings? You mention that "Bad" ratings often traced back to POS tagging errors, but this suggests the classifier is brittle with respect to upstream errors, which is an important limitation to discuss.
