### Summary and Contributions

This paper presents a method and a tool for automatically annotating grammatical error correction (GEC) data with edit spans and error types. The primary goal is to enable a more fine-grained analysis of GEC system performance beyond a single aggregate score. The contributions of this work, as I see them, are:

1.  **A practical tool and methodology for detailed GEC evaluation:** The main contribution is a complete, open-source pipeline that takes unannotated source and hypothesis sentences and produces a detailed, error-typed evaluation. This addresses a well-known limitation of standard GEC metrics and will be a valuable resource for researchers wanting to diagnose their systems' strengths and weaknesses.

2.  **A comprehensive, multi-level error analysis of the CoNLL-2014 shared task:** The authors apply their tool to re-evaluate the CoNLL-2014 systems, providing the first detailed public analysis of its kind. This empirical analysis reveals insightful patterns, such as specific systems excelling at certain error types (e.g., CUUI on orthography, CAMB on prepositions) and a widespread failure to correct "Unnecessary" token errors.

3.  **A novel, transparent, and dataset-agnostic error classification framework:** The authors propose a rule-based classifier for assigning error types. Its primary advantage, as argued in the paper, is that it does not require labeled training data and is deterministic, making it transparent and applicable across different datasets without retraining.

### Strengths

1.  **Addresses a Clear and Important Need:** The GEC field has long been constrained by evaluation metrics that report a single F-score, obscuring the nuanced performance of different systems. This paper directly tackles this problem by providing a means to conduct standardized, fine-grained error analysis. The release of the tool significantly enhances its impact and utility for the community.

2.  **Sound and Well-Justified Methodological Choices:** The authors make pragmatic and well-reasoned decisions. They adopt a state-of-the-art method for edit extraction (Felice et al., 2016) rather than reinventing it. The choice of a rule-based classifier is convincingly argued for on the grounds of transparency, determinism, and independence from training data, which are significant advantages for a standardized evaluation tool.

3.  **Thorough and Insightful Experimental Analysis:** The application of the tool to the CoNLL-2014 data is more than just a proof-of-concept; it is a significant empirical contribution. The paper uncovers several interesting and actionable findings. For example, the analysis of performance on multi-token edits (Table 8) highlights a key area for future GEC research, and the detection vs. correction analysis (Figure 1) provides a different perspective on system capabilities. These results provide a new benchmark and a roadmap for future system development.

4.  **Methodologically Careful Evaluation:** The authors demonstrate rigor in their evaluation setup. A key step is reprocessing the gold-standard reference data with their automatic annotation pipeline (Section 4.1) to ensure a fair and direct comparison. Showing that this does not significantly alter the overall system rankings (Table 4) lends strong credibility to their subsequent detailed analysis based on the automatically generated annotations.

### Weaknesses

1.  **Lack of Detail on the Core Technical Contribution:** The paper's main technical novelty is the rule-based classifier, yet its implementation is treated as a black box. The authors state that it classifies edits based on "automatic POS tags" and other features, but provide no examples of the rules themselves. For instance, how does the system differentiate a complex `VERB:TENSE` error from a `VERB:FORM` error? How are ambiguities (e.g., `ring -> rings` as `NOUN:NUM` vs. `VERB:SVA`) handled? This lack of detail hinders reproducibility and makes it difficult to assess the sophistication and potential limitations of the classifier itself.

2.  **Limited and Indirect Evaluation of the Classifier:** The classifier's performance is assessed via a small-scale manual evaluation of "appropriateness" by 5 experts on 200 edits. While the results are positive (96.3% "Good" or "Acceptable"), this evaluation is subjective. The paper dismisses comparison to other classifiers by stating there is no gold standard for their new scheme. However, a more informative evaluation could have involved mapping their proposed error categories to an existing, labeled schema (like the original CoNLL-2014 one) and reporting F1 scores. Even with an imperfect mapping, this would provide a more direct, quantitative comparison against prior work (e.g., the 50-70 F1 reported for ML classifiers) and a clearer sense of the classifier's accuracy.

3.  **Potential Overstatement of Independence from Upstream Errors:** The paper rightly notes that many "Bad" classifications stem from errors in the underlying NLP tools (e.g., POS tagger). However, the impact of these cascading errors is not systematically analyzed. The quality of the entire pipeline is fundamentally dependent on the accuracy of the edit extractor and the linguistic annotation tools. While the overall system rankings seem stable, a more detailed analysis of how annotation errors might affect the distribution of error types would strengthen the paper. For example, are certain error types more susceptible to being misclassified due to common POS tagging mistakes?

### Questions to Authors

1.  Could you provide 2-3 concrete examples of the rules in your classifier? For example, what specific logic (in terms of POS tags, lemmas, etc.) is used to identify a `VERB:SVA` error versus a `NOUN:NUM` error, especially in ambiguous cases? This would greatly improve the clarity and reproducibility of your core contribution.

2.  The manual evaluation measures "appropriateness," which is valuable. However, to better position your work relative to previous ML-based typing approaches, have you considered performing an extrinsic evaluation by mapping your error scheme to the CoNLL-2014 scheme and reporting F1-scores against the gold reference types? This would provide a useful, if imperfect, quantitative benchmark.

3.  Your analysis shows that systems using MT were most successful at multi-token edits. Do you have any insights into whether this is purely a strength of the GEC systems, or if the underlying edit extraction algorithm (Felice et al., 2016) might have its own biases in identifying single vs. multi-token edits that could influence this result?
