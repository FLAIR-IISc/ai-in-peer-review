### Summary and Contributions

This paper presents a method and a tool for automatically annotating Grammatical Error Correction (GEC) data with error types. The process involves two main stages: first, extracting edits between original and corrected sentences using a linguistically-enhanced alignment algorithm from prior work, and second, classifying these edits using a novel, rule-based framework that relies on automatic linguistic features (POS tags, lemmas, etc.). The primary contribution is the development and release of this tool, which enables detailed, error-type-specific evaluation of GEC systems, a capability largely missing from current evaluation metrics. A significant secondary contribution is the comprehensive re-evaluation of the CoNLL-2014 shared task submissions, which provides new, granular insights into the strengths and weaknesses of different GEC architectures that were obscured by a single aggregate score.

### Strengths

1.  **High Practical Utility and Addresses a Clear Need:** The GEC community has long acknowledged the limitations of single-score evaluation. This work directly addresses this gap by providing a practical tool to dissect system performance. The ability to automatically annotate system hypotheses and re-process gold references in a consistent manner makes fine-grained analysis accessible and standardized. The re-analysis of the CoNLL-2014 shared task is a powerful demonstration of the tool's utility, revealing valuable insights such as the surprising failure of several systems on "Unnecessary" token errors and the superiority of MT-based systems for multi-token edits.

2.  **Sound and Well-Justified Methodology:** The authors make sensible design choices. They build upon a state-of-the-art edit extraction method (Felice et al., 2016) for the first step. For the second, their decision to create a deterministic, rule-based classifier is well-argued. It circumvents the need for labeled training data, making the tool dataset-agnostic, and offers transparency, which is a significant advantage over opaque machine learning classifiers. The manual evaluation of the classifier, while small, shows strong results (>95% "Good" or "Acceptable") from expert raters, lending credibility to the approach's effectiveness.

3.  **Thorough and Insightful Analysis:** The paper goes far beyond simply describing the tool. The detailed breakdown of the CoNLL-2014 results in Tables 5-8 is excellent. The analysis at different tiers (Operation, General Type, Detailed Type), as well as the specific investigations into multi-token errors and detection vs. correction, provide a multi-faceted view of system performance. The observation that reprocessing the gold reference with their automatic method does not significantly alter system rankings is a crucial finding that validates their evaluation methodology. This level of analysis sets a new standard for GEC system evaluation papers.

### Weaknesses

1.  **Limited Evaluation of the Error Classifier and Error Propagation:** The primary novelty is the rule-based error classifier, but its evaluation is somewhat limited. While the manual evaluation of 200 edits is a good start, it is small-scale. The paper notes that "Bad" classifications can often be traced to POS tagger mistakes, but it does not quantify the impact of such upstream errors. The performance of the underlying tools (spaCy, NLTK) can vary across different domains or learner proficiency levels. A more thorough analysis of the classifier's failure modes and its robustness to noise from the linguistic annotation pipeline would significantly strengthen the paper's claims.

2.  **Lack of Comparison with Existing Error Taxonomies:** The paper introduces a "new framework" of 25 error types but does not sufficiently situate it within the context of existing, well-established error annotation schemes (e.g., the one used for the original CoNLL-2014 gold data, or other taxonomies from the SLA/GEC literature). A discussion justifying the design choices for this new schema—why these 25 categories, why not others, and how they map to or improve upon previous schemes—is missing. Without this context, it is difficult to judge the novelty and appropriateness of the proposed taxonomy itself, separate from the tool that implements it.

3.  **The "Auto Reference" Justification Sidesteps a Deeper Issue:** The paper argues for re-processing the gold reference to ensure a fair comparison, as automatic alignment can differ from manual alignment (e.g., one multi-token edit vs. two single-token edits). While this creates a methodologically sound comparison *within their system's paradigm*, it avoids the question of how well the entire pipeline (alignment + typing) reproduces human judgments end-to-end. The evaluation in Section 3.3 assesses only the typing step on automatically-aligned edits. An end-to-end comparison against a manually annotated gold standard, measuring how often the full pipeline produces the exact same edits (span and type), would provide a more complete picture of the system's fidelity to human annotation.

### Questions to Authors

1.  Regarding the classifier's performance, can you provide a more detailed breakdown of the 3.7% "Bad" ratings from the human evaluation? How many were due to clear POS tagging errors versus inherent ambiguities or flaws in the rule set? Are there particular error types that are more prone to misclassification?

2.  Could you elaborate on the design principles of your error typology? How does it relate to the original 28 error types used in the CoNLL-2013/14 annotations? Was the goal to create a scheme that was more amenable to automatic classification, and if so, what trade-offs were made in terms of linguistic descriptiveness?

3.  Your finding that overall F0.5 scores are stable between gold and auto references is important. Does this stability also hold at the level of individual error types? For instance, could the automatic alignment process systematically merge edits in a way that inflates one error category at the expense of another compared to the manual gold alignment, even if the overall score remains similar?
