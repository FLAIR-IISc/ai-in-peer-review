### Summary of Contributions

This paper presents a method and a tool for the automatic annotation of grammatical error correction (GEC) data with explicit edit spans and error types. The primary contributions of this work are:

1.  **A novel, rule-based error classification framework.** This framework is designed to be dataset-agnostic, relying on automatically generated linguistic features (POS tags, lemmas, etc.) rather than trained models. This makes it transparent, deterministic, and portable across different GEC datasets.
2.  **A detailed, fine-grained re-analysis of the CoNLL-2014 shared task.** By applying their tool to the system outputs, the authors provide the first comprehensive breakdown of system performance by error type. This analysis reveals previously hidden strengths and weaknesses of different GEC approaches (e.g., MT vs. classifier-based) and offers valuable insights for future research.

### Strengths

1.  **Solves a significant and persistent problem in GEC evaluation.** The inability to easily perform fine-grained error analysis has been a major limitation since the CoNLL-2014 shared task. This work provides a practical and well-motivated solution that allows researchers to move beyond a single F-score and understand *why* a system performs as it does. The planned release of the tool is a valuable contribution to the community.

2.  **The methodology is sound and transparent.** The decision to use a rule-based classifier is well-justified, correctly identifying the limitations of dataset-dependent ML classifiers. The core idea of "automating the annotation guidelines" is elegant. Furthermore, the decision to re-process the gold reference with the same automatic pipeline to ensure a fair "apples-to-apples" comparison is a critical and insightful step that demonstrates a deep understanding of the evaluation problem. The validation in Table 4, showing that system rankings are preserved, strongly supports this methodological choice.

3.  **The analysis is extensive, insightful, and actionable.** The paper goes far beyond a simple proof-of-concept. The breakdowns by operation type (Table 5), general error type (Table 6), and even more specific examples (Table 7) provide a wealth of new information. Key findings—such as the failure of five teams to correct any "Unnecessary" errors, the superiority of MT systems on multi-token edits, and the detailed comparison of system performance on specific categories like determiners or verb forms—are highly valuable and can directly inform the design of future hybrid GEC systems.

4.  **The classifier evaluation is pragmatic and convincing.** Lacking a gold standard for their new schema, the authors' use of manual evaluation by five domain experts is an appropriate and effective strategy. The result that over 96% of classifications were deemed "Good" or "Acceptable" is a strong endorsement of their rule-based approach, especially considering the noise from the upstream automatic edit extraction.

### Weaknesses

1.  **The pipeline's accuracy is heavily dependent on upstream NLP tools, and the impact of this is not fully quantified.** The authors rightly note that most "Bad" classifications in their manual evaluation were due to POS tagging errors. This highlights a critical vulnerability. The entire framework's reliability hinges on the performance of spaCy. While this is an inherent challenge for any system using such features, the paper would be stronger if it included a more detailed error analysis. For example, for the 3.7% of "Bad" ratings, could the authors quantify what proportion was due to POS errors vs. parser errors vs. flaws in the rules themselves? This would help users of the tool understand its potential failure modes.

2.  **The paper lacks detail about the ruleset itself, hindering reproducibility and understanding.** The core methodological contribution is the "rule-based framework," yet the paper provides no examples of the actual rules. How are different error types distinguished? For instance, the distinction between a `VERB:TENSE` error and a `VERB:SVA` error can be subtle and often relies on syntactic context. A few illustrative examples of the logic (e.g., "if source and target lemmas are the same, source POS is VBZ and target is VBP, and the dependency head is a plural noun, classify as VERB:SVA") would significantly improve the paper's clarity. Without this, the classifier remains a black box, which undermines the claimed benefit of transparency over ML models.

3.  **The error schema, while practical, has some potentially ambiguous or overly broad categories.** The paper notes that compromises were made for practicality, which is reasonable. However, some categories seem very broad. For example, `VERB:TENSE` is said to include "inflectional and periphrastic tense, modal verbs and passivization." Lumping these distinct linguistic phenomena together might obscure important system differences. A system might be excellent at inflectional tense but fail completely on modal verb choice. Similarly, `OTHER` is a catch-all that could hide interesting error patterns. A brief discussion of these limitations and the trade-offs involved would be beneficial.

### Questions to Authors

1.  Your manual evaluation of the classifier was based on a random sample of 200 edits. Given the long-tail distribution of error types in GEC, could you provide a breakdown of how many instances of each error type were present in this sample? It is possible that the high performance is driven by high-frequency, easy-to-classify types (e.g., `DET`, `NOUN:NUM`), while the classifier's performance on rarer types remains less certain.

2.  In Section 4.1, you show that re-processing the reference doesn't significantly change the *overall* F0.5 scores. This is a strong result. Did you also investigate if it disproportionately affects the scores for specific error types? For example, human annotators might segment complex phrasal edits (e.g., `WO`, `OTHER`) differently than the automatic alignment algorithm, which could change the relative rankings for those specific categories, even if the overall score remains stable.
