**Summary**
This paper introduces a tool for the automatic annotation and fine-grained evaluation of grammatical error correction (GEC) systems. The proposed method first uses an existing linguistically-enhanced alignment algorithm to extract edits between original and corrected sentences. It then classifies these edits using a novel, rule-based framework that relies on automatic linguistic features (POS tags, lemmas, etc.). The authors claim this approach is dataset-independent and transparent. To demonstrate its utility, the tool is applied to re-evaluate the system outputs from the CoNLL-2014 shared task, providing a detailed breakdown of performance across 25 error types, which was not possible with the original evaluation.

**Contributions**
1.  **A detailed error analysis of the CoNLL-2014 shared task systems.** This analysis provides novel insights into the specific strengths and weaknesses of different GEC architectures (e.g., MT-based, classifier-based) on various error types, which is a valuable resource for the community.
2.  **A rule-based method for classifying GEC edits.** The paper proposes a new set of rules for assigning error types to edits based on automatically derived linguistic features.
3.  **A publicly available tool for GEC evaluation.** The release of the software will allow other researchers to perform similar fine-grained analyses on their own systems.

**Strengths**
1.  **Addresses a clear need in the community.** The lack of standardized, fine-grained error analysis in GEC evaluation is a well-known problem. This work provides a practical solution to enable researchers to look beyond a single F-score and understand system behavior in more detail. The re-analysis of the CoNLL-2014 task is a compelling demonstration of the tool's value.
2.  **The provided analysis is thorough and insightful.** The breakdown of system performance by operation type (Missing, Replacement, Unnecessary), general error type, and even multi-token edits yields interesting and actionable conclusions. For example, the finding that many systems completely fail to handle "Unnecessary" token errors or that MT-based systems are superior at multi-token edits is valuable information for future GEC research.
3.  **The tool is being released publicly.** This is a welcome contribution that promotes reproducibility and allows the community to build upon this work.

**Weaknesses**
1.  **The evaluation of the core technical contribution is insufficient.** The main novelty of this work is the rule-based classifier, yet its evaluation is based on a small-scale, subjective manual rating of 200 edits. While the high agreement and "Good"/"Acceptable" ratings are noted, this is not a substitute for a rigorous, quantitative evaluation. The authors should have created a gold-standard dataset annotated with their new scheme and reported standard metrics (precision, recall, F1) for each error category. This would provide a much clearer picture of the classifier's performance and its failure modes. Comparing subjective ratings to F1 scores from prior work (while admitting they are incomparable) is unconvincing.
2.  **The justification for a rule-based approach over established ML methods is weak.** The authors criticize ML classifiers for being biased to their training data, but rule-based systems are subject to the developers' own biases and are notoriously brittle. The system's heavy reliance on the output of upstream tools (POS tagger, parser) is a significant vulnerability, as acknowledged by the authors when they state that most "Bad" classifications are due to POS tagging errors. The claim that the method is "dataset-independent" is not substantiated; the rules were likely developed and refined based on the authors' experience with common learner corpora (like FCE and CoNLL) and may not generalize well to learner text from different L1 backgrounds or proficiency levels without modification.
3.  **The claim that automatic annotations are "qualitatively as good as human annotations" is an overstatement.** The evidence presented in Section 4.1 shows only that system *rankings* are preserved when replacing the gold reference with an automatically generated one. This does not mean the annotations are of equivalent quality. It is entirely possible that the automatic process introduces systematic errors that affect all systems similarly, thus preserving their relative ranking while being objectively worse than the manual gold standard. A direct comparison of the automatic annotations against the original gold annotations, analyzing the types and frequency of disagreements, would be required to support such a strong claim.

**Questions to Authors**
1.  The primary evaluation of your new rule-based classifier (Section 3.3) is a subjective rating task. Could you elaborate on why this was chosen over creating a small gold-annotated test set and reporting standard classification metrics (e.g., per-category F1-score)? The latter would provide a more objective measure of the classifier's accuracy and allow for a more grounded comparison against potential ML baselines.
2.  You argue that your approach is "dataset-independent." Have you performed any experiments to validate this claim? For instance, have you applied your tool to GEC corpora with significantly different characteristics from CoNLL/FCE (e.g., from learners with a non-European L1) to see if the classifier's performance, or the distribution of errors it finds, remains plausible?
3.  The conclusion drawn from Table 4—that automatic annotations are "qualitatively as good as human" ones—seems premature. Can you provide a more direct analysis of the differences between your automatically generated reference and the original CoNLL-2014 gold reference? For example, what percentage of edits match exactly? For those that don't, what are the most common sources of disagreement in terms of span boundaries and error labels?
