### Summary and Contributions

This paper presents a method and a publicly available tool for automatically annotating grammatical error correction (GEC) data with edit spans and error types. The process involves two main stages: 1) extracting edits using a pre-existing linguistically-enhanced alignment algorithm (Felice et al., 2016), and 2) classifying these edits using a novel, deterministic, rule-based framework that relies on automatic linguistic markup (POS tags, lemmas, etc.). The main goal is to enable fine-grained, error-type-specific evaluation of GEC systems, a capability lacking in current standard metrics. As a large-scale case study, the authors apply their tool to re-evaluate the system outputs from the CoNLL-2014 shared task, providing the first comprehensive breakdown of system performance by error type.

The primary contributions of this work are:
1.  **A Novel Rule-Based Error Annotation Tool:** The core contribution is the methodology and tool for automatically annotating GEC edits. Its rule-based, dataset-independent nature is a significant advantage over trained classifiers, offering transparency and removing the need for labeled training data for new annotation schemes.
2.  **A Comprehensive Re-analysis of the CoNLL-2014 Shared Task:** The paper provides a valuable and insightful analysis of the CoNLL-2014 systems. This goes beyond a simple proof-of-concept and delivers new knowledge to the GEC community, highlighting specific strengths and weaknesses of different GEC architectures (e.g., MT vs. classifier-based) on a wide range of error types.
3.  **A Methodological Proposal for Standardized GEC Evaluation:** The work implicitly and explicitly advocates for a new evaluation paradigm where both system hypotheses and gold references are processed with the same automatic annotation pipeline. This ensures a fair and direct comparison, resolving mismatches in edit granularity that have complicated GEC evaluation.

### Strengths

1.  **Addresses a Clear and Important Problem:** The GEC community has long recognized that a single F-score is insufficient for understanding system performance. The inability to robustly calculate precision, recall, and F-score for specific error types has been a major limitation, particularly since the influential CoNLL-2014 shared task. This paper provides a practical and well-motivated solution to this long-standing problem.

2.  **Sound and Transparent Methodology:** The choice of a rule-based classifier over a machine learning approach is well-justified. The authors correctly point out the limitations of ML classifiers, such as dataset bias and lack of transparency. Their deterministic approach, based on explicit linguistic features, ensures that results are reproducible and the reasons for any given classification are always traceable. This is a significant strength in the context of building a reliable evaluation tool.

3.  **Methodological Rigor in Evaluation:** The decision to re-process the gold reference data using the same automatic pipeline as the system hypotheses (Section 4.1) is crucial and demonstrates a deep understanding of the evaluation problem. This "auto vs. auto" comparison eliminates discrepancies in edit segmentation (e.g., one multi-token edit vs. two single-token edits) and ensures that systems are evaluated fairly on a consistent representation of errors. The validation in Table 4, showing minimal score changes, provides strong evidence for the validity of this approach.

4.  **Extensive and Insightful Analysis:** The detailed breakdown of the CoNLL-2014 results is excellent and provides numerous valuable insights. The analyses at different granularities—by operation (Table 5), general error type (Table 6), detailed error type (Section 4.4), multi-token edits (Table 8), and detection vs. correction (Figure 1)—collectively form a significant contribution. For example, identifying that MT-based systems excel at multi-token edits while classifier-based systems are strong on specific categories like `VERB:FORM` and `ORTH` provides actionable information for future work on hybrid GEC systems.

### Weaknesses

1.  **Limited Evaluation of the Classifier:** The evaluation of the rule-based classifier itself is a notable weakness. While the manual evaluation by 5 experts is a reasonable first step, it is small-scale (200 edits) and the results, while positive, are subjective. The paper dismisses comparison with previous ML classifiers as "incomparable," but this sidesteps a direct assessment of quality. A more convincing evaluation could have involved training an ML classifier on the paper's own 25-category typology using a manually-annotated dataset and comparing its performance to the rule-based system. Without a more quantitative evaluation against a gold standard, the true accuracy of the error typing remains an open question.

2.  **Overstated Criticism of the M2 Scorer:** The paper's critique of the M2 scorer (Section 4.1) feels somewhat one-sided. While it correctly identifies that M2 merges edits to maximize scores, framing this as "overestimation" and an "exploit" is a strong claim. The M2 scorer was designed with the explicit goal of being more flexible regarding edit granularity, acknowledging that multiple valid sets of edits can achieve the same correction. The proposed scorer, which relies on a strict, pre-determined tokenization of edits, is simpler but also more rigid and potentially penalizes legitimate correction variations. The paper would be stronger if it framed its scoring method as a stricter, alternative evaluation rather than an implicitly superior one.

3.  **Lack of Justification for the Error Typology:** The paper introduces a new 25-category error scheme but provides little justification for its specific design choices. Error typology design is a notoriously complex and subjective task. The authors do not discuss how their scheme relates to or improves upon other widely used typologies, such as the original 28-type scheme from the CoNLL-2013/14 tasks. A discussion of why certain categories were included, excluded, or merged (e.g., collapsing all modal verb errors into `VERB:TENSE`) would strengthen the paper's contribution by grounding the typology in linguistic or pedagogical principles.

4.  **Unexamined Reliance on Upstream Tools:** The entire pipeline's performance is critically dependent on the accuracy of its components: the aligner, POS tagger, lemmatizer, and parser. The authors acknowledge that POS tagging errors are a primary source of "Bad" classifications. However, the paper does not analyze the potential for cascading errors or the brittleness of the rules to systematic errors from these upstream tools (e.g., how a common POS tagging confusion impacts a specific rule). This is a practical limitation that could affect the tool's reliability on different domains or text types where the linguistic tools may perform less well.

### Questions to Authors

1.  Regarding the classifier evaluation: Could you provide more details on the qualitative nature of the "Bad" and "Acceptable" ratings? Were there any recurring patterns of error beyond incorrect POS tags? For instance, did your rules struggle with highly idiomatic or complex syntactic constructions?
2.  Regarding the error typology: Could you briefly justify the decision to create a new typology rather than adapting an existing one, such as the one used in the CoNLL-2014 shared task itself? What are the key advantages of your proposed scheme over the original one?
3.  The paper argues that its strict edit matching is an advantage over the M2 scorer's flexibility. However, could this rigidity not unfairly penalize systems that produce a valid correction through a different (but equally correct) set of edits? For example, correcting "he walk" to "he has walked" could be one edit (`walk -> has walked`) or two (`(insert has)`, `walk -> walked`). How does your system ensure consistency in such cases, and do you see any value in a more flexible matching strategy?
