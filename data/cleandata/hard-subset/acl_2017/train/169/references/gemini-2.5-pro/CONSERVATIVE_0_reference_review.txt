**Summary of the paper**
This paper presents a method for automatically annotating grammatical error correction data with error types, with the goal of enabling more fine-grained system evaluation. The proposed method consists of two steps: first, extracting edits using an existing linguistically-enhanced alignment algorithm, and second, classifying these edits using a novel, rule-based framework. This framework relies on automatically generated linguistic features (POS tags, lemmas, etc.) and is therefore dataset-independent. The authors use their tool to re-evaluate the systems from the CoNLL-2014 shared task, providing a detailed breakdown of performance by error type, which has not been possible before.

**Contributions**
1.  The primary contribution is the detailed, error-type-based analysis of the CoNLL-2014 shared task systems. This analysis provides valuable and novel insights into the comparative strengths and weaknesses of different GEC architectures on specific grammatical phenomena.
2.  A secondary contribution is the development and release of an open-source tool for automatically annotating GEC edits. This tool could serve as a useful utility for researchers wishing to perform similar error analyses on their own systems.

**Strengths**
1.  **Addresses a Clear and Important Problem:** The paper correctly identifies a significant gap in standard GEC evaluation. An overall F-score conceals crucial information about system performance on different error types. By providing a method for detailed error analysis, this work enables a much deeper understanding of GEC systems, which is valuable for the community.
2.  **Comprehensive and Insightful Analysis:** The application of the proposed tool to the CoNLL-2014 data is thorough. The analyses presented in Section 4—breaking down performance by operation type, general error categories, multi-token edits, and detection vs. correction—are genuinely illuminating. For instance, the finding that many systems completely fail to handle "Unnecessary" token errors is a significant and non-obvious result.
3.  **Pragmatic Methodological Choice:** The decision to re-process the gold reference data using the same automatic pipeline as the system hypotheses is a sound and pragmatic solution to ensure a fair, like-for-like comparison. This avoids penalizing systems for mismatches that arise from differences in annotation methodology rather than actual correction errors.

**Weaknesses**
1.  **Insufficient Validation of the Core Method:** The central technical contribution—the rule-based error classifier—lacks rigorous validation. The evaluation consists of a small-scale manual study where 5 researchers rated the "appropriateness" of 200 classifications. While the agreement and ratings are reported as high, this is not a substitute for a standard, quantitative evaluation against a gold-standard dataset. The authors argue no such dataset exists for their new scheme, but the standard approach in such cases is to create a small, manually annotated test set to report precision and recall for the proposed classifier. Without this, the reliability of the entire analysis rests on a subjective assessment of a very small sample of data, which is not sufficient to endorse this new method.
2.  **Overstated Claims Regarding Annotation Quality:** The paper claims that its "auto annotations are qualitatively as good as human annotations" based on the evidence in Table 4, which shows that overall system rankings are preserved when switching from the gold reference to the auto-generated reference. This is a significant overstatement. The preservation of system-level F-score correlation does not prove annotation-level quality. It is entirely possible that the automatic method introduces systematic errors (e.g., misclassifying certain error types, failing to identify complex edits) that affect all systems in a similar way, thus preserving their relative rankings while still being of lower quality than the manual gold standard. Discarding gold-standard human annotation is a major step that requires much stronger justification than provided.
3.  **Brittleness of the Rule-Based Approach:** The proposed classifier is entirely dependent on the quality of its input features, which are derived from off-the-shelf tools (spaCy, NLTK). The authors acknowledge that errors from the POS tagger can lead to misclassifications, but the potential impact of this is downplayed. Rule-based systems are notoriously brittle in the face of noisy or out-of-domain input. The paper lacks a sensitivity analysis or a detailed error analysis of the classifier itself. What are the most common failure modes? How frequently do upstream errors cause misclassifications? This methodological weakness makes it difficult to assess the robustness of the tool.
4.  **Limited Justification for the New Error Typology:** The paper introduces a new 25-category error scheme but provides little rationale for its design choices, beyond being a "compromise between informativeness and practicality." How does this scheme improve upon or relate to other, well-established error typologies in the Second Language Acquisition (SLA) or GEC fields? The classification seems primarily driven by what is easily achievable with POS tags, rather than being motivated by linguistic or pedagogical principles. The novelty of the scheme itself, therefore, appears limited.

**Questions to Authors**
1.  Your central claim about the quality of the automatic annotations rests on the preservation of system rankings. Could you provide a more direct comparison to demonstrate quality? For example, what is the agreement (e.g., edit-level F1 score) between your tool's annotations and the original gold CoNLL-2014 annotations for the reference sentences? While the error schemes differ, many edit spans and general categories should be comparable, and this would provide a clearer measure of what information is lost by discarding the manual annotations.
2.  Could you elaborate on why a small-scale, subjective rating study was chosen to evaluate the classifier over the more standard approach of creating a small gold-standard annotated set and reporting precision/recall? A quantitative evaluation would provide a much stronger and more convincing validation of your proposed method.
3.  The rule-based approach is highly dependent on the accuracy of the spaCy POS tagger. Have you measured how tagger errors propagate and affect the final error type distribution? For example, for the 3.7% of cases rated as "Bad" by your experts, what proportion was due to upstream tool errors versus flawed rules in your classifier?
