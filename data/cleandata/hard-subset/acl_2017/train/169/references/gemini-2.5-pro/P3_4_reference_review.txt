### **Summary and Contributions**

This paper presents a method and a tool for automatically annotating Grammatical Error Correction (GEC) system outputs with explicit edit spans and error types. The process involves two main steps: first, extracting edits between original and corrected sentences using an existing linguistically-enhanced alignment algorithm; second, classifying these edits using a novel, rule-based framework that relies on automatic linguistic markup (POS tags, lemmas, etc.). The primary contribution is the development of this transparent, dataset-agnostic annotation tool, which enables detailed, error-type-specific evaluation of GEC systems. As a secondary contribution, the authors apply this tool to conduct an extensive and insightful re-analysis of the CoNLL-2014 shared task, revealing system-specific strengths and weaknesses that were obscured by the original aggregate scores.

### **Strengths**

1.  **Addresses a Critical Need in GEC Evaluation:** The paper tackles a well-known and significant limitation in GEC evaluation. Standard metrics like M2 provide an overall score but offer no insight into *what kind* of errors a system is good at correcting. This work provides a practical solution to this problem, enabling researchers to perform diagnostic, fine-grained analysis. The commitment to release the tool significantly enhances its potential impact on the community.

2.  **Sound and Transparent Methodological Choices:** The design of the error classifier is a key strength. By opting for a rule-based, deterministic system that relies only on standard automatic markup, the authors create a tool that is highly interpretable and dataset-agnostic. This avoids the black-box nature and training-data dependency of machine learning classifiers, making the tool more generalizable. Furthermore, the decision to re-process the gold reference data using the same automatic pipeline (Section 4.1) is a crucial and methodologically sound step that ensures a fair and direct comparison between system hypotheses and the reference.

3.  **Extensive and Insightful Analysis:** The detailed re-evaluation of the CoNLL-2014 shared task is a major highlight and convincingly demonstrates the utility of the proposed tool. The paper provides a wealth of new information, breaking down performance by operation type (Missing/Unnecessary/Replacement), specific error categories (Table 6), multi-token edits, and the distinction between error detection and correction. The findings are insightful; for example, the observation that five teams' systems failed to correct any "Unnecessary" errors, or that MT-based systems excel at complex multi-token edits, are valuable contributions to the field's understanding of different GEC architectures.

4.  **Credible Evaluation of the Classifier:** Given that the proposed error schema is new, there is no pre-existing gold standard for evaluation. The authors address this pragmatically with a manual evaluation by five GEC researchers. The results, where over 96% of classifications were deemed "Good" or "Acceptable," along with a solid inter-annotator agreement (0.724 κ), provide strong evidence for the classifier's effectiveness and validate the rule-based approach.

### **Weaknesses**

1.  **Overstated Claim on Annotation Quality:** In Section 4.1, the authors claim that since their automatic processing of the reference data preserves system rankings, their "auto annotations are qualitatively as good as human annotations." This is a significant overstatement. The experiment demonstrates that the tool is *consistent* and that its biases affect all systems in a similar way, thus preserving *relative rankings*. It does not, however, prove that the absolute accuracy, nuance, or correctness of the automatic annotations matches that of a trained human annotator. A more measured claim about the tool's reliability for comparative evaluation would be more appropriate.

2.  **High Dependency on Upstream NLP Tools:** The entire framework's performance is fundamentally capped by the accuracy of its dependencies, particularly the spaCy POS tagger and parser. The authors acknowledge this, noting that most "Bad" classifications in their manual evaluation stemmed from POS tagging errors. However, the paper lacks a quantitative sensitivity analysis. It would be valuable to understand how error propagation from the tagger affects different error categories. For instance, are VERB:SVA classifications more brittle to tagging errors than, say, SPELL or ORTH classifications? This analysis is critical for understanding the tool's limitations.

3.  **Limited Novelty of Individual Components:** The main contribution of this work lies in the successful integration and application of its components, rather than in deep technical novelty. The edit extraction component is adopted directly from prior work (Felice et al., 2016). The use of rule-based systems for error classification is a well-established (though less common recently) paradigm. The error schema itself, while well-organized, is largely a systematic formalization of error types commonly discussed in GEC literature. The primary value is therefore in the engineering, the creation of a public tool, and the resulting analysis, rather than a new algorithmic breakthrough.

4.  **Small Scale of Manual Classifier Evaluation:** While the manual evaluation is a necessary and well-executed step, the sample size of 200 edits is relatively small, especially when distributed across a schema of 25 main error types. While the overall results are very positive, a larger-scale study would provide more statistical confidence in the classifier's performance across the full range of less frequent error types.

### **Questions to Authors**

1.  Regarding the claim in Section 4.1, could you please reconsider the statement that the auto-annotations are "qualitatively as good as human annotations"? Would you agree that a more precise claim is that the tool is sufficiently reliable for robust *comparative system evaluation*, as demonstrated by the stable rankings, without making claims about equivalence to human-level quality on an absolute scale?

2.  The discussion of the M2 scorer's tendency to maximize TPs by preferring multiple smaller edits over a single larger one is interesting. Does your tool offer any flexibility in handling such alignment divergences? For instance, could it report "equivalent" edits where a single hypothesis edit maps to multiple reference edits that produce the same final string?

3.  The performance of the entire system hinges on the quality of spaCy's models. Did you consider the impact of using different underlying NLP toolkits or models (e.g., Stanza, or different spaCy models trained on different domains) on the final classification results and system rankings? A brief discussion on the robustness of the findings to the choice of preprocessing tools would strengthen the paper.
