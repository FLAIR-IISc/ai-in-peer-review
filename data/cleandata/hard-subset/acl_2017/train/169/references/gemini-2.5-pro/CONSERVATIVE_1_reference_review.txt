### Summary and Contributions
This paper introduces a method for automatically annotating grammatical error correction data with explicit error types, aiming to facilitate more detailed, fine-grained system evaluation than is possible with existing metrics like M2. The proposed method consists of two stages: first, it uses an established, linguistically-enhanced alignment algorithm to extract edits, and second, it classifies these edits using a novel, rule-based framework that relies on automatic linguistic features (e.g., POS tags, lemmas). The authors demonstrate the utility of their tool by performing a comprehensive, error-type-based analysis of the systems submitted to the CoNLL-2014 shared task.

As I see them, the main contributions of this work are:
1.  **A detailed error-type analysis of the CoNLL-2014 shared task systems.** This is the first analysis of its kind and provides valuable insights into the specific strengths and weaknesses of different GEC architectures, which were previously obscured by single F-scores.
2.  **A publicly available tool for automatic error annotation and evaluation.** This tool has the potential to become a useful resource for the GEC community, enabling standardized and detailed analysis of new and existing systems.
3.  **A rule-based, dataset-agnostic error classification framework.** The framework is transparent and does not require labeled training data, which distinguishes it from machine learning-based approaches.

### Strengths
1.  **Addresses a Clear and Important Gap in GEC Evaluation.** The primary motivation for this work is strong. The GEC field has long relied on holistic metrics that offer little diagnostic value. By enabling evaluation at the level of specific error types (e.g., Verb Tense vs. Determiner errors), this work allows for a much deeper understanding of system performance. The analysis presented in Section 4 is compelling and reveals interesting patterns, such as certain systems excelling at specific error types while failing completely at others. This level of detail is crucial for guiding future research.
2.  **Release of a Practical Tool.** The authors' commitment to releasing their tool is a significant strength. A reusable, open-source tool for detailed error analysis will be a valuable asset to the community, promoting reproducibility and allowing researchers to easily benchmark their systems against others on a more granular level.
3.  **Methodological Transparency.** The choice of a rule-based system for error classification, while perhaps lacking in novelty, offers complete transparency. The deterministic nature of the classifier means that its decisions are always interpretable, which is a desirable property for an evaluation tool. This contrasts with machine learning models, whose decisions can be opaque and subject to biases from their training data.

### Weaknesses
1.  **Insufficient Validation of the Core Classification Method.** The central technical contribution—the rule-based error classifier—lacks rigorous empirical validation. The authors' evaluation consists of a small-scale manual study where 5 researchers rated the "appropriateness" of 200 automatically labeled edits. While the results (96% rated "Good" or "Acceptable") seem positive, this is not a substitute for evaluation against a gold-standard annotated corpus. The authors argue that no such corpus exists for their new schema, but this is a circular argument. A standard approach would have been to manually annotate a sufficiently large test set according to their proposed schema and report precision, recall, and F1-score for their classifier against this benchmark. The provided comparison to F1-scores of previous classifiers is acknowledged by the authors as "incomparable" and thus holds little weight.
2.  **Problematic Treatment of Gold-Standard Data.** The decision to discard the manual annotations in the CoNLL-2014 gold reference and replace them with automatically generated ones is highly questionable. The justification provided is that this ensures a fair comparison, and that overall system rankings do not change significantly. However, stable rankings do not prove annotation quality. This approach may simply introduce a consistent set of errors and biases across all systems, preserving their relative order while potentially distorting the absolute evaluation. The claim that this finding means the "auto annotations are qualitatively as good as human annotations" is a significant overstatement and is not supported by the evidence presented. A direct comparison and error analysis between the original gold reference edits and the auto-generated ones is conspicuously absent.
3.  **Limited Novelty of the Underlying Approach.** While the specific rule set is new, the fundamental approach of using rules based on linguistic features like POS tags for error classification is a well-established, traditional method. The paper positions this as an advantage (dataset-agnosticism, transparency), but it does not provide a strong argument or empirical comparison to demonstrate that this is superior to more modern, data-driven methods. For example, a simple baseline classifier trained on the linguistic features of the existing CoNLL-2014 training data could have provided a valuable point of comparison to substantiate the claims about the advantages of the rule-based approach. Without such a comparison, the contribution feels more engineering-focused than a methodological advance.

### Questions to Authors
1.  The justification for creating and evaluating against an "auto reference" hinges on the stability of system rankings. This is indirect evidence of quality. Could you provide a more direct analysis of the information lost when converting the human-annotated gold reference to your automatic format? Specifically, what is the agreement (e.g., F1-score) between the original gold edits and the edits generated by your tool on the reference sentences? This would provide a clearer measure of your tool's fidelity to human judgments.
2.  While I understand that a gold standard for your specific 25-category schema did not exist, why was a more robust evaluation not undertaken? For example, by manually annotating a larger sample (e.g., 1000+ edits) to allow for a more reliable, quantitative evaluation of your classifier's accuracy per category, rather than relying on a subjective "appropriateness" rating.
3.  The paper argues that machine learning classifiers are biased towards their training corpora. While true, this does not preclude their use as a powerful baseline. Did you consider training a standard multi-class classifier on the original CoNLL-2014 training data (using its error types) and comparing its performance on the test set against a version of your rule-based system mapped to the CoNLL error types? This would provide a more direct, in-domain comparison to an established methodology.
