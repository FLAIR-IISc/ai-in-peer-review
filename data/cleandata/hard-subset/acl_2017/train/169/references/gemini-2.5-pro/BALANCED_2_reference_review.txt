### Summary and Contributions

This paper addresses a significant gap in the evaluation of Grammatical Error Correction (GEC) systems: the lack of fine-grained, automatic analysis of performance across different error types. The authors propose a two-stage method to automatically annotate parallel (original and corrected) sentences. First, it uses a pre-existing linguistically-enhanced alignment algorithm to identify edit spans. Second, it classifies these edits using a novel, rule-based framework that relies on automatic linguistic features (POS tags, lemmas, etc.). The authors apply this method to provide the first detailed error analysis of all participating systems in the CoNLL-2014 shared task. The main contributions of this work, as I see them, are:

1.  **A comprehensive and insightful empirical analysis of CoNLL-2014 GEC systems.** This is the primary contribution. The detailed breakdown of system performance by error type (Tables 5, 6, 8) offers a wealth of novel insights into the relative strengths of different GEC architectures (e.g., MT vs. classifier-based). This analysis is a valuable resource for the GEC community, highlighting common failure points and suggesting promising avenues for future research and system combination.
2.  **A practical, dataset-agnostic tool for automated error annotation.** The paper introduces and validates a rule-based system for error typing. Its key advantages are transparency (rules are explicit), determinism, and independence from labeled training data, making it applicable to any parallel GEC corpus. The public release of this tool is a significant practical contribution that can facilitate more standardized and detailed evaluations in future GEC research.
3.  **A simplified and more transparent evaluation methodology.** By pre-annotating both system hypotheses and references with the same tool, the evaluation is simplified to a direct comparison of edits. The paper provides a compelling argument and empirical evidence (Table 4) that re-annotating the gold reference does not significantly alter system rankings, justifying this cleaner approach.

### Strengths

1.  **High-Impact Empirical Analysis:** The detailed evaluation of the CoNLL-2014 systems is a major strength. The paper moves beyond single F-scores to reveal a much richer and more nuanced picture of system performance. For instance, the finding that five teams failed to correct any "Unnecessary" token errors is both surprising and actionable. Similarly, the analysis of multi-token edits (Section 4.5) and the comparison of detection versus correction performance (Section 4.6) provide valuable diagnostics that were previously unavailable. This analysis will likely be widely cited and used by researchers to guide the development of future GEC systems.
2.  **Well-Motivated and Practical Tool:** The paper clearly articulates the need for fine-grained evaluation and presents a practical solution. The choice of a rule-based, dataset-agnostic classifier is well-justified, as it avoids the domain-specificity and opacity of machine learning models, which are critical features for a standardized evaluation tool. The public release of the tool greatly enhances the paper's contribution to the community.
3.  **Sound Methodological Choices:** The authors demonstrate sound experimental design. A key insight is the decision to re-process the gold-standard reference annotations with their automatic tool to ensure a fair comparison with the automatically annotated system outputs. This is a crucial step that is often overlooked. They rightly validate this choice by showing that overall system rankings remain stable (Table 4), lending credibility to their entire analysis pipeline.
4.  **Clarity and Presentation:** The paper is well-written, clearly structured, and easy to follow. The motivation is established effectively in the introduction, the methodology is described logically, and the results are presented in extensive, informative tables. This clarity makes the paper's complex analysis accessible and its conclusions compelling.

### Weaknesses

1.  **Limited Evaluation of the Error Classifier:** The primary technical contribution, the rule-based classifier, is evaluated on a small scale (200 random edits) with subjective human ratings ("Good", "Acceptable", "Bad"). While the reported inter-annotator agreement is good and the overall results are positive (>95% Good/Acceptable), this evaluation lacks depth. It is difficult to gauge the classifier's performance on rare but important error types, or to understand its systematic failure modes beyond blaming upstream tools like the POS tagger. The authors claim their approach is more effective than ML classifiers (which score 50-70 F1) but then state the results are incomparable. While true, this leaves the claim of superiority unsubstantiated. A more thorough qualitative analysis of failure cases or a contrastive evaluation would strengthen this aspect of the paper.
2.  **Unexplored Sensitivity to Upstream NLP Tools:** The proposed pipeline is a cascade of several automatic tools (aligner, POS tagger, lemmatizer, parser). Errors from any of these components will propagate and affect the final error type distribution. The paper acknowledges this possibility but does not investigate the robustness of the framework. How much would the final analysis (e.g., Table 6) change if a different POS tagger or parser were used? For a tool intended for standardized evaluation, understanding its sensitivity to these underlying dependencies is crucial for reproducibility and reliability.
3.  **Moderate Technical Novelty:** The paper's strength lies in its application and analysis, not in groundbreaking technical novelty. The edit extraction relies on prior work, and the concept of rule-based classification based on linguistic features is not new. While the engineering of these components into a new, coherent framework and error schema is a valuable contribution, the paper should be primarily judged on its empirical and practical impact rather than as a major methodological advance.
4.  **Limited Justification for the Error Schema:** The paper presents a new 25-category error schema as a "compromise between informativeness and practicality". However, the rationale behind specific design choices is sparse. For example, the decision to classify a modal verb error like `could -> should` as `VERB:TENSE` is mentioned but not deeply defended against alternatives. A more detailed comparison with pre-existing error schemas (such as the one used for the original CoNLL-2014 annotations) and a clearer articulation of the design principles would help readers understand the trade-offs made and the linguistic grounding of the proposed schema.

### Questions to Authors

1.  Regarding the classifier evaluation: Beyond POS tagging errors, were there other systematic failure modes for the rule-based classifier identified in your manual analysis of the "Bad" cases? For instance, did it struggle with highly ambiguous edits or complex multi-token edits? Understanding these limitations is important for potential users of your tool.
2.  Regarding the error schema: Could you elaborate on the differences between your proposed schema and the one used to manually annotate the original CoNLL-2014 data? What were the main motivations for your design choices (e.g., creating new categories, merging existing ones)?
3.  Regarding robustness: Have you performed any sensitivity analysis by swapping the underlying linguistic tools (e.g., using a different parser or POS tagger) to see how much the final error type distributions and system comparisons change? This would help in assessing the stability of the proposed evaluation framework.
