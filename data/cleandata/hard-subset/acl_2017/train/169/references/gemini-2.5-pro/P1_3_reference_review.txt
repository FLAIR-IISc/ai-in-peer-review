### Summary and Contributions

This paper introduces a methodology and a corresponding tool to automatically annotate Grammatical Error Correction (GEC) data with detailed error types. The process involves two steps: first, extracting edits between original and corrected sentences using an existing linguistically-enhanced alignment algorithm, and second, classifying these edits using a novel, rule-based framework that relies on automatic linguistic features like POS tags and lemmas. The primary value of this work is demonstrated through an extensive re-analysis of the CoNLL-2014 shared task, providing the first-ever detailed, F-score-based evaluation of system performance across a rich typology of 25 error categories.

In my view, the main contributions of this work are, in order of importance:
1.  **A comprehensive and insightful error analysis of the CoNLL-2014 shared task systems.** This analysis provides the GEC community with a new, fine-grained understanding of the strengths and weaknesses of different GEC architectures (e.g., MT, classifiers, rules) on specific grammatical phenomena. The findings presented in Section 4 are valuable for guiding future research in the field.
2.  **A practical, dataset-independent tool for GEC error annotation and evaluation.** The rule-based, transparent, and deterministic nature of the proposed classifier is a significant practical advantage over ML-based approaches that require domain-specific training. The planned release of this tool represents a valuable resource for standardizing error analysis in future GEC work.

### Strengths

1.  **Addresses a critical gap in GEC evaluation.** The paper effectively tackles the long-standing problem that aggregate GEC scores (like M2) obscure system-specific strengths and weaknesses. By enabling precision, recall, and F-score calculation for individual error types, this work facilitates a much deeper and more meaningful analysis of GEC system performance. The analyses presented are compelling; for instance, the finding that several top systems failed to correct any "Unnecessary" token errors (Table 5) or the clear distinction between which systems performed best on categories like `VERB:FORM` versus `WO` errors (Table 6) are novel and highly informative insights.
2.  **The proposed methodology is sound, transparent, and well-justified.** The choice of a rule-based classifier built on standard linguistic features is a pragmatic one. It avoids the black-box nature and dataset dependency of machine learning classifiers, and as the authors note, it is transparent and deterministic. The small-scale manual evaluation provides strong evidence for the classifier's quality, with over 96% of its outputs being rated as "Good" or "Acceptable" by human experts.
3.  **The paper provides an exemplary demonstration of the tool's utility.** The authors do not merely describe their tool but apply it to conduct a thorough and valuable scientific investigation. The re-evaluation of the CoNLL-2014 task is comprehensive, breaking down performance by operation type, general error categories, multi-token edits, and even the distinction between error detection and correction. This extensive analysis serves as a significant contribution in its own right.
4.  **The work promotes reproducibility and provides a resource for the community.** The authors' commitment to releasing the tool is a major strength. It will enable other researchers to perform similar fine-grained analyses on their own systems and datasets, fostering a more standardized and detailed evaluation culture in GEC. The proposed practice of reprocessing the gold reference to create an "auto reference" for fair comparison is a thoughtful methodological point that improves the rigor of the evaluation.

### Weaknesses

1.  **The evaluation of the automatic classifier is limited.** While the manual evaluation is a good starting point, its small scale (200 instances) and lack of granularity are limitations. The paper reports an overall "Bad" rating of 3.7% but provides no information on how these failures are distributed across the 25 error types. It is possible that the classifier is highly accurate on common types but fails consistently on rarer or more ambiguous ones. A more detailed breakdown of the manual evaluation results, or even a confusion matrix between the top-rated category and the system's choice, would substantially strengthen the validation of the core tool.
2.  **The potential impact of cascading errors from upstream tools is underexplored.** The entire pipeline is dependent on the performance of the alignment algorithm and the linguistic annotation tools (spaCy for POS tagging, parsing, etc.). The paper acknowledges that a POS tagging error can lead to a misclassification but does not analyze the sensitivity of the system to such errors. How would the results change if a different tagger or a newer version of spaCy were used? A discussion of this dependency or a sensitivity analysis would make the claims about the tool's robustness more convincing.
3.  **The rationale behind the error typology design is not fully explained.** The paper presents its 25 error categories as a "compromise between informativeness and practicality," which is a reasonable goal. However, the process by which this specific set of categories was developed is opaque. It is unclear how it relates to or improves upon previous error schemes (such as the one used in the original CoNLL-2014 annotation). A more detailed justification for the chosen categories and their granularity would be beneficial for readers to understand the linguistic assumptions underpinning the evaluation.
4.  **The critique of the M2 scorer could be nuanced.** The paper states that the M2 scorer "tends to overestimate performance" by merging false positives and maximizing true positives through its flexible matching algorithm. While these mechanics are correctly described, framing this as "overestimation" is a strong claim. The M2 scorer was designed with a different philosophy: to be lenient towards variations in annotation granularity and phrasing. The authors' simpler exact-match approach has clear benefits in terms of simplicity and transparency, but presenting the M2 scorer's design as a flaw, rather than a different set of trade-offs, could be seen as slightly uncharitable.

### Questions to Authors

1.  Regarding the manual evaluation of your classifier: Could you provide any further details on the distribution of the 200 edits across your error typology? For example, were there specific error categories where human raters were more likely to disagree with the system's classification (i.e., rating it "Acceptable" or "Bad")?
2.  Your method of creating an "auto reference" by re-annotating the gold data is a key step for ensuring a fair comparison. Does this re-annotation process systematically alter the distribution of error types in the reference set? For instance, is the automatic aligner more prone to merging or splitting edits compared to the original human annotators, and if so, how does this affect the prevalence of certain error types?
3.  The classifier relies on spaCy v1.6, which is now several years old. Have you investigated how using a more modern and potentially more accurate version of spaCy would impact the error classifications and the overall results of the CoNLL-2014 analysis? This speaks to the broader question of the tool's stability across different versions of its dependencies.
