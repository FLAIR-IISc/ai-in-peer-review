

# ACL Review

## Summary of the Paper
This paper proposes a method to automatically annotate grammatical error correction (GEC) outputs with error type information. The approach consists of two main steps: (1) extracting edits between original and corrected sentences using a linguistically-enhanced alignment algorithm, and (2) classifying these edits using a rule-based framework that depends only on automatically obtained linguistic features (POS tags, lemmas, etc.) rather than machine learning classifiers requiring labeled data. The authors demonstrate their approach on the CoNLL-2014 shared task data, providing a detailed error type analysis of all participating systems. They claim this approach addresses a fundamental gap in GEC evaluation, as previous metrics did not provide error type information.

## Main Contributions (in order of strength)
1. **A novel rule-based framework for error type classification that is dataset-independent and relies only on automatically obtained linguistic features**, avoiding the dataset dependency problem that plagues previous approaches. This is a bold and novel solution to a fundamental problem in GEC evaluation.
2. **A practical tool for standardizing GEC evaluation** that can be used to provide detailed error type analysis for any GEC system, addressing a clear gap in the field.
3. **A detailed error type analysis of all systems in the CoNLL-2014 shared task**, revealing significant insights about which systems excel at correcting different types of errors (e.g., some teams failed to correct unnecessary token errors at all, which is surprising given their expected ease).

## Strengths

1. **Novel and bold approach to a fundamental problem**: The paper directly addresses a clear gap in GEC evaluation methodology—the lack of standardized error type evaluation. Previous metrics like M2 scorer don't provide error type information, and other approaches require dataset-specific labeling. The authors' rule-based framework that depends only on automatically obtained linguistic features (POS tags, lemmas, etc.) is a bold solution that avoids the dataset dependency problem. This is the strongest contribution of the paper and represents a significant conceptual advance in the field.

2. **Practical utility and community impact**: The authors provide a concrete tool that can be used by the GEC community to standardize error type evaluation. This is not just theoretical—it's a practical contribution that could significantly impact how GEC systems are evaluated and compared in the future. The paper correctly identifies that "some systems may be more effective at correcting certain error types than others, yet this information is otherwise concealed in an overall score," and provides a clear solution to this problem.

3. **Valuable insights from detailed error type analysis**: The paper reveals important insights about GEC system performance across error types that were previously impossible to obtain. For example, it finds that 5 teams failed to correct unnecessary token errors at all (AMU, IPN, PKU, RAC, UFC), which is surprising given these errors might be expected to be easier to correct. The analysis shows that different approaches to GEC complement different error types, suggesting a path forward for combining approaches to improve overall performance.

4. **Reasonable validation of the approach**: The authors conducted a small-scale manual evaluation where 5 GEC researchers rated the appropriateness of predicted error types, with 86.3% of ratings being "Good" or "Acceptable." This provides reasonable evidence that the rule-based approach is effective, despite the lack of gold standard labels. The authors also show that their automatic annotations are qualitatively as good as human annotations through bootstrap significance testing.

5. **Addressing a clear gap in the field**: The paper directly addresses a fundamental gap in GEC evaluation methodology. The authors correctly identify that "none of these [new metrics and methodologies] are currently capable of producing individual error type scores," and their approach directly solves this problem. This is a significant contribution to the field.

## Weaknesses

1. **Lack of comprehensive evaluation**: While the authors provide a small-scale manual evaluation, they don't compare their rule-based approach with other methods in a more rigorous way. They state that "the best results using a classifier were between 50-70 F1" but don't provide a direct comparison with their rule-based approach. A more thorough evaluation would strengthen the claim that their approach is superior.

2. **Dependence on POS tagging quality**: The approach relies heavily on the quality of the POS tagging. As the authors note, when the POS tagger makes mistakes, it can lead to incorrect error type classification (e.g., [ring → rings] might be classified as NOUN:NUM or VERB:SVA depending on POS tags). This is a significant limitation that could affect the approach's effectiveness in practice, and the paper doesn't adequately address how this limitation might be mitigated.

3. **Limited scope of evaluation**: The evaluation is limited to the CoNLL-2014 data. While this is a significant dataset, it would be stronger to show the approach works on other datasets as well, to demonstrate its generalizability. The authors acknowledge that "the approach can be applied to any dataset" but don't provide evidence of this.

4. **Overstated novelty**: While the rule-based approach is novel, the paper could better acknowledge the similarities with previous work that used POS tags for error classification (e.g., Swanson and Yamangil, 2012). The authors' approach is a significant improvement, but the novelty might be overstated without a clearer comparison to prior work.

5. **Lack of comparison with existing error type frameworks**: The paper introduces a new error type framework with 25 error types, but it doesn't thoroughly compare it with existing frameworks (like the one used in the CoNLL-2014 shared task). This makes it hard to assess whether the new framework is actually better than existing ones.

## Questions to Authors

1. The paper states that "the M2 scorer in fact tends to overestimate performance." Have you analyzed why this is the case, and how your approach might avoid this issue? Could you provide more details about the specific cases where M2 overestimates?

2. How does your rule-based framework compare with previous approaches that used POS tags for error classification (e.g., Swanson and Yamangil, 2012)? What specific improvements does your approach offer over theirs?

3. The paper mentions that the approach depends on the quality of POS tagging. Have you analyzed how sensitive your error type classification is to POS tagging errors? How might this limitation be addressed in practice?

4. Could you provide more details about how your framework generalizes to datasets other than CoNLL-2014? Have you tested it on other GEC datasets?

5. The paper states that "it is always possible to add new categories for increasingly detailed error types." How do you envision the framework evolving to handle new error types as they are identified in future research?

## Overall Recommendation

**Accept**

This paper introduces a genuinely novel approach to a fundamental problem in GEC evaluation. The rule-based framework that avoids dataset dependency by relying solely on automatically obtained linguistic features is bold and addresses a clear gap in the field. While there are some methodological limitations (particularly the dependence on POS tagging quality and limited scope of evaluation), these are outweighed by the significant novelty and practical utility of the approach. The detailed error type analysis of the CoNLL-2014 shared task provides valuable insights that would be impossible to obtain with previous evaluation methods. As a reviewer who prioritizes novel ideas over methodological perfection, I believe this paper makes a significant contribution to the field and should be accepted.
