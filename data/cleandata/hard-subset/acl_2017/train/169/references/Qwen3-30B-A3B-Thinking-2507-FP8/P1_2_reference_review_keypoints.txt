- Proposes a dataset-agnostic, rule-based error typing framework for GEC that requires no labeled training data.  
- Presents an automatic two-stage annotation pipeline using linguistically-enhanced edit extraction and rule-based typing from automatic linguistic properties.  
- Demonstrates the pipeline by performing a detailed error-type analysis of CoNLL-2014 systems.  
- Fills an important gap in GEC evaluation by enabling per-error-type analysis of system strengths and weaknesses.  
- Uses a simple, reproducible, dataset-agnostic method relying only on automatic linguistic properties and plans to release the tool.  
- Provides empirical validation: a small human evaluation showing 86.3% of annotations rated "Good" or "Acceptable."  
- Produces actionable insights into system performance (e.g., CAMB excelled in 10 categories; PKU best on ADJ errors).  
- Highlights limitations of existing metrics by showing M2 scorer can overestimate performance through merged edits.  
- Limited direct comparison with prior machine-learning error-typing approaches, making relative performance unclear.  
- Human evaluation sample (200 examples) is small, limiting confidence in annotation reliability.  
- Paper lacks discussion on how error-type analysis would concretely drive GEC system improvements.  
- Claim that M2 overestimates performance is supported only by a single example rather than systematic evidence.  
- The error-type taxonomy may miss nuanced distinctions (e.g., treating modal choice as tense), indicating potential framework limitations.  
- How does the rule-based approach compare (F1) with previous ML methods, and how to standardize comparisons across schemes?  
- What is the impact of POS tagging errors on error-type classification, and which error types are most affected?  
- Can the authors provide systematic evidence that M2 overestimates performance across multiple examples?  
- Why did five teams fail to correct any unnecessary token errors, and does this reveal a fundamental limitation of current GEC systems?  
- How can this error-type analysis be integrated into GEC model development to improve systems beyond evaluation?  
- Recommendation: accept with minor revisions, addressing evaluation, comparisons, and discussion of practical impact.
