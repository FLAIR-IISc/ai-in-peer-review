

# Review of "Automatic Annotation and Evaluation of Error Types for Grammatical Error Correction"

## Summary
This paper proposes a method to automatically annotate grammatical error correction (GEC) data with explicit error type information. The authors argue that previous GEC evaluation methods lacked the ability to provide detailed error type analysis, as systems were evaluated only with overall scores. Their approach consists of two main components: 1) edit extraction using a linguistically-enhanced alignment algorithm (Felice et al., 2016), and 2) error typing using a rule-based framework that classifies edits based on automatically obtained properties like POS tags and lemmas. They validate their approach through a small-scale manual evaluation and apply it to analyze the CoNLL-2014 shared task data.

## Main Contributions
1. A rule-based framework for automatically annotating error types in GEC data without requiring labeled training data
2. A detailed analysis of error type performance across GEC systems in the CoNLL-2014 shared task, revealing system strengths and weaknesses
3. A practical tool for automatic error type annotation that can be used to standardize GEC evaluation

## Strengths
1. **Addressing a genuine community need**: The paper identifies a real gap in GEC evaluation - the inability to analyze system performance by error type. The authors demonstrate that their method enables this analysis, which could guide future system development by showing which error types each system handles well or poorly.

2. **Methodological transparency**: The rule-based approach is fully transparent and dataset-independent, which is a significant advantage over machine learning approaches that require different models for different datasets. This makes the framework easier to apply consistently across different GEC datasets.

3. **Practical demonstration of utility**: The paper successfully applies their method to the CoNLL-2014 data, revealing interesting patterns (e.g., CAMB excelled at 10 error types while CUUI was strong in others), which would not have been possible with previous evaluation methods. This demonstrates clear value for the GEC community.

4. **Validation through manual evaluation**: While they can't validate against gold standard labels (as they're creating a new framework), they conducted a small but reasonable manual evaluation with 5 researchers rating 200 edits, showing high agreement (86.3% "Good" or "Acceptable" ratings), which provides reasonable evidence for the framework's quality.

5. **Reproducibility**: The authors state they will release the tool with the paper, which is a significant contribution to the community and enhances the practical value of their work.

## Weaknesses
1. **Lack of rigorous comparison with existing methods**: The paper claims their rule-based approach is superior to previous machine learning approaches but provides insufficient evidence. They mention that previous classifiers achieved 50-70 F1, but their manual evaluation shows 86.3% "Good" or "Acceptable" ratings - these metrics are not directly comparable, and the paper doesn't provide a fair comparison showing how their approach outperforms existing methods.

2. **Limited validation of the error type framework**: The paper claims the framework is "dataset-independent" but only validates it on CoNLL-2014 and FCE-test data. There's no evidence showing how well it generalizes to other GEC datasets with different error distributions, which is crucial for claiming it's truly dataset-independent.

3. **Questionable novelty**: The edit extraction component relies heavily on Felice et al. (2016), which the authors cite as their basis. The error typing component is rule-based, which is not particularly novel in NLP. The main contribution appears to be the application of existing methods to GEC evaluation rather than the methods themselves, which is a significant limitation for a paper claiming to introduce a new approach.

4. **Insufficient evidence that the method solves the stated problem**: While they show their method can be applied to CoNLL-2014 data, they don't demonstrate that this leads to better insights than existing evaluation methods. The paper claims their approach "rectifies" the situation where error type analysis was impossible, but doesn't provide evidence that their annotations actually enable more useful analysis than previous approaches.

5. **Overstated claims about the M2 scorer**: The paper claims the M2 scorer "tends to overestimate performance," but this is a contentious claim without sufficient evidence. They provide a theoretical explanation but don't demonstrate this through empirical comparison, which weakens their argument.

## Questions to Authors
1. How does your rule-based error typing framework compare in accuracy to existing machine learning approaches for error type classification? You mention previous classifiers achieved 50-70 F1, but your manual evaluation shows 86.3% "Good" or "Acceptable" ratings - how do these metrics compare, and why can't you directly compare them?

2. The paper claims your error type framework is dataset-independent, but you only validated it on CoNLL-2014 and FCE-test data. Could you provide evidence that it would work equally well on other GEC datasets with different error distributions?

3. The paper states that your approach "is analogous to automating the annotation guidelines given to human annotators." Can you elaborate on how your rule-based approach specifically aligns with or differs from the annotation guidelines used in existing GEC datasets?

4. How does your method for error type annotation compare to the framework proposed by Ground Truth for Grammatical Error Correction (Napoles et al., 2015) in terms of practicality and usefulness for the GEC community?

5. The paper claims that your approach "makes evaluation much more straightforward" by treating edit extraction and evaluation as separate tasks. How does this compare to other recent evaluation frameworks like the one proposed by Felice and Briscoe (2015) in terms of practical benefits for researchers?

## Recommendation
I recommend rejection. While the paper addresses a genuine need in the GEC community and provides a practical tool for error type analysis, the contributions lack sufficient novelty and the evidence provided to support the claims is inadequate. The rule-based approach is not novel, the validation is limited in scope, and the paper makes overstated claims about the superiority of their method without rigorous comparison to existing approaches. The GEC community would benefit more from a paper with stronger evidence of novelty and more comprehensive validation.
