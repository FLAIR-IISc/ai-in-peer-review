

# ACL Review

## Summary of Paper
This paper proposes a method for automatically annotating grammatical error correction (GEC) data with error type information, addressing a gap in the literature where systems are evaluated using overall scores without detailed error type analysis. The authors present a two-step approach: (1) using a linguistically-enhanced alignment algorithm to extract edits between original and corrected sentences, and (2) classifying these edits using a new rule-based framework that depends only on automatically obtained properties like POS tags. They demonstrate their method by applying it to the CoNLL-2014 shared task data, enabling detailed error type evaluation for all participating teams.

## Main Contributions
1. A method for automatically annotating GEC data with error type information, enabling detailed error type analysis of system output
2. A rule-based error typing framework that is dataset-independent and requires no labeled training data
3. A comprehensive error type analysis of the CoNLL-2014 shared task systems, revealing different system strengths across error types

## Strengths

1. **Addresses a genuine gap in GEC evaluation**: The paper correctly identifies that previous GEC evaluations have lacked detailed error type analysis, making it difficult to understand system strengths and weaknesses. The authors provide a practical solution to this problem that could significantly benefit the field.

2. **Novel approach to error typing**: The rule-based framework that relies solely on automatically obtained properties (POS tags, lemmas, etc.) is a clean and elegant solution to the problem of dataset-dependent error typing. The claim that this approach is "analogous to automating the annotation guidelines" is well-justified and represents a significant contribution.

3. **Strong empirical validation**: The small-scale manual evaluation with 5 GEC researchers shows that the rule-based approach achieves high agreement (86.3% "Good" or "Acceptable" ratings), which is impressive given the noise from automatic edit extraction. The comparison with the M2 scorer is insightful and helps explain why the M2 scorer may produce inflated scores.

4. **Valuable analysis of CoNLL-2014 systems**: The detailed error type analysis of all participating teams provides important insights into system strengths and weaknesses across different error categories, which would not be possible with standard evaluation metrics.

## Weaknesses

1. **Critical missing material**: The paper references Table 7 ("Table 7 shows the breakdown of Determiner errors for two teams...") and Figure 1 ("Figure 1 hence shows how each team's score for detection differed..."), but neither are included in the submission. This is a severe issue as Table 7 is supposed to provide a "representative example of detailed error type performance" and Figure 1 is central to the "Detection vs. Correction" analysis. The absence of these key figures undermines the paper's credibility and the validity of the claims made in Sections 4.4 and 4.6.

2. **Numerous formatting and typographical errors**: The paper contains numerous inconsistencies in formatting, typos, and poorly formatted references that indicate a lack of attention to detail. Examples include:
   - "spaCy2 v1.6" (should be "spaCy v2.1.6")
   - "NLTK.3" (should be "NLTK" followed by a footnote reference)
   - "Universal Dependency tags.4" (should be "Universal Dependency tags" followed by footnote reference)
   - "dictionary5" (should be "dictionary" followed by footnote reference)
   - Inconsistent use of "CoNLL-2014" vs. "CoNLL2014"
   - Missing periods and inconsistent capitalization in references
   - "Damerau-Levenshtein1 algorithm" (should be "Damerau-Levenshtein algorithm")

3. **Overstated claims without sufficient evidence**: The paper claims "Our approach is analogous to automating the annotation guidelines given to human annotators" but provides no specific examples or detailed explanation of how this automation works. The claim that "it is always possible to determine precisely why an edit was assigned to a particular error category" lacks concrete examples or justification.

4. **Incomplete references section**: The references are poorly formatted with missing periods, inconsistent capitalization, and incorrect punctuation. For example: "Human evaluation of gram, Edward Gillian, Gillian.,? \Q2015\E, Gillian., " and "The amu system in the conll-2014 shared task: Grammatical error correction by dataintensive and feature-rich statistical machine translation, Marcin Junczys-Dowmunt, Roman Grundkiewicz., Proceedings of the Eighteenth Confer-, Junczys.Dowmunt and Grundkiewicz.,? 2014, Junczys.Dowmunt and Grundkiewicz., " These errors suggest the paper was not properly proofread.

5. **Limited evaluation of the rule-based classifier**: While the paper evaluates the classifier with 5 researchers on 200 examples, it lacks a detailed analysis of where the rules fail. The paper states that "whenever raters judged an edit as 'Bad', this could usually be traced back to a mistake made by the POS tagger" but doesn't provide examples of these cases or discuss how to improve the rule-based framework to handle them.

## Questions to Authors

1. Could you please provide the missing Table 7 and Figure 1, as they are critical to the claims made in Sections 4.4 and 4.6?

2. The paper claims the error typing framework is "entirely dataset independent," but the evaluation is limited to two datasets (FCE-test and CoNLL-2014). Can you provide examples of how the framework would handle datasets with different error types or annotation schemes?

3. The paper states that "it is always possible to determine precisely why an edit was assigned to a particular error category." Could you provide specific examples of this transparency in action, compared to machine learning approaches?

4. The paper claims that "none of these are currently capable of producing individual error type scores" (referring to existing GEC metrics). Could you provide a more thorough comparison with recent work that has attempted to address this issue (e.g., the work by Felice and Briscoe, 2015; Napoles et al., 2015)?

## Recommendation
Reject

## Justification
While the paper addresses a genuine gap in GEC evaluation and presents a methodologically sound approach to automatic error type annotation, the presence of critical missing material (Table 7 and Figure 1) and numerous formatting and typographical errors makes it impossible to properly evaluate the paper's claims. The missing figures undermine the paper's most significant contribution (the detailed error type analysis), and the formatting errors suggest a lack of attention to detail that is unacceptable for an ACL submission. As a perfectionist reviewer who meticulously examines every aspect of a paper, I cannot recommend acceptance when the paper contains such fundamental omissions and inconsistencies. The paper's technical merits are overshadowed by these substantial presentation flaws.
