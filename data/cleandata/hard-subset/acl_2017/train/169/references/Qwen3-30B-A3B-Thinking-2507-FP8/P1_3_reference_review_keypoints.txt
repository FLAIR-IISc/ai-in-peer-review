- Proposes a rule-based, dataset-agnostic framework to classify GEC error types using only automatic linguistic features.
- Introduces a method to automatically annotate GEC system outputs with error-type labels for detailed error analysis.
- Applies the annotation method to analyze CoNLL-2014 systems, revealing system-specific strengths and weaknesses.

- Novel evaluation methodology enabling error-type analysis of system outputs previously unavailable.
- Robust validation via human evaluation: >95% predictions rated Good/Acceptable and κ = 0.724, outperforming prior ML F1s.
- Demonstrates that the M2 scorer overestimates performance by favoring many small edits over single multi-token edits.
- Practical utility: authors will release the tool and show actionable insights from CoNLL-2014 analysis.

- Core methodology lacks novelty and heavily builds on Felice et al. (2016), making the contribution mainly an application.
- Claim of dataset independence is overstated since the 25 error-type framework is GEC-specific and may not generalize across annotation schemes.
- Lacks comprehensive comparisons or ablation studies against other rule-based approaches to isolate the contribution of specific rules.
- Does not sufficiently analyze how the insights could be used to improve GEC systems in practice.
- Limited discussion of the tradeoffs and limitations of the 25-type framework and some questionable categorizations (e.g., modal vs. tense).

- How were the specific 25 error types chosen, and were alternative, more comprehensive frameworks considered?
- Can the authors provide concrete CoNLL-2014 sentence examples where M2 scores are substantially higher than the proposed approach?
- How would adding new error categories be managed without disrupting consistency of existing classifications?

- Overall recommendation: valuable, well-validated contribution with limited novelty; recommend acceptance with minor revisions.
