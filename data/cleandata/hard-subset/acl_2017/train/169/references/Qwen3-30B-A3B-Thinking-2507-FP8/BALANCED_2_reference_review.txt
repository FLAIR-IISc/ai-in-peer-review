

# Review for ACL

## Summary of the Paper

This paper proposes a method to automatically annotate grammatical error correction (GEC) system outputs with error type information, addressing a significant gap in GEC evaluation. The authors argue that previous GEC evaluations (including the CoNLL-2014 shared task) have lacked detailed error type analysis because systems were not required to annotate their outputs with error types. Their approach consists of two main components: (1) linguistically-enhanced edit extraction using Felice et al.'s (2016) alignment algorithm, and (2) a rule-based error typing framework that classifies edits based solely on automatically obtained linguistic features (POS tags, lemmas, etc.) without requiring labeled training data. They release a tool implementing this approach and apply it to the CoNLL-2014 shared task data to analyze error type performance across different systems.

## Main Contributions (in order of strength)

1. **A rule-based error typing framework that is dataset-independent and relies only on automatically obtained linguistic features** - This is the most significant contribution as it enables standardized error type evaluation across different GEC systems and datasets without requiring manual annotation or labeled training data.

2. **A practical tool for automatic annotation of GEC system outputs** - The authors release a tool that makes their approach immediately applicable to the GEC community for detailed error analysis.

3. **A comprehensive error type analysis of CoNLL-2014 shared task systems** - The paper demonstrates the value of their approach by revealing system strengths and weaknesses at the error type level, providing new insights that were previously unavailable.

## Strengths

1. **Addresses a genuine gap in GEC evaluation**: The paper identifies a real limitation in current GEC research and evaluation practices that has persisted since the CoNLL-2014 shared task. The lack of error type analysis has prevented researchers from understanding which error types systems are good or bad at correcting, which is crucial for developing more effective GEC systems. The authors' solution is both practical and elegant.

2. **Strong empirical validation of the error typing framework**: The manual evaluation with 5 GEC researchers is well-designed and provides convincing evidence that the rule-based approach is reliable (86.3% of predictions rated "Good" or "Acceptable"). This is particularly impressive given that the evaluation was conducted despite the noise introduced by automatic edit extraction.

3. **Valuable insights from the CoNLL-2014 analysis**: The paper reveals several important findings, such as the surprising fact that 5 teams failed to correct any unnecessary token errors (which should theoretically be easier to correct than other types), and that multi-token error correction remains a significant challenge for current systems. These insights have direct implications for GEC research and development.

4. **Practical utility of the released tool**: The authors provide a working tool that can be immediately used by the GEC community to perform detailed error analysis on existing datasets and system outputs, significantly lowering the barrier to entry for this kind of analysis.

5. **Insight into M2 scorer limitations**: The paper provides a compelling explanation for why the M2 scorer tends to overestimate performance (by treating multiple edits as a single edit to maximize true positives), which is an important contribution to understanding existing evaluation metrics.

## Weaknesses

1. **Lack of direct comparison to previous error typing approaches**: While the authors correctly note that previous approaches used machine learning classifiers requiring dataset-specific training, they don't provide a direct comparison of their rule-based approach with these previous methods. A comparison (even if using different metrics) would strengthen the paper by demonstrating the relative advantages of their approach.

2. **Limited discussion of the framework's limitations**: The paper acknowledges that the framework cannot distinguish between very fine-grained error types (e.g., tense vs. modal errors), but doesn't thoroughly explore the full scope of limitations. For example, how does the framework handle errors requiring semantic understanding (e.g., "I have a book" vs. "I have books" - NOUN:NUM vs. NOUN:POSS)? A more comprehensive discussion of where the framework might fail would strengthen the paper.

3. **Small-scale manual evaluation**: While the 86.3% agreement rate is impressive, the manual evaluation only included 200 edits (100 from FCE-test and 100 from CoNLL-2014). Given the paper's claim about detailed error type analysis, a larger evaluation would better establish the framework's reliability across different error types and datasets.

4. **Insufficient explanation for observed performance patterns**: The paper identifies interesting patterns (e.g., why some teams failed at unnecessary token errors), but doesn't provide sufficient analysis of the underlying reasons. This limits the paper's utility for guiding future GEC system development.

5. **Limited discussion of practical impact**: While the paper demonstrates the framework's utility for analysis, it doesn't provide clear guidance on how GEC developers should use this error type analysis to improve their systems. The paper would benefit from more specific recommendations for system improvement based on the error type analysis.

## Questions to Authors

1. The authors claim their framework is dataset-independent, but how would it handle error types specific to certain datasets or language varieties (e.g., errors common in non-native English writing but not in other contexts)? Could the framework be extended to handle such domain-specific errors?

2. The paper shows that multi-token error correction is a significant challenge for current systems. How do the authors suggest GEC systems could be improved specifically for multi-token errors, based on the error type analysis they provide?

3. The authors note that "it is always possible to add new categories for increasingly detailed error types," but acknowledge this creates practical challenges. What is their recommended approach for balancing the trade-off between detail and practicality when defining error types for different GEC applications?

## Additional Comments

- The paper would benefit from a clearer explanation of how their error typing framework differs from previous approaches. While they mention that previous approaches required labeled training data, a more detailed comparison would strengthen the paper.

- The title "Automatic Annotation and Evaluation of Error Types" slightly overstates the paper's focus. The paper primarily focuses on the annotation aspect, with evaluation being the application of this annotation. Consider revising the title to better reflect this focus.

- The discussion of the relationship between error detection and correction (Section 4.6) is insightful but would be more valuable if it included specific recommendations for how GEC developers could use this information to improve their systems.

- The paper mentions that "few teams attempted to correct NOUN:POSS errors," but doesn't explore why this might be the case. Was it a technical limitation, or simply a lack of focus in the shared task?

## Overall Recommendation

This paper makes a valuable contribution to the GEC field by addressing a significant gap in evaluation methodology. The rule-based error typing framework is well-conceived, well-validated, and immediately applicable to the GEC community. The analysis of CoNLL-2014 systems provides genuinely new insights that could guide future research.

However, the paper would be significantly strengthened by addressing the weaknesses noted above, particularly by providing a more thorough comparison with previous error typing approaches, expanding the discussion of the framework's limitations, and offering more concrete guidance on how the error type analysis can be used to improve GEC systems.

I recommend **weak acceptance** with the understanding that the authors would address these weaknesses in the camera-ready version. The paper's contribution is valuable, but it could be even stronger with the suggested improvements.
