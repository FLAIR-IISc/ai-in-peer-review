

# Review of "Automatic Annotation and Evaluation of Error Types for Grammatical Error Correction"

## Summary of the Paper
This paper addresses a critical gap in Grammatical Error Correction (GEC) evaluation by proposing a method to automatically annotate error correction data with error type information. The authors note that previous shared tasks (like CoNLL-2014) evaluated systems using overall scores without detailed error type analysis, as systems weren't required to annotate their outputs with error types. Their approach consists of two main steps: (1) automatically extracting edits between original and corrected sentences using a linguistically-enhanced alignment algorithm, and (2) classifying these edits into error types using a rule-based framework that depends solely on automatically obtained properties (POS tags, lemmas, etc.) rather than labeled training data.

## Main Contributions
1. **A dataset-independent rule-based error typing framework** that relies only on automatically obtained linguistic properties, eliminating the need for labeled training data and avoiding dataset-specific classifier training.
2. **Demonstration of the value of detailed error type analysis** through application to the CoNLL-2014 shared task data, revealing nuanced system strengths and weaknesses across different error types.
3. **A practical tool for automatic annotation** that will be released with the paper, enabling the community to apply detailed error type evaluation to existing and future GEC datasets.

## Strengths

1. **Addresses a significant gap in GEC evaluation:** The paper correctly identifies a real problem in the field - the lack of detailed error type analysis in GEC evaluation. Previous work has focused on overall scores, but different systems may have different strengths in correcting different error types, and this information is valuable for system development. The authors properly contextualize this by referencing the CoNLL-2014 shared task, which was the first major GEC evaluation.

2. **Technical soundness of the error typing framework:** The rule-based approach is simple, transparent, and dataset-independent, which is a significant advantage over machine learning approaches that require different classifiers for different datasets. The authors provide convincing evidence for the quality of their error typing through a small-scale human evaluation where 86.3% of predicted error types were rated as "Good" or "Acceptable" by 5 GEC researchers.

3. **Practical demonstration of the method's value:** The authors successfully apply their method to the CoNLL-2014 shared task data, revealing nuanced insights about system performance across different error types that would be hidden in overall scores. This demonstrates the practical utility of their approach and provides meaningful analysis that wasn't previously possible.

4. **Clear explanation of advantages over existing evaluation methods:** The paper provides a clear and convincing explanation of how their approach differs from and improves upon existing methods like the M2 scorer. They demonstrate that M2 scorer tends to overestimate performance by merging edits, while their method provides more consistent and transparent results.

5. **Validation through multiple complementary analyses:** The authors validate their approach through multiple complementary analyses: a small-scale human evaluation of error types, application to a large existing dataset (CoNLL-2014), and comparison with existing evaluation metrics. This multi-faceted validation strengthens their claims about the quality and utility of their method.

## Weaknesses

1. **Limited novelty in edit extraction component:** The edit extraction method they use (Felice et al., 2016) is not novel - it's a previously published method. While the authors acknowledge this, the main novelty of the paper lies in the error typing framework, not the edit extraction. This is a limitation because edit extraction is an important part of the pipeline, and the paper doesn't offer any significant improvements to this component.

2. **Error typing framework has limitations in capturing certain error types:** The framework relies heavily on POS tags, which may not capture all error types well. For example, they mention that "Word Order" errors are hard to characterize with POS alone, and they have to handle them as a special case. This suggests the framework might not be comprehensive for all error types, particularly those that require semantic understanding.

3. **Lack of comparative analysis with alternative error typing approaches:** While the authors mention that ML-based approaches require dataset-specific classifiers, they don't provide a detailed comparison of their rule-based approach with these alternatives. A more thorough comparison would strengthen their claim of novelty and advantage.

4. **Small-scale human evaluation:** The human evaluation of the error types is based on only 200 randomly chosen edits rated by 5 experts. While the results are positive (86.3% "Good" or "Acceptable"), this is still a relatively small evaluation. A more extensive evaluation would provide stronger evidence for the quality of the error typing.

5. **Limited discussion of error handling when POS tagging fails:** The paper mentions that "Bad" ratings often correspond to POS tagging mistakes, but doesn't provide a systematic analysis of how often this happens or how to handle such cases in practice. This is important for understanding the robustness of their approach.

## Questions to Authors

1. The paper states that the error typing framework is "dataset independent," but it has 25 fixed error types. How would you handle a dataset with error types that don't fit into this framework? What would be the process for adding new error types while maintaining the framework's consistency?

2. In Table 3, the inter-annotator agreement is 0.724 κfree. How does this compare to typical inter-annotator agreement for error type annotation in GEC? Is this level of agreement considered good for this task?

3. The paper claims that M2 scorer tends to overestimate performance. Can you provide specific examples from the CoNLL-2014 data that demonstrate this overestimation, and explain how your method avoids it?

## Overall Assessment

This paper makes a valuable contribution to the GEC field by providing a practical method for detailed error type analysis that was previously impossible. The rule-based error typing framework is technically sound, dataset-independent, and validated through human evaluation. While the novelty is somewhat limited in the edit extraction component, the overall contribution is significant for the field, as it enables a more nuanced understanding of system performance that can guide future research. The paper is well-written, clearly argues the need for their approach, and demonstrates its practical value through application to a major shared task. The method is immediately applicable to the community, and the authors' intention to release the tool with the paper further enhances its impact.
