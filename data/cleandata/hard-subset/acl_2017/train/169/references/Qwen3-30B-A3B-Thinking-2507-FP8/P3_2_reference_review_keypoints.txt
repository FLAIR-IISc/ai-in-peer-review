- Main contribution: Proposes a dataset-agnostic, rule-based error-typing framework using only automatic linguistic features, plus a tool and analysis of CoNLL-2014 systems.  
- Contribution: Demonstrates framework on CoNLL-2014 and provides standardized error-type evaluation tool to be released.  
- Strength: Addresses a real gap by enabling per-error-type analysis rather than only overall GEC scores.  
- Strength: Method is practical and well-motivated, relying on POS/lemma features and showing 86.3% "Good" or "Acceptable" in a small manual check.  
- Strength: CoNLL-2014 analysis yields meaningful insights (e.g., some teams never fix unnecessary tokens; most systems struggle with multi-token edits).  
- Strength: Tool is practical and modular, allowing evaluation at different granularities (e.g., "R:NOUN").  
- Weakness: Lacks direct comparison to ML-based error-typing methods (e.g., Swanson & Yamangil 2012).  
- Weakness: Evaluation of typing quality is limited (200 edits), with potential vulnerabilities from upstream POS tagger errors.  
- Weakness: Overstates the CoNLL-2014 “mismatch” claim since the original task did not require error-type annotations.  
- Weakness: Unclear handling and alignment of multi-token edits, weakening interpretation of multi-token results.  
- Question: How does the rule-based framework compare to Swanson & Yamangil (2012) maximum entropy classifiers under the same evaluation?  
- Question: How are annotator edge cases (e.g., tense vs. modal) handled automatically by the rules?  
- Question: How does the error-typing framework relate to fluency evaluation, given the paper’s emphasis on fluency over mere grammaticality?  
- Overall assessment: Valuable, well-executed, dataset-agnostic contribution with practical tooling; recommended for acceptance with minor revisions addressing comparisons and evaluation gaps.
