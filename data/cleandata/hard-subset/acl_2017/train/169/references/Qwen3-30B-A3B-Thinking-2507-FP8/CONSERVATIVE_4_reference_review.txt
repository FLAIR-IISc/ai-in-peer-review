

# Review for ACL Submission

## Summary
This paper proposes a method for automatically annotating grammatical error correction (GEC) output with error type information, addressing a gap noted in the CoNLL-2014 shared task where systems were evaluated without error type annotations. The approach consists of two parts: 1) extracting edits using a linguistically-enhanced alignment algorithm (Felice et al., 2016), and 2) classifying these edits into a new rule-based error type framework based on automatically obtained linguistic properties like POS tags.

## Main Contributions (as I see them)
1. A new rule-based framework for error type classification that depends only on automatic linguistic markup rather than labeled training data
2. A tool for automatically annotating GEC output with error type information
3. A detailed error type analysis of the CoNLL-2014 shared task systems

## Strengths
1. The paper identifies a genuine gap in GEC evaluation methodology - the lack of detailed error type analysis for system output, which is important for understanding system strengths and weaknesses.
2. The tool they provide could be genuinely useful for the GEC community, particularly for researchers who want to analyze system performance beyond overall scores.
3. The small-scale manual evaluation shows reasonable agreement between their rule-based classification and human judgment (86.3% "Good" or "Acceptable" ratings), suggesting the framework is plausible.

## Weaknesses
1. **Lack of novelty**: The main contribution is a rule-based error type framework that is not substantially different from existing approaches. The CoNLL-2014 shared task already had an error type framework, and the paper fails to convincingly demonstrate why their framework is superior or necessary. Rule-based classification is a well-established technique, not a novel contribution.

2. **Misleading claims about existing methods**: The paper states "none of these [metrics] are currently capable of producing individual error type scores," which is inaccurate. The M2 scorer (Dahlmeier and Ng, 2012) and other metrics do provide error type information, just not in the exact format the authors propose. This overstates the novelty of their contribution.

3. **Inadequate evaluation**: The evaluation of their classifier is extremely limited - only 200 examples rated by 5 experts. This is insufficient to establish the quality of their framework, especially since they admit that many errors stem from POS tagger mistakes rather than their classification rules. The paper doesn't compare their framework to existing error type frameworks in terms of coverage, utility, or practicality.

4. **No evidence of practical impact**: The paper claims their approach "enables us to automatically annotate system hypothesis corrections with the same alignment and error type information as the reference," but fails to demonstrate how this leads to better GEC systems or improved evaluation. The analysis of CoNLL-2014 systems shows interesting patterns, but doesn't prove that their framework is superior to existing evaluation methods.

5. **Overstated significance**: The paper claims their method "rectifies this situation" and "enables a more detailed evaluation," but doesn't provide evidence that their approach is significantly better than existing methods for the practical goal of improving GEC systems. The paper's main contribution is a framework for analysis, not a method for improving correction performance.

## Questions to Authors
1. How does your error type framework differ in practical utility from the error type framework used in the CoNLL-2014 shared task? What specific limitations did the existing framework have that your framework addresses?

2. The paper claims that machine learning approaches are "biased towards their particular training corpora" and "may not be entirely compatible with each other." However, you don't provide any empirical evidence that your rule-based approach is actually more accurate or reliable than ML approaches for error type classification. Can you provide comparative results on a standard dataset?

3. You state that "all 5 raters individually considered at least 95% of our rule-based error types to be either 'Good' or 'Acceptable'." Given that the raters were aware that edit boundaries were determined automatically (which could be "unusual"), how do you account for potential bias in their ratings? Could the high agreement be partly due to raters not challenging the automatically extracted edits?

4. Your paper claims that the M2 scorer "tends to overestimate performance" because it merges edits to maximize true positives. However, the M2 scorer is widely used and accepted as the standard in GEC evaluation. How do you reconcile your claim with the fact that the M2 scorer has been validated against human judgments in multiple studies?

## Overall Recommendation
This paper presents a useful tool for GEC evaluation but overstates the novelty and significance of its contributions. The rule-based error type classification is not sufficiently novel, and the evaluation of the framework is inadequate. The paper would be significantly strengthened by a more rigorous comparison to existing error type frameworks, a more comprehensive evaluation of the classifier's accuracy, and clearer evidence of how this approach leads to better GEC systems rather than just more detailed analysis.

As a reviewer who prioritizes methodological soundness and established approaches, I find the paper's claims about novelty and impact unsubstantiated. While the tool itself may be useful, the paper doesn't provide sufficient evidence to justify its publication as a novel contribution to the field. I recommend rejection due to lack of sufficient novelty and inadequate evaluation of the proposed method.
