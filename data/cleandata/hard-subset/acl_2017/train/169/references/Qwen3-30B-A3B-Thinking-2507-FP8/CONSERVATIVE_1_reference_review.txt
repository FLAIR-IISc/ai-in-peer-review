

# ACL Review: "Automatic Annotation and Evaluation of Error Types for Grammatical Error Correction"

## Summary
The paper proposes a method for automatically annotating grammatical error correction (GEC) system outputs with error type information. The approach consists of two main components: (1) linguistically-enhanced alignment to extract edits between original and corrected sentences, and (2) a rule-based framework to classify those edits into error types based on automatically obtained properties (POS tags, lemmas, etc.). The authors validate their approach through a small manual evaluation (5 raters on 200 examples) and apply it to the CoNLL-2014 shared task data to provide detailed error type analysis for all participating teams.

## Main Contributions (as I see them)
1. **Practical tool for detailed error type analysis**: The paper provides a valuable tool for GEC researchers to analyze system performance in more detail than standard metrics allow, revealing system strengths and weaknesses across different error types.
2. **Rule-based error classification framework**: The authors propose a dataset-independent rule-based approach for error classification that relies only on automatically obtained properties, avoiding the need for labeled training data.
3. **Application to CoNLL-2014 data**: The paper demonstrates the utility of their method by providing the first detailed error type analysis for all teams in the CoNLL-2014 shared task.

## Strengths
1. **Practical utility**: The method provides genuinely useful insights into GEC system performance that aren't visible through standard metrics. For example, the paper reveals that CAMB excelled at many error types while CUUI was strong at specific error types like VERB:FORM, and that 5 teams failed to correct any unnecessary token errors. This level of detail is valuable for the GEC community to understand system capabilities.

2. **Methodological soundness of the rule-based approach**: The authors provide a clear rationale for why a rule-based approach is superior to machine learning approaches for error classification. They argue that ML approaches are dataset-dependent and require labeled training data, while their rule-based approach is dataset-independent and relies only on automatically obtained information. The manual evaluation showing 86.3% of error types were rated "Good" or "Acceptable" provides some evidence for the quality of their classification.

3. **Validation through application**: The paper successfully demonstrates the value of their approach by applying it to the CoNLL-2014 data, showing how their method reveals insights that aren't visible through standard metrics. The detailed error type analysis across teams is particularly compelling and adds value to the GEC community.

## Weaknesses
1. **Lack of rigorous validation**: The authors claim their rule-based approach is "dataset-independent," but they only validate it on a small scale (200 examples from two datasets: FCE-test and CoNLL-2014). This is insufficient to support the claim of dataset independence. A more thorough validation across multiple datasets with multiple annotators would be needed. The paper doesn't provide evidence that the approach would work well on newer GEC datasets (e.g., BEA-2019, JFLEG).

2. **Questionable novelty**: The paper builds on existing work for edit extraction (Felice et al., 2016) and the error type framework is largely based on prior work (Swanson and Yamangil, 2012). The authors claim novelty in the rule-based error classification, but this seems like a straightforward extension rather than a significant innovation. The paper doesn't clearly articulate how this is different from or better than existing approaches.

3. **Insufficient comparison with established baselines**: The paper claims that their approach "bypasses bias effects altogether" and is superior to machine learning approaches, but doesn't provide a proper comparison. They mention that ML approaches had 50-70 F1 scores, but this is incomparable because they used different metrics and error schemes. Without a fair comparison on standard datasets, the claim of superiority is unsubstantiated.

## Questions to Authors
1. The paper claims that "none of these are currently capable of producing individual error type scores." Can you provide evidence that no existing work has produced individual error type scores for GEC? For example, what about the work by Felice and Briscoe (2015) or others?

2. The authors claim their approach is "dataset-independent" but only tested on two datasets. What specific evidence do you have that your approach would work well on other GEC datasets (e.g., BEA-2019, JFLEG, etc.)?

3. The paper states that "all 5 raters individually considered at least 95% of our rule-based error types to be either 'Good' or 'Acceptable'." How would you handle cases where raters disagreed with the error type classification? The paper doesn't discuss inter-rater disagreement beyond the κfree score.

4. The paper claims that their approach "bypasses bias effects altogether," but this seems like an overstatement. How do you account for potential bias introduced by the POS tagger or other automatic tools used in your pipeline?

5. How would you modify your framework to handle newer GEC datasets like BEA-2019, which have different error type annotations? Would you need to create new rules for these datasets?

## Final Recommendation
The paper provides a useful tool for GEC researchers to analyze system performance in more detail, which is valuable for the community. However, the lack of rigorous validation, questionable novelty, and insufficient comparison with established baselines prevent me from recommending acceptance at this stage. The authors should provide more comprehensive validation across multiple datasets and a proper comparison with existing error type classification approaches before the paper can be considered for acceptance.
