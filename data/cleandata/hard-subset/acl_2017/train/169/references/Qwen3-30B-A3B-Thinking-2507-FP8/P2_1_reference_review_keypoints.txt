- Identifies a gap in GEC evaluation: inconsistent annotations prevent detailed error-type analysis.
- Proposes a two-step automatic annotation: linguistically-enhanced alignment followed by rule-based error-type classification using only automatic linguistic features.
- Main contributions: dataset-agnostic rule-based typing framework, a released annotation tool, and a detailed CoNLL-2014 systems analysis.
- Novel and practical solution addressing a long-standing pain point in GEC evaluation.
- Rigorous small-scale manual evaluation: 5 researchers rated 200 edits with >95% "Good" or "Acceptable" and 86.3% "Good".
- Application to CoNLL-2014 yields actionable insights (e.g., CAMB excels in many categories; several teams fail on unnecessary token errors).
- Clearly demonstrates advantages over existing metrics (e.g., M2 scorer overestimates by merging edits).
- Lacks direct quantitative comparison to prior ML-based error-typing approaches on the same datasets/metrics.
- Evaluation of generalizability is limited (only 200 edits from FCE-test and CoNLL-2014).
- Insufficient justification for the practical utility of very fine-grained categories (e.g., NOUN:POSS).
- No ablation studies to separate the impact of alignment vs. error-typing components.
- Requests: show performance on other datasets (BEA-2019, JFLEG) to support dataset-agnostic claims.
- Questions how the framework handles datasets with different error distributions and potentially irrelevant categories.
- Asks for elaboration on trade-offs between taxonomy comprehensiveness and implementation practicality.
- Seeks explanation for some systems' poor performance on seemingly simple error types (e.g., zero unnecessary-error corrections).
- Overall: valuable, practical contribution; recommends acceptance with minor revisions to address generalizability and taxonomy justification.
