- Summary: Identifies lack of automatic error-type annotation in GEC and proposes a two-step method: linguistically-enhanced edit extraction and a rule-based error-typing framework using automatic properties.

- Contribution 1: Dataset-agnostic, training-free method for detailed error-type evaluation in GEC.

- Contribution 2: Demonstrates method by analyzing CoNLL-2014 systems to reveal system strengths by error type.

- Contribution 3: Plans to release a tool to enable this analysis for the community.

- Strength 1: Addresses a genuine evaluation gap by enabling granular error-type analysis without requiring system annotations.

- Strength 2: Practical approach that is dataset-agnostic and does not need labeled training data, unlike ML-based methods.

- Strength 3: Strong manual validation with 5 GEC experts showing high agreement (86.3% "Good" or "Acceptable").

- Strength 4: Analysis yields meaningful insights (e.g., CAMB strong on Missing, CUUI on ORTH), demonstrating practical utility.

- Strength 5: Shows automatic processing of references produces comparable results to manual annotation (non-significant difference).

- Weakness 1: Lacks comparison to existing ML-based error-typing methods in terms of accuracy and practicality.

- Weakness 2: Does not adequately address ambiguous edits that could map to multiple error types (e.g., VERB:INFL vs. VERB:TENSE).

- Weakness 3: Limited analysis of how errors from POS taggers and lemmatizers propagate to error-type classification.

- Weakness 4: Evaluation is limited to CoNLL-2014; generalizability to other datasets (FCE, BEA) is not shown.

- Weakness 5: Limited discussion on concrete practical implications and how analyses can guide GEC system improvements.

- Question 1: How does the rule-based system resolve ambiguous multi-type edits—please provide examples and resolution strategy?

- Question 2: How are POS/tagging errors handled, and have you measured their impact on error-type classification, especially out-of-domain?

- Question 3: Can you provide results on another GEC dataset (e.g., FCE) to demonstrate generalizability?

- Overall assessment: Valuable, well-validated contribution that merits acceptance with minor revisions to address comparisons, ambiguity handling, and generalizability.
