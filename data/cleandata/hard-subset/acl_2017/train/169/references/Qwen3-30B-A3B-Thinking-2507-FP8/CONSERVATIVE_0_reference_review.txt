

# Review of "Automatic Annotation and Evaluation of Error Types for Grammatical Error Correction"

## Summary
This paper proposes a method for automatically annotating grammatical error correction (GEC) data with error type information. The approach consists of two main steps: 1) extracting edits between original and corrected sentences using a linguistically-enhanced alignment algorithm, and 2) classifying the extracted edits using a rule-based framework that relies solely on automatically obtained properties (POS tags, lemmas, etc.) rather than labeled training data. The authors apply their method to the CoNLL-2014 shared task data to enable detailed error type analysis of participating systems.

## Main Contributions (as I see them)
1. **Rule-based error typing framework** that relies only on automatically obtained properties (POS tags, lemmas) rather than requiring labeled training data.
2. **Demonstrated application** of this method to provide detailed error type analysis of systems in the CoNLL-2014 shared task.
3. **Tool release** that enables standardized error type evaluation for GEC systems.

## Strengths

1. **Clear identification of a genuine research gap**: The paper correctly identifies that previous GEC shared tasks (like CoNLL-2014) did not provide a mechanism for detailed error type analysis, limiting the ability to understand system strengths and weaknesses across different error categories. This is a valid problem worth addressing.

2. **Practical utility**: The method enables more detailed evaluation of GEC systems, which could help researchers identify specific areas where their systems excel or struggle. The ability to break down performance by error type (e.g., NOUN:NUM, VERB:SVA) provides valuable insights that aren't possible with standard metrics.

3. **Transparency of the rule-based approach**: The authors' claim that their method is entirely deterministic and transparent is a valid advantage over machine learning approaches, which can be black boxes. This transparency could be valuable for researchers trying to understand why a system makes specific corrections.

## Weaknesses

1. **Inadequate evidence for novelty and superiority**: The authors claim their rule-based approach is superior to machine learning methods, but they provide insufficient evidence to support this. They cite previous work that achieved "50-70 F1" but don't provide a direct comparison on the same dataset with the same evaluation metrics. The manual evaluation they conducted (95% "Good" or "Acceptable" ratings) doesn't demonstrate that their method is more accurate than human annotation or other approaches - it only shows that raters found the predicted error types reasonable. This is a critical weakness as it undermines the main contribution of the paper.

2. **Insufficient methodological validation**: The paper states there are no gold standard labels against which to evaluate classifier performance, but the CoNLL-2014 shared task did have gold standard error type annotations. Why didn't the authors use these to directly evaluate their method against human annotations? Instead, they conducted a small-scale manual evaluation of the predicted error types, which doesn't address the core question of how well their method matches human error type annotations. This is a fundamental flaw in their validation strategy.

3. **Overstated claims about the method's advantages**: The paper claims their approach "is analogous to automating the annotation guidelines given to human annotators" and "bypasses bias effects altogether." However, they provide no evidence for these claims. Human error type annotation relies on context, intended meaning, and other factors beyond POS tags that the authors' method doesn't capture. The paper doesn't quantify the "bias" they claim to bypass or demonstrate how their method is more consistent than human annotation.

4. **Lack of comprehensive evaluation**: The authors only evaluated their method on 200 randomly chosen edits from two datasets (FCE-test and CoNLL-2014). They don't provide error rates by error type, nor do they test the method across multiple datasets. Without this broader evaluation, it's impossible to assess the generalizability of their approach.

5. **Inconsistent claims about dataset independence**: The paper claims the approach is "entirely dataset independent," but they only tested it on two datasets (FCE-test and CoNLL-2014). Dataset independence is a strong claim that requires evaluation across multiple diverse datasets, which the authors haven't provided.

## Questions to Authors

1. The paper states that "several new metrics and methodologies for GEC have been proposed since the end of the CoNLL-2014 shared task" but none can produce individual error type scores. However, the CoNLL-2014 shared task itself included error type annotations. Why couldn't existing error type annotations be used directly for evaluation rather than developing a new method?

2. You claim your approach is "analogous to automating the annotation guidelines given to human annotators." How do you reconcile this with the fact that human annotators use more nuanced criteria than just POS tags (e.g., context, intended meaning), which your method doesn't capture? What specific annotation guidelines would you be automating?

3. You state that "all 5 raters individually considered at least 95% of our rule-based error types to be either 'Good' or 'Acceptable'." How does this compare to human inter-annotator agreement on the same error types? Without this comparison, it's impossible to determine whether your method is actually performing well or if the raters are simply being lenient.

4. The paper claims that "the M2 scorer in fact tends to overestimate performance." You provide a theoretical explanation for this, but you don't provide empirical evidence showing that M2 scores are indeed inflated relative to human judgment. How did you establish that the M2 scorer is overestimating performance?

5. You mention that "only 3 teams were successful at handling WO errors (CAMB, IITB and UMC)." However, the CoNLL-2014 shared task paper (Ng et al., 2014) already reported detailed error type performance for all teams. Why did this analysis not exist before your work, and how does your method provide a significant advancement over what was already available?

## Overall Recommendation

This paper identifies a genuine need for more detailed error type analysis in GEC evaluation. However, the proposed method lacks sufficient validation and the claims about its advantages are not well-supported by evidence. The authors have not provided a rigorous comparison with existing approaches, and their evaluation strategy is fundamentally flawed. While the tool they develop may be useful for researchers, the paper fails to convincingly demonstrate that their approach is superior to existing methods or even that it's a significant improvement over the status quo.

I recommend **rejection** due to insufficient evidence for the main claims of the paper. The method appears to be an incremental extension of prior work (notably Swanson and Yamangil, 2012) without providing compelling evidence of its advantages. The authors should provide a more rigorous comparison with existing methods, evaluate their approach on multiple datasets, and demonstrate how it outperforms human annotation rather than just showing that raters found the predicted error types "reasonable."
