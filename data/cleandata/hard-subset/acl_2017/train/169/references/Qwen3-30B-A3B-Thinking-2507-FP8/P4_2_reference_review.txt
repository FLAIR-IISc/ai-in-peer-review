

# Review of "Automatic Annotation and Evaluation of Error Types for Grammatical Error Correction"

## Summary and Main Contributions

This paper proposes a method for automatically annotating grammatical error correction (GEC) system outputs with error type information. The authors argue that previous GEC evaluation has focused on overall correction performance without detailed error type analysis, as systems were not required to annotate their output with error type information. Their approach consists of two main components: (1) automatically extracting edits between original and corrected sentences using a linguistically-enhanced alignment algorithm, and (2) classifying these edits according to a new rule-based error typing framework designed for error type evaluation.

Main contributions (as assessed objectively):

1. A rule-based error typing framework that is dataset-agnostic, relying only on automatically obtained linguistic properties (POS tags, lemmas, etc.) rather than requiring labeled training data for each dataset.

2. A method for automatically annotating GEC system outputs with error type information, enabling detailed error type analysis that was previously impossible.

3. A detailed error type analysis of the CoNLL-2014 shared task systems, revealing system-specific strengths and weaknesses across different error types.

## Strengths

1. The paper addresses a genuine gap in the GEC evaluation literature. Previous work has focused on overall correction performance without providing detailed error type analysis, which could inform system development. The authors successfully demonstrate how their method enables this detailed analysis, providing concrete examples of different systems' strengths (e.g., CAMB excelling at DET errors, CUUI at ORTH errors) that would not have been visible with standard metrics.

2. The proposed error typing framework is novel in its approach. Unlike machine learning-based approaches that require labeled data for each dataset, their rule-based framework is dataset-agnostic and relies solely on automatically obtained properties. The manual evaluation by 5 GEC researchers showed high agreement (86.3% "Good" or "Acceptable" ratings), which provides strong validation for the framework's quality and consistency.

3. The authors provide a thorough demonstration of their method's utility by applying it to the CoNLL-2014 shared task data, which constitutes the largest collection of publicly available GEC system output. This analysis reveals previously hidden insights about system performance across different error types, showing that different systems have complementary strengths (e.g., CAMB's hybrid MT approach for missing errors, CUUI's classifier for ORTH errors). This detailed analysis could guide future system development.

## Weaknesses

1. The paper fails to clearly establish how much this method improves upon existing evaluation approaches. While the authors claim previous metrics cannot provide individual error type scores, they don't provide a detailed comparison with existing methods or demonstrate how their approach differs in practice. The paper doesn't show how their framework's output would change the evaluation results compared to standard metrics.

2. The evaluation of the error typing framework is limited in scope. The manual evaluation by 5 researchers covers only 200 edits (100 from FCE-test and 100 from CoNLL-2014), which is a small sample size for comprehensive validation. The paper doesn't provide a detailed analysis of the "Bad" cases (3.7% overall), which would help understand the framework's limitations and error patterns.

3. The paper doesn't sufficiently address the limitations of their approach. It doesn't discuss how errors in the POS tagger (which is a prerequisite for their framework) propagate to error typing. For example, the paper mentions that when raters judged an edit as "Bad," this could usually be traced back to a mistake made by the POS tagger, but doesn't provide a systematic analysis of these cases. The paper also doesn't discuss how their framework handles ambiguous cases where the same edit could be classified in multiple ways.

## Questions to Authors

1. The paper states that "whenever raters judged an edit as 'Bad', this could usually be traced back to a mistake made by the POS tagger." Could you provide specific examples from your manual evaluation where this occurred, and how these cases would be handled in practice?

2. The authors claim their framework is "analogous to automating the annotation guidelines given to human annotators." How does your rule-based framework specifically differ from the annotation guidelines used in previous shared tasks like CoNLL-2014? What specific aspects of the annotation guidelines does your framework automate?

3. The paper states that "the M2 scorer in fact tends to overestimate performance" compared to your method. Could you provide more concrete evidence for this claim, perhaps with specific examples from the CoNLL-2014 data where the M2 scorer produced inflated scores?
