Summary
This paper addresses event detection (ED) on ACE 2005 by explicitly leveraging event argument annotations to guide an attention mechanism used in trigger classification. The model computes attention over a fixed-size sentence context based on the current token and combines word and entity-type attentions. Crucially, in training it supervises attention with “gold” attention derived from the gold arguments using two strategies: S1 (only argument words) and S2 (arguments and nearby context via a Gaussian). The ED classifier is a simple feed-forward network over the trigger embedding and attention-weighted context. Experiments on ACE 2005 show substantial gains over an unsupervised-attention baseline and competitive or better performance than strong published systems. The authors also augment training with FrameNet-derived events to alleviate sparsity, further improving F1.

Main contributions (as I see them)
1) A simple and effective way to inject argument supervision into ED via supervised attention, demonstrating that explicit argument signals improve trigger classification.
2) A systematic ablation isolating the effect of argument supervision (unsupervised attention vs entity-only attention vs gold attention), and a comparison of two supervision strategies (S1 vs S2).
3) Competitive state-of-the-art F1 on ACE 2005 for ED, and evidence that weak external supervision from FrameNet further helps.

Strengths
- Clear and well-motivated insight with empirical validation: The paper identifies a plausible reason joint models have limited impact on ED (imbalance and indirect use of arguments) and proposes a targeted remedy. The ablation in Section 4.2 is particularly convincing: ANN-Gold1/2 (oracle attentions) vastly outperform ANN and ANN-ENT, showing that arguments—not just entities—carry decisive cues for ED.
- Strong empirical gains with a simple model: On ACE 2005, ANN-S2 achieves 71.7 F1, outperforming Liu’s FN-based 70.7 and Nguyen’s joint 69.3, with higher precision than most baselines (Table 3). The architecture is straightforward (no complex structured inference), suggesting the contribution is accessible and reproducible.
- Supervised attention design is thoughtful and tested: Two complementary strategies (S1 more precise, S2 broader coverage) yield the expected precision/recall trade-off; S2 achieves the best F1 by boosting recall, consistent with the intended behavior.
- Sensible use of external data to address sparsity: Augmenting with FrameNet examples (after mapping frame elements to arguments and adding entity labels) increases F1 further (ANN-S2 from 71.7 to 71.9, Table 4), and improves the weak baseline ANN (64.6 to 66.2), reinforcing the paper’s diagnosis that data sparsity limits unsupervised attention.
- Ablations isolate the role of entities vs arguments: ANN-ENT (uniform attention over entities) is a nice control that shows entities alone are insufficient, strengthening the causal claim that argument supervision is what helps.

Weaknesses
- Fairness and supervision mismatch in comparisons: The proposed approach leverages gold argument annotations to supervise attention during training, whereas several compared pipeline baselines do not use argument annotations at all; some joint baselines do, but for different objectives. This raises the question whether the comparison is apples-to-apples in terms of supervision. A more direct comparison would include a strong neural baseline augmented with features derived from gold arguments (e.g., indicator embeddings for presence/role types) trained purely for ED.
- Evaluation protocol clarity and comparability: The paper states it follows the Li et al. (2013)/Chen et al. (2015) split (529/30/40) but does not list document IDs, and Table 3 mixes systems trained/evaluated under potentially different splits (e.g., Skip-CNN). Without uniform splits, it is hard to ascribe gains solely to the method. The statistical test is mentioned but lacks details (which comparisons, test unit, variance estimation).
- Under-specified implementation details: The context window size n used for attention is not stated; how sentences longer than n are trimmed is important for ED. The transformation from word to entity space (we) is described conceptually but lacks parameterization details. Training details like early stopping, number of epochs, and optimization hyperparameters beyond Adadelta are sparse, which hampers reproducibility.
- Limited analysis: The paper would benefit from error analysis and per-type results to verify the claim that arguments help disambiguate polysemous triggers (e.g., End-Position vs Attack). There is also no qualitative examination of learned attentions vs gold, nor sensitivity studies for λ, the Gaussian window size, or σ.
- Practicality and dependence on gold arguments/entities: The approach assumes gold arguments are available for supervising attention in training; in low-resource domains this may not hold. Additionally, the model uses entity-type embeddings as inputs—are these gold entities at test time? Many prior works vary in whether they use gold or predicted entities; the paper should clarify and align comparisons accordingly.

Questions to authors
- Supervision parity: Which baselines (especially the neural ones) use gold argument annotations during training? Can you include a baseline that feeds gold argument indicators (or role types) as features to the ED classifier without attention to control for supervision amount?
- Entities at test time: Do you use gold entity mentions/types or predicted ones for computing entity-type attention and inputs on dev/test? If predicted, with which system and what accuracy? If gold, how do you ensure fairness vs baselines?
- Data splits and significance: Please provide the exact doc lists for train/dev/test and confirm that all compared numbers use the same split. For the t-test, what is the unit of analysis (document-level F1, bootstrap over sentences?), and which comparisons are statistically significant at p ≤ 0.05?
- Hyperparameters and architecture: What context window size n is used? How sensitive are results to n, λ, and the Gaussian parameters (μ, σ, window)? What is the exact parameterization for computing we?
- FrameNet augmentation: How did you ensure no leakage between FrameNet-derived data and ACE test (e.g., duplicated sentences)? What is the noise level introduced by the RPI system’s entity labeling, and does using predicted vs gold entities for FN data change the effect?

Overall, the paper targets a relevant problem with a simple, effective idea and provides solid empirical evidence. Addressing the supervision fairness, clarifying evaluation details, and adding analysis would further strengthen the submission.
