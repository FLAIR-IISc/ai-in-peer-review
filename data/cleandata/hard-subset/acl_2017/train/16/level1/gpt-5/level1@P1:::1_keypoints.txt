- Paper summary: ED on ACE2005 using supervised attention guided by gold argument annotations (S1: arguments only; S2: arguments + nearby words via Gaussian) with a feed-forward trigger classifier.
- Contribution: Introduces supervised attention using gold arguments that yields consistent improvements and a new SOTA on ACE2005.
- Contribution: Empirically shows arguments provide signal beyond entity types via ablations (ANN vs ANN-ENT vs ANN-Gold) and two supervision strategies.
- Contribution: Diagnoses why prior joint trigger-argument models give limited ED gains (loss imbalance, indirect use of arguments) and proposes an alternative that avoids these issues.
- Strength: Clear empirical gains and competitive SOTA (ANN-S2 71.7 F1; +7.1 over unsupervised ANN), supporting the benefit of explicit argument supervision.
- Strength: Well-motivated supervision and informative ablations that isolate the effect of argument-informed attention (unsupervised attention ineffective; entity types insufficient).
- Strength: Simple, general, architecture-agnostic mechanism (MSE supervision on attention) with interpretable S1/S2 precision–recall trade-off.
- Strength: Notable precision improvements useful for downstream extraction (e.g., 78.0–81.4 P vs 73.2 baseline).
- Strength: FrameNet data augmentation yields consistent F1 gains and reduces supervised vs unsupervised attention gap, supporting data-sparsity claims.
- Weakness: Limited novelty—supervised attention is an established idea and the work adapts it straightforwardly; encoder is a simple MLP with fixed window.
- Weakness: Reliance on gold annotations in training and unclear whether gold entities/types are used at test; robustness to predicted entities is not shown.
- Weakness: Narrow context modeling—fixed, position-agnostic window with no sequential or syntactic modeling and no sensitivity analysis to window size.
- Weakness: Limited analysis—no quantitative test-time attention alignment to arguments, no per-event-type results, no qualitative visualizations, and unclear statistical significance protocol.
- Weakness: Baseline coverage/fairness concerns—missing stronger baselines (CNN/RNN with attention, joint/multi-task models with loss reweighting) to isolate the effect of supervised attention vs encoder capacity.
- Question: Do you use gold entity mentions/types at test time; please report results with predicted entities to assess robustness?
- Question: What is the context window size n and how sensitive are results to n; did you try position embeddings, dependency neighborhoods, or RNN/CNN encoders with the same supervision?
- Question: How well do learned attentions align to gold argument tokens at test time (average attention mass on arguments; top-k correctness)?
- Question: Can you compare to a joint or multi-task trigger–argument model with loss reweighting to directly test your diagnosis about joint training failures?
- Question: Please detail significance testing (number of runs, seeds, variance) and provide per-type precision/recall to show which event types benefit most.
