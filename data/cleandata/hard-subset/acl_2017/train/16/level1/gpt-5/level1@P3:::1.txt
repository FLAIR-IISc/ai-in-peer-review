Summary
This paper addresses event detection (ED) in ACE 2005 by explicitly leveraging annotated argument information through supervised attention. The model computes attention over a fixed-size context of words and entity types, combining word- and entity-based attention, and then learns to match this attention to “gold” attention derived from event arguments via two strategies: S1 (arguments only) and S2 (arguments plus nearby words via a Gaussian). The ED classifier is a simple feed-forward network over the attended context and the candidate token. Experiments on ACE 2005 report gains over prior work; ablations show that supervising attention with arguments helps, and further augmentation with FrameNet-induced events yields small additional gains.

Main contributions (as I see them)
1) Empirical contribution: a simple supervised-attention scheme that injects gold argument supervision into ED training yields measurable improvements on ACE 2005, surpassing strong published systems.
2) Methodological contribution: a lightweight mechanism to exploit argument information for ED without full joint inference, via attention supervision with two alternative gold-attention constructions (S1/S2).
3) Diagnostic analysis: comparisons against variants (unsupervised attention, entity-only attention, gold attention at train/test) to disentangle the effect of arguments vs entities.

Strengths
- Conceptual clarity and practicality: Turning argument annotations into a supervision signal for attention is a clean compromise between fully joint EE and pure ED. It is easy to implement, adds minimal overhead, and does not require argument information at test time.
- Solid empirical gains and strong precision: ANN-S2 reaches 71.7 F1 on ACE 2005, exceeding Liu et al. (2016a; 70.7) and Nguyen & Grishman (2016), with notably higher precision. The improvement over the unsupervised-attention baseline is substantial.
- Diagnostic ablations: The inclusion of ANN-ENT and ANN-Gold1/2 convincingly supports the central claim that argument supervision, not merely entity/type information, drives the gains.
- Sensible supervision strategies: Comparing S1 vs S2 gives insight into precision-recall trade-offs: argument-only supervision (S1) yields higher precision, while spreading attention around arguments (S2) improves recall. This aligns with the intended inductive bias.
- Data augmentation check: Repeating experiments with FrameNet-derived events shows the approach is compatible with external weak supervision and retains its advantage.

Weaknesses
- Modest novelty: Supervised attention is not new (e.g., Mi et al., 2016), and the technical contribution is incremental—deriving gold attention from known argument spans. The paper’s originality lies mainly in applying this idea to ED and in the specific S1/S2 instantiations.
- Weak neural baseline and incomplete baselines: The core ED model is a 3-layer ANN over bagged context; the unsupervised-attention baseline (64.6 F1) lags well behind published CNN/RNN baselines (~69 F1). To fairly assess the contribution, the method should be compared against strong sequence encoders (CNN/BiLSTM) with the same inputs and also reported as an add-on to them.
- Reliance on gold entity types and unclear comparability: The model uses entity type embeddings from annotated entities. It is unclear whether entities are gold at test time (as often in ACE trigger-only setups) or predicted; the FN augmentation uses predicted entities, creating a mismatch. Results with predicted entities (or without entity features) are important to assess robustness and fairness against prior work.
- Construction of attention supervision for negatives: For non-trigger candidates, S1/S2 resort to averaging over entities, which creates qualitatively different supervision between positive and negative instances. This may conflate attention sharpness with label polarity rather than teaching the model to find argument cues generally. An ablation that removes attention supervision for negatives or uses alternative neutral supervision is needed.
- Missing details and reproducibility concerns: The fixed context window length n is not reported; no analysis of sensitivity to n, λ, or Gaussian width; training and optimization hyperparameters are only partially specified; significance testing is claimed without test details; reference formatting is incomplete; and figures/footnotes are occasionally unclear. Code/data mapping for FN-to-ACE roles is only briefly sketched.

Questions to authors
- Do you use gold or predicted entity mentions/types at test time on ACE 2005? Please report results under both settings, and also an ablation without entity features to isolate the benefit of argument supervision from entity-type cues.
- What is the context window size n? How sensitive are results to n, λ, and the Gaussian window/σ in S2?
- How exactly is α* constructed for negative (NA) candidates? Have you tried training the attention loss only on positive triggers, or alternative neutral supervision for negatives? How does that affect performance?
- Can you compare against stronger neural baselines (e.g., CNN or BiLSTM encoders) with and without supervised attention? Does your supervision still yield gains on top of those?
- Please provide per-type F1, especially for ambiguous triggers (e.g., fire) where argument cues should help most, and attention visualizations to substantiate the interpretability claim.
- For the FN augmentation, what is the final size of additional training instances, and how are FN frames mapped to ACE types? Any filtering heuristics beyond core FEs?

Additional comments
- The analysis that joint models underperform on ED due to argument-trigger imbalance is plausible but speculative; consider an experiment that reweights losses in a joint model to verify the hypothesis.
- Clarify the t-test setup (test statistic, paired samples, number of runs).
- Paper quality could be improved by fixing typos/footnote formatting and providing implementation details; releasing code would aid reproducibility.

Overall, the paper presents a simple and effective idea with promising results, but would benefit from stronger baselines, clearer evaluation settings around entity usage, and deeper analysis of the supervision signal.
