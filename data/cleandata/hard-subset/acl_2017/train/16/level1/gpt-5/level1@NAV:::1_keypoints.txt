- Summary: Proposes supervised attention to focus ED trigger classifiers on gold argument tokens and nearby context, evaluated on ACE 2005 with analyses of unsupervised vs supervised attention and two supervision strategies.

- Contribution 1: Empirically shows argument-aware supervised attention yields consistent, statistically significant ED gains (notably precision) over prior systems on ACE 2005.

- Contribution 2: Ablations demonstrate that arguments (not entities) drive the gains and that supervised attention mitigates weaknesses of unsupervised attention under data sparsity.

- Contribution 3: Systematically compares attention supervision strategies (arguments-only vs arguments+neighborhood) and includes modest robustness checks via FrameNet-based augmentation.

- Strength 1: Strong empirical results — ANN-S2 achieves best-reported F1 on ACE 2005 with statistically significant improvements.

- Strength 2: Convincing ablation comparisons (ANN vs ANN-ENT vs ANN-Gold1/2) validate the central hypothesis about argument supervision.

- Strength 3: The supervised-attention mechanism is simple, general, and could be plugged into various ED architectures.

- Strength 4: Provides sensible analysis attributing joint models’ limitations to data imbalance and indirect argument use.

- Weakness 1: Baseline classifier is a simple MLP without sequence modeling; unclear if gains hold versus modern contextual encoders (CNN/RNN/biLSTM).

- Weakness 2: Training relies on gold argument spans and entity-type embeddings; unclear whether test-time uses gold or predicted entities, affecting realism.

- Weakness 3: Supervision design choices (Gaussian window, hyperparameters) are heuristic with no sensitivity analysis or qualitative attention inspection.

- Weakness 4: Limited domain robustness — FrameNet augmentation yields small improvements and no out-of-domain or noisy-supervision evaluation.

- Question 1: Do you use gold or predicted entity mentions/types at test time on ACE; please report both settings?

- Question 2: What is the fixed context window length n, how sensitive are results to n and λ, and is there variance across random seeds?

- Question 3: How does performance vary by event subtype — are gains concentrated on ambiguous trigger lexemes?

- Question 4: Can you supervise attention with automatically predicted arguments to assess robustness to noisy supervision?
