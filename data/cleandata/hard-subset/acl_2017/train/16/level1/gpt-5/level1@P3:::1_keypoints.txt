- Leverages annotated argument information via supervised attention for event detection on ACE 2005.  
- Computes attention over a fixed context of words and entity-type embeddings, combining word- and entity-based attention.  
- Supervises attention to match “gold” attention from event arguments using two strategies: S1 (arguments only) and S2 (arguments + Gaussian).  
- ED classifier is a simple feed-forward network over the attended context and the candidate token.  
- Reports empirical gains on ACE 2005 and shows that supervising attention with arguments improves performance.  
- Augmenting training with FrameNet-induced events yields small additional gains.  

- Empirical contribution: simple supervised-attention scheme that yields measurable improvements, surpassing strong published systems.  
- Methodological contribution: lightweight way to exploit argument information without full joint inference via attention supervision (S1/S2).  
- Diagnostic contribution: ablations (unsupervised, entity-only, gold attention) disentangle effects of arguments vs entities.  

- Strength: conceptually clear, practical, easy to implement, and does not require argument info at test time.  
- Strength: solid empirical gains and high precision (ANN-S2 reaches 71.7 F1, surpassing some prior work).  
- Strength: diagnostic ablations convincingly show argument supervision drives gains, not just entity types.  
- Strength: S1 vs S2 analysis clarifies precision-recall trade-offs (S1 → higher precision, S2 → higher recall).  
- Strength: compatible with external weak supervision (FrameNet augmentation) and retains advantage.  

- Weakness: limited novelty; supervised attention idea is incremental and similar to prior work (e.g., Mi et al., 2016).  
- Weakness: weak neural baseline and incomplete comparisons—unsupervised-attention baseline lags behind CNN/RNN baselines (~64.6 vs ~69 F1).  
- Weakness: unclear whether entity mentions/types are gold or predicted at test time; mixing gold/predicted across experiments hurts comparability.  
- Weakness: attention supervision for negative (NA) candidates averages over entities, potentially conflating attention sharpness with label polarity.  
- Weakness: missing experimental details (context window n, sensitivity to n/λ/σ, full hyperparameters, t-test setup) and reproducibility concerns.  
- Weakness: brief/unclear description of FN-to-ACE role mapping and augmentation filtering heuristics.  

- Question: Are entity mentions/types gold or predicted at test time? please report both and an ablation without entity features.  
- Question: What is the context window size n, and how sensitive are results to n, λ, and S2’s Gaussian width σ?  
- Question: How is α* constructed for negative (NA) candidates? Have you tried training attention loss only on positives or using neutral supervision for negatives?  
- Question: Can you compare against stronger encoders (CNN/BiLSTM) with and without supervised attention to show additive gains?  
- Question: Please provide per-type F1 (especially ambiguous triggers) and attention visualizations to support interpretability claims.  
- Question: For FrameNet augmentation, what is the final number of additional instances and how are FN frames mapped to ACE types? Any extra filtering?  

- Additional: The claim that joint models underperform due to argument-trigger imbalance is speculative; consider experiments reweighting joint losses.  
- Additional: Clarify t-test details (statistic, pairing, number of runs) and improve paper quality (fix typos/formatting, release code).
