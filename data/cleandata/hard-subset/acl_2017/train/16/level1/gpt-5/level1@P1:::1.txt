Summary and claimed contributions
- The paper addresses event detection (ED) on ACE 2005 by explicitly leveraging annotated event arguments through supervised attention. A simple feed-forward classifier scores each token as a trigger; contextual representations are obtained via attention over nearby words and entity types. During training, the attention mechanism is guided by “gold attention” vectors derived from argument annotations with two strategies: S1 (only arguments) and S2 (arguments plus neighboring words via a Gaussian).
- As I see it, the main contributions are:
  1) A supervised attention scheme that uses gold argument annotations to bias ED models to focus on informative context, yielding consistent improvements and new SOTA on ACE 2005.
  2) Empirical insight that arguments matter for ED beyond entity types, demonstrated via ablations (ANN vs ANN-ENT vs ANN-Gold) and via two alternative supervision strategies (S1, S2).
  3) A practical diagnosis of why prior joint models have limited ED gains (loss imbalance and indirect use of arguments for trigger pre-prediction), and an alternative that side-steps these issues.

Strengths
- Clear empirical gains and competitive SOTA. ANN-S2 achieves 71.7 F1 on ACE 2005, modestly exceeding the strongest prior reported number (Skip-CNN, 71.3). The gains over a comparable neural baseline without supervised attention (ANN) are substantial (+7.1 F1), supporting the central claim that explicit argument supervision helps ED.
- Well-motivated supervision and informative ablations. The comparison among ANN (unsupervised attention), ANN-ENT (uniform attention over entities), and ANN-Gold1/2 (gold attention at train/test) convincingly shows: (i) unsupervised attention is ineffective under data sparsity; (ii) entity-type signals alone are insufficient; and (iii) argument-informed attention is crucial. This isolates the contribution of argument supervision cleanly.
- Simple, general mechanism. The method is architecture-agnostic: supervising an attention vector via an extra MSE loss is easy to implement and could be plugged into stronger encoders (CNN/RNN), suggesting broader applicability beyond the particular MLP used here. The two supervision strategies (S1 and S2) offer a precision–recall trade-off that is intuitively interpretable.
- Precision improvements. The method notably improves precision (e.g., 78.0–81.4 P vs 73.2 baseline), which is valuable for downstream extraction pipelines where false triggers are costly.
- Data augmentation study. Augmenting with FrameNet events yields consistent (if small) F1 gains for all variants and supports the paper’s claim that attention learning suffers from ED data sparsity; additional data narrows the gap between supervised and unsupervised attention.

Weaknesses
- Limited novelty relative to supervised attention literature and ED baselines. While applying supervised attention to ED is new to my knowledge, supervising attention with gold signals (e.g., Mi et al., 2016 for NMT) is established. The approach is a straightforward adaptation. Moreover, the encoder is a simple MLP with a fixed window; comparisons to stronger neural encoders with attention (e.g., CNN/LSTM with attention but without gold supervision) are missing, making it hard to attribute gains to supervision vs encoder capacity.
- Reliance on gold annotations during training and potentially at test time. The attention supervision requires gold argument spans in training, which are not available in realistic settings. More critically, the model uses entity-type embeddings for contextual entities; it is not clearly stated whether these are gold entities at test time (as is common in ACE ED) or predicted. If gold entities are used at test, reported results overstate practical performance; robustness to predicted entities is unknown.
- Narrow context modeling. The attention is computed over a fixed-length, position-agnostic context window, without sequential modeling or syntactic structure. Many triggers rely on long-distance or dependency-mediated cues; prior work (CNNs, RNNs, dependency features) exploits this. The method may underperform when arguments are distant or outside the window. No sensitivity analysis on window size n, nor experiments with dependency-based context, are provided.
- Limited analysis and diagnostics. The paper does not quantify how well learned attentions align to arguments at test time (e.g., attention mass on true arguments), lacks per-event-type results or error analysis, and provides no qualitative attention visualizations. The improvement over the strongest prior system is small (0.4 F1), yet the significance testing setup (test repetitions, random seeds) is not described. The FN augmentation pipeline uses an external entity tagger; potential label noise or leakage isn’t analyzed.
- Baseline coverage and fairness. The unsupervised attention baseline (ANN) underperforms substantially; stronger baselines such as a CNN/RNN with attention but no supervision, or a multi-task model that jointly predicts triggers and arguments with rebalanced losses (addressing the authors’ critique of joint training), are absent. These would more directly test the core hypothesis.

Questions to authors
- Do you use gold entity mentions/types at test time? If yes, please report results with predicted entities (e.g., from a standard ACE NER) to assess robustness and practical utility.
- What is the context window size n, and how sensitive are results to n? Did you try position embeddings, dependency-based neighborhoods, or bidirectional RNN/CNN encoders with the same supervised attention?
- How closely do learned attentions align to argument tokens at test time (without using gold arguments)? Please report average attention mass on gold arguments and the proportion of cases where a top-k attended token is an argument.
- Can you compare to a joint or multi-task trigger-argument model with loss reweighting to mitigate the imbalance you identify? This would directly test your diagnosis of why prior joint models underperform on ED.
- Please detail your significance testing protocol (number of runs, random seeds, variance). Also provide per-type precision/recall to understand which event types benefit most.
