Summary and contributions:
The paper proposes using supervised attention to explicitly leverage gold event arguments when training an event detection (ED) model. The key idea is to guide attention to argument tokens (and nearby context) so that the trigger classifier focuses on informative regions, addressing the observation that joint ED/AE methods tend to help AE much more than ED. The method is evaluated on ACE 2005, with analyses contrasting unsupervised vs supervised attention and two supervision strategies.

Contributions as I see them (in order of strength):
1) Empirical demonstration that argument-aware, supervised attention yields consistent and statistically significant ED gains over strong prior systems on ACE 2005, with clear precision improvements.
2) A careful ablation establishing that arguments (not entities per se) drive the gains, and that supervised attention overcomes the weakness of unsupervised attention under data sparsity.
3) A systematic comparison of attention supervision strategies (arguments-only vs arguments-plus-neighborhood), plus a modest robustness check via FrameNet-based data augmentation.

Strengths:
- Strong empirical gains: ANN-S2 achieves the best reported F1 on ACE 2005, and the precision improvements are substantial. The t-test (p ≤ 0.05) strengthens the claim.
- Convincing ablations: The ANN vs ANN-ENT vs ANN-Gold1/2 comparisons directly validate the central hypothesis that argument supervision is critical and that entities alone are insufficient.
- Simple, general mechanism: Supervised attention is conceptually straightforward and could be plugged into diverse ED architectures beyond the presented MLP.
- Sensible analysis of joint models’ limitations: The paper pinpoints data imbalance and indirect use of arguments in prior joint approaches, motivating the proposed direction.

Weaknesses:
- Baseline strength: The core classifier is a simple MLP without sequence modeling; comparisons to more recent CNN/RNN/biLSTM baselines (beyond those cited) could be stronger. It is unclear if the improvement would hold against a carefully tuned contextual encoder with modern features.
- Reliance on gold annotations: Training requires gold argument spans, and the model also uses entity type embeddings; it is not clearly stated whether test-time uses gold or predicted entities on ACE, which affects realism.
- Limited analysis of supervision design: S1/S2 choices (Gaussian window, hyperparameters) are heuristic; there is no sensitivity analysis or qualitative attention inspection to support interpretability claims.
- Generalization/domain robustness: Improvements with noisy FrameNet augmentation are small; there is no evaluation on out-of-domain ACE genres or with noisy argument supervision.

Questions to authors:
- Do you use gold or predicted entity mentions/types at test time on ACE? Please report both settings.
- What is the fixed context window length n, and how sensitive are results to n and λ? Any variance over multiple random seeds?
- How does performance vary by event subtype (per-class F1)? Are gains concentrated on triggers with ambiguous lexemes?
- Can you supervise attention with automatically predicted arguments to assess robustness to noisy supervision?
