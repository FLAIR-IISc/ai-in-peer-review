Summary
The paper addresses event detection (ED) in ACE 2005 by explicitly leveraging argument annotations to supervise an attention mechanism. The model forms attention over contextual words and entity types based on the candidate trigger, and learns attention jointly with the ED classifier via an additional mean-squared error loss to match “gold” attentions derived from argument positions. Two supervision strategies are explored: S1 focuses only on argument tokens; S2 distributes attention to arguments and neighboring words using a Gaussian profile. The method achieves improved F1 over prior systems on ACE 2005 and shows modest additional gains with FrameNet augmentation.

Main contributions (as assessed)
1) A supervised attention framework that uses gold argument locations to directly guide context selection for ED, avoiding indirect use of arguments typical of joint ED+AE models.
2) An empirical study of attention supervision strategies (argument-only vs argument+neighbors) and an analysis indicating argument information is beneficial for ED beyond entities alone.
3) State-of-the-art ED performance on ACE 2005 under the reported setup.

Strengths
1) Clear, targeted use of argument supervision for ED. The paper isolates a concrete limitation of joint models (arguments benefiting AE more than ED) and proposes a direct supervisory signal for attention. The joint loss (classification + attention MSE) is simple and technically sound. Empirically, supervised attention (ANN-S1/S2) substantially outperforms the unsupervised-attention baseline (ANN; 64.6 F1) by 6–7 F1 points and modestly improves over strong prior methods (best reported 71.3 vs 71.7 F1).
2) Evidence that argument information, not just entity presence, drives gains. The oracle experiments (ANN-Gold1/2) show large improvements when attention uses true argument cues, and ANN-ENT (uniform attention over entities) underperforms, supporting the claim that entities alone are insufficient and argument supervision adds discriminative value.
3) Attention supervision strategies are well-motivated and behave as expected. S1 yields higher precision (argument-focused) whereas S2 achieves higher recall (broader, noisier context), aligning with the design hypotheses and offering a tunable precision–recall trade-off.
4) Practical inference without arguments at test time. The model uses argument annotations only to supervise attention during training; at test, it operates like a standard ED classifier. This preserves practicality while benefiting from richer supervision.
5) Additional data results are consistent and robust. Augmenting with FrameNet-derived events improves all variants slightly (e.g., ANN: 64.6→66.2; ANN-S2: 71.7→71.9), supporting the claim that data sparsity is a limiting factor for learning reliable attention and that the approach leverages extra data coherently.

Weaknesses
1) Limited architectural competitiveness of the baseline and attribution of gains. The main model is a simple feed-forward network over fixed windows. Strong recent baselines (CNN/RNN with position features or non-consecutive convolutions) are more expressive. The reported improvements over the best prior (0.4 F1 over Skip-CNN) are small; without comparisons that control for architecture (e.g., supervised attention atop CNN/RNN baselines), it is hard to attribute gains uniquely to supervised attention rather than other design/implementation choices.
2) Evaluation clarity and fairness. It is not clearly stated whether gold or predicted entity mentions/types are used at test time; this can substantially affect performance and comparability. The Skip-CNN row reports only F1 with missing P/R, and exact data splits/seeds are not fully specified. The claim of statistical significance (t-test, p≤0.05) lacks details (number of runs, variance), weakening confidence in significance.
3) Incomplete ablations and sensitivity analyses. The method sums word- and entity-based attentions without learned weighting; there is no ablation removing entity attention, nor a learned fusion alternative. Sensitivity to λ, context window size, and Gaussian parameters (w, σ) is not analyzed beyond a single setting, leaving uncertainty about robustness to hyperparameters and randomness.
4) Data handling and coverage concerns. Context trimming/padding may exclude distant arguments; the paper does not report how often gold arguments fall outside the window or the impact on attention supervision quality. The FrameNet augmentation relies on automatically labeled entities (RPI system) and restricts to core frame elements; the amount and noise level of added data are not quantified, limiting interpretability of those results.
5) Presentation quality. The manuscript contains numerous typographical and formatting errors, incomplete citations, and inconsistencies in tables/figures (e.g., missing values), which detract from clarity and make it harder to assess and reproduce results.

Questions to Authors
- Do you use gold or predicted entity mentions/types at test time? If predicted, please specify the system and report results under both settings to align with prior work.
- What fraction of gold arguments falls within your fixed context window after trimming? How does coverage affect performance, and did you consider dynamic windows or full-sentence encoders?
- Provide ablations: (a) remove entity attention; (b) learn a scalar to weight word vs entity attention instead of summation; (c) replace the feed-forward classifier with CNN/RNN while keeping supervised attention to test whether the gains persist.
- Report sensitivity to λ, window size, and Gaussian parameters, and variance across multiple random seeds. How robust are results?
- Clarify the statistical significance testing protocol (number of runs, metric distribution). Are improvements over Liu (2016) FN-based and Skip-CNN significant under repeated runs?
- For the FrameNet augmentation: how many additional instances were added, with what positive/negative balance, and what was the estimated noise rate after automatic entity labeling?
