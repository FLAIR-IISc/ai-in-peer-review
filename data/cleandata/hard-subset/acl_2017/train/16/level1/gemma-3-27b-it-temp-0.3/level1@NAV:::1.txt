## Review of "Exploiting Argument Information to Improve Event Detection via Supervised Attention Mechanisms"

This paper addresses the event detection (ED) task, arguing that existing methods neglect valuable information contained within event arguments. The authors propose a neural network approach that explicitly incorporates argument information via supervised attention mechanisms, training the attention based on annotated arguments. They investigate different attention strategies and report state-of-the-art performance on the ACE 2005 dataset.

The primary contribution of this work is the demonstration that explicitly modeling argument information *with supervised attention* can improve event detection performance. While joint ED/AE models exist, the authors convincingly argue that these models don't effectively leverage arguments for ED specifically, and their experimental results support this claim. A secondary contribution lies in the systematic investigation of different attention strategies for incorporating argument information, providing insights into how best to utilize this signal. Finally, achieving a new state-of-the-art on the ACE 2005 dataset is a positive, albeit incremental, contribution.

**Strengths:**

1. **Clear Motivation & Problem Definition:** The paper clearly articulates the limitations of existing approaches and convincingly motivates the need for explicitly leveraging argument information for ED. The example provided with "fired" is particularly effective in illustrating the ambiguity that arguments can resolve.
2. **Sound Methodology:** The proposed supervised attention mechanism is a reasonable approach to incorporating argument information. The use of gold attention vectors as supervision is a sensible strategy.
3. **Strong Empirical Results:** The paper demonstrates consistent improvements over strong baselines, including state-of-the-art methods. The ablation study (comparing different attention strategies) provides valuable insights.
4. **Thorough Experimentation:** The authors explore data augmentation with FrameNet, further demonstrating the robustness of their approach.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Novelty of Attention Mechanism:** While the application of supervised attention to argument information in ED is novel, the attention mechanism itself is fairly standard. The paper doesn't introduce any new architectural components or attention formulations.
2. **ACE 2005 Dataset Limitation:** The reliance on a single, relatively old dataset (ACE 2005) limits the generalizability of the findings. Evaluating on more diverse and modern datasets would strengthen the conclusions.
3. **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** The significant difference in optimal λ values for S1 and S2 suggests potential sensitivity to hyperparameter tuning. A more detailed analysis of hyperparameter influence would be beneficial.



**Questions to Authors:**

1. Could you provide a more detailed analysis of the types of events where your approach shows the greatest improvement? Are there specific event categories that particularly benefit from argument information?
2.  How does the performance of your model degrade when the quality of argument annotations is reduced (e.g., with noisy or incomplete argument labels)?
3.  Have you explored alternative methods for constructing gold attention vectors beyond S1 and S2?
