### Summary of the Paper

This paper addresses the task of Event Detection (ED), which involves identifying and classifying event triggers in text. The central hypothesis is that event arguments, though part of a separate downstream task, provide critical contextual clues for correctly identifying triggers, especially for ambiguous words. The authors argue that previous pipeline methods ignore this information, while joint learning methods use it ineffectively for the ED subtask.

To address this, the paper proposes a neural network model that explicitly leverages argument information through a novel *supervised attention mechanism*. During training, the model is not only optimized for the event classification task but is also simultaneously trained to reproduce "gold" attention distributions derived from the locations of annotated arguments in the sentence. Two strategies for creating these gold attentions are explored: one that focuses sharply on argument words (S1) and another that uses a Gaussian spread around them (S2). Experiments on the ACE 2005 dataset show that this supervised attention approach significantly outperforms an unsupervised attention baseline and achieves a new state-of-the-art F1 score, demonstrating the effectiveness of explicitly guiding the model's focus towards event arguments.

### Main Contributions

1.  **A novel method for incorporating argument information into event detection via supervised attention.** The core contribution is the idea of treating attention learning as a supervised task, where the supervisory signal is derived from the locations of annotated arguments. This is an elegant and effective way to inject rich structural information into a neural trigger classifier without resorting to complex joint inference frameworks, which the authors show have historically struggled to improve ED.
2.  **Strong empirical validation and state-of-the-art results on a standard benchmark.** The paper demonstrates the effectiveness of the proposed method with a comprehensive set of experiments on the ACE 2005 dataset. The proposed model (ANN-S2) achieves a new state-of-the-art F1 score of 71.7, significantly outperforming previous feature-based, representation-based, and joint models.
3.  **A clear analysis of the limitations of prior work and the benefits of the proposed approach.** The paper provides a well-reasoned motivation, highlighting that previous joint models, despite their theoretical appeal, have failed to meaningfully improve ED performance. The ablation studies in Table 2 convincingly support the paper's core assumption that argument information, not just general entity information, is key to the performance gains.

### Strengths

1.  **Excellent Motivation and Intuition.** The paper is motivated by a clear and insightful observation about the shortcomings of existing event detection models, particularly joint models. The example of the trigger "fired" (Attack vs. End-Position) is very effective at illustrating why argument information is crucial. The proposed solution—forcing the model to "pay attention" to arguments—is intuitive, simple, and directly addresses the identified problem.
2.  **Novelty of the Supervised Attention Mechanism.** While attention mechanisms are common, using them in a *supervised* manner for this task is novel. Instead of letting the model learn attention weights solely through the backpropagated classification error (which they show performs poorly, likely due to data sparsity), the authors add a direct loss term that guides the attention mechanism. This provides a strong and explicit learning signal that proves highly effective.
3.  **Thorough and Convincing Experimental Results.** The paper achieves a new state-of-the-art result on the competitive ACE 2005 benchmark. The performance gains are substantial (e.g., +2.4 F1 over the best previous system) and are shown to be statistically significant. The authors include important baselines, such as their own model with standard (unsupervised) attention, which highlights the critical contribution of the supervision. The analysis in Section 4.2 further strengthens their claims by isolating the impact of argument information versus general entity information.
4.  **Clarity and Readability.** The paper is very well-written and easy to follow. The model architecture is explained clearly with a helpful diagram, the motivation is laid out logically, and the experimental setup and results are presented in a straightforward manner. This makes the paper's contributions easy to understand and appreciate.

### Weaknesses

1.  **Dependence on Annotated Arguments in Training Data.** The proposed method's primary strength is also a potential limitation. It requires training data to be annotated with not only event triggers but also their corresponding arguments to create the supervisory signal. This limits its direct applicability to datasets that lack this rich annotation. The paper does not discuss the method's robustness to scenarios with missing or noisy argument annotations, which would be common in lower-resource settings or when using arguments predicted by an upstream system.
2.  **Simplicity of the Base Model Architecture.** The event detector is a standard three-layer feed-forward neural network (ANN). While sufficient to demonstrate the effectiveness of the supervised attention mechanism, it is a relatively simple architecture compared to recurrent (BiLSTM) or convolutional (CNN) models that were state-of-the-art for sequence/token classification tasks at the time. It is unclear if the performance gains are entirely attributable to the supervised attention, or if they would be complementary to a stronger base model. Integrating the proposed attention mechanism into a more powerful architecture like a BiLSTM would make the results even more compelling.
3.  **Ambiguity in the FrameNet Augmentation Process.** The process of generating extra training data from FrameNet is not fully detailed. Specifically, it is not clear how the "gold" attention vectors are constructed for this augmented data, given that both the arguments (core frame elements) and entities (labeled by a separate system) are automatically generated and thus noisy. This noise in the supervisory signal could have complex effects, and a more detailed description and analysis of this process would be beneficial.
4.  **Strong Claims about Unsupervised Attention.** The paper concludes that "unsupervised-attention mechanisms do not work well for ED" based on the poor performance of the ANN baseline. While the supervised version is clearly superior on this dataset, the performance gap is very large (64.6 vs 71.7 F1). This may be less an indictment of unsupervised attention in general and more a reflection of the data sparsity of ACE 2005. A more nuanced conclusion would be that on this specific, sparse dataset, unsupervised attention fails to learn a useful signal, and requires the strong guidance provided by argument supervision.

### Questions to Authors

1.  Your method relies on gold-standard argument annotations during training to create the supervision signal for the attention mechanism. How do you expect the model to perform if this supervision is noisy? For example, what would happen if you used arguments predicted by a baseline system to generate the "gold" attention vectors during training? This would be a crucial test for applying the method in a more realistic pipeline setting.
2.  Could you please clarify how the gold attention vectors were constructed for the augmented training data from FrameNet? Since both the arguments (frame elements) and entity types were generated automatically, this supervisory signal is inherently noisy. Did you apply the same S1/S2 strategies, and how did you account for the potential noise during training?
3.  The base model is a relatively simple ANN. Have you considered integrating your supervised attention mechanism into a more powerful sequential model, such as a BiLSTM, which might be better suited for capturing sequential context? It would be interesting to know if the gains from supervised attention are additive with the gains from a stronger base architecture.
