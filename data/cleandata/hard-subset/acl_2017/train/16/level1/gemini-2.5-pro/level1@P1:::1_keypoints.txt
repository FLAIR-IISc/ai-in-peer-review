- Paper tackles Event Detection (ED) by using event arguments as explicit contextual cues for trigger classification.
- Central idea: supervise the model’s attention to focus on annotated arguments during training to disambiguate triggers.
- Proposes a supervised attention mechanism with two gold-attention strategies: sharp focus on arguments (S1) and Gaussian spread (S2).
- Model is trained to reproduce gold attention distributions alongside the event classification loss.
- Experiments on ACE 2005 show large gains over unsupervised attention and a new state-of-the-art F1 (71.7).
- Contribution: novel supervised-attention method to inject argument information into trigger classification without complex joint inference.
- Contribution: strong empirical validation and statistically significant improvements on a standard benchmark.
- Contribution: clear analysis showing argument information (not just entity info) drives performance gains.
- Strength: well-motivated intuition and effective example (e.g., “fired”) illustrating the need for argument context.
- Strength: novelty in supervising attention directly to provide a strong learning signal on a sparse dataset.
- Strength: thorough experiments, useful baselines, ablation studies, and clear presentation.
- Weakness: requires gold argument annotations for training, limiting applicability to datasets without arguments.
- Weakness: base detector is a simple three-layer ANN, leaving open whether gains generalize to stronger architectures (e.g., BiLSTM).
- Weakness: unclear details on how gold-attention vectors were constructed for noisy FrameNet-augmented data.
- Weakness: strong general claim that unsupervised attention “does not work well for ED” may be overstated given dataset sparsity.
- Question: how robust is the method to noisy argument supervision (e.g., using predicted arguments instead of gold)?
- Question: how exactly were gold attention vectors constructed for the automatically generated FrameNet augmentation?
- Question: have you tried integrating supervised attention with stronger sequential models (e.g., BiLSTM) to see if gains are additive?
