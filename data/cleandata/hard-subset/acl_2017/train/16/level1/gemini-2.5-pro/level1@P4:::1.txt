**Summary and Contributions**

This paper addresses the task of Event Detection (ED), which involves identifying and classifying event triggers. The central thesis is that event arguments, though not part of the ED task definition, provide crucial contextual clues that are underexploited by existing methods, particularly joint models for event extraction. To remedy this, the authors propose a neural network model that incorporates argument information explicitly through a supervised attention mechanism. During training, the model's attention component is guided by a "gold" attention vector derived from annotated arguments, using an auxiliary loss function. This encourages the model to focus on argument words when classifying a potential trigger. The authors experiment with two strategies for constructing these gold attention vectors. Experiments on the ACE 2005 dataset show that this approach achieves a new state-of-the-art result.

The main contributions of this work are:
1.  A novel method for explicitly incorporating annotated argument information into a neural event detection model via a supervised attention mechanism. This provides a direct way to guide the model's focus using external linguistic knowledge.
2.  A clear analysis motivating the work, highlighting that prior joint models for event extraction have failed to translate the availability of argument information into significant gains for the ED subtask.
3.  State-of-the-art empirical performance on the standard ACE 2005 benchmark for event detection, demonstrating the efficacy of the proposed technique.

**Strengths**

1.  **Strong Motivation and Problem Formulation:** The paper is very well-motivated. It begins by identifying a clear and plausible gap in existing research: while joint trigger and argument extraction models exist, they have shown minimal benefit for the ED subtask. The authors provide quantitative evidence for this claim (Table 1) and a compelling qualitative example (the ambiguous trigger "fired"). This provides a strong justification for developing a method that focuses specifically on leveraging argument information for ED.

2.  **Novelty of the Proposed Method:** The core technical contribution—using supervised attention to integrate argument information—is novel for the task of event detection. While the underlying neural architecture and attention mechanism are standard, the idea of creating an auxiliary training objective based on the locations of known arguments is an innovative and intuitive way to inject valuable supervisory signal into the model. This contrasts with more implicit methods used in joint models and represents a targeted way to address the identified problem.

3.  **Comprehensive and Rigorous Evaluation:** The experimental evaluation is thorough and supports the paper's claims effectively.
    *   The authors first conduct an experiment with "oracle" gold attentions (ANN-Gold1/2) to validate their central hypothesis that argument information is highly beneficial for ED.
    *   The main results show a significant improvement over an unsupervised attention baseline and establish a new state-of-the-art on the ACE 2005 dataset, outperforming strong recent methods. The use of a t-test to confirm statistical significance adds to the rigor.
    *   The paper also includes experiments augmenting the training data with FrameNet, demonstrating that the proposed model benefits from additional data and can be integrated with other performance-enhancing techniques.

4.  **Clarity and Readability:** The paper is well-written and easy to follow. The proposed architecture is explained clearly with a helpful diagram (Figure 1), and the motivation, methodology, and experimental setup are all presented in a logical and understandable manner.

**Weaknesses**

1.  **Weak Unsupervised Baseline:** The performance of the unsupervised attention baseline (the "ANN" model) is surprisingly low (64.6 F1). This is considerably weaker than other contemporary neural network-based ED systems reported in the literature, such as Skip-CNN (71.3 F1) or even the older Chen et al. (2015) model (69.1 F1). This raises a concern about the strength of the base model architecture. A stronger baseline would provide a more convincing measure of the value added by the supervised attention component. It is difficult to discern whether the large performance gain comes purely from the supervised attention or if the supervision is compensating for a suboptimal base model.

2.  **Dependence on Argument Annotations:** The proposed method's primary limitation is its reliance on training data annotated with both event triggers and their corresponding arguments. This requirement limits its applicability to richly annotated corpora like ACE 2005. The method cannot be directly applied to datasets where only trigger information is available, or in a semi-supervised setting where a large amount of text might be annotated only for triggers. The paper does not discuss potential adaptations or the implications of this data requirement.

3.  **Limited Technical Novelty of Model Components:** While the *application* of supervised attention is novel for this task, the underlying components are not. The base model is a standard multi-layer perceptron, and the attention mechanism is a conventional dot-product attention. The concept of supervised attention itself has been explored in other areas, such as NMT (Mi et al., 2016), which the paper cites. The contribution is thus more about a novel training paradigm for an existing task rather than a new model architecture.

**Questions to Authors**

1.  The unsupervised attention baseline (ANN) performs significantly worse than other recent neural ED models. Could you elaborate on the potential reasons for this performance gap? Does this suggest that the underlying ANN architecture is less effective for this task than the CNN/RNN models used by competitors, and if so, how might your supervised attention approach perform when integrated into a stronger base architecture like a CNN or Bi-RNN?

2.  Your method requires argument annotations during training. How do you see this approach being extended to settings with limited or no argument annotations? For instance, could pseudo-arguments be generated by a separate system, or could the model be trained in a multi-task fashion on a combination of datasets with and without argument labels?

3.  Could you provide a qualitative analysis or visualization of the learned attention weights on test examples? It would be instructive to see if the model learns to focus on specific entity types or semantic roles that frequently act as arguments, and how it behaves on sentences where the contextual clues are more subtle or distant from the trigger candidate. This would offer insight into the generalization capability of the learned attention mechanism.
