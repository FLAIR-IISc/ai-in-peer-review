- Proposes supervised attention for Event Detection that trains attention to focus on annotated argument words via a loss against a gold attention vector.  
- Claims this injects argument knowledge directly into ED models to disambiguate triggers.  
- Main contribution: novel application of supervised attention to leverage event argument annotations as a simple alternative to complex joint inference.  
- Empirical claim: achieves state-of-the-art on ACE 2005 with thorough ablations and external-data experiments validating the hypothesis.  
- Strength: clear motivation and intuitive hypothesis that focusing on arguments disambiguates triggers, supported by examples and tests.  
- Strength: elegant method—using a supervised loss to guide attention—yields significant gains over unsupervised baseline and prior work.  
- Strength: strong experimental validation isolating argument information as the improvement source.  
- Weakness: requires training data with argument annotations, limiting applicability in datasets or low-resource settings.  
- Weakness: unsupervised baseline (ANN) is weaker than previous models, so gains may partly stem from baseline simplicity.  
- Weakness: gold attention construction strategies (S1, S2) are heuristic; more nuanced or data-driven schemes (e.g., role-weighting) could strengthen the approach.  
- Question: How can the method be adapted when argument annotations are unavailable—e.g., via semi-supervised or pseudo-argument prediction?  
- Question: Have you tried integrating supervised attention into stronger backbones (e.g., BiLSTMs) to confirm gains are not due to a weak base classifier?
