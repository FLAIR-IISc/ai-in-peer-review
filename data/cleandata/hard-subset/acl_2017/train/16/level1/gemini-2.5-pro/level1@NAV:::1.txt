**Summary**
This paper addresses the task of Event Detection (ED). The authors argue that event arguments, while containing crucial information for disambiguating trigger words, are often ignored or used ineffectively by existing ED models. To address this, they propose a neural network architecture featuring a supervised attention mechanism. This mechanism is explicitly trained to focus on argument words by minimizing a loss between its generated attention weights and a "gold" attention vector derived from ground-truth argument annotations, thereby directly injecting argument knowledge into the ED model.

**Main Contributions**
1.  The primary contribution is the novel application of a supervised attention mechanism to explicitly leverage annotated event arguments for the ED task. This provides a direct and effective method for guiding the model's focus, offering a simpler alternative to complex joint inference frameworks.
2.  The paper provides a strong empirical demonstration on the ACE 2005 dataset that this explicit supervision leads to state-of-the-art performance. The thorough analysis, including ablations and experiments with external data, convincingly validates the authors' core hypothesis.

**Strengths**
1.  **Clear Motivation and Hypothesis:** The paper effectively identifies a weakness in prior work, noting that joint models often fail to improve ED. The core hypothesis—that explicitly focusing on arguments can disambiguate triggers—is intuitive, well-supported by examples, and directly tested.
2.  **Effective and Elegant Method:** Using a supervised loss to guide the attention mechanism is a clever and direct solution to the stated problem. The resulting performance gains over both an unsupervised baseline and previous state-of-the-art models are significant and clearly demonstrate the method's efficacy.
3.  **Thorough Experimental Validation:** The experiments are well-designed and convincing. The analysis in Table 2 successfully isolates the impact of argument information, confirming it as the source of improvement. The model's performance against a strong set of baselines solidifies its contribution to the field.

**Weaknesses**
1.  **Dependence on Annotated Arguments:** The method’s core strength is also its main limitation. It requires training data with both trigger and argument annotations to generate the supervisory signal. This restricts its applicability to datasets lacking argument labels and is a potential weakness in low-resource settings.
2.  **Potentially Weak Unsupervised Baseline:** The base model (ANN) with unsupervised attention performs significantly worse than prior work. This raises the question of whether the impressive gains are partially due to a weak baseline architecture rather than solely the supervised attention. The underlying ANN detector is simpler than other contemporary models (e.g., RNNs, more complex CNNs).
3.  **Ad-hoc Gold Attention Strategies:** The two strategies (S1, S2) for constructing gold attention vectors are reasonable but feel heuristic. The paper could be strengthened by exploring more nuanced or data-driven approaches to creating this supervisory signal, for instance, by weighting arguments based on their roles.

**Questions to Authors**
1.  How might this approach be adapted for settings where argument annotations are unavailable during training? Could a semi-supervised method, where a baseline model predicts pseudo-arguments to generate a noisy attention signal, be viable?
2.  Have you considered integrating the supervised attention mechanism into more powerful backbone architectures (e.g., BiLSTMs) that have proven effective for sequence classification tasks, to ensure the performance gains are independent of the choice of the base classifier?
