### Summary of the Paper

This paper addresses the task of Event Detection (ED), which involves identifying event triggers and classifying their types. The authors hypothesize that event arguments, which are often ignored or used ineffectively in prior ED models, provide crucial contextual clues for disambiguating trigger words. The core contribution is a novel method to explicitly leverage argument information through a supervised attention mechanism. Instead of letting the attention weights be learned implicitly, the model is trained with an auxiliary loss that encourages the attention mechanism to focus on words annotated as event arguments. The authors propose two strategies for creating these "gold" attention signals. The model, a simple feed-forward neural network, is evaluated on the ACE 2005 dataset, where it is shown to significantly outperform an unsupervised attention baseline and establish a new state-of-the-art, demonstrating the effectiveness of explicitly supervising the model's focus using argument information.

### Main Contributions

1.  **A novel supervised attention framework for Event Detection:** The primary contribution is the idea of using argument annotations to directly supervise an attention mechanism for the ED task. This provides a simple yet highly effective method for injecting explicit, linguistically-motivated structural information into a neural model, guiding it to focus on the most salient parts of the context for trigger classification.
2.  **An insightful analysis of the limitations of prior work:** The paper provides a clear and compelling analysis of why previous joint event extraction models have failed to significantly improve ED performance, despite using argument information. The authors correctly identify the data imbalance and the indirect flow of information as key issues, which strongly motivates their proposal for a more direct and explicit use of arguments for ED.
3.  **State-of-the-art performance on a standard benchmark:** The proposed method achieves the best reported F1 score on the widely-used ACE 2005 dataset, demonstrating its empirical strength. The gains are particularly notable in precision, suggesting the model learns to make more confident and accurate predictions.

### Strengths

1.  **Novelty and Elegance of the Method:** The central idea of supervising the attention mechanism is both novel for this task and conceptually elegant. It sidesteps the complexities of full joint inference models while still effectively transferring knowledge from argument annotations to the ED task. By framing the problem as "teaching the model where to look," the authors have found a very direct way to exploit available annotations. The investigation of two different supervision strategies (S1 and S2) further adds to the depth of the technical contribution.
2.  **Strong and Well-Supported Empirical Results:** The paper presents a comprehensive set of experiments that convincingly demonstrate the model's effectiveness. The proposed ANN-S2 model achieves a new state-of-the-art F1 score of 71.7 on the ACE 2005 test set, a significant improvement over previous methods. The results are further boosted to 71.9 with data augmentation from FrameNet. The performance gains are substantial and statistically significant.
3.  **Thorough Ablation Studies and Analysis:** The experimental design is a major strength. The authors include a crucial experiment in Section 4.2 to validate their core hypothesis. By comparing against an entity-only attention model (ANN-ENT) and oracle models using gold arguments at test time (ANN-Gold1/2), they convincingly show that the performance boost comes specifically from *argument* information, not just general entity information. This level of rigorous analysis strengthens the paper's conclusions significantly. The comparison between the S1 (high precision) and S2 (high recall) strategies also provides useful insights into the precision/recall trade-off inherent in the supervision design.

### Weaknesses

1.  **Weak Unsupervised Baseline:** The main unsupervised attention baseline (ANN) performs surprisingly poorly, achieving only 64.6 F1. This is considerably lower than other contemporary neural network-based ED systems like Chen et al. (2015) (69.1 F1) or Nguyen and Grishman (2015), which also use neural architectures (CNNs in their case) without explicit argument supervision. This raises a concern that the base neural architecture (a simple ANN) might be relatively weak, which could exaggerate the perceived gains from adding the supervised attention loss. The impressive final results are undeniable, but it is unclear how much of the gain is from the supervised attention versus how much a more powerful base architecture (e.g., a BiLSTM or CNN) might have achieved with unsupervised attention alone.
2.  **Dependence on Rich Annotations:** The proposed method's success is fundamentally tied to the availability of training data with both event triggers and their corresponding arguments annotated. While effective on ACE 2005, this limits its applicability to datasets that lack such rich annotation. The authors demonstrate a workaround using FrameNet, but this required a non-trivial pipeline of its own (mapping frame elements to arguments, running an external tool for entity tagging), introducing potential noise. The paper would be strengthened by a discussion of the method's robustness to sparse or noisy argument annotations, which is a more realistic scenario for many domains.
3.  **Lack of Qualitative Analysis and Visualization:** The paper proves *that* the method works through quantitative results but offers little insight into *how* it works on a qualitative level. Visualizing the learned attention weights for a few key examples (e.g., the ambiguous trigger "fired" from the introduction) would be extremely powerful. Comparing the attention maps from the unsupervised ANN model with the supervised ANN-S2 model could provide concrete visual evidence that the supervision is working as intended and leading to better linguistic understanding. Similarly, an error analysis discussing the types of events that are now correctly classified would add significant depth.

### Questions to Authors

1.  The performance of your unsupervised attention baseline (ANN, 64.6 F1) is notably lower than other neural ED systems from around the same time period. Could you comment on why this might be? Have you considered applying your supervised attention technique to a stronger base architecture, such as the CNNs used by Chen et al. (2015) or Nguyen & Grishman (2015), to see if the gains hold?
2.  Your method relies on argument annotations for supervision. How sensitive is the model's performance to the quality and quantity of these annotations? For instance, what is the performance impact if, during training, you only use 50% or 25% of the available argument annotations to generate the supervision signal? This would help gauge the method's utility in lower-resource settings.
