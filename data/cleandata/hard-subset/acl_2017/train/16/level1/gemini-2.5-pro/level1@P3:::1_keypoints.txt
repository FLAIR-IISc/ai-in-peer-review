- Paper proposes supervising an attention mechanism with event-argument annotations to improve Event Detection (ED) by teaching the model which context words to focus on.
- Introduces two strategies for creating gold attention signals and trains a simple feed-forward neural network, evaluated on ACE 2005.
- Main contribution: a supervised attention framework that uses argument annotations to guide ED attention, injecting explicit linguistic structure.
- Main contribution: analysis identifying data imbalance and indirect information flow as reasons prior joint models underused argument information.
- Main contribution: achieves state-of-the-art F1 on ACE 2005, with notable precision gains.
- Strength: the supervised-attention idea is novel, elegant, and simpler than full joint inference while effectively leveraging argument annotations.
- Strength: strong empirical results with ANN-S2 reaching 71.7 F1 (71.9 with FrameNet augmentation), statistically significant improvements.
- Strength: thorough ablation studies (entity-only, oracle variants) that validate gains come specifically from argument information and explore S1/S2 precision–recall trade-offs.
- Weakness: the unsupervised attention baseline is weak (64.6 F1) compared to contemporaries, raising concerns the base ANN may inflate perceived gains.
- Weakness: heavy reliance on richly annotated argument data limits applicability; FrameNet augmentation required a noisy external pipeline.
- Weakness: lacks qualitative analyses/visualizations of attention maps and an error analysis to show how and why supervision helps.
- Questions: Why is the unsupervised ANN baseline substantially weaker than other neural ED systems, and have you tried supervised attention on stronger architectures (e.g., CNNs/BiLSTMs)?
- Questions: How sensitive is performance to the quality/quantity of argument annotations (e.g., using only 50% or 25% of annotations)?
