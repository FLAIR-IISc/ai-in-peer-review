- Central idea: use supervised attention guided by annotated event arguments to improve event trigger detection.
- Contribution: novel method to inject argument information into ED via an auxiliary supervised-attention loss.
- Contribution: clear analysis showing prior joint models underutilize argument information for the ED subtask.
- Contribution: achieves state-of-the-art results on ACE 2005, demonstrating empirical effectiveness.
- Strength: strong motivation and problem formulation with quantitative and qualitative evidence that arguments help disambiguate triggers.
- Strength: novel application of supervised attention for ED, providing an intuitive way to inject external linguistic signals.
- Strength: comprehensive, rigorous evaluation including oracle-attention experiments, statistical significance testing, and FrameNet augmentation.
- Strength: clear, readable presentation with helpful diagrams and logical exposition.
- Weakness: weak unsupervised-attention baseline (ANN) that performs worse than several contemporary neural ED models, raising concerns about base model strength.
- Weakness: dependence on richly annotated argument data (e.g., ACE 2005), limiting applicability to datasets without argument labels.
- Weakness: limited technical novelty in model components—standard MLP and dot-product attention; supervised attention idea exists in other areas.
- Question: why does the ANN baseline underperform compared to CNN/RNN baselines, and how would supervised attention perform on a stronger base architecture?
- Question: how can the method be extended to settings with limited/no argument annotations (e.g., pseudo-arguments, multi-task learning)?
- Question: can the authors provide qualitative attention visualizations to show what the model learns and how it handles subtle/distant contextual clues?
