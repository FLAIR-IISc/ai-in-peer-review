

# Review for ACL 2017

## Summary and Contributions
The paper proposes using argument information explicitly for event detection (ED) via supervised attention mechanisms. The authors argue that existing methods either ignore arguments or exploit them indirectly in joint models for event extraction. Their main contributions are: (1) identifying the problem with current joint models in effectively leveraging argument information for ED; (2) introducing a supervised attention mechanism that explicitly uses argument information to improve ED; and (3) demonstrating significant performance gains on ACE 2005 dataset through systematic evaluation.

## Strengths
1. The paper provides a compelling and well-supported analysis of why existing joint models fail to effectively use argument information for ED, backed by empirical data in Table 1. The authors correctly identify two key reasons: unbalanced data (more arguments than triggers) and the fact that argument information is primarily used for AE rather than ED in joint models. This analysis establishes a strong theoretical foundation for their approach.

2. The proposed supervised attention mechanism is technically sound and well-motivated. The ablation study in Table 2 clearly demonstrates the effectiveness of argument information for ED, showing significant improvements over baselines that don't use this information (ANN-S1/2 outperform ANN by 6.2/7.1 F1 points). This provides strong empirical validation for their core insight.

3. The systematic investigation of two attention strategies (S1: focus only on argument words; S2: focus on arguments and surrounding words) offers valuable practical insights. The paper effectively explains the precision-recall trade-offs between these strategies, with S1 achieving higher precision (81.4% vs. 78.0%) while S2 achieves higher recall (66.3% vs. 62.4%).

4. The experimental results (Table 3) demonstrate consistent and significant improvements over state-of-the-art methods, with ANN-S2 achieving the best F1 score (71.7%) on ACE 2005. The t-test showing statistical significance (p < 0.05) against all baselines strengthens the claim of superiority.

5. The augmentation with FrameNet (Table 4) provides additional evidence for the effectiveness of their approach, particularly in addressing data sparseness issues. The results show that their method benefits from external data, with ANN-S2+FrameNet achieving 71.9 F1 (vs. 71.7 without augmentation), demonstrating robustness.

## Weaknesses
1. The paper fails to address a critical practical limitation: argument annotations are not available during inference for new documents. The proposed method relies on gold attention vectors constructed from annotated arguments, but these would not be available in real-world applications where the goal is to detect events without prior argument annotations.

2. The distinction between "explicit" exploitation of arguments and existing joint learning approaches is not sufficiently clear. The authors claim joint methods use arguments "indirectly," but it's not evident why their supervised attention approach is fundamentally different from joint methods that might incorporate attention mechanisms to leverage arguments.

3. The paper lacks detailed error analysis to understand when the proposed approach succeeds or fails compared to baselines. Without this analysis, it's difficult to assess the specific scenarios where the method provides the most benefit.

4. The evaluation is limited to ACE 2005 without sufficient analysis of performance on specific event types, particularly rare ones. The paper doesn't address how the method performs on low-resource event types or in cross-domain settings.

5. The comparison to state-of-the-art methods is incomplete. The paper doesn't include more recent approaches published in 2016-2017 (e.g., recent neural network-based methods) that might provide a more comprehensive evaluation of their contribution.

## Questions to Authors
1. How can the proposed approach be applied in a real-world scenario where argument annotations are not available for test documents? The paper relies on gold attention vectors from annotated arguments, but these would not be available in practice.

2. Can the authors clarify the fundamental distinction between their "explicit" use of argument information and existing joint learning approaches that might also use attention mechanisms to leverage arguments?

3. The paper mentions using FrameNet events as extra training data, but FrameNet events don't have the same structure as ACE events. How were the FrameNet annotations processed to make them compatible with the ACE dataset for training?
