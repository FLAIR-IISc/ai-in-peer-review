

This paper proposes a novel approach to event detection (ED) by explicitly leveraging argument information through supervised attention mechanisms. The key contribution is recognizing that argument information, often ignored or indirectly utilized in existing methods, provides crucial disambiguation for ambiguous event triggers. The authors introduce two supervised attention strategies (S1: focusing only on argument words; S2: focusing on arguments and surrounding words) and demonstrate their effectiveness through comprehensive experiments on ACE 2005.

The main contributions are: (1) the insightful analysis showing that existing joint methods fail to effectively utilize argument information for ED due to data imbalance and implementation constraints; (2) the supervised attention mechanism that directly incorporates argument information into ED through gold attention vectors; (3) the empirical demonstration of significant performance gains over state-of-the-art methods, achieving the best F1 score (71.7%) on ACE 2005.

Strengths: The paper makes a compelling case for why argument information matters for ED, supported by a clear example showing how arguments resolve ambiguity (e.g., "fired" as End-Position vs. Attack). The ablation studies (Table 2) convincingly demonstrate that argument information is necessary for improved ED, as systems using gold attention (ANN-Gold1/2) significantly outperform those without (ANN/ANN-ENT). The method is conceptually simple yet effective, with ANN-S2 achieving a statistically significant improvement over all baselines (p<0.05). The paper also provides a thorough analysis of why joint approaches fail to improve ED performance.

Weaknesses: The paper doesn't adequately explain why the unsupervised attention model (ANN) performs poorly (64.6 F1) compared to supervised models (70.8-71.7 F1), despite the authors' claim about data sparseness. The computational complexity analysis is missing, which would be important for practical adoption. The paper also lacks error analysis to understand where the model fails, which would provide deeper insights into the approach's limitations. Additionally, the reason why S2 achieves higher recall than S1 (71.7 vs. 70.8 F1) despite lower precision isn't thoroughly explained.

Questions to Authors: How does your approach scale to datasets with more event types or different linguistic structures beyond ACE 2005? Could you provide more details on the computational overhead of your supervised attention mechanism compared to standard ED models?
