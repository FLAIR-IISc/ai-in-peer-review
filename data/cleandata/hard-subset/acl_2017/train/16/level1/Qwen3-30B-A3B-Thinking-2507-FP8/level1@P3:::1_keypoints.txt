- Proposes leveraging argument information explicitly via supervised attention to improve event detection (ED).

- Contributions: shows argument information resolves trigger ambiguity (e.g., "fired"), introduces a supervised attention mechanism using arguments, and systematically compares two attention strategies (S1, S2).

- Strength: Strong conceptual insight that argument information is underutilized and can disambiguate triggers.

- Strength: Robust empirical validation with ablation studies and state-of-the-art F1 (71.7 on ACE 2005) with statistical significance.

- Strength: Systematic investigation of two attention strategies, explaining differences in precision vs. recall.

- Weakness: Method relies on argument information at training and assumes availability at test time, which is unrealistic for real-world ED.

- Weakness: Claims about joint models not leveraging arguments are unsupported and some cited joint models do use argument info.

- Weakness: Inconsistent treatment of data sparsity—blaming sparsity for unsupervised attention failure while using sparse FrameNet to augment performance without explanation.

- Weakness: Missing comparisons to recent neural ED approaches (e.g., Chen et al. 2016, Zhao et al. 2016), weakening claims of superiority.

- Question: How would the approach work in real-world inference when argument information is unavailable—predict arguments jointly or modify training?

- Question: Can the authors provide concrete literature examples showing pre-prediction of triggers without using argument information to support their claim?

- Question: How exactly does FrameNet data alleviate data sparsity for ED given FrameNet’s own sparsity?

- Question: Why does ANN-S2 yield higher recall but lower precision than ANN-S1, and in what scenarios is that trade-off preferable?

- Overall recommendation: Interesting and well-validated conceptual contribution, but practical limitations and unsupported claims merit rejection unless addressed.
