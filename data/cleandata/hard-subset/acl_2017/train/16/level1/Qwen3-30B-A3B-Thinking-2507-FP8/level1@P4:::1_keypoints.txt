- Proposes using supervised attention that explicitly leverages argument information to improve event detection (ED).
- Contributions: diagnoses joint models' failure to use arguments effectively, introduces supervised attention for arguments, and reports performance gains on ACE 2005.
- Strength: Presents a convincing empirical analysis showing joint models underuse arguments due to unbalanced data and AE-focused usage.
- Strength: Technically sound supervised attention with ablation study showing large F1 gains when using argument information.
- Strength: Compares two attention strategies (S1: argument words only; S2: arguments + context) and explains their precision–recall trade-off.
- Strength: Demonstrates consistent, statistically significant improvements over baselines (ANN-S2 achieves best F1 on ACE 2005).
- Strength: Shows robustness to data sparsity via FrameNet augmentation, yielding slight additional gains.
- Weakness: Method depends on gold argument annotations (attention vectors) that are not available at inference in real-world use.
- Weakness: Unclear distinction between the claimed “explicit” argument exploitation and what joint learning/attention-based joint models could achieve.
- Weakness: Lacks detailed error analysis to show when and why the approach succeeds or fails relative to baselines.
- Weakness: Evaluation is limited to ACE 2005 and lacks per-event-type, rare-event, and cross-domain analyses.
- Weakness: Comparison set is incomplete—omits some recent (2016–2017) neural methods for a fuller SOTA comparison.
- Question: How can the method be applied when argument annotations are unavailable at test time?
- Question: Please clarify how this “explicit” use of arguments fundamentally differs from joint models that might incorporate attention.
- Question: How were FrameNet events transformed or aligned to be compatible with ACE annotations for training?
