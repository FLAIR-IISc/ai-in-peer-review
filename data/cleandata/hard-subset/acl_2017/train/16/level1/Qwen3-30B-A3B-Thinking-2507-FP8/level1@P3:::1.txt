

# Peer Review for ACL 2017

## Summary and Contributions

This paper proposes to leverage argument information explicitly for event detection (ED) via supervised attention mechanisms. The authors argue that existing approaches either ignore arguments or use them indirectly in joint models, and they demonstrate that explicit use of arguments can significantly improve ED performance. The main contributions, as I see them, are: (1) the compelling demonstration that argument information is crucial for resolving ambiguity in event detection (e.g., "fired" as End-Position vs. Attack), (2) the supervised attention mechanism that explicitly uses argument information to guide attention during ED, and (3) the systematic investigation of two attention strategies (S1 and S2) that produce significant improvements over state-of-the-art.

## Strengths

1. **Conceptual Insight**: The paper makes a strong, well-illustrated argument that argument information is underutilized in ED. The "fired" example is particularly compelling and demonstrates a clear, practical benefit of using argument information. This insight addresses a genuine gap in the literature, as most prior work either ignores arguments or uses them indirectly in joint models where ED performance doesn't improve.

2. **Empirical Validation**: The experimental results are robust and well-structured. The ablation study in Table 2 conclusively shows that argument information (not just entity information) is necessary for the improvements. The main results in Table 3 demonstrate that the proposed approach (ANN-S2) achieves the best F1 score (71.7) on ACE 2005, significantly outperforming previous state-of-the-art methods (e.g., Liu's FN-Based at 70.7). The statistical significance (p < 0.05) strengthens these claims.

3. **Systematic Investigation**: The paper thoroughly investigates two distinct attention strategies (S1 and S2) and provides a clear explanation for their different performance characteristics (higher precision vs. higher recall). This systematic approach demonstrates a deep understanding of the problem space and adds value beyond a simple methodological proposal.

## Weaknesses

1. **Argument Information Availability**: The core limitation is that the proposed method relies on argument information being available during training. However, in real-world ED scenarios, argument information would not be available at test time, as arguments are part of the event extraction task (not the detection task). The paper does not address how this limitation would be overcome in practical applications. This undermines the practical significance of the contribution, as the method cannot be applied without the very information it's supposed to help detect.

2. **Mischaracterization of Joint Models**: The paper claims that joint models fail to use argument information for ED because "when pre-predicting potential triggers, almost all existing approaches do not leverage any argument information." However, the authors do not provide evidence for this claim. In fact, some joint models (e.g., Li et al. 2013, Nguyen et al. 2016) explicitly use argument information to inform trigger prediction. The paper's analysis of why joint models underperform on ED is superficial and not sufficiently supported.

3. **Inconsistent Treatment of Data Sparsity**: The paper claims that data sparsity is the reason unsupervised attention (ANN) performs poorly (Section 4.3), yet they use FrameNet data to augment performance (Section 4.4). However, FrameNet data is also sparse for many event types, as noted by Liu et al. (2016a). The paper doesn't explain why FrameNet data, which is similarly sparse, improves performance when unsupervised attention mechanisms fail due to sparsity. This creates a contradiction in the paper's own reasoning.

4. **Lack of Comparison with Recent Neural Approaches**: The paper compares with several state-of-the-art methods, but it misses several recent neural approaches to ED that might have used argument information in different ways. For example, the paper does not compare with work by Chen et al. (2016) or Zhao et al. (2016), which also explore neural approaches to event detection. This omission weakens the claim that the proposed method is the best.

## Questions to Authors

1. How would your approach handle real-world ED scenarios where argument information is unavailable during inference? Could you propose a way to predict argument information alongside event triggers, or would you need to modify the training procedure?

2. In your analysis of joint models, could you provide specific examples from the literature where the pre-prediction of triggers does not leverage argument information? The claim that "almost all existing approaches do not leverage any argument information" needs stronger empirical support.

3. The paper states that FrameNet data is used to "alleviate the data-sparseness problem for ED." However, FrameNet data is also sparse for many event types. How does FrameNet data specifically address the data sparsity issue that the paper claims prevents unsupervised attention from working well?

4. The paper mentions that ANN-S2 achieves higher recall than ANN-S1, but at the cost of slightly lower precision. Could you elaborate on why this trade-off occurs and whether there are scenarios where the higher recall of ANN-S2 would be more valuable than the higher precision of ANN-S1?

## Overall Recommendation

This paper makes a valuable conceptual contribution by demonstrating that argument information is useful for event detection, particularly for ambiguous triggers. The experimental results are strong, and the proposed supervised attention mechanism is well-motivated. However, the method's reliance on argument information that would not be available at test time significantly limits its practical applicability. The paper also contains some unsubstantiated claims about joint models and inconsistent treatment of data sparsity. While the contribution is interesting, the limitations prevent it from being a major advance in the field. I recommend rejection, but with the understanding that the core insight about argument information being useful for ED could be developed into a stronger contribution with further work addressing the limitations above.
