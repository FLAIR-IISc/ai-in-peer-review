

# Review of "Exploiting Argument Information to Improve Event Detection via Supervised Attention Mechanisms"

## Summary and Contributions
This paper proposes a novel approach to event detection (ED) by explicitly leveraging argument information through supervised attention mechanisms. The authors argue that existing ED methods either ignore arguments or use them indirectly in joint learning frameworks, despite arguments providing crucial clues for ambiguous trigger words. Their main contributions are: (1) identifying the problem of argument information underutilization in ED; (2) proposing a supervised attention mechanism that directly uses argument information to improve ED; and (3) systematically evaluating different attention strategies for this purpose. The approach constructs gold attention vectors based on annotated arguments and uses them to supervise the attention mechanism during training.

## Strengths

1. **Novel insight on argument information**: The paper makes a compelling case that arguments are underutilized in ED despite their clear value for ambiguous triggers (e.g., "fired" as End-Position vs. Attack). This is well-argued with concrete examples and the critical observation that joint models primarily benefit AE, not ED, because arguments are used for AE but not for ED. This insight addresses a genuine gap in the literature and provides a clear rationale for the proposed approach.

2. **Effective and well-motivated methodology**: The supervised attention mechanism is elegantly designed and directly addresses the identified problem. The two attention strategies (S1 and S2) provide a systematic exploration of how to best leverage argument information. The ablation study in Section 4.2 effectively demonstrates that argument information is indeed crucial for ED (ANN-Gold1/2 significantly outperform ANN-ENT), which strongly supports the paper's central claim.

3. **Strong empirical results**: The proposed approach (ANN-S2) achieves state-of-the-art performance on ACE 2005 (71.7% F1 vs. previous best 70.7%), with a statistically significant improvement (p<0.05). The results are comprehensive, including ablation studies, comparison with multiple baselines (both pipeline and joint methods), and augmentation with FrameNet data. The consistent improvement across different metrics (especially precision, which is notably higher than state-of-the-art) demonstrates robustness.

## Weaknesses

1. **Insufficient justification for joint model limitations**: The paper claims that joint models fail to improve ED because they use arguments for AE, not ED. However, the paper doesn't provide a detailed analysis of why existing joint models can't be adapted to use arguments for ED. For instance, why couldn't joint models incorporate the attention mechanism proposed in this paper? Without addressing this, the criticism of joint models remains somewhat superficial.

2. **Limited discussion of attention construction**: The paper describes S1 and S2 strategies but doesn't sufficiently address edge cases. How are gold attention vectors constructed when there are multiple arguments? What happens when no arguments are present (as mentioned in S1)? The paper states "for candidates without any annotated arguments in context, we force all entities to average the whole attention" but doesn't explain why this specific approach was chosen or how it affects model performance.

3. **Limited comparison with more recent work**: The paper cites Nguyen et al. (2016) as the best joint model, but doesn't compare with more recent state-of-the-art approaches that might have been published after the cited works. For example, the paper doesn't include comparisons with methods like those proposed by Li et al. (2017) or others that might have improved upon the cited baselines.

## Questions to Authors

1. How would your supervised attention mechanism integrate with existing joint models to directly use arguments for ED rather than just for AE? Would this require significant architectural changes?

2. In cases with multiple arguments, how do you handle the gold attention vector construction? Does the model learn to attend to multiple argument regions simultaneously?

3. Could you provide more details on the FrameNet processing pipeline? Specifically, how did you handle frame elements that don't correspond directly to ACE event arguments, and how did you evaluate the quality of the automatically extracted FrameNet events?

## Overall Assessment
This paper makes a valuable contribution by identifying and addressing a significant gap in the event detection literature. The proposed supervised attention mechanism is well-motivated, the experimental evaluation is thorough, and the results demonstrate clear improvements over state-of-the-art methods. The paper's core insight—that argument information should be explicitly used for ED rather than ignored or used indirectly—is compelling and well-supported. While the weaknesses noted above are important, they don't undermine the paper's main contribution. The work provides a clear, actionable approach that could influence future event detection research. I recommend acceptance.
