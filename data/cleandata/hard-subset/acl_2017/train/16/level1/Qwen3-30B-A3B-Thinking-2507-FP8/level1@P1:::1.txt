

# Peer Review

## Summary
The paper proposes a novel approach to event detection (ED) that explicitly leverages argument information through a supervised attention mechanism. The authors argue that arguments provide significant clues for ED, especially for ambiguous trigger words, and that existing approaches either ignore arguments or use them indirectly in joint models. They introduce a neural network model with a supervised attention mechanism where argument words are encouraged to receive higher attention. The authors systematically investigate two strategies for constructing gold attention vectors based on annotated arguments. Experiments on the ACE 2005 dataset show that their approach achieves state-of-the-art performance with a 71.7 F1 score.

## Main Contributions (as I see them)
1. The key contribution is the supervised attention mechanism that explicitly uses argument information to improve ED, which represents a novel approach to the problem.
2. The systematic investigation of different attention strategies (S1 and S2) to construct gold attention vectors based on argument information.
3. The empirical demonstration that argument information is beneficial for ED, challenging the common practice of ignoring arguments in ED models.

## Strengths
1. The paper makes a compelling case for why argument information is valuable for ED, with clear examples (e.g., "fired" as ambiguous trigger word) that effectively illustrate the problem. This is a strong motivation for the work and addresses an important gap in the literature.

2. The experimental results are robust and show significant improvements over state-of-the-art methods (71.7 F1 vs 70.7 for Liu's FN-Based approach). The t-test showing statistical significance (p < 0.05) provides strong evidence for the effectiveness of the proposed method.

3. The analysis of why joint models don't improve ED much is insightful and valuable. The authors correctly identify that joint models use arguments for argument extraction (AE) but not for ED, which explains the limited improvements in ED performance. This is a meaningful contribution to the field's understanding of the problem.

4. The method is elegant and relatively simple, using a supervised attention mechanism that can be added to existing ED frameworks without major architectural changes. This makes it easy to adopt and integrate with other systems.

## Weaknesses
1. The paper lacks sufficient evidence for the claim that existing joint models don't leverage argument information for ED. The authors state this as a fact but don't provide specific examples from the literature to support this claim. Without concrete evidence, this key premise of the paper is not fully justified.

2. The paper doesn't compare with more recent state-of-the-art methods published after 2016. For example, it cites Liu et al. (2016) as the best-reported feature-based system, but doesn't compare with methods like the 2016 ACL paper by Chen et al. or other subsequent work that might have surpassed these results, limiting the significance of the claimed "state-of-the-art" performance.

3. The FrameNet augmentation approach is mentioned as crucial to their results but isn't explained in sufficient detail. The paper doesn't address how they handle the mismatch between FrameNet and ACE 2005 event types and argument structures, which could significantly affect the quality of the augmented data and the validity of their results.

4. The paper doesn't adequately address the data sparsity problem for ED. While they mention it as a reason why unsupervised attention doesn't work well, they don't provide a thorough analysis of the data statistics or how their method specifically addresses this problem beyond using FrameNet augmentation.

## Questions to Authors
1. Could you provide specific examples from the literature (e.g., from Li et al., 2013 or Nguyen et al., 2016) that illustrate how existing joint models implement their attention mechanisms without leveraging argument information for ED?

2. The paper claims state-of-the-art performance, but doesn't compare with methods published after 2016. Could you clarify if you've compared with more recent work, and if not, why you chose to focus on the methods cited in the paper?

3. Could you elaborate on how you handle the mismatch between FrameNet and ACE 2005 event types and argument structures during the augmentation process? Specifically, how do you map FrameNet frame elements to ACE event arguments?
