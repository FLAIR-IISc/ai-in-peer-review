This paper proposes a neural network approach to event detection (ED) that explicitly leverages argument information via supervised attention mechanisms. The authors argue that existing joint models for event extraction (ED and argument extraction) don’t effectively utilize argument information for ED, and propose a method to directly incorporate argument clues into the detection process using attention. They demonstrate improvements over state-of-the-art methods on the ACE 2005 dataset.

**Contributions:**

1. **Highlighting the underutilization of argument information in existing joint models for ED:** The paper correctly points out that existing joint ED/AE models often achieve limited improvements in ED performance despite including argument information. The analysis of why this occurs – data imbalance and indirect utilization of arguments – is a significant contribution, framing the need for the proposed work.
2. **Supervised attention mechanism for integrating argument information into ED:**  The proposed approach of using supervised attention, guided by annotated arguments, to focus on relevant parts of the sentence for event detection is a valuable technique.  The exploration of different strategies for constructing "gold attentions" (S1 and S2) adds to the contribution.
3. **State-of-the-art performance on ACE 2005:** Achieving the best reported F1 score on the widely used ACE 2005 dataset provides strong empirical evidence supporting the effectiveness of the proposed method.

**Strengths:**

1. **Clear Motivation and Problem Definition:** The paper clearly articulates the problem of inefficient argument utilization in existing joint models. The motivating example (Mohamad fired Anwar…) is well-chosen and effectively illustrates how argument information can disambiguate event triggers.
2. **Sound Technical Approach:**  The proposed supervised attention mechanism is a logical and well-defined way to integrate argument information. The use of gold attention vectors as supervision is a sensible strategy.  The different strategies (S1 and S2) for creating these vectors demonstrate thoughtful consideration of how argument information can be best utilized.
3. **Comprehensive Experimental Evaluation:**  The paper includes a thorough experimental evaluation, comparing the proposed approach to several state-of-the-art methods. The ablation studies (ANN, ANN-S1, ANN-S2) effectively demonstrate the contributions of the different components of the proposed system.  The augmentation with FrameNet data further strengthens the evaluation.
4. **Strong Empirical Results:** The reported performance improvements on the ACE 2005 dataset are significant and convincing. The t-test result indicates the statistical significance of the improvements.
5. **Well-Written and Organized:** The paper is generally well-written and organized, making it easy to understand the proposed approach and experimental results.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Novelty of Attention Mechanism:** While the application of supervised attention to argument-aware event detection is a reasonable approach, the core attention mechanism itself is not novel. The primary contribution lies in *how* the attention is supervised with argument information, rather than the attention mechanism itself. This should be acknowledged more explicitly.
2. **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** The significant difference in performance between ANN-S1 and ANN-S2 (and the differing lambda values) suggests a sensitivity to hyperparameters. A more detailed analysis of hyperparameter tuning and sensitivity would be beneficial. While the authors mention tuning, the process isn’t described in detail.
3. **FrameNet Augmentation Details:**  The description of the FrameNet augmentation process is somewhat brief.  Specifically, the process of applying RPI to add entity information is not fully explained, and the potential impact of RPI’s performance on the overall results isn’t discussed. Were there any errors introduced during entity labeling that might have affected results?
4. **Lack of Error Analysis:**  The paper would benefit from a more detailed error analysis. Identifying the types of events or triggers that the proposed method still struggles with would provide valuable insights and suggest directions for future research.



**Questions to Authors:**

1.  Could you provide more details about the hyperparameter tuning process, including the range of values explored and the specific criteria used for selection?
2.  Could you elaborate on the process of using RPI to add entity information to the FrameNet data? What was the performance of RPI on this task, and did you observe any errors that might have influenced the results?
3.  Could you provide a more detailed error analysis, identifying the types of events or triggers that the proposed method still struggles with?
4.  Given that the attention mechanism isn’t novel, how do you believe this work advances the field beyond simply applying a known technique to a new task?



**Additional Comments:**

*   The citation style is a little inconsistent (e.g., sometimes including "pages" after conference proceedings, sometimes not).
*   The concluding paragraph repeats information already stated in the introduction and summary of contributions. It could be more concise or provide a more forward-looking perspective.
