- Paper proposes using event arguments via supervised attention for event detection and reports state-of-the-art results on ACE 2005.  
- Primary contribution: showing that explicitly incorporating argument information with supervised attention improves ED performance.  
- Secondary contribution: systematic comparison of different supervised attention strategies.  
- Strength: Clear motivation and problem definition, arguing why joint ED/AE models fail to leverage arguments for ED.  
- Strength: Strong empirical results (SOTA on ACE 2005) with informative ablation studies.  
- Strength: Well-defined, logically structured model using gold attention vectors as supervision.  
- Strength: Thorough experimentation including baselines, ablations, and FrameNet augmentation.  
- Weakness: Limited novelty in core components—neural ED and attention mechanisms are not themselves new.  
- Weakness: Performance sensitive to λ (attention vs. ED loss) with seemingly arbitrary chosen values.  
- Weakness: Evaluation restricted to ACE 2005, raising generalization concerns to other datasets/domains.  
- Weakness: Limited qualitative analysis of learned attention weights; few examples shown to explain model behavior.  
- Question: Please provide a detailed sensitivity analysis for the λ hyperparameter.  
- Question: Please show examples of attention weight distributions for different event types.  
- Question: Have more sophisticated attention mechanisms (e.g., self-attention) been explored?  
- Question: What is the computational overhead introduced by the attention mechanism in practical settings?  
- Additional comment: References are well-formatted and relevant.  
- Additional comment: Writing is generally clear and concise.  
- Additional comment: Consider adding discussion of error cases and future directions in the conclusion.
