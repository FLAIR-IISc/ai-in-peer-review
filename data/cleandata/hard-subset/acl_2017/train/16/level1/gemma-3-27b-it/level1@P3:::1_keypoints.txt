- Paper argues existing event detection (ED) models underutilize annotated argument information and motivates using arguments explicitly.  
- Proposes a supervised attention mechanism guided by annotated arguments to improve trigger identification and classification.  
- Explores two strategies (S1, S2) for constructing gold attention vectors to supervise attention.  
- Evaluates on ACE 2005 and reports state-of-the-art performance with extensive experiments and ablation studies.  
- Augments experiments with FrameNet data to bolster empirical findings.  
- Clear motivation and illustrative examples (e.g., "fired") showing arguments help disambiguate triggers.  
- Methodology is sensible: supervised attention is an appropriate way to incorporate argument signals.  
- Comprehensive experimental setup comparing against multiple baselines and including ANN, ANN-ENT, ANN-S1, ANN-S2 ablations.  
- Strong empirical gains on a standard benchmark demonstrate practical effectiveness.  
- Paper is well-written, organized, and situates itself within related work.  
- Attention mechanism itself lacks novelty; main contribution is supervision strategy rather than a new attention model.  
- Performance is sensitive to how gold attention is constructed (S1 vs. S2), indicating robustness concerns.  
- Reliance on ACE 2005 (limited size, annotation issues) raises questions about generalizability; additional datasets would help.  
- Paper lacks detailed error analysis to reveal common failure modes and guide future improvements.  
- Questions: why does S2 outperform S1, computational complexity and scalability, alternative methods for gold attention construction, and handling class imbalance explicitly?  
- Additional issues: incomplete references, Figure 2 needs clearer labels, and the paper should include example model predictions to show attention behavior.
