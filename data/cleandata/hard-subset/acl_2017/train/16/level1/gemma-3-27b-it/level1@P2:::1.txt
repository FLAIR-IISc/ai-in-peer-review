This paper tackles event detection (ED) by explicitly incorporating argument information via supervised attention mechanisms. The authors argue that existing joint event and argument extraction models don’t fully leverage arguments for ED, and propose a neural network architecture where attention is directed towards argument words during trigger classification. They explore different attention strategies and report state-of-the-art results on the ACE 2005 dataset.

**Contributions:**

1. **Highlighting the underutilization of argument information in joint ED models:** The paper correctly points out that while joint models *include* arguments, they don't necessarily *use* them effectively for improving event detection performance. The observation, backed by the performance comparison in Table 1, is a valuable contribution.
2. **Supervised attention mechanism for incorporating argument clues:** Introducing a supervised attention mechanism, guided by annotated arguments, is a reasonable and potentially effective approach. The different attention strategies (S1 and S2) offer a systematic exploration of how to leverage argument information.
3. **Strong empirical results on ACE 2005:** The reported state-of-the-art performance on the ACE 2005 dataset provides evidence that the proposed approach is competitive and effective.  Augmenting with FrameNet data further strengthens these results.

**Strengths:**

1. **Clear motivation and problem definition:** The paper clearly articulates the problem of underutilized argument information in ED and provides a compelling justification for their approach. The example with “fired” effectively illustrates the benefit of considering arguments for disambiguation.
2. **Systematic exploration of attention strategies:** Investigating different strategies (S1 and S2) for constructing the "gold" attention vectors demonstrates a thoroughness in exploring the design space. This allows for a nuanced understanding of how different argument representations influence performance.
3. **Solid experimental validation:**  The experiments are well-designed, with comparisons to strong baselines and state-of-the-art methods.  The inclusion of FrameNet augmentation and ablation studies (comparing ANN, ANN-S1, and ANN-S2) provides a comprehensive evaluation. The t-test further supports the significance of the reported improvements.
4. **Well-written and organized:** The paper is generally well-written and easy to follow. The architecture is clearly explained, and the experimental setup is described in detail.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited novelty of the core technical contribution:** While the application to event detection is novel, the use of attention mechanisms in neural networks is not. The supervised attention approach, while well-executed, isn't a groundbreaking technical innovation in itself. The core novelty lies in applying this to the specific problem of leveraging argument information for ED.
2. **Lack of analysis on *why* the attention mechanism works:** The paper demonstrates *that* the attention mechanism improves performance, but offers limited insight into *why* certain words receive higher attention. Visualizing the attention weights or conducting a more detailed error analysis could reveal which types of arguments are most helpful and how the model is leveraging them.
3. **Dependence on ACE 2005:** The evaluation is solely based on the ACE 2005 dataset. While this is a standard benchmark, it would be beneficial to see results on other datasets to assess the generalizability of the approach. ACE 2005 is also relatively small and may not fully capture the complexity of real-world event detection tasks.
4. **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** The paper mentions hyperparameter tuning, but doesn’t detail the process extensively. A sensitivity analysis of the λ parameter (controlling the trade-off between ED and attention loss) would be helpful to understand the robustness of the results.



**Questions to Authors:**

1. Can you provide examples of attention weights learned by the model, highlighting which argument words receive the most attention for different event types?
2.  Did you observe any specific event types where the argument information was particularly helpful or detrimental?
3.  Could you discuss the computational cost of the attention mechanism, and how it scales with the length of the sentence and the number of arguments?
4.  Have you experimented with other methods for constructing "gold" attention vectors besides S1 and S2?



**Additional Comments:**

* The discussion of related work could be more comprehensive, especially regarding other attention-based approaches in NLP.
* The code and data used for the experiments should be made publicly available to facilitate reproducibility.
* Consider adding a brief discussion of the limitations of the approach and potential directions for future research.



Overall, this is a solid paper that presents a reasonable and well-executed approach to improving event detection by explicitly leveraging argument information. While the technical novelty is somewhat limited, the clarity of the presentation, the systematic experiments, and the strong empirical results make this a worthwhile contribution. I recommend acceptance.
