Contributions:
- Points out that joint event-argument models underutilize argument information for event detection.
- Introduces supervised attention guided by annotated arguments and compares two strategies (S1, S2).
- Reports state-of-the-art results on ACE 2005, improved further with FrameNet augmentation.

Strengths:
- Clear motivation and illustrative example showing why arguments help disambiguate triggers.
- Systematic exploration of attention strategies with ablation comparisons (ANN, ANN-S1, ANN-S2).
- Strong experimental validation including baselines, augmentation, ablations, and t-test significance.
- Well-written and clearly organized presentation.

Weaknesses:
- Limited technical novelty: supervised attention is not a fundamentally new technique, novelty mainly in application.
- Insufficient analysis of why attention improves performance; lacks attention visualizations and detailed error analysis.
- Evaluation restricted to ACE 2005, raising concerns about generalizability to other datasets.
- Missing sensitivity analysis for hyperparameters (notably the λ trade-off between ED and attention loss).

Questions to authors:
- Can you provide examples or visualizations of learned attention weights for different event types?
- Which event types benefit most or least from using argument information?
- What is the computational cost and how does it scale with sentence length and number of arguments?
- Have you tried other ways to construct gold attention vectors beyond S1 and S2?

Additional comments:
- Expand related work coverage on other attention-based NLP approaches.
- Release code and data to enable reproducibility.
- Add a discussion of limitations and directions for future work.

Overall:
- Solid, well-executed paper with strong empirical results; recommend acceptance.
