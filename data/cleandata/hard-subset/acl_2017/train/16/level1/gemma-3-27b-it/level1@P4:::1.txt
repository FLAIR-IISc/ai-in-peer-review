This paper addresses the event detection (ED) task, arguing that event arguments provide valuable clues often overlooked in existing approaches. The authors propose a neural network-based approach that explicitly exploits argument information via supervised attention mechanisms, investigating different attention strategies. They report state-of-the-art results on the ACE 2005 dataset.

The primary contribution of this work is the demonstration of the effectiveness of explicitly incorporating argument information, guided by supervised attention, for improving event detection performance. While joint ED and AE models exist, the authors convincingly argue (and demonstrate empirically) that these models don’t effectively leverage argument information *for* ED. The proposed approach, by focusing attention on arguments during ED, achieves improved results. A secondary contribution is the systematic investigation of different attention strategies within the proposed framework. Finally, achieving a new state-of-the-art on the ACE 2005 dataset provides a strong empirical validation of the proposed method.

**Strengths:**

1. **Clear Motivation and Problem Definition:** The paper clearly articulates the limitations of existing ED approaches, particularly joint ED and AE models, and convincingly argues for the benefit of explicitly leveraging argument information. The authors effectively highlight why previous joint models haven’t successfully used arguments for ED.
2. **Solid Empirical Results:** The proposed method achieves state-of-the-art performance on the ACE 2005 dataset, demonstrating its effectiveness. The ablation studies (comparing different attention strategies and the impact of FrameNet data) provide valuable insights into the contribution of each component.
3. **Well-Defined Approach:** The proposed architecture, combining context representation learning with event detection and supervised attention, is clearly explained and logically structured. The use of gold attention vectors as supervision is a reasonable and effective approach.
4. **Thorough Experimentation:**  The paper includes a comprehensive experimental setup, including comparisons with several baselines and ablation studies. The inclusion of the FrameNet augmentation further strengthens the results.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Novelty in Core Components:** While the combination of argument information and attention is effective, the individual components – neural networks for ED, attention mechanisms – are not novel. The novelty lies in the *application* to this specific problem and the demonstration of its effectiveness, rather than a breakthrough in underlying techniques.
2. **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** The performance gains seem sensitive to the choice of the λ parameter (trade-off between the attention loss and event detection loss). A more detailed analysis of the hyperparameter space and its impact on performance would be beneficial.  The justification for choosing λ=1.0 for S1 and λ=5.0 for S2 feels somewhat arbitrary.
3. **ACE 2005 Dataset Limitation:** The exclusive evaluation on the ACE 2005 dataset is a limitation. While it’s a widely used benchmark, the results may not generalize to other datasets or domains. Evaluation on a more diverse set of datasets would strengthen the claims.
4. **Limited Analysis of Attention Weights:** While the paper demonstrates the improvement in F1 score, a more detailed qualitative analysis of the learned attention weights would provide further insight into *how* the model is leveraging argument information. Showing examples of attention distributions for different event types could illustrate the model’s behavior.



**Questions to Authors:**

1. Could you provide a more detailed analysis of the hyperparameter space, specifically for the λ parameter? What is the sensitivity of the results to different values of λ?
2. Could you provide examples of attention weight distributions for a few different event types, demonstrating how the model focuses on relevant arguments?
3.  Have you explored the possibility of using more sophisticated attention mechanisms (e.g., self-attention) in your model?
4. What is the computational overhead introduced by the attention mechanism, and is it significant for practical applications?

**Additional Comments:**

* The references are well-formatted and relevant.
* The writing is generally clear and concise.
* Consider including a discussion of potential error cases and future directions in the conclusion.
