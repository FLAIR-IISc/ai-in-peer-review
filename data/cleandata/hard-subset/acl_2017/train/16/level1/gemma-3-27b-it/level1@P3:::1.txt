This paper addresses the event detection (ED) task, arguing that existing approaches underutilize argument information. The authors propose a neural network-based approach with supervised attention mechanisms, explicitly leveraging annotated arguments to improve event trigger identification and categorization. They present extensive experiments on the ACE 2005 dataset, demonstrating state-of-the-art performance.

**Contributions:**

1. **Highlighting the Under-Exploitation of Argument Information:** The paper convincingly argues that while arguments contain crucial clues for event detection, they are largely ignored or indirectly utilized in existing ED systems, particularly in joint ED and AE models. This observation provides a strong motivation for the proposed work.
2. **Supervised Attention Mechanism for Argument Exploitation:** The introduction of a supervised attention mechanism, guided by annotated arguments, is a sensible and potentially effective approach to integrate argument information into the ED process.  The exploration of different strategies (S1 and S2) for constructing "gold" attention vectors adds to the contribution.
3. **Strong Empirical Results:** The paper reports state-of-the-art performance on the ACE 2005 dataset, validating the effectiveness of the proposed method. The ablation studies and experiments with FrameNet augmentation provide further insights into the method’s strengths.

**Strengths:**

1. **Clear Motivation and Problem Definition:** The paper clearly articulates the problem of underutilized argument information in ED and provides a compelling justification for its research. The example provided with "fired" effectively illustrates the benefit of considering arguments for disambiguation.
2. **Sound Methodology:** The use of supervised attention is a well-motivated and appropriate technique for incorporating argument information. The exploration of two different strategies for generating the gold attention vectors demonstrates a thoughtful approach to model design.
3. **Comprehensive Experiments and Analysis:** The authors conducted extensive experiments on the ACE 2005 dataset, comparing their approach with multiple baselines and state-of-the-art methods. The inclusion of ablation studies (ANN, ANN-ENT, ANN-S1, ANN-S2) provides valuable insights into the contribution of each component. The augmentation with FrameNet data further strengthens the empirical evaluation.
4. **Strong Performance:** Achieving state-of-the-art results on a widely used benchmark demonstrates the practical effectiveness of the proposed approach. The reported improvements over existing methods are substantial.
5. **Well-written and Organized:** The paper is well-written, clearly structured, and easy to follow. The related work section provides a good overview of the existing literature.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Novelty in the Attention Mechanism:** While the application of supervised attention to ED with argument information is valuable, the attention mechanism itself isn’t particularly novel. Standard attention mechanisms are widely used in NLP, and the core innovation lies in *how* the attention is supervised. The paper could benefit from a more nuanced discussion of the novelty compared to existing attention-based methods.
2. **Sensitivity to Gold Attention Construction:** The performance seems quite sensitive to the chosen strategy for constructing gold attention vectors (S1 vs. S2).  While both strategies improve performance, the difference in results suggests that the method is not robust to variations in how argument information is represented in the attention mechanism.  Further investigation into more robust attention supervision techniques would be beneficial.
3. **ACE 2005 Dataset Limitations:** The ACE 2005 dataset is known to have limitations, including relatively small size and potential annotation inconsistencies. While the authors address this somewhat with the FrameNet augmentation, it would be valuable to acknowledge these limitations and discuss the potential for generalizing the results to more diverse and challenging datasets.  Evaluations on other ED datasets would strengthen the findings.
4. **Lack of Error Analysis:** The paper lacks a detailed error analysis. Identifying common error cases and analyzing the reasons for the errors could provide valuable insights into the strengths and weaknesses of the proposed approach and suggest avenues for future improvement.



**Questions to Authors:**

1.  Regarding the sensitivity to the gold attention construction strategies (S1 vs. S2), could you elaborate on why S2 consistently outperforms S1? What characteristics of the arguments and surrounding context contribute to this difference in performance?
2.  Could you discuss the computational complexity of the proposed approach, particularly the attention mechanism? How does it scale with the length of the sentence and the number of potential trigger candidates?
3.  Have you considered other methods for constructing the gold attention vectors beyond S1 and S2? For instance, could you explore using learned weights or attention distributions derived from a separate model trained specifically on argument-trigger relationships?
4.  The paper briefly mentions data imbalance between triggers and arguments. While addressed indirectly via the attention mechanism, have you explored explicit techniques for handling this imbalance, such as weighted loss functions or data augmentation?




**Additional Comments:**
