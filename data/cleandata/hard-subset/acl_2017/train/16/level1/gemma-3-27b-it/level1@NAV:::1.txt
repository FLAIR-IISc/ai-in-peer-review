## Review for "Exploiting Argument Information to Improve Event Detection via Supervised Attention Mechanisms"

This paper addresses the event detection (ED) task, arguing that existing methods underutilize argument information. The authors propose a neural network approach with supervised attention mechanisms, specifically designed to leverage arguments for improved ED performance. They investigate different attention strategies and demonstrate state-of-the-art results on the ACE 2005 dataset, also augmenting with FrameNet data.

The primary contribution of this work, as I see it, is the focused empirical investigation demonstrating the benefit of *explicitly* incorporating argument information into an attention-based ED model. While joint models have touched on this, the authors convincingly argue that previous approaches haven't truly leveraged arguments for ED, and their supervised attention mechanism offers a more direct approach. A secondary contribution lies in the systematic exploration of different attention strategies (S1 & S2) and their impact on performance, offering insights into how argument information can be best integrated. The positive results on ACE2005, and the improvement with FrameNet augmentation, provide further validation.

**Strengths:**

1. **Strong Empirical Results:** The paper achieves state-of-the-art performance on the ACE 2005 dataset, a well-established benchmark for ED. This provides strong evidence of the effectiveness of the proposed approach.
2. **Clear Motivation & Argument:** The authors clearly articulate the limitations of existing joint models in effectively utilizing argument information for ED and provide a compelling rationale for their approach.
3. **Systematic Investigation:** The systematic exploration of different attention strategies (S1 & S2) contributes to a deeper understanding of how to best incorporate argument information into the ED model. The comparison highlights trade-offs between precision and recall.
4. **Data Augmentation:** The inclusion of experiments leveraging data from FrameNet demonstrates the adaptability and robustness of the proposed approach and addresses the data sparsity issue in ED.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Novelty in Core Architecture:** The underlying neural network architecture (ANN with attention) is fairly standard. The novelty resides primarily in the application of supervised attention specifically targeting argument information.  The paper could benefit from a more detailed discussion of the architectural choices and their relationship to the specific problem.
2. **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** The optimal value of λ (trade-off parameter between loss functions) differs between the two attention strategies (S1 and S2), indicating some sensitivity and potentially requiring careful tuning for different datasets or scenarios. A discussion of this sensitivity would be beneficial.
3. **Analysis of Attention Weights:** While the paper demonstrates performance gains, it lacks detailed analysis of *what* the attention mechanism is learning. Visualizing or analyzing the attention weights would provide further insight into whether the model is truly focusing on relevant argument information.



**Questions to Authors:**

1.  Can you provide examples of attention weights learned by the model, illustrating how it focuses on argument words during event detection?
2.  The paper mentions that only 10% of ACE 2005 triggers are entities. How does the model handle situations where the trigger *is* an entity, and how does that impact the performance?
3. What is the computational overhead introduced by the supervised attention mechanism compared to a baseline model without attention?

**Additional Comments:**

*   The paper would benefit from a clearer definition of "core elements" in FrameNet when discussing data augmentation.
*   Consider adding a brief discussion of potential limitations and future research directions, such as exploring different attention mechanisms or extending the approach to event argument extraction.
