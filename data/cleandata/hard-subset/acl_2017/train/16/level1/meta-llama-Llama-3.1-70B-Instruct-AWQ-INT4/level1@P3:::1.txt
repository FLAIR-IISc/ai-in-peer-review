**Summary of the Paper**

This paper proposes a novel approach to improve event detection (ED) by exploiting argument information via supervised attention mechanisms. The authors argue that existing joint methods, which tackle ED and argument extraction (AE) simultaneously, do not effectively utilize annotated arguments for ED. They introduce a neural network-based approach that uses supervised attention to focus on argument words and demonstrate its effectiveness on the ACE 2005 dataset.

**Main Contributions**

1. **Analysis of joint methods**: The authors provide a thorough analysis of the limitations of joint methods in utilizing annotated arguments for ED.
2. **Supervised attention-based ED model**: The authors propose a novel approach that uses supervised attention to focus on argument words, which is shown to be effective in improving ED performance.
3. **Investigation of different attention strategies**: The authors investigate two strategies to construct gold attentions using annotated arguments and demonstrate their effectiveness in improving ED performance.

**Strengths**

1. **Effective utilization of argument information**: The authors propose a novel approach that effectively utilizes annotated arguments for ED, which is a significant improvement over existing joint methods.
2. **Improved performance on ACE 2005 dataset**: The authors demonstrate the effectiveness of their approach on the ACE 2005 dataset, achieving state-of-the-art performance.
3. **Thorough analysis and investigation**: The authors provide a thorough analysis of the limitations of joint methods and investigate different attention strategies, demonstrating the robustness of their approach.

**Weaknesses**

1. **Limited generalizability**: The authors only evaluate their approach on the ACE 2005 dataset, which may not be representative of other datasets or domains.
2. **Dependence on annotated arguments**: The authors' approach relies heavily on annotated arguments, which may not be available in all datasets or domains.
3. **Computational complexity**: The authors' approach may be computationally expensive, particularly when processing large datasets.

**Questions to Authors**

1. How do you plan to extend your approach to other datasets or domains, where annotated arguments may not be available?
2. Can you provide more insights into the computational complexity of your approach and potential optimizations?
3. How do you plan to address the issue of data sparsity, which is a common problem in ED tasks?

**Additional Comments**

The authors' approach is a significant improvement over existing joint methods, and their thorough analysis and investigation demonstrate the robustness of their approach. However, the limitations of their approach, such as limited generalizability and dependence on annotated arguments, need to be addressed in future work.
