**Summary of the Paper**

This paper proposes a novel approach to improve event detection (ED) by exploiting argument information via supervised attention mechanisms. The authors argue that argument information is crucial for ED, but it is either ignored or indirectly used in existing approaches. They propose a neural network-based approach that uses a supervised attention mechanism to focus on argument words when performing ED. The approach is evaluated on the ACE 2005 dataset and achieves state-of-the-art performance.

**Main Contributions**

1. **Exploiting argument information for ED**: The authors propose a novel approach to use argument information explicitly for ED, which is different from existing approaches that either ignore or indirectly use argument information.
2. **Supervised attention mechanism**: The authors introduce a supervised attention mechanism to focus on argument words when performing ED, which is shown to be effective in improving ED performance.
3. **Systematic evaluation**: The authors conduct a thorough evaluation of their approach on the ACE 2005 dataset and compare it with state-of-the-art approaches, demonstrating the effectiveness of their approach.

**Strengths**

1. **Novel approach**: The authors propose a novel approach to use argument information explicitly for ED, which is different from existing approaches.
2. **Effective use of attention mechanism**: The authors demonstrate the effectiveness of their supervised attention mechanism in improving ED performance.
3. **Thorough evaluation**: The authors conduct a thorough evaluation of their approach on the ACE 2005 dataset and compare it with state-of-the-art approaches.

**Weaknesses**

1. **Limited dataset**: The authors only evaluate their approach on the ACE 2005 dataset, which may not be representative of all ED tasks.
2. **Dependence on annotated arguments**: The authors' approach relies on annotated arguments, which may not always be available in real-world scenarios.
3. **Computational complexity**: The authors' approach may be computationally expensive due to the use of supervised attention mechanisms.

**Questions to Authors**

1. How do you plan to extend your approach to other ED tasks or datasets?
2. Can you provide more details on how you handle cases where annotated arguments are not available?
3. How do you plan to reduce the computational complexity of your approach?
