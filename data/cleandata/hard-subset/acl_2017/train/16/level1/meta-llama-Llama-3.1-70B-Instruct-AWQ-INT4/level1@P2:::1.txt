**Summary of the Paper**

The paper proposes a novel approach to event detection (ED) by exploiting argument information explicitly via supervised attention mechanisms. The authors argue that existing joint methods that tackle ED and argument extraction (AE) simultaneously often favor AE due to the unbalanced data and indirect use of argument information. In contrast, the proposed approach focuses on ED and uses annotated arguments to construct gold attention vectors, which serve as supervision to train the attention mechanism. The experimental results on the ACE 2005 dataset demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed approach, achieving the best F1 score among state-of-the-art methods.

**Main Contributions**

1. The authors propose a novel approach to ED by exploiting argument information explicitly via supervised attention mechanisms.
2. They introduce two strategies to construct gold attention vectors using annotated arguments, which serve as supervision to train the attention mechanism.
3. The proposed approach achieves the best F1 score on the ACE 2005 dataset, outperforming state-of-the-art methods.

**Strengths**

1. The paper addresses a significant limitation of existing joint methods that tackle ED and AE simultaneously, which often favor AE due to the unbalanced data and indirect use of argument information.
2. The proposed approach is novel and effective, achieving the best F1 score on the ACE 2005 dataset.
3. The authors provide a thorough analysis of the experimental results, including a comparison with state-of-the-art methods and an investigation of the effects of using different attention strategies.
4. The paper is well-written and easy to follow, with clear explanations of the proposed approach and experimental setup.

**Weaknesses**

1. The paper relies heavily on the ACE 2005 dataset, which may not be representative of other datasets or domains.
2. The authors do not provide a detailed analysis of the computational complexity of the proposed approach, which may be a concern for large-scale datasets.
3. The paper does not investigate the use of other attention mechanisms or strategies, which may be more effective or efficient.
4. The authors do not provide a clear explanation of how the proposed approach can be applied to other NLP tasks or domains.
