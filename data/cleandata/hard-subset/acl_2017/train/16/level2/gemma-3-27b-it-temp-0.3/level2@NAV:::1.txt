## Review of "Exploiting Argument Information to Improve Event Detection via Supervised Attention Mechanisms"

This paper addresses the event detection (ED) task, arguing that existing methods overlook valuable information contained within event arguments. The authors propose a novel approach that explicitly exploits argument information via supervised attention mechanisms, aiming to improve ED performance. They systematically investigate different attention strategies and demonstrate state-of-the-art results on the ACE 2005 dataset.

The primary contribution of this work is the explicit incorporation of argument information into a supervised attention mechanism for event detection. While joint ED and argument extraction models exist, the authors convincingly argue these often indirectly leverage arguments, benefiting argument extraction more than detection. The proposed approach directly focuses on using arguments to inform trigger classification, a valuable insight. A secondary contribution lies in the systematic investigation of different attention strategies within this framework, providing a nuanced understanding of how argument information can be best utilized. Finally, achieving state-of-the-art results on ACE 2005 provides empirical validation of the approach.

**Strengths:**

*   **Novelty of Approach:** The explicit focus on leveraging argument information *specifically* for event detection, rather than as a byproduct of joint learning, is a strong point.
*   **Strong Empirical Results:** Achieving state-of-the-art performance on a standard benchmark (ACE 2005) demonstrates the effectiveness of the proposed method.
*   **Clear Motivation & Analysis:** The paper provides a clear rationale for why argument information is valuable for ED, supported by an analysis of existing joint models.
*   **Systematic Evaluation:** The investigation of different attention strategies provides a more comprehensive understanding of the approach.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Limited Generalizability:** The experiments are solely conducted on the ACE 2005 dataset. Evaluating the approach on other datasets or domains would strengthen the findings.
*   **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** The choice of λ (trade-off between loss functions) seems crucial, and the justification for the specific values chosen (1.0 and 5.0) is limited. A more thorough hyperparameter analysis would be beneficial.
*   **FrameNet Augmentation Details:** The description of how FrameNet data was processed and integrated is somewhat brief. More details on the entity linking process would be helpful.
*   **Lack of Error Analysis:** A qualitative error analysis would provide insights into the types of events where the approach excels and where it struggles.

**Questions to Authors:**

1.  Could you provide a more detailed explanation of the rationale behind the chosen values for the hyperparameter λ in the experiments?
2.  What types of errors does the proposed model still make? Could you provide examples of misclassified events and discuss potential reasons for these errors?
3.  Have you considered exploring other methods for incorporating argument information beyond supervised attention, such as graph neural networks?
