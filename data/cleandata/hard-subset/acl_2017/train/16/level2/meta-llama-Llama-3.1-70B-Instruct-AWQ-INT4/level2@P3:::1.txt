**Summary of the Paper**

This paper proposes a novel approach to event detection (ED) by exploiting argument information explicitly via supervised attention mechanisms. The authors argue that existing methods either ignore or indirectly use argument information, leading to suboptimal performance. The proposed approach uses a neural network-based model with a supervised attention mechanism to focus on argument words, which are expected to provide significant clues for ED. The model is trained on the ACE 2005 dataset and achieves state-of-the-art performance.

**Main Contributions**

1. **Analysis of joint models**: The authors analyze the limitations of joint models in ED, which often favor argument extraction (AE) over ED due to the unbalanced data and indirect use of argument information.
2. **Supervised attention-based ED model**: The authors propose a novel approach to ED by using supervised attention mechanisms to focus on argument words, which are expected to provide significant clues for ED.
3. **Investigation of attention strategies**: The authors investigate two strategies to construct gold attentions using annotated arguments and demonstrate their effectiveness in improving ED performance.

**Strengths**

1. **Novel approach to ED**: The proposed approach is a significant departure from existing methods, which either ignore or indirectly use argument information.
2. **State-of-the-art performance**: The proposed approach achieves state-of-the-art performance on the ACE 2005 dataset, outperforming existing methods.
3. **Thorough analysis**: The authors provide a thorough analysis of joint models and their limitations, which is a valuable contribution to the field.

**Weaknesses**

1. **Limited dataset**: The authors only evaluate their approach on the ACE 2005 dataset, which may not be representative of other datasets or domains.
2. **Dependence on annotated arguments**: The proposed approach relies heavily on annotated arguments, which may not always be available or accurate.
3. **Computational complexity**: The authors do not provide a detailed analysis of the computational complexity of their approach, which may be a concern for large-scale datasets.

**Questions to Authors**

1. How do you plan to extend your approach to other datasets or domains, where annotated arguments may not be available or accurate?
2. Can you provide a more detailed analysis of the computational complexity of your approach and its implications for large-scale datasets?
3. How do you plan to address the potential overfitting of the supervised attention mechanism to the training data?

**Additional Comments**

The authors provide a thorough analysis of joint models and their limitations, which is a valuable contribution to the field. The proposed approach is a significant departure from existing methods and achieves state-of-the-art performance on the ACE 2005 dataset. However, the authors should consider extending their approach to other datasets or domains and providing a more detailed analysis of the computational complexity of their approach.
