**Summary of the Paper**

This paper proposes a novel approach to improve event detection (ED) by exploiting argument information via supervised attention mechanisms. The authors argue that existing joint methods for event detection and argument extraction (AE) do not effectively utilize argument information for ED, leading to insignificant improvements. They introduce a neural network-based approach that uses annotated argument information to construct gold attention vectors, which are then used to supervise the attention mechanism. The proposed approach is evaluated on the ACE 2005 dataset and achieves state-of-the-art performance.

**Main Contributions**

1. The authors propose a novel approach to exploit argument information for ED via supervised attention mechanisms.
2. They introduce two strategies to construct gold attention vectors using annotated arguments.
3. The proposed approach achieves state-of-the-art performance on the ACE 2005 dataset.

**Strengths**

1. The paper presents a novel and well-motivated approach to improve ED by exploiting argument information.
2. The authors provide a thorough analysis of existing joint methods and their limitations.
3. The proposed approach is evaluated on a widely used benchmark dataset, and the results demonstrate its effectiveness.
4. The paper is well-written and easy to follow, with clear explanations of the proposed approach and its components.

**Weaknesses**

1. The paper assumes that annotated argument information is available, which may not always be the case in real-world applications.
2. The proposed approach requires additional computational resources to construct gold attention vectors and supervise the attention mechanism.
3. The authors do not provide a detailed analysis of the computational complexity of the proposed approach.
4. The paper could benefit from a more detailed discussion of the limitations of the proposed approach and potential avenues for future work.

**Questions to Authors**

1. How do the authors plan to adapt the proposed approach to scenarios where annotated argument information is not available?
2. Can the authors provide more details on the computational complexity of the proposed approach and its scalability to large datasets?
3. How do the authors plan to extend the proposed approach to other tasks, such as event argument extraction or event coreference resolution?
4. Can the authors provide more insights into the performance of the proposed approach on different event types or genres of text?

**Additional Comments**

The paper presents a well-motivated and effective approach to improve ED by exploiting argument information. However, the authors should consider addressing the limitations of the proposed approach and providing more details on its computational complexity and scalability. Additionally, the authors could benefit from a more detailed discussion of the potential applications and extensions of the proposed approach.
