**Summary of the Paper:**

This paper proposes a novel approach to improve event detection (ED) by exploiting argument information explicitly via supervised attention mechanisms. The authors argue that argument information is crucial for ED, but existing joint methods either ignore it or use it indirectly. They propose a neural network-based approach that uses a supervised attention mechanism to focus on argument words, which are expected to acquire more attention than other words. The experimental results on the ACE 2005 dataset demonstrate that the proposed approach outperforms state-of-the-art methods, achieving the best F1 score.

**Main Contributions:**

1. The authors analyze the problem of joint models on the task of ED and propose to use annotated argument information explicitly for this task.
2. They introduce a supervised attention-based ED model that focuses on argument words.
3. They investigate two strategies to construct gold attentions using annotated arguments.

**Strengths:**

1. The paper provides a thorough analysis of the limitations of existing joint methods and proposes a novel approach to address these limitations.
2. The experimental results demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed approach, achieving state-of-the-art performance on the ACE 2005 dataset.
3. The paper provides a detailed description of the proposed approach, including the architecture of the neural network and the supervised attention mechanism.

**Weaknesses:**

1. The paper assumes that argument information is crucial for ED, but does not provide a thorough theoretical justification for this assumption.
2. The experimental results are limited to the ACE 2005 dataset, and it is unclear whether the proposed approach would generalize to other datasets.
3. The paper does not provide a detailed analysis of the computational complexity of the proposed approach, which could be a limitation for large-scale applications.

**Questions to Authors:**

1. Can you provide a more detailed theoretical justification for the assumption that argument information is crucial for ED?
2. How does the proposed approach handle cases where argument information is not available or is noisy?
3. Can you provide a more detailed analysis of the computational complexity of the proposed approach and its implications for large-scale applications?
