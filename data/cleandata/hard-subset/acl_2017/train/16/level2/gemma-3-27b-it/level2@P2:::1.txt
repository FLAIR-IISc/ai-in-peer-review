This paper tackles event detection (ED) by explicitly exploiting argument information via supervised attention mechanisms. The authors argue that existing joint ED and argument extraction models indirectly utilize argument information, leading to insignificant improvements in ED performance. They propose a neural network-based approach where attention is supervised by annotated arguments, aiming to focus on relevant information for trigger classification. The paper reports state-of-the-art results on the ACE 2005 dataset.

**Contributions:**

1. **Highlighting the underutilization of argument information in joint ED models:** The authors correctly point out that existing joint models, while incorporating arguments, primarily benefit argument extraction and offer minimal gains for ED. This observation is a valuable contribution, framing the motivation for their work.
2. **Supervised attention mechanism for ED leveraging argument information:** The proposed method of directly incorporating argument information through supervised attention is a reasonable and potentially effective approach to address the identified limitation of joint models.
3. **Empirical validation and state-of-the-art results:** The paper demonstrates improvements over existing state-of-the-art methods on the ACE 2005 dataset, providing empirical evidence for the effectiveness of the proposed approach.

**Strengths:**

1. **Clear Motivation and Problem Definition:** The paper clearly articulates the problem of underutilized argument information in existing approaches to event detection. The introductory examples effectively illustrate how argument information can disambiguate event triggers.
2. **Well-Defined Approach:** The proposed supervised attention mechanism is clearly explained, and the implementation details (gold attention construction strategies, joint loss function) are well-defined. The use of two distinct strategies for constructing gold attention vectors provides a degree of robustness to the approach.
3. **Comprehensive Experimental Evaluation:** The authors conducted a thorough experimental evaluation, comparing their approach against several baselines and state-of-the-art methods. The inclusion of augmentation with FrameNet data further strengthens the results and demonstrates the model’s adaptability.
4. **Strong Empirical Results:** Achieving state-of-the-art results on a widely used benchmark dataset (ACE 2005) provides strong support for the effectiveness of the proposed method. The ablation study in section 4.3 clearly demonstrates the contribution of argument information.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Novelty of the Attention Mechanism:** The supervised attention mechanism itself is not entirely novel. Supervised attention has been extensively used in other NLP tasks (e.g., machine translation). The novelty lies in its application *specifically* to ED and the way it’s supervised by argument information, but this contribution could be emphasized more strongly.
2. **Reliance on ACE 2005 Dataset:** While ACE 2005 is a standard benchmark, the results may not generalize well to other datasets or event detection scenarios with different characteristics. Exploring the performance on other datasets would increase the robustness of the findings.
3. **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** The performance difference between the two gold attention construction strategies (S1 and S2) and the specific lambda values suggests potential sensitivity to hyperparameters. A more detailed analysis of hyperparameter tuning and its impact on performance would be beneficial.
4. **Limited Error Analysis:** The paper lacks a detailed error analysis. Identifying the types of events where the model performs poorly and the common errors it makes would provide valuable insights for future improvement. For instance, are there specific event types where argument information is less helpful?



**Questions to Authors:**

1.  Could you elaborate on the computational cost associated with the supervised attention mechanism compared to standard ED models? 
2. How does the performance of your model vary across different event types in the ACE 2005 dataset? Are certain event types more sensitive to argument information than others?
3.  The paper mentions potential issues with FN data quality. Could you describe the filtering or preprocessing steps applied to the FN data, and how they impact the results?
4.  While you mention that the approach can be extended to other datasets, can you speculate on the expected performance drop on datasets with less-detailed argument annotation?




**Additional Comments:**

*   The paper is well-written and generally easy to follow.
*   Consider adding a discussion of potential limitations and future work, specifically addressing the generalizability of the approach to other datasets and the potential for incorporating more sophisticated argument representations.
*   The citation style appears slightly inconsistent (e.g., some citations include page numbers, others do not). Ensuring consistency would improve the paper’s polish.
