This paper addresses the event detection (ED) task, arguing that explicit utilization of argument information can improve performance. The work proposes a neural network approach with supervised attention mechanisms, leveraging annotated arguments to guide attention during training. The primary contributions are: (1) an analysis highlighting the limitations of joint ED and argument extraction models, specifically regarding the underutilization of argument information for ED; (2) a supervised attention-based ED model designed to explicitly incorporate argument information; and (3) demonstration of improved performance on the ACE 2005 dataset, achieving state-of-the-art results.

**Strengths:**

1. **Novelty of Approach:** The explicit focus on leveraging argument information *specifically* for event detection, as opposed to relying on it indirectly through joint learning, is a valuable contribution. The observation that joint models often prioritize argument extraction due to data imbalance and implementation details is insightful and well-articulated.
2. **Strong Empirical Results:** The paper demonstrates clear improvements over existing state-of-the-art methods on the ACE 2005 dataset, which is a widely used benchmark for event detection. The reported gains are significant and provide strong evidence supporting the effectiveness of the proposed approach.
3. **Well-Motivated Design:** The use of supervised attention, guided by annotated arguments, is a logical and well-motivated approach. The two proposed strategies (S1 and S2) for constructing gold attention vectors offer flexibility and allow for investigation of different ways to incorporate argument information.
4. **Thorough Experimentation:** The paper presents a comprehensive set of experiments, including ablation studies to demonstrate the impact of argument information and augmentation with data from FrameNet. The inclusion of a t-test to confirm statistical significance further strengthens the results.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Generalizability:** The experiments are solely conducted on the ACE 2005 dataset. While this is a standard benchmark, it raises concerns about the generalizability of the findings to other datasets with different characteristics (e.g., different event types, annotation schemes, or domains).
2. **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** The choice of the hyperparameter λ in the joint loss function (Equation 9) appears crucial for performance, with different values used for S1 and S2. The paper does not provide a detailed justification for these specific values or an analysis of their sensitivity. A more thorough hyperparameter search and analysis would be beneficial.
3. **Complexity of Implementation:** The described approach involves multiple components (context representation learning, event detector, attention mechanisms) and requires careful tuning of various parameters. The implementation complexity might limit its adoption by researchers with limited resources.
4. **Insufficient Error Analysis:** While the paper reports overall performance improvements, it lacks a detailed error analysis to identify specific types of events or scenarios where the proposed approach still struggles. Understanding these limitations would be valuable for future research.

**Questions to Authors:**

1.  Could the authors elaborate on the computational cost of the proposed approach compared to existing state-of-the-art methods?
2.  How sensitive are the results to the choice of word embeddings (Skip-gram model on the NYT corpus)? Have the authors experimented with other pre-trained embeddings?
3.  Regarding the FrameNet data augmentation, how were inconsistencies between FrameNet event types and ACE event types handled?
4.  Can the authors provide more insights into the differences in performance between strategies S1 and S2? Specifically, what types of events benefit more from each strategy?

**Additional Comments:**

*   The discussion of related work is relatively brief. Expanding on existing approaches to argument-aware event detection, even if those approaches treat argument information indirectly, would provide a more complete context for the proposed work.
*   The notation used in the mathematical formulations is generally clear, but some minor clarifications could improve readability. For example, specifying the dimensions of the matrices and vectors would be helpful.
