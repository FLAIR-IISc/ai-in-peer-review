This paper proposes a method for event detection (ED) that explicitly leverages argument information via supervised attention mechanisms. The authors argue that existing joint ED and argument extraction models don’t effectively utilize argument information for ED, and propose a model that focuses attention on argument words during event trigger classification. They demonstrate improvements over state-of-the-art results on the ACE 2005 dataset.

**Contributions:**

1. **Highlighting the under-utilization of argument information in joint ED models:** The paper effectively points out a key weakness in current joint models – despite jointly training on arguments and triggers, the benefit to *ED* performance is minimal. This is a valuable observation.
2. **Supervised Attention Mechanism for Argument Exploitation:** The proposed approach of directly incorporating argument information through supervised attention is a sensible and well-motivated idea. The exploration of different strategies for constructing “gold” attention vectors (S1 and S2) is a positive aspect.
3. **Strong Empirical Results:** The paper achieves state-of-the-art performance on the ACE 2005 dataset, demonstrating the effectiveness of the proposed method. The augmentation with FrameNet data further strengthens the results.

**Strengths:**

1. **Clear Motivation and Problem Definition:** The paper clearly articulates the problem and provides a convincing argument for why argument information is important for ED, despite being largely ignored in existing approaches. The example provided ("Mohamad fired Anwar...") effectively illustrates the point.
2. **Sound Methodology:** The use of attention mechanisms is appropriate for the task, and the supervised learning approach with “gold” attention vectors is a reasonable way to guide the model to focus on relevant arguments. The two strategies for constructing gold attention vectors provide a useful comparison.
3. **Thorough Experimentation:** The paper includes a comprehensive experimental evaluation, including comparisons to strong baselines and ablation studies (ANN, ANN-S1, ANN-S2) to demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed approach. The inclusion of FrameNet data further enhances the robustness of the results.
4. **Strong Performance:** Achieving state-of-the-art results on a standard benchmark dataset is a significant accomplishment and validates the effectiveness of the proposed approach.
5. **Well-written and Organized:** The paper is generally well-written and logically organized, making it easy to follow the authors’ line of reasoning.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Novelty in Attention Mechanism:** While the application of attention to ED with argument information is valuable, the attention mechanism itself is fairly standard. The core novelty lies in *how* the attention is supervised, rather than the attention mechanism itself.  The paper would benefit from more explicitly acknowledging this.
2. **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** The optimal value of λ (the weighting between loss functions) differs between S1 and S2 (1.0 vs. 5.0). This suggests some sensitivity to hyperparameters, and it is unclear how these values were determined. A sensitivity analysis could be beneficial.
3. **ACE 2005 Dataset Limitations:** While ACE 2005 is a standard benchmark, it is a relatively small and dated dataset. Evaluating the approach on more recent and larger datasets would provide a more comprehensive assessment of its generalizability.
4. **Lack of Error Analysis:** The paper lacks detailed error analysis. Examining examples where the model fails could provide insights into the limitations of the approach and potential areas for improvement. What kinds of events or arguments does it still struggle with?



**Questions to Authors:**

1. Could you elaborate on the process used to select the value of λ for S1 and S2? Was a grid search performed, or was it based on intuition?
2. What are some examples of cases where the proposed method fails, and what do you believe are the reasons for these failures?
3.  The paper mentions the imbalance in the number of arguments vs. triggers. Did you explore any techniques to address this imbalance during training, beyond the use of attention?
4. Could you comment on the computational complexity of the proposed method compared to other state-of-the-art approaches?



**Additional Comments:**

*   The reference formatting appears inconsistent. Please ensure consistency throughout the paper.
*   The phrase "on earth" in "ANN-Gold1/2 on earth benefits from entities or arguments" is informal and should be removed.



Overall, this is a strong paper that presents a valuable contribution to the field of event detection. The authors effectively identify a weakness in existing approaches and propose a sensible solution that achieves state-of-the-art results. While the novelty of the attention mechanism itself is limited, the way it is supervised and applied to leverage argument information is significant. I recommend acceptance.
