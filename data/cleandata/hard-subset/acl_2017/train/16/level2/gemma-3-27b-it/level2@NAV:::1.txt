## Review for “Exploiting Argument Information to Improve Event Detection via Supervised Attention Mechanisms”

This paper addresses the event detection (ED) task, arguing that existing methods overlook valuable information contained within event arguments. The authors propose a supervised attention mechanism to explicitly leverage arguments during ED, demonstrating improvements over state-of-the-art methods on the ACE 2005 dataset. The primary contribution is a novel approach to incorporating argument information into an ED system via supervised attention, showing a demonstrable performance gain. A secondary contribution lies in the systematic investigation of different attention strategies within their proposed model. Finally, achieving a new best F1 score on the ACE 2005 dataset is a notable result.

**Strengths:**

*   **Clear Motivation:** The paper effectively argues for the importance of argument information in resolving ambiguity during event detection, providing a concrete example to illustrate the point.
*   **Strong Empirical Results:** The proposed model achieves state-of-the-art performance on a standard benchmark dataset (ACE 2005). The comparisons to both pipeline and joint methods are well-presented and highlight the benefits of the proposed approach.
*   **Systematic Investigation:** The authors explore different attention strategies, providing a comprehensive analysis of their impact on performance.
*   **Addressing Data Imbalance:** The authors acknowledge the data imbalance between triggers and arguments in the training corpus and address the potential impact on joint models.
*   **Augmentation with FrameNet:** The augmentation with FrameNet data further demonstrates the robustness of their approach.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Limited Analysis of Attention Weights:** While the paper demonstrates performance gains with supervised attention, a more in-depth analysis of *where* the model is focusing its attention would strengthen the findings. Visualizing attention weights for specific examples could provide valuable insights.
*   **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** The paper lacks detail on the sensitivity of the model to hyperparameter tuning, particularly the crucial λ parameter used in the joint loss function. A more rigorous hyperparameter search and discussion of its impact would be beneficial.
*   **Generalizability:** The experiments are limited to the ACE 2005 dataset. Exploring the model's performance on other datasets or domains would provide a better understanding of its generalizability.
*   **Presentation could be more concise:** The paper contains some repetition, particularly in describing the motivation and related work.

**Questions to Authors:**

1.  Can you provide visualizations of the learned attention weights for a few representative examples to illustrate which arguments are most influential in event detection?
2.  What was the range of values explored for the hyperparameter λ, and how did the performance vary across this range?
3.  Do you anticipate the performance gains from argument information to be more or less pronounced in datasets with different characteristics (e.g., different event types, data size, or annotation style)?
