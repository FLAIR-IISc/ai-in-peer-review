- Contribution: Analyzes limitations of joint event detection and argument extraction models, arguing they underutilize argument information for ED.  
- Contribution: Proposes a supervised attention-based ED model that explicitly incorporates annotated argument information.  
- Contribution: Demonstrates state-of-the-art performance on the ACE 2005 dataset.

- Strength: Novelty in explicitly leveraging argument information for ED rather than relying on joint learning, with a clear critique of joint-model bias.  
- Strength: Strong empirical results with significant gains on the ACE 2005 benchmark.  
- Strength: Well-motivated supervised attention design using annotated arguments and two strategies (S1, S2) for gold attention.  
- Strength: Thorough experimentation including ablations, FrameNet augmentation, and statistical significance testing.

- Weakness: Limited generalizability due to experiments only on ACE 2005.  
- Weakness: Hyperparameter sensitivity (λ in joint loss) with insufficient justification or sensitivity analysis.  
- Weakness: Implementation complexity from multiple components and many tunable parameters may hinder adoption.  
- Weakness: Insufficient error analysis to identify specific event types or scenarios that remain challenging.

- Question: What is the computational cost of the proposed approach compared to existing state-of-the-art methods?  
- Question: How sensitive are results to the choice of word embeddings and were other pre-trained embeddings evaluated?  
- Question: How were inconsistencies between FrameNet and ACE event types handled during augmentation?  
- Question: Can the authors clarify which event types or cases benefit more from strategies S1 versus S2?

- Additional comment: Related work discussion is brief and should expand on prior argument-aware ED approaches for fuller context.  
- Additional comment: Mathematical notation is generally clear but would benefit from specifying matrix and vector dimensions.
