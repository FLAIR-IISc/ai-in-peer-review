**Summary of the paper and main contributions**

This paper addresses the task of Event Detection (ED). The authors argue that event arguments, while providing crucial contextual clues for identifying and classifying event triggers, have been ineffectively utilized by prior methods, particularly joint models for event and argument extraction. To address this, the paper proposes a novel method to explicitly incorporate argument information into a neural ED model using a supervised attention mechanism. During training, the attention component is explicitly supervised to focus on the locations of annotated arguments, effectively teaching the model to identify the salient contextual cues needed to disambiguate trigger words.

The main contributions of this work are:

1.  A novel and effective method for event detection that uses supervised attention to explicitly leverage argument annotations during training. This directly addresses a well-motivated weakness in prior joint models and provides a new direction for incorporating auxiliary information into task-specific architectures.
2.  A strong empirical validation on the ACE 2005 benchmark, demonstrating that the proposed approach achieves a new state-of-the-art F1 score. The paper includes a thorough ablation analysis and an insightful oracle experiment that strongly support the central hypothesis.

**Strengths**

1.  **Excellent Motivation and Novelty:** The paper is exceptionally well-motivated. The introduction clearly articulates a plausible reason for the failure of prior joint models to improve ED performance: the benefits of joint learning were primarily flowing from triggers to arguments, not the other way around. The proposed supervised attention mechanism is an elegant and direct solution to this problem. It operationalizes the intuition that arguments provide critical context, and the method for doing so is novel for this task.
2.  **Strong and Convincing Empirical Results:** The proposed model, ANN-S2, achieves a new state-of-the-art F1 score of 71.7 on the ACE 2005 dataset, significantly outperforming a range of strong recent baselines. The performance gain is not marginal, and the high precision achieved (78.0) is particularly impressive. These results strongly substantiate the authors' claims about the effectiveness of their method.
3.  **Thorough Experimental Design:** The experiments are well-designed and provide compelling evidence for the paper's claims. The comparison between the unsupervised attention model (`ANN`) and the supervised versions (`ANN-S1`/`ANN-S2`) clearly isolates the benefit of the proposed supervision. Furthermore, the inclusion of the `ANN-Gold` oracle experiments provides a powerful validation of the core hypothesis that argument information is indeed highly valuable, lending significant credibility to the entire approach.
4.  **Clarity and Readability:** The paper is very well-written, clear, and easy to follow. The model architecture, training objective, and experimental setup are explained logically and concisely. The motivation is laid out persuasively in the introduction, making the paper's contributions immediately understandable.

**Weaknesses**

1.  **Reliance on Argument Annotations for Training:** The primary limitation of the proposed method is its requirement for gold-standard argument annotations during the training phase. This limits its direct applicability to datasets that are annotated for both event triggers and their arguments, such as ACE 2005. For many real-world scenarios or datasets annotated only for events, this method cannot be used out-of-the-box. The paper would be strengthened by a discussion of this limitation and potential ways to mitigate it, for instance, by using a pre-trained argument extractor to generate noisy "pseudo-gold" attention signals.
2.  **Unclear Impact of Noisy Supervision from FrameNet:** For the data augmentation experiments, the paper relies on an external system to provide entity tags and maps FrameNet "core frame elements" to arguments. This process inevitably introduces noise and errors into the supervision signal for the attention mechanism. However, the paper does not provide any analysis of the quality of these automatically generated labels or discuss how robust the supervised attention mechanism is to such noise. This makes it difficult to fully assess the model's behavior in a more realistic, lower-resource setting where perfect supervision is unavailable.
3.  **Simplicity of the Base Model:** The underlying event detector is a relatively simple 3-layer feed-forward network. While the paper demonstrates that this is sufficient to achieve state-of-the-art results when combined with supervised attention, it is unclear how the proposed mechanism would interact with more powerful and standard context encoders like BiLSTMs or more advanced CNNs. Integrating the supervised attention mechanism into a stronger baseline (e.g., the Bi-RNN from Nguyen et al., 2016) could potentially lead to even greater gains and would make the contribution more broadly applicable. A brief discussion on this point would be beneficial.
