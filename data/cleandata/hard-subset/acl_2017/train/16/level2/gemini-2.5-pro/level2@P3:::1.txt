### Summary of the Paper
This paper addresses the task of Event Detection (ED), which involves identifying and classifying event trigger words. The central hypothesis is that event arguments, which are often underutilized in existing ED models, provide critical information for disambiguating trigger words. The work critiques prior joint event extraction models, arguing they fail to effectively leverage argument information for the ED subtask.

To address this, the paper proposes a neural network architecture that incorporates argument information explicitly through a supervised attention mechanism. During training, the model's attention distribution over the context of a trigger candidate is optimized not only through the classification objective but also by minimizing its distance to a "gold" attention vector. This gold attention is constructed from the annotated arguments in the training data, thereby forcing the model to focus on context words that are known to be participants in an event. The work explores two strategies for constructing these gold attentions. Experiments on the ACE 2005 dataset show that this supervised attention approach significantly outperforms an unsupervised attention baseline and achieves a new state-of-the-art result, demonstrating the effectiveness of explicitly modeling argument information for event detection.

### Main Contributions
1.  **A Novel Method for Supervised Attention in Event Detection:** The primary contribution is the proposal to supervise a neural attention mechanism using annotated event arguments. This provides a simple yet effective method for injecting task-specific structural knowledge (the relationship between triggers and arguments) into an end-to-end model, guiding it to focus on the most salient contextual clues for trigger classification.

2.  **Strong Empirical Validation of the Core Hypothesis:** The work provides a compelling empirical demonstration that explicitly using argument information benefits the ED task. Through a series of well-designed ablation studies, it shows that models using argument-supervised attention significantly outperform those with unsupervised attention or those that heuristically focus on all entities, validating the initial hypothesis that argument information is key for trigger disambiguation.

### Strengths
1.  **Clear Motivation and Sound Rationale:** The paper is built on a clear, intuitive, and well-argued premise. The introductory example of the ambiguous trigger "fired" effectively illustrates the problem, and the analysis of why previous joint models failed to improve ED performance provides a strong justification for the proposed approach.

2.  **Methodological Elegance and Novelty:** The concept of using ground-truth arguments to supervise an attention mechanism is a novel and elegant contribution to the event detection task. Instead of relying on complex feature engineering or multi-task frameworks that can be difficult to balance, this method integrates crucial information via a straightforward modification to the loss function, making the model more interpretable and targeted.

3.  **Thorough and Convincing Experimental Evaluation:** The empirical evaluation is comprehensive and robust. The paper includes comparisons against strong, recent state-of-the-art systems and employs carefully designed ablation studies. The inclusion of an unsupervised attention baseline (`ANN`) and oracle models using gold attention at test time (`ANN-Gold1/2`) effectively isolates and quantifies the impact of the proposed supervised mechanism. The results show significant improvements, establishing a new state-of-the-art on a standard benchmark.

4.  **High Readability and Clarity:** The paper is well-written, well-structured, and easy to follow. The proposed model, experimental setup, and results are presented with clarity, making the work accessible and its contributions easy to appreciate.

### Weaknesses
1.  **Dependence on Argument Annotations During Training:** The proposed method's primary strength is also a potential limitation. It relies on the availability of event argument annotations in the training corpus to construct the gold attention supervision. This may limit its direct applicability to datasets where only event triggers are annotated. A discussion on potential adaptations for settings with limited or no argument annotations (e.g., using distant supervision or bootstrapping to generate pseudo-arguments) would strengthen the paper by addressing the method's generalizability.

2.  **Lack of Qualitative Error Analysis:** While the quantitative results are strong, the paper would benefit from a qualitative analysis of the model's predictions. The central motivation is that argument information helps disambiguate difficult trigger words. An analysis of specific examples where the supervised model succeeds and the unsupervised baseline fails (and vice-versa) would provide deeper insight into the model's behavior and confirm that it is indeed solving the problem it was designed for.

3.  **Unexplored Fusion of Attention Signals:** The model computes separate attention vectors for words (`αw`) and entity types (`αe`) and combines them via a simple sum (`α = αw + αe`). The rationale for this specific design choice is not fully elaborated. It would be beneficial to know if other fusion methods (e.g., a learned weighted average, concatenation followed by a projection, or a gating mechanism) were considered and why the simpler summation was chosen. This would add more depth to the methodological description.

### Questions to Authors
*   The supervised attention mechanism currently requires annotated arguments for training. Could this approach be adapted for scenarios with only trigger annotations, perhaps by using a baseline model to predict arguments and generate "pseudo-gold" attentions in a bootstrapping or self-training framework?
*   The analysis shows a clear quantitative improvement from the supervised attention. Does a qualitative review of the model's predictions confirm that the improvement is particularly pronounced for ambiguous trigger words, as motivated in the introduction? Providing a few examples would be very illustrative.
