1. Paper tackles Event Detection (ED) and argues event arguments are crucial for disambiguating trigger words.  
2. Proposes supervising a neural attention mechanism with gold attention vectors derived from annotated arguments.  
3. Trains attention by minimizing distance to gold attention alongside the classification loss; explores two gold-attention construction strategies.  
4. Experiments on ACE 2005 show large gains over unsupervised attention and achieve new state-of-the-art results.  
5. Main contribution: a simple, novel supervised-attention method that injects argument structure into ED models.  
6. Empirically validates the hypothesis via ablation studies showing argument-supervised attention outperforms baselines and oracle comparisons.  
7. Strength: clear motivation and intuitive examples (e.g., ambiguous triggers like “fired”) that justify the approach.  
8. Strength: methodological elegance—uses annotated arguments to guide attention through a straightforward loss modification.  
9. Strength: thorough experimental evaluation with strong baselines, ablations, and oracle models isolating the effect of supervised attention.  
10. Strength: paper is well-written, clear, and accessible.  
11. Weakness: method depends on argument annotations during training, limiting applicability to datasets without argument labels.  
12. Weakness: lacks qualitative error analysis showing concrete examples where supervised attention resolves ambiguity.  
13. Weakness: fusion of word and entity-type attentions (simple sum) is not justified or compared to alternative fusion methods.  
14. Question: can the approach be adapted for datasets with only trigger annotations via bootstrapping or pseudo-gold arguments?  
15. Question: do qualitative examples confirm the gains are driven by disambiguating ambiguous triggers, and can a few examples be provided?
