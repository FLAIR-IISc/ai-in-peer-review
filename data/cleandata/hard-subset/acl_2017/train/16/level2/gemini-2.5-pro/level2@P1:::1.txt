**Summary and Contributions**

This paper addresses the task of event detection (ED), arguing that event arguments, while often ignored or used indirectly in prior work, provide crucial information for correctly identifying and classifying event triggers. The authors propose a neural network model that explicitly leverages this information through a supervised attention mechanism. The model learns to attend to relevant parts of the context by being trained with a "gold" attention signal derived from the locations of annotated arguments in the training data. The authors explore two different strategies for constructing these gold attention vectors. Experiments on the ACE 2005 dataset show that this approach significantly outperforms an unsupervised attention baseline and achieves a new state-of-the-art result, which is further improved by augmenting the training data with information from FrameNet.

The primary contributions of this work are:
1.  A novel method for explicitly incorporating event argument information into the event detection task using supervised attention. This provides a direct and effective way to guide the model's focus, addressing a key limitation of previous pipeline and joint models.
2.  A thorough experimental analysis demonstrating the effectiveness of the proposed approach. The paper not only shows state-of-the-art results on a standard benchmark but also includes insightful ablations that validate the core hypothesis that argument information is critical for ED.

**Strengths**

1.  **Clear Motivation and Strong Hypothesis:** The paper is exceptionally well-motivated. The introduction clearly articulates a weakness in existing joint learning models for event extraction: they improve argument extraction (AE) but provide little to no benefit for event detection (ED). The authors provide a plausible and well-reasoned explanation for this phenomenon (unbalanced data, indirect use of information), which directly motivates their proposal to use argument information explicitly for ED. The example of the ambiguous trigger "fired" is a compelling illustration of the core idea.

2.  **Novel and Elegant Method:** The central idea of using supervised attention is both novel for this task and elegant in its implementation. While attention mechanisms are now standard, using ground-truth annotations (argument locations) to directly supervise the attention weights is a clever way to inject task-specific structural knowledge into a neural model. This approach is more direct than typical multi-task or joint learning frameworks and neatly sidesteps the issues of unbalanced loss functions that the authors identify.

3.  **Comprehensive and Convincing Experiments:** The empirical evaluation is strong and well-structured.
    *   The experiments in Section 4.2 ("Correctness of Our Assumption") provide convincing evidence for the paper's central claim by comparing against models that use no argument information or only entity information. This initial study effectively validates the premise of the work before moving to the main results.
    *   The proposed models are compared against a strong and representative set of recent state-of-the-art systems, and the reported improvements are significant, establishing a new SOTA on a widely-used benchmark.
    *   The authors also demonstrate the robustness of their approach by incorporating external data from FrameNet, showing that the model benefits from additional (albeit noisy) training signals, further boosting performance.

**Weaknesses**

1.  **Lack of Clarity in Attention Mechanism:** The description of how the two attention vectors, `αw` (from words) and `αe` (from entities), are combined in Section 3.1.3 is unclear. The paper states, "We combine αw and αe to obtain the final attention vector, α = αw+αe." Since `αw` and `αe` are both outputs of a softmax function, they are probability distributions that sum to 1. Adding them would result in a vector that sums to 2, which is not a valid attention distribution. This detail is critical to the model's implementation and reproducibility. It is also not well-justified why a single combined attention vector `α` is used to weight both the contextual words and contextual entities, rather than using `αw` and `αe` separately.

2.  **Handling of Negative Instances in Supervised Attention:** The paper explains how gold attention vectors (`α*`) are constructed for positive trigger candidates based on their annotated arguments. However, it is not specified how `α*` is created for the vast majority of tokens, which are negative examples (i.e., belonging to the 'NA' class). If the attention supervision loss `D(θ)` is only applied to positive examples, this is an important detail that should be stated, as it could significantly impact training dynamics. If some default `α*` is used for negative instances, this strategy should be described and justified.

3.  **Dependence on Gold Annotations:** The proposed method relies on having gold-standard argument annotations in the training data to generate the supervision signal. This is a potential limitation for applying the method to datasets or domains where only trigger annotations are available. Furthermore, the model uses gold entity types as input features during both training and testing. This is a common practice in ACE evaluations but represents an oracle setting. The performance in a more realistic end-to-end pipeline, where entity types are predicted by a separate system, is unknown and would be a valuable point of discussion.

**Questions to Authors**

1.  Could you please clarify the attention combination step `α = αw+αe`? Is the resulting vector `α` subsequently normalized to form a valid probability distribution before being used in Equations (4) and (5)? Have you experimented with alternative combination strategies, such as using `αw` to weight words and `αe` to weight entities separately?

2.  How are the gold attention vectors (`α*`) constructed for trigger candidates that are not actual triggers (i.e., 'NA' instances)? Is the attention loss term `D(θ)` in Equation (9) computed for all tokens in the training set, or only for tokens that are annotated as positive event triggers?

3.  The model uses gold entity type information as input. While this allows for a fair comparison with previous work, it would be insightful to understand the model's sensitivity to this feature. Could you comment on the expected performance if predicted, rather than gold, entity types were used?
