**Summary and Contributions:**
This paper addresses the task of Event Detection (ED) by proposing a novel method to explicitly leverage event argument information. The authors argue that existing methods either ignore arguments or use them in joint models that primarily benefit argument extraction, not detection. The core contribution is a neural network model with a supervised attention mechanism. During training, this mechanism is explicitly guided by a loss function to focus on the locations of annotated arguments, thereby teaching the model to identify words that provide strong contextual clues for disambiguating and classifying event triggers. A secondary contribution is a comprehensive experimental validation on the ACE 2005 dataset, which demonstrates that this approach achieves state-of-the-art performance.

**Strengths:**
1.  **Well-Motivated and Novel Approach:** The paper provides a clear and convincing rationale for why prior work fails to effectively use argument information for ED. The proposed solution—supervising an attention mechanism with argument locations—is an elegant and direct way to inject this valuable information into the model.
2.  **Strong Empirical Performance:** The method achieves a new state-of-the-art F1 score on the widely used ACE 2005 benchmark. The improvements over strong baselines, including previous joint and representation-based models, are significant (e.g., 71.7 F1 vs. the previous best of 71.3), as shown in Table 3.
3.  **Thorough Experimental Analysis:** The authors effectively validate their core hypothesis in Section 4.2 by showing that models using gold argument information substantially outperform baselines, and that this benefit comes specifically from arguments, not just entities. This strengthens the foundation of their proposed method.

**Weaknesses:**
1.  **Reliance on Annotated Arguments:** The method's effectiveness is contingent on the availability of training data with annotations for both event triggers and their corresponding arguments. This requirement could limit the approach's applicability to datasets where only trigger information is available. The paper does not discuss potential adaptations for settings with partial or no argument labels.
2.  **Simplicity of the Base Architecture:** The underlying model is a standard Artificial Neural Network (ANN). While the supervised attention mechanism is novel, the base architecture lacks the sophistication of more recent sequence-based models (e.g., RNNs, Transformers) commonly used for NLP tasks. It is unclear if the gains would persist when integrated into more powerful base models.
3.  **Limited Ablation on Attention Strategies:** The paper proposes two strategies (S1 and S2) for generating gold attention but provides limited analysis on the hyperparameter choices (e.g., the Gaussian parameters for S2, the weighting parameter λ). A more detailed investigation or visualization of the learned attention patterns would provide greater insight into why the S2 strategy is more effective.

**Questions to Authors:**
1.  Your method requires gold argument annotations during training. How robust is the model to noise in this supervision signal? For instance, what is the expected performance if the attention is supervised using arguments predicted by an imperfect, off-the-shelf argument extraction system?
2.  Could you provide qualitative examples or attention visualizations for ambiguous trigger candidates from the test set? This would help illustrate how the learned attention successfully focuses on key arguments to make a correct prediction, reinforcing the initial motivation.
