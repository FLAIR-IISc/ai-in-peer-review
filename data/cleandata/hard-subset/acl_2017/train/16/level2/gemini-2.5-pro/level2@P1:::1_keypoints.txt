- Paper proposes supervised attention for event detection that uses gold argument locations to guide attention, with two strategies to build gold attention vectors.
- Reports state-of-the-art results on ACE 2005 and further improvement by augmenting training data with FrameNet.
- Contribution: a novel, explicit method to incorporate event argument information into ED via supervised attention.
- Contribution: thorough empirical evaluation and ablation studies that validate the importance of argument information for ED.
- Strength: Clear motivation and well-articulated hypothesis that joint models underuse argument info due to unbalanced losses; illustrated by ambiguous triggers like “fired.”
- Strength: Method is novel and elegant—supervising attention with ground-truth argument locations injects structural knowledge directly.
- Strength: Strong, comprehensive experiments: initial validation of the assumption, comparisons to recent SOTA, and robustness gain from external FrameNet data.
- Weakness: Unclear description of how αw and αe are combined (α = αw + αe would not be a valid distribution unless normalized) and why a single α weights both words and entities.
- Weakness: Unclear handling of negative/NA instances—how gold attention α* is constructed for non-trigger tokens and whether the attention loss is applied to all tokens or only positives.
- Weakness: Method depends on gold argument annotations and uses gold entity types as input, creating an oracle setting and leaving end-to-end performance with predicted entities unknown.
- Question: Please clarify whether α = αw + αe is normalized and whether alternatives (e.g., separate weighting for words and entities) were tried.
- Question: How are α* vectors defined for NA instances, and is the attention supervision loss D(θ) computed for all tokens or only annotated triggers?
- Question: Can the authors comment on model sensitivity when using predicted (not gold) entity types and expected end-to-end performance?
