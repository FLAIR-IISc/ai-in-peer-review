Summary and claimed contributions
- The paper proposes to improve event detection (ED) by explicitly leveraging annotated argument information through supervised attention. The model computes attention over a fixed-size context around each trigger candidate using both contextual words and entity types, and trains the attention mechanism to match gold “argument-centric” targets constructed from ACE argument annotations (two strategies: S1 focusing only on argument tokens; S2 also spreading mass to neighboring tokens via a Gaussian).
- The work empirically demonstrates that supervising attention with arguments substantially helps ED compared to an unsupervised attention baseline, and reports state-of-the-art F1 on ACE 2005. It also explores augmentation with FrameNet-derived events.
- A secondary contribution is the analysis claiming that prior joint ED+AE methods help AE more than ED and motivating direct use of arguments for ED.

Strengths
- Clear, focused idea with a simple implementation: supervising attention with argument positions credibly injects useful inductive bias for ED. The contrast between unsupervised attention (ANN) and supervised attention (ANN-S1/S2) is compelling and isolates the benefit of argument supervision.
- Systematic exploration of attention targets: comparing S1 vs. S2 (hard vs. smoothed attention around arguments) provides useful insight; S2 trades a bit of precision for recall, consistent with the intuition that nearby context is helpful albeit noisier.
- Sensible upper-bound ablations: ANN-Gold1/2 that use gold attentions at test time illustrate the potential when argument information is directly available, strengthening the claim that arguments are informative for ED beyond generic entity presence (vs. ANN-ENT).
- Competitive results on ACE 2005 with a relatively lightweight model; the approach outperforms several strong baselines and prior work, and retains high precision. The FrameNet augmentation experiment further shows consistent gains and suggests the method benefits from more data.
- The paper addresses a practically relevant limitation of joint models (arguments helping AE more than ED) and proposes a direct remedy aligned with the core hypothesis.

Weaknesses
- Limited novelty relative to existing supervision paradigms: the method is essentially adding explicit argument supervision to a context-weighting mechanism. While supervising attention is interesting, the overall architecture (feed-forward ANN with weighted sums) is standard, and the conceptual step (teach the model to focus on argument positions) is straightforward. The claim of “explicitly using arguments for ED” is largely equivalent to injecting argument supervision signals during training.
- Small margin over prior SOTA and unclear statistical support: the best F1 (71.7) only slightly exceeds Skip-CNN’s 71.3. The paper claims significance (t-test, p ≤ 0.05) but does not report variance, number of runs, or seeds. Given high variance typically seen on ACE, a single-run comparison is insufficient to establish significance.
- Heavy reliance on entity annotations and unclear test-time assumptions: the model uses contextual entity types (Ce) during both training and testing. Are these gold entities at test time (as in some ACE ED settings) or predicted entities? Several baselines differ in entity usage; comparability and practical applicability (when gold entities are unavailable) are not discussed. The FrameNet augmentation uses automatically predicted entities, introducing a mismatch relative to main experiments with gold entities.
- Incomplete model specification and ablations: key details are missing or under-specified (e.g., how we is computed for entity attention—what transformation is used; the exact context window size n; whether positional embeddings are used; normalization and handling of multiple arguments). Ablations disentangling the contribution of word attention vs. entity attention (αw vs. αe vs. αw+αe) are absent; ANN-ENT (uniform attention over entities) is not a strong control for entity-based attention.
- The analysis of why joint models do not help ED is largely speculative. The data imbalance and pre-prediction arguments are plausible but not empirically validated. A simple test (reweight losses in a joint model; or provide predicted argument cues as features to a baseline ED system) would strengthen this claim.
- Fairness and completeness of baselines: It would be informative to compare against a baseline that uses predicted arguments (from a separate AE system) as features without attention to assess whether attention supervision per se brings additional benefit. Also, report results against Nguyen et al. (2016) and other baselines under the exact same split and resource conditions.

Questions to authors
- Do you assume gold entity mentions/types at test time for ACE? If yes, please report results with predicted entities to assess robustness and practical applicability. If no, specify the entity recognizer used and its performance.
- How is we computed precisely? Is there a separate transformation matrix from word space to an “entity-type attention space”? Is it learned jointly? Please specify shapes, activation functions, and whether parameters are shared with Ww.
- What is the context window size n? Did you try larger windows and/or positional embeddings to model proximity to the candidate trigger beyond the Gaussian smoothing in S2?
- How many random seeds/runs were used for each reported number? Please report mean and standard deviation and re-run competitors (when feasible) under the same split for a fair t-test.
- Can you provide an ablation of αw-only, αe-only, and αw+αe? Also, a variant without entity inputs would clarify how much the method depends on entity annotations.
- How sensitive are S2’s results to μ, σ, and the window size for the Gaussian? Did you tune these on dev, and how robust is performance?

Additional comments
- Clarify the training/test pipeline for the FrameNet-augmented setup and whether entity annotations there are gold or predicted. Discuss any domain mismatch effects.
- Several typos and reference formatting issues should be fixed; ensure all tables report consistent metrics and that “Skip-CNN” reports P/R/F1 if available.
- Consider releasing code and the processing scripts for FrameNet-derived data to facilitate reproducibility.

Overall: The paper presents a clear and useful idea that yields competitive results, but the novelty is moderate and several methodological and evaluation details need clarification. With stronger ablations, robustness experiments (predicted entities), and statistically grounded comparisons, this would be a solid contribution.
