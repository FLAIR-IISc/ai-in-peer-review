Summary and claimed contributions
- The paper proposes using supervised attention to explicitly exploit argument information for event detection (ED), hypothesizing that arguments provide strong cues for disambiguating triggers.
- It introduces two supervision strategies for attention (S1: argument-only, S2: argument-plus-neighborhood via Gaussian smoothing) and a joint loss that aligns learned attention with gold attention.
- Experiments on ACE 2005 show state-of-the-art F1, with consistent gains over baselines and prior work; augmentation with FrameNet further improves recall.

Contributions (as I see them; strongest first)
1) Demonstration that argument-supervised attention substantially improves ED performance over unsupervised attention and prior systems on ACE 2005.
2) A simple, general supervision scheme for attention (S1/S2) that can be plugged into neural ED models without requiring arguments at test time.
3) Empirical analysis clarifying why prior joint models yield limited ED gains, and a targeted remedy focused on ED.

Strengths
- Strong empirical gains: ANN-S2 achieves the best reported F1 on ACE 2005, with statistically significant improvements and notably higher precision than prior SOTA. The comparative table and t-test bolster credibility.
- Well-designed ablations: Comparing ANN, ANN-ENT, ANN-Gold1/2 cleanly isolates the value of argument supervision beyond entity information, supporting the central claim.
- Practicality: The model uses gold arguments only to supervise attention during training; no arguments are required at inference, making deployment feasible.
- Transparent setup: Hyperparameters, training details, and attention-supervision construction are clearly described; the FrameNet augmentation pipeline is documented.

Weaknesses
- Potential reliance on gold entities at test time: The approach “exploits annotated entity information”; it is unclear whether entity types are gold or predicted at inference on ACE 2005. Using gold entities would inflate results; the FN experiments use predicted entities, but ACE settings need clarification.
- Baseline strength: The core ANN with a fixed window and no sequence model is relatively weak versus contemporary BiLSTM/CNN architectures; comparisons to stronger attention-based or RNN/CNN baselines would better substantiate novelty.
- Mapping and noise in FN augmentation: Details of mapping FrameNet core elements to ACE argument schema and the quality of automatic entity labels are sparse; impact on precision/recall trade-offs could be better analyzed.
- Limited analysis: No per-event-type breakdown, error analysis, or attention visualizations are provided to understand where the method helps or fails.

Questions to authors
- Do you use gold or predicted entity types at test time on ACE 2005? If predicted, with which model and what is its performance? Please report ED performance with predicted entities.
- How sensitive are results to window size, λ, and S2’s Gaussian parameters? Any robustness study?
- Can you provide per-event-type results and qualitative attention examples to illustrate disambiguation of ambiguous triggers?
- How is the FN-to-ACE argument mapping validated, and what proportion of FN-added samples are noisy?
