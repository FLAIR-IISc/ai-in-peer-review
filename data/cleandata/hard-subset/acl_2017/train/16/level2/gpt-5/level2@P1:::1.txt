Summary and Contributions
This paper proposes a neural event detection (ED) model that explicitly leverages annotated event arguments via supervised attention. The key idea is to train attention mechanisms so that context words corresponding to annotated arguments (and optionally their neighbors) receive higher attention when classifying a trigger candidate. The model combines word and entity-type contexts and jointly optimizes a classification loss and an attention supervision loss. Experiments on ACE 2005 show the approach outperforms prior systems; additional experiments use FrameNet-derived data to mitigate sparsity.

As I see it, the main contributions are:
1) An explicit use of argument annotations to supervise attention for ED, leading to consistent gains over strong baselines and prior SOTA on ACE 2005.
2) A careful empirical study including ablations (unsupervised attention, entity-only attention, gold-attention oracles) that isolates the utility of argument supervision and compares two supervision strategies (arguments only vs. arguments plus nearby words).
3) An evaluation of augmentation with FrameNet events adapted to provide argument/entity signals, showing further improvements and supporting the data sparsity hypothesis.

Strengths
- Clear, focused insight and motivation: The paper identifies a plausible reason why prior joint models have not improved ED (imbalance and indirect use of argument information for trigger pre-prediction) and proposes a direct, testable alternative: supervising attention with argument annotations. This is conceptually simple and addresses a concrete gap.

- Strong empirical improvements with solid ablations: On ACE 2005, ANN-S2 achieves the best reported F1 (71.7) over Liu et al. (2016a,b), Nguyen & Grishman (2016), and Nguyen et al. (2016), with a statistically significant margin (p ≤ 0.05). The supervised attention variants (S1/S2) improve substantially over the same backbone with unsupervised attention (64.6 → 70.8/71.7), showing the effect is not due to the classifier capacity. Oracle variants (ANN-Gold1/2) highlight the ceiling and isolate where gains come from.

- Evidence that arguments matter beyond entity presence: The ANN-ENT control (averaging attention over entities) underperforms the gold-attention or supervised-attention variants, supporting the claim that argument-specific supervision provides signal beyond generic entity information.

- Interpretability and design transparency: The attention formulation is simple (dot-product attention with a supervised target), making it easy to diagnose and extend. The contrast between S1 (argument-only) and S2 (Gaussian around arguments) cleanly trades precision vs recall, aligning with the hypothesis about noise and coverage.

- Data augmentation analysis: Incorporating FrameNet-derived events increases F1 further and improves recall, providing additional, independent evidence for the authors’ data sparsity explanation for weak unsupervised attention.

Weaknesses
- Assumptions and supervision realism: The model relies on gold entity types (for entity-type embeddings) and gold argument positions to supervise attention during training. It is unclear whether entity information at test time is gold or predicted; many ACE setups assume gold entities, but this affects comparability and practical applicability. When augmenting with FN, entities are predicted (RPI system), creating a mismatch with ACE test conditions if gold entities are used there. Please clarify and, if possible, report results with predicted entities across the board.

- Limited novelty in mechanism and lack of stronger encoders: The supervision strategies are adapted from supervised attention work (e.g., Mi et al., 2016), and the context encoder is a fixed-window, dot-product attention over embeddings without sequence modeling (e.g., BiLSTMs/CNNs). While the empirical gains are strong, the architectural novelty is moderate. A comparison to a BiLSTM/CNN baseline with the same supervised-attention supervision would better isolate the contribution of the supervision vs the specific encoder.

- Incomplete experimental details and sensitivity: Key hyperparameters and settings are missing or under-specified (e.g., the exact fixed context length n; early stopping; epochs; initialization specifics; runtime). The choice of λ differs across S1/S2 without a sensitivity analysis; similarly, the Gaussian window size and σ are fixed without robustness checks. Reporting performance variance across random seeds would strengthen the claims.

- Fairness and completeness of comparisons: Table 3 reports “Skip-CNN (2016) N/A 71.3” (formatting unclear). Also, Table 4 augments only the proposed systems with FrameNet, but not prior FN-augmented systems beyond citing Liu et al. (2016a). A direct side-by-side of your +FrameNet results with Liu et al. (2016a)’s FN setup under the same split would help avoid confounds.

- Limited analysis: The paper would benefit from error analysis by event type, attention visualizations, and a breakdown by trigger ambiguity or distance to arguments. The diagnosis of why joint models underperform ED remains speculative; a small experiment (e.g., reweighting losses in a joint setting or using argument-informed pre-prediction) would substantiate this claim.

Questions to Authors
- What entity information is available at test time on ACE: gold entity mentions/types or predicted ones? If gold, can you provide results with predicted entities (e.g., using the same RPI system) for a more realistic setting?

- What is the fixed context length n used in experiments? How sensitive are results to n?

- For negative (NA) trigger candidates, do you supervise attention with averaged entities only, or do you include arguments of other events present in the same sentence? How does this choice affect precision/recall?

- How many additional training instances result from the FrameNet augmentation, and how does the class distribution shift? Is there a mapping quality assessment from FN frames to ACE types?

- How robust are results to λ, Gaussian window size, and σ? Please provide a sensitivity plot or at least a range.

Additional Comments
- Please clarify the t-test protocol (paired over instances? multiple runs?) and report mean±std over several random seeds.

- Some references have formatting issues (years/authors/venues). A proofreading pass for citations and tables (e.g., Table 3 formatting) would improve clarity.

Overall, this paper presents a simple, well-motivated, and empirically effective way to inject argument supervision into ED. While the mechanism is modestly novel, the careful ablations and consistent gains make it a valuable contribution. Addressing clarity on entity assumptions, adding robustness analyses, and tightening comparisons would strengthen the submission.
