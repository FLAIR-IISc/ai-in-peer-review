Summary and claimed contributions
The paper addresses event detection (ED) and proposes to explicitly exploit argument information via supervised attention. The key idea is to construct gold attention targets from argument annotations and train a neural ED classifier to align its attention to these targets. Two supervision strategies are explored: (S1) attention on argument tokens only; (S2) attention on arguments and neighboring tokens via a Gaussian around argument positions. The approach integrates word- and entity-type attention, and evaluates on ACE 2005, with additional augmentation using events derived from FrameNet. Reported results show improvements over strong baselines and prior state of the art.

As seen by this review, the main contributions are:
1) A supervised attention formulation for ED that directly uses argument annotations to guide attention during training, with two concrete supervision strategies (S1/S2).
2) An empirical study demonstrating that argument-informed attention improves ED F1 on ACE 2005 over both an unsupervised attention baseline and prior reported systems, with additional analysis on FrameNet-based augmentation.
3) A clear diagnostic comparison indicating that gains stem from argument supervision rather than entity-type information alone (ANN-ENT vs ANN-Gold1/2).

Strengths
- Clear and well-motivated problem framing: The paper convincingly argues that prior “joint” EE models have benefited AE more than ED and provides quantitative context (Table 1). The rationale for directly supervising attention using arguments is coherent and targeted at ED’s ambiguity issues (e.g., “fired” example).

- Methodological simplicity and plausibility: The supervised attention setup is straightforward to implement and does not require arguments at test time. The two supervision strategies are sensible: S1 provides precise supervision; S2 broadens contextual cues via a parameterized neighborhood.

- Empirical gains and significance: On ACE 2005, the supervised models outperform the unsupervised attention baseline substantially (e.g., ANN 64.6 F1 vs ANN-S2 71.7 F1), and surpass prior systems including Liu’s FN-based (70.7 F1) and Nguyen’s joint model (69.3 F1). The claim of significance is supported by a t-test (p ≤ 0.05).

- Diagnostic comparisons: The inclusion of ANN-ENT and ANN-Gold1/2 helps isolate the effect of argument supervision vs. generic entity cues, supporting the core hypothesis that arguments, not just entities, drive the gains.

- Sensible analysis of precision/recall trade-offs: The observation that S1 favors precision while S2 improves recall is consistent with the supervision designs and helps interpret model behavior.

Weaknesses
- Limited baseline strength and comparability: The primary internal baseline (ANN with unsupervised attention) underperforms substantially, potentially due to data sparsity, but also because it lacks stronger sequence/context encoders (e.g., CNNs/RNNs used in Chen 2015, Nguyen & Grishman 2016). While external baselines are cited, the comparison to Skip-CNN is incomplete (Table 3 lacks Skip-CNN’s F1), making the “best” claim less crisp. A stronger internal encoder would better isolate the contribution of supervised attention.

- Reliance on gold entities and argument annotations for supervision: The approach assumes availability of argument annotations during training. While acceptable on ACE, this may limit portability to domains without argument labels. The paper partly addresses this via FrameNet augmentation but still requires frame element labels and applies an external entity tagger for FN data, which introduces noise. Discussion of how performance degrades with weaker or silver argument supervision would strengthen the case.

- Attention formulation details and potential mismatch: The method sums αw and αe to form α, but it is unclear whether α is renormalized before computing cw/ce and before computing the supervised loss against α*. Since α* is normalized, the scale mismatch could impact training. Clarification and/or an ablation (sum vs. concatenation vs. learned mixture with normalization) would improve transparency.

- Incomplete ablations: While ANN-ENT is informative, more granular ablations would help: (a) removing entity attention within the supervised regime (only word-based attention); (b) supervising only αw or only αe; (c) varying the Gaussian window/σ in S2; (d) replacing entity types with automatically predicted entity types on ACE to match test-time constraints.

- Reproducibility and setup clarity: Some experimental details are missing or scattered: the exact context window size n used in CRL; tokenization/preprocessing; whether gold entities are used at test time on ACE (prior work often does, but explicit confirmation is helpful); and how λ was selected (grid, ranges). The mapping and coverage between FN frames and ACE types, and class balance effects when adding FN, are only briefly described.

Questions to authors (for rebuttal)
- Is α renormalized after summing αw and αe? If not, how is the scale handled when computing the squared loss against α*? Any sensitivity observed when normalizing vs. not?

- What is the exact context window size n and its impact on performance? Any results for larger/smaller n?

- On ACE 2005, are gold entity types used at test time? If so, can results with predicted entities be reported for completeness?

- For Skip-CNN, what is the F1 on the same split? If not available, can the paper re-run or approximate to provide a fuller comparison?

- For S2, how sensitive are results to window size and σ? Was µ fixed at 0 for all experiments?

Suggestions for improvement
- Integrate a stronger context encoder (e.g., BiLSTM/CNN) beneath the attention layer to ensure that improvements are not limited by a weak base model. This seems feasible within standard computing budgets.

- Add ablations on attention normalization and on the relative contributions of word vs. entity attention. Also, report attention quality analysis (e.g., how often top-k attended tokens align with true arguments).

- Provide results with silver/noisy argument supervision (e.g., arguments predicted by a state-of-the-art AE model) to demonstrate broader applicability when gold arguments are unavailable.

- Expand the comparisons to include F1 for Skip-CNN on the same split or clearly qualify the “best” claim. Include runtime/parameter counts to contextualize practical trade-offs.

- Improve clarity and presentation: fix citation and typographical issues; explicitly report all hyperparameters and preprocessing choices; add an error analysis highlighting common failure modes (e.g., polysemy not resolved by nearby arguments).

Overall assessment
The paper presents a focused and effective idea—supervising attention with argument annotations—that yields consistent improvements and is easy to adopt. With stronger baselines, clearer normalization/ablation details, and a more complete comparison, the contribution would be solidly compelling for ACL. The work is promising and relevant to the ED community, with actionable paths to strengthen the submission.
