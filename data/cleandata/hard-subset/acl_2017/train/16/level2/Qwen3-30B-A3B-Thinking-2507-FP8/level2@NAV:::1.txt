

This paper proposes a novel approach to event detection (ED) by explicitly leveraging argument information through supervised attention mechanisms. The main contributions are: (1) identifying that existing joint models indirectly use argument information for ED (focusing on AE instead), (2) introducing a supervised attention mechanism to directly incorporate argument information into ED, and (3) demonstrating state-of-the-art performance on ACE 2005 with statistically significant improvements.

Strengths: The paper makes a compelling case for why argument information should be explicitly used for ED, correctly identifying the key limitation in existing joint models where arguments primarily benefit argument extraction rather than event detection. The proposed supervised attention mechanism is well-motivated and implemented with clear technical details. The experimental results are strong, showing that both attention strategies (S1 and S2) significantly outperform unsupervised attention (ANN) and state-of-the-art baselines, with ANN-S2 achieving the best F1 score (71.7% vs. 70.7% for Liu's FN-Based). The paper also provides a thorough analysis of the trade-offs between S1 (higher precision) and S2 (higher recall).

Weaknesses: The paper evaluates only on ACE 2005, which limits the generalizability of the findings. It would be valuable to test on additional datasets like TAC KBP to demonstrate broader applicability. The paper does not adequately explain why unsupervised attention (ANN) performs poorly (64.6% F1), which is a critical aspect of the proposed method's novelty. Additionally, the computational cost of the proposed approach relative to existing methods is not discussed, which is important for practical adoption.

The paper has a strong theoretical foundation and clear experimental validation, but would be significantly strengthened by addressing the dataset limitation, providing deeper analysis of the unsupervised attention performance gap, and discussing computational efficiency. The authors should also consider addressing how their approach would perform in settings where argument annotations are unavailable or noisy.

Overall, this is a solid contribution to the event detection literature with clear practical value. With additional experiments and analysis, it could be a strong paper for acceptance.
