

This paper proposes a supervised attention mechanism to explicitly exploit argument information for event detection (ED), arguing that existing joint methods only indirectly use arguments for ED. The authors claim to outperform state-of-the-art methods by directly leveraging argument information through supervised attention mechanisms.

Main Contributions:
1. Analysis showing that joint models primarily benefit argument extraction (AE) but not event detection (ED), as they indirectly use arguments for AE rather than explicitly for ED.
2. A supervised attention mechanism that explicitly uses argument information during ED training, with two different attention strategies (S1: focusing only on argument words; S2: focusing on arguments and surrounding words).
3. Demonstration that the proposed approach achieves state-of-the-art results on ACE 2005 dataset.

Strengths:
1. The paper makes a compelling argument that existing joint methods don't properly leverage argument information for ED, which is a valid observation. The analysis of why joint methods underperform on ED (unbalanced data and pre-prediction of triggers without argument information) is insightful and well-supported.

2. The experimental results are thorough and well-designed, with clear ablation studies showing the benefits of argument information. The comparison between S1 and S2 strategies demonstrates a good understanding of the trade-offs between precision and recall.

3. The use of FrameNet to augment training data is well-justified and shows the robustness of the approach to data sparsity issues. The results demonstrate that their method benefits from additional data, which is a strong point.

4. The paper provides a clear explanation of the attention mechanism and how it differs from standard attention approaches, with detailed descriptions of the supervised training procedure.

Weaknesses:
1. The claim that the proposed method "outperforms state-of-the-art" is overstated. The paper only compares to a limited set of baselines (Li's joint, Liu's PSL, Liu's FN-based, Nguyen's joint, and Skip-CNN), but fails to compare to more recent state-of-the-art approaches that might better leverage argument information. For example, they don't compare to methods like "Joint Event Extraction via Recurrent Neural Networks" (Nguyen et al., 2016) or "Leveraging FrameNet to Improve Automatic Event Detection" (Liu et al., 2016a), which are actually cited in the paper as baselines.

2. The paper doesn't sufficiently differentiate their "supervised attention" from other attention mechanisms. The distinction between "supervised" and "unsupervised" attention isn't clearly justified, and it's unclear why their approach would work better than other attention-based methods. The paper claims "data sparseness is an important issue" but doesn't provide evidence that their supervised approach specifically addresses this problem better than alternatives.

3. The paper makes a fundamental assumption that argument information is available during training, but fails to address the practical limitation that argument annotations would not be available in a real-world ED system. The paper doesn't discuss how their method would perform in a setting where argument information is not available during inference, which is a critical limitation for practical applications.

4. The paper's ANN baseline (without argument information) performs poorly (F1=64.6) compared to other baselines like Liu's PSL (F1=69.4), making the claimed improvements over the baseline somewhat misleading. The paper should have compared to stronger baselines to properly demonstrate the novelty and effectiveness of their approach.

5. There's no ablation study showing the specific contribution of the supervised attention mechanism versus other components. The paper doesn't isolate how much of the improvement comes from the attention mechanism versus other aspects of the model.

Questions to Authors:
1. Why did you choose to compare only to the specific baselines listed in Table 3 rather than to more recent state-of-the-art methods that might better leverage argument information for ED?
2. How would your approach perform in a setting where argument information is not available during training (i.e., the standard ED setting where only triggers are annotated)?
3. Can you provide more evidence to justify why your supervised attention mechanism is fundamentally different from other attention-based approaches, and why it specifically addresses the data sparsity problem better than alternatives?
