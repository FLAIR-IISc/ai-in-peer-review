- Proposes leveraging argument information for event detection via supervised attention mechanisms.
- Contributions: (1) notes existing joint models use argument info indirectly, (2) introduces supervised attention to incorporate arguments directly, (3) reports state-of-the-art results on ACE 2005 with significant improvements.
- Argues convincingly that argument information should be explicitly used for event detection rather than primarily aiding argument extraction.
- Supervised attention mechanism is well-motivated and described with clear technical details.
- Strong experimental results: both S1 and S2 outperform unsupervised attention and baselines; ANN-S2 achieves best F1 (71.7% vs. 70.7%).
- Provides useful analysis of trade-offs: S1 yields higher precision, S2 yields higher recall.
- Evaluation limited to ACE 2005, reducing confidence in generalizability; additional datasets like TAC KBP are needed.
- Does not sufficiently explain why unsupervised attention (ANN) performs poorly (64.6% F1), a key gap in analysis.
- Omits discussion of computational cost and efficiency relative to existing methods.
- Unclear how the approach handles unavailable or noisy argument annotations.
- Overall: solid theoretical and empirical contribution with practical value; would be stronger with broader evaluation, deeper analysis of ANN, and efficiency discussion.
