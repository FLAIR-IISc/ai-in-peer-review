

Summary: This paper proposes a novel approach for event detection (ED) that explicitly exploits argument information via supervised attention mechanisms. The main contributions are: (1) identifying the problem of existing joint methods not effectively using argument information for ED; (2) introducing a supervised attention-based ED model that leverages argument information through two attention strategies (S1: focusing only on argument words; S2: focusing on arguments and surrounding words); and (3) demonstrating significant improvements over state-of-the-art methods on ACE 2005.

Strengths:
1. The paper makes a compelling case for the importance of argument information in event detection, particularly for ambiguous trigger words, with a well-chosen example demonstrating how argument information resolves ambiguity. This insight addresses a clear gap in the literature, as existing joint methods primarily benefit argument extraction rather than event detection.

2. The proposed supervised attention mechanism is well-motivated and systematically evaluated. The paper conducts thorough comparisons between the two attention strategies (S1 and S2), providing valuable insights into their trade-offs (S1 achieves higher precision but lower recall, while S2 achieves higher recall with slightly lower precision).

3. The experimental results are strong and well-justified. The proposed approach achieves the best F1 score on ACE 2005 (71.7), significantly outperforming state-of-the-art methods. The paper also demonstrates that the method benefits from FrameNet augmentation, with the best system (ANN-S2 + FrameNet) achieving 71.9 F1.

4. The paper provides a clear and convincing explanation for why existing joint methods fail to improve ED performance (unbalanced data favoring AE, and arguments not being effectively used for ED in the pre-prediction phase). This analysis is more thorough than previous work on the topic.

5. The paper's systematic ablation studies (comparing ANN, ANN-ENT, ANN-Gold1, ANN-Gold2) provide strong evidence for the necessity of argument information rather than just entity information for ED.

Weaknesses:
1. The paper does not sufficiently address how the proposed approach handles cases with no annotated arguments for a trigger candidate. The paper mentions that "for candidates without any annotated arguments in context, we force all entities to average the whole attention," but it does not provide performance metrics or analysis of how this handling impacts overall results. This is particularly important since the ACE 2005 dataset contains many triggers without annotated arguments.

2. The paper does not provide a detailed error analysis to identify which event types benefit most from the proposed approach. Without this, it's difficult to assess the specific strengths and limitations of the method, particularly for event types that are inherently more ambiguous.

3. The paper does not adequately address the computational cost of the proposed method compared to existing approaches. The supervised attention mechanism requires additional training for the attention model, which may increase training time and resource requirements. This is particularly relevant for practitioners considering adoption of the method.

4. The paper's comparison with state-of-the-art methods could be more comprehensive. While the paper compares with several strong baselines, it does not include the most recent methods from 2016-2017 that might have built upon or improved upon the approaches mentioned. This weakens the claim of being "state-of-the-art."

5. The paper does not sufficiently discuss the potential for overfitting to the argument information, especially given the relatively small size of the ACE 2005 dataset (5300 triggers). The authors acknowledge data sparseness as an issue but don't provide evidence that their approach mitigates this problem beyond the FrameNet augmentation results.

Questions to Authors:
1. The paper mentions that for candidates without annotated arguments, "all entities are forced to average the whole attention." Could the authors provide performance metrics specifically for triggers with and without arguments to better understand this handling?

2. The paper demonstrates that the proposed approach benefits from FrameNet augmentation, but FrameNet annotations are not perfect. How does the paper handle errors in the FrameNet-based event annotations that might propagate to the training data?

3. Given the paper's focus on argument information, could the authors provide a more detailed analysis of which specific event types or trigger words benefit most from the argument information?

4. The paper mentions that the ACE 2005 dataset contains 9800 arguments and 5300 triggers. How does the paper handle the imbalance between the number of arguments and triggers during training, particularly when using the supervised attention mechanism?
