Paper proposes supervised attention using annotated arguments to improve event detection, arguing joint methods underuse argument info and demonstrating gains on ACE 2005.  
Contributions: analysis of why joint ED-argument methods fail (unbalanced data, indirect argument use).  
Contributions: supervised attention mechanism guiding ED with gold argument-based attention vectors (S1: argument words; S2: argument + context).  
Contributions: extensive experiments claiming state-of-the-art F1 (71.7) on ACE 2005 with ANN-S2.  

Strength: Convincing analysis and empirical evidence that argument information is underutilized and why joint methods don't help ED.  
Strength: Supervised attention is simple and effective; two strategies (S1, S2) and ablation studies clarify argument importance.  
Strength: Strong experimental results with statistically significant improvement over prior best (70.7 → 71.7) and discussion of S1 vs S2 precision–recall trade-off.  
Strength: Clear organization and thorough related-work comparison that contextualizes the contribution.  

Weakness: Insufficient explanation for poor performance of unsupervised attention baseline (ANN, F1=64.6); data-sparseness claim lacks strong support.  
Weakness: Comparison with state-of-the-art is incomplete—omits several recent relevant methods that might outperform cited baselines.  
Weakness: Superficial explanation for why ANN-S2 yields higher recall but lower precision; lacks detailed error-type analysis (e.g., ambiguous triggers).  
Weakness: FrameNet augmentation is not fully justified or described—unclear relevance to ACE 2005 and effects of processing choices (e.g., core elements only).  

Question: Please provide concrete test-set examples where argument information corrected ambiguous triggers (with error analysis).  
Question: How exactly does FrameNet augmentation fail to resolve data sparsity—why is the F1 gain from 64.6 to 66.2 so modest?  
Question: Why were some recent methods (e.g., Nguyen & Grishman 2015; Liu et al. 2016b) omitted from comparisons—were they unavailable, incompatible, or outperforming cited baselines?
