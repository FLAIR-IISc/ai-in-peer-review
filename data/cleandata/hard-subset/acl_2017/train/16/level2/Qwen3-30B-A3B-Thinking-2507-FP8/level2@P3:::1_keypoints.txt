- Paper identifies that existing joint methods do not effectively use argument information for event detection.  
- Proposes a supervised attention-based ED model that leverages argument information via two strategies: S1 (argument words only) and S2 (arguments plus surrounding words).  
- Shows significant improvements on ACE 2005 (best F1 71.7; 71.9 with FrameNet augmentation).  

- Arg information is important for disambiguating ambiguous triggers, supported by a clear example.  
- The supervised attention mechanism is well-motivated and systematically evaluated, with clear S1 vs S2 trade-offs (S1: higher precision; S2: higher recall).  
- Experimental results are strong and claim state-of-the-art performance on ACE 2005.  
- The paper provides a convincing analysis for why joint AE+ED methods fail (data imbalance favoring AE and lack of pre-prediction use of arguments).  
- Ablation studies (ANN, ANN-ENT, ANN-Gold1, ANN-Gold2) support the necessity of argument information beyond entity information.  

- Handling of candidates without annotated arguments is under-explained and lacks performance analysis.  
- No detailed error analysis by event type to show which types benefit most from the method.  
- Computational cost and additional training overhead of the supervised attention mechanism are not reported.  
- Comparison against recent 2016–2017 methods is missing, weakening the SOTA claim.  
- Potential overfitting to argument information and issues from ACE 2005’s small size are not sufficiently addressed.  

- Please provide performance metrics separately for triggers with and without annotated arguments.  
- How are errors in FrameNet-based annotations handled to avoid propagating noisy labels?  
- Which specific event types or trigger words benefit most from leveraging argument information?  
- How is the imbalance between 9,800 arguments and 5,300 triggers handled during training, especially for the supervised attention?
