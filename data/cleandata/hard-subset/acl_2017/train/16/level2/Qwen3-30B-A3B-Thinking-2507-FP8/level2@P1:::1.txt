

Summary: This paper proposes a novel approach to event detection (ED) that explicitly leverages argument information through supervised attention mechanisms. The authors argue that existing methods either ignore argument information or use it indirectly in joint learning frameworks, and they demonstrate that explicitly incorporating argument information can significantly improve ED performance. The main contributions are: (1) a thorough analysis of why joint methods fail to improve ED performance, (2) a supervised attention mechanism that uses annotated arguments to guide attention during ED, and (3) extensive experiments showing the proposed approach achieves state-of-the-art results on ACE 2005.

Strengths:
1. The paper makes a compelling argument about the underutilization of argument information in ED. The analysis of why joint methods don't improve ED (unbalanced data and indirect use of arguments) is well-supported by empirical evidence from Table 1 and provides a clear rationale for their approach.

2. The proposed supervised attention mechanism is conceptually simple yet effective. The two strategies for constructing gold attention vectors (S1: focusing only on argument words; S2: focusing on arguments and surrounding words) provide a thoughtful exploration of different ways to incorporate argument information, and the ablation studies (Table 2) effectively demonstrate the importance of argument information.

3. The experimental results are strong, with the proposed ANN-S2 model achieving the best F1 score (71.7) on ACE 2005 compared to state-of-the-art methods. The improvement over the previous best method (Liu's FN-Based at 70.7) is statistically significant (p<0.05), and the authors provide a clear explanation for the precision-recall trade-off between S1 and S2.

4. The paper is well-organized and clearly written, making it easy to follow the authors' rationale and methodology. The comparison with related work (Section 5) is thorough and helps situate the contribution within the broader literature.

Weaknesses:
1. The paper doesn't sufficiently explain why the unsupervised attention baseline (ANN) performs poorly (F1=64.6). While the authors suggest data sparseness as the reason, this isn't thoroughly supported. A comparison with other unsupervised attention mechanisms or a more detailed analysis of the data sparseness problem would strengthen the paper.

2. The comparison with state-of-the-art methods appears incomplete. For example, the paper doesn't compare with the recently published "Event Detection and Domain Adaptation with Convolutional Neural Networks" (Nguyen and Grishman, 2015) or the more recent "A Probabilistic Soft Logic Based Approach" (Liu et al., 2016b), which might have achieved better results than the methods cited.

3. The explanation for why ANN-S2 outperforms ANN-S1 (higher recall but lower precision) is somewhat superficial. A more detailed analysis of the types of errors made by each model, especially for ambiguous triggers like "fired" in the example, would provide deeper insights into the trade-offs.

4. The augmentation with FrameNet is interesting but not fully justified. The paper doesn't explain why FrameNet events are relevant to ACE 2005 or how the processing steps (e.g., using only core frame elements) affect the results. More details about the FrameNet data and its alignment with ACE 2005 would strengthen this contribution.

Questions to authors:
1. The paper states that argument information is crucial for resolving ambiguous triggers like "fired" (End-Position vs. Attack). Could the authors provide specific examples from the test set where argument information led to correct classification, along with the associated error analysis?

2. The paper claims that "data sparseness is the reason that causes unsupervised attention mechanisms to be ineffective to ED" based on the modest improvement from FrameNet augmentation (F1 from 64.6 to 66.2). However, this improvement is relatively small. Could the authors elaborate on why the data sparseness issue isn't fully resolved by FrameNet augmentation?

3. The paper states that "the proposed approach achieves the best performance on ACE 2005." However, the comparison doesn't include some recent methods published around the same time. Could the authors clarify why these methods weren't included in the comparison?
