- Proposes leveraging argument information explicitly for event detection, showing arguments disambiguate ambiguous triggers.  
- Introduces a neural framework with supervised attention to focus on argument words for ED.  
- Demonstrates significant gains on ACE 2005 with ablation studies and FrameNet augmentation.

- Novel insight: explicitly using argument information for ED (not just joint extraction) is valuable and underexplored.  
- Clear, simple and reproducible supervised-attention architecture with well-explained attention mechanisms.  
- Strong empirical results: substantial F1 improvements over baselines with statistical significance.  
- Thorough experimental setup and comparisons to feature- and representation-based baselines; robust augmentation with FrameNet.  
- Well-written and organized presentation with clear motivation and related work coverage.

- Attention combination is simplistic: summing word- and entity-level attention may not capture interactions optimally.  
- Limited exploration of supervised attention strategies; role/type-aware or distance-aware strategies not investigated.  
- Evaluation limited to ACE 2005, raising generalization concerns to other datasets or languages.  
- Lacks detailed error analysis to identify common failure modes and guide improvements.

- Why sum word- and entity-level attention vectors; were other combination methods tried and what were the results?  
- Why choose Gaussian parameters in S2 (µ=0, σ=1, window=3); was sensitivity analysis conducted?  
- What common error types does the model still make; can examples of misclassifications be provided?  
- Do authors expect similar benefits from argument information in languages with richer morphology or different syntax?

- Reference list has placeholders/incomplete entries (e.g., Baker et al.,? 1998).  
- Add discussion of computational complexity of the proposed approach.  
- Improve clarity of Figure 2’s example annotation by directly highlighting arguments.

- Overall assessment: strong, well-motivated paper with valuable contributions and convincing results; recommend acceptance.
