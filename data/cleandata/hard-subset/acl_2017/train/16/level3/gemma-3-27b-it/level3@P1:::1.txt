This paper addresses the event detection (ED) task, arguing that event arguments, frequently overlooked in existing methods, provide valuable clues for identifying and categorizing events. The authors propose a neural network approach incorporating a supervised attention mechanism to explicitly exploit argument information.  They systematically investigate different attention strategies and demonstrate state-of-the-art performance on the ACE 2005 dataset.

The primary contribution of this work is the effective integration of argument information into a neural network framework for event detection via supervised attention. While joint event and argument extraction models exist, the authors convincingly argue these primarily benefit argument extraction, not detection, and often indirectly utilize argument information. This work directly addresses this gap by focusing attention on argument-related words during the detection phase. A second contribution lies in the empirical demonstration of the value of this approach, achieving significant performance gains over strong baselines and state-of-the-art methods on a standard benchmark.  Finally, the systematic investigation of different attention strategies provides insights into the influence of argument information and offers a useful analysis for future research.

**Strengths:**

1. **Novelty and Importance of Argument Exploitation:** The core idea of explicitly leveraging argument information for event detection is a significant contribution.  Most prior work neglects this information or uses it indirectly through joint models. The authors clearly articulate why argument information is valuable (disambiguating trigger words) and convincingly demonstrate its benefits.
2. **Strong Empirical Results:** The proposed approach achieves state-of-the-art results on the ACE 2005 dataset, consistently outperforming established baselines, including both feature-based and representation-based methods, as well as existing joint models.  The gains are substantial and statistically significant, supporting the effectiveness of the proposed approach.
3. **Clear and Well-Defined Framework:** The architecture of the proposed model is relatively straightforward, making it easy to understand and potentially extend. The use of supervised attention provides a clear mechanism for incorporating argument information.
4. **Thorough Experimental Evaluation:** The authors conduct a comprehensive set of experiments, including comparisons with several strong baselines and an ablation study to demonstrate the impact of different components of their model (e.g., different attention strategies, augmentation with FrameNet data).
5. **Demonstrated Benefit of Data Augmentation:** The inclusion of data from FrameNet further improves performance, highlighting the challenge of data sparsity in event detection and the potential benefits of leveraging external resources.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Simplicity of Attention Mechanism:** While the supervised attention mechanism is effective, its implementation (summing two attention vectors) appears somewhat simplistic. Exploring more sophisticated attention mechanisms, such as multiplicative interactions or hierarchical attention, could potentially yield further improvements.
2. **Limited Exploration of Attention Strategies:**  The authors investigate two attention strategies (S1 and S2), but a more extensive exploration of different strategies for constructing gold attention vectors might uncover more optimal ways to leverage argument information. For example, weighting arguments based on their semantic role or distance to the trigger.
3. **Dependence on ACE 2005:** The evaluation is primarily limited to the ACE 2005 dataset. While this is a standard benchmark, the results may not generalize to other datasets with different characteristics. Evaluating on additional datasets would strengthen the conclusions.
4. **Lack of Error Analysis:** A detailed error analysis would be beneficial for understanding the limitations of the model and identifying areas for future improvement. For example, investigating cases where the model fails to correctly identify events despite the presence of informative arguments.



**Questions to Authors:**

1.  Could you elaborate on the rationale for summing the two attention vectors (αw and αe) rather than using a more complex combination method?
2.  Have you considered weighting the arguments differently when constructing the gold attention vectors (e.g., based on their semantic role or distance to the trigger)? If not, why?
3.  Do you have any insights into the types of events or triggers where the proposed model performs particularly well or poorly?
4.  Could you discuss the computational complexity of the proposed approach compared to other state-of-the-art methods?

**Additional Comments:**

- The paper is well-written and clearly organized.
- The related work section provides a good overview of the relevant literature.
- The code and data used in the experiments should be made publicly available to facilitate reproducibility.
- The use of a t-test to demonstrate statistical significance is appreciated.



Overall, this is a strong paper with a novel and well-motivated approach to event detection. The experimental results are compelling, and the authors provide a clear and concise presentation of their work. I recommend acceptance.
