- Addresses event detection by explicitly leveraging event arguments with supervised attention in a neural network, achieving state-of-the-art on ACE 2005.
- Main contributions: direct integration of argument information for detection via supervised attention, strong empirical gains, and systematic investigation of attention strategies.
- Novelty: explicitly exploiting argument info for ED (beyond joint models) to disambiguate triggers.
- Strong empirical results: significant, statistically significant improvements over strong baselines and prior SOTA.
- Clear framework: straightforward architecture and a supervised attention mechanism that is easy to understand and extend.
- Thorough evaluation: comprehensive comparisons, ablations, and analysis of attention strategies (including FrameNet augmentation).
- Data augmentation benefit: incorporating FrameNet helps mitigate data sparsity and improves performance.
- Weakness: attention mechanism is simplistic (summing two vectors) — more sophisticated variants could help.
- Weakness: limited exploration of attention strategies (only S1 and S2); alternatives like role- or distance-weighting not studied.
- Weakness: evaluation is primarily on ACE 2005, so generalizability to other datasets is uncertain.
- Weakness: lacks detailed error analysis to identify failure modes and guide improvements.
- Question: Why sum αw and αe instead of a more complex combination method?
- Question: Were arguments weighted differently in gold attention vectors (e.g., by semantic role or distance)?
- Question: Which event/trigger types does the model perform particularly well or poorly on?
- Question: What is the computational complexity compared to other state-of-the-art methods?
- Additional: Paper is well-written and clearly organized.
- Additional: Related work section provides a good overview.
- Additional: Code and data should be released to enable reproducibility.
- Additional: Use of t-test for statistical significance is appropriate and appreciated.
- Overall recommendation: Strong paper; recommend acceptance.
