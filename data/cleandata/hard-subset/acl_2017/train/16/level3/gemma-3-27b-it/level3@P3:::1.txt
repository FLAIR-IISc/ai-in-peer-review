This paper tackles event detection (ED) by explicitly leveraging argument information, a frequently overlooked aspect in existing approaches. The authors argue that arguments provide crucial clues for identifying and categorizing events, especially when trigger words are ambiguous. They propose a neural network framework with supervised attention mechanisms, trained to focus on argument words, and demonstrate significant performance gains on the ACE 2005 dataset.

**Main Contributions:**

1. **Highlighting the Importance of Argument Information:** The paper convincingly argues for and demonstrates the benefit of explicitly incorporating argument information into event detection. This is a valuable contribution, as prior work largely ignored or indirectly utilized this data. The provided example with "fired" illustrates the point effectively.
2. **Effective Supervised Attention Framework:** The proposed framework, employing supervised attention, provides a clear and effective method for incorporating argument information. The use of attention mechanisms is well-motivated and contributes significantly to the observed performance improvements.
3. **Solid Experimental Validation:** The authors present a thorough experimental evaluation on the ACE 2005 dataset, demonstrating that their approach outperforms state-of-the-art methods. The inclusion of ablation studies (comparing different attention strategies) and data augmentation (using FrameNet) strengthens the results.

**Strengths:**

1. **Novelty and Insight:** The central idea of explicitly leveraging argument information for event detection is a novel contribution. While joint models exist, the authors correctly point out that these models often don’t effectively utilize arguments for *improving* ED, instead primarily focusing on argument extraction itself. The analysis of why previous joint methods failed to fully benefit from argument information is insightful.
2. **Clear and Concise Framework:** The proposed framework is relatively simple and easy to understand. The architecture is clearly presented in Figure 1, and the explanations of the attention mechanisms are straightforward. This clarity facilitates reproducibility and potential extensions of the work.
3. **Strong Empirical Results:** The experimental results are compelling. The reported F1 score improvements over baseline systems are substantial, and the ablation studies demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed attention mechanisms. The use of t-tests further supports the significance of the findings.
4. **Thorough Experimental Setup:** The experimental setup is well-defined, with clear descriptions of the dataset, hyperparameter tuning, and evaluation metrics. The inclusion of comparisons to both feature-based and representation-based state-of-the-art methods provides a comprehensive benchmark. The augmentation with FrameNet data demonstrates robustness.
5. **Well-Written and Organized:** The paper is well-written and logically organized. The introduction clearly motivates the problem and the proposed approach, and the related work section provides a good overview of the existing literature.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Naive Attention Combination:** The attention vector is computed as a simple sum of two part-wise attention vectors (αw + αe). This seems somewhat simplistic and may not optimally capture the interactions between word-level and entity-level attention. A more sophisticated combination mechanism, such as a learned weighting or a gating mechanism, could potentially improve performance.
2. **Limited Attention Strategy Exploration:** The two proposed supervised attention strategies (S1 and S2) are relatively straightforward. While they demonstrate the effectiveness of supervised attention, more complex and nuanced strategies might yield even better results. Exploring strategies that consider the *type* of argument (role) or the distance between words could be beneficial.
3. **Dependence on ACE 2005:** While ACE 2005 is a standard benchmark, the results may not generalize to other datasets or event extraction tasks. Evaluating the approach on more diverse datasets would strengthen the conclusions.
4. **Lack of Error Analysis:** The paper lacks a detailed error analysis. Identifying the types of errors made by the model (e.g., misclassifying trigger words, incorrectly identifying event types) could provide valuable insights for future improvements.

**Questions to Authors:**

1.  Could you elaborate on the rationale behind simply summing the word-level and entity-level attention vectors? Have you experimented with other combination mechanisms, and if so, what were the results?
2.  What considerations led you to choose the specific parameters for the Gaussian distribution in strategy S2 (µ = 0.0, σ = 1.0, window size = 3)? Did you perform sensitivity analysis on these parameters?
3.  What types of errors does the model still make frequently? Providing examples of misclassifications could help guide future research.
4.  Do you anticipate that the benefits of using argument information will be as significant in languages with richer morphology or different syntactic structures?



**Additional Comments:**

*   The reference list appears to be incomplete (e.g., there is a placeholder for "Baker et al.,? 1998").
*   It would be helpful to include a discussion of the computational complexity of the proposed approach.
* The annotation of the example in Figure 2 is a bit unclear. Highlighting the arguments in the sentence directly might be more effective.



Overall, this is a strong paper that presents a valuable contribution to the field of event detection. The authors have identified a previously underutilized source of information (argument information) and developed an effective framework for incorporating it into event detection models. Despite a few minor weaknesses, the paper is well-written, well-motivated, and supported by compelling experimental results. **I recommend acceptance.**
