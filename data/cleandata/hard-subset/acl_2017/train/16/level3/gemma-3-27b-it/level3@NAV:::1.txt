## Review of "Exploiting Argument Information to Improve Event Detection via Supervised Attention Mechanisms"

This paper addresses the event detection (ED) task, arguing that leveraging argument information – often overlooked – can significantly improve performance. The authors propose a neural network approach with supervised attention mechanisms, focusing on different attention strategies to effectively incorporate argument clues. The paper demonstrates a clear framework and achieves state-of-the-art results on the ACE 2005 dataset.

The primary contribution of this work, in my view, is the **demonstration of the effectiveness of explicitly incorporating argument information into an attention-based event detection framework.** While joint event and argument extraction exists, this work highlights that simply *using* arguments in the ED process (even with supervised attention) offers substantial gains, outperforming existing joint methods on the ED task specifically. The systematic investigation of different attention strategies, while incremental, is a valuable step in understanding how to best leverage these argument cues. Furthermore, the solid experimental validation and comparative analysis with existing state-of-the-art methods bolster the claim of improvement.

**Strengths:**

*   **Strong Performance:** The proposed model significantly outperforms established baselines and achieves a new state-of-the-art F1 score on the ACE 2005 dataset. This provides strong empirical evidence supporting the effectiveness of the approach.
*   **Clear and Simple Framework:** The proposed architecture is relatively straightforward and easy to understand, making it accessible and potentially adaptable to other event detection scenarios.
*   **Effective Use of Supervised Attention:** The supervised attention mechanism, guided by annotated arguments, allows the model to focus on relevant contextual information, leading to improved event detection accuracy. The empirical results support the claim that the model learns to attend to argument words.
*   **Well-Defined Experiments:** The experimental setup is rigorous, with a clear comparison to existing methods and a well-defined development set for hyperparameter tuning.
*   **Analysis of Attention Strategies:** The investigation into different strategies (S1 & S2) provides valuable insights into how to best utilize argument information.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Naive Attention Vector Computation:** The method of summing two part-wise attention vectors appears somewhat simplistic. A more sophisticated attention mechanism, such as incorporating more complex interactions between words and arguments, might yield further improvements.
*   **Limited Complexity of Attention Strategies:** While the exploration of S1 and S2 is a good starting point, the strategies themselves are relatively straightforward. Exploring more complex strategies incorporating context or dependency parsing could potentially lead to more nuanced attention weighting.
*   **Data Dependency:** The strong performance is demonstrated on ACE 2005, a specific dataset. The generalizability of the approach to other datasets with different event types or argument structures could be further investigated.

**Questions to Authors:**

*   Could you elaborate on why the summation of two attention vectors was chosen over alternative methods for combining them? Did you experiment with other combination methods (e.g., concatenation, element-wise multiplication)?
*   What is the computational overhead associated with the supervised attention mechanism compared to a standard attention mechanism without supervision?
*   Could you analyze the learned attention weights to provide further insights into which types of arguments are most influential for event detection?
