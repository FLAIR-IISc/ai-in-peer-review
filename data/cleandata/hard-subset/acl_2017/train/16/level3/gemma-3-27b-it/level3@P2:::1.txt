This paper tackles the event detection (ED) task, arguing that event arguments, often ignored in existing approaches, provide valuable clues for identifying and categorizing events. The authors propose a neural network-based approach that explicitly exploits argument information via supervised attention mechanisms. Experimental results on the ACE 2005 dataset demonstrate that their approach advances the state-of-the-art, achieving the best reported F1 score.

The primary contribution of this work is the explicit incorporation of argument information into an event detection framework using supervised attention. While joint event and argument extraction models exist, the authors convincingly argue that these often indirectly leverage arguments, with benefits primarily accruing to argument extraction rather than event detection. By focusing attention directly on arguments during event detection, the authors achieve significant performance improvements. A second contribution lies in the systematic investigation of different attention strategies to supervise the attention mechanism, providing insight into how best to leverage argument information. Finally, the thorough experimental evaluation, including ablation studies and comparison to strong baselines, provides convincing evidence of the effectiveness of the proposed approach.

**Strengths:**

1. **Novelty and Importance of Argument Exploitation:** The paper addresses a crucial and often overlooked aspect of event detection – the role of event arguments. Existing methods largely ignore or indirectly utilize this information. The authors’ focus on explicitly incorporating argument information represents a valuable contribution to the field. The claim that joint models primarily benefit argument extraction is well-supported by the literature cited in Table 1.
2. **Strong Empirical Results:** The proposed approach achieves state-of-the-art performance on the ACE 2005 dataset. The improvements over strong baselines, as presented in Table 3, are significant and demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed method. The ablation studies in Section 4.2 further validate the importance of utilizing argument information.
3. **Clear and Well-Defined Framework:** The proposed architecture is relatively straightforward and easy to understand. The use of supervised attention is clearly explained, and the two proposed strategies (S1 and S2) for constructing gold attention vectors are logically presented. The implementation details provided are sufficient for reproducibility.
4. **Thorough Experimental Evaluation:** The authors conduct a comprehensive set of experiments, including comparisons to relevant baselines, ablation studies to analyze the contribution of different components, and experiments to augment the training data with information from FrameNet. This thoroughness strengthens the credibility of the results.
5. **Demonstrated Generalizability:** Augmenting with FrameNet data shows the method’s ability to leverage external knowledge, further bolstering the results and suggesting broader applicability.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Naïve Attention Vector Computation:** The attention vector is simply the sum of two part-wise attention vectors. This approach seems somewhat simplistic and may not fully capture the complex interactions between words and arguments. Exploring more sophisticated attention mechanisms, such as multiplicative interactions or hierarchical attention, could potentially yield further performance gains.
2. **Limited Exploration of Attention Strategies:** While the authors investigate two strategies for constructing gold attention vectors, these strategies are relatively basic. Exploring more complex strategies that consider the semantic relationships between arguments and the trigger word, or that incorporate contextual information beyond the immediate vicinity of the arguments, could potentially lead to further improvements.
3. **Dataset Limitation:** Evaluation is limited to the ACE 2005 dataset. While this is a standard benchmark, it is relatively small and may not fully reflect the challenges of event detection in more diverse and realistic scenarios. Evaluating the approach on other datasets would strengthen the generalizability of the findings.
4. **Lack of Error Analysis:** While the quantitative results are strong, the paper lacks a detailed error analysis. Investigating the types of errors made by the proposed approach and identifying potential areas for improvement would provide valuable insights.

**Questions to Authors:**

1. Could you elaborate on the rationale behind summing the two attention vectors (word and entity) instead of employing a more complex interaction mechanism?
2. Were any other strategies for creating the “gold attention” considered beyond S1 and S2? If so, could you briefly describe them and explain why they were not chosen?
3. How sensitive is the performance of the model to the hyperparameters, particularly λ, used for combining the loss functions? Did you perform a more extensive hyperparameter search?
4. Could you provide some examples of error cases where the model fails to correctly identify event types, and discuss potential reasons for these failures?



**Additional Comments:**

- The citation style is inconsistent.
- The paper would benefit from a discussion of the computational complexity of the proposed approach.
- A visualization of the attention weights could provide further insight into how the model is leveraging argument information.

**Overall Assessment:**

Despite the minor weaknesses, this is a strong paper that presents a novel and effective approach to event detection. The authors convincingly demonstrate the importance of exploiting argument information and provide a clear and well-supported framework for doing so. The experimental results are compelling, and the thorough evaluation strengthens the credibility of the findings. I recommend acceptance.
