- Proposes supervised attention for event detection that uses gold argument annotations to focus attention on argument words for trigger classification.
- Introduces two heuristic supervision strategies: S1 (uniform over arguments) and S2 (Gaussian blur including surrounding words).
- Implements a simple neural trigger classifier with supervised attention that outperforms an unsupervised attention baseline.
- Reports a new state-of-the-art F1 on ACE 2005 (e.g., ANN-S2 71.7 vs. unsupervised 64.6).
- Ablation studies show the performance gain stems from supervised argument information, not just attention or entity signals.
- Experimental evaluation is comprehensive, comparing many strong baselines and showing robustness including FrameNet-augmented training.
- Attention combination is a naive sum of word and entity attentions (α = αw + αe), a design choice that is unjustified and possibly suboptimal.
- Supervision strategies (S1/S2) are heuristic rather than learned, and may not reflect optimal attention patterns.
- Reliance on gold argument annotations during training limits applicability in low-resource settings; FrameNet augmentation may introduce noise.
- Requests clarification on whether alternative attention-combination methods (e.g., concatenation or learned gating) were tried and their performance.
- Asks why supervising attention is less susceptible to joint-model bias toward argument extraction despite using the same annotations.
- Asks about sensitivity to S2 hyperparameters (Gaussian σ, window) and whether supervisory attention distributions could be learned or made data-driven.
