### Summary of the paper
This paper proposes a novel method to improve event detection (ED) by explicitly leveraging information from event arguments. The authors argue that while arguments provide crucial clues for disambiguating event triggers, existing pipeline methods ignore them, and joint learning methods fail to utilize them effectively for the ED subtask. The proposed model is a neural network that classifies trigger candidates. Its core contribution is a supervised attention mechanism that is trained to focus on words that are annotated as event arguments. The supervision signal is derived from the gold-standard argument annotations in the training data, for which the authors propose two heuristic strategies (one focusing only on arguments, the other also including surrounding words). Experiments on the ACE 2005 dataset demonstrate that this supervised approach significantly outperforms an unsupervised attention baseline and achieves a new state-of-the-art F1 score.

### Main Contributions
1.  **A novel and effective framework for incorporating argument information into event detection via supervised attention.** The paper's primary contribution is the idea of using gold argument annotations to directly supervise an attention mechanism for the ED task. This provides a direct and explicit way to leverage a rich information source that previous methods have struggled to exploit effectively for trigger identification, as convincingly argued in the introduction.

2.  **Strong empirical results establishing a new state-of-the-art on a standard benchmark.** The paper presents a thorough experimental evaluation on the ACE 2005 dataset. The proposed method significantly outperforms strong baselines and a range of previous state-of-the-art systems. The ablation studies clearly demonstrate that the performance gain comes from the supervised use of argument information, not just from the attention mechanism itself.

### Strengths
1.  **Clear motivation and problem analysis.** The paper does an excellent job of motivating the work. It clearly articulates why argument information is useful for ED (e.g., for disambiguating trigger words like "fired") and provides empirical evidence (Table 1) to support its claim that existing joint models, while theoretically promising, have failed to translate this information into significant gains for the ED subtask. This analysis effectively carves out a niche for the proposed approach.

2.  **Simple, elegant, and effective model.** The proposed method is conceptually simple and easy to understand. By formulating the problem as supervising an attention mechanism, the authors found an elegant way to inject argument information directly into the trigger classification model. The remarkable performance improvement (e.g., F1 score of 71.7 for ANN-S2 vs. 64.6 for the unsupervised baseline) underscores the power of this simple idea.

3.  **Comprehensive experimental validation.** The experiments are well-designed and convincing. The authors compare their model against a wide array of recent and strong baselines, including both feature-based and neural methods. The inclusion of the experiment in Section 4.2 (ANN vs. ANN-ENT vs. ANN-Gold1/2) is particularly effective in demonstrating that the improvement is due to specific argument information, not just general entity information. Finally, augmenting the training data with FrameNet shows the robustness and potential for further extension of the model.

### Weaknesses
1.  **Oversimplified attention combination.** The method combines the attention scores from contextual words (`αw`) and contextual entities (`αe`) by simple summation (`α = αw + αe`). This is a rather naive approach that treats both information sources as equally important and additive. This design choice is not justified, and it is plausible that a more sophisticated combination, such as concatenation followed by a linear projection or a learned gating mechanism, could yield better performance by allowing the model to weigh the two attention signals differently.

2.  **Heuristic nature of supervision strategies.** The two strategies (S1 and S2) for creating the "gold" attention signals are based on simple heuristics (a uniform distribution over arguments or a fixed Gaussian blur around them). While shown to be effective, these strategies are not learned from data and may not represent the optimal attention patterns. The paper could be strengthened by exploring or at least discussing more principled, data-driven approaches to generating the supervisory signal, or by analyzing the model's sensitivity to the hyper-parameters of these heuristics (e.g., the Gaussian variance in S2).

3.  **Reliance on gold argument annotations during training.** The proposed supervised method is fundamentally dependent on the availability of argument annotations in the training corpus. This could limit its applicability in low-resource settings or on datasets where only trigger information is annotated. While the authors demonstrate a workaround using automatically labeled data from FrameNet, this process introduces noise and relies on external tools. A more thorough discussion of this limitation and the model's robustness to noisy argument annotations would have been beneficial.

### Questions to Authors
1.  Could you elaborate on the design choice of simply summing the word and entity attention vectors (`α = αw + αe`)? Have you experimented with alternative combination methods, such as concatenation or a learned gating mechanism, and how did they perform in comparison?
2.  The paper argues that joint models are biased towards argument extraction due to the larger number of argument annotations. Your supervised attention model also relies heavily on these same annotations. Could you clarify why supervising the attention mechanism is less susceptible to this bias compared to optimizing a joint loss function for both triggers and arguments?
3.  How sensitive is the model's performance to the hyper-parameters of the S2 strategy (e.g., the Gaussian's standard deviation `σ` and window size)? Have you considered learning these attention distributions or exploring other methods to generate the supervisory signal?
