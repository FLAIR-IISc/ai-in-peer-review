### Summary

This paper addresses the task of event detection (ED), focusing on identifying and classifying event triggers. The authors argue that event arguments, which are often ignored or used indirectly in prior work, provide crucial information for disambiguating trigger words. The core proposal is a neural network framework that explicitly leverages this argument information through a supervised attention mechanism. In this setup, the attention weights generated by the model are trained to align with "gold" attention vectors constructed from the annotated arguments in the training data. The authors propose and evaluate two strategies for creating these gold attention signals. Experiments on the ACE 2005 dataset show that this approach significantly outperforms an unsupervised attention baseline and achieves state-of-the-art results compared to previous methods.

### Contributions

1.  **A Novel Method for Explicitly Using Argument Information for Event Detection**: The primary contribution is the proposal to use annotated arguments as direct supervision for an attention mechanism in an event detection model. The paper convincingly argues that previous pipeline methods ignore this information, while joint models often fail to effectively leverage it for the ED subtask. This work provides a simple and direct method to inject this valuable signal, demonstrating that focusing on how information flows between subtasks can be more effective than complex joint inference.

2.  **A Simple and Effective Neural Framework with Strong Empirical Validation**: The paper presents a straightforward neural architecture that is easy to understand and likely to be reproducible. Despite its simplicity, the model achieves a new state-of-the-art F1 score on the standard ACE 2005 benchmark. The authors provide a strong empirical case through a series of well-designed experiments, including an initial study to validate their core assumption, comprehensive comparisons against strong baselines, and an analysis using external data from FrameNet.

### Strengths

1.  **Addresses a Clear Gap in Prior Work**: The paper's motivation is excellent. It correctly identifies that argument information is an underutilized resource for the ED task. The analysis in the introduction (Table 1), which shows that state-of-the-art joint models improve argument extraction but provide negligible gains for event detection, is a compelling justification for this work. The proposed method provides an elegant solution to this specific problem.

2.  **Simple and Effective Model Design**: The proposed framework is clear and well-conceived. By framing the problem as supervising the model's attention, the authors avoid the complexities and potential optimization challenges of full joint-inference models. The significant performance jump from the unsupervised baseline (`ANN`, F1=64.6) to the supervised versions (`ANN-S2`, F1=71.7) provides powerful evidence that this targeted supervision is the key driver of the model's success.

3.  **Thorough Experimental Evaluation**: The experiments are solid and convincingly support the paper's claims. The authors first use "gold" attention during testing (Table 2) to establish an upper-bound for their assumption, showing that argument information is indeed highly beneficial. They then demonstrate that their supervised attention mechanism can successfully learn this signal and outperform strong, recently published baselines (Table 3). Finally, the experiments incorporating FrameNet data show that the approach is robust and benefits from additional, albeit noisy, training data.

4.  **High-Precision Results**: A noteworthy advantage of the proposed method is its high precision (78.0 for ANN-S2, and 81.4 for ANN-S1). This is a significant improvement over many prior systems and is a valuable characteristic for real-world applications, where high-confidence extractions are often preferred. The paper provides a plausible explanation for this, suggesting that focusing on arguments provides precise, disambiguating information.

### Weaknesses

1.  **Naive Combination of Attention Vectors**: The final attention vector `α` is computed as a simple, unweighted sum of the contextual word attention `αw` and the contextual entity attention `αe` (`α = αw + αe`). This is a rather naive fusion strategy that assumes both information sources contribute equally in all contexts. It is plausible that for certain trigger candidates, one attention source is more reliable than the other. A more sophisticated combination method, such as a learned weighted average or a gating mechanism, could potentially yield further improvements by allowing the model to dynamically balance the two sources.

2.  **Simplicity of Supervision Strategies**: The two proposed strategies (S1 and S2) for constructing the "gold" attention vectors are reasonable heuristics but are somewhat simplistic. S1 is a hard, binary assignment, while S2 uses a fixed Gaussian distribution to "blur" the attention around arguments. These heuristics might not fully capture the complex relationships between arguments and triggers. For instance, some argument roles might be more indicative of an event type than others, suggesting a role-weighted supervision signal could be more effective. The paper would be strengthened by a discussion of the limitations of these strategies or an ablation study on the S2 hyperparameters (e.g., window size, sigma).

### Questions to Authors

1.  Regarding the combination of word attention (`αw`) and entity attention (`αe`), you use a simple sum. Have you considered or experimented with more complex fusion methods, such as a weighted sum or a gating mechanism, that could learn the relative importance of these two attention sources for a given instance?

2.  The two strategies for constructing gold attention (S1 and S2) are well-motivated but based on heuristics. Could you comment on the sensitivity of the model to the hyper-parameters in S2 (e.g., the Gaussian's standard deviation and window size)? Did you explore other, potentially more data-driven or linguistically-motivated, ways to generate the supervision signal?

### Overall Recommendation

This is a well-written and well-executed paper that makes a clear and valuable contribution. It identifies an important and under-exploited source of information for event detection and proposes a simple, novel, and highly effective method to leverage it. The experiments are thorough and the results are state-of-the-art. While there are opportunities to explore more sophisticated attention mechanisms and supervision strategies, the presented framework is strong and convincing as is. The paper's strengths decisively outweigh its minor weaknesses. I strongly recommend acceptance.
