**Summary**

This paper addresses the task of Event Detection (ED) by proposing a novel approach that explicitly leverages information from event arguments—a rich signal that is often underutilized by prior methods. The authors introduce a neural network framework featuring a supervised attention mechanism. This mechanism is trained to focus on annotated argument words in the context of a trigger candidate, thereby guiding the model to make more accurate event type predictions. The primary contribution is a simple yet highly effective method for integrating argument information directly into the ED task, demonstrating that explicit supervision of attention is a powerful technique for this problem. The comprehensive experiments, which establish a new state-of-the-art on the ACE 2005 dataset, serve as a significant secondary contribution.

**Strengths**

1.  **Novel and Effective Use of Argument Information:** The paper's core strength lies in its well-motivated proposal to use argument information for ED. The authors correctly identify that previous joint learning models failed to significantly improve ED, and their approach provides a direct and effective solution. By using arguments to supervise an attention mechanism, the model learns to focus on the most salient contextual clues, leading to substantial performance gains over strong baselines.

2.  **Simplicity and Clarity of the Framework:** The proposed model is elegant in its simplicity. Rather than introducing a highly complex architecture, the paper presents a straightforward modification to a standard neural classifier. This clarity makes the approach easy to understand, appreciate, and likely reimplement, which is a valuable contribution to the community.

3.  **Strong Empirical Results and Analysis:** The experimental evaluation is thorough and convincing. The model significantly outperforms previous state-of-the-art methods on the widely used ACE 2005 benchmark. The ablation studies effectively validate the central hypothesis, showing that supervised attention is superior to unsupervised attention and that focusing on arguments is more beneficial than focusing on all entities.

**Weaknesses**

1.  **Naive Combination of Attention Vectors:** The final attention vector is computed as a simple summation of the word-based and entity-based attention vectors. This approach is somewhat naive, as it treats both information sources with equal importance. A more sophisticated fusion method, such as a learned gating mechanism or concatenation followed by a projection layer, could potentially yield better performance by dynamically weighing the two attention signals.

2.  **Straightforward Supervision Strategies:** While effective, the two proposed strategies (S1 and S2) for constructing the gold attention vectors are heuristic and relatively simple. Strategy S1 assigns uniform attention to all arguments, while S2 applies a fixed Gaussian distribution around them. It is possible that more nuanced or data-driven strategies for generating the supervisory signal could lead to further improvements.
