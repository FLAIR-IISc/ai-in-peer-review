- Proposes a neural event detection model that uses annotated event arguments to supervise an attention mechanism during training.  
- Introduces two heuristic strategies (S1 uniform, S2 Gaussian) to create "gold" attention signals from argument annotations.  
- Trains with both event classification loss and an auxiliary attention supervision loss to steer attention to argument locations.  
- Demonstrates strong empirical gains on ACE 2005, achieving a new state-of-the-art F1 (71.7) and 71.9 with FrameNet augmentation.  
- Main contribution: a novel, simple, and effective use of supervised attention to inject argument information into trigger classification.  
- Main contribution: thorough empirical validation and ablation/oracle studies showing argument information drives improvements.  
- Main contribution: a simple, easy-to-implement framework that yields large gains without major model complexity.  
- Strength: clear motivation and well-argued hypothesis about why arguments disambiguate triggers.  
- Strength: significant performance improvement and high precision over recent baselines.  
- Strength: simple framework likely easy to adapt to other pipelines.  
- Weakness: naive combination of word and entity attention via simple sum (α = αw + αe) may miss dynamic weighting opportunities.  
- Weakness: gold-attention construction strategies (S1, S2) are heuristic and do not account for differing informativeness of arguments.  
- Weakness: reliance on a fixed context window may miss long-distance argument-trigger dependencies.  
- Question: Have you tried alternative fusion methods (concatenation, learned gating) for combining αw and αe, and how sensitive is performance to this choice?  
- Question: Is the ANN base architecture comparable to other recent neural ED systems (e.g., Skip-CNN), or might supervised attention be compensating for a simpler base model?  
- Question: How were S2’s Gaussian parameters and window size (3) chosen, and how sensitive is the model to µ/σ and window size?
