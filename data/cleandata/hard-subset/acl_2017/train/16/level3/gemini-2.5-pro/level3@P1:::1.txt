**Summary and Contributions**

This paper addresses the task of event detection (ED), arguing that event arguments, while providing crucial disambiguation clues, are often ignored or used ineffectively in prior work. The authors propose a neural network model that explicitly leverages annotated argument information through a supervised attention mechanism. During training, the model is guided not only by the standard event classification loss but also by an additional loss term that encourages its attention mechanism to focus on the locations of known event arguments in the training data. The authors explore two different strategies for creating these "gold" attention signals. Experiments on the ACE 2005 dataset show that this approach significantly improves performance, achieving a new state-of-the-art result.

The main contributions of this work are:

1.  **A Novel and Effective Use of Supervised Attention for Event Detection:** The primary contribution is the simple yet powerful idea of using annotated arguments as direct supervision for an attention mechanism in an ED model. This provides a direct and explicit way to inject valuable argument information into the trigger classification task, addressing a well-motivated shortcoming of previous pipeline and joint-learning approaches.

2.  **Strong Empirical Validation and Analysis:** The paper presents a comprehensive set of experiments that convincingly demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed method. The model significantly outperforms strong recent baselines on the standard ACE 2005 benchmark. The ablation studies, particularly the comparison with an entity-focused baseline (ANN-ENT) and oracle experiments (ANN-Gold), provide clear evidence supporting the central hypothesis that argument information is key.

**Strengths**

1.  **Clear Motivation and Well-Founded Hypothesis:** The paper is built on a strong, intuitive, and well-articulated motivation. The introduction clearly explains why argument information is useful for ED (e.g., for ambiguous triggers like "fired") and provides a convincing analysis of why previous joint models have largely failed to leverage this information to improve ED performance. The proposed solution directly and elegantly addresses this identified gap.

2.  **Significant Performance Improvement:** The experimental results are a major strength. The proposed model (ANN-S2) achieves a state-of-the-art F1 score of 71.7 on the ACE 2005 dataset, outperforming recent and competitive methods, including feature-based, joint-learning, and other representation-based approaches. The further improvement to 71.9 with FrameNet data augmentation reinforces the model's strength and robustness. The high precision of the models is also a noteworthy advantage.

3.  **Simple and Effective Framework:** The proposed framework is clear, straightforward to implement, and effective. By adding a supervision signal to a standard attention mechanism within a simple feed-forward network, the authors achieve substantial gains without introducing excessive model complexity. This simplicity makes the approach appealing and likely easy to adapt or integrate into other event extraction pipelines.

**Weaknesses**

1.  **Simplistic Attention Vector Combination:** The final attention vector `α` is calculated as a simple sum of the contextual word attention `αw` and the contextual entity attention `αe`. This is a somewhat naive combination that treats both sources of information equally. It is possible that a more sophisticated combination, such as a learned gating mechanism or concatenation followed by a dense layer, could allow the model to dynamically weigh the importance of word versus entity context, potentially leading to further improvements.

2.  **Heuristic Nature of Gold Attention Strategies:** The two strategies (S1 and S2) for constructing the gold attention vectors are reasonable but heuristic. S1 assigns uniform weight to all arguments, and S2 applies a fixed Gaussian distribution around them. These strategies do not account for the possibility that some arguments may be more informative than others for identifying a specific event type. A more nuanced approach, perhaps weighing arguments by their role or learning the distribution, might yield better supervision signals.

3.  **Use of a Fixed Context Window:** The model relies on a fixed-length context window around the candidate trigger. This approach may fail to capture relevant long-distance dependencies, particularly if a crucial argument appears far from the trigger in a long sentence. While common, this is a limitation compared to models that process the entire sentence, such as those based on recurrent neural networks (RNNs), which could potentially be combined with the proposed supervised attention idea.

**Questions to Authors**

1.  Regarding the combination of attention vectors (`α = αw + αe`), have you experimented with alternative fusion methods, such as concatenation or a weighted sum with a learned gating parameter? How sensitive is the model to this specific design choice?

2.  The performance of the baseline unsupervised attention model (ANN) is surprisingly low (64.6 F1) compared to other recent neural methods. The paper attributes this to data sparsity. Could you clarify if the base architecture of your ANN is comparable to that of other state-of-the-art systems like Skip-CNN? Is it possible that the supervised attention is not just adding information but also compensating for a simpler base model?

3.  The S2 strategy uses a standard normal distribution to spread attention around arguments. How did you choose the window size of 3, and have you analyzed the model's sensitivity to the parameters of this Gaussian distribution (i.e., µ and σ)?
