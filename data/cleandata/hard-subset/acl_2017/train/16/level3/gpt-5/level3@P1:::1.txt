Summary
The paper addresses event detection (ED) in ACE 2005 and proposes to leverage event argument information explicitly via supervised attention. The model computes attention over contextual words and entity types, combining two attention vectors and supervising them with “gold” attention constructed from annotated arguments using two strategies: (S1) focusing only on argument tokens; (S2) diffusing attention to tokens around arguments via a Gaussian. A simple ANN classifier consumes the weighted context and candidate trigger embeddings. Experiments on ACE 2005 show notable gains over a comparable unsupervised-attention baseline and improvements over several state-of-the-art systems. Additional experiments with FrameNet-based augmentation support the hypothesis that data sparsity limits unsupervised attention and further improve F1.

Contributions (as I see them)
1) Demonstration that explicit supervision from argument annotations via supervised attention yields consistent and significant improvements for ED, clarifying why joint ED/AE methods have not historically improved ED.
2) A systematic empirical study of argument-informed attention supervision strategies (argument-only vs. argument+neighborhood) including ablations (ANN, ANN-ENT, Gold1/2), with statistically significant gains on ACE 2005.
3) A simple and practical neural framework that is easy to integrate with standard ED pipelines and benefits further from data augmentation (FrameNet).

Strengths
1) Clear and compelling motivation and problem formulation. The paper identifies a concrete gap: prior joint models largely help AE, not ED, often due to data imbalance and indirect use of arguments for ED. The proposed supervised attention explicitly aligns the ED model with argument cues, making the rationale easy to follow and operationalize.

2) Strong empirical evidence with careful ablations. The baseline without argument supervision (ANN: 64.6 F1) underperforms, while supervising attention with arguments (ANN-S2: 71.7 F1, ANN-S1: 70.8 F1) yields substantial gains and surpasses prior work (e.g., Liu FN-based: 70.7 F1; Nguyen joint: 69.3 F1). The comparisons to ANN-ENT and Gold1/Gold2 convincingly show that (i) entities alone are insufficient and (ii) argument supervision is the key driver. The significance test (p ≤ 0.05) further supports the result.

3) Simple, well-specified approach likely to be reproducible and broadly useful. The architecture, training procedure, and hyperparameters are clearly described. The approach requires minimal changes to common ED neural models and can be implemented without complex inference or joint decoding.

4) Insightful analysis of supervision strategies and error trade-offs. The study of S1 vs. S2 provides useful guidance: S1 yields higher precision, S2 yields higher recall. This aligns with the intuition about focus vs. context diffusion. The FrameNet augmentation experiment strengthens the data-sparsity argument by showing unsupervised attention improves with more data.

Weaknesses
1) Limited novelty in modeling; attention design is simplistic. The attention combination is a simple sum of word- and entity-based attentions. No learned gating, dependency-aware attention, or structural constraints are explored. The supervision strategies (S1/S2) are straightforward and heuristic; more principled or learnable supervision (e.g., learned diffusion, syntax-/role-aware kernels) may further improve performance.

2) Dependence on gold argument annotations for supervision at training time may limit applicability. Many corpora lack argument labels, and the approach’s gains rely on those labels. While test-time does not require arguments, the cost/availability of annotated argument supervision is not discussed, nor are alternatives (weak supervision, distant supervision, or self-training).

3) Entity usage and evaluation setup need clearer fairness guarantees. The method exploits entity type embeddings and ACE entity annotations. It is not explicit whether gold entity mentions/types are used at test time (common but important to state for comparability). Also, Skip-CNN is cited with incomplete metrics (N/A), and it’s unclear whether all baselines use the same splits and resources (especially FrameNet augmentation).

4) Analysis depth is limited. There is little per-event-type breakdown, qualitative attention visualizations, or error analysis to understand where supervised attention helps most (e.g., ambiguous triggers, long-distance arguments). Sensitivity to key hyperparameters (context window, λ, Gaussian parameters) and robustness across random seeds are not reported.

5) Potential truncation issues and context window constraints. The fixed-length context may drop relevant arguments outside the window; the impact and mitigation strategies are not quantified.

Questions to Authors
- Do you use gold entity mentions/types at test time or predicted ones? If predicted, which system is used and how does ED performance change with realistic NER/EL errors?
- How often are annotated arguments outside the chosen context window, and how does this affect S1/S2 supervision quality and final performance?
- Did you try learning a gating or mixing coefficient between word- and entity-based attentions instead of summation? Any attempts with syntax-aware or dependency-based attention?
- How sensitive are the results to λ, window size, and Gaussian parameters (µ, σ, window)? Please report dev-set sweeps and variance across multiple random seeds.
- Can you provide per-type F1 or confusion analyses to show which event types benefit most from argument supervision?

Overall recommendation
Accept. The paper presents a clear and effective idea—explicitly supervising attention with argument annotations—to improve ED. Despite the modeling simplicity and reliance on gold arguments for training, the empirical gains, ablations, and analysis make a solid, practical contribution that should interest the ED/IE community.
