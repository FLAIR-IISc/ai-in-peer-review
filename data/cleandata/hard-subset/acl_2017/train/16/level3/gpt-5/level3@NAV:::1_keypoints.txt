- Summary: Proposes supervised-attention event detection that uses argument-derived attention targets, achieving SOTA on ACE 2005 with additional gains from FrameNet augmentation.

- Primary contribution: Demonstrates that explicit argument supervision substantially improves ED F1 over unsupervised attention and strong baselines.

- Secondary contribution: Introduces a simple, transparent, and reproducible architecture that fuses word and entity contexts via supervised attention.

- Tertiary contribution: Provides systematic comparison of two attention-supervision strategies and ablation studies isolating where gains come from.

- Strength: Strong empirical gains with statistical significance (ANN 64.6 → ANN-S2 71.7 F1), outperforming prior best systems.

- Strength: Conceptually compelling use of argument signals to resolve trigger ambiguity and exploit underused information.

- Strength: Simple, well-explained three-layer ANN using standard embeddings and minimal feature engineering.

- Strength: Careful ablations and FrameNet augmentation show arguments (not just entities) drive improvements and boost recall.

- Weakness: Attention fusion is simplistic (α = αw + αe); no learnable fusion (gating, MLP, multi-head, role-aware) explored.

- Weakness: Method depends on gold argument annotations during training; scalability and robustness to noisy/low-resource settings are unclear.

- Weakness: Limited exploration of supervision strategies and hyperparameters (role-agnostic Gaussian width; no role-specific analysis).

- Weakness: Evaluation limited to ACE 2005 with insufficient per-event-type/error analysis and no cross-domain tests.

- Question: Did you try learnable fusion between word- and entity-based attentions (gated sum, concatenation+MLP, etc.)?

- Question: How sensitive is performance to entity typing errors or removal of entity types?

- Question: Can role-specific attention targets (e.g., higher weight for discriminative roles) improve over S1/S2?

- Question: Any cross-domain evaluation beyond ACE 2005?

- Overall recommendation: Accept — addresses an important gap with a clean approach and strong, well-supported results.
