- Proposes supervised attention for event detection using annotated arguments (S1: argument-only; S2: Gaussian diffusion) combining word- and entity-based attentions.  
- Uses a simple ANN classifier on weighted context and trigger embeddings and evaluates on ACE 2005.  
- Shows significant F1 gains over unsupervised-attention baseline and several prior systems; FrameNet augmentation further improves results.  
- Contribution: Demonstrates explicit argument-supervised attention consistently improves ED and explains why joint ED/AE often fails to help ED.  
- Contribution: Provides a systematic empirical study and ablations of supervision strategies (S1 vs S2, ANN, ANN-ENT, Gold1/2) with statistical significance.  
- Contribution: Offers a simple, reproducible neural framework that integrates easily with standard ED pipelines and benefits from data augmentation.  
- Strength: Clear motivation and formulation pinpointing why prior joint models underutilized argument cues for ED.  
- Strength: Strong empirical evidence and careful ablations showing large, significant performance improvements (ANN: 64.6 → ANN-S2: 71.7 F1).  
- Strength: Method is simple, well-specified, and likely reproducible with minimal changes to existing models.  
- Strength: Insightful analysis of S1 vs S2 trade-offs (S1 higher precision, S2 higher recall) and support for data-sparsity argument via FrameNet experiments.  
- Weakness: Modeling novelty is limited; attention combination is simplistic and supervision strategies are heuristic rather than learned or structure-aware.  
- Weakness: Requires gold argument annotations at training time, limiting applicability where such labels are unavailable; alternatives not explored.  
- Weakness: Unclear fairness regarding entity usage and evaluation — whether gold entities/types are used at test time and whether baselines/resources are comparable.  
- Weakness: Analysis lacks per-event-type breakdowns, attention visualizations, detailed error analysis, and robustness checks (hyperparameter sensitivity, seed variance).  
- Weakness: Fixed context window may truncate relevant arguments; impact and mitigation are not quantified.  
- Questions: Are gold entity mentions/types used at test time, and how does ED perform with predicted entities?  
- Questions: How often are annotated arguments outside the context window and how does that affect supervision quality?  
- Questions: Were learnable gating or syntax-/dependency-aware attention variants tried instead of simple summation?  
- Questions: How sensitive are results to λ, window size, Gaussian parameters, and random seeds — please report sweeps and variance?  
- Questions: Can the authors provide per-type F1 or confusion analyses to show which event types benefit most?  
- Overall recommendation: Accept — clear, effective idea with solid empirical gains despite simplicity and reliance on gold training annotations.
