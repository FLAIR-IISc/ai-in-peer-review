Summary
This paper addresses event detection (ED) by explicitly leveraging argument information via supervised attention. The key idea is to supervise an attention mechanism to focus on annotated arguments (and optionally their neighborhoods) when classifying each token as a trigger type. The model builds word-level and entity-type attentions conditioned on the trigger candidate, combines them, and feeds the resulting contextual representation into a simple ANN classifier. Two supervision strategies are explored: S1 (attention mass only on arguments) and S2 (a Gaussian-smoothed distribution over arguments and nearby words). Experiments on ACE 2005 show consistent gains over an unsupervised-attention baseline and small but significant improvements over prior state-of-the-art systems; additional gains are reported when augmenting training with FrameNet-derived events.

Main contributions (as I see them)
1) Demonstration that supervising attention with argument annotations meaningfully improves ED over both unsupervised attention and strong baselines, with clear precision/recall trade-offs between two supervision strategies.
2) A simple, effective framework for injecting argument information into ED without full joint inference, including a pragmatic construction of “gold” attention targets and a joint loss that regularizes attention.
3) Empirical evidence on ACE 2005 (and with FrameNet augmentation) that the approach matches or exceeds prior state of the art while maintaining high precision.

Strengths
- Clear problem motivation and gap: The paper convincingly argues that existing joint models benefit AE much more than ED and that argument information is underused for ED. The analysis of data imbalance and pre-prediction stages in joint pipelines is insightful and motivates a targeted ED solution.

- Simple, well-scoped method with clear supervision signal: Supervising attention directly on annotated arguments is intuitive and easy to reproduce. The two strategies (S1 vs. S2) make the role of argument focus vs. contextual smoothing explicit, and the results align with the hypotheses (S1 higher precision, S2 higher recall).

- Solid empirical validation and ablations: The paper includes (i) a clean unsupervised-attention baseline (ANN), (ii) an entity-only attention variant (ANN-ENT), and (iii) “oracle” gold-attention conditions (ANN-Gold1/2) to validate the utility of argument information. These help isolate the contribution of argument supervision and show that entities alone are insufficient.

- Competitive to state of the art with statistical significance: ANN-S2 achieves the best reported F1 on ACE 2005 (71.7 vs. prior best 71.3), and the paper reports significance (p ≤ 0.05). The approach also yields notably higher precision, which is valuable in downstream EE pipelines.

- Practical augmentation with FrameNet: Incorporating FN-derived events further improves recall and F1, and the paper details the additional processing needed (mapping frame elements to arguments and automatic entity labeling). This increases the method’s practical appeal under data sparsity.

Weaknesses
- Limited methodological novelty in attention: The attention computation and supervision are fairly straightforward (single-layer projections; dot-product attention; simple sum of word- and entity-type attentions; squared-error supervision). This is a pragmatic design, but it leaves performance potentially on the table compared to more expressive mechanisms (learned gating between channels, multi-head or structured attention, syntax-aware attention, margin-based or entropy-regularized objectives).

- Modest absolute gains over the best prior systems: The improvement over the strongest published baseline (Skip-CNN 71.3 F1) is small (0.4–0.6 F1, depending on setup), and while statistically significant, practical significance could be discussed more thoroughly. Additionally, the benefit from FN augmentation is also incremental (+0.2 F1 for ANN-S2).

- Comparability and use of gold entities: The model uses entity-type embeddings and, as common in ACE evaluations, likely relies on gold entity annotations at test time. This may inflate results relative to real-world settings or systems using predicted entities. Clarifying whether test-time uses gold or predicted entities and aligning with the evaluation setup of compared baselines are important for fairness.

- Limited analysis of when/why attention helps: The paper would benefit from deeper error analysis (e.g., per-event-type improvements, ambiguous triggers, long-distance arguments, cross-sentence effects) and qualitative attention visualizations. This would substantiate the claim that arguments are especially helpful for disambiguating polysemous triggers.

- Missing details/ablations: The choice of λ per strategy is noted but not fully justified; no ablation on attention-channel combination (sum vs. learned weights), context window size, or sensitivity to Gaussian parameters in S2 is provided. Runtime/complexity and robustness to noisy argument labels (outside ACE) are not discussed.

Questions to Authors
- Test-time entity information: Do you use gold entity mentions/types at test time on ACE 2005? If yes, can you report results with predicted entities to gauge robustness and comparability?

- Attention combination: Why choose a simple sum of word- and entity-type attentions? Did you try learned scalar or vector gating, concatenation followed by a projection, or normalization schemes that preserve probability mass?

- Sensitivity analyses: How sensitive are results to λ, the context window size n, and the Gaussian parameters (σ, window) in S2? Please provide a brief sensitivity plot/table.

- Per-type breakdown: Which event subtypes benefit most/least from supervised attention? Are gains concentrated on ambiguous trigger lemmas (e.g., “fire,” “charge”)?

- Noise tolerance: On FrameNet augmentation, what is the estimated noise rate in the automatically mapped triggers/arguments, and how does the model’s performance vary if you inject noise into ACE argument labels (e.g., drop or corrupt a fraction)?

Additional comments
- The paper is clearly written and easy to follow. Some references and formatting need cleanup (e.g., missing years, inconsistent citation formatting).
- Consider adding qualitative attention heatmaps to illustrate S1 vs. S2 behavior.
- Future work: structured or syntax-aware supervised attention; multi-task training with AE to co-regularize attention without overwhelming ED; learned gating between word/entity contexts.

Overall recommendation
Accept. The paper addresses an important and underexplored angle—explicitly leveraging argument supervision for ED—via a simple, effective approach with careful ablations and competitive results. While methodological novelty is limited and gains over the strongest baselines are modest, the contribution is clear, well-supported, and useful to the ED community.
