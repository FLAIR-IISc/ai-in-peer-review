Summary
This paper targets event detection (ED) and argues that arguments provide strong signals that are often ignored or only indirectly exploited in joint EE systems. The authors propose a simple supervised-attention framework that explicitly guides the model to focus on argument tokens (and optionally their neighborhoods) when classifying trigger candidates. Concretely, they build attention over contextual words and entity types, supervise that attention using gold argument annotations via two strategies (S1: arguments-only; S2: arguments plus Gaussian neighborhood), and train the detector jointly with the attention mechanism. On ACE 2005, their best model (S2) achieves state-of-the-art F1 and significantly improves over an unsupervised-attention baseline. They also show complementary gains when augmenting training with events derived from FrameNet.

Main contributions (as I see them)
1) Demonstrating that explicit, supervised attention over annotated arguments substantially improves ED performance and precision, addressing a long-standing gap where arguments benefit AE more than ED.
2) A clear, simple ED architecture that fuses word and entity context with attention supervision and achieves strong results on ACE 2005, including with FrameNet augmentation.
3) An empirical study comparing two straightforward supervision strategies for attention (arguments-only vs. arguments-plus-neighborhood), with analyses of precision/recall trade-offs.

Strengths
- Effective use of argument information for ED (primary): The paper tackles an important and underused signal. Supervised attention guided by arguments leads to large gains over an unsupervised-attention baseline (64.6 F1 to 71.7 F1; Table 3), and the arguments-only/plus-neighborhood comparison is insightful (S1 higher precision; S2 higher recall). This directly addresses the cited phenomenon that joint models help AE more than ED.
- Simple, transparent framework that generalizes: The architecture is easy to understand and implement (MLP over context representations with attention over words and entity types). Despite its simplicity, it achieves SOTA F1 on ACE 2005 and strong precision, indicating that the idea is robust and not dependent on heavy engineering.
- Supervised attention is used effectively: The construction of gold attention targets from argument annotations is principled and yields consistent improvements. The joint loss with a tunable λ is standard yet effective; results show statistically significant gains vs. prior work.
- Solid empirical evaluation: The paper follows established ACE 2005 splits and compares against strong baselines (joint models, CNN/RNN, PSL). The augmentation experiment with FrameNet (following Liu et al., 2016a) is a useful external validation; gains persist with extra data, and the observed precision/recall trade-offs are plausible and well explained.
- Diagnostic comparisons support the thesis: ANN-ENT vs. ANN-Gold1/2 (Table 2) shows that mere entity information is insufficient; explicit argument supervision is key. This is an important empirical point.

Weaknesses
- Limited methodological novelty: Supervised attention is a known mechanism (e.g., in NMT), and leveraging argument cues for ED has been explored indirectly in joint models. The core novelty here is applying supervised attention to ED with argument-derived supervision, which is incremental conceptually.
- Naive attention fusion: The final attention vector is α = αw + αe without normalization or learned weighting. This fixed, additive fusion could be suboptimal. A learned, gated, or bilinear combination, or normalization, might further improve results and interpretability.
- Supervision strategies are simplistic: S1 and S2 are straightforward heuristics. More expressive strategies (e.g., weighting by argument role type, distance-aware or dependency-path-aware kernels, multi-head/self-attention with supervision, or supervision over spans rather than tokens) may yield stronger gains. The paper would benefit from exploring or at least ablating such choices.
- Potential evaluation ambiguity regarding entity information: The model uses entity type embeddings in context. It is unclear whether gold entity mentions/types are used at test time (common in ACE ED setups but not universal). If gold entities are used, fairness vs. some baselines may be affected; if predicted entities are used, the prediction quality should be reported.
- Baseline strength and ablations: The unsupervised-attention ANN baseline performs poorly, possibly underrepresenting what stronger encoders (CNN/RNN/transformer) with unsupervised or self-attention could achieve. Ablations for (i) αw-only vs. αe-only vs. αw+αe, (ii) renormalizing α, (iii) effect of λ and Gaussian window/σ, and (iv) removing entity inputs would clarify where gains come from.

Questions to authors (optional)
- What entity information is used at test time—gold ACE entity mentions/types or automatically predicted ones? If predicted, please report the NER/typing accuracy and its impact on ED. If gold, discuss comparability with baselines that do not assume gold entities.
- α = αw + αe doubles the mass of a probability vector; did you try renormalizing α or learning a scalar/binary gate to combine αw and αe? Any performance difference?
- Do you have ablations for αw-only vs. αe-only vs. their sum? This would quantify the contribution of entity attention relative to word attention.
- How sensitive is performance to λ, Gaussian window size, and σ? Any per-event-type analysis indicating which event types benefit most from argument supervision?
- During training, how do you construct attention supervision for negative (NA) candidates that occur in sentences with annotated arguments of other triggers? Do you risk supervising attention toward arguments of other events, and if so, how is that handled?

Overall recommendation
Accept. The paper delivers a clear, effective framework that explicitly leverages argument information for ED via supervised attention, yielding strong and statistically significant improvements on ACE 2005. While the method is conceptually simple and could be further refined (attention fusion and supervision strategies), the idea is important, underused for ED, and validated by solid experiments.
