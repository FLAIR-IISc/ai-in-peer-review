- Paper proposes supervising attention to focus on annotated arguments (S1: arguments only; S2: Gaussian-smoothed neighborhood) for token-level event trigger classification.
- Model builds word-level and entity-type attentions conditioned on trigger candidates, combines them, and uses an ANN classifier.
- Experiments on ACE 2005 show consistent gains over unsupervised-attention baselines and small but significant improvements over prior state of the art; FrameNet augmentation yields additional gains.

Main contributions:
- Shows that supervising attention with argument annotations improves ED and yields precision/recall trade-offs between S1 and S2.
- Provides a simple framework to inject argument information into ED without full joint inference, including construction of gold attention targets and joint loss regularization.
- Empirically matches or exceeds prior SOTA on ACE 2005 while maintaining high precision, with further gains from FrameNet augmentation.

Strengths:
- Clear motivation: argues argument information is underused for ED and analyzes limitations of joint AE–ED pipelines.
- Intuitive, reproducible supervision signal with two complementary strategies (S1 vs. S2) aligning with expected precision/recall behavior.
- Solid empirical validation and ablations, including unsupervised baseline, entity-only variant, and oracle attention conditions.
- Statistically significant, competitive results (ANN-S2 best reported F1 on ACE 2005) and notably higher precision.
- Practical FrameNet augmentation pipeline described, addressing data sparsity.

Weaknesses:
- Limited methodological novelty in attention design (simple projections, dot-product, sum of attentions, squared-error loss).
- Modest absolute gains over strongest baselines (≈0.4–0.6 F1) and small improvement from FN augmentation.
- Potential reliance on gold entity annotations at test time may inflate results and hinder comparability to systems using predicted entities.
- Limited analysis of where attention helps (per-type breakdown, long-distance arguments, ambiguous triggers) and few qualitative visualizations.
- Missing ablations/sensitivity analyses (λ choice, attention-channel fusion methods, S2 Gaussian parameters, runtime, robustness to noisy labels).

Questions to authors:
- Do you use gold entity mentions/types at test time on ACE 2005, and can you report results with predicted entities?
- Why sum word- and entity-type attentions rather than learned gating/concatenation/projection or alternative normalization?
- How sensitive are results to λ, context window size n, and Gaussian parameters in S2—can you provide sensitivity numbers?
- Which event subtypes benefit most/least from supervised attention—are gains concentrated on ambiguous triggers?
- What is the estimated noise rate in FrameNet mappings, and how does performance change when injecting noise into ACE argument labels?

Additional comments:
- Paper is clearly written but needs reference/citation formatting fixes.
- Add qualitative attention heatmaps to illustrate S1 vs. S2 behavior.
- Suggest future work on structured/syntax-aware supervised attention, multi-task AE–ED training, and learned gating between contexts.

Overall recommendation:
- Accept.
