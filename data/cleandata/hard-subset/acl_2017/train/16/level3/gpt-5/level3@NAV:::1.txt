Summary: The paper proposes a supervised-attention framework for event detection that explicitly leverages argument information during training. The model learns attention over contextual words and entity types, supervised by gold argument-derived attention targets, and achieves new state-of-the-art results on ACE 2005, with further gains from FrameNet augmentation.

Contributions (as I see them):
- Primary: A clear demonstration that explicit argument supervision materially improves event detection, with substantial F1 gains over both unsupervised attention and strong baselines.
- Secondary: A simple, transparent architecture that fuses word and entity contexts via supervised attention and is easy to reproduce.
- Tertiary: A systematic comparison of two attention-supervision strategies (argument-only vs. argument-with-neighborhood) and ablations (ANN, ANN-ENT, Gold1/2) that isolate where gains come from.

Strengths:
- Strong empirical gains with clear evidence. Supervised attention improves F1 from 64.6 (ANN) to 71.7 (ANN-S2), outperforming prior best systems (e.g., 70.7 with Liu’s FN-based). Significance is supported by a t-test (p ≤ 0.05).
- Conceptually compelling and underused signal. Focusing attention on arguments addresses trigger ambiguity (e.g., “fired”) and exploits information often ignored by pipeline ED, aligning well with the ED problem structure.
- Simple, well-explained framework. The three-layer ANN with supervised attention is easy to implement, requires minimal feature engineering, and uses standard embeddings and entity types.
- Careful ablation and augmentation. Comparisons with ANN-ENT and gold-attention variants convincingly show that arguments, not just entities, drive the gains; FrameNet augmentation further boosts recall (to 71.9 F1).

Weaknesses:
- Attention fusion is simplistic. The final attention α = αw + αe is a naive sum; no learnable fusion (e.g., gating, bilinear pooling, multi-head, or role-aware attention) is explored, possibly leaving performance on the table.
- Supervision depends on gold arguments. The method requires argument annotations during training; the scalability to low-resource or cross-domain settings is unclear, and the impact of noisy argument labels is not quantified.
- Limited exploration of supervision strategies. S1/S2 are straightforward; hyperparameters (e.g., Gaussian width) are fixed and role-agnostic, with no analysis of role-specific or type-aware supervision.
- Generalization and error analysis. Results are confined to ACE 2005; more granular analysis (per-event-type, ambiguity categories) and domain transfer would strengthen claims.

Questions to authors:
- Did you try learnable fusion between word- and entity-based attentions (e.g., a gated sum or concatenation + MLP)? Any results?
- How sensitive is performance to entity typing errors? What happens if entity types are noisy or removed?
- Can role-specific attention targets (e.g., higher weight for discriminative roles) improve over S1/S2?
- Any cross-domain evaluation beyond ACE 2005?

Overall recommendation: Accept. The paper addresses an important gap with a clean approach and strong, well-supported results.
