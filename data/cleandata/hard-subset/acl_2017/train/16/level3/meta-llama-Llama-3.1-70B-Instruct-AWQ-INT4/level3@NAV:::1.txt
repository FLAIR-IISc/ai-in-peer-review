**Summary of the Paper**

This paper proposes a novel approach to event detection (ED) by leveraging argument information, which is often ignored in existing methods. The authors argue that arguments provide significant clues for identifying and categorizing events, especially for ambiguous trigger words. They introduce a supervised attention mechanism to exploit argument information explicitly for ED. The proposed approach is evaluated on the ACE 2005 dataset and achieves state-of-the-art performance.

**Main Contributions**

1. **Exploiting argument information**: The authors propose to use argument information explicitly for ED, which is a novel approach in the field.
2. **Supervised attention mechanism**: The authors introduce a supervised attention mechanism to learn attention weights that focus on argument words, which is effective in improving ED performance.
3. **Systematic evaluation**: The authors conduct a thorough evaluation of their approach on the ACE 2005 dataset, demonstrating its effectiveness and superiority over state-of-the-art methods.

**Strengths**

1. **Novel approach**: The paper proposes a novel approach to ED by leveraging argument information, which is a significant contribution to the field.
2. **Effective supervised attention mechanism**: The proposed supervised attention mechanism is effective in improving ED performance, demonstrating the importance of argument information in ED.
3. **State-of-the-art performance**: The paper achieves state-of-the-art performance on the ACE 2005 dataset, demonstrating the effectiveness of the proposed approach.

**Weaknesses**

1. **Limited attention strategies**: The paper only explores two simple attention strategies, which may not be sufficient to fully capture the complexity of argument information.
2. **Naive computation of attention vector**: The attention vector is computed as the sum of two part-wise attention vectors, which may not be optimal.
3. **Limited evaluation**: The paper only evaluates the proposed approach on a single dataset (ACE 2005), which may not be sufficient to demonstrate its generalizability.

**Questions to Authors**

1. Can you explore more complex attention strategies to further improve ED performance?
2. How can you optimize the computation of the attention vector to better capture the complexity of argument information?
3. Can you evaluate the proposed approach on more datasets to demonstrate its generalizability?
