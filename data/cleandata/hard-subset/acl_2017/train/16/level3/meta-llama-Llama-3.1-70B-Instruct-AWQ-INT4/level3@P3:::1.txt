This paper proposes a novel approach to leverage argument information to improve event detection via supervised attention mechanisms. The authors argue that existing joint methods that tackle event detection and argument extraction simultaneously often fail to utilize argument information effectively, leading to insignificant improvements in event detection. In contrast, the proposed approach explicitly exploits argument information through supervised attention mechanisms, resulting in significant performance gains.

The main contributions of this paper are threefold. Firstly, the authors analyze the limitations of existing joint methods and propose to use annotated argument information explicitly for event detection. Secondly, they introduce a supervised attention-based event detection model that effectively utilizes argument information. Finally, they investigate two strategies to construct gold attentions using annotated arguments, demonstrating the effectiveness of the proposed approach.

The proposed approach is based on a neural network architecture that consists of two components: Context Representation Learning (CRL) and Event Detector (ED). The CRL component learns representations of contextual words and entities through attention mechanisms, while the ED component assigns an event type to each candidate trigger based on the learned contextual representations. The authors employ a supervised attention mechanism to encourage argument words to acquire more attention than other words.

The experimental results demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed approach. The authors conduct experiments on the ACE 2005 dataset and show that their approach outperforms state-of-the-art methods with significant gains. They also investigate the effectiveness of two strategies to construct gold attentions using annotated arguments and demonstrate that both strategies lead to improved performance.

One of the strengths of this paper is its clear and simple framework that effectively leverages argument information to improve event detection. The authors provide a thorough analysis of the limitations of existing joint methods and propose a novel approach that addresses these limitations. The experimental results are also well-presented and demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed approach.

However, there are some weaknesses in this paper. One of the limitations is that the attention vector is naively computed as the sum of two part-wise attention vectors, which may not be optimal. Additionally, the two proposed supervised-attention strategies are straightforward, and more complex strategies might yield better results.

Overall, this paper presents a solid contribution to the field of event detection. The proposed approach is effective, and the experimental results demonstrate its superiority over state-of-the-art methods. The authors provide a clear and thorough analysis of the limitations of existing joint methods and propose a novel approach that addresses these limitations.

Questions to Authors:

1. How do the authors plan to extend their approach to more complex event detection tasks, such as event extraction with multiple event triggers and arguments?
2. Can the authors provide more details on the hyper-parameter tuning process, especially for the λ parameter in the joint loss function?
3. How do the authors plan to address the issue of data sparsity in event detection, especially when the training data is limited?
