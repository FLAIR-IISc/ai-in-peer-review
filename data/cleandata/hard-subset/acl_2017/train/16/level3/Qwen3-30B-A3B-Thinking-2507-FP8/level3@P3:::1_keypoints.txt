- Paper identifies underutilization of argument information in event detection and shows arguments provide strong cues for ambiguous triggers.  
- Proposes a simple neural framework that explicitly leverages argument information via supervised attention.  
- Systematically investigates different supervised attention strategies to demonstrate effectiveness.

- Novel insight: argument information is crucial for ED and has been largely overlooked, supported by examples and experiments.  
- Simple but effective: the proposed supervised-attention framework is straightforward and yields state-of-the-art results; ablations show arguments drive the gains.  
- Strong experimental validation on ACE 2005 with comparisons to SOTA and FrameNet augmentation demonstrating robustness.  
- Clear explanation why joint learning methods often fail to improve ED (they use arguments for AE but not ED).

- Attention combination is simplistic (α = αw + αe); more sophisticated combination methods might improve performance.  
- Limited exploration of attention strategies (only S1 and S2); more complex strategies were not investigated.  
- No detailed error analysis to show where the method succeeds or fails relative to baselines.  
- Limited discussion of how noisy or incomplete argument annotations affect performance in practical settings.

- Why sum word and entity attention—what ablations justify this over concatenation or learned weighting, and how sensitive is the model to this choice?  
- What more sophisticated attention strategies were considered or planned for future work?  
- How does the method perform when events have no annotated arguments or when argument annotations are missing/incorrect?

- Overall recommendation: strong paper, well-motivated and well-executed with substantial gains; recommend acceptance despite minor concerns.
