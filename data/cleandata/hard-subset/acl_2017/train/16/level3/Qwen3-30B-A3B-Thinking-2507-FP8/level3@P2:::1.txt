

# Review for "Exploiting Argument Information to Improve Event Detection via Supervised Attention Mechanisms"

## Summary and Main Contributions
The paper addresses event detection (ED) by proposing to explicitly leverage argument information, which has been largely ignored in prior work. The authors demonstrate that argument information provides crucial clues for identifying and categorizing events, especially for ambiguous trigger words. They introduce a supervised attention mechanism that uses argument information to guide the attention process during ED. The main contributions are: (1) identifying the underutilization of argument information in ED and demonstrating its importance; (2) proposing a supervised attention mechanism that explicitly incorporates argument information; (3) systematically investigating two attention strategies (S1 and S2) for the proposed model, which achieve state-of-the-art results on the ACE 2005 benchmark.

## Strengths
1. **Significant Gap Addressed**: The paper identifies a crucial gap in the literature - that argument information, which is highly relevant to ED, has been largely ignored in prior work. The authors convincingly demonstrate through examples that arguments provide essential clues for ambiguous triggers (e.g., "fired" as End-Position vs. Attack), which is a compelling motivation that has been overlooked in the field. This is a substantial contribution to the understanding of event detection.

2. **Strong Empirical Validation**: The experimental results are thorough and convincing. The authors systematically compare their approach against both pipeline and joint methods, demonstrating significant improvements (71.7 F1 vs. 69.4 for the best prior work). The t-test showing statistical significance (p < 0.05) strengthens their claims. The ablation studies (Table 2) clearly show that argument information is necessary for the performance gains, not just entity information.

3. **Clear and Effective Methodology**: The proposed supervised attention mechanism is conceptually simple yet effective. The approach of using gold attention vectors constructed from annotated arguments as supervision to train the attention mechanism is elegant and well-motivated. The two attention strategies (S1 and S2) are clearly defined and their trade-offs (precision vs. recall) are well-explained in the results.

## Weaknesses
1. **Simplistic Attention Mechanism**: The attention mechanism computes the final attention vector as the simple sum of two separate attention vectors (α = αw + αe). While the paper justifies this approach, it's relatively naive and doesn't explore more sophisticated combinations of the two attention components. A more nuanced approach (e.g., learning weights for combining these attention vectors) could potentially yield better results.

2. **Limited Exploration of Attention Strategies**: The paper proposes only two attention strategies (S1 and S2), which are straightforward and lack complexity. The authors acknowledge this limitation but don't explore whether more sophisticated attention strategies (e.g., incorporating argument roles or using more complex attention functions) might yield even better results. This represents an opportunity for future work that the current paper doesn't fully exploit.

3. **Insufficient Explanation of Joint Method Limitations**: While the paper identifies that joint methods don't significantly improve ED performance, the explanation (unbalanced data and indirect use of arguments) is somewhat superficial. A deeper analysis of why argument information doesn't translate to better ED in joint approaches would strengthen the paper's contribution and provide more insight for the community.

## Questions to Authors
1. The attention mechanism combines two attention vectors (αw and αe) by simple summation. Have you considered learning a weighted combination of these vectors (e.g., through a small neural network) to potentially achieve better performance?

2. The paper demonstrates that using argument information via supervised attention significantly improves ED performance. How would your approach perform if the argument annotations were incomplete or noisy (e.g., missing arguments or incorrect argument roles)?

3. The authors note that their approach achieves higher precision than state-of-the-art methods. Can you elaborate on why this might be the case, particularly given that S1 (which focuses only on argument words) achieves higher precision than S2?

## Overall Recommendation
This is a strong paper that makes a meaningful contribution to the event detection literature by demonstrating the importance of argument information and proposing a simple yet effective method to leverage it. The empirical results are convincing, and the approach is well-motivated and clearly presented. While there are opportunities for improvement in the attention mechanism and exploration of attention strategies, these do not detract from the paper's overall contribution. The work is well-suited for ACL 2017 and should be accepted.
