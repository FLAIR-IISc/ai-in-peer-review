- Paper identifies underutilization of argument information in event detection and shows arguments provide crucial disambiguating clues for ambiguous triggers.  
- Proposes a supervised attention mechanism that uses annotated argument information to guide attention during event detection.  
- Systematically investigates two attention strategies (S1 and S2) and reports state-of-the-art results on ACE 2005.

- Strength: Addresses a significant gap by demonstrating the importance of arguments for ED with convincing examples and motivation.  
- Strength: Strong empirical validation with comparisons to pipeline and joint methods, significant F1 improvement (71.7 vs. 69.4) and statistical significance.  
- Strength: Methodology is clear and effective; using gold attention vectors from annotated arguments is elegant and ablation studies confirm argument information is key.

- Weakness: Attention combination is simplistic (α = αw + αe) and more sophisticated learned combinations were not explored.  
- Weakness: Only two straightforward attention strategies (S1, S2) are explored; richer strategies (e.g., role-aware or complex attention functions) are not investigated.  
- Weakness: Explanation for why joint methods fail to improve ED is superficial and lacks deeper analysis.

- Question: Have you considered learning a weighted or neural combination of αw and αe instead of simple summation?  
- Question: How does the approach perform with incomplete or noisy argument annotations (missing arguments or incorrect roles)?  
- Question: Why does the approach yield higher precision (particularly S1 focusing on argument words) compared to prior work?

- Overall recommendation: Strong, well-motivated paper with convincing results; recommended for acceptance (ACL 2017).
