

This paper proposes an innovative approach to event detection (ED) by explicitly leveraging argument information, which has been largely ignored or indirectly used in previous work. The authors make a compelling case that arguments provide crucial clues for identifying event triggers, especially for ambiguous words, and that existing joint methods for event detection and argument extraction fail to effectively utilize this information for ED. The main contributions are: (1) identifying and analyzing the fundamental reason why joint methods underperform on ED (unbalanced data and indirect use of arguments), (2) proposing a supervised attention mechanism to explicitly exploit argument information for ED, and (3) systematically investigating different attention strategies to optimize this mechanism.

Strengths: The paper's most significant contribution is its insightful analysis of why joint methods fail to improve ED performance. The authors demonstrate through empirical evidence (Table 1) that while joint methods achieve substantial improvements on argument extraction (AE), they barely improve ED (less than 0.2 points), contrary to theoretical expectations. This analysis identifies a critical gap in the field that the paper successfully addresses. The proposed supervised attention mechanism is elegantly simple yet highly effective, achieving state-of-the-art performance on ACE 2005 with an F1 score of 71.7, outperforming previous best methods by 2.3 points. The ablation study (Table 2) provides strong evidence for the value of argument information, showing that systems using gold attention based on arguments significantly outperform those using only entity information. The systematic exploration of two attention strategies (S1 and S2) demonstrates how different ways of incorporating argument information affect precision and recall, offering valuable insights for future work.

The experimental design is thorough and well-justified. The authors properly handle the data sparseness problem by incorporating FrameNet data (Table 4), showing that their approach benefits from additional training data. The implementation is clear, with detailed descriptions of the attention mechanisms and model architecture. The results are consistently strong across different evaluation metrics, and the t-test confirms statistical significance.

Weaknesses: The supervised attention mechanism, while effective, is somewhat simplistic. The attention vector is computed as the sum of two part-wise attention vectors (word and entity attention), without exploring more sophisticated interactions between these components. This naive combination might limit the potential of the approach, as more complex attention mechanisms could better model the relationship between arguments and triggers. The two proposed attention strategies (S1 and S2) are straightforward and lack innovation; more sophisticated strategies (e.g., using attention to model dependencies between arguments and triggers) might yield further improvements. The paper does not sufficiently explain why their approach works better than previous methods, beyond stating that argument information is useful. A deeper analysis of which specific aspects of argument information contribute most to performance would strengthen the contribution.

The evaluation is limited to a single dataset (ACE 2005), and the paper does not discuss how the approach might generalize to other datasets or event types with different argument structures. While the FrameNet augmentation is a good addition, it doesn't address potential limitations of the approach on different domains.

Questions to Authors: How would your approach perform on datasets with different event type distributions or more diverse argument structures? Could you provide a more detailed analysis of which specific event types benefit most from argument information? Have you considered more sophisticated attention mechanisms that might better model the relationship between arguments and triggers, such as hierarchical attention or attention over argument roles?

Overall, this paper makes a significant contribution to the event detection field by addressing a critical gap in the literature and demonstrating that argument information can substantially improve ED performance. The proposed approach is novel, well-motivated, and empirically validated with strong results. While the attention mechanism could be more sophisticated, the simplicity of the approach is also a strength, making it easy to implement and understand. The paper is clearly written, well-structured, and makes a compelling case for the importance of argument information in event detection. The experimental results are convincing, and the work represents a meaningful step forward in the field. I recommend acceptance.
