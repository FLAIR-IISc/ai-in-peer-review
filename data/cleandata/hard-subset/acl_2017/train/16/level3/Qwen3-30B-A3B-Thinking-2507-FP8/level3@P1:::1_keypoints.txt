- Proposes leveraging argument information explicitly for event detection (ED), arguing prior work ignored or indirectly used arguments.  
- Identifies why joint ED and argument extraction (AE) methods underperform: unbalanced data and indirect use of argument information.  
- Introduces a supervised attention mechanism to explicitly exploit argument information for ED.  
- Systematically investigates two attention strategies (S1, S2) to optimize argument-based attention.  
- Empirical analysis shows joint methods improve AE substantially but barely improve ED (<0.2 points), highlighting a key gap.  
- Achieves state-of-the-art on ACE 2005 (F1 71.7), outperforming prior best by 2.3 points.  
- Ablation study demonstrates gold argument-based attention yields significant gains over using only entity information.  
- Incorporates FrameNet data to mitigate data sparsity and shows additional training data benefits the approach.  
- Experimental design and implementation are clear, results consistent, and improvements are statistically significant.  
- Attention mechanism is somewhat simplistic (sum of word and entity attentions) and may miss richer interactions.  
- Attention strategies S1 and S2 are straightforward and lack exploration of more sophisticated mechanisms modeling argument-trigger dependencies.  
- Lacks deeper analysis on which specific argument aspects or event types drive the gains.  
- Evaluation limited to ACE 2005; generalization to other datasets/domains remains untested.  
- Questions: How does the approach perform on datasets with different event distributions, which event types benefit most, and have more sophisticated attention variants been considered?  
- Overall recommendation: significant, novel contribution with strong empirical support; recommends acceptance.
