

**Summary and Contributions**  
This paper addresses event detection (ED), a key component of event extraction, by leveraging argument information typically ignored in existing approaches. The authors identify that while arguments provide crucial clues for ambiguous triggers (e.g., "fired" as End-Position vs. Attack), prior work either neglects arguments or uses them indirectly in joint models. Their main contributions are: (1) a systematic analysis of why joint ED-argument extraction models fail to improve ED (due to data imbalance and implementation choices), (2) a supervised attention mechanism that explicitly integrates argument information into ED, and (3) empirical validation of this approach on ACE 2005, achieving state-of-the-art results.  

**Strengths**  
1. **Novel insight into argument utilization**: The paper compellingly demonstrates that arguments are underutilized in ED, not because they lack value, but because existing joint models inadvertently prioritize argument extraction over ED. The authors' analysis of data imbalance (9800 arguments vs. 5300 triggers in ACE 2005) and implementation flaws (pre-predicting triggers without argument context) is insightful and well-supported by Table 1. This addresses a critical gap in the literature.  
2. **Strong empirical results**: The proposed model (ANN-S2) achieves 71.7 F1 on ACE 2005, outperforming prior state-of-the-art by 1.0 F1 points. Crucially, the gains are statistically significant (p < 0.05), and the approach improves precision over competitors (76.8% vs. 66.0% for Nguyen's joint model), addressing a key weakness in prior work. The ablation study (Table 2) conclusively proves argument information is necessary for ED, not merely entity information.  
3. **Rigorous experimental design**: The authors systematically test two attention strategies (S1: focus only on arguments; S2: extend attention to nearby words) and validate their approach with FrameNet augmentation (Table 4). The experiments are well-motivated, with careful hyperparameter tuning and comparisons against both pipeline and joint baselines. The use of gold attention vectors for supervision (a novel application in ED) is particularly effective.  

**Weaknesses**  
1. **Simplistic attention mechanism**: The attention vector combines word and entity attention via simple summation (α = αw + αe), which lacks theoretical justification. More sophisticated approaches (e.g., learnable gating, dynamic weighting) could better capture nuanced context-argument relationships. The paper does not explore why this design works or how it compares to alternatives, weakening the methodological contribution.  
2. **Superficial exploration of attention strategies**: The two proposed strategies (S1/S2) are straightforward extensions of prior work (e.g., Gaussian smoothing from Mi et al. 2016). The authors do not test more complex variants (e.g., event-type-specific attention, hierarchical attention), which might yield further gains. This limits the paper's novelty, as the core innovation lies in *using* arguments, not *how* to use them.  
3. **Limited analysis of argument utility**: While the paper proves arguments improve ED, it does not investigate *which* arguments or roles are most informative (e.g., Role = Position vs. Person). Understanding this could guide future work and explain why S2 (broader attention) trades precision for recall. The lack of qualitative analysis (e.g., error cases where arguments resolved ambiguity) leaves room for deeper insight.  

**Questions to Authors**  
1. The paper states only 10% of triggers are entities. How does this impact the entity attention mechanism (αe)? Could the model leverage non-entity arguments more effectively?  
2. For strategy S2, why was a Gaussian window size of 3 chosen? Did varying this hyperparameter (e.g., 2 or 4) significantly affect performance?  
3. In FrameNet augmentation (Section 4.4), how did you align FrameNet frame elements with ACE 2005 argument roles? Did core elements (e.g., "Person") consistently map to ACE roles?  

**Overall Recommendation**  
This paper makes a valuable contribution by identifying a fundamental flaw in joint event extraction approaches and proposing a simple yet effective solution. The core insight—arguments are crucial for ED but underutilized—deserves attention, and the results robustly validate the approach. While the attention mechanism could be refined, the empirical impact is substantial. The paper is well-written, experiments are thorough, and the limitations are reasonable for a strong submission. I recommend acceptance.
