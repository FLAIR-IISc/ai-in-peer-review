

Strengths:  
This paper addresses a significant gap by leveraging argumentative information—often overlooked in event detection—to enhance model performance. The proposed framework is novel, well-structured, and elegantly simple. Crucially, the integration of supervised attention mechanisms (a technique widely validated in tasks like machine translation) yields substantial improvements over strong baselines. The experimental design is rigorous, with comprehensive results demonstrating the approach’s efficacy across multiple benchmarks. The clarity of the methodology and the robustness of the evaluation make the contribution highly compelling.

Weaknesses:  
The primary limitation lies in the attention mechanism’s implementation. Specifically, the attention vector is computed as a simple summation of two component vectors (one per argument part), which lacks theoretical grounding and may not optimally capture nuanced argument interactions. A more sophisticated derivation—such as concatenation, gating, or multiplicative fusion—could better model dependencies between argument components and potentially yield further gains. Additionally, while the two proposed strategies for supervised attention are logically sound, they are relatively basic. The authors could explore more advanced variants (e.g., context-aware attention or hierarchical mechanisms) to strengthen the methodological contribution. The paper would benefit from a discussion of why simpler approaches were prioritized over more complex alternatives, including a brief analysis of computational trade-offs.

General Discussion:  
Despite these minor limitations, the paper presents a highly effective and well-executed framework. The core idea of utilizing argumentative features for event detection is both timely and impactful, directly addressing a meaningful gap in the literature. The experimental results are thorough and convincingly support the proposed approach’s superiority over existing methods. The clarity of the writing and the soundness of the evaluation mitigate the weaknesses noted. Given the novelty of the approach, the strong empirical results, and the paper’s overall contribution to the field, I recommend acceptance. To further enhance the work, the authors should address the attention mechanism’s design in the revision (e.g., by experimenting with alternative fusion strategies and discussing their rationale), but this is not essential for acceptance. The paper is a valuable addition to ACL 2017.
