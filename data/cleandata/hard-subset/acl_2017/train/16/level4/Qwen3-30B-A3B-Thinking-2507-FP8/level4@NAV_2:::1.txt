

Summary: This paper addresses event detection (ED) by explicitly leveraging argument information, which is typically ignored in existing approaches despite providing crucial clues for ambiguous triggers. The main contributions are: (1) a clear analysis of why joint models fail to benefit ED (due to data imbalance and indirect argument usage), (2) a supervised attention mechanism that explicitly integrates argument information into ED, and (3) state-of-the-art performance on ACE 2005 with significant gains over prior methods.

Strengths:
1. The paper identifies a critical oversight in ED literature—ignoring argument information that directly aids trigger disambiguation—and provides a theoretically sound solution via supervised attention. The analysis of joint model limitations (data imbalance and indirect argument usage) is compelling and well-supported by Table 1.
2. Experimental results are robust and comprehensive: the proposed approach (ANN-S2) achieves the highest F1 score (71.7%) on ACE 2005, significantly outperforming all baselines (p < 0.05) and demonstrating clear gains over unsupervised attention (ANN: 64.6% vs. ANN-S2: 71.7%).
3. The framework is both simple and effective, leveraging established techniques (supervised attention from machine translation) in a novel context. The ablation studies (Table 2) conclusively prove that argument information—not just entity information—drives the performance improvement.

Weaknesses:
1. The attention mechanism combines word and entity attention vectors via simple summation (α = αw + αe), which may not optimally balance their contributions. A more nuanced approach (e.g., learnable fusion weights) could better capture context-specific relevance.
2. The proposed attention strategies (S1/S2) are relatively straightforward; more sophisticated methods (e.g., adaptive attention based on argument roles or hierarchical context modeling) were not explored, potentially limiting performance gains.
3. The paper attributes the success of supervised attention to data sparsity but does not provide direct evidence (e.g., ablation on attention training data size). The improvement from FrameNet augmentation (Table 4) partially supports this, but the core mechanism remains under-analyzed.
