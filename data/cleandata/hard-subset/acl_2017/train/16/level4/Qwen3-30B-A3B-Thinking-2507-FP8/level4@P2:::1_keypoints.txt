- Proposes leveraging argument information explicitly for event detection, with contributions: analysis of joint ED-argument failures, a supervised attention mechanism, and SOTA results on ACE 2005.  
- Shows arguments are underutilized in prior work; joint models favor argument extraction due to data imbalance and implementation choices.  
- Presents strong empirical results: ANN-S2 achieves 71.7 F1 on ACE 2005, +1.0 over prior SOTA, with significant precision gains and statistical significance.  
- Ablation study demonstrates argument information is necessary for ED improvements, not just entity information.  
- Rigorous experiments: compares two attention strategies (S1/S2), uses FrameNet augmentation, careful hyperparameter tuning, and gold attention supervision.  
- Attention mechanism is simplistic (α = αw + αe) with no theoretical justification; more sophisticated weighting could help.  
- Exploration of attention strategies is limited and omits variants like event-type-specific or hierarchical attention.  
- Limited analysis of which argument roles drive improvements and lacks qualitative/error-case analysis explaining S2’s precision–recall tradeoff.  
- Question: Only 10% of triggers are entities—how does this affect the entity attention αe and can non-entity arguments be better leveraged?  
- Question: Why choose a Gaussian window size of 3 for S2, and how sensitive are results to this hyperparameter?  
- Question: How were FrameNet frame elements aligned to ACE 2005 argument roles, and did core elements map consistently?  
- Overall recommendation: Valuable and well-executed contribution with clear insight and empirical impact; recommend acceptance.
