- Summary: Proposes using argument information to improve event detection, identifying joint-model limitations, introducing supervised attention for arguments, and achieving SOTA on ACE 2005.  
- Contribution 1: Analyzes how existing joint AE/ED models underutilize arguments due to data imbalance and indirect training.  
- Contribution 2: Introduces a supervised attention mechanism (ANN-S1/S2) that explicitly incorporates gold argument annotations into ED.  
- Contribution 3: Systematically compares attention strategies and shows S2 plus FrameNet augmentation yields top performance.  
- Strength 1: Novel insight that argument information disambiguates ambiguous triggers and is theoretically and practically significant.  
- Strength 2: Effective, simple methodology with strong empirical gains (ANN-S2: 71.7 F1 vs. prior 69.4).  
- Strength 3: Rigorous evaluation including ablations, strategy comparison, FrameNet augmentation, and statistically significant results.  
- Weakness 1: Attention fusion is simplistic (α = αw + αe) and may miss interactions between word and entity contexts.  
- Weakness 2: Limited exploration of attention strategies; more sophisticated alternatives (role-aware, dependency-based) were not tested.  
- Weakness 3: Data sparsity hurts unsupervised attention; reliance on external FrameNet data gives only modest gains.  
- Question 1: Why does simple summation fusion outperform alternatives—are there comparison experiments (e.g., concatenation or gating)?  
- Question 2: How do the authors reconcile "entity information is not enough" with S1 using only argument words rather than entity types?  
- Question 3: Why does FrameNet augmentation yield only slight improvement—are noisy auto-extracted FrameNet events the cause?  
- Recommendation: Strong accept—well-motivated, clearly presented, empirically convincing work with minor limitations.
