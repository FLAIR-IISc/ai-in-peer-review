- Summary: Proposes supervised attention that uses annotated argument information to improve event detection, arguing existing joint models fail to benefit ED from arguments.

- Contribution 1: Identifies that current joint event extraction methods do not properly utilize argument information for ED, despite arguments disambiguating triggers.

- Contribution 2: Proposes a neural ED model with supervised attention trained on gold attention vectors derived from annotated arguments.

- Contribution 3: Systematically investigates two attention strategies (S1: argument words only; S2: arguments + surrounding words) and evaluates them on ACE 2005.

- Strength 1: Strong evidence that arguments are valuable for ED, with analysis showing joint models improve AE but yield minimal ED gains (<0.2 points).

- Strength 2: Supervised attention is simple and effective, producing large F1 gains (e.g., ANN 64.6 → ANN-S2 71.7) and outperforming prior SOTA with statistical significance.

- Strength 3: Clear comparison of S1 vs S2 demonstrates a precision/recall trade-off useful for practitioners (S1 higher precision, S2 higher recall).

- Weakness 1: Insufficient evidence that supervised attention outperforms joint models that explicitly incorporate argument information during trigger prediction.

- Weakness 2: Attention mechanism is simplistic (α = αw + αe) with no exploration or justification of more complex combination methods.

- Weakness 3: Experiments are limited to ACE 2005, so generalization to other datasets or domains is not demonstrated.

- Question 1: Why would supervised attention outperform joint models like Nguyen et al. (2016), and how does it overcome their limitations?

- Question 2: Have you tried alternative combinations (weighted sums, attention gates) for word/entity attention, and what were the results?

- Question 3: Can you discuss potential limitations and expected generalization of your approach to other datasets or domains?
