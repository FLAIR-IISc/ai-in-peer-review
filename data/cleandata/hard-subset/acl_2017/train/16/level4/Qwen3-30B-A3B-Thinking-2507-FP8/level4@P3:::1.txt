

**Summary**  
This paper addresses event detection (ED), a key component of event extraction, by proposing a novel approach to explicitly leverage argument information for improving ED performance. The authors observe that while existing joint models for event extraction (ED + argument extraction, AE) typically treat arguments as auxiliary to AE, arguments actually provide crucial clues for ambiguous event triggers. The authors argue that current joint models indirectly use arguments for AE but fail to benefit ED, and they propose a supervised attention mechanism to directly incorporate argument information into ED.

**Main Contributions**  
1. The paper identifies a significant limitation in existing joint event extraction methods: they do not properly utilize argument information for ED, which is the primary contribution. The authors demonstrate that arguments provide essential clues for ambiguous triggers (e.g., "fired" could be misclassified as Attack rather than End-Position without context from arguments like "former protege").

2. The authors propose a neural network-based ED model with a supervised attention mechanism that explicitly uses argument information. The model is trained with gold attention vectors constructed from annotated arguments, rather than learning attention unsupervised.

3. The authors systematically investigate two attention strategies (S1: focusing only on argument words; S2: focusing on arguments and surrounding words) and demonstrate their effectiveness through comprehensive experiments on ACE 2005.

**Strengths**  
1. The paper makes a compelling argument for why argument information is valuable for ED, supported by concrete examples and analysis of existing joint models' limitations. The observation that joint models achieve significant improvements on AE but minimal gains on ED (often <0.2 points) is well-documented with evidence from Table 1, revealing a fundamental flaw in current approaches that the authors correctly identify.

2. The proposed supervised attention mechanism is both simple and effective. The authors demonstrate a clear performance gain over unsupervised attention (ANN: 64.6 F1 → ANN-S2: 71.7 F1) and state-of-the-art methods (ANN-S2 achieves 71.7 F1, outperforming all baselines including Liu's FN-Based approach at 70.7 F1). The t-test confirmation of statistical significance adds weight to the claim.

3. The systematic evaluation of two attention strategies provides valuable insights into how argument information should be incorporated. The authors correctly observe that S1 (focusing only on arguments) yields higher precision while S2 (including surrounding words) achieves higher recall, offering a useful trade-off for practitioners.

**Weaknesses**  
1. The paper does not sufficiently address why supervised attention with argument information would outperform joint models that already incorporate arguments. The authors claim joint models indirectly use arguments for AE but not for ED, yet they don't provide evidence that their approach would outperform joint models that explicitly include argument information during ED prediction (e.g., models that pre-predict triggers using argument information).

2. The attention mechanism's design is relatively simple, using a straightforward summation of word and entity attention vectors (α = αw + αe). The authors acknowledge this but don't provide a thorough analysis of why more complex attention mechanisms (e.g., weighted combinations or attention gates) might not yield better results. This limits the generalizability of their approach.

3. The paper's claim that argument information is crucial for ED is partially supported by the ANN-Gold1/2 vs. ANN-ENT comparison, but the evidence is limited to a single dataset (ACE 2005). The authors don't demonstrate whether this effect generalizes to other datasets or domains, which would strengthen their argument.

**Questions to Authors**  
1. Could you clarify why your supervised attention approach would outperform joint models that have been designed to incorporate argument information during trigger prediction (e.g., Nguyen et al. 2016)? Specifically, how does your method overcome the limitations you identify in joint models?

2. The attention mechanism combines word and entity attention through simple summation. Have you experimented with alternative approaches (e.g., weighted sum or attention gates) that might better capture the relationship between arguments and triggers? If so, how did they perform?

3. The paper demonstrates improvements on ACE 2005, but the argument that argument information is universally crucial for ED would be stronger with evidence from additional datasets or domains. Could you discuss potential limitations of your approach in other contexts?
