

**Summary and Contributions**  
This paper addresses event detection (ED), a key component of event extraction, by proposing to explicitly leverage argument information—often ignored in prior work—to improve detection performance. The authors make three key contributions: (1) they identify and analyze the limitations of existing joint models for ED, demonstrating that arguments are underutilized for ED (not just AE); (2) they introduce a supervised attention mechanism to explicitly incorporate argument information into ED; and (3) they systematically evaluate different attention strategies, achieving state-of-the-art results on ACE 2005.

**Strengths**  
1. **Novel and impactful insight**: The authors persuasively argue that argument information provides critical clues for ambiguous triggers (e.g., "fired" in "Mohamad fired Anwar..."), which existing methods largely overlook. Their analysis of joint models reveals why arguments fail to benefit ED: data imbalance and the indirect use of arguments in training. This insight is both theoretically sound and practically significant.  

2. **Effective methodology**: The supervised attention mechanism elegantly integrates argument information into ED by using gold-standard argument annotations as supervision. The proposed approach (ANN-S1/S2) significantly outperforms baselines (e.g., ANN-S2 achieves 71.7 F1 vs. 69.4 for the best prior system), demonstrating the value of their core idea. The method is simple yet effective, avoiding unnecessary complexity.  

3. **Rigorous evaluation**: The experiments thoroughly validate the approach across multiple dimensions: (a) ablation studies confirm argument information's necessity (ANN-Gold1/2 outperform ANN-ENT by 1.5+ F1); (b) the two attention strategies (S1/S2) are systematically compared, with S2 achieving the best results; and (c) augmentation with FrameNet data further validates the method's robustness. The t-test confirms statistical significance (p < 0.05).  

**Weaknesses**  
1. **Attention mechanism design**: The attention vector is computed as a simple sum of word and entity attention vectors (α = αw + αe). While effective, this approach lacks nuance—it does not account for potential interactions between word and entity contexts. A more sophisticated fusion mechanism (e.g., gating or multiplicative interactions) might better capture how arguments and surrounding text jointly inform ED.  

2. **Limited exploration of attention strategies**: The two proposed strategies (S1/S2) are relatively straightforward. S1 focuses solely on argument words, while S2 extends attention to neighboring words using a Gaussian kernel. More complex strategies—such as learning attention weights from argument roles or incorporating dependency parsing—could yield further gains. The authors acknowledge this limitation but do not explore alternatives.  

3. **Data sparsity concerns**: The paper notes that unsupervised attention (ANN) performs poorly due to data scarcity (64.6 F1 vs. 71.7 for ANN-S2). While FrameNet augmentation helps (F1 rises to 71.9), the reliance on external data weakens the core contribution. A more principled approach to handling sparsity (e.g., data augmentation with argument-aware synthetic examples) would strengthen the work.  

**Questions to Authors**  
1. Why does the simple summation (α = αw + αe) work better than alternative fusion methods (e.g., concatenation or gating)? Could the authors share experiments comparing these?  

2. The paper states that "entity information is not enough" (Table 2), yet S1 uses only argument words (not entity types). How do the authors reconcile this with the fact that arguments are entities?  

3. The FrameNet augmentation (Table 4) shows modest gains (71.7 → 71.9 F1). Given the effort to process FrameNet data, could the authors elaborate on why improvements are limited? Is this due to noise in the automatically extracted FrameNet events?  

**Overall Recommendation**  
This paper makes a valuable contribution by demonstrating that argument information—often ignored in ED—can significantly boost performance when explicitly incorporated via supervised attention. The method is well-motivated, the experiments are thorough, and the results are compelling. While the attention mechanism design and strategy exploration have room for refinement, these are minor limitations given the strong empirical results. The work is clearly presented, and the analysis of joint models' shortcomings is insightful. I recommend acceptance.
