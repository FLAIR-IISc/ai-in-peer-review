- Summary: Tackles event detection by explicitly leveraging argument information; contributions are (1) analysis of joint model failures (data imbalance, indirect argument use), (2) supervised attention integrating arguments into ED, and (3) state-of-the-art results on ACE 2005.

- Strength: Identifies overlooked role of argument information and provides a theoretically sound supervised-attention solution, with joint-model limitations well-supported (Table 1).

- Strength: Strong experiments—ANN-S2 achieves 71.7% F1 on ACE 2005, significantly beating baselines (p<0.05) and improving over unsupervised attention (64.6%→71.7%).

- Strength: Simple, effective framework applying supervised attention from MT to ED; ablations (Table 2) show argument information, not just entity info, drives improvements.

- Weakness: Fusion of word and entity attention by simple summation (α = αw + αe) may not optimally balance contributions; learnable fusion weights could help.

- Weakness: Attention strategies (S1/S2) are relatively basic; more sophisticated approaches (role-adaptive or hierarchical attention) were not explored.

- Weakness: Claims about data sparsity driving supervised-attention gains lack direct evidence (no ablation on attention training data size); FrameNet augmentation offers partial but incomplete support.
