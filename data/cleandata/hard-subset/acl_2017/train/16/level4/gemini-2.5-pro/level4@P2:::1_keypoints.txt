- Proposes supervised attention that forces the ED model to focus on annotated event arguments by minimizing loss to gold attention vectors; reports SOTA on ACE 2005 with Gaussian-smoothed supervision.  
- Contribution: Introduces a novel, explicit way to integrate argument information into ED via supervised attention as an alternative to joint extraction models.  
- Contribution: Provides thorough empirical validation, comparing unsupervised, supervised, and oracle attention setups and showing robustness with FrameNet-augmented data.  
- Strength: Clear motivation and hypothesis explaining why argument information helps disambiguate triggers (e.g., “fired” example).  
- Strength: Simple, elegant model design that yields substantial F1 improvements over strong baselines.  
- Strength: Solid experimental setup including ablation studies that isolate the effect of argument information and comparisons to recent SOTA methods.  
- Weakness: Final attention is obtained by naively summing word and entity attentions, lacking a dynamic fusion mechanism that could adaptively weight sources.  
- Weakness: Gold-attention strategies (S1 binary, S2 fixed Gaussian) are heuristic and manually defined; more nuanced or data-driven schemes might improve results.  
- Weakness: Method depends on annotated arguments during training, limiting applicability to datasets without argument annotations; FrameNet auto-labeling impact not fully analyzed.  
- Question: Why choose summation to combine word and entity attentions, and were alternative fusion methods (e.g., learned gating) explored?  
- Question: Please provide error analysis showing which event types benefit most from supervised attention and an example where ANN-S2 succeeds but the baseline fails.
