- Proposes supervised attention using annotated arguments to guide event trigger detection, with two strategies for generating gold attention; achieves SOTA on ACE 2005.
- Contribution: introduces a simple secondary-loss supervised attention mechanism to explicitly incorporate argument information into ED.
- Contribution: provides clear empirical validation (ablation and oracle experiments) that direct argument supervision, not just attention, drives performance gains.
- Strength: well-motivated and elegant solution addressing shortcomings of prior joint models by directly supervising attention on arguments.
- Strength: strong empirical results — ANN-S2 attains 71.7 F1 vs. 64.6 for unsupervised ANN, with experiments isolating argument utility.
- Strength: clearly written, easy-to-follow architecture and intuitive descriptions of two gold attention construction strategies.
- Weakness: combines word- and entity-attention by simple summation (α = αw + αe) without justification; more principled fusion (gating/concat) might be better.
- Weakness: method depends on richly annotated argument data, limiting applicability to datasets like ACE 2005; no discussion of partial/no-annotation settings.
- Weakness: limited exploration of gold-attention construction — S1/S2 treat all arguments equally and do not consider role-specific weighting or learned attention shapes.
- Question: why use simple summation for α = αw + αe, and were alternative fusion methods (concatenation, gating) tried?
- Question: what is the quality of the FrameNet→ACE mappings and automatic entity tags, and how sensitive is performance to annotation noise?
- Question: given S1 yields higher precision and S2 higher recall, did you try hybrid attention shapes (peak at argument + smaller neighbor peaks) to balance the trade-off?
