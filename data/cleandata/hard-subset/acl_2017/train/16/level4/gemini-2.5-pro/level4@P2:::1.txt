### Summary of the Paper

This paper addresses the task of Event Detection (ED), which involves identifying event trigger words and classifying their types. The authors' central hypothesis is that event arguments, which are often underutilized or ignored in previous ED models, provide crucial information for disambiguating triggers and improving classification accuracy. To exploit this, they propose a neural network model that incorporates a supervised attention mechanism. In this framework, the model is explicitly trained to focus on argument words by minimizing a loss function between its learned attention weights and pre-defined "gold" attention vectors derived from the annotated arguments in the training data. The authors evaluate their approach on the ACE 2005 dataset, demonstrating that their model, particularly with a Gaussian-smoothed attention supervision strategy, achieves a new state-of-the-art F1 score, outperforming previous pipeline and joint-learning methods.

### Contributions

1.  **A Novel Method for Explicitly Integrating Argument Information into Event Detection:** The primary contribution is the proposal to use a supervised attention mechanism to force an ED model to focus on event arguments. While prior work has attempted to use arguments through joint trigger/argument extraction models, this paper provides a compelling analysis of why those methods often fail to improve ED performance and presents a direct and effective alternative. This explicit supervision is a novel way to inject valuable, targeted information into the ED task.

2.  **Thorough Empirical Validation and Analysis:** The paper provides a strong empirical case for its claims. The authors systematically compare unsupervised attention, supervised attention, and oracle-like "gold" attention systems to clearly demonstrate the value of argument information (Table 2). The final results show significant improvements over several strong state-of-the-art systems (Table 3). The inclusion of experiments with FrameNet data further strengthens the findings by showing the approach is robust even when augmented with automatically labeled, and potentially noisy, data.

### Strengths

1.  **Clear Motivation and Well-Founded Hypothesis:** The paper is motivated by a clear and insightful observation: previous joint models for event extraction have improved argument extraction but have had a negligible, or even negative, impact on event detection. The authors' analysis of data imbalance and the direction of information flow in these models provides a strong justification for their work. The example of the trigger "fired" (End-Position vs. Attack) effectively illustrates why argument information is essential for disambiguation.

2.  **Effective and Elegant Model:** The proposed solution is simple, intuitive, and highly effective. Instead of designing a complex joint inference architecture, the authors elegantly integrate argument information as a supervisory signal for an attention mechanism within a standard ED model. This design choice makes the contribution of the supervised attention component clear and unambiguous. The substantial gains in F1 score (e.g., 71.7% for ANN-S2 vs. 64.6% for the unsupervised baseline) are compelling evidence of the method's success.

3.  **Solid and Comprehensive Experimental Setup:** The experiments are well-designed and thorough. The authors include a crucial ablation study (Section 4.2) that validates their core assumption by comparing models with no argument information (ANN), general entity information (ANN-ENT), and oracle argument information (ANN-Gold1/2). This comparison effectively isolates the contribution of arguments specifically, rather than just entities in general. The comparison against a strong suite of recent state-of-the-art methods further contextualizes the significance of the results.

### Weaknesses

1.  **Oversimplified Attention Combination:** The final attention vector is calculated by simply summing the word-based attention and entity-based attention vectors (`α = αw + αe`). This approach gives equal, fixed weight to both information sources. It is plausible that the relative importance of a word's lexical content versus its entity type could vary depending on the context. A more sophisticated fusion method, such as a learned gating mechanism or concatenation followed by a dense layer, might allow the model to dynamically weigh these signals and could potentially yield better performance.

2.  **Heuristic-Based Gold Attention Strategies:** The two strategies for constructing gold attentions (S1 and S2) are reasonable heuristics but are manually defined. Strategy S1 uses a hard, binary assignment, while S2 uses a fixed Gaussian distribution. This raises the question of whether more nuanced or data-driven approaches could be better. For instance, not all arguments are equally important; perhaps the "gold" attention weights could be modulated by the argument's role or its frequency in relation to a given event type. While the proposed strategies are shown to be effective, their heuristic nature represents a potential area for future improvement.

3.  **Dependence on Annotated Arguments During Training:** The method's core strength is also a potential limitation: it relies on the availability of annotated event arguments in the training corpus. While this is true for standard benchmarks like ACE 2005, it might limit the model's applicability to datasets where only event triggers are annotated. The experiments using FrameNet partially address this by showing a way to automatically generate argument labels, but the quality of these labels and their impact on the model are not fully analyzed.

### Questions to Authors

1.  Could you elaborate on the design choice of summing the word and entity attention vectors? Have you explored alternative fusion methods, such as concatenation or a learned gating mechanism, that might weigh these two information sources more dynamically?

2.  Could you provide a brief error analysis? It would be insightful to know for which specific event types the supervised attention model provides the largest improvements over the unsupervised baseline, and which types remain challenging for your model. An example of a sentence where ANN-S2 succeeds and the baseline ANN fails would be particularly helpful for understanding the practical impact of the guided attention.
