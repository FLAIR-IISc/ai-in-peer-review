**Summary and Contributions**

This paper addresses the task of Event Detection (ED) by proposing a method to explicitly leverage event argument information, which the authors argue is underutilized in previous approaches. The core of the work is a neural network model featuring a supervised attention mechanism. This mechanism is trained to assign higher importance to words that are annotated as event arguments, thereby guiding the model to focus on the most salient contextual clues for identifying and classifying event triggers. The authors systematically investigate two strategies for generating the supervisory "gold" attention signals. Experiments conducted on the standard ACE 2005 dataset show that this approach achieves a new state-of-the-art performance.

The main contributions of this work are:
1.  The primary contribution is the novel application of a supervised attention mechanism to directly incorporate annotated argument information for the task of event detection. This provides an effective solution to a well-articulated problem where prior joint models often failed to leverage argument information to benefit the ED sub-task.
2.  The paper presents a strong and valuable empirical validation of its central hypothesis. The proposed model significantly outperforms previous state-of-the-art methods on the ACE 2005 benchmark, and the thorough ablation studies convincingly demonstrate the effectiveness of using explicit argument supervision over unsupervised attention or general entity information.

**Strengths**

1.  **Well-motivated and elegant solution:** The paper clearly identifies a key limitation in prior work—the failure to effectively use argument information for trigger detection. The proposed solution of using supervised attention is an intuitive, clean, and direct way to address this problem. The motivation is well-supported by an analysis of pipeline and joint models in the introduction.
2.  **State-of-the-art performance:** The experimental results are strong and convincing. The method establishes a new state-of-the-art F1 score on the widely-used ACE 2005 dataset, demonstrating a clear and significant improvement over a comprehensive set of recent and competitive baselines.
3.  **Thorough experimental analysis:** The authors conduct a series of well-designed experiments that support their claims. The comparison in Section 4.2 between unsupervised attention, entity-focused attention, and the proposed supervised models effectively isolates the benefits of using argument information. Furthermore, augmenting the training with FrameNet data demonstrates the model's robustness and helps validate the hypothesis that data sparsity is a key challenge for unsupervised attention mechanisms.
4.  **Clarity and simplicity:** The paper is well-written, and the proposed framework is explained clearly. The architecture is straightforward and not overly complex, which makes the work easy to understand and likely to be reproducible.

**Weaknesses**

1.  **Oversimplified attention combination:** The final attention vector is derived from a simple element-wise summation of the word attention and entity attention vectors (`α = αw + αe`). This fusion strategy is rather basic and may not be optimal. A more sophisticated combination, such as a learned weighted average or a gating mechanism, could potentially capture a more nuanced interaction between these two information sources and lead to better performance.
2.  **Heuristic-based supervision strategies:** The two strategies (S1 and S2) for constructing the gold attention vectors are heuristic and relatively straightforward. While shown to be effective, these strategies treat all arguments with equal importance (S1) or based on proximity (S2), ignoring that certain arguments may be more semantically indicative of an event type than others. The paper could be strengthened by discussing or exploring more data-driven approaches to generating the supervisory signal.
3.  **Lack of qualitative error analysis:** The paper presents strong aggregate results but lacks a qualitative analysis of the model's errors. It would be insightful to understand for which types of events or trigger words the model succeeds or fails. An analysis of specific examples where the supervised attention mechanism corrects an error made by the baseline (or fails to do so) would provide a deeper understanding of the method's limitations and strengths.

**Questions to Authors**

1.  Could you elaborate on the design choice of using a simple summation to combine the word and entity attention vectors? Have you explored other fusion techniques, such as concatenation followed by a projection or a gating mechanism, to learn the interaction between these two signals?
2.  The gold attention construction strategies are manually designed. Do you think it would be possible to learn the importance of different arguments when creating the supervisory signal? For example, could the attention weight assigned to an argument depend on its specific entity type or semantic role, and could this be beneficial?
