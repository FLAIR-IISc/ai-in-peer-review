- Proposes supervised attention that leverages annotated event arguments to guide event detection, with two strategies for creating gold attention.
- Achieves a new state-of-the-art F1 on ACE 2005, demonstrating strong empirical gains.
- Main contribution: novel application of supervised attention to directly incorporate argument information for ED.
- Contribution: thorough ablation studies showing explicit argument supervision outperforms unsupervised/entity-focused attention.
- Strength: Well-motivated, elegant, and intuitive solution addressing prior underuse of argument information.
- Strength: Strong, convincing experimental results with significant improvement over competitive baselines.
- Strength: Comprehensive experimental analysis, including comparisons and FrameNet augmentation to mitigate data sparsity.
- Strength: Clear writing and a simple, reproducible architecture.
- Weakness: Attention fusion is an overly simple element-wise summation (α = αw + αe) that may be suboptimal.
- Weakness: Gold attention strategies (S1, S2) are heuristic, treating arguments equally or by proximity and ignoring semantic importance.
- Weakness: Paper lacks qualitative error analysis to show where the model succeeds or fails on specific event/trigger types.
- Question: Why was simple summation used to combine word and entity attention, and were learned fusion methods (projection, gating) explored?
- Question: Could the supervisory attention be learned or weighted by argument properties (e.g., entity type or semantic role) instead of manual heuristics?
