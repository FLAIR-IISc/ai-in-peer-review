### **Summary and Main Contributions**

This paper addresses the task of Event Detection (ED), proposing that event argument information, which is often ignored or used indirectly, can provide strong signals for identifying and classifying event triggers. The core idea is to use annotated arguments from the training data to explicitly supervise an attention mechanism within a neural network-based classifier. The model learns to assign higher attention to words that are likely to be arguments, thereby using this information to better disambiguate event triggers. The authors propose two strategies for generating these "gold" attention signals for supervision. Experiments on the ACE 2005 dataset show that this supervised attention approach significantly outperforms an unsupervised attention baseline and achieves a new state-of-the-art result.

In my view, the main contributions of this work are:

1.  **A novel and effective method for explicitly incorporating argument information into a neural Event Detection model.** The use of a supervised attention mechanism, trained with a secondary loss function to mimic gold attention patterns derived from annotated arguments, is a simple yet clever way to guide the model's focus towards salient contextual clues.
2.  **A clear analysis and empirical validation of the central hypothesis that direct argument information benefits ED.** The authors provide a convincing motivation by showing that previous joint models failed to improve ED. More importantly, the ablation studies (Table 2) and the stark performance difference between the unsupervised and supervised attention models (Table 3) provide strong evidence that directly supervising the attention with argument locations is the key to their performance gains.

### **Strengths**

1.  **Well-motivated and elegant approach.** The paper starts with a clear and compelling observation: despite the intuition that arguments should help ED, prior joint models have failed to deliver significant improvements on this subtask. The authors' hypothesis about unbalanced training objectives and indirect information flow is plausible. Their proposed solution—using supervised attention to directly focus on arguments—is an elegant and direct response to this identified problem.
2.  **Strong empirical results and insightful experiments.** The proposed method (ANN-S2) achieves a state-of-the-art F1 score on the standard ACE 2005 test set, significantly outperforming previous methods. The experimental design is particularly strong because it includes crucial comparisons that validate the authors' claims. The massive performance gap between the unsupervised `ANN` model (64.6 F1) and the supervised `ANN-S2` model (71.7 F1) convincingly demonstrates that the supervision, rather than just the attention mechanism itself, is the source of the improvement. Furthermore, the experiments with oracle `ANN-Gold1/2` systems in Table 2 effectively isolate and confirm the utility of argument information over general entity information.
3.  **The paper is clearly written and the method is easy to follow.** The architecture is explained well, and the motivation for each component is clear. The two strategies for constructing gold attention vectors are intuitive and well-described. This clarity makes the work easy to understand and likely straightforward to reproduce.

### **Weaknesses**

1.  **The combination of attention vectors is overly simplistic and lacks justification.** The final attention vector is calculated as a simple sum of the word-based attention and the entity-based attention (`α = αw + αe`). This is a strong heuristic assumption that these two information sources are equally important and can be combined additively. It is possible that a more principled combination method, such as a learned gating mechanism or concatenation of the resulting context vectors, could yield better performance or provide more insight into the relative importance of each attention stream.
2.  **The applicability of the method is limited to datasets with argument annotations.** The core contribution relies on the availability of annotated arguments in the training set to create the supervision signal. This dependency limits the method's applicability to richly annotated corpora like ACE 2005. The paper does not discuss how this approach might be adapted for settings with partial or no argument annotations, which would be a valuable direction for future work. The FrameNet experiment mitigates this slightly, but still relies on pre-existing frame element annotations.
3.  **The exploration of strategies for constructing gold attentions is somewhat limited.** The two proposed strategies, S1 (binary) and S2 (Gaussian), are reasonable starting points. However, they treat all arguments equally. It is conceivable that certain argument roles are more indicative of an event type than others. A more sophisticated strategy might involve weighting arguments based on their role type or learning the shape of the attention distribution rather than fixing it to a Gaussian.

### **Questions to Authors**

1.  Regarding the combination of attention from words and entities, could you elaborate on the design choice of simple summation (`α = αw + αe`)? Have you experimented with alternative fusion methods, such as concatenating the two resulting context vectors (`cw` and `ce`) before the final classification layer, or using a gating mechanism to dynamically weight the two attention scores?
2.  The FrameNet augmentation experiments required you to map FrameNet's frame elements to ACE arguments and to automatically tag entities. Could you provide some analysis on the quality of these automatic annotations? How sensitive is your model's performance to noise in the argument or entity information in this augmented training data?
3.  Your analysis suggests that the S1 strategy leads to higher precision while S2 leads to higher recall. This is an interesting trade-off. Did you experiment with a hybrid approach that might combine the precise focus of S1 with the broader context of S2, perhaps by creating a gold attention vector with a high peak at the argument itself and smaller peaks for its neighbors?
