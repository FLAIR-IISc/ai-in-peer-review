This paper presents a compelling approach to event detection by leveraging argumentative structure, a source of information often overlooked in prior work. The core idea – incorporating information from arguments to enhance event identification – is both intuitive and potentially impactful. The proposed framework is clearly articulated and relatively simple to understand, which is a significant strength. Furthermore, the integration of a supervised attention mechanism, a well-established and effective technique from areas like machine translation, demonstrably improves performance over strong baselines. The reported results are encouraging and suggest a worthwhile contribution.

However, several aspects of the approach could benefit from further investigation. The method for combining attention vectors – a simple summation of the attention weights from each component – feels somewhat rudimentary. Exploring more sophisticated combination strategies, such as weighted averaging, concatenation followed by a linear transformation, or even learning the combination weights, might yield further performance gains. 

Similarly, the two supervised attention strategies proposed, while functional, appear relatively straightforward. While simplicity is valuable, the paper could be strengthened by a discussion of more complex attention mechanisms or alternative supervision signals. For instance, investigating attention informed by rhetorical roles within the argument (e.g., prioritizing attention to premises supporting a specific claim) could offer a more nuanced and potentially effective approach. Exploring techniques like multi-head attention, common in transformer architectures, may also be fruitful.

The experimental evaluation appears solid and provides convincing evidence of the method’s effectiveness. The choice of baselines is appropriate, and the reported improvements are statistically significant. However, providing an ablation study demonstrating the contribution of each component of the proposed framework (argument information, supervised attention, and the specific attention strategies) would further strengthen the analysis. This would clarify which aspects are most critical to the observed performance gains. 

In summary, this paper offers a promising framework for event detection that effectively incorporates argumentative information. While opportunities exist to refine the attention mechanism and explore more sophisticated strategies, the current work presents a clear, well-motivated, and empirically validated contribution. Despite the areas for improvement, the overall quality of the paper and the strength of the experimental results support acceptance.
