This paper tackles event detection (ED) and argues for the beneficial use of argument information, which is often overlooked or indirectly exploited in existing approaches. The proposed framework is clear and leverages a supervised attention mechanism – a successful technique widely adopted in tasks like machine translation – to achieve significant performance gains over baselines.

**Strengths:**

The primary strength of this work lies in its explicit incorporation of argument information into the event detection process. Prior work, as the authors correctly point out, often either ignores argument information entirely or utilizes it only indirectly through joint learning approaches that appear to prioritize argument extraction. By focusing attention on arguments during event detection, the authors demonstrate a clear performance improvement. The supervised attention mechanism is a well-motivated choice, and the experimental results convincingly show its effectiveness. The ablation study (Table 2) clearly demonstrates the benefit of including argument information. Furthermore, the inclusion of FrameNet data demonstrates a thoughtful approach to addressing data sparsity issues in event detection.

A second strength is the thoroughness of the experimental evaluation. The authors compare their method against several state-of-the-art approaches and provide a detailed analysis of the results, including a discussion of the different attention strategies.

**Weaknesses:**

The proposed method for combining the word and entity attention vectors – simple summation – feels somewhat rudimentary. While functional, more sophisticated combination methods (e.g., concatenation followed by a linear layer, or element-wise multiplication) might yield further improvements.

The two proposed strategies for constructing "gold attentions" (S1 and S2) are relatively straightforward. While the experiments demonstrate their effectiveness, exploring more complex strategies for capturing the nuanced relationship between arguments and trigger words could potentially lead to even better performance. For instance, considering the syntactic dependency between the trigger and its arguments could be explored.

Finally, the paper lacks a detailed error analysis. Identifying common error cases and discussing potential reasons for those errors could offer valuable insights and suggest avenues for future research.

**Questions to Authors:**

1. Could you elaborate on the computational cost of incorporating the argument information through the attention mechanism? Does it significantly increase the inference time compared to baseline methods?
2. What is the sensitivity of the results to the hyperparameter λ in equation 9? Did you perform a more comprehensive hyperparameter search for λ?

**General Discussion:**

Despite some minor limitations, this paper presents a compelling and effective framework for improving event detection by explicitly leveraging argument information. The experimental results are solid, and the improvements over state-of-the-art methods are significant. The work is well-motivated and clearly presented. I recommend this paper for acceptance.

**Additional Comments:**

*   Throughout the paper, "exiting" should be "existing".
*   In the abstract, "state-of-the-arts" should be "state of the art".
*   The formatting of Table 1 is slightly unusual; consider aligning the numbers more clearly.
*   The reference list is missing some formatting (e.g. journal titles).
