This paper explores leveraging argument information – often overlooked in existing methods – to enhance event detection performance. The proposed framework is clear and employs a supervised attention mechanism, a technique successfully applied in other NLP tasks like machine translation. Experimental results demonstrate a significant performance improvement over baselines.

**Contributions:** The primary contribution is the explicit incorporation of argument information into an event detection model via supervised attention. A secondary contribution lies in the systematic investigation of different attention strategies within this framework. The authors demonstrate the effectiveness of their approach, achieving state-of-the-art results on the ACE 2005 dataset.

**Strengths:**

1.  **Novelty in Argument Exploitation:** The paper addresses a key gap in existing event detection research by explicitly utilizing argument information. While joint methods exist, the authors convincingly argue that these often indirectly leverage arguments, primarily benefiting argument *extraction* rather than event *detection*. This re-framing and focused approach are valuable.
2.  **Effective Use of Supervised Attention:** The application of supervised attention, guided by annotated arguments, proves to be a successful strategy. The results clearly indicate that focusing the model's attention on relevant arguments improves event detection accuracy.
3.  **Strong Empirical Results:** The proposed model achieves state-of-the-art performance on the ACE 2005 dataset, demonstrating the practical effectiveness of the approach. The comprehensive experimental setup, including comparisons to various baselines and ablation studies (examining different attention strategies), strengthens these findings.
4.  **Clear and Well-Structured Presentation:** The paper is well-written and logically organized, making it easy to understand the proposed method and experimental results.

**Weaknesses:**

1.  **Attention Vector Combination:** The method of simply summing the word and entity attention vectors appears simplistic. Exploring more sophisticated combination methods (e.g., concatenation, gating mechanisms) might yield further performance gains.
2.  **Limited Attention Strategy Exploration:** While the authors investigate two attention strategies, both are relatively straightforward. Investigating more complex or adaptive attention mechanisms could potentially lead to improved results. The benefit of using external data from FrameNet could also be investigated more thoroughly. 
3. **Lack of Error Analysis:** While the paper demonstrates strong overall performance, it lacks a detailed error analysis. Understanding the types of events or situations where the model still struggles would provide valuable insights for future research.



**Questions to Authors:**

1.  Could you elaborate on why you chose a simple summation to combine the word and entity attention vectors? Did you experiment with other combination methods?
2.  What is the computational overhead of incorporating the attention mechanism? Is it significantly higher than baseline methods, and if so, how could it be mitigated?
3.  Can you provide examples of cases where the model correctly detects the event type due to the attention mechanism focusing on relevant arguments?

**Additional Comments:**

*   The discussion of related work could be expanded to include more recent advances in attention mechanisms and event detection.
*   The hyperparameter settings should be discussed more thoroughly; a justification for the chosen values would be helpful.
