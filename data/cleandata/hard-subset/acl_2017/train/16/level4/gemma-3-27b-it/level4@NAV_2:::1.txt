This paper investigates the use of argument information – often overlooked in prior work – to improve event detection (ED) performance. The proposed framework is clear and leverages a supervised attention mechanism, a technique proven effective in other NLP tasks like machine translation. The experimental results demonstrate significant performance gains over baseline systems.

**Strengths:**

The primary strength of this work lies in its explicit exploitation of argument information for event detection. Existing methods largely ignore or indirectly utilize arguments, and this paper convincingly demonstrates their value through a well-designed supervised attention mechanism. The attention mechanism effectively focuses the model on relevant argument words during event type classification. The framework’s clarity and simplicity are also notable strengths, making the approach easily understandable and reproducible. Finally, the reported performance improvements on the ACE 2005 dataset are substantial and demonstrate the efficacy of the proposed method, outperforming several state-of-the-art approaches.

**Weaknesses:**

The current implementation of the attention mechanism relies on a simple summation of individual attention vectors. While functional, this approach may not fully capture the complex interactions between contextual words and entities. Exploring more sophisticated attention aggregation methods could potentially yield further improvements. Additionally, the proposed strategies for constructing gold attention vectors (S1 and S2) are relatively straightforward. While they provide a reasonable starting point for supervised attention, investigating more complex or nuanced strategies could lead to enhanced performance.  The gains from FrameNet augmentation are modest, suggesting a potential limitation in how the external data is integrated.

**Questions to Authors:**

1.  Could you elaborate on the rationale behind choosing a simple summation for combining the word and entity attention vectors? Have you experimented with alternative combination methods (e.g., concatenation, gating mechanisms)?
2.  Beyond strategies S1 and S2, did you explore other methods for generating the gold attention vectors, particularly those that might incorporate semantic relationships between words and arguments?
3.  Given the relatively modest improvements from the FrameNet data augmentation, could you provide more details on the filtering or pre-processing steps applied to the FN data, and whether alternative integration strategies were considered?

**General Discussion:**

Despite minor areas for potential improvement, this paper presents a valuable contribution to the field of event detection. The proposed framework is effective, the experiments are well-conducted, and the results are compelling. The work is a solid addition to the literature and is suitable for acceptance.
