## Review: Exploiting Argument Information to Improve Event Detection via Supervised Attention Mechanisms

This paper tackles the event detection (ED) task, arguing that event arguments, often overlooked, provide valuable clues for improved performance. The authors propose explicitly exploiting argument information via supervised attention mechanisms, investigating different attention strategies within their model. The approach demonstrates strong results, achieving state-of-the-art performance on the ACE 2005 dataset.

**Contributions:**

The primary contribution of this work is the demonstration that explicitly modeling argument information through supervised attention can significantly improve event detection performance. While joint event and argument extraction models exist, this work highlights that previous approaches haven’t effectively leveraged arguments *for* event detection, and proposes a targeted solution.  A secondary contribution lies in the systematic investigation of different strategies for constructing “gold” attention vectors based on annotated arguments, providing insights into how to best incorporate this information into the learning process. Finally, the strong empirical results on the ACE 2005 dataset establish the effectiveness of the proposed approach and provide a new strong baseline for future work.

**Strengths:**

1. **Novelty and Insightful Motivation:** The paper identifies a key gap in existing event detection research – the underutilization of argument information. The authors convincingly argue that arguments provide useful signals for disambiguating event triggers, and this motivation forms a strong foundation for their work.
2. **Strong Empirical Performance:** The proposed approach achieves state-of-the-art results on the challenging ACE 2005 dataset. The performance gains over existing methods, particularly the improvements in precision reported in the experiments, are significant and demonstrate the effectiveness of the approach.
3. **Clear and Well-Defined Methodology:** The proposed model is clearly described, and the use of supervised attention mechanisms is well-justified. The two strategies for constructing gold attention vectors are straightforward to understand and provide a useful comparison.
4. **Thorough Experimental Evaluation:** The authors perform a comprehensive set of experiments, including comparisons with strong baselines and ablation studies to validate the effectiveness of different components of their model. The inclusion of experiments augmenting the data with FrameNet resources further strengthens the evaluation. 

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Simplicity of Attention Combination:** The method of simply summing the word and entity attention vectors may be overly simplistic. Exploring more sophisticated combination techniques (e.g., gating mechanisms, concatenation followed by a linear transformation) could potentially yield further improvements.
2. **Limited Exploration of Attention Strategies:** While the paper investigates two strategies for constructing gold attention vectors, the exploration of different attention mechanisms feels somewhat limited. More diverse or learned attention strategies might provide further gains.  The reliance on a Gaussian distribution for S2 seems arbitrary; justification for this choice could be strengthened.
3. **Dependence on ACE 2005:** The evaluation is heavily focused on the ACE 2005 dataset.  While a standard benchmark, the results might not generalize to other event detection datasets with different characteristics. Evaluation on a more diverse set of datasets would increase confidence in the robustness of the approach. 
4. **Lack of Error Analysis:** The paper lacks a detailed error analysis. Identifying specific cases where the model fails would provide valuable insights into the limitations of the approach and guide future research.

**Questions to Authors:**

1.  Could you elaborate on the rationale for choosing a Gaussian distribution in strategy S2 for constructing the gold attention vectors? Were alternative distributions considered, and if so, why were they rejected?
2.  Have you experimented with more sophisticated methods for combining the word and entity attention vectors, such as gating mechanisms or concatenation followed by a learned transformation? If so, what were the results?
3.  Do you have any observations about the types of events or triggers where your approach performs particularly well or poorly? 

**Additional Comments:**

*  The paper is generally well-written and easy to follow. 
*  Consider adding a table summarizing the key differences between your approach and related joint event and argument extraction models.
*  The reference formatting appears inconsistent; please ensure adherence to ACL style guidelines.



Overall, this is a strong paper that presents a novel and effective approach to event detection. The clear motivation, strong empirical results, and thorough evaluation make it a valuable contribution to the field. Despite some minor weaknesses, the strengths of the paper outweigh its shortcomings, and I recommend acceptance.
