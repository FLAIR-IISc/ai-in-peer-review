Summary and Contributions
- This paper addresses event detection (ED) and argues that event arguments provide valuable cues that are often ignored or only indirectly used by prior methods. The authors propose a neural ED model that explicitly leverages argument information via supervised attention.
- Main contributions (as I see them):
  1) Introducing supervised attention to directly incorporate annotated argument information into ED, rather than relying on joint models where arguments mostly benefit AE.
  2) Systematically evaluating two simple supervision strategies for attention (argument-only vs. argument-plus-neighborhood).
  3) Demonstrating substantial gains over baseline models on ACE 2005, with a clear and implementable architecture.

Strengths
- Clear, effective idea: The paper targets a well-motivated gap—arguments are informative for trigger disambiguation but are underused in ED—and provides a direct mechanism (supervised attention) to exploit them.
- Simple, transparent architecture: The model is easy to understand and reproduce. The separation between context representation learning and the event detector, with attention guided by argument supervision, is clean and practical.
- Empirical gains: The supervised attention variants significantly outperform the unsupervised attention baseline. The results suggest that the proposed supervision materially improves ED, particularly in recall when attention is spread to words surrounding arguments.
- Solid experimental setup: The paper follows standard ACE 2005 splits and compares to strong baselines. The analysis contrasting supervised and unsupervised attention helps validate the central claim that argument information is crucial to ED.
- Use of multiple attention strategies: Evaluating both argument-only and neighborhood-aware attention supervision provides insight into precision–recall trade-offs and indicates robustness of the approach.

Weaknesses
- Attention fusion is simplistic: The final attention is a simple sum of word-based and entity-based attentions. More principled fusion (e.g., learned gating, multiplicative interactions, or multi-head mechanisms) could be explored and might yield further gains.
- Supervision strategies are basic: Both attention supervision strategies are straightforward (uniform over arguments; Gaussian around arguments). The paper could benefit from exploring richer supervision, such as role-aware weighting, distance-sensitive decay tuned per role, or supervision derived from syntactic/semantic structure (e.g., dependencies from trigger to arguments).
- Limited analysis of design choices: The paper stops short of deeper ablations that could clarify where the gains come from (e.g., contribution of entity types vs. word context, sensitivity to window size and Gaussian parameters, or error analysis on ambiguous triggers). Such analysis would strengthen the causal link between argument supervision and improved ED.

Overall Assessment
Despite room for architectural and analytical enhancements, this paper presents a clear and effective framework that directly addresses a salient limitation in prior ED work. The supervised attention mechanism is well-motivated and yields meaningful improvements over baselines on ACE 2005. The methodology is simple yet impactful, and the experiments are sound. I recommend acceptance.
