Summary
This paper explores leveraging argument information—often overlooked yet crucial—to enhance event detection. The proposed framework is clear and straightforward, incorporating supervised attention to guide the model toward informative context. The approach yields significant improvements over a strong baseline.

Strengths
- Clear and simple framework: The overall design is easy to follow and well motivated, making the contribution accessible and reproducible.
- Effective use of supervised attention: The method leverages attention supervision, a technique with demonstrated success in tasks like machine translation, to focus on informative tokens and arguments for event detection.
- Empirical gains: The system outperforms the baseline by a substantial margin, indicating that argument information is indeed beneficial. The experiments appear solid and carefully conducted.

Weaknesses and Suggestions
- Attention composition: The attention vector is computed as the sum of two attention vectors from each part. This simple aggregation may overlook interactions between the parts and could dilute informative signals. Consider alternatives such as:
  - Learned weighted combinations or gating mechanisms to balance contributions dynamically.
  - Concatenation followed by a projection layer to allow the model to learn richer interactions.
  - Bilinear or tensor-based fusion to capture cross-part dependencies.
  - Multi-head attention to model different types of relevance signals.
  An ablation comparing these alternatives against simple summation would clarify trade-offs.
- Supervised attention strategies: The two proposed supervision strategies are straightforward. While this simplicity is appealing, it may underutilize the available supervision signal. Potential extensions include:
  - Span-level or role-aware supervision that ties attention to specific argument roles rather than individual tokens.
  - Margin-based or posterior-regularization objectives to enforce stronger alignment between attention and supervision.
  - Curriculum learning or confidence-weighted supervision to mitigate noise in the supervision signals.
  - Multi-task learning with auxiliary objectives (e.g., argument role labeling) to strengthen the attention guidance.
- Clarification of supervision details: The paper would benefit from more detail on how supervision signals are derived (e.g., seed words, argument markers, or external annotations), their noise characteristics, and their impact on performance. Sensitivity analyses (e.g., varying the strength of the supervision loss, the quality/quantity of supervision) would make the findings more robust.
- Analysis: While the empirical improvements are clear, additional qualitative analysis—such as visualizing attention distributions for successes and failures—could help demonstrate how the supervised attention changes model behavior and where it is most effective.

Overall Assessment
This paper presents an effective and well-executed framework for event detection by incorporating argument information via supervised attention. Despite some design choices that could be further refined—particularly in attention composition and supervision strategies—the approach is sound, the experiments are solid, and the performance gains are compelling. I lean toward acceptance.

Minor Comments
- Provide implementation details (e.g., training hyperparameters, initialization, and regularization) to aid reproducibility.
- Report variance across runs if possible, to establish statistical robustness.
- If space permits, include a brief error analysis to identify common failure modes and inform future work.
