Summary
This paper addresses event detection (ED) by explicitly leveraging event argument information via supervised attention. The model represents each trigger candidate with contextual word and entity embeddings, computes two attention distributions (word-based and entity-type-based) conditioned on the candidate, and trains the attention mechanism using “gold” attention derived from annotated arguments. Two supervision strategies are studied: (S1) focusing attention on argument tokens only; (S2) distributing attention around arguments using a Gaussian kernel. The approach is evaluated on ACE 2005, with additional experiments augmenting training data using FrameNet-derived events. Results show improvements over prior systems and over an unsupervised attention baseline.

Main contributions (as I see them)
1) An explicit, supervised-attention formulation that uses annotated arguments to guide contextual focus for ED, rather than relying on indirect use of arguments in joint models.  
2) A systematic comparison of simple supervision strategies for attention (arguments-only vs. arguments-plus-neighbors) and evidence that argument supervision improves ED, including ablations contrasting argument vs. entity information.  
3) Competitive performance on ACE 2005 and further gains with FrameNet augmentation, suggesting the approach is effective and complementary to data augmentation.

Strengths
- Clear motivation and empirical evidence for argument utility in ED. The paper convincingly illustrates that ambiguous triggers benefit from argument context and substantiates this with ablations: systems using gold argument-derived attention (ANN-Gold1/2) substantially outperform those without argument guidance (ANN, ANN-ENT), showing that entities alone are insufficient while arguments are highly informative.

- Simple, modular method with measurable gains. The supervised attention mechanism is easy to implement and integrate. Both supervised variants (ANN-S1, ANN-S2) markedly improve over the unsupervised-attention baseline. The S1/S2 comparison is insightful: S1 yields higher precision, S2 higher recall, aligning with their design intuition.

- Competitive state-of-the-art results on ACE 2005. ANN-S2 achieves the best reported F1 (71.7), slightly outperforming strong neural baselines (e.g., Skip-CNN). With FrameNet augmentation, the approach improves further (to 71.9 F1). The authors also report significance testing.

- Solid experimental setup and useful analyses. The paper follows common ACE splits, reports key hyperparameters, and explores data augmentation via FrameNet, shedding light on why unsupervised attention underperforms (data sparsity) and how extra data affects precision/recall.

Weaknesses
- Limited methodological novelty. Supervised attention with oracle-derived signals is a straightforward extension of attention mechanisms applied in other tasks. The contribution is mainly in the application to ED and the explicit use of gold arguments for attention supervision, rather than in a fundamentally new model.

- Dependence on gold supervision and entity annotations. Training attention from gold arguments presumes argument annotations are available—which they are in ACE but not in many real settings. Moreover, entity types are used as inputs; it is unclear whether gold or predicted entities are used at test time. This affects practicality and the fairness of comparisons. An evaluation with predicted entities (and noisy argument supervision) would strengthen claims of robustness.

- Simple attention combination and loss formulation. The final attention is the unweighted sum of word- and entity-based attentions, with MSE used to match gold attention. A learned gating/weighting, a normalization-aware divergence (e.g., KL), or ranking-based losses could be more appropriate. An ablation on the combination scheme and loss would clarify design choices.

- Modest margin over the strongest baseline and reporting details. The improvement over Skip-CNN is small (0.4 F1). Although a t-test is reported, variance across random seeds is not provided, and it is not entirely clear that all systems use identical resources (e.g., entity annotations). A per-type breakdown, confidence intervals, and multiple-run averages would better support the significance claim.

- Missing or under-specified details. The context window size n and sensitivity analyses are not reported; the proportion of triggers without in-window arguments (where S1 backs off to entity averaging) is unknown; and there is limited qualitative/error analysis to illustrate when the supervised attention helps or fails.

Questions to authors
- Do you use gold entity mentions/types at test time? If so, please also report results with predicted entities to assess practical performance and ensure comparability to baselines that do not rely on gold entities.

- What is the context window size n, and how sensitive are results to n? How often do triggers lack any in-window arguments, and how does performance vary with trigger–argument distance?

- How many random seeds/runs were used for each result, and how was the t-test conducted? Please report mean±std over multiple runs.

- Can your attention supervision be learned from noisy/automatic arguments (e.g., predicted by an AE system)? A degradation/robustness study would assess applicability beyond ACE.

- Have you tried learned weighting between word- and entity-based attentions, or alternative attention supervision losses (e.g., KL divergence to target distribution)? Any preliminary results?

Additional comments
- Consider adding per-event-type precision/recall to clarify where supervised attention helps most (e.g., disambiguating polysemous triggers).

- Some writing issues can be polished (e.g., “state-of-the-arts” → “state of the art”, “p 6 0.05” → “p ≤ 0.05”). Please also verify reference formatting and completeness.

Overall recommendation
Leaning accept. The idea is incremental but well-motivated, the method is simple and effective, and the empirical results are solid with useful analyses. Addressing the concerns on entity/argument supervision assumptions, reporting variance, and adding robustness experiments would further strengthen the paper.
