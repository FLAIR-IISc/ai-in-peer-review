- Paper addresses event detection using supervised attention guided by annotated arguments, with word- and entity-type-based attentions and two supervision strategies (S1 arguments-only, S2 Gaussian around arguments); evaluated on ACE2005 and with FrameNet augmentation.

- Main contribution: explicit supervised-attention formulation using annotated arguments to guide ED.

- Main contribution: systematic comparison of attention supervision strategies (S1 vs S2) and ablations showing argument supervision improves ED over entity-only signals.

- Main contribution: Competitive ACE2005 performance and additional gains from FrameNet data augmentation.

- Strength: Clear motivation and empirical evidence that argument information disambiguates triggers; ablations show gold-argument attention greatly helps.

- Strength: Simple, modular method that is easy to implement and yields measurable gains; S1 improves precision, S2 improves recall.

- Strength: Strong reported results on ACE2005 (ANN-S2 71.7 F1; 71.9 with FrameNet) with significance testing.

- Strength: Solid experimental setup (standard splits, hyperparameters) and useful analyses on data augmentation and unsupervised attention limitations.

- Weakness: Limited methodological novelty—straightforward extension of attention with oracle-derived supervision.

- Weakness: Heavy dependence on gold argument/entity annotations; unclear whether gold or predicted entities are used at test time, raising practicality and fairness concerns.

- Weakness: Simple attention combination (unweighted sum) and MSE loss; alternatives like learned gating or KL/ranking losses not explored.

- Weakness: Modest improvement over strongest baseline (≈0.4 F1); lacks variance across runs, per-type breakdown, and multi-run averages to support significance claims.

- Weakness: Missing/under-specified details (context window size n, sensitivity analyses, rate of triggers without in-window arguments, limited qualitative/error analysis).

- Question: Do you use gold entity mentions/types at test time; please report results with predicted entities.

- Question: What is the context window size n, sensitivity to n, how often triggers lack in-window arguments, and how does performance vary with trigger–argument distance?

- Question: How many random seeds/runs were used, how was the t-test conducted; please report mean±std over multiple runs.

- Question: Can attention supervision be learned from noisy/automatic arguments (e.g., predicted by an AE system); include a robustness/degradation study.

- Question: Have you tried learned weighting between word- and entity-based attentions or alternative supervision losses (e.g., KL divergence); any preliminary results?

- Additional comment: Add per-event-type precision/recall to show where supervised attention helps most.

- Additional comment: Fix minor writing issues and reference formatting.

- Overall recommendation: Leaning accept — incremental but well-motivated and effective; addressing supervision assumptions, variance reporting, and robustness experiments would strengthen the paper.
