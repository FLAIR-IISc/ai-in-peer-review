- Proposes a neural event detection (ED) model that explicitly uses annotated event argument information through supervised attention.  
- Main contributions: introducing supervised attention for ED, evaluating two supervision strategies (argument-only vs. argument+neighborhood), and showing substantial gains on ACE 2005 with a clear architecture.  
- Addresses an underused signal in ED by directly leveraging arguments for trigger disambiguation.  
- Model architecture is simple, transparent, and easy to reproduce, separating context representation and event detection.  
- Supervised attention variants significantly outperform unsupervised attention, improving particularly on recall when attention includes neighboring words.  
- Experimental setup is solid, using standard ACE 2005 splits and strong baselines, with analysis contrasting supervised vs. unsupervised attention.  
- Evaluating both argument-only and neighborhood-aware supervision reveals precision–recall trade-offs and approach robustness.  
- Final attention fusion is simplistic (simple sum of word- and entity-based attentions) and could benefit from more principled fusion mechanisms.  
- Attention supervision strategies are basic (uniform and Gaussian); richer options like role-aware or syntax-informed supervision are unexplored.  
- Limited deeper ablations and analyses (e.g., entity type vs. word context contributions, sensitivity to window/Gaussian parameters, error analysis) weaken causal claims.  
- Overall: a well-motivated, simple, and effective method that yields meaningful improvements and merits acceptance despite opportunities for architectural and analytical extensions.
