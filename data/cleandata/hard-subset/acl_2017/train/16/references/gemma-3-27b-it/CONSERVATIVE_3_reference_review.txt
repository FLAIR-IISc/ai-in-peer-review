This paper proposes a neural network approach to event detection (ED) that explicitly leverages argument information through a supervised attention mechanism. The authors argue that existing joint ED and argument extraction (AE) models implicitly utilize arguments for AE, not ED, and that their method directly addresses this issue. They present experimental results on ACE 2005, demonstrating improvements over baseline methods, including state-of-the-art approaches.

The primary contribution of this work is the attempt to directly incorporate argument information into the ED process via supervised attention. While the idea of explicitly using argument information is reasonable, the presentation lacks sufficient justification for *why* their proposed approach is fundamentally different or superior to existing joint models. The authors claim existing methods don’t leverage argument information for ED, but joint models, by definition, consider dependencies between triggers and arguments. The proposed method simply re-frames this as an attention mechanism, without demonstrating a clear advantage in how argument information is utilized.

**Strengths:**

1. **Clear Problem Statement:** The paper clearly articulates the potential benefits of explicitly leveraging argument information for event detection, and identifies a perceived gap in how existing joint models handle this aspect.
2. **Empirical Results:** The experimental results on the ACE 2005 dataset demonstrate that the proposed approach achieves competitive performance and outperforms some existing methods. The ablation studies exploring different attention strategies are also a positive aspect.
3. **Thorough Experimentation:** The authors explore the use of FrameNet data for augmentation, showing some improvements, and provide a reasonable experimental setup.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Novelty:** The core idea of using attention to focus on relevant context words, including arguments, is not particularly novel. Attention mechanisms are widely used in NLP, and applying them to incorporate argument information feels incremental rather than groundbreaking. The claim that previous joint methods *don't* use argument information for ED is debatable and not sufficiently substantiated.
2. **Insufficient Comparison to Strong Baselines:** The comparisons to state-of-the-art methods are not comprehensive. While some baselines are included, the lack of comparison to more recent and powerful models (e.g., those leveraging Transformers) weakens the claim of significant improvement. A more thorough comparison is needed to demonstrate the true effectiveness of the proposed approach.
3. **Overreliance on ACE 2005:** The evaluation is solely based on the ACE 2005 dataset. This raises concerns about the generalizability of the results. Testing on other event detection datasets would strengthen the findings.
4. **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** The varying lambda values for S1 and S2 strategies (1.0 vs. 5.0) raise concerns about hyperparameter tuning and the sensitivity of the model to these parameters. The justification for these specific values is unclear.
5. **Unclear Advantage of Supervised Attention:** The benefit of *supervised* attention is not convincingly demonstrated. The gold attention construction methods (S1 and S2) seem somewhat arbitrary, and the improvements gained compared to a standard attention mechanism are not substantial enough to justify the added complexity.

**Questions to Authors:**

1. Can you provide a more detailed explanation of why you believe existing joint models do not leverage argument information for ED? Specifically, how does your supervised attention mechanism differ in its utilization of arguments compared to the joint inference process in models like Li et al. (2013) or Nguyen et al. (2016)?
2. Have you experimented with more recent state-of-the-art models for event detection, such as those based on Transformers (e.g., BERT, RoBERTa)? If so, how does your approach compare to these models?
3. What is the rationale behind the chosen values for the hyperparameter lambda in the S1 and S2 strategies? Did you perform a more extensive hyperparameter search?
4. Could you provide a more intuitive explanation for why the S2 strategy, which considers words around arguments, performs better than the S1 strategy, which focuses solely on argument words?
5.  Have you investigated the impact of different gold attention construction strategies beyond S1 and S2?
