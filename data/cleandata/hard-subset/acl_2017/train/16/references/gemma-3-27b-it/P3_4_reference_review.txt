## Review for “Exploiting Argument Information to Improve Event Detection via Supervised Attention Mechanisms”

**Summary:**

This paper addresses the event detection (ED) task, arguing that incorporating event argument information can improve performance, particularly in disambiguating trigger words. The authors identify a weakness in existing joint event detection and argument extraction (EE/AE) models – that they tend to focus more on argument extraction due to data imbalance and indirect argument usage. They propose a novel neural network approach that explicitly leverages argument information through a supervised attention mechanism, training the attention based on annotated arguments. Experimental results on ACE 2005 demonstrate state-of-the-art performance, with further improvements when augmented with data from FrameNet.

**Main Contributions (as I see them):**

1. **Empirical Demonstration of Argument Utility for ED:** The paper convincingly demonstrates, through a series of controlled experiments, that explicitly incorporating argument information benefits event detection, contrasting this with the limited gains observed in traditional joint ED/AE models. This is a valuable empirical finding.
2. **Supervised Attention Framework for Argument Incorporation:** The proposed supervised attention mechanism provides a practical and effective way to integrate argument information into an ED model. The two strategies for constructing "gold attentions" offer flexibility and reveal interesting trade-offs.
3. **Comprehensive Experimental Evaluation:** The authors provide a thorough evaluation, including comparisons with strong baselines, ablation studies (demonstrating the impact of different attention strategies), and data augmentation with FrameNet, bolstering the credibility of their findings.

**Strengths:**

1. **Clear Motivation and Problem Definition:** The authors clearly articulate the limitations of existing approaches and convincingly motivate the need for explicitly leveraging argument information for ED. The example provided with “fired” is particularly effective.
2. **Sound Methodology:** The proposed supervised attention mechanism is well-motivated and logically sound. The use of gold attentions as supervision is a clever way to guide the model's focus toward relevant arguments.
3. **Strong Empirical Results:** The reported results on the ACE 2005 dataset demonstrate significant improvements over state-of-the-art methods, validating the effectiveness of the proposed approach. The ablation studies in Section 4.2 are particularly insightful.
4. **Thorough Experimental Setup:** The authors meticulously describe the dataset, hyperparameter settings, and evaluation metrics, facilitating reproducibility. The inclusion of FrameNet data augmentation further strengthens the evaluation.
5. **Well-Written and Organized:** The paper is well-structured, clearly written, and easy to follow. The diagrams (Figures 1 and 2) effectively illustrate the model architecture and attention mechanism.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Novelty of Core Components:** While the combination of supervised attention with argument information is valuable, the individual components (neural network architecture, attention mechanism) are not novel. The primary contribution lies in *how* these components are combined and applied to the specific problem of argument-aware ED. This should be more clearly acknowledged.
2. **Dependence on Annotation Quality:** The performance of the model heavily relies on the quality of argument annotations in the training data.  The paper doesn’t adequately address potential issues arising from noisy or inconsistent annotations.  A discussion of annotation error and its potential impact would be valuable.
3. **Lack of Analysis of Attention Weights:** While the paper demonstrates *that* the attention mechanism works, it provides limited analysis of *what* the model is attending to. Visualizing attention weights for specific examples would provide further insight into the model's behavior and help understand how it utilizes argument information. Are the attentions truly focused on arguments as intended, or are other factors influencing the attention weights?
4. **Limited Discussion of Generalizability:** The evaluation is performed solely on the ACE 2005 dataset. It's unclear how well the approach would generalize to other datasets with different event schemas or annotation styles. A brief discussion of potential challenges and limitations in generalizing to new domains would be beneficial.



**Questions to Authors:**

1.  Could you provide examples of attention weight visualizations for a few representative sentences, highlighting how the model attends to arguments and other relevant words?
2.  How sensitive is the performance of the model to variations in the quality of argument annotations? Have you considered any strategies for handling noisy or incomplete annotations?
3.  How does the performance of the proposed model compare to existing joint ED/AE models when applied to a downstream AE task? (Even if the focus is on ED, showing that using argument information for ED doesn't *harm* AE would be reassuring).
4.  The paper mentions data imbalance as a potential reason for the poor performance of joint models.  Did you explore any techniques (e.g., class weighting, oversampling) to address this imbalance in the joint models you compared against?




**Additional Comments:**

*   The comparison to state-of-the-art models in Table 3 could be improved by including the F1 scores for the NA class, as this is often a significant component of overall performance in ED.
*   Consider adding a brief discussion of related work on attention mechanisms in other NLP tasks, to better contextualize the proposed approach.



**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper that presents a valuable contribution to the field of event detection. While the individual components are not entirely novel, the authors effectively combine them to address a specific problem – leveraging argument information for ED – and demonstrate significant improvements over existing methods.  The paper is well-written, thoroughly evaluated, and makes a compelling case for the effectiveness of the proposed approach. I recommend acceptance, pending minor revisions to address the weaknesses outlined above.
