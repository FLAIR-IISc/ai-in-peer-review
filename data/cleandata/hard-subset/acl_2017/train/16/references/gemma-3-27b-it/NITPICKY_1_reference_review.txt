This paper proposes a supervised attention mechanism to incorporate argument information into event detection (ED). The authors argue that existing joint ED and argument extraction models don’t effectively leverage argument information for *improving* ED performance, and instead, arguments primarily benefit argument extraction. They present a model where attention weights are learned based on annotated arguments, aiming to focus the model on relevant context for trigger classification.

The primary contribution, as I see it, is the explicit attempt to address the under-utilization of argument information in existing joint models for event detection. While the idea isn't entirely novel – attention mechanisms are common – applying them specifically to leverage argument cues for *improving ED*, rather than just as part of a joint loss, is a reasonable direction. A secondary contribution is the empirical demonstration, on ACE 2005, that incorporating argument information via supervised attention does indeed yield performance gains, surpassing existing state-of-the-art methods.

**Strengths:**

1. **Clear Motivation:** The paper clearly articulates the problem – that argument information, despite being present in training data, isn't effectively used to boost ED performance in existing joint models. The provided example with “fired” is compelling and effectively illustrates the potential benefit of considering arguments.
2. **Sound Methodology:** The proposed supervised attention mechanism is logically sound and reasonably implemented. The use of gold attention vectors as supervision is a sensible approach to guide the learning process. The two strategies (S1 and S2) for constructing gold attention offer interesting variations in how argument influence is modeled.
3. **Empirical Validation:** The experiments on ACE 2005 demonstrate tangible improvements over strong baselines. Achieving state-of-the-art performance is a positive indicator. The inclusion of FrameNet augmentation further strengthens the results and showcases the model’s adaptability.
4. **Comprehensive Related Work:** The related work section provides a good overview of existing approaches to event detection, including both pipeline and joint methods, and correctly positions the current work within this landscape.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Lack of Ablation Study Rigor:** While the paper compares different attention strategies (S1 and S2), a more systematic ablation study is needed. For instance, what happens if the entity type embeddings are removed? What is the contribution of *just* the attention mechanism, independent of the FrameNet augmentation? The current presentation makes it difficult to isolate the impact of each component.
2. **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** The difference in lambda values (1.0 vs 5.0) for S1 and S2 feels arbitrary and lacks justification. A more thorough hyperparameter search and reporting would strengthen the validity of the results.  Why were those specific values chosen? A sensitivity analysis would be valuable.
3. **Formatting and Presentation Issues:**  The paper contains numerous minor formatting inconsistencies. For example, inconsistent spacing after punctuation, varying font sizes (particularly in equations), and a generally cluttered layout detract from readability. Table 3’s formatting is particularly poor, making it difficult to quickly compare results. Also, the use of “†” to denote systems employing argument information is unconventional and could be more clearly explained.
4. **Limited Error Analysis:**  The paper lacks a detailed error analysis.  What types of events does the model still struggle with? What are common error cases?  Understanding the limitations of the model would provide valuable insights for future work. Simply stating "achieves the best performance" isn't sufficient.
5. **Overstated Claims:** The claim of “remarkable gains” feels overstated given the magnitude of the improvements. While statistically significant, the F1 score improvements are relatively modest (e.g., 70.8% vs. 69.4%).

**Questions to Authors:**

1. Could you provide a more detailed explanation for the choice of lambda values for strategies S1 and S2?  What hyperparameter search was conducted?
2. Can you perform a more rigorous ablation study to isolate the contributions of each component of your proposed model (attention mechanism, entity embeddings, FrameNet augmentation)?
3.  Please provide a qualitative error analysis.  Can you describe specific error cases where the model fails and explain why?
4. Why were the word embeddings pre-trained on the NYT corpus and not on ACE 2005 itself, or a corpus more representative of the event detection domain?

**Additional Comments:**

* Please thoroughly proofread the paper for formatting inconsistencies and typographical errors.
* Equation (6) is missing a closing parenthesis.
* The citation style is inconsistent (e.g., "Bengio et al.,? 2003" vs. "Baker et al.,? 1998").



Overall, this paper presents a reasonably sound approach to leveraging argument information for event detection. However, the lack of rigorous ablation studies, formatting issues, and overstated claims prevent me from recommending acceptance in its current form. A major revision addressing these weaknesses is necessary.
