This paper proposes a supervised attention mechanism to incorporate argument information into event detection (ED), arguing that existing joint ED and argument extraction models fail to effectively leverage arguments for improving ED performance. The authors claim contributions in (1) analyzing the limitations of joint models, (2) introducing a supervised attention-based ED model, and (3) achieving state-of-the-art results on ACE 2005.

The primary contribution, as I see it, is a somewhat incremental improvement to event detection through a reasonably intuitive attention mechanism guided by argument annotations. While the observation that joint models may not fully exploit argument information is not entirely novel, framing it and attempting to address it with supervised attention is a reasonable, if not groundbreaking, approach. The empirical results demonstrate a tangible, though not overwhelming, performance gain.

**Strengths:**

1. **Clear Motivation:** The paper identifies a potential weakness in existing joint models – their limited use of argument information for *improving* event detection specifically, rather than just argument extraction. This is a valid point and justifies the proposed investigation. The initial analysis presented in Table 1, while not entirely conclusive, provides some evidence to support this claim.
2. **Sound Methodology:** The proposed supervised attention mechanism is logically sound. Using annotated arguments to guide attention weights during training is a sensible approach to encourage the model to focus on relevant context. The two strategies for constructing 'gold' attention vectors (S1 and S2) offer a reasonable exploration of different ways to represent argument importance.
3. **Empirical Validation:** The experimental results on ACE 2005 demonstrate consistent improvements over strong baselines, including state-of-the-art methods. While the gains are not massive, they are statistically significant and support the effectiveness of the proposed approach. The ablation study with FrameNet data further strengthens the findings.
4. **Well-Written and Organized:** The paper is generally well-written and clearly organized. The presentation of the method and experimental setup is straightforward and easy to follow.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Novelty:** While the method is sound, the core idea of using attention mechanisms in NLP is far from novel. The specific application to ED with argument-guided attention doesn't represent a significant breakthrough. It feels more like a well-executed application of an established technique to a specific problem.
2. **Insufficient Analysis of Joint Models:** The authors assert that joint models "indirectly" use arguments for ED. However, the analysis is superficial. A deeper investigation into *why* joint models underperform on ED despite having access to argument information is needed. Simply pointing to data imbalance is insufficient; the specific architectural choices within those joint models likely play a significant role.
3. **Dependence on ACE 2005:** The evaluation is almost entirely focused on the ACE 2005 dataset. While this dataset is a standard benchmark, its limited size and specific characteristics might not generalize well to other event detection scenarios. Evaluation on a more diverse set of datasets would strengthen the conclusions.
4. **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** The substantial difference in performance between ANN-S1 and ANN-S2 (and their corresponding results with FrameNet) suggests a significant sensitivity to the `lambda` hyperparameter. The paper lacks a thorough discussion of the hyperparameter tuning process and the robustness of the results to different parameter settings. The choice of λ=5.0 for S2 feels somewhat arbitrary.



**Questions to Authors:**

1. Could you provide a more detailed analysis of the joint models' architectures and training procedures to understand why they struggle to leverage argument information for event detection?
2. How sensitive are the results to different values of the `lambda` hyperparameter? Can you provide a more comprehensive ablation study to demonstrate the robustness of the chosen values?
3. Have you considered evaluating the proposed approach on other event detection datasets, such as those from SemEval or TAC KBP? If so, what were the results? If not, why not?
4. The paper mentions data imbalance as a potential reason for the poor performance of joint models. Have you tried explicitly addressing this imbalance during training (e.g., through weighted loss functions or data augmentation)? If so, what was the impact on performance?



**Additional Comments:**

The reference list is extensive but could benefit from more recent and relevant works on attention mechanisms and event detection. The formatting of the references is inconsistent in places.
