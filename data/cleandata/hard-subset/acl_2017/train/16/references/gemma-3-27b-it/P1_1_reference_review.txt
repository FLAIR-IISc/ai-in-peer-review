## Peer Review: Exploiting Argument Information to Improve Event Detection via Supervised Attention Mechanisms

**Summary:**

This paper tackles the event detection (ED) task, arguing that event arguments, often overlooked in existing approaches, provide valuable clues for identifying and categorizing events, particularly in cases of ambiguous trigger words. The authors propose a neural network-based approach with a supervised attention mechanism that focuses on argument words during event detection. They demonstrate improvements over state-of-the-art methods on the ACE 2005 dataset, and further improve performance by augmenting the training data with events from FrameNet.

**Main Contributions (as I see them):**

1. **Empirical Demonstration of Argument Utility:** The paper convincingly demonstrates, through experimentation, that explicitly incorporating argument information *can* improve event detection performance.  This is a valuable finding, as it challenges the common practice of treating arguments as irrelevant to the ED task.
2. **Supervised Attention Mechanism for Argument Focus:** The proposed supervised attention mechanism, guided by gold argument annotations, provides a practical method for incorporating argument information into a neural network ED model.  The comparison with unsupervised attention highlights the benefit of leveraging labeled argument data.
3. **Strong Empirical Results:** The paper achieves strong performance on the ACE 2005 dataset, establishing a new state-of-the-art result and providing compelling evidence for the effectiveness of the proposed approach.



**Strengths:**

1. **Clear Motivation and Problem Definition:** The paper clearly articulates the problem of ambiguous event triggers and the potential benefits of leveraging argument information, offering a compelling rationale for their approach. The initial example with "fired" is particularly effective.
2. **Thorough Experimental Evaluation:** The authors conduct a comprehensive evaluation, comparing their method against multiple baselines, including both pipeline and joint models. The ablation studies (ANN, ANN-S1, ANN-S2) effectively isolate the impact of the proposed attention mechanism and different argument exploitation strategies.
3. **Data Augmentation with FrameNet:**  The incorporation of FrameNet data demonstrates a thoughtful approach to addressing data sparsity, a known challenge in event detection.  The reported improvements further strengthen the results.
4. **Well-Written and Organized:** The paper is generally well-written, logically organized, and easy to follow. The inclusion of a clear task description and a detailed explanation of the proposed approach facilitates understanding.
5. **Reproducibility:** The authors mention specific datasets, embedding sources and hyperparameter settings, which facilitates reproducibility.


**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Novelty of the Core Method:** While the application to event detection is valuable, the underlying supervised attention mechanism itself is not particularly novel.  Attention mechanisms are widely used in NLP, and the core idea of weighting inputs based on relevance is well-established. The main innovation lies in *where* the attention is applied (to argument words) rather than *how* it’s applied.
2. **Dependence on High-Quality Argument Annotations:** The performance of the supervised attention mechanism is directly tied to the quality of the argument annotations. In real-world scenarios, argument annotations may be noisy or incomplete, potentially limiting the effectiveness of the approach.  The paper doesn’t sufficiently address the robustness of the method to annotation errors.
3. **Modest Gains over Strong Baselines:** While the proposed method achieves state-of-the-art performance, the improvements over existing systems (especially Liu's FN-Based approach) are relatively modest.  This raises the question of whether the complexity of the proposed approach is fully justified by the gains achieved.
4. **Limited Error Analysis:** The paper lacks a detailed error analysis.  Identifying specific types of errors (e.g., misclassification of particular event types, failure to detect arguments) would provide valuable insights into the strengths and weaknesses of the approach and suggest avenues for future research.



**Questions to Authors:**

1. **Annotation Robustness:** How robust is the proposed approach to noisy or incomplete argument annotations? Have you experimented with different levels of annotation noise to assess its impact on performance?
2. **Generalizability:** Do you anticipate that the benefits of exploiting argument information will be consistent across different datasets and event types?  Have you considered evaluating the method on other event detection datasets?
3. **Efficiency:** What is the computational overhead of the supervised attention mechanism compared to a standard attention mechanism or a baseline model without attention?
4. **Error Analysis Details:** Could you provide a more detailed error analysis, outlining the most common types of errors made by the model and suggesting potential solutions?
5. **Comparison with Joint Models:** Given that the paper frames its approach as a response to the limitations of joint ED-AE models, could you provide a more direct comparison with a recent, strong joint model, potentially incorporating argument information into the joint framework?



**Additional Comments:**

*   The citation style is inconsistent (e.g., "Bengio et al.,? 2003" vs. "Ahn.,? 2006").
*   The mention of hyperparameter settings (e.g. λ=1.0) appears late in the experimental section. It could be useful to provide these settings earlier.
*   The hyperlink to the NYT corpus used for word embeddings (in section 3.1.1) is broken.




Overall, this is a solid and well-executed paper that makes a valuable contribution to the field of event detection. While the core method is not groundbreaking, the empirical demonstration of argument utility and the strong experimental results are compelling.  Addressing the weaknesses outlined above, particularly regarding annotation robustness and a more detailed error analysis, would further strengthen the paper.  I recommend acceptance.
