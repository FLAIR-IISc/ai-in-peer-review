This paper tackles event detection (ED) by explicitly leveraging argument information, a component often overlooked in joint event detection and argument extraction models. The core idea – that arguments provide crucial contextual clues for ambiguous event triggers – is intuitively appealing and forms the primary contribution. The authors propose a supervised attention mechanism to focus on argument words during ED, demonstrating performance gains on the ACE2005 dataset. A secondary contribution lies in the empirical analysis revealing the limitations of existing joint models, specifically their tendency to prioritize argument extraction over event detection due to data imbalance.

**Strengths:**

1. **Novelty of Approach:** The explicit incorporation of argument information *specifically* for event detection, rather than as a byproduct of joint learning, is a genuinely novel idea. Existing work largely ignores arguments during the initial trigger identification phase. This shift in perspective is valuable and opens up a new avenue for research.
2. **Strong Empirical Results:** The paper demonstrates consistent and statistically significant improvements over strong baselines, including state-of-the-art methods. The performance gains, while perhaps not massive, are compelling evidence that the proposed approach is effective. The augmentation with FrameNet data further strengthens these results.
3. **Insightful Analysis of Joint Models:** The authors correctly identify a key problem with existing joint models: the data imbalance between triggers and arguments leads to a bias towards argument extraction. This analysis provides a clear rationale for the proposed approach and highlights a weakness in current methodologies.
4. **Clear and Well-Structured Presentation:** The paper is well-written and easy to follow. The methodology is clearly explained, and the experimental setup is sufficiently detailed to allow for replication. The use of figures aids understanding.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Scope of Evaluation:** The evaluation is limited to the ACE2005 dataset. While this is a standard benchmark, it’s crucial to assess the generalizability of the approach to other datasets and languages. The robustness of the method to variations in argument annotation quality also remains unclear.
2. **Supervised Attention – Reliance on Gold Arguments:** While the supervised attention mechanism is effective, it fundamentally relies on the availability of high-quality argument annotations during training. This limits its applicability to scenarios where such annotations are scarce or unavailable. Exploring methods for weakly supervised or unsupervised argument-aware attention would be a valuable extension.
3. **Attention Mechanism Details:** The specifics of the attention mechanism could be more thoroughly investigated. The choice of the attention function and the combination of word and entity attention seem somewhat arbitrary. A more detailed ablation study exploring different attention configurations would be beneficial. The normalization applied to the gold attention vectors (α') is not fully explained and warrants more discussion.
4. **Potential for Bias:** Because the model is explicitly trained to focus on annotated arguments, there's a risk of overfitting to the specific characteristics of the ACE2005 annotation scheme. This could lead to reduced performance on datasets with different annotation guidelines.



**Questions to Authors:**

1.  How sensitive is the performance of your model to the quality of the argument annotations? Have you experimented with noisy or incomplete argument annotations?
2.  Could you provide a more detailed ablation study on the attention mechanism, exploring different attention functions and configurations? Specifically, what impact does removing the entity attention component have on performance?
3.  Do you have any insights into *why* the proposed approach works? Are there specific types of events or trigger words where the argument information is particularly helpful? Can you provide examples of cases where the model correctly identifies an event due to the argument information?
4.  What are the computational costs associated with your approach, particularly the attention mechanism? Is it feasible to scale this approach to very large datasets?

**Additional Comments:**

*   The discussion of related work is adequate but could benefit from a more in-depth comparison with recent advances in contextualized word embeddings (e.g., BERT, RoBERTa) and their potential application to this task.
*   The hyperparameter settings, while provided, could be justified with more detailed explanations.



Overall, this is a promising paper that introduces a novel and effective approach to event detection. The strong empirical results and insightful analysis of existing methods make it a valuable contribution to the field. Despite some limitations, the potential impact of this work is significant, and I recommend acceptance. The novelty of the idea outweighs the current lack of extensive evaluation.
