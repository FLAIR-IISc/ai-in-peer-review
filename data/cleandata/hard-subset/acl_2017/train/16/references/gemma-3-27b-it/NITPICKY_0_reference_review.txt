The paper proposes a supervised attention mechanism to incorporate argument information into event detection (ED). The authors argue that existing joint ED and argument extraction models don't effectively utilize argument information for ED, and instead focus on argument extraction due to data imbalance. They propose a neural network that learns to attend to argument words during ED, training this attention mechanism with “gold” attention vectors derived from annotated arguments.

The primary contribution, as I see it, is the explicit attempt to leverage argument information *during* the ED process via a supervised attention mechanism. While the idea is reasonable, the execution and presentation are significantly flawed.

**Strengths:**

1. **Motivation is plausible:** The authors correctly identify a potential issue with current joint models – a focus on argument extraction due to data imbalance. Explicitly addressing this issue is a valid research direction.
2. **Clear Problem Statement:** The paper clearly defines the event detection task and its relationship to event argument extraction, providing necessary background for readers unfamiliar with the ACE dataset.
3. **Experimental Setup:** The experimental setup, including the use of FrameNet data for augmentation, is fairly standard and allows for reasonable comparisons to existing methods.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Lack of Novelty in Method:** The proposed approach, a supervised attention mechanism, is not particularly novel. Supervised attention has been extensively used in NLP. The application to ED, while potentially beneficial, lacks a groundbreaking methodological innovation. The core idea – “pay attention to argument words” – is intuitive and hardly constitutes a significant advancement.
2. **Implementation Details are Vague and Inconsistent:** The description of the "gold attention" construction (S1 and S2) is confusing. Specifically, the purpose of strategy S1 seems redundant; if arguments are known, why not directly incorporate them into the feature representation instead of forcing attention? Strategy S2's Gaussian distribution approach feels ad-hoc and lacks justification. The hyperparameter tuning for λ is glossed over, and the choice of 0.6 for dropout feels arbitrary. The paper mentions a “Skip-gram model” for word embeddings but doesn’t detail training specifics (corpus size, hyperparameters).  The sentence “The current candidate trigger word w0 is not included in the context.” is perplexing; why not?
3. **Insufficient Ablation Studies:**  The ablation study (ANN, ANN-ENT, ANN-S1, ANN-S2) is insufficient. Simply adding attention mechanisms isn't enough. A critical control experiment is missing: a baseline that *incorporates argument embeddings directly into the input representation* without attention. This would isolate the benefit of the attention mechanism from the benefit of simply providing argument information. Furthermore, the impact of the different gold attention strategies (S1 vs. S2) is minimal and lacks a clear explanation. The reported results are incremental at best, casting doubt on the effectiveness of the proposed attention mechanism.
4. **Presentation & Writing Quality:** The paper suffers from numerous minor stylistic issues. There are instances of awkward phrasing, redundant sentences, and inconsistent terminology. Table 1 is poorly formatted and makes it difficult to extract information quickly. The references are inconsistently formatted.  The repeated mention of “remarkably gains” is hyperbolic and unprofessional.
5. **Statistical Significance:** While a t-test is mentioned, the specific results and significance levels aren't detailed within the table.  It’s crucial to include p-values to support claims of “significant” improvement.

**Questions to Authors:**

1.  Why was a direct incorporation of argument embeddings into the input representation (instead of using attention) not explored as a baseline?
2.  Please provide a more detailed justification for the choice of the Gaussian distribution and its parameters in strategy S2 for constructing gold attention vectors. What sensitivity analysis was conducted on the sigma parameter?
3.  Can you provide detailed hyperparameter tuning results (e.g., the range of λ values explored and their corresponding performance) for both S1 and S2?
4.  Why is the candidate trigger word excluded from the context window?



**Additional Comments:**

*   The formatting of the references is inconsistent and needs to be standardized.
*   Please proofread the paper carefully to address the numerous stylistic issues and awkward phrasing.
*   The authors should be more specific about the computational resources used for training and evaluation.



**Overall Assessment:**

While the motivation behind this work is reasonable, the lack of methodological novelty, insufficient experimental validation, and poor presentation significantly detract from its contribution. The improvements over existing methods are incremental and lack a clear justification for the added complexity of the proposed attention mechanism. Therefore, I recommend **rejection**.  A substantial revision addressing the weaknesses outlined above, including more rigorous experimentation and clearer presentation, would be necessary for reconsideration.
