## Peer Review: Exploiting Argument Information to Improve Event Detection via Supervised Attention Mechanisms

**Summary:**

This paper tackles the event detection (ED) task, arguing that event arguments, often ignored in existing approaches, provide valuable clues for identifying and categorizing events, particularly for ambiguous trigger words. The authors propose a neural network-based approach with a supervised attention mechanism to explicitly leverage argument information during ED. They train this attention mechanism by creating "gold attentions" based on annotated arguments, and demonstrate improvements over state-of-the-art methods on the ACE 2005 dataset, including augmenting the training data with FrameNet events.

**Main Contributions (as I see them):**

1. **Empirical Demonstration of Argument Utility:** The paper provides compelling empirical evidence, through experiments and ablation studies, demonstrating that incorporating argument information *does* improve event detection performance. This is a valuable contribution, as it challenges the common practice of ignoring arguments in many ED systems.
2. **Supervised Attention Framework:**  The proposed supervised attention mechanism offers a practical and relatively straightforward way to integrate argument information into a neural network ED model.  The "gold attention" strategy, while conceptually simple, appears to be effective.
3. **Comprehensive Experimental Evaluation:** The authors present a thorough evaluation, including comparisons with several strong baselines and an ablation study to understand the contribution of different components, as well as data augmentation using FrameNet.

**Strengths:**

1. **Clear Motivation and Problem Definition:** The paper clearly articulates the problem – that existing ED methods underutilize argument information – and provides a convincing rationale for why this information should be beneficial. The example with “fired” is particularly effective.
2. **Strong Empirical Results:** The proposed method consistently outperforms strong baselines, and the gains are statistically significant. The use of both ACE 2005 and FrameNet data further strengthens the results.
3. **Well-Designed Experiments:** The ablation studies (Table 2) are well-executed and provide insights into the effectiveness of different components (ANN, ENT, Gold1, Gold2).  This helps justify the design choices made by the authors.
4. **Reproducibility:** The authors provide sufficient details regarding the dataset, hyperparameter settings, and evaluation metrics to facilitate replication of the work.
5. **Practical Approach:**  The supervised attention mechanism is relatively easy to implement and integrate into existing ED pipelines.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Novelty of the Method:** While effective, the core method (a neural network with attention) isn’t radically new. The primary novelty lies in *how* the attention is supervised, using argument information. The paper would benefit from a more nuanced discussion of related work on attention mechanisms in NLP and a clearer articulation of what distinguishes this approach.
2. **Attention Mechanism Details:** The paper lacks a detailed analysis of *what* the attention mechanism is actually learning. While the results show it improves performance, it's unclear *which* argument words are receiving the most attention, and why. Visualizing attention weights or providing examples would be helpful.
3. **FrameNet Augmentation Details:** The description of how the FrameNet data was processed and integrated is somewhat brief.  More detail on the entity linking process with RPI and how potential inconsistencies were handled would be beneficial.
4. **Generalizability:** The experiments are limited to the ACE 2005 dataset. While this is a common benchmark, it would be valuable to see how well the approach generalizes to other event detection datasets or domains.



**Questions to Authors:**

1. **Attention Visualization:** Can you provide examples of attention weights learned by the model, highlighting which argument words receive the most attention for specific event triggers?
2. **FrameNet Data Quality:**  What steps were taken to ensure the quality of the entity linking performed on the FrameNet data? Were there any manual validation steps to correct errors in entity linking?
3. **Error Analysis:**  Can you perform an error analysis to identify common types of errors made by the model, and whether these errors are related to difficulties in identifying event arguments or trigger words?
4. **Computational Cost:** How does the addition of the argument-aware attention mechanism impact the computational cost (training time and inference time) of the model?
5. **Alternative Attention Strategies:** Did you explore other ways to construct "gold attentions" beyond S1 and S2? For example, using a weighted scheme based on dependency distance?




**Additional Comments:**

* The paper is generally well-written and easy to follow.
* The tables are well-formatted and informative.
* It would be beneficial to include a discussion of the limitations of the approach and potential directions for future research. For example, exploring methods for handling noisy or incomplete argument information.
* Minor: "ACL" should be defined at first use ("Association for Computational Linguistics").



**Overall Assessment:**

This is a solid and valuable contribution to the field of event detection. While the method itself isn’t groundbreaking, the empirical demonstration of argument utility and the practical supervised attention framework are significant. The authors have conducted a thorough evaluation and provided compelling evidence of the effectiveness of their approach.  I recommend acceptance with minor revisions addressing the weaknesses outlined above.
