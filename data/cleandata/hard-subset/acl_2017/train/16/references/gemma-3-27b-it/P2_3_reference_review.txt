## Review for "Exploiting Argument Information to Improve Event Detection via Supervised Attention Mechanisms"

**Summary:**

This paper addresses the event detection (ED) task, arguing that event arguments, often ignored in existing approaches, contain valuable information for improving trigger identification and categorization. The authors propose a neural network model incorporating a supervised attention mechanism that focuses on argument words during event detection. They demonstrate improved performance on the ACE 2005 dataset, both with and without augmenting training data from FrameNet.

**Main Contributions (as I see them):**

1. **Empirical Demonstration of Argument Utility:** The paper provides a convincing empirical analysis showing that explicitly incorporating argument information *can* improve event detection performance, addressing a gap in prior work that primarily focused on joint ED/AE without significant ED gains.
2. **Supervised Attention Framework:**  The introduction of a supervised attention mechanism specifically designed to focus on argument words is a reasonable and well-executed approach to leverage argument information.
3. **Comprehensive Experimental Evaluation:**  The authors conduct a thorough set of experiments, including ablation studies (comparing different attention strategies) and data augmentation (using FrameNet), strengthening the validity of their findings.

**Strengths:**

1. **Clear Motivation and Problem Definition:** The paper clearly articulates the problem – that existing joint models don’t effectively utilize argument information for ED – and provides a compelling rationale for addressing it.  The example with “fired” is particularly effective in illustrating the ambiguity resolution benefit of considering arguments.
2. **Strong Empirical Results:** The experimental results consistently demonstrate performance improvements over strong baselines, including both pipeline and joint methods. The consistent gains across different settings (with and without FrameNet augmentation) lend credibility to the findings. The statistical significance test adds further weight.
3. **Well-Designed Attention Mechanism:** The supervised attention mechanism is logically sound and appropriately implemented. The exploration of different strategies for creating “gold” attention vectors (S1 and S2) is a valuable addition.
4. **Thorough Experimentation:** The inclusion of FrameNet data augmentation and the systematic comparison of different strategies demonstrate a commitment to rigorous evaluation and analysis. The ablation study provides insight into the contribution of the different components of the model.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Novelty of Core Components:** While the *combination* of argument exploitation and supervised attention is a reasonable approach, the individual components (attention mechanisms, neural networks for ED) are not novel. The novelty resides in the specific application to leverage argument information *for* ED. A stronger statement of the paper's contribution would acknowledge this.
2. **Attention Visualization & Analysis:** While the paper demonstrates performance gains from the attention mechanism, it lacks any visualization or more detailed analysis of *what* the model is actually attending to.  Showing examples of the learned attention weights would provide more insight into the model’s behavior and confirm that it's focusing on relevant argument information.
3. **Sensitivity to Hyperparameter λ:** The choice of λ (the weight for the attention loss) seems somewhat arbitrary. The paper could benefit from a more detailed discussion of how this parameter affects performance and potentially a sensitivity analysis.
4. **Limited Error Analysis:**  A more in-depth error analysis would be beneficial. Identifying the types of events or trigger words where the model struggles would provide valuable insights for future work.



**Questions to Authors:**

1. **Attention Visualization:** Could you provide examples of learned attention weights for specific sentences? This would help us understand which words the model is focusing on and validate that it's indeed attending to relevant argument information.
2. **λ Sensitivity:**  Can you elaborate on the process of selecting the value of λ? Did you perform a sensitivity analysis to determine the optimal range for this parameter, and if so, what were the results?
3. **Error Analysis:** Could you provide a more detailed error analysis, identifying the types of events or trigger words where the model performs poorly?
4. **Generalizability:** Do you anticipate the performance gains observed on ACE 2005 to generalize to other datasets with different characteristics (e.g., different event types, argument schemas, or data sizes)?




**Additional Comments:**

* The paper is generally well-written and clearly organized.
* The references are comprehensive.
* The link to the FN data (line 189) is broken.
* The footnote referencing the percentage of triggers that are entities (line 205) is helpful.



**Overall Assessment:**

I recommend **acceptance** with minor revisions. The paper presents a solid contribution to the field of event detection by demonstrating the value of argument information and proposing a reasonable approach for leveraging it.  Addressing the weaknesses outlined above – particularly providing attention visualizations and conducting a more thorough error analysis – would further strengthen the paper. The experimental results are strong and the paper is well-written and clearly presented.
