## Review for "Exploiting Argument Information to Improve Event Detection via Supervised Attention Mechanisms"

**Summary:**

This paper addresses the event detection (ED) task, arguing that event arguments, often ignored in existing approaches, can provide crucial clues for improving performance, particularly in cases of ambiguous triggers. The authors propose a neural network based approach with a supervised attention mechanism, focusing attention on argument words during event detection. Experiments on the ACE2005 dataset demonstrate that their approach outperforms state-of-the-art methods, and benefits from augmentation with data from FrameNet.

**Main Contributions (as I see them):**

1. **Empirical Demonstration of Argument Utility:** The paper provides a solid empirical analysis showing that incorporating argument information, even with a relatively simple attention mechanism, demonstrably improves event detection performance. This is a valuable contribution, as it challenges the prevailing trend of treating ED and AE as largely independent tasks.
2. **Supervised Attention Framework for Argument Incorporation:**  The supervised attention approach provides a clear and conceptually simple way to integrate argument information into a neural network-based ED system. While not groundbreaking in its attention mechanism itself, the application within the context of argument-aware ED is effective.
3. **Thorough Experimental Evaluation:** The authors present a comprehensive experimental setup, including comparisons to several baselines and an ablation study, along with augmentation using FrameNet data, strengthening the validity of their findings.



**Strengths:**

1. **Strong Empirical Results:** The experimental results consistently show improvements over existing state-of-the-art methods on the ACE2005 dataset. The reported gains, while not massive, are statistically significant and demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed approach.
2. **Clear Motivation and Problem Definition:** The paper effectively articulates the problem – the under-utilization of argument information in ED – and provides a convincing rationale for why arguments are useful, especially in resolving trigger ambiguity.  The example with "fired" is particularly illustrative.
3. **Well-Structured and Readable:** The paper is clearly written and well-organized, making it easy to understand the proposed approach and the experimental setup. The inclusion of diagrams (e.g., Figure 1) is helpful.
4. **Comprehensive Baseline Comparison:**  The comparison against a range of relevant baseline systems (both joint and pipeline) lends credibility to the claims of improved performance.  Including both feature-based and embedding-based baselines is a good practice.
5. **Effective Use of FrameNet Augmentation:**  The inclusion of FrameNet data to address data sparsity further strengthens the work and shows a practical approach to improving performance in a low-resource setting.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Novelty in Core Technique:** The supervised attention mechanism itself is not novel.  While its application to incorporating argument information is effective, the core technical innovation is relatively incremental.  The paper would benefit from more deeply contextualizing the work within the broader landscape of attention mechanisms and ED.
2. **Simplified Argument Representation:** The paper primarily treats arguments as binary indicators (present/absent) within the attention mechanism. It doesn’t explore more nuanced representations of argument roles or the semantic relationships between arguments and the trigger, which might yield further improvements.
3. **ACE2005 Dataset Limitation:**  The exclusive reliance on the ACE2005 dataset, while standard for this task, limits the generalizability of the findings. Testing the approach on other ED datasets (if available) would be beneficial.  The ACE2005 dataset is also relatively small, potentially hindering the demonstration of more significant gains.
4. **Discussion of Error Analysis is Lacking:** While the results are positive, the paper lacks detailed error analysis. Understanding *where* the proposed approach still fails would provide valuable insights and direction for future research.  



**Questions to Authors:**

1.  Could you elaborate on why you chose to use a simple additive attention mechanism (Equation 2 & 3) instead of exploring more sophisticated attention variants (e.g., self-attention, transformer-based attention)?  Was this a deliberate design choice to maintain simplicity, or were there specific computational or performance considerations?
2.  The paper mentions that only 10% of triggers in ACE2005 are entities.  Did you investigate whether explicitly masking or down-weighting entity-based trigger candidates during the attention calculation could further improve performance?
3.  You mention that arguments can be helpful to disambiguate trigger words. Can you provide some specific examples from the ACE2005 dataset where the argument information was particularly crucial for correctly identifying the event type? (e.g., a case where the model would have misclassified the event without the argument information).
4.  What is the computational overhead of incorporating the supervised attention mechanism? How does it compare to the baseline ANN model in terms of training and inference time?

**Additional Comments:**

*   The formatting of equations could be improved for better readability.
*   Consider adding a discussion of related work on incorporating external knowledge (beyond FrameNet) into event detection.



**Overall Assessment:**

This is a solid and well-executed paper that makes a valuable contribution to the field of event detection. While the core technical innovation is incremental, the empirical demonstration of the utility of argument information and the effective integration of this information via supervised attention are noteworthy. I recommend **acceptance** with minor revisions to address the weaknesses outlined above.
