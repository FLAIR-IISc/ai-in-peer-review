This paper tackles the event detection (ED) task, arguing that event arguments, often overlooked in existing methods, provide valuable clues for identifying and categorizing events. The authors propose a supervised attention mechanism to explicitly leverage argument information, constructing "gold attentions" based on annotated arguments to train the model.  Experiments on the ACE 2005 dataset demonstrate improvements over state-of-the-art approaches, and further gains are achieved by augmenting the data with events from FrameNet.

The primary contribution of this work is the explicit incorporation of argument information into an attention-based neural network for event detection. While the idea of utilizing arguments isn’t entirely novel (joint models exist, albeit with limited ED gains), the focused, supervised attention mechanism applied *specifically* to enhance ED performance is a worthwhile contribution. A second contribution lies in the investigation of different strategies for constructing “gold attentions” from annotated arguments. The comparison between these strategies (S1 and S2) offers insights into how best to leverage argument information for this task. Finally, the comprehensive experimental evaluation, including augmentation with FrameNet data, strengthens the validity of the findings.

**Strengths:**

1. **Clear Motivation and Problem Statement:** The paper clearly articulates the limitations of existing event detection methods that ignore argument information and persuasively argues for the benefits of explicitly utilizing it. The example provided with “fired” immediately illustrates the ambiguity that arguments can resolve.
2. **Novel Attention Mechanism:** The proposed supervised attention mechanism, trained with gold attention vectors derived from arguments, is a well-motivated and potentially effective approach.  The idea of using annotated arguments as a supervisory signal for attention weights is insightful.
3. **Strong Empirical Results:** The experimental results on ACE 2005 demonstrate that the proposed approach outperforms several state-of-the-art methods. The gains are substantial enough to suggest a meaningful contribution to the field. The inclusion of FrameNet augmentation further enhances the results and demonstrates the model’s adaptability.
4. **Thorough Evaluation:** The experiments are well-designed and include comparisons with both pipeline and joint methods, as well as ablation studies with different attention strategies. This provides a comprehensive assessment of the proposed approach.
5. **Well-Written and Organized:** The paper is generally well-written and easy to follow. The architecture is clearly explained with a helpful figure, and the experimental setup is detailed enough for reproducibility.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Novelty of Joint Modeling Concept:** While the *implementation* of argument utilization via attention is novel, the core idea of leveraging both event triggers and arguments isn't entirely new, as acknowledged by the authors with a discussion of joint models.  The paper doesn’t sufficiently differentiate itself from previous joint learning approaches beyond the attention mechanism. A more detailed comparison of *how* the proposed approach differs in its utilization of argument information compared to existing joint methods would be beneficial.
2. **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** The optimal value of λ (the weight for the attention loss) differs between strategies S1 and S2 (1.0 vs. 5.0). This suggests sensitivity to hyperparameters and raises questions about the robustness of the model. A more in-depth analysis of hyperparameter tuning and its impact on performance would be valuable.
3. **FrameNet Augmentation Details:** The description of how FrameNet data was processed and integrated is somewhat brief. More details about the entity annotation process (using the RPI system) and potential noise introduced by this step would strengthen the evaluation. The impact of different levels of FrameNet data augmentation could also be explored.
4. **Error Analysis:** While the paper presents performance numbers, it lacks a detailed error analysis. Understanding *what kinds* of events the model still struggles with and *why* would provide valuable insights for future research.  For example, are there specific event types where argument information is less helpful?
5. **Lack of Computational Complexity Analysis**: The paper doesn’t provide any discussion of the computational cost associated with the proposed approach. Given the use of attention mechanisms and the integration of argument information, it’s important to consider whether the method is scalable to larger datasets or more complex models.



**Questions to Authors:**

1. Can you provide a more detailed comparison of how the proposed approach utilizes argument information differently from existing joint event extraction models? Specifically, how does the attention mechanism enable a more effective use of arguments for ED compared to global inference in joint models?
2. What is the rationale for the different optimal values of λ for strategies S1 and S2?  Did you explore a wider range of λ values, and if so, what were the results?
3. Could you provide more details on the entity annotation process using the RPI system for the FrameNet data? What is the precision and recall of this entity annotation?
4.  Have you performed any error analysis to understand the types of errors the model makes? Are there specific event types where the model consistently struggles?
5. What is the computational complexity of the proposed approach, and how does it scale with the size of the input sentence and the number of arguments?

**Additional Comments:**

*  The citation style appears inconsistent (e.g., sometimes including page numbers, sometimes not).
* In the introduction, consider providing a clearer definition of “multivocal” words and their relevance to event detection.



Overall, this is a solid paper with a clear contribution to the field of event detection. While the novelty of the core idea could be strengthened with a more thorough comparison to existing joint models, the proposed attention mechanism and the comprehensive experimental evaluation make this a worthwhile contribution. Addressing the weaknesses and answering the questions above would further enhance the paper.  I recommend acceptance with minor revisions.
