This paper presents a novel approach to event detection (ED) by explicitly incorporating argument information via a supervised attention mechanism. The core idea – that event arguments provide crucial clues for disambiguating event triggers – is insightful and addresses a notable gap in existing literature, where joint ED and argument extraction models often show limited improvement in ED performance. The paper convincingly argues that previous joint approaches haven't truly *utilized* argument information for ED, but rather indirectly through a combined loss function and pre-prediction stages.

**Main Contributions:**

1.  **A Novel Framing of Argument Utility for ED:** The central contribution lies in recognizing that argument information is underutilized in existing joint models and proposing a direct approach to leverage it for improved ED. This reframing is a significant shift in perspective.
2.  **Supervised Attention Mechanism:** The proposed supervised attention mechanism, trained with gold argument labels, is a reasonable and well-motivated technique to focus the model's attention on relevant contextual information.
3.  **Empirical Validation:** The paper demonstrates improvements over strong baselines on the ACE 2005 dataset, establishing the effectiveness of the proposed method. Augmenting with FrameNet data further strengthens the results.

**Strengths:**

1.  **Bold and Insightful Idea:** The paper tackles a fundamental issue in event extraction - the disconnect between argument and trigger information – with a genuinely novel approach. While the methodology is sound, the core idea is what stands out.
2.  **Clear Motivation & Justification:** The authors provide a compelling argument, backed by table 1, for why joint methods haven’t significantly improved ED. The analysis of unbalanced data and pre-prediction limitations is well-articulated.
3.  **Solid Experimental Evaluation:** The experimental setup is comprehensive, including comparisons with strong baselines and ablation studies (via different attention strategies and FrameNet augmentation). The results consistently demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed approach.
4.  **Well-Written and Organized:** The paper is clearly written and easy to follow. The architecture is explained well, and the experimental details are provided sufficiently.

**Weaknesses:**

1.  **Limited Error Analysis:** While the paper presents strong overall results, a deeper error analysis would be valuable. Understanding *where* the model still struggles – specific event types or sentence structures – would provide insights for future work.
2.  **Attention Mechanism Detail:** The construction of "gold attentions" (especially S2) feels somewhat ad-hoc. While it performs well, a stronger theoretical justification or exploration of alternative attention weighting schemes would be beneficial. The hyperparameter tuning for λ could be better explained/motivated.
3.  **Generalizability:** The experiments are limited to the ACE 2005 dataset. Demonstrating performance on other event extraction datasets would strengthen the claim of generalizability.
4.  **Joint vs. Pipeline comparison context:** The pipeline systems used for comparison are somewhat dated. More recent and sophisticated pipeline approaches would provide a more challenging benchmark.

**Questions to Authors:**

1.  Can you provide a more detailed error analysis of your system? Specifically, what types of events are most often misclassified, and what are common error patterns?
2.  Could you elaborate on the rationale behind the Gaussian distribution used in strategy S2 for constructing gold attentions? What other weighting schemes were considered and why were they discarded?
3.  Have you explored other methods for addressing the data imbalance issue in joint models, beyond the supervised attention mechanism?
4.  What is the computational overhead of the attention mechanism compared to a baseline ED model without attention?
