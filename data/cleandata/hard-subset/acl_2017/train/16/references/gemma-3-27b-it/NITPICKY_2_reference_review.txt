The paper proposes a supervised attention mechanism to incorporate argument information into event detection (ED). The authors argue that existing joint ED and argument extraction (AE) models inadequately utilize argument information for ED, and propose a model where attention weights are learned based on annotated arguments. They present experimental results on ACE 2005, demonstrating improvements over baseline and state-of-the-art methods.

The primary contribution of this work is the exploration of supervised attention as a means of directly leveraging argument information for event detection. While the idea isn't entirely novel – attention mechanisms are prevalent – its specific application to guide attention *towards* arguments within the context of ED is a reasonable direction. A secondary contribution is the empirical demonstration, on ACE 2005, that incorporating argument information via supervised attention can indeed boost ED performance. Finally, the augmentation with FrameNet data provides a small, but noteworthy, addition to the experimental setup.

**Strengths:**

1. **Clear Motivation:** The paper clearly articulates the limitations of existing joint models regarding the utilization of argument information for ED. The example provided with "fired" is persuasive and effectively illustrates the potential benefit of considering arguments.
2. **Solid Experimental Results:** The reported results on ACE 2005 are encouraging. The proposed approach consistently outperforms several baselines and state-of-the-art methods. The inclusion of FrameNet augmentation further strengthens the evaluation.
3. **Well-Defined Approach:** The proposed architecture is clearly described, and the use of supervised attention with gold attention vectors is a logical approach to achieving the stated goal. The two strategies (S1 and S2) for constructing gold attentions are thoughtful and allow for a nuanced comparison.
4. **Comprehensive Comparison:** The paper compares against a reasonable set of baselines, including both pipeline and joint approaches. This provides a good benchmark for assessing the effectiveness of the proposed method.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Lack of Novelty in Core Methodology:** While the application of supervised attention to argument-aware ED is a sensible idea, the core methodology (ANNs, attention mechanisms) lacks significant novelty. The paper doesn’t introduce fundamentally new architectural components or learning algorithms. The innovation lies more in *how* these existing techniques are combined than in the techniques themselves.
2. **Over-reliance on ACE 2005:** The evaluation is almost entirely limited to the ACE 2005 dataset. This raises concerns about the generalizability of the findings. Event detection performance can vary significantly across datasets, and the ACE 2005 dataset is relatively small and may not fully represent the complexity of real-world event detection tasks.
3. **Insufficient Ablation Study:** Although two attention strategies are proposed, the ablation study is limited. It would be beneficial to investigate the individual contributions of the word embedding table and entity type embedding table to better understand their impact on performance. Furthermore, a more detailed analysis of the learned attention weights could provide valuable insights into the model's behavior.
4. **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** The paper mentions hyperparameter tuning, but the specific settings for key parameters (e.g., λ) are not thoroughly justified. A sensitivity analysis demonstrating the impact of these parameters on performance would strengthen the results.
5. **Formatting & Presentation:** Several minor formatting inconsistencies exist. For example, inconsistent use of italics, missing spaces around punctuation, and occasionally awkward phrasing. The table captions are sometimes unclear. These details detract from the overall professionalism of the paper. Specifically, Table 1 has inconsistent formatting. The labels “Methods” appear twice.



**Questions to Authors:**

1. Could you elaborate on the computational complexity of the proposed approach compared to the baseline methods?
2. How sensitive is the performance of the model to the quality of the annotated arguments in the training data? Would noisy or incomplete argument annotations significantly degrade performance?
3.  Can you provide a visualization of the learned attention weights for a few representative examples? This would help to understand which words/entities the model is focusing on.
4. Why was the window size fixed to 3 in strategy S2? Was this parameter tuned, and if so, what was the impact of different window sizes?



**Additional Comments:**

*   The reference list is somewhat incomplete and lacks proper formatting.
*   The abbreviation "ED" is used extensively, but not always consistently defined.
*   The explanation of “core” elements in FrameNet is brief and assumes a level of familiarity with FrameNet that may not be present in all reviewers.
*   The use of "remarkably gains" in the introduction is overly enthusiastic and should be toned down.
