This paper proposes a supervised attention mechanism to incorporate argument information into event detection (ED). The authors argue that existing joint ED and argument extraction models fail to effectively utilize argument information for ED, and their proposed approach addresses this by explicitly focusing on arguments during the attention learning process. They present experiments on the ACE 2005 dataset, demonstrating improvements over baseline and state-of-the-art methods.

The primary contribution of this work is the *idea* of explicitly modeling argument information through supervised attention for event detection, a departure from existing joint models that implicitly leverage arguments. A secondary contribution is the empirical demonstration of this approach on a standard benchmark (ACE 2005), showing performance gains. A tertiary, though less significant, contribution is the exploration of two strategies (S1 and S2) for constructing “gold” attention vectors based on annotated arguments.

**Strengths:**

1. **Identified a valid problem:** The authors correctly point out that joint models often prioritize argument extraction over event detection due to imbalanced data and the way arguments are used during inference. This observation is insightful and justifies the need for a more focused approach.
2. **Clear and well-structured presentation:** The paper is generally well-written and easy to follow. The motivation is clearly articulated, and the proposed approach is presented in a logical manner with a helpful diagram (Figure 1).
3. **Empirical results are promising:** The reported improvements on ACE 2005 are encouraging, and the ablation study (Table 3) supports the claim that incorporating argument information via supervised attention is beneficial. The augmentation with FrameNet data further reinforces the effectiveness of the approach.
4. **Thorough experimental setup:** The paper includes detailed descriptions of the experimental setup, hyperparameter settings, and comparison with relevant baselines, making the results reproducible.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Lack of novelty in the method itself:** The core technical contribution—using supervised attention—is not particularly novel. Supervised attention has been extensively used in NLP, and its application to event detection, while sensible, doesn't represent a significant methodological breakthrough. The attention mechanism itself is quite standard.
2. **Overstated contributions regarding “explicit” argument usage:** While the authors claim their method *explicitly* uses argument information, the approach still relies on the *existence* of annotated arguments during training. This is not fundamentally different from how joint models utilize argument annotations; the difference lies in *how* that information is incorporated. The phrasing suggests a more substantial departure from previous work than actually exists.
3. **Insufficient analysis of attention weights:** The paper lacks a detailed analysis of the learned attention weights. While the authors show that using argument information improves performance, they don’t provide compelling evidence that the attention mechanism is actually focusing on the *relevant* parts of the sentence. Visualizing attention weights for specific examples would be highly valuable.
4. **Minor inconsistencies in notation and presentation:** Equation 4 & 5 use 'α' which is previously defined as a single attention vector, to denote a matrix. The text lacks rigor in defining what 'Cw' and 'Ce' are - are they indices, matrices of embedding vectors? Throughout the paper a consistent formalism should be maintained.
5. **Table formatting errors:** Table 1 has some weird spacing and hyphenation issues. Specifically, there’s a hyphen dangling mid-sentence in the table description.

**Questions to Authors:**

1. Can you provide visualizations of the learned attention weights for several example sentences, illustrating which words the model is attending to when predicting an event type? This would provide stronger evidence that the attention mechanism is effectively leveraging argument information.
2. The paper claims “arguments are actually used for AE, not for ED in existing joint methods.” Is this claim supported by a thorough analysis of attention weights in those methods? Or is this merely an assumption?
3. Could you elaborate on the rationale behind choosing a Gaussian distribution (S2) for weighting the words around arguments? What other distribution functions were considered and why was the Gaussian distribution preferred?
4. Please address the inconsistencies in notation, as highlighted in the weaknesses section.



**Additional Comments:**

*  The reference list is formatted inconsistently.
* The choice of λ=5.0 for ANN-S2 seems arbitrary. What was the basis for this selection?
* The authors should consider reporting confidence intervals for their results.
