- Summary: Proposes supervised attention trained with annotated arguments to explicitly incorporate argument information for event detection and reports SOTA on ACE 2005 with FrameNet augmentation.

- Contribution: Empirical analysis showing existing joint ED/AE models provide limited direct benefit to ED despite AE gains.

- Contribution: Introduces supervised attention using gold argument supervision to directly leverage argument information for ED.

- Contribution: Demonstrates competitive, statistically significant improvements on ACE 2005, enhanced by FrameNet augmentation.

- Strength: Clear motivation and illustrative example showing argument info can resolve event ambiguity.

- Strength: Thorough experimental setup with strong baselines, ablations, and FrameNet augmentation.

- Strength: Solid empirical results with statistically significant gains supporting the approach.

- Strength: Detailed and clearly explained methodology (context representation, attention, training).

- Strength: Insightful analysis explaining why joint models may fail to improve ED (data imbalance, indirect argument use).

- Weakness: Attention mechanism itself is not novel; main novelty is supervising attention with argument labels.

- Weakness: Evaluation limited to ACE 2005, leaving generalizability to other datasets untested.

- Weakness: Sensitivity to hyperparameter λ (different optimal values for settings) suggests tuning issues.

- Weakness: Lacks detailed error analysis identifying event types or patterns where the model fails.

- Question: Please clarify the novelty beyond supervised attention weighting and differences from standard attention mechanisms.

- Question: Have you evaluated on datasets beyond ACE 2005, or what challenges do you foresee in generalizing?

- Question: Provide details of hyperparameter tuning (search space and selection criteria) used in experiments.

- Question: Provide a detailed error analysis identifying event types and argument types that help or hinder ED.

- Question: What is the added computational cost of the supervised attention compared to a baseline ED model?

- Additional comment: Writing is clear and well-organized.

- Additional comment: Related work could be expanded to cover more recent advances in event extraction and attention mechanisms.

- Overall assessment: Solid, convincing paper with useful contributions; recommend acceptance with minor revisions addressing weaknesses and questions.
