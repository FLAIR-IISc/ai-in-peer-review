## Peer Review: Exploiting Argument Information to Improve Event Detection via Supervised Attention Mechanisms

**Summary:**

This paper tackles the event detection (ED) task, arguing that event arguments, often ignored in existing methods, can provide valuable clues for identifying and categorizing events, particularly resolving ambiguity in trigger words. The authors identify a weakness in joint ED and argument extraction (AE) models – they tend to favor AE due to data imbalance and indirect use of arguments for ED.  To address this, they propose a supervised attention mechanism that explicitly incorporates argument information into a neural network-based ED model, training the attention based on gold-standard arguments. Experiments on ACE 2005, with and without FrameNet data augmentation, demonstrate improved performance compared to state-of-the-art methods.

**Main Contributions (as I see them):**

1. **Empirical Analysis of Joint Models:** The paper provides a compelling analysis highlighting the limitations of existing joint ED and AE models, specifically their tendency to prioritize argument extraction and fail to effectively leverage argument information for improving event detection performance.  This observation is valuable for the community.
2. **Supervised Attention Mechanism for ED:** The proposed supervised attention mechanism offers a practical approach to directly incorporating argument information into the ED process. The use of “gold” attention derived from annotated arguments is a reasonable and potentially effective technique.
3. **Strong Empirical Results:**  The reported performance gains on the ACE 2005 dataset, achieving state-of-the-art results, provide solid evidence supporting the effectiveness of the proposed approach.



**Strengths:**

1. **Clear Motivation & Problem Definition:** The paper clearly articulates the problem – the underutilization of argument information in event detection – and provides a convincing rationale for addressing it.  The example with "fired" effectively illustrates the benefit of considering arguments.
2. **Well-Defined Methodology:** The proposed model is described in a clear and organized manner, with a helpful diagram (Figure 1) illustrating the architecture.  The two strategies for constructing gold attention vectors (S1 and S2) offer different approaches to leverage argument information, allowing for a nuanced evaluation.
3. **Comprehensive Experimental Evaluation:** The authors conduct a thorough set of experiments, comparing their approach to both baseline systems and state-of-the-art methods.  The inclusion of FrameNet data augmentation and ablation studies strengthens the validity of the results.  The t-test provides statistical significance to the findings.
4. **Significant Performance Improvement:** Achieving state-of-the-art performance on ACE 2005 is a significant accomplishment. The reported gains in precision, recall and F1-score demonstrate the effectiveness of the approach.
5. **Reproducibility:** The paper provides sufficient detail regarding the experimental setup, hyperparameter tuning, and dataset preparation which increases the potential for reproducibility.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Novelty of the Attention Mechanism:** While effective, the supervised attention mechanism itself isn't fundamentally novel. Attention mechanisms are widely used in NLP, and applying it to ED with argument information is a natural extension. The core contribution lies more in *how* the attention is supervised and the empirical demonstration of its benefit in the specific context of ED.
2. **Dependency on Annotation Quality:** The effectiveness of the proposed approach heavily relies on the quality of argument annotations in the training data. Errors or inconsistencies in the annotations could negatively impact the learned attention mechanism and overall performance. This is not explored in the paper.
3. **Lack of Error Analysis:** The paper lacks a detailed error analysis.  Understanding *where* the model still struggles (e.g., specific event types, ambiguous triggers, complex sentence structures) would provide valuable insights and guide future research.
4. **Limited Generalizability Discussion:** While ACE 2005 is a standard benchmark, the discussion of the approach's generalizability to other datasets or domains is limited. The performance improvements observed may not translate directly to other event detection tasks with different characteristics.



**Questions to Authors:**

1. **Annotation Sensitivity:**  How sensitive is the performance of your model to the quality of argument annotations? Have you explored any methods for mitigating the impact of noisy or incomplete annotations?
2. **Error Analysis:** Could you provide a more detailed error analysis, identifying common types of errors made by the model and discussing potential reasons for these errors?
3. **Generalizability:**  How do you anticipate the performance of your model on other event detection datasets (e.g., those with different event types or domain characteristics)? Have you considered any techniques for improving the model's generalizability?
4. **Computational Cost:** What is the computational overhead introduced by the supervised attention mechanism compared to a standard event detection model? Is this overhead significant for large-scale applications?
5.  **Ablation Study Details:** Could you provide a more detailed breakdown of the results for the different attention strategies (S1 and S2), including the specific values of λ used and the impact on precision, recall, and F1-score for each strategy?




**Additional Comments:**

*  The references are formatted consistently, but a few are slightly incomplete (e.g., missing page numbers).
*  Consider adding a section discussing the limitations of the proposed approach and outlining potential avenues for future research.
*  The discussion of related work could be more comprehensive, particularly regarding recent advances in attention mechanisms and event detection.
