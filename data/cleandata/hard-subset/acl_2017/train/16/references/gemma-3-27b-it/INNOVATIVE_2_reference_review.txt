This paper proposes a supervised attention mechanism to incorporate argument information into event detection (ED). The authors motivate their approach by observing that existing joint ED and argument extraction (AE) models don't significantly improve ED performance, and hypothesize this is due to data imbalance and ineffective argument utilization. They propose explicitly attending to argument words during ED, training the attention mechanism with gold argument labels.

The primary contribution is a conceptually simple yet potentially impactful idea: directly leveraging argument information via supervised attention for event detection. While attention mechanisms are commonplace, applying them *specifically* to argument words as a means to disambiguate event triggers is a novel direction. The experimental results, showing improvements over strong baselines, provide preliminary evidence for this idea. A secondary contribution lies in the analysis of why joint ED/AE models struggle to improve ED, pinpointing data imbalance and superficial argument use.

**Strengths:**

1. **Novelty of the Approach:** The core idea of supervised attention focused on argument words is genuinely novel within the event detection landscape. Most work focuses on improved feature engineering or more sophisticated joint models, but this direct attention approach feels fresh and promising.
2. **Clear Motivation and Problem Definition:** The paper clearly articulates the problem – existing joint models aren't effectively using argument information for ED – and provides a compelling rationale for why their approach might address this. The example with "fired" is particularly effective in illustrating the potential benefit.
3. **Strong Empirical Results:** The experimental results on ACE 2005 demonstrate a clear performance improvement over strong baselines, including recent state-of-the-art methods. The gains, while not massive, are statistically significant and support the effectiveness of the proposed approach.
4. **Thorough Experimental Setup:** The inclusion of FrameNet data augmentation and a comparison against several recent approaches strengthens the evaluation. The authors demonstrate a good understanding of the existing literature.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Analysis of Attention Weights:** The paper demonstrates *that* the attention mechanism improves performance, but offers little insight into *how*. Visualizing and analyzing the learned attention weights would be extremely valuable.  Are the attention weights consistently focused on relevant arguments? Are there failure cases where the attention mechanism misfires? Without this analysis, it's difficult to fully understand the effectiveness of the approach.
2. **Supervision Reliance & Generalizability:** The supervised attention relies heavily on accurate argument annotations.  While ACE 2005 is a benchmark, the performance is tied to the quality of those annotations.  How would this approach perform on datasets with noisier or less reliable argument labels? Could the attention mechanism be adapted for semi-supervised or unsupervised learning?
3. **Modest Gains:** While statistically significant, the performance gains aren’t enormous. The authors do not deeply explore error analysis to reveal *where* the gains are most prominent. A clearer understanding of the types of events or trigger words where the approach excels would be beneficial.
4. **Lack of Ablation Study:** A more detailed ablation study would be helpful to determine the relative contribution of different components of the model. For instance, what is the impact of using entity type embeddings versus just word embeddings in the attention mechanism?

**Questions to Authors:**

1. Can you provide visualizations of the learned attention weights for several example sentences, highlighting which words are receiving the most attention?
2. How sensitive is the performance of the model to errors in argument annotation? Have you experimented with artificially noisy argument labels?
3. What types of event triggers benefit the most from the inclusion of argument information via attention? Can you provide a more detailed error analysis?
4. Can you discuss the computational cost of adding the attention mechanism, and how it scales with sentence length?
