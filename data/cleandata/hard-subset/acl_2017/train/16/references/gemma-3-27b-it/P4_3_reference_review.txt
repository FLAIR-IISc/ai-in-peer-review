This paper proposes a supervised attention mechanism to improve event detection (ED) by explicitly leveraging argument information. The authors argue that existing joint ED and argument extraction models don't effectively use argument information for ED, and that a model focused on explicitly attending to arguments can improve performance. They present a neural network approach with a supervised attention layer trained on gold argument annotations and demonstrate improvements on the ACE 2005 dataset, achieving state-of-the-art results.

The main contributions of this work, in order of strength, are: (1) The identification of a deficiency in existing joint ED/AE models – namely, that argument information is not effectively leveraged for improving ED performance; (2) The proposed supervised attention mechanism that explicitly focuses on argument words during event detection; and (3) The demonstration of competitive performance on the ACE 2005 dataset, achieving state-of-the-art results through the proposed approach and augmentation with FrameNet data.

**Strengths:**

1. **Insightful Problem Identification:** The core argument that joint models don’t effectively utilize argument information for ED is a strong point. The authors convincingly explain why this might be the case – data imbalance and the way joint models typically pre-predict triggers and arguments – and position their work as addressing this gap. This justifies the proposed approach and provides valuable context.
2. **Clear and Well-Motivated Method:** The proposed supervised attention mechanism is conceptually straightforward and well-motivated by the central argument. The process of constructing "gold attentions" is clearly explained, with two different strategies offered. The use of supervised learning for the attention mechanism is a reasonable and logical approach.
3. **Strong Empirical Results:** The experimental results on ACE 2005 demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed approach. Achieving state-of-the-art performance, and consistently outperforming strong baselines, provides strong evidence supporting the claims made in the paper. The ablation study (comparing different attention strategies) further strengthens these results.
4. **Comprehensive Experiments:** The authors demonstrate that augmenting the model with data from FrameNet improves performance. This demonstrates the robustness of the method and adds further credibility to the results.
5. **Detailed Explanation of Implementation:** The paper provides sufficient details about the experimental setup, hyperparameter tuning, and dataset processing allowing for reproducibility.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Novelty of the Attention Mechanism:** While the application of supervised attention to leverage argument information is a good idea, the attention mechanism itself is not novel. It's a standard attention mechanism applied in a specific way. The core novelty resides in the *application* to argument information, rather than the attention mechanism itself.
2. **Dependence on Gold Argument Annotations:** The supervised nature of the attention mechanism relies entirely on accurate gold argument annotations. While ACE 2005 is a standard dataset, annotation errors are possible, and the model's performance is thus constrained by the quality of the annotations. This limits the potential for application in scenarios where gold argument annotations are unavailable.
3. **Limited Analysis of Attention Weights:** The paper would benefit from a more in-depth analysis of the learned attention weights. Visualizing or analyzing which argument words receive the most attention for different event types could provide further insight into the model's behavior and confirm that it is indeed learning to attend to relevant arguments.
4. **Data Sparseness Considerations:** While the authors address data sparseness by augmenting with FrameNet data, the discussion could be expanded. ACE 2005 is a relatively small dataset. More analysis of how different event types are affected by data sparsity, and how the attention mechanism mitigates this, would be beneficial.



**Questions to Authors:**

1. Could you provide examples of attention weights learned by the model for different event types and sentences? Specifically, can you showcase instances where the model successfully attends to the most relevant argument words?
2. How sensitive is the performance of the model to errors in the gold argument annotations? Have you considered performing an analysis with noisy argument annotations to evaluate robustness?
3.  The paper mentions a potential issue with data imbalance in joint models. Have you experimented with re-weighting the loss function to address this imbalance directly, and if so, how did that compare to the proposed approach?
4.  The FrameNet augmentation is a positive result. Could you explore the influence of the FrameNet data quality and coverage on the final performance? Is there a saturation point where adding more FN data doesn’t yield further improvement?

**Additional Comments:**

*   The citation style is inconsistent (e.g., some references have pages listed, others do not).
*   The introduction is a bit repetitive, especially when defining event extraction and its components. Consider streamlining this section.
*   The discussion of related work could be expanded to include more recent approaches to event detection.
