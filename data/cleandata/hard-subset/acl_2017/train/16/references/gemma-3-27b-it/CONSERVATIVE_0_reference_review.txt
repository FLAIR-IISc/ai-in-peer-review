This paper proposes a supervised attention mechanism to incorporate argument information into event detection (ED). The authors motivate their work by observing that joint ED and argument extraction (AE) models haven't significantly improved ED performance, attributing this to data imbalance and the way arguments are currently utilized (indirectly, through AE). They argue that explicitly focusing on arguments during ED can improve performance, and demonstrate this with a neural network model trained with gold attention weights derived from annotated arguments.

**Contributions (as I see them):**

1.  **Empirical Observation & Analysis:** The paper highlights a valid and interesting observation: existing joint ED/AE models do not demonstrably improve ED performance despite improvements in AE. The analysis attributing this to data imbalance and indirect argument use is plausible, and worth investigating.
2.  **Straightforward Application of Attention:** The proposed supervised attention mechanism is a relatively simple and understandable way to incorporate argument information. While not a fundamentally new architectural component, its application to this specific problem is a reasonable approach.
3.  **Demonstrated Improvement:** The experiments on ACE 2005 demonstrate a performance improvement over established baselines, indicating the approach has some merit.

**Strengths:**

1.  **Clear Motivation & Problem Definition:** The authors clearly articulate the limitations of existing joint models and motivate the need for a more direct approach to utilizing argument information for ED.
2.  **Sound Experimental Setup:** The experiments are conducted on a standard benchmark dataset (ACE 2005) and compared against several relevant baselines, including both pipeline and joint approaches. The inclusion of FrameNet data augmentation is also a good practice.
3.  **Rigorous Ablation Study:** The experimentation with different attention strategies (S1 and S2) and the ablation study (ANN, ANN-S1, ANN-S2) provide insights into the effectiveness of the proposed approach and the contribution of each component.
4.  **Significant Performance Gain:** The reported performance improvements, while not massive, are statistically significant and demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed approach compared to state-of-the-art methods.

**Weaknesses:**

1.  **Lack of Novelty in Core Methodology:** The core technical contribution – using supervised attention – is not novel in itself. Attention mechanisms are widely used in NLP. The novelty lies in *how* it’s applied, but the application feels incremental rather than groundbreaking.
2.  **Limited Generalizability:** The experiments are limited to the ACE 2005 dataset. It's unclear whether the observed improvements would generalize to other datasets or domains. The authors do not address this limitation adequately. Testing on more diverse datasets would be crucial.
3.  **Over-reliance on Gold Attentions:** The model relies heavily on gold attention weights derived from annotated arguments. This raises concerns about whether the model is truly learning to identify relevant argument information or simply memorizing the provided supervision. A more robust evaluation would involve evaluating the model's ability to generalize to unseen examples where gold attentions are not available.
4.  **Insufficient Error Analysis:** The paper lacks a thorough error analysis. Understanding *where* the model still struggles would provide valuable insights for future research. For example, are there specific event types or sentence structures that are particularly challenging?




**Questions to Authors:**

1.  How sensitive is the performance of your model to the quality of the annotated arguments? Are there any instances where incorrect or ambiguous argument annotations negatively impact the model's performance?
2.  Have you investigated the use of alternative methods for generating attention weights, such as reinforcement learning or self-attention, instead of relying solely on gold attentions?
3.  Could you provide a more detailed analysis of the types of errors made by your model? Specifically, what are the most common misclassifications, and what factors contribute to these errors?
4.  How would you expect your model to perform on datasets with different characteristics than ACE 2005 (e.g., different event types, different sentence lengths, different levels of annotation quality)?




**Additional Comments:**

*   The writing is generally clear and concise, but some sections could benefit from further clarification (e.g., the explanation of the attention mechanisms).
*   The reference list appears complete.
*   The ablation study is well-designed and provides valuable insights.



**Overall Assessment:**

This paper presents a reasonable and well-executed approach to incorporating argument information into event detection. The empirical results are promising, but the lack of novelty in the core methodology and the limited generalizability raise concerns. I believe the paper is currently *borderline acceptable*. Addressing the weaknesses identified above, particularly by expanding the evaluation to more diverse datasets and providing a more thorough error analysis, would significantly strengthen the submission.
