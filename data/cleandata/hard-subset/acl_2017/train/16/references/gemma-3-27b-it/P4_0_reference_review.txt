This paper proposes a supervised attention mechanism to improve event detection (ED) by explicitly leveraging argument information. The authors argue that existing joint models for event extraction (ED and argument extraction) do not effectively utilize argument information for ED, and instead favor argument extraction due to data imbalance. They present a neural network model where attention weights are learned under the supervision of annotated arguments, guiding the model to focus on relevant context for trigger classification.

The primary contribution of this work lies in framing argument information as a direct supervisory signal for improving event detection, and implementing this idea through a supervised attention mechanism. While attention mechanisms are common in NLP, the specific application to ED guided by argument annotations is a worthwhile contribution. A second contribution is the empirical demonstration on the ACE 2005 dataset that incorporating argument information *via* this supervised attention approach yields performance improvements over state-of-the-art methods. Finally, the ablation studies and investigation of different strategies for constructing "gold" attention vectors (S1 and S2) provide insights into the effectiveness of the approach.

**Strengths:**

1. **Clear Motivation & Problem Definition:** The paper clearly articulates the limitations of existing joint models in effectively utilizing argument information for ED, and convincingly motivates the need for a dedicated approach. The observation that joint models often prioritize argument extraction due to data imbalance is insightful.
2. **Novelty of Approach:** The supervised attention mechanism applied to ED, guided by argument annotations, is a novel and reasonable approach. It moves beyond simply considering arguments as features and instead uses them to directly shape the attention mechanism.
3. **Strong Empirical Results:** The experimental results on the ACE 2005 dataset demonstrate that the proposed approach outperforms several state-of-the-art methods, including both pipeline and joint models. The gains are significant enough to warrant attention.
4. **Thorough Evaluation:** The authors conduct a series of experiments, including ablation studies and comparisons with different attention strategies (S1 and S2), demonstrating a good understanding of the model's behavior and the impact of different design choices.
5. **Augmentation with FrameNet:** Exploring the use of FrameNet data to alleviate data sparsity is a valuable addition and demonstrates the practical applicability of the proposed approach.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Analysis of Attention Weights:** While the authors demonstrate performance improvements, they lack a detailed qualitative analysis of the learned attention weights. Visualizing and interpreting the attention maps could provide further insights into *how* the model utilizes argument information and whether the learned attention aligns with linguistic intuition.  
2. **Dependence on Annotation Quality:** The performance of the supervised attention mechanism heavily relies on the accuracy of the argument annotations. The paper doesn’t address the potential impact of noisy or inconsistent argument annotations on the model's performance.
3. **Generalizability to Other Datasets:** The evaluation is limited to the ACE 2005 dataset. It's unclear whether the observed improvements would generalize to other event extraction datasets with different characteristics (e.g., different event types, annotation schemes).
4. **Comparison to Stronger Baselines:** While comparisons to several state-of-the-art systems are made, some newer and potentially stronger baselines (e.g., models based on transformers like BERT or RoBERTa fine-tuned for event detection) are not included. This makes it difficult to assess the true competitive advantage of the proposed approach.

**Questions to Authors:**

1. Can you provide examples of attention weights learned by the model, illustrating how it focuses on argument words and their surrounding context?
2. How robust is the proposed approach to noisy or incomplete argument annotations? Have you conducted any experiments to assess its performance under such conditions?
3. Have you experimented with different strategies for constructing the "gold" attention vectors (S1 and S2), and are there any other promising approaches you explored but didn't include in the paper due to space constraints?
4. How does the performance of the proposed approach compare to more recent models based on transformers (e.g., BERT, RoBERTa) fine-tuned for event detection on the ACE 2005 dataset?



**Additional Comments:**

*   The paper is well-written and easy to follow.
*   The related work section is comprehensive and provides a good overview of the relevant literature.
*   The inclusion of the experimental setup details and hyperparameter settings is helpful for reproducibility.
* Consider adding a discussion of the limitations of the work and potential directions for future research in the conclusion.
