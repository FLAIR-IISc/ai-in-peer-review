This paper proposes a supervised attention mechanism to incorporate argument information into event detection, arguing that existing joint models inadequately utilize this information. The core idea – explicitly focusing on arguments to disambiguate event triggers – is a compelling and, to my knowledge, novel direction. The experimental results demonstrate improvements over strong baselines, suggesting the approach has merit.

**Main Contributions (as I see them):**

1. **Highlighting the Underutilized Potential of Argument Information in Event Detection:** The paper convincingly argues that arguments are valuable clues often overlooked by current event detection systems, particularly joint models. This reframing of the problem is the strongest contribution.
2. **Introducing a Supervised Attention Mechanism for Argument Incorporation:** The proposed architecture, while not groundbreaking in its components (attention, ANNs), offers a specific and practical way to leverage argument information, trained with gold attention labels.
3. **Empirical Validation on ACE 2005:** The results on a standard benchmark demonstrate the effectiveness of the approach, providing support for the core hypothesis.

**Strengths:**

1. **Novelty of the Core Idea:** The explicit focus on utilizing argument information *during* event detection, rather than relying on it indirectly through joint learning, is a genuinely fresh perspective. Most previous work focuses on better argument extraction within a joint framework, but this paper targets improving *detection* by looking at arguments.
2. **Clear Motivation and Explanation:** The paper clearly articulates the limitations of existing joint models and provides a strong rationale for why arguments are often ignored in the event detection phase. The example with "fired" is particularly illustrative.
3. **Solid Experimental Setup:** The authors use a standard benchmark, compare against strong baselines, and perform ablation studies. The inclusion of FrameNet data as augmentation is also a good addition.
4. **Strong Performance Gains:** Achieving state-of-the-art results on ACE 2005 is a significant accomplishment, further solidifying the value of the proposed approach.
5. **Well-Written and Organized:** The paper is generally well-structured and easy to follow.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Analysis of Attention Weights:** While the paper demonstrates that the model *can* learn to attend to arguments, it lacks a detailed analysis of *which* arguments are most influential and *why*. Visualizing and interpreting the learned attention weights could provide valuable insights.
2. **Reliance on Gold Arguments:** The supervised attention mechanism relies heavily on accurate argument annotations during training. The paper doesn’t sufficiently address how the approach might perform with noisy or incomplete argument information, a realistic scenario in many applications. It would be beneficial to explore techniques for learning attention with less-than-perfect supervision.
3. **Modest Gains in Some Experiments:** While overall performance is strong, the gains from FrameNet augmentation are relatively small. Further investigation into why this is the case (e.g., domain mismatch) would be helpful.
4. **Lack of Error Analysis:** A detailed error analysis would help identify the types of events or situations where the model still struggles, providing direction for future research. For instance, are there specific trigger words where argument information is less helpful?

**Questions to Authors:**

1. Can you provide examples of learned attention weights that highlight the model’s ability to focus on relevant arguments?
2. How sensitive is the performance of your model to the quality of the argument annotations? Have you experimented with noisy argument labels or strategies for learning attention with imperfect supervision?
3. Could you discuss the limitations of your approach, particularly in scenarios where argument information is sparse or unreliable?
4. What are the potential computational costs associated with using argument information, especially for long sentences with many potential arguments?



**Additional Comments:** The reference formatting is a bit inconsistent. Also, the concluding sentence feels slightly repetitive. Despite these minor issues, this is a very promising paper with a novel and well-supported idea. I lean towards acceptance.
