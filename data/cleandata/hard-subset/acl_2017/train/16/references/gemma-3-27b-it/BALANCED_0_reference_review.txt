This paper addresses the event detection (ED) task, arguing that incorporating argument information can improve performance, particularly for ambiguous triggers. The authors identify a weakness in existing joint event detection and argument extraction (EE/AE) models – that they often benefit AE more than ED, potentially due to data imbalance and the way argument information is utilized. They propose a supervised attention mechanism to explicitly leverage argument information for ED, training the attention based on annotated arguments.  The primary contributions are: (1) identifying the limitations of existing joint models in effectively utilizing argument information for ED, (2) proposing a supervised attention mechanism to directly incorporate argument information into ED, and (3) achieving state-of-the-art results on the ACE 2005 dataset, demonstrating the effectiveness of the proposed approach.

**Strengths:**

1. **Clear Problem Identification and Motivation:** The paper clearly articulates the problem of limited benefit of joint EE/AE models for ED, backed by a concise review of existing work (Table 1). The authors convincingly argue that current joint models indirectly utilize argument information, primarily for AE, and that a direct approach focusing on ED could be more effective. This is a strong starting point and provides a solid rationale for the proposed work.

2. **Novelty of the Supervised Attention Mechanism:**  The core idea of using supervised attention based on annotated arguments is a novel approach to leveraging argument information for ED. The explicit design to focus attention on argument-related words during training is a worthwhile contribution. The exploration of different gold attention construction strategies (S1 and S2) adds further nuance.

3. **Strong Empirical Results:** The experimental results demonstrate that the proposed method achieves state-of-the-art performance on the ACE 2005 dataset. The ablation studies (comparing ANN, ANN-S1, and ANN-S2) provide insights into the contributions of the different components of the proposed model, especially the supervised attention mechanism. The augmentation with FrameNet data further strengthens the results.

4. **Well-Structured and Clear Presentation:** The paper is well-organized and clearly written. The architecture diagram (Figure 1) effectively illustrates the proposed approach. The experimental setup and hyperparameter settings are adequately described, allowing for reproducibility.



**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Analysis of Attention Weights:** While the paper introduces a supervised attention mechanism, there's a lack of detailed analysis of the learned attention weights. Visualizing attention weights on specific examples (especially ambiguous cases like the one presented in the introduction) would help demonstrate *how* the model is utilizing argument information and validate the intuition behind the approach. Understanding what the model is attending to would build further confidence in the method.

2. **Sensitivity to Hyperparameter λ:** The optimal value of the hyperparameter λ, controlling the trade-off between the event detection loss and the attention loss, appears to differ between strategies S1 and S2 (λ=1.0 for S1 and λ=5.0 for S2).  A more thorough analysis of the sensitivity of the model’s performance to this parameter, and a discussion of why different values are optimal for different strategies, would be beneficial.

3. **Over-reliance on ACE 2005:** While achieving SOTA on ACE 2005 is commendable, the evaluation is limited to a single dataset. Event detection datasets vary considerably in their characteristics.  Evaluating the method on other event detection datasets (even if with limited scope) would help assess its generalizability and robustness.  This is particularly important given the domain-specificity that often exists in event detection.

4. **Missing Error Analysis:** A more detailed error analysis would be insightful.  What types of events does the model still struggle with?  What are common error patterns? Understanding the limitations of the model would inform future research directions.




**Questions to Authors:**

1. Could you provide examples of learned attention weights for ambiguous trigger words, demonstrating how the argument information influences the model's prediction?

2. What is the rationale behind the different optimal values of λ for strategies S1 and S2?  Is there a theoretical basis for this difference?

3.  What are the computational costs associated with the proposed approach, particularly the supervised attention mechanism, compared to baseline methods?

**Additional Comments:**

*   The phrase "supervised attention" is used frequently. While understandable, it might be useful to briefly define or clarify what constitutes "supervision" in this context, i.e., the gold argument annotations.
*   The connection to FrameNet is briefly mentioned. Expanding on how FrameNet data can complement the argument information, and potentially improve performance on specific event types, could be valuable.
*   Consider adding a discussion of potential limitations related to the quality of argument annotations in the training data.



**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper that addresses a relevant and important problem in event extraction. The proposed supervised attention mechanism is a novel and effective approach to leveraging argument information for event detection. The experimental results are compelling, and the paper is well-written. Despite some minor weaknesses regarding analysis and generalization, the contributions are significant and warrant acceptance. I recommend **acceptance** with minor revisions to address the points raised in the weaknesses section.
