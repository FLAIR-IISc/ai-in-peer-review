## Review for "Exploiting Argument Information to Improve Event Detection via Supervised Attention Mechanisms"

**Summary:**

This paper addresses the event detection (ED) task within event extraction, arguing that existing methods largely ignore valuable information contained in event arguments. The authors propose a neural network-based approach that explicitly incorporates argument information through a supervised attention mechanism. The core idea is to train an attention model to focus on argument words during event detection, using annotated arguments to create "gold" attention weights. Experiments on the ACE 2005 dataset, with and without augmentation from FrameNet, demonstrate that the proposed method outperforms state-of-the-art approaches.

**Main Contributions (as I see them):**

1. **Empirical Demonstration of Argument Utility:** The paper convincingly demonstrates through experiments that incorporating argument information *can* improve event detection performance. While the intuition is reasonable, the empirical validation is a key contribution, especially given the prevailing trend of ignoring arguments in joint ED/AE models.
2. **Supervised Attention Framework for Argument Integration:**  The proposed supervised attention mechanism provides a simple yet effective way to leverage argument information.  The use of “gold” attention weights derived from annotated arguments offers a clear and practical training signal.
3. **Comprehensive Experimental Evaluation:** The authors provide a fairly thorough experimental evaluation, including comparisons with several baselines and a data augmentation strategy. This strengthens the credibility of the findings.

**Strengths:**

1. **Clear Motivation and Problem Definition:** The paper clearly articulates the problem – the underutilization of argument information in event detection – and motivates the proposed approach effectively. The example provided with "fired" is particularly insightful.
2. **Solid Experimental Results:** The proposed method consistently outperforms strong baselines on the ACE 2005 dataset, and the gains are statistically significant. The ablation studies (e.g., comparing S1 and S2 attention strategies) provide valuable insights into the effectiveness of different approaches.
3. **Well-Structured and Readable:** The paper is well-organized, clearly written, and easy to follow. The figures and tables are informative and well-presented.
4. **Practical Implementation Details:** Providing details like hyperparameter settings and the data preprocessing steps increases the reproducibility of the results.
5. **Addressing Previous Work Limitations:** The authors effectively critique existing joint ED/AE methods, highlighting why they haven't yielded substantial improvements in ED.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Novelty of Core Method:** While the application to event detection is valuable, the core idea of using supervised attention is not entirely novel. Supervised attention mechanisms have been widely used in various NLP tasks, including machine translation and question answering. The specific contribution lies in applying this technique to explicitly incorporate argument information for ED.  However, the novelty of this application needs to be more clearly positioned within the broader context of attention mechanisms in NLP.
2. **Dependence on Annotation Quality:** The approach relies heavily on the quality of argument annotations. Errors in argument labeling will directly impact the accuracy of the "gold" attention weights and, consequently, the performance of the model. The paper doesn’t sufficiently discuss the potential impact of annotation errors or explore methods to mitigate them (e.g., using noisy labels or self-training).
3. **ACE 2005 Dataset Limitations:** ACE 2005 is a relatively old dataset, and its event typology may not fully reflect the complexities of real-world events. While it serves as a valuable benchmark, the generalizability of the findings to more modern and diverse datasets remains uncertain.  Evaluation on other datasets would strengthen the conclusions.
4. **Lack of Error Analysis:** The paper lacks a detailed error analysis.  Understanding *where* the model fails (e.g., specific event types, ambiguous triggers) would provide valuable insights for future improvement.



**Questions to Authors:**

1.  **Annotation Robustness:**  How sensitive is the performance of your model to errors in argument annotation? Have you experimented with introducing noise into the argument annotations to assess the model’s robustness?
2.  **Generalizability:** Have you considered evaluating your approach on other event detection datasets, such as those from SemEval or more recent datasets based on social media or news articles?  How might the performance differ on these datasets?
3. **Attention Visualization:** Can you provide examples of the learned attention weights?  Visualizing the attention patterns would help to better understand how the model is leveraging argument information.
4. **Complexity Analysis:** What is the computational complexity of the proposed method compared to the baselines?  Does the addition of the attention mechanism significantly increase training or inference time?




**Additional Comments:**

*   The discussion of related work could be expanded to include more recent advances in attention mechanisms and their applications in NLP.
*   Consider discussing the potential benefits of incorporating argument *role* information into the attention mechanism, beyond simply identifying the argument mentions themselves.



**Overall Assessment:**

This is a solid and well-executed paper that makes a valuable contribution to the field of event extraction. The authors have convincingly demonstrated that argument information can be effectively leveraged to improve event detection performance. While the core method is not groundbreaking, the application to this specific task and the thorough experimental evaluation are commendable. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions addressing the weaknesses outlined above.
