## Review: Exploiting Argument Information to Improve Event Detection via Supervised Attention Mechanisms

**Summary:**

This paper tackles the event detection (ED) task, arguing that event arguments, often ignored in existing methods, provide valuable clues for identifying event triggers, especially ambiguous ones. The authors propose a neural network approach with a supervised attention mechanism, focusing the model’s attention on argument words during training. They demonstrate improved performance on the ACE 2005 dataset, outperforming state-of-the-art methods.

**Main Contributions (as I see them):**

1. **Empirical Demonstration of Argument Utility for ED:** The paper convincingly demonstrates, through experimentation, that explicitly leveraging argument information *does* improve event detection performance. While intuitively plausible, this wasn’t a given, especially given previous work showing limited gains from joint ED/AE models. This is the strongest contribution.
2. **Supervised Attention Framework for Argument Incorporation:**  The proposed supervised attention mechanism provides a practical and effective way to incorporate argument information into an ED model.  The use of gold argument annotations for attention supervision is a reasonable and well-motivated approach.
3. **Comprehensive Experimental Evaluation:** The paper includes thorough experiments on a standard benchmark dataset (ACE 2005), comparison with multiple baselines (including state-of-the-art methods), and exploration of data augmentation with FrameNet.

**Strengths:**

1. **Strong Empirical Results:** The reported performance improvements over existing state-of-the-art methods on the ACE 2005 dataset are substantial and statistically significant.  The results provide strong evidence for the effectiveness of the proposed approach.
2. **Clear Motivation and Problem Definition:** The paper clearly articulates the problem of underutilizing argument information in event detection, providing a compelling motivation for their work. The example provided with “fired” is particularly helpful in illustrating the issue.
3. **Well-Structured and Readable:** The paper is well-written, logically organized, and easy to follow.  The architecture diagram (Figure 1) is clear and informative.
4. **Thorough Experimental Setup:** The authors clearly describe the experimental setup, hyperparameter tuning, and baseline methods, which allows for reproducibility and facilitates comparison with other approaches.
5. **Investigation of Attention Strategies:** Comparing different strategies for creating gold attentions (S1 and S2) adds depth to the analysis and provides insight into the optimal ways to leverage argument information.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Novelty of Core Techniques:** The core components – neural networks, attention mechanisms, and supervised learning – are not novel in themselves.  The novelty lies in the *application* of supervised attention specifically to incorporate argument information for ED. While effective, the technique isn’t a fundamentally new architectural innovation.
2. **Dependence on Accurate Argument Annotations:**  The performance of the system heavily relies on the quality of argument annotations in the training data.  Errors or inconsistencies in these annotations could negatively impact the learned attention weights and overall performance. The authors do not extensively discuss the potential impact of annotation errors.
3. **ACE 2005 as a Limited Testbed:** While ACE 2005 is a standard benchmark, it is relatively small and may not fully represent the complexity of real-world event detection scenarios.  Evaluating the approach on larger and more diverse datasets would further strengthen the claims.
4. **Lack of Ablation Study on Attention:** While strategies S1 and S2 are compared, a more detailed ablation study specifically focused on the attention mechanism itself (e.g., removing attention entirely, using different attention functions) could provide further insight into its contribution.



**Questions to Authors:**

1. **Annotation Error Analysis:**  Could you provide a discussion of potential annotation errors in the ACE 2005 dataset and how they might affect the performance of your system? Did you perform any error analysis on the attention weights to identify cases where the model attends to incorrect or irrelevant words?
2. **Generalizability to Other Datasets:** Have you experimented with your approach on other event detection datasets besides ACE 2005? If so, what were the results? If not, what challenges do you anticipate in adapting the model to different datasets?
3. **Attention Visualization:** Could you provide examples of learned attention weights, visually highlighting the words that the model attends to for different event triggers? This would provide valuable qualitative insight into how the model is utilizing argument information.
4. **Computational Cost:** What is the computational overhead of the supervised attention mechanism compared to a standard ED model without attention?




**Additional Comments:**

*   The references are generally well-cited, though a few appear to be incomplete (e.g., Ahn,?).
*   The paper would benefit from a brief discussion of potential applications of the approach beyond event detection, such as improving event argument extraction or relation extraction.



**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper that presents a well-motivated and effective approach to improving event detection by explicitly incorporating argument information. While the core techniques are not entirely novel, the empirical results are compelling, and the supervised attention framework is a practical and valuable contribution.  I recommend acceptance with minor revisions addressing the weaknesses and questions raised above.
