- Paper addresses event detection by leveraging annotated event arguments via a supervised attention mechanism and shows improvements on ACE 2005 with FrameNet augmentation.
- Main contribution: empirical demonstration that argument information improves event detection performance.
- Main contribution: a supervised attention framework that trains attention using "gold attentions" derived from arguments.
- Main contribution: thorough experimental evaluation including strong baselines, ablations, and data augmentation with FrameNet.
- Strength: clear motivation and illustrative examples (e.g., ambiguous triggers like “fired”).
- Strength: strong, statistically significant empirical gains on benchmarks.
- Strength: well-designed ablation studies that clarify component contributions (ANN, ENT, Gold1, Gold2).
- Strength: sufficient detail for reproducibility (datasets, hyperparameters, metrics).
- Strength: practical, easy-to-implement approach suitable for integration into ED pipelines.
- Weakness: limited methodological novelty beyond supervised attention application to arguments.
- Weakness: lacks analysis/visualization of learned attention weights and which argument words are prioritized.
- Weakness: insufficient detail on how FrameNet data was processed and entity linking (RPI) inconsistencies handled.
- Weakness: experiments confined to ACE 2005, raising questions about cross-dataset/domain generalizability.
- Questions for authors: provide attention visualizations and examples of attended argument words.
- Questions for authors: describe steps taken to ensure FrameNet entity-linking quality and any manual validation.
- Questions for authors: provide error analysis distinguishing errors due to argument vs. trigger identification.
- Questions for authors: report computational cost impact of the argument-aware attention (training/inference).
- Questions for authors: whether alternative schemes for constructing gold attentions (e.g., distance-weighted) were explored.
- Additional comments: paper is well-written, tables clear, suggest discussing limitations and handling noisy/incomplete arguments; minor fix: define "ACL" at first use.
- Overall assessment: solid, valuable contribution recommending acceptance with minor revisions.
