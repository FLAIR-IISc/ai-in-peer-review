This paper proposes a supervised attention mechanism to improve event detection (ED) by explicitly leveraging argument information. The authors argue that existing joint ED and argument extraction models indirectly utilize arguments and that focusing on argument information directly can boost ED performance. They construct gold attention vectors based on annotated arguments and train an event detector with this supervision. Experimental results on ACE 2005 demonstrate improvements over state-of-the-art methods, and the authors further show gains by augmenting the training data with events from FrameNet.

**Contributions:**

1. **Highlighting the Underutilization of Argument Information in Joint Models:** The paper effectively points out a crucial observation: while joint models for event extraction *include* argument information, they don’t necessarily *utilize* it effectively for the task of event detection. This framing of the problem is a significant contribution as it motivates the proposed approach.
2. **Supervised Attention Mechanism for ED:** The introduction of a supervised attention mechanism, specifically designed to focus on argument words during event detection, is the core technical contribution. Utilizing gold attention vectors derived from annotated arguments is a sensible approach to guide the model's learning.
3. **Empirical Validation and Data Augmentation:** The authors provide a comprehensive experimental evaluation on ACE 2005, demonstrating the effectiveness of their approach. The positive results with FrameNet data augmentation further strengthen their findings.

**Strengths:**

1. **Clear Motivation and Problem Definition:** The paper clearly articulates the limitations of existing joint models and provides a strong rationale for focusing on explicit argument utilization for ED. The illustrative example with "fired" is particularly effective.
2. **Sound Technical Approach:** The proposed supervised attention mechanism is well-motivated and implemented. The use of gold attention vectors provides a strong supervisory signal for learning relevant attention weights. The consideration of two strategies (S1 and S2) for constructing gold attention vectors demonstrates thoughtful experimentation.
3. **Strong Experimental Results:** The paper demonstrates consistent performance improvements over strong baselines on a standard benchmark dataset. The statistical significance test further supports the validity of the results.
4. **Comprehensive Experiments:** The inclusion of experiments with FrameNet data augmentation adds robustness to the findings and demonstrates the generalizability of the approach.
5. **Well-Written and Organized:** The paper is generally well-written and clearly organized, making it easy to follow the proposed approach and understand the experimental results.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Novelty of the Attention Mechanism:** While effective, the supervised attention mechanism itself isn’t inherently novel. Supervised attention has been widely used in other NLP tasks (e.g., machine translation, image captioning). The novelty lies in its *application* to event detection with argument-specific supervision, but the core mechanism itself lacks substantial innovation.
2. **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** The difference in performance between ANN-S1 and ANN-S2, and the need to tune different lambda values for each, suggests potential sensitivity to hyperparameters. A more thorough analysis of hyperparameter impact would strengthen the results.
3. **Limited Analysis of Attention Weights:** The paper would benefit from a more detailed analysis of the learned attention weights. Visualizing attention distributions for different event types and trigger words would provide insights into what the model is learning and further validate the effectiveness of the approach.
4. **Lack of Error Analysis:** A detailed error analysis would help identify the types of errors the model still makes and suggest potential directions for future work. For instance, are there specific event types or trigger words that the model consistently struggles with?

**Questions to Authors:**

1. Could you provide a more detailed analysis of the learned attention weights? Specifically, can you visualize attention distributions for different event types and example sentences?
2. How sensitive is the performance of your model to the choice of the hyperparameter λ? Did you explore a wider range of values?
3. Can you discuss the computational complexity of your approach compared to the baselines?
4. What types of errors does the model still make? A brief error analysis would be valuable.
