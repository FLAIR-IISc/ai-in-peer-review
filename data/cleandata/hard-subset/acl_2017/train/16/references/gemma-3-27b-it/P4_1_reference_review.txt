This paper proposes a supervised attention-based neural network approach to event detection (ED), arguing that leveraging event argument information can improve performance, particularly for ambiguous trigger words. The authors observe that joint ED and argument extraction (AE) models haven’t significantly improved ED performance, attributing this to data imbalance (more arguments than triggers) and the way arguments are used – primarily for AE rather than directly benefiting ED. Their approach explicitly focuses on argument information during ED via a supervised attention mechanism, training the model to attend to argument words.

The primary contribution of this work is the introduction of a supervised attention mechanism for ED that explicitly incorporates argument information. While attention mechanisms are commonly used, applying them in this context – specifically, using annotated arguments to *supervise* attention for trigger classification – is a sensible and potentially impactful idea. A secondary contribution lies in the empirical demonstration on the ACE 2005 dataset, showing performance gains over baseline and state-of-the-art methods. The exploration of different strategies (S1 & S2) for constructing "gold" attention vectors represents a further, though less substantial, contribution.

**Strengths:**

1. **Clear Motivation and Problem Definition:** The paper clearly articulates the problem – the limited improvement of ED in joint models – and provides a convincing argument for why explicitly incorporating argument information might help. The example with "fired" is well-chosen and effectively illustrates the potential benefit.
2. **Sound Technical Approach:** The proposed supervised attention mechanism is logically sound and well-integrated into a neural network architecture. The use of gold attention vectors as supervision is a reasonable strategy for guiding the learning process.
3. **Comprehensive Experiments:** The experiments are thorough, with comparisons to strong baselines (including state-of-the-art methods) and ablation studies to evaluate the effectiveness of different components (S1 vs. S2, FrameNet augmentation). The use of a development set for hyperparameter tuning and a separate test set for final evaluation is good practice.
4. **Strong Empirical Results:** The results demonstrate a clear improvement over existing methods, providing evidence to support the authors’ hypothesis. The statistical significance test further strengthens the findings.
5. **Well-written and Organized:** The paper is generally well-written and easy to follow. The architecture is clearly described with a helpful figure.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Novelty of the Attention Mechanism:** While the *application* of supervised attention to ED with argument information is novel, the attention mechanism itself is not. It's a standard attention mechanism adapted to the task. The paper would benefit from more discussion of how this approach differs from, and improves upon, other attention-based models for sequence labeling or similar tasks.
2. **Gold Attention Construction Strategies:** The S1 and S2 strategies for constructing gold attention vectors seem somewhat arbitrary. The justification for the Gaussian distribution in S2 is weak, and the performance difference between the two strategies isn’t substantial. A more thorough exploration of different gold attention construction methods or a more theoretically grounded justification for the chosen strategies would be beneficial.
3. **Lack of Error Analysis:** The paper lacks a detailed error analysis. Understanding *where* the model still fails could provide valuable insights for future work. For example, are there specific event types or sentence structures that are particularly challenging?
4. **FrameNet Augmentation Detail:** While the augmentation with FrameNet data is mentioned, the details of processing the data (handling differing annotation schemes, ensuring data quality) are glossed over. More transparency here would be appreciated.



**Questions to Authors:**

1.  Could you elaborate on the novelty of your attention mechanism compared to existing attention mechanisms used in sequence labeling or other NLP tasks? Specifically, how does your application address potential limitations of standard attention in this context?
2.  What motivated the choice of a Gaussian distribution in the S2 gold attention construction strategy? Were other distributions considered, and if so, why were they rejected?
3.  Can you provide a more detailed error analysis, including examples of cases where the model fails and potential reasons for those failures?
4.  Can you provide more detail on the processing steps taken when augmenting the training data with FrameNet events, particularly regarding the alignment of annotation schemes and quality control measures?

**Additional Comments:**

*   The paper references a Github repository for the FrameNet data. Providing a direct link in the main text would be helpful.
*   The discussion of related work could be expanded to include more recent attention-based models for sequence labeling.
