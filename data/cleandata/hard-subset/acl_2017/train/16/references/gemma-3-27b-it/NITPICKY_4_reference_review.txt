This paper proposes a neural network approach to event detection (ED) that explicitly leverages argument information via a supervised attention mechanism. The authors argue that existing joint ED and argument extraction (AE) models don't effectively utilize arguments for ED, and propose training an attention mechanism to focus on argument words when identifying event triggers. They present experimental results on ACE 2005, demonstrating improved performance compared to baselines and state-of-the-art methods.

The primary contribution, as *I* see it, is the empirical demonstration that explicitly incorporating argument information, even with a relatively simple attention mechanism, can improve event detection performance. While the idea isn't revolutionary, the observation that joint models haven't fully exploited arguments for ED is insightful, and the results support this claim. A secondary contribution is the thorough experimental evaluation on ACE 2005, including ablation studies and augmentation with FrameNet data.

**Strengths:**

1. **Empirical Results:** The paper demonstrates consistent and statistically significant improvements over strong baselines on the ACE 2005 dataset. The improvement, while not massive, is notable and suggests the proposed approach is effective. The use of FrameNet augmentation provides further evidence of the method’s robustness.
2. **Clear Motivation and Problem Statement:** The authors clearly articulate the problem of underutilized argument information in joint ED-AE models. The example provided with “fired” effectively illustrates how argument information can disambiguate event types.
3. **Thorough Experimental Setup:** The experimental setup is comprehensive, including comparisons to several state-of-the-art methods and ablation studies (ANN-S1 vs. ANN-S2) that help understand the impact of different attention strategies.
4. **Well-Structured Presentation:** The paper is generally well-written and organized. The figures are clear and effectively illustrate the proposed model architecture.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Lack of Novelty in Method:** The proposed approach, a supervised attention mechanism, is a relatively standard technique in NLP. The novelty lies primarily in the *application* of this technique to explicitly leverage argument information for ED, but the core method itself isn’t groundbreaking. The authors don't adequately address how their attention mechanism differs substantially from existing attention models applied to sequence labeling tasks.
2. **Attention Mechanism Details:** The implementation details of the attention mechanism are somewhat vague.  Equation 2 and 3 define the attention weights, but lack explanation regarding the choice of the hyperbolic tangent activation function *f(.)*. Why this function over ReLU or others?  The impact of the transformation matrix Ww is not fully explored. Furthermore, the combination of αw and αe (α = αw + αe) is a simplistic summation.  More sophisticated combination strategies (e.g., concatenation, weighted averaging) weren't explored.
3. **Limited Error Analysis:** While the paper presents quantitative results, it lacks a detailed qualitative error analysis. Examining specific examples where the model fails would provide valuable insights into its limitations and potential areas for improvement. Why does the model still misclassify events?  Are there particular types of events or argument structures that are problematic?
4. **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** The paper states hyperparameters were tuned on the development set, but the sensitivity of the model to different hyperparameter settings (e.g., λ, embedding dimension, learning rate) is not discussed. A sensitivity analysis would strengthen the robustness of the findings.
5. **Formatting and Typos:** Numerous minor formatting inconsistencies exist throughout the paper. For instance, inconsistent use of bolding, spacing around equations, and minor grammatical errors (e.g., "exiting" instead of "existing" in the introduction). While individually minor, these issues detract from the overall polish of the paper. Specifically, the reference list is poorly formatted.

**Questions to Authors:**

1.  Can you elaborate on the rationale behind choosing the hyperbolic tangent activation function for the hidden representation calculation (Equation 1)?
2.  Have you explored more sophisticated methods for combining the word and entity attention vectors (αw and αe) beyond simple summation? If so, what were the results?
3.  Can you provide a detailed qualitative error analysis, including examples of misclassified events and a discussion of the common error patterns?
4.  How sensitive is the performance of the model to different values of the hyperparameter λ? Please provide a sensitivity analysis.
5.  Please provide a corrected reference list.



**Additional Comments:**

* The paper frequently uses "ANN" to refer to a specific model architecture. It would be clearer to use a more descriptive name.
* The phrase “remarkable gains” is used frequently and feels overstated given the magnitude of the improvements.
* The figure captions are too brief and could provide more context.



Overall, this paper presents a sound empirical investigation of the benefits of explicitly incorporating argument information into event detection. However, the lack of methodological novelty and the presence of numerous minor flaws prevent a strong recommendation for acceptance. I recommend rejection, but encourage the authors to address the weaknesses outlined above and resubmit to a future conference.
