## Review for "Exploiting Argument Information to Improve Event Detection via Supervised Attention Mechanisms"

**Summary:** This paper addresses the event detection (ED) task, arguing that event arguments, often ignored in existing methods, provide valuable clues for identifying and categorizing events, particularly for ambiguous triggers. The authors propose a neural network-based approach using a supervised attention mechanism to explicitly leverage argument information during ED. They demonstrate improvements over state-of-the-art methods on the ACE 2005 dataset, and also show benefits from augmenting training data with events from FrameNet.

**Main Contributions (as I see them):**

1. **Empirical Demonstration of Argument Utility for ED:** The paper convincingly shows, through experiments, that incorporating argument information *does* improve event detection performance, a claim that isn't universally established in prior work which often focuses on joint extraction without isolating the benefit of argument awareness for the ED component.
2. **Supervised Attention Framework:** The introduction of a supervised attention mechanism tailored for argument information is a reasonable and potentially effective approach for focusing the model's attention on relevant contextual cues.
3. **Comprehensive Experimental Evaluation:** The thorough experimentation, including ablation studies (demonstrating different attention strategies) and data augmentation with FrameNet, provides strong support for the proposed method and offers valuable insights.



**Strengths:**

1. **Clear Motivation and Problem Definition:** The paper clearly articulates the problem and justifies why leveraging argument information is a worthwhile pursuit, especially in the context of ambiguous event triggers. The example with "fired" is very effective.
2. **Solid Experimental Results:** The results consistently demonstrate improvements over strong baselines. The inclusion of both joint models and pipeline systems provides a robust comparison. The statistical significance test adds further confidence.
3. **Well-Structured and Readable:**  The paper is generally well-written and easy to follow. The architecture is clearly explained and visualized in Figure 1. The methodology is presented logically.
4. **Thorough Ablation Studies:**  Investigating different attention strategies (S1 and S2) allows for a nuanced understanding of how the proposed method performs under different conditions. This strengthens the validity of the findings.
5. **Data Augmentation Effectiveness:** The positive results with FrameNet augmentation demonstrate the approach's ability to benefit from external knowledge, addressing the common issue of data sparsity in ED.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Novelty of the Core Technique:** While effective, the core technical contribution – a supervised attention mechanism – isn't radically novel. Attention mechanisms are widely used in NLP. The specific application to argument-aware event detection is valuable, but the underlying mechanism itself doesn’t represent a significant breakthrough.  The novelty resides more in *where* it's applied and *how* it's supervised.
2. **Dependency on Argument Annotation Quality:** The performance of the model is directly tied to the accuracy of the argument annotations in the training data. The paper doesn’t discuss the impact of noisy or imperfect argument annotations on the results. This is a significant limitation.
3. **Focus on ACE2005:**  The evaluation is limited to a single dataset (ACE2005). While this allows for fair comparison with existing work, it's unclear how well the method generalizes to other event detection datasets with different characteristics (e.g., different event types, document genres).
4. **Implementation Details:**  The paper could benefit from more detailed implementation details. For example, the specific architecture of the ANN (number of layers, activation functions) is not fully specified. Providing this information would enhance reproducibility.




**Questions to Authors:**

1. **Argument Annotation Noise:** How sensitive is your model to noisy or inaccurate argument annotations in the training data? Have you considered any techniques to mitigate the impact of annotation errors?
2. **Generalizability:** Do you have any preliminary results or insights regarding the generalizability of your approach to other event detection datasets beyond ACE2005?
3. **Error Analysis:** Can you provide a more detailed error analysis, identifying the types of events where your method performs particularly well or poorly?  Are there specific types of ambiguities where the argument information is most helpful?
4. **Efficiency:** What is the computational overhead introduced by the attention mechanism? Is there a trade-off between performance gains and computational cost?

**Additional Comments:**

*  The referencing is generally good, but a few references are missing year information (e.g., Baker et al. 1998).
* Consider discussing limitations in the conclusion.




**Overall Assessment:**

This is a solid and well-executed paper that makes a valuable contribution to the field of event detection. The empirical evidence convincingly demonstrates the benefits of explicitly leveraging argument information. While the core technique isn’t groundbreaking, the application and thorough evaluation are commendable. I recommend **Acceptance** with minor revisions addressing the weaknesses and questions outlined above.
