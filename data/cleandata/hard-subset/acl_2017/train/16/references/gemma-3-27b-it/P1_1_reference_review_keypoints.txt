- Summary: Proposes supervised attention over argument words for event detection, showing improved results on ACE 2005 and further gains using FrameNet augmentation.

- Contribution 1: Empirically demonstrates that incorporating argument information improves event detection, especially for ambiguous triggers.

- Contribution 2: Introduces a supervised attention mechanism guided by gold argument annotations to focus on argument words.

- Contribution 3: Achieves state-of-the-art performance on ACE 2005 and shows benefit from FrameNet data augmentation.

- Strength 1: Clear motivation and problem framing, with effective examples (e.g., "fired") illustrating ambiguity.

- Strength 2: Thorough experimental evaluation with multiple baselines and ablation studies isolating attention effects.

- Strength 3: Thoughtful use of FrameNet to address data sparsity and improve performance.

- Strength 4: Well-written, organized paper with clear task description and method explanation.

- Strength 5: Provides dataset, embedding, and hyperparameter details to facilitate reproducibility.

- Weakness 1: Limited novelty in the attention mechanism itself; novelty is mainly in applying attention to arguments.

- Weakness 2: Method depends on high-quality argument annotations and lacks analysis of robustness to noisy/incomplete annotations.

- Weakness 3: Improvements over strong baselines are modest, raising questions about complexity vs. gain.

- Weakness 4: Lacks a detailed error analysis to identify common failure modes and guide future work.

- Question 1: How robust is the approach to noisy or incomplete argument annotations, and were noise experiments performed?

- Question 2: Will the benefits generalize across other datasets and event types, and have other datasets been tried?

- Question 3: What is the computational overhead of the supervised attention compared to standard attention or no-attention baselines?

- Question 4: Can the authors provide a detailed error analysis outlining common errors and possible fixes?

- Question 5: How does this approach compare directly with recent strong joint ED–AE models that incorporate argument information?

- Additional comment 1: Citation style is inconsistent and should be fixed.

- Additional comment 2: Hyperparameter settings (e.g., λ=1.0) are mentioned late and should be presented earlier.

- Additional comment 3: Broken hyperlink to the NYT corpus used for embeddings should be corrected.

- Overall: Solid, well-executed paper with valuable empirical findings and a recommendation for acceptance, pending robustness and error-analysis improvements.
