## Review for ACL Submission: Exploiting Argument Information to Improve Event Detection via Supervised Attention Mechanisms

**Summary:** This paper investigates the use of event argument information to improve event detection (ED) performance. The authors argue that existing joint ED and argument extraction (AE) models don't effectively leverage argument information for ED, and propose a supervised attention mechanism that focuses on argument words during ED. They demonstrate improved performance on the ACE 2005 dataset, and further augment their results by incorporating data from FrameNet.

**Main Contributions (as I see them):**

1. **Empirical Observation & Motivation:** The paper highlights a valid and important observation – existing joint models don’t demonstrably improve ED performance despite jointly modeling arguments. This provides a strong motivation for exploring alternative approaches to leveraging argument information specifically for ED.
2. **Supervised Attention Mechanism:** The introduction of a supervised attention mechanism focused on argument words offers a conceptually sound and practically effective way to incorporate argument information into the ED process. The use of gold attention as supervision is a reasonable approach.
3. **Strong Experimental Results:** The authors demonstrate consistent and significant performance gains on ACE 2005, outperforming existing state-of-the-art methods, and show further improvement with FrameNet augmentation.

**Strengths:**

1. **Clear Problem Statement & Motivation:** The paper clearly articulates the limitations of existing joint models for ED and presents a compelling argument for explicitly leveraging argument information.  The example provided ("Mohamad fired Anwar...") effectively illustrates the ambiguity that arguments can resolve.
2. **Well-Designed Approach:**  The supervised attention mechanism is a logical and well-structured solution to the identified problem. The two strategies for constructing gold attention (S1 and S2) provide a reasonable exploration of different ways to utilize argument information.
3. **Thorough Experimental Evaluation:** The authors conducted a comprehensive set of experiments, including ablation studies (comparing ANN, ANN-S1, and ANN-S2) and comparisons with state-of-the-art systems. The inclusion of FrameNet data augmentation further strengthens the evaluation.
4. **Strong Performance:** The reported results demonstrate a clear and substantial improvement over existing methods, establishing the effectiveness of the proposed approach.  The t-test provides statistical significance.
5. **Well-Written & Organized:** The paper is generally well-written and easy to follow. The organization is logical, and the details of the method are clearly presented.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Novelty of Attention Mechanism:** While effective, the proposed supervised attention mechanism isn't fundamentally novel.  Attention mechanisms are now ubiquitous in NLP, and the application here, while beneficial, doesn’t represent a significant breakthrough in attention itself. The novelty lies in *where* it's applied and *how* the supervision is crafted.  This should be more explicitly acknowledged.
2. **Reliance on Gold Arguments:** The supervised approach relies on perfectly annotated arguments during training.  Real-world applications often involve noisy or incomplete argument annotations. The paper doesn’t discuss the robustness of the approach to argument annotation errors, or potential strategies for handling them (e.g., using predicted arguments as weak supervision).
3. **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** The paper mentions hyperparameter tuning but doesn’t provide sufficient detail about the search space or justification for the chosen values, particularly for λ in the joint loss function. A more detailed discussion of hyperparameter sensitivity would be valuable.  Specifically, how does performance change with different λ values?
4. **Limited Error Analysis:**  While the results are strong, a more detailed error analysis would provide deeper insights into the strengths and weaknesses of the approach.  What types of events does it still struggle with? What are common error patterns? Understanding the failure cases would be beneficial.
5. **FrameNet Augmentation Details:** The description of processing the FrameNet data is somewhat brief. More details about how the RPI Joint Information Extraction System was used to label entities would be helpful.



**Questions to Authors:**

1.  You mention that existing joint models achieve "insignificant" improvements on ED.  Could you quantify this insignificance more precisely?  What is the average improvement (or lack thereof) observed across different joint models?
2.  How sensitive is the performance of your model to the value of the λ hyperparameter? Could you provide a graph or table showing the performance (F1 score) as a function of λ?
3.  How robust is your model to errors in argument annotation? Have you experimented with using noisy or incomplete argument annotations, or employing a weak supervision approach?
4.  Can you provide a more detailed error analysis? What types of event triggers are most often misclassified by your model, and what are the common error patterns?
5.  Regarding the FrameNet data augmentation, how was the RPI Joint Information Extraction System used to label ACE entity mentions in the FN data? What are the limitations of this approach?



**Additional Comments:**

* The related work section is relatively brief.  Expanding it to include more recent work on attention mechanisms and event extraction would be beneficial.
* The paper would benefit from a discussion of the computational cost of the proposed approach compared to existing methods.



**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong submission that addresses an important problem in event extraction. The proposed supervised attention mechanism is a well-designed and effective solution, and the experimental results are compelling.  While the novelty of the attention mechanism itself is limited, the application to the ED task with argument-aware supervision is a valuable contribution. Addressing the weaknesses outlined above would further strengthen the paper.  I recommend acceptance.
