- Summary: Argues that event arguments improve event detection and proposes a supervised attention mechanism that leverages arguments, showing improvements on ACE2005 and with FrameNet augmentation.

- Contribution: Empirically demonstrates that incorporating argument information improves event detection, isolating this benefit from joint extraction approaches.

- Contribution: Proposes a supervised attention framework tailored to focus on argument-aware contextual cues.

- Contribution: Provides comprehensive experimental evaluation including ablation studies and FrameNet data augmentation.

- Strength: Clear motivation and problem definition with an effective example (e.g., "fired").

- Strength: Strong experimental results against joint and pipeline baselines, with statistical significance testing.

- Strength: Well-structured, readable paper with a clear architecture diagram.

- Strength: Thorough ablation studies comparing different attention strategies (S1 and S2).

- Strength: Data augmentation with FrameNet is effective and helps address data sparsity.

- Weakness: Core technique has limited novelty—attention is standard; novelty is mainly in application/supervision.

- Weakness: Performance depends on argument annotation quality; impact of noisy annotations is not analyzed.

- Weakness: Evaluation is limited to ACE2005, so generalizability to other datasets/genres is unclear.

- Weakness: Some implementation details (e.g., ANN layers, activations) are missing, hindering reproducibility.

- Question: How sensitive is the model to noisy or inaccurate argument annotations, and can this be mitigated?

- Question: Any preliminary results or insights on generalizability to other event detection datasets beyond ACE2005?

- Question: Can the authors provide a detailed error analysis showing which event types/ambiguities benefit most from argument information?

- Question: What is the computational overhead of the attention mechanism and the trade-off between performance gains and cost?

- Additional comment: A few references lack year information (e.g., Baker et al. 1998).

- Additional comment: Consider discussing limitations explicitly in the conclusion.

- Overall assessment: Solid, well-executed paper with convincing evidence for argument-aware ED; recommend acceptance with minor revisions.
