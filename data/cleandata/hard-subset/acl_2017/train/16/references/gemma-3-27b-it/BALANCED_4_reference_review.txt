This paper addresses the event detection (ED) task, arguing that existing joint event detection and argument extraction (AE) models haven't fully leveraged argument information to improve ED performance. The authors propose a supervised attention mechanism to explicitly focus on argument words during ED, constructing gold attention vectors based on annotated arguments and jointly training the attention mechanism with the event detector.  They demonstrate improved performance on the ACE 2005 dataset, and further augment results using FrameNet data.

The primary contribution of this work is the exploration of *explicitly* incorporating argument information into a supervised attention mechanism for event detection. While joint models exist, the authors convincingly argue that they indirectly utilize argument information, primarily benefiting AE. This focused approach of directly attending to arguments for ED is a valuable contribution. A second, supporting contribution is the empirical demonstration on ACE 2005 that this approach yields improvements over both pipeline and existing joint methods. The inclusion of FrameNet data augmentation is a third contribution, demonstrating further robustness and performance gains.

**Strengths:**

1. **Clear Motivation and Problem Statement:** The paper effectively highlights the limitations of existing joint models and clearly articulates the argument for explicitly leveraging argument information for ED. The example with "fired" is particularly insightful in illustrating the ambiguity that argument information can resolve.
2. **Well-Designed Approach:** The proposed supervised attention mechanism is logically sound and well-integrated into a neural network architecture.  The two strategies for constructing gold attention vectors (S1 and S2) are reasonable and offer different ways to guide the attention learning process.
3. **Strong Empirical Results:**  The experimental results on ACE 2005 demonstrate a clear performance improvement over strong baselines, including state-of-the-art joint models and pipeline systems. The ablation studies (ANN, ANN-S1, ANN-S2) effectively isolate the contribution of the proposed attention mechanism.
4. **Thorough Evaluation:**  The authors conduct a comprehensive evaluation, including ablation studies, data augmentation with FrameNet, and comparison with multiple baselines. This strengthens the validity of their findings.
5. **Good Writing and Clarity:** The paper is well-written and easy to understand. The figures and tables are clear and effectively illustrate the proposed approach and experimental results.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Novelty of Attention Mechanism:** While the *application* to event detection with argument information is novel, the supervised attention mechanism itself is a relatively standard technique in NLP. The paper could benefit from a more nuanced discussion of the novelty relative to existing attention-based models.  What specifically about the approach or the gold attention construction makes it particularly effective for this task?
2. **Sensitivity to Hyperparameters:** The experimental setup mentions careful hyperparameter tuning, but the specific values used for λ (especially for S2) aren't thoroughly justified. It’s unclear why 5.0 was chosen for λ in ANN-S2. A sensitivity analysis of λ, or a more principled method for its selection (e.g., using a validation set), would strengthen the results.
3. **Lack of Error Analysis:** The paper focuses on overall performance metrics (P, R, F1).  A more detailed error analysis, examining specific types of errors made by the proposed model and the baselines, could provide valuable insights into the strengths and weaknesses of the approach. For example, are there specific event types where the model struggles?  Are there cases where argument information is misleading?
4. **FrameNet Augmentation Detail:** While augmentation with FrameNet is a positive contribution, the process of converting FN events to the ACE format and adding entity information could be described in greater detail. This would improve reproducibility and allow readers to better understand the potential impact of this preprocessing step.



**Questions to Authors:**

1.  Could you elaborate on the novelty of the supervised attention mechanism in the context of existing attention-based models? What specific aspects are tailored to the problem of event detection with argument information?
2.  What is the rationale behind setting λ = 5.0 for ANN-S2?  Did you perform a sensitivity analysis of this parameter?
3.  Can you provide a qualitative error analysis, illustrating examples of events that the proposed model correctly identifies due to argument information, and examples where it fails despite argument information?
4. Could you provide more details on how you leveraged RPI Joint Information Extraction System to label entity mentions on the FN data, particularly how you handled potential discrepancies between FN and ACE entity types?

**Additional Comments:**

*   The reference list is comprehensive.
*   Minor: In the introduction, the phrase "exiting methods" should be "existing methods."



Overall, this is a solid and well-executed paper that addresses an important problem in event extraction. The proposed approach is logical, the experimental results are strong, and the writing is clear. Addressing the weaknesses outlined above, particularly regarding the novelty of the attention mechanism, hyperparameter sensitivity, and error analysis, would further strengthen the contribution of this work. I recommend acceptance.
