## Peer Review: Exploiting Argument Information to Improve Event Detection via Supervised Attention Mechanisms

**Summary:**

This paper addresses the event detection (ED) task, arguing that leveraging event arguments – often ignored in existing methods – can improve performance, especially in disambiguating trigger words. The authors identify a weakness in joint ED and argument extraction models where the imbalance in training data (more arguments than triggers) leads to a focus on argument extraction at the expense of event detection. They propose a supervised attention mechanism that focuses on argument words during event detection, training the attention with "gold" attention vectors derived from annotated arguments. Experiments on ACE 2005, with and without FrameNet data augmentation, demonstrate improvements over state-of-the-art methods.

**Main Contributions (as I see them):**

1. **Empirical Analysis of Joint Models:** The paper provides a compelling empirical observation and analysis of why existing joint ED and AE models often fail to significantly improve ED performance, attributing it to data imbalance and the indirect use of arguments. This is a valuable insight into the behavior of these models.
2. **Supervised Attention Mechanism for ED:** The proposed supervised attention mechanism, trained with gold attention vectors derived from argument annotations, is a reasonable and potentially effective approach to directly incorporate argument information into event detection.
3. **Strong Experimental Results:** The paper demonstrates strong experimental results on the ACE 2005 dataset, outperforming several baselines, suggesting the proposed approach is a viable method for improving ED performance.

**Strengths:**

1. **Clear Problem Statement & Motivation:** The paper clearly articulates the problem of ineffective argument utilization in current event detection methods and provides a strong motivation for their proposed approach. The example with "fired" is particularly effective in illustrating the benefit of considering arguments.
2. **Well-Designed Experimental Setup:** The experimental setup appears robust, with comparisons to multiple baselines, including both pipeline and joint methods, and the inclusion of data augmentation with FrameNet. The clear description of hyperparameter tuning is also a strength.
3. **Effective Use of Supervised Attention:** The concept of using gold attention vectors, constructed from annotated arguments, to directly supervise the attention mechanism is a clever and intuitive approach. The exploration of different strategies (S1 and S2) for constructing these vectors adds depth to the work.
4. **Solid Empirical Validation:**  The consistently reported performance improvements across different experimental settings (with and without FrameNet) provides strong evidence supporting the effectiveness of the proposed approach. The statistical significance test further strengthens this claim.
5. **Thorough Related Work Discussion:** The related work section provides a good overview of existing approaches to event detection and argument extraction, accurately positioning the authors' contribution within the field.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Novelty of the Attention Mechanism:** While the application of supervised attention to event detection using argument information is a reasonable approach, the attention mechanism itself is relatively standard. The core novelty lies in *how* the attention is supervised, rather than the attention mechanism *itself*. This limits the overall impact of the work.
2. **Dependence on High-Quality Annotations:** The method's performance relies heavily on the accuracy and completeness of argument annotations in the training data. The "gold" attention vectors are only as good as the underlying argument annotations. This could be a limitation in scenarios with noisy or incomplete annotations. A discussion of potential robustness to annotation errors would be beneficial.
3. **Lack of Ablation Study on Attention Strategies:** While two attention construction strategies (S1 & S2) are explored, a more detailed ablation study examining the impact of different parameters within these strategies (e.g., the Gaussian window size in S2) would provide further insights into their effectiveness.
4. **Focus Solely on ACE 2005:**  The evaluation is limited to the ACE 2005 dataset.  While this is a common benchmark, it would be valuable to assess the generalizability of the approach to other datasets with different characteristics (e.g., different event types, different document genres).



**Questions to Authors:**

1.  How sensitive is the performance of your model to the quality of argument annotations? Have you explored any techniques to mitigate the impact of noisy or incomplete argument annotations?
2.  Could you provide a more detailed ablation study, investigating the influence of different parameters (e.g., window size in S2) on the performance of your proposed strategies?
3.  Do you have any insights into *why* strategy S2 consistently outperforms S1? Is it simply a matter of providing more information, or is there something specific about the distribution of attention weights that contributes to the improvement?
4.  Have you considered extending the proposed approach to jointly perform event detection *and* argument extraction? Even if joint training doesn't improve ED performance directly, the supervised attention mechanism could potentially benefit argument extraction.




**Additional Comments:**

*   The writing is generally clear and concise, but some sections (e.g., Section 3.1) could benefit from further simplification and explanation of the mathematical notation.
*   Consider adding a visualization of the attention weights for a few example sentences to illustrate how the model is focusing on argument words.
*   The link to the GitHub repository for Liu et al. (2016a)'s FN data is appreciated.



Overall, this is a solid paper that presents a well-motivated and empirically validated approach to improving event detection by effectively leveraging argument information. While the novelty of the attention mechanism itself is limited, the proposed method and the insightful analysis of existing joint models contribute meaningfully to the field. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions.
