This paper proposes a neural network approach to event detection (ED) that explicitly leverages argument information via a supervised attention mechanism. The authors argue that existing joint event detection and argument extraction (EE) models don't effectively utilize argument information for ED, and propose a model where argument words receive higher attention during trigger classification. They present experimental results on the ACE 2005 dataset, demonstrating improvements over baseline systems.

The primary contribution of this work, as I see it, is a reasonably well-executed investigation into the potential benefits of directly incorporating argument information into an event detection model. While the idea isn’t entirely novel – the authors correctly identify that joint models haven't fully realized this potential – the supervised attention mechanism provides a concrete approach to address it. The experimental setup, including ablation studies and comparisons to existing state-of-the-art methods, is generally sound. A secondary contribution is the observation, supported by experiments, that existing joint models are indeed biased towards argument extraction due to data imbalance.

**Strengths:**

1. **Clear Motivation and Problem Statement:** The paper clearly articulates the shortcomings of existing joint models regarding ED and argues convincingly for the importance of explicitly utilizing argument information. The analysis of why joint models struggle with ED, pointing to data imbalance and the indirect use of arguments, is insightful.
2. **Sound Methodology:** The proposed supervised attention mechanism is a sensible approach to incorporate argument information. The use of gold attention vectors for training, coupled with experimentation with different strategies (S1 and S2), demonstrates a thorough exploration of the technique.
3. **Empirical Validation:** The experimental results on the ACE 2005 dataset demonstrate a tangible improvement in ED performance compared to several baselines, including established joint models. The inclusion of FrameNet data further strengthens the findings.
4. **Comprehensive Experimental Design:** The ablation studies (ANN, ANN-ENT, ANN-S1, ANN-S2) are helpful in dissecting the contribution of each component of the proposed model and verifying the effectiveness of the attention mechanism.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Novelty:** While the application of supervised attention to leverage argument information is a reasonable direction, the underlying neural network architecture and attention mechanism itself are fairly standard. The core innovation lies in *how* attention is applied, not in a fundamentally new architectural component.
2. **Over-reliance on ACE 2005:** The evaluation is solely based on the ACE 2005 dataset. While a standard benchmark, it's a relatively small dataset, and the results may not generalize well to other event types or domains. A demonstration on a more diverse dataset would significantly strengthen the claims.
3. **Sensitivity to Hyperparameters:** The description of hyperparameter tuning is brief. The choice of λ (the weighting factor for the attention loss) appears crucial, and more discussion on its selection and sensitivity would be valuable. A more rigorous hyperparameter search and analysis is needed.
4. **Lack of Error Analysis:** The paper lacks a detailed error analysis. Understanding *where* the proposed model still fails would provide valuable insights for future research. Specifically, analyzing the types of events where argument information is most (and least) helpful would be informative.



**Questions to Authors:**

1.  Could you provide a more detailed explanation of the hyperparameter tuning process, specifically regarding the selection of λ? Did you explore a wider range of values, and how did the performance vary?
2.  The paper mentions that only 10% of triggers in ACE 2005 are entities. How does the performance of your model change when tested on datasets with a higher proportion of event triggers that are also entities?
3.  Could you provide a qualitative error analysis, highlighting specific examples where the model succeeds or fails, particularly in cases where argument information seems to be crucial or misleading?
4.  Given the limited novelty of the core architecture, what are the key insights gained from this work that could inform the design of more advanced event detection models?



**Additional Comments:**

The writing is generally clear and concise. Consider adding a section discussing the computational complexity of the proposed method. Also, citing more recent work on attention mechanisms in NLP would provide valuable context.
