## Review for "Exploiting Argument Information to Improve Event Detection via Supervised Attention Mechanisms"

**Summary:**

This paper tackles the Event Detection (ED) task within Event Extraction (EE). The authors argue that existing joint ED and Argument Extraction (AE) models underutilize argument information for improving ED performance, primarily because they are hampered by data imbalance and indirect argument usage (arguments used for AE, which then *implicitly* benefits ED). They propose a supervised attention mechanism to explicitly incorporate argument information into ED, training the attention weights using annotated arguments as “gold” supervision. The method is evaluated on the ACE 2005 dataset, demonstrating improvements over state-of-the-art methods, and further benefits from augmentation with FrameNet data.

**Main Contributions (as I see them):**

1. **Empirical Analysis of Joint Models:** The paper provides a compelling empirical analysis highlighting the limited benefits of existing joint ED/AE models on the *ED* task itself, despite gains in AE. This is a valuable observation and motivates the proposed approach.
2. **Supervised Attention for Argument Incorporation:** The proposed supervised attention mechanism is a reasonable and potentially effective approach to directly leverage argument information for ED. The use of "gold" attention weights derived from annotated arguments is a novel training technique.
3. **Strong Experimental Results:** The paper demonstrates competitive performance on the ACE 2005 benchmark, achieving state-of-the-art results, especially when combined with FrameNet data.



**Strengths:**

1. **Clear Motivation and Problem Definition:** The authors clearly articulate the problem of underutilized argument information in ED and provide a strong rationale for their approach. The example provided ("Mohamad fired Anwar...") effectively illustrates the ambiguity that argument information can resolve.
2. **Well-Designed Experimental Setup:** The experimental setup is thorough, including comparisons to strong baselines (both pipeline and joint methods) and ablation studies investigating different attention strategies.  The inclusion of FrameNet augmentation provides a more comprehensive evaluation.
3. **Solid Empirical Results:** The reported results consistently demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed approach, surpassing existing methods on the ACE 2005 dataset. The gains are statistically significant, adding to the credibility of the findings.
4. **Detailed Methodology:**  The paper provides a detailed description of the proposed model, including the context representation learning module, the attention mechanism, and the training procedure. The explanations are clear and easy to follow.
5. **Insightful Discussion of Joint Models:** The authors' analysis of why joint models struggle to improve ED performance (data imbalance, indirect argument usage) is insightful and provides a good justification for their proposed solution.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Novelty of Attention Mechanism:** While the application of supervised attention to leverage arguments is a valid contribution, the attention mechanism itself (specifically, the form of calculating attention values) isn’t particularly novel. It's a standard dot-product attention mechanism, and the core innovation lies in *how* that attention is trained with argument-based supervision, rather than the attention architecture itself.  This should be acknowledged.
2. **Reliance on ACE 2005:** The evaluation is limited to the ACE 2005 dataset. While it's a widely used benchmark, the results might not generalize to other event extraction datasets with different characteristics. Evaluation on a more diverse set of datasets would strengthen the claims of generalizability.
3. **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** The difference in optimal λ values for S1 and S2 (1.0 vs. 5.0) suggests some sensitivity to hyperparameter tuning.  A more in-depth analysis of the influence of λ and other key hyperparameters could provide valuable insights into the model's behavior.
4. **Limited Error Analysis:**  The paper lacks a detailed error analysis. Identifying specific types of events where the model struggles or common error patterns would provide a deeper understanding of the method’s strengths and weaknesses.




**Questions to Authors:**

1.  **Novelty Clarification:** Could you elaborate on the specific novelty of your approach beyond the supervised learning of attention weights using argument information?  Specifically, how does your attention mechanism differ from standard attention mechanisms used in similar tasks?
2. **Generalizability:**  Have you considered evaluating your approach on other event extraction datasets beyond ACE 2005? If so, what were the results? If not, what are the potential challenges of applying your method to other datasets?
3. **Hyperparameter Tuning:**  Can you provide a more detailed description of the hyperparameter tuning process, including the search space and the criteria used to select the optimal values?
4. **Error Analysis:** Can you provide a more detailed error analysis, identifying specific types of events where the model performs poorly and the common error patterns observed?  Are there particular types of arguments that are more effective in improving ED performance?
5. **Computational Complexity:** What is the added computational cost of incorporating the supervised attention mechanism compared to a baseline ED model without attention?



**Additional Comments:**

*   The writing is generally clear and well-organized.
*   The related work section could be expanded to include more recent advances in event extraction and attention mechanisms.




**Overall Assessment:**

This is a solid and well-executed paper that addresses an important problem in event extraction. The empirical analysis, proposed method, and experimental results are all convincing. While the novelty of the attention mechanism itself is limited, the supervised learning approach to incorporate argument information is a valuable contribution. I recommend **acceptance** with minor revisions addressing the weaknesses and questions raised above.
