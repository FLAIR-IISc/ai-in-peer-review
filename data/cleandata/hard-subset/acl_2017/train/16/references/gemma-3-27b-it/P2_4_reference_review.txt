## Review: Exploiting Argument Information to Improve Event Detection via Supervised Attention Mechanisms

**Summary:**

This paper investigates the use of event arguments to improve event detection (ED) performance. The authors argue that existing joint ED and argument extraction models don’t effectively leverage argument information for *improving* ED, and propose a supervised attention mechanism that focuses on argument words during ED. They demonstrate improvements over state-of-the-art methods on the ACE 2005 dataset, and show further gains by augmenting training data with events from FrameNet.

**Main Contributions (as I see them):**

1. **Empirical Demonstration of Argument Utility for ED:** The paper convincingly demonstrates, through experiments, that explicitly incorporating argument information *can* improve event detection performance, a finding that challenges the prevailing observation that joint models don't significantly benefit ED.
2. **Simple and Effective Attention Mechanism:** The proposed supervised attention mechanism is relatively straightforward to implement, yet provides a measurable performance boost. This suggests a practical way to incorporate argument knowledge into ED systems.
3. **Systematic Evaluation and Ablation:**  The authors present a thorough set of experiments, including ablation studies (different attention strategies, FrameNet augmentation) which contribute to a better understanding of the proposed method's components and effectiveness.


**Strengths:**

1. **Clear Motivation and Problem Definition:** The paper articulates a clear issue with current joint models – they don’t effectively use argument information to improve ED – and offers a compelling motivation for addressing it. The example provided with “fired” effectively illustrates the ambiguity that argument information can resolve.
2. **Strong Empirical Results:**  The results consistently show improvements over strong baselines (including recent joint models and strong pipeline systems) on the ACE 2005 dataset.  The use of t-tests to demonstrate statistical significance is also appreciated.
3. **Well-Structured and Readable:** The paper is well-written, logically organized, and easy to follow. The figures clearly illustrate the proposed architecture.
4. **Thorough Experiments:** The paper includes ablation studies to investigate the effect of different attention strategies (S1 and S2) and the benefits of FrameNet augmentation, demonstrating a solid experimental evaluation.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Novelty of the Core Method:** While effective, the supervised attention mechanism itself isn’t radically new. Attention mechanisms are now commonplace in NLP. The primary novelty lies in *applying* a supervised attention to argument information specifically for ED, but the core technique isn’t a breakthrough.
2. **Dataset Limitations:**  The evaluation is solely on the ACE 2005 dataset. While ACE 2005 is a standard benchmark, a more diverse evaluation (e.g., different domains, different event schemas) would strengthen the generalizability claims.
3. **Lack of Error Analysis:**  The paper would benefit from a more in-depth error analysis. Understanding *where* the model still fails, and *why*, would provide valuable insights for future work. For example, are there specific event types where argument information is particularly helpful or unhelpful?
4. **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** The tuning of the `lambda` hyperparameter for the loss function (especially for S2) isn't fully justified.  More exploration of the effect of `lambda` and some discussion about its sensitivity would be beneficial.



**Questions to Authors:**

1.  Could you provide a more detailed error analysis, specifically focusing on event types where the proposed model performs particularly well or poorly?
2.  How sensitive is the performance to the choice of the `lambda` hyperparameter in the joint loss function (Equation 9), particularly for the S2 strategy? Could you provide a learning curve or sensitivity analysis?
3.  Have you investigated the potential for using more sophisticated attention mechanisms (e.g., self-attention, transformers) instead of the relatively simple attention mechanism proposed in this work?
4.  The FrameNet augmentation improves performance.  Do you observe any specific event types benefiting more significantly from this augmentation, and if so, why?



**Additional Comments:**

*   The formatting is generally good, but referencing style should be checked for consistency.
*   The connection to related work is sufficient, but could be slightly expanded to acknowledge more recent advancements in attention mechanisms and their application to sequence labeling tasks.




**Overall Assessment:**

I recommend **acceptance** of this paper. While the proposed method isn't a radical departure from existing techniques, the authors convincingly demonstrate that explicitly leveraging argument information through supervised attention can significantly improve event detection performance. The thorough experimentation, clear presentation, and strong empirical results make this a valuable contribution to the field. The weaknesses are relatively minor and could be addressed in a revised version of the paper.
