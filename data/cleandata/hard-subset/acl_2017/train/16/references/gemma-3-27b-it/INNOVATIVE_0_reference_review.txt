This paper proposes a supervised attention mechanism to incorporate argument information into event detection (ED). The authors motivate their work by observing that existing joint ED and argument extraction models don't significantly improve ED performance, hypothesizing this is due to data imbalance and the way arguments are used (indirectly, through pre-prediction). Their approach directly leverages argument information via a supervised attention mechanism, training the attention weights based on annotated arguments.

The primary contribution is the *idea* of explicitly modeling argument information for event detection via supervised attention. While attention mechanisms are common, applying them in this specific way – using argument annotations to directly supervise attention towards relevant context – is a novel and potentially impactful direction. A secondary contribution is the empirical demonstration, on ACE2005, that this approach outperforms several baselines, including strong joint models.

**Strengths:**

1. **Novelty of Approach:** The core idea of directly supervising attention with argument information is genuinely novel. Most existing work either ignores arguments for ED or treats them as a secondary component in a joint model. This paper proposes a targeted approach that focuses on leveraging arguments specifically for *improving event detection*, which is a worthwhile pursuit.
2. **Strong Empirical Results:** The results on ACE2005 are competitive, achieving state-of-the-art performance. While ACE2005 is a relatively old dataset, it remains a standard benchmark for event detection, and achieving a new best result is a significant accomplishment.
3. **Clear Motivation and Analysis:** The paper clearly articulates the problem with existing joint models – they don't effectively leverage argument information for ED – and provides a plausible explanation (data imbalance, indirect use of arguments). This analysis strengthens the rationale for the proposed approach.
4. **Well-Defined Strategies for Gold Attention:**  The two strategies (S1 and S2) for creating "gold" attention vectors are reasonable and provide a good starting point for exploring different ways to incorporate argument information.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Ablation Study:** While the paper compares to several baselines, the ablation study of the proposed method is limited. It would be valuable to see results with only *part* of the argument information used, or a more detailed analysis of the impact of the different attention strategies (S1 vs S2). It is unclear if the gains are entirely attributable to the supervised attention mechanism.
2. **ACE2005 Dataset:** The reliance solely on ACE2005 is a concern. This dataset is known to be challenging but also relatively small. Evaluating on more modern and larger datasets would strengthen the findings and demonstrate the generalizability of the approach.
3. **Implementation Details:** The implementation details are somewhat sparse.  For instance, the specifics of the neural network architecture (number of layers, hidden unit sizes) aren’t fully transparent, and the hyperparameter search process could be clarified.  This limits reproducibility.
4. **Potential for Over-Supervision:** While the core idea is appealing, the supervised attention mechanism could potentially *over-supervise* the model, leading to overfitting to the annotated arguments. The authors do not address this potential issue.



**Questions to Authors:**

1. Could you provide a more detailed ablation study, evaluating the contribution of different components of your proposed approach (e.g., the supervised attention mechanism, the different attention strategies)?
2. Have you experimented with evaluating your model on more recent event detection datasets? If not, what are the challenges in adapting your method to these datasets?
3. Could you provide a more thorough description of the neural network architecture and hyperparameter tuning process?
4. Have you explored methods to mitigate potential overfitting issues that might arise from the supervised attention mechanism? For example, techniques like dropout, regularization, or data augmentation?



**Additional Comments:**

The paper is well-written and clearly presented. Despite the minor weaknesses, the novelty of the approach and the strong empirical results on a standard benchmark make this a promising contribution. I believe the potential benefits of this work outweigh the limitations, and I would lean towards acceptance pending satisfactory responses to the questions above.
