## Peer Review: Exploiting Argument Information to Improve Event Detection via Supervised Attention Mechanisms

**Summary:**

This paper tackles the event detection (ED) task, arguing that event arguments, often ignored in existing methods, contain valuable information for improving trigger identification and classification. The authors identify a limitation in joint ED and argument extraction (AE) models – that they are often biased towards AE due to data imbalance and indirect utilization of argument information. To address this, they propose a neural network-based approach employing a supervised attention mechanism that explicitly focuses on arguments during ED.  They demonstrate improved performance on the ACE 2005 dataset, and show further gains when augmenting training data with FrameNet events.

**Main Contributions (as claimed by authors, in order):**

1.  Analysis of the limitations of joint ED and AE models, arguing for explicit argument utilization for ED.
2.  A supervised attention-based ED model that leverages annotated arguments.
3.  Performance improvements on the ACE 2005 dataset, achieving state-of-the-art results.

**My Assessment of Contributions:**

1. **Novel Application of Supervised Attention:** The most significant contribution is the practical application of supervised attention focusing on arguments for event detection. While attention mechanisms are common, applying them specifically to argument information in this manner is a valuable contribution.
2. **Empirical Validation & Strong Results:** The empirical results on ACE2005 are convincing and demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed approach, achieving SOTA performance.
3. **Insightful Analysis of Joint Models:**  The critique of joint ED and AE models, pointing out the data imbalance and indirect argument usage, provides a useful perspective and motivates the proposed approach.



**Strengths:**

1.  **Strong Empirical Results:** The paper achieves state-of-the-art performance on the ACE 2005 dataset, providing strong evidence for the effectiveness of the proposed approach.  The gains over existing methods are notable.
2.  **Clear Motivation and Problem Definition:** The authors clearly articulate the problem of under-utilizing argument information in ED and provide a compelling rationale for their approach.  The example provided in the introduction effectively illustrates the potential benefit of considering arguments.
3.  **Well-Defined Methodology:** The proposed approach is clearly described, with a detailed explanation of the architecture and the supervised attention mechanism.  The use of both S1 and S2 strategies for creating gold attention vectors is a thoughtful exploration of different approaches.
4. **Thorough Experimentation:** The authors conduct a comprehensive set of experiments, including ablation studies (ANN, ANN-ENT, ANN-S1, ANN-S2) and data augmentation (FrameNet), to demonstrate the effectiveness of their approach and analyze the contribution of different components.

**Weaknesses:**

1.  **Limited Novelty in Core Components:**  The core components of the system – a neural network for ED and attention mechanisms – are not novel in themselves. The primary novelty lies in *how* these components are applied and combined to specifically exploit argument information. The paper could benefit from more explicitly acknowledging this.
2.  **Data Dependency and Generalizability:**  The model is evaluated solely on the ACE 2005 dataset. It is unclear how well the approach would generalize to other datasets with different characteristics (e.g., different event types, annotation schemes, or domains).
3.  **Hyperparameter Sensitivity & Analysis:** The paper states hyperparameters were tuned on the development set, but the justification for specific choices (e.g., λ=1.0 for S1, λ=5.0 for S2) is not provided. A more detailed analysis of hyperparameter sensitivity would strengthen the paper.  Why are different lambda values optimal for S1 and S2?
4. **Presentation of Results:** Table 3 includes a "Skip-CNN" method without sufficient context. The reader needs more explanation of where these results are from and how comparable they are to the other methods presented.



**Questions to Authors:**

1.  **Generalizability:** Have you considered evaluating your approach on other event detection datasets? If not, could you discuss the potential challenges and limitations of applying your method to different datasets?
2.  **Hyperparameter Tuning:** Could you provide a more detailed explanation of the hyperparameter tuning process, including the range of values explored and the criteria used to select the optimal values for λ in S1 and S2? What impact did different values of lambda have on performance?
3.  **Computational Cost:**  What is the computational overhead of incorporating the attention mechanism, especially compared to a baseline model without attention?  How does this impact scalability to larger datasets?
4. **Error Analysis:** Can you provide a qualitative error analysis? What types of events or triggers does the model still struggle with, and can you identify specific cases where argument information would have been helpful but was not effectively utilized?

**Additional Comments:**

*   The paper could benefit from a discussion of related work on attention mechanisms in other NLP tasks, to provide a broader context for the proposed approach.
*   Consider adding a small example demonstrating how the attention mechanism highlights relevant arguments for a specific event trigger.
*   Clarify the source and comparability of the “Skip-CNN” results in Table 3.
