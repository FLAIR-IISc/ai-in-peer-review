This paper addresses the event detection (ED) task, arguing that leveraging event argument information can improve performance, a resource often ignored in existing approaches. The authors propose a supervised attention mechanism to explicitly incorporate argument information into a neural network-based ED model. They demonstrate improvements over state-of-the-art methods on the ACE 2005 dataset, and also show the benefit of augmenting training data with events from FrameNet.

**Contributions:**

1. **Highlighting the Underutilization of Argument Information:** The paper correctly points out that despite being readily available in training data, argument information is largely ignored in existing ED systems, particularly in joint ED/AE models where its benefit is indirect. This observation and analysis are the strongest contribution, framing the motivation for the work.
2. **Supervised Attention Mechanism for ED:** The introduction of a supervised attention mechanism to explicitly focus on argument words during ED is a reasonable and well-motivated approach. The two proposed strategies for constructing “gold attentions” offer different perspectives on incorporating argument information.
3. **Empirical Validation and Data Augmentation:** The experimental results on ACE 2005 demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed approach, and the use of FrameNet data for augmentation further strengthens the findings. The authors provide a fairly comprehensive set of experiments and comparisons.

**Strengths:**

1. **Clear Motivation and Problem Definition:** The paper clearly articulates the problem of underutilized argument information in ED and provides a compelling argument for its potential benefits. The example provided with “fired” effectively illustrates the point.
2. **Sound Technical Approach:** The proposed supervised attention mechanism is a sensible way to incorporate argument information. The use of gold attentions as supervision is a reasonable training strategy.
3. **Comprehensive Experiments:** The authors perform a thorough set of experiments, comparing their approach against strong baselines and evaluating the impact of data augmentation. The inclusion of ablation studies (ANN, ANN-ENT, ANN-S1, ANN-S2) is particularly valuable for understanding the contribution of each component.
4. **Strong Empirical Results:** The reported results demonstrate a clear improvement over existing state-of-the-art methods on the ACE 2005 dataset, which is a widely used benchmark for ED.
5. **Reproducibility:** The authors provide sufficient details regarding the experimental setup and hyperparameter settings, enhancing the reproducibility of their results.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Novelty of the Attention Mechanism:** While the application of supervised attention to ED is a valid contribution, the attention mechanism itself isn’t novel. It’s a standard attention mechanism applied in a specific context. The core novelty lies in *where* the attention is directed (towards arguments), not *how* the attention is computed.
2. **FrameNet Data Augmentation Details:** The description of how FrameNet data was processed and integrated is a bit sparse. Specifically, how entity information was added to the FN data (using RPI Joint Information Extraction System) and the potential impact of errors introduced by this system on the results aren't adequately discussed.
3. **Error Analysis:** While the authors present quantitative results, there’s a lack of qualitative error analysis. Understanding *where* the model still struggles (e.g., specific event types, ambiguous triggers despite argument information) would provide valuable insights and directions for future work.
4. **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** The choice of λ (trade-off between loss functions) appears somewhat arbitrary. A more systematic exploration of the hyperparameter space, and a discussion of its impact on performance, would be beneficial. The different values chosen for S1 and S2 (1.0 vs 5.0) seem oddly specific without further justification.
5. **Lack of Comparison to Recent Transformer-Based Models:** The landscape of NLP is rapidly evolving with the prevalence of transformer-based models (e.g., BERT, RoBERTa). A comparison to recent ED systems based on these models would provide a more up-to-date assessment of the proposed approach's performance.



**Questions to Authors:**

1. Could you provide more details on the processing of the FrameNet data, specifically regarding the entity information added using the RPI system? What steps were taken to mitigate potential errors introduced by this system?
2. Can you elaborate on the rationale behind the specific values chosen for the hyperparameter λ in the different experimental settings (S1 and S2)? Did you perform a hyperparameter search?
3. Would you be willing to share some examples of cases where the model still fails to correctly identify the event type despite having access to relevant argument information? This would help in understanding the limitations of the approach.
4. How do you think the proposed method would perform when integrated with pre-trained language models like BERT or RoBERTa? Have you considered conducting experiments with these models?

**Additional Comments:**

*   The writing is generally clear and well-organized.
*   The related work section provides a good overview of existing approaches to event detection.
*   The figure captions are descriptive and helpful.



**Overall Assessment:**

This is a solid paper that addresses a relevant problem in event detection and proposes a reasonable solution. While the attention mechanism itself is not novel, the application to explicitly leverage argument information is a valuable contribution. The strong empirical results and thorough experiments support the authors' claims. However, the lack of error analysis, limited discussion of FrameNet data processing, and absence of comparison to recent transformer-based models are weaknesses.

I recommend **acceptance** with minor revisions addressing the weaknesses mentioned above. Specifically, the authors should elaborate on the FrameNet data processing, provide a discussion of hyperparameter sensitivity, and include some qualitative error analysis. A brief discussion about potential integration with pre-trained language models would also strengthen the paper.
