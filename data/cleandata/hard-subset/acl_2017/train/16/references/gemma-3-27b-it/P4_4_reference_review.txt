This paper tackles the event detection (ED) task, arguing that event arguments, often ignored in existing methods, provide valuable clues for identifying and categorizing events, particularly in cases of ambiguous trigger words. The authors propose a supervised attention mechanism to explicitly incorporate argument information into a neural network-based ED model. They construct “gold attentions” based on annotated arguments to supervise the learning process and demonstrate improvements on the ACE 2005 dataset, achieving state-of-the-art results.

The main contributions of this work, as I see them, are: 1) the insightful observation and empirical demonstration that argument information is underutilized in current ED systems and can significantly improve performance; 2) the proposal and implementation of a supervised attention mechanism specifically designed to leverage argument information for ED; and 3) the achievement of state-of-the-art results on the ACE 2005 benchmark dataset.

**Strengths:**

1. **Novelty of Focusing on Argument Information:** The paper correctly identifies a gap in existing research where argument information is largely ignored in ED, despite its potential value. The hypothesis that arguments can disambiguate event triggers is intuitively strong and supported by the example provided in the introduction. This shift in focus is a significant contribution.
2. **Sound Technical Approach:** The proposed supervised attention mechanism is a logically sound approach to incorporating argument information. The use of “gold attentions” provides a clear and effective supervision signal for learning the importance of different words in the context of a trigger candidate. The architecture appears well-motivated and consistent with current neural network approaches to sequence labeling.
3. **Strong Empirical Results:** The experimental results on the ACE 2005 dataset are compelling. The authors demonstrate consistent improvements over existing state-of-the-art methods, and the ablation study (comparing ANN, ANN-S1, and ANN-S2) provides evidence that the proposed attention mechanism is indeed contributing to the improved performance. The augmentation with FrameNet further strengthens the results.
4. **Clear Presentation and Motivation:** The paper is well-written and clearly articulates the problem, proposed solution, and experimental setup. The motivation for the work is strong and the connection between the observed shortcomings of existing methods and the proposed approach is clearly explained.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Analysis of Attention Weights:** While the authors demonstrate that the attention mechanism improves performance, there is limited qualitative analysis of *what* the model is actually attending to. Visualizing the learned attention weights, particularly for ambiguous trigger words, would provide valuable insight into whether the model is indeed focusing on relevant arguments as intended.
2. **Dataset Dependency:** The results are primarily based on the ACE 2005 dataset, which is relatively small and may not generalize to other event detection datasets with different characteristics. Demonstrating performance on additional datasets would strengthen the claim that the proposed approach is broadly applicable.
3. **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** The choice of λ in the joint loss function (Equation 9) seems somewhat arbitrary. A more systematic investigation of the optimal λ value, and a discussion of its sensitivity, would be beneficial. It's unclear why different values were chosen for S1 and S2.
4. **Lack of Comparison to More Recent Models:** The comparisons are made against models published in 2013-2016. Given the rapid progress in NLP, it would be helpful to compare against more recent state-of-the-art models, potentially including those based on transformers (e.g., BERT, RoBERTa).

**Questions to Authors:**

1. Can you provide examples of attention weights learned by the model for ambiguous trigger words? Do the attention weights indeed focus on relevant arguments?
2. Have you experimented with other datasets besides ACE 2005? If so, what were the results? If not, do you plan to evaluate the model on other datasets?
3. What is the rationale behind the different λ values used for ANN-S1 and ANN-S2? Did you perform a hyperparameter search to determine the optimal values?
4. How would you characterize the computational complexity of the proposed approach compared to existing methods?



**Additional Comments:**

- The discussion of related work is fairly brief. It would be helpful to provide a more detailed overview of recent advances in event detection, including those based on transformers.
- The notation could be improved for clarity. For example, defining the dimensions of the matrices used in the attention mechanism would be helpful.
