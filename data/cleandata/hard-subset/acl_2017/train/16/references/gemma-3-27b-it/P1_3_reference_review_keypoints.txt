- Summary: Proposes supervised attention that explicitly uses annotated arguments to improve event detection, addressing bias in joint ED+AE models; shows SOTA on ACE2005 and gains with FrameNet augmentation.

- Contribution 1: Analyzes limitations of joint ED and AE models, arguing they underutilize arguments due to data imbalance and indirect usage.

- Contribution 2: Introduces a supervised attention-based ED model that leverages annotated argument information (S1 and S2 strategies for gold attention).

- Contribution 3: Demonstrates empirical improvements on ACE2005, claiming state-of-the-art performance.

- Assessment 1: Novel application of supervised attention focused on arguments is a valuable practical contribution.

- Assessment 2: Empirical results on ACE2005 are convincing and show strong performance gains.

- Assessment 3: Provides a useful critique of joint models that motivates the proposed explicit-argument approach.

- Strength 1: Strong empirical results with notable gains over prior methods.

- Strength 2: Clear motivation and illustrative example showing the benefit of argument information.

- Strength 3: Well-defined methodology with detailed architecture and attention supervision strategies.

- Strength 4: Thorough experimentation including ablations (ANN variants) and data augmentation (FrameNet).

- Weakness 1: Core components (neural ED + attention) are not novel; novelty is mainly in their specific application and combination.

- Weakness 2: Evaluation limited to ACE2005, leaving generalizability to other datasets/domains unclear.

- Weakness 3: Hyperparameter choices (e.g., λ=1.0 for S1, λ=5.0 for S2) lack justification and sensitivity analysis.

- Weakness 4: Presentation issue: “Skip-CNN” results in Table 3 lack context and comparability information.

- Question 1: Have you evaluated or considered evaluating the approach on other event detection datasets, and what challenges would arise?

- Question 2: Provide details on hyperparameter tuning ranges and why different λ values are optimal for S1 vs S2; how sensitive is performance to λ?

- Question 3: What is the computational overhead of the supervised attention component versus a baseline, and how does it scale?

- Question 4: Can you include qualitative error analysis showing which event types/triggers remain challenging and when argument info fails?

- Additional comment 1: Add discussion of related work on attention mechanisms across NLP to contextualize the approach.

- Additional comment 2: Include a small example showing how attention highlights relevant arguments for a specific trigger.

- Additional comment 3: Clarify the source and comparability of the “Skip-CNN” results in Table 3.
