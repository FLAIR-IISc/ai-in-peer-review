This paper proposes a neural network approach to event detection (ED) that explicitly incorporates argument information via a supervised attention mechanism. The authors motivate their work by observing that joint ED and argument extraction models have not significantly improved ED performance, and hypothesize this is due to data imbalance and the indirect use of argument information in existing joint models. They construct “gold attentions” based on annotated arguments to supervise the learning of the attention mechanism.

The primary contribution of this work, as I see it, is the empirical demonstration – albeit on a single, relatively small dataset – that explicitly modeling argument information *can* improve event detection performance. The supervised attention mechanism itself is a fairly standard technique, and the gold attention construction is a straightforward application of existing ideas. A secondary contribution is the observation that existing joint models appear to disproportionately benefit argument extraction, aligning with the authors’ hypothesis about data imbalance.

**Strengths:**

1. **Empirical Improvement:** The reported results on ACE 2005 show consistent improvements over strong baselines, including state-of-the-art methods. While the gains aren't enormous, they are statistically significant and suggest the proposed approach is worthwhile.
2. **Clear Motivation and Hypothesis:** The authors articulate a clear reason for investigating the explicit use of argument information in ED, based on limitations of previous joint models. The hypothesis regarding data imbalance is plausible and a good starting point for further investigation.
3. **Thorough Experimentation:** The authors present a reasonably thorough set of experiments, including ablation studies (ANN, ANN-S1, ANN-S2) and comparisons to several existing state-of-the-art systems. The inclusion of FrameNet augmentation is a good attempt to address data sparsity.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Generalizability:** The evaluation is confined to the ACE 2005 dataset. While a standard benchmark, its limited size and specific characteristics raise concerns about the generalizability of the findings. Event detection performance can vary significantly across domains and datasets.  The authors need to demonstrate that the method transfers to other datasets.
2. **Lack of Novelty in Method:** The core technical contributions – the use of a supervised attention mechanism and gold attention construction – are not particularly novel. Similar techniques have been successfully applied in various NLP tasks. The paper lacks a strong theoretical justification for *why* this particular approach should be effective beyond the intuitive idea that arguments provide useful clues.
3. **Attention Mechanism Details:** The choice of using the sum of attention vectors αw and αe feels somewhat arbitrary.  There's little justification given for this design decision, and other combination strategies (e.g., concatenation, weighted averaging) haven't been explored.  Furthermore, the hyperparameters for the Gaussian distribution used in S2 (µ=0.0, σ=1.0) were not sufficiently justified.
4. **Limited Error Analysis:** The paper lacks a detailed error analysis. Understanding *where* the model still struggles (e.g., which event types are most often misclassified, what kinds of arguments are most helpful) would provide valuable insights and suggest avenues for future work.



**Questions to Authors:**

1.  Have you experimented with different ways of combining the word and entity attention vectors (e.g., concatenation, weighted averaging) instead of simply summing them? If so, what were the results?
2.  Could you provide a more detailed error analysis, identifying the types of events for which the model performs poorly and the characteristics of those instances?
3.  How sensitive are the results to the hyperparameters of the Gaussian distribution used in strategy S2? Have you performed an ablation study to determine the optimal values for µ and σ?
4.  Have you considered evaluating your approach on other event detection datasets, such as those from SemEval or other shared tasks?  If not, what are the challenges of applying this method to other datasets?



**Additional Comments:**

The paper is well-written and clearly presented. The references are comprehensive. However, given the limited novelty of the method and the lack of evaluation on diverse datasets, I recommend a rejection with encouragement to expand the evaluation and explore more novel technical contributions. The empirical results are promising, but require further validation before a strong recommendation can be made.
