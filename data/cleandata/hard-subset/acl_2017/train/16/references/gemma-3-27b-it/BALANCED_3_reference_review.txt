This paper proposes a supervised attention mechanism to improve event detection (ED) by explicitly leveraging argument information. The authors argue that existing joint ED and argument extraction (AE) models haven’t effectively utilized argument information for ED, as they are often dominated by the larger number of argument annotations during training and pre-predict triggers without considering arguments. They introduce a model where attention weights are learned based on annotated arguments, effectively forcing the model to focus on argument-related words during trigger classification.

The main contributions of this work, as I see them, are: 1) A compelling analysis highlighting the limitations of current joint ED/AE models regarding effective utilization of argument information for the ED task, and a clear articulation of why arguments are largely ignored during trigger prediction. This is a valuable insight. 2) A supervised attention mechanism, specifically designed to incorporate argument information into the ED process. The two proposed strategies (S1 & S2) for constructing “gold” attention vectors are reasonable and offer different approaches to leveraging argument annotations. 3) Demonstrably improved performance on the ACE 2005 dataset compared to existing state-of-the-art methods, demonstrating the effectiveness of their approach.  Augmenting with FrameNet data further strengthens the results.

**Strengths:**

1. **Insightful Problem Analysis:** The paper clearly identifies a weakness in existing joint models – that argument information is not effectively used to *improve* event detection, but primarily benefits argument extraction. This is a crucial observation and forms a strong motivation for the proposed work. The authors provide a table illustrating the relatively small gains in ED when using joint methods, making their argument quite convincing.
2. **Clear and Well-Motivated Approach:** The proposed supervised attention mechanism is conceptually simple and well-motivated. The idea of using annotated arguments to guide attention during trigger classification is intuitive and aligns with the paper’s central argument. The description of the attention mechanisms and the joint training process is clear and easy to follow.
3. **Strong Empirical Results:** The experimental results on ACE 2005 are impressive. The proposed models outperform several strong baselines, including both pipeline and joint methods. The ablation studies (ANN, ANN-S1, ANN-S2) provide useful insights into the contribution of the supervised attention mechanism and the two strategies for constructing gold attention vectors. The inclusion of FrameNet data and the corresponding results further validate the robustness of the approach.
4. **Thorough Experimentation:**  The authors conducted a reasonably thorough set of experiments, including comparisons to state-of-the-art methods and ablation studies. They clearly describe the experimental setup, hyperparameter settings, and evaluation metrics. The use of statistical significance tests (t-test) strengthens the validity of the results.
5. **Well-Written and Organized:** The paper is well-written, logically organized, and easy to read. The introduction clearly states the problem and the proposed solution. The related work section provides a good overview of existing approaches.



**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Novelty of Attention Mechanism:** While the application of supervised attention to leverage argument information for ED is a reasonable idea, the attention mechanism itself isn't particularly novel. Attention mechanisms are widely used in NLP. The core novelty lies in *how* the attention is supervised with argument information, but the underlying mechanism is standard. This point could be acknowledged more directly.
2. **Sensitivity to Gold Attention Construction:** The performance differences between ANN-S1 and ANN-S2 suggest that the choice of strategy for constructing gold attention vectors is important. The paper doesn't fully explore the reasons behind these differences or investigate other potential strategies. A more detailed analysis of the impact of different gold attention construction methods would be beneficial. What happens if the arguments are incorrectly annotated?
3. **ACE 2005 Dataset Limitation:** The experiments are limited to the ACE 2005 dataset. While this is a widely used benchmark, it’s relatively small and may not fully represent the diversity of real-world event detection scenarios. Evaluating the approach on other datasets or domains would strengthen the generalizability claims.
4. **Lack of Error Analysis:** The paper lacks a detailed error analysis. Understanding *where* the model still fails (e.g., specific event types, ambiguous triggers) would provide valuable insights for future research. Analyzing the attention weights learned by the model could also reveal interesting patterns and inform further improvements.
5. **Hyperparameter Tuning Detail:** Although hyperparameters are mentioned, a more detailed description of the tuning process would be helpful.



**Questions to Authors:**

1. Could you elaborate on why you chose a simple additive attention mechanism instead of more complex attention variants (e.g., self-attention, multi-head attention)? Did you experiment with other attention mechanisms?
2. What is the impact of noisy or incorrect argument annotations on the performance of your model? Have you conducted any experiments to evaluate the robustness of your approach to annotation errors?
3. Could you provide a more detailed analysis of the differences between ANN-S1 and ANN-S2? Why does S2 achieve higher recall at the cost of precision?
4. Did you explore methods for dynamically weighting the loss function between the ED task and the attention supervision task (i.e., adjusting λ during training)?

**Additional Comments:**

*   The references are well-formatted and comprehensive.
*   The figure captions are clear and informative.
*   Minor stylistic improvements could be made to enhance readability (e.g., breaking up long paragraphs).



**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper that addresses an important problem in event detection. The authors present a clear and well-motivated approach that achieves impressive results on a challenging benchmark dataset. While the novelty of the attention mechanism itself is limited, the application to leveraging argument information for ED is a valuable contribution. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions, specifically addressing the weaknesses outlined above. The authors should expand on the discussion of the attention mechanism's novelty, investigate alternative gold attention construction strategies, and consider including an error analysis and evaluation on additional datasets.
