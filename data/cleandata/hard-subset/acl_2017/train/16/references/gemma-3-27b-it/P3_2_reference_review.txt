## Review for ACL Submission: "Exploiting Argument Information to Improve Event Detection via Supervised Attention Mechanisms"

**Summary:**

This paper tackles the event detection (ED) task, arguing that leveraging event arguments, often ignored in existing methods, can improve performance, particularly for ambiguous trigger words. The authors identify a weakness in joint ED-AE models – they often prioritize argument extraction due to data imbalance – and propose a supervised attention mechanism that explicitly focuses on argument information during event detection. They demonstrate improvements over baseline and state-of-the-art methods on the ACE 2005 dataset, and further augment performance by incorporating data from FrameNet.

**Main Contributions (as I see them):**

1. **Empirical Demonstration of Argument Utility for ED:**  While intuitively plausible, the paper provides a solid empirical justification for incorporating argument information *specifically* for the ED task.  The ablation studies clearly show that focusing on arguments (even with a simple supervised attention mechanism) yields significant gains over methods ignoring them.
2. **Identification of a Limitation in Joint ED-AE Models:** The authors highlight a critical issue – the data imbalance between triggers and arguments in standard datasets – that likely hinders the performance of joint models on ED. This is a valuable observation for the community.
3. **A Simple Yet Effective Supervised Attention Mechanism:** The proposed approach, while not conceptually groundbreaking, is a practical and effective way to leverage argument information. The use of 'gold' attentions as supervision is a straightforward and effective technique.

**Strengths:**

1. **Strong Empirical Results:** The paper consistently outperforms strong baselines and state-of-the-art methods on ACE 2005. The gains are statistically significant and demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed approach. The augmentation with FrameNet data further validates the approach.
2. **Clear Motivation and Problem Statement:** The paper clearly articulates the problem – the underutilization of argument information in ED – and provides a compelling motivation for their approach. The example with "fired" is particularly effective in illustrating the benefit of argument awareness.
3. **Thorough Evaluation:** The authors provide a comprehensive evaluation, including ablation studies (ANN, ANN-ENT, ANN-S1, ANN-S2) that help to isolate the impact of different components of their model. This rigor is commendable.
4. **Well-Written and Organized:**  The paper is generally well-written, logically structured, and easy to follow. The description of the method is clear and concise.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Novelty of the Core Method:** The core technical contribution – supervised attention – is not entirely novel. Supervised attention has been used extensively in other NLP tasks. The novelty lies in applying it *specifically* to the argument-aware ED problem, but the underlying mechanism itself is not a breakthrough.
2. **Dependence on Gold Annotations:** The supervised attention relies heavily on the availability of high-quality argument annotations.  While ACE 2005 provides these, the approach may not generalize well to scenarios with limited or noisy argument annotations. The authors acknowledge this implicitly by augmenting with FrameNet data. Further discussion on robustness to annotation quality would be valuable.
3. **Limited Analysis of Attention Weights:**  While the paper *uses* attention, there's a lack of deeper analysis of *what* the attention mechanism is actually learning. Visualizing attention weights for specific examples, or conducting a qualitative analysis of which arguments receive the most attention, would provide greater insights into the model's behavior.
4. **Lack of Comparison to More Recent ED Models:** The comparison to state-of-the-art is somewhat dated.  There have been advancements in ED based on transformers and pre-trained language models (e.g., BERT) since the cited SOTA papers. A comparison to these more recent methods would strengthen the paper.



**Questions to Authors:**

1. **Robustness to Annotation Noise:** How sensitive is the performance of your model to errors or inconsistencies in the argument annotations?  Have you conducted any experiments to assess this sensitivity?
2. **Attention Weight Analysis:** Could you provide examples of attention weights learned by your model, and discuss what these weights reveal about the model's reasoning process?  Are there any surprising or counterintuitive patterns?
3. **Comparison to Transformer-Based Models:**  How does your model compare to more recent ED models based on transformers (e.g., BERT)?  If you haven't conducted this comparison, what are the anticipated challenges and potential benefits of integrating your approach with a transformer-based architecture?
4. **Scalability:** The method seems to have a computational complexity related to the number of words in the context window. How does performance scale as the context window size increases?



**Additional Comments:**

*   The notation could be slightly improved for clarity (e.g., consistently defining symbols before use).
*   The sentence "Only 10% of triggers in ACE 2005 are entities" feels a little out of place and needs more contextualization.




**Overall Assessment:**

This is a solid and well-executed paper that makes a valuable contribution to the field of event detection. While the core technical innovation is incremental, the empirical results are strong, and the paper identifies an important limitation of existing joint models.  The authors effectively demonstrate the utility of argument information for ED. I recommend **acceptance** with minor revisions addressing the weaknesses and questions raised above.
