- Paper proposes using supervised attention guided by gold argument annotations to improve event detection (ED), evaluated on ACE 2005 with strong gains.  
- Key contribution: analysis showing joint AE+ED models underuse arguments due to data imbalance and indirect argument use.  
- Key contribution: a supervised attention mechanism that explicitly prioritizes argument words for ED.  
- Key contribution: systematic comparison of two attention strategies (S1: higher precision, S2: higher recall).  
- Strength: insightful and well-supported analysis explaining why joint methods fail to improve ED.  
- Strength: strong experimental results and statistically significant improvement over prior state-of-the-art (ANN-S2 F1 71.7%).  
- Strength: clear, simple methodological contribution with practical implementation details and ablation studies.  
- Weakness: supervised attention mechanism is incremental and closely resembles prior supervised attention work (e.g., Mi et al., 2016).  
- Weakness: reliance on gold-standard argument information for training limits practical deployment; no evaluation when arguments are unavailable.  
- Weakness: FrameNet augmentation yields only marginal gains, weakening the claim that external data alleviates sparsity.  
- Weakness: lack of error analysis showing *why* the approach resolves ambiguous triggers in concrete cases.  
- Question: How does the model perform when argument information is not available during training (pipeline scenario)?  
- Question: Why did FrameNet augmentation produce only marginal improvement compared to prior work (e.g., Liu et al., 2016a)?  
- Question: Can the authors compare against more recent transformer-based ED methods?  
- Recommendation: Accept with minor revisions to better address novelty concerns and practical limitations of needing argument annotations for training.
