

# Review of "Exploiting Argument Information to Improve Event Detection via Supervised Attention Mechanisms"

## Summary of the Paper
This paper addresses Event Detection (ED), a key subtask of Event Extraction (EE), by proposing a novel approach that explicitly leverages argument information to improve ED performance. The authors identify a critical limitation in existing joint models for EE (which tackle both ED and Argument Extraction simultaneously): while these models achieve improvements on Argument Extraction (AE), they provide minimal gains for ED. The authors argue this is because existing joint methods primarily use arguments for AE, not for ED. To address this, they propose a supervised attention mechanism that incorporates argument information directly into ED. The approach constructs "gold attention" vectors based on annotated arguments (using two strategies: S1 focusing only on argument words, S2 considering words around arguments) to train an attention mechanism that prioritizes argument words during ED. The method is evaluated on the ACE 2005 benchmark, showing significant improvements over state-of-the-art approaches.

## Main Contributions (as I see them)
1. **Insightful analysis of joint models' limitations**: The paper correctly identifies and thoroughly analyzes why existing joint models fail to effectively utilize argument information for ED, which is a significant contribution to the field.
2. **Novel supervised attention mechanism for ED**: The proposed approach of using argument information through supervised attention mechanisms provides a clear, effective way to incorporate argument information into ED without requiring joint training with AE.
3. **Systematic evaluation of attention strategies**: The paper systematically explores two different attention strategies (S1 and S2) and demonstrates that S2 (considering words around arguments) generally outperforms S1, providing valuable insights for future work.

## Strengths

1. **Significant and Well-Motivated Problem Identification**: The authors correctly identify a critical gap in existing joint models for event extraction. Their analysis of why joint models don't effectively use argument information for ED (due to data imbalance and implementation choices) is thorough and convincing. The "fired" example in the introduction effectively illustrates the problem, and the analysis in Table 1 provides strong empirical evidence for their claim.

2. **Comprehensive and Rigorous Experimental Evaluation**: The paper conducts a well-designed set of experiments on the widely used ACE 2005 benchmark. The ablation studies (Table 2) convincingly demonstrate that argument information is crucial for ED, and the comparison with multiple state-of-the-art methods (Table 3) shows consistent improvements. The inclusion of FrameNet augmentation (Table 4) further strengthens the argument about the importance of data sparsity in ED.

3. **Clear and Well-Structured Presentation**: The paper is well-written, with a logical flow from problem statement to approach to evaluation. The architecture diagram (Figure 1) effectively illustrates the proposed approach, and the tables (especially Tables 2-4) clearly present the experimental results and comparisons. The authors provide sufficient technical details for reproducibility.

4. **Practical and Complementary Approach**: The proposed supervised attention mechanism can be easily integrated into existing ED systems (as shown with the ANN baseline), making it a practical contribution that doesn't require a complete overhaul of current approaches. This makes the work immediately applicable to the community.

5. **Strong Theoretical Justification**: The paper provides a solid theoretical foundation for their approach, explaining why argument information is useful for ED (particularly for ambiguous triggers) and how their attention mechanism captures this relationship.

## Weaknesses

1. **Limited Novelty in Core Technique**: While the insight about leveraging argument information for ED is novel, the core technique (supervised attention) is not particularly innovative. Supervised attention mechanisms have been used in other NLP tasks (e.g., machine translation, text summarization), and the paper doesn't sufficiently distinguish how their application to ED differs from or improves upon existing attention mechanisms. This makes the technical contribution somewhat incremental rather than transformative.

2. **Potential Data Leakage Concerns**: The paper constructs "gold attention" vectors based on annotated arguments to supervise the attention mechanism during training. However, this means the model has access to information (argument positions) that wouldn't be available during real-world ED (where arguments are not known at test time). The paper doesn't adequately address this potential data leakage issue or provide evidence that the model doesn't overfit to argument information that's only present in training data.

3. **Insufficient Comparison with Attention-Based ED Approaches**: While the paper compares against multiple state-of-the-art methods, it doesn't directly compare against other attention-based ED approaches that might have used attention in a similar way. For example, the Skip-CNN approach (Nguyen and Grishman, 2016) uses non-consecutive convolution, which might implicitly capture some attention-like effects. The paper would be stronger with a more detailed comparison against such methods.

4. **Lack of Detailed Error Analysis**: The paper presents strong overall results but doesn't provide a detailed error analysis to understand *why* their approach works better. For instance, they don't analyze which specific event types or ambiguous trigger words benefit most from their approach, or where their method fails. This would provide deeper insights into the limitations and strengths of their approach.

5. **Limited Discussion on Practical Implementation**: The paper doesn't discuss the practical implementation challenges of their approach. For example, how would this approach scale to larger datasets or different event types? How would it handle cases where argument information is sparse or missing? These are important considerations for real-world deployment.

## Questions to Authors

1. The paper uses "gold attention" vectors constructed from annotated arguments to supervise the attention mechanism during training. However, in a real-world ED system, argument information would not be available. How do you ensure that this supervised attention mechanism doesn't overfit to the argument information that's only present in the training data and not available at test time?

2. The paper mentions using FrameNet to augment training data, but doesn't provide details on how the FrameNet data is processed (especially regarding the lack of argument and entity information). Could you elaborate on how you handled this processing, particularly how you identified triggers and arguments from FrameNet?

3. The paper claims that the proposed approach outperforms state-of-the-art methods, but doesn't provide specific examples of ambiguous trigger words where their method succeeded while others failed. Could you provide a few concrete examples from the ACE 2005 dataset that demonstrate the advantage of your approach over existing methods?

## Overall Recommendation
This is a well-written paper that makes a valuable contribution by identifying a significant limitation in existing joint models for event extraction and proposing a practical solution. The experimental results are strong and demonstrate clear improvements over state-of-the-art approaches. However, the core technical contribution (supervised attention) is somewhat incremental, and the potential data leakage issue needs to be addressed.

I recommend **weak acceptance** with the following conditions:
1. The authors need to address the data leakage concern by providing a more thorough explanation of how their approach avoids overfitting to argument information.
2. The authors should include a detailed error analysis to better understand where their approach excels and where it might have limitations.
3. The authors should compare against more attention-based ED approaches (like Skip-CNN) to better position their work in the literature.

With these revisions, this paper would make a strong contribution to the field of event extraction. The insight about leveraging argument information for ED is important and the proposed approach is practical and effective.
