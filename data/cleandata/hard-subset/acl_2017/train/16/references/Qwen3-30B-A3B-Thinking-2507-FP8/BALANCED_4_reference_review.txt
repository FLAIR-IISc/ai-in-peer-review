

# Review of "Exploiting Argument Information to Improve Event Detection via Supervised Attention Mechanisms"

## Summary of the Paper
This paper addresses event detection (ED), a key subtask of event extraction, by proposing a novel approach that explicitly leverages argument information to improve performance. The authors identify a key limitation in existing joint event extraction methods: while these methods use argument information, they primarily benefit argument extraction (AE) rather than event detection (ED). The paper argues that argument information can provide crucial clues for ambiguous trigger words (e.g., "fired" could be "End-Position" or "Attack"), and proposes a supervised attention mechanism that explicitly focuses on argument words during ED. The authors introduce two strategies for constructing "gold attention" vectors from annotated arguments (S1: focus only on argument words; S2: focus on arguments and surrounding words) and train an attention mechanism using these as supervision. The approach is evaluated on the ACE 2005 benchmark and augmented with FrameNet data.

## Main Contributions
1. The paper identifies and analyzes a key limitation in existing joint event extraction methods: argument information is not effectively utilized for ED, only for AE.
2. The authors propose a supervised attention mechanism that explicitly leverages argument information to improve ED performance, with two specific strategies for constructing gold attention vectors.
3. The experimental results demonstrate significant improvements over state-of-the-art methods on the ACE 2005 benchmark, with statistical significance confirmed by t-tests.

## Strengths

1. **Insightful Analysis of Existing Methods**: The paper provides a clear and well-supported analysis of why joint methods fail to improve ED performance (Table 1 and the following discussion). The authors correctly identify that joint models suffer from data imbalance (more annotated arguments than triggers) and that arguments are used indirectly for AE rather than ED. This analysis addresses a significant gap in the literature that has been overlooked.

2. **Strong Experimental Results**: The proposed approach achieves state-of-the-art results on the ACE 2005 benchmark (71.7 F1 for ANN-S2 vs. 70.7 for the previous best), with statistical significance confirmed by t-tests. The results in Table 3 clearly demonstrate the effectiveness of leveraging argument information, with ANN-S2 outperforming all baselines. The comparison between S1 and S2 also provides useful insights about precision/recall tradeoffs.

3. **Thorough Analysis of Argument Information's Importance**: Section 4.2 provides a compelling analysis that establishes the importance of argument information specifically for ED. By comparing systems that use argument information (ANN-Gold1/2) versus entity information (ANN-ENT), the authors convincingly show that entity information alone is insufficient and that argument information is necessary for improved ED performance.

4. **Practical Consideration of Data Sparseness**: The paper appropriately addresses the data sparseness issue in ED by using FrameNet data for augmentation (Section 4.4). The results show that their approach benefits from external data, with ANN-S2 + FrameNet achieving 71.9 F1. This demonstrates practical applicability beyond just the ACE 2005 dataset.

5. **Clear and Well-Structured Presentation**: The paper is well-written with a logical flow from problem statement to methodology to evaluation. The architecture (Figure 1) and experimental setup are clearly explained, making the approach easy to follow.

## Weaknesses

1. **Novelty Claim Overstated**: While the application to event detection is novel, the core mechanism (supervised attention) builds directly on existing work (Mi et al., 2016). The paper doesn't sufficiently differentiate its approach from existing attention-based methods for ED, such as Liu et al. (2016a). A more detailed comparison with these methods would strengthen the novelty claim.

2. **Insufficient Detail on Gold Attention Construction**: The paper lacks sufficient detail on how gold attention vectors are constructed from argument annotations. Figure 2 provides a limited example, but the paper doesn't explain how this would generalize to contexts with multiple arguments, different argument roles, or arguments that span multiple words. This is crucial for reproducibility and understanding the approach.

3. **Inadequate Comparison with Joint Methods**: The paper states that joint methods "only make remarkable improvements to AE, but insignificant to ED" but doesn't provide a clear comparison between their approach and existing joint methods (e.g., Nguyen et al., 2016). It's unclear how their approach differs from these joint methods or why it would be better. A direct comparison would strengthen the paper's claims.

4. **Limited Analysis of Data Sparseness**: While the paper claims "data sparseness is an important issue of ED," it doesn't provide a clear analysis of this phenomenon. The poor performance of ANN (64.6 F1) is mentioned, but it's not clear if this is due to data sparseness or other factors. A more detailed analysis of data sparseness and how their approach specifically addresses it would be valuable.

5. **Lack of Scalability Analysis**: The paper's experiments are limited to the ACE 2005 dataset, with no analysis of how the approach would perform on more complex event structures or on other datasets. A comparison with other datasets would strengthen the paper's claims about generalizability.

## Questions to Authors

1. How exactly are the gold attention vectors constructed from the annotated arguments? Specifically, what is the mapping from argument mentions to attention values in the context? The paper mentions "an example of S1" in Figure 2, but it's not clear how this would generalize to all cases, especially for contexts with multiple arguments or different argument roles.

2. The paper states that "the data sparseness is an important issue of ED" but doesn't provide evidence beyond the poor performance of ANN. How do you measure data sparseness in your context, and how does your approach specifically address this issue?

3. How does your supervised attention mechanism differ from existing attention-based methods for ED, such as Liu et al. (2016a)? What specific improvements does it offer over these methods?

4. The paper uses FrameNet data for augmentation but only uses core frame elements. What would happen if you used all frame elements? Why did you choose to use only core elements?

5. The paper claims that argument information is crucial for ED, but it doesn't analyze which types of events benefit most from argument information. For example, do events with clear arguments (e.g., "Die" with "He" as Person) benefit more than events with ambiguous arguments?

## Overall Recommendation

The paper addresses an important limitation in existing event extraction methods and proposes a novel approach that effectively leverages argument information for event detection. The experimental results are strong and demonstrate significant improvements over state-of-the-art methods. However, the novelty claim is overstated, and the paper lacks sufficient detail on key aspects of the method (gold attention construction) and comparison with relevant prior work.

I recommend **weak acceptance** with the understanding that the authors should address the weaknesses identified above, particularly regarding novelty, methodological details, and comparison with existing approaches. The paper has good potential to be accepted after these revisions, as it makes a meaningful contribution to the field with strong experimental results.

The authors should clarify the novelty of their approach, provide more detail on how gold attention vectors are constructed, and provide a more thorough comparison with existing attention-based methods for ED. Addressing these issues would significantly strengthen the paper and make it a valuable contribution to the ACL community.
