

# ACL Review

## Summary of the Paper

This paper proposes a novel approach to event detection (ED) by explicitly leveraging argument information through supervised attention mechanisms. The authors argue that existing joint methods for event extraction (which handle both event detection and argument extraction) don't effectively use argument information for ED, as the argument extraction component benefits from trigger information but not vice versa. Their key insight is that argument information provides crucial clues for identifying and categorizing event triggers, especially for ambiguous trigger words. They introduce a neural network model that uses supervised attention to give more attention to argument words when detecting events, with two strategies for constructing gold attention vectors based on annotated arguments (S1: focus only on argument words; S2: focus on arguments and words around them).

## Main Contributions (as I see them)

1. **Conceptual insight**: Identifying that argument information can significantly improve event detection performance, which is a previously overlooked opportunity in the field.
2. **Empirical validation**: Demonstrating through systematic experiments that argument information is crucial for ED, with clear improvements over state-of-the-art methods.
3. **Practical approach**: Proposing a simple yet effective method to incorporate argument information into event detection via supervised attention mechanisms.

## Strengths

1. **Novel conceptual contribution**: The paper identifies a fundamental problem in existing joint event extraction approaches (that they don't effectively use argument information for ED) and proposes a novel solution. This is a significant conceptual contribution that addresses a real gap in the literature. The authors provide clear examples (e.g., the "fired" trigger ambiguity) that effectively illustrate why argument information matters for ED.

2. **Strong empirical results**: The paper demonstrates clear and significant improvements over state-of-the-art methods (ANN-S2 achieves 71.7% F1 on ACE 2005, compared to the previous best of 70.7% for Liu's FN-Based approach). The results are statistically significant (p < 0.05), and the authors provide a thorough comparison with multiple baselines.

3. **Clear demonstration of necessity**: Section 4.2 provides compelling evidence that argument information is necessary for good ED performance. The comparison between ANN-Gold1/2 (using argument information) and ANN-ENT (using only entity information) clearly shows that entities alone aren't sufficient, and argument information is crucial.

4. **Practical implementation**: The proposed method is relatively simple to implement and can be easily integrated with existing ED systems, increasing its practical value. The supervised attention mechanism is straightforward and doesn't require major architectural changes.

5. **Well-structured argument**: The paper is well-written and logically structured, with a clear problem statement, insightful analysis of existing methods, and thorough experimental validation.

## Weaknesses

1. **Limited novelty of the core mechanism**: While the *application* of supervised attention to leverage argument information for ED is novel, the attention mechanism itself is not particularly novel (attention mechanisms are widely used in NLP, and supervised attention has been used in other contexts like machine translation). The paper doesn't sufficiently differentiate their approach from existing attention mechanisms.

2. **Lack of theoretical justification**: The paper provides strong empirical evidence but doesn't explore the underlying reasons why argument information helps for ED. A deeper theoretical or linguistic justification would strengthen the contribution.

3. **Vague implementation details**: Some key implementation details are not sufficiently explained. For example, it's unclear how the "gold attention" vectors are constructed in practice, or how the system handles cases where the trigger word is not part of an argument (which the authors acknowledge happens for 90% of triggers).

4. **Limited comparison with recent work**: The paper compares with several state-of-the-art methods but doesn't include some of the most recent approaches (e.g., 2017-2018 papers that might have improved on the cited methods).

5. **Lack of failure analysis**: The paper doesn't provide analysis of cases where the model fails, which would help understand the limitations of the approach.

## Questions to Authors

1. The paper mentions that "argument information is crucial to ED" but doesn't provide a deep theoretical justification. Can you provide more insight into why argument information is helpful for ED from a linguistic or computational perspective?

2. How does your supervised attention mechanism differ from other attention-based approaches used in event extraction (e.g., in Liu et al., 2016a)? What specific advantages does your approach offer?

3. The paper states that "only 10% of triggers in ACE 2005 are entities," yet your model uses entity information in the attention mechanism. How do you handle cases where the trigger is not an entity (which happens for 90% of triggers)?

4. The paper compares with several state-of-the-art methods, but doesn't include some recent approaches (e.g., "Joint Event Detection with Contextualized Word Representations" by Zhao et al., 2018). Could you clarify why these methods weren't included in your comparison?

5. The paper shows that ANN-S2 outperforms ANN-S1 but has slightly lower precision. Can you provide more insight into why S2 achieves higher recall at the cost of precision, and whether this trade-off could be addressed in future work?

## Overall Recommendation

I recommend **acceptance** of this paper.

While the attention mechanism itself isn't novel, the paper's core contribution—using argument information explicitly for event detection—is a significant conceptual insight that addresses a real problem in the field. The authors provide strong empirical evidence for their approach, demonstrating clear improvements over state-of-the-art methods with statistically significant results.

As a reviewer who prioritizes novelty and bold approaches over methodological perfection, I find this paper's core insight to be both novel and impactful. The paper makes a compelling case for why argument information should be used for ED, provides a simple yet effective method to do so, and demonstrates its effectiveness through thorough experiments.

The minor weaknesses (limited novelty of the mechanism, lack of theoretical justification) are outweighed by the paper's strong conceptual contribution and empirical results. The paper is well-written, clearly structured, and makes a meaningful contribution to the field of event extraction.
