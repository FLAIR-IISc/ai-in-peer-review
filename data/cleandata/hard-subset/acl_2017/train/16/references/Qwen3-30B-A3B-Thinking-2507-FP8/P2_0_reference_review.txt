

# Review of "Exploiting Argument Information to Improve Event Detection via Supervised Attention Mechanisms"

## Summary
This paper proposes a novel approach to event detection (ED) that explicitly leverages argument information through a supervised attention mechanism. The authors argue that existing methods either ignore argument information or use it indirectly in joint models that primarily benefit argument extraction (AE) rather than ED. Their approach trains an attention mechanism using gold-standard argument information to focus on argument words when detecting event triggers. They introduce two strategies for constructing "gold attention vectors" (S1: only attention to arguments; S2: attention to arguments and surrounding words) and demonstrate that their method (ANN-S2) achieves state-of-the-art performance on ACE 2005 (71.7% F1).

## Main Contributions
1. **Insight on argument information for ED**: The paper effectively identifies and demonstrates that argument information is underutilized in ED and provides crucial clues for ambiguous trigger words.
2. **Supervised attention mechanism**: The core technical contribution is the supervised attention mechanism that trains attention weights using argument information as supervision.
3. **Systematic evaluation of attention strategies**: The paper thoroughly compares different attention strategies and their impact on precision/recall trade-offs.

## Strengths

1. **Strong problem statement and motivation**: The paper clearly articulates the gap in existing literature—joint models don't properly leverage argument information for ED, despite arguments being crucial for ambiguous triggers. The "fired" example effectively illustrates the problem, making the motivation compelling.

2. **Rigorous empirical evaluation**: The experimental results are convincing and well-structured. The ablation studies (Table 2) conclusively demonstrate that argument information significantly improves ED performance compared to methods that don't use it. The main results (Table 3) show statistically significant improvements over state-of-the-art methods (71.7% vs. 69.4% F1 for Liu et al. 2016), with a t-test confirming significance.

3. **Practical implementation and integration**: The proposed approach is relatively simple to implement and integrates well with existing ED frameworks. The authors provide clear implementation details and hyper-parameter settings, making it easy for others to replicate.

4. **Ablation studies demonstrating value of argument information**: The paper systematically compares approaches with and without argument information (ANN vs. ANN-Gold1/2), providing strong evidence that the argument information itself is the key differentiator, not just the attention mechanism.

## Weaknesses

1. **Limited novelty of the attention mechanism**: While the application to ED is novel, the supervised attention mechanism itself is not innovative. Supervised attention has been used in other NLP tasks (e.g., neural machine translation by Mi et al. 2016), and the paper doesn't sufficiently differentiate their approach from these prior applications. The main contribution is the application to ED with argument supervision, not the mechanism itself.

2. **Inadequate comparison to recent attention-based ED work**: The paper compares to state-of-the-art methods but doesn't adequately address recent attention-based approaches like Skip-CNN (Nguyen and Grishman, 2016), which also uses attention mechanisms. The comparison in Table 3 is somewhat superficial, as it doesn't show how their approach differs from these attention-based baselines.

3. **Lack of qualitative analysis**: The paper lacks qualitative examples showing how the supervised attention mechanism changes the model's behavior. Providing specific examples where the model correctly identifies an event trigger because it focused on argument words would strengthen the argument about the value of argument information.

4. **Insufficient discussion of limitations**: The paper doesn't adequately address limitations of the approach. For instance, how does the method perform when argument information is scarce or noisy? How does it handle cases where arguments don't provide clear clues for event type? This is particularly important since the approach relies on argument annotations.

## Questions to Authors

1. How does your supervised attention mechanism compare to other attention mechanisms used in recent ED work (e.g., the attention mechanisms in Skip-CNN or other recent neural models)?

2. Can you provide specific qualitative examples showing how the supervised attention mechanism specifically helps with ambiguous trigger words (like "fired" in your example)?

3. How robust is your approach when argument information is not available (e.g., for new domains where arguments are not annotated)?

## Overall Assessment

This is a solid paper that makes a valuable contribution to event detection by demonstrating how argument information can be effectively leveraged for ED. The empirical results are strong and well-supported by ablation studies. While the core attention mechanism isn't novel, the application to ED with argument supervision is innovative and well-justified. The paper would be strengthened by addressing the weaknesses noted above, particularly by providing qualitative examples and a more thorough comparison to recent attention-based ED approaches. The contribution is significant but not groundbreaking, making it a good fit for ACL as a solid technical contribution to a well-established area.

I recommend acceptance with minor revisions to address the weaknesses mentioned above, particularly regarding the novelty discussion and qualitative analysis. The paper makes a clear and valuable contribution to the field of event detection.
