

# ACL Review: "Exploiting Argument Information to Improve Event Detection via Supervised Attention Mechanisms"

## Summary of the Paper
This paper addresses the task of Event Detection (ED), which involves identifying event triggers and categorizing them into event types. The authors argue that event arguments (participants in an event) provide valuable clues for ED, especially for ambiguous trigger words, but most existing methods ignore this information. They propose a neural network-based approach that uses a supervised attention mechanism to explicitly leverage argument information for ED. The key idea is to construct "gold attentions" from annotated arguments to guide the attention mechanism during training, so that argument words receive more attention. The authors evaluate their approach on the ACE 2005 dataset, comparing with several state-of-the-art methods, and demonstrate that their method achieves better performance, particularly with their proposed supervised attention mechanisms.

## Main Contributions
1. **Identification of a key gap in existing joint event extraction methods**: The paper clearly identifies that existing joint methods for event extraction (which handle both ED and argument extraction) do not effectively use argument information to improve ED, despite the potential value of argument information for ED.
2. **Novel supervised attention mechanism for ED**: The authors propose a supervised attention mechanism that explicitly incorporates argument information into ED, using gold attention vectors constructed from annotated arguments to guide the attention weights during training.
3. **Systematic evaluation of attention strategies**: The paper introduces and evaluates two different strategies for constructing gold attention vectors (focusing only on argument words vs. focusing on argument words and surrounding words), demonstrating their effectiveness through ablation studies and comparisons.

## Strengths

1. **Clear identification of a meaningful research gap**: The paper effectively demonstrates the problem with existing joint methods through a compelling example (the word "fired" being ambiguous between "End-Position" and "Attack"). The authors convincingly argue that argument information should be explicitly used for ED, not just for argument extraction. This is a significant contribution to the field as it addresses a fundamental limitation in current approaches.

2. **Strong empirical results on a standard benchmark**: The proposed method achieves state-of-the-art results on the ACE 2005 benchmark (ANN-S2 achieves 71.7 F1, outperforming previous best of 70.7). The improvements are statistically significant (as indicated by t-tests), and the ablation studies (Table 2) provide clear evidence that argument information is valuable for ED, with the supervised attention mechanisms making a substantial difference.

3. **Thorough analysis of attention strategies**: The paper systematically investigates two different attention strategies (S1 and S2), providing insights into their trade-offs (S1 achieves higher precision, S2 achieves higher recall). This shows careful consideration of different ways to leverage argument information and adds depth to the contribution.

4. **Clear explanation of the method**: The paper provides a detailed explanation of the proposed approach, including the architecture (Figure 1), the supervised attention mechanism, and the gold attention construction strategies. The mathematical formulations are well-presented and easy to follow.

5. **Effective use of FrameNet data for augmentation**: The paper demonstrates that their approach benefits from FrameNet data augmentation, achieving further improvements (71.9 F1 with FrameNet vs. 71.7 without), which provides additional evidence for the effectiveness of their method.

## Weaknesses

1. **Lack of comparison with recent state-of-the-art methods**: The paper compares with methods up to 2016 (e.g., Nguyen et al., 2016), but the field has advanced significantly since then. Recent transformer-based approaches (e.g., BERT-based models) have achieved substantial improvements in event detection. Without comparison to these methods, it's unclear how the proposed approach stands against current state-of-the-art. This is a major limitation as ACL expects submissions to be evaluated against the most recent advances.

2. **Arbitrary choice of attention strategies**: The paper proposes two strategies for constructing gold attention vectors (S1 and S2), but the choice appears somewhat arbitrary. The authors don't provide a strong theoretical justification for why these specific strategies would work better than alternatives (e.g., why use a Gaussian distribution for S2?). This weakens the contribution as the proposed strategies lack a principled foundation.

3. **Limited evaluation on multiple datasets**: The paper only evaluates on ACE 2005, which is a relatively small dataset. Without testing on additional datasets (e.g., TAC KBP, ERE, or more recent benchmarks), it's unclear whether the proposed method generalizes well to different domains and event types. This limits the perceived impact of the work.

4. **Insufficient discussion of failure cases**: The paper doesn't analyze cases where the proposed method fails. Understanding the limitations and failure modes would provide valuable insights for future work and strengthen the paper's contribution.

5. **Inadequate discussion of data sparseness**: While the paper mentions data sparseness as a problem for unsupervised attention, it doesn't provide a thorough analysis of how the supervised attention mechanism specifically addresses this issue compared to other approaches (e.g., data augmentation techniques).

## Questions to Authors

1. How does your approach compare with recent state-of-the-art methods that use transformer-based models (e.g., BERT) for event detection? Have you considered integrating your supervised attention mechanism with such models?

2. Could you provide a stronger theoretical justification for your choice of attention strategies (S1 and S2)? Why did you choose a Gaussian distribution for S2, and why not use a different distribution or a more adaptive weighting scheme?

3. Have you evaluated your method on additional event detection datasets (e.g., TAC KBP, ERE) to demonstrate its generalizability beyond ACE 2005? If not, could you discuss how you would expect your method to perform on other datasets?

4. The paper mentions that data sparseness is a problem for unsupervised attention mechanisms. Could you quantify how much of the improvement from your supervised attention mechanism is due to better handling of data sparseness versus the explicit use of argument information?

## Overall Recommendation

This paper addresses a meaningful gap in event detection research and presents a novel approach to leveraging argument information. The empirical results are strong on the ACE 2005 benchmark, and the ablation studies provide good evidence for the value of argument information. However, the lack of comparison with recent state-of-the-art methods and the limited evaluation on a single dataset are significant weaknesses that prevent the paper from meeting ACL's high standards.

**Decision: Reject (with strong recommendation for revision)**

The paper has potential but requires substantial revision to address the weaknesses identified above. Specifically, the authors should:

1. Compare their approach with recent transformer-based state-of-the-art methods for event detection.
2. Provide a more principled justification for their attention strategies.
3. Evaluate their method on at least one additional event detection dataset.
4. Discuss failure cases to better understand the limitations of their approach.

With these revisions, the paper could make a strong contribution to the field. As it stands, the lack of comparison with recent methods is a critical weakness that prevents it from being accepted at ACL. I encourage the authors to address these issues in a revised submission.
