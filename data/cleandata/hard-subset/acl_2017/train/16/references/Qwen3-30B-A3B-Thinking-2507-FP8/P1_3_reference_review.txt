

# Peer Review for ACL Submission

## Summary and Main Contributions

This paper addresses the task of event detection (ED) in the ACE framework, arguing that existing methods underutilize argument information, which can provide crucial clues for identifying event triggers, especially for ambiguous words. The authors propose a supervised attention mechanism that explicitly leverages annotated argument information to improve ED performance. They demonstrate that joint methods (which simultaneously tackle event detection and argument extraction) often fail to improve ED because they indirectly use arguments for argument extraction rather than for event detection.

The main contributions of this work, in order of strength, are:

1. **Novel insight into the problem of joint methods**: The authors provide a clear analysis of why joint methods fail to improve ED performance (unbalanced data favoring AE task and indirect use of arguments for ED), which is a significant conceptual contribution to the field.

2. **Supervised attention mechanism for ED**: The authors propose a novel neural network approach with a supervised attention mechanism that explicitly uses argument information to improve ED, which is the core technical contribution.

3. **Systematic investigation of attention strategies**: The authors systematically evaluate two different strategies for constructing gold attention vectors from argument information (S1: focusing only on argument words; S2: focusing on arguments and surrounding words), providing valuable insights into how argument information should be utilized.

## Strengths

1. **Novel and insightful problem analysis**: The paper makes a compelling argument that existing joint methods don't effectively use argument information for ED, which is a significant conceptual contribution. The authors clearly demonstrate that joint methods only marginally improve ED (less than 0.2 F1 points) while making substantial improvements to AE (over 1.9 points), and they provide a well-argued explanation for this phenomenon. This analysis is both novel and important for the field, as it identifies a key limitation in existing approaches that has been overlooked.

2. **Strong experimental validation**: The authors conduct thorough experiments on the ACE 2005 benchmark, comparing their approach with multiple state-of-the-art methods. Their supervised attention mechanism (ANN-S2) achieves the best F1 score (71.7%) on the standard ACE 2005 test set, significantly outperforming the previous best method (Liu's FN-Based approach at 70.7%). The results are convincing and well-documented, with statistical significance testing (t-test, p<0.05) confirming the superiority of their approach.

3. **Clear demonstration of argument information's importance**: The ablation study (Table 2) conclusively demonstrates that argument information is crucial for ED. Systems using gold attention vectors constructed from arguments (ANN-Gold1/2) significantly outperform systems without argument information (ANN, ANN-ENT), with ANN-Gold2 achieving 70.8% F1 vs. 64.6% for ANN. This provides strong empirical evidence for their core hypothesis.

## Weaknesses

1. **Limited novelty of the supervised attention mechanism**: While the application of supervised attention to ED is novel, the mechanism itself is relatively straightforward and builds directly on existing attention mechanisms (e.g., Mi et al., 2016). The paper doesn't clearly distinguish how this supervised attention differs from prior work on attention in neural networks. The main innovation is the *application* of attention to ED with argument information, not the attention mechanism itself, which somewhat limits the technical contribution.

2. **Lack of ablation studies on the attention mechanism components**: The paper doesn't provide sufficient ablation studies to demonstrate the necessity of each component of their proposed model. For example, they don't show how much of the improvement comes from the supervised attention versus the neural network architecture. It's unclear whether the attention mechanism is truly necessary or if the same improvement could be achieved by other means (e.g., better feature engineering).

3. **Potential data leakage in the argument information construction**: The paper constructs gold attention vectors using annotated arguments in the training data. However, it's not clear whether the argument information used for constructing gold attentions is the same as the argument information used in the joint models they compare against. If the joint models also use this argument information (which they likely do), then the comparison might be unfair, as the authors are essentially using information that joint models already have access to but don't properly leverage for ED.

## Questions to Authors

1. The paper states that joint methods "usually only make remarkable improvements to AE, but insignificant to ED." However, the table comparing joint and pipeline methods shows that Nguyen's joint model (2016) actually achieved a slightly higher ED F1 (69.3) than Chen's pipeline (69.1). How do you explain this exception to your general claim, and does this affect your analysis of why joint methods don't improve ED?

2. The paper uses FrameNet data to augment training, but FrameNet is a different resource with different annotation schemes than ACE. How did you handle the differences in annotation schemes between ACE and FrameNet when processing FrameNet events for training? Did you use any mapping between FrameNet frame elements and ACE event arguments?

3. The paper claims that "the data sparseness problem is the reason that causes unsupervised attention mechanisms to be ineffective to ED." However, the unsupervised attention model (ANN) performed poorly (F1=64.6), while the supervised attention models performed much better (ANN-S1: 70.8, ANN-S2: 71.7). How do you distinguish between the effects of data sparseness and the effectiveness of the supervised attention mechanism itself? Could the improvement be solely due to the supervised attention, rather than the data augmentation?

## Additional Comments

The paper is well-written, clearly structured, and makes a compelling case for the importance of argument information in event detection. The experimental setup follows standard ACE 2005 evaluation protocols, and the results are convincing. However, the paper would benefit from a more detailed comparison with recent state-of-the-art methods (e.g., those using transformers or BERT-based approaches, which were not covered in the comparison). 

The authors should also clarify how the supervised attention mechanism differs from the attention mechanisms used in previous joint models, as this is crucial to understanding the novelty of their contribution. 

The paper makes a valuable contribution to the field by providing a clear analysis of why joint methods underperform for ED and offering a practical solution that significantly improves performance. While the technical novelty of the attention mechanism itself is limited, the application to ED and the clear demonstration of argument information's importance make this a strong contribution to the field.
