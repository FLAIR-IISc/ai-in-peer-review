

# ACL Review

## Summary of the Paper
This paper proposes a novel approach to event detection (ED) by explicitly leveraging argument information through supervised attention mechanisms. The authors argue that existing joint models for event extraction (which tackle both ED and argument extraction) fail to effectively use argument information for ED, instead primarily using it for argument extraction. They propose a neural network-based approach where argument words are given more attention during ED by using annotated arguments as supervision for the attention mechanism. The paper presents two strategies for constructing "gold attention vectors" based on argument information and demonstrates that their approach achieves state-of-the-art results on the ACE 2005 benchmark.

## Main Contributions (in order of strength)
1. The key insight that argument information should be explicitly leveraged for event detection (not just for argument extraction), which has been overlooked in previous joint models
2. The supervised attention mechanism that directly incorporates argument information into the event detection process
3. The empirical demonstration that this approach achieves significant improvements over state-of-the-art methods on ACE 2005

## Strengths

1. **Novel Conceptual Approach**: This paper identifies a fundamental gap in how argument information is used in event extraction systems. The insight that argument information should be explicitly used for event detection (rather than just for argument extraction) is a significant conceptual contribution. The example with "fired" being misclassified as Attack instead of End-Position is compelling and clearly demonstrates the value of argument information for ED. This represents a paradigm shift from previous joint models.

2. **Strong Empirical Results**: The paper demonstrates that their approach achieves the best performance on ACE 2005 (71.7 F1), significantly outperforming the previous best (70.7 F1). The improvements are statistically significant (p < 0.05), which is crucial for establishing the value of a new method. The results are robust across different attention strategies (S1 and S2), with ANN-S2 achieving the highest F1 score.

3. **Clear Problem Statement and Motivation**: The paper effectively identifies a specific problem with existing joint models (they don't actually use arguments for ED, only for AE) and provides a clear explanation of why this matters. The analysis of Table 1 showing that joint models achieve only insignificant improvements on ED (less than 0.2 points) while making remarkable improvements on AE (over 1.9 points) is particularly convincing.

4. **Well-Defined Methodology**: The supervised attention mechanism is clearly explained, with two distinct strategies (S1 and S2) for constructing gold attention vectors. The implementation details are sufficient for reproducibility, and the ablation studies (Table 2) provide evidence that argument information is indeed crucial for ED.

## Weaknesses

1. **Lack of Deep Analysis**: While the results are strong, the paper doesn't provide deep analysis of *why* their approach works better than previous methods. Specific examples of cases where their model correctly identified events that other models missed would strengthen the paper significantly. Without this, it's difficult to fully understand the nature of the improvement.

2. **Limited Theoretical Justification**: The paper relies heavily on empirical results without providing strong theoretical justification for why the supervised attention mechanism should work better. The argument is largely based on observation rather than theory, which would be strengthened by a more rigorous analysis of the attention mechanism's impact on the model's decision process.

3. **Comparison to Alternative Approaches**: The paper compares to several state-of-the-art methods, but doesn't thoroughly compare to other approaches that might have used argument information in different ways. For example, it would be valuable to see how their approach compares to models that use argument information through different mechanisms (e.g., graph-based approaches or different attention strategies).

4. **Data Sparseness Explanation**: While the paper mentions data sparseness as a problem for ED, it doesn't fully explain how the supervised attention mechanism specifically addresses this issue better than other methods. The improvement with FrameNet augmentation (Table 4) is noted, but the connection between their approach and data sparseness isn't sufficiently developed.

## Questions to Authors

1. Can you provide specific examples of sentences where your model correctly identified an event trigger that previous models misclassified, and explain why the argument information was crucial for this correct identification?

2. The paper mentions that the data sparseness problem is a reason why unsupervised attention mechanisms don't work well for ED. Can you elaborate on how your supervised attention mechanism specifically addresses this data sparseness issue, compared to other approaches?

3. The paper presents two strategies for constructing gold attention vectors (S1 and S2). Can you provide more insight into why S2 (which focuses on both arguments and words around them) performs better overall than S1, despite having lower precision?

## Overall Recommendation

**Accept**

This paper makes a significant conceptual contribution to the event extraction field by identifying and addressing a fundamental gap in how argument information is used in existing systems. The insight that argument information should be explicitly leveraged for event detection (not just for argument extraction) is novel and important. The supervised attention mechanism is a clear, well-executed approach to implementing this insight, and the empirical results demonstrate significant improvements over state-of-the-art methods.

While the paper has some weaknesses in terms of depth of analysis and theoretical justification, these are outweighed by the novelty of the core idea and the strength of the empirical results. The paper represents a bold approach that prioritizes a significant new concept over methodological perfection, which aligns with the values of this reviewer. The contribution is substantial enough to warrant acceptance, and the paper would be a valuable addition to the ACL literature.
