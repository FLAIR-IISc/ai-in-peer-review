- Summary: Proposes supervised attention using annotated argument information (S1: argument words, S2: arguments+surroundings) to improve event detection, outperforming prior methods on ACE 2005, especially in precision.

- Contribution — insight: Identifies that joint ED/AE models do not effectively use argument information for event detection.

- Contribution — methodology: Introduces a supervised attention mechanism that explicitly incorporates argument information into ED.

- Contribution — validation: Provides systematic experiments and ablation studies showing argument-guided attention improves ED.

- Strength — problem identification: Convincing analysis (dataset stats, examples) showing why argument info should help ED.

- Strength — empirical results: Ablations and comparisons show significant gains and statistical significance, particularly higher precision for ANN-S2.

- Strength — attention design: Two well-motivated attention strategies (S1/S2) illustrating a precision–recall tradeoff.

- Weakness — theoretical grounding: Lacks deep theoretical or linguistic justification for why argument roles inform event types.

- Weakness — novelty: Supervised attention is a straightforward adaptation and insufficiently differentiated from prior attention-based ED and supervised-attention work.

- Weakness — implementation detail gaps: Missing specifics on how gold attention vectors are constructed, how argument words are weighted, and handling arguments outside the context window.

- Weakness — failure analysis: Limited analysis of failure cases or scenarios where argument information is misleading or misannotated.

- Question: How are gold attention vectors constructed and how are multi-word argument phrases weighted (e.g., "former protege")?

- Question: Can the authors provide linguistic/semantic justification for why argument information determines event types?

- Question: How does the supervised attention approach compare in training time and computational complexity to other attention-based ED models?

- Question: Why does the unsupervised ANN perform poorly (64.6 F1) and how exactly does supervised attention address data sparsity?

- Overall assessment: Well-written, valuable contribution demonstrating the utility of argument information for ED, but needs clearer theoretical motivation, more implementation details, and deeper error analysis before being a stronger ACL paper.
