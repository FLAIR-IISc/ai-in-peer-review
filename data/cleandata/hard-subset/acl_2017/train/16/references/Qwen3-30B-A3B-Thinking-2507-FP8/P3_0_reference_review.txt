

# Peer Review: "Exploiting Argument Information to Improve Event Detection via Supervised Attention Mechanisms"

## Summary
This paper proposes a novel approach to event detection (ED) by explicitly leveraging argument information through supervised attention mechanisms. The authors argue that existing joint models for event extraction (which handle both ED and argument extraction) fail to improve ED because they primarily use argument information for argument extraction rather than ED. They introduce a supervised attention mechanism that learns to focus on argument words when identifying event triggers, using annotated arguments to construct "gold attention" vectors. The method is evaluated on ACE 2005, showing significant improvements over state-of-the-art approaches.

## Main Contributions (as I see them)
1. **Key insight**: The critical observation that argument information can be explicitly leveraged to improve ED, rather than being used indirectly in joint models.
2. **Supervised attention mechanism**: A simple yet effective approach to incorporate argument information directly into ED through supervised attention.
3. **Strong empirical results**: Demonstrated state-of-the-art performance on ACE 2005 with significant improvements over prior work.

## Strengths

1. **Significant Insight and Problem Identification**: The paper makes a compelling argument that joint models fail to improve ED because they don't properly leverage argument information for ED. The "fired" example clearly illustrates how argument information can resolve ambiguity, which is a valuable contribution to the field. The authors correctly identify the two key reasons why joint models underperform on ED (data imbalance and indirect use of arguments), which provides strong theoretical grounding for their approach.

2. **Effective and Well-Justified Methodology**: The supervised attention approach is elegantly simple and directly addresses the identified problem. The two attention strategies (S1 and S2) provide a thoughtful exploration of how argument information can be incorporated, with S2's extension to words around arguments being particularly insightful. The ablation studies (Table 2) convincingly demonstrate that argument information is crucial for ED performance.

3. **Strong Experimental Results**: The method achieves the best F1 score (71.7%) on ACE 2005, significantly outperforming previous state-of-the-art approaches (70.7% for Liu's FN-Based). The results are statistically significant (t-test p < 0.05). The augmentation with FrameNet data (Table 4) further demonstrates the robustness of the approach.

4. **Clear Analysis of Limitations**: The authors provide a thoughtful analysis of why unsupervised attention fails (data sparsity), which is well-supported by the results (ANN performs poorly compared to ANN-S1/S2).

## Weaknesses

1. **Limited Novelty of Core Mechanism**: While the insight about argument information is valuable, the supervised attention mechanism itself is not novel. Supervised attention has been used in other NLP tasks (e.g., machine translation by Mi et al. 2016), and the paper doesn't sufficiently differentiate this approach from existing attention mechanisms in the ED literature. The method would be stronger if it incorporated more novel architectural elements.

2. **Insufficient Analysis of S2 vs. S1**: The paper observes that S2 achieves higher recall but lower precision than S1, but provides no deep analysis of why this occurs. A more detailed exploration of how words around arguments contribute to ED (e.g., through case studies of specific events) would strengthen the paper significantly.

3. **Ambiguity in "Explicit" vs. "Indirect" Usage**: The paper claims their approach explicitly uses argument information, but it's unclear how this differs from other joint models. The authors themselves note that joint models like Nguyen et al. (2016) are "indirect" in their use of argument information, yet their method is fundamentally a joint model. The distinction needs to be clarified to avoid confusion.

4. **Limited Comparison with Recent Work**: The paper compares with state-of-the-art methods up to 2016, but misses more recent work (e.g., 2017-2019 ACL papers on event extraction). For example, the 2018 paper by Li et al. on joint event extraction with multi-task learning would provide a stronger baseline.

## Overall Assessment
This is a strong paper that makes a valuable contribution to the event extraction literature by addressing a critical gap in how argument information is used for event detection. The insight that argument information can directly improve ED is well-argued and supported by compelling evidence. While the supervised attention mechanism itself isn't highly novel, the application to ED is well-executed and effective.

The paper's main weaknesses are the limited novelty of the core mechanism and insufficient analysis of the S2 vs. S1 performance difference. However, these do not undermine the overall contribution, which is significant and well-supported.

## Recommendation
Accept. This paper makes a meaningful contribution to the field by demonstrating that argument information can be effectively leveraged to improve event detection, addressing a limitation in current joint models. The methodology is sound, the results are convincing, and the paper is well-written and clearly argued.

## Questions to Authors
1. How does your supervised attention mechanism differ from the attention mechanisms used in recent joint models (e.g., Nguyen et al. 2016)? Why does your approach work better for ED?

2. Can you provide more detailed analysis on why S2 (which includes words around arguments) achieves higher recall but lower precision compared to S1? What specific aspects of the context around arguments help with event detection?

3. The paper states that "data sparseness is an important issue of ED" but doesn't provide strong evidence that this is the primary reason for ANN's poor performance. Could you elaborate on this?
