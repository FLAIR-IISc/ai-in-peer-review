

# Review of "Exploiting Argument Information to Improve Event Detection via Supervised Attention Mechanisms"

## Summary
This paper addresses the Event Detection (ED) task, which involves identifying event triggers and categorizing them into event types. The authors argue that event arguments (which are not part of the ED task) can provide valuable clues for ED, particularly for ambiguous triggers. They observe that existing joint methods for ED and Argument Extraction (AE) do not effectively use argument information for ED, as they primarily focus on AE. The authors propose a novel approach that explicitly uses argument information for ED through a supervised attention mechanism, where argument words are given higher attention. They evaluate their approach on the ACE 2005 dataset and demonstrate improvements over state-of-the-art methods.

## Main Contributions (as assessed)

1. The authors identify a key limitation in existing joint approaches to ED (that they don't effectively use argument information for ED) and propose a novel way to exploit argument information for ED through supervised attention mechanisms. This addresses a genuine gap in the literature.

2. They systematically investigate different attention strategies (S1 and S2) for using argument information to improve ED performance, providing empirical evidence for their effectiveness.

3. They demonstrate that their approach significantly outperforms state-of-the-art methods on the ACE 2005 dataset, with a notable improvement in precision (71.7% vs. 69.4% for the previous best method).

## Strengths

1. **Clear Identification of a Real Problem**: The authors effectively identify a genuine limitation in current approaches to ED. Their analysis of joint methods (Table 1) shows that existing approaches don't effectively leverage argument information for ED, despite arguments providing valuable clues for ambiguous triggers. This is a well-motivated problem that addresses a real gap in the literature.

2. **Strong Experimental Validation**: The paper provides thorough experimental validation, including ablation studies (Table 2) showing that systems using argument information significantly outperform those without. The comparison against multiple state-of-the-art methods (Table 3) demonstrates clear improvements, with ANN-S2 achieving the best performance (71.7% F1) and significantly outperforming previous methods (p < 0.05).

3. **Novel Application of Attention Mechanisms**: While attention mechanisms have been used in other NLP tasks, the authors' specific application to ED using argument information as supervision is novel and well-motivated. Their explanation of how argument information helps disambiguate triggers (e.g., "fired" as End-Position vs. Attack) is clear and convincing.

4. **Practical Demonstration of Value**: The paper provides a clear example (the "fired" example) that demonstrates the practical value of using argument information for ED. This makes the contribution tangible and understandable.

5. **Clear Methodology**: The paper is well-organized and clearly explains the proposed approach, including the construction of gold attention vectors (S1 and S2 strategies) and how they are used to train the attention mechanism.

## Weaknesses

1. **Limited Novelty of Technical Approach**: While the idea of using argument information for ED is novel, the technical approach (supervised attention) is not particularly novel. Supervised attention mechanisms have been used in other NLP tasks (e.g., machine translation, as cited in Mi et al., 2016), and the authors don't sufficiently justify why their specific application to ED is significantly different or more novel than prior work in other domains.

2. **Lack of Comparison with Other Data Sparseness Solutions**: The authors note that unsupervised attention performs poorly due to data sparseness (Section 4.2), but they don't compare their supervised attention approach against other methods that address data sparseness (e.g., using external knowledge sources or transfer learning). Their use of FrameNet (Section 4.4) is mentioned but not thoroughly evaluated against other data augmentation strategies.

3. **Limited Evaluation Scope**: The evaluation is limited to the ACE 2005 dataset, which is a standard benchmark but not sufficient to demonstrate the generalizability of the approach. The paper doesn't evaluate on other datasets or in different domains, which would strengthen the claim of the approach's broader applicability.

4. **Insufficient Discussion of Computational Cost**: The paper doesn't discuss the computational cost or scalability of the proposed approach compared to baseline methods. Given that the supervised attention mechanism adds complexity to the model, this is an important consideration for practical applications.

5. **Ambiguous Comparison with Previous Work**: While the authors claim their approach differs from existing joint methods, they don't provide a thorough comparison with any prior work that might have used argument information for ED. The paper doesn't adequately address how their work is distinct from previous attempts to incorporate argument information.

## Questions to Authors

1. How does your supervised attention mechanism differ from attention mechanisms used in other NLP tasks (e.g., machine translation), and why is your specific application to ED considered novel?

2. The paper mentions that unsupervised attention performs poorly due to data sparseness, but your supervised attention approach requires gold attention vectors constructed from annotated arguments. How does your approach address the data sparseness problem when gold attention vectors are not available for new datasets or domains?

3. How does your approach compare to other methods that address data sparseness in ED (e.g., using external knowledge sources or transfer learning), and why is your supervised attention mechanism specifically advantageous for this problem?

4. The paper uses FrameNet data to augment performance, but doesn't provide detailed analysis of how much additional data this provides or how it compares to other data augmentation strategies. Could you provide more details on the quantity and quality of the FrameNet data used?

5. How does the computational complexity of your supervised attention mechanism compare to standard ED models, and what are the implications for real-world applications with large-scale datasets?
