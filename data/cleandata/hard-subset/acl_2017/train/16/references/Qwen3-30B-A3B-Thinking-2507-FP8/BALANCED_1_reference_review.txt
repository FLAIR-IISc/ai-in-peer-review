

# ACL Review

## Summary of the Paper

This paper proposes a novel approach for Event Detection (ED) that explicitly leverages argument information through a supervised attention mechanism. The authors argue that existing joint methods for ED and Event Argument Extraction (AE) don't effectively use argument information for ED, as argument information is primarily used for AE rather than ED. They propose a neural network-based approach where argument words are expected to acquire more attention than other words. The attention mechanism is trained using gold attention vectors constructed from annotated arguments. The authors systematically evaluate their approach on ACE 2005 and show that their method outperforms state-of-the-art approaches.

## Main Contributions

1. A novel approach to explicitly leverage argument information for ED using supervised attention mechanisms, addressing a gap in the literature where existing joint methods use arguments indirectly for AE rather than ED.
2. A systematic investigation of two strategies (S1 and S2) for constructing gold attention vectors based on annotated arguments, with analysis of their different effects on ED performance.
3. Empirical demonstration that argument information significantly improves ED performance, with their proposed method achieving state-of-the-art results on ACE 2005.

## Strengths

1. **Strong Motivation and Problem Identification**: The paper clearly identifies a significant gap in the literature - that existing joint methods for ED and AE don't effectively use argument information for ED. The authors provide a compelling example showing how argument information resolves ambiguity in trigger words (e.g., "fired" as End-Position vs. Attack), which makes the problem well-motivated and relevant. The analysis of why joint methods underperform on ED (due to unbalanced data and indirect use of argument information) is insightful and well-supported.

2. **Comprehensive Experimental Evaluation**: The paper provides a thorough experimental evaluation. They compare against multiple state-of-the-art methods (Li's joint, Liu's PSL, Liu's FN-Based, Nguyen's joint, Skip-CNN) and conduct ablation studies to validate their assumptions. The use of FrameNet to augment training data further strengthens their claims. The results clearly demonstrate that their method (ANN-S2) outperforms all baselines, with significant improvements in F1 score (71.7% vs. 70.7% for the previous best).

3. **Clear and Well-Structured Presentation**: The paper is exceptionally well-written and logically organized. The problem statement is clear, the proposed approach is explained thoroughly with sufficient technical detail, and the experiments are presented in a way that clearly demonstrates the effectiveness of their method. The distinction between the two attention strategies (S1 and S2) is particularly well-explained, and the paper effectively addresses the limitations of existing approaches.

## Weaknesses

1. **Limited Comparison with More Recent Methods**: While the paper compares against several state-of-the-art methods, it doesn't compare against more recent approaches published after 2016 (e.g., Liu et al. 2017, or other recent ACL/EMNLP papers). For instance, the paper cites Liu et al. (2016), but there have been significant advances in event detection since then. This weakens the claim that their method is "state-of-the-art" as the comparison is not against the very latest methods.

2. **Lack of Analysis of Why Argument Information Helps**: While the paper demonstrates that argument information helps with ED, it doesn't provide a deep analysis of *why* it helps. For example, it doesn't analyze which specific event types or trigger words benefit most from argument information. A deeper analysis of the types of events where argument information is most helpful would strengthen the paper's contribution and provide more valuable insights to the community.

3. **Potential Confounding Effects with FrameNet Data**: The paper shows that their method benefits from FrameNet data augmentation, but the results in Table 4 reveal that even the basic ANN model (without the attention mechanism) benefits from FrameNet (F1 from 64.6 to 66.2). This suggests that the improvement might be partially due to the additional data rather than the attention mechanism itself. The paper doesn't clearly disentangle these effects, which weakens the claim that the attention mechanism is the primary source of improvement.

## Questions to Authors

1. The paper claims their method achieves state-of-the-art performance on ACE 2005, but doesn't compare against more recent methods published after 2016 (e.g., Liu et al. 2017 or other recent ACL/EMNLP papers). Could you provide a comparison with these more recent state-of-the-art methods to strengthen your claim?

2. The paper demonstrates that argument information helps with ED, but doesn't analyze which specific event types or trigger words benefit most from this information. Could you provide a detailed analysis of the specific event types or trigger words where argument information provides the most improvement?

3. In Table 4, the basic ANN model also benefits from FrameNet data (F1 from 64.6 to 66.2). How much of the improvement from ANN-S2 to ANN-S2+FrameNet is due to the attention mechanism vs. the additional data from FrameNet? Could you provide a more detailed ablation study to disentangle these effects?

## Additional Comments

The paper makes a valuable contribution to the event extraction literature by identifying a key gap in how argument information is used in ED. The supervised attention mechanism is novel and well-motivated, and the experimental results are convincing. The main weaknesses are related to the comparison with more recent methods and the lack of deeper analysis of why argument information helps.

The paper is well-written, addresses a meaningful problem, and provides strong empirical evidence for their approach. With minor revisions addressing the questions above, this paper would make a significant contribution to the field.

## Overall Recommendation

I recommend **acceptance** with minor revisions. The paper makes a novel contribution to the event detection literature with a well-motivated approach and strong empirical results. The main weaknesses are not fundamental flaws but rather opportunities to strengthen the paper further, and the questions I've raised can be addressed in the author response period. The contribution is significant enough to warrant acceptance at ACL, and the paper is well-written and clearly presented.
