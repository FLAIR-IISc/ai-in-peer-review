

# Peer Review: "Exploiting Argument Information to Improve Event Detection via Supervised Attention Mechanisms"

## Summary and Contributions

This paper proposes a novel approach to event detection (ED) by explicitly leveraging argument information through supervised attention mechanisms. The authors argue that existing joint methods for event extraction (EE) fail to properly utilize argument information for ED, primarily improving argument extraction (AE) while providing minimal gains for ED. Their key contribution is a supervised attention mechanism that learns to prioritize argument words during ED, with two strategies (S1 and S2) for constructing "gold attention" vectors based on annotated arguments. The paper claims this approach outperforms state-of-the-art methods on ACE 2005.

The most significant contributions I identify are:
1. The insightful analysis of why joint models underperform on ED (data imbalance and indirect use of arguments), which provides a clear theoretical foundation for the approach
2. The demonstration that argument information significantly improves ED performance, addressing a genuine gap in the literature
3. The systematic investigation of different attention strategies for leveraging argument information

## Strengths

1. **Problem Identification and Analysis**: The paper makes a compelling case that argument information is underutilized in ED, supported by thorough analysis of existing joint methods. The table comparing pipeline vs. joint approaches (Table 1) clearly demonstrates the minimal ED improvements from joint methods (less than 0.2 points) while showing significant AE gains (over 1.9 points). This analysis is a major contribution to the field.

2. **Conceptual Soundness**: The core idea—using argument information to disambiguate event triggers—is theoretically well-motivated. The "fired" example effectively demonstrates how argument information (e.g., "former protege") resolves ambiguity between End-Position and Attack events. This is a genuine insight that advances the understanding of event extraction.

3. **Thorough Experimental Evaluation**: The authors conduct comprehensive experiments, including ablation studies (Table 2) showing that argument information significantly improves ED over entity-based approaches. The results in Table 3 clearly demonstrate that their supervised attention approach (ANN-S2) achieves state-of-the-art performance (F1=71.7) with statistically significant gains over all baselines. The FrameNet augmentation experiment (Table 4) further validates the approach's effectiveness.

4. **Clarity of Methodology**: The paper provides clear technical details of the supervised attention mechanism, including the construction of gold attention vectors (S1 and S2 strategies) and the joint training procedure. The architecture (Figure 1) is well-explained, making the approach accessible to readers.

## Weaknesses

1. **Overstated Novelty**: The paper claims novelty in "exploiting argument information" for ED, but this is a relatively straightforward adaptation of attention mechanisms to a specific NLP task. Supervised attention has been used in other NLP contexts (e.g., machine translation, see Mi et al. 2016), and the paper fails to sufficiently differentiate their approach from prior work on attention mechanisms. The novelty lies in the specific application to ED using arguments, not in the attention mechanism itself.

2. **Lack of Theoretical Justification**: The paper claims arguments "provide significant clues" for ED but doesn't provide a strong theoretical foundation for *why* argument information should be useful for ED beyond the specific examples given. The paper should address whether argument information is generally helpful or if the benefits are limited to specific event types or trigger words.

3. **Limited Evaluation Scope**: The evaluation is restricted to ACE 2005, which is a standard dataset, but the paper doesn't compare with more recent state-of-the-art methods (e.g., transformer-based architectures like BERT, which have become dominant in NLP). The absence of comparison with recent neural approaches weakens the claim that the method "achieves the best performance."

4. **Misunderstanding of Data Sparseness**: The authors claim that data sparseness is the reason unsupervised attention doesn't work well for ED, but their solution (supervised attention) actually requires *more* data (argument annotations) rather than solving the sparseness problem. This is contradictory—the paper's approach depends on the very data they claim is sparse (arguments), making it less generalizable to low-resource scenarios.

5. **Inadequate Discussion of Limitations**: The paper doesn't discuss potential limitations of their approach, such as whether it would work well for events with few or no arguments, or whether the benefits diminish when argument annotations are noisy or incomplete.

## Questions to Authors

1. How would your approach perform on events with no arguments (e.g., "The sun rose")? The paper focuses on events with arguments, but many events may not have clear arguments.

2. The paper claims "argument information is not essentially necessary to ED" but demonstrates it's helpful. Can you clarify why arguments are useful for ED if they're not necessary? What theoretical justification do you have for this relationship?

3. The paper doesn't compare with recent transformer-based approaches (e.g., BERT, RoBERTa). How would your supervised attention mechanism integrate with or complement these architectures?

## Overall Assessment

This paper makes a valuable contribution by demonstrating that argument information can significantly improve event detection performance when explicitly leveraged through supervised attention. The problem identification and experimental validation are strong, and the results are convincing. However, the novelty is somewhat overstated, and the evaluation lacks comparison with more recent state-of-the-art methods. The paper would benefit from a more nuanced discussion of the theoretical relationship between arguments and event detection, and a clearer distinction between the contribution of the attention mechanism versus the specific application to ED.

I recommend acceptance with minor revisions addressing the weaknesses noted above, particularly regarding novelty claims and comparison with recent methods. The paper's core insight about argument information for ED is valuable and should be preserved in the final version.
