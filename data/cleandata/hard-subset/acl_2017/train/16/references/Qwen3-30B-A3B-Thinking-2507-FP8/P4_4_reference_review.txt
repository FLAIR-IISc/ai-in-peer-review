

# Review of "Exploiting Argument Information to Improve Event Detection via Supervised Attention Mechanisms"

## Summary of the Paper and Main Contributions

The paper addresses event detection (ED) in the ACE event extraction framework, arguing that annotated argument information is underutilized in existing ED approaches despite being valuable for identifying event triggers, especially for ambiguous words. The authors propose a novel approach that explicitly leverages argument information through a supervised attention mechanism, where argument words are expected to receive higher attention during ED.

The main contributions of this work, as objectively assessed, are:

1. **Demonstrating that argument information is crucial for ED performance**: The paper provides empirical evidence that using argument information leads to significant improvements in ED performance (71.7 F1 vs 64.6 F1 for the baseline), and shows that this improvement comes specifically from argument information, not just entity information.

2. **Introducing a supervised attention mechanism for ED**: The authors propose a novel way to incorporate argument information into ED by using it as supervision for the attention mechanism, where argument words are expected to receive higher attention.

3. **Systematically investigating different attention strategies**: The paper explores two different strategies for constructing gold attention vectors from annotated arguments (S1 and S2), and analyzes their effects on performance, providing valuable insights for future work.

## Strengths

1. **Strong empirical evidence for the core claim**: The paper provides compelling evidence that argument information is valuable for ED. The ablation study (Table 2) shows that systems using argument information (ANN-Gold1/2) significantly outperform those without (ANN and ANN-ENT), with a 7.2 F1 point improvement. This is the strongest argument for the paper's contribution, as it directly validates the authors' core hypothesis.

2. **Clear analysis of why joint models underperform on ED**: The authors provide a well-supported analysis of why existing joint models (which tackle both ED and AE simultaneously) don't effectively use argument information for ED. This analysis is backed by data from Table 1, which shows that joint models achieve only marginal improvements on ED (less than 0.2 points) while making significant improvements on AE (over 1.9 points). This analysis establishes a solid foundation for the proposed approach.

3. **Significant performance gains over strong baselines**: The proposed method (ANN-S2) achieves 71.7 F1 on ACE 2005, which is a 2.3 F1 point improvement over the previous best system (Liu's FN-Based) and a 7.1 F1 point improvement over the baseline ANN. The t-test showing statistical significance (p < 0.05) adds credibility to these results, demonstrating that the improvements are not due to chance.

## Weaknesses

1. **Limited comparison to recent state-of-the-art**: While the paper compares to several strong baselines, it omits several recent state-of-the-art methods for event detection. For example, the paper mentions "Liu's FN-Based" as the best-reported feature-based system but does not compare to more recent work like "Event Detection via Dynamic Multi-Pooling Convolutional Neural Networks" (Chen et al., 2015) or "A Probabilistic Soft Logic Based Approach to Exploiting Latent and Global Information in Event Classification" (Liu et al., 2016b), which could have provided a more complete comparison.

2. **Lack of qualitative analysis of the attention mechanism**: While the paper introduces a supervised attention mechanism, it doesn't provide qualitative analysis of how the attention works. The paper doesn't show examples of which words receive higher attention in the model, or how the attention differs between correct and incorrect predictions. This omission limits the reader's understanding of why the mechanism works.

3. **Unclear novelty compared to existing attention mechanisms**: The paper claims novelty in using supervised attention for ED, but it doesn't sufficiently distinguish this from existing attention mechanisms in NLP. The paper cites Mi et al. (2016) on "Supervised attentions for neural machine translation," which is very similar to the proposed approach. The paper should clarify how their supervised attention mechanism differs from or builds upon these existing techniques.

## Questions to Authors

1. The paper states that "event arguments seem to be not essentially necessary to ED" but argues they provide clues for ambiguous triggers. Could the authors provide more specific examples of ambiguous triggers where argument information was particularly helpful, beyond the "fired" example given in the introduction?

2. The paper mentions that "the data sparseness is an important issue of ED" (Zhu et al., 2014; Liu et al., 2016a), and uses FrameNet data to address this. However, the FrameNet data they use is automatically extracted from FrameNet, which may introduce noise. Could the authors provide more details about the quality of the FrameNet data and how they handled potential errors in this data?

3. The paper states that "only 10% of triggers in ACE 2005 are entities." Could the authors explain why they believe this is relevant to their decision not to use entity information of the candidate trigger in computing attention vectors? How might this change if the percentage was higher?

## Additional Comments

The paper is well-written and clearly structured, with a logical flow from problem statement to methodology to evaluation. The experimental design is sound, with appropriate baselines and ablation studies. The use of ACE 2005 as a benchmark dataset is appropriate for the task. The paper makes a clear contribution to the field by demonstrating the value of argument information for ED, which has been overlooked in previous work.
