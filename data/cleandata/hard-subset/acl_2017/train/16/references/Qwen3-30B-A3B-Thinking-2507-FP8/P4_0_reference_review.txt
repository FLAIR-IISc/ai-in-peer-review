

# Review of "Exploiting Argument Information to Improve Event Detection via Supervised Attention Mechanisms"

## Summary of the Paper
This paper addresses the event detection (ED) task in the ACE event extraction program. The authors argue that while most existing methods treat ED as a standalone task, ignoring annotated event arguments, these arguments can provide significant clues for identifying and categorizing events, especially for ambiguous trigger words. They observe that joint methods (which tackle ED and argument extraction simultaneously) have shown only marginal improvements on ED (less than 0.2 points) while significantly improving argument extraction. The authors propose a neural network-based approach using a supervised attention mechanism to explicitly leverage argument information for ED. Their approach constructs "gold attentions" based on annotated arguments and uses these as supervision to train the attention mechanism. They evaluate their method on the ACE 2005 dataset and demonstrate state-of-the-art performance.

## Main Contributions
1. The paper identifies and analyzes a problem with existing joint methods for event extraction - that they fail to effectively use argument information for event detection despite having access to annotated arguments.
2. The authors propose a supervised attention mechanism that explicitly leverages argument information to improve event detection performance, with two different strategies for constructing gold attention vectors.
3. The authors demonstrate significant improvements over state-of-the-art approaches on the ACE 2005 benchmark dataset, with their best model achieving 71.7 F1 (compared to 70.7 for the previous state-of-the-art).

## Strengths
1. **Clear Problem Identification and Motivation:** The paper provides a compelling analysis of why existing joint methods fail to leverage argument information for event detection. The empirical evidence in Table 1 clearly demonstrates that joint methods show only marginal improvements on ED (less than 0.2 points) while achieving significant gains on argument extraction. The example with the word "fired" effectively illustrates the practical value of argument information for resolving ambiguity in event detection.

2. **Well-Designed Experimental Evaluation:** The paper presents a thorough experimental evaluation with multiple ablation studies. The comparison in Table 2 clearly demonstrates that systems using argument information (ANN-Gold1/2) significantly outperform systems without argument information (ANN/ANN-ENT), which validates the core assumption of the paper. The comparison with state-of-the-art methods in Table 3 shows statistically significant improvements (p < 0.05), with the proposed approach achieving the best F1 score (71.7) compared to the previous state-of-the-art (70.7).

3. **Effective Use of FrameNet Data:** The paper demonstrates how to augment the ACE 2005 dataset with events from FrameNet to further improve performance. The results in Table 4 show that the proposed approach benefits from this augmentation (ANN-S2 + FrameNet achieves 71.9 F1), which provides additional evidence of the approach's effectiveness and robustness.

## Weaknesses
1. **Limited Novelty of the Core Mechanism:** While the paper identifies an important problem with existing joint methods, the core mechanism (supervised attention) is not novel. Attention mechanisms have been widely used in NLP for years (e.g., Bahdanau et al., 2014), and the paper does not sufficiently differentiate their specific application from existing attention-based approaches for event detection. The paper would benefit from a more detailed comparison with attention-based methods like Skip-CNN (Nguyen and Grishman, 2016) which also uses attention mechanisms.

2. **Insufficient Analysis of Why Joint Methods Fail to Leverage Arguments:** The paper identifies that joint methods fail to use argument information for ED, but provides limited analysis of why this is the case. The authors state that "in implementation, joint models usually pre-predict several potential triggers and arguments first and then make global inference," but don't provide a detailed architectural analysis of existing joint methods to show exactly where argument information is not being used for ED. A more thorough analysis would strengthen the paper's claim about the limitation of existing approaches.

3. **Lack of Analysis on Attention Mechanism's Impact on Specific Error Cases:** The paper demonstrates that the proposed approach achieves higher precision than state-of-the-art methods, but doesn't provide any analysis of why this is the case. It would be valuable to examine specific error cases where the proposed approach outperforms previous methods to better understand the mechanism's strengths.

## Questions to Authors
1. The paper claims that joint methods "do not leverage any argument information" when pre-predicting potential triggers. Can the authors provide a more detailed analysis of the architecture of existing joint methods (e.g., Li et al., 2013; Nguyen et al., 2016) to show exactly where argument information is not being used for ED?

2. The proposed approach achieves higher precision than state-of-the-art methods. Can the authors provide more insight into why their approach achieves higher precision, perhaps by analyzing specific examples where their model is more precise?

3. The paper uses FrameNet as external data to augment performance. How does the quality of the automatically extracted events from FrameNet compare to manually annotated ACE data, and how might this affect the performance improvements observed in Table 4?
