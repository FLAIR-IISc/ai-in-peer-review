

# ACL Review: "Exploiting Argument Information to Improve Event Detection via Supervised Attention Mechanisms"

## Summary of the Paper
This paper proposes a method to improve event detection (ED) by explicitly incorporating argument information through a supervised attention mechanism. The authors argue that existing joint models for event extraction (which handle ED and argument extraction together) do not properly leverage argument information for ED, and propose a neural network-based approach where the attention mechanism is trained to focus on argument words. They introduce two strategies for constructing "gold attention" vectors based on annotated arguments and evaluate their method on ACE 2005, claiming state-of-the-art results.

## Main Contributions (as I see them)
1. The observation that argument information is underutilized in ED (though this is not particularly novel)
2. A supervised attention mechanism that uses argument information to improve ED performance
3. Experimental results showing marginal improvement on ACE 2005

## Strengths
1. **The problem statement is reasonable**: The paper correctly identifies that existing joint models for event extraction don't properly leverage argument information for ED. This is a valid observation about the current state of the field.

2. **The experimental results are consistent with the claim**: The authors demonstrate that their supervised attention approach does improve ED performance over baseline methods on ACE 2005, which is a well-established benchmark.

3. **The method is well-explained**: The paper provides clear details about the architecture and implementation of the supervised attention mechanism, making the approach reproducible.

## Weaknesses
1. **Lack of novelty**: The core idea of using argument information for ED is not new. The authors cite previous joint models that attempt to incorporate arguments, but their "supervised attention" mechanism is a minor variation of existing attention mechanisms in NLP (which have been used extensively for years). The paper doesn't clearly explain why their specific approach is fundamentally different from established methods. The "supervised attention" is essentially using the gold standard arguments to train the attention mechanism, which is a common practice in supervised learning, not a novel contribution.

2. **Marginal performance gains**: The claimed improvement (71.7 F1 vs. 70.7 F1 for the previous state-of-the-art) is extremely marginal. The paper overstates this as "remarkable gains," but a 1-point improvement on F1 is not significant in the context of event detection research. The paper doesn't provide sufficient evidence to justify claiming a "remarkable" improvement, especially given the limited evaluation scope.

3. **Limited evaluation**: The paper only evaluates on ACE 2005, which is a well-known dataset but not the most challenging. There's no comparison with more recent state-of-the-art methods beyond 2016 (the paper cites work up to 2016, but significant advances have been made since). The paper lacks thorough ablation studies to isolate the contribution of the supervised attention mechanism versus simply using argument information.

4. **Questionable practicality**: The method relies on using gold-standard argument information during training, which is not available in real-world scenarios. The paper doesn't address how this would generalize to cases where argument information is not available, making the practical utility of the method questionable.

5. **Overclaiming of contributions**: The paper claims "the best performance" on ACE 2005, but the improvement is marginal. The authors also overstate the significance of their approach by claiming that existing joint methods "do not properly leverage argument information for ED," but don't provide sufficient evidence beyond a table showing small improvements on ED.

## Questions to Authors
1. How does your method handle cases where argument information is not available during inference (e.g., for new event types or domains where arguments haven't been annotated)? The paper only evaluates on ACE 2005, which has annotated arguments, but real-world applications would not have this information.

2. Given that the improvement over previous state-of-the-art is only 1 point (71.7 vs. 70.7 F1), what evidence do you have that this would translate to meaningful improvements in other settings or with more challenging datasets?

3. The paper claims that existing joint models "do not properly leverage argument information for ED," but the table in the paper shows that joint models do provide some improvement to ED (albeit small). Can you provide more specific evidence for this claim beyond the marginal improvements shown in the table?

4. The paper uses FrameNet data for augmentation, but FrameNet and ACE have different annotation schemes. How do you ensure that the FrameNet data is compatible with the ACE annotation scheme, and what impact does this have on the results?

5. The paper doesn't discuss any limitations of the proposed approach. What are the main limitations, and how would you address them in future work?

## Recommendation
**Reject**

While the paper addresses a valid problem and shows some improvement over baselines, the contribution is marginal and lacks sufficient novelty. The proposed method is a minor variation of existing attention mechanisms, and the performance gains are not significant enough to justify the claim of "remarkable gains" or "state-of-the-art" performance. The paper does not provide sufficient evidence to overcome my skepticism about the novelty and significance of the approach. For a paper to be accepted at ACL, it should either present a fundamentally new approach with substantial evidence of its superiority, or provide significant insights that change the way we think about the problem. This paper fails to do either.
