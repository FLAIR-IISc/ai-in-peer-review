- Paper tackles ACE event detection, arguing existing joint methods underuse argument information and proposing a supervised attention mechanism that leverages annotated arguments to improve ED.  
- Contribution: novel analysis explaining why joint methods fail to improve ED (data imbalance and indirect use of arguments).  
- Contribution: a supervised attention neural model that explicitly uses argument information for ED.  
- Contribution: systematic comparison of two gold-attention construction strategies (S1: arguments only; S2: arguments + context).  
- Strength: insightful problem analysis showing joint methods mainly boost AE not ED (marginal ED gains vs large AE gains).  
- Strength: strong experimental results on ACE 2005 — ANN-S2 achieves 71.7% F1 and is statistically significant over prior work.  
- Strength: ablation shows argument information is crucial (ANN-Gold2 70.8% vs ANN 64.6%).  
- Weakness: supervised attention mechanism itself is relatively straightforward and not clearly differentiated from prior attention work.  
- Weakness: lacks detailed ablations isolating the contribution of supervised attention vs other architectural choices.  
- Weakness: possible data leakage/unfair comparison if gold attentions use argument annotations that joint baselines also had access to.  
- Question: how do you explain Nguyen 2016 as an exception where a joint model slightly outperformed a pipeline, and does it affect your analysis?  
- Question: how were FrameNet annotations mapped or aligned to ACE when using FrameNet for augmentation?  
- Question: how do you disentangle gains from supervised attention vs data augmentation/sparsity effects—could improvements be due solely to supervision?  
- Additional: paper is well-written and follows ACE evaluation but should compare to transformer/BERT-based methods and clarify novelty relative to prior attention uses.
