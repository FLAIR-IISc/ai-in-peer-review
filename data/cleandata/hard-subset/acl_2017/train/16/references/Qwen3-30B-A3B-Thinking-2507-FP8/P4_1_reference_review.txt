

# Review for ACL Submission

## Summary of the Paper
This paper proposes a novel approach to event detection (ED) that explicitly leverages argument information through a supervised attention mechanism. The authors argue that existing joint models for event extraction (which tackle both event detection and argument extraction) fail to effectively utilize argument information for improving ED performance, as the argument information primarily benefits argument extraction rather than event detection. The authors introduce a neural network model that uses annotated argument information to create "gold attentions" that supervise the attention mechanism during training, thereby guiding the model to focus more on argument-related words when detecting event triggers. The approach is evaluated on the ACE 2005 dataset, with additional experiments using FrameNet data for augmentation.

## Main Contributions
1. The key insight that argument information can be explicitly utilized for event detection, which differs from how existing joint models have treated argument information (primarily for argument extraction).
2. The introduction of a supervised attention mechanism that uses annotated argument information to guide attention weights during training, with systematic investigation of two different attention strategies (S1 and S2).
3. Demonstrated significant improvements over state-of-the-art methods on the ACE 2005 benchmark dataset, with ANN-S2 achieving the best performance (71.7 F1).

## Strengths

1. **Clear identification of a research gap**: The paper effectively identifies a specific limitation in existing joint models for event extraction (Table 1), showing that while joint models improve argument extraction significantly, they provide minimal improvement for event detection. The authors' analysis that this is due to argument information not being effectively leveraged for ED, but rather for AE, is well-supported by the data and provides a strong motivation for their approach.

2. **Rigorous experimental validation**: The paper provides thorough experimental validation across multiple baselines (including state-of-the-art methods like Li's joint model, Liu's PSL, and Nguyen's joint model) and systematically investigates different attention strategies. The results clearly demonstrate that the proposed approach significantly outperforms existing methods (with statistical significance shown through t-tests), and the ablation study (Table 2) effectively shows the importance of argument information for ED.

3. **Well-structured and clear presentation**: The paper is well-organized, with a clear explanation of the problem, the proposed solution, and thorough experimental results. The technical details of the supervised attention mechanism are explained clearly, including the two different strategies (S1 and S2) for constructing gold attention vectors. The architecture diagram (Figure 1) effectively illustrates the model components.

## Weaknesses

1. **Limited novelty compared to related work**: While the paper identifies a gap in existing joint models, the core idea of using attention mechanisms for event detection is not entirely novel. The paper does not sufficiently distinguish its approach from prior work that has used attention mechanisms or entity information for ED (e.g., Liu et al. 2016a, which uses FrameNet to augment data). The paper mentions ANN-ENT (which uses entity information) as a baseline, but doesn't clearly articulate why argument information (a specific type of entity information) is superior to general entity information for ED.

2. **Insufficient discussion of limitations**: The paper does not thoroughly address the limitations of the proposed approach. For instance, how does the method perform when argument information is missing or ambiguous in the training data? How does the approach scale to other event extraction datasets beyond ACE 2005? The authors don't discuss potential failure cases or scenarios where the supervised attention mechanism might not work as well.

3. **Questionable necessity of supervised attention**: The paper claims that unsupervised attention mechanisms don't work well for ED due to data sparsity. However, the paper doesn't provide a clear explanation of why supervised attention specifically solves the data sparsity issue rather than other approaches (like using more training data or better pretraining). The results with FrameNet augmentation (Table 4) show that simply adding more data improves performance, which suggests that data sparsity might be the primary issue rather than the attention mechanism itself.

## Questions to Authors

1. The paper states that "argument information is necessary for ED" (Table 2), but ANN-ENT (which uses entity information) performs worse than ANN-Gold1/2. Could you clarify why argument information (a specific type of entity information) is more useful than general entity information for ED, and how the authors differentiate between arguments and entities in their model?

2. The paper claims that "the data sparseness problem is the reason that causes unsupervised attention mechanisms to be ineffective to ED" (Section 4.3). However, Table 4 shows that simply adding more training data (via FrameNet) improves ANN's performance from 64.6 to 66.2 F1. How does your supervised attention mechanism specifically address the data sparsity issue beyond what data augmentation can achieve?

3. In Table 3, ANN-S1 achieves higher precision (81.4) than ANN-S2 (78.0), but lower recall (62.4 vs 66.3). What are the practical implications of this trade-off for real-world event detection systems, and how might the authors balance precision and recall in future work?
