- Proposes supervised attention using annotated arguments (two gold-attention strategies S1: argument words, S2: argument+context) to improve event detection (ED).
- Identifies gap: joint event extraction models fail to effectively use argument information for ED due to data imbalance and indirect argument use.
- Reports strong empirical result: 71.7% F1 on ACE 2005, beating baseline ANN (64.6% F1) and prior compared methods.
- Main contributions: novel explicit use of argument information, a supervised-attention mechanism for ED, and empirical ablation demonstrating argument importance.
- Strength: convincingly motivates the problem and shows joint models’ shortcomings (Table 1).
- Strength: strong empirical performance and ablation studies showing argument signals are crucial.
- Strength: method is conceptually simple and easy to implement; gold-attention idea is elegant.
- Strength: systematic analysis of attention strategies S1 vs S2 (S1 favors precision, S2 favors recall).
- Weakness: inadequate justification/comparison for poor unsupervised attention performance; no comparison to modern unsupervised/self-attention methods.
- Weakness: outdated comparisons—missing recent SOTA (e.g., Liu et al., 2018) that report higher ACE 2005 scores.
- Weakness: lacks qualitative analysis or attention visualizations to show the mechanism focuses on argument words.
- Weakness: superficial treatment of FrameNet data augmentation and potential noise/false positives from automatic extraction.
- Weakness: no analysis of failure cases (events without arguments or ambiguous arguments).
- Questions for authors: provide comparisons with standard unsupervised/self-attention methods; explain omission of recent SOTA comparisons; supply qualitative examples showing attention focus.
- Recommendation: accept with minor revisions, contingent on updated comparisons and added qualitative/diagnostic analyses.
