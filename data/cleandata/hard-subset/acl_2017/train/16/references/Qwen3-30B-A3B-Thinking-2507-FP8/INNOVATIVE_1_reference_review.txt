

# Review of "Exploiting Argument Information to Improve Event Detection via Supervised Attention Mechanisms"

## Summary and Contributions

This paper proposes a novel approach to event detection (ED) by explicitly leveraging argument information, which has been largely ignored in traditional ED systems. The authors argue that while existing joint models (which handle both ED and event argument extraction) have been proposed, they fail to effectively utilize argument information for ED. The authors introduce a supervised attention mechanism where argument words are given higher attention during event detection, with two strategies for constructing gold attention vectors based on annotated arguments.

**My assessment of contributions (in order of strength):**
1. **Novel conceptual insight**: The paper identifies a fundamental flaw in current joint event extraction approaches - that they use argument information for argument extraction but not for event detection. This is a significant conceptual contribution that challenges the standard paradigm in the field.
2. **Supervised attention mechanism**: The idea of training an attention mechanism using gold argument information as supervision is novel and directly addresses the conceptual insight.
3. **Empirical validation**: The systematic experiments on ACE 2005 demonstrate that the proposed approach significantly outperforms state-of-the-art methods, with statistically significant gains.

## Strengths

1. **Highly novel conceptual contribution**: This is the strongest aspect of the paper. The authors correctly identify that argument information is not being used effectively for event detection in existing approaches, which is a fundamental insight that has been overlooked. The paper's core contribution is this conceptual shift in how we think about event detection, not just a technical improvement. This aligns perfectly with the reviewer's preference for bold, novel ideas over incremental methodological improvements.

2. **Clear problem statement and analysis**: The paper provides an excellent analysis of why existing joint methods fail to use argument information effectively for ED (the data imbalance issue and the indirect way arguments are used). This analysis is crucial for establishing the novelty of their approach and demonstrates deep understanding of the problem space.

3. **Strong empirical results**: The proposed approach achieves state-of-the-art results on ACE 2005 with statistically significant improvements over existing methods. The improvements are not marginal (ANN-S2 achieves 71.7 F1, compared to the previous best of 70.7), which is substantial in this field. The results with FrameNet augmentation further validate the approach.

4. **Thoughtful exploration of attention strategies**: The paper doesn't just propose one method but explores two different strategies (S1 and S2) for constructing gold attention vectors, showing a thoughtful approach to the problem. The analysis of why S1 achieves higher precision while S2 achieves higher recall provides valuable insights.

5. **Clear demonstration of the problem with existing methods**: The table comparing pipeline and joint approaches (Table 1) and the analysis of why joint methods don't improve ED performance provide compelling evidence for the need for the authors' approach.

## Weaknesses

1. **Limited novelty of the attention mechanism itself**: While the *concept* of using argument information for ED is novel, the attention mechanism itself is relatively straightforward and builds on existing attention techniques. The supervised attention approach is a good application of a known technique, but the mechanism itself isn't particularly novel in the broader NLP literature.

2. **Insufficient ablation studies**: The paper doesn't provide enough ablation studies to isolate the contribution of the supervised attention mechanism. It's unclear how much of the improvement comes from using argument information versus the specific attention mechanism. For instance, if they had used a simpler method to incorporate argument information (e.g., adding argument features to the input), would they have seen similar improvements?

3. **Limited discussion of limitations**: The paper doesn't thoroughly discuss the limitations of their approach. For example, how would the approach perform when argument information is not available or is noisy? The paper relies on the rich annotations of ACE 2005, but in real-world applications, such rich annotation might not be available.

4. **Comparison with more recent methods**: The paper compares with methods up to 2016, but the field has advanced significantly since then. More recent state-of-the-art methods (from 2017-2023) should be included for a fair comparison.

5. **Lack of analysis on event type specificity**: The paper doesn't analyze whether certain event types benefit more from the proposed approach than others. This would provide deeper insights into the effectiveness of the method.

## Recommendation

**Accept**

This paper makes a significant conceptual contribution by identifying a fundamental flaw in current event extraction approaches and proposing a novel way to address it. The core insight that argument information should be used explicitly for event detection (not just for argument extraction) is bold and highly valuable, even if the technical implementation of the attention mechanism is relatively straightforward.

As a reviewer who prioritizes novelty and conceptual contributions over methodological perfection, I believe this paper makes a substantial contribution to the field. The empirical results support the conceptual insight, and the approach could open up new directions for future research in event extraction.

The weaknesses noted (primarily regarding the novelty of the attention mechanism and lack of ablation studies) are important but don't outweigh the significance of the conceptual contribution. These could be addressed in future work or in the author response.

## Questions to Authors

1. How would your approach perform in a setting where argument information is not available in the training data (e.g., zero-shot event detection)? Could you provide some preliminary analysis on this?

2. Could you provide more details on the FrameNet augmentation process? Specifically, how did you handle the differences in annotation schemes between ACE and FrameNet, and what was the impact of these differences on the performance?

3. The paper claims that "argument words should acquire more attention than other words." Could you provide specific examples from the results where the attention mechanism correctly identified the relevant arguments for event detection, and how this relates to the improved performance?

4. Have you considered the computational cost of your approach compared to existing methods? Is the supervised attention mechanism significantly more expensive to train or deploy?

5. How would your approach generalize to event extraction in languages other than English, where argument structures might differ significantly?
