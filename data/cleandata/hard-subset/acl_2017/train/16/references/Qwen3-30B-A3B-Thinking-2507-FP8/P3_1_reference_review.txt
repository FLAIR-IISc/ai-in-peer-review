

# Peer Review: "Exploiting Argument Information to Improve Event Detection via Supervised Attention Mechanisms"

## Summary
This paper proposes a novel approach to event detection (ED) by explicitly leveraging argument information through supervised attention mechanisms. The authors argue that existing joint event extraction models (which simultaneously perform ED and argument extraction) fail to effectively utilize argument information for ED, as they primarily benefit argument extraction. They introduce a neural network-based ED model that uses annotated argument information to guide attention, with two strategies for constructing "gold attention" vectors (S1: focusing only on argument words; S2: focusing on arguments and surrounding words). The authors demonstrate that their approach outperforms state-of-the-art methods on ACE 2005, particularly in precision.

## Main Contributions (as I see them)
1. **Important Insight**: The paper correctly identifies a critical gap in existing joint event extraction models—arguing that they do not effectively use argument information for event detection, despite having access to it.
2. **Novel Methodology**: The supervised attention mechanism that explicitly incorporates argument information into ED represents a clean and principled approach to a well-documented problem.
3. **Empirical Validation**: The systematic experiments, including ablation studies comparing models with and without argument information, provide strong evidence supporting their core hypothesis.

## Strengths

1. **Insightful Problem Identification**: The paper makes a compelling case for why existing joint models fail to benefit ED from argument information. The analysis of the ACE 2005 dataset (9800 arguments vs. 5300 triggers) and the clear examples (e.g., "fired" as End-Position vs. Attack) effectively demonstrate the core problem. The authors correctly identify that joint models primarily use argument information for AE, not ED, which is a significant contribution to understanding the limitations of current approaches.

2. **Strong Experimental Validation**: The ablation study (Table 2) is particularly convincing, showing that models using gold attention vectors based on argument information (ANN-Gold1/2) significantly outperform models using only entity information (ANN-ENT) or no argument information (ANN). The comparison with state-of-the-art methods (Table 3) demonstrates clear improvements, especially in precision (ANN-S2 achieves 78.0% precision vs. 77.6% for Liu's FN-Based approach), with statistical significance confirmed by t-tests.

3. **Well-Motivated Attention Strategies**: The two attention strategies (S1 and S2) are well-justified and provide a nice exploration of how argument information can be incorporated. The trade-off between precision and recall (S1 has higher precision but lower recall than S2) is well-explained and demonstrates thoughtful design.

## Weaknesses

1. **Lack of Theoretical/Linguistic Justification**: While the paper provides empirical evidence that argument information helps, it fails to provide a strong theoretical or linguistic justification for *why* arguments would be informative for event detection. The example of "fired" and "former protege" is illustrative but lacks deeper linguistic grounding. Why should the presence of a specific argument role (e.g., Position) help distinguish between event types? Without this, the approach risks being seen as a statistical trick rather than a linguistically motivated improvement.

2. **Questionable Novelty**: The supervised attention mechanism is a relatively straightforward adaptation of existing attention mechanisms. The paper cites Mi et al. (2016) on supervised attention for machine translation, and the core idea of using gold attention vectors is not entirely new. The authors don't sufficiently differentiate their approach from prior work that has used attention for ED (e.g., Liu et al., 2016a; Nguyen and Grishman, 2016), which already incorporate contextual information.

3. **Insufficient Implementation Details**: The paper lacks crucial details about how the "gold attention vectors" are constructed from argument information. For example:
   - How are arguments identified in the training data? Are they identified using a separate model or directly from the annotated arguments?
   - How exactly is the attention weight assigned to each word? The paper mentions "all argument words obtain the same attention," but doesn't explain how this is implemented in practice.
   - How are arguments handled when they appear outside the fixed context window (n)?

4. **Limited Analysis of Failure Cases**: The paper doesn't analyze cases where the approach fails. Understanding failure modes would be valuable for assessing the limitations of the method and guiding future work. For example, are there cases where argument information is misleading (e.g., when arguments are misannotated or ambiguous)?

## Questions to Authors

1. Could you provide more details about how the gold attention vectors are constructed from the annotated arguments? Specifically, how are attention weights assigned to words that are not arguments but are part of the argument phrase (e.g., "former protege" in your example)?

2. The paper claims that "argument information is crucial" for ED, but doesn't provide linguistic evidence for why this is the case. Could you elaborate on the linguistic or semantic relationship between event arguments and event types that would explain this phenomenon?

3. How does your supervised attention mechanism compare to other attention-based ED approaches in terms of training time and computational complexity? Given that attention mechanisms are computationally intensive, this is an important practical consideration.

4. The paper mentions that the unsupervised attention model (ANN) performs poorly (64.6 F1), but doesn't fully explain why. Is this solely due to data sparsity, or are there other factors? How does your supervised attention approach specifically address the data sparsity issue?

## Overall Assessment

This is a well-written paper that makes an important observation about the limitations of existing joint event extraction models. The proposed approach is novel in its explicit use of argument information for event detection, and the empirical results are strong. However, the paper would be significantly strengthened by addressing the weaknesses noted above, particularly by providing a clearer theoretical justification for the approach and more detailed implementation information. The contribution is valuable but not revolutionary, and the novelty could be more clearly articulated.

The paper makes a meaningful contribution to the event extraction literature by demonstrating that argument information can be effectively leveraged for event detection, and the supervised attention mechanism provides a principled way to incorporate this information. With some clarifications and deeper analysis, this could be a strong paper for ACL.
