

# Review of "Exploiting Argument Information to Improve Event Detection via Supervised Attention Mechanisms"

## Summary
This paper proposes a method to improve event detection (ED) by explicitly incorporating event argument information through supervised attention mechanisms. The authors argue that existing joint methods for event extraction (which handle both ED and argument extraction) don't effectively use argument information for ED, and they propose a novel approach where annotated arguments serve as "gold attention" to train an attention mechanism that focuses on argument words during ED. The paper evaluates their approach on the ACE 2005 dataset, claiming state-of-the-art results.

## Main Contributions (as I see them)
1. Demonstrating empirical improvements on ACE 2005 by incorporating argument information through attention mechanisms (though the novelty of this specific approach is questionable)
2. Providing a systematic comparison of different attention strategies for incorporating argument information
3. Showing that argument information is useful for ED through empirical evidence

## Strengths

1. **Clear demonstration of argument utility for ED**: The paper provides a compelling example (the ambiguous "fired" trigger) that effectively illustrates why argument information could be valuable for ED. This is a strong point that makes the problem statement well-motivated.

2. **Strong empirical results on ACE 2005**: The paper shows that their method (ANN-S2) achieves 71.7 F1 on ACE 2005, which is a clear improvement over the previous state-of-the-art (70.7 F1). The t-test showing statistical significance adds credibility to these results.

3. **Thorough analysis of attention strategies**: The paper provides a clear comparison between the two attention strategies (S1 and S2), explaining their different trade-offs between precision and recall. This is valuable for understanding the practical implications of the method.

## Weaknesses

1. **Lack of novelty in the core approach**: The paper's central idea of using attention mechanisms to focus on relevant information is not novel. Attention mechanisms have been widely used in NLP for years, including for event detection (as referenced in the paper by Nguyen and Grishman, 2016). The authors don't sufficiently differentiate their supervised attention approach from existing attention-based methods, making the contribution appear incremental rather than novel.

2. **Insufficient evidence for the core claim about existing methods**: The paper claims that "annotated arguments are actually used for AE, not for ED in existing joint methods," but provides only limited evidence (Table 1 showing small ED improvements). This is a critical claim that lacks thorough empirical support. The authors don't analyze how exactly joint methods process arguments during ED, making their claim seem like an assumption rather than a well-supported observation.

3. **No comparison with simpler alternative approaches**: The paper doesn't compare their supervised attention mechanism with simpler approaches that directly incorporate argument information as features (e.g., adding argument information as additional features to a standard ED model). Without this comparison, it's impossible to determine whether the attention mechanism is truly necessary or if a simpler approach would achieve similar results. This is a significant oversight for a paper claiming to propose a new approach.

4. **Overstated problem with unsupervised attention**: The paper claims that unsupervised attention (ANN) performs poorly due to "data sparseness," but doesn't provide sufficient evidence for this claim. The authors don't show that their supervised attention specifically addresses the data sparseness problem better than other established techniques (like using external data sources as they do with FrameNet in Section 4.4).

5. **Limited evaluation scope**: The evaluation is confined to ACE 2005, which, while a standard benchmark, doesn't demonstrate broader applicability. The paper doesn't test on other datasets or in different settings to show that the approach generalizes well.

## Questions to Authors

1. The paper claims that existing joint methods don't use argument information for ED, but doesn't provide specific evidence from the cited joint methods (e.g., Li et al., 2013; Nguyen et al., 2016) showing how they process arguments during ED. Can you provide specific examples or code snippets from these methods that demonstrate this?

2. Why is a supervised attention mechanism necessary for incorporating argument information when a simpler approach (using argument information as features in a standard ED model) might be equally effective? Please provide a comparison between your approach and such a baseline.

3. The paper states that "ANN performs unexpectedly poorly" (Section 4.3), but doesn't provide a thorough analysis of why unsupervised attention fails. How does this failure differ from the known challenges of data sparseness in ED, and why does supervised attention specifically solve this problem?

4. The paper uses FrameNet to augment training data, but doesn't explain why FrameNet is a suitable source for this task. How does FrameNet's annotation scheme align with ACE's event types and argument roles, and how does this alignment specifically benefit the ED task?

## Overall Recommendation

This paper presents a method that achieves good results on ACE 2005 by incorporating argument information, but the contribution is not sufficiently novel or rigorously validated. The core idea of using attention mechanisms to focus on relevant information is not new, and the paper lacks a proper comparison with simpler, more established approaches for incorporating argument information. The claim about existing joint methods not using argument information for ED is not sufficiently supported. While the empirical results are strong, the paper fails to convincingly demonstrate that the proposed approach is fundamentally better than existing methods for the problem it addresses.

I recommend rejection, as the paper presents an incremental improvement without sufficient novelty or rigorous validation against appropriate baselines. The authors would need to substantially strengthen their contribution by demonstrating the novelty of their approach and providing a more thorough comparison with simpler alternatives before this work would be suitable for ACL.
