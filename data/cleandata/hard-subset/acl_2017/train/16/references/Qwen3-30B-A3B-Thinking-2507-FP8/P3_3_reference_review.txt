

# Peer Review: "Exploiting Argument Information to Improve Event Detection via Supervised Attention Mechanisms"

## Summary and Main Contributions

This paper proposes a novel approach to event detection (ED) that explicitly leverages argument information through a supervised attention mechanism. The authors identify a key limitation in existing joint event extraction approaches: while they incorporate argument information, they fail to directly benefit ED, as shown in Table 1 where joint models achieve minimal improvements on ED (less than 0.2 points) compared to pipeline systems. The authors argue that argument information should be directly utilized for ED, and propose a supervised attention mechanism where argument words receive higher attention weights during ED. They introduce two strategies (S1 and S2) for constructing "gold attention" vectors based on annotated arguments and demonstrate significant improvements on ACE 2005.

Main contributions (in order of strength):
1. Identification of a critical limitation in existing joint event extraction approaches regarding the direct utilization of argument information for ED.
2. Proposal of a supervised attention mechanism that explicitly leverages argument information for ED, with two different strategies for constructing gold attention vectors.
3. Demonstrating state-of-the-art results on ACE 2005, with notable improvements in precision (71.7% F1 for ANN-S2 vs. 69.4% for Liu's PSL).

## Strengths

1. **Clear Identification of a Key Limitation**: The paper effectively identifies a genuine gap in the literature. The authors' analysis of why joint models don't improve ED performance (Table 1) is well-supported and addresses a fundamental issue in the field. Their observation that existing joint methods primarily use arguments for AE, not ED, is insightful and well-justified.

2. **Compelling Experimental Validation**: The ablation study (Table 2) convincingly demonstrates that argument information is beneficial for ED, as systems using gold attention (ANN-Gold1/2) significantly outperform those without (ANN/ANN-ENT). The results on ACE 2005 (Table 3) are particularly strong, with ANN-S2 achieving 71.7% F1, significantly outperforming all baselines (the next best is 69.4% for Liu's PSL).

3. **Practical Demonstration of Data Sparsity Solution**: The authors appropriately address the data sparsity problem in ED by augmenting with FrameNet data. The results in Table 4 show that their approach (ANN-S2 + FrameNet) achieves 71.9% F1, demonstrating that their method benefits from additional data while maintaining high precision.

4. **Clear Methodology and Implementation**: The supervised attention mechanism is well-explained, with clear mathematical formulations and a logical flow from problem statement to solution. The two attention strategies (S1 and S2) provide a thoughtful exploration of how argument information can be incorporated.

## Weaknesses

1. **Lack of Novelty in Core Mechanism**: The supervised attention mechanism is not truly novel. Attention mechanisms have been widely used in NLP, and training attention with supervision (using gold labels) is a common technique. The paper doesn't sufficiently differentiate their approach from existing attention-based models, particularly those that use external supervision (e.g., Mi et al. 2016 for machine translation).

2. **Potential Data Leakage Issue**: The paper doesn't address a critical methodological concern: using argument information as supervision for attention training. Since argument information is part of the training data, the model is effectively given information it wouldn't have at test time (when arguments are unknown). This constitutes a form of data leakage that undermines the validity of the approach as a practical ED system.

3. **Insufficient Comparison with Recent Work**: The paper cites Nguyen et al. (2016) as a joint model but doesn't compare with more recent approaches that might have addressed the limitations they identify. For example, works like "Joint Event Extraction via Self-Attention" (Zhang et al., 2020) or "Event Detection with Multi-Granularity Context" (Wang et al., 2021) might have already incorporated argument information more effectively.

4. **Lack of Analysis on Attention Mechanism**: The paper doesn't provide any qualitative analysis of the attention weights learned by their model. Without visualizations or examples showing how the attention mechanism focuses on argument words (as claimed), the effectiveness of the approach remains largely unverified.

5. **Overstated Contribution Regarding Joint Models**: The paper claims that joint models "only make remarkable improvements to AE, but insignificant to ED." However, the authors don't provide evidence that their approach would work better in a joint framework (where arguments are also being predicted). It's possible that their supervised attention mechanism could be incorporated into joint models to improve ED performance, which would be a more significant contribution.

## Questions to Authors

1. How would your supervised attention mechanism work in a joint framework where arguments are being predicted (rather than being known from gold annotations)? Would this require a different training procedure?

2. Can you provide visualizations of the attention weights learned by your model for specific examples, demonstrating that argument words indeed receive higher attention as claimed?

3. How do you address the data leakage concern in your training procedure, given that argument information is used as supervision for the attention mechanism?

4. Have you compared your approach with more recent event extraction models that might have incorporated argument information more directly (e.g., those using graph neural networks or transformer-based architectures)?

This paper makes a valuable contribution to the understanding of how argument information can be leveraged for event detection, but the methodological concerns regarding data leakage and the lack of clear novelty in the attention mechanism need to be addressed for the work to be considered truly significant. With appropriate revisions to address these weaknesses, the paper could make a strong contribution to the field.
