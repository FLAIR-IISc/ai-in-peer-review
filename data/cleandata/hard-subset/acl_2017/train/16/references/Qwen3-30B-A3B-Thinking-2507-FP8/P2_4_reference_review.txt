

# ACL Review: Exploiting Argument Information to Improve Event Detection via Supervised Attention Mechanisms

## Summary and Main Contributions

This paper addresses event detection (ED) in the context of event extraction, arguing that annotated argument information - typically ignored or only indirectly leveraged in existing approaches - can provide valuable clues for identifying event triggers, especially for ambiguous words. The authors propose a supervised attention mechanism that explicitly uses argument information to guide the attention process in their ED model, rather than treating argument information as a byproduct of joint learning.

The main contributions of the paper, in order of strength, are:
1. The insightful analysis of why existing joint models fail to improve ED (unbalanced data and indirect use of arguments), which identifies a genuine gap in the literature.
2. A simple but effective supervised attention mechanism that explicitly incorporates argument information into ED, with two different attention strategies (S1 and S2).
3. Thorough experimental validation demonstrating that their approach outperforms state-of-the-art methods on ACE 2005, with statistically significant improvements.

## Strengths

1. **Clear Identification of a Real Gap in the Literature**: The paper provides a compelling analysis of why existing joint methods (which simultaneously tackle ED and AE) fail to improve ED performance. The authors correctly point out that the abundance of annotated arguments compared to triggers leads to unbalanced training, and that arguments are only indirectly used for ED in joint models. This is a well-justified critique of the current state of the art.

2. **Strong Experimental Validation**: The paper includes thorough experiments to validate their core hypothesis. The ablation study in Section 4.2 (Correctness of Our Assumption) clearly demonstrates that systems using argument information significantly outperform those that don't, even when controlling for entity information. The results in Table 3 show meaningful improvements over state-of-the-art methods (71.7% F1 vs. 70.7% for Liu's FN-Based approach), with statistically significant gains (p < 0.05).

3. **Simple but Effective Approach**: The proposed supervised attention mechanism is conceptually straightforward and doesn't require complex architectures. The authors demonstrate that by using gold attention vectors constructed from annotated arguments as supervision, they can effectively guide the attention mechanism to focus on relevant words, which directly addresses their core insight.

4. **Systematic Analysis of Different Attention Strategies**: The paper doesn't just propose one solution but systematically investigates two different attention strategies (S1 and S2) and analyzes their trade-offs (precision vs. recall). This shows a comprehensive understanding of the problem and provides practical guidance for future work.

## Weaknesses

1. **Limited Novelty of the Core Approach**: While the application to ED is novel, the supervised attention mechanism itself is not new to NLP. Supervised attention has been used in machine translation (Mi et al., 2016), and the paper doesn't sufficiently differentiate their approach from existing work in other domains. The novelty is primarily in the application to ED with argument information, not in the attention mechanism itself.

2. **Marginal Performance Gains**: The improvements over state-of-the-art are relatively small (0.7-1.0 F1 points), which in the context of event extraction (where gains of 2-3 points are often considered significant) may not be sufficient to justify the paper's claims of "remarkable gains." For example, ANN-S2 achieves 71.7% F1 compared to Liu's FN-Based approach at 70.7%, which is a 1.0-point improvement. This is a modest improvement that may not be considered significant by the ACL community.

3. **Lack of Detailed Error Analysis**: The paper demonstrates statistical significance in the results but doesn't provide any error analysis to show *why* their approach works better. It would be valuable to see specific examples of ambiguous triggers where their method correctly identifies the event type while baselines fail, which would strengthen the argument for the usefulness of argument information.

4. **Unclear Practical Impact**: The paper doesn't discuss how the proposed approach would scale to new domains or languages, or how the supervised attention mechanism would be implemented in a production system. The focus is primarily on the ACE 2005 dataset, which limits the generalizability of the findings.

5. **Questionable Value of FrameNet Augmentation**: The paper uses FrameNet data as additional training data, but the results in Table 4 show only marginal improvements (71.9% F1 for ANN-S2 + FrameNet vs. 71.7% for ANN-S2). The paper doesn't convincingly argue why FrameNet augmentation is necessary or how it specifically benefits their approach beyond the general data augmentation benefits.

## Questions to Authors

1. The paper claims "remarkable gains" over state-of-the-art methods, but the improvements are marginal (0.7-1.0 F1 points). In the context of event extraction research, what threshold would you consider "remarkable" for performance gains, and how do your results meet this threshold?

2. The paper demonstrates that argument information is useful for ED, but doesn't provide specific examples of ambiguous triggers where their approach succeeds while baselines fail. Could you provide a few representative examples to illustrate when and why your method works better?

3. The authors claim that "argument words should acquire more attention than other words," but the paper doesn't explain why argument words are more informative for event detection than other contextual words. Could you elaborate on the theoretical basis for this assumption?

## Overall Assessment

This paper makes a valid contribution by identifying a genuine gap in the literature regarding the use of argument information for event detection and proposing a simple yet effective solution. The experimental validation is thorough and provides evidence for the core hypothesis. However, the novelty of the approach is limited, and the performance gains, while statistically significant, are relatively modest in the context of event extraction research.

The paper would be significantly strengthened by a more detailed error analysis showing specific cases where their method succeeds, a clearer discussion of the novelty relative to existing attention mechanisms in NLP, and a more compelling justification for the magnitude of the performance gains.

Given the paper's clear insight, thorough validation, and reasonable contribution to the field, I recommend acceptance, though the marginal improvements over state-of-the-art are a concern that should be addressed in the author response.
