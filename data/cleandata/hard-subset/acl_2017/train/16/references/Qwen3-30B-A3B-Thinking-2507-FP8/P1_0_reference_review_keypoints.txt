- Proposes using supervised attention that explicitly leverages argument information to improve event detection (ED).
- Identifies why existing joint models fail to improve ED: indirect use of arguments and data imbalance.
- Main contributions: analysis of joint-model limitations, a supervised attention mechanism with two gold-attention strategies (S1, S2), and strong ACE 2005 results.
- Strong motivation and insight: clear argument that annotated arguments provide crucial clues for ambiguous event triggers.
- Clear methodology: conceptually simple supervised attention with systematic evaluation and ablation study showing argument importance.
- Strong empirical results: ANN-S2 achieves highest reported ACE 2005 F1 (71.7%), statistically significant improvements, and gains from FrameNet augmentation.
- Provides theoretical/empirical insight into why unsupervised attention fails for ED due to data sparseness and how supervision helps.
- Limited novelty: supervised attention is not a novel concept and related prior work has used argument information indirectly.
- Marginal performance gains: only a 1.0 F1 improvement over prior SOTA, statistically significant but modest in practical terms.
- Limited evaluation scope: experiments only on ACE 2005; no results on TAC KBP, ERE, or comparisons to recent post-2016 methods.
- Assumes availability of annotated arguments and lacks analysis of robustness to missing or noisy argument annotations.
- Ambiguous distinction from joint models: unclear how supervised attention fundamentally differs from joint learning approaches.
- Question on robustness: how does the method handle missing or incorrect argument information during training?
- Question on definition: unclear which tokens are treated as "argument words" and how they’re determined.
- Question on relation to joint models: clarify how this approach differs from or relates to prior joint ED/argument models.
- Overall recommendation: accept with minor revisions, asking for clearer novelty framing and better contextualization of performance gains.
