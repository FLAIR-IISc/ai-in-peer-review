

# ACL Review

## Summary
This paper proposes a supervised attention mechanism to explicitly leverage argument information for improving event detection (ED) performance. The authors argue that existing joint methods for event extraction (ED and argument extraction) do not effectively use argument information for ED, only for argument extraction. They propose a neural network-based approach where argument words are given higher attention through supervised attention mechanisms, with gold attention vectors constructed from annotated arguments. The paper evaluates their approach on ACE 2005, showing improvements over state-of-the-art methods, and also explores augmentation with FrameNet data.

## Main Contributions (as I see them)
1. A supervised attention mechanism for event detection that explicitly uses argument information
2. Two strategies for constructing gold attention vectors from argument information
3. Systematic evaluation of the proposed approach on ACE 2005 with FrameNet augmentation

## Strengths
1. **Valid Identification of a Gap**: The paper correctly identifies an important limitation in existing joint approaches for event extraction - that argument information is not effectively used for event detection, only for argument extraction. This is a valid observation supported by the empirical evidence in Table 1.

2. **Novel Methodological Approach**: The proposed supervised attention mechanism is a creative way to incorporate argument information directly into the event detection process, rather than relying on indirect methods through joint learning. The two attention strategies (S1 and S2) provide useful variations to explore.

3. **Comprehensive Experimental Evaluation**: The paper provides a thorough experimental evaluation, including comparisons with multiple state-of-the-art methods, ablation studies, and augmentation with FrameNet data. The results consistently show improvements over baseline methods, particularly with the supervised attention mechanisms.

## Weaknesses
1. **Numerous Technical and Formatting Inconsistencies**: The paper contains multiple technical errors and formatting issues that undermine its professionalism and clarity. For example:
   - Equation errors: "αke = exp(we eTk )∑ i exp(we e T i )" should be "α_k^e = exp(w_e e_k^T)/∑_i exp(w_e e_i^T)" (with proper notation for vector operations)
   - Inconsistent terminology: "ACE 2005" vs. "ACE2005" (e.g., "ACE20051" in the text)
   - Typographical errors: "lacks of" instead of "lacks" (Section 4.4), "on the contrast" instead of "on the contrary" (Section 5)
   - Unclear numerical values: In Strategy S1, it's not specified what numerical value is assigned to argument words (e.g., 1.0 vs. 0.5)
   - Inconsistent citation formats throughout the references (e.g., "Ahn.,? 2006" should be "Ahn, 2006")

2. **Insufficient Justification for Key Claims**: The paper states "only 10% of triggers in ACE 2005 are entities" (Section 3.2.1) but provides no citation or evidence for this statistic, which is critical to their argument. Without this, the claim lacks credibility.

3. **Ambiguous Experimental Design in Key Comparisons**: The comparison between ANN-ENT and ANN-Gold1/2 (Section 4.2) is confusing because ANN-ENT is supposed to use entity information, and since all arguments are entities, it's unclear why ANN-Gold1/2 would outperform ANN-ENT. The authors don't adequately explain this distinction, making the experimental comparison less convincing.

4. **Inconsistent Treatment of FrameNet Data**: The paper states that "their data can not be used in the proposed approach without further processing, because it lacks of both argument and entity information" (Section 4.4), but then they proceed to use FrameNet data without clarifying how they obtained the missing argument and entity information. The process for obtaining this information (using RPI Joint Information Extraction System) is mentioned but not adequately explained.

5. **Missing Methodological Details**: The paper lacks sufficient detail about key hyperparameters. For example, in Section 3.2.2, they state "we use the standard normal distribution to model attention distributions of words around arguments, which means that µ = 0.0, σ = 1.0, and the window size is set to 3" but don't explain why these values were chosen or whether they were tuned on the development set.

## Questions to Authors
1. In Strategy S1, you state "all argument words in the given context obtain the same attention, whereas other words get no attention." What is the numerical value assigned to argument words (e.g., 1.0) and to non-argument words (e.g., 0.0)? Please clarify this in the paper.

2. In Section 3.2.2, you state that you use "the standard normal distribution" with µ = 0.0, σ = 1.0, and window size = 3. Were these values tuned on the development set, or were they fixed? If tuned, what was the range of values considered?

3. In Section 4.2, you compare ANN-ENT with ANN-Gold1/2 to claim that argument information is necessary for ED. However, since all arguments are entities, it's unclear why ANN-Gold1/2 would outperform ANN-ENT. Could you clarify the distinction between these two approaches?

4. You claim "only 10% of triggers in ACE 2005 are entities" (Section 3.2.1) but provide no citation. Could you provide the source for this statistic?

5. In Section 4.4, you state that you "only use core elements" from FrameNet. What is the precise definition of "core" elements in FrameNet (e.g., FrameNet classifies frame elements into three groups: core, peripheral and extra-thematic)? Please clarify this in the paper.

## Overall Recommendation
I recommend rejection. While the paper identifies a valid problem and proposes a potentially useful method, the numerous technical inconsistencies, formatting errors, and lack of sufficient detail significantly undermine the paper's quality and credibility. As a perfectionist reviewer, I find these minor flaws too numerous to overlook, even though the core idea has merit. The paper would need substantial revision to address these issues before it could be considered for publication.
