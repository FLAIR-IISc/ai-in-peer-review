

# Review of "Exploiting Argument Information to Improve Event Detection via Supervised Attention Mechanisms"

## Summary
This paper proposes a novel approach to event detection (ED) by explicitly leveraging argument information through a supervised attention mechanism. The authors correctly identify that existing joint models for event extraction primarily use argument information for argument extraction (AE), not for event detection (ED), which explains why joint models show minimal improvements on ED. They propose a simple but effective method where gold attention vectors are constructed based on annotated arguments, and the attention mechanism is trained to mimic these gold vectors. The approach achieves state-of-the-art results on ACE 2005, with their best model (ANN-S2) reaching 71.7 F1 compared to the previous best of 70.7.

## Main Contributions (in order of strength)
1. **Conceptual innovation**: The core contribution is the insight that argument information should be explicitly used for ED (not just for AE), challenging the prevailing joint model approach and providing a clear explanation for why joint models fail to improve ED.
2. **Novel implementation**: The supervised attention mechanism that uses argument information as explicit supervision for attention calculation is a clever and effective implementation of the conceptual insight.
3. **Strong experimental validation**: The systematic ablation studies (Table 2) demonstrate that argument information is indeed crucial for ED, and the results show significant improvements over state-of-the-art.

## Strengths
1. **Novel conceptual contribution**: This is the strongest point of the paper. The authors correctly diagnose a fundamental problem in the field (joint models use arguments for AE, not ED) and propose a fresh direction for ED research. This is not just a minor methodological tweak but a genuine conceptual shift in how we think about event detection.
   
2. **Clear and compelling justification**: The paper provides a well-structured argument with a strong example (the "fired" trigger ambiguity) and a thorough analysis of why joint models fail to help ED (unbalanced data and indirect use of arguments). This makes the contribution feel significant rather than incremental.

3. **Effective implementation with meaningful results**: The supervised attention mechanism is simple but effective, and the results are meaningful in the context of the field (1.0 F1 point improvement over state-of-the-art). The ablation study in Table 2 provides strong evidence that the improvement comes from using argument information, not other factors.

4. **Addresses a real problem**: The paper correctly identifies that data sparseness is a key issue in ED, and their approach (using argument information to improve attention) directly addresses this problem. The FrameNet augmentation results (Table 4) further support this.

## Weaknesses
1. **Modest absolute improvement**: While 1.0 F1 point is meaningful in this field, the paper could have provided more context on how significant this improvement is compared to the field's typical progress. The paper claims "remarkable gains," but the field often sees 0.5-1.0 point improvements as significant, so this is a bit of an overstatement.

2. **Limited analysis of which cases benefit**: The paper provides one strong example (the "fired" trigger), but doesn't provide a systematic analysis of which specific event types or trigger words benefit most from argument information. A more detailed case study would strengthen the contribution.

3. **FrameNet alignment justification**: The paper states that "frames in FN are analogous to events in ACE," but doesn't clearly explain how the different taxonomies (ACE's 33 event types vs. FrameNet's frame types) are aligned. This is a critical gap in the methodology that should be addressed.

## Questions to Authors
1. The paper states FrameNet frames are analogous to ACE events, but ACE and FrameNet have different event taxonomies. Could you elaborate on how you mapped FrameNet frames to ACE event types for the augmentation? What specific alignment strategy did you use?

2. The paper claims "argument words should acquire more attention than other words" as the core idea. Could you provide more examples beyond the "fired" case to demonstrate how argument information resolves ambiguity in different event types?

3. The precision improvement (78.0 vs 77.6) is small but notable. Could you provide specific examples where your approach correctly identifies an event that previous methods misclassified, particularly for ambiguous triggers?

## Overall Recommendation
This paper makes a significant conceptual contribution to the event extraction field by proposing a novel way to use argument information for event detection. The insight that argument information should be explicitly used for ED (not just for AE) is fresh, well-justified, and leads to meaningful improvements. While the absolute performance gain is modest (1.0 F1 point), this is a meaningful improvement in the context of the field, and the paper provides strong evidence that the improvement comes from the novel idea, not from other factors.

I believe this paper should be accepted. The conceptual novelty and the clear direction it provides for future work outweigh the minor weaknesses. This is the kind of paper that can influence the field's approach to event detection, which is exactly what I look for as a reviewer prioritizing novelty over methodological perfection.

**Recommendation: Accept**
