

# Peer Review: "Exploiting Argument Information to Improve Event Detection via Supervised Attention Mechanisms"

## Summary and Main Contributions

This paper proposes a novel approach to improve event detection (ED) by explicitly leveraging argument information through supervised attention mechanisms. The authors identify a critical gap in existing joint models for event extraction: while these models simultaneously handle event detection and argument extraction, they primarily use argument information to improve argument extraction rather than event detection. The authors argue that argument information can provide crucial clues for identifying and categorizing events, especially for ambiguous trigger words.

The main contributions of this work are:
1. A novel supervised attention mechanism that explicitly exploits argument information for event detection, rather than indirectly through joint models.
2. Two strategies (S1 and S2) for constructing gold attention vectors based on annotated arguments, which are used to supervise the attention mechanism.
3. Comprehensive experiments demonstrating that the proposed approach significantly outperforms state-of-the-art methods on the ACE 2005 benchmark dataset.

## Strengths

1. **Significant Gap Identification**: The paper convincingly identifies a critical oversight in existing joint event extraction methods. The analysis in Section 1, particularly Table 1 showing that joint methods achieve only marginal improvements on ED (less than 0.2 points) while significantly improving AE (over 1.9 points), is compelling evidence for the authors' claim that arguments are not effectively used for ED in current approaches. This is a well-motivated and important contribution to the field.

2. **Clear and Effective Methodology**: The proposed supervised attention mechanism is conceptually simple yet effective. The two attention strategies (S1 and S2) provide a systematic way to incorporate argument information, and the implementation details are well-explained. The ablation study in Section 4.2 (Table 2) provides strong evidence that argument information is crucial for ED, as systems using gold attention (ANN-Gold1/2) significantly outperform those without (ANN and ANN-ENT).

3. **Strong Experimental Results**: The results in Table 3 are impressive, with the proposed ANN-S2 achieving 71.7% F1 on ACE 2005, significantly outperforming state-of-the-art methods (e.g., Liu's FN-Based at 70.7% F1). The paper also demonstrates the effectiveness of FrameNet augmentation in Table 4, showing that the proposed approach benefits from additional training data, which is valuable for addressing data sparsity in ED.

## Weaknesses

1. **Limited Novelty**: While the supervised attention mechanism is novel for ED, the paper doesn't sufficiently differentiate it from existing attention-based methods. The authors cite Mi et al. (2016) as inspiration for their attention strategy but don't clearly articulate how their approach differs or improves upon this work specifically for ED. The paper would benefit from a more thorough comparison with existing attention-based ED methods to establish true novelty.

2. **Insufficient Analysis of Why the Approach Works**: The paper demonstrates that the proposed approach works well, but doesn't provide sufficient insight into *why* it works. For instance, the authors don't analyze how the attention weights learned by their model correspond to actual event-relevant words. A qualitative analysis showing examples where the attention mechanism correctly focuses on argument words would strengthen the paper's claims.

3. **Lack of Discussion on Limitations**: The paper assumes perfect argument annotations, but doesn't address how the approach would perform with noisy or incomplete argument annotations, which is common in real-world scenarios. The authors should discuss this limitation and potentially present experiments with varying levels of annotation quality.

4. **Overstated Claims About Joint Models**: The paper claims that joint models "do not effectively use argument information for ED," but doesn't provide a detailed comparison of how their approach differs from joint models in terms of argument usage. The paper would benefit from a more nuanced discussion of the relationship between their approach and joint models, rather than presenting them as mutually exclusive paradigms.

## Questions to Authors

1. How would your supervised attention mechanism handle cases where argument annotations are missing or incomplete for certain triggers? Would the model fall back to the unsupervised attention strategy in such cases?

2. Could you provide more detailed analysis of the attention weights learned by your model? Specifically, how do the attention weights correlate with the actual importance of words for event detection, and how does this differ between S1 and S2 strategies?

3. In Table 4, you show that FrameNet augmentation improves performance, but the gains are modest (e.g., ANN-S2 + FrameNet: 71.9% vs. ANN-S2: 71.7%). Given that FrameNet is a different resource with different annotation schemes, how do you explain the limited improvement from augmentation?

## Overall Recommendation

This paper makes a meaningful contribution to the event extraction field by addressing a significant gap in how argument information is used for event detection. The proposed supervised attention mechanism is well-motivated, clearly explained, and demonstrates strong empirical results. While the novelty could be better established and more analysis of why the approach works would strengthen the paper, the core contribution is valuable and well-supported by the experiments. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions to address the limitations noted above. The paper's clear writing, strong empirical results, and important theoretical insight make it a worthy contribution to ACL.
