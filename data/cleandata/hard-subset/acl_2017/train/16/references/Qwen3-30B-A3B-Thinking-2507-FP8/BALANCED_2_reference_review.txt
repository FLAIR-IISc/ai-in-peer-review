

# Review of "Exploiting Argument Information to Improve Event Detection via Supervised Attention Mechanisms"

## Summary of the Paper
This paper proposes a novel approach to event detection (ED) that explicitly leverages argument information through a supervised attention mechanism. The authors identify a significant gap in existing event extraction systems: while joint methods for event extraction (which handle both event detection and argument extraction) exist, they typically don't effectively utilize argument information to improve event detection. The authors argue that argument information can provide crucial clues for identifying and categorizing events, especially for ambiguous trigger words. Their approach constructs "gold attention" vectors based on annotated arguments to supervise the attention mechanism, rather than using unsupervised attention. They evaluate their method on the ACE 2005 dataset and also incorporate FrameNet data for additional training.

## Main Contributions (as I see them)
1. **Novel insight into argument information utilization**: The paper correctly identifies and analyzes the problem of underutilizing argument information in event detection systems, which is a significant contribution to the field.
2. **Supervised attention mechanism for ED**: The authors propose a neural network-based approach with a supervised attention mechanism that explicitly incorporates argument information for event detection, with two different strategies for constructing gold attention vectors.
3. **State-of-the-art performance**: The proposed approach achieves the best performance on the widely used ACE 2005 benchmark dataset, with significant improvements over previous state-of-the-art methods, particularly in precision.

## Strengths

1. **Important and novel insight**: The paper correctly identifies a critical gap in existing event extraction systems - that argument information is available but not effectively utilized for improving event detection. This insight is valuable and well-justified through examples and analysis of previous joint methods. The authors effectively demonstrate that existing joint models only marginally improve ED performance because they don't explicitly use argument information for ED (as shown in Table 1).

2. **Strong empirical results**: The experimental results are impressive and clearly demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed approach. The ANN-S2 model achieves 71.7 F1 on ACE 2005, significantly outperforming the previous state-of-the-art (70.7 for Liu's FN-Based approach). The precision improvement (78.0% vs. 77.6% for the previous best) is particularly notable and addresses a key challenge in event detection.

3. **Systematic investigation of attention strategies**: The authors carefully design and evaluate two different strategies for constructing gold attention vectors (S1: focusing only on argument words; S2: focusing on argument words and their surrounding context). The comparative analysis of these strategies provides valuable insights into how different attention mechanisms affect performance, explaining why S1 achieves higher precision while S2 achieves higher recall.

4. **Clear explanation of data sparsity issue**: The paper correctly identifies that data sparsity is a key reason why unsupervised attention mechanisms perform poorly for event detection (Table 3), and demonstrates that incorporating argument information through supervised attention helps address this issue. The results with FrameNet augmentation (Table 4) further support this claim.

5. **Reproducibility**: The authors provide clear implementation details, hyperparameter settings, and experimental setup, making the approach easy to reproduce.

## Weaknesses

1. **Limited comparison with recent state-of-the-art methods**: While the paper compares with several previous methods (Li et al., 2013; Liu et al., 2016a,b; Nguyen et al., 2016), it does not include comparisons with some of the most recent advances in event detection, such as transformer-based models or more sophisticated attention mechanisms (e.g., those using BERT or similar architectures). This weakens the claim that the proposed method achieves "state-of-the-art" performance.

2. **Lack of attention visualization**: The paper doesn't provide visualizations of the attention weights to demonstrate that the model is indeed focusing on argument words as intended. Without such evidence, it's difficult to verify that the supervised attention mechanism is working as claimed. This is a significant omission given the centrality of the attention mechanism to the proposed approach.

3. **Ambiguity in gold attention construction**: The description of how gold attention vectors are constructed (particularly for cases with no arguments in the context) is somewhat ambiguous. For example, the paper states "for candidates without any annotated arguments in context, we force all entities to average the whole attention" but doesn't clarify what this means or how it's implemented. This lack of clarity makes it difficult to fully reproduce the method.

4. **Insufficient ablation study**: The paper doesn't include a thorough ablation study to isolate the contribution of different components. For example, it doesn't clearly separate the contribution of the supervised attention mechanism from the contribution of using FrameNet data. This makes it difficult to determine what aspect of the approach is most responsible for the performance gains.

5. **Limited analysis of failure cases**: The paper doesn't provide any analysis of cases where the proposed method fails, which would help understand the limitations of the approach and guide future work.

## Questions to Authors

1. Could you provide attention visualization examples showing that the supervised attention mechanism is indeed focusing on argument words as intended? This would strengthen the claim about the effectiveness of the supervised attention mechanism.

2. How does your method compare with recent transformer-based event detection approaches (e.g., those using BERT or similar architectures)? Given the rapid advances in transformer models for NLP, it would be valuable to see how your approach compares with these more recent methods.

3. Could you clarify the exact procedure for handling cases where there are no annotated arguments in the context (as mentioned in S1)? Specifically, what does "forcing all entities to average the whole attention" mean in practice?

4. Can you provide a more detailed ablation study showing the contribution of each component (supervised attention, FrameNet augmentation, S1 vs S2 strategies)? This would help better understand what aspects of your approach are most beneficial.

5. Could you discuss the limitations of your approach and provide examples of cases where it fails? Understanding the failure cases would be valuable for future work.

## Overall Recommendation
The paper makes a valuable contribution by correctly identifying and addressing a significant gap in existing event extraction systems. The proposed supervised attention mechanism is well-motivated and demonstrates impressive results on the ACE 2005 benchmark. However, the paper would be significantly strengthened by addressing the weaknesses noted above, particularly by including attention visualizations, providing clearer descriptions of the gold attention construction, and conducting a more thorough ablation study.

While the paper has strong merits, the lack of comparison with recent state-of-the-art methods and the absence of attention visualizations prevent it from being a clear candidate for acceptance at ACL. I recommend a **weak reject** with the understanding that with the suggested revisions, the paper would be competitive for acceptance.

The authors should address the questions above in their response, particularly focusing on providing attention visualizations, clarifying the gold attention construction, and including a more thorough ablation study. If these issues are adequately addressed, the paper would be a strong candidate for acceptance at ACL.
