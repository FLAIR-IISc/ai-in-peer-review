

# Peer Review: "Exploiting Argument Information to Improve Event Detection via Supervised Attention Mechanisms"

## Summary and Contributions

This paper proposes a novel approach to event detection (ED) by explicitly leveraging argument information through supervised attention mechanisms. The authors argue that existing joint methods for ED and argument extraction fail to effectively use argument information for ED, as they primarily use arguments to improve argument extraction (AE) rather than ED. Their core contribution is a supervised attention mechanism that directly incorporates argument information to improve ED performance. The paper introduces two strategies for constructing "gold attention" vectors based on annotated arguments (S1: only arguments get attention; S2: arguments plus surrounding words get attention). The authors demonstrate that their approach outperforms state-of-the-art methods on ACE 2005 with significant improvements.

Main contributions in order of strength:
1. The insightful analysis of why joint methods fail to improve ED (they use arguments indirectly for AE, not directly for ED).
2. The supervised attention mechanism that explicitly leverages argument information for ED.
3. Systematic evaluation of different attention strategies and their trade-offs between precision and recall.

## Strengths

1. **Compelling theoretical insight**: The paper makes a strong case that argument information is underutilized in ED, supported by the critical observation in Table 1 that joint methods achieve negligible improvements on ED (less than 0.2 F1 points) while making significant gains on AE. This analysis challenges the common practice of ignoring arguments in ED and provides a clear theoretical foundation for the proposed approach.

2. **Simple yet effective method**: The supervised attention mechanism is straightforward to implement and integrates well with existing ED frameworks. The paper demonstrates clear empirical gains (71.7% F1 for ANN-S2 vs. 69.4% for the previous best method), with statistically significant improvements (p < 0.05). The method's simplicity is a strength, as it doesn't require complex modifications to existing ED pipelines.

3. **Thorough evaluation of attention strategies**: The paper provides a thoughtful comparison of S1 (higher precision) and S2 (higher recall) strategies, offering practitioners valuable insights into the trade-offs between precision and recall when incorporating argument information. This level of analysis is often missing in similar papers.

## Weaknesses

1. **Lack of theoretical justification for why arguments help ED**: While the paper provides an example (the ambiguous "fired" trigger), it doesn't systematically explain *why* argument information should help with trigger identification. The connection between arguments and event types needs stronger theoretical grounding beyond the single example. Without this, the approach may seem like an empirical trick rather than a principled method.

2. **Limited comparison with recent state-of-the-art methods**: The paper compares against methods from 2013-2016, but doesn't evaluate against more recent neural approaches (e.g., BERT-based models). Given that the field has advanced significantly since these works, this limits the paper's relevance to current research. For instance, the Skip-CNN baseline (71.3% recall) is now outdated.

3. **Insufficient ablation study**: The paper doesn't isolate the contribution of the supervised attention mechanism from other components. It's unclear how much of the improvement comes from the attention mechanism versus other aspects of the model (like entity information). A proper ablation study would strengthen the claim that argument information is the key factor.

4. **Limited discussion of practical implications**: The paper assumes argument annotations are available, but doesn't address how this would work in domains where such annotations don't exist. The authors mention using FrameNet as external data, but the marginal improvement (only 0.2 F1 points) suggests this augmentation isn't as impactful as claimed.

5. **Overstated novelty**: The paper claims to be the first to use argument information explicitly for ED, but the approach resembles supervised attention mechanisms used in other NLP tasks (e.g., machine translation). The novelty is limited to applying this concept specifically to ED with argument information.

## Questions to Authors

1. The paper states that only 10% of triggers are entities, but then uses entity information in the model. How does the model handle triggers that aren't entities, especially since the entity information might be misleading for non-entity triggers?

2. The paper uses FrameNet to augment training data, but the improvement is marginal (0.2 F1 points for ANN-S2). Given that FrameNet is a large resource, why does this augmentation have such a small impact on performance?

3. How would your approach perform when combined with more recent neural architectures (e.g., BERT-based models) that have become the standard in NLP? Would the supervised attention mechanism still provide significant gains?

## Overall Assessment

This is a solid paper that makes a meaningful contribution to event detection by demonstrating the value of argument information. The approach is well-motivated, well-executed, and shows clear empirical gains. While the paper has some limitations in terms of novelty, theoretical grounding, and comparison to more recent methods, the core insight is valuable and the method is simple and effective. With minor revisions addressing the weaknesses noted above, this paper would be a strong contribution to ACL.

**Recommendation: Accept** (with minor revisions)
