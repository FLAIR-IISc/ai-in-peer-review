

# Review for ACL Conference

## Summary and Main Contributions

This paper proposes a novel approach to improve event detection (ED) by explicitly exploiting argument information through supervised attention mechanisms. The authors argue that while existing joint models for ED and argument extraction (AE) typically use argument information indirectly (benefiting AE more than ED), argument information can provide crucial clues for ED, especially for ambiguous trigger words. Their main contributions are: (1) identifying the limitation of existing joint models in utilizing argument information for ED, (2) introducing a supervised attention mechanism that explicitly leverages argument information, and (3) demonstrating significant improvements over state-of-the-art methods on the ACE 2005 benchmark.

## Strengths

1. **Strong Motivation and Problem Identification**: The paper provides a compelling analysis of why existing joint models fail to effectively use argument information for ED. The authors clearly demonstrate this through the "fired" example (End-Position vs. Attack), showing how argument information resolves ambiguity. Their analysis of the two key reasons for poor ED performance in joint models (data imbalance and lack of argument information in trigger pre-prediction) is well-justified and forms a solid foundation for their approach.

2. **Clear and Well-Designed Experiments**: The experimental evaluation is thorough and well-structured. The authors conduct systematic comparisons against multiple state-of-the-art methods (both pipeline and joint approaches) and provide ablation studies that convincingly demonstrate the value of their approach. The results in Table 3 are particularly strong, with ANN-S2 achieving 71.7% F1, significantly outperforming the previous best (Liu's FN-Based at 70.7%). The additional experiments with FrameNet augmentation further validate the approach's robustness.

3. **Novel Technical Approach**: The supervised attention mechanism represents a novel application of attention in event detection. The two attention strategies (S1 and S2) are well-motivated and systematically evaluated. The paper's innovation lies in using gold attention vectors constructed from annotated arguments to train the attention mechanism, which directly addresses the problem identified in the introduction. This is a clear technical contribution that goes beyond simply applying attention mechanisms to ED.

4. **Relevance to the Field**: Event detection is a fundamental task in event extraction, and improving ED is crucial for downstream applications. The paper addresses a meaningful problem in this space with clear practical implications. The ACE 2005 benchmark is widely used in the field, making the results directly comparable to existing work.

## Weaknesses

1. **Limited Novelty of Core Mechanism**: While the application of supervised attention to ED is novel, the attention mechanism itself builds on existing work (e.g., Mi et al., 2016). The paper doesn't sufficiently articulate how this supervised attention differs from other attention mechanisms used in NLP, which might make it seem less novel to some reviewers. The core mechanism is an application of existing techniques rather than a novel technique itself.

2. **Lack of Qualitative Analysis**: The paper provides strong quantitative results but lacks qualitative examples showing how the attention mechanism actually works. It would be valuable to see specific examples where the model correctly identifies an event trigger thanks to the attention mechanism, especially in ambiguous cases. Without this, it's difficult to assess the actual impact of the attention mechanism beyond the numbers.

3. **Insufficient Analysis of Failure Cases**: The paper doesn't analyze cases where the model fails, which would help identify the limitations of the approach. For instance, what types of events or sentence structures are particularly challenging for the model? This information would be valuable for future work and for understanding the scope of the approach's applicability.

4. **Unclear Relationship Between Supervised Attention and Data Sparseness**: The paper mentions data sparseness as a problem for unsupervised attention (Table 3 shows ANN performs poorly), but it's not clear how the supervised attention mechanism specifically addresses this issue. The FrameNet augmentation helps, but it's not clear how supervised attention interacts with this data augmentation to improve performance.

## Questions to Authors

1. How does your supervised attention mechanism specifically differ from other attention mechanisms used in NLP (e.g., in machine translation or other event extraction tasks)? What is the precise novelty of your approach to attention mechanisms?

2. Could you provide a few qualitative examples showing how the attention mechanism helps the model identify event triggers in ambiguous cases, particularly where argument information was crucial (like the "fired" example)?

3. The paper states that data sparseness is a problem for unsupervised attention, but it's not clear how your supervised attention mechanism specifically addresses this issue. Could you elaborate on the relationship between your approach and data sparseness?

## Recommendation

This is a strong paper with a clear problem statement, well-designed experiments, and significant improvements over state-of-the-art methods. The main contributions are well-motivated and well-validated. The paper makes a meaningful contribution to the event detection literature by demonstrating that argument information can be explicitly leveraged to improve ED performance.

The main weaknesses (lack of qualitative analysis, limited novelty of core mechanism, insufficient failure case analysis) are important but do not undermine the paper's overall contribution. With reasonable revisions addressing these points, this paper would be an excellent fit for ACL.

I recommend **acceptance** with minor revisions. The paper's main contribution—demonstrating that argument information can be explicitly used to improve ED through supervised attention—is significant and well-supported by the experimental results. The improvements over state-of-the-art methods are substantial (71.7% vs. 70.7% F1) and the ablation studies convincingly show the value of argument information for ED.
