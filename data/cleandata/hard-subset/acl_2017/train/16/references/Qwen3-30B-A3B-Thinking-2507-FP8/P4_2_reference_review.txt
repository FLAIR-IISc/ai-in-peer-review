

# Review of "Exploiting Argument Information to Improve Event Detection via Supervised Attention Mechanisms"

## Summary of the Paper
This paper addresses the Event Detection (ED) task in event extraction, arguing that argument information (typically used for Event Argument Extraction) can provide crucial disambiguation clues for ED, especially for ambiguous trigger words. The authors identify that existing joint models for ED and Event Argument Extraction (AE) don't effectively use argument information for ED due to data imbalance and indirect usage (arguments are primarily used for AE). They propose a supervised attention mechanism that explicitly uses annotated argument information to guide the attention of the ED model, so that argument words receive higher attention weights. The paper introduces two strategies for constructing gold attention vectors (S1: only focus on argument words; S2: focus on arguments and surrounding words) and evaluates the approach on the ACE 2005 benchmark.

## Main Contributions
1. **Analysis of why joint models fail to effectively use argument information for ED**: The paper provides a clear and well-supported explanation for the limited improvement in ED performance when using joint models (data imbalance and indirect usage of arguments), which is a significant theoretical contribution.

2. **Supervised attention mechanism for explicit argument exploitation in ED**: The core technical contribution is the novel supervised attention mechanism that learns to focus on argument words during ED, directly using annotated arguments as supervision. This represents a fundamentally different approach from existing joint models.

3. **Systematic evaluation of attention strategies**: The paper provides a thorough comparison of different attention strategies (S1 vs. S2) and their impact on precision and recall, offering valuable insights for future work.

## Strengths

1. **Clear and well-justified problem statement**: The paper effectively identifies a gap in the literature (joint models not using arguments effectively for ED) with a compelling example ("fired" as End-Position vs. Attack) and a strong rationale based on data imbalance and implementation details. The Table 1 comparison of pipeline vs. joint methods clearly demonstrates the problem.

2. **Novel technical approach with strong theoretical grounding**: The supervised attention mechanism represents a meaningful contribution that directly addresses the identified problem. Unlike previous joint models that use arguments indirectly for AE, this approach explicitly uses arguments for ED through a supervised mechanism, which is theoretically sound and well-motivated.

3. **Comprehensive experimental evaluation**: The paper provides a thorough evaluation on the standard ACE 2005 benchmark, including ablation studies (ANN vs. ANN-Gold1 vs. ANN-Gold2) that clearly demonstrate the value of argument information. The comparison with multiple state-of-the-art methods (Li's joint, Liu's PSL, Liu's FN-Based, Nguyen's joint, Skip-CNN) is appropriate and shows significant improvements (ANN-S2 achieves 71.7 F1, outperforming all baselines).

4. **Strong empirical results with statistical significance**: The proposed method (ANN-S2) achieves the best performance on ACE 2005 (71.7 F1), with statistically significant improvement over all baselines (p < 0.05). The paper also demonstrates the value of argument information through the ANN-Gold1/2 vs. ANN-ENT comparison.

5. **Additional validation through FrameNet augmentation**: The experiments with FrameNet data further validate the approach, showing that the benefits of argument information are consistent even when addressing data sparsity issues.

## Weaknesses

1. **Outdated comparison with state-of-the-art methods**: The paper compares with methods up to 2016, but there have been significant advances in event extraction since then (e.g., Transformer-based models like BERT, ERNIE, and other recent neural architectures). This limits the perceived novelty and impact of the contribution.

2. **Lack of detailed analysis of failure cases**: While the paper demonstrates overall improvements, it doesn't provide specific examples of cases where the approach failed or where argument information was insufficient. A case study would strengthen the argument for when the approach works best.

3. **Insufficient discussion of computational efficiency**: The paper doesn't discuss the computational cost of the proposed approach compared to baselines, which is important for practical adoption. The attention mechanism adds complexity that might impact scalability.

4. **Limited analysis of the attention mechanism's contribution**: While the paper compares S1 and S2, it doesn't provide a thorough ablation study of the attention mechanism itself (e.g., what happens if the attention mechanism is removed or replaced with a different approach). This makes it difficult to isolate the contribution of the attention mechanism.

5. **Unclear generalizability to other datasets**: The paper focuses exclusively on ACE 2005, without discussing how the approach might generalize to other event extraction datasets or settings. The use of FrameNet as auxiliary data is mentioned, but the paper doesn't address whether the approach would work well with other external resources.

## Questions to Authors

1. The paper compares with methods up to 2016. How would your approach perform against more recent state-of-the-art event extraction methods (e.g., those using Transformer architectures like BERT or ERNIE)?

2. Could you provide more details on the FrameNet augmentation process? Specifically, how many additional training samples were added, and how did the quality of these automatically extracted event samples affect the results?

3. The paper mentions that "only 10% of triggers in ACE 2005 are entities." How does this affect the performance of the entity-based attention mechanism, and would the approach work well in domains where a higher percentage of triggers are entities?

4. The paper states that ANN performs poorly (64.6 F1) compared to baselines. Is this due to insufficient training data or a fundamental limitation of the unsupervised attention approach, and how does your supervised approach specifically address this limitation?

5. The paper demonstrates that S2 outperforms S1 in F1, but S1 has higher precision. Could you provide specific examples where S1's higher precision is particularly valuable (e.g., in applications where false positives are more costly than false negatives)?
