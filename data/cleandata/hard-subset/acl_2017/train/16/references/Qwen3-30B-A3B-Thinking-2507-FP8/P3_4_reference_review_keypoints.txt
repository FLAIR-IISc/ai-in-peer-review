- Proposes supervised attention mechanisms that explicitly leverage argument information to improve event detection (ED), with two strategies (S1, S2) for constructing gold attention vectors.

- Contribution: Analyzes why joint event extraction models underperform on ED, attributing issues to data imbalance and indirect use of arguments.

- Contribution: Demonstrates that explicitly using argument information significantly improves ED performance on ACE 2005.

- Contribution: Systematically investigates different supervised attention strategies for leveraging argument information.

- Strength: Clearly identifies and analyzes the problem that argument information is underutilized in ED, supported by comparative results (Table 1).

- Strength: Conceptually sound idea that arguments help disambiguate event triggers, illustrated with motivating examples.

- Strength: Thorough experimental evaluation including ablations, significance testing, and FrameNet augmentation, showing state-of-the-art gains (ANN-S2 F1=71.7).

- Strength: Methodology and architecture are clearly described, with detailed construction of gold attention vectors and joint training.

- Weakness: Novelty is overstated — supervised attention is a straightforward adaptation and not sufficiently differentiated from prior attention work.

- Weakness: Lacks strong theoretical justification for why argument information is generally useful for ED beyond specific examples.

- Weakness: Evaluation scope is limited to ACE 2005 and does not compare against recent transformer-based models (e.g., BERT/RoBERTa).

- Weakness: Claims about data sparseness are inconsistent since supervised attention relies on argument annotations, which can be sparse.

- Weakness: Insufficient discussion of limitations, such as performance on events with few/no arguments or noisy/incomplete argument annotations.

- Question: How does the approach perform on events lacking arguments (e.g., "The sun rose")?

- Question: Clarify the theoretical relationship between arguments and ED given the claim that arguments are helpful but not essential.

- Question: How would the supervised attention mechanism integrate with or complement transformer-based architectures like BERT/RoBERTa?

- Overall assessment: Valuable contribution demonstrating argument-informed supervised attention improves ED, but novelty and comparison to recent methods need stronger treatment; recommend acceptance with minor revisions.
