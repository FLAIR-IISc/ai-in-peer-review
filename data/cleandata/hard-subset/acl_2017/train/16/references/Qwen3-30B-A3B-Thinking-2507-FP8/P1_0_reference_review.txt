

# Peer Review: "Exploiting Argument Information to Improve Event Detection via Supervised Attention Mechanisms"

## Summary and Main Contributions

This paper addresses event detection (ED), a key component of event extraction, by proposing a novel approach that explicitly leverages argument information to improve performance. The authors identify a key limitation in existing methods: while joint models for event extraction (which simultaneously perform ED and argument extraction) have been proposed, they typically only yield marginal improvements for ED because they use argument information indirectly. The paper argues that arguments provide valuable clues for identifying event triggers, especially for ambiguous words, and proposes a supervised attention mechanism that explicitly incorporates this information.

The main contributions (in order of strength) are:
1. A compelling analysis of why existing joint models fail to improve ED performance despite using argument information (data imbalance and indirect usage).
2. A novel supervised attention mechanism that explicitly uses annotated arguments to guide attention during ED, with two specific strategies for constructing gold attention vectors.
3. Empirical demonstration that the proposed approach significantly outperforms state-of-the-art methods on the ACE 2005 benchmark, with the highest reported F1 score.

## Strengths

1. **Strong Motivation and Insight**: The paper provides a clear and well-justified argument for why argument information should be explicitly used for ED, addressing a real limitation in existing approaches. The analysis of why joint models primarily improve argument extraction rather than event detection (due to data imbalance and indirect usage) is insightful and well-supported by the data (Table 1). This is a significant contribution to the field as it identifies a meaningful gap in current research.

2. **Clear Methodology and Evaluation**: The proposed supervised attention mechanism is conceptually simple and well-explained. The two attention strategies (S1 and S2) are well-motivated and systematically evaluated. The ablation study (Table 2) convincingly demonstrates that argument information is crucial for ED performance, with ANN-Gold1/2 significantly outperforming systems that only use entity information.

3. **Strong Empirical Results**: The experimental results on ACE 2005 are compelling. ANN-S2 achieves the highest F1 score (71.7%), significantly outperforming all baselines (including state-of-the-art methods like Liu's FN-Based at 70.7%). The authors also demonstrate how augmenting with FrameNet data further improves performance, which provides additional evidence for the effectiveness of their approach. The statistical significance (p<0.05) of the results adds credibility.

4. **Insight into Data Sparseness Problem**: The paper provides valuable insight into why unsupervised attention mechanisms fail for ED (data sparseness), and how incorporating argument information via supervision solves this problem. This theoretical understanding complements the empirical results and adds depth to the contribution.

## Weaknesses

1. **Limited Novelty**: While the approach is well-executed, the core idea of using supervised attention to incorporate auxiliary information is not particularly novel. Supervised attention mechanisms have been used in other NLP tasks (e.g., machine translation), and the paper doesn't sufficiently distinguish their approach from existing work. The claim that "this paper proposes to exploit argument information explicitly for ED" is somewhat misleading, as some joint models (e.g., Li et al., 2013) do attempt to use arguments for ED, albeit indirectly.

2. **Marginal Performance Gains**: The improvement over the previous state-of-the-art (71.7% vs. 70.7% F1) is only 1.0 point, which is statistically significant but not particularly large in the context of the field. The paper overstates the significance of this improvement as "remarkable," when in fact it's a modest gain. More context on how this compares to the typical improvements reported in the literature would strengthen the claim.

3. **Limited Evaluation Scope**: The evaluation is restricted to ACE 2005, which is a standard benchmark but not comprehensive. The paper doesn't evaluate on other event extraction datasets (e.g., TAC KBP, ERE), which would better demonstrate the generalizability of the approach. The lack of comparison with more recent state-of-the-art methods (post-2016) is also a limitation.

4. **Lack of Analysis on Argument Identification**: The paper assumes that annotated arguments are available and correctly identified during training, but doesn't address how this approach would handle cases where argument information is missing or incorrect. This is a significant practical limitation that should be discussed.

5. **Ambiguous Relationship with Joint Models**: The paper criticizes existing joint models for "indirectly" using arguments for ED, but it's unclear how their proposed supervised attention mechanism differs fundamentally from joint models. If the attention mechanism uses argument information during training, isn't this also a form of joint learning? The paper needs to clarify this distinction.

## Questions to Authors

1. How would your approach handle cases where argument information is missing or incorrect in the training data? Have you conducted experiments to evaluate the robustness of your method to argument annotation errors?

2. The paper states that "argument words should acquire more attention than other words," but doesn't clearly define which words are considered "argument words" in the context. Could you provide more details on how this is determined?

3. Could you clarify the relationship between your approach and joint models (e.g., Nguyen et al., 2016)? Your paper claims existing joint models use arguments "indirectly," but wouldn't your supervised attention mechanism also be a form of joint learning?

## Overall Recommendation

The paper makes a meaningful contribution by identifying a limitation in existing joint models for event extraction and proposing a practical solution using supervised attention. The insights are valuable, the methodology is sound, and the empirical results are strong. However, the novelty is somewhat limited, and the performance gains, while statistically significant, are modest. The paper would benefit from more thorough comparison with recent state-of-the-art methods and a clearer distinction from existing joint models.

I recommend acceptance with minor revisions, particularly to address the novelty concerns and provide more context on the significance of the performance gains.
