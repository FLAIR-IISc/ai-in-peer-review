

# ACL Review

## Summary
This paper proposes a novel approach to event detection (ED) by explicitly incorporating argument information through a supervised attention mechanism. The authors argue that existing joint methods for event extraction don't effectively use argument information for ED (only for argument extraction), and they claim that by using gold attention vectors constructed from annotated arguments, their model can significantly improve ED performance. They present two attention strategies (S1 and S2) and demonstrate their approach on the ACE 2005 dataset, claiming state-of-the-art results.

## Main Contributions (as assessed by reviewer)
1. The authors claim to have identified a limitation in existing joint event extraction methods (that they don't properly leverage argument information for ED).
2. They propose a supervised attention mechanism to explicitly use argument information for ED.
3. They demonstrate modest improvements over state-of-the-art methods on ACE 2005.

## Strengths

1. **Clear problem statement**: The paper effectively identifies a potential limitation in current joint event extraction approaches (that they don't properly leverage argument information for ED). The examples provided (e.g., "fired" being ambiguous) help illustrate the problem well.

2. **Systematic experimental design**: The paper conducts thorough experiments including ablation studies (comparing ANN, ANN-ENT, ANN-Gold1, ANN-Gold2) and comparisons with multiple state-of-the-art methods. The use of both ACE 2005 and FrameNet augmentation for data sparseness is a reasonable approach.

3. **Theoretical justification**: The authors provide a reasonable explanation for why argument information might be helpful for ED (particularly for ambiguous trigger words), which is well-supported by their examples.

## Weaknesses

1. **Lack of novelty**: The core idea of using attention mechanisms to focus on relevant parts of text is not new. Attention mechanisms have been widely used in NLP for years (e.g., in machine translation, text classification). The paper does not convincingly demonstrate how their specific application of attention to event detection is novel or significantly different from existing work. The "supervised attention" approach is essentially training an attention mechanism with gold standard attention vectors, which is not a new concept.

2. **Overstated claims about joint methods**: The paper claims that existing joint methods don't effectively use argument information for ED, but the evidence provided (Table 1) is insufficient to support this claim. The marginal improvements in ED performance for joint methods could be due to many factors other than not using argument information for ED. The authors' analysis of why joint methods don't work well for ED is speculative rather than evidence-based.

3. **Problematic use of "gold attention vectors"**: The approach of using gold attention vectors constructed from annotated arguments as supervision is fundamentally flawed. This essentially hard-codes the attention into the model, which doesn't reflect how a real-world system would work. The paper doesn't address how this would generalize to new data where argument information might not be available or might be noisy. The method is essentially cheating by using the same information (arguments) that the model is supposed to learn to identify.

4. **Modest performance improvements**: The claimed improvements over state-of-the-art methods are minimal (e.g., 71.7% F1 for ANN-S2 vs. 70.7% for Liu's FN-Based). In event detection research, such small improvements are not typically considered significant, especially without strong statistical evidence of their significance.

5. **Lack of error analysis**: The paper provides no analysis of when their approach fails or why it might not generalize to other datasets or event types. Understanding the limitations of a proposed method is crucial for assessing its value, and this is missing from the paper.

## Questions to Authors

1. The paper claims that joint methods don't effectively use argument information for ED, but the evidence provided (Table 1) is insufficient. Could you provide more rigorous evidence or analysis to support this claim?

2. The "gold attention vectors" approach is problematic as it essentially hard-codes the attention. How would your approach work in a real-world setting where argument information is not available during inference? 

3. The paper claims "remarkable gains" over state-of-the-art methods, but the F1 improvements are minimal (e.g., 1.0 points). Could you provide statistical significance testing to demonstrate that these improvements are meaningful?

4. The paper uses FrameNet data for augmentation, but FrameNet events are not directly comparable to ACE 2005 events. How did you ensure that the FrameNet data is relevant to the ACE 2005 event types and argument structures?

## Overall Recommendation
The paper presents a reasonable idea that argument information might be useful for event detection, but the methodology is flawed and the claimed improvements are not substantial. The use of "gold attention vectors" as supervision is particularly problematic as it doesn't reflect a realistic implementation. The paper does not provide sufficient evidence to support its novel claims, and the improvements over state-of-the-art are marginal at best. I would recommend rejection for lack of novelty and insufficient evidence for the claimed contributions. The paper would need to substantially revise its methodology (eliminating the use of gold attention vectors), provide stronger evidence for the importance of argument information, and demonstrate more significant improvements to be considered for acceptance.
