

# Review of "Exploiting Argument Information to Improve Event Detection via Supervised Attention Mechanisms"

## Summary
This paper proposes a method to improve event detection (ED) by explicitly leveraging argument information through a supervised attention mechanism. The authors argue that existing joint methods for event extraction (which handle both event detection and argument extraction) fail to properly utilize argument information for ED, and they introduce a neural network approach that uses gold attention vectors constructed from annotated arguments to train the attention mechanism. The method is evaluated on the ACE 2005 benchmark, with results showing marginal improvements over state-of-the-art methods.

## Main Contributions (as I see them)
1. Identification of a valid limitation in existing joint event extraction approaches regarding their use of argument information for event detection (though this is not particularly novel)
2. Introduction of a supervised attention mechanism that uses argument information for event detection (though the method is relatively simple and not a significant methodological innovation)
3. Empirical demonstration that argument information can improve event detection performance (though the improvements are marginal)

## Strengths

1. **Valid observation about joint models**: The paper correctly identifies that existing joint event extraction methods typically show only marginal improvements on event detection (ED) while significantly improving argument extraction (AE). The explanation about data imbalance (more arguments than triggers) and the indirect use of arguments for ED is reasonable.

2. **Clear experimental setup**: The paper follows standard practices for event extraction evaluation on ACE 2005, with proper data splits and comparisons against multiple state-of-the-art methods. The ablation studies comparing different attention strategies (S1 vs S2) provide useful insights.

3. **Good analysis of argument information**: The paper provides a compelling example (the "fired" trigger) showing how argument information can resolve ambiguity in event detection, which supports their core claim.

## Weaknesses

1. **Marginal performance improvement**: The paper claims "remarkable gains" with their approach, but the improvement over state-of-the-art is only 1.0 F1 point (71.7% vs 70.7% for Liu's FN-Based approach). This is a very small improvement in the context of NLP, especially for a paper claiming significant advances. The paper does not provide sufficient statistical evidence to show this difference is meaningful (a t-test is mentioned but no p-value is provided).

2. **Lack of methodological novelty**: The proposed supervised attention mechanism is a straightforward application of attention mechanisms rather than a significant methodological innovation. The idea of using gold labels to supervise attention is not novel in NLP, and the paper doesn't clearly articulate how this differs from existing approaches that might implicitly learn attention to arguments.

3. **Questionable premise about "indirect" use of arguments**: The authors claim that existing joint models use arguments "indirectly" for ED, but in joint models, argument information is directly used to help detect triggers (e.g., by providing context for trigger candidates). The authors' claim that arguments are "not essentially necessary" for ED yet can provide "significant clues" is contradictory.

4. **Insufficient ablation studies**: The paper doesn't provide enough ablation studies to show that the improvement is specifically due to the supervised attention mechanism. For example, it's unclear whether the improvement comes from the attention mechanism itself or from the additional data augmentation with FrameNet (which they use in their best results).

5. **Overstated claims**: The paper repeatedly claims "remarkable gains" and "significant improvements" when the actual gains are marginal. This overstatement undermines the credibility of the paper, especially given the small performance improvement.

## Questions to Authors

1. Can you provide statistical significance testing (p-values) to demonstrate that the 1.0 F1 point improvement over state-of-the-art is meaningful, rather than just a small fluctuation?

2. How does your "supervised attention" mechanism fundamentally differ from existing methods that might implicitly learn to focus on argument words during training? The paper doesn't clearly distinguish between these approaches.

3. The paper states that argument information is not available during event detection (since ED is the first step), yet your method requires gold argument information to train the attention mechanism. How do you plan to apply this method in real-world scenarios where argument information is not available during inference?

4. Why does the paper claim "remarkable gains" when the improvement is only 1.0 F1 point, which is well below the typical threshold for claiming significant improvements in event extraction literature?

5. The paper shows that ANN-S2 (with supervised attention) outperforms ANN (without supervised attention), but ANN itself performs poorly (F1=64.6). What is the specific reason for this poor performance, and how does supervised attention specifically address this issue?

## Overall Recommendation
I cannot recommend acceptance of this paper. While the paper identifies a valid limitation in existing joint event extraction approaches, the proposed solution lacks methodological novelty, the claimed improvements are marginal, and the paper makes overstated claims about the significance of its contribution. The paper would need to demonstrate substantially larger improvements with rigorous statistical validation, provide stronger evidence of methodological novelty, and better address the question of how to use argument information for event detection in real-world scenarios where argument information is not available during inference.
