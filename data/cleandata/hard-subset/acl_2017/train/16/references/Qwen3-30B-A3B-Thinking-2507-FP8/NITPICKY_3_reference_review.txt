

# ACL Review

## Summary
This paper proposes a novel approach to event detection (ED) by explicitly leveraging argument information through supervised attention mechanisms. The authors argue that existing joint models for event extraction (EE) fail to properly utilize argument information for ED, instead only improving argument extraction (AE). They propose a neural network model that uses gold-standard argument annotations to train a supervised attention mechanism, which focuses on argument words during ED. The authors demonstrate their approach on the ACE 2005 dataset, showing improvements over state-of-the-art methods.

## Main Contributions (as I see them)
1. The analysis of why joint methods fail to improve ED despite using argument information (unbalanced data and indirect use of arguments)
2. A supervised attention mechanism that explicitly uses argument information for ED (with two different attention strategies)
3. Demonstrated improvements over state-of-the-art methods on ACE 2005, with a clear analysis of precision/recall trade-offs

## Strengths

1. **Novel Perspective on Joint Models**: The paper provides a clear and insightful analysis of why joint models fail to improve ED despite using argument information. The two key reasons identified (unbalanced data favoring AE and indirect use of arguments) are well-justified with evidence from the literature and their own experiments. This analysis is valuable for the field, as most prior work focused on joint models without critically examining why ED improvements were limited.

2. **Clear Experimental Design and Analysis**: The paper demonstrates a systematic experimental approach to validate the authors' claims. The comparison between systems with and without argument information (Table 2) provides strong evidence for the importance of argument information in ED. The analysis of precision/recall trade-offs between the two attention strategies (S1 and S2) is particularly insightful and well-supported by the data.

3. **Strong Performance Gains**: The proposed approach achieves state-of-the-art results on ACE 2005 (71.7 F1), significantly outperforming previous methods. The paper provides thorough comparisons with multiple baselines, including both pipeline and joint approaches, and includes a statistical significance test (t-test, p<0.05) to validate the improvements.

## Weaknesses

1. **Technical Ambiguity in Attention Mechanism**: The paper's core contribution is the supervised attention mechanism, but the description is confusing and contains mathematical inconsistencies. In Equation (2), the notation α_k^w = exp(w w^T_k) / ∑_i exp(w w^T_i) is ambiguous - it's unclear what w^T_k represents (is it the transpose of the embedding vector for word k?). The paper should use standard notation (e.g., w_k^T w) to avoid confusion. Additionally, the paper states "we combine α_w and α_e to obtain the final attention vector, α = α_w + α_e" but doesn't explain why addition is appropriate rather than a more sophisticated combination (e.g., weighted sum).

2. **Inconsistent Terminology and Citations**: The paper contains several formatting inconsistencies that undermine its professionalism. For example:
   - ACE is incorrectly defined as "Automatic Context Extraction" (it's "Automatic Content Extraction")
   - Inconsistent citation formats (e.g., "Li's joint (2013)" vs. "Li et al., 2013")
   - In Table 1, the authors refer to "Li's joint (2013)" but the citation is "Li et al., 2013" - these should be consistent
   - References contain typographical errors like "Baker et al.,? 1998" (should be "Baker et al., 1998")

3. **Lack of Clarity on FrameNet Integration**: The paper states that they "use events from FrameNet (FN) as extra training data" but fails to clarify how FrameNet events are converted to ACE-style event annotations. The paper mentions "treating frame elements annotated in FN corpus as event arguments" but doesn't explain how they handle cases where FrameNet frame elements don't correspond to ACE event arguments. This is a significant gap in the methodology description, especially since this augmentation is claimed to be an important part of their approach.

4. **Inconsistent Context Description**: The paper states "the current candidate trigger word w0 is not included in the context" but then describes the context as [w_{-n/2}, ..., w_{-1}, w_1, ..., w_{n/2}], which implies w0 is not part of the context (as expected). However, the description of the context in Section 3.1 is confusing because it uses w0 in the context description, creating ambiguity. The paper should be more precise about the context definition.

5. **Lack of Explanation for Poor Baseline Performance**: The paper states "ANN performs unexpectedly poorly" but doesn't provide a clear explanation for why their baseline (unsupervised attention) performs worse than other state-of-the-art methods. The paper attributes it to "data sparseness" but doesn't provide evidence or analysis to support this claim. A more thorough explanation would strengthen their argument for why supervised attention is necessary.

## Questions to Authors

1. In Figure 2, the gold attention vector for S1 is shown with "fired" as the trigger and "Anwar" as an argument. However, the paper states "fired" is the trigger and "Anwar" is the argument. In the ACE 2005 annotation scheme, "fired" would be the trigger and "Anwar" would be the argument (Role = Person). However, the paper states "Anwar, his former protege" - the phrase "former protege" is likely the argument (Role = Position), not "Anwar" alone. Please clarify the exact argument annotation used in this example.

2. The paper states "only 10% of triggers in ACE 2005 are entities." This is a critical claim that should be supported by a specific statistic from the ACE 2005 documentation. Please provide the exact reference to this statistic.

3. The paper claims "the current candidate trigger word w0 is not included in the context" but then uses w0 in the context description. Please clarify the exact definition of the context to avoid confusion.

## Additional Comments

1. The paper's title "Exploiting Argument Information to Improve Event Detection via Supervised Attention Mechanisms" is slightly misleading. The paper doesn't just "exploit" argument information - it uses gold-standard argument information to train the attention mechanism, which is a supervised approach. The title could be more precise.

2. The paper's Table 3 has a minor inconsistency: the paper states "ANN-S2 outperforms ANN-S1" (which is true for F1: 71.7 vs. 70.8), but then says "ANN-S1 could achieve higher precision" (which is correct: 81.4 vs. 78.0), but the phrasing "ANN-S2 outperforms ANN-S1" could be misinterpreted as outperforming in all metrics.

3. The paper uses "ACE 2005 dataset" without a space in the text (e.g., "ACE 2005 dataset" vs. "ACE2005") - this should be consistent throughout the paper.

4. In Section 3.2.2, the paper states "For candidates without any annotated arguments in context, we force all entities to average the whole attention." This is unclear. What does "force all entities to average the whole attention" mean? How is this different from the standard attention calculation?

## Recommendation

I recommend **rejection**. While the paper presents a novel idea that addresses a real problem in the field (why joint models don't improve ED), it contains multiple minor but significant flaws that undermine its credibility. The technical ambiguities in the attention mechanism, inconsistent terminology, and unclear methodology (particularly regarding FrameNet integration) are serious issues that would require substantial revision. The paper is well-written overall and makes a valuable contribution to understanding why joint models fail for ED, but these technical inconsistencies are too significant to overlook in a top-tier venue like ACL.

The paper's core idea is promising, but the execution is marred by these technical inconsistencies that would make it difficult for other researchers to reproduce or build upon the work. The minor flaws identified are not just typos or formatting issues, but fundamental ambiguities in the model description that affect the paper's technical credibility. As a perfectionist reviewer, I cannot recommend acceptance with these issues present.
