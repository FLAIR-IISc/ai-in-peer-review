

# Peer Review: "Exploiting Argument Information to Improve Event Detection via Supervised Attention Mechanisms"

## Summary
This paper addresses a critical gap in event detection (ED) research by proposing a novel approach to explicitly leverage argument information for improving ED performance. The authors argue that while existing joint methods for event detection and argument extraction (AE) typically focus on improving AE, they fail to effectively use argument information for ED. The proposed method introduces a supervised attention mechanism that uses annotated arguments to guide the model to focus on relevant words during ED. The paper presents two strategies for constructing "gold attention" vectors (S1: focusing only on argument words; S2: focusing on arguments and surrounding words) and demonstrates significant improvements over state-of-the-art approaches on ACE 2005.

## Main Contributions
1. A novel conceptual insight that argument information is valuable for ED but is underutilized in existing joint approaches
2. A simple yet effective supervised attention mechanism that explicitly leverages argument information
3. Comprehensive experimental validation demonstrating significant improvements over state-of-the-art methods

## Strengths

1. **Significant Conceptual Contribution**: The paper identifies a genuine limitation in existing approaches that has been overlooked in the literature. The authors correctly observe that while joint methods claim to address both ED and AE, they primarily improve AE while providing minimal gains for ED. This is well-justified with their analysis of data imbalance and implementation issues in joint models.

2. **Strong Experimental Validation**: The authors provide thorough experimental validation, including ablation studies that demonstrate the necessity of argument information (ANN-Gold1/2 significantly outperform ANN and ANN-ENT). The results are statistically significant (p < 0.05) and the proposed approach achieves the best F1 score (71.7) on ACE 2005, outperforming previous state-of-the-art methods by 1.0 points.

3. **Clear Methodological Innovation**: The supervised attention mechanism is conceptually simple but effective. The two attention strategies (S1 and S2) provide a principled way to explore different levels of attention granularity, with S2 achieving better recall while S1 achieves higher precision. This demonstrates thoughtful design of the attention mechanism.

4. **Thorough Analysis of the Problem**: The paper's analysis of why existing joint methods fail to improve ED (data imbalance and indirect use of arguments) is insightful and provides a strong theoretical foundation for their approach.

## Weaknesses

1. **Lack of Theoretical Justification**: While the paper demonstrates that supervised attention works well, it lacks a strong theoretical argument for *why* this specific approach outperforms unsupervised attention. The claim that data sparsity causes unsupervised attention to fail is plausible but not rigorously established. The authors should provide more evidence that supervised attention is the optimal solution to the data sparsity problem.

2. **Limited Comparison with Recent Work**: The paper compares only with methods up to 2016, missing more recent transformer-based approaches (e.g., BERT, RoBERTa) that have significantly advanced the field. This is a major limitation as the state-of-the-art has evolved substantially since the cited works.

3. **Ambiguity in Technical Distinction**: The paper claims their approach differs from previous joint methods in "explicitly" using argument information, but doesn't provide a clear technical distinction. Previous joint methods also use argument information (though indirectly), and it's not clear how the supervised attention mechanism is fundamentally different.

4. **Superficial FrameNet Augmentation**: The FrameNet augmentation approach is somewhat superficial. The authors treat FrameNet frame elements as event arguments without addressing potential mismatches between FrameNet and ACE argument structures. They also don't discuss how the automatically extracted FrameNet data might introduce noise.

## Questions to Authors

1. How does your supervised attention approach technically differ from previous joint methods that also use argument information? Could you provide a clear technical distinction?

2. Why didn't you compare your approach with more recent state-of-the-art methods (e.g., BERT-based approaches) that have emerged since 2016?

3. The paper claims that arguments are especially useful for ambiguous triggers, but doesn't provide detailed analysis of which specific event types benefit most from your approach. Could you provide such an analysis?

4. In the FrameNet augmentation section, you mention using RPI Joint Information Extraction System to label entities. How does this impact the quality of your augmented data, and did you analyze potential errors introduced by this process?

## Overall Assessment

This is a strong paper with a valuable conceptual contribution that addresses a genuine gap in the literature. The supervised attention mechanism is well-designed and demonstrates significant improvements over state-of-the-art methods. However, the lack of comparison with more recent approaches and the insufficient theoretical justification for the method's success are notable weaknesses. With minor revisions addressing these concerns, this paper would make a valuable contribution to the field.

**Recommendation: Weak Accept** (with the expectation that the authors will address the weaknesses in the author response, particularly regarding comparison with more recent methods and clearer technical distinction from previous work).
