- Proposes supervised attention using annotated arguments to focus on argument words for event trigger detection.  
- Claims existing joint models underutilize argument information for ED and that explicit use can improve ED performance.  
- Introduces two attention strategies (S1: argument words; S2: argument words plus surrounding context).  
- Achieves state-of-the-art on ACE 2005 (71.7% F1) with statistically significant gains; improved further with FrameNet augmentation.  
- Ablation studies show argument information is crucial; unsupervised attention (ANN) performs poorly, attributed to data sparsity.  
- Strength: clear, compelling insight and problem identification illustrated with disambiguating examples.  
- Strength: simple, well-justified supervised attention methodology that directly addresses the problem.  
- Strength: strong experimental results with significance testing and analysis of limitations.  
- Weakness: supervised attention itself is not novel and is similar to prior work in other NLP tasks.  
- Weakness: insufficient analysis of why S2 yields higher recall but lower precision compared to S1.  
- Weakness: unclear distinction between the claimed “explicit” usage and other joint models’ “indirect” use of arguments.  
- Weakness: comparison set omits more recent (2017–2019) relevant baselines.  
- Overall: strong, well-supported contribution; recommendation to accept.  
- Questions for authors: clarify how your supervised attention differs from prior joint-model attentions and why it helps ED; provide deeper analysis of S2 vs S1 behavior; provide stronger evidence that data sparsity explains ANN's poor performance.
