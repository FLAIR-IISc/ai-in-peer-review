- Main contribution: Analyzes why joint AE+ED methods fail (unbalanced data, indirect use of arguments) and shows argument info can improve ED.
- Main contribution: Proposes a supervised attention mechanism that explicitly leverages argument information for event detection.
- Main contribution: Systematically evaluates different supervised-attention strategies (S1, S2) for ED.

- Strength: Novel and important insight that explicit argument information is crucial for resolving ambiguous triggers in ED, supported by examples and empirical gains.
- Strength: Strong empirical validation — ablation studies and comparisons show significant, statistically significant improvements on ACE 2005 (ANN-S2 best).
- Strength: Clear, well-structured model and experimental setup with two gold-attention construction strategies and detailed architecture/hyperparameters.
- Strength: Addresses data sparsity by augmenting training with FrameNet, showing consistent performance improvements.

- Weakness: Attention mechanism itself is relatively straightforward and similar to prior supervised/standard attention methods, limiting methodological novelty.
- Weakness: Lacks deeper analysis of why the approach works (e.g., per-event-type effects, detailed behavior of attention changes); S2 vs S1 differences not fully explored.
- Weakness: Limited evaluation scope — only ACE 2005 tested, no cross-dataset or low-resource evaluations to show generality.

- Question: How does your supervised attention differ concretely from existing attention mechanisms (e.g., Mi et al., 2016) and why is it particularly suited for ED?
- Question: Which specific event types benefit most from argument information (e.g., End-Position vs. Attack)?
- Question: Have you considered more dynamic uses of argument information (e.g., graph neural networks modeling trigger-argument relations)?

- Recommendation: Strong paper with important insight and solid results; recommend acceptance with minor revisions to provide deeper analysis, broader evaluation, and clearer differentiation from prior attention work.
