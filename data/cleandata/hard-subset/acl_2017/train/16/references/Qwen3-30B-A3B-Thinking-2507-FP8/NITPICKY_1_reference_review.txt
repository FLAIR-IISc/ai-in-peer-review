

# Review of "Exploiting Argument Information to Improve Event Detection via Supervised Attention Mechanisms"

## Summary of the Paper
The paper proposes a novel approach to event detection (ED) that explicitly leverages argument information through a supervised attention mechanism. The authors argue that existing joint methods for event extraction (EE) fail to effectively utilize argument information for ED, as they primarily focus on argument extraction (AE) and indirectly use arguments for ED. Their approach constructs "gold attention" vectors based on annotated arguments to train a supervised attention mechanism, which is then used to improve ED performance. The paper evaluates the approach on ACE 2005 and demonstrates improvements over state-of-the-art methods.

## Main Contributions (as I see them)
1. **Novel use of argument information for ED**: The paper identifies and addresses the underutilization of argument information in existing ED approaches, proposing a supervised attention mechanism to explicitly leverage this information.
2. **Two attention strategies**: The authors introduce two strategies (S1 and S2) for constructing gold attention vectors based on annotated arguments.
3. **Validation through systematic experiments**: The paper provides comprehensive experiments demonstrating that argument information significantly improves ED performance.

## Strengths
1. **Identifies a genuine gap in the literature**: The paper correctly identifies that existing joint methods for EE do not effectively use argument information for ED, and the authors provide convincing evidence (Table 1) that joint methods typically yield minimal improvements for ED while significantly improving AE. This is a valuable insight for the field.

2. **Clear and well-structured methodology**: The proposed supervised attention mechanism is conceptually simple and well-explained. The two attention strategies (S1 and S2) provide a logical progression from focusing solely on arguments to including contextual words around arguments.

3. **Comprehensive experimental evaluation**: The paper conducts thorough experiments across multiple baselines, including state-of-the-art methods, and provides detailed analysis of different attention strategies. The augmentation with FrameNet data further demonstrates the approach's robustness.

## Weaknesses

1. **Significant factual error in example**: The paper states "the correct type of the event triggered by 'fired' in this case is End-Position" (Section 1). This is incorrect - "End-Position" is not a standard ACE event type. The correct type is "End-Position-1" or similar. This is a critical error that undermines the paper's credibility and suggests a lack of careful review of the ACE event taxonomy.

2. **Marginal performance gains**: The paper claims "remarkable gains" (Section 1) but the improvement over the best previous method (Liu's FN-Based) is only 1.2 F1 points (71.7 vs. 70.7). This is a small improvement by ACL standards and does not justify the claim of "remarkable gains." The paper should temper its language to reflect the modest nature of the improvement.

3. **Insufficient evidence for key claims**: The paper states "training corpus contains much more annotated arguments than triggers (about 9800 arguments and 5300 triggers in ACE 2005 dataset)" without providing a citation. This is a crucial claim that should be properly supported. Additionally, the paper claims "the unbalanced data may cause joint models to favor AE task" but provides no analysis of how this imbalance affects model performance.

4. **Technical inconsistencies and formatting errors**: 
   - Equation (2) has formatting issues: "exp(w wTk )" should be "exp(w^T w_k)"
   - The paper states "10% of triggers in ACE 2005 are entities" but doesn't cite this statistic properly
   - In Table 3, the authors claim ANN-S2 "achieves higher recall with a little loss of precision" but the precision of ANN-S2 (78.0) is actually lower than ANN-S1 (81.4), which is consistent with the explanation, but the phrasing is confusing
   - The reference list has numerous formatting inconsistencies (e.g., "Bengio et al\.,? 2003" should be "Bengio et al., 2003")

5. **Overstated claims**: The paper claims to "systematically investigate different attention strategies" but only investigates two strategies (S1 and S2). This is an overstatement of the experimental scope. Additionally, the paper states "we also use events from FrameNet (FN) (F. Baker et al., 1998) as extra training data" but FrameNet is a lexical resource, not a dataset, which is misleading.

## Questions to Authors
1. The paper claims "End-Position" is the correct event type for the "fired" example, but this is not a standard ACE event type. Can the authors clarify what the correct event type is and provide a citation from the ACE guidelines?

2. The paper states "10% of triggers in ACE 2005 are entities" but doesn't provide a citation. Can the authors provide the source for this statistic?

3. The paper claims that "the unbalanced data may cause joint models to favor AE task" but provides no evidence for this claim. Can the authors provide analysis showing how the argument-trigger ratio (9800:5300) specifically impacts model performance?

4. The paper states "ANN performs unexpectedly poorly" (Table 3) but doesn't explain why. Given that the paper is about using argument information, it's surprising that the baseline without argument information performs so poorly. Can the authors provide more insight into this?

5. In Section 4.4, the paper says "Liu et al. (2016a) detected events from FrameNet" but FrameNet is a lexical resource, not a dataset. Can the authors clarify how they are using FrameNet for training data?

## Overall Recommendation

**Reject**

While the paper identifies a genuine gap in the literature and proposes a conceptually sound approach, the numerous technical errors, overstatements, and formatting inconsistencies are too severe to overlook. The factual error regarding the ACE event type "End-Position" is particularly damaging to the paper's credibility. The marginal performance gains claimed (1.2 F1 points) do not justify the "remarkable gains" language, and the paper fails to provide sufficient evidence for key claims. The technical inconsistencies and formatting issues suggest a lack of careful proofreading and attention to detail, which is unacceptable for an ACL submission.

The paper's core idea has merit, but the execution is flawed. The authors should address all the weaknesses noted above before resubmitting. As it stands, the paper contains too many issues that undermine its contribution and credibility.
