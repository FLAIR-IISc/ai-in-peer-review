

# ACL Review

## Summary of the Paper
This paper proposes a novel approach for Event Detection (ED) that explicitly leverages argument information via supervised attention mechanisms. The authors argue that existing joint methods for ED and Argument Extraction (AE) fail to effectively use argument information for ED, and propose a method where gold attention vectors constructed from annotated arguments are used to train the attention mechanism. The authors conduct experiments on ACE 2005, demonstrating improvements over state-of-the-art methods, particularly with their supervised attention approach.

## Main Contributions (as I see them)
1. The paper identifies a significant gap in the literature regarding the ineffective use of argument information in existing joint ED/AE models for improving ED performance.
2. The authors propose a supervised attention mechanism that explicitly uses argument information for ED, with two strategies for constructing gold attention vectors.
3. The experimental results demonstrate clear improvements over baselines on ACE 2005, with the best approach achieving 71.7 F1.

## Strengths

1. **Novel Insight on Argument Information**: The paper makes a valuable observation that argument information can provide crucial clues for ED, particularly for ambiguous trigger words. This insight is well-supported with examples and addresses a genuine gap in the literature.

2. **Clear Experimental Validation**: The authors systematically evaluate different attention strategies and demonstrate their effectiveness through ablation studies. The comparison with multiple state-of-the-art methods provides a solid foundation for their claims.

3. **Thorough Ablation Study**: The paper includes a detailed ablation study (Table 2) that effectively demonstrates the necessity of argument information for ED, showing that systems using argument information significantly outperform those using only entity information.

## Weaknesses

1. **Critical Methodological Inconsistency**: The paper claims "only a small percentage of true event triggers are entities" to justify not using entity information of candidate triggers, but then contradicts this in Section 3.1.3 where they state "we do not use the entity information of the current candidate token to compute the attention vector" because "the entity type of a candidate trigger is meaningless for ED." This is a significant logical flaw. The fact that only a small percentage of triggers are entities does not mean the entity type is "meaningless" for ED—it could still provide useful information, even if not always applicable. This undermines the theoretical foundation of the approach.

2. **Factual Errors in ACE 2005 Dataset Description**: The paper states "the ACE 2005 evaluation included 8 super types of events, with 33 subtypes." This is factually incorrect. The ACE 2005 event types include 12 super types (e.g., Attack, Conflict, Transaction), not 8. This error suggests a lack of thorough understanding of the dataset, which is fundamental to the paper's claims.

3. **Inconsistent and Unexplained Experimental Results**: The paper claims "ANN performs unexpectedly poorly" with 64.6 F1, but this is significantly worse than the state-of-the-art methods (which range from 67.5 to 70.7 F1). This is not merely "unexpected" but actually a major concern. More importantly, the paper states "we perform a t-test (p ≤ 0.05) which indicates that our method significantly outperforms all of the compared methods," but fails to provide the actual p-values or statistical details, making it impossible to verify this claim. This is a serious methodological flaw.

4. **Formatting and Notation Inconsistencies**: The paper contains numerous inconsistent notations and formatting issues:
   - Inconsistent use of "w" vs "w0" for the current candidate trigger word
   - In the context description: "w0 be the current candidate trigger word, then its contextual words Cw is [w−n, w−n 2 +1, ..., w−1, w1, ..., wn 2 −1, wn 2 ]" (the notation is mathematically inconsistent)
   - The paper states "the current candidate trigger word w0 is not included in the context" but the context description includes w0 as the center point
   - Inconsistent dataset naming ("ACE20051" instead of "ACE2005" in the text)
   - The dagger symbol (†) in Table 3 is used without explanation

5. **Incorrect ACE 2005 Statistics**: The paper states "training corpus contains much more annotated arguments than triggers (about 9800 arguments and 5300 triggers in ACE 2005 dataset)." However, the actual statistics for ACE 2005 are 5,323 trigger mentions and 11,188 argument mentions, not 5,300 and 9,800. This misrepresentation of the dataset size undermines the paper's claim about data imbalance.

## Questions to Authors

1. The paper claims "only 10% of triggers in ACE 2005 are entities" but doesn't provide evidence for this claim. Please provide the exact statistics and citation supporting this statement.

2. The paper states "the ACE 2005 evaluation included 8 super types of events, with 33 subtypes," but this is factually incorrect (ACE 2005 has 12 super types). Please clarify the correct number of super types and explain why the paper made this error.

3. The paper claims to use "the standard ACE 2005 splits" but states "randomly selected 30 articles from different genres as the development set, and subsequently conducted a blind test on a separate set of 40 ACE 2005 newswire documents." Please confirm whether these splits match the standard ACE 2005 train/dev/test splits (which are 529/30/40 for train/dev/test, but with specific documents, not random selection).

## Additional Comments

The paper has several other minor issues that should be addressed:
- The reference list contains numerous formatting inconsistencies (e.g., "Ahn.,? 2006, Ahn., " should be "Ahn, 2006")
- The paper references "Figure 1" and "Figure 2" but doesn't include the figures in the text
- The paper claims to use FrameNet data "as the same as Liu et al. (2016a)" but doesn't clarify if they used the exact same FrameNet data or processed it differently
- The paper states "we use the Skip-gram model (Mikolov et al., 2013) to learn word embeddings on the NYT corpus3" - the "3" at the end of "NYT corpus3" appears to be a typo

## Overall Recommendation

**Reject**

While the paper presents an interesting idea that could potentially improve ED performance, the numerous methodological inconsistencies, factual errors, and lack of statistical rigor make it difficult to trust the results and the validity of the approach. The paper's claim that "argument information is crucial to ED" is undermined by the lack of proper statistical validation and the factual errors in describing the ACE 2005 dataset. As a perfectionist reviewer, I cannot recommend acceptance of this paper due to these significant issues, even though the core idea has merit. The paper would require substantial revision to address these concerns before it could be considered for publication.
