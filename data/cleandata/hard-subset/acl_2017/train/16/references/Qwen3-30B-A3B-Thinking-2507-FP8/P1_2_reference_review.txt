

# Peer Review: "Exploiting Argument Information to Improve Event Detection via Supervised Attention Mechanisms"

## Summary
This paper addresses a gap in event detection (ED) research by proposing a novel approach to explicitly leverage argument information for ED. The authors argue that while existing joint event extraction methods (combining ED and argument extraction) are common, they fail to effectively utilize argument information for ED due to unbalanced data and indirect use of arguments. They introduce a supervised attention mechanism that uses annotated arguments to guide attention weights, with two strategies for constructing "gold attention" vectors (S1: focusing only on argument words; S2: focusing on arguments and surrounding words). The method is evaluated on ACE 2005, where it achieves state-of-the-art performance (71.7% F1), significantly outperforming previous approaches.

## Main Contributions
1. **Novel use of argument information for ED**: The paper identifies and addresses the key problem that existing joint models don't effectively use argument information for ED despite its potential value for resolving ambiguous triggers.
2. **Supervised attention mechanism for ED**: The authors propose a simple yet effective method to incorporate argument information through supervised attention, with thorough empirical validation of different attention strategies.
3. **Demonstration of argument importance**: The ablation studies (Table 2) provide strong evidence that argument information is genuinely crucial for ED, not just beneficial.

## Strengths

1. **Clear identification of a genuine research gap**: The authors convincingly demonstrate that existing joint methods fail to use argument information for ED (Table 1), providing a strong motivation for their approach. Their analysis of why joint methods underperform on ED (unbalanced data, indirect use of arguments) is insightful and well-supported.

2. **Strong empirical results**: The proposed method (ANN-S2) achieves the best performance on ACE 2005 (71.7% F1), significantly outperforming state-of-the-art methods (Table 3). The ablation studies (Table 2) provide compelling evidence that argument information is crucial, with ANN-S1/2 significantly outperforming ANN (64.6% vs. 70.8% F1).

3. **Conceptual simplicity and elegance**: The supervised attention mechanism is straightforward to implement and understand, making it accessible to the community. The idea of using gold attention vectors constructed from annotated arguments as supervision is both novel and elegant.

4. **Thorough analysis of attention strategies**: The authors systematically investigate two attention strategies (S1 and S2), providing valuable insights into when each approach is preferable (S1 for precision, S2 for recall), which adds depth to their contribution.

## Weaknesses

1. **Inadequate justification for poor unsupervised attention performance**: The paper claims the unsupervised attention baseline (ANN) performs poorly (F1=64.6) due to data sparseness, but provides no comparative evidence with standard unsupervised attention mechanisms (e.g., self-attention in transformers). Without this, the need for supervised attention remains somewhat unproven.

2. **Outdated state-of-the-art comparison**: The paper compares against methods from 2013-2016 (e.g., Li et al., 2013; Nguyen et al., 2016), while the field has advanced significantly since then. Recent state-of-the-art methods like Liu et al. (2018) and others have reported higher scores on ACE 2005. This undermines the claim that their approach is "the best performance on the widely used benchmark."

3. **Lack of qualitative analysis**: The paper doesn't provide examples showing how the attention mechanism actually focuses on argument words as intended. A qualitative analysis would strengthen the claim that the attention mechanism works as designed.

4. **Superficial treatment of FrameNet augmentation**: The paper uses FrameNet events to augment training data but doesn't adequately address potential issues with the quality of automatically extracted FrameNet events (e.g., false positives), which could affect the results. The processing of FrameNet events (Section 4.4) is somewhat cursory.

5. **No discussion of failure cases**: The paper doesn't analyze cases where their method fails, particularly for events with no arguments or ambiguous arguments. This limits the understanding of the method's boundaries.

## Questions to Authors

1. The paper claims unsupervised attention performs poorly (ANN: 64.6% F1) due to data sparseness, but doesn't compare with standard unsupervised attention mechanisms (e.g., self-attention in transformers). Could you provide a comparison with such methods to better justify the need for supervised attention?

2. Recent work (e.g., Liu et al., 2018) reports higher F1 scores on ACE 2005 (72.5%+). Why isn't your method compared against these more recent state-of-the-art approaches?

3. Could you provide qualitative examples showing how the attention mechanism focuses on argument words as intended, particularly for ambiguous triggers like "fired" in the example?

## Overall Recommendation
This paper makes a valuable contribution by addressing a genuine gap in event extraction research. The supervised attention mechanism is conceptually sound, and the empirical results are strong. However, the outdated comparison with state-of-the-art methods and lack of qualitative analysis are significant weaknesses. With careful revision to address these concerns and provide more current comparisons, this paper could be a strong contribution to ACL. I recommend **acceptance with minor revisions**.
