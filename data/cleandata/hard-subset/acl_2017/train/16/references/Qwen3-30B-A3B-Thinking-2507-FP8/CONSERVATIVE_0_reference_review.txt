

# ACL Review: "Exploiting Argument Information to Improve Event Detection via Supervised Attention Mechanisms"

## Summary of the Paper
The paper proposes a novel approach to Event Detection (ED) by explicitly leveraging argument information through a supervised attention mechanism. The authors claim that existing joint models for event detection and argument extraction don't effectively use argument information for ED, and they propose to use annotated arguments as supervision for attention mechanisms to improve ED performance. The core idea is to construct "gold attention" vectors based on annotated arguments and train an attention mechanism to mimic these attention patterns.

## Main Contributions (in order of strength)
1. Identification of the problem that joint models don't effectively use argument information for ED
2. Introduction of a supervised attention mechanism for ED that uses argument information as supervision
3. Experimental validation of the approach on ACE 2005 dataset with two attention strategies

## Strengths
1. **Problem Identification**: The paper correctly identifies that joint models for event extraction show minimal improvement for ED compared to AE (as shown in Table 1), which is a valid observation that deserves attention.

2. **Systematic Experimental Design**: The paper conducts thorough ablation studies (ANN vs. ANN-ENT vs. ANN-Gold1 vs. ANN-Gold2) to validate the core assumption that argument information is useful for ED. The experiments are well-structured and provide evidence for their main claim.

3. **Use of Standard Benchmark**: The experiments are conducted on the widely accepted ACE 2005 benchmark, and the paper compares against multiple strong baselines, which is good practice.

## Weaknesses
1. **Lack of Novelty**: The core idea of using attention mechanisms for event detection is not novel, and the specific approach proposed (using argument information as supervision) represents only a minor modification of existing attention-based methods. Attention mechanisms have been widely used in NLP for years, and the paper doesn't sufficiently differentiate itself from prior work that has used attention for similar tasks.

2. **Marginal Performance Gains**: The reported improvements (e.g., from 69.3 to 71.7 F1) are marginal and not impressive by current standards in the field. The paper's claim of "remarkable gains" is overstated, as the gains are less than 2.5 F1 points compared to the best baseline. In a field where small improvements (0.5-1.0 F1 points) are often considered significant, this represents a very modest improvement.

3. **Mischaracterization of Joint Learning**: The paper fundamentally misunderstands how joint learning works. The claim that "annotated arguments are actually used for AE, not for ED in existing joint methods" is incorrect. In joint learning, argument information is used to help detect triggers (i.e., for ED), just not in the direct way the authors propose. The authors have mischaracterized the standard approach, which undermines their entire premise.

4. **Insufficient Theoretical Justification**: The paper lacks a strong theoretical justification for why supervised attention would work better than unsupervised attention. The explanation provided ("data sparseness is an important issue of ED") is weak and doesn't sufficiently justify the proposed approach. Without a clear theoretical basis, the method appears to be an ad-hoc modification rather than a principled improvement.

5. **Overstated Claims**: The paper makes several overstated claims about the significance of the approach. The claim that "the proposed approach is effective for ED task, and it outperforms state-of-the-art approaches with remarkable gains" is not supported by the data presented. The gains are marginal, not remarkable.

## Questions to Authors
1. How does your supervised attention mechanism differ from the attention mechanisms used in recent neural network-based event detection models (e.g., Nguyen and Grishman, 2016; Liu et al., 2016a), which also use attention to focus on relevant parts of the input?

2. Can you provide a more convincing theoretical justification for why argument information should be used as supervision for attention mechanisms in ED, rather than using standard unsupervised attention? The current explanation regarding data sparseness is insufficient.

3. Given that your improvements are marginal (only 2.4 F1 points), how do you justify the claim of "remarkable gains" in the paper?

4. The paper claims that joint models don't effectively use argument information for ED, but this contradicts the standard understanding of joint learning. Can you clarify how argument information is not being used for ED in existing joint models?

5. Why do you believe that the supervised attention mechanism would generalize well to other datasets or event types beyond ACE 2005, given that the improvements are marginal and the approach seems highly dataset-specific?

## Additional Comments
The paper's main contribution is actually a minor modification to existing attention-based approaches rather than a significant methodological advance. The authors' characterization of the problem with joint models is flawed, and their proposed solution doesn't represent a substantial improvement over the state of the art. The marginal performance gains do not justify the claims made in the paper, and the paper would benefit from a more critical assessment of the novelty and significance of the proposed approach.
