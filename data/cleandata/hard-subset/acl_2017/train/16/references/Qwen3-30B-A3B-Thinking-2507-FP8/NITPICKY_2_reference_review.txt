

# ACL Review: "Exploiting Argument Information to Improve Event Detection via Supervised Attention Mechanisms"

## Summary of the Paper and Contributions

The paper proposes a novel approach for Event Detection (ED) that leverages argument information through supervised attention mechanisms. The authors argue that existing joint methods for Event Extraction (EE) don't effectively use argument information for ED, and propose a model that uses gold attention vectors constructed from annotated arguments to train an attention mechanism specifically for ED. The claimed contributions are:

1. Analysis of why joint methods don't work well for ED (arguing that arguments are not effectively used for ED in existing joint models)
2. A supervised attention-based ED model that explicitly uses argument information
3. Systematic investigation of different attention strategies (S1 and S2) for the proposed model

## Strengths

1. **Novel Perspective on Argument Information**: The paper presents a compelling argument that argument information can be useful for ED, which is a fresh perspective compared to the existing literature that mostly focuses on using arguments for AE rather than ED. This is the strongest contribution of the paper.

2. **Systematic Exploration of Attention Strategies**: The authors systematically investigate two different strategies (S1 and S2) for constructing gold attention vectors from argument information, providing valuable insights into how argument information can be leveraged for ED.

3. **Comprehensive Evaluation on ACE 2005**: The paper conducts thorough experiments on the widely used ACE 2005 benchmark dataset, which is a standard for event extraction research.

## Weaknesses

1. **Critical Methodological Ambiguity in Attention Construction**: The paper lacks clear details on how the gold attention vectors are constructed for training. In Section 3.2.2, the authors describe strategies S1 and S2 for constructing gold attention vectors, but fail to specify the exact numerical values used. For example, in S1, they state "all argument words in the given context obtain the same attention, whereas other words get no attention" but don't specify whether this means 1.0 for argument words and 0.0 for others. This is a fundamental flaw as the exact implementation of the supervision signal is critical to the model's performance.

2. **Inconsistent Claims and Results Regarding Data Sparseness**: The paper claims "data sparseness is an important issue of ED" and that unsupervised attention mechanisms don't work well because of this. However, Table 4 shows that ANN-S1 + FrameNet achieves only 70.9 F1 compared to ANN-S1's 70.8 F1, which is a minimal improvement (0.1 points). This contradicts the paper's claim that data sparseness is the main problem for unsupervised attention, as the FrameNet augmentation didn't significantly improve the performance of the supervised attention model. The paper fails to reconcile this contradiction.

3. **Lack of Statistical Significance Details**: The paper mentions "we also perform a t-test (p < 0.05), which indicates that our method significantly outperforms all of the compared methods." However, it doesn't provide the exact p-values or the detailed results of the t-test. Without these details, it's impossible to verify the statistical significance of the reported performance differences.

4. **Poorly Explained Hyperparameter Selection**: The paper sets λ to 1.0 for ANN-S1 and 5.0 for ANN-S2 but provides no justification for these values. The paper lacks a sensitivity analysis of λ, which is a critical hyperparameter for the joint loss function. This omission makes it difficult to assess the robustness of the proposed approach.

5. **Formatting and Typographical Inconsistencies**: The paper contains numerous minor formatting and typographical errors that undermine its professionalism and credibility:
   - "event arguments seem to be not essentially necessary to ED" (should be "are not essentially necessary")
   - "we have analyzed that arguments are capable of providing significant clues to ED" (should be "for ED")
   - In Table 1: "both join- methods achieved" (missing "joint")
   - In Table 3: "Ngyen's joint model" (should be "Nguyen's joint model")
   - In references: "Ahn.,? 2006" (should be "Ahn, 2006")
   - Missing Figure 1 (described in text but not included)

## Questions to Authors

1. In Section 3.2.2, for strategy S1, what exact numerical values are used for the gold attention vector (e.g., 1.0 for argument words and 0.0 for others)? Please provide the precise implementation details.

2. The paper claims that "data sparseness is an important issue of ED," but the results in Table 4 show minimal improvement from FrameNet augmentation for ANN-S1 (70.9 F1 vs 70.8 F1). How do you reconcile this claim with your experimental results?

3. The paper sets λ to 1.0 for ANN-S1 and 5.0 for ANN-S2 without justification. Please provide a sensitivity analysis of λ and explain how these specific values were determined.

4. The paper mentions a t-test with p < 0.05 but doesn't provide the exact p-values. Please provide the detailed statistical results to support your claim of statistical significance.

5. The paper describes the model architecture in Figure 1, but this figure is missing from the submission. Please provide the figure or describe the model architecture in sufficient detail to allow for proper evaluation.

## Final Recommendation

I must recommend rejection for this paper. While the paper presents a novel perspective on leveraging argument information for ED, the methodological ambiguities, inconsistent claims, and numerous formatting errors are too severe to overlook. The lack of clear implementation details for the key innovation (the supervised attention mechanism) makes it impossible to evaluate the technical merit of the approach. Additionally, the paper's failure to reconcile its claims about data sparseness with the experimental results undermines the credibility of its main contribution.

As a perfectionist reviewer, I cannot recommend acceptance when the paper lacks critical details that would allow other researchers to replicate the work or properly evaluate its claims. The numerous formatting and typographical errors also indicate a lack of attention to detail that is unacceptable for an ACL submission.

The paper has the potential to be valuable, but it requires significant revision to address the methodological ambiguities and inconsistencies before it can be considered for publication.
