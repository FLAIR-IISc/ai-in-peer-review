### Summary of the Paper and Main Contributions

This paper addresses the task of Event Detection (ED), which involves identifying and classifying event triggers in text. The authors' central argument is that event argument information, while often ignored or ineffectively used in ED-only or joint ED-Argument Extraction (AE) models, provides crucial cues for disambiguating trigger words. They propose a neural network-based approach that explicitly leverages argument annotations from the training data via a supervised attention mechanism. The model learns to attend to context words, but during training, this attention mechanism is guided by an additional loss term that encourages it to produce attention scores similar to "gold" attention vectors. These gold vectors are constructed to highlight the positions of annotated event arguments. Experiments on the ACE 2005 dataset show that this method achieves a new state-of-the-art performance.

In my view, the main contributions of this work are:
1.  **A novel and effective method for incorporating argument information into ED.** The idea of supervising an attention mechanism using annotations from a related but distinct task (argument extraction) is an elegant and direct way to inject this valuable information. This contrasts favorably with prior joint models where the benefit to ED has been marginal.
2.  **State-of-the-art results on a standard benchmark.** The proposed model significantly outperforms a comprehensive set of prior methods on the widely-used ACE 2005 dataset. The empirical gains are substantial and statistically significant.
3.  **An insightful analysis of the shortcomings of joint models for ED.** The introduction provides a clear and compelling motivation by analyzing why joint ED-AE models often fail to improve ED performance (e.g., data imbalance favoring AE, indirect information flow), which frames the paper's contribution effectively.

### Strengths

1.  **Clear Motivation and Strong Rationale:** The paper is exceptionally well-motivated. The analysis in the introduction regarding the limitations of previous joint models is convincing and clearly establishes the need for a more direct method to leverage argument information for ED. The core idea is intuitive: if arguments help disambiguate a trigger, the model should learn to pay attention to them. The proposed supervised attention mechanism is a direct and logical implementation of this idea.
2.  **Impressive Empirical Performance:** The proposed method achieves a new state-of-the-art F1 score on the ACE 2005 dataset, surpassing several strong recent baselines, including feature-based, CNN-based, and RNN-based joint models. An F1 score of 71.7 (and 71.9 with FrameNet data) is a significant improvement in this challenging task. The high precision achieved by the models is particularly noteworthy.
3.  **Thorough and Convincing Experiments:** The evaluation is comprehensive. The authors first conduct an experiment (Section 4.2) to explicitly validate their core assumption that argument information is crucial, showing that a model using gold argument locations ("ANN-Gold1/2") dramatically outperforms models that do not. This strengthens their motivation. Furthermore, they compare two different strategies for constructing the gold attention signal (S1 and S2), providing a useful analysis of the precision/recall trade-off associated with each. The inclusion of experiments with FrameNet data further demonstrates the robustness and potential of the approach.
4.  **High Clarity and Readability:** The paper is well-written, clearly structured, and easy to follow. The proposed method is explained in sufficient detail, and the connection between the motivation, method, and experiments is very clear. This makes the paper's contributions easy to understand and appreciate.

### Weaknesses

1.  **Limited Architectural Novelty and a Potentially Weak Baseline:** The base model is a standard three-layer ANN with an attention mechanism. The novelty lies entirely in the supervised training signal for the attention. While this signal is highly effective, the base architecture is simpler than many contemporary models (e.g., CNNs, RNNs). More importantly, the unsupervised attention baseline ("ANN") performs quite poorly with an F1 score of 64.6. This is substantially lower than other recent neural baselines like Skip-CNN (71.3 F1) and Nguyen's joint RNN (69.3 F1). This performance gap raises the question of whether the impressive gains of the supervised model are solely due to the novel supervision or are partially inflated by starting from a weaker-than-SOTA baseline architecture.
2.  **Dependence on Rich Annotations:** The method's key strength—its use of argument annotations—is also a practical limitation. It requires training data to be annotated for both event triggers and their corresponding arguments. While this is the case for ACE 2005, many real-world or domain-specific scenarios may have datasets where only triggers are labeled, as argument annotation is significantly more expensive. The paper does not discuss this limitation or the method's applicability in such settings.
3.  **Unclear Impact of Noise in Data Augmentation:** For the FrameNet augmentation experiments, arguments and entities are generated automatically using FrameNet's structure and an external entity tagger. This process is inherently noisy. While the authors acknowledge that this noise may hurt precision, the paper would benefit from a more in-depth analysis of its impact. For example, some measure of the quality of the automatically generated "gold" attentions from the FrameNet data would help in understanding their effect on the final model performance.

### Questions to Authors

1.  Your unsupervised attention baseline "ANN" achieves a 64.6 F1 score, which is considerably lower than other recent neural models like Skip-CNN (reported at 71.3 F1). Could you elaborate on why this baseline's performance is not closer to the state of the art for neural ED models? Have you considered applying your supervised attention mechanism to a stronger base architecture (e.g., a CNN or Bi-RNN) to test if the improvements are additive and can push the state-of-the-art even further?
2.  For trigger candidates that have no annotated arguments in the context, your S1 strategy defaults to creating a gold attention vector where all entities in the context receive equal attention. This seems like a strong heuristic. Did you experiment with alternative strategies, such as using a uniform attention distribution over all words, or simply excluding these instances from the attention-supervision loss component? How sensitive is the model's performance to this design choice?
3.  The proposed method relies on the availability of argument annotations during training. How do you see this method being applied to datasets or domains where such annotations are scarce or unavailable? Could the model be adapted to a semi-supervised setting, for instance, by using a model's own high-confidence argument predictions to generate pseudo-gold attentions?
