### **Summary and Contributions**

This paper addresses the task of Event Detection (ED), focusing on identifying and classifying event triggers. The authors' central argument is that event arguments, though part of a separate subtask (Argument Extraction), provide crucial information for disambiguating trigger words, yet are underutilized by existing methods. They critique both pipeline systems (which ignore arguments) and joint learning systems (which they claim are biased towards argument extraction and fail to meaningfully improve ED).

The primary contribution is a novel method to explicitly leverage argument information for ED using a **supervised attention mechanism**. The core idea is to train a neural network not only to classify event types but also to learn an attention distribution over the context that mirrors the locations of ground-truth arguments. This is achieved by defining a "gold" attention vector based on annotated arguments and adding a squared-error loss term to the main classification objective, encouraging the model's attention to focus on argument words. The authors propose and evaluate two strategies for constructing these gold attentions. A secondary contribution is a strong empirical result, achieving a new state-of-the-art F1 score on the widely-used ACE 2005 benchmark.

### **Strengths**

1.  **Clear, Well-Motivated Problem Formulation:** The paper's introduction is exceptionally clear and persuasive. The analysis of why previous joint models failed to significantly improve ED (unbalanced data favoring AE, indirect use of argument information) is insightful and provides a strong justification for the proposed work. The example of the trigger "fired" (End-Position vs. Attack) perfectly illustrates the core problem and the potential of argument information to solve it.

2.  **Novel and Elegant Method:** The idea of supervising the attention mechanism using argument annotations is both novel and elegant. It directly addresses the identified shortcoming of previous work by forcing the model to learn what contextual words are important for ED, using the argument annotations as an explicit supervisory signal. This avoids the balancing issues inherent in traditional joint loss functions while still effectively integrating information from the related AE task. The method is simple to understand and implement, adding only a secondary loss term to a standard neural classification model.

3.  **Strong and Thorough Empirical Evaluation:** The experimental results are convincing and well-supported by a series of carefully designed experiments.
    *   **State-of-the-Art Performance:** The proposed model (ANN-S2) achieves the best performance on the ACE 2005 dataset, outperforming a comprehensive set of recent and strong baselines. The reported F1-score of 71.7 is a significant improvement over prior work.
    *   **Assumption Verification:** Section 4.2 provides a crucial ablation study that validates the paper's core hypothesis. By comparing a baseline (ANN), an entity-focused model (ANN-ENT), and oracle models using gold arguments (ANN-Gold1/2), the authors effectively demonstrate that the performance gain comes specifically from focusing on *arguments*, not just entities in general.
    *   **Robustness:** The experiments augmenting the training data with FrameNet show that the method is not only effective on its own but also benefits from additional, albeit noisier, data sources. This demonstrates the approach's potential for scalability and robustness.

### **Weaknesses**

1.  **Limited Technical Novelty in Model Architecture:** While the *supervision method* is novel, the underlying neural architecture itself (word/entity embeddings, a non-linear transformation, an attention layer, and a feed-forward classifier) is quite standard. The primary innovation lies in the training objective and the formulation of the gold attention, not in the network design. This is a minor weakness, as the conceptual contribution is strong, but it's worth noting that the architectural contribution is limited.

2.  **Dependency on Argument Annotations for Training:** The method's main strength is also its main limitation. It fundamentally requires a training corpus that is annotated for both event triggers and their corresponding arguments. While this is the case for ACE 2005, it limits the direct applicability of this technique to datasets or domains where only trigger information is available. The FrameNet experiment shows a path to using automatically-derived arguments, but the core training process still relies on their availability.

3.  **Unclear Attention Combination and Heuristics:**
    *   In Section 3.1.3, the word attention (`αw`) and entity attention (`αe`) vectors are combined via a simple, unweighted sum (`α = αw + αe`). It is not specified if this sum is followed by a re-normalization step. A simple addition could lead to an imbalanced contribution from the two sources. A more principled approach, like a learned weighted average or concatenation, might be more robust.
    *   The fallback strategy for Strategy S1 (when no arguments are present in the context, attention is averaged over all entities) feels like a somewhat arbitrary heuristic. It's unclear how often this case occurs and what impact this design choice has on the final performance.

### **Questions to Authors**

1.  Regarding the attention combination `α = αw + αe`: Have you experimented with other combination strategies, such as a gated mechanism or a learned weighted sum, that might better balance the contributions from word-level and entity-level signals?

2.  The supervised attention approach is data-hungry by nature, as it learns from argument patterns. Could you comment on how the model's performance degrades as the availability of argument annotations in the training set decreases? An experiment on a subset of the training data with argument annotations removed would provide valuable insight into the method's robustness in lower-resource scenarios.

3.  For the S1 gold attention strategy, when a trigger candidate has no annotated arguments in its context window, you average attention over all entities. What percentage of training instances fall into this category? Have you explored alternative fallbacks, such as using a uniform attention distribution or simply excluding these instances from the attention loss calculation?
