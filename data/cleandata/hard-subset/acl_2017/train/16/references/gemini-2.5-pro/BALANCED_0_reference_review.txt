**Summary of the Paper and Contributions**

This paper addresses the task of Event Detection (ED), which involves identifying and classifying event-triggering words in text. The central argument is that existing methods, particularly joint models for event detection and argument extraction, fail to effectively leverage annotated argument information to improve ED performance. The authors propose a novel approach to explicitly incorporate this information through a supervised attention mechanism. Their model learns a context representation by attending to words and entities around a trigger candidate. Crucially, during training, this attention mechanism is directly supervised using a "gold" attention distribution derived from the ground-truth argument annotations, encouraging the model to focus on words that are likely to be arguments. This is implemented via a joint loss function combining the standard classification loss and an attention supervision loss.

In my view, the main contributions of this work are:
1.  **A novel supervised attention framework for Event Detection:** The core contribution is the method of using ground-truth argument labels to create an explicit supervision signal for an attention mechanism. This is a clever and direct way to inject valuable structural information into the ED model, representing a departure from indirect joint learning paradigms.
2.  **State-of-the-art empirical results on a standard benchmark:** The proposed method (specifically ANN-S2) achieves a new state-of-the-art F1-score on the widely used ACE 2005 dataset, demonstrating its practical effectiveness and advancing the performance on this challenging task.
3.  **A clear and insightful analysis of the limitations of prior work:** The paper provides a convincing motivation by analyzing why previous joint models have struggled to improve ED, citing issues like data imbalance and the indirect flow of information from arguments to triggers. This analysis helps contextualize the paper's contribution and highlights a genuine research gap.

**Strengths**

1.  **Strong Motivation and Intuition:** The paper is built on the clear and compelling intuition that arguments provide significant contextual clues for disambiguating event triggers (e.g., the "fired" example). The analysis in the introduction (Table 1) effectively highlights a real shortcoming in prior joint models, providing a strong justification for the proposed research direction.
2.  **Novelty and Simplicity of the Method:** The idea of supervising the attention mechanism is both novel for this task and elegantly simple. Rather than designing a complex joint inference model, the authors use a straightforward auxiliary loss to guide the model's focus. This makes the approach easy to understand and likely easy to implement and integrate into other neural architectures. The two proposed strategies for constructing gold attention (S1 and S2) are sensible and well-explained.
3.  **Thorough and Convincing Experimental Evaluation:** The experiments are well-designed and systematically validate the authors' claims. The inclusion of an unsupervised attention baseline (ANN) clearly demonstrates the benefit of supervision. The oracle experiments using gold arguments at test time (ANN-Gold1/2) provide an effective upper-bound analysis and confirm the underlying hypothesis that argument information is indeed crucial. Finally, achieving state-of-the-art results against strong and recent baselines firmly establishes the method's effectiveness.
4.  **Strong Analysis and Reproducibility:** The paper provides good analysis of its results, for instance, by contrasting the precision/recall trade-offs of the S1 and S2 strategies. The authors also test their model with external data from FrameNet, showing that the approach is robust and benefits from more training data. The clear description of the model and hyper-parameters should facilitate reproducibility.

**Weaknesses**

1.  **Lack of Qualitative Analysis of Learned Attention:** The central claim is that the model *learns* to attend to argument-like words at test time, when no gold arguments are available. However, the paper provides no evidence to support this. A key piece of missing analysis is a qualitative inspection of the learned attention weights on test set examples. Does the model successfully focus on words that are semantically plausible arguments for a given trigger? Without this analysis, it is hard to be certain that the model has learned a generalizable attention policy rather than simply memorizing correlations between specific triggers and context words that happened to be arguments in the training set.
2.  **Relatively Weak Baseline Architecture:** The base model (ANN) is a simple feed-forward network. Its performance (F1=64.6) is substantially lower than other contemporary neural baselines like Skip-CNN (F1=71.3). While the supervised approach clearly provides a massive boost over the authors' own unsupervised baseline, it is possible that some of the reported gains are due to the relative weakness of this baseline's attention mechanism. The work could be made stronger by showing that supervised attention can also improve a more powerful base architecture (e.g., a BiLSTM or CNN-based model).
3.  **Potential for More Sophisticated Gold Attention Schemes:** The two strategies (S1 and S2) for creating gold attention are heuristic and treat all arguments equally. This might be suboptimal, as certain argument roles could be more indicative for specific event types than others. For example, for a `Transport` event, an `Artifact` or `Vehicle` argument might be a stronger signal than a `Time` argument. The paper could be improved by discussing or experimenting with more nuanced supervision schemes, perhaps weighting arguments by their role or type.
4.  **Unclear Formulation of Entity Attention:** The description of how the contextual entity attention vector `αe` is calculated in Section 3.1.3 is confusing. It states that the entity information of the candidate trigger is not used, but instead, a vector `we` is used as the "attention source," which is derived by "transforming w from the word space into the entity type space." The exact mechanism of this transformation is not specified. Is it a learned linear projection? This lack of clarity makes a part of the model difficult to understand and reproduce.

**Questions to Authors**

1.  Could you provide a qualitative analysis of the learned attention distributions on some examples from the development or test set? Specifically, for a correctly classified trigger, does the model assign high attention to words that are plausible (but unannotated at test time) arguments? This would greatly strengthen the claim that the model learns a generalizable strategy.
2.  The performance of your unsupervised baseline ANN (F1=64.6) is significantly below other neural methods from the same period. Do you have insights into why this is the case? Have you considered applying your supervised attention technique to a stronger base model, such as the CNN model from Chen et al. (2015) or Nguyen and Grishman (2015), to verify that the gains are independent of the base architecture's strength?
3.  Regarding the calculation of contextual entity attention (Equation 3), could you please clarify how the vector `we` is computed from the candidate trigger's word embedding `w`? What is the specific function or projection used for this transformation into the "entity type space"?
