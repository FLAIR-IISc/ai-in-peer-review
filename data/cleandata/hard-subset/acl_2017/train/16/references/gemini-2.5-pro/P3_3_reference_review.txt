### Summary and Contributions

This paper addresses the task of Event Detection (ED), arguing that existing methods, particularly joint models for event and argument extraction, fail to effectively leverage argument information to improve ED performance. The authors hypothesize that this is due to data imbalance and an indirect information flow in joint models. To remedy this, they propose a neural network model that explicitly uses argument annotations to guide an attention mechanism. The core idea is to create "gold" attention distributions based on the locations of annotated arguments in the training data and use these to supervise the model's attention layer via an auxiliary loss function. This forces the model to learn to focus on words that are likely to be arguments when classifying a potential trigger.

As I see them, the main contributions are:

1.  **A Novel Supervised Attention Mechanism for ED:** The primary contribution is the method of supervising an attention mechanism using task-specific structural annotations (i.e., argument locations). This provides a direct and explicit signal to the model about what contextual information is important, which is a departure from standard unsupervised attention mechanisms that learn implicitly from the final classification loss.

2.  **Insightful Analysis of Joint Models:** The paper provides a compelling analysis of why previous joint models for event extraction have shown significant gains for Argument Extraction (AE) but not for ED. The identification of the data imbalance problem and the observation that information primarily flows from triggers to arguments (and not vice versa) is a valuable insight that clearly motivates the need for a new approach.

3.  **Strong Empirical Results:** The proposed method achieves state-of-the-art F1 score on the standard ACE 2005 dataset, demonstrating the effectiveness of explicitly modeling argument information for ED.

### Strengths

1.  **Well-Motivated and Intuitive Approach:** The paper's core premise is very strong. The example of the ambiguous trigger "fired" clearly illustrates why argument information ("former protege") is crucial for disambiguation. The subsequent analysis of prior work's shortcomings (Table 1) provides a solid foundation for the proposed solution. Supervising attention with argument locations is an elegant and direct way to inject this crucial information into the model.

2.  **Thorough and Convincing Experimental Design:** The experiments are well-structured to validate the authors' claims. The inclusion of the `ANN` (unsupervised attention) baseline is critical, as it allows for a direct measurement of the benefit of supervision; the large performance gap between `ANN` and `ANN-S1/S2` strongly supports the central claim. Furthermore, the `ANN-ENT` baseline cleverly disentangles the effect of arguments from just any entity, and the `ANN-Gold1/2` experiments effectively demonstrate an upper bound of performance, confirming that access to argument information is indeed highly beneficial.

3.  **Strong Results and Analysis:** The proposed model, `ANN-S2`, achieves a new state-of-the-art F1 score on ACE 2005, significantly outperforming previous methods. The analysis of the precision/recall trade-off between the S1 (hard attention on arguments) and S2 (Gaussian-smoothed attention) strategies is insightful. It correctly identifies that the focused nature of S1 leads to high precision, while the broader context included by S2 improves recall, leading to a better overall F1 score. The further experiments with FrameNet data also show the robustness and potential for further improvement of the model.

### Weaknesses

1.  **Practical Applicability and the "Chicken-and-Egg" Problem:** The method's main weakness is its reliance on gold-standard argument annotations during training. In a standard pipeline, ED precedes Argument Extraction (AE). This model requires argument labels to train the ED component, creating a dependency loop. The authors do not discuss how this model would be trained or applied in a realistic scenario where gold arguments are not available for new, unannotated text. This limits the method's applicability to settings where a fully annotated corpus (with both triggers and arguments) already exists. Could the model be trained using arguments predicted by a weaker system in a bootstrapping fashion? This critical issue of practicality is not addressed.

2.  **Relatively Weak Baseline Architecture:** The base model (`ANN`) is a simple feed-forward network that processes each trigger candidate independently. Its performance (64.6 F1) is substantially lower than contemporary state-of-the-art systems like Nguyen and Grishman's Skip-CNN (71.3 F1). While the supervised attention provides a massive boost, it raises the question of whether this technique is compensating for the weakness of the base model. The impressive gains might be less pronounced if the supervised attention mechanism were applied to a stronger, more context-aware architecture (e.g., a BiLSTM or a more advanced CNN). The experiments do not demonstrate that the contribution is orthogonal to the choice of the base model.

3.  **Lack of Clarity on Some Methodological Details:** The description of the attention mechanism could be more detailed. For instance, the contextual word attention (`αw`) and entity attention (`αe`) are combined via simple addition (`α = αw + αe`). The motivation for this specific combination strategy over others (e.g., concatenation followed by a projection, or a gating mechanism) is not provided or ablated. Additionally, the computation of `we` in Equation 3, which transforms the trigger word embedding into an "entity type space," is somewhat opaque and feels ad-hoc without further justification.

### Questions to Authors

1.  Could you elaborate on the practical deployment of your model? Given the dependency on argument annotations for training the ED model, how do you envision this approach being used in a real-world setting for a new domain where such annotations are not available? Have you considered iterative or joint training schemes where noisy, predicted arguments are used as supervision?

2.  The unsupervised `ANN` baseline performs significantly worse than other contemporary neural models. Do you believe the substantial gains from your supervised attention would hold if applied to a more powerful base architecture, such as the Bi-directional RNN used by Nguyen et al. (2016) or the Skip-CNN by Nguyen and Grishman (2016)? This would help clarify whether the contribution is a general technique for improving ED or a specific fix for a simpler model architecture.
