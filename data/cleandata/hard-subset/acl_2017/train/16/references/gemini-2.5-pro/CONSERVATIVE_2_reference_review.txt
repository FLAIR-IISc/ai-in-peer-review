### Summary of the Paper
This paper proposes a method to improve event detection (ED) by explicitly using annotated event argument information from the training data. The authors argue that existing joint event detection and argument extraction (AE) models fail to effectively leverage this information for the ED subtask. Their proposed method uses a standard neural network classifier for ED but incorporates a supervised attention mechanism. This attention mechanism is trained to focus on words that are annotated as event arguments. The supervision is provided by a "gold" attention vector created heuristically from the argument labels. The model is trained with a joint loss function that combines the standard classification loss with a squared error loss between the model's attention weights and the gold attention vector. Experiments on the ACE 2005 dataset show that this approach significantly outperforms an unsupervised attention baseline and achieves state-of-the-art results compared to previous methods.

### Main Contributions
1.  **A Method for Direct Argument Supervision in ED:** The primary contribution is a straightforward method for injecting supervision from argument labels directly into an event detection model. This is achieved by adding a loss term that encourages an attention mechanism to focus on annotated argument words.
2.  **State-of-the-Art Performance on ACE 2005:** The paper presents strong empirical results on the standard ACE 2005 benchmark, outperforming several recent and competitive baselines in the F1 score.
3.  **Analysis of Joint Models' Weakness for ED:** The work is well-motivated by an insightful analysis of prior joint learning models, presenting evidence that they improve AE but provide negligible benefit to ED, justifying the need for a more direct approach.

### Strengths
1.  **Clear Motivation and Problem Formulation:** The paper's premise is well-founded and convincing. The authors clearly articulate why argument information should be beneficial for disambiguating triggers and provide evidence from prior work (Table 1) that existing joint models fail to capitalize on this. This provides a strong justification for their proposed direct supervision approach.
2.  **Methodological Simplicity and Soundness:** The proposed model is an incremental but sensible modification of a standard neural architecture. Rather than introducing a complex and unproven framework, the authors augment a simple feed-forward network with a supervised attention loss. This approach is easy to understand, reproduce, and is methodologically sound. The effectiveness of this simple addition is compelling.
3.  **Strong Empirical Results:** The experiments are thorough and demonstrate the method's effectiveness. The proposed supervised models (ANN-S1 and ANN-S2) show a substantial improvement of over 7 points in F1-score compared to the identical but unsupervised baseline (ANN). Furthermore, the final model (ANN-S2) achieves a new state-of-the-art result on the ACE 2005 dataset, surpassing a range of strong contemporary systems, including feature-based, representation-based, and joint models. The significance of the improvement is also validated with a t-test.

### Weaknesses
1.  **Limited Novelty of the Core Mechanism:** The idea of "supervised attention" is not new, and the authors cite prior work applying it to machine translation. The novelty here is restricted to its application for ED, using argument labels as the source of supervision. While the application is clever and effective, the work can be seen as a successful application of an existing technique rather than a fundamental methodological contribution.
2.  **Heuristic Nature of the Supervision Signal:** The method's success is entirely dependent on the quality of the "gold attention" vectors. The two strategies proposed (S1 and S2) are purely heuristic. S1's binary assignment is simplistic, while S2's Gaussian smoothing introduces additional hyperparameters (window size, µ, σ) that are set without justification beyond being "standard". The paper lacks an analysis of how sensitive the model is to these design choices or an exploration of more principled methods for deriving the supervision signal from the argument annotations.
3.  **Evaluation on a Single Benchmark:** All claims are based on results from a single dataset, ACE 2005. While this is a crucial benchmark for the task, the generality of the findings is not established. It is possible that the specific properties of ACE 2005 (e.g., annotation style, density of arguments) make it particularly amenable to this approach. Validation on at least one other dataset (e.g., TAC KBP) would be required to make a stronger claim about the method's general utility.
4.  **Lack of Qualitative Analysis:** The paper argues that its method improves the model's focus, but it offers no qualitative evidence to support this. Visualizing the learned attention weights from the unsupervised versus the supervised models on illustrative examples (such as the ambiguous "fired" case from the introduction) would provide direct insight into whether the model is truly learning to leverage argument clues as intended. Without this analysis, the attention mechanism remains a black box that simply leads to better scores.

### Questions to Authors
1.  The hyperparameter λ, which balances the two loss terms, is set to 1.0 for strategy S1 and 5.0 for S2. This is a significant difference. Could you provide a sensitivity analysis for λ for both strategies? How critical is the choice of this parameter to achieving the reported performance?
2.  The "ANN-Gold" experiments in Section 4.2 use oracle argument information at test time. This does not represent a realistic use case. Could you clarify the purpose of this experiment? Have you considered a more practical evaluation where arguments predicted by a separate, state-of-the-art system are used to generate attention at test time? This would give a better sense of the approach's utility in a true pipeline system.
3.  The paper states that for trigger candidates without arguments in the context, S1 forces all entities to receive attention. How often does this situation occur in the training and test sets? Does this fallback strategy have a noticeable impact on performance, and do the supervised models struggle more on these instances compared to the unsupervised baseline?
