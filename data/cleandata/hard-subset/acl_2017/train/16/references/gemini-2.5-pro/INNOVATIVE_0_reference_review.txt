### Summary and Contributions

This paper addresses the task of Event Detection (ED), focusing on improving trigger identification and classification by leveraging event argument information. The authors begin with the insightful observation that previous joint models for Event Detection and Argument Extraction (AE) have successfully improved AE performance but have yielded negligible gains for ED. They hypothesize this is due to data imbalance and an indirect flow of information where arguments do not effectively inform trigger prediction.

To remedy this, the paper proposes a novel and direct method to exploit argument information for ED. The core contribution is a **supervised attention mechanism**. Instead of allowing attention weights to be learned implicitly as a byproduct of the main classification task, the authors create "gold" attention vectors directly from the annotated argument locations in the training data. The model is then trained with a joint loss function that penalizes both incorrect event classification and deviations between the model's learned attention and the gold attention. This forces the model to explicitly learn to focus on words that are arguments when making predictions about a trigger candidate.

In my view, the main contributions are:
1.  **The conceptual innovation of supervised attention for this task.** This is a novel and clever method for injecting a strong, explicit supervisory signal from one type of annotation (arguments) to guide a related task (trigger detection). It represents a new way to think about integrating different information sources in a neural architecture.
2.  A clear and compelling analysis of a recognized but poorly understood deficiency in prior state-of-the-art joint models, providing a strong motivation for a new research direction in event extraction.

### Strengths

1.  **High Novelty and Conceptual Significance:** The central idea of supervising an attention mechanism using argument locations is a bold and significant contribution. Standard attention is unsupervised and learned implicitly. By creating an explicit learning target for the attention weights, this work provides a new, direct, and powerful paradigm for knowledge injection. This approach is not an incremental tweak to an existing architecture but a fundamentally different way to frame the learning problem, which I value highly.

2.  **Strong and Clear Motivation:** The paper's introduction is excellent. It clearly identifies a failure point in existing SOTA joint models—their inability to leverage argument information to improve ED. The authors' analysis of why this might be the case (data imbalance, model architecture) is insightful and provides a perfectly compelling rationale for their proposed solution. They are not just building a better model; they are solving a well-defined problem.

3.  **Elegant and Effective Method:** The proposed method is powerful in its simplicity. The creation of gold attention vectors from argument spans and the addition of a squared-error loss term is a straightforward yet highly effective way to operationalize the core idea. The fact that this relatively simple mechanism, applied to a standard ANN, leads to state-of-the-art results underscores the strength of the underlying concept.

4.  **Convincing Ablation and Analysis:** The experiments in Section 4.2 ("Correctness of Our Assumption") are crucial and well-executed. By comparing a baseline unsupervised attention model (`ANN`), a model that attends to all entities (`ANN-ENT`), and oracle models using gold attentions (`ANN-Gold1/2`), the authors provide compelling evidence for their main claim. The results strongly suggest that it is the specific signal from *arguments*, not just entities in general, that is key to the performance improvement.

### Weaknesses

While I am very positive about this paper, I will note a few areas that could be stronger, though I do not consider them grounds for rejection given the novelty of the work.

1.  **Simplicity of the Base Model:** The core event detector is a simple three-layer ANN. While this is perfectly adequate for demonstrating the power of the supervised attention mechanism, it is less complex than the CNN and RNN architectures used by some of the compared SOTA methods. This leaves open the question of how this mechanism would perform when integrated with more powerful, contemporary architectures. However, using a simpler base model also has the benefit of isolating the gains from the novel contribution, which is a valid research choice.

2.  **Heuristic Nature of Gold Attention Schemes:** The two strategies for constructing gold attention vectors, S1 (hard attention on arguments) and S2 (Gaussian-smoothed attention), are effective but heuristic. They rely on pre-defined rules and hyperparameter choices (e.g., Gaussian variance, window size). This is a reasonable first step for a new idea, but a deeper exploration of more principled or learnable methods for generating the supervisory signal could strengthen the approach further.

3.  **Underwhelming Impact of FrameNet Augmentation:** The experiments using FrameNet data to augment training show very marginal F1 score improvements (+0.2 for the best model). This section feels less impactful than the core contribution and is complicated by the introduction of potential noise from external annotation tools. The paper's primary claims are strongly supported by the ACE 2005 results alone, and this additional experiment adds little to the main story.

### Questions to Authors

1.  The trade-off hyperparameter λ is set to 1.0 for the S1 strategy and 5.0 for the S2 strategy. This is a significant difference. Could you provide some intuition for why the "blurry" attention signal from S2 requires a five-fold stronger weight in the loss function compared to the hard signal from S1? Is this because the S2 signal is weaker per-token and thus requires more overall emphasis to guide the learning? How sensitive is the model's performance to this parameter?

2.  Could you provide a qualitative error analysis? It would be very insightful to see one or two concrete examples where the supervised attention model correctly identifies an event trigger (especially an ambiguous one like the "fired" example) that the unsupervised baseline misses. This would provide a more tangible illustration of precisely how the learned attention is helping.
