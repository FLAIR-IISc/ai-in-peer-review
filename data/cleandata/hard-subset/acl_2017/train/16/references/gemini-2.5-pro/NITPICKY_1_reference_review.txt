### Summary and Contributions

This paper proposes to improve event detection (ED) by explicitly using annotated argument information, which is often ignored in pipeline ED systems or, as the authors argue, ineffectively used in joint extraction models. The primary contribution is a neural network model for ED that employs a supervised attention mechanism. The core idea is to generate "gold" attention vectors from the argument annotations in the training data and use these to directly supervise the model's attention layer via an auxiliary loss term. The authors test two strategies for creating these gold attentions. Experiments on the ACE 2005 dataset show that this approach achieves state-of-the-art performance on the specific data split used by the authors.

As I see them, the contributions are:
1.  A novel and intuitive method for explicitly incorporating argument information into an ED model by framing it as a supervision signal for an attention mechanism.
2.  An empirical demonstration that this supervised attention approach significantly outperforms an unsupervised attention baseline and achieves a new state-of-the-art result on a previously used, albeit non-standard, split of the ACE 2005 dataset.

### Strengths

1.  **Well-motivated Problem and Intuitive Solution:** The paper provides a clear and compelling motivation. The analysis in the introduction, particularly Table 1, effectively argues that prior joint models failed to leverage argument information for the ED subtask. The proposed solution—to directly supervise attention using argument locations—is a simple, elegant, and logical response to this identified problem.

2.  **Novelty of the Approach:** To my knowledge, the idea of creating a direct supervision signal from argument annotations to train an attention mechanism for event detection is novel. It represents a clever way to transfer knowledge from one annotation type (arguments) to a related task (trigger detection) without the complexities of a full joint inference model.

3.  **Strong Reported Performance:** The proposed model, particularly ANN-S2, achieves an F1 score of 71.7, surpassing previous state-of-the-art results on this dataset split. The significant boost in precision compared to many prior models is particularly impressive and suggests the supervised attention is effective at focusing the model on highly indicative contextual clues.

### Weaknesses

Despite the promising core idea, the paper is marred by a substantial number of flaws in its methodology, presentation, and experimental design. These issues are severe enough to undermine the work's clarity, reproducibility, and the reliability of its conclusions.

1.  **Critical Lack of Methodological Clarity and Precision:** The description of the proposed model in Section 3 is frustratingly vague and contains numerous notational errors, making the work impossible to reproduce.
    *   **Undefined Hyperparameters:** The context window size, denoted by `n`, is a critical hyperparameter of the model, yet its value is never stated.
    *   **Incorrect or Ambiguous Equations:** Equation 1 (`wk = f(wk Ww)`) is a circular definition. Equations 4 and 5 (`cw = CwαT`) describe a mathematically invalid matrix multiplication given the likely dimensions of the matrices. The transformation of a word vector `w` into an entity-space vector `we` for Equation 3 is mentioned but never defined. The S2 strategy for gold attention is described only in prose; a formal equation is necessary.
    *   **Poor Notation:** The notation is inconsistent (e.g., `w0` vs `w`). The description of the context window `[w−n, w−n/2 +1, ..., wn/2]` is non-standard and confusing.

2.  **Weak Baseline and Questionable Experimental Design:**
    *   The unsupervised attention baseline (ANN) achieves an F1 score of only 64.6. This is significantly lower than other contemporary neural ED models (e.g., Chen et al. 2015 reported 69.1 F1), suggesting the base architecture is weak. This weak baseline may artificially inflate the perceived gains of the supervised attention mechanism. The authors should justify why their base model is so much weaker than comparable systems.
    *   The use of oracle systems (ANN-Gold1/2) that leverage gold-standard argument information at *test time* in Section 4.2 is presented as proof of the paper's main assumption. This is misleading. These experiments merely establish an upper bound and do not validate the effectiveness of a realistic system that must operate without such oracle information.

3.  **Extremely Poor Manuscript Preparation:** The paper exhibits a lack of care that is unacceptable for a top-tier conference.
    *   The reference section is completely malformed, filled with BibTeX errors, extraneous characters, and inconsistent formatting (e.g., "Bengio et al\.,? 2003, Bengio et al\.," and "\Q2010\E"). This is a major presentational flaw.
    *   Numerous typos and minor errors are present throughout the text (e.g., "join-t methods" in Table 1, inconsistent hyphenation of "state-of-the-art", "p 6 0.05" instead of "p < 0.05", "Ngyuen" instead of "Nguyen").
    *   Table formatting is poor; the headers in Table 1 are misaligned with the data columns.

While the central idea of this paper has merit, the execution and presentation are deeply flawed. The methodological description is too incomplete and imprecise for the work to be reproduced or properly assessed. The combination of a weak baseline, misleading oracle experiments, and abysmal formatting significantly detracts from the paper's contribution.

### Questions to Authors

1.  Could you please provide the missing methodological details required for reproducibility? Specifically: (a) the context window size `n`; (b) the precise mathematical formulations for Equations 1, 4, and 5; (c) the definition of the transformation used to obtain `we` for Equation 3; and (d) a formal equation for the S2 gold attention construction strategy.

2.  Your unsupervised ANN baseline scores 64.6 F1, which is 4-5 points lower than other neural ED models from the same time period. Can you provide an analysis of why the baseline is comparatively weak? How can you be certain that the large gains from your supervised method are not primarily due to correcting for deficiencies in a weak base model?

3.  The decision to sum the word and entity attention vectors (`α = αw + αe`) is unconventional. What was the justification for this over more standard fusion methods like concatenation followed by a dense layer, or a gating mechanism? Have you performed any ablation studies on this design choice?
