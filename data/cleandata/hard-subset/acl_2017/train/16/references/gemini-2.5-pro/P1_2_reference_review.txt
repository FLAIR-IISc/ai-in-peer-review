**Summary and Contributions**

This paper addresses the task of Event Detection (ED), arguing that existing methods, particularly joint ED and Argument Extraction (AE) models, fail to effectively leverage annotated argument information to improve ED performance. The authors propose a neural network-based ED model that incorporates an attention mechanism trained with explicit supervision. This supervision is derived from "gold" attention vectors constructed from the locations of annotated arguments in the training data, forcing the model to focus on context words that are likely arguments. The model is evaluated on the ACE 2005 dataset, where it demonstrates significant improvements over baseline models and achieves new state-of-the-art results.

From my perspective, the main contributions of this work are:
1.  **A novel and effective method for integrating argument information into event detection.** The core idea of using a supervised attention mechanism, where the supervision signal directly encodes the location of arguments, is an intuitive and powerful way to address the well-motivated problem of trigger ambiguity.
2.  **A compelling analysis of the shortcomings of prior joint learning approaches for ED.** The paper clearly identifies why joint models have historically boosted AE but not ED performance, citing data imbalance and the indirect flow of information from arguments to triggers. This provides a strong rationale for the proposed explicit approach.
3.  **Strong empirical results and thorough validation on a standard benchmark.** The proposed method significantly outperforms a range of strong baselines, including previous state-of-the-art systems. The experimental design is sound, featuring an insightful "oracle" experiment that validates the central hypothesis before testing the full model.

**Strengths**

1.  **Strong motivation and an elegant, well-justified solution.** The paper opens with a clear example ("fired") that perfectly illustrates the problem of trigger ambiguity and the potential for arguments to resolve it. The proposed supervised attention mechanism is a direct and logical solution to this problem, creating a tight and convincing narrative throughout the paper.
2.  **Thorough and well-designed experiments.** The experimental setup is a major strength. The authors first use an oracle experiment (ANN-Gold1/2) in Section 4.2 to confirm their core hypothesis that access to argument locations dramatically improves ED. This is excellent practice. The main results in Table 3 demonstrate a clear and significant performance gain over their own baseline (ANN) and several state-of-the-art systems. The gains, particularly in precision, are substantial and statistically significant.
3.  **Clear and insightful analysis.** The paper is well-written and easy to follow. The authors' analysis of why their unsupervised attention baseline (ANN) performs poorly (due to data sparsity) is plausible and further strengthens the case for their supervised approach. The discussion comparing the S1 and S2 strategies (precision vs. recall trade-off) is also insightful and demonstrates a good understanding of the model's behavior.
4.  **Simplicity and replicability.** While the idea of supervised attention exists elsewhere, its application here is novel and effective. The approach is a relatively simple modification to a standard attention-based classifier, which makes the work easy to understand, appreciate, and likely replicate.

**Weaknesses**

1.  **Dependency on argument annotations creates limitations.** The entire method hinges on the availability of argument annotations during training to generate the supervisory signal. This limits its applicability to datasets that lack such annotations (e.g., datasets annotated only for triggers). While this is acceptable for the ACE benchmark, it is a significant practical limitation that is not discussed. The work would be stronger if it addressed potential avenues for relaxing this constraint, such as using noisy argument labels or semi-supervised approaches.
2.  **Lack of qualitative error analysis.** The paper presents strong aggregate scores but misses an opportunity to provide qualitative evidence that the model is working as intended. An error analysis showing specific examples of ambiguous triggers (like the "fired" example) that the supervised model correctly classifies while the baseline fails would be highly compelling. Without this, it is harder to be certain that the performance gain comes from better resolving ambiguity, as hypothesized.
3.  **Heuristic nature of "gold" attention.** The two strategies for constructing gold attention vectors, S1 and S2, are reasonable but simplistic heuristics. S1 treats all arguments as equally important, while S2 adds a simple proximity-based prior. It is likely that the true importance of a contextual word depends on more nuanced factors (e.g., the argument's semantic role). The paper could benefit from a discussion on the limitations of these heuristics or an exploration of more sophisticated strategies for generating the supervision signal.
4.  **The base model architecture is relatively simple.** The core classifier is a standard feed-forward neural network, and the attention mechanism is a standard formulation. This is not a major weakness, as the contribution lies in the training method, not the architecture. However, the poor performance of the unsupervised ANN baseline (64.6 F1) is notably lower than other contemporary neural models like Skip-CNN (71.3 F1). This raises the question of whether the performance gains are partially due to the supervised attention fixing a weaker base model, rather than solely demonstrating the superiority of the supervision strategy over other advanced architectures.

**Questions to Authors**

1.  Regarding the unsupervised baseline (ANN), its F1 score of 64.6 is significantly lower than other neural SOTA methods like Skip-CNN. Could this be due to architectural differences (ANN vs. CNN) rather than just the lack of supervision on attention? Could you clarify how your base model compares architecturally to these other methods and comment on whether the supervised attention approach would yield similar gains if applied to a stronger baseline like a CNN or RNN?
2.  In testing, the model relies on its learned attention weights without gold argument labels. Could you provide a visualization of the learned attention weights for a test sentence? This would be very helpful to qualitatively verify that the model has indeed learned a general strategy of focusing on plausible (but unannotated) arguments to correctly classify an ambiguous trigger.
3.  How sensitive is the model's performance to the hyperparameter λ, which balances the classification loss and the attention supervision loss? The paper sets it to 1.0 for S1 and 5.0 for S2, but a sensitivity analysis would provide greater insight into the stability and robustness of the joint training procedure.
