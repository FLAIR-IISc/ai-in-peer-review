### Summary and Contributions

This paper tackles the task of Event Detection (ED), arguing that existing methods, including joint models for Event Detection and Argument Extraction (AE), fail to effectively leverage annotated argument information to improve ED performance. The authors diagnose this failure, suggesting it stems from data imbalance and the indirect way arguments are used in joint frameworks. To remedy this, they propose a conceptually novel approach: using a supervised attention mechanism. Instead of allowing the attention mechanism to be learned in a purely unsupervised fashion, they create "gold" attention distributions from the annotated argument words in the training data and add an explicit loss term to train the model's attention to mimic these distributions. This directly forces the model to focus on context words that are known to be salient arguments, thereby improving its ability to identify and classify event triggers.

In my view, the main contributions of this work are:

1.  **A New Paradigm for Information Transfer in Event Extraction:** The primary contribution is the introduction of supervised attention as a mechanism to explicitly inject knowledge from event arguments into the event detection task. This represents a bold and creative alternative to traditional joint modeling, sidestepping common issues like data imbalance while still enabling information flow between related subtasks.
2.  **An Insightful Critique of Joint Models:** The paper provides a compelling analysis of why prior joint ED-AE models have largely failed to boost ED performance. This careful problem diagnosis is a valuable contribution in itself, as it clarifies a key challenge in the field and motivates the search for new solutions like the one proposed.

### Strengths

1.  **High Conceptual Novelty:** The standout strength of this paper is its core idea. While attention mechanisms are ubiquitous, their training is almost always unsupervised. The proposal to supervise attention using annotations from a different (but related) task is a significant conceptual leap. It presents a new and promising direction for exploiting diverse annotations within a single model without resorting to complex, and often brittle, joint inference frameworks. This is exactly the kind of bold, thought-provoking work the community should encourage.
2.  **Strong Motivation and Clear Hypothesis:** The paper is exceptionally well-motivated. The authors don't just present a new method; they first build a strong case for why it's needed by dissecting the limitations of prior work. The example of the ambiguous trigger "fired" is illustrative, and the empirical data in Table 1 powerfully supports their claim that joint models have not solved this problem. This clear problem formulation makes the proposed solution feel both elegant and necessary.
3.  **Thorough and Convincing Experimental Design:** The experiments are well-structured to validate the authors' central hypothesis. The inclusion of the unsupervised baseline (`ANN`) versus the supervised models (`ANN-S1/S2`) provides a direct and powerful demonstration of the proposed technique's efficacy. Furthermore, the "oracle" experiments (`ANN-Gold1/2`) are a clever addition, effectively establishing an empirical upper bound and confirming that access to argument information is indeed transformative for the task. The fact that the final model achieves state-of-the-art results validates its practical utility.

### Weaknesses

While I am enthusiastic about this paper, there are areas where the approach could be refined or explored more deeply. These are not reasons for rejection but rather opportunities for future work that build on this strong foundation.

1.  **Heuristic Construction of "Gold" Attention:** The two strategies for creating the gold attention vectors, while functional, are somewhat heuristic. The binary S1 strategy is quite rigid, and the Gaussian-based S2 strategy relies on fixed hyperparameters (window size, µ, σ). This feels like a simplification of a more complex relationship between arguments and triggers. The work could be strengthened by exploring more nuanced or even learnable methods for defining the target attention distribution. However, given the novelty of the overall framework, this simplification is an acceptable starting point.
2.  **Lack of Qualitative Analysis:** The paper provides strong quantitative results but would benefit from a qualitative analysis of the learned attention. For instance, visualizing the attention weights produced by the unsupervised `ANN` model versus the supervised `ANN-S2` model on challenging examples (like the "fired" sentence) would provide compelling visual evidence that the supervision is working as intended. This would help readers build a more intuitive understanding of *why* the model's performance improves.
3.  **Potential Brittleness of the Loss Combination:** The final loss is a simple linear combination of the ED loss and the attention supervision loss, balanced by a hyperparameter λ. This approach can be sensitive to the choice of λ, and the paper notes that different values were needed for S1 and S2. While the authors found settings that work well, the method might be more robust if a more principled way to combine these objectives (e.g., uncertainty weighting) were explored.

### Questions to Authors

1.  The motivation for the work is centered on ambiguous triggers like "fired". Did you perform any error analysis to confirm that your supervised attention method disproportionately improves performance on these specific types of challenging cases compared to the unsupervised baseline?
2.  The λ hyperparameter seems crucial for balancing the two loss terms. How sensitive were the final results to this parameter? Is there a reasonably wide range of values for λ that produce good results, or does it require very precise tuning?
