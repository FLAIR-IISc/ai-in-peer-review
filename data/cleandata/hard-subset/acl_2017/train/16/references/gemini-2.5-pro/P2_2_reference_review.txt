### **Summary and Contributions**

This paper addresses the task of Event Detection (ED), focusing on the observation that prior joint Event Detection and Argument Extraction (AE) models have failed to significantly improve ED performance, despite having access to argument information. The authors hypothesize this is because argument information is used indirectly and is outweighed by the AE task during joint training.

To rectify this, the authors propose a neural network model that explicitly leverages argument annotations to improve ED. The core contribution is a **supervised attention mechanism**. Instead of learning attention weights in a purely unsupervised manner, the model is trained with an auxiliary objective that encourages its attention mechanism to focus on words that are annotated as arguments in the training data. The model learns to represent a trigger candidate's context by attending to words and entities, and this attention is guided by "gold" attention vectors constructed from argument labels. The paper presents two strategies for creating these gold attentions: one focusing strictly on argument words (S1) and another using a Gaussian distribution to include surrounding words (S2).

As I see them, the main contributions are:
1.  **A novel and effective method for incorporating argument information into ED via supervised attention.** This is a direct and intuitive way to force a model to learn the connection between specific contextual patterns (arguments) and event types, addressing a well-articulated shortcoming of previous joint models.
2.  **Strong empirical validation demonstrating the effectiveness of the proposed method.** The paper provides a well-designed set of experiments, including an insightful ablation study that confirms their core assumption about the importance of argument information (Section 4.2). The model achieves a new state-of-the-art F1 score on the ACE 2005 benchmark.

### **Strengths**

1.  **Clear Motivation and Well-Defined Problem.** The paper does an excellent job of motivating the work. The introduction clearly explains why argument information should be useful for ED (e.g., the ambiguous "fired" example) and provides quantitative evidence (Table 1) that existing joint models fail to leverage this information effectively for the ED subtask. The authors' reasoning for this failure (data imbalance, indirect information flow) is plausible and provides a strong foundation for their proposed solution.

2.  **Elegant and Intuitive Method.** The proposed supervised attention mechanism is a simple yet powerful idea. Rather than relying on complex joint inference or structured prediction, it integrates the supervision signal directly into the neural architecture via an additional loss term. This approach is fully differentiable and fits naturally within standard deep learning training paradigms. The exploration of two different supervision strategies (S1 and S2) is also a good contribution, showing that the approach is not overly sensitive to the exact formulation of the gold attention.

3.  **Thorough Experimental Setup and Analysis.** The experimental evaluation is comprehensive and convincing.
    *   The experiments in Section 4.2 ("Correctness of Our Assumption") are particularly strong. By comparing a standard unsupervised attention model (`ANN`) against an entity-focused model (`ANN-ENT`) and oracle models using gold attentions (`ANN-Gold1/2`), the authors provide compelling evidence that it is specifically the *argument* information, not just general entity information, that provides the performance boost.
    *   The main results (Table 3) show a significant improvement over both the unsupervised baseline and previous state-of-the-art systems. The final model (`ANN-S2`) achieves an F1 score of 71.7, surpassing the previous best by 1.0 point. The substantial gain in precision is particularly noteworthy.
    *   The augmentation with FrameNet data further validates the approach, showing that it can benefit from additional, albeit noisy, supervision and that the performance gains are not solely an artifact of the ACE 2005 dataset.

### **Weaknesses**

1.  **Strong Supervision Requirement.** The primary weakness of this method is its reliance on argument-annotated data for training. While this is available for the ACE 2005 dataset, it limits the approach's applicability to other ED datasets or low-resource scenarios where only trigger annotations are available. The method effectively shifts the problem from joint inference at test time to using AE labels as a privileged training signal for a pipeline ED model. This dependency should be more explicitly discussed as a limitation.

2.  **Potentially Weak Baseline.** The performance of the unsupervised attention baseline (`ANN`) is quite low (64.6 F1). This is significantly below other reported neural ED models from around the same period, such as Chen et al. (2015) (69.1 F1) or Nguyen and Grishman (2015). While the architecture is similar to Liu et al. (2016a), the low baseline performance may exaggerate the gains achieved by the supervised attention. A stronger unsupervised baseline would make the demonstrated improvements even more compelling.

3.  **Ambiguity in Attention Formulation.** The paper computes separate attention vectors for words (`αw`) and entities (`αe`) and then combines them via simple addition (`α = αw + αe`) before applying the supervision loss. The rationale for this specific combination is not well-justified. It is unclear how the error signal from the joint loss `D(θ)` is distributed between the parameters governing `αw` and `αe`. An ablation study comparing the performance of supervising only `αw`, only `αe`, or their sum would have strengthened the architectural design choices.

### **Questions to Authors**

1.  The performance of your unsupervised baseline (`ANN`) at 64.6 F1 seems low compared to contemporary neural methods. For instance, Chen et al. (2015) achieve 69.1 F1 with a CNN-based model. Could you comment on whether this might be due to architectural choices or hyperparameter tuning, and how a stronger baseline might affect the relative gains reported for `ANN-S1` and `ANN-S2`?

2.  Regarding the attention mechanism, you combine word and entity attentions by summing them (`α = αw + αe`). Did you experiment with other combination methods (e.g., concatenation, gating) or with supervising only one of the attention components? Could you provide more intuition on why this simple sum is an effective choice?

3.  Your method's main requirement is the availability of argument annotations during training. Have you considered how this approach might be adapted to settings with only partial or no argument labels? For instance, could one use a weakly supervised model to generate "pseudo-gold" arguments and then use those to train the supervised attention mechanism in an iterative fashion?
