### Summary

This paper addresses the task of Event Detection (ED), which involves identifying and classifying event trigger words in text. The central argument is that existing methods, including joint models for event and argument extraction, do not effectively leverage annotated argument information to improve ED. To address this, the authors propose a neural network model that explicitly uses argument information via a supervised attention mechanism. During training, "gold" attention vectors are created based on the positions of annotated arguments in the context of a trigger candidate. The model's attention mechanism is then trained to replicate these gold attentions through an additional loss term. This forces the model to learn to focus on words that are likely to be arguments when classifying a potential trigger. The authors evaluate their approach on the ACE 2005 dataset, showing significant improvements over state-of-the-art methods. They also demonstrate that the method benefits from augmentation with external data from FrameNet.

### Contributions

1.  **A novel method for explicitly incorporating argument information into the Event Detection task using a supervised attention mechanism.** The core contribution is the formulation of a training objective that directly uses argument annotations to guide an attention mechanism. This is achieved by constructing "gold" attention distributions from ground-truth arguments and adding a squared error loss term to the main classification loss, compelling the model to attend to argument words.
2.  **A thorough empirical validation of the hypothesis that direct argument supervision benefits ED.** The paper provides a clear experimental progression, starting with an analysis that isolates the effect of argument information (Table 2) and demonstrates its utility beyond just general entity information. This is followed by a comprehensive comparison against strong baselines (Table 3), achieving state-of-the-art results on the ACE 2005 benchmark.

### Strengths

1.  **Well-motivated and clear problem formulation.** The paper starts with a clear and compelling motivation, observing that prior joint learning models for event extraction have primarily improved argument extraction (AE) while offering minimal gains for event detection (ED). The authors provide a plausible explanation for this phenomenon (unbalanced data, indirect use of arguments) and propose a direct solution. The example of the ambiguous trigger "fired" effectively illustrates why argument context is crucial for ED.
2.  **Technically sound and clearly described methodology.** The proposed architecture, while based on a standard ANN, introduces the supervised attention mechanism in a clear and reproducible manner. The paper details two distinct strategies (S1 and S2) for constructing the gold attention vectors and explains how they are integrated into a joint loss function. This clarity allows for a straightforward understanding of the technical novelty.
3.  **Comprehensive and rigorous experimental evaluation.** The experiments are well-designed to support the paper's claims. First, an initial experiment (Section 4.2) validates the core assumption that argument information is more beneficial than general entity information for ED. Second, the main results (Table 3) show that the proposed method significantly outperforms a wide range of recent and competitive baselines, including both feature-based and representation-based models. The use of a t-test to confirm statistical significance is a good practice. Finally, the experiments incorporating FrameNet data (Section 4.4) demonstrate the robustness and extensibility of the approach.

### Weaknesses

1.  **Dependency on argument annotations during training.** The method's primary strength is also a potential limitation. It requires training data to be annotated with both event triggers and their corresponding arguments to create the supervision signal for the attention mechanism. This might limit its applicability in scenarios or on datasets where only trigger information is available. While the model does not require argument annotations at test time, its performance relies on having learned from them during training, which assumes a specific type of rich annotation that may not always be available.
2.  **Potential for noise in augmented data processing.** The augmentation with FrameNet data is a valuable addition, but the process of preparing this data introduces potential sources of noise. The paper states that core frame elements are treated as arguments and an external system is used to label entities. The performance of this external entity tagger and the appropriateness of mapping all "core" frame elements to ACE-style arguments are not analyzed. It is unclear how noise or mismatches from this automatic annotation process might affect the quality of the "gold" attention signals and, consequently, the model's training.
3.  **The base model architecture is relatively simple.** The event detector is a standard three-layer ANN. While the focus of the paper is on the novel supervised attention mechanism, it is unclear how this technique would perform when integrated with more powerful and contemporary encoders like BiLSTMs or Transformers, which are often better at capturing complex contextual dependencies. The performance gains shown are impressive, but it is an open question whether these gains are additive with a stronger base architecture or if a more powerful model might implicitly learn similar dependencies without direct supervision.

### Questions to Authors

1.  The supervised attention mechanism relies on gold argument annotations during training, but at test time, it must generalize to identify salient words without such annotations. Could you elaborate on the mechanism by which the model learns to generalize? Does it learn to identify features of words or phrases that are characteristic of arguments (e.g., specific entity types, syntactic roles), and if so, have you performed any analysis to confirm this?
2.  Regarding the FrameNet augmentation, could you provide more detail on the potential impact of noise introduced by the automatic entity and argument annotation process? For instance, what is the accuracy of the RPI system on this data, and did you perform any error analysis on how incorrect entity tags or mismatched frame elements might have affected the "gold" attention signals used for training?
3.  Have you considered applying your supervised attention mechanism to more recent and powerful neural architectures, such as BiLSTMs or Transformer-based models, which are now common for sequence classification tasks? It would be interesting to know if the significant performance gains you observed on an ANN-based model would also manifest on these stronger base models.
