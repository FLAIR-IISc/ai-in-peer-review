### **Summary and Contributions**

This paper addresses the task of Event Detection (ED) by proposing a method to explicitly leverage annotated event arguments from the training data, information which the authors argue is underutilized by previous pipeline and joint learning methods. The core idea is to use a supervised attention mechanism within a neural network classifier. During training, "gold" attention vectors are created based on the locations of annotated arguments for a given trigger candidate. The model's attention mechanism is then trained, via a joint loss function, to mimic these gold attentions, effectively forcing the model to focus on context words that are known to be salient (i.e., arguments).

The main contributions of this work are:
1.  **A Novel Application of Supervised Attention for Event Detection:** The primary contribution is the method itself. By framing the problem as supervising the model's attention mechanism with argument locations, the authors provide a direct and explicit way to inject argument information into the ED task, successfully addressing a well-motivated gap in prior work.
2.  **State-of-the-Art Empirical Performance:** The proposed model, particularly the variant using a smoothed Gaussian attention (ANN-S2), achieves a new state-of-the-art F1 score on the standard ACE 2005 benchmark, outperforming several recent and strong baselines.
3.  **Insightful Analysis of Joint Models:** The paper provides a compelling analysis of why previous joint event extraction models have failed to significantly improve ED performance, attributing it to data imbalance and the typical flow of information in those models. This analysis provides strong motivation for their work.

### **Strengths**

1.  **Well-Motivated and Intuitive Approach:** The paper's core premise is strong and clearly articulated. The example of the ambiguous trigger "fired" convincingly illustrates why argument information is crucial for ED. The subsequent analysis of why joint models fail to leverage this for ED is insightful and justifies the need for a new approach. The proposed solution—directly supervising the attention mechanism—is an elegant and direct way to address this identified problem.
2.  **Strong and Thorough Experimental Validation:** The empirical evaluation is comprehensive and provides robust support for the authors' claims.
    *   The "Correctness of Our Assumption" experiment (Section 4.2) is particularly effective. Using oracle models (ANN-Gold1/2) that leverage gold argument information at test time clearly demonstrates the upper bound of this information's utility and validates the central hypothesis.
    *   The main results show a significant improvement over an unsupervised attention baseline (64.6 vs. 71.7 F1) and a clear win over previous state-of-the-art systems. The gains are not marginal, suggesting a meaningful advance.
    *   The inclusion of experiments with FrameNet augmentation further strengthens the paper by addressing the known data sparsity issue in ED and demonstrating that their method's gains are complementary to data augmentation techniques.
3.  **Clarity and Reproducibility:** The paper is well-written and the proposed method is described in sufficient detail to allow for reproducibility. The model architecture is straightforward, and the two strategies for constructing gold attentions are clearly explained. The authors also point to the specific data splits and external resources used, adhering to good scientific practice.

### **Weaknesses**

1.  **Limited Novelty in the Core Mechanism:** While the *application* of supervised attention to ED is novel and effective, the technique of supervising attention weights is not new in itself (as acknowledged by the citation to Mi et al., 2016 for NMT). The underlying attention calculation is a standard dot-product attention, and the overall classifier is a simple feed-forward network. The contribution is therefore more about a novel and successful application of existing techniques to a new problem formulation rather than a fundamental methodological advance in neural architectures.
2.  **Oversimplified Attention Fusion:** In Section 3.1.3, the method for combining word-level and entity-level attention (`α = αw + αe`) is a simple summation. This seems suboptimal, as it doesn't allow the model to dynamically weight the importance of the two information sources. More critically, this single, combined attention vector `α` is then used to weight both the contextual word embeddings (`Cw`) and the contextual entity embeddings (`Ce`). This assumes that the words and entity types that are most salient for a given trigger are perfectly aligned, which may not be the case. A more sophisticated model might learn two separate attention distributions or use a gating mechanism for fusion. This aspect of the model feels underexplored.
3.  **Potential Mismatch in FrameNet Data Augmentation:** The process for adapting FrameNet data is reasonable but introduces potential noise and conceptual mismatches. The arguments ("core frame elements") in FrameNet are not always equivalent to arguments in ACE. For example, FrameNet's granularity and semantic definitions can differ. Furthermore, entity types are annotated using an external, non-perfect system. While the results show a net positive effect, the paper would be stronger with a brief discussion of this potential domain mismatch and an analysis of its impact (e.g., how much noise might be introduced and how it affects precision vs. recall).

### **Questions to Authors**

1.  Regarding the attention mechanism in Section 3.1.3: Could you elaborate on the design choice of summing the word and entity attention vectors and then applying the same resulting vector to both context types? Have you experimented with alternative approaches, such as learning separate attention distributions for words and entities, or using a more complex fusion method like a gated sum?
2.  Your analysis of the S1 (hard attention) and S2 (Gaussian) strategies shows an interesting precision/recall trade-off. Could you provide a brief error analysis? For instance, what types of ambiguous triggers does the higher-precision S1 model correctly classify that the S2 model misses, and conversely, what new event types does the higher-recall S2 model correctly identify?
3.  The performance of the unsupervised attention baseline (ANN) is quite low (64.6 F1), significantly below other recent neural models like Nguyen and Grishman (2016) which report an F1 of 71.3. While data sparsity is a valid reason, could there be other architectural or hyperparameter factors contributing to this large performance gap? Does your unsupervised baseline use the same architecture (apart from the supervised loss term) as the state-of-the-art models you compare against?
