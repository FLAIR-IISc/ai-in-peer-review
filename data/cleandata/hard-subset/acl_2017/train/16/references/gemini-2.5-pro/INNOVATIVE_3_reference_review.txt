### Summary and Contributions

This paper addresses the task of Event Detection (ED), specifically focusing on how to better leverage event argument information to improve the identification and classification of event triggers. The authors astutely observe that prior joint learning models, which train ED and argument extraction (AE) together, have yielded significant gains for AE but only marginal improvements for ED. They hypothesize this is due to data imbalance and the fact that argument information is not explicitly used to inform trigger prediction in these models.

The primary and most significant contribution of this work is the proposal of a **novel supervised attention mechanism** to explicitly integrate argument information into the ED task. Instead of learning attention weights in an unsupervised manner, the model is trained to produce attention distributions that mimic a "gold" distribution derived from the annotated arguments in the training data. This reframes the problem of information integration in a creative and direct way.

A secondary contribution is the clear and compelling analysis of the shortcomings of existing joint models for the ED subtask, which provides a strong motivation for their new direction.

### Strengths

1.  **High Novelty and a Bold New Direction:** The core idea of using argument annotations to directly supervise an attention mechanism for trigger detection is highly novel and thought-provoking. While attention mechanisms are common, using supervision from a related but distinct annotation layer (arguments) to guide the attention for another task (trigger detection) is a clever and unconventional approach. It sidesteps the complexities of joint inference models and instead injects the necessary information directly where it is needed. This is precisely the kind of bold, conceptual leap that has the potential to open up new research directions in multi-task learning and information extraction.

2.  **Excellent Problem Formulation and Motivation:** The paper begins with a sharp, data-driven analysis of a real and underexplored problem in the field. The observation, supported by Table 1, that state-of-the-art joint models fail to improve ED is both surprising and insightful. The authors' diagnosis—attributing this failure to data imbalance and the one-way flow of information in typical joint architectures—is convincing and sets the stage perfectly for their proposed solution. This strong framing makes the work feel not just technically sound, but also important and well-grounded.

3.  **Conceptually Simple and Effective Method:** The proposed method is elegant in its simplicity. By defining a target attention distribution and adding a straightforward squared-error loss term, the authors cleanly integrate the supervisory signal without overly complicating the model architecture. The empirical results strongly validate this approach. The substantial F1-score improvement over the unsupervised attention baseline (ANN) from 64.6 to 71.7 (for ANN-S2) demonstrates that the supervised signal is highly effective. Furthermore, achieving a new state-of-the-art result on the ACE 2005 benchmark confirms the method's practical value.

4.  **Strong Supporting Analysis:** The experiments in Section 4.2 ("Correctness of Our Assumption") are a key strength. By comparing against a model that focuses attention on all entities (ANN-ENT) versus models with gold argument information (ANN-Gold1/2), the authors provide compelling evidence that the performance gain comes specifically from *argument* information, not just general entity information. This level of careful ablation strengthens the paper's central claim significantly.

### Weaknesses

While I am very enthusiastic about this paper, there are a few points that could be considered limitations or areas for future exploration. I view these as minor in the face of the paper's novelty.

1.  **Dependence on Full Event Annotations:** The proposed method requires gold-standard argument annotations during the training phase to generate the supervisory signal. This implicitly ties the ED training process to the availability of a corpus fully annotated for both triggers and arguments. While this is also true for joint models, it limits the method's direct applicability to settings where only trigger-level annotations might be available, potentially restricting its use on more lightly annotated datasets.

2.  **Heuristic Nature of "Gold Attention":** The two strategies for constructing the gold attention vectors (S1 and S2) are reasonable heuristics, but they are ultimately artificial constructs. There is little theoretical grounding for why a binary distribution or a Gaussian-smoothed one is the optimal way to represent the influence of arguments. While these strategies demonstrably work, the paper could benefit from a brief discussion of this limitation or potential alternatives. However, for a foundational paper introducing a new concept, this kind of heuristic approach is an acceptable and necessary first step.

3.  **Modest Gains from FrameNet Augmentation:** The section on augmenting the training data with FrameNet feels somewhat less impactful than the core contribution. The performance gains are marginal (e.g., 71.7 to 71.9 F1 for ANN-S2), and the process of adapting the FrameNet data (using an external tool for entities, selecting only core frame elements) introduces several confounding variables. While interesting, this part of the evaluation does not add as much weight to the paper's central thesis as the experiments on the original ACE data do.

### Questions to Authors

1.  The trade-off hyperparameter λ is set to 1.0 for the S1 strategy and 5.0 for the S2 strategy. This is a notable difference. Could you provide some intuition for why the model requires a much stronger weight on the attention loss for the "blurry" Gaussian supervision (S2) compared to the "sharp" binary supervision (S1)? Does this suggest anything about the relative difficulty of learning these two types of distributions?

2.  Could you provide any qualitative examples or error analysis? It would be particularly insightful to see an example where the unsupervised attention mechanism fails but the supervised one succeeds. Conversely, are there cases where focusing heavily on arguments (as enforced by your supervision) might be misleading and cause errors that a more flexible, unsupervised attention might have avoided?
