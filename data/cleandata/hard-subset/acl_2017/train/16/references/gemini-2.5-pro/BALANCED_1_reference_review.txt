### Summary and Contributions

This paper proposes a method to improve Event Detection (ED) by explicitly leveraging event argument information from the training data. The authors argue that previous pipeline methods ignore this information, while joint models use it ineffectively for the ED subtask. The core of their proposal is a neural network with a supervised attention mechanism. During training, this mechanism is explicitly guided via an auxiliary loss function to focus on the annotated event arguments in the context of a trigger candidate. This forces the model to learn the importance of argument-like words for identifying and classifying event triggers. The authors experiment with two strategies for creating this "gold" attention signal. Their method achieves state-of-the-art results on the standard ACE 2005 benchmark, demonstrating the value of directly incorporating argument information into the ED task.

As I see them, the main contributions are:

1.  **A novel and effective method for incorporating argument information into Event Detection via supervised attention.** This is a clever approach that directly addresses a well-motivated weakness in prior work. By treating the attention weights as trainable parameters with their own supervision signal derived from argument annotations, the model explicitly learns to focus on the most salient parts of the context for ED.

2.  **A thorough empirical validation that demonstrates the effectiveness of the proposed technique.** The paper presents strong results, outperforming several competitive baselines and achieving a new state-of-the-art on the ACE 2005 dataset. The experiments are well-designed, including an analysis that validates the core assumption about the importance of argument information, and a clear ablation between unsupervised and supervised attention that isolates the gains from the proposed method.

### Strengths

1.  **Clear Motivation and Intuitive Approach:** The paper is built on a strong and well-explained intuition: event arguments provide crucial context for disambiguating event triggers (e.g., "fired" as `End-Position` vs. `Attack`). The analysis of why joint models often fail to leverage this for ED is insightful and provides a compelling justification for the proposed method. The idea of supervising the attention mechanism is an elegant and direct solution to this problem.

2.  **Strong Empirical Performance:** The proposed model, particularly ANN-S2, achieves state-of-the-art performance on the ACE 2005 dataset, surpassing previous feature-based, representation-based, and joint models. An F1 score of 71.7 (and 71.9 with FrameNet augmentation) is a solid improvement. The substantial gain in precision over many prior methods is also a significant result, suggesting the model is more confident in its predictions.

3.  **Systematic and Convincing Experimental Design:** The evaluation is thorough and builds a strong case for the method. The "Correctness of Our Assumption" section (4.2), which uses oracle gold attentions, provides an excellent upper-bound analysis and confirms that access to argument locations is highly beneficial. The direct comparison between the unsupervised `ANN` model and the supervised `ANN-S1`/`ANN-S2` models clearly demonstrates that the performance gain comes from the proposed supervision technique, not just the base architecture.

4.  **Clarity and Readability:** The paper is well-written, clearly structured, and easy to follow. The model architecture, training objective, and experimental setup are explained in sufficient detail. Figure 1 provides a helpful overview of the model.

### Weaknesses

1.  **Potential Mismatch Between Training and Inference Signals:** The model is trained to rely on gold argument locations, but at test time, it must generalize to identify salient context for unseen sentences without such guidance. While the strong results suggest this generalization is successful, the paper lacks a qualitative analysis to show *what* the model has actually learned. Visualizing the learned attention weights on test set examples could demonstrate whether the model successfully focuses on words that are plausible arguments, thereby providing deeper insight into *why* the method works so well and how robust this generalization is.

2.  **Dependence on Richly Annotated Training Data:** The method's core strength is its reliance on argument annotations. This also implies a key limitation: it requires training data where both triggers and their arguments are labeled. This may not be available for all domains or in low-resource scenarios. The FrameNet experiment provides a creative way to generate proxy labels, but a more direct discussion of the method's applicability and potential adaptations for datasets with only trigger annotations would strengthen the paper's contribution.

3.  **Ambiguity in Handling Multi-word Spans:** The process for constructing the gold attention vectors is underspecified for multi-word arguments. Figure 2 only illustrates single-word arguments. For an argument phrase like "his former protege", it is unclear whether all words in the span receive attention, or only the headword. This detail is important for reproducibility and for understanding the precise nature of the supervision signal being provided to the model.

4.  **Limited Architectural Novelty:** The base architecture—an ANN classifier fed by context vectors produced by an attention mechanism—is a relatively standard model for this type of task. The primary novelty is confined to the addition of the supervised loss term for the attention weights. While this is an impactful contribution, the underlying model for representation learning and classification is not novel in itself.

### Questions to Authors

1.  Regarding the construction of the gold attention vectors (`α*`): How are multi-word argument spans handled? For instance, for the argument "his former protege," do all three words receive equal attention under strategy S1, or is there a heuristic to identify a headword?

2.  The performance gap between the unsupervised attention model (ANN, F1=64.6) and the supervised model (ANN-S2, F1=71.7) is substantial. This strongly suggests that the standard unsupervised attention is ineffective for this task and dataset. Could you provide a brief qualitative analysis or visualization of the attention weights from both the unsupervised and supervised models on a development set example? This would help to visually demonstrate what the supervised model learns to focus on that the unsupervised one misses.

3.  In Section 3.1.3, you state that the entity type of the candidate trigger is not used because few triggers are entities. However, the entity type of a word could still be a useful feature (e.g., a `PERSON` is more likely to be an `Elect` trigger than a `Die` trigger). Was this design choice empirically validated on the development set, or was it based on assumption?
