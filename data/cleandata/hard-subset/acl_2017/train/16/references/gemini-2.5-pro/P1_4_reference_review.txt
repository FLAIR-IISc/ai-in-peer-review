### **Summary and Contributions**

This paper addresses the task of Event Detection (ED), which involves identifying and classifying event trigger words. The authors make a compelling case that event arguments, though typically ignored in standalone ED systems, provide crucial clues for disambiguating trigger words. They critique existing joint event extraction models, arguing that these models fail to effectively leverage argument information to improve ED performance, primarily due to data imbalance and one-directional information flow during training.

To address this, the paper proposes a neural network model that explicitly incorporates argument information into the ED task via a supervised attention mechanism. The model is trained to focus on context words that are annotated as arguments for a given trigger candidate. This supervision is achieved by adding a loss term that minimizes the difference between the model's learned attention weights and a pre-defined "gold" attention vector derived from ground-truth argument annotations. Experiments on the ACE 2005 dataset show that this approach significantly outperforms an unsupervised attention baseline and achieves a new state-of-the-art result, demonstrating the effectiveness of explicitly modeling argument information for event detection.

From my perspective, the main contributions of this work are:

1.  **A novel and highly effective method for integrating argument information into the event detection task.** The concept of using supervised attention to force the model to focus on argument locations is intuitive, well-motivated, and empirically powerful. It offers a direct way to leverage argument annotations for ED, a problem that prior joint models have struggled to solve effectively.
2.  **A strong empirical result and a thorough set of experiments.** The proposed model achieves a new state-of-the-art F1 score on the widely-used ACE 2005 benchmark. The authors' experimental setup is robust, featuring a convincing ablation study that validates their core hypothesis and demonstrates the necessity of supervision for the attention mechanism in this task setting.
3.  **An insightful analysis of the limitations of prior joint learning approaches for ED.** The introduction clearly articulates why existing joint models provide minimal benefit to ED, setting a strong foundation for the paper's novel approach and highlighting a subtle but important problem in the event extraction literature.

### **Strengths**

1.  **Excellent Motivation and Intuition:** The paper is exceptionally well-motivated. The introductory example of the ambiguous trigger "fired" clearly illustrates the problem, and the proposed solution of using arguments for disambiguation is a direct and elegant response. This clarity of purpose makes the paper easy to follow and appreciate.
2.  **Strong Empirical Performance:** The proposed method (ANN-S2) achieves an F1 score of 71.7, which surpasses all previously reported results on this specific ACE 2005 data split. The improvement over strong baselines, including both feature-based and recent neural models, is a significant achievement and a clear indicator of the method's effectiveness. Notably, the model achieves a very high precision (78.0), suggesting it learns reliable cues for classification.
3.  **Comprehensive Experimental Validation:** The authors conduct a well-designed series of experiments to support their claims. The analysis in Section 4.2 is particularly compelling; it systematically shows that an unsupervised attention model fails, that gold argument information is highly predictive (ANN-Gold models), and that this benefit comes specifically from arguments rather than just general entity information (comparison to ANN-ENT). This rigor adds significant weight to the paper's conclusions.
4.  **Clarity and Readability:** The paper is well-written, clearly structured, and easy to understand. The model architecture, training procedure, and experimental setup are all described with sufficient detail.

### **Weaknesses**

1.  **Dependence on Argument Annotations:** The method's primary strength—the use of supervised attention—is also a potential limitation. It requires training data to be annotated with both event triggers and their corresponding arguments. This may limit its applicability to datasets that lack argument annotations, which is not uncommon. The paper would be strengthened by a discussion of this limitation and potential avenues for future work, such as using weakly supervised signals or pseudo-arguments from an external system.
2.  **Potential Training-Test Mismatch:** The model is trained using a strong supervision signal (gold argument locations) that is entirely absent at test time. While the results demonstrate that the learned attention mechanism generalizes well, there is no analysis of *how* well it generalizes. An error analysis or a qualitative study showing whether the learned attention at test time correctly focuses on (unseen) arguments would provide deeper insight and further bolster the paper's claims.
3.  **Simplicity of the Base Architecture:** The core classifier is a standard multi-layer perceptron (ANN). While its effectiveness in this paper is undeniable, the field has largely moved towards more complex sequential models (LSTMs, Transformers) that can capture broader sentence context. It is unclear whether the performance gains are solely due to the supervised attention mechanism or if the comparison is against slightly dated architectures. Integrating this supervised attention idea into a more powerful, modern base architecture could be a valuable extension.
4.  **Unconventional Combination of Attention Vectors:** In Section 3.1.3, the final attention vector α is computed by summing two softmax-normalized vectors (`α = αw + αe`). This is an unusual operation, as the resulting vector's elements will no longer sum to one. While it may function empirically, the motivation is unclear compared to more standard fusion techniques like a learned gate or concatenation. This detail could use further justification.

### **Questions to Authors**

1.  The final attention vector `α` is calculated by summing two probability distributions (`αw` and `αe`). Could you clarify the reasoning behind this choice over other methods for combining information, such as a weighted average, a gating mechanism, or concatenating the context representations derived from each attention vector?
2.  Your model's performance gain seems to stem from the high-quality supervision provided by gold argument annotations. The experiments with additional FrameNet data showed only a minor F1 improvement (71.7 to 71.9 for ANN-S2). Does this suggest that the quality of the supervision signal is much more critical than the sheer quantity of training instances for this task? How do you think your model would perform if trained with noisy, automatically-generated argument labels instead of gold ones?
3.  Have you performed any analysis on the test set to examine where the learned attention mechanism focuses? It would be very insightful to see if the model successfully learns to attend to words that are (unseen) arguments, especially in cases with ambiguous triggers like the "fired" example from the introduction. This could provide strong qualitative evidence for your method's effectiveness.
