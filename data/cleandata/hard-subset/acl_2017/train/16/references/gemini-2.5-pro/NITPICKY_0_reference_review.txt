**Summary of the Paper**
This paper proposes a method to improve event detection (ED) by explicitly leveraging annotated argument information from the training data. The authors argue that previous joint event extraction models failed to effectively use argument information to benefit the ED subtask. To address this, they introduce a neural network model with a supervised attention mechanism. The core idea is to train the model's attention layer to focus on words that are likely to be event arguments. This is achieved by defining a "gold" attention vector for each training instance based on the locations of annotated arguments and adding a loss term that minimizes the difference between the model's predicted attention and this gold standard. The authors evaluate two strategies for constructing these gold attentions and demonstrate that their method achieves a new state-of-the-art result on the ACE 2005 dataset.

**Contributions**
1.  The primary contribution is the novel application of supervised attention for the task of event detection. By creating a gold-standard attention distribution from argument annotations, the method provides a direct and explicit signal to guide the model's focus, which is a departure from previous joint models where such guidance was implicit and, as the authors argue, ineffective.
2.  The paper provides a thorough empirical analysis, including an oracle study to demonstrate the potential of using argument information, an ablation of different supervision strategies, and experiments with external data augmentation from FrameNet. This analysis validates the effectiveness of the proposed supervised attention mechanism.

**Strengths**
1.  **Well-Motivated and Intuitive Idea:** The paper's core premise is well-argued and compelling. The introduction effectively uses an example ("fired") and data from prior work (Table 1) to build a strong case that argument information is a valuable but underutilized signal for ED. The proposed solution—to directly supervise the model's attention to focus on arguments—is an elegant and intuitive way to address this identified gap.
2.  **Novelty of the Approach:** While attention mechanisms are common, the use of a *supervised* attention mechanism for ED, where the supervision signal is derived from a distinct but related annotation layer (i.e., arguments), is a novel contribution. It presents a new paradigm for integrating multiple sources of information in extraction tasks.
3.  **Thorough Experimental Setup:** The authors perform a series of well-designed experiments. The inclusion of an oracle experiment (Section 4.2), while needing better framing, successfully establishes an upper bound for the utility of argument information. The comparison between the two supervision strategies (S1 and S2) provides insight into different ways of modeling the "gold" attention. Finally, augmenting the training data with FrameNet demonstrates the model's robustness and potential for further gains.

**Weaknesses**
1.  **Critical Methodological Flaws and Ambiguities:** The proposed approach has several unclear or questionable components.
    *   The combination of the word and entity attention vectors via simple addition (`α = α_w + α_e`) is mathematically dubious. Since both `α_w` and `α_e` are outputs of separate softmax functions, their element-wise sum `α` will not form a valid probability distribution (i.e., its elements will not sum to 1). This makes the interpretation of `α` as attention weights problematic and calls into question the subsequent weighted sum operations and the supervision loss calculation.
    *   Key details are omitted. In Section 3.1.3, the paper states that `we` is calculated by "transforming w from the word space into the entity type space," but the mechanism for this transformation is never specified.
    *   The construction of gold attention vectors for the majority of cases (negative trigger candidates, or triggers whose arguments are outside the context window) is not clearly defined, which is a critical detail for reproducibility and understanding the model's behavior.

2.  **Weak Baseline and Marginal SOTA Improvement:** The main baseline, `ANN` (unsupervised attention), performs very poorly (64.6 F1). This is significantly lower than other contemporary neural models like Chen et al. (2015), which reported 69.1 F1. This weak baseline exaggerates the gains achieved by the proposed supervised models. The final state-of-the-art result (71.7 F1) represents only a marginal 0.4 F1 improvement over the previous best (Skip-CNN), and the claim of statistical significance (p < 0.05) is difficult to assess without knowing the variance of the results.

3.  **Misleading Framing of "Oracle" Experiment:** The experiment in Section 4.2 ("Correctness of Our Assumption") is presented as a validation of the paper's core assumption. However, it uses gold argument information at *test time* (`ANN-Gold1/2`), making it an oracle experiment. While useful for showing the theoretical potential of perfect argument information, it does not validate that the proposed *learning method* can effectively capture and use this information from training data. The section's conclusion that "argument information is very useful for this task" is trivial; the real question is whether their model can learn it.

4.  **Poor Presentation and Numerous Formatting Errors:** The paper suffers from a lack of polish that detracts from its scientific contribution. There are numerous typos ("exiting methods," "Ngyuen"), grammatical errors, and awkward phrases ("gives us an enlightenment"). The mathematical notation for the context window in Section 3.1 is confusing and likely incorrect. Most critically, the entire References section is unformatted and appears to be a raw copy-paste from a BibTeX file, with stray LaTeX commands, misplaced punctuation, and inconsistent formatting. This level of carelessness is unacceptable for a top-tier conference submission.

**Questions to Authors**
1.  Could you please justify the decision to combine attention vectors via element-wise addition (`α = α_w + α_e`)? Have you experimented with other fusion methods that produce a valid probability distribution, such as a learned gate or simply using one of the attention vectors?
2.  Your unsupervised attention baseline `ANN` achieves an F1 score of 64.6, which is substantially lower than other neural ED methods from the same time period. Could you please elaborate on why this baseline is so weak? Does this suggest a fundamental issue with the unsupervised attention component of your architecture?
3.  How exactly are gold attention vectors (`α*`) constructed for the vast majority of training instances where the candidate token is not a true event trigger? Is `α*` a zero vector, a uniform vector, or something else? Clarity on this point is essential.
