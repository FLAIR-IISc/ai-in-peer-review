### Summary of the Paper
This paper tackles the task of Event Detection (ED), which involves identifying and classifying event trigger words. The authors make a compelling observation that most prior work, including sophisticated joint models for event and argument extraction, fails to effectively leverage argument information to improve the ED subtask. They argue this is because existing joint methods either have imbalanced loss functions or do not use argument information when predicting trigger candidates.

To address this, the paper introduces a novel approach that explicitly uses annotated arguments to guide the learning process for ED. The core contribution is a **supervised attention mechanism**. Instead of allowing a neural network's attention layer to be trained implicitly via the main classification loss, the authors add a direct supervisory signal. They construct "gold" attention vectors from the ground-truth argument locations in the training data and add a loss term that encourages the model's learned attention to mimic these gold distributions. The method is tested on the ACE 2005 dataset, where it significantly outperforms an unsupervised attention baseline and achieves new state-of-the-art results, demonstrating that explicitly modeling the link between arguments and triggers is highly effective for ED.

### Main Contributions
1.  **A Novel Training Paradigm via Supervised Attention:** The primary contribution is the introduction of a supervised attention mechanism for Event Detection. This is a conceptually bold and elegant idea that directly injects knowledge from argument annotations to guide an intermediate component of the neural model. It represents a new and promising direction for leveraging rich, structured annotations that are often ignored or used only indirectly.
2.  **A Clear Diagnosis of a Key Problem in Joint Event Extraction:** The paper provides a sharp analysis of why previous state-of-the-art joint models have failed to improve ED. By identifying the issues of data imbalance and the lack of argument information in the trigger prediction step, the authors provide a strong, evidence-based motivation for their new approach.

### Strengths
1.  **Excellent Novelty and Conceptual Elegance:** The idea of supervising the attention mechanism directly is the paper's greatest strength. In an era of increasingly complex and often inscrutable neural architectures, this work presents a simple, interpretable, and powerful method for injecting explicit, task-specific knowledge into a model. It moves the conversation beyond just *using* attention to *teaching* attention what to focus on. This is a significant conceptual leap that could inspire similar work in other NLP tasks with complex, multi-part annotations.
2.  **Strong and Well-Motivated Problem Formulation:** The authors do an outstanding job of motivating their work. The analysis in the introduction (Table 1) clearly illustrates a real, documented shortcoming in prior research. The paper doesn't just propose a new method; it first diagnoses a problem and then proposes a direct and targeted solution. This makes the contribution feel necessary and impactful, not just incremental.
3.  **Convincing and Thorough Experimental Validation:** The experiments are well-designed and effectively support the paper's claims. The "Correctness of Our Assumption" section (Table 2), using gold argument information at test time, serves as a powerful oracle experiment that validates the core hypothesis: argument information is indeed crucial for ED. The main results (Table 3) not only show a substantial improvement over the unsupervised baseline but also establish a new state-of-the-art, demonstrating practical utility. The high precision of the proposed model is particularly noteworthy.

### Weaknesses
1.  **Heuristic Nature of Gold Attention Construction:** The strategies for creating the gold attention vectors (S1 and S2) are reasonable but heuristic. S1 is a hard binary assignment, and S2 applies a simple Gaussian smoothing. While effective, this simplification misses potential nuances. For example, it treats all arguments as equally important, whereas certain argument roles might be more indicative of an event's type than others. While I see this as a minor flaw and a clear avenue for future work rather than a fatal one, a brief discussion of these limitations would strengthen the paper.
2.  **Dependence on Co-annotated Data:** The proposed method is contingent on the availability of training data where both event triggers and their corresponding arguments are annotated. This is true for ACE 2005 but may not be the case for other ED datasets or in lower-resource scenarios. This practical limitation slightly narrows the immediate applicability of the method, though the underlying concept remains broadly relevant.
3.  **Static, Training-Only Supervision:** The supervision from arguments is only applied during the training phase. At inference, the model relies on the hope that the learned attention patterns will generalize to unseen sentences. The paper does not explore a more dynamic approach where, for instance, predicted arguments from a full extraction system could be used to guide the ED module at test time. This is less a criticism of the current work and more a comment on its positioning as a pipeline-style component rather than a truly integrated joint model.

### Questions to Authors
1.  The hyper-parameter λ, which balances the classification and attention losses, is set to 1.0 for the hard-assignment strategy (S1) and 5.0 for the Gaussian-smoothed strategy (S2). This is a substantial difference. What is your intuition for this? Does the "blurry" signal from S2 require a much stronger push to be learned effectively compared to the clean but sparse signal from S1?
2.  Have you considered incorporating argument *role* information into the gold attention construction? It seems plausible that some roles (e.g., a `Person` argument for a `Die` event) are more informative than others (e.g., a `Place` argument). Could assigning different weights to different roles in the gold attention vector lead to further improvements?
