### Summary and Contributions

This paper addresses the task of Event Detection (ED), focusing on improving trigger identification and classification by leveraging event argument information. The authors' central thesis is that while arguments provide crucial context for disambiguating event triggers, existing joint Event Extraction models fail to effectively channel this information to the ED sub-task. They propose a novel approach that explicitly uses annotated arguments to train a neural ED model via a "supervised attention mechanism." In this framework, "gold" attention distributions are created from argument annotations in the training set, and the model is trained not only to classify the event type but also to replicate these gold attention patterns. The authors demonstrate through extensive experiments on the ACE 2005 dataset that their method significantly outperforms previous state-of-the-art systems.

In my view, the main contributions of this work are:
1.  **A compelling diagnosis of a key limitation in joint event extraction models.** The paper provides a clear and empirically supported argument (Table 1) that existing joint models, despite their theoretical appeal, primarily benefit argument extraction (AE) while offering negligible improvements for ED. This identifies a significant gap in the literature.
2.  **A novel and effective method for injecting argument knowledge into an ED model.** The proposed supervised attention mechanism is an elegant way to force the model to learn the importance of argument-bearing words for trigger classification. The formulation of generating gold attention labels from argument spans is a simple but powerful idea for this task.
3.  **State-of-the-art results on a standard benchmark, backed by strong analysis.** The paper presents a comprehensive evaluation, including comparisons with strong baselines, an "oracle" experiment to validate their core hypothesis, and data augmentation experiments using FrameNet, ultimately achieving a new state-of-the-art F1 score on the ACE 2005 dataset.

### Strengths

1.  **Clear Motivation and Strong Premise:** The paper is exceptionally well-motivated. The "fired" example immediately clarifies the problem, and the analysis of why joint models fail is insightful and convincing. The authors correctly identify that data imbalance and the typical information flow in joint models are likely culprits, providing a solid foundation for their proposed solution.
2.  **Methodological Soundness:** The proposed supervised attention mechanism is intuitive and well-designed. It directly addresses the identified problem by creating an explicit supervisory signal from the very information source they claim is underutilized (arguments). The two proposed strategies for creating gold attentions (S1 and S2) are sensible, and the analysis of their respective impacts on precision and recall is a strong point of the experimental section.
3.  **Thorough and Rigorous Evaluation:** The experimental setup is comprehensive.
    *   The "Correctness of Our Assumption" section (4.2), which uses oracle gold attentions at test time, is an excellent piece of analysis that effectively demonstrates the upper-bound potential of leveraging argument information and validates the paper's core premise.
    *   The comparison against a wide range of state-of-the-art methods, including both feature-based and neural approaches, is robust. The reported gains are significant, and the high precision of the proposed model is a notable advantage.
    *   The inclusion of FrameNet augmentation experiments not only pushes performance further but also demonstrates the method's applicability and robustness when dealing with additional, potentially noisy, training data.

### Weaknesses

1.  **Limited Discussion of the Method's Practicality and Relation to Pipelines:** The proposed method relies on gold argument annotations during training. While effective, this sidesteps the full event extraction problem. At inference time, the model has learned a *proxy* for identifying arguments to help ED. An important missing comparison is against a more traditional pipeline: (1) train a baseline argument extractor, (2) run it to generate predicted arguments, and (3) use these predictions as explicit features (e.g., BIO tags) for a downstream ED model. Such a comparison would help situate the contribution more clearly. Is supervised attention simply a more elegant way of feature engineering, or does it capture dependencies that a feature-based pipeline cannot?
2.  **Lack of Qualitative Error Analysis:** The paper presents strong quantitative results but lacks a qualitative analysis of the improvements. The "fired" example is used for motivation, but the paper never confirms whether the proposed model actually solves this specific ambiguity better than the baselines. Including a few examples of correctly classified triggers (that baselines get wrong) and showing the learned attention weights would provide powerful, concrete evidence of the mechanism's success and offer deeper insight into what the model has learned.
3.  **Potentially Weak Unsupervised Baseline:** The unsupervised attention baseline (ANN) performs at 64.6 F1, which is considerably lower than other contemporary neural ED models like Chen et al. (2015) (69.1 F1) and Nguyen & Grishman (2016) (71.3 F1). While architectural differences exist, this large gap raises the concern that the baseline model may be sub-optimally implemented or designed, which could potentially inflate the perceived gains from adding supervised attention. A stronger baseline would make the claims of improvement even more convincing.

### Questions to Authors

1.  In Section 3.1.3, the final attention vector is computed as `α = αw + αe`, an element-wise sum of two separate softmax outputs. This means the resulting vector `α` does not form a valid probability distribution (i.e., its elements do not sum to 1). Could you clarify the motivation for this combination strategy and did you experiment with alternatives, such as a weighted average, a gated combination, or re-normalizing the sum?
2.  Your unsupervised attention baseline (ANN) underperforms other neural ED models from the same era by a significant margin. Could you elaborate on the potential architectural or hyper-parameter choices that might explain this performance gap? Establishing a stronger baseline would further solidify the impressive gains achieved by your supervised method.
3.  The proposed method relies on gold arguments during training. How do you expect the model's performance to be affected if the supervision signal for attention came from a noisy source, such as the output of an automatic argument extraction system? This seems crucial for understanding the method's robustness and potential application in settings without fully annotated data.
