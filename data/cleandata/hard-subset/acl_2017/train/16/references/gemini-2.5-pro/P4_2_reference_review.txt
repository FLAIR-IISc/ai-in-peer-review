### Summary of the Paper
This paper addresses the task of Event Detection (ED), which involves identifying and classifying event trigger words. The authors hypothesize that event arguments, which are typically ignored in ED-only systems or ineffectively utilized in joint event extraction models, provide crucial contextual clues for disambiguating trigger words. To test this, they propose a neural network-based ED model that explicitly leverages argument information through a supervised attention mechanism.

The core of the proposed method is to train the model's attention layer using supervision derived from annotated arguments in the training data. The authors create "gold" attention vectors where words annotated as arguments are assigned high attention scores. The model is then trained with a joint loss function that combines a standard cross-entropy loss for event classification and a squared error loss that encourages the model's learned attention to mimic the "gold" attention. The authors experiment with two strategies for creating these gold attention vectors: one focusing solely on argument words (S1) and another that also gives weight to words in their immediate vicinity (S2). The model is evaluated on the ACE 2005 dataset, where it is shown to outperform previous state-of-the-art methods. The authors also demonstrate that augmenting the training data with automatically processed data from FrameNet further improves performance.

### Contributions
1.  **A Novel Method for Event Detection Using Supervised Attention:** The primary contribution is the proposal of a supervised attention mechanism to explicitly incorporate event argument information into the Event Detection task. This approach directly addresses a well-motivated weakness in prior work, where argument information is either ignored or ineffectively used in joint models. The supervision signal is cleverly constructed from existing argument annotations in the training corpus.
2.  **State-of-the-Art Empirical Results:** The paper presents a comprehensive set of experiments on the widely-used ACE 2005 benchmark. The results demonstrate that the proposed method significantly outperforms previous state-of-the-art systems, establishing a new performance benchmark for the task.
3.  **Analysis of Argument Information Utility:** The paper provides a clear analysis and empirical validation (Section 4.2) of its core hypothesis: that argument information is critical for ED. By comparing models with and without access to this information, the authors provide strong evidence for their approach's design.

### Strengths
1.  **Clear Motivation and Strong Rationale:** The paper is motivated by a well-articulated and insightful observation: existing joint event extraction models provide significant gains for argument extraction (AE) but minimal to no improvement for event detection (ED). The authors provide plausible explanations for this phenomenon (e.g., unbalanced data, indirect use of argument information), which builds a compelling case for their proposed solution of explicitly using argument information for ED.
2.  **Novel and Technically Sound Method:** The use of supervised attention is a novel and elegant way to integrate domain-specific knowledge (the location of arguments) into a neural model. The mechanism is straightforward to implement and is grounded in the clear intuition that arguments are key disambiguating features for triggers. The joint loss function, combining classification and attention supervision, is a technically sound way to train the model.
3.  **Thorough and Convincing Experimental Evaluation:** The experiments are well-designed and systematically validate the paper's claims.
    *   The experiments in Section 4.2 directly test the central hypothesis by comparing against models using "gold" argument information, confirming the utility of this information.
    *   The main results (Table 3) show a significant improvement over both an unsupervised attention baseline and multiple strong, contemporary state-of-the-art systems. The performance gain is substantial, particularly in precision.
    *   The inclusion of experiments with FrameNet augmentation further demonstrates the model's robustness and provides insights into its behavior when faced with data sparsity.

### Weaknesses
1.  **Generalization of the Learned Attention:** The model learns to focus on arguments during training, but at test time, ground-truth arguments are unavailable. The method relies on the assumption that the learned attention mechanism generalizes to focus on words that are likely to be arguments. While the strong results suggest this is happening, the paper lacks any qualitative or quantitative analysis to confirm it. An analysis of the attention weights on test set examples would significantly strengthen the paper by providing insight into what the model has actually learned.
2.  **Simplicity of the Base Architecture:** The event detector is a standard three-layer Artificial Neural Network (ANN). While the focus of the paper is on the novel attention mechanism, it is unclear how this contribution interacts with more powerful, modern architectures like BiLSTMs or CNNs, which are used by some of the baseline systems. It is possible that the performance gains attributed to the supervised attention are partially confounded with the choice of the base classifier. An ablation study applying supervised attention to a stronger backbone would better isolate and demonstrate the impact of the core contribution.
3.  **Dependence on Annotation Quality and Density:** The proposed method is fundamentally dependent on the existence and quality of argument annotations in the training set. The paper does not discuss the limitations of this approach in low-resource scenarios or on datasets where argument annotations might be sparse or non-existent. Furthermore, when augmenting with FrameNet data, arguments and entities are annotated automatically, which introduces noise. The paper acknowledges this hurts precision but does not analyze the sensitivity of the supervised attention mechanism to such noise.

### Questions to Authors
1.  Could you provide a qualitative analysis of the learned attention weights on some test set examples? It would be particularly insightful to see what the model focuses on for both correct and incorrect predictions, as this would help verify that the attention mechanism has successfully generalized to identify salient, argument-like context words without direct supervision at test time.
2.  The base model for your event detector is a feed-forward neural network. Have you considered applying the supervised attention mechanism to more powerful sequential encoders like BiLSTMs, which are common in state-of-the-art NLP models? Would you expect the gains from supervised attention to be additive when combined with a stronger base architecture?
