**Summary and Contributions**

This paper addresses the task of Event Detection (ED), arguing that event argument information, while beneficial, is not effectively utilized for ED in existing joint event extraction models. The authors propose a neural network model that incorporates a supervised attention mechanism. The core idea is to train the attention layer to focus on words that are annotated as event arguments in the training data. This is achieved by defining a "gold" attention distribution based on argument locations and adding a loss term that minimizes the difference between the model's attention and this gold distribution. The authors show that this approach achieves state-of-the-art performance on the ACE 2005 dataset.

From my perspective, the main contributions of this work are:
1.  The novel proposal to use argument annotations as direct supervision for an attention mechanism in an ED model. This provides an explicit and targeted way to leverage argument information for trigger classification.
2.  An empirical demonstration that this method of explicitly integrating argument information leads to significant performance gains in ED, outperforming prior pipeline and joint models.
3.  An analysis of two different strategies for constructing the "gold" attention signal (hard vs. smoothed), providing insights into what form of supervision is most effective.

**Strengths**

1.  **Well-Motivated and Novel Idea:** The paper's primary motivation is clear and compelling. The analysis in the introduction, supported by Table 1, effectively argues that existing joint models fail to leverage argument information for the ED subtask. The proposed solution—using supervised attention—is a direct and clever way to address this identified gap. This is a departure from previous work and introduces a new perspective on how different annotated components in a complex task can be used to guide a model's focus.

2.  **Strong Empirical Results:** The proposed model, particularly ANN-S2, achieves a new state-of-the-art F1 score (71.7) on the widely used ACE 2005 dataset, surpassing previous feature-based, representation-based, and joint models. The further improvement to 71.9 F1 with FrameNet augmentation solidifies this claim. The significant boost in precision over many prior works is also a notable strength.

3.  **Insightful Analysis Experiments:** The authors conduct a useful set of experiments to validate their core hypothesis. The comparison in Table 2 between a model with no supervision (ANN), a model attending to all entities (ANN-ENT), and oracle models using gold arguments (ANN-Gold1/2) provides strong evidence that it is specifically *argument* information, not just entity information, that is crucial. This is a well-designed experiment that isolates the variable of interest and strengthens the paper's central claim.

**Weaknesses**

1.  **Lack of Clarity and Reproducibility:** The description of the model contains several ambiguities and instances of confusing notation that severely hinder reproducibility.
    *   **Equation 1:** The notation `wk = f(wk Ww)` is highly confusing, as `wk` is used for both the input embedding and the output hidden representation.
    *   **Equation 2 & 3:** The attention computation `exp(w wTk )` is non-standard and poorly explained. It appears to be a dot-product attention, but the notation `w wTk` is unclear. It should be written explicitly, e.g., as `exp(h_w^T h_k)`.
    *   **Attention Combination:** The paper states `α = αw+αe`. It is not specified if this is a simple element-wise addition and, critically, whether the resulting vector `α` is re-normalized to sum to one before being used as attention weights. This is a crucial missing detail.
    *   **Equation 4 & 5:** The expression `cw = CwαT` is ambiguous. Given the likely dimensions of the context matrix `Cw` and the attention vector `α`, this should probably be `c_w = C_w^T α`. The current notation is imprecise.
    *   **Context Definition:** The definition of contextual words in Section 3.1 is unclear (`[w−n, w−n 2 +1, ...]`). This seems to contain a typo and should be clarified.

2.  **Weak Baseline and Evaluation Gaps:**
    *   The performance of the unsupervised attention baseline (ANN) is very low (64.6 F1), significantly underperforming other neural ED models from the same period (e.g., Chen et al., 2015, at 69.1 F1). This raises the concern that the base architecture may be suboptimal, and the supervised attention is compensating for an otherwise weak model. The impressive gains might be partially attributable to a poor starting point.
    *   The reporting of the significance test is vague ("we also perform a t-test (p 6 0.05)"). The authors should specify the number of runs, the variance, and the exact p-value. The '6' is also a typo for '<'.
    *   In Table 3, the result for Skip-CNN (2016) is listed with an F1 of "N/A" and a Recall of "71.3". The original paper reports an F1 of 71.3. This is a citation error that suggests a lack of carefulness.

3.  **Major Presentation and Formatting Issues:** The paper suffers from a substantial number of presentation flaws that detract from its quality and professionalism.
    *   **References:** The reference section is completely corrupted. Entries contain BibTeX artifacts (`\.,`), spurious characters (`?`), incorrect formatting (`Ahn.,? 2006, Ahn.,`), and undefined commands (`\Q2010\E`). This is unacceptable for a camera-ready submission and should have been fixed prior to submission.
    *   **Table Formatting:** Table 1 is broken, with text wrapping incorrectly ("join-t methods").
    *   **Typos and Grammar:** The paper contains numerous typos and grammatical errors (e.g., "exiting methods" instead of "existing", "on earth benefits" instead of "actually benefits", "Ngyuen" instead of "Nguyen"). While minor individually, their high frequency suggests a lack of careful proofreading.

**Questions to Authors**

1.  Could you please clarify the ambiguous mathematical notations in Section 3, specifically in Equations 1, 2, 4, 5, and the process of combining `αw` and `αe`? A precise and standard formulation is needed for reproducibility.

2.  Can you provide some justification for the low performance of your unsupervised ANN baseline (64.6 F1) compared to other contemporary neural ED models? Does your architecture have specific properties that make it highly dependent on attention supervision?

3.  Could you please provide more details on your significance testing procedure (e.g., number of runs, standard deviation) to substantiate the claim of statistically significant improvement?
