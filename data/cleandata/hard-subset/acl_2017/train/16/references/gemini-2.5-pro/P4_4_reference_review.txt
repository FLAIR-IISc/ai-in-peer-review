### Summary and Contributions

This paper addresses the task of Event Detection (ED), which involves identifying and classifying event trigger words. The authors hypothesize that event argument information, which is available in annotated training corpora like ACE 2005, is underutilized by existing methods. They observe that pipeline methods ignore it, while joint event extraction models often fail to leverage it effectively to improve ED performance.

To address this, the paper proposes a neural network model for ED that incorporates a supervised attention mechanism. Instead of learning attention weights over context words in an unsupervised manner, the model is explicitly trained to produce attention distributions that match "gold" distributions constructed from annotated event arguments. The final model is trained with a joint loss function that combines the standard cross-entropy loss for trigger classification and a squared error loss to enforce the supervised attention. The authors test their model on the ACE 2005 dataset, showing significant improvements over state-of-the-art systems.

The main contributions of this work are:

1.  A novel method for explicitly incorporating event argument information into an ED model using a supervised attention mechanism. This provides a direct and effective way to leverage argument annotations to disambiguate trigger words during training.
2.  A strong empirical validation on the ACE 2005 dataset, including a well-designed ablation study that clearly justifies the core assumption that argument information is more valuable than general entity information. The proposed method achieves state-of-the-art performance on this benchmark.
3.  An investigation into two distinct strategies for constructing the "gold" attention distributions for supervision (a binary strategy and a Gaussian-smoothed one), providing insights into how to best model the influence of arguments.

### Strengths

1.  **Well-motivated and sound proposal.** The paper's motivation is clear and compelling. The initial analysis in Section 1, which highlights the shortcomings of joint models in improving ED performance, effectively identifies a gap in existing research. The proposed solution—using supervised attention to force the model to focus on arguments—is a direct and logical way to address this identified problem. The technical soundness of this motivation is further reinforced by the experiment in Section 4.2 ("Correctness of Our Assumption"), which provides strong evidence that oracle argument information (ANN-Gold models) dramatically outperforms both unsupervised attention and attention focused only on entities. This establishes a high potential upper bound for the proposed approach.

2.  **Strong empirical results and thorough evaluation.** The proposed model, particularly ANN-S2, achieves a new state-of-the-art F1-score on the standard ACE 2005 test set, outperforming a range of recent and competitive baselines. The improvement from 70.7 F1 (the previous best) to 71.7 F1 is substantial for this task. The experimental setup is thorough, including a comparison against the model's unsupervised counterpart (ANN), which demonstrates the effectiveness of the supervision, and an analysis of different supervision strategies (ANN-S1 vs. ANN-S2). The further augmentation with FrameNet data, which pushes the F1 score to 71.9, showcases the robustness and scalability of the method.

3.  **High clarity and potential for reproducibility.** The paper is well-written, with a logical flow that is easy to follow. The model architecture (Figure 1) and the supervised attention mechanism are described in sufficient detail. The authors are transparent about their experimental setup, including dataset splits and hyper-parameter settings (Section 4.1), which is commendable and should facilitate future attempts to reproduce or build upon this work.

### Weaknesses

1.  **Limited applicability and evaluation of the learned attention.** The method's core strength—reliance on gold argument annotations for supervision—is also a limitation. It is only applicable to datasets where both triggers and their corresponding arguments are annotated. While effective on ACE 2005, its utility on datasets annotated only for triggers is unclear. More importantly, the paper does not evaluate how well the *learned* attention mechanism performs at test time. Since gold arguments are not available during inference, the model's performance relies on its ability to generalize the learned attention patterns. An analysis (e.g., quantitative or qualitative) of whether the model's attention at test time actually highlights true argument words would provide crucial insight into the mechanism's success and its ability to generalize beyond the training signal.

2.  **Unconventional formulation of the attention combination.** In Section 3.1.3, the final attention vector `α` is calculated by adding two separate attention vectors, `αw` and `αe`, which are both outputs of softmax functions. This means `α` is a vector of positive values that sums to 2.0, not a standard probability distribution. This choice of `α = αw + αe` is not justified or compared against more conventional combination methods, such as element-wise averaging, concatenation followed by a projection, or a learned gating mechanism. Without justification, it is unclear whether this specific, unusual formulation is critical to the model's performance or simply an arbitrary design choice that could be improved.

3.  **Confounding factors in the FrameNet augmentation experiment.** The data augmentation experiments in Section 4.4 rely on an external, high-performance joint information extraction system to annotate entities in the FrameNet data. The performance of this system introduces a potential confounding variable. The quality of these automatically generated entity labels is not discussed, nor is their potential impact on the supervised attention mechanism. The improvements seen from FrameNet data could be partially attributed to patterns learned from this powerful external model rather than solely from the augmented event data. This dependency limits the generality of the data augmentation approach to settings where a similarly high-quality tool is available.

### Questions to Authors

1.  Could you provide the rationale for combining the word and entity attention vectors via addition (`α = αw + αe`), as opposed to more standard methods like averaging or a learned gating mechanism? Have you experimented with alternative combination strategies, and if so, how did they perform?

2.  The success of your model hinges on the quality of the learned attention at inference time. Could you provide a quantitative or qualitative analysis of how well the model's predicted attention (`α`) aligns with the actual argument locations in the development or test set? This would help clarify whether the model is learning to identify arguments or if the supervision is acting as a more general form of regularization.

3.  In your FrameNet augmentation experiment, what was the performance of the external RPI system when generating entity labels for the FrameNet sentences? How sensitive is your supervised attention mechanism to noise in these automatically generated entity and argument (i.e., frame element) annotations?
