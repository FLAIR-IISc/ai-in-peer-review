### Summary

This paper proposes a method to improve event detection (ED) by explicitly using event argument annotations from the training data. The authors argue that previous joint models for event detection and argument extraction (AE) have failed to leverage argument information effectively for the ED sub-task. Their proposed solution is a neural network model for ED that incorporates a supervised attention mechanism. During training, "gold" attention vectors are constructed based on the locations of annotated arguments, and the model is trained with a joint loss function that encourages its learned attention weights to mimic these gold attentions, in addition to the standard classification loss for identifying event triggers. The authors test two strategies for creating these gold attentions and show that their best model achieves a new state-of-the-art result on the ACE 2005 dataset.

### Contributions

1.  The primary contribution is the proposal of a supervised attention mechanism for event detection, where the supervision signal is derived directly from annotated event arguments in the training corpus. This provides a direct method for injecting argument information into the ED task, as opposed to indirect methods used in joint models.
2.  The paper provides an empirical analysis demonstrating that this proposed method improves ED performance on the ACE 2005 benchmark, outperforming several strong pipeline and joint-learning baselines from the period.

### Strengths

1.  **Clear Motivation and Problem Analysis:** The paper provides a convincing motivation for its approach. The analysis in the introduction (Table 1), which highlights that prior joint models significantly improved AE but offered negligible gains for ED, is a strong and clear justification for exploring alternative ways to leverage argument information for the ED task.
2.  **Methodological Simplicity:** The proposed method is straightforward and intuitive. It directly translates the core hypothesis—that arguments provide important contextual clues for triggers—into a trainable mechanism. The architecture is based on standard components (ANN, attention), making it relatively easy to understand and reproduce.
3.  **Thorough Empirical Comparison:** The authors compare their method against a comprehensive suite of contemporary state-of-the-art systems, including feature-based, representation-based, and joint models. This provides a solid context for evaluating the performance of the proposed approach. The inclusion of experiments with FrameNet augmentation further strengthens the empirical validation.

### Weaknesses

1.  **Limited Novelty and Heuristic Design:** The core mechanism, "supervised attention," is not a novel concept in itself, having been explored in other areas like NMT (e.g., Mi et al., 2016). The novelty is its application to ED using argument labels. However, the implementation feels somewhat ad-hoc and heuristic. The two strategies for constructing "gold" attention vectors (S1 and S2) are manually designed and lack a strong theoretical grounding. For instance, S1 assigns uniform weight to all arguments, ignoring their potential differences in importance, while S2 relies on a Gaussian distribution with fixed hyperparameters. This approach feels more like a form of engineered supervision rather than a principled, learned mechanism.
2.  **Questionable Comparison Framework and Practicality:** The method relies on the availability of full event argument annotations during training. This makes it fundamentally different from the pipeline baselines it is compared against (e.g., Chen et al., 2015; Liu et al., 2016a), which are designed to operate with only trigger annotations. By using this additional supervision, the proposed model has an inherent advantage. A more rigorous comparison would involve adapting a baseline model to also leverage this argument information, perhaps via a multi-task learning objective, to ensure the comparison is made on equal footing in terms of supervision signals. This reliance also limits the method's applicability to datasets that lack argument annotations.
3.  **Marginal Performance Gains:** While the paper claims state-of-the-art performance, the margin of improvement is small. The best model (ANN-S2) achieves an F1 score of 71.7, a modest improvement over Liu et al. (2016a)'s 70.7 and Nguyen and Grishman (2016)'s 71.3. Given the use of stronger supervision compared to these pipeline methods, this small gain raises questions about the overall impact and efficiency of the proposed technique. The authors report statistical significance, but a more detailed analysis of the results would be beneficial.
4.  **Flawed Ablation/Motivation Study:** The experiment in Section 4.2, intended to demonstrate the "correctness of our assumption," is not a valid ablation. The ANN-Gold1/2 systems use oracle argument information at *test time*. This only confirms the initial intuition that knowing the arguments is helpful, which is the premise of the paper, not a result. It shows an upper bound for a perfect attention mechanism but does not validate the effectiveness of the proposed *learning* procedure. The poor performance of the unsupervised attention baseline (ANN) is attributed to data sparsity, which seems plausible but also highlights the brittleness of the base architecture without the strong supervision signal.

### Questions to Authors

1.  The construction of the gold attention signal seems critical to the method's success. Could you elaborate on the choice of the S1 and S2 strategies? Have you explored other, perhaps more nuanced or data-driven, methods for generating this supervision signal, such as weighting arguments by their role or frequency?
2.  The paper contrasts its approach with joint models. However, the proposed method could also be viewed as a form of multi-task learning (classifying triggers and regressing attention weights). How do you see your approach differing from a more standard MTL setup where an auxiliary loss related to arguments is used, and why is direct attention supervision superior?
3.  How sensitive is the model's performance to the hyperparameter `lambda`, which balances the classification and attention losses? The paper provides the final values used, but a sensitivity analysis would provide valuable insight into the stability and robustness of the joint training procedure.
