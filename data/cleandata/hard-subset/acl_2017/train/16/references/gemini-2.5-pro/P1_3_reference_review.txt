### Paper Summary

This paper addresses the task of Event Detection (ED), which involves identifying and classifying event trigger words. The authors make a key observation that most prior work, including joint models for event extraction, fails to effectively leverage annotated argument information to improve the performance of the ED subtask. They argue this is due to data imbalance and the indirect way information flows from arguments to triggers in joint models.

To address this, the paper proposes a novel approach that explicitly incorporates argument information into a neural ED model using a supervised attention mechanism. The core idea is to train the model's attention layer to focus on words that are annotated as arguments in the training data. The authors define "gold" attention vectors based on argument locations and add a loss term to penalize the difference between the model's learned attention and this gold standard. This forces the model to learn which contextual words (i.e., potential arguments) are most indicative of a particular event type.

The proposed model is evaluated on the ACE 2005 dataset, where it achieves a new state-of-the-art result. The authors also conduct experiments augmenting their training data with information from FrameNet, further improving performance and demonstrating the robustness of their approach.

### Contributions

1.  **A Novel Method for Incorporating Argument Information into ED:** The primary contribution is the proposal of a supervised attention mechanism for event detection. This is a simple yet effective method to directly leverage argument annotations from the training data to guide the ED model, addressing a key limitation of previous pipeline and joint models. The idea of creating a "gold" attention target from structured annotations is insightful and could be applicable to other NLP tasks.

2.  **State-of-the-Art Performance on a Standard Benchmark:** The paper provides strong empirical evidence for its claims, achieving the best-reported F1 score on the widely-used ACE 2005 dataset. The thorough experimental setup, including ablation studies and comparisons against a strong suite of recent models, convincingly demonstrates the effectiveness of the proposed technique.

3.  **Insightful Analysis of Joint Event Extraction Models:** The paper provides a clear and compelling analysis of why existing joint models for event extraction often fail to improve ED performance, despite improving argument extraction. This observation regarding data imbalance and information flow provides strong motivation for their work and is a valuable insight for the community.

### Strengths

1.  **Well-Motivated and Intuitive Approach:** The paper is built on a very clear and intuitive premise: words that serve as arguments provide strong clues for identifying the event trigger. The example of "fired" being disambiguated by the argument "former protege" is an excellent illustration. The proposed solution—directly supervising the model's attention to focus on these arguments—is an elegant and direct way to operationalize this intuition.

2.  **Strong and Thorough Experimental Evaluation:** The experimental design is a major strength.
    *   The experiments in Section 4.2, using "gold" argument information at test time (ANN-Gold1/2), provide a clear upper-bound analysis and effectively prove the assumption that argument information is highly beneficial for ED.
    *   The comparison between the unsupervised attention baseline (ANN) and the supervised versions (ANN-S1/S2) clearly isolates the contribution of the proposed supervision technique, showing a dramatic improvement in F1-score (e.g., 64.6 to 71.7).
    *   The analysis of the two different supervision strategies (S1 and S2) and their trade-offs between precision and recall is insightful.
    *   The inclusion of experiments with FrameNet data further strengthens the paper's conclusions about data sparsity and demonstrates the model's versatility.

3.  **Significant Improvement in Precision:** While the overall F1-score improvement over the previous state-of-the-art is modest, the paper reports a very high precision (e.g., 78.0 for ANN-S2). In many real-world applications of event extraction, high precision is more critical than high recall to avoid populating a knowledge base with incorrect facts. This is a significant practical advantage of the proposed method.

### Weaknesses

1.  **Modest F1-Score Gain Over Strongest Baseline:** The final F1-score of 71.7 for the ANN-S2 model is only a 0.4 point improvement over the Skip-CNN model (71.3). While the authors note this is statistically significant, the absolute gain is small. The paper's narrative of "remarkable gains" is better supported by the comparison to their own unsupervised baseline rather than the prior state-of-the-art. The strength of the result lies more in the novel technique and the precision boost than in a massive leap in the F1 headline number.

2.  **Complexity and Justification of the Attention Mechanism:** The formulation of the final attention vector as `α = αw + αe` (a sum of word-based and entity-based attention) and its subsequent use to weight both the word context matrix (`Cw`) and the entity context matrix (`Ce`) feels somewhat arbitrary and is not well-justified. It is unclear why a simple sum is the best way to combine these signals, or why the same combined attention vector should be applied to two different representations (words and entity types). A simpler architecture, such as learning separate attentions or concatenating the final context vectors, might be more standard and should be discussed or compared against.

3.  **Potential for Information Leak in FrameNet Augmentation:** For the FrameNet experiments, the authors use an external tool (RPI Joint Information Extraction System) to label entities. It is not specified whether this RPI system was trained on any part of the ACE 2005 dataset. If the system was trained on data that overlaps with the ACE test set, this could represent a form of indirect information leak, potentially inflating the reported results for the "+FrameNet" models.

### Questions to Authors

1.  Could you elaborate on the design choice of adding the word and entity attention vectors (`α = αw + αe`) rather than using another fusion method? Have you experimented with alternatives, for instance, concatenating the final context representations (`cw` and `ce`) before feeding them to the event detector?

2.  Regarding the FrameNet augmentation experiments, can you confirm that the RPI system used for entity tagging was not trained on any portion of the ACE 2005 development or test sets? This is crucial for ensuring the fairness of the evaluation.

3.  The two strategies for creating gold attention (S1 and S2) are heuristic. Have you considered more data-driven or role-dependent methods for constructing the gold attention vector? For example, could different argument roles (e.g., `Person` vs. `Place`) receive different weights in the gold supervision signal?
