### Paper Summary and Contributions

This paper addresses the task of Event Detection (ED), focusing on identifying and classifying event triggers. The authors' central premise is that event arguments, while typically used in the separate task of Argument Extraction (AE), provide crucial contextual clues for disambiguating event triggers. They argue that existing joint ED-AE models fail to effectively leverage this information for the ED subtask, primarily due to data imbalance and an asymmetric information flow where triggers help AE but not vice-versa.

To remedy this, the paper proposes a neural network model for ED that explicitly incorporates argument information through a supervised attention mechanism. During training, the model is guided by "gold" attention distributions constructed from the annotated argument locations in the training data. This forces the model to learn to pay more attention to words that are likely to be arguments when classifying a potential trigger. The main contributions of this work are:

1.  **A Novel Supervised Attention Mechanism for Event Detection:** The core contribution is the method of using annotated argument locations to create a supervisory signal for an attention layer. This provides a direct and explicit way to inject knowledge from arguments into the ED task, addressing a well-motivated weakness of prior joint models.
2.  **Strong Empirical Performance and a New State-of-the-Art:** The proposed model (specifically, ANN-S2) achieves a state-of-the-art F1 score of 71.7 on the standard ACE 2005 benchmark, outperforming previous feature-based and representation-based methods.
3.  **Thorough Analysis and Ablation Studies:** The paper provides a compelling analysis of why joint models underperform on ED and conducts well-designed experiments (Table 2) to validate its core assumption—that argument information, not just general entity information, is key to improving ED performance.

### Strengths

1.  **Well-Motivated and Intuitive Approach:** The paper's motivation is exceptionally clear and convincing. The observation that joint models have historically failed to improve ED is insightful, and the reasons provided (data imbalance, one-way information flow) are plausible. The proposed solution—to directly supervise the model's attention using argument locations—is an elegant and intuitive way to force the model to learn the connection between arguments and triggers. The example of "fired" being disambiguated by the argument "former protege" perfectly illustrates the problem and the proposed solution.

2.  **Strong and Significant Empirical Results:** The method achieves a new state-of-the-art F1 score on the widely used ACE 2005 dataset. The improvement over strong contemporary baselines like Liu et al. (2016a) and Nguyen and Grishman (2016) is notable (71.7 vs. 70.7 and 71.3-recall respectively). The high precision of the proposed models (81.4 for ANN-S1 and 78.0 for ANN-S2) is particularly impressive and suggests that the method is effective at reducing ambiguity. The fact that the approach also benefits from additional, noisier data from FrameNet further demonstrates its robustness.

3.  **Excellent Experimental Design and Analysis:** The authors conduct a series of well-designed experiments to validate their claims. The comparison in Table 2 is particularly effective. By comparing the baseline `ANN`, an entity-aware `ANN-ENT`, and two oracle models (`ANN-Gold1/2`), they convincingly demonstrate that it is specifically the *argument* information that provides the performance boost, not just the presence of entities. This isolates the contribution of their core idea effectively. The comparison between the two supervision strategies (S1 and S2) also provides valuable insight into the trade-off between precision and recall.

### Weaknesses

1.  **High Reliance on Annotated Arguments:** The method's primary strength is also its main limitation. The supervised attention mechanism fundamentally requires argument annotations in the training data. This limits its applicability to datasets like ACE 2005 where both triggers and arguments are labeled. The paper does not discuss how this approach would fare on datasets or in scenarios where only trigger annotations are available, which is a common setting for ED. This reliance makes the method less generalizable than approaches that do not depend on argument labels.

2.  **Weakness of the Unsupervised Baseline:** The baseline `ANN` model, which uses unsupervised attention, achieves a surprisingly low F1 score of 64.6. This is significantly lower than other neural ED models from the same time period (e.g., Chen et al., 2015, reported 69.1 F1). This weak baseline performance may exaggerate the gains achieved by the supervised attention mechanism. While the supervised models clearly outperform SOTA, the massive +7.1 F1 point jump from `ANN` to `ANN-S2` might be partially due to the ineffectiveness of the specific unsupervised attention formulation used, rather than purely the benefit of supervision.

3.  **Incomplete Comparison to State-of-the-Art:** In Table 3, the Skip-CNN model (Nguyen and Grishman, 2016) is listed as a top-performing representation-based approach, but only its Recall score (71.3) is reported. Without the Precision and F1 scores, it is difficult to make a direct and fair comparison. While the proposed method's F1 score of 71.7 is very strong, a complete comparison against this key baseline would have strengthened the paper's claims further.

### Questions to Authors

1.  Could you please clarify the performance of the `ANN` baseline? Its F1 score of 64.6 seems low compared to other neural approaches from 2015-2016. Does this reflect a weakness in the underlying model architecture or is it indicative of the difficulty for unsupervised attention to learn meaningful patterns on the ACE dataset without explicit guidance?

2.  The method's reliance on argument annotations for supervision is a potential limitation. Have you considered any strategies to adapt this approach for settings where argument annotations are unavailable or sparse? For instance, could pseudo-arguments generated by a separate, pre-trained argument extraction system be used to create a noisy supervisory signal?

3.  For the sake of a complete comparison, could you provide the Precision and F1 scores for the Skip-CNN (2016) model in Table 3? This would help to more precisely situate your results relative to this important baseline.
