**Summary of the Paper**
This paper addresses the task of Event Detection (ED). The authors argue that event argument information, while available in standard datasets like ACE 2005, is underutilized by existing methods. They observe that pipeline methods for ED typically ignore arguments, and joint models for Event Extraction (EE) often fail to significantly improve ED performance. To address this, they propose a neural network model for ED that incorporates a supervised attention mechanism. The core idea is to create "gold" attention vectors from the ground-truth argument annotations in the training set and use these to explicitly supervise the model's attention layer. This supervision is intended to force the model to focus on context words that are arguments when classifying a candidate trigger. The authors test their method on the ACE 2005 dataset, showing that it outperforms an unsupervised attention baseline and achieves state-of-the-art results compared to several established methods.

**Contributions**
1.  The primary contribution is the application of a supervised attention mechanism to Event Detection, using annotated argument locations as the supervision signal.
2.  The paper presents an empirical study on the ACE 2005 dataset, demonstrating that this approach improves performance over an unsupervised baseline and surpasses prior state-of-the-art results.

**Strengths**
1.  The paper is well-motivated and clearly written. The initial example of the ambiguous trigger "fired" effectively illustrates the potential benefits of leveraging argument information for ED. The analysis of why existing joint models may not significantly boost ED performance is plausible and provides a solid justification for exploring a more direct approach.
2.  The experimental results are strong. The proposed model (ANN-S2) achieves a new state-of-the-art F1 score on the standard ACE 2005 test set, outperforming a range of recent and competitive baselines, including both feature-based and neural methods. The reported gains appear to be statistically significant.
3.  The authors include a useful set of experiments in Section 4.2 ("Correctness of Our Assumption") that use oracle argument information (ANN-Gold1/2). These experiments effectively establish an upper-bound for this type of approach and convincingly demonstrate that access to argument locations is indeed highly beneficial for the task, which validates the paper's core premise.

**Weaknesses**
1.  **Limited Technical Novelty:** The core technical contribution—supervising an attention mechanism with an external signal—is not new. The authors cite Mi et al. (2016) for a similar approach in NMT, and the general concept of using auxiliary supervision on intermediate layers is a well-established technique. While its application to ED using argument information is novel, the method itself can be seen as an incremental adaptation of existing ideas rather than a fundamental innovation.
2.  **Weak Unsupervised Baseline:** The performance of the unsupervised attention baseline (ANN) is 64.6 F1, which is notably lower than several of the contemporary baselines presented in Table 3 (e.g., Liu's PSL at 69.4, Nguyen's joint model at 69.3, Skip-CNN at 71.3). The architecture is a simple 3-layer ANN, which may lack the representational power of the CNN and RNN models it is compared against. This weak baseline significantly inflates the perceived benefit of adding the supervised attention component (a gain of >7 F1 points). The true gain of the supervised attention signal might be more modest if it were integrated into a stronger, state-of-the-art base architecture.
3.  **Heuristic Nature of "Gold" Attention:** The method for constructing the gold attention vectors (S1 and S2) is based on heuristics. The S1 strategy (a binary mask) is quite rigid, and the S2 strategy (Gaussian smoothing) introduces hyper-parameters (window size, µ, σ) that seem arbitrary. This approach treats the mere presence and location of an argument as the key signal, ignoring potentially more important information like the argument's semantic type or its specific role. It is not clear that this simplistic, location-based supervision is the optimal way to inject argument knowledge into the model.
4.  **Practical Limitations and Dependencies:** The proposed method is fundamentally dependent on the availability of argument annotations during training. This limits its direct applicability to datasets that lack this information. While the authors attempt to mitigate this by augmenting with FrameNet data, this requires a non-trivial preprocessing pipeline involving a separate argument/entity extraction system, which introduces its own complexities and potential for noise. The paper does not analyze the model's robustness to noisy or incomplete argument annotations, which is a critical factor for real-world scenarios.
5.  **Unconvincing Comparison with Joint Models:** The paper motivates its approach by criticizing joint models for not improving ED. However, the proposed method is a pipeline system that simply uses AE annotations as features for an ED model. A more direct and compelling comparison would be to integrate the proposed supervised attention loss into a state-of-the-art joint model architecture. This would test whether the explicit supervision signal is superior to the implicit signal learned through joint backpropagation, thereby isolating the contribution of the proposed mechanism from the architectural choice of a pipeline versus a joint model.

**Questions to Authors**
1.  Could you provide more detail on the architecture of the "ANN" baseline? Given its performance is substantially below other neural baselines from 2016, what is the justification for using it as the base model? How does your supervised attention perform when added to a stronger base model, such as the Skip-CNN from Nguyen and Grishman (2016)?
2.  The creation of gold attention vectors seems heuristic. Have you considered alternative formulations? For example, instead of a uniform weight for all arguments, have you explored weighting arguments by their proximity to the trigger, or by the importance of their semantic role (if available)? How sensitive is the S2 model to the choice of Gaussian hyper-parameters?
3.  How does your approach compare conceptually and empirically to a standard multi-task learning setup? One could frame ED and AE as two tasks, and train a model jointly but with a weighted loss function that encourages the model to perform well on ED. This seems like a more standard way to leverage the AE signal without resorting to constructing an artificial "gold" attention map.
