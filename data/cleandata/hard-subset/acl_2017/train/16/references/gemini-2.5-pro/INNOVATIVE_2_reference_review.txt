**Summary and Contributions**
This paper addresses the task of Event Detection (ED), focusing on how to effectively leverage event argument information to improve performance. The authors insightfully observe that prior joint learning models for Event Detection and Argument Extraction (AE) have yielded significant gains for AE but only marginal improvements for ED. They hypothesize this is due to an imbalance in training data and the indirect way argument information is incorporated.

The paper's main contribution is a novel and elegant proposal to use argument information *explicitly* for ED through a **supervised attention mechanism**. Instead of leaving the model to discover the relationship between triggers and arguments implicitly, the authors use the annotated arguments in the training set to construct "gold" attention distributions. These distributions directly supervise the learning of the model's attention layer, forcing it to focus on words that are arguments when classifying a candidate trigger. This reframes the problem from just learning *what* the event is, to learning *where to look* to determine what the event is. A secondary contribution is the thorough analysis motivating this approach and the empirical validation showing it achieves state-of-the-art results on the ACE 2005 benchmark.

**Strengths**

1.  **High Novelty and Conceptual Elegance:** The central idea of using supervised attention is a significant and creative contribution. While attention mechanisms are common, supervising them using annotations from a related sub-task (argument extraction) to benefit the primary task (event detection) is a bold and clever approach. It breaks away from the standard paradigms of either ignoring argument information (pipeline models) or hoping for implicit learning (joint models). This introduces a new, direct way to inject rich, task-specific structural knowledge into a neural network, a concept that could inspire similar work in other structured prediction tasks.

2.  **Strong Motivation and Clear Problem Formulation:** The paper is exceptionally well-motivated. The introduction clearly articulates a specific, observable problem with existing state-of-the-art joint models (Table 1) and provides a compelling, well-reasoned explanation for this phenomenon (unbalanced data, indirect information flow). The "fired" example is perfectly chosen to illustrate how arguments can resolve trigger ambiguity. This strong setup makes the proposed solution feel not just innovative, but necessary.

3.  **Convincing Empirical Validation and Analysis:** The paper achieves a new state-of-the-art F1 score on the widely-used ACE 2005 dataset, significantly outperforming previous methods. More importantly, the experiments are well-designed to support the central claim. The analysis in Section 4.2 ("Correctness of Our Assumption") is particularly strong, using oracle experiments (ANN-Gold1/2) and a targeted baseline (ANN-ENT) to convincingly demonstrate that it is *argument* information, specifically, that provides the crucial benefit, not just entity information in general. The high precision achieved by the models (especially ANN-S1) further validates the hypothesis that arguments provide precise, disambiguating clues.

**Weaknesses**

1.  **Indirectness of the Mechanism at Inference Time:** The primary conceptual weakness is that the supervision signal exists only during training. At test time, when gold arguments are unavailable, the model relies on the hope that the learned attention mechanism has generalized successfully to identify salient argument-like words on its own. The paper demonstrates *that* it works (via improved F1 scores), but provides no analysis of *how* it works at inference. Does the learned attention mechanism on test data actually highlight the true (but unannotated) arguments? An analysis of the learned attention weights on test examples would significantly strengthen the claim that the model is truly exploiting argument-like information, rather than just learning a better-regularized representation.

2.  **Weakness of the Unsupervised Baseline:** The base model (ANN), which uses an unsupervised attention mechanism, performs very poorly (64.6 F1), lagging far behind contemporary state-of-the-art systems from 2016 which were already achieving ~70-71 F1. While the final supervised models (ANN-S1/S2) do surpass these competitors, the massive performance gap between the paper's baseline and its final models (e.g., 64.6 to 71.7) might be partially attributed to the weakness of the unsupervised attention baseline itself, rather than purely the benefit of supervision. Applying the supervised attention technique to a stronger base architecture (e.g., the Bi-RNN or CNNs used by the SOTA methods they compare against) would provide a more robust measure of its specific contribution.

3.  **FrameNet Augmentation Is Underdeveloped:** The section on augmenting the data with FrameNet feels somewhat peripheral. The process for annotating the required argument and entity information is reliant on heuristics (using only core frame elements) and an external tool which itself is a joint model of the type the paper initially critiques. Furthermore, the resulting performance gain is minimal (e.g., 71.7 to 71.9 for ANN-S2). This section adds little to the paper's core thesis and could have been omitted or replaced with deeper analysis of the primary model.

**Questions to Authors**

1.  Could you provide a qualitative analysis of the learned attention weights on a few representative examples from the test set? Specifically, for ambiguous triggers like "fired", does the model learn to place high attention on words that are syntactically and semantically plausible arguments, even without direct supervision?

2.  The performance of your unsupervised attention baseline ("ANN") is substantially lower than other contemporary neural models. This suggests that the attention mechanism struggles without supervision. Have you considered applying your supervised attention loss to a stronger base model, such as the Bi-RNN from Nguyen et al. (2016)? This would help clarify if the supervised attention provides a consistent boost on top of any architecture, or if it is primarily effective at rescuing a weaker base model.
