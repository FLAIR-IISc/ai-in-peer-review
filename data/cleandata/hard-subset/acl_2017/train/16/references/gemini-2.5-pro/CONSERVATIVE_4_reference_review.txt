### Summary and Contributions

This paper addresses the task of Event Detection (ED), focusing on identifying and classifying event triggers. The authors observe that existing pipeline methods ignore valuable information from event arguments, while joint models for event detection and argument extraction (AE) often improve AE but provide negligible benefits to ED. The central hypothesis is that explicitly leveraging argument information can significantly improve ED, especially for ambiguous triggers.

The main contribution is a novel method for incorporating argument information into an ED model via a supervised attention mechanism. During training, the model's attention scores over the context words are explicitly guided towards "gold" attention distributions derived from annotated argument locations. This is achieved through a joint loss function that combines the standard classification loss for ED with a square error loss between the model's attention and the gold attention. The authors propose and evaluate two strategies for constructing these gold attentions. Experiments on the ACE 2005 dataset show that this approach achieves a new state-of-the-art result, outperforming previous pipeline and joint methods.

### Strengths

1.  **Well-motivated and Intuitive Approach:** The paper's motivation is clear and compelling. The analysis in the introduction (Table 1), which highlights the failure of joint models to improve ED, effectively frames the problem. The core idea that event arguments provide crucial context for disambiguating trigger words (e.g., "fired" as *End-Position* vs. *Attack*) is highly intuitive and well-supported by the provided example. This provides a strong foundation for the proposed method.

2.  **Strong Empirical Results:** The proposed method (ANN-S2) achieves a state-of-the-art F1 score of 71.7 on the standard ACE 2005 test set, surpassing several strong, recently published baselines. The improvement over the authors' own unsupervised baseline is substantial (+7.1 F1), and the gains over prior work are statistically significant. The high precision (78.0%) is also a notable advantage over many competing methods. The further gains achieved by augmenting with FrameNet data reinforce the robustness of the approach.

3.  **Thorough Experimental Analysis:** The authors provide a dedicated section (4.2) to validate their core assumption. The comparison between a standard unsupervised attention model (ANN), a model focusing on all entities (ANN-ENT), and oracle models using gold argument information at test time (ANN-Gold1/2) provides convincing evidence that it is specifically the *argument* information, not just general entity information, that drives the performance improvement. This rigorous ablation strengthens the paper's claims.

### Weaknesses

1.  **Dependence on Annotated Arguments for Training:** The proposed supervision signal is entirely dependent on the availability of gold-standard argument annotations in the training data. This is a significant practical limitation. While the ACE 2005 corpus provides this, many other event datasets may only contain trigger annotations. The paper does not discuss the applicability of the method in scenarios where argument annotations are unavailable or sparse, which reduces the generality of the proposed technique. The reliance on this specific type of supervision makes it an incremental improvement for a particular data condition rather than a broadly applicable new method.

2.  **Limited Architectural Novelty:** The core model architecture—an attention mechanism feeding into a simple multi-layer ANN—is quite standard. The primary novelty lies in the application of a supervised loss to the attention weights. This concept of supervising an intermediate network component is not new in itself (e.g., Mi et al., 2016, for NMT). While its application to ED using argument information is novel, the work could be strengthened by positioning itself more clearly within this broader paradigm of "supervising the intermediate steps" of a neural network. The contribution feels more like a clever training strategy or a form of regularization for a standard model rather than a fundamentally new architecture for the task.

3.  **Potentially Weak Unsupervised Baseline:** The performance of the unsupervised attention baseline (ANN) is surprisingly low at 64.6 F1. This is considerably below other recent neural methods presented in Table 3, such as Liu's FN-Based approach (70.7) and Nguyen's joint model (69.3). This raises the concern that the underlying base model might be suboptimal for the task, and that the very large gains from supervision are partially due to correcting for the deficiencies of a weak baseline. It is unclear if a more powerful, state-of-the-art base architecture (e.g., using BiLSTMs or more advanced CNNs) would see similarly large gains from the proposed supervision technique. A stronger baseline would make the specific contribution of supervised attention clearer.

### Questions to Authors

1.  The unsupervised ANN baseline performs significantly worse than other contemporary neural approaches. Could you comment on why this might be? Does this suggest that the chosen base architecture is not competitive on its own, and if so, have you considered applying the supervised attention technique to a stronger base model (e.g., one similar to Chen et al. (2015) or Nguyen & Grishman (2016))?

2.  The core contribution relies heavily on the availability of argument annotations during training. How do you see this method being applied in settings with limited or no argument annotations? Would a semi-supervised approach, perhaps using a jointly trained model to generate "pseudo" arguments for supervision, be a viable direction?

3.  For the FrameNet augmentation experiments, the argument and entity labels were generated automatically. This introduces potential noise into the supervision signal for the attention mechanism. Did you analyze the impact of this noise? For instance, the precision of the augmented models drops slightly compared to their non-augmented counterparts (e.g., 81.4 -> 80.1 for ANN-S1). Is this drop attributable to noise from the automatically labeled arguments?
