### **Summary and Contributions**

This paper addresses the task of Event Detection (ED), focusing on improving trigger identification and classification by leveraging event argument information. The authors astutely observe that prior joint models for event extraction, while aiming to model triggers and arguments together, have yielded significant improvements for argument extraction (AE) but only marginal gains for ED. They hypothesize this is due to data imbalance and an indirect flow of information from arguments to triggers.

To rectify this, the paper proposes a novel method to *explicitly* use argument information for ED via a supervised attention mechanism. The core idea is to train a neural network's attention layer not just implicitly through the main classification task, but also directly by supervising it with "gold" attention vectors derived from annotated argument locations in the training data. This forces the model to learn to focus on words that are likely arguments when making a prediction about a trigger candidate. The authors experiment with two strategies for creating these gold attention vectors and demonstrate that their method achieves a new state-of-the-art F1 score on the ACE 2005 dataset.

As I see them, the main contributions are:

1.  **A Novel Training Paradigm for Attention:** The primary contribution is the proposal and successful application of *supervised attention* for Event Detection. This is an elegant method to inject explicit, structured knowledge (i.e., the location of arguments) into a neural model, directly guiding the learning process of the attention mechanism. It presents a compelling alternative to more complex joint inference models for channeling information between related subtasks.
2.  **Strong Empirical Results and Analysis:** The paper achieves a new state-of-the-art result on a standard benchmark (ACE 2005), which is a significant accomplishment. The authors provide a thorough experimental evaluation, including crucial "oracle" experiments (ANN-Gold1/2) that validate their core hypothesis and well-designed ablations that demonstrate the effectiveness of their specific method over an unsupervised baseline.
3.  **Insightful Motivation:** The paper provides a clear and convincing analysis of a key problem in the field: the failure of joint models to substantially benefit the ED subtask. Their diagnosis of data imbalance and indirect information flow provides strong motivation for their focused approach.

### **Strengths**

1.  **Well-Motivated and Elegant Method:** The core idea is both intuitive and novel for this task. The problem formulation is excellent; the paper clearly identifies a shortcoming in existing literature (joint models not helping ED) and proposes a direct solution. Supervising the attention mechanism is a more targeted and potentially more efficient way to leverage argument information than full joint modeling, which can be hampered by complexity and optimization challenges.
2.  **Convincing Experimental Validation:** The experimental design is very strong. The inclusion of the ANN-Gold systems, which use ground-truth argument locations at test time, provides a clear upper-bound analysis and strongly supports the central claim that argument information is highly beneficial for ED. The significant performance gap between the unsupervised baseline (ANN) and the supervised versions (ANN-S1/S2) provides compelling evidence for the proposed method's efficacy.
3.  **State-of-the-Art Performance:** The model (ANN-S2) surpasses previous state-of-the-art systems, including both pipeline and joint models. The F1-score improvement is meaningful, and the particularly high precision achieved by the ANN-S1 model suggests that this method is very good at leveraging high-quality evidence to reduce false positives.
4.  **Clarity and Readability:** The paper is very well-written and easy to follow. The motivation is laid out clearly in the introduction with a perfect illustrative example ("fired"). The methodology is described logically, and the experiments are presented in a way that builds a convincing narrative.

### **Weaknesses**

1.  **Dependence on Argument Annotations:** The primary weakness of this approach is its reliance on argument annotations during training. This limits its applicability to richly annotated corpora like ACE 2005. It cannot be directly applied to datasets where only event triggers are annotated. The paper does not discuss this significant limitation or explore potential workarounds, such as using a pre-trained argument extraction system to generate noisy "pseudo-gold" attention labels for training.
2.  **Underperforming Unsupervised Baseline:** The unsupervised attention baseline (ANN) performs surprisingly poorly, achieving only a 64.6 F1 score. This is substantially lower than several other neural network-based ED models from the same time period that did not use argument information (e.g., Chen et al. 2015 reports 69.1, Nguyen's joint model 69.3). This raises a concern that the base model architecture may be suboptimal. While the gains from supervised attention are still clear, the low performance of the baseline may exaggerate the absolute effectiveness of the proposed technique compared to a stronger base model.
3.  **Unclear Attention Combination Method:** In Section 3.1.3, the authors state that the final attention vector is calculated as `α = αw + αe`. This is a methodologically questionable step. Attention vectors are typically probability distributions that sum to 1. Adding two such vectors results in a vector whose elements no longer sum to 1, and its interpretation as a set of weights is unclear. The paper does not specify if a re-normalization step (e.g., softmax) is applied after the addition. This ambiguity about a core model detail is a notable weakness.
4.  **Limited Architectural Novelty:** While the training method is novel, the underlying neural network architecture itself is fairly standard for classification tasks (embedding layers, attention-weighted context vector, ANN classifier). The main contribution lies squarely in the *supervision signal* for the attention, not in the model architecture. This is a minor point but worth noting.

### **Questions to Authors**

1.  Could you please clarify the poor performance of your unsupervised ANN baseline (64.6 F1), which is several points below other contemporary neural ED systems? Does this indicate that the base architecture is not fully competitive, and if so, how might the gains from supervised attention translate to a stronger base model?
2.  Regarding the combination of word and entity attentions, `α = αw + αe`, could you elaborate on this operation? Is the resulting vector `α` re-normalized to form a valid probability distribution before being used to compute the context representations in equations (4) and (5)? If not, what is the justification for this additive approach?
3.  The main limitation of this work is its requirement for argument-annotated training data. Have you considered how this method might be adapted for settings where only trigger annotations are available? For instance, would it be feasible to use an off-the-shelf argument extractor to generate pseudo-gold attention labels, and how do you think the noise from such a system would impact performance?
