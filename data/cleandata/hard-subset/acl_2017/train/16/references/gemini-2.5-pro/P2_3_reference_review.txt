### **Summary and Contributions**

This paper addresses the task of Event Detection (ED), which involves identifying and classifying event trigger words. The authors make a key observation: while event arguments contain crucial information for disambiguating trigger words, existing joint models for Event Detection and Argument Extraction (AE) have largely failed to leverage this information to improve ED performance. They hypothesize this is due to data imbalance and model architectures that don't directly feed argument information into the trigger detection component.

To remedy this, the paper proposes a novel approach that explicitly uses argument annotations to improve ED. The core contribution is a **supervised attention mechanism** within a neural network classifier. During training, this mechanism is explicitly taught to focus on annotated argument words by adding a loss term that minimizes the difference between the model's learned attention weights and a pre-defined "gold" attention vector derived from the ground-truth arguments. The paper explores two strategies for creating these gold vectors. The second key contribution is achieving **state-of-the-art performance** on the standard ACE 2005 benchmark, demonstrating the empirical effectiveness of this focused supervision.

### **Strengths**

1.  **Clear Motivation and Intuitive Premise:** The paper is exceptionally well-motivated. The introduction clearly identifies a plausible gap in existing research: joint models improve AE but not ED. The example of the trigger "fired" (End-Position vs. Attack) being disambiguated by the argument "former protege" is compelling and makes the core idea intuitive and easy to grasp. This clear problem statement makes the proposed solution feel necessary and logical.

2.  **Novel and Elegant Method:** The use of supervised attention to inject knowledge from a related annotation layer (arguments) into the primary task (trigger detection) is a clever and novel technique. Instead of a complex joint inference model, the authors propose a simple and elegant solution: directly guiding the model's focus using a dedicated loss function. This form of targeted supervision is a valuable contribution, offering a new perspective on how to exploit rich annotations that might otherwise be ignored in a pipeline setting. The two proposed strategies for constructing gold attention vectors (S1 and S2) are sensible and well-explained.

3.  **Thorough and Convincing Experimental Evaluation:** The experimental setup is strong and provides compelling evidence for the paper's claims.
    *   The "Correctness of Our Assumption" section (4.2) is a very effective ablation-style study. By showing that using gold argument information directly at test time (ANN-Gold1/2) drastically improves performance, the authors convincingly demonstrate the value of argument information for ED.
    *   The main results (Table 3) show a significant improvement over their baseline and achieve a new state-of-the-art F1 score, outperforming several strong recent systems.
    *   The inclusion of experiments with FrameNet data augmentation further validates the robustness of the approach, showing that it also benefits from additional, albeit noisy, training data.

4.  **Clarity of Presentation:** The paper is well-written, well-structured, and easy to follow. The model architecture is described clearly, and the figures effectively illustrate the key concepts (e.g., Figure 1 for the model, Figure 2 for the gold attention construction).

### **Weaknesses**

1.  **Very Weak Unsupervised Baseline:** My main concern is the surprisingly poor performance of the `ANN` baseline (64.6 F1). This score is significantly lower than most of the state-of-the-art systems it is compared against, including older feature-based methods and other neural approaches. For instance, Liu et al. (2016b) reported 69.4 F1, and Nguyen et al. (2016) reported 69.3 F1. The authors attribute this to data sparseness being insufficient for unsupervised attention, but other neural models seem to cope better. A weak baseline can inflate the perceived gains of the proposed method. It is difficult to ascertain whether the large improvement comes solely from the novel supervised attention, or if the supervision is compensating for a suboptimal base architecture.

2.  **Limited Practicality due to Supervision Requirement:** The method's reliance on gold-standard argument annotations during training is a significant limitation. This restricts its applicability to datasets like ACE 2005 that are dual-annotated for both triggers and arguments. It cannot be directly applied to new domains or datasets where only trigger information is available. While this is a valid research setup for demonstrating the principle, the paper would be stronger if it discussed this limitation and potential ways to mitigate it (e.g., using arguments from a pre-trained AE system to generate weak supervision).

3.  **Potentially Suboptimal Context Representation:** The Context Representation Learning component models the context as a weighted sum of word and entity embeddings (`cw` and `ce`). This approach collapses the entire context into single vectors, losing all positional information of the attended-to words relative to the trigger candidate. More sophisticated context encoders, such as BiLSTMs or CNNs, are standard in NLP and preserve this sequential information, which is often crucial. This simple "bag-of-words" style attention representation might be another reason for the weak baseline performance.

### **Questions to Authors**

1.  The performance of your `ANN` baseline is considerably lower than prior neural approaches. Could you please provide more analysis on why this might be? Have you considered strengthening the baseline with a more standard sequential encoder like a BiLSTM over the context to ensure the performance gains are robustly attributable to the supervised attention mechanism itself?

2.  The method's requirement for argument annotations during training limits its applicability. Do you have any thoughts on how this approach could be adapted for settings where such annotations are not available? For instance, have you considered a semi-supervised or iterative approach where a model's own argument predictions are used to create "pseudo-gold" attention vectors in a subsequent training pass?

3.  Could you clarify the final input vector to the ANN detector? Based on Figure 1, it appears to be a concatenation of the candidate trigger embedding and the two context vectors (`cw`, `ce`). Was this design choice (a weighted sum that loses positional information) deliberate? How does it compare to attention mechanisms that modulate the hidden states of a recurrent network?
