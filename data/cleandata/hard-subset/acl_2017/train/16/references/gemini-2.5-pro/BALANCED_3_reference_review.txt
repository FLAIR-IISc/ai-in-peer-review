**Summary of the paper**

This paper addresses the task of event detection (ED), which involves identifying and classifying event trigger words. The authors argue that existing methods, particularly joint learning models for event detection and argument extraction (AE), fail to effectively leverage argument information to improve ED performance. To address this, they propose a neural network model that explicitly uses argument annotations from the training data to supervise an attention mechanism. The core idea is to train the model's attention to focus on words that are arguments of a given trigger candidate. This is accomplished by defining a "gold" attention distribution based on the annotated arguments and adding a loss term that encourages the model's learned attention to mimic this gold distribution. The authors experiment with two strategies for creating these gold attentions. Their experiments on the ACE 2005 dataset show that this supervised attention approach significantly outperforms an unsupervised attention baseline and achieves new state-of-the-art results, demonstrating the value of explicitly modeling argument information for event detection.

**Main Contributions**

1.  **A novel application of supervised attention for event detection.** The primary contribution is the clever formulation of a supervised learning signal for an attention mechanism, derived directly from the annotated event arguments in the training data. This provides a direct and effective way to inject task-specific knowledge—that arguments are strong indicators for trigger types—into a neural model, addressing a well-motivated weakness in prior work.
2.  **Strong empirical validation of the proposed method.** The paper presents state-of-the-art results on the standard ACE 2005 benchmark, outperforming a range of competitive feature-based, neural, and joint models. The comprehensive experiments and ablations effectively demonstrate the superiority of the proposed supervised approach over unsupervised attention and other prior methods.
3.  **A clear analysis of the utility of argument information for ED.** The paper provides a convincing analysis, backed by oracle experiments (ANN-Gold), that confirms its central hypothesis: argument information is a powerful and underutilized signal for disambiguating and identifying event triggers.

**Strengths**

1.  **Clear motivation and intuitive approach.** The paper is exceptionally well-motivated. It begins by identifying a clear gap in existing research: joint ED-AE models improve AE but provide negligible benefits to ED. The hypothesis that arguments can disambiguate trigger words (e.g., "fired") is compelling and well-illustrated. The proposed solution—supervising the attention mechanism to focus on arguments—is an elegant and direct method to incorporate this intuition into the model.
2.  **Thorough and convincing experimental evaluation.** The experimental results are strong and well-supported. The authors achieve a new state-of-the-art on ACE 2005, significantly outperforming strong and recent baselines. The evaluation includes several insightful ablation studies. In particular, the experiments in Table 2 provide compelling evidence for the paper's core claim by showing that oracle access to argument locations (ANN-Gold) yields massive performance gains, justifying the effort to learn to predict these locations via supervised attention. The comparison between the unsupervised `ANN` model and the supervised `ANN-S1/S2` models clearly isolates and proves the value of the proposed supervision.
3.  **Good analysis of results.** The authors provide thoughtful analysis of their findings. For instance, the discussion on the precision/recall trade-off between the S1 (hard attention on arguments) and S2 (soft Gaussian attention) strategies is insightful and helps understand the model's behavior. The subsequent experiments involving data augmentation from FrameNet further demonstrate the robustness and potential of the approach, while also highlighting the data-hungry nature of unsupervised attention models.

**Weaknesses**

1.  **Mismatch between training and inference and lack of qualitative analysis.** The model is trained using gold argument locations, but at test time, it must infer where to attend without this information. The paper's claims would be significantly strengthened by a qualitative analysis showing that the learned attention mechanism successfully generalizes. For example, does the model, when making a correct prediction on a test instance, actually place high attention on the (unlabeled) arguments? Visualizing a few attention maps for test set examples would provide crucial evidence that the model is working as hypothesized, rather than using the supervision signal as a mere regularizer that improves performance for other reasons.
2.  **Limited novelty of the core technical components.** While the application is novel and impactful, the individual technical pieces—a feed-forward network for classification, attention mechanisms, and the concept of supervised attention—are well-established. The primary novelty lies in the creative formulation of the supervision signal from argument annotations for the ED task. This is a solid contribution, but the work does not introduce a new model architecture or learning algorithm. The contribution is more in problem formulation and engineering than in fundamental deep learning methodology.
3.  **Unclear details in the model description.** Section 3.1.3, which describes the calculation of the entity attention vector (`αe`), is confusing. The paper states it uses `we`, "calculated by transforming `w` from the word space into the entity type space," as the attention source. It is unclear how this transformation is learned (e.g., via a separate projection matrix) and what the motivation is for this step, especially after arguing that a trigger's own entity type is not useful. A clearer explanation and justification for this design choice are needed.
4.  **Dependence on rich annotations.** The method's success is contingent on having training data annotated with both event triggers and their corresponding arguments. While this is true for ACE 2005, many IE datasets only contain trigger or entity annotations. The paper could benefit from a brief discussion of this limitation and the applicability of the method in settings with less comprehensive annotations.

**Questions to Authors**

1.  To address the potential mismatch between training supervision and inference-time behavior, could you provide a qualitative analysis? For instance, could you visualize the learned attention weights for a few correctly classified test-set examples to demonstrate whether the model is indeed focusing on semantically plausible argument-like words, as your hypothesis suggests?
2.  In Section 3.1.3, could you please clarify the mechanism for transforming the trigger word representation `w` into `we` (a representation in the entity type space)? What is the architecture of this transformation, and what was the rationale for projecting the trigger word into the entity space rather than using its word embedding directly to attend over the context entity embeddings?
3.  Your method achieves excellent results by leveraging argument annotations during training. How do you see your approach being adapted to a more common, low-resource scenario where a dataset might only be annotated with event triggers, but not arguments?
