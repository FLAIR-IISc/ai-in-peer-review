### Review Summary

This paper proposes a novel method for the event detection (ED) task by explicitly leveraging annotated argument information from the training data. The central argument is that existing methods, especially joint models, fail to effectively use arguments to improve ED. The authors introduce a supervised attention mechanism where the model is trained not only to classify event triggers but also to produce attention weights that mimic a "gold" attention distribution derived from the locations of annotated arguments. The model is based on a standard neural classifier that attends over the context of a trigger candidate. Experiments on the ACE 2005 dataset show that this supervised attention approach significantly outperforms an unsupervised attention baseline and achieves a new state-of-the-art result, demonstrating the value of explicitly guiding the model's focus using argument information.

### Main Contributions

1.  **A Novel Supervised Attention Framework for Event Detection:** The primary contribution is the idea of supervising the attention mechanism in a neural ED model using annotated arguments. This provides a direct and effective way to inject knowledge about the relationship between triggers and arguments into the ED task, addressing a well-motivated weakness in prior joint modeling approaches.
2.  **Strong Empirical Validation and State-of-the-Art Results:** The paper presents a thorough empirical evaluation on the ACE 2005 benchmark, establishing a new state-of-the-art. The carefully designed experiments, including insightful ablations and "oracle" tests, provide compelling evidence that explicitly modeling argument information is highly beneficial for ED and that the proposed method is an effective way to do so.

### Strengths

1.  **Clear Motivation and an Elegant Solution:** The paper is exceptionally well-motivated. The introduction clearly articulates a specific and important problem: existing joint EE models improve argument extraction (AE) but provide little to no benefit for ED. The proposed solution is an elegant and intuitive way to tackle this problem directly by forcing the ED model to learn "what to look for" (i.e., arguments) when making a prediction.
2.  **Convincing Experimental Results:** The results are strong and well-supported. The large performance gap between the unsupervised baseline (ANN, F1 64.6) and the supervised models (ANN-S2, F1 71.7) decisively demonstrates the effectiveness of the proposed technique. The model not only improves upon its own baseline but also outperforms a suite of strong, contemporary state-of-the-art systems.
3.  **Thorough Ablation and Analysis:** The authors have done an excellent job of designing experiments to validate their claims. The "oracle" experiments with ANN-Gold1/2 (Section 4.2) provide a clear upper bound and confirm the core hypothesis that argument information is vital. The comparison with ANN-ENT successfully isolates the contribution of arguments over general entity information. This level of detailed analysis strengthens the paper's conclusions significantly.
4.  **High Clarity and Readability:** The paper is very well-written, with a logical flow that is easy to follow. The methodology is explained clearly, and the motivations behind design choices (e.g., the two strategies for constructing gold attentions) are well-justified. This makes the paper's contributions easy to understand and appreciate.

### Weaknesses

1.  **Limited Technical Novelty in the Base Architecture:** While the concept of supervised attention is the key contribution, the underlying model architecture is fairly standard. It consists of a feed-forward network with a conventional attention mechanism. The paper's novelty is therefore concentrated in the training objective and the method of generating the supervision signal, rather than in architectural innovation.
2.  **Dependence on Argument Annotations:** The method's reliance on annotated arguments in the training set constrains its applicability. It cannot be used for ED datasets that lack argument annotations. While the paper focuses on *exploiting* available information, a discussion on the method's limitations and potential adaptations for settings with partial or no argument annotation (e.g., using distant supervision to generate pseudo-arguments) would strengthen its positioning.
3.  **Indirect Comparison to Joint Models:** The paper motivates its approach by highlighting the failures of joint models to improve ED, yet the proposed model is evaluated as a pipeline system. A more direct investigation would be to integrate the supervised attention mechanism into a state-of-the-art joint model (like Nguyen et al., 2016) to see if it can fix the identified issue within that framework. The current setup makes it hard to distinguish whether the improvement comes from the supervised attention itself or from decoupling the ED task from the joint training objective.
4.  **Unexplored Design Choices for Gold Attention:** The two strategies for constructing gold attention vectors (S1 and S2) are sensible heuristics but also simplistic. It is unclear how sensitive the model's performance is to these specific choices. For instance, S1 gives all arguments equal weight, ignoring their type or syntactic distance. S2 uses a fixed Gaussian which may not be optimal. An analysis of alternative, perhaps more data-driven, strategies for creating the gold attention signal would make the contribution more robust.

### Questions to Authors

1.  How does your training procedure handle trigger candidates that are negative examples (i.e., belong to the 'NA' class)? Do these instances contribute to the attention loss term `D(θ)` in the joint loss function? If so, what is their corresponding "gold attention" vector `α*` defined to be?
2.  The final attention vector `α` is a simple sum of the word-level attention `αw` and the entity-level attention `αe`. Did you experiment with alternative combination methods, such as a learned gating mechanism or a weighted average, to potentially better balance the influence of these two information sources?
3.  Given that the unsupervised attention model performs poorly due to data sparsity, have you considered alternative ways to improve it that do not rely on gold arguments? For example, one could pre-train the model on a larger corpus using a self-supervised objective to learn more meaningful attention patterns before fine-tuning on the ED task. This could serve as a stronger baseline and further clarify the unique benefits of supervising with explicit argument structure.
