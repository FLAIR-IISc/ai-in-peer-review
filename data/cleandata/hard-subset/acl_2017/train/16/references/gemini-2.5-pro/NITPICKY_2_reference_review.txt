### Review

**Summary of the paper**

This paper proposes a method to improve event detection (ED) by explicitly leveraging argument information from the training data. The authors argue that previous joint event detection and argument extraction (AE) models have failed to significantly improve ED performance because of data imbalance and the indirect way argument information is used. To address this, they propose a neural network-based ED model that incorporates a supervised attention mechanism. The core idea is to train the model's attention to focus on words that are annotated as event arguments. They define two strategies for creating "gold" attention distributions based on argument locations and add a loss term to the training objective that penalizes the divergence between the model's learned attention and this gold distribution. Experiments on the ACE 2005 dataset show that this method achieves state-of-the-art results, with further gains when augmented with data from FrameNet.

**Main Contributions (as perceived by the reviewer)**

1.  A specific method for supervising an attention mechanism for event detection, where the supervision signal is derived directly from the locations of annotated event arguments. This provides a direct and explicit way to inject argument information into the ED model.
2.  A compelling analysis motivating the work, which highlights a key weakness in existing joint models for ED—that they primarily benefit AE and offer little improvement for ED.
3.  Empirically, the proposed method achieves new state-of-the-art F1 scores on the standard ACE 2005 benchmark for event detection.

**Strengths**

1.  **Well-Motivated and Intuitive Approach:** The paper provides a clear and convincing motivation. The example of the ambiguous trigger "fired" effectively illustrates the potential of argument information for ED. The analysis of why prior joint models fail to improve ED (Section 1, Table 1) is insightful and provides a strong foundation for the proposed work. The core idea of "forcing" the model to attend to arguments is simple and intuitive.
2.  **Strong Empirical Performance:** The proposed model, particularly ANN-S2, achieves a new state-of-the-art F1 score of 71.7 on the ACE 2005 dataset, outperforming a range of strong recent baselines. The reported improvements are shown to be statistically significant. The high precision achieved by the models is also a noteworthy advantage.
3.  **Thorough Experimental Analysis:** The authors conduct a good set of experiments to validate their claims. The comparison in Section 4.2 between the baseline ANN, an entity-aware model (ANN-ENT), and the oracle models (ANN-Gold1/2) provides strong evidence that it is specifically the *argument* information, not just entity information, that is beneficial. The analysis of the precision/recall trade-off between the S1 and S2 supervision strategies is also valuable.

**Weaknesses**

1.  **Significant Lack of Clarity and Precision in Methodology:** The description of the core model architecture is plagued with ambiguities and apparent errors, which raises serious concerns about reproducibility.
    *   **Attention Formulation:** The attention score calculation in Equations (2) and (3) is confusing. The summation index is `i` while the term being summed uses `k` (`exp(w wTk ) / ∑ i exp(w w T i )`). This is a critical typo. It should likely be `exp(w_c^T w_k) / ∑_j exp(w_c^T w_j)`, where `w_c` is the representation of the trigger candidate.
    *   **Contextual Representation:** The weighted sum in Equations (4) and (5) is not well-defined. `Cw` is a matrix of word embeddings and `αT` is a column vector. The matrix-vector product `CwαT` is dimensionally incorrect. A standard formulation would be a sum of embedding vectors weighted by attention scores, e.g., `c_w = ∑_k α_k w_k`. This lack of precision in the model's fundamental equations is a major flaw.
    *   **Attention Combination:** The paper states `α = αw + αe`. This element-wise addition means the resulting vector `α` is no longer a valid probability distribution (i.e., it does not sum to 1). It is not specified whether a re-normalization step is applied. Using an unnormalized vector as weights in a weighted sum is methodologically unsound.

2.  **Unprofessional Presentation and Formatting:** The paper suffers from a startling number of formatting and typographical errors, which detracts significantly from its quality and suggests a lack of careful preparation.
    *   **References:** The bibliography is in a state of disarray. It is riddled with extraneous characters (e.g., "Ahn.,? 2006, Ahn.,"), BibTeX artifacts ("et al\.,?", "\Q2010\E"), and inconsistent formatting. This is well below the standard expected for a submission to a top-tier conference.
    *   **Missing Figures:** Figure 1, which describes the core architecture of the approach, is referenced multiple times throughout the paper but is not included in the provided text. This makes it impossible to fully assess the proposed model. Figure 2 is also referenced but not present.
    *   **Typos and Grammatical Errors:** The text contains numerous errors, such as "exiting methods" (Introduction), "does hardly benefit" (Introduction), "on earth benefits" (Section 4.2), and "p 6 0.05" (Section 4.3). While minor individually, their high frequency is distracting and unprofessional.

3.  **Ambiguity in Experimental Setup:**
    *   The oracle systems `ANN-Gold1` and `ANN-Gold2` are described as using "gold attentions in both training and testing procedure". This implies that ground-truth argument locations are required at test time, which is an unrealistic setting. While useful for establishing an upper bound, this should be explicitly framed as an oracle experiment to avoid misleading the reader about the model's practical capabilities.
    *   When augmenting with FrameNet data, an external system is used to tag entities. The performance of this system is not reported, nor is there any discussion of how errors from this pre-processing step might impact the final results.

**Questions to Authors**

1.  Could you provide the correct and unambiguous mathematical formulations for your attention calculation (Eq. 2), the combination of word and entity attentions, and the final context vector computation (Eq. 4)?
2.  Please clarify the experimental setting for ANN-Gold1/2. Are these intended as oracle experiments that use ground-truth information at test time?
3.  The reference list contains severe formatting issues. Can you confirm that these will be meticulously corrected for any future version of the paper?
