### **Summary and Contributions**

This paper addresses the task of Event Detection (ED), focusing on identifying and classifying event trigger words. The central hypothesis is that event arguments, which are typically ignored in pipeline ED models or ineffectively used in joint ED+AE models, provide crucial information for disambiguating trigger words. To exploit this, the authors propose a neural network model with a supervised attention mechanism. During training, the model's attention distribution over the context is explicitly supervised to match a "gold" attention distribution derived from the locations of annotated event arguments. The model is evaluated on the ACE 2005 dataset, where it achieves a new state-of-the-art result, further improved by augmenting the training data with information from FrameNet.

As I see them, the main contributions of this work are:
1.  **A novel and intuitive method for incorporating argument information into the ED task.** The use of annotated arguments to directly supervise an attention mechanism is a clever and explicit way to force the model to focus on context words that are most relevant for disambiguating triggers. This contrasts well with previous joint models where the benefit to ED was marginal.
2.  **A strong empirical validation and analysis.** The paper presents a new state-of-the-art F1 score on the standard ACE 2005 benchmark. The authors provide a thorough ablation study, including an oracle experiment (ANN-Gold), that convincingly demonstrates the utility of argument information.
3.  **An insightful diagnosis of the shortcomings of prior joint models for ED.** The paper provides a compelling argument for why joint learning has historically failed to improve ED, citing data imbalance and the failure to use argument information during the trigger prediction phase. This analysis effectively motivates the need for the proposed approach.

### **Strengths**

1.  **Well-motivated and elegant core idea.** The paper's premise is clear, intuitive, and strongly motivated. The "fired" example in the introduction perfectly illustrates the problem of trigger ambiguity and how arguments can resolve it. The proposed solution—using arguments as a supervisory signal for attention—is an elegant way to inject this knowledge directly into the ED model.
2.  **Novel application of supervised attention for event detection.** While supervised attention has been explored in other areas like NMT, its application to ED using argument spans as the supervisory signal is novel. The two proposed strategies for constructing the gold attention vectors (S1: hard attention on arguments; S2: Gaussian-smoothed attention) are sensible and well-explained.
3.  **Thorough and convincing experimental setup.** The experiments are well-designed to support the paper's claims.
    *   The "Correctness of Our Assumption" section (4.2) is particularly strong. Using oracle knowledge of argument locations at test time (ANN-Gold systems) provides a clear upper-bound analysis and powerfully demonstrates the potential of leveraging this information.
    *   The main results are benchmarked against a comprehensive list of recent and strong state-of-the-art systems.
    *   The experiments with FrameNet augmentation successfully address the potential counterargument that the baseline model's poor performance is solely due to data scarcity, showing that even with more data, the supervised approach provides benefits.

### **Weaknesses**

1.  **The baseline model (ANN) is surprisingly weak.** The unsupervised attention model achieves an F1 score of only 64.6, which is significantly lower than all contemporary baselines reported in Table 3 (e.g., Liu's PSL at 69.4, Nguyen's joint model at 69.3). The authors attribute this to data scarcity, but it might also suggest that the base architecture (a simple MLP over aggregated context) is not competitive. This weakness inflates the perceived improvement from the supervised attention mechanism (a >7 point F1 gain). It would be more convincing to show that this supervised loss can also improve a stronger baseline architecture, such as the CNNs used by Chen et al. (2015) or Nguyen & Grishman (2016).
2.  **Unclear handling of training instances for non-trigger candidates.** The paper explains how to construct a gold attention vector (`α*`) for a true trigger candidate based on its annotated arguments. However, it is not clear how `α*` is constructed for the vast majority of training instances, which are negative examples (i.e., tokens that are not triggers). If `α*` is a zero vector or a uniform distribution for these negative instances, the attention loss `D(θ)` might be dominated by these uninformative signals. A clearer explanation of the training process, especially for negative samples, is needed to fully assess the method's soundness. This is a critical detail for reproducibility and understanding the learning dynamics.
3.  **Limited technical novelty in the model architecture.** The core novelty lies in the formulation of the joint loss function and the creation of the gold attention signal. The underlying neural architecture—an MLP classifier fed with an attention-weighted context representation—is quite standard. Furthermore, the method for combining the word- and entity-based attentions (`α = αw + αe`) is a simple summation, which may be suboptimal. More sophisticated combination strategies (e.g., gating, concatenation) were not explored.

### **Questions to Authors**

1.  Could you please clarify how the gold attention vector `α*` is constructed during training for a token that is *not* an annotated event trigger? For a sentence with a single "End-Position" event triggered by "fired", what is the target attention vector `α*` when the model is processing the candidate token "Mohamad" or "Anwar"?
2.  The performance of the unsupervised ANN baseline is substantially lower than other recent neural models. Have you considered applying your supervised attention loss to a stronger, state-of-the-art ED architecture (e.g., the Skip-CNN from Nguyen & Grishman, 2016)? Demonstrating a gain on top of a more competitive baseline would strengthen the paper's claims about the general utility of this supervision technique.
3.  The paper states that for candidates without arguments in context, the S1 strategy forces all *entities* to average the attention. Could you elaborate on the motivation for this heuristic? Why entities specifically, and not, for example, a uniform distribution over all context words? Does this choice significantly impact performance?
