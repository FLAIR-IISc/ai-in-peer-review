### Summary and Contributions

This paper addresses the task of Event Detection (ED), which involves identifying and classifying event trigger words. The authors hypothesize that existing joint learning models for Event Extraction fail to leverage argument information effectively to improve ED performance. They propose a neural network-based approach that explicitly incorporates argument information by training an attention mechanism under direct supervision. During training, "gold" attention vectors are constructed based on the locations of annotated event arguments in the context of a trigger candidate. The model's loss function is then a combination of the standard classification loss and an attention loss, which penalizes the deviation of the model's learned attention from the gold attention. Experiments on the ACE 2005 dataset show that this supervised attention method significantly outperforms an unsupervised attention baseline and achieves state-of-the-art results compared to previous methods.

In my view, the main contributions of this work are:

1.  **A novel training paradigm for Event Detection using supervised attention.** The primary contribution is the method of using annotated argument locations as a supervisory signal to guide a model's attention mechanism. This provides a direct and explicit way to inject knowledge about informative contextual words (i.e., arguments) into the ED model, which is a departure from previous pipeline and joint modeling approaches.
2.  **An empirical demonstration of the value of argument information for ED.** Through a series of well-designed experiments, the paper provides strong evidence for its central claim: that explicit use of argument information can substantially improve ED performance. The ablation studies effectively show that the performance gain comes from focusing on arguments, not just entities in general.
3.  **Achievement of state-of-the-art performance on a standard benchmark.** The proposed model sets a new state-of-the-art F1 score on the ACE 2005 dataset, demonstrating the practical effectiveness of the approach.

### Strengths

1.  **Well-motivated and sound methodology.** The paper's motivation is clear and compelling. It identifies a plausible weakness in prior joint models—that they do not effectively channel argument information to benefit the ED sub-task—and proposes a direct solution. The idea of supervising attention with argument locations is intuitive and technically sound. This direct supervision forces the model to learn the correlation between certain contextual patterns (arguments) and specific event types, which is particularly useful for ambiguous triggers, as highlighted in the introduction.

2.  **Strong and convincing empirical results.** The experimental results strongly support the paper's claims. The proposed supervised attention models (ANN-S1 and ANN-S2) achieve a significant improvement of over 6-7 F1 points compared to the identical model architecture trained with unsupervised attention (ANN). This large gap convincingly demonstrates the efficacy of the proposed training method. Furthermore, the final model (ANN-S2) surpasses prior state-of-the-art systems, including both pipeline and joint models, on a standard benchmark.

3.  **Thorough experimental analysis.** The paper includes a dedicated section (4.2) to validate its core assumption, which is a key strength. By comparing baselines with oracle "gold" attention models, the authors effectively isolate the impact of argument information and show its superiority over using just entity information. The comparison between the two supervision strategies (S1 and S2) and the corresponding analysis of the precision-recall trade-off also add depth to the results. The inclusion of experiments with FrameNet data augmentation further demonstrates the model's robustness.

### Weaknesses

1.  **Limited architectural novelty.** While the training method is novel for this task, the underlying model components are standard. The classifier is a basic ANN, and the attention mechanism is a conventional dot-product attention. The concept of supervised attention has been explored in other NLP tasks (e.g., machine translation), as cited by the authors. Therefore, the novelty is confined to the application and the specific formulation of the supervisory signal for ED, rather than in the development of a new neural architecture.

2.  **Dependence on argument annotations and unclear positioning.** The method's effectiveness is contingent on the availability of argument annotations in the training set. This limits its applicability to datasets that have this rich level of annotation. The paper frames its contribution as an improvement over joint models, but the proposed model is not a joint model; it performs only ED. It is more accurately a pipeline ED component that leverages training-time annotations typically used for a subsequent Argument Extraction (AE) component. This framing could be clearer, as the model does not perform AE at test time and relies on the learned attention to implicitly find argument-like words.

3.  **Potential issues with noisy supervision in data augmentation.** For the FrameNet augmentation experiments, the paper relies on an external system to generate entity and argument labels. This process inevitably introduces noise. The "gold" attention vectors created from this noisy data may be inaccurate, potentially degrading the quality of the supervision signal. The paper does not provide an analysis of the quality of these automatic annotations or discuss how the model handles this noise. This lack of analysis makes it difficult to fully assess the robustness of the supervised attention method when applied to less clean, automatically-labeled data.

### Questions to Authors

1.  A key part of your method is learning an attention mechanism that is supposed to mimic focusing on arguments. Have you performed a qualitative or quantitative analysis of the learned attention weights on the test set? Specifically, for correctly predicted trigger words, does the model's attention distribution strongly correlate with the positions of the actual, unannotated arguments in the sentence?
2.  Your method improves ED by leveraging AE annotations during training. How do you envision this improved ED model benefiting a full, end-to-end Event Extraction system? Have you considered whether the significant gains in ED would translate to downstream improvements in AE when your model is used as the first stage in a pipeline?
3.  Regarding the FrameNet augmentation, the automatic annotation of arguments and entities is a potential source of significant noise. Could you comment on the estimated accuracy of the annotations produced by the RPI system on this data? How does this noise impact the supervised training signal, and have you explored any techniques to mitigate its effects (e.g., down-weighting the attention loss for augmented data)?
