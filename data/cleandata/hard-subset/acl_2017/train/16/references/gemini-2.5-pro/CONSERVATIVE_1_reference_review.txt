### **Summary of the Paper and Contributions**

This paper addresses the task of Event Detection (ED), arguing that existing methods, including joint models for event and argument extraction, fail to effectively leverage annotated argument information to improve ED performance. The authors propose a neural network model that incorporates a supervised attention mechanism. The core idea is to explicitly train the model's attention component to focus on argument words when classifying a potential event trigger. This is achieved by defining a "gold" attention vector based on argument annotations in the training data and adding a loss term that encourages the model's learned attention to mimic this gold standard. The authors evaluate their approach on the ACE 2005 dataset, showing that it outperforms previous state-of-the-art methods.

From my perspective, the main contributions of this work are:

1.  **A novel method for integrating argument information into an ED model via supervised attention.** This provides an alternative to complex joint inference frameworks, offering a straightforward way to guide the model's focus using readily available annotations.
2.  **An empirical demonstration of improved performance on the standard ACE 2005 benchmark.** The proposed model achieves a new state-of-the-art F1 score, surpassing a number of strong, established baselines.
3.  An exploration of different heuristic strategies for constructing the supervision signal for the attention mechanism.

### **Strengths**

1.  **The paper is well-motivated and the central hypothesis is plausible.** The authors begin with a clear analysis of a perceived weakness in prior work: joint models improve argument extraction (AE) but provide little benefit to ED. Their hypothesis that argument information is underutilized and could disambiguate triggers (e.g., "fired") is compelling. The proposed solution of using arguments to directly supervise an attention mechanism is an intuitive and logical approach to test this hypothesis.

2.  **The proposed method achieves state-of-the-art results on a standard benchmark.** The final model (ANN-S2) reports an F1 score of 71.7 (and 71.9 with FrameNet data), which moderately improves upon previously published results (e.g., 70.7 and 71.3). In a well-studied task like ACE event detection, achieving a statistically significant improvement over strong prior work is a notable accomplishment and suggests the method has merit.

3.  **The approach is relatively simple and conceptually elegant.** Compared to traditional joint models that often rely on complex structured prediction or global inference, this method integrates argument information through a simple secondary loss term. This approach of using auxiliary supervision to guide a neural model's internal representations is a sound and established technique, and its application here is novel and well-executed.

### **Weaknesses**

1.  **The main baseline for comparison is unconvincingly weak.** The unsupervised attention model (`ANN`) serves as the primary baseline to demonstrate the efficacy of the supervised approach. However, its reported F1 score of 64.6 is substantially lower than all other recent neural network-based methods cited in the paper (which score between 69.3 and 71.3). This significant performance gap raises concerns about the strength of the base architecture or its implementation. Without a competitive unsupervised baseline, it is difficult to ascertain how much of the reported performance gain stems from the supervision itself versus simply bringing a weak model up to par with more standard architectures. The impressive gains over `ANN` may be partially inflated due to this weak starting point.

2.  **The experimental validation of the core assumption is flawed.** The experiments in Section 4.2 ("Correctness of Our Assumption") use models (`ANN-Gold1/2`) that are given access to gold argument information at *test time*. This constitutes an oracle experiment, which demonstrates the potential upper bound of performance if arguments were known perfectly, but it does not validate the efficacy of the proposed *learning* method. It is misleading to frame this as a direct comparison showing that argument information is "useful," as this setup is not achievable in a real-world scenario. A more appropriate analysis would involve examining the attention weights of the trained model on unseen test data to see if it has learned to focus on syntactically and semantically plausible argument-like words.

3.  **The construction of the "gold" attention signal is entirely heuristic and lacks thorough analysis.** The paper proposes two strategies, S1 (binary) and S2 (Gaussian), for creating the supervision signal. These methods rely on arbitrary choices (e.g., why a Gaussian distribution? How was the window size of 3 chosen? Why µ=0.0 and σ=1.0?). There is no ablation study or sensitivity analysis to justify these specific hyperparameters or to explore alternatives. It is unclear how robust the model's performance is to these choices. The heuristic nature of this core component of the method feels under-motivated and insufficiently validated.

4.  **The approach has limited applicability.** The method's reliance on training data annotated with both event triggers and their corresponding arguments restricts its use to datasets like ACE 2005. While the authors attempt to mitigate this by using FrameNet data, they must resort to automatically labeling arguments, which introduces noise. The paper acknowledges that this noisy supervision hurts precision but provides no deeper analysis of the impact. This reliance on rich annotations is a significant practical limitation compared to methods that only require trigger labels.

### **Questions to Authors**

1.  Could you elaborate on the architecture of your `ANN` baseline? Given its performance is substantially below other recent neural ED systems (e.g., Chen et al., 2015; Nguyen and Grishman, 2016), can you explain why this might be the case? A stronger baseline would make the contribution of the supervised attention more clear.

2.  Regarding the experiment in Section 4.2, do you agree that using gold arguments at test time is an oracle condition? Would it be possible to provide a qualitative analysis of the attention maps produced by your trained ANN-S2 model on test examples to show that it has learned to focus on semantically relevant context words without oracle information?

3.  How were the hyperparameters for the S2 gold attention strategy (Gaussian distribution, window size=3, etc.) selected? Have you performed any sensitivity analysis to determine how these choices impact final performance?
