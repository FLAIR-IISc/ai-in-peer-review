Summary and contributions
This paper targets ACE-style event detection (ED) and argues that event arguments provide strong cues for trigger identification and type disambiguation. The core idea is to supervise an attention mechanism with gold argument positions during training so that the model learns to focus on argument-bearing tokens when classifying a candidate trigger. Two strategies are proposed to construct supervision signals: S1 focuses attention solely on annotated arguments; S2 spreads attention to arguments and their nearby tokens via a Gaussian kernel. A simple ANN classifier consumes the attended context (words and entity types). Experiments on ACE 2005 show sizable F1 gains over prior systems; augmenting training with FrameNet-derived events gives further improvements.

As I see it, the main contributions are:
1) Empirical demonstration that supervising attention with argument annotations substantially improves ED over unsupervised attention and several prior systems on ACE 2005.
2) A simple attention scheme that combines word- and entity-type context, with two practical supervision targets (S1/S2) offering a precision/recall trade-off.
3) A pragmatic augmentation using FrameNet events to address data sparsity, showing complementary gains to the proposed supervision.

Strengths
- Clear, motivated use of argument information for ED: The paper articulates why arguments help disambiguate ambiguous triggers and operationalizes this via supervised attention trained only on gold arguments; at test time no arguments are used, avoiding leakage. The large gains from ANN to ANN-S1/S2 (F1 +6–7 points) support the hypothesis that arguments are powerful supervision for ED.

- Effective and simple method: The attention is computed via lightweight transformations and dot products, and the supervision is easy to construct from ACE annotations. The two target designs (S1 vs. S2) produce interpretable precision/recall behavior (S1 higher precision; S2 higher recall), which is a useful practical knob.

- Solid experimental evidence on ACE 2005: The paper compares to multiple prior systems (Li et al., Nguyen et al., Liu et al., Skip-CNN), uses a common split protocol, and includes ablations (unsupervised attention baseline; entity-only attention; oracle attention in train/test). The ANN-ENT vs. ANN-Gold1/2 comparison supports the claim that entities alone are insufficient; argument-specific supervision matters.

- Data augmentation with FrameNet: Re-using FN-derived events (with core frame elements as arguments) yields further recall improvements, suggesting the method benefits from more (even noisy) supervision and is not overfit to ACE.

- Implementation details for reproducibility: Hyperparameters, optimization, embeddings, and S2 windowing are described, facilitating replication.

Weaknesses
- Limited novelty relative to prior supervised attention: Supervising attention with gold alignments has been explored (e.g., for NMT), and extending it to ED using ACE arguments is a fairly direct adaptation. The classifier is a basic ANN without contextual encoders (CNN/BiLSTM), so part of the gains may stem from complementing a relatively weak baseline rather than from a fundamentally new modeling contribution. A comparison where supervised attention is plugged into a stronger encoder would better situate the contribution.

- Methodological ambiguities: It is unclear whether the combined attention α = αw + αe is renormalized before being used for context pooling and before computing the supervision loss. Since αw and αe are each softmaxed, α sums to 2, while the gold α* is normalized; a scaling mismatch could affect training unless addressed. Additionally, for negative candidates (non-triggers) the gold attention is defined as uniform over entities; this design may inadvertently encourage attending to entities even for negatives. The paper does not analyze alternative targets (e.g., zero attention, uniform over all tokens) or the impact of this choice on false positives.

- Evaluation clarity and completeness: The ACE split is one of several in the literature; although you cite comparable works, Table 3 has formatting inconsistencies (e.g., “Skip-CNN N/A 71.3” with no P/R/F1 labeling), and the statistical testing setup (number of runs/seeds, paired test) is not specified. It is also unclear whether gold entities are used at test for all systems (common in ED work but a strong assumption); no results with automatic entities are reported, limiting realism.

- Missing ablations and analysis: There is no ablation of the entity attention (αe) vs. word attention (αw) vs. their combination; no sensitivity to λ; no per-event-type analysis; no attention visualizations to validate that the model indeed focuses on arguments; and the fixed context length n is not reported nor varied. These would strengthen the causal story about why the method works and where it helps most.

- Unsubstantiated claim about joint models: The paper attributes modest ED gains in joint models to argument/trigger annotation imbalance and pre-prediction design, but does not provide controlled experiments (e.g., reweighting losses or injecting argument info into trigger pre-prediction) to support this explanation. This discussion would benefit from empirical backing or more cautious framing.

Questions to authors
1) Do you renormalize α = αw + αe before using it for cw/ce and in the attention loss? If not, how do you reconcile the scale difference with α*, which is normalized?

2) How do you construct gold attention for negative candidates in sentences that contain other events? Is it always uniform over entities? Did you try zero attention or uniform-over-all-tokens, and how did this affect precision/recall?

3) What fixed context length n did you use? How sensitive are results to n?

4) Please report results with automatic entity mentions on ACE test (e.g., from the RPI system) to estimate robustness without gold entities.

5) In Table 3, what does the 71.3 for Skip-CNN denote (F1?) and under which features/settings? Also, how many runs were averaged for your systems and what was the exact statistical test?

6) Could you ablate αw vs. αe vs. αw+αe and report the effect of varying λ? Visualizing learned attention would also help validate the intended behavior.

Overall recommendation
The paper presents a well-motivated and effective application of supervised attention to ED, with clear empirical gains and practical value. The methodological novelty is modest and several clarifications/ablations are missing, but the results and analysis are promising. I lean toward acceptance if the authors address the methodological ambiguities, strengthen comparisons/ablations, and clarify evaluation details.
