- Paper proposes event detection on ACE 2005 by supervising attention with gold argument annotations (S1: arguments only, S2: arguments + Gaussian neighborhood) and optionally augmenting training with FrameNet-derived events.
- Main claimed contributions: explicit supervised attention using gold arguments, empirical ablations isolating argument supervision effects, and simple reusable attention supervision strategies (hard masks or Gaussian smoothing).
- Clear and focused motivation: argument-centric intuition for disambiguating ambiguous triggers (e.g., “fired”) is well illustrated and plausible.
- Strong empirical gains on ACE 2005: ANN-S2 achieves reported best F1 (71.7) and substantial improvement over the model’s unsupervised-attention baseline (64.6 → 71.7).
- Informative ablations and controls: comparisons to unsupervised attention (ANN), entity-only attention (ANN-ENT), and oracle attention bounds (ANN-Gold1/2) support the utility of argument supervision and the S1/S2 precision–recall tradeoff.
- Method is simple and general: supervised attention via argument masks is lightweight, encoder-agnostic, and potentially reusable in other tasks (relation extraction, SRL).
- FrameNet augmentation provides additional evidence that external argument-annotated data improves recall and attention learning.
- Novelty is moderate: channeling argument supervision into attention is incremental relative to prior joint AE/ED work and uses a straightforward dot-product attention with MSE loss.
- Baseline strength and fairness concerns: core classifier is a shallow ANN and the unsupervised-attention baseline is weaker than prior neural models, making it unclear how much gains stem from supervision versus architecture differences.
- Test-time entity/argument supervision is unclear: paper uses annotated entity type embeddings but doesn’t clarify whether gold entities are available at test time or if predicted entities are used, raising comparability issues.
- Potential data leakage or evaluation confusion: attention is supervised with gold arguments during training but test-time use of learned attention should be clarified to ensure no gold information is used at inference.
- Reproducibility details missing or underspecified: context window size n, handling of multi-token triggers, class imbalance handling, entity type inventory, early stopping, random seeds, and variance reporting are not fully described.
- Statistical claims need support: t-test/significance statements lack details on how variance was measured (number of runs, mean/std).
- Limited qualitative analysis: paper lacks concrete learned attention heatmaps, failure cases, and per-event-type breakdowns to validate that attention supervision causes the gains.
- Requested experiments/clarifications: specify test-time entity usage, report context window and multi-word trigger handling, provide run averages and stddevs, integrate supervised attention into stronger encoders (CNN/RNN) to measure incremental gains, and include qualitative attention examples and per-type error analysis.
- Overall recommendation: acknowledges useful idea and solid empirical/ablation support but notes moderate novelty and methodological concerns; reviewer leans to weak accept if authors clarify entity usage, strengthen comparisons, and address reproducibility, otherwise borderline.
