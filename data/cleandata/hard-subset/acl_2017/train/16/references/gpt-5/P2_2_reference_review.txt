Summary and contributions
This paper targets ACE-style event detection (ED) and argues that gold event arguments, though not strictly required for trigger classification, provide strong cues that can be exploited directly rather than indirectly via joint models. The authors propose a simple neural ED model with a supervised attention mechanism: attention weights over context words/entities are trained to match “gold attention” constructed from annotated arguments (two strategies: S1 focusing only on argument tokens; S2 spreading attention to neighbors via a Gaussian). The attention supervision is combined with the ED loss. Experiments on ACE 2005 show gains over the authors’ own baseline and modest improvements over selected prior systems. They also show further gains by augmenting training with FrameNet-derived data.

As I see it, the main contributions are:
1) A direct, training-time use of gold argument supervision to shape attention for ED, avoiding the complexity of joint inference while leveraging argument information specifically for trigger classification.
2) An empirical analysis demonstrating that supervised attention guided by arguments yields consistent improvements over unsupervised attention and entity-only attention, with two strategies (S1 high-precision, S2 higher-recall).
3) Additional experiments showing further improvements from FrameNet augmentation and a discussion on why joint models may under-improve ED.

Strengths
- Clear and focused idea with a simple implementation. Supervising attention with argument annotations is a direct, intuitive way to inject useful supervision where ED needs it (around potential arguments). The method is easier to implement than joint models and does not require arguments at test time.
- Solid ablations that isolate where gains come from. The comparison among ANN (unsupervised attention), ANN-ENT (entity-only), ANN-Gold1/2 (gold attention at test), and the proposed supervised attention (ANN-S1/S2) is informative. The large gap between ANN-ENT and ANN-Gold1/2 convincingly shows that entity types alone are not sufficient; argument supervision is the key signal.
- Competitive results on ACE 2005. The proposed model outperforms or matches strong prior systems (e.g., slight F1 improvement over Skip-CNN and Liu et al. FN-based). The precision/recall trade-off between S1 and S2 is reasonable and supports the design choice.
- Sensible use of external data. The FrameNet augmentation replicates a known strategy, and the results fit the narrative: more (noisier) positives raise recall slightly and depress precision slightly, improving F1 overall. This also supports the claim that unsupervised attention may suffer from data sparsity.
- Reasonable discussion on why prior joint methods may not help ED as much. The imbalance in annotated instances and the pipeline pre-prediction behavior plausibly explain previous findings and motivate the proposed approach.

Weaknesses
- Limited novelty relative to existing supervised attention literature and joint EE work. Training attention to match known supervision (here, arguments) closely follows prior supervised attention ideas (e.g., Mi et al., 2016) and leverages the same ACE annotations that joint models use. Conceptually, the paper repurposes argument supervision from joint learning to attention shaping; while useful, the methodological novelty is incremental.
- Baselines and comparability leave some uncertainty. The main neural baseline (ANN with unsupervised attention) is relatively weak compared to contemporary CNN/RNN models trained under the same setup. The comparison to Skip-CNN shows a marginal improvement (0.4 F1), and the table reports incomplete metrics for Skip-CNN. It is unclear whether all reported prior numbers use exactly the same split/settings (though the paper claims so). Given small gains, stronger, re-run baselines under identical conditions would strengthen the case.
- Use of entity information at test time is unclear and potentially unfair. The model uses entity type embeddings and constructs attention partly over entity sequences. Are gold entities used at test time on ACE (as some prior ED works do), or predicted entities? For FrameNet augmentation, entities are predicted by an external system, creating a mismatch. Without a clear statement and matched baselines, it is hard to assess fairness and robustness to realistic settings without gold entities.
- Incomplete methodological details and limited qualitative analysis. Important details are missing or terse: the fixed context window size n, how argument spans are mapped to token-level gold attention, how “we” (the transformed vector for entity attention) is computed, and whether multiple runs and variance were considered (the t-test is claimed but setup not described). There is no error analysis or attention visualization to validate that attention indeed focuses on arguments and helps disambiguate triggers, especially for polysemous words like “fired.”
- Modest absolute gains and limited generalization evidence. Improvements over the strongest prior baseline are small. The approach is evaluated only on ACE 2005 newswire; no cross-domain or cross-split evaluation is provided. Since the method relies on argument supervision available only in training, its benefit may diminish in low-resource or domain-transfer settings without aligned argument annotations.

Questions to authors
- Do you use gold entity mentions/types at test time on ACE 2005? If so, are all competing baselines in Table 3 also using gold entities at test time? If not, please report results with predicted entities for a fair comparison.
- What is the exact context window size n and sentence handling (e.g., trimming, padding across sentence boundaries)? How sensitive are results to n?
- How are multi-token argument spans converted to token-level gold attention for S1/S2? Are all tokens in the span assigned equal attention before normalization?
- How is the “we” vector (used to attend over entity types) derived from the current word embedding? Please specify the transformation and parameters.
- How many random seeds/runs were used for each reported number, and how was the t-test conducted (paired over runs)? Please provide means and standard deviations or confidence intervals.
- Can you provide qualitative analyses/visualizations showing attention distributions for correctly disambiguated triggers versus errors?

Overall, this paper presents a clean, well-motivated use of supervised attention with argument supervision for ED. The idea is incremental but practical; results are competitive, though gains over the strongest prior work are modest and some experimental details/comparability questions remain. Clarifications and stronger baselines would materially strengthen the submission.
