Summary
The paper addresses event detection (ED) in ACE 2005 by explicitly leveraging annotated event arguments during training via supervised attention. The model represents each token as a trigger candidate and computes attention over its contextual words and contextual entity types. Gold arguments are used to construct target attention distributions under two strategies: S1 (attention only on argument tokens) and S2 (attention on arguments and nearby words via a Gaussian). The attention is trained with a squared error loss jointly with the ED classifier. At test time, no gold arguments are used; the learned attention guides context aggregation. Experiments on ACE 2005, with and without FrameNet augmentation, show improved F1 over several reported baselines.

Contributions (as assessed by the reviewer)
1) An explicit, supervised-attention formulation that uses gold argument annotations to guide the attention mechanism for ED training, rather than relying on joint ED/AE or unsupervised attention.
2) Empirical validation that argument information (beyond entity types alone) materially benefits ED, via controlled baselines including entity-only attention and oracle gold-attention systems.
3) Competitive results on ACE 2005 and further gains with FrameNet augmentation, with analysis of precision/recall trade-offs between attention strategies.

Strengths
- Clear motivation and problem framing: The paper articulates a plausible reason why prior joint ED/AE approaches have limited ED gains (imbalance in supervision and indirect use of arguments for ED). The hypothesis that arguments disambiguate ambiguous triggers is supported by examples and subsequent experiments.

- Method simplicity and focused novelty: The supervised attention mechanism is straightforward, draws on established supervised-attention ideas (e.g., Mi et al., 2016), and adapts them to ED by constructing gold attention from argument spans. This provides a direct pathway for argument signals to influence the ED model without requiring a full joint IE system.

- Empirical validation of the central claim: The oracle variants (ANN-Gold1/2) outperform systems without argument supervision, and the entity-only baseline (ANN-ENT) underperforms the argument-aware oracles. This isolates the effect of argument supervision beyond generic entity cues.

- Competitive performance and analysis: The supervised attention systems (especially S2) improve F1 on ACE 2005 over several reported baselines, with statistically significant gains (t-test, p ≤ 0.05). The paper also explores the precision-recall trade-off between S1 and S2 and demonstrates further improvements from FrameNet augmentation, consistent with prior work on data sparsity.

- Use of standard resources and comparability: The dataset split follows prior work; word embeddings and training details are described to a reasonable extent, aiding replicability in principle.

Weaknesses
- Attention combination and normalization unclear: The paper defines αw and αe as normalized attention distributions, then combines them as α = αw + αe and uses α both to weight context and as the target of the supervised loss against α*. It is not stated whether α is renormalized before use. Without renormalization, α does not sum to 1, which misaligns with α* and may affect both the loss and the context aggregation. This is a potential technical flaw or at least a clarity issue that needs resolution.

- Use of entity annotations at test time: The approach “exploits annotated entity information,” but it is unclear whether test-time entity types are gold or predicted. If gold entities are used at test time, this inflates performance and reduces realism; if predicted, the source and quality of predictions should be specified with an assessment of the impact on ED. Comparisons may be unfair if baselines differ in their use of gold entities.

- Baseline strength and ablations: The unsupervised attention baseline (ANN) is weak relative to prior representation-based methods; stronger neural baselines (e.g., CNNs/RNNs with comparable features) are not reported for direct comparison within the same framework. Ablations isolating the contribution of supervised attention versus entity context, word-only attention, or architectural choices (e.g., removing αe or varying the attention scoring function) are missing, limiting attribution of gains.

- Reproducibility and reporting gaps: Several implementation details are insufficiently specified, including the exact context window size n, training epochs/early stopping, random seed stability, and whether results are averaged across runs. The t-test methodology is not fully described (e.g., across which runs). No per-event-type results or qualitative attention visualizations are provided to corroborate that attention focuses on arguments at test time.

- Comparative reporting issues: For Skip-CNN, only recall is shown, preventing a clean F1 comparison. The rationale regarding why joint models underperform on ED is plausible but presented as conjecture without controlled experiments (e.g., reweighting joint losses) to substantiate the claim.

Questions to Authors (optional)
- Do you renormalize α after combining αw and αe? If not, how do you reconcile the scale mismatch with the gold attention α* and its use in context weighting? Please clarify the exact computation used in both training and inference.

- What entity information is used at test time? Gold ACE entities, or outputs from an entity recognizer/linker? If predicted, which system is used and what is the ED performance with gold vs predicted entities?

- What is the exact context window size n, and how sensitive are results to n? Can you report an ablation removing αe (entity attention) or using only αw to quantify the incremental value of entity-based attention?

- How many runs were performed, and what is the variance of F1? Please provide standard deviations and details of the t-test.

- Can you provide per-event-type performance and qualitative attention heatmaps to demonstrate that the learned attention indeed focuses on argument tokens at test time?

Overall, the paper presents a clear and focused idea—supervising attention with argument annotations for ED—with evidence of benefit on ACE 2005. Addressing the technical and reporting issues above would strengthen confidence in the method’s soundness and generalizability.
