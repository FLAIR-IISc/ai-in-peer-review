Summary and contributions
This paper addresses event detection (ED) in ACE 2005 by explicitly leveraging annotated argument information via supervised attention. The model computes attention over contextual words and entity types conditioned on a candidate trigger, and trains the attention to match “gold” attention distributions derived from gold arguments. Two supervision strategies are explored: (S1) focusing attention on argument tokens only; (S2) also distributing attention to neighboring tokens via a Gaussian window. Experiments show improvements over strong baselines and prior work; additional gains are reported when augmenting training with FrameNet-derived events.

As I see it, the main contributions (in order of strength) are:
1) A simple supervised-attention formulation that injects argument supervision into ED and yields new SOTA F1 on ACE 2005 compared to prior feature- and representation-based methods.
2) A careful contrast of different attention supervision strategies (S1 vs S2) with empirical analysis of precision/recall trade-offs and an “oracle” study demonstrating the utility ceiling of argument information for ED.
3) An external data augmentation study using FrameNet, adapted to the proposed setup, showing complementary gains.

Strengths
1) Clear, intuitive motivation and targeted mechanism. The paper’s central hypothesis—arguments help disambiguate triggers—maps cleanly to a supervised attention target. The supervision is derived directly from gold arguments without complex pipeline dependencies, making the idea easy to implement and reason about.

2) Strong empirical results and useful ablations. On ACE 2005, ANN-S2 achieves the highest reported F1 among compared systems, and both supervised-attention variants substantially outperform the unsupervised-attention baseline. The “oracle” experiments (ANN-Gold1/2) convincingly show that argument signals are indeed predictive for ED, beyond what entity information alone provides (ANN-ENT).

3) Analysis of precision/recall behavior. The comparison between S1 (higher precision) and S2 (higher recall) is insightful and consistent with the design of the supervision strategies. This gives practitioners guidance on tailoring the method to application needs.

4) External data augmentation is thoughtfully integrated. The FrameNet study, including treatment of frame elements as arguments and adding entity annotations, demonstrates that the approach can leverage broader supervision and remains beneficial under added noise.

5) Conceptual clarity and simplicity. The model architecture (feed-forward ED classifier, attention over contextual words and entity types) is straightforward and reproducible. Many implementation details are reported (embedding sizes, dropout, λ values per strategy), and optimization choices are standard.

Weaknesses
1) Limited novelty relative to existing attention work. The core idea—supervising attention with external alignments/labels—is known in sequence modeling; the novelty here is its application to ED with argument-derived targets. While the empirical gains are solid, the methodological contribution is incremental.

2) Practicality and reliance on gold annotations. Training requires gold argument spans and gold entities; testing appears to rely on gold entity types as contextual features. This raises questions about deployment: How does performance change if entities (and potentially arguments for pretraining) are predicted rather than gold? The current setup may overestimate real-world performance and comparability depends on consistent use of gold entities across baselines.

3) Insufficient substantiation of claims about joint models. The paper attributes the limited ED gains in joint models to data imbalance and to argument signals not being used for trigger pre-prediction. These are plausible but not empirically tested here (e.g., by modifying a joint model’s loss weighting or pre-selector). Without such evidence, the causal claims remain speculative.

4) Missing ablations and sensitivity studies. The contribution of αw vs αe, the attention combination scheme (αw + αe), and the sensitivity to λ, context window size n, and Gaussian parameters are not explored. Such ablations would strengthen understanding of what drives gains. Similarly, analysis of error types (e.g., ambiguous polysemous triggers) and attention visualizations would improve interpretability.

5) Comparative baselines and statistical rigor. While the paper compares against several strong systems, reporting is uneven (e.g., Skip-CNN table lacks precision; t-test details are minimal: test used, sample size/variance, multiple comparisons). Given the modest absolute F1 gains over the strongest baseline, clearer statistical reporting would help.

Questions to authors
- Do you use gold entity types at test time? If not, what tagger is used and how sensitive is performance to entity errors? Please report results with predicted entities.
- How much does each attention source contribute? Provide ablations for using only αw, only αe, their concatenation vs sum, and no attention (uniform).
- How sensitive are results to λ, context window length n, and the Gaussian window/hyperparameters for S2? Any signs of overfitting?
- Can you provide qualitative analyses (attention heatmaps) and error breakdowns, especially for ambiguous triggers like “fired”?
- For the FrameNet augmentation, how are frames mapped to ACE subtypes and how noisy are the mapped labels/arguments? Any filtering heuristics?
- Could your thesis about joint models be tested by reweighting ED vs AE losses or by injecting your supervised-attention signal into a joint architecture?

Additional comments and suggestions
- Consider a setting where argument supervision is weak/noisy (e.g., distant supervision from dependency roles) to assess robustness.
- Evaluate on settings without gold entities at test time and on cross-domain splits to assess generalization.
- Improve reference formatting and fix minor typos; ensure all compared results are reported with consistent metrics.
- Include runtime/complexity and parameter counts; the simplicity is a strength worth quantifying.

Overall recommendation: Weak Accept. The idea is simple but well-motivated, the empirical gains are credible and practically relevant, and the study is thorough enough to be useful to the community. Addressing the practicality and ablation concerns would strengthen the paper further.
