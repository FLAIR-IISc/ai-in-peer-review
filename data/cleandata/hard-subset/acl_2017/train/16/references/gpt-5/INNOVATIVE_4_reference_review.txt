Summary
This paper addresses event detection (ED) by explicitly leveraging annotated argument information through supervised attention. The core idea is to construct “gold” attention distributions from argument annotations (two strategies: argument-only and argument+neighbors via a Gaussian), and jointly train an attention module and a simple ANN classifier so that the model learns to focus on argument-bearing context when classifying triggers. Experiments on ACE 2005 show consistent gains over a baseline with unsupervised attention and modest improvements over prior SOTA; additional training data from FrameNet yields further gains.

Main contributions (as I see them)
1) A conceptually clear use of supervised attention to inject argument supervision directly into ED, addressing a long-standing gap where arguments mainly benefited AE in joint systems but not ED.
2) Two practical strategies to convert argument annotations into target attentions and a joint loss to train attention and the ED classifier.
3) Empirical evidence on ACE 2005 (and with FN augmentation) that argument-supervised attention improves ED, with higher precision and competitive or better F1 than strong baselines.

Strengths
- Novelty in supervision signal: Using gold argument positions to supervise attention for ED is a simple but fresh idea that directly bridges AE supervision to ED, differing from prior joint approaches that implicitly use arguments and often fail to help ED. This conceptual move is likely to inspire similar cross-task supervision in other IE settings.
- Methodological simplicity and clarity: The approach is lightweight (no complex structured inference), easy to implement, and orthogonal to encoder choices, which makes it appealing for adoption and extension.
- Consistent empirical gains: Supervised attention variants (S1/S2) substantially outperform the unsupervised-attention baseline; S2 achieves the best F1 on ACE 2005 against reported SOTA, with strong precision. The analysis contrasting S1 vs S2 (precision–recall trade-off) is sensible.
- Diagnostic comparisons: The “ANN-ENT” and “ANN-Gold” variants provide useful evidence that mere entity information is insufficient and that argument-focused supervision is the driver, supporting the paper’s central claim.
- External data augmentation: The integration with FrameNet further validates the approach’s robustness to additional training data and gives a realistic path to improving ED under data sparsity.

Weaknesses
- Modest SOTA gains and baseline strength: Improvements over the strongest prior systems are small (e.g., ~0.4–1.0 F1), and the in-house baseline ANN is relatively weak compared to modern CNN/RNN encoders. It’s unclear how much of the improvement would persist if plugged into stronger encoders (e.g., biLSTM/CNN with rich features).
- Reliance on gold argument supervision at training time: While legitimate on ACE, many ED datasets lack argument annotations. The generality and portability of the approach may be limited; the paper would benefit from discussion or experiments on settings without gold arguments (e.g., distant supervision or predicted arguments for attention supervision).
- Incomplete analysis of attention behavior: The paper claims that attention focuses on arguments, but provides no qualitative visualizations or quantitative alignment metrics to confirm that learned attentions indeed attend to arguments at test time. An ablation disentangling word-attention vs entity-attention contributions (αw vs αe) is also missing.
- Some technical and reporting details are unclear: 
  - The combination α = αw + αe is not renormalized; does this scaling matter? 
  - The context window n is not specified in the main text (beyond padding/truncation). 
  - It’s unclear whether entity types are gold at test time (prior works often use gold entities).
  - Significance testing details are sparse (against which systems, how many runs?).
  - The Skip-CNN comparison lists “N/A” for precision/F1; fairness of this comparison is questionable.
- Limited comparison to stronger joint ED/AE models: The claim that joint methods underuse arguments for ED is plausible, but the paper does not empirically show that plugging argument signals into trigger pre-prediction in a joint model narrows the gap. A stronger baseline (e.g., joint RNN with trigger-aware argument features leveraged during trigger scoring) would solidify the argument.

Questions to Authors
- Do you use gold entity mentions/types at test time? If not, how are entities obtained, and how does performance change with predicted entities?
- How is the attention vector normalized after combining αw and αe (α = αw + αe)? Did you try renormalizing to a simplex, and does it affect performance?
- What is the context window size n? How sensitive are results to n?
- Can you provide qualitative attention visualizations and quantitative stats showing that, at test time, the learned attention aligns with argument tokens more than chance?
- Ablations: What is the impact of using only αw or only αe? What is the effect of λ, and of the Gaussian window size/σ in S2?
- For the t-test, which systems and how many random restarts are compared? Please report variance.
- Could you plug the supervised attention into a stronger encoder (e.g., biLSTM/CNN) to verify that gains persist?

Overall recommendation: Weak Accept. The paper’s key idea—explicitly supervising attention with argument annotations for ED—is novel, practical, and empirically beneficial, even if gains are modest and analysis/baselines could be stronger. The concept is impactful enough to merit acceptance, and the shortcomings seem addressable.
