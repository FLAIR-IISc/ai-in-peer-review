Summary and claimed contributions
This paper addresses event detection (ED) in ACE 2005 by explicitly exploiting annotated argument information to guide an attention mechanism. The model computes attention over contextual words and entity types relative to each trigger candidate, and trains the attention with supervision derived from gold argument spans. Two supervision strategies are explored: S1 (attention only on argument tokens) and S2 (a Gaussian-smoothed distribution around argument tokens). A simple feed-forward classifier (ANN) consumes the attended context representations alongside the candidate token to predict event type. The paper includes ablations to validate the usefulness of argument information, compares to several prior methods on ACE 2005, and examines augmentation with FrameNet-derived training instances.

Contributions as assessed:
1) An explicit use of gold argument annotations to supervise attention for ED, decoupling test-time ED from argument prediction and demonstrating improved performance on ACE 2005. This is the core methodological contribution.
2) An empirical study comparing unsupervised attention, entity-only attention, and argument-supervised attention (S1/S2), providing evidence that argument information (not merely entities) is beneficial for ED and that smoothing around argument spans (S2) can improve recall.
3) A demonstration of state-of-the-art or near state-of-the-art performance on ACE 2005, with additional analysis of augmentation via FrameNet-derived examples.

Strengths
- Clear hypothesis backed by targeted ablations: The paper explicitly tests the central claim that argument information aids ED. Comparisons among ANN (unsupervised attention), ANN-ENT (entity-only), and oracle-like ANN-Gold1/2 establish that arguments provide stronger signals than entities alone. The supervised attention models (ANN-S1/S2) outperform the unsupervised baseline substantially in F1 (e.g., 71.7 vs. 64.6), aligning with the stated hypothesis.
- Method is simple, modular, and test-time efficient: The approach uses a lightweight attention mechanism supervised during training; at inference it does not require argument annotations. This addresses a limitation of many joint models where ED may not benefit from argument data during trigger pre-selection, while avoiding the runtime complexity of joint inference.
- Competitive results on a standard benchmark: On ACE 2005, ANN-S2 slightly surpasses strong prior systems (e.g., Skip-CNN) and reports statistically significant improvements (t-test, p ≤ 0.05). The precision/recall trade-off between S1 and S2 is analyzed, providing interpretable behavior consistent with the supervision design.
- Attention supervision strategies are well-motivated and interpretable: The two schemes (argument-only vs. Gaussian around arguments) correspond to intuitive priors about where useful context resides. The formalization via a squared-error loss on attention is straightforward and reproducible.
- Comprehensive experimental details: The paper specifies hyperparameters, pretrained embeddings, optimization, and the use of FrameNet augmentation, facilitating replication under the stated setting.

Weaknesses
- Dependence on gold entities at test time and unclear realism: The context representation includes entity type embeddings for all contextual tokens, and experiments “exploit the annotated entity information” on ACE. This suggests gold entity labels are used at test time, which inflates realism and may limit applicability. When augmenting with FrameNet, predicted entities are added, but core ACE results do not report performance with predicted entities for a fairer comparison.
- Limited novelty relative to prior joint models and attention literature: While supervising attention with gold arguments is a direct and useful idea, prior joint ED-AE methods already leverage argument information (albeit indirectly) and attention mechanisms are established in NLP. The main novelty lies in applying supervised attention to ED with argument supervision; this is incremental rather than a fundamentally new modeling paradigm.
- Modest improvements over strongest baselines and incomplete baseline coverage: The gain over the best reported neural baselines appears small (e.g., 71.7 vs. 71.3 F1), and statistical testing details (number of runs, variance) are not provided. Some baseline numbers (e.g., Skip-CNN precision) are incomplete, and more recent sequence models (e.g., BiLSTM-based ED without joint AE) are not directly compared in the same split.
- Potential supervision mismatch and fallback heuristic: For candidates without arguments in the context window, the method forces attention to average over entities. This introduces a heuristic that may bias the model and could be a source of noise; its impact is not isolated via ablation. Additionally, no quantitative evaluation of attention quality (alignment with arguments) is provided, so it is unclear how faithfully the model learns the intended attention distribution.
- Dataset split and reproducibility concerns: The paper follows a particular ACE 2005 split (as in prior work), but does not report multiple random seeds, standard deviations, or code availability. The claim that joint models underperform on ED due to loss imbalance is plausible but not empirically substantiated within this paper.

Questions to authors
1) Do the ACE 2005 main results use gold entity types at test time? If so, please report results with predicted entities to assess practical utility and fairness against baselines that may also rely on gold entities.
2) How sensitive are results to the window size n and to the Gaussian parameters (µ, σ, w) in S2? Please include an ablation or sensitivity analysis.
3) What is the proportion of trigger candidates without any arguments in the context window, and what is the effect of the “average-over-entities” fallback on performance?
4) How many random runs were used to compute the reported scores and p-values? Please provide mean±std over multiple seeds.
5) Can you report an intrinsic evaluation of attention quality (e.g., alignment F1 between learned α and gold argument masks), and an example-based qualitative analysis?
6) Could a stronger sequence encoder (e.g., BiLSTM/CNN over the sentence) with the same supervised attention improve performance further? Have you tried sequence encoders to replace the MLP?
7) For FrameNet augmentation, what is the noise rate of automatically detected events and predicted entities, and how does performance vary with the amount of added FN data?

Overall, the paper is clearly written in terms of method and experimental setup, though there are formatting and citation issues. The approach is technically sound and the empirical evaluation supports the main claim, with caveats regarding reliance on gold entities and modest gains over strong baselines.
