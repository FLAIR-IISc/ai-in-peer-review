Summary and claimed contributions
This paper addresses event detection (ED) in ACE 2005 by explicitly exploiting annotated event arguments to supervise an attention mechanism. The model builds contextual representations for a trigger candidate via attention over surrounding words and entity types, and adds a supervised loss that encourages attention to concentrate on annotated arguments (S1) or arguments plus nearby words via a Gaussian kernel (S2). A simple feed-forward classifier predicts the event type. Experiments on ACE 2005 show gains over several prior systems; modest additional gains are reported when augmenting with FrameNet-derived events.

My view of the main contributions (in order):
1) A straightforward supervised-attention scheme that uses gold argument spans to guide attention during ED training.
2) An empirical study comparing unsupervised attention, supervised attention with two strategies (S1/S2), and augmentation with noisy FrameNet-derived events on ACE 2005.

Strengths
- Clear, intuitive motivation: Ambiguous triggers can be disambiguated by nearby arguments; using argument supervision to bias attention is sensible and aligns with prior findings that trigger-argument dependencies are informative.
- Simple method that plugs into a standard architecture: The approach is easy to implement (auxiliary squared-error loss on attention), does not require complex structured inference, and illustrates a clean way to leverage existing argument annotations during ED training.
- Consistent improvements over weak baselines: Supervised attention markedly improves a vanilla ANN baseline (F1 64.6 → 70.8/71.7), supporting the core claim that argument-informed attention helps ED.
- Comparison of attention strategies: The S1 vs S2 analysis (precision/recall trade-off) is informative and offers practical guidance on how to construct target attentions.
- Use of external data: The attempt to leverage FrameNet-derived events to address data sparsity, and the reported effect on recall, are useful even if gains are modest.

Weaknesses
- Limited novelty and positioning: Supervised attention is not new; the contribution is essentially using gold argument positions as attention targets—an incremental extension of established attention supervision ideas. The paper’s critique of joint models (imbalance, indirect use of arguments) is speculative and not empirically verified with controlled comparisons.
- Questionable experimental rigor and fairness of comparisons: The baseline ANN is weak (64.6 F1) compared to contemporaneous strong ED baselines (e.g., CNN/RNN with position/dependency features). There is no comparison to a simple multi-task/joint trigger-argument model trained on the same data, or to adding argument-indicator features (e.g., binary masks for gold argument tokens) without attention. It is unclear whether all compared systems use gold entities at test; the proposed model relies on entity type embeddings from ACE annotations.
- Potential leakage and realism concerns: The method requires gold argument annotations to supervise attention during training. If such annotations are available, a principled joint training objective over triggers and arguments is a natural and established alternative. The paper does not evaluate robustness when argument annotations are absent or noisy during training, nor when only predicted entities/arguments are available (a more realistic setting).
- Insufficient analysis and ablations: No ablation separates contributions of word- vs entity-based attention, or tests different ways of combining αw and αe (sum vs learned weighting, normalization). There is no per-type analysis, error breakdown, or sensitivity to context window size λ and Gaussian parameters. The t-test significance claim lacks details (number of runs, variance).
- Clarity and technical details: Some choices are ad hoc (e.g., if no arguments in context, “force all entities to average the whole attention”). It is unclear whether α = αw + αe is renormalized before weighting context. The use of gold vs predicted entities at test time is not clearly stated. References and writing quality have numerous issues, which raises reproducibility concerns; no code/data release is mentioned.

Questions to Authors
1) Do you use gold entity mentions/types at test time for ACE 2005? If not, which entity recognizer is used, and how sensitive is performance to entity errors?
2) How does your method compare to a simple alternative that uses gold argument indicator masks as additional input features (without attention supervision), and to a multi-task model that jointly predicts triggers and argument roles with shared encoders?
3) Is α renormalized after combining αw and αe? Did you try learning a gating/weighting between word- and entity-based attention?
4) How many random seeds/runs were used to report final scores and significance? Please provide mean±std and details of the t-test.
5) What fraction of positive trigger instances have at least one gold argument within your fixed window? How does performance vary with this fraction or when arguments are outside the window?
6) Can you report per-event-type results and an error analysis to substantiate the claim that arguments help disambiguate polysemous triggers?
7) In the FrameNet augmentation, you create arguments from core FEs and label entities with the RPI system. What is the noise level, and how does your model handle this? Did you try curriculum or down-weighting noisy attention supervision?

Overall, while the idea is intuitive and results are positive, the methodological novelty is limited and the experimental evaluation lacks stronger baselines and analyses. I encourage the authors to strengthen comparisons against joint/multi-task alternatives, clarify the evaluation setting (gold/predicted entities), and provide deeper ablations and error analyses.
