Summary and claimed contributions
- This paper proposes a supervised attention mechanism for event detection (ED) that explicitly leverages annotated argument information during training. Attention weights over context words/entities are supervised using “gold attention” derived from argument annotations (two strategies: S1 focus on argument tokens; S2 Gaussian-smoothed around arguments). The model is a simple MLP over the current token embedding and two context summaries (words and entity types) obtained via attention-weighted sums.
- The authors argue that prior joint ED+AE models underutilize arguments for ED. They report gains on ACE 2005 over several baselines and show modest further improvements when augmenting training with FrameNet-derived examples.

Main contributions (as I see them)
1) An explicit supervision signal for attention using argument annotations for ED, with two alternative constructions (S1/S2).
2) An empirical study on ACE2005 suggesting supervised attention helps over an unsupervised attention baseline, and a small improvement over selected prior work.
3) A simple way to augment with FrameNet by mapping frame elements to arguments and adding predicted ACE entity mentions.

Strengths
- Clear motivation for supervising attention with argument signals: The ambiguity example (“fired”) illustrates why arguments can disambiguate triggers. The two gold-attention strategies (strict vs smoothed) are reasonable and lead to complementary precision/recall behavior (S1 higher precision, S2 higher recall).
- Simplicity and ease of integration: The approach is architecturally simple (attention-weighted averages + MLP) and could be adapted to stronger encoders. The additional loss D(θ) is straightforward and does not require joint inference machinery.
- Ablations that probe the role of arguments: The comparison between ANN (unsupervised attention), ANN-ENT (uniform attention on entities), and ANN-Gold1/2 (oracle attentions) is useful. It supports the claim that arguments (not just entity types) are informative for ED.
- External data augmentation study: The FrameNet augmentation experiment is sensible and shows some recall gains (expected with more positive examples), providing additional evidence that data sparsity affects attention learning.

Weaknesses
- Evaluation protocol and comparability issues: The train/dev/test split is “randomly selected,” not the commonly adopted fixed split used in much prior ACE ED work. Reported baselines (e.g., Li 2013, Nguyen & Grishman 2016) may not be directly comparable under this split, yet the paper compares numbers across different splits and claims SOTA with very small margins (e.g., 71.7 vs 71.3). This undermines the significance of the headline claims. A fair comparison requires using the same split or reproducing baselines on your split.
- Under-specified and sometimes inconsistent methodology:
  - The context window size n is never specified; the index notation “[w−n, w−n/2+1, …]” is unclear/typo-ridden. Padding/truncation details and sentence boundary handling are underspecified.
  - The entity-attention source “we” is said to be “transforming w from the word space into the entity type space,” but no mapping (matrix, dimensionality) is defined, nor is its training detailed.
  - Attention equations: αw uses a dot product “w wTk” after a nonlinearity; the notation and dimensions are unclear. The same α (sum of αw and αe) is used to weight both word and entity contexts, though αe is derived from entities; the alignment and normalization details are not fully justified.
  - It is stated that “all arguments are entities,” which is not true in ACE (arguments can be Entities, Values, and Times). This affects the design of ANN-ENT and the interpretation of results.
- Use of gold resources and potential leakage: It appears gold entity types are used (at least during training) for ACE experiments, but it is unclear whether gold entities are also used at test time. Several prior works evaluate with predicted entities; if this paper uses gold entities at test time, comparisons are unfair. For FrameNet augmentation, entity mentions are predicted by an IE system; for ACE, the source (gold vs predicted) is not clearly stated.
- Baseline strength and architecture limitations: The main ED model is an MLP over attention-weighted averages, without contextual encoders (e.g., BiLSTM/CNN) that are standard for capturing syntax and long-range dependencies. The unsupervised attention baseline (ANN) performs poorly, possibly due to this weak encoder, making the relative gains less informative. A stronger baseline with a modern encoder and unsupervised attention would make the case more compelling.
- Statistical testing and effect sizes: Gains over strong baselines are small (≤0.6 F1) and the paper claims significance (t-test, p ≤ 0.05) without describing the test setup (randomization/bootstrap, number of runs, variance, multiple comparisons). With different splits, significance claims against published numbers are not meaningful.

Questions to authors
- Splits and comparability: What exact documents are in your train/dev/test? Can you report results on the standard split used by Li (2013) / Chen (2015) / Nguyen & Grishman (2016), or reproduce their systems on your split?
- Entities at test time: Do you use gold or predicted entity types for ACE test evaluation? If gold, can you provide results with predicted entities to compare fairly to methods that don’t rely on gold entities?
- Context window: What is the exact context window size n? How are sentence boundaries handled? Are tokens beyond the sentence clipped?
- Attention details: Please define the transformation producing we explicitly (matrix, dimensions). Are αw and αe separately normalized before summation? Is α re-normalized after α = αw + αe?
- Training details: Do you fine-tune word embeddings? Number of epochs, early stopping criterion, random seeds, number of runs, gradient clipping? Please report mean±std over multiple runs.
- ACE argument types: How do you handle Value/Time arguments in ACE for constructing gold attention? Are they included or ignored?

Additional comments (formatting/technical nits)
- ACE stands for Automatic Content Extraction, not “Automatic Context Extraction.”
- Numerous typos/formatting errors: malformed equations and indices (e.g., “[w−n , w−n 2 +1 …]”), inconsistent spacing in formulas, footnotes misplaced, “t methods,” etc.
- Reference list is highly inconsistent and in places corrupted (e.g., “Ahn.,? 2006,” “\Q2010\E,” incomplete venues, duplicated author names). Please fix to ACL style.
- Table 3: “Skip-CNN (2016) N/A 71.3” is missing precision and possibly F1; clarify what metric is shown. Indicate if all numbers are on the same split and whether they use gold entities.
- Claim “Only 10% of triggers in ACE 2005 are entities” needs a citation or your own count; also clarify how non-entity arguments (Time/Value) are treated.
- The attention visualization or qualitative examples would strengthen the argument that attention focuses on arguments and helps disambiguation.
- Consider evaluating per-event-type to show improvements on ambiguous triggers, which is central to the motivation.

Overall, while the idea of supervising attention with argument annotations is reasonable and the study is interesting, the paper in its current form suffers from significant clarity, evaluation, and presentation issues that limit confidence in the claims and reproducibility.
