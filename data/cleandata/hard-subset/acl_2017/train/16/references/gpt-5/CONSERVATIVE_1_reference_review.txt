Summary and contributions
- The paper proposes using supervised attention to inject argument information into event detection (ED). Attention over contextual words and entity types is trained to align with gold argument positions, under two strategies (S1: arguments only; S2: arguments and their neighbors via a Gaussian).
- A simple feed-forward classifier consumes the attention-weighted context representation to classify each token into ACE event types. The method is evaluated on ACE 2005 and modestly outperforms prior reported results; an augmentation experiment with FrameNet-derived data yields minor additional gains.
- Empirical analyses show that attending to arguments improves ED compared to unsupervised attention or entity-only attention, supporting the premise that arguments carry useful signal for trigger classification.

Strengths
- Clear and intuitive idea: supervising attention with gold argument spans is a straightforward way to bias the model toward context that should be informative for resolving ambiguous triggers. The comparison against unsupervised attention and entity-only attention helps isolate the contribution of argument information.
- Simple architecture and training objective: the method is easy to implement and does not require complex joint inference or structured decoding, which facilitates reproducibility in principle and may reduce engineering overhead compared to many joint EE systems.
- Empirical gains on ACE 2005: The best variant (ANN-S2) edges out prior results on the reported split and achieves notably higher precision than several baselines, aligning with the claim that argument-focused context improves specificity in trigger classification.
- Diagnostic oracle experiments: The “Gold” attention systems (using arguments at test time) convincingly demonstrate the potential upper bound of argument guidance for ED, which helps justify the overall direction.

Weaknesses
- Evaluation fairness and realism: The approach relies on entity type embeddings and uses ACE entity annotations; it is not clearly stated whether gold entity mentions/types are used at test time. If so, the comparison to baselines that do not rely on gold entities (e.g., some neural baselines) is not apples-to-apples, and the setup is not reflective of realistic deployment. Results with predicted entities (and a variant without entity features) are needed to assess robustness.
- Limited and potentially weak baseline: The main neural baseline (ANN with unsupervised attention) is underpowered relative to standard sequence models (CNNs/LSTMs with position features, or contemporary contextual encoders). Showing gains over a weak baseline does not convincingly establish the benefit of the proposed supervision. A stronger baseline without attention, and stronger sequence models under the same feature regime, are needed.
- Narrow empirical scope and small margins: All core results are on ACE 2005 with one split; no cross-domain or additional datasets (e.g., other ACE genres, Rich ERE/KBP) are included. The claimed improvements over the strongest prior (Skip-CNN) are small (≤0.4 F1), and the augmentation with FrameNet yields negligible gains (~0.2 F1). Statistical testing details (test type, sample definition) are not provided, making significance claims hard to assess.
- Methodological clarity gaps: Key details are underspecified or potentially problematic: the context window size n is not reported; how αw and αe are combined and normalized is ambiguous (α = αw + αe without renormalization can skew magnitudes); handling of candidates with no arguments (averaging attention over entities) seems ad hoc; training stops/seed variance and hyperparameter sensitivity (λ, window size, Gaussian parameters) are not analyzed. Without these, reproducibility and interpretability suffer.
- Lack of analysis of learned attention: Since the key novelty is supervised attention, the paper should assess alignment quality (e.g., how often high attention falls on arguments at test time), sensitivity to noisy/missing arguments, and error categories where attention helps or hurts. As is, it’s unclear whether the learned attention generalizes beyond encoding priors about entities rather than true argument-focused cues.

Questions to authors
- Do you use gold entity mentions/types at test time? If yes, please report results with predicted entities (and without any entity features) to assess realism and isolate the contribution of entity features vs. argument-supervised attention.
- What is the context window size n, and how sensitive are results to n? How is α normalized after combining αw and αe (is there a renormalization)? Please clarify.
- What is the exact data split for ACE 2005 (document IDs), and do all baselines use the same split and the same use of gold/predicted entities?
- How many runs were averaged, with what variance? Provide details of the statistical test (test type, unit of analysis, p-values).
- Can you compare against a stronger non-attention baseline (same features but no attention), and against stronger sequence models (e.g., CNN/LSTM with position features) under the same feature conditions?
- Please provide attention analysis: proportion of top-k attention mass on gold arguments at test time; performance breakdown for triggers with/without nearby arguments; qualitative error analysis where attention helps vs. hurts.
- For the FrameNet augmentation, what fraction of added instances are noisy? Can you show learning curves or ablations to quantify the tradeoff between added positives and label noise?

Overall, while the idea is sensible and the preliminary results are encouraging, the experimental design and reporting leave important questions about fairness, robustness, and generalization. Strengthening the baselines, clarifying the use of entities, and providing deeper analyses of the learned attention would be necessary to support strong claims.
