Summary
This paper addresses event detection (ED) by explicitly leveraging argument annotations to supervise an attention mechanism. The model computes attention over contextual words and entity types for each trigger candidate, with two strategies to construct “gold” attention from annotated arguments: S1 (only argument tokens) and S2 (arguments plus nearby tokens with a Gaussian decay). The attention is trained jointly with a feed-forward classifier via an auxiliary loss. Experiments on ACE 2005 show that supervised attention improves over an unsupervised-attention baseline and achieves small gains over strong prior systems. Additional experiments augment training with FrameNet-derived events.

Main contributions (as I see them)
- Primary: Empirical demonstration that supervising attention with gold argument locations yields consistent and meaningful gains for ED over unsupervised attention and competitive baselines on ACE 2005.
- Secondary: A simple attention supervision formulation that combines word- and entity-type-based attention and two practical strategies (S1, S2) for constructing gold attention targets from argument annotations.
- Tertiary: Analyses comparing S1 vs. S2 and a small augmentation study with FrameNet-derived training data.

Strengths
- Clear motivation and problem framing: The paper identifies a plausible reason why prior joint ED/AE approaches yield limited ED gains (imbalance and indirect use of arguments) and proposes a direct, test-time-agnostic way to exploit arguments via attention supervision. The argument is intuitive and well-illustrated (e.g., “fired” ambiguity).
- Simple, general method: The supervised attention loss is easy to implement, does not require changing the inference-time inputs (no arguments needed at test time), and should be reusable across IE settings where argument spans are available in training data.
- Solid empirical improvements: On ACE 2005, supervised attention (especially S2) improves substantially over the unsupervised-attention baseline (F1: 71.7 vs. 64.6). Compared with prior reported systems, the best model slightly improves SOTA F1 (71.7 vs. ~71.3) and notably increases precision, consistent with the hypothesis that focusing on argument-related context reduces spurious triggers.
- Useful ablations and sanity checks: The “Correctness of Our Assumption” section separates the effect of using entities vs. arguments (ANN-ENT vs. ANN-Gold), supporting the claim that arguments, not just entity presence, are helpful. The S1 vs. S2 comparison provides insights into precision–recall trade-offs.
- Practical augmentation study: Although gains are modest, the FrameNet augmentation experiment is pragmatic and shows the approach remains effective when additional noisy supervision is introduced.

Weaknesses
- Limited novelty relative to supervised attention literature and prior ED/AE integration: While the application to ED is valuable, the idea of supervising attention using available annotations (e.g., Mi et al., 2016) is known. The formulation (squared error to a target attention) and the architecture (dot-product attention with a feed-forward classifier) are straightforward adaptations; the contribution is more empirical than methodological.
- Fairness and realism of entity usage: The model uses gold entity type annotations for ACE (Section 3.1.2). Many prior ED works either avoid gold entities or compare both gold and predicted-entity settings. It is unclear whether all compared baselines used gold entities, and how much of the gain stems from using entity-type attention. This threatens comparability and external validity (what happens with predicted entities?).
- Incomplete ablations and analysis: Key components lack thorough ablation:
  - Effect of removing the entity-type attention channel (only word attention).
  - Sensitivity to λ and attention window/hyperparameters; choice of squared error vs. KL divergence.
  - Variance across random seeds; the t-test is mentioned without details (number of runs, metrics per run).
  - Per-type performance to verify the hypothesized benefits on ambiguous triggers.
  Attention visualizations would also help substantiate that the model focuses on arguments at test time.
- Baseline strength and architecture choice: The core ED model is a shallow ANN with fixed-length context and no sequence encoder (no CNN/RNN/transformer). It is unsurprising that a supervised attention signal helps such a model a lot, but it remains unclear whether the gains hold when plugged into stronger contextual encoders (e.g., BiLSTM/CNN models that already capture position and dependency cues). A direct comparison by adding the same supervised attention objective to a modern encoder would strengthen claims.
- FrameNet augmentation adds little and lacks quality control: The FN mapping and entity labeling introduce noise, but there is no analysis of label quality, overlap with ACE, or per-type effects. The overall F1 gain is marginal (71.7 → 71.9), making it hard to draw strong conclusions.

Questions to Authors
- Entity supervision: Are gold entity types used at test time on ACE 2005? If so, can you report results with predicted entities (e.g., from RPI system) for a more realistic setting and to compare fairly with baselines that do not use gold entities?
- Variance and significance: How many random seeds/runs were used for each system? Please report mean±std and clarify how the t-test was computed.
- Ablations: What is the impact of removing entity-type attention (word-only) or word attention (entity-only)? How sensitive are results to λ, attention window size, and the Gaussian parameters? Have you tried KL divergence or cross-entropy for attention supervision?
- Stronger encoders: What happens if you replace the ANN with a BiLSTM/CNN (e.g., skip-CNN or BiLSTM) and add your supervised attention? Do gains persist over those stronger baselines?
- Attention behavior: Can you provide qualitative attention maps on dev examples, especially for ambiguous triggers, to show the model focuses on argument tokens at test time?
- Data splits and comparability: Do your train/dev/test splits and feature setups strictly match those of the cited baselines (e.g., use of gold entities, preprocessing)? If not, please align or provide matched re-runs.
- FrameNet augmentation: How are frames mapped to ACE types, and what is the estimated noise level? Which ACE types benefit most from FN data?

Additional comments/suggestions for improvement
- Include per-type F1 and an error analysis highlighting where supervised attention helps/hurts.
- Clarify the construction of we (mapping w into entity space) and provide more implementation details to aid reproducibility (context window n, tokenizer, pretraining corpus size, learning rate schedule).
- Consider reweighting joint ED/AE training or knowledge distillation from an AE model as baselines to directly address your stated imbalance concern.
