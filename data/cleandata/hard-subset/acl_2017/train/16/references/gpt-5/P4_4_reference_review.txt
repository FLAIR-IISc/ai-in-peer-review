Summary
The paper proposes a neural event detection (ED) model that explicitly leverages annotated argument information via supervised attention. The core idea is to construct “gold” attention distributions from argument annotations during training and to train an attention mechanism (over contextual words and entity types) to match these distributions while jointly optimizing the trigger classification objective. Two supervision strategies are explored: S1 focuses attention on argument tokens only; S2 spreads attention to argument tokens and nearby words via a Gaussian kernel. Experiments on ACE 2005 show that supervised attention improves over an unsupervised-attention baseline and compares favorably to prior pipeline and joint models. The authors also augment training with FrameNet-derived events and report further gains.

Main contributions (as assessed)
1) A supervised attention framework that uses gold argument annotations to guide attention for ED, improving test-time ED without requiring arguments at inference time.
2) Empirical evidence, via controlled ablations (oracle attention and supervision strategies), that argument information is a strong signal for ED, especially for ambiguous triggers.
3) Competitive or superior results on ACE 2005 compared to cited contemporaneous baselines, with analysis of precision/recall trade-offs and augmentation using FrameNet.

Strengths
- Clear, focused problem framing: The paper identifies a plausible gap in prior joint models (arguments benefiting AE more than ED) and formulates a direct, test-time–agnostic way to transfer argument information to ED through supervised attention. The setup and motivation are coherent.
- Method simplicity and concreteness: The training signal for attention is well defined (two strategies for constructing gold attention), and the joint loss is straightforward to implement. The approach integrates naturally with a standard ANN classifier and uses only annotated arguments during training.
- Convincing ablations about argument utility: The inclusion of oracle systems (ANN-Gold1/2) and a control using entities only (ANN-ENT) isolates the effect of arguments versus entities. These comparisons substantiate the claim that arguments, not just entities, improve ED.
- Empirical gains and precision/recall analysis: Supervised attention variants (especially S2) outperform the unsupervised attention baseline and reported baselines on ACE 2005 in F1. The paper carefully discusses why S1 yields higher precision and S2 higher recall, consistent with their supervision design.
- External data augmentation: The FrameNet augmentation experiment is a useful addition, showing that more (noisy) positive data enhances recall and overall F1, and giving a partial explanation for the weakness of unsupervised attention under data sparsity.

Weaknesses
- Limited novelty and modeling depth: The model is a shallow classifier with windowed attention over words and entity types. Compared to contemporaneous sequence models (e.g., BiLSTMs/CNNs) and modern attention architectures, the technical novelty is modest: supervising attention using gold argument positions is a direct application of multi-task/supervision ideas rather than a new architectural insight.
- Baseline coverage and fairness: The main baseline (ANN with unsupervised attention) is weak, and the paper lacks a strong non-attention baseline with the same inputs (e.g., no-attention windowed MLP/CNN) and stronger sequence models using comparable features. Some reported external baselines differ in assumptions/resources (e.g., domain adaptation, joint training), making direct comparisons difficult. The Skip-CNN result is partially reported (missing P/F1).
- Use of gold entity information and evaluation assumptions: The approach relies on annotated entity types as features. It is unclear whether test-time evaluation assumes gold entities or system-predicted entities; this materially affects comparability with prior work. The paper should clearly state the entity setting and ensure comparisons match it.
- Attention construction and loss details: The attention combination α = αw + αe is not stated to be re-normalized before being used for context pooling or compared to α*. Since αw and αe are softmax distributions, their sum is not a probability distribution; using it directly for weighted sums and MSE against normalized targets may be inconsistent. Additionally, the choice of squared error over distributions is suboptimal compared to cross-entropy/KL, and sensitivity to λ is not explored.
- Experimental rigor and reproducibility: The ACE split is randomly selected (though following prior work), but results over multiple random seeds/runs are not reported. The significance test is mentioned without details. Hyperparameter sensitivity and attention quality analysis (e.g., alignment accuracy with arguments) are missing. Code/data processing for FrameNet augmentation and the entity tagger use are not fully specified, which limits reproducibility.

Questions to Authors
- Do you use gold entity mentions/types at test time? If so, which compared systems also assume gold entities, and are all numbers in Table 3 under the same assumption?
- Is α re-normalized after α = αw + αe before computing cw/ce and before the attention loss? If not, how do you reconcile comparing a non-normalized α to a normalized α*? Did you test a normalized variant?
- Why choose squared error between attention distributions rather than cross-entropy/KL? Did you compare these losses?
- How are gold attention vectors constructed for negative trigger candidates (NA) that have no associated arguments in the sentence? Does averaging attention over entities bias the model toward entities even for non-triggers?
- How many runs and seeds were used to report means/variances? Please provide standard deviations or confidence intervals and details of the t-test (paired over instances or runs?).
- What is the evaluation protocol for trigger matching (token-level exact match vs. span-level) and subtype classification? Please clarify to ensure comparability.

Additional comments
- Please include a no-attention baseline with identical inputs to quantify the net contribution of attention. A sequence model baseline (BiLSTM/CNN) with the same features would also strengthen the case.
- An error analysis (e.g., per-event-type gains, ambiguous triggers like “fire”) and qualitative inspection of learned attention would help validate the mechanism.
- References need cleanup (formatting and completeness).
