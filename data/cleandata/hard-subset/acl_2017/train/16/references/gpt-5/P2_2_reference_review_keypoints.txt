- Proposes supervised attention for ACE-style event detection using gold argument annotations (S1: argument tokens, S2: Gaussian-smoothed) to shape trigger classification without joint inference.
- Contributions: direct training-time use of gold argument supervision for attention; empirical analysis showing supervised attention outperforms unsupervised/entity-only attention; FrameNet augmentation experiments.
- Simple, focused method that's easy to implement and does not require argument labels at test time.
- Strong ablation study comparing ANN, ANN-ENT, ANN-Gold1/2, and ANN-S1/S2, showing argument supervision drives gains.
- Competitive results on ACE 2005 with modest F1 improvements and reasonable S1/S2 precision–recall trade-offs.
- FrameNet augmentation sensibly increases recall (at slight precision cost), supporting claims about data sparsity and noisy positives.
- Reasonable discussion of why prior joint models may under-help ED due to annotation imbalance and pipeline pre-prediction effects.
- Limited novelty: approach is incremental relative to prior supervised-attention and joint EE work.
- Baselines and comparability issues: main neural baseline may be weak; some prior results lack complete metrics and identical settings are unclear.
- Potential unfairness: unclear whether gold entity mentions/types are used at test time, creating comparability and realism concerns.
- Missing methodological details: context window size, span-to-token attention mapping, computation of the "we" entity vector, and run variance reporting are terse or absent.
- Lack of qualitative analysis: no attention visualizations or error analysis to show how attention disambiguates triggers (e.g., polysemy cases).
- Modest absolute gains and limited generalization evidence beyond ACE 2005; unclear performance in low-resource or cross-domain settings.
- Question: Are gold entities/types used at test time, and are competing baselines matched in that setting?
- Question: What is the exact context window size n, sentence trimming/padding policy, and sensitivity to n?
- Question: How are multi-token argument spans converted to token-level gold attention in S1/S2?
- Question: How is the "we" vector for entity-type attention derived from the current word embedding (transformation/parameters)?
- Question: How many random seeds/runs were used, and are means, standard deviations, or confidence intervals available; how was the t-test performed?
- Question: Can the authors provide qualitative analyses/visualizations of attention distributions for correct vs. erroneous trigger predictions?
- Overall: Clean, practical idea with competitive but modest gains; clarifications, stronger baselines, and more analysis are needed to strengthen the paper.
