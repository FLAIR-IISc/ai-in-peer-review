Summary and claimed contributions
This paper tackles event detection (ED) by explicitly leveraging annotated argument information during training via supervised attention. The model computes attention over contextual words and entity types for each trigger candidate; the attention is guided by “gold” attention derived from argument annotations (two strategies: S1 only arguments; S2 arguments plus nearby words via a Gaussian). At test time, the model does not require arguments. On ACE2005, the supervised-attention variants outperform an unsupervised-attention baseline and several prior methods; the authors also show further gains when augmenting training with FrameNet-derived instances.

Main contributions as I see them:
1) An explicit, training-time supervision signal for attention using gold argument positions for ED, demonstrating that arguments can improve ED without joint inference at test time.
2) Two alternative supervision strategies (argument-only vs. argument+context) with empirical analysis of precision/recall trade-offs.
3) Empirical results on ACE2005 showing competitive-to-SOTA F1 and a straightforward augmentation with FrameNet events.

Strengths
- Clear, focused idea with practical value: Using supervised attention to inject argument-based supervision into ED is simple, intuitive, and decouples training-time use of arguments from test-time requirements. This addresses a known limitation of prior joint models that help AE more than ED.
- Empirical gains on a standard benchmark: The supervised attention models (ANN-S1/ANN-S2) show sizable improvements over the unsupervised-attention baseline (64.6 to 71.7 F1) and are at least competitive with, and in the authors’ reporting better than, several prior methods on ACE2005. The S1 vs. S2 comparison is informative, linking attention supervision scope to precision/recall trade-offs.
- Sensible data augmentation story: Incorporating FrameNet-derived instances further improves recall and F1, reinforcing the claim that ED is data-sparse and that the proposed attention supervision benefits from more positive examples.
- Conceptual generalizability: The approach (supervising attention with known supporting spans) is potentially applicable to other IE components where span-level annotations exist (e.g., relation arguments, slots), making the contribution broader than ED alone.

Weaknesses
- Baseline coverage and fairness: The primary baseline is a relatively weak unsupervised-attention MLP (ANN). There is no comparison against modern CNN/RNN baselines under the same setup (e.g., a strong CNN/RNN with no attention, or with self-attention) nor an “entity-aware” baseline that uses context-entity features without attention. This makes it hard to isolate how much improvement comes from supervised attention versus architectural choices. The Skip-CNN comparison in Table 3 is incomplete (missing precision/F1), and the paper does not ensure apples-to-apples comparisons or report variance across runs.
- Reliance on gold entities at test time: The model uses entity type embeddings in context. It appears experiments use ACE gold entity types at test time (as is common in some prior work), which overestimates real-world performance. There is no evaluation with predicted entities or an ablation removing entity information to understand the contribution of the entity attention vs. word attention pathways.
- Technical clarity and missing details: Several implementation specifics are underspecified:
  - Attention definition and normalization: αw and αe are softmax-normalized over positions, but α = αw + αe is used without renormalization; the supervised target α* is normalized. This mismatch can affect the squared-error supervision. Please clarify whether α is renormalized.
  - The “we” vector is said to be “transforming w from the word space into the entity type space,” but the transformation (parameters, dimensionalities) is not fully defined.
  - Handling of candidates with no arguments: For S1/S2, candidates “without any annotated arguments in context” average entity attention. This includes negative (NA) candidates and positive candidates with distant arguments; this design choice could blur the supervision signal.
- Statistical claims without supporting evidence: The t-test claim (p ≤ 0.05) lacks details: number of runs, random seeds, and the distribution of scores. No standard deviations or confidence intervals are reported.
- Limited analysis: There is no error analysis, per-type breakdown, or inspection of learned attention. Given the centrality of attention supervision, qualitative/quantitative analyses (e.g., attention alignment with arguments for held-out data) would strengthen the case.
- Related work positioning: While the paper cites supervised attention (e.g., Mi et al., 2016), it could better contrast with alternatives that exploit argument signals during training (e.g., multi-task objectives predicting argument indicators, contrastive or span-level supervision) and with joint ED-AE models that incorporate argument cues earlier in trigger pre-prediction.

Questions to authors
- Do you renormalize α = αw + αe before computing cw/ce and before the supervised attention loss? If not, why is squared error appropriate when α* sums to 1 and α sums to 2?
- What exactly is the transformation producing we (dimensions, parameters)? Is there a separate matrix from Ww?
- What entity information is used at test time—gold ACE entity types or predicted types? If gold, can you report results with predicted entities to assess robustness?
- Can you provide stronger in-house baselines: (a) ANN without attention (average/concat pooling), (b) a CNN or BiLSTM baseline, and (c) an entity-aware baseline without attention? This would help attribute gains to supervised attention.
- For S1/S2, how are gold attention targets constructed for negative candidates (NA)? Are there any negatives that co-occur in sentences with other events and thus arguments—how is supervision handled then?
- How many random seeds were used and what are mean/std across runs? Please substantiate the significance claim.
- Can you include an ablation removing entity attention (αe) to quantify its contribution relative to word-based attention?

Overall assessment
Soundness: Reasonable, but some technical details and evaluation choices need clarification. Originality: Moderate; supervising attention with argument spans is a natural extension of supervised attention ideas to ED, and the framing (arguments used only at training) is appealing. Significance: Moderate; the empirical gains on ACE2005 are promising, but stronger baselines and realistic settings (predicted entities) are needed to fully establish impact. Clarity: Generally understandable but with several missing details and reference/reporting issues.

With the above clarifications and stronger baselines, this work could make a solid contribution.
