Summary and contributions
This paper proposes a neural event detection (ED) model that explicitly leverages annotated event arguments through supervised attention. The key idea is to supervise token-level attention weights so that argument tokens (and optionally their neighborhoods) receive higher attention when classifying a trigger candidate. Two supervision strategies are explored: S1 (attention mass on argument tokens only) and S2 (Gaussian-smoothed attention over arguments and nearby words). The model learns contextual representations by combining attention-weighted word and entity-type embeddings and feeds them to an MLP classifier. Experiments on ACE 2005 show consistent gains over unsupervised attention and reported prior systems; augmenting training with FrameNet-derived events yields further improvements.

As I see them, the main contributions are:
1) A simple, effective supervision scheme for attention in ED using gold argument annotations, with two strategies (S1/S2), yielding state-of-the-art F1 on ACE 2005.
2) Empirical evidence that argument information, not merely entity presence, is crucial for ED, via clean ablations (unsupervised attention vs entity-averaged attention vs gold attention).
3) A practical data augmentation pathway using FrameNet frames (core frame elements as arguments), demonstrating robustness to additional noisy supervision.

Strengths
- Clear and targeted methodological novelty: Supervising attention with gold arguments is a direct, well-motivated way to inject argument information into trigger classification. Compared to typical joint models that indirectly influence ED via global inference, this paper operationalizes the intuition in a minimal change to a standard ED pipeline. The two strategies (S1 vs S2) capture complementary inductive biases (precision vs recall), and results reflect that.

- Strong empirical gains with careful ablations: The ablation study (ANN vs ANN-ENT vs ANN-Gold1/2) convincingly shows that (a) unsupervised attention underperforms; (b) “entity-only” attention is insufficient; and (c) explicit argument supervision is key. The main models (ANN-S1/S2) outperform both symbolic and neural prior work on the commonly used ACE 2005 split, with particularly notable precision improvements, which is valuable for downstream extraction pipelines.

- Practicality and simplicity: The approach is lightweight (adds a supervised attention loss), avoids complex global inference or structured prediction, and thus should be easy to integrate into existing ED systems.

- Data augmentation with FrameNet: Reusing frame elements as arguments for attention supervision is a clever way to mitigate data sparsity. The paper shows consistent, statistically meaningful improvements when adding this training signal, lending external validity to the approach.

- Sound problem motivation: The analysis of why prior joint models often help AE more than ED (imbalance in supervision and lack of argument use in trigger pre-prediction) is plausible, and the proposed method directly addresses this gap.

Weaknesses
- Dependence on gold argument annotations during training limits generality: The approach presumes access to argument spans in training data. Many ED corpora either lack argument annotations or have them only for a subset of types/domains. The paper stops short of evaluating whether supervising attention with noisy/predicted arguments preserves gains, which is critical for practicality beyond ACE.

- Baselines and architecture strength: The core encoder is a shallow attention over a fixed window with dot-product scoring and an MLP classifier. This is weaker than contemporary sequence encoders (e.g., BiLSTM/CNN with position features, or syntactic/contextualized encoders). It is unclear whether the improvements would persist over a stronger baseline; the claim that unsupervised attention fails due to data sparsity may be confounded by architectural limitations.

- Entity usage and fairness of comparison: The model leverages annotated entity types in ACE; it is not clearly stated whether gold entities are used at test time. If so, the comparison with methods that use predicted entities (or none) is not apples-to-apples. Even if prior work also used gold entities, the paper should clearly disclose and, ideally, report results with predicted entities.

- Limited analysis: The paper lacks per-event-type results, coverage analysis (how often arguments fall within the attention window), attention quality inspection (are high-attention words indeed arguments?), and sensitivity studies (λ, window size, Gaussian σ/µ). The claim that S2 improves recall due to surrounding words is plausible but not substantiated with detailed error analysis.

- FrameNet augmentation details: The procedure uses an external joint IE system to annotate entities on FN data, but no quality control or noise analysis is provided. Potential domain or label leakage is not discussed. Statistical testing is mentioned but not described (what test, which systems).

Questions to authors
- Do you use gold entity mentions/types at test time on ACE 2005? If not, which NER/typing system is used, and what is the delta vs using gold? Please report both settings for fair comparison.

- What fraction of training trigger candidates have at least one gold argument within the fixed context window? How does performance vary with/without in-window arguments?

- How sensitive are results to λ, window size n, and Gaussian parameters (σ, window width) in S2? Please provide development curves.

- Can the supervised attention be trained with automatically predicted arguments (from a separate AE model) and retain most gains? This would demonstrate applicability when gold arguments are unavailable.

- Please provide per-event-type P/R/F1 and examples of attention visualizations to substantiate that the mechanism focuses on arguments and helps disambiguate polysemous triggers.

- What statistical test did you use to establish significance (paired bootstrap/approximate randomization)? Against which baselines?

Overall, the paper presents a simple and effective idea with solid empirical gains and a convincing ablation that highlights the value of argument supervision for ED. Addressing the concerns about reliance on gold arguments/entities and strengthening baselines/analysis would substantially increase its impact and generality.
