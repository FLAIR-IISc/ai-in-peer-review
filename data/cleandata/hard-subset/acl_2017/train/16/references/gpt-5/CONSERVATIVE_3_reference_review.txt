Summary and contributions
This paper tackles ACE-style event detection (trigger identification and classification) by explicitly leveraging annotated argument information via supervised attention. The core idea is to bias an attention mechanism to focus on argument tokens (and optionally nearby words) during training, using gold arguments to construct target attention distributions. A shallow ANN classifier consumes a context representation obtained by attention-weighted sums over word and entity-type embeddings. On ACE 2005, the method outperforms an unsupervised-attention baseline and several older baselines; adding automatically harvested FrameNet instances yields modest additional gains.

As I see it, the main contributions are:
1) An empirical demonstration that supervising attention with gold argument indicators improves ED over an unsupervised attention baseline and some prior systems on ACE 2005.
2) A simple, task-specific supervision strategy for attention (argument-only vs. argument-plus-neighborhood) that does not require arguments at test time.

Strengths
- Clear hypothesis and mechanism: The paper articulates a concrete, intuitive hypothesis—argument information is useful for ED—and operationalizes it with supervised attention. The two supervision strategies (S1, S2) are easy to understand and implement.

- Some targeted ablations: The comparisons among ANN (unsupervised attention), ANN-ENT (entity-only averaging), and oracle variants using gold attentions (ANN-Gold1/2) are useful to isolate the value of argument supervision versus generic entity information and to illustrate an upper bound.

- Practical training-time supervision: Using gold arguments to supervise attention but not requiring them at test is a sensible way to exploit existing annotations without changing inference-time requirements.

- Additional data experiment: Augmenting with FrameNet frames (with core frame elements treated as arguments) demonstrates the approach can incorporate extra supervision; the gains on recall are consistent with the intuition about more positive samples.

Weaknesses
- Limited novelty: Supervised attention is not new, and the adaptation here—supervising attention toward gold arguments—is a straightforward application. The classifier itself is a simple feed-forward model with dot-product attention and no sequence modeling. As such, the methodological contribution is incremental.

- Outdated and weak baselines; missing strong contemporaries: The strongest comparisons are to pre-Transformer systems (e.g., Li 2013; Nguyen 2016; Skip-CNN). The in-house ANN baseline is notably weak (64.6 F1), and improvements over such a baseline are not compelling. There are no comparisons to stronger encoders (BiLSTMs/CRFs) or modern pretrained models (e.g., BERT/SpanBERT-based ED, DYGIE++, OneIE), nor is the proposed attention supervision layered atop a stronger encoder to show additive benefit. Claims of state-of-the-art performance are not convincing without these.

- Evaluation and reproducibility concerns: The paper reports “randomly selected” dev/test splits without listing document IDs or random seeds, hindering reproducibility and fairness. It is unclear whether all compared baselines used the same split and resources. Statistical significance claims (t-test) lack details about the unit of analysis and whether results are averaged over multiple runs; no variances or multi-seed results are reported.

- Reliance on gold entities and argument-rich supervision: The model relies on annotated entity types as inputs. Using gold entities at test time is unrealistic in many settings; results with predicted entities are not reported. The approach’s supervision requires gold argument spans, which are not available in many ED datasets, limiting applicability. It is also unclear how performance changes when arguments lie outside the truncated context window.

- Methodological details and analyses are thin: Fixed context truncation may discard arguments; no analysis quantifies how often this happens or its impact. The Gaussian smoothing parameters and window are set ad hoc, with no sensitivity study. No qualitative analyses (e.g., attention visualizations) or error analyses are provided to support interpretability claims or to diagnose failure modes. Training details (epochs/early stopping) are sparse.

- Potential fairness issues in external data augmentation: In the FrameNet augmentation, arguments are mapped from core frame elements and entities are added using an external system. The mapping procedure, coverage, and noise characteristics are not rigorously evaluated; gains are modest, raising questions about the robustness of the pipeline.

Questions to authors (optional)
- Do you use gold ACE entity mentions/types at test time? If yes, please report results with predicted entities to reflect a realistic setting and compare fairly to baselines that do not assume gold entities.

- Please provide exact train/dev/test document lists and random seeds to make the results reproducible. Were all external baselines re-run on your split, or are numbers copied from their papers?

- What is the unit and protocol for your t-tests? Are results averaged over multiple random initializations? Please report mean±std over multiple runs.

- Sensitivity analyses: How sensitive are results to λ, the Gaussian window size and σ, and the context window length? What fraction of triggers has arguments outside the truncated context, and how does performance degrade in those cases?

- Can you show results with a stronger encoder (e.g., BiLSTM/CNN or a pretrained Transformer) and demonstrate that supervised attention still provides gains?

- How many triggers in ACE 2005 have no annotated arguments within the sentence/context window? What is the impact on those cases, especially under S1 vs. S2?

Overall, while the idea is plausible and the results against older baselines are encouraging, the novelty is limited and the experimental validation is not sufficiently rigorous against strong, established baselines and realistic settings. I cannot recommend acceptance in its current form.
