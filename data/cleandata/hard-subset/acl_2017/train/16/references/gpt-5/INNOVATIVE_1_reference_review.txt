Summary and Contributions
This paper proposes a supervised attention mechanism for event detection (ED) that explicitly leverages annotated argument information during training. The key idea is to construct “gold” attention maps from labeled arguments and train the attention module to focus on those positions, thereby directly injecting argument cues into trigger classification. A simple feed-forward ED model consumes context representations built via attention over words and entity types. Two supervision strategies are explored: S1 (attention concentrated on argument tokens) and S2 (a Gaussian spread around arguments). Experiments on ACE 2005, with and without FrameNet-based augmentation, show improvements over prior pipeline and joint systems.

As I see it, the main contributions are:
1) Conceptual: introducing argument-supervised attention as a direct, explicit way to use argument annotations to improve ED, contrasting with joint models that often fail to boost ED.
2) Empirical: consistent gains on ACE 2005, especially in precision, with analysis comparing supervised vs. unsupervised attention and the effect of FrameNet augmentation.
3) Practical/architectural: a simple, training-time supervision signal (gold attention) that can be plugged into a lightweight model and potentially other ED architectures.

Strengths
1) Clear, novel use of supervision: Directly supervising attention with argument annotations is an elegant and bold idea for ED. It shifts from indirect dependency exploitation (joint inference) to a targeted signal that aligns well with how humans disambiguate triggers. This is a meaningful conceptual contribution likely to generalize to other IE settings.
2) Strong empirical signal on ACE 2005: The supervised attention variants (ANN-S1/S2) substantially outperform the unsupervised attention baseline and reach or surpass reported SOTA, with notably higher precision. The comparison against representative pipeline and joint systems strengthens the case that the method helps where joint ED/AE often under-delivers for ED.
3) Sensible design choices and analysis: Two supervision strategies (argument-only vs. argument + neighborhood) provide useful insights into precision–recall trade-offs (S1 higher precision, S2 higher recall). The ablations (ANN vs. ANN-S1/S2) isolate the benefit of argument supervision. The FrameNet augmentation experiment supports the hypothesis that data sparsity hinders unsupervised attention.
4) Simplicity and potential for broader impact: The method is easy to implement and does not depend on heavy structured inference. The supervision signal (gold attention) is interpretable and could be incorporated into more powerful architectures or related tasks (e.g., relation extraction).
5) Plausible diagnosis of prior joint models: The argument that joint models are biased by annotation imbalance and pre-prediction pipelines is well-motivated and supported by the reported numbers and qualitative discussion.

Weaknesses
1) Evaluation setting and fairness: It appears the model uses gold entity mentions/types at test time (as is common in some ACE settings). This inflates realism and complicates comparison to baselines that may use predicted entities. Please clarify and, if possible, report results with predicted entities to assess robustness.
2) Baseline strength and architecture limitations: The primary baseline (ANN with unsupervised attention) is a relatively weak feed-forward model. Stronger modern baselines (e.g., BiLSTM/CNN encoders without attention, or attention over contextualized embeddings) are missing. It is unclear how much of the gain is attributable to the supervision vs. architecture choice. A comparison to a non-attention strong encoder or to joint models augmented with auxiliary argument prediction would strengthen claims.
3) Potential distribution mismatch and analysis gap: Supervising attention on gold arguments may cause the model to over-rely on argument positions seen in training, without evidence that it attends to comparable cues at test time (when no arguments are available). There is no quantitative alignment analysis (e.g., correlation between learned attention and true arguments at test) or qualitative visualization to demonstrate that the learned attention behaves as intended.
4) Reproducibility and statistical rigor: Details on the data split (random vs. standard splits), number of runs, variance, and exact significance testing are sparse. Some reported baselines (e.g., Skip-CNN) are incomplete (missing precision), and different works often use different ACE setups. A clearer apples-to-apples comparison and multiple seeds with mean±std would increase confidence.
5) FrameNet augmentation pipeline: The extra data requires mapping frame elements to arguments and automatic entity labeling. While reasonable, this introduces noise and domain mismatch; the observed gains are modest. More analysis on the quality of the FN-to-ACE mapping and error categories would help.

Questions to Authors (Optional)
- Do you use gold or predicted entity mentions/types at test time? If gold, can you provide results with predicted entities to assess robustness?
- How sensitive are results to λ, the context window size, and the Gaussian parameters (µ, σ, window) in S2? Please provide a sensitivity analysis.
- Can you quantify whether the learned attention at test time aligns with argument positions (e.g., attention mass on true arguments) and provide some visualizations?
- How does your approach compare to (i) a strong BiLSTM/CNN encoder without attention, and (ii) a multi-task model that predicts argument indicators as an auxiliary task (without supervising attention directly)?
- What fraction of triggers in ACE have no annotated arguments within your fixed window, and how does performance vary across such cases?
- Will you release code and the processed FrameNet-derived training set to facilitate reproducibility?

Overall, I find the core idea novel and impactful for ED, and I am inclined to recommend acceptance despite the evaluation and baseline limitations.
