Summary and Contributions
This paper targets event detection (ED) and proposes to explicitly exploit argument annotations to supervise an attention mechanism that guides a trigger classifier toward informative context. The model builds attention over both words and entity types around a trigger candidate, and trains a joint loss to match “gold” attention vectors derived from argument annotations (two strategies: argument-only; argument plus Gaussian-decayed neighborhood). Experiments on ACE 2005 show gains over prior systems; augmenting training with FrameNet-derived events further improves recall.

As I see them, the main contributions are:
1) Conceptual: Reframing argument annotations as supervision for trigger-centric attention in ED, explicitly using arguments to guide ED rather than relying on joint EE to indirectly help ED. This is a crisp and potentially general idea.
2) Method: A simple supervised-attention ED architecture combining word and entity-type contexts, with two supervision strategies (argument-only and Gaussian neighborhood) and a joint loss.
3) Empirical: Competitive/best ACE 2005 results, analyses indicating unsupervised attention underperforms under data sparsity, and that argument supervision substantially boosts ED; additional gains with FN augmentation.

Strengths
- Novel, focused use of argument supervision for ED: The paper makes a clear and bold design choice—use arguments to supervise attention during ED training—addressing a known shortcoming of joint models (they help AE much more than ED). This explicit supervision signal is a neat idea likely to generalize to other IE tasks.
- Simple and practical: The attention supervision integrates cleanly into a straightforward neural ED model and requires no heavy global inference or complex pipelines. It should be easy to reproduce and extend.
- Evidence that attention needs supervision under data sparsity: The unsupervised-attention baseline performs poorly, while supervised attention variants achieve strong gains. This supports the argument that ED’s limited data makes unsupervised attention unreliable, motivating the proposed supervision.
- Precision/recall trade-offs are well characterized: The two supervision strategies exhibit intuitive behavior (argument-only yields higher precision; argument+neighbors yields higher recall), offering useful guidance for application-dependent choices.
- External data augmentation: Incorporating FN-derived events (after mapping frame elements to arguments) demonstrates the approach can benefit from weakly/noisily supervised data while still improving overall F1.

Weaknesses
- Limited methodological novelty beyond the supervision signal: While the central idea is nice, the model itself is quite basic (context windows, dot-product attentions, simple fusion of word/entity attentions). Alternatives like contextual encoders (biLSTM/CNN/self-attention) or richer attention formulations are not explored, which weakens the technical depth.
- Comparison fairness and experimental clarity: It’s unclear whether gold entity mentions/types are used at test time in the main ACE experiments. The paper states it exploits annotated entity information “following existing work,” but does not specify test-time settings. If gold entities are used, this should be made explicit and aligned fairly with baselines; if predicted, the tagger and its accuracy should be reported. This affects the credibility of the reported SOTA.
- Reporting gaps and missing analyses: Some tables are incomplete or inconsistent (e.g., Table 3 shows “N/A” for precision in a baseline; Table 2 is referenced but not shown in the provided text). The claimed t-test lacks details (what pairs, what variance assumptions). There is no per-class breakdown, no error analysis, and limited ablation beyond the attention supervision factor. The paper would benefit from qualitative attention visualizations to confirm that attention indeed focuses on arguments.
- Ad-hoc choices with limited sensitivity study: The Gaussian neighborhood supervision (S2) uses default μ/σ/window without sensitivity analysis; the choice of linearly summing word and entity attentions is not justified relative to other fusion options. The trade-off parameter λ is fixed per strategy without broader exploration.
- Scope vs. claims: The diagnosis that joint models underperform on ED because they implicitly favor AE is plausible, but the paper does not present controlled experiments against strong modern joint architectures under identical settings to decisively attribute the gap to the proposed reasons.

Questions to Authors (for rebuttal)
- What entity annotations are used at test time in the main ACE 2005 results—gold or predicted? If predicted, which system and with what performance? Please ensure fairness relative to baselines.
- Please provide the full results for Table 2 (ANN, ANN-ENT, ANN-Gold1/2), including P/R/F1, to substantiate the argument vs. entity effect.
- Can you add qualitative attention heatmaps to demonstrate that supervised attention focuses on arguments (and, for S2, on nearby words)?
- How sensitive are results to λ and to the Gaussian parameters (μ, σ, window)? Any evidence that other fusion schemes for αw and αe (e.g., learned gating) help?
- Have you tried adding supervised attention on top of a stronger contextual encoder (e.g., biLSTM/CNN)? If so, does the supervision still yield gains?

Recommendation
Overall, I find the central idea—using argument annotations to supervise attention for ED—novel enough for this domain and practically impactful, despite the simplicity of the model and some reporting gaps. With clarifications on entity usage and improved analysis, this could be a solid contribution that opens a useful direction. I lean toward acceptance for its conceptual novelty and potential influence, while encouraging the authors to strengthen experimental clarity and analysis.
