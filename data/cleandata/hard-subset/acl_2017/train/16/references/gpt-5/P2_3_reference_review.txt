Summary
This paper addresses event detection (ED) in ACE 2005 by explicitly exploiting event argument annotations to guide attention over context. The model represents each token as a trigger candidate and computes attention over surrounding words and entity types via a simple similarity-based mechanism. Crucially, it supervises the attention using gold argument locations: (S1) attending only to argument tokens, and (S2) attending to arguments and nearby words with a Gaussian decay. The attention loss is combined with the ED classification loss. Experiments on ACE 2005 show substantial gains over an unsupervised-attention baseline and improvements over reported prior systems. The authors also augment training with FrameNet-derived events (processed to add arguments/entities) and report further gains.

Main contributions (as I see them)
1) Introducing supervised attention for ED using gold argument spans as supervision, showing that argument-aware attention improves trigger classification over unsupervised attention and reported baselines.
2) A careful comparison of two supervision strategies (argument-only vs. argument-plus-neighborhood), with ablations (ANN, ANN-ENT, ANN-Gold) that support the claim that arguments, not just entities, are beneficial.
3) An attempt to leverage FrameNet-derived data to alleviate sparsity for ED and showing that the proposed approach benefits from this augmentation.

Strengths
- Clear central idea with direct supervision signal: The paper translates a well-motivated intuition—arguments provide strong cues for trigger disambiguation—into an explicit supervisory signal on attention. This is novel in the ED setting (prior “joint” models implicitly use argument information but do not directly supervise attention to arguments), and it is simple to implement and explain.

- Solid empirical gains and reasonable controls: The supervised-attention variants (ANN-S1/S2) substantially outperform the unsupervised attention baseline. The ANN-ENT and ANN-Gold ablations support the claim that arguments (beyond entity presence) are crucial, and that the supervised mechanism leverages them. The gains over published systems are competitive to state of the art, with statistical testing reported.

- Practical training objective: The squared-loss attention supervision is an intuitive auxiliary objective equivalent to multi-task supervision of a soft argument mask. It is likely robust with modest data sizes and avoids the instability of joint inference pipelines.

- Simple architecture and reproducible hyperparameters: The model is lightweight (feed-forward with attention over a fixed window) and the hyperparameters for embeddings, hidden size, dropout, and λ are reported, which can facilitate reproduction.

- Sensible exploration of attention targets: Comparing S1 vs. S2 and analyzing their precision/recall trade-offs provides insight into how localized vs. broader contextual supervision affects ED.

Weaknesses
- Limited novelty relative to joint learning and prior supervised attention: While supervising attention with gold alignments is known in other tasks (e.g., NMT), the main novelty here is its application to ED. The architectural choices (dot-product attention over a fixed window; summing word and entity attentions) are straightforward, and the paper does not compare against equally simple alternative auxiliary tasks (e.g., predicting argument masks or roles directly as an auxiliary head) or stronger modern encoders (biLSTMs/CNNs) with the same supervision.

- Baseline strength and fairness: The primary baseline (ANN with unsupervised attention) performs weakly. There is no in-house reimplementation of stronger neural baselines (e.g., CNN/LSTM without attention, CNN with distance-to-entity features, or joint RNN models) on the same split. Some reported baselines (e.g., Skip-CNN) are incomplete (only one metric shown in Table 3) and may not be strictly comparable due to different splits/settings. This makes it harder to isolate the incremental value of the proposed supervision over a strong encoder.

- Use of gold entities and supervision realism: The model uses entity type embeddings of contextual entities; it appears these are gold ACE entities at test time, which inflates realism and may overstate practical performance. Moreover, for negative trigger candidates or candidates lacking annotated arguments in the window, the supervision backs off to averaging entity attention, which may inject noise; the impact is not analyzed.

- Insufficient detail on FrameNet augmentation: The mapping from FrameNet frames to ACE event types is delegated to prior work, but for this paper the crucial step is adding arguments and entity labels to that data. The process for aligning frame elements to ACE argument roles/types and the quality/noise of automatically recognized entities are not quantified. Without a noise analysis, it is hard to interpret the modest gains and precision drop.

- Missing qualitative analysis and diagnostics: The paper lacks attention visualizations, per-type breakdowns, or error analyses to substantiate the claim that the model truly focuses on arguments and disambiguates polysemous triggers. No ablation is provided for combining αw and αe (e.g., learned weighting vs. fixed sum), for window size, or for removing entity attention altogether.

Questions to authors
1) Do you assume gold entity mentions/types at test time? If not, what NER/typing system is used, and with what performance? How sensitive are results to entity quality?

2) How do you construct gold attention for negative (NA) trigger candidates and for sentences with multiple overlapping events/arguments? If a candidate is NA but near arguments of another event, does the supervision encourage attention to those arguments?

3) What is the fixed context window size n, and how sensitive are results to n? Have you tried sequence encoders (CNN/biLSTM) with the same attention supervision?

4) How is αw + αe chosen vs. learning a gating or concatenation? Can you report an ablation comparing word-only attention, entity-only attention, and learned fusion?

5) For FrameNet augmentation, please detail: (a) the mapping of frames to ACE types used, (b) how frame elements are aligned to ACE argument roles, (c) entity recognition/typing accuracy on FN data, and (d) the noise level (estimated false positives) of the added events.

6) Can you provide per-event-type P/R/F1 and a few attention heatmaps to illustrate that the model focuses on arguments and nearby words as intended?

Overall, the paper presents a clear and effective idea—supervising attention with gold arguments—for ED, with encouraging gains. Strengthening baselines, clarifying reliance on gold entities, and adding qualitative/diagnostic analyses would significantly improve the rigor and impact.
