Summary and claimed contributions
This paper tackles event detection (ED) and proposes to use annotated argument information explicitly via supervised attention. The model treats each token as a trigger candidate, computes attention over contextual words and entity types conditioned on the candidate, and trains the attention to match “gold” attention distributions derived from argument annotations. Two supervision strategies are explored: S1 (only argument tokens get attention) and S2 (arguments plus nearby words via a Gaussian). The attention supervision is combined with the ED cross-entropy loss. Experiments on ACE05 show improvements over prior systems; an augmentation with FrameNet-derived data yields small further gains.

As I see them, the main contributions are:
1) A simple, explicit way to inject argument supervision into ED via supervised attention, showing consistent gains over a variety of baselines.
2) An empirical study demonstrating that argument-based supervision helps ED beyond entity information alone, with ablations contrasting gold, supervised, and unsupervised attention.
3) A practical exploration of leveraging FrameNet as extra training data for ED (albeit with modest impact).

Strengths
- Clear, focused idea with direct supervision: The use of gold arguments to supervise attention is conceptually clean, requires no changes to the ED label space, and is easy to integrate into standard neural classifiers. The approach is plausibly better aligned with ED than joint ED+AE models that often benefit AE more than ED. The paper’s analysis of why joint methods may under-serve ED (loss imbalance; trigger pre-prediction not using arguments) is reasonable and motivates the design.

- Solid empirical gains on a standard benchmark: On ACE05, the supervised attention model (ANN-S2) achieves F1 71.7, improving over several strong baselines (Liu FN-based 70.7; Nguyen joint 69.3; Skip-CNN 71.3). The precision/recall trade-off between S1 and S2 is also informative, and overall results suggest the method is competitive with or better than contemporaneous systems.

- Ablations substantiate the central claim: The comparison of ANN vs ANN-ENT vs ANN-Gold1/2 shows (i) unsupervised attention underperforms, (ii) entity-only attention is insufficient, and (iii) argument-focused signals are key. This isolates the source of improvement more convincingly than many EE papers.

- Implementation simplicity and portability: The method uses standard components (embeddings, a feed-forward classifier, attention losses), making it appealing for practitioners and easy to reproduce in principle. The explicit gold attention construction is straightforward and transparent (S1 and S2).

Weaknesses
- Baseline strength and fairness: The core ANN baseline (F1 64.6) is quite weak relative to modern CNN/RNN encoders, undercutting some internal comparisons. The supervised-attention model slightly edges Skip-CNN (71.7 vs 71.3), but the comparison is not apples-to-apples in architecture or features. It would strengthen the case to show gains when adding attention supervision on top of a stronger encoder (e.g., CNN/RNN/BiLSTM), and to control for identical inputs and resources across systems.

- Reliance on gold entities and argument annotations: The approach depends on entity type embeddings and gold entity mentions at test time (as is common in ACE ED evaluations) and requires gold argument annotations for attention supervision during training. This limits generality to settings where such supervision is available. Moreover, S1’s fallback (averaging attention over all entities when no argument lies in the context window) could inject noise; the impact is not quantified.

- Attention formulation leaves ambiguities: The paper computes αw and αe via separate softmaxes and sums them (α = αw + αe), but does not clarify whether α is renormalized before use or before computing the supervision loss. Since α* is normalized, comparing it to a sum of two distributions may misalign scales. This is a potential technical flaw unless addressed (e.g., via a final softmax). Also, transforming the current word embedding into “entity space” to attend over entity types is somewhat ad hoc and its contribution is not ablated.

- Limited architectural capacity and context handling: The context representation is a weighted sum over a fixed window, without sequence encoders or syntactic paths. ED often benefits from long-range and structural context; it’s unclear how often arguments fall outside the window (and how that affects supervision). Reporting window size, coverage of argument positions, and performance sensitivity would improve the study.

- Statistical testing and reporting details: The paper claims significant improvements (t-test, p ≤ 0.05) but does not specify the unit of analysis (documents vs sentences vs mentions) or variance estimation procedure, which is important given the small test set. Some comparisons (e.g., Skip-CNN) lack complete metrics. Reproducibility would benefit from code and split details.

Questions to authors
- How is α handled after α = αw + αe? Is there a renormalization step before computing cw/ce and the attention loss? If not, how do you reconcile scale mismatch with normalized gold α*?

- What is the fixed window size n, and what fraction of gold arguments for positive triggers fall within this window? How sensitive are results to window size?

- Do you use gold entity mentions and types at test time, and is this aligned with all compared baselines? Have you evaluated performance with automatically predicted entities?

- Can you report results when adding supervised attention on top of a stronger encoder (e.g., BiLSTM/CNN), to verify the benefit is additive to commonly used architectures?

- What is the exact setup for the significance test (test unit, number of runs/seeds, variance)? Are improvements over Skip-CNN and Liu FN-based significant under that test?

- For the FrameNet augmentation: how many examples are added, what is their noise rate, and how much do they contribute specifically to the supervised-attention loss (given arguments are derived from frame elements and entities from an external tagger)?

Overall assessment
The paper proposes a simple and well-motivated way to inject argument information into ED via supervised attention and shows competitive or better results on ACE05 with clear ablations. There are technical and experimental weaknesses (attention normalization, baseline strength, dependence on gold entities) that should be addressed, but the core idea is novel in this context and practically useful. With clarifications and stronger baselines, this would be a solid contribution.
