Summary and contributions
- The paper proposes to exploit annotated argument information explicitly for event detection (ED) via a supervised attention mechanism. Gold “attention” distributions are constructed from ACE argument annotations (two strategies: S1 argument-only; S2 argument-plus-neighborhood), and the attention module is trained jointly with a simple feed-forward ED classifier.
- The authors report improved ED performance on ACE 2005 versus several published baselines, and show additional small gains when augmenting training with automatically extracted FrameNet events.
- The paper argues that joint ED+AE approaches underutilize argument information for ED and provides a focused alternative that supplies argument supervision directly to ED.

Strengths
- Clear problem focus and rationale: The paper articulates a concrete hypothesis—argument information is directly useful for ED—and designs a simple mechanism to inject that information. This is a sensible, targeted alternative to joint models and aligns with prior observations about ambiguous triggers.
- Simple, interpretable supervision signal: Supervising attention with argument distributions is straightforward to implement and interpret. The S1 vs. S2 comparison provides some insight into the trade-off between precision (argument-only) and recall (wider context).
- Positive results on ACE 2005: The reported gains over the authors’ ANN baseline are substantial (about +7 F1), and the S2 variant marginally surpasses several published numbers cited for the same dataset. The FrameNet augmentation experiments further show the approach is compatible with external supervision and robust to some noise.
- Empirical check on unsupervised attention: The failure of the unsupervised attention baseline is informative. Although not surprising given data sparsity, it usefully motivates the proposed supervision.

Weaknesses
- Limited novelty and relation to prior work: Supervising attention with gold alignments/labels is not new (e.g., Mi et al., 2016 for NMT) and using argument annotations to aid ED has been a long-standing idea in joint models. The main contribution is the straightforward application of supervised attention to ED. The methodological advance is incremental.
- Comparability and potential use of gold resources at test time: It appears the model uses ACE entity type annotations as features (entity type embeddings) on ACE. Are these gold entity mentions/types used at test time? Several comparison baselines may rely on predicted entities or different resources. Without a clear statement and a “predicted-entity” setting, the fairness of the comparisons is uncertain.
- Experimental rigor and reproducibility: The split is described as “following” prior work but also “randomly selected,” with no document lists or seeds. The t-test claim (p ≤ 0.05) is not credible without multiple runs or folds; the paper does not describe how variance was obtained. The attention supervision for negative candidates (NA) is under-specified and could bias learning. Important hyperparameters (context window n) and details of “transforming w to entity space” are omitted.
- Baselines and ablations are insufficient: The main baseline is a simple ANN with an underperforming unsupervised attention. There is no strong neural baseline without attention (e.g., BiLSTM/CNN with context) trained on the same features, nor an ablation without entity features, nor a variant training without argument supervision but with the same architecture. This makes it hard to isolate the source of gains. The “ANN-ENT” baseline that averages attention over all entities is not competitive or standard.
- Analysis depth: No per-type or ambiguity-focused analyses, no error analysis, and no calibration/attention quality assessment (e.g., how often attention aligns to true arguments). It remains unclear whether improvements are concentrated on ambiguous triggers, as hypothesized.

Questions to authors
- Do you use gold ACE entity mentions/types at test time on ACE 2005, or predicted entities? If gold, please report results with predicted entities to make comparisons fair.
- Please provide the exact train/dev/test document lists (and random seeds) to enable replication, and clarify whether your split matches those used in cited baselines.
- How is statistical significance computed? Over multiple random initializations on the same split? How many runs?
- How do you construct supervised attention for negative (NA) candidates in sentences that also contain other events/arguments? Do you ever assign argument-derived attention to NA candidates, and if so, could that leak event information?
- What is the context window length n? How often are gold arguments outside the window and thus invisible to attention? Any sensitivity analysis on n, λ, and the S2 Gaussian parameters?
- Can you compare against a strong non-attention neural baseline (e.g., BiLSTM/CNN) with the same inputs, and provide an ablation removing entity embeddings?
- Do you have per-event-type gains, especially on ambiguous trigger lemmas? Any qualitative evaluation of the learned attention alignment to arguments?

Additional comments
- Writing, references, and clarity require attention (typos, inconsistent citations). Releasing code/splits would materially improve reproducibility. Overall, the idea is reasonable but the novelty is limited and the experimental rigor needs strengthening to support the claims.
