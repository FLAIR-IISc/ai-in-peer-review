Summary
This paper targets event detection (ED) in ACE 2005 and proposes a neural model that explicitly leverages annotated event arguments to supervise an attention mechanism. The model computes attention over contextual words and entity types with respect to a candidate trigger. During training, “gold” attention vectors are constructed from ACE argument annotations via two strategies: S1 (only argument tokens) and S2 (Gaussian-smoothed attention around arguments). A joint loss combines trigger classification and squared-error attention supervision. Results on ACE 2005 show sizable gains over a feed-forward baseline and improvements over several published systems. The authors further augment training with FrameNet-derived events (with core frame elements as pseudo-arguments), showing modest additional gains.

Main contributions (as I see them)
1) An explicit, supervised-attention formulation that uses gold argument positions to guide what context the ED model should focus on, yielding improved ED performance without requiring arguments at test time.
2) A simple, effective training protocol with two supervision strategies (S1/S2), and an analysis showing S2 trades some precision for recall; plus a study with FrameNet augmentation illustrating data sparsity effects on attention learning.
3) Empirical results on ACE 2005 that match or surpass prior reported systems under the stated setup.

Strengths
- Clear, targeted idea with practical impact: Using argument annotations to supervise attention is a natural way to inject high-value supervision into ED, addressing the common finding that joint models tend to help AE more than ED. The approach is conceptually simple, easy to implement, and uses supervision already available in ACE training.
- Strong empirical gains on ACE 2005: The supervised-attention variants (especially S2) substantially improve F1 over the unsupervised baseline (64.6 → 71.7) and surpass several reported systems (e.g., Liu et al. 2016a 70.7; Nguyen & Grishman 2016 71.3). The consistent improvements across S1/S2 strengthen the claim that argument-guided attention helps ED.
- Sensible analysis of precision/recall trade-off: The comparison between S1 (higher precision) and S2 (higher recall) aligns with intuition about supervision breadth (strict argument-only versus argument neighborhood), which adds credibility and interpretability.
- Data augmentation study is informative: Using FrameNet events highlights that unsupervised attention struggles under data sparsity and that more positive samples help recall. The processing steps (using core FEs and adding entity labels) make the augmentation reproducible in principle.
- Method interpretability: Attention supervision grounded in argument positions makes the model’s focus human-understandable, a desirable property in information extraction.

Weaknesses
- Limited novelty relative to existing supervised attention literature: The core technical move—using gold token spans to supervise attention—is borrowed from prior work (e.g., supervised attentions in NMT). The novelty lies primarily in applying it to ED with argument spans; methodologically this is incremental.
- Comparability and evaluation clarity: It is unclear whether all comparisons use identical train/dev/test splits and identical access to gold entities at test time (common in ACE ED but must be explicit). The Skip-CNN row has incomplete metrics (N/A), complicating fair comparison. Significance testing details (number of runs, variance) are not provided.
- Incomplete ablations and methodological details: Key details are underspecified, e.g., how αw and αe are combined (sum of two softmaxes without renormalization?), the exact transform from word to entity-type space for αe, whether embeddings are fixed or fine-tuned, and whether attention normalization matches the supervised loss. Ablations such as removing entity-type features, supervising only word or only entity attention, or comparing to sequence encoders (BiLSTM/CNN with attention) are missing.
- Generality and robustness: Results are only on ACE 2005 English. There is no error analysis, per-type performance, or attention visualizations to show the model truly attends to arguments at test. Hyperparameter robustness for λ, Gaussian window/σ is not explored.
- Practical assumptions: The model relies on entity type contexts; while standard in ACE ED, the paper should clarify whether gold or predicted entities are used at test time, as reliance on gold entities can inflate performance and limit practical deployment. The FrameNet augmentation further depends on an external entity tagger, adding noise and engineering effort.

Questions to authors
- What entity information is available at test time on ACE 2005 in your experiments—gold entity mentions/types or predicted? Do all baselines use the same setting?
- Do you renormalize attention after summing αw and αe? If not, how do you reconcile the discrepancy between normalized gold attention (α*) and potentially unnormalized α in the squared-error loss?
- How exactly is the “transforming w from the word space into the entity type space” implemented for αe (parameters, dimensions)? Is there a separate matrix from Ww?
- Are your train/dev/test splits identical to those used by each compared system, or are numbers copied from their papers with potentially different splits? If the latter, can you re-run strong baselines under your split?
- Can you provide ablations: (i) without entity-type features; (ii) only word-attention supervision vs only entity-attention supervision; (iii) replacing the MLP with a BiLSTM/CNN under the same supervision?
- How sensitive are results to λ and to S2’s Gaussian window/σ? Any stability analysis over multiple random seeds?
- Do you have qualitative attention visualizations showing that, at test time, the model focuses on argument-like context around ambiguous triggers?
