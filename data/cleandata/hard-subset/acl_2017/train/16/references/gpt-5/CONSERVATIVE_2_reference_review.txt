Summary and main contributions
- The paper proposes to exploit annotated event arguments to improve event detection (ED) by supervising an attention mechanism. During training, “gold” attention targets are constructed from argument spans; the model is trained to align attention with these spans while classifying triggers. At test time, attention is computed without gold arguments.
- Two supervision strategies are explored: focusing attention solely on argument tokens (S1) and diffusing attention around arguments via a Gaussian kernel (S2).
- Experiments on ACE 2005 indicate gains over selected baselines; modest additional gains are reported when augmenting training with FrameNet-derived data.

Strengths
- Clear intuition and direct use of argument supervision for ED: The paper highlights a genuine gap in many joint models—arguments help AE more than ED—and proposes a simple, direct way to bias ED toward argument-bearing regions. The idea is straightforward and plausible.
- Competitive performance and precision gains: On ACE 2005, the best variant (ANN-S2) slightly outperforms reported baselines and exhibits notably higher precision, which is relevant for downstream pipelines sensitive to false positives.
- Comparative analysis of supervision strategies: The S1 vs. S2 comparison is informative and consistent with the stated hypotheses (S1 improves precision by focusing strictly on arguments; S2 increases recall by allowing context).
- Use of external data: The FrameNet augmentation is handled reasonably, with a clear description of how frame elements are mapped and how entities are obtained. Although gains are modest, the directionality makes sense and supports the data sparsity argument.

Weaknesses
- Limited novelty and incremental technical contribution: Supervised attention itself is not new, and leveraging arguments to help ED has long been the rationale for joint models. The proposed approach mainly repurposes supervised attention to ED with argument supervision. Methodologically, the architecture (bag-of-context via attention + feed-forward ANN) is simplistic relative to contemporaneous CNN/RNN baselines, which weakens the claim of methodological advance.
- Fairness and completeness of baselines: The core baseline (ANN) with unsupervised attention is weak, and stronger established baselines (e.g., well-tuned CNN/RNN/biLSTM models with position embeddings) are missing. The comparison to Skip-CNN is incomplete (only recall reported in the table), and it’s unclear whether all baselines are trained with the same supervision (e.g., whether they use gold entities at test time as this work seems to do). Since the proposed method uses argument annotations as additional supervision (albeit only to train attention), comparisons to pipelines that do not exploit argument supervision may be unfavorable to the latter.
- Evaluation protocol and statistical rigor: There is no report of multiple runs or variance; the “t-test (p ≤ 0.05)” is underspecified (paired over what units? across seeds?). Small absolute F1 differences (e.g., 0.4 over Skip-CNN) require careful statistical treatment. The fixed-length context truncation/padding is under-specified (n not given), which could affect whether arguments are even visible to the model, and thus reproducibility and interpretability suffer.
- Ambiguities in the attention supervision and inputs: 
  - The paper computes separate word and entity attentions and combines them (α = αw + αe), but the gold attention construction is described primarily over argument words. It is unclear how α* is defined when supervising both word and entity attentions jointly, and how supervision is applied to the combined vector vs. components. 
  - For candidates without arguments in context, S1 “forces all entities to average the attention,” which seems ad hoc and conflates word-level and entity-level signals.
  - Entity information for ACE test appears to be gold; when augmenting with FN, entities are predicted automatically. The impact of using predicted vs. gold entities on ED performance is not quantified, leaving questions about the practical robustness of the approach.
- Analysis is limited: Beyond showing aggregate F1, there is little error analysis or per-type breakdown to substantiate the claim that arguments particularly help with ambiguous triggers. No ablations remove entity types, remove attention entirely, or replace supervised attention with simpler position-based heuristics, so it is hard to isolate what truly drives the gains.

Questions to authors
- Do you use gold entity mentions and types at test time on ACE 2005? If so, what is the performance when using predicted entities (to match the FN augmentation scenario)? Please quantify the sensitivity to entity quality.
- What is the context window size n, and how often are argument spans truncated out of the window? How does performance vary with n?
- How exactly is attention supervision applied when α = αw + αe? Is α* defined over tokens only, entities only, or both? Are αw and αe supervised separately or only the sum?
- Please report averages and standard deviations over multiple random seeds, and clarify the unit of the t-test (per-document? per-run?). Are the gains over Skip-CNN statistically significant under this protocol?
- Can you compare to stronger neural baselines (e.g., biLSTM/CNN with position embeddings and without argument supervision) and to a variant of your model without any attention? This would help disentangle the contribution of supervised attention vs. other architectural choices.
- Provide per-event-type precision/recall to support the claim that arguments help disambiguate polysemous triggers.

Additional comments
- The references are poorly formatted and in several cases incomplete; please fix for camera-ready.
- Clarify whether hyperparameters (e.g., λ) were tuned on the dev set and whether any test-set information leaked via multiple tries.
- Consider qualitative attention visualizations to demonstrate the model indeed attends to argument spans for ambiguous cases.

Overall, while the intuition is sensible and the results are promising, the contribution feels incremental and the evaluation lacks rigor against stronger baselines and under realistic settings (predicted entities).
