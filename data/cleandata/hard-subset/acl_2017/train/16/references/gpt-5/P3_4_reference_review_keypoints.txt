- Paper proposes supervising attention for event detection on ACE 2005 using argument annotations, with dot-product attention over a local context and two supervision schemes (S1: arguments, S2: Gaussian-smoothed).
- Contributions: (1) supervised attention formulation for ED improving over unsupervised attention; (2) systematic comparison of S1 vs S2 and oracle analysis showing value of argument info; (3) FrameNet-based data augmentation mapping frame elements to arguments.

Strengths
- Clear motivation: explicitly teaching attention to focus on arguments plausibly addresses why joint AE/ED helps AE more than ED.
- Empirical gains: supervised attention variants improve substantially over the authors’ unsupervised baseline and slightly beat some prior state-of-the-art, with higher precision.
- Oracle analysis: ANN-Gold vs ANN-ENT comparisons quantify the potential ceiling and show gains stem from argument supervision, not just entity cues.
- Simple and general mechanism: attention supervision is model-agnostic and straightforward to implement for related IE tasks.
- Sensible extra-data use: FrameNet augmentation is carefully adapted and helps both supervised and unsupervised setups, supporting the data-sparsity argument.

Weaknesses
- Limited novelty: attention supervision is known in other domains; main novelty is its application to ED without architectural innovation.
- Weak baseline and missing ablations: unsupervised ANN baseline is notably weak and several ablations (word-only, entity-only, window size, position embeddings) are missing.
- Assumptions and potential leakage: likely use of gold entity types at test time and mismatch in entity sourcing for FrameNet vs ACE may limit applicability and fairness of comparisons.
- Insufficient attention/error analysis: lacks quantitative attention-alignment metrics, per-event-type breakdowns, and detailed error analyses on ambiguous triggers.
- Clarity/reproducibility issues: notational/formatting problems, under-specified hyperparameters (e.g., window n), and incomplete FrameNet→ACE mapping details hinder reproducibility.

Questions for authors
- Do you use gold entity mentions/types at test time on ACE? Please report results with predicted entities and clarify baseline assumptions.
- How are gold attention vectors constructed for negative candidates and sentences with multiple events; do you assign attention to other events’ arguments?
- What is the context window size n, sensitivity to n, and how often do arguments fall outside the window?
- Can you provide attention-alignment metrics (e.g., proportion of attention mass on true arguments) and per-event-type performance?
- Please detail the statistical testing protocol: number of runs, variance, exact baselines compared—are gains consistent?
- How do you map FrameNet frames to ACE subtypes, what fraction of augmented labels are noisy, and is there filtering beyond core elements?

Overall summary
- Focused, practical idea that yields modest but consistent gains on ACE 2005; paper would benefit from stronger baselines, clearer evaluation assumptions, and deeper analyses to increase impact and rigor.
