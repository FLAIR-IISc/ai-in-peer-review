Summary
This paper proposes a supervised attention mechanism for event detection (ED) that explicitly leverages annotated event arguments during training. Instead of relying on joint EE models where arguments mostly help AE, the authors construct “gold attention” vectors from argument spans and train the attention module and ED classifier jointly. Two supervision strategies are explored: S1 (focus only on argument tokens) and S2 (spread attention to neighborhoods via a Gaussian). The model combines attention over contextual words and entity types, and is trained on ACE 2005, with additional experiments augmenting training with FrameNet-derived events. Results show clear gains over prior SOTA on ACE 2005, with especially strong precision.

Contributions
- A novel, explicit use of argument annotations to supervise attention for ED, shifting from joint EE’s indirect use of arguments toward a direct mechanism that emphasizes argument-bearing context.
- A simple, general training recipe for supervised attention in ED (gold attention construction via S1/S2) that yields strong improvements and favorable precision/recall trade-offs.
- Empirical evidence on ACE 2005 (and augmented with FrameNet) that supervised attention substantially outperforms unsupervised attention and prior SOTA methods.

Strengths
- Clear conceptual novelty: Using argument labels to supervise attention for ED is a fresh, bold idea relative to prior joint and pipeline approaches. It targets a well-identified gap (arguments helping AE more than ED) with a direct, elegant mechanism.
- Strong empirical gains and high precision: On ACE 2005, ANN-S2 achieves the best F1 (71.7) vs. Liu’s FN-based 70.7 and Nguyen & Grishman’s methods (~69–71). ANN-S1 yields even higher precision (81.4 P), which is valuable for downstream EE pipelines where precision is critical.
- Diagnostic design and analysis: The “correctness” study (ANN vs ANN-ENT vs ANN-Gold1/2) thoughtfully isolates the value of argument supervision beyond entity information and unsupervised attention, supporting the core thesis.
- Practical training-time supervision, no test-time cost: Argument supervision is only used to guide attention during training; the inference-time model does not require arguments, preserving practical usability.
- Simple, extensible approach: The method is architecture-agnostic (attention supervision can be bolted onto stronger encoders) and uses minimal heuristics (S1/S2), making it an appealing direction for future ED research.

Weaknesses
- Fairness and realism of entity usage: The model uses entity type embeddings for context. Are these gold entities at test time? Many comparisons in ACE use gold entities, but practical deployment relies on predicted entities. The paper should clarify and, ideally, report results with system entities to ensure robustness and fairness to baselines using predicted entities.
- Limited encoder strength and generality evidence: The ED backbone is a simple MLP. While the attention idea is promising, it should be tested with stronger encoders (e.g., BiLSTM/CNN/Transformers) to demonstrate that gains persist and are not an artifact of a weak baseline. An ablation with word-only attention (no entity attention) would further isolate contributions.
- Evaluation clarity and statistical rigor: The paper reports a single run with a t-test claim but no variance across multiple seeds, which is important for NN-based models on small datasets like ACE 2005. More rigorous reporting (mean±std over seeds) would strengthen the conclusions.
- Attention supervision coverage and windowing: The approach relies on a fixed context window. It is unclear how often arguments for a trigger fall outside that window or are truncated, and how that affects both attention supervision and performance. This could be a source of silent bias.
- Presentation issues and reproducibility details: Some tables and references are inconsistent/unclear (e.g., the Skip-CNN row, missing/garbled citation metadata). Key hyperparameters (context length n), the proportion of triggers with in-window arguments, and training time are not reported, which hinders reproducibility.

Questions to Authors
- Do you use gold or system entity mentions/types at test time on ACE 2005? If gold, can you report results with predicted entities for a more realistic and comparable setting?
- What is the fixed context length n, and what fraction of gold arguments for trigger candidates falls outside this window? How sensitive are the results to n?
- How often do trigger candidates have no arguments in the context, and what is the impact of the “average all entities” backup rule on those cases?
- Could you provide results with stronger encoders (e.g., BiLSTM/CNN), and an ablation with only word attention (no entity attention) to test generality and isolate the contribution of entity attention?
- For the FN-augmented experiments, you use automatically predicted entities. Does this mismatch (gold entities in ACE vs predicted in FN) affect model behavior? Can you harmonize the setting (e.g., use system entities on ACE) and report results?
- Can you report mean±std over multiple random seeds, and per-event-type breakdowns to show which event types benefit most (e.g., those with ambiguous triggers)?
- Any qualitative attention visualizations to verify that supervised attention indeed focuses on arguments and their neighborhoods as intended?

Overall, I find the central idea novel and impactful for ED. Despite some experimental and presentation gaps, the contribution is clear and forward-looking. I lean toward acceptance.
