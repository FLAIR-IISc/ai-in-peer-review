Summary and claimed contributions
- The paper addresses event detection (ED) in ACE 2005 by explicitly exploiting annotated argument information via supervised attention. It trains an attention mechanism to put higher weights on argument tokens (and optionally their vicinity), then feeds attention-weighted contextual word and entity representations into a simple feed-forward classifier. Two supervision strategies are explored: S1 (arguments only) and S2 (arguments plus Gaussian neighborhood). The model is further augmented with FrameNet-derived events as extra training data.
- Key contributions (as I see them):
  1) A simple, explicit use of gold argument annotations to supervise attention for ED, yielding consistent gains over an unsupervised attention baseline and prior SOTA on ACE 2005.
  2) Empirical analysis and ablations that isolate the effect of argument supervision (ANN vs ANN-S1/S2; comparisons to an entity-only attention variant; and +FrameNet augmentation).
  3) A straightforward supervision strategy for attention (hard argument masks or Gaussian-smoothed masks) that is potentially reusable beyond ED.

Strengths
- Clear, focused motivation with concrete examples. The ambiguity of triggers like “fired” is well illustrated, and the argument-centric intuition maps naturally to attention. This grounding increases plausibility that the proposed signal is relevant to ED.
- Empirical improvements on a widely used benchmark. On ACE 2005, ANN-S2 achieves the best reported F1 among compared systems (71.7), exceeding several strong baselines (e.g., Skip-CNN, Nguyen et al.’s joint RNN, Liu’s PSL/FN-based). The gains over the model’s own unsupervised-attention baseline are substantial (64.6 → 71.7), indicating the argument supervision is the driver rather than incidental tuning.
- Ablation and control comparisons are informative. The comparisons to:
  • ANN (unsupervised attention) show data sparsity hinders learning attention without supervision.
  • ANN-ENT (uniform attention over entities) demonstrate that entity information alone is insufficient; true argument supervision matters.
  • ANN-Gold1/2 (using gold attention at both train and test) bound potential gains and reinforce the utility of argument signal.
  The S1 vs S2 trade-off (precision vs recall) is also well analyzed.
- Simple, general mechanism. Supervising attention with gold argument masks is architecturally lightweight, compatible with many encoders, and conceptually clean. This lowers the barrier to adoption and potential impact beyond ED (e.g., relation extraction, SRL).
- Additional evidence from external data. The FrameNet augmentation experiment (carefully adapted to provide arguments and entities) shows further recall gains and supports the claim that better supervision/data helps both the model and attention learning.

Weaknesses
- Novelty is moderate. Using argument information to help ED has a long history in joint models; the contribution here is to channel that supervision directly into an attention mechanism. While useful, the technical novelty is incremental: a dot-product attention with MSE to a hand-constructed target is a straightforward application of supervised attention. The paper would benefit from stronger methodological innovations (e.g., more expressive attention, structured supervision, or end-to-end joint training with AE that avoids the imbalance issue the authors raise).
- Baseline strength and fairness of comparisons. The core classifier is a shallow ANN with bagged contextual representations, which underperforms modern CNN/RNN encoders. The unsupervised attention baseline (ANN) is notably weak (F1 64.6), far below prior neural baselines (e.g., Chen et al. 2015 ~69, Skip-CNN ~71). It is therefore unclear how much of the improvement over SOTA derives from the supervision versus architectural differences. A stronger apples-to-apples comparison would integrate the same supervised attention into a competitive CNN/RNN baseline and report deltas.
- Reliance on gold entities and unclear test-time supervision. The model uses entity type embeddings of “annotated entities” following prior work. It is not clearly stated whether gold entities are available at test time (many ED papers do assume gold entities, but this must be explicit), and whether any system-predicted entities are used. Given attention combines word and entity channels, test-time availability materially affects results and comparability. Similarly, while the attention module is supervised with gold arguments at training, at test time there are no gold arguments—clarify that only learned attention is used and ensure no leakage.
- Missing or underspecified details that affect reproducibility. The fixed context window size n is not specified; handling of multi-token triggers is not described; class imbalance handling (beyond softmax) is not discussed; selection and size of entity type inventory is unspecified; and early stopping/seed variance are not reported. The t-test significance claim lacks details on how variance across runs was measured; single-run numbers with random initialization are fragile on ACE 2005.
- Limited qualitative analysis of attention. The paper argues that attention focuses on arguments, but provides only schematic illustrations. Examples of learned attention heatmaps, error analysis (e.g., when attention is misdirected), and per-type breakdowns would strengthen the causal claim that attention supervision, not other confounds, yields the gains.

Questions to authors
- Do you use gold entity annotations at test time, or system-predicted entities? Please specify for all compared systems to ensure fairness.
- What is the exact context window size n, and how sensitive are results to n? How do you handle multi-word triggers?
- How many random seeds/runs were averaged for the reported numbers? Please report mean and standard deviation, and clarify the statistical test protocol.
- Can you integrate the same supervised attention into a stronger encoder (e.g., CNN/RNN) and report the incremental gain? This would better isolate the contribution of supervised attention.
- Please provide qualitative examples of learned attention on success and failure cases, and a per-event-type breakdown to show where argument supervision helps most.

Overall recommendation
- The paper presents a simple and useful idea with solid empirical gains and informative ablations, but with moderate novelty and some concerns about baseline strength, fairness, and missing details. I lean toward weak accept if the authors clarify the entity usage, strengthen comparisons, and address reproducibility; otherwise, borderline.
