Summary:
The paper addresses event detection (ED) in ACE by explicitly leveraging annotated argument information during training via supervised attention. The model treats each token as a trigger candidate, computes attention over a fixed-size context for both words and entity types, and trains the attention mechanism to match “gold” attention derived from annotated arguments. Two supervision strategies are explored: S1 (attention only on argument tokens) and S2 (attention on arguments and nearby words via a Gaussian). An ANN classifier consumes the attended context representations to predict event types. Experiments on ACE 2005 show gains over prior systems, with further improvements when augmenting training with FrameNet-derived events.

Main contributions (as assessed):
1) A supervised attention scheme for ED that uses annotated arguments to guide attention during training, enabling the detector to focus on argument-relevant context at test time without arguments.
2) Empirical demonstration that argument-informed attention improves ED over unsupervised attention and several prior baselines, including an exploration of two attention supervision strategies (S1 and S2) and augmentation with FrameNet data.
3) An ablation showing the relative contribution of argument supervision vs. general entity information, suggesting arguments (not just entities) drive the improvements.

Strengths:
- Clear, focused methodological idea with simple implementation. The design—dot-product attention between the candidate and each contextual item, plus supervised loss to align attention with argument positions—is straightforward and well-motivated for disambiguating ambiguous triggers. The addition of entity-type context is also reasonable within ACE’s setting.
- Consistent empirical gains and high precision. On ACE 2005, supervised attention variants (particularly S2) improve F1 over reported baselines, with notable precision increases. The comparison to an otherwise identical ANN without attention supervision isolates the benefit of argument-based attention.
- Solid ablations isolating the value of argument information. The paper contrasts models that: (i) do not use arguments, (ii) collapse attention over entities (ANN-ENT), and (iii) use gold argument attention even at test time (ANN-Gold). This supports the claim that arguments, not simply entity presence, provide key signals for ED.
- Exploration of supervision strategies. Comparing S1 vs. S2 shows a precision/recall trade-off aligned with the intended behavior (S1 more precise, S2 higher recall). This gives useful insight into how to best construct attention supervision for ED.
- Data augmentation with FrameNet. Re-using frames as events and core frame elements as arguments is sensible; the observed improvements (with expected precision costs) strengthen the case that data sparsity hampers unsupervised attention and that the proposed method scales with more data.

Weaknesses:
- Limited novelty beyond applying supervised attention to ED using gold arguments. Supervised attention as a concept is established; the novelty lies in the choice of supervision (arguments) and the task. While useful, the technical contribution is incremental.
- Reliance on gold argument annotations during training. The method’s supervision requires argument labels, which may not be available for many ED settings. The paper does not discuss how performance degrades when arguments are noisy/predicted during training, nor how to adapt when only triggers are annotated.
- Missing or ambiguous technical details. Key aspects are underspecified: the fixed context window size n is not reported; the transformation from word space to entity-type space (we) lacks a precise definition; after combining αw and αe by addition, normalization is not discussed (α then sums to 2), which matters for both representation scaling and the attention loss. These details affect reproducibility and interpretability.
- Comparability and robustness concerns. It is unclear whether all baselines use identical resources (e.g., gold entity mentions at test time). Some baseline numbers (e.g., Skip-CNN) are incomplete. No results across multiple random seeds or splits are reported, and the significance testing procedure is not described (test set size, test used, number of runs), raising concerns about variance.
- Limited analysis. The paper lacks error analysis, per-type performance, or qualitative inspection of learned attention to confirm that the model focuses on informative arguments in practice. This would strengthen the causal link between argument supervision and observed gains.

Questions to Authors:
- What is the exact context window size n used in all experiments? How sensitive are results to n?
- How is we computed? Is there a separate transformation matrix from word embeddings to the entity-type space? Please provide the precise formula and parameterization.
- After computing αw and αe (both normalized), you sum them to get α. Do you re-normalize α before using it to form cw/ce and in the attention loss? If not, why, and how does this affect training stability?
- What entity information is used at test time: gold ACE entity types or predicted ones? If gold, can you report results with predicted entities to gauge practical performance?
- How many random runs were conducted for each result? Please report mean and variance and clarify the statistical test used for significance claims.
- Can you provide an ablation removing entity-type attention (using only word attention) and another removing attention supervision but keeping entities, to disentangle their contributions?
- For the FrameNet augmentation, what is the scale of added data relative to ACE training instances, and how was class distribution handled? Any domain mismatch issues observed?

Overall, the paper presents a clear and effective application of supervised attention to ED with argument-based supervision, yielding solid empirical gains on ACE. The main limitations are modest novelty, reliance on argument annotations for supervision, and missing technical/reproducibility details. Addressing these points would strengthen the work.
