- Proposes supervising attention for event detection using annotated argument spans (S1: argument-only; S2: argument + neighbor diffusion via Gaussian), jointly optimizing ED and attention-matching losses.  
- Model treats every token as a trigger candidate and inputs candidate embedding plus attention-weighted contextual words and entity-type representations; evaluated on ACE 2005 with FrameNet augmentation.  
- Contribution: explicit use of labeled argument spans to supervise attention, yielding consistent gains over an unsupervised-attention baseline and slight SOTA improvements on ACE 2005.  
- Contribution: systematic comparison of two supervision strategies (S1 vs S2) showing a precision–recall tradeoff and demonstrating unsupervised attention suffers from data sparsity, partially mitigated by FrameNet augmentation.  
- Contribution: a lightweight, efficient architecture that fuses contextual word and entity-type attention for ED.  
- Strength: Clear, intuitive motivation that argument information helps disambiguate triggers and a principled mechanism to inject that supervision into attention.  
- Strength: Competitive empirical results (S2 F1 ≈ 71.7) with notably higher precision and substantial gains over the unsupervised-attention baseline.  
- Strength: Sensible analyses/ablations showing S1 vs S2 trade-offs and FrameNet experiments supporting data-sparsity claims.  
- Strength: Simple, efficient model design (no heavy sequence encoders) focused on supervision strategy rather than capacity.  
- Strength: Combining attention over contextual words and entity types appears to boost precision.  
- Weakness: Limited novelty—supervising attention with gold labels is conceptually straightforward and incremental relative to prior work.  
- Weakness: Baseline and comparability concerns—core baseline is weak and comparisons to prior work may not be apples-to-apples (splits, resources, entity use).  
- Weakness: Reliance on gold entities at test time; unclear how much performance holds with predicted entities (only FN augmentation used predicted entities).  
- Weakness: Risk of overfitting to argument supervision since attention is trained on gold argument positions and generalization/robustness across domains is not thoroughly evaluated.  
- Weakness: Missing ablations/error analysis isolating entity vs word attention, attention-loss weight λ, S2 window/σ, context length, and lacking per-class/error breakdowns.  
- Weakness: Clarity/presentation issues and incomplete reproducibility details (notation typos, preprocessing/hyperparameter search, code).  
- Question: Are reported numbers on identical ACE 2005 splits/conditions (e.g., gold entities) as compared prior work—please clarify and provide matched-setting results.  
- Question: What is performance with predicted entities (e.g., RPI) on ACE dev/test and sensitivity to entity tagging errors?  
- Question: What happens when removing entity-type attention (αe=0) or word attention (αw=0), and when supervising only words vs only entities?  
- Question: How was λ chosen for S1/S2, and what are sensitivity analyses for λ, S2 window size/σ, and context length n?  
- Question: Can you provide per-class results, analyses on ambiguous triggers, and any cross-domain/genre evaluations to support generalization claims?
