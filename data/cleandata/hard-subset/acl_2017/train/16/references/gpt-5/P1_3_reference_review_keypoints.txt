- Proposes supervised attention for event detection that uses annotated arguments to guide attention over context tokens and entity types, with two gold-attention strategies (S1 argument-only, S2 Gaussian-smoothed).
- Combines attention supervision loss with standard ED cross-entropy and shows gains on ACE 2005; FrameNet augmentation further improves recall.
- Main contribution: simple supervised attention using argument spans that yields consistent gains and matches/exceeds prior ACE 2005 results.
- Provides diagnostic experiments (including oracle “Gold” attention) that isolate the benefit of argument supervision beyond generic entity signals.
- Introduces a practical, noisy data augmentation using FrameNet-derived events that boosts recall.
- Strength: Clear, practical idea with measurable impact and sizable improvements over an unsupervised attention baseline.
- Strength: Diagnostic analysis (ANN vs ANN-ENT vs ANN-Gold) supports the core claim that argument supervision is the key signal.
- Strength: Lightweight, efficient architecture that is easy to reproduce and integrate into other models.
- Strength: Comparison of S1 and S2 highlights a precision–recall tradeoff, matching intuition about strict vs. smoothed attention.
- Weakness: Limited novelty given existing supervised attention and joint EE literature; lacks controlled comparison to strong joint models.
- Weakness: Baselines are weak or dated and reporting is inconsistent, making comparisons to stronger CNN/RNN encoders unclear.
- Weakness: Relies on gold entity types (likely at test time), raising concerns about real-world applicability and missing results with predicted entities.
- Weakness: Limited analysis of learned attention alignment and per-event-type effects, reducing insight into when the method helps.
- Weakness: Reproducibility concerns—dev split randomness, sparse hyperparameter tuning/details, and unclear statistical testing methodology.
- Asks for robustness tests with predicted entities and reporting of results with an off-the-shelf ACE entity tagger.
- Requests sensitivity analysis for λ, attention window size, and Gaussian parameters (μ, σ).
- Requests ablations separating word-based vs. entity-based attention and investigating learned combinations vs. simple sum.
- Requests per-event-type P/R/F1 and quantitative alignment of learned attention peaks with true arguments at test time.
- Requests exact dev/test document IDs, preprocessing/tokenization details, and clarification of entity label usage relative to prior work.
- Questions whether a competitive CNN/LSTM encoder with the same attention supervision would further improve results or justify the feed-forward choice.
- Overall: A clear, well-motivated application with solid empirical gains and useful diagnostics, but moderate novelty and needing stronger baselines, predicted-entity experiments, and expanded analyses; leaning toward acceptance with requested clarifications/experiments.
