- Paper proposes supervised attention for event detection using annotated argument positions to guide attention over context and entity types.

- Model treats each token as a trigger candidate and builds context representations by attending over surrounding words and entity types.

- Two attention supervision strategies: S1 (arguments-only) and S2 (arguments plus neighbors via a Gaussian spread).

- ED classifier is a three-layer feedforward network trained jointly with an attention MSE loss.

- Experiments on ACE 2005 show modest F1 gains over prior systems; FrameNet-derived extra data improves recall.

- Contribution: a simple, explicit use of argument annotations to supervise attention, improving trigger classification vs. unsupervised attention.

- Contribution: two practical supervision strategies (S1, S2) with empirical comparison showing S2 increases recall.

- Contribution: empirical analyses including oracle-attention variants and FrameNet augmentation demonstrating consistent gains.

- Strength: clear, well-motivated idea addressing why joint ED/AE models may underperform and offering a focused remedy.

- Strength: oracle experiments show argument locations substantially help disambiguate triggers, supporting core claim.

- Strength: competitive results on ACE 2005 with improved precision and consistent gains when augmented with FrameNet.

- Strength: supervised attention loss is simple, lightweight, and modular for existing ED models.

- Strength: FrameNet augmentation suggests weakly labeled data can help attention-based models.

- Weakness: methodological novelty is limited—supervised attention is a straightforward application of existing ideas.

- Weakness: unsupervised attention baseline underperforms common ED architectures, raising fairness concerns about reported gains.

- Weakness: comparisons may mix results from different splits; statistical significance claims are unconvincing without rerunning baselines and reporting variance.

- Weakness: key methodological details are underspecified (how α = αw + αe is normalized, entity attention computations, entity embedding transforms).

- Weakness: context window size, whether embeddings are updated, and other hyperparameters/sensitivities are not reported.

- Weakness: reliance on gold entity mentions/types at test time is unclear and may be an unrealistic oracle; no results with predicted entities.

- Weakness: analysis gaps—no ablation studies quantifying supervised attention vs. entity channel, no sensitivity analyses for λ/σ/window, no attention visualizations, ad hoc S1 fallback unexamined.

- Weakness: reproducibility concerns—missing random seeds, exact split, code, pretrained embeddings, and FrameNet processing details.

- Questions for authors: are entity mentions/types at test time gold or predicted, and can results with predicted entities be reported?

- Questions for authors: how is α combined and normalized; what are the exact transformations and computations for entity attention?

- Questions for authors: are baseline numbers recomputed on the same split or copied; can baselines be rerun with mean±std over multiple runs?

- Questions for authors: what is the context window n and how sensitive are results to n, λ, σ, and S2 window size?

- Questions for authors: are word embeddings updated during training and how does freezing affect results?

- Questions for authors: please provide ablations (λ=0, remove entity channel, word-only attention) and attention visualizations and per-event-type performance.
