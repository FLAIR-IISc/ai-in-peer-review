Summary and Contributions
This paper tackles event detection (ED) in ACE 2005 with the hypothesis that argument information provides strong cues for disambiguating triggers. The authors propose a neural ED model with supervised attention: during training, attention over context words/entities is guided by “gold” attention derived from argument annotations (two strategies: S1 focuses only on arguments; S2 also diffuses attention to neighboring tokens via a Gaussian). At test time, the learned attention operates without gold arguments. Experiments on ACE 2005 and augmentation with FrameNet (FN) show improvements over several baselines, with notable precision gains.

As I see them, the primary contributions are:
1) A supervised attention mechanism for ED that explicitly leverages argument annotations during training to guide where the model should attend in context.
2) Empirical evidence (ablation with ANN, ANN-ENT, ANN-Gold1/2; S1 vs S2) substantiating that argument information—not just entity presence—is beneficial for ED, and that attention supervision improves over unsupervised attention.
3) A simple procedure to incorporate FN-derived event data (mapping frame elements to arguments) to alleviate data sparsity, showing additional gains.

Strengths
1) Clear and well-motivated use of argument supervision. The paper articulates the ambiguity of triggers (e.g., “fired”) and positions argument cues as disambiguators. The ablation with ANN-Gold1/2 vs ANN and ANN-ENT isolates the value of argument guidance, showing sizable improvements when argument-derived attention is available or learned via supervision.

2) Strong empirical precision and competitive SOTA F1. On ACE 2005, ANN-S2 achieves the best reported F1 among compared methods, while ANN-S1 attains particularly high precision. The precision gains support the claim that argument-focused attention helps reduce false positives for ambiguous triggers.

3) Thoughtful attention supervision design with two strategies. S1 (arguments-only) and S2 (arguments + neighbors via Gaussian) capture complementary trade-offs (precision vs recall). The analysis aligns with the reported results (S1 higher precision; S2 higher recall), indicating the design matches the intended behavior.

4) Data augmentation with FrameNet is practical and informative. The mapping from frames to events and from core frame elements to arguments, plus injecting entity labels with an external system, is a reasonable way to create additional training signal. The observed gains (especially improved recall) corroborate the data sparsity hypothesis and the utility of external supervision.

5) Simplicity and generality. The method is conceptually simple (dot-product attention with supervised targets, combined word/entity attention) and could be adapted to other event-centric tasks or used as an auxiliary loss in more complex architectures.

Weaknesses
1) Limited methodological novelty and relatively simple architecture. Supervised attention has prior art (e.g., in NMT), and the application here is a fairly direct adaptation. The underlying ED model is a feed-forward network over attention-weighted context, without sequence modeling (CNN/RNN/Transformers) or syntactic structure—this may limit broader impact. Demonstrating the approach atop stronger encoders would strengthen the contribution.

2) Evaluation clarity and fairness concerns. Several details are unclear: the fixed context window length n is not specified; αw and αe are simply summed without learned weighting; λ differs across S1 (1.0) and S2 (5.0) with no sensitivity study. It is also unclear whether gold entities are used at test time on ACE (many works differ); if gold entities are used, comparisons to baselines that do not use gold entities may be unfair. The Skip-CNN entry lacks complete metrics (only recall). The t-test claim lacks multiple runs/seed variance details.

3) Supervision assumptions and realism. The ANN-Gold1/2 settings, while illustrative, use gold arguments at test time and are thus unrealistic; they risk overstating the achievable performance if taken at face value (the paper correctly treats them as analysis, but readers could be confused). For FN augmentation, argument and entity labels are automatically induced; the noise profile and its effect on the supervised attention are not analyzed.

4) Missing analyses that would solidify claims. The paper would benefit from: (a) per-type breakdowns showing larger gains on ambiguous triggers; (b) attention quality evaluation (do learned attentions align with gold arguments without supervision at test?); (c) ablations on window size, λ, and the relative contribution of word vs entity attentions; (d) robustness to predicted (not gold) entity mentions on ACE; and (e) comparisons to a multi-task joint model with ED+AE where loss weights are adjusted to address the imbalance the authors highlight.

5) Presentation and reference quality. There are numerous typos and formatting issues; some references are incomplete or malformed. Figures are referenced but not sufficiently described for reproducibility; several implementation details (e.g., early stopping, number of epochs, random seeds) are missing.

Questions to Authors
- Do you use gold entity mentions/types at test time on ACE 2005? If yes, can you report results with predicted entities to assess practical robustness and comparability?
- What is the fixed context window size n? Did you explore sensitivity to n and to the window size for S2’s Gaussian diffusion?
- How was λ chosen for S1 vs S2? Please provide a sensitivity analysis or validation curves. Did you try learning a weight to combine αw and αe instead of summation?
- How many random seeds/runs were used? Please report mean ± std and clarify how significance was established.
- For FN augmentation: what proportion of the FN-derived events are noisy (false positives) after your processing? Any filtering? How does attention supervision behave with noisy argument/entity annotations?
- Did you compare to a joint ED+AE multi-task model with rebalanced losses or curriculum learning to test your hypothesis about joint models’ bias toward AE?
- Can you provide per-event-type performance and error analysis, especially on triggers with high lexical ambiguity?

Overall, this is a well-motivated and empirically promising paper with clear evidence that argument-guided attention helps ED. However, the methodological novelty is modest, and several evaluation and clarity issues need attention. Strengthening the experimental rigor and situating the approach atop stronger encoders or in a multi-task setting would significantly improve the paper.
