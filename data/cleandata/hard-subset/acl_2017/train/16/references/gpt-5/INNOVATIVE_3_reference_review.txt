Summary
This paper targets event detection (ED) and proposes to explicitly leverage annotated event arguments during training via supervised attention. The model computes attention over contextual words and entity types conditioned on a trigger candidate, and it trains this attention to match “gold” attention constructed from annotated arguments (two strategies: argument-only; argument plus nearby words). The ED classifier then uses the attention-weighted contextual representations. Experiments on ACE 2005 show consistent improvements over a comparable ANN baseline and modest but positive gains over prior state-of-the-art. The authors also augment training with FrameNet-derived events, adapting frame elements as arguments, and observe additional improvements.

Contributions (as I see them)
1) Introduce argument-supervised attention for ED: using gold arguments to directly supervise where the model should attend during training, with two concrete strategies for constructing target attention distributions.
2) Empirical evidence and analysis that explicit, training-time argument supervision substantially helps ED compared to unsupervised attention and entity-only signals; the approach achieves state-of-the-art results on ACE 2005.
3) A practical pipeline to exploit FrameNet events as additional weakly labeled training data (mapping frame elements to arguments) to mitigate data sparsity, showing further recall gains.

Strengths
- Conceptual novelty and clarity: The core idea—directly supervising attention with gold arguments—is simple, targeted, and addresses a real gap in prior joint models (which tended to help AE more than ED). This is a bold reframing: use arguments not via joint inference but as supervision for where ED models should look. The two target-attention strategies (argument-only; argument + Gaussian vicinity) are concrete and well-motivated.

- Consistent empirical gains and new SOTA: On ACE 2005, supervised attention variants (ANN-S1/S2) markedly outperform an unsupervised-attention ANN baseline and edge out prior state-of-the-art systems in F1, with especially strong precision. The analysis that unsupervised attention struggles under data sparsity, and that supervising attention alleviates this, is persuasive and supported by results.

- Training-time use of arguments avoids test-time oracle dependence: Unlike “gold attention at test” ablations (used only for analysis), the main models learn attention from arguments during training but do not require arguments at test time. This balances practicality with leveraging rich supervision.

- Ablative insight on attention design: Comparing S1 vs S2 reveals a precision–recall trade-off (arguments-only yields higher precision; including neighboring words increases recall). This is a useful, generalizable insight about attention target construction.

- Data augmentation with FrameNet: The paper operationalizes a reasonable method to bring in extra supervision despite missing argument/entity annotations, and shows that even noisy external supervision can increase recall without severely harming precision, yielding higher F1.

Weaknesses
- Modest margins over strong baselines and potentially unfair comparisons: The final SOTA improvements are small (e.g., 71.7 vs 71.3), and the strongest baseline used here (ANN) is relatively weak architecturally (no CNN/RNN encoders or contextualized representations), which may underplay what unsupervised attention could achieve with stronger encoders. A fairer test would compare supervised vs unsupervised attention within stronger architectures (e.g., CNN/RNN with non-consecutive convolutions or BiRNNs).

- Heavy reliance on gold entities/types and unclear test-time setup: The model uses entity type embeddings for attention; it is not clear whether gold entity mentions/types are available at test time (common but not universal in ACE ED setups). If predicted entities are required, performance may degrade; if gold entities are used, this should be stated and matched to baselines. Clarity on this is crucial for fair comparison.

- Evaluation protocol and statistical reporting: The random split protocol can yield variance; stronger evidence would include multiple random seeds/splits or standard splits, and details on the t-test (test set size, paired setup) are missing. Also, reporting only F1 for Skip-CNN is incomplete; the comparison table has a missing precision figure and an “N/A”, making it hard to assess relative precision/recall trade-offs.

- Limited analysis of attention quality and alternatives: While S1/S2 are compared, there’s no qualitative or quantitative evaluation of learned attention (e.g., alignment with arguments, error cases) or exploration of alternative combinations (αw vs αe vs learned gating, removing entity attention, or multi-head variants). The simple sum α = αw + αe is ad hoc.

- Causal claims about joint models: The claim that joint methods underperform on ED due to data imbalance and trigger pre-prediction ignoring arguments is plausible but not empirically tested. An additional baseline (e.g., a joint model with loss reweighting or argument-aware trigger candidate scoring) would strengthen the argument.

Questions to Authors
- What entity resources are used at test time? Are entity mentions and types gold in evaluation, or predicted? If predicted, with which system, and how much does ED performance drop vs gold?

- How does the method perform with stronger encoders (e.g., CNNs with skip-grams, BiRNNs) under both unsupervised and supervised attention? Does the supervised attention still yield gains?

- Can you provide attention visualizations and quantitative alignment metrics (e.g., proportion of attention mass on arguments) to show that supervision meaningfully shapes attention at test time?

- How sensitive are results to λ, window size, and Gaussian parameters? Did you try learning a gating between αw and αe instead of summation?

- For FrameNet augmentation, what is the noise rate in mapped arguments, and how does performance change if you filter by confidence? Will you release the processed FN data and code?

Overall, I find the core idea novel and impactful for ED, with supportive empirical results. Despite some methodological and evaluation limitations, the paper proposes a clear new direction—supervising attention with argument annotations—that I believe is worthy of acceptance.
