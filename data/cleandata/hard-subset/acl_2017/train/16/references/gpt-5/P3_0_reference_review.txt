Summary and main contributions
This paper addresses ACE-style event detection (ED) by explicitly exploiting argument annotations to supervise a neural attention mechanism. The model computes attention over contextual words and entity types relative to a candidate trigger; during training, the attention is encouraged to align with gold arguments (S1: argument-only attention; S2: argument plus nearby words via a Gaussian). The attention-weighted context representations, together with the candidate token, are fed into a feed-forward classifier. The authors also augment training with FrameNet-derived events.

Contributions as I see them:
1) A simple supervised-attention formulation for ED that uses gold argument spans to shape token-level attention during training, thereby injecting trigger–argument supervision directly into trigger classification.
2) Empirical evidence on ACE 2005 that argument-supervised attention improves over an unsupervised-attention baseline and is competitive with, or better than, contemporary CNN/RNN and joint models; analysis contrasting entity-only vs argument-based signals.
3) A pragmatic data augmentation experiment using FrameNet events to mitigate data sparsity, and a comparison of two attention supervision strategies (S1 vs S2).

Strengths
- Clear and plausible modeling idea with direct supervision signal: The paper convincingly argues that arguments provide disambiguating evidence for triggers and that joint models often do not pass this signal to ED pre-prediction. Supervising attention with argument spans is a neat, low-complexity way to inject this information into trigger classification without full joint inference.

- Empirical gains and strong precision: On ACE 2005, the supervised attention variants (ANN-S1/S2) substantially improve over the unsupervised-attention baseline (F1: 64.6 → 70.8/71.7). ANN-S2 attains the best reported F1 among compared systems and notably higher precision, aligning with the hypothesis that argument-guided attention focuses on informative context.

- Argument vs entity ablation: The comparison between averaging over entities (ANN-ENT) and using argument supervision (ANN-Gold1/2) demonstrates that entity presence alone is insufficient; argument information is the driver. This ablation supports the central claim.

- Sensible exploration of attention supervision strategies: The two strategies (argument-only vs argument+neighbors) lead to predictable precision/recall trade-offs, suggesting the supervision is behaving as intended and offering practitioners a tunable bias.

- Data augmentation analysis: The FrameNet experiment, while modest, shows that adding noisy external data especially helps the weaker unsupervised-attention baseline, lending support to the paper’s data sparsity diagnosis regarding attention learning.

Weaknesses
- Limited novelty relative to supervised attention literature and ED practice: Supervising attention using gold annotations has precedent (e.g., in MT), and using argument information for ED is not new conceptually (joint models leverage it). The innovation here is mainly the specific use of argument spans to supervise token attention in a trigger classifier. While useful, the technical novelty is incremental.

- Incomplete methodological clarity and ablations:
  - Combining attentions: α = αw + αe is not guaranteed to be normalized; it’s unclear whether re-normalization occurs before weighted sums. The relative weighting between word-attention and entity-attention is hard-coded (sum) rather than learned; ablations (αw only, αe only, learned gating) are missing.
  - Context size n is not specified; sensitivity to window size, λ, and Gaussian σ/window is not reported.
  - Details for computing we (transform from word to “entity type space”) and e representations used in Eq. (3) need clarification for reproducibility.

- Baseline and comparability concerns:
  - The main baseline (ANN) itself uses unsupervised attention; a no-attention baseline (simple context pooling/concatenation) is missing, making it hard to attribute gains specifically to attention supervision vs attention per se.
  - Comparisons across systems rely on “following” prior splits; yet the dev set is randomly selected and the exact split for training/test may differ. This undermines direct comparability with reported numbers from other papers unless the same documents are used.
  - Skip-CNN results are reported partially (“N/A 71.3”); it’s unclear if 71.3 is F1 or recall, and thus whether the claim of best F1 is robust.

- Reliance on gold entity types and argument annotations during training; unclear test-time assumptions: The model uses entity types as part of the context representation and attention. It appears to assume gold entities at test time (as common in ACE ED setups), but this should be made explicit and aligned with compared systems. The dependence reduces practicality in settings without high-quality entity annotations.

- Limited analysis and diagnostics: No attention visualizations, error analyses by event type, or robustness tests (e.g., with predicted entities, cross-domain) are provided. Given the stated goal of helping disambiguate ambiguous triggers, a breakdown on polysemous triggers or long-range arguments would strengthen the paper.

Questions to authors
1) Do you re-normalize α after summing αw and αe? If not, what is the motivation, and how sensitive are results to a learned or tuned weighting between the two attention sources?
2) What is the context window size n, and how sensitive are results to n? Please provide an ablation on n, λ, and the Gaussian σ/window.
3) What exactly is we in Eq. (3)? Please specify the transformation and whether entity type embeddings for Ce are transformed similarly before dot products.
4) What is the “no-attention” baseline performance with the same classifier and inputs (e.g., mean/max pooling or concatenation of context and entity embeddings without attention)?
5) Which entity annotations are used at test time (gold vs predicted)? Do all compared baselines also use gold entities? If possible, provide results with predicted entities to assess practical robustness.
6) Are your ACE train/dev/test splits identical to those used in the reported prior work, or only similar in protocol? If not identical, can you report numbers of prior methods on your split or provide your code/splits for reproducibility?
7) For the Skip-CNN line in Table 3, is 71.3 F1? If not, please clarify and revisit the “best F1” claim.
8) Can you share attention heatmaps or statistics showing that attention indeed peaks on argument tokens at test time, and qualitative examples where attention corrects mislabeling of ambiguous triggers?

Additional comments
- The FrameNet augmentation pipeline introduces predicted entities; a brief estimate of noise and its effect (e.g., by filtering) would be useful.
- The paper would benefit from minor proofreading and more precise reference formatting.
