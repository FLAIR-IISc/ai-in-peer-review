Summary and main contributions
This paper addresses event detection (ED) in ACE by explicitly exploiting annotated argument information via supervised attention. The model computes attention over contextual words and entity types, and uses gold argument positions to supervise the attention during training. Two supervision strategies are proposed: S1 focuses attention on argument tokens; S2 also diffuses attention to neighboring words with a Gaussian kernel. The ED classifier is a simple feed-forward network over the current token, a weighted sum of contextual word embeddings, and contextual entity-type embeddings. Experiments on ACE 2005 (with and without FrameNet augmentation) show improvements over several prior pipeline and joint systems.

As I see them, the primary contributions are:
1) A supervised attention mechanism that leverages gold argument annotations to guide context focusing for ED, with two variants (S1, S2), yielding consistent gains.
2) An empirical finding, supported by ablations, that argument information is much more beneficial to ED than generic entity information, and that unsupervised attention is ineffective at ACE-scale data.
3) State-of-the-art F1 on ACE 2005 under the reported setup, and further gains when augmented with FrameNet-derived events.

Strengths
- Clear motivation and insight: The paper identifies a plausible reason why prior joint ED+AE systems help AE more than ED (imbalance and indirect use of arguments for trigger pre-prediction) and proposes a direct mechanism to make arguments inform ED. The motivation is supported with concrete examples (e.g., “fired”) and quantitative comparisons.

- Simple, effective mechanism: The supervised attention loss against gold argument-derived targets is straightforward to implement and integrates cleanly with a basic ED classifier. The two strategies (hard vs Gaussian-smoothed attention) are reasonable design points, with a clear precision–recall trade-off observed (S1 higher precision, S2 higher recall).

- Empirical gains and diagnostic experiments: The paper provides:
  - A baseline without attention (ANN) and with unsupervised attention, showing limited effectiveness of unsupervised attention at ACE scale.
  - A control that uses entity-only attention averaging (ANN-ENT), indicating that entities alone do not account for the gains; argument supervision matters.
  - Results with and without FrameNet augmentation, showing complementary benefits.
  - Improvements over several strong prior systems, including FN-augmented baselines.

- Practical training-time supervision: The approach only requires arguments at training time; at test time, attention is learned and applied without gold argument inputs. This makes the idea applicable whenever training data provides argument labels (as in ACE).

Weaknesses
- Comparability and reliance on gold entities: The model uses contextual entity type embeddings from ACE annotations at test time. It is unclear whether all compared systems also used gold entity mentions/types, and the practical impact when switching to predicted entities is not reported. This limits conclusions about robustness and downstream utility.

- Limited baselines and architecture capacity: The ED classifier is a shallow feed-forward network over a fixed context window. Stronger contemporary baselines (BiLSTM/CNN with contextualized encoders, or transformer-style encoders) are not compared under the same split. The poor performance of unsupervised attention may be partly due to the weak encoder rather than data size alone.

- Incomplete reporting and potential inconsistencies: 
  - Table 3 lists Skip-CNN with only recall; no F1 is reported, making “best” claims harder to verify.
  - The paper states “randomly selected 30 dev / 40 test” following prior work, but it is not explicit whether the exact same splits as compared papers were used. Given sensitivity to splits, this affects fairness.
  - Statistical testing (t-test p ≤ 0.05) is mentioned without details on runs, seeds, or variance.

- Ablation gaps on attention design: While there is a comparison to entity-only attention averaging, there is no ablation of the entity attention channel itself (e.g., word-only attention vs combined word+entity attention), or of the choice to sum word and entity attentions, window size, σ and λ sensitivity, or removal of the Gaussian smoothing. This limits interpretability of which components contribute most.

- Data processing for FrameNet augmentation: The procedure for mapping FrameNet frames/elements to ACE event types and arguments is inherited from prior work, but details are sparse. Using an external entity tagger for FN data introduces domain noise; the impact of this noise is discussed qualitatively (precision drop) but not quantified.

Questions to authors
- Do you use gold entity mentions and types at test time on ACE 2005? Do all compared systems (Li 2013; Nguyen 2016; Liu 2016a/b; Skip-CNN) also assume gold entities at test time? Please clarify to ensure fair comparison.

- How sensitive are the results to the context window size n, the Gaussian σ/window in S2, and the λ trade-off? Could you provide a brief sensitivity analysis or report default choices and ranges?

- What is the effect of removing the entity attention channel (i.e., using only word-context attention) or, conversely, removing word attention and using only entity attention? Also, what happens if you concatenate rather than sum αw and αe?

- How many random seeds/runs were used for each score and the t-test? Please report mean±std across runs to substantiate significance claims.

- For the FrameNet augmentation: 
  - Which subset of frames/types was used, and how were they mapped to ACE event types? 
  - Approximately what fraction of the augmented samples are noisy (incorrect labels) by a manual spot check?
  - Did you filter out frames with multiple lexical units that do not correspond to ACE triggers?

- Could you provide per-type P/R/F1 to understand which event subtypes benefit most from argument-supervised attention (e.g., those with distinctive roles like End-Position vs ambiguous triggers)?

Additional comments and suggestions
- Include attention visualizations on representative examples to show that the model focuses on arguments and their neighborhoods as intended.

- Evaluate with predicted entities on ACE to assess practicality, and/or add a joint variant that uses predicted arguments at test time to see whether training-time supervision transfers.

- Consider stronger encoders (BiLSTM/CNN) to disentangle the effect of supervised attention from encoder capacity, and to improve the unsupervised-attention baseline.

- Clean up references and table formatting for clarity, and explicitly state dataset splits to aid reproducibility.

Overall, the paper offers a clear, well-motivated idea—using argument-supervised attention for ED—and shows promising gains. Addressing comparability, ablations, and reporting gaps would strengthen the work and its impact.
