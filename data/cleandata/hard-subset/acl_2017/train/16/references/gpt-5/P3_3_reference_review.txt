Summary
This paper addresses event detection (ED) by explicitly leveraging annotated argument information via a supervised attention mechanism. The model treats each token as a trigger candidate and builds context representations by attending over surrounding words and entity types. The attention is supervised during training using argument annotations: S1 assigns attention only to argument tokens; S2 spreads attention to arguments and nearby tokens via a Gaussian. The ED classifier is a three-layer feedforward network trained jointly with an attention MSE loss. Experiments on ACE 2005 show gains over several prior systems; using FrameNet-derived extra data further improves recall.

Main contributions (as I see them)
1) A simple, explicit use of argument annotations to supervise attention for ED, demonstrating that argument-focused attention improves trigger classification over an unsupervised attention baseline.
2) Two practical supervision strategies for attention (arguments-only and arguments-plus-neighbors), with empirical comparison and evidence that expanding attention improves recall.
3) Empirical results on ACE 2005, including an “oracle attention” analysis and a FrameNet augmentation study, showing consistent if modest gains over prior work.

Strengths
- Clear, well-motivated idea with practical impact: The paper pinpoints a plausible reason joint ED/AE models have yielded limited ED gains (imbalance and indirect use of arguments) and proposes a focused remedy. The design—using argument positions to supervise attention—is conceptually clean and easy to incorporate into standard architectures.

- Empirical evidence that arguments matter: The “oracle” variants (ANN-Gold1/2) that use argument locations at test time substantially outperform variants without argument information, isolating the value of arguments for disambiguating triggers. This strengthens the core claim independently of architectural choices.

- Competitive results on ACE 2005: ANN-S2 slightly improves F1 over strong contemporaneous baselines, and substantially improves precision. The gains persist when augmented with FrameNet. The recall boost of S2 vs. S1 is consistent with the intended behavior of the Gaussian spreading, giving a coherent story.

- Simplicity and modularity: The supervised attention loss is lightweight and can be added to existing ED models without complex joint inference or structured learning. This increases the potential for adoption.

- FrameNet augmentation experiment: Although modest, the study suggests that additional weakly labeled instances help both unsupervised and supervised attention, supporting the paper’s hypothesis about data sparsity.

Weaknesses
- Limited novelty relative to supervised attention literature and joint EE: Supervised attention is established (e.g., in NMT); using gold argument spans to supervise attention feels like a straightforward application. While the paper’s focus on ED (rather than AE) is useful, the methodological contribution is incremental.

- Baseline underperformance and fairness concerns: The unsupervised attention baseline (ANN) performs markedly below widely reported CNN/RNN ED systems, suggesting the architecture is weak and possibly inflating the relative gains from supervision. Moreover, several comparisons appear to use numbers quoted from prior work that may not be on the identical random split; the 0.4 F1 improvement over Skip-CNN is small, and the claim of statistical significance (p ≤ 0.05) is unconvincing without rerunning baselines on the same split and reporting variance across multiple random seeds/splits.

- Incomplete/murky methodological details:
  - Attention combination: α is defined as αw + αe, yet αw and αe are softmax distributions; their sum is not normalized, but α is directly used for weighted sums. Is there a renormalization? If not, the magnitude of α depends on the number of channels, confounding interpretation.
  - Entity stream: It is unclear how entity attention is computed (are entity embeddings transformed like words? what is the transformation for entity types?), and what role it plays at test time.
  - Context window n is not specified; sensitivity analyses are missing.
  - Are word embeddings updated during training?
  - Several equations and definitions for entities (e_k transforms) are underspecified.

- Reliance on gold entity mentions/types at test time: The model uses entity types in the context. Do experiments use gold ACE entity mentions? If so, this is a strong oracle and may limit practical applicability. No results with predicted entities are provided to assess robustness.

- Analysis gaps:
  - No ablation to quantify the contribution of supervised attention vs. the entity channel vs. word-only attention.
  - No sensitivity to λ, Gaussian parameters (σ, window), or attention supervision strategy beyond S1 vs. S2.
  - No qualitative attention visualizations to demonstrate that the model indeed focuses on arguments or their neighborhoods.
  - The S1 fallback (“if no arguments in context, average attention over entities”) is ad hoc and might inject noise; its impact is not analyzed.

- Reproducibility: Important details (random seeds, exact split, code, pretrained embeddings) are not provided. The FrameNet augmentation requires additional processing (core elements and an IE system for entities); without releasing this data, reproducibility is challenging.

Questions to Authors
- What entity information is used at test time? Are entity mentions/types gold or predicted? If gold, can you report results with predicted entities to assess robustness?

- How exactly is α combined and normalized? After α = αw + αe, do you renormalize α before computing cw and ce? Please clarify the transformations for entity embeddings and the attention computation for entities.

- Are baseline numbers (Li 2013, Liu 2016, Nguyen 2016, Skip-CNN) recomputed on your exact split, or copied from the papers? If copied, can you rerun at least the strongest baselines on your split and report mean±std over multiple runs?

- What is the context window size n, and how sensitive are results to n, λ, σ, and window size for S2?

- Do you update word embeddings during training? Any difference if frozen?

- Can you provide ablations: supervised attention off (λ=0) but keeping the same ED classifier; removing the entity attention channel; using only word attention with/without supervision?

- Can you include attention visualizations and per-event-type performance to show where the method helps most (e.g., ambiguous triggers like “fired”)?
