Summary and Claimed Contributions
This paper addresses event detection (ED) in ACE 2005 by explicitly leveraging annotated argument information via supervised attention. The model builds context representations by attending over neighboring words and entity types conditioned on a trigger candidate, and it supervises the attention weights using gold argument positions during training. Two supervision strategies are explored: (S1) focusing attention only on argument tokens, and (S2) distributing attention over arguments and nearby words via a Gaussian kernel. Experiments on ACE 2005, with and without additional FrameNet-derived data, show improvements over several reported baselines.

As I see them, the main contributions (in order of strength) are:
1) A simple, well-motivated way to use annotated arguments to supervise attention for ED, rather than relying on joint models that primarily benefit argument extraction.
2) Empirical evidence that argument-aware supervision improves trigger classification on ACE 2005, including analysis of two attention-supervision strategies and a study with additional FN data.
3) A lightweight architecture demonstrating that even basic attention over words and entity types can yield gains when properly supervised.

Strengths
- Clear motivation and problem framing: The paper convincingly argues why joint models can underutilize argument information for ED and motivates a direct, supervised-attention alternative. The example of “fired” (End-Position vs. Attack) is illustrative and grounded in ACE.
- Conceptual simplicity with measurable gains: The method is straightforward to implement (dot-product attention over words and entity types, trained with an auxiliary MSE loss) and yields improvements over the authors’ unsupervised-attention baseline and several prior systems, with especially strong precision.
- Exploration of supervision strategies: Comparing S1 vs. S2 provides insight into precision–recall trade-offs (arguments-only vs. arguments-plus-neighborhoods), which is useful for practitioners.
- Analysis supporting the argument-usefulness hypothesis: The “correctness” study (ANN vs. ANN-ENT vs. ANN-Gold1/2) cleanly isolates that argument supervision, not generic entity awareness, drives the observed gains.
- Data augmentation with FrameNet: Although modest, the FN experiments add credibility that the approach benefits from more positive supervision and is not fragile to an expanded training set.

Weaknesses
- Limited novelty relative to the literature on supervised attention: The technique closely mirrors prior supervised attention ideas (e.g., for NMT), applied here to ED with gold arguments as supervision. From a 2025 perspective, the architectural core (shallow ANN with attention over fixed windows) is dated compared to transformer-based models with contextualized encoders and span-based formulations. The paper would be stronger if the core idea were integrated into a more competitive backbone and compared to contemporary ED baselines.
- Evaluation comparability and potential over-reliance on gold annotations: It appears gold entity types are used at test time (“we exploit the annotated entity information”), which can inflate performance and complicate comparisons with works using predicted entities. The “correctness” study uses gold arguments at test time in ANN-Gold1/2, which is unrealistic (though positioned as an oracle). Clarifying and controlling for these factors is essential for fair comparison.
- Missing or incomplete comparisons and reporting: The Skip-CNN row is malformed (only recall shown); it is unclear whether all reported baselines used the same splits and resources (e.g., FN). No comparisons to stronger neural baselines with contextualized encoders are provided. Statistical significance claims are made from one split without details (e.g., variance across different random splits).
- Ablation and sensitivity gaps: The contribution of word vs. entity-type attention (αw vs. αe) is not disentangled; α = αw + αe is used without exploring weights or gating. There is limited hyperparameter sensitivity analysis for λ, window size, Gaussian parameters, or attention temperature. More comprehensive ablations would strengthen the causal claims.
- Clarity and reproducibility issues: Notation is occasionally inconsistent; tables have formatting problems; references contain errors. Important implementation details (e.g., whether entity recognition is gold or predicted at test; pre-processing; exact splits; code and pretrained resources) are insufficiently specified for replication.

Questions to Authors
- Do you use gold entity mentions/types at test time? If so, can you report results with predicted entities to align with more realistic settings and to fairly compare with baselines that do not rely on gold entities?
- How sensitive are results to λ, window size n, and the Gaussian parameters (µ, σ, w)? Did you try a learnable combination α = β αw + (1−β) αe or a small gating network?
- What is the exact attention normalization temperature and are the transformed representations w and e layer-normalized? Any gradient stability issues when adding D(θ)?
- How was statistical significance assessed (number of runs/seeds)? Can you provide mean and variance over multiple random initializations/splits?
- Will you release code, trained models, and the FN-preprocessed data (including the entity labels produced by the RPI system) to ensure reproducibility?

Suggestions for Improvement
- Evaluate with predicted entity mentions/types and report both “with-gold” and “with-predicted” results. This will clarify the method’s robustness and comparability.
- Integrate the supervised-attention objective into a stronger backbone (e.g., BERT/SpanBERT/DeBERTa encoders with span classification) and compare against contemporary ED baselines on ACE 2005 and additional datasets (e.g., ERE, TAC KBP, MAVEN).
- Provide fuller ablations: remove αe or αw; learnable mixing weights; different attention functions; alternative supervision distributions; and attention visualization/error analysis to show the supervision changes model behavior beyond label memorization.
- Improve evaluation rigor: multi-run averages with standard deviations; cross-validation over multiple document splits; careful control over external resources and comparable settings with prior work.
- Clarify and fix presentation issues (tables, references, notation) and add implementation detail sufficient for exact replication.

Overall, the idea of supervising attention with gold arguments for ED is reasonable and empirically helpful, but the methodological novelty and evaluation rigor are currently insufficient for acceptance at a top venue. I encourage strengthening the study along the lines above.
