- Paper proposes supervised attention for event detection that uses annotated arguments to guide attention over context words and entity types, improving ACE 2005 performance.
- Main contributions: supervised attention leveraging arguments, empirical validation including ablations with gold attention, and a simple efficient architecture with analysis of attention strategies.

Strengths:
- Intuitive and well-motivated idea: argument information helps disambiguate triggers (e.g., “fired”).
- Strong empirical gains over an unsupervised-attention baseline (64.6 → 71.7 F1), showing the benefit of attention supervision.
- Competitive with or slightly better than reported SOTA on ACE 2005 (e.g., ANN-S2 vs. Nguyen & Grishman 2016).
- Ablations demonstrate arguments add value beyond entity types alone (ANN-ENT vs ANN-Gold variants).
- Practical at test time since it does not require arguments for inference—only for training supervision.

Weaknesses:
- Limited methodological novelty: supervised attention is a straightforward extension of existing ideas.
- Requires gold argument annotations during training and may rely on gold entities at test time, limiting generalizability.
- Comparability/statistical rigor unclear: dataset splits, number of runs/seeds, and t-test details are not fully reported.
- Missing analyses: no per-type error analysis, attention visualizations, or ablations for key design choices (only-words vs only-entities, window size, λ, alternative attention forms).
- FrameNet augmentation is noisy and gives marginal gains (71.7 → 71.9) without deeper analysis.

Questions for authors:
- Do you use gold or predicted entity mentions/types at test time; if predicted, which system and performance?
- What is the context window length n and sensitivity of results to n; how often do arguments fall outside the window?
- Why combine word and entity attentions into a single α instead of separate attentions and fusion; any ablations with only αw or αe?
- How many random seeds/runs were used; what are mean/std F1 and full details of the t-test?
- In S1, why fallback to averaging attention over entities when no arguments are present; were alternatives tried?
- Can attention supervision be learned from predicted arguments (from an AE model) instead of gold annotations?

Additional minor points:
- Improve clarity (typos, figure refs, notation), report full P/R/F1 for all baselines, include qualitative attention examples, and release code for reproducibility.

Overall recommendation:
- Solid empirical paper with moderate novelty but valuable demonstration that argument-supervised attention improves event detection; needs clarification on entity usage, statistical reporting, and more analyses.
