Summary and main contributions
This paper addresses event detection (ED) in ACE 2005 by explicitly exploiting argument annotations to supervise an attention mechanism. The model computes attention over a fixed window of contextual words and entity types around a trigger candidate via dot-product attention with the candidate, and trains the attention with a loss that encourages higher mass on annotated arguments (S1) or on arguments plus nearby words via a Gaussian smoothing (S2). The attended context representations (for words and entity types) are concatenated with the candidate’s embedding and fed to a simple feed-forward classifier. The paper further augments training with FrameNet-derived “events,” where frame elements are treated as arguments.

As I see it, the main contributions are:
1) A supervised attention formulation for ED that uses argument annotations as explicit supervision signals, yielding improvements over unsupervised attention and competitive results on ACE 2005.
2) A systematic comparison of attention supervision strategies (S1 vs S2) and an oracle analysis (gold attention at test time) demonstrating the value of argument information for ED.
3) An augmentation experiment using FrameNet frames as additional (noisy) training data by mapping frame elements to arguments and adding predicted ACE entity mentions.

Strengths
- Clear motivation and focused technical idea: The paper articulates a plausible reason why prior joint models have helped AE more than ED (imbalance and indirect use of arguments) and proposes a simple yet targeted remedy: teach the attention where to look using argument supervision. This design directly addresses the stated problem and is well aligned with the ED task’s needs.
- Empirical gains over strong baselines on ACE 2005: The supervised attention variants improve substantially over the authors’ unsupervised-attention baseline (ANN) and slightly outperform published state-of-the-art numbers (e.g., Skip-CNN), with higher precision. The improvements are consistent across S1/S2 variants and remain when augmented with FrameNet, suggesting robustness.
- Insightful oracle analysis: The comparison to ANN-Gold1/2 (using gold attention at test time) quantifies the ceiling if argument locations were known, and the contrast to ANN-ENT (entity-only heuristic attention) convincingly shows that argument supervision, not merely entity cues, drives the gains.
- Simple, general mechanism: The supervised attention loss is model-agnostic and could be layered on other ED architectures. The technique of constructing target attention distributions (including smoothed variants) is straightforward to implement and potentially reusable for related IE tasks.
- Sensible use of extra data: The FrameNet augmentation experiment is carefully adapted by mapping core frame elements to arguments and adding entity mentions with an external system. It helps both supervised and unsupervised attention setups and supports the claim that data sparsity undermines unsupervised attention in ED.

Weaknesses
- Limited novelty w.r.t. supervised attention: The core technical idea—attention supervision using task-specific “rationales”—is known (e.g., in NMT and other tasks). The contribution here is its application to ED via ACE arguments. The attention mechanism itself is a standard dot-product with a simple feed-forward classifier; no architectural innovation beyond the supervision is introduced. The paper would be stronger with integration into stronger ED architectures (CNN/BiLSTM) for a fairer assessment of the supervision signal’s added value.
- Weak baseline and missing ablations: The unsupervised attention baseline (ANN) is markedly weaker than prior neural baselines (64.6 F1 vs ~70+), amplifying the perceived gains of supervision. There is no ablation disentangling: only word attention, only entity-type attention, removal of entity features, varying window size n, or using position embeddings. Without these, it is hard to assess which components matter and how reliant the method is on gold entities.
- Assumptions and potential leakage: The model appears to use gold ACE entity types at test time, which is common in some ED setups but limits practical applicability and comparability to works using predicted entities. For FrameNet augmentation, arguments are from frame elements and entities are automatic, whereas on ACE test entities appear gold—this mismatch should be discussed and controlled. Also, for negative candidates and sentences with multiple events, the gold attention construction (e.g., averaging over all entities when no arguments are in context) may inject noise or bias; this needs clearer justification and analysis.
- Insufficient analysis of attention quality and error patterns: While the oracle analysis is useful, there is no qualitative/quantitative evaluation of learned attention alignment to arguments (e.g., attention overlap metrics), per-type performance, or error breakdowns on ambiguous triggers—key to validating the claimed mechanism. The t-test claim lacks detail (test setting, variance, number of runs), and improvements over best prior are small.
- Clarity and presentation issues: Some notational/formatting problems (missing figures, inconsistent references), and key hyperparameters are under-specified (e.g., context window n). The mapping from FrameNet frames to ACE event types and handling of subtype mismatches are not fully explained. These issues hinder reproducibility.

Questions to authors
1) Do you use gold entity mentions and types at test time on ACE 2005? If so, can you report results with predicted entities to assess practical robustness, and clarify whether the compared baselines use the same assumption?
2) How are gold attention vectors constructed for negative trigger candidates and for sentences with multiple event mentions? Do you ever assign attention to arguments of other events, and could that confound training?
3) What is the context window size n, and how sensitive are results to n? Do arguments frequently fall outside the window?
4) Can you provide attention alignment metrics (e.g., proportion of attention mass on true arguments) and per-event-type results to substantiate the mechanism’s effectiveness on ambiguous triggers?
5) Please detail the statistical testing protocol (number of random runs, variance, exact baselines compared). The absolute gains over the best prior are small; are they consistent across runs?
6) For FrameNet augmentation, how do you map frames to ACE subtypes, and what proportion of augmented labels are noisy? Any filtering beyond “core” elements?

Overall, this paper presents a focused and practically useful idea—explicitly supervising attention with argument annotations—that yields modest but consistent gains on ACE 2005 and clarifies why arguments help ED. Strengthening baselines, clarifying evaluation assumptions, and adding deeper analysis would significantly improve the paper’s impact and rigor.
