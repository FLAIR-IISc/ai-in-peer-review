Summary
The paper tackles event detection (ED) in ACE 2005 by explicitly leveraging annotated argument information through supervised attention. The model treats each token as a trigger candidate and builds a simple neural classifier whose input includes the candidate embedding and an attention-weighted representation of contextual words and entity types. The key idea is to supervise the attention mechanism using “gold” attention derived from annotated arguments: S1 attends only to argument tokens; S2 also diffuses attention to neighboring tokens via a Gaussian kernel. The ED loss and attention-matching loss are jointly optimized. Experiments on ACE 2005 show improvements over a weak unsupervised-attention baseline and modest gains over reported state-of-the-art systems. Augmenting with FrameNet-derived events further improves recall.

Main contributions (as I see them)
1) A simple, explicit use of labeled argument spans to supervise attention for ED, resulting in consistent gains over a baseline without argument supervision and slightly surpassing prior results on ACE 2005.
2) A systematic comparison of two attention supervision strategies (argument-only vs argument-plus-neighbors) and a demonstration that unsupervised attention struggles under data sparsity, partially mitigated by augmenting with FrameNet.
3) An effective, lightweight architecture that fuses contextual word and entity-type attention for ED.

Strengths
- Clear and intuitive problem motivation and design: The paper articulates why argument information should help ED (especially for ambiguous triggers) and proposes a direct mechanism to inject that supervision into the attention module, avoiding the indirect dependence of prior joint models. This is a principled and task-salient use of attention supervision.
- Competitive performance on ACE 2005: The proposed supervised-attention variants (especially S2) achieve state-of-the-art or near-SOTA results (F1 71.7) relative to reported numbers, with notably higher precision. The gains over the unsupervised attention baseline are substantial, supporting the method’s effectiveness.
- Sensible ablations and analysis: Comparing S1 vs S2 reveals a precision–recall trade-off that matches intuition (argument-only attention is more precise; argument-smoothed attention yields higher recall). The FrameNet augmentation experiment further supports the claim that data sparsity limits unsupervised attention, as adding data improves the baseline and supervised models.
- Simplicity and efficiency: The model architecture is straightforward (no heavy sequence encoders), which eases training and makes the contribution focused on supervision strategy rather than model capacity.
- Use of entity-type context: Combining attention over contextual words and entity types is a reasonable choice for ED and appears to contribute to precision.

Weaknesses
- Limited novelty: Supervising attention with gold labels, including distributing attention around labeled spans (akin to Mi et al., 2016 for NMT), is conceptually straightforward. The technical contribution is incremental; the main novelty is applying it to ED with two specific supervision schemes.
- Baseline and comparability concerns: The core baseline (ANN with unsupervised attention) is relatively weak; stronger neural baselines (e.g., CNN/RNN sentence encoders without attention or with self-attention) trained under the same split are not provided. The comparisons to published systems may not be strictly apples-to-apples due to possible differences in splits, resources, or use of entity information. The reported significance tests against published numbers are not convincing without paired comparisons on identical outputs.
- Reliance on gold entities at test time: The model exploits entity type embeddings for contextual tokens, and ACE evaluation often uses gold entities; however, it remains unclear how much the gains persist with predicted entities, which is crucial for realistic end-to-end settings. The FN augmentation does use predicted entities, but the main ACE test results appear to use gold, potentially inflating performance and masking robustness.
- Risk of overfitting to argument supervision: During training, attention is directly fit to gold argument positions for the same event mention. While test-time does not use arguments, there is a risk the model is learning to key on argument patterns that may not generalize across domains or genres. The improvements over SOTA are modest; additional evaluations (e.g., cross-domain, per-class analysis, ambiguous trigger subsets) would strengthen the generalization claim.
- Missing ablations and error analysis: The paper lacks ablations to isolate contributions from entity vs word attention, the effect of the attention loss weight λ, window size and σ for S2, and the context window length. An error breakdown (by event type, trigger ambiguity, or error category) is absent, limiting interpretability of where supervised attention helps or fails.
- Clarity and presentation: Notation and writing have several inconsistencies/typos; some details (e.g., whether arguments outside the fixed window are ignored, how often that happens) are missing. References are inconsistently formatted. Reproducibility details (code, preprocessing, hyperparameter search ranges) are not provided.

Questions to authors
- Evaluation protocol: Are all reported numbers strictly on the same ACE 2005 split and conditions (e.g., gold entities at test) as the compared prior work? Please clarify splits and resources for each baseline, and provide results of your model under the exact settings used by the strongest baselines.
- Entity dependence: What is the performance when using predicted entities (e.g., from the RPI system) on ACE dev/test? How sensitive is the method to entity tagging errors?
- Attention components: What are the impacts of removing entity-type attention (αe=0) or word attention (αw=0)? Also, what is the performance with supervised attention only on words (no entity supervision) vs only on entities?
- Hyperparameters: How was λ chosen for S1 (1.0) and S2 (5.0)? Please report sensitivity analyses (λ, window size, σ) and the effect of context length n.
- Generalization and robustness: Can you provide per-class results and analysis on ambiguous triggers (e.g., “fire” cases) to substantiate the claim that argument supervision resolves ambiguity? Any cross-domain/genre evaluation within ACE?

Overall, this is a well-motivated, simple approach that empirically improves ED by directly supervising attention with argument annotations. The novelty is moderate, and stronger baselines/comparability and robustness analyses would strengthen the case.
