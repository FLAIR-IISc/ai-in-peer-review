- Proposes embeddings for words, phrases, and concepts by mapping ontology concepts to atomic phrases and training with a skip-gram style model.
- Evaluated on concept similarity and relatedness using UMLS and Yago.
- Strength: Targets non-compositional phrases and offers a plausible method for training phrase embeddings.
- Strength: Trained embeddings are competitive or superior at identifying concept similarity in some settings.
- Strength: Released software could benefit biomedical NLP researchers.
- Weakness: Methodary is not very novel—essentially a tweak to skip-gram.
- Weakness: Full model does not consistently perform best; Choi baseline substantially outperforms on the Mayo datasets and trends similarly in Table 6.
- Weakness: On the larger UMNSRS dataset the model is at best competitive with simpler baselines (Chiu).
- Concern: Describing phrase occurrences as “distant supervision” is misleading—occurrences provide context but not traditional supervised signals.
- Issue: Notation introduced in Section 3.2 (e.g., E_W) is never used later in the paper.
- Concern: Using a single global β to control compositionality is surprising; cross-validated β values (zero for PM+CL and WikiNYT) counterintuitively argue against compositionality.
- Clarification needed: Experimental setup for Table 4 is unclear—how similarities are computed given many-to-many concept↔phrase mappings.
- Concern: Table 5 shows averaging phrase vectors (approximate phr) performs best, questioning the utility of the concept ontology versus starting from seed phrases.
