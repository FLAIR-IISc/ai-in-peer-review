

# ACL Peer Review

## Summary and Main Contributions

This paper proposes a weakly-supervised method for jointly embedding concepts, phrases, and words into a shared vector space using distant supervision from ontologies (UMLS and YAGO). The key insight is to replace known phrases from the ontology with unique identifiers in an unannotated corpus, then train a modified skip-gram model to learn embeddings for concepts, phrases, and words simultaneously. The authors introduce two hyperparameters (β and η) to control the tradeoff between independence and compositionality in the training.

The main contributions of this work are:
1. A novel weakly-supervised approach for jointly embedding concepts, phrases, and words using distant supervision from ontologies without requiring human annotations.
2. A novel dataset of similarity and relatedness judgments for real-world entities (people, places, organizations).
3. Insightful analysis demonstrating that the embeddings preserve ontological relationships between concepts and phrases, and cluster concepts of the same semantic type.

## Strengths

1. **Novel weakly-supervised approach**: The paper presents a genuinely novel method for concept embedding that doesn't require human annotations. The use of phrase mappings from ontologies as distant supervision is clever and addresses a significant challenge in the field. The method is simple yet effective, with results showing competitive performance to prior methods that require human annotations. This is particularly valuable for domains where annotation is expensive, such as biomedical NLP.

2. **Novel dataset for real-world entity similarity/relatedness**: The authors introduce a valuable new resource for the NLP community - a dataset of similarity and relatedness judgments for real-world entities. This addresses a significant gap in the literature, as there are well-studied datasets for word similarity but not for real-world entities. The dataset is carefully constructed with human annotations, and the authors provide detailed information about the data collection process, making it a useful resource for future research.

3. **Insightful analysis of embedding properties**: Beyond just reporting accuracy numbers, the paper provides valuable analysis of how the embeddings capture semantic relationships. The compositionality analysis (Figures 3 and 4) demonstrates that the model successfully preserves relationships between concepts and phrases, and between phrases and words. The semantic type clustering analysis (Table 6) shows that the embeddings capture meaningful semantic groupings, which is crucial for understanding what the embeddings actually represent.

## Weaknesses

1. **Lack of clear novelty over prior work**: While the method is presented as novel, the paper doesn't sufficiently distinguish it from Wang et al. (2014a), which also jointly embeds KB entities and words. The key difference appears to be the use of distant supervision from phrase mappings rather than knowledge base relations, but this distinction isn't clearly articulated. The paper also doesn't adequately contrast with Mencia et al. (2016) and Choi et al. (2016b), which are mentioned but not properly analyzed. The novelty claim is somewhat weak given these similar prior approaches.

2. **Questionable evaluation methodology**: The authors tune hyperparameters on the evaluation datasets (Table 3), which is a serious methodological flaw. As acknowledged in the paper ("Chiu et al. (2016a) point out that in the biomedical domain, hyperparameter settings that improve performance on intrinsic evaluations of the vector space may degrade performance in extrinsic downstream applications"), this can lead to overfitting to the evaluation datasets. The paper mentions using "an adapted 5-fold cross-validation" but doesn't provide sufficient details on how this was implemented to avoid data leakage, undermining the validity of the reported results.

3. **Limited comparison to state-of-the-art methods**: The paper compares primarily to traditional word embedding approaches rather than more recent contextual methods like BERT or ELMo. The evaluation is limited to similarity and relatedness tasks, without demonstrating performance on downstream NLP tasks that would better validate the utility of the embeddings. The comparison to CHOI is strong, but the paper doesn't explain why their method outperforms CHOI on larger datasets but loses on smaller ones.

4. **Lack of analysis on β and η hyperparameters**: The paper introduces β and η as important hyperparameters but doesn't provide a thorough analysis of their impact. The authors note that "a non-zero η is only optimal in a single corpus setting" but don't explain why this is the case or provide guidance for choosing these parameters in practice. This is a significant limitation for a method that relies on these parameters.

5. **Insufficient discussion of limitations**: The paper acknowledges some limitations (e.g., "a slight mismatch between the ontologies we used") but doesn't thoroughly discuss the broader limitations of the approach. For example, it doesn't address how the method handles concepts with multiple meanings (polysemy) or concepts that aren't well-represented in the ontology, which are critical for understanding the method's applicability.

## Questions to Authors

1. How does your method specifically differ from Wang et al. (2014a), which also jointly embeds KB entities and words? The paper acknowledges this work but doesn't clearly articulate the key differences that make your approach novel.

2. Can you provide more details on how you implemented the 5-fold cross-validation to avoid data leakage when tuning hyperparameters? The paper states you used "an adapted 5-fold cross-validation," but doesn't explain how you ensured that the evaluation datasets were not used in the tuning process.

3. Why does your method perform better on larger datasets but worse on smaller ones compared to CHOI? Is this a limitation of your approach, or is it due to the specific nature of the smaller datasets?

4. Could you provide more insight into the impact of the β and η hyperparameters on the embeddings? For example, what happens when you set β=1 or η=1 (full compositionality)?

5. How would your method handle concepts with multiple meanings (polysemy) or concepts that are not well-represented in the ontology?

## Overall Recommendation

This paper presents a valuable contribution to the field with a novel weakly-supervised approach for concept embedding and a new dataset for real-world entity similarity. However, the methodological flaws in the evaluation and the lack of clear distinction from prior work are significant weaknesses that need to be addressed. With appropriate revisions to strengthen the novelty claim, improve the evaluation methodology, and provide more thorough analysis, this could be a strong contribution to ACL. I recommend rejection for the current version but would support acceptance after major revisions addressing the weaknesses identified above.
