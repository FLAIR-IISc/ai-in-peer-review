

# ACL Review

## Summary
This paper proposes a weakly-supervised method for jointly embedding concepts, phrases, and words using distant supervision from an ontology. The authors claim to learn embeddings for concepts by training on contexts of their representative phrases, without requiring human annotations. The paper presents a novel dataset of similarity and relatedness for real-world entities, and demonstrates that their approach performs competitively with prior methods that require human annotations.

## Main Contributions (as I see them)
1. A weakly-supervised method for jointly embedding concepts, phrases, and words using distant supervision from an ontology
2. A novel dataset of similarity and relatedness for real-world entities
3. An analysis showing that the model preserves ontological links between concepts and phrases

## Strengths
1. The core idea of using distant supervision from ontology mappings to train concept embeddings is well-motivated and addresses a genuine need in the field. The paper clearly explains why word-level embeddings are insufficient for certain concepts (e.g., "the Big Apple" vs. "New York City").

2. The model architecture is well-organized and clearly explained, with appropriate attention to the relationship between concepts, phrases, and words. The introduction of ╬▓ and ╬Ę hyperparameters to control compositionality is a thoughtful design choice.

3. The supplemental material provides thorough details about hyperparameter tuning and dataset construction, which is valuable for reproducibility.

## Weaknesses

1. **Severely inadequate inter-annotator agreement for the new dataset**: The authors claim to present a novel dataset of similarity and relatedness for real-world entities, but report an overall Fleiss' ╬║ of 0.24 for both similarity and relatedness datasets, indicating "fair" agreement between annotators. However, ╬║=0.24 is at the very low end of "fair" agreement (0.21-0.40), and is barely above chance agreement (0.0-0.20). This is an unacceptably low level of agreement for a dataset intended to serve as a gold standard for evaluation. The authors should have discarded the dataset or improved the annotation process to achieve higher agreement (╬║ > 0.60). This fundamental flaw undermines the value of the main contribution (the new dataset).

2. **Critical mathematical errors in the paper**: Several equations contain significant notational errors that would make the method difficult to reproduce:
   - Equation (4): "logŽā(~c ┬Ę ~o)ŌłÆ Ōłæ nŌłłN logŽā(~c ┬Ę ~n) ] 1 |Cs|" should be "logŽā(~c ┬Ę ~o)ŌłÆ Ōłæ nŌłłN logŽā(~c ┬Ę ~n) ] / |Cs|"
   - Equation (6): "logŽā(~p ┬Ę Wp)" should be "logŽā(~p ┬Ę W╠äp)" to indicate the average embedding
   - Equation (7): "logŽā(~c ┬Ę Pc)" should be "logŽā(~c ┬Ę P╠äc)"
   These errors are not minor; they would lead to incorrect implementation of the method.

3. **Inconsistent and poorly defined terminology**: 
   - The paper uses "concept" without consistently defining it. The footnote in Section 2 is insufficient as it's only in the related work section.
   - The term "CUI" (Concept Unique Identifier) is used throughout without definition (it's defined in the reference list but not in the main text).
   - The paper states "We also present a novel dataset of similarity and relatedness of real-world entities, identified both by Wikipedia page and text phrase," but the dataset is constructed using YAGO entities, not directly from Wikipedia pages. This is misleading.

4. **Inconsistent formatting and grammar issues**: The paper contains numerous minor formatting inconsistencies and grammatical errors throughout:
   - "Lou GehrigŌĆÖs disease" uses a right single quote instead of an apostrophe
   - "Patient was diagnosed with chronic obstructive lung disease in December." (missing period at end of sentence)
   - "most concepts has cosine similarity" (subject-verb agreement error: "has" should be "have")
   - "WORDind indicates word embeddings trained alone using the skipgram model" (should be "skip-gram")
   - "cui2vec" should be "CUI2Vec" to match capitalization in references
   - Multiple instances of inconsistent hyphenation (e.g., "tradeoff" vs. "trade-off")

5. **Inadequate discussion of limitations**: The paper acknowledges some limitations but fails to address the most significant one: the extremely low inter-annotator agreement for their new dataset. The authors state "Overall Fleiss' ╬║ for our annotations was 0.24 for both datasets, indicating fair agreement between annotators" but don't provide sufficient justification for why this level of agreement is acceptable for their evaluation dataset. Given that ╬║=0.24 is at the lower end of "fair" agreement, this is a critical omission.

## Questions to Authors
1. How do you justify using a dataset with Fleiss' ╬║=0.24 as a benchmark for evaluating concept embeddings? Given that this level of agreement is barely above chance, how can the results from this dataset be considered reliable?

2. Can you provide a detailed explanation of why the mathematical equations in Sections 3.2.1 and 3.2.2 contain critical notational errors that would affect implementation? What steps will you take to correct these errors in the final version?

3. The paper states "We also present a novel dataset of similarity and relatedness of real-world entities, identified both by Wikipedia page and text phrase." However, the dataset is constructed using YAGO entities, not directly from Wikipedia pages. Can you clarify this discrepancy and how the dataset relates to Wikipedia pages?

## Recommendation
**Reject**

While the core idea of the method is interesting and well-motivated, the paper contains significant methodological flaws that undermine its main contributions. The extremely low inter-annotator agreement (╬║=0.24) for the novel dataset fundamentally undermines its value as a benchmark. Additionally, the critical mathematical errors in the paper would make the method difficult to reproduce, and the inconsistent terminology and formatting issues indicate a lack of attention to detail that would be expected in an ACL publication. These issues collectively outweigh the paper's contributions, and I would recommend rejection.
