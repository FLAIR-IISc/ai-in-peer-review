

# Peer Review: A Weakly-Supervised Method for Jointly Embedding Concepts, Phrases, and Words

## Summary
This paper proposes a weakly-supervised method for jointly embedding concepts, phrases, and words using structured knowledge from ontologies as distant supervision. The approach replaces phrases mapped to concepts in a corpus with unique identifiers, then trains embeddings using a modified skip-gram model with negative sampling. The method introduces hyperparameters (β and η) to control compositionality between phrases and words, and concepts and phrases. The authors evaluate on biomedical concept similarity/relatedness tasks and introduce a new dataset for real-world entity similarity/relatedness.

## Main Contributions
1. A weakly-supervised method for concept embedding that requires no human annotations beyond existing ontology mappings.
2. A novel dataset of similarity/relatedness judgments for real-world entities (people, places, organizations).
3. Demonstration that concept embeddings trained with this method can achieve competitive performance with methods requiring human annotations.

## Strengths

1. **Novel weakly-supervised approach**: The paper correctly identifies a critical gap in the literature—many concepts don't have lexical overlap between their different textual forms (e.g., "Lou Gehrig's disease" and "amyotrophic lateral sclerosis"). The use of ontology mappings as distant supervision is clever and addresses the need for scalable concept representation without human annotations. This approach is particularly valuable for domains where human annotation is expensive or impractical.

2. **Valuable new dataset**: The authors' creation of a novel dataset for real-world entity similarity/relatedness is a significant contribution. As they note, while there are well-studied datasets for word similarity, comparable datasets for real-world entities are scarce. Their crowdsourced dataset (688 pairs each for similarity and relatedness) fills an important gap in the literature and will benefit future research in entity representation learning.

3. **Competitive performance**: On the larger biomedical datasets (UMNSRS), the proposed method achieves the highest correlation with human judgments for similarity tasks and competitive performance on relatedness. This demonstrates that weak supervision can yield embeddings nearly as good as those from methods requiring substantial human annotation (like CHOI, which uses ICD-9 codes).

## Weaknesses

1. **Critical evaluation methodology flaw**: The paper's hyperparameter tuning process (Section 5.2) uses an adapted 5-fold cross-validation that still tunes on the evaluation datasets, violating standard NLP evaluation practices. The authors acknowledge this issue in Section 6 ("we are still tuning on the test set") but fail to provide a proper solution or analysis of how this might affect their results. This is a major methodological flaw that undermines the validity of their comparisons.

2. **Inadequate comparison with Wang et al. (2014a)**: The paper cites Wang et al. (2014a) as the "closest approach," but fails to adequately explain why their method is better or how it differs in practice. Wang et al. also jointly embeds KB entities and words, but their method relies on binary relations in the knowledge base (which the authors correctly note are "highly incomplete"). The paper doesn't analyze how the authors' distant supervision approach might address this limitation of Wang et al.'s work.

3. **Insufficient analysis of ontology mapping quality**: The paper treats ontology mappings as perfect supervision, but doesn't analyze how errors in the mappings (e.g., incorrect phrase-concept associations) might affect the embeddings. For instance, if UMLS or YAGO contains incorrect mappings, how does this propagate to the embeddings? Without this analysis, it's unclear how robust the method is to real-world ontology quality issues.

4. **Misleading claims about CHOI comparison**: The paper claims their method outperforms CHOI on larger biomedical datasets, but CHOI requires human annotation (ICD-9 codes, etc.). The paper states CHOI "relies on human annotation via Wikipedia links," but doesn't quantify the annotation effort required. This comparison is misleading because CHOI's human annotation cost is significantly higher than the authors' weakly-supervised approach.

5. **Confusing compositionality analysis**: The analysis in Section 5.4 (Figures 3-4) is poorly explained and doesn't clearly support the paper's claims. The authors show concepts have high similarity to their mean phrasal embeddings but lower similarity to the average of words in their phrases, but don't provide a clear interpretation of why this pattern is expected or how it relates to their method's strengths. The MAP analysis (Figure 4) shows mixed results without clear implications.

## Questions to Authors

1. How did you handle cases where ontology mappings are incorrect or incomplete (e.g., a phrase that should map to concept A but is incorrectly mapped to concept B in the ontology)? Did you analyze the impact of such errors on the final embeddings?

2. The paper claims that the proposed method requires "no human annotations," but the ontology mappings themselves likely required some human curation (e.g., UMLS is built on human-annotated data). Can you clarify the exact annotation effort required compared to prior methods like CHOI or DEVINE?

3. Given the critical issue of hyperparameter tuning on the evaluation data (Section 5.2), did you attempt to re-evaluate your best-performing model using proper train/validation/test splits? If not, how confident can you be that your results aren't overfit to the evaluation datasets?

## Overall Assessment

This paper makes a valuable contribution with its weakly-supervised method for concept embedding and the introduction of a new entity similarity/relatedness dataset. However, the critical evaluation methodology flaw (hyperparameter tuning on test data) significantly undermines the validity of the results. The comparison with Wang et al. (2014a) is also insufficiently developed. While the method shows promise, these issues need to be addressed before the paper can be accepted. With revisions addressing these concerns, this paper could be a strong contribution to the field.
