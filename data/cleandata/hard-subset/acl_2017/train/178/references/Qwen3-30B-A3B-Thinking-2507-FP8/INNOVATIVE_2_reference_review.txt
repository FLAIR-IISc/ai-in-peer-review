

# ACL Review

## Summary
This paper introduces a weakly-supervised method for jointly embedding concepts, phrases, and words into a shared vector space using distant supervision from ontology mappings. The approach replaces phrases with unique identifiers in the training corpus and adapts skip-gram training to jointly learn embeddings for concepts, phrases, and words. The method requires no human annotations for concepts (only using structured knowledge from UMLS and YAGO ontologies), and the authors evaluate it on biomedical concepts and introduce a new dataset of similarity/relatedness for real-world entities.

## Main Contributions (in order of strength)

1. **Novel weakly-supervised approach for concept embeddings without human annotation**: The paper's core contribution is a method that learns concept embeddings using only ontology mappings as distant supervision, eliminating the need for human annotations for concepts. This is a significant conceptual advance over prior work that required manual concept annotation.

2. **Novel dataset of similarity and relatedness for real-world entities**: The authors create the first comparable dataset for evaluating embeddings of real-world entities (people, places, organizations), filling an important gap in the literature.

3. **Demonstration of competitive performance without human annotation**: The method achieves competitive results with prior methods that require human annotations for concepts, particularly on larger biomedical datasets.

## Strengths

1. **Strong novelty of the approach**: This is the paper's strongest point. The use of structured knowledge from ontologies as distant supervision to learn concept embeddings without human annotations is genuinely novel. Previous work either relied on word composition (which fails for non-compositional phrases like "Lou Gehrig's disease") or required human annotation for concepts. The paper clearly articulates this gap in the introduction and provides a compelling solution.

2. **Practical significance**: The method scales to larger vocabularies without additional annotation effort, which is a major practical advantage over prior approaches. This makes the method more applicable to real-world scenarios where human annotation is expensive.

3. **Valuable new dataset**: The creation of a novel dataset for real-world entity similarity/relatedness is a significant contribution to the field. The paper properly justifies the need for such a dataset and provides thorough details about the data collection process.

4. **Demonstration of semantic structure preservation**: The paper provides evidence that the embeddings preserve ontological structure (concepts cluster by semantic type, concepts are close to their representative phrases), which is an important property for semantic embeddings.

## Weaknesses

1. **Lack of clear evidence for the "joint embedding" advantage**: The paper claims to jointly embed concepts, phrases, and words, but the evaluation shows that APPROXPHRco (approximating concepts as average of phrase embeddings) performs nearly identically to CONCEPTco (direct concept embeddings). Figure 3a shows high similarity between concepts and their mean phrasal embeddings, suggesting the joint embedding might not be adding significant value. The paper doesn't convincingly demonstrate why the joint embedding approach is better than the simpler phrase averaging approach.

2. **Limited differentiation from Wang et al. (2014a)**: The paper states that Wang et al. (2014a) is the closest approach but doesn't clearly articulate how their method differs in a meaningful way. Wang et al. also jointly embeds entities and words but relies on binary relations in knowledge bases (which are incomplete) and requires human annotation via Wikipedia links. The paper should more clearly explain the conceptual and practical differences that make their approach superior.

3. **Counterintuitive results on real-world entities**: The paper's evaluation on real-world entities shows that phrase-based concept approximation outperforms direct concept embeddings (Table 5). This contradicts the main premise of the paper, which is that direct concept embeddings are valuable. The authors don't adequately explain this unexpected finding, which weakens their main claim.

## Questions to Authors

1. The paper claims to jointly embed concepts, phrases, and words, but the results (Table 4) show that APPROXPHRco (approximating concepts as average of phrase embeddings) performs nearly identically to CONCEPTco (direct concept embeddings). How does this align with the claim that "joint embedding" is a key contribution? Can you provide more evidence that the joint embedding approach provides a meaningful advantage over simpler phrase averaging?

2. The paper states that Wang et al. (2014a) is the closest approach, but their method relies on binary relations in the knowledge base (which are incomplete) and requires human annotation via Wikipedia links. How does your method fundamentally differ from Wang et al. (2014a) in a way that makes your approach more novel and effective?

3. The results on real-world entity datasets (Table 5) show that phrase-based concept approximation outperforms direct concept embeddings. This seems to contradict the main premise of the paper. Can you explain this unexpected finding and how it impacts the value of your approach?

4. Figure 3a shows high similarity between concepts and their mean phrasal embeddings, suggesting that the concept embedding is largely redundant with the average of its phrase embeddings. However, Figure 4a shows that MAP scores for concepts and phrases are skewed toward 0, suggesting concepts aren't particularly near their individual representative phrases. How do you reconcile these two findings?

## Overall Recommendation

I recommend acceptance of this paper. The novelty of the weakly-supervised approach for concept embeddings without human annotation is significant and addresses a clear gap in the literature. The creation of a new dataset for real-world entities is also a valuable contribution. While there are some weaknesses in the evaluation and explanation of the joint embedding advantage, these are outweighed by the novelty and practical significance of the approach. The paper introduces a genuinely new direction for concept embeddings that doesn't require human annotation, which is a substantial contribution to the field.

I would encourage the authors to address the questions above in their response, particularly regarding the evidence for the joint embedding advantage and the counterintuitive results on real-world entities, as these are important for strengthening the paper's main claims.
