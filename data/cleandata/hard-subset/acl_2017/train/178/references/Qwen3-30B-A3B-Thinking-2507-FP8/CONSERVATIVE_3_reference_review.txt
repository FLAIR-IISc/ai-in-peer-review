

# Review of "A Weakly-Supervised Method for Jointly Embedding Concepts, Phrases, and Words"

## Summary
The paper proposes a weakly-supervised approach to jointly embed concepts, phrases, and words into a shared vector space using distant supervision from ontologies (UMLS and YAGO). The method replaces phrases from the ontology with unique identifiers in the corpus, then trains embeddings using a modified skip-gram model. The authors claim their embeddings preserve semantic relationships between concepts and phrases, and perform competitively with prior methods that require human annotations.

## Main Contributions (as I see them)
1. Novel dataset of similarity and relatedness for real-world entities (people, places, organizations) - a significant contribution addressing a gap in the literature
2. Method for weakly-supervised joint embedding of concepts, phrases, and words using ontology mappings
3. Evidence that embeddings preserve some semantic relationships between concepts and phrases

## Strengths
1. **Novel dataset for real-world entities**: The authors created a well-constructed dataset of 688 similarity/relatedness pairs for real-world entities (people, places, organizations), which is a valuable contribution to the field. The annotation process was carefully designed with validation questions and quality control measures.

2. **Demonstrated scalability**: The method requires only distant supervision from ontologies and can scale to large vocabularies without additional annotation costs, which is a practical advantage.

3. **Competitive performance on larger biomedical datasets**: On the larger UMNSRS datasets, their method achieves the highest correlation with human judgments on the similarity task, suggesting meaningful semantic capture.

## Weaknesses

1. **Lack of clear advantage over simpler baselines**: The paper's most significant claim is that their joint embedding method outperforms prior approaches requiring human annotation, but the evidence is weak. The APPROXPHRco baseline (using phrase embeddings directly) consistently performs as well as or better than the joint embedding approach (CONCEPTco), as shown in Tables 4 and 5. For example, on the UMNSRS Similarity task, APPROXPHRco (0.637) outperforms CONCEPTco (0.653) only marginally, while on the general-domain Similarity task, APPROXPHRco (0.766) significantly outperforms CONCEPTco (0.750). The paper fails to convincingly justify the added complexity of their joint embedding method when a simpler approach is equally effective.

2. **Insufficient validation against established baselines**: The paper compares against a variety of baselines but doesn't adequately address the most relevant comparison: the CHOI method, which uses manual annotations and outperforms their method on smaller biomedical datasets (Mayo SRS and Mini Mayo SRS) with large margins (0.817 vs 0.593 for Mayo SRS). The paper doesn't explain why a weakly-supervised approach would be preferable when a supervised approach with human annotation achieves better results on the smaller datasets where annotation is feasible.

3. **Inconsistent evidence about preserving semantic relationships**: The authors claim their method "preserves the links between concepts of the same type and between concepts and their representative phrases," but the MAP analysis (Figure 4) contradicts this. The MAP scores for concepts and phrases are skewed toward 0 with peaks at 0.5 and 1, suggesting most concepts are not especially near their representative phrases in the vector space. The compositionality analysis (Figure 3) also shows that phrases have high similarity to their constituent words (0.5 average cosine similarity), which contradicts the authors' claim that their method can handle non-compositional phrases like "Lou Gehrig's disease."

4. **Lack of extrinsic evaluation**: The paper acknowledges known issues with similarity/relatedness tasks for evaluating embeddings (citing Chiu et al., 2016b; Faruqui et al., 2016), but doesn't provide any extrinsic evaluations (e.g., entity linking, classification tasks) that would provide stronger evidence of the embeddings' utility. The authors note that "applications to other intrinsic tasks such as analogy completion or downstream NLP tasks would provide additional strong evidence," but they don't provide any such evidence.

5. **Methodological concerns with distant supervision**: The paper doesn't adequately address the limitations of distant supervision. The approach relies on the assumption that "any occurrence of a known phrase signifies an occurrence of each concept it can refer to," which may not hold true in practice. The paper doesn't analyze error cases where this assumption fails, nor does it provide evidence that the method handles cases where phrases refer to multiple concepts.

## Questions to Authors

1. The APPROXPHRco baseline (using phrase embeddings directly) consistently outperforms the joint embedding approach (CONCEPTco) on the real-world entity datasets (Table 5). Why should researchers use your more complex joint embedding method when a simpler approach is clearly better?

2. On the smaller biomedical datasets (Mayo SRS and Mini Mayo SRS), the CHOI method (which uses manual annotations) significantly outperforms your approach. How can you claim your weakly-supervised method is competitive with methods requiring human annotation when it's demonstrably worse on the datasets where human annotation would be feasible?

3. Figure 4 shows that most concepts are not especially near their representative phrases in the vector space (MAP scores skewed toward 0). How do you reconcile this with your claim that "the source ontology structure is reflected in the organization of our embeddings: concepts and their representative phrases are embedded close to one another"?

4. The paper acknowledges that "the lack of train/test splits on the similarity and relatedness tasks" is a problem, yet the authors still tune hyperparameters on these evaluation datasets. How do you justify this methodological flaw, especially when they acknowledge it's a known issue?

5. The authors state "our embeddings are competitive with prior models that required human annotations for concepts," but the CHOI method (which uses human annotations) outperforms their method on smaller datasets. How do you reconcile this claim with the evidence presented in Table 4?

## Overall Recommendation

I recommend rejection. While the novel dataset is valuable, the methodological claims are not sufficiently supported by the evidence provided. The paper fails to demonstrate a clear advantage of their joint embedding approach over simpler baselines, and the evaluation methodology has significant limitations. The paper makes an unproven claim about the superiority of their weakly-supervised approach without providing adequate evidence to support it. The field would benefit more from a paper that thoroughly validates a simpler, more established approach with a strong dataset rather than a new method with questionable advantages.
