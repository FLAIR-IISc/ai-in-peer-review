- Summary: Proposes a weakly-supervised method that replaces ontology-mapped phrases with IDs and trains a modified skip-gram to jointly embed concepts, phrases, and words; evaluated on biomedical tasks and a new real-world entity similarity dataset.

- Contribution: Novel weakly-supervised approach using ontology mappings as distant supervision for concept embeddings.

- Contribution: Introduces a new dataset of similarity/relatedness judgments for real-world entities.

- Contribution: Shows concept embeddings can be learned without direct human annotation while preserving semantic relationships.

- Strength: Novel and well-motivated use of ontology mappings for distant supervision, avoiding costly human concept annotation.

- Strength: Competitive results on biomedical similarity/relatedness tasks, outperforming some word embeddings and approaching human-annotated baselines on larger sets.

- Strength: Provides insightful analysis of embedding structure and compositionality between concepts, phrases, and words.

- Weakness: Performance gains over prior work are modest and not consistently superior on smaller datasets.

- Weakness: Serious methodological flaw: hyperparameter tuning was performed on evaluation sets, which can invalidate reported results.

- Weakness: Contradictory findings: phrase-based approximation outperforms direct concept embeddings on the real-world dataset, undermining main claims.

- Weakness: Overstated novelty due to similarities with prior joint entity-word embedding work (Wang et al., 2014a), and ontology mappings are themselves human-curated.

- Question: How does this method concretely differ from Wang et al. (2014a), and what justifies claimed advantages?

- Question: If ontology mappings are human-curated, how is this meaningfully different from methods that require human annotations?

- Question: Why does APPROXPHRco outperform CONCEPTco on the real-world dataset despite claims that direct concept embeddings are superior?

- Question: How will you address the hyperparameter-tuning-on-test-sets flaw, and would proper train/test splits change the results?

- Overall assessment: Interesting and valuable idea with a useful new dataset, but claims are weakened by methodological issues, marginal gains, and contradictory results; needs corrected evaluation and clearer comparison to prior work.
