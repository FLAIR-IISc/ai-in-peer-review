

# ACL Review: "A Weakly-Supervised Method for Jointly Embedding Concepts, Phrases, and Words"

## Summary of the Paper
The paper proposes a weakly-supervised method for jointly embedding concepts, phrases, and words into a shared vector space. The approach leverages structured knowledge from ontologies (UMLS and YAGO) to provide distant supervision for training embeddings without requiring human annotations. The method replaces phrases in a corpus with unique identifiers based on their concept mappings, then trains embeddings using a modified skip-gram model with additional parameters to control compositionality. The authors evaluate their method on biomedical concept similarity/relatedness tasks and introduce a new dataset for real-world entity similarity/relatedness. They claim competitive performance compared to prior methods that require human annotation.

## Main Contributions (as I see them)
1. A novel weakly-supervised training method for jointly embedding concepts, phrases, and words using distant supervision from ontologies
2. A new dataset of similarity/relatedness judgments for real-world entities
3. Analysis showing that the embedding space preserves some aspects of the ontology structure (concept-phrase relationships and semantic type clustering)

## Strengths

1. **Novel Weakly-Supervised Approach**: The paper presents a genuinely weakly-supervised method that doesn't require human annotations for concepts, which is a significant contribution. Most prior work on concept embeddings relies on human annotations (e.g., using CUIs in medical records), while this approach leverages existing ontologies (UMLS, YAGO) to provide distant supervision. This is particularly valuable in domains where human annotation is expensive or unavailable.

2. **Valuable New Dataset**: The introduction of a new dataset for real-world entity similarity and relatedness is a significant contribution. As the authors note, there are few comparable datasets for entities, while most existing datasets focus on words. The careful data collection process using Mechanical Turk with validation questions and analysis of inter-annotator agreement (Fleiss' κ = 0.24) adds credibility to this contribution.

3. **Competitive Performance on Larger Datasets**: On the larger biomedical datasets (UMNSRS Similarity and Relatedness with 566 and 587 concept pairs), the proposed method achieves the highest correlation with human judgments on the similarity task (0.653), outperforming prior methods that require human annotations. This demonstrates the practical value of the approach for real-world applications where large-scale semantic modeling is needed.

## Weaknesses

1. **Lack of Clear Novelty Distinction**: The paper doesn't sufficiently differentiate its approach from Wang et al. (2014a), which is the closest related work. Wang et al. also propose methods for jointly embedding KB entities and words, though they rely on binary relations between entities (which the authors rightly note are incomplete). The paper should more clearly articulate how their distant supervision approach (using phrase-to-concept mappings) improves upon Wang et al.'s method, particularly in terms of requiring less structured knowledge.

2. **Evaluation Methodology Concerns**: The evaluation methodology has significant issues that undermine the claimed results. The paper uses the same data for hyperparameter tuning and evaluation, which is problematic as noted by Chiu et al. (2016b) and Faruqui et al. (2016). The authors acknowledge this limitation but don't adequately address it. The use of 5-fold cross-validation for tuning is insufficient to prevent data leakage, and the evaluation results may be inflated due to this issue.

3. **Inconsistent Performance and Lack of Downstream Validation**: The method performs well on larger biomedical datasets but is outperformed by CHOI on the smaller MayoSRS and Mini Mayo SRS datasets. CHOI uses additional human annotations (ICD9 codes), making the comparison less fair. More concerning is the lack of evaluation on downstream tasks beyond similarity/relatedness (e.g., entity linking, classification), which would provide stronger evidence of the embeddings' utility.

4. **Inadequate Explanation of Compositionality Parameters**: The paper introduces β and η hyperparameters to control compositionality, but the analysis shows these parameters have a negative impact on performance when increased. This is counterintuitive and suggests the method isn't effectively capturing compositionality. The paper doesn't provide a good explanation for this behavior, which weakens the proposed model's theoretical foundation.

5. **Limited Generalizability Demonstration**: The paper primarily evaluates on biomedical data (UMLS) and real-world entities (YAGO), but doesn't demonstrate the method's effectiveness on general-domain concepts. The authors note this as a limitation but don't provide any evidence that their method works well in a broader context, which would strengthen the claim of general applicability.

## Questions to Authors

1. The paper claims that the method "requires no human annotations," but relies on ontologies that contain human-curated mappings. How does this differ from previous work that also used ontologies (e.g., Wang et al., 2014a)? What specific aspect of the ontology does the method require that makes it "weaker" supervision than prior approaches?

2. The authors acknowledge the evaluation limitations regarding similarity/relatedness tasks (as noted by Chiu et al., 2016b), but don't provide evidence of performance on downstream tasks. What specific downstream NLP tasks would you recommend evaluating on to demonstrate the practical utility of these embeddings?

3. The analysis shows that increasing β and η (to encourage compositionality) consistently decreases performance. Could you provide a more detailed explanation for this counterintuitive result, and discuss whether the model might be capturing some non-compositional aspects of concepts that are important for the tasks?

4. The paper demonstrates results on both biomedical (UMLS) and general-domain (YAGO) data, but doesn't show how the method might generalize to other domains. Could you provide a brief analysis of how the method might perform on a domain with different characteristics (e.g., legal or financial) without requiring additional human annotation?

## Additional Comments

The paper's supplemental material provides good detail on hyperparameter tuning and dataset construction, which is helpful for reproducibility. The implementation (cui2vec) as part of the submission is a positive contribution. The discussion of semantic type clustering (Table 6) is interesting, though the poor performance on "sign or symptom" suggests limitations in capturing certain semantic categories.

The paper has a strong methodological contribution with the weakly-supervised approach and valuable new dataset, but the evaluation methodology concerns and lack of clear novelty distinction from prior work are significant weaknesses that need to be addressed for consideration at ACL.

## Overall Recommendation

I recommend rejection of this paper in its current form. The main contributions are valuable but not sufficiently novel or compelling, and the evaluation methodology has serious issues that undermine the claimed results. With substantial revisions addressing the evaluation concerns, better differentiation from prior work, and additional evidence of downstream utility, this could be a strong submission. The authors should focus on demonstrating how their method provides a clear advantage over prior work in terms of both performance and practical utility, while addressing the evaluation limitations.
