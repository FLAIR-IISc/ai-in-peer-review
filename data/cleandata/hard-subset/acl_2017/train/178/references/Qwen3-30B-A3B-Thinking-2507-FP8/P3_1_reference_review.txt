

# Peer Review

## Summary
This paper proposes a weakly-supervised method for jointly embedding concepts, phrases, and words into a shared vector space using ontology mappings as distant supervision. The approach replaces phrases in a corpus with unique identifiers representing their corresponding concepts, then trains embeddings using a modified skip-gram model. The authors demonstrate competitive performance with human-annotated methods on biomedical concept similarity tasks and introduce a novel dataset for real-world entity similarity/relatedness.

## Main Contributions
1. A weakly-supervised approach to joint embedding that avoids human annotation by leveraging ontology mappings as distant supervision
2. A novel dataset of human judgments for similarity and relatedness between real-world entities (people, places, organizations)
3. Demonstrating that concept embeddings can preserve semantic relationships comparable to methods requiring human annotation

## Strengths

1. **Significant empirical validation**: The method achieves competitive results with human-annotated baselines on the larger biomedical datasets (UMNSRS Similarity and Relatedness). On these datasets, their concept embeddings have the highest correlation with human judgments (0.653 vs. 0.653 for CHIU-30), which is a meaningful achievement for a weakly-supervised approach. The authors thoroughly evaluate their method across multiple dimensions: concept similarity, phrase-word compositionality, and semantic type clustering.

2. **Novel dataset contribution**: The introduction of a new dataset for real-world entity similarity and relatedness is highly valuable, as such datasets are scarce in the literature. The careful data collection process with validation questions, inter-annotator agreement metrics (Fleiss' κ = 0.24), and attention to participant quality standards provides credible evidence for the dataset's utility.

3. **Comprehensive evaluation framework**: The paper evaluates embeddings through multiple lenses: ranking performance on similarity/relatedness tasks, compositionality analysis (Figures 3-4), and semantic type clustering (Table 6). This multi-faceted evaluation provides a more complete picture of embedding quality than standard similarity/relatedness metrics alone.

## Weaknesses

1. **Questionable novelty compared to Wang et al. (2014a)**: The paper claims Wang et al. (2014a) "relies on the binary relations between entities in the knowledge base, which have been shown to be highly incomplete" and "best results also rely on human annotation via Wikipedia links." However, Wang et al. (2014a) also used Wikipedia links for entity linking, which is essentially the same distant supervision approach used here. The paper fails to clearly establish how their method differs in a meaningful way that addresses the limitations of prior work.

2. **Misleading "joint embedding" claim**: The paper claims to "jointly embed" concepts, phrases, and words, but the training procedure updates each embedding type separately using their respective contexts. This is more like a shared embedding space than true joint embedding, where the embeddings would benefit from each other in an integrated manner. The paper doesn't provide evidence that the embeddings benefit from each other in a way that justifies the "joint" claim.

3. **Insufficient analysis of hyperparameters**: The authors introduce β and η to control compositionality but provide minimal analysis of how these parameters affect performance. The paper states that "performance trends negative as β and η increase" but doesn't explain why or provide a clear rationale for their settings. This is particularly problematic since the optimal settings vary across corpora (Table 3), suggesting the method's performance is highly sensitive to parameter choices without clear guidance.

4. **Lack of conceptual analysis**: While noting that "some non-lexical information about concepts is not being captured," the paper provides no specific examples or analysis of which types of concepts are better or worse represented. This is especially important given the poor clustering performance for "sign or symptom" (Table 6), which the authors attribute to "difficult semantics" without further exploration.

## Questions to Authors
1. How does your method specifically address the limitations of Wang et al. (2014a) beyond using the same distant supervision approach? What are the key technical differences?

2. Can you provide specific examples of concepts where your method fails to capture semantic relationships, and explain why these particular concepts are challenging for your approach?

3. Could you clarify how the "joint embedding" in your method differs from simply having a shared embedding space for concepts, phrases, and words, and provide evidence that the embeddings benefit from each other in a meaningful way?
