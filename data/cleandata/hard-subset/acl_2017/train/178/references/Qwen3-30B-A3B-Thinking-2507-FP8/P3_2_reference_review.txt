

# Peer Review: "A Weakly-Supervised Method for Jointly Embedding Concepts, Phrases, and Words"

## Summary
This paper proposes a weakly-supervised method for jointly embedding concepts, phrases, and words using structured knowledge from ontologies (UMLS and YAGO) as distant supervision. The approach replaces ontology-mapped phrases with unique identifiers in a corpus, then trains embeddings using a modified skip-gram model with two hyperparameters (β and η) controlling compositionality. The authors evaluate on biomedical concept similarity/relatedness tasks and introduce a novel dataset for real-world entity similarity/relatedness.

## Main Contributions (as I see them)
1. A method for weakly-supervised joint embedding of concepts, phrases, and words that eliminates the need for human annotations of concepts
2. A novel dataset of human judgments for similarity and relatedness of real-world entities
3. Demonstration that weakly-supervised concept embeddings can achieve competitive performance with supervised methods

## Strengths
1. **Novel dataset creation**: The authors create a much-needed dataset for real-world entity similarity/relatedness (688 pairs each for similarity and relatedness), with careful data collection methodology including validation questions and inter-annotator agreement assessment. This fills a significant gap in the literature, as existing datasets focus on words, not entities.

2. **Demonstrated feasibility of weak supervision**: The paper shows that a weakly-supervised approach using only ontology mappings (without human annotation of concepts) can achieve competitive results with supervised methods. On the larger biomedical datasets, their approach achieves the highest correlation with human judgments on the similarity task, which is impressive given the lack of human supervision.

3. **Clear articulation of the many-to-many relationship**: The paper effectively demonstrates the challenge of representing concepts with multiple textual forms (e.g., "Lou Gehrig's disease" and "amyotrophic lateral sclerosis") and provides a clear framework for addressing this through ontology mappings, which is a well-articulated contribution to the field.

## Weaknesses
1. **Limited novelty**: The core idea of using ontology mappings for distant supervision is similar to Mintz et al. (2009) and Wang et al. (2014a), which the authors acknowledge. The main novelty appears to be the joint embedding framework with β and η parameters, but these are relatively minor modifications to standard word2vec. The paper doesn't clearly demonstrate how their approach fundamentally differs from or improves upon prior work.

2. **Questionable evaluation methodology**: The authors admit they tune hyperparameters on the test set (using 5-fold cross-validation), which violates standard evaluation practices. They also acknowledge Chiu et al. (2016b) that intrinsic evaluations like similarity tasks often don't correlate with downstream performance, but don't provide evidence of better performance on extrinsic tasks. This significantly weakens their claims of effectiveness.

3. **Lack of theoretical justification for hyperparameters**: The β and η parameters are introduced without strong theoretical basis. The paper shows small values of η (concept-phrase compositionality) are optimal, but doesn't explain why. The hyperparameter tuning is ad-hoc, and the paper doesn't provide insight into how these parameters should be set in general or why they're necessary.

4. **Confusing terminology**: The paper uses "concept" to mean both a canonical abstract concept (e.g., a medical condition) and a specific textual form (e.g., "Big Apple" for New York City), which creates significant confusion. This is inconsistent with standard usage in the biomedical domain, where "concept" refers to the medical condition, not the phrase.

5. **Limited scope of evaluation**: The paper primarily evaluates on biomedical concepts and a new dataset for real-world entities, but doesn't evaluate on general domain concepts or tasks. The results on the general-domain entity dataset show that phrase-based approximations outperform the direct concept embeddings, suggesting the method may not be as effective for general concepts as claimed.

## Questions to Authors
1. How does your method specifically differ from Wang et al. (2014a), which also jointly embeds entities and words using knowledge base structure? The authors cite Wang et al. as "closest approach" but don't clearly articulate the differences.

2. The paper shows that concept embeddings are less effective than phrase embeddings for the real-world entity dataset. What is the theoretical explanation for this, and how does it align with your claim that "concepts and their representative phrases are embedded close to one another"?

3. Given the concerns about intrinsic evaluation not correlating with downstream performance (Chiu et al., 2016b), what downstream NLP tasks would you expect your embeddings to perform well on, and have you tested this?

## Overall Assessment
This paper makes a valuable contribution through the creation of a novel dataset for real-world entity similarity/relatedness, which fills an important gap in the literature. The weakly-supervised approach demonstrates feasibility for concept embedding without human annotation, which is promising. However, the limited novelty of the core method, questionable evaluation methodology, and confusing terminology prevent this from being a strong contribution to the field. The paper would be significantly strengthened by addressing these weaknesses, particularly by providing evidence of better performance on downstream tasks and clarifying the theoretical justification for the key innovations.
