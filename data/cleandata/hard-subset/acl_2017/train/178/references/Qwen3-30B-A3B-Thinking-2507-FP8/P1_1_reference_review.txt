

# Peer Review

## Summary and Main Contributions

This paper proposes a weakly-supervised method to jointly embed concepts, phrases, and words into a shared vector space using distant supervision from ontology mappings. The key idea is to treat phrases linked to the same concept as evidence that those phrases represent the same underlying concept, training concept embeddings based on the contexts of these phrases. The authors demonstrate their approach on biomedical concepts (using UMLS) and real-world entities (using YAGO), and introduce a novel dataset of similarity and relatedness judgments for real-world entities.

In my assessment, the main contributions are:
1. A novel weakly-supervised method for concept embedding that avoids the need for human annotation of concepts
2. A new dataset of similarity and relatedness judgments for real-world entities (a significant contribution given the scarcity of such datasets)
3. Empirical evidence that concept embeddings can be learned effectively without direct human annotation of concepts

## Strengths

1. **Significant methodological contribution to concept representation**: The paper addresses a meaningful problem in NLP: concepts often have multiple textual forms that aren't easily compositional (e.g., "the Big Apple" vs. "New York City"). The weakly-supervised approach using ontology mappings as distant supervision is novel and practical, as it avoids the need for expensive human annotation of concepts. The authors demonstrate this works for both biomedical concepts (UMLS) and real-world entities (YAGO), showing broad applicability.

2. **Valuable new dataset**: The authors introduce a novel dataset of similarity and relatedness judgments for real-world entities (people, places, organizations), which is a significant contribution to the field. Prior work has focused on words or biomedical concepts, but this dataset fills a gap in evaluating embeddings for general real-world entities. The careful data collection process and analysis of inter-annotator agreement (Fleiss' κ = 0.24) demonstrate the authors' attention to quality.

3. **Strong empirical results**: The approach achieves competitive results with methods that require human annotation (e.g., CHOI, DEVINE, MENCIA) on biomedical similarity and relatedness tasks. The results on the larger datasets (UMNSRS) show their concept embeddings outperforming prior methods in similarity tasks. The analysis of embedding compositionality (Figures 3 and 4) provides valuable insights into how the model organizes concepts, phrases, and words.

## Weaknesses

1. **Unclear advantage over simpler alternatives**: The paper claims to learn concept embeddings, but the results in Table 5 show that PHRASEco (using the learned embedding of a phrase directly) consistently outperforms CONCEPTco (the jointly trained concept embeddings) on the real-world entity dataset. This undermines the main contribution, as it suggests that simply using the phrase embeddings (which requires no additional training) is superior to the proposed concept embeddings. The paper fails to explain why this is the case or how their concept embeddings provide additional value.

2. **Problematic comparison with prior work**: The comparison with prior work is problematic because the baselines use different data sources and evaluation settings. For example, CHOI uses ICD9 codes (which are different from UMLS CUIs), and MENCIA uses MeSH headings. The authors claim to use "UMLS CUIs" as their concept identifiers, but the baselines don't use the same concept set, making direct comparison difficult. The paper should have used consistent concept sets for all methods to make a fair comparison.

3. **Evaluation methodology concerns**: The authors tune hyperparameters using 5-fold cross-validation on the evaluation datasets (UMNSRS, MayoSRS), which risks overfitting to the evaluation data. While they mention "adapted 5-fold cross-validation," they don't explain how this differs from standard practice or how they prevent data leakage. This is particularly concerning given the small size of the biomedical evaluation datasets (e.g., MayoSRS has only 101 pairs).

4. **Lack of analysis on limitations**: The paper doesn't adequately address the limitations of their approach. For example, they note that their method works for both abstract biomedical concepts and concrete real-world entities, but they don't discuss how well it handles concepts with high polysemy or concepts that don't have clear textual forms in the ontology. The analysis of semantic type clustering (Table 6) shows poor performance for "sign or symptom," but they don't explore why this is the case.

## Questions to Authors

1. The results in Table 5 show that PHRASEco (using phrase embeddings directly) outperforms CONCEPTco (jointly trained concept embeddings) on the real-world entity dataset. Can you explain why this is the case, and what specific advantages concept embeddings provide over phrase embeddings in your model?

2. The paper states that "we assume that any occurrence of a known phrase signifies an occurrence of each concept it can refer to." However, in the case of UMLS, a single phrase can refer to multiple concepts (e.g., "diabetes" can refer to both "diabetes mellitus" and "diabetes insipidus"). How does the model handle this ambiguity, and how does it affect the quality of the concept embeddings?

3. The authors note that "the extreme similarity observed in our compositionality analysis" suggests "some non-lexical information about concepts is not being captured." What specific information might be missing, and how could the model be improved to capture it?

## Overall Recommendation

This paper presents a novel approach to concept embedding that could be valuable for the community, particularly given the new dataset. However, the lack of clear advantages of the concept embeddings over simpler phrase-based approaches and the problematic comparison with prior work significantly weaken the contribution. The authors should address these concerns before publication. I recommend rejection, but would reconsider with major revisions addressing the weaknesses outlined above.
