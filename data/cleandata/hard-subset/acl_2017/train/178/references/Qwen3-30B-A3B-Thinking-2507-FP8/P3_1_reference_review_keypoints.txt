- Summary: Proposes a weakly-supervised method that replaces phrases with concept IDs and trains a modified skip-gram to jointly embed concepts, phrases, and words, showing competitive biomedical similarity results and introducing a real-world entity similarity dataset.

- Contribution 1: Weakly-supervised joint embedding approach using ontology mappings as distant supervision to avoid human annotation.

- Contribution 2: New human-judged dataset for similarity and relatedness of real-world entities (people, places, organizations).

- Contribution 3: Empirical demonstration that concept embeddings can preserve semantic relationships comparable to human-annotated methods.

- Strength 1: Strong empirical validation—competitive or top correlations on biomedical datasets with multi-faceted evaluations (similarity, compositionality, clustering).

- Strength 2: Valuable and carefully collected novel dataset with validation checks and inter-annotator agreement reporting.

- Strength 3: Comprehensive evaluation framework including ranking, compositionality analysis, and semantic type clustering.

- Weakness 1: Novelty is unclear relative to Wang et al. (2014a), which used similar distant supervision via Wikipedia links.

- Weakness 2: "Joint embedding" claim is misleading since embeddings are updated separately and evidence of mutual benefit is lacking.

- Weakness 3: Insufficient analysis of hyperparameters β and η and their corpus-dependent sensitivities.

- Weakness 4: Lacks detailed conceptual analysis or examples explaining poor representation for certain semantic types (e.g., "sign or symptom").

- Question 1: How does this method specifically differ from and address limitations of Wang et al. (2014a)?

- Question 2: Can you provide concrete examples of concepts the method fails on and explain why?

- Question 3: Clarify how "joint embedding" differs from a shared embedding space and provide evidence of embeddings benefiting each other.
