

# ACL Review: A Weakly-Supervised Method for Jointly Embedding Concepts, Phrases, and Words

## Summary of the Paper
The paper proposes a weakly-supervised method for jointly embedding concepts, phrases, and words into a shared vector space. The key insight is to use existing ontology mappings (from UMLS or YAGO) to treat phrases as "words" in the training corpus, allowing the model to learn embeddings for concepts without human annotations. The approach modifies skip-gram with negative sampling to incorporate the ontology structure, with hyperparameters controlling the tradeoff between phrase-word compositionality and concept-phrase independence. The authors evaluate on biomedical concept similarity/relatedness tasks and introduce a new dataset of similarity/relatedness for real-world entities (people, places, organizations).

## Main Contributions (as I see them)
1. A novel weakly-supervised method for jointly embedding concepts, phrases, and words using only ontology mappings as supervision (no human annotations required).
2. A new dataset for evaluating similarity and relatedness of real-world entities (people, places, organizations), addressing a gap in the literature.
3. Experimental validation showing competitive performance with prior methods that require human annotations, particularly on larger datasets.

## Strengths

1. **Genuine weak supervision approach**: The paper successfully demonstrates a method that requires no human annotations beyond existing ontology mappings. This is a significant advantage over prior work like CHOI (which requires manual annotation of medical codes) or MENCIA (which requires manual tagging of PubMed abstracts). The authors effectively show that their approach can scale to larger vocabularies without additional annotation costs, which is particularly valuable for resource-constrained domains like biomedical NLP.

2. **Novel evaluation dataset**: The introduction of a new dataset for evaluating similarity and relatedness of real-world entities fills an important gap in the literature. The authors clearly acknowledge the lack of such datasets for entities (as opposed to words) and provide a detailed methodology for collecting and validating the data, including the use of validation questions to filter annotators. This dataset will be valuable for future work in entity representation learning.

3. **Comprehensive experimental evaluation**: The paper provides a thorough evaluation across multiple corpora (biomedical and general domain), multiple datasets (biomedical concept similarity/relatedness and the new real-world entity dataset), and multiple baselines. The authors also include insightful analyses of how well the model preserves ontological relationships (Figures 3-4), providing evidence beyond just task performance that their embeddings capture meaningful semantic structure.

## Weaknesses

1. **Questionable novelty compared to prior work**: The paper's approach is very similar to Wang et al. (2014a), which also jointly embeds KB entities and words using a modified skip-gram approach. The authors claim their method avoids the "highly incomplete" binary relations used by Wang et al., but they don't sufficiently demonstrate why their approach is superior or how it specifically overcomes this limitation. The paper would benefit from a more thorough comparison and differentiation from Wang et al.'s work.

2. **Evaluation methodology concerns**: The authors use Spearman's correlation on similarity/relatedness datasets, which as noted by Chiu et al. (2016b) and Faruqui et al. (2016), has well-known limitations for evaluating distributional semantic models. More critically, they tune hyperparameters using the test set (via 5-fold cross-validation), which is problematic for proper evaluation. The paper mentions this issue in the discussion but doesn't adequately address it in their experimental methodology. This undermines the reliability of their performance claims.

3. **Inconsistent performance claims**: The paper claims their method is "competitive with prior models that required human annotations," but Table 4 shows CHOI (which requires human annotation) outperforms their method on the smaller Mayo SRS and Mini Mayo SRS datasets. The authors don't adequately explain this discrepancy or provide a nuanced analysis of when their method is competitive versus when it's not, which weakens their main contribution claim.

4. **Contradictory results in embedding quality analysis**: The paper's Figure 3 and Figure 4 analyses suggest that the model isn't consistently preserving the ontological relationships it claims to capture. For example, Figure 3b shows high variance when comparing phrases to the average of their words, and Figure 4a shows a bi-modal distribution indicating that most phrases are either very close or very distant from their component words. These findings contradict the paper's claim that their embeddings "preserve the links between concepts of the same type and between concepts and their representative phrases."

## Questions to Authors

1. The paper claims their method is competitive with prior methods that require human annotations, but CHOI (which requires human annotation) outperforms their method on the Mayo SRS and Mini Mayo SRS datasets. Can the authors provide a more nuanced comparison of when their method is competitive versus when it's not, and why this pattern emerges?

2. The authors mention that they use "distant supervision" but don't fully address the limitations of this approach. How does their method handle cases where the ontology mappings are incorrect or incomplete? How does this affect the quality of the embeddings, and what measures do they take to mitigate this?

3. The inter-annotator agreement for the new entity similarity/relatedness dataset (Fleiss' κ = 0.24) is quite low. How do the authors justify using this dataset for evaluation given the low agreement among annotators, and what steps could be taken to improve agreement in future work?

4. The paper claims their method jointly embeds concepts, phrases, and words, but Table 5 shows that APPROXPHRco (approximating concepts as average of phrase embeddings) outperforms CONCEPTco (direct concept embeddings) on the real-world entity dataset. How do the authors reconcile this finding with their core claim that direct concept embeddings are superior?

## Overall Recommendation

The paper presents a method with genuine potential for reducing annotation costs in concept embedding tasks, and the new entity dataset is a valuable contribution to the field. However, the paper has significant weaknesses in its novelty claims, evaluation methodology, and consistency of results. The authors need to address these issues before the work can be considered for publication.

I recommend **weak rejection**. The paper has a solid foundation but requires substantial revisions to strengthen its novelty claims, address evaluation methodology concerns, and provide more nuanced analysis of the results. With these improvements, the paper could make a valuable contribution to the field.

## Additional Comments

- The paper would benefit from a more detailed discussion of how the β and η hyperparameters affect the quality of the embeddings across different datasets.
- The authors should clarify how their method handles phrases that map to multiple concepts (which is common in ontologies) and how this affects the training process.
- The claim that their method "preserves the links between concepts and their representative phrases" (as stated in the abstract and conclusion) is not fully supported by the evidence presented in Figures 3-4.
- The paper's discussion of the "mismatch between ontologies" (UMLS for biomedical concepts vs. YAGO for real-world entities) is interesting but not sufficiently developed as a limitation or opportunity for future work.
