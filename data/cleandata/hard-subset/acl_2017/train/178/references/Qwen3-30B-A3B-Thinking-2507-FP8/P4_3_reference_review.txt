

# Review of "A Weakly-Supervised Method for Jointly Embedding Concepts, Phrases, and Words"

## Summary of the Paper
The paper proposes a weakly-supervised method for jointly embedding concepts, phrases, and words into a shared vector space. The approach uses ontology mappings between concepts and phrases as distant supervision, replacing phrases with unique identifiers in the training corpus, and then trains a modified skip-gram model to embed concepts, phrases, and words simultaneously. The authors evaluate their method on biomedical concept similarity/relatedness tasks and introduce a new dataset of similarity/relatedness judgments for real-world entities (people, places, organizations) collected via crowdsourcing.

## Main Contributions
1. A weakly-supervised method for concept embedding that requires no human annotations for concepts, using only ontology mappings as distant supervision.
2. A novel dataset of similarity and relatedness judgments for real-world entities (people, places, organizations).
3. Experimental validation showing competitive performance with methods requiring human annotations, particularly on larger biomedical datasets.

## Strengths

1. **Practical approach to concept embedding without human annotation**: The paper presents a technically sound and well-motivated method for learning concept embeddings without requiring human annotations for concepts. The use of distant supervision from ontology mappings is clever and addresses a significant practical limitation in prior work. The method is scalable to large vocabularies without additional annotation cost, which is a substantial practical advantage.

2. **Comprehensive evaluation framework**: The authors evaluate their method on multiple established biomedical datasets (MayoSRS, Mini Mayo SRS, UMNSRS) and introduce a new dataset for real-world entity similarity/relatedness. This provides a more complete picture of the method's capabilities than previous work. The results on larger biomedical datasets demonstrate competitive performance with prior methods, particularly on the similarity task.

3. **Insightful analysis of embedding properties**: The paper includes thorough analysis of how well the model preserves ontological relationships (Figures 3-4), showing that concepts are well-represented by their phrases (high similarity to mean phrase embeddings) while phrases show more variability in their relationship to component words. The semantic type clustering analysis (Table 6) also provides valuable evidence that the embeddings capture meaningful semantic relationships.

## Weaknesses

1. **Limited novelty compared to prior work**: The paper acknowledges Wang et al. (2014a) as the closest approach, which also jointly embeds KB entities and words. However, the paper doesn't sufficiently differentiate its contribution from Wang et al.'s work. The key difference (using phrase mappings instead of binary relations) is not clearly articulated as a novel contribution, and the method appears to build directly on Wang et al.'s approach without making a strong case for its novelty.

2. **Performance limitations on smaller datasets**: The method is outperformed by CHOI (which uses human annotations) on the smaller biomedical datasets (MayoSRS and Mini Mayo SRS). The paper doesn't adequately address why their method performs worse on these datasets, which are important for evaluating intrinsic quality of embeddings. This undermines the claim that their method "preserves several semantic properties comparably to recent methods that require human-annotated data."

3. **Questionable benefit of joint embedding**: The paper claims to jointly embed concepts, phrases, and words, but the experimental results don't clearly demonstrate the advantage of this joint approach. The APPROXPHRco baseline (which simply averages phrase embeddings) performs nearly as well as the CONCEPTco method (jointly embedded concepts), suggesting that the joint embedding might not provide significant additional value beyond simpler approaches.

## Questions to Authors

1. The paper states that Wang et al. (2014a) is the closest approach, but their method relies on binary relations in the knowledge base. How exactly does your method differ from Wang et al.'s in terms of the training signal, and why do you believe your approach is more robust or effective?

2. The paper shows that APPROXPHRco (averaging phrase embeddings) performs nearly as well as CONCEPTco (jointly embedded concepts). What evidence do you have that the joint embedding provides additional value beyond simply averaging phrase embeddings?

3. The authors note that the performance of the method varies across different corpora (PUBMED, CLINICAL, PM+CL, WIKINYT) with different hyperparameters. How do you explain these differences, and is there a principled way to select optimal hyperparameters for a given application?

## Additional Comments
The paper's evaluation of semantic type clustering (Table 6) shows mixed results, with the CHOI method performing better overall. The authors should clarify whether their method's performance is consistent across semantic types or if it particularly struggles with certain types (e.g., "sign or symptom" which is poorly clustered for all methods). The paper also mentions that the CHOI method uses additional human annotations (ICD9 codes), but doesn't provide sufficient context about how much additional annotation effort this represents compared to their method.

The supplementary material provides details about hyperparameter tuning, but the paper would benefit from a more concise explanation of why certain hyperparameter choices were made, especially given the relatively small differences in performance across settings.
