

# Review of "A Weakly-Supervised Method for Jointly Embedding Concepts, Phrases, and Words"

## Summary of the Paper
This paper proposes a novel weakly-supervised method for jointly embedding concepts, phrases, and words into a shared vector space. The key insight is to use structured knowledge from ontologies (UMLS and YAGO) as distant supervision to train concept embeddings without human annotations. The authors replace phrases in a corpus with unique identifiers corresponding to their concepts, then train embeddings using a modified skip-gram model. They evaluate the embeddings on biomedical similarity/relatedness tasks and introduce a new dataset for real-world entity similarity/relatedness.

## Main Contributions (in order of strength)
1. **Novel training paradigm using distant supervision from ontologies**: The core contribution is the use of phrases linked to concepts in ontologies as distant supervision to learn concept embeddings without human annotations.
2. **Creation of a new dataset for real-world entity similarity/relatedness**: Fills a significant gap in the field as no comparable datasets existed for entities.
3. **Demonstration of broad applicability**: Shows the method works for both biomedical concepts (UMLS) and general real-world entities (YAGO).

## Strengths
1. **Truly novel training approach**: The use of ontological phrases as distant supervision for concept embedding is a bold, novel paradigm that directly addresses a key limitation in the field. Prior work either required human annotations (Choi et al., 2016) or relied on incomplete knowledge graph structure (Wang et al., 2014a). This is the most significant contribution of the paper.

2. **Competitive performance without human annotations**: The method achieves competitive performance with prior methods that require human annotations (e.g., CHOI), especially on larger datasets. The fact that it can match or outperform methods with human annotations using only distant supervision from ontologies is impressive and demonstrates the value of the approach.

3. **Novel dataset creation**: The authors created a new dataset of similarity and relatedness for real-world entities (people, places, organizations), which is a significant contribution. As they note, there are no comparable datasets for entities, so this fills an important gap in the field.

4. **Broad applicability beyond biomedical domain**: The method works for both biomedical concepts (UMLS) and general real-world entities (YAGO), demonstrating versatility. This is a strong point as it shows the method's potential for wider adoption.

## Weaknesses
1. **Limited novelty in model architecture**: While the training paradigm is novel, the model itself is largely a modification of existing word2vec. The core approach is still a variant of skip-gram with negative sampling, which is not particularly novel. The addition of hyperparameters (β and η) for compositionality is a minor extension.

2. **Questionable evaluation methodology**: The authors tune hyperparameters on the same datasets they're evaluating on, which is a methodological flaw. As they acknowledge in the discussion, the evaluation method (Spearman's correlation on similarity tasks) has known issues (Chiu et al., 2016b), and they don't adequately address this.

3. **Limited analysis of the embeddings**: The paper lacks in-depth analysis of what the embeddings actually capture. There's no exploration of how the embeddings could be used for downstream NLP tasks (e.g., entity linking), which would provide stronger evidence of their utility beyond similarity/relatedness tasks.

4. **Low inter-annotator agreement on the new dataset**: The Fleiss' κ of 0.24 for the new entity similarity/relatedness dataset indicates only fair agreement between annotators, which is concerning for a dataset meant for evaluation. This might undermine the value of their new dataset.

## Questions to Authors
1. You acknowledge that the evaluation method has known issues (Chiu et al., 2016b), but don't address how your results might be affected. Could you discuss how you would evaluate these embeddings in a way that avoids the pitfalls of similarity/relatedness tasks?

2. You mention that your embeddings preserve semantic relationships between concepts, phrases, and words, but don't explore how these embeddings could be used for downstream NLP tasks. Could you discuss potential applications (e.g., entity linking) and how your embeddings might be useful in those contexts?

3. The Fleiss' κ of 0.24 for your new dataset is concerning. Could you provide more details about the annotation process and why the agreement was so low? Would you consider collecting more annotations to improve the dataset quality?

4. Your method works for both biomedical and general entities, but you don't discuss why it might work better for one domain than the other. Could you elaborate on the differences in performance between the biomedical and general-domain tasks?

## Recommendation
**Accept**

As a reviewer who prioritizes novelty and bold approaches, I find this paper highly compelling. The core idea of using ontological phrases as distant supervision for concept embedding without human annotations is a significant conceptual leap that addresses a real limitation in the field. While the model architecture is not fundamentally new, the training paradigm is bold and novel, and the results demonstrate its effectiveness.

I'm willing to overlook the methodological limitations (like the evaluation approach and low inter-annotator agreement) because the paper introduces a genuinely new direction for concept embedding. The creation of a new dataset for real-world entity similarity/relatedness is also a valuable contribution that fills an important gap in the field.

The paper demonstrates that concept embeddings can be learned effectively without human annotations, which is a major step forward. I believe this paper would be a strong addition to ACL, particularly for researchers interested in semantic representation learning and knowledge integration.
