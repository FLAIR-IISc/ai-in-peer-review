

# Review of "A Weakly-Supervised Method for Jointly Embedding Concepts, Phrases, and Words"

## Summary of the Paper and Main Contributions

This paper proposes a weakly-supervised method for jointly embedding concepts, phrases, and words into a shared vector space. The authors leverage structured knowledge from ontologies (UMLS and YAGO) as distant supervision to train embeddings without requiring human annotations. The core idea is to replace phrases in a corpus with unique identifiers corresponding to their canonical concepts, then train a modified skip-gram model to jointly embed concepts, phrases, and words.

**Main contributions (ordered by strength):**
1. A novel weakly-supervised method for concept embedding using distant supervision from ontologies, eliminating the need for human annotations.
2. A new dataset of similarity and relatedness judgments for real-world entities (people, places, organizations), addressing a gap in the literature.
3. Experimental validation showing competitive performance on biomedical concept similarity and relatedness tasks compared to methods that require human annotations.

## Strengths

1. **Innovative use of distant supervision:** The paper presents a clever approach to concept embedding that leverages existing ontological knowledge as distant supervision. This is particularly valuable in domains like biomedicine where manual annotation is expensive and time-consuming. The method of replacing phrases with concept identifiers and training a modified skip-gram model is well-motivated and addresses a genuine problem in semantic representation learning.

2. **Significant contribution of the new dataset:** The authors create a novel dataset of similarity and relatedness judgments for real-world entities, which fills a notable gap in the literature. Previous work has focused on word similarity, but there are limited resources for evaluating entity similarity, especially for non-biomedical entities. The dataset was constructed with careful crowdsourcing procedures, including validation questions to ensure quality (with 658 shared pairs for similarity and relatedness), and the authors provide detailed documentation of their data collection process.

3. **Competitive results on biomedical tasks:** The method achieves competitive performance on biomedical concept similarity and relatedness tasks compared to approaches that require human annotation. The results on the larger UMNSRS datasets are particularly strong, with the authors' method achieving the highest correlation with human judgments on the similarity task (0.653 vs. 0.653 for CHIU-30). This demonstrates that weak supervision from ontologies can yield meaningful semantic representations without human annotation.

## Weaknesses

1. **Lack of clear novelty differentiation:** The paper fails to adequately distinguish its approach from Wang et al. (2014a), which also jointly embeds KB entities and words. The authors mention this work but don't clearly explain how their method differs or why it's superior. The key difference appears to be that Wang et al. uses binary relations between entities, while this paper uses phrase-to-concept mappings, but this distinction is not sufficiently articulated. The paper should provide a more detailed comparison of the core methodological differences.

2. **Questionable evaluation methodology:** The paper uses Spearman's rank correlation for evaluation, which is problematic as noted by Chiu et al. (2016b) and Faruqui et al. (2016). More critically, the authors admit they're tuning on the test set (using 5-fold cross-validation on the evaluation data), which is a major methodological flaw. This undermines the validity of the reported results, as the evaluation data is being used for hyperparameter tuning. The authors should provide results with a proper train/dev/test split.

3. **Incomplete comparison with relevant prior work:** The paper omits comparisons with several important recent methods, such as BabelNet embeddings (Camacho-Collados et al., 2015), which has been used for concept embedding with similar goals. The comparison with Choi et al. (2016b) is limited to their specific method, but they don't address why their method underperforms on smaller datasets (Mayo SRS and Mini Mayo SRS) despite claiming "comparable" performance. The paper should include comparisons with more recent state-of-the-art methods for concept embedding.

4. **Overstated claims:** The paper claims their embeddings "preserve several semantic properties comparably to recent methods that require human-annotated data." However, on the smaller Mayo SRS and Mini Mayo SRS datasets, their method is outperformed by Choi et al. (2016b) (0.593 vs. 0.817 for Mayo SRS). This is a significant limitation that's not adequately addressed, and the paper should temper its claims to reflect this nuanced performance profile.

5. **Insufficient analysis of limitations:** The paper shows that concepts have high similarity to their average phrase embeddings but doesn't explore why this is the case or how to improve the model to better capture semantic information. The authors mention this as a limitation but don't provide concrete suggestions for improvement or analyze the conditions under which the model performs well or poorly.

## Questions to Authors

1. The paper mentions Wang et al. (2014a) as the closest approach but doesn't clearly explain how the proposed method differs. Can you provide a more detailed comparison of the key methodological differences, especially regarding the use of phrase-to-concept mappings versus binary relations between entities?

2. The evaluation methodology uses 5-fold cross-validation on the evaluation datasets, meaning the test set is used for tuning. How would the results change if you used a proper train/dev/test split with the dev set for tuning and the test set reserved for final evaluation? Could you provide these results?

3. Your method achieves competitive performance on larger biomedical datasets but is outperformed by Choi et al. (2016b) on smaller datasets. What specific limitations of your approach might explain this discrepancy, and how could these be addressed in future work?

4. The new entity similarity dataset is limited to 688 pairs per task. Given the importance of this contribution, how do you plan to expand the dataset to make it more comprehensive for future research?

## Overall Assessment

This paper presents an interesting approach to concept embedding that leverages distant supervision from ontologies. The method is well-motivated, and the creation of a new dataset for entity similarity evaluation is a valuable contribution. However, the paper suffers from several significant weaknesses, particularly regarding the evaluation methodology and the lack of clear differentiation from prior work.

While the results on the larger biomedical datasets are promising, the paper's claims are overstated due to the underperformance on smaller datasets and the questionable evaluation methodology. The authors should provide a more balanced assessment of their method's strengths and limitations.

## Recommendation

I recommend **Rejection** with the following reasoning: The paper has valuable contributions in the form of a novel weakly-supervised method and a new dataset, but the methodological flaws in the evaluation (tuning on the test set) and the insufficient differentiation from prior work undermine the significance of the claims. The authors should address these issues before the paper can be considered for acceptance.

## Suggestions for Improvement

1. **Revise evaluation methodology:** Use a proper train/dev/test split to avoid tuning on the test set. Report results on the test set that haven't been used for hyperparameter tuning.

2. **Provide clearer differentiation from prior work:** Conduct a more detailed comparison with Wang et al. (2014a) and other relevant methods, explaining the key differences and advantages of the proposed approach.

3. **Expand the comparison with state-of-the-art methods:** Include comparisons with more recent concept embedding approaches, such as those using BabelNet or other knowledge graph embedding techniques.

4. **Moderate the claims:** Adjust the claims about performance to reflect the nuanced results (e.g., "competitive on larger datasets" rather than "comparable across the board").

5. **Expand the analysis of limitations:** Provide a deeper analysis of why the model underperforms on smaller datasets and what specific aspects of semantic representation it captures or misses.

With these revisions, the paper could make a significant contribution to the field. As it stands, the methodological flaws prevent the paper from meeting ACL's high standards for publication.
