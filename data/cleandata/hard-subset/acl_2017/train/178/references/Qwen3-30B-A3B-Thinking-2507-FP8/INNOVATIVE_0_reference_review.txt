

# ACL Review

## Summary of the Paper
This paper proposes a weakly-supervised method for jointly embedding concepts, phrases, and words into a shared vector space using distant supervision from structured knowledge (ontologies) and an unannotated corpus. The authors replace phrases from a corpus with unique identifiers corresponding to concepts in an ontology, then train a modified skip-gram model to learn embeddings for words, phrases, and concepts simultaneously. The method introduces two hyperparameters to control the tradeoff between compositionality (how phrases are represented by their constituent words) and concept-phrase relationships. The authors evaluate on biomedical concept similarity/relatedness tasks and introduce a new dataset for real-world entity similarity/relatedness.

## Main Contributions (in order of strength)
1. A novel weakly-supervised approach for concept embedding that requires no human annotations for concept representations (only using existing ontologies)
2. A new dataset for evaluating similarity and relatedness of real-world entities (people, places, organizations)
3. Demonstrated preservation of ontological relationships in the embedding space (concepts and phrases clustering together, semantic type clustering)

## Strengths

1. **Truly novel approach to concept embedding**: The paper presents a genuinely novel method for concept embedding that doesn't require human annotations for the concept embeddings themselves. Most prior work on concept embedding (e.g., DeVine et al., Choi et al., Mencia et al.) requires human-annotated data (tagging corpora with concepts or using manually annotated codes). The distant supervision approach using phrases from ontologies is clever and could be applied to many domains beyond biomedical. This represents a significant methodological shift in how we can build concept embeddings without costly human annotation.

2. **Competitive results with human-annotated methods**: The paper demonstrates that the method achieves competitive results on similarity/relatedness tasks compared to methods that require human annotations, particularly on the larger UMNSRS datasets (where their concept embeddings achieve the highest correlation with human judgments). This is impressive given the absence of human annotations for the concept embeddings themselves. The results on the biomedical datasets show the method can capture semantic properties comparable to more resource-intensive approaches.

3. **Valuable new dataset for real-world entities**: The introduction of a novel dataset for similarity and relatedness of real-world entities (people, places, organizations) is a significant contribution to the field. The authors correctly identify that there are no established datasets for this evaluation, and they provide a carefully constructed dataset with appropriate validation. This dataset will be valuable for future research in concept embedding and semantic similarity.

4. **Strong theoretical insight**: The paper demonstrates that the embeddings preserve ontological relationships (concepts and phrases are embedded close to each other, concepts of the same semantic type cluster together). This is a meaningful theoretical contribution showing that the model captures actual semantic structure rather than just optimizing for a particular evaluation metric.

## Weaknesses

1. **Insufficient differentiation from Wang et al. (2014a)**: The paper claims novelty but fails to sufficiently differentiate from Wang et al. (2014a), which is explicitly acknowledged as the closest approach. Wang et al. also proposed methods for jointly embedding KB entities and words, though they used knowledge graph structure. The paper doesn't clearly articulate how their method addresses the limitations of Wang et al.'s approach (incomplete knowledge base relations) or how their distant supervision technique differs fundamentally. This weakens the novelty claim.

2. **Misleading "no human annotations" claim**: The paper claims "no human annotations" but uses ontologies (UMLS and YAGO) that are themselves human-annotated resources. This is misleading since the ontologies contain significant human annotation (e.g., concept mappings, semantic types). The paper should clarify that the method requires no *additional* human annotations beyond what's already present in the ontologies.

3. **Limited scope of evaluation**: The method is evaluated primarily on biomedical concepts and a small set of real-world entities. The paper mentions they would like to apply it to "more abstract data in the general domain," but this remains unexplored. The authors should demonstrate the method's applicability to a broader range of concepts beyond biomedical and concrete entities to better establish the generalizability of their approach.

4. **Questionable practical utility**: The paper shows that for real-world entities, APPROXPHRco (approximating concepts as the average of their phrase embeddings) outperforms the direct concept embeddings. This suggests the joint embedding approach might not be necessary for these entities, which weakens the core contribution. The authors should address why their joint embedding approach is beneficial when a simple phrase average works better for real-world entities.

## Questions to Authors

1. How does your method specifically differ from Wang et al. (2014a) in a way that addresses the limitations of their approach (incomplete knowledge base relations)? The authors acknowledge Wang et al. as the closest approach but don't clearly articulate the key differences.

2. The paper claims "no human annotations" but uses ontologies that contain human-annotated information. Could you clarify the exact nature of the human annotations required for your method versus prior methods that require human annotations for concept embeddings?

3. The results show that APPROXPHRco (approximating concepts as the average of their phrase embeddings) outperforms the direct concept embeddings on the real-world entity dataset. Why does this happen, and what does it say about the effectiveness of your joint embedding approach for these entities?

4. The paper mentions that the model could be applied to "more abstract data in the general domain, similar to previous work with BabelNet." Could you provide a brief example of how the method would work with BabelNet concepts, which are more abstract than the UMLS/YAGO concepts used in the paper?

## Overall Recommendation

Accept. This paper introduces a genuinely novel approach to concept embedding that requires no human annotations for the concept representations themselves, which is a significant methodological contribution. The competitive results on biomedical datasets and the introduction of a valuable new dataset for real-world entity similarity/relatedness make this a strong contribution to the field. While there are some weaknesses in the novelty claim and evaluation scope, the core innovation and results are compelling enough to warrant acceptance, especially given the emphasis on novelty in the conference guidelines. The authors should address the weaknesses in the author response, but they don't undermine the core contribution.
