

# Review of "A Weakly-Supervised Method for Jointly Embedding Concepts, Phrases, and Words"

## Summary and Contributions

The paper proposes a method to jointly embed concepts, phrases, and words using distant supervision from ontologies (UMLS and YAGO) without human annotations. The key idea is to replace phrases in a corpus with unique identifiers corresponding to concepts from the ontology, then train word embeddings on this modified corpus. The authors claim their method can produce embeddings that capture semantic properties comparable to methods requiring human annotations.

As a skeptical reviewer, I assess the contributions as follows (in order of perceived significance):

1. **Novel dataset of similarity/relatedness for real-world entities** - This is the most substantive contribution, though its quality is questionable.
2. **A method that builds on existing word2vec techniques** - The method itself is not truly novel but rather a modification of established approaches.
3. **Claimed competitive performance against methods requiring human annotations** - This claim is overstated based on the evidence provided.

## Strengths

1. **Novel dataset for entity similarity/relatedness**: The paper does provide a new dataset of 688 pairs for both similarity and relatedness tasks for real-world entities (people, places, organizations), which fills a gap in the literature. While the inter-annotator agreement (Fleiss' κ = 0.24) is low, the dataset itself is a useful resource for future work.

2. **Clear description of the method**: The proposed approach is well-described and builds on established techniques (word2vec), making it relatively straightforward to understand and implement.

3. **Practical implementation**: The authors provide a software implementation (cui2vec) and make their code available, which is a positive contribution to the community.

## Weaknesses

1. **Mischaracterization of the method as "weakly-supervised" when it relies on human-annotated ontologies**: The paper repeatedly claims their method requires "no human annotations," but the UMLS and YAGO ontologies themselves are heavily human-annotated knowledge bases. The paper states "We use known phrases as distant supervision in distributional similarity training over an unannotated corpus," but the "known phrases" are derived from the ontology, which is human-annotated. This mischaracterization undermines the paper's main claim of being a truly weakly-supervised approach.

2. **Questionable evaluation methodology**: The paper acknowledges they are "tuning on the test set" (Section 6), which is a serious methodological flaw. The evaluation datasets are used for hyperparameter tuning, which invalidates the results. The authors attempt to mitigate this with 5-fold cross-validation, but as noted in the supplemental material, this is "not equivalent to simply ranking the full dataset" and "the validation numbers skew worse than the overall numbers." This approach is unacceptable for a paper claiming to present a novel method with competitive results.

3. **Lack of convincing evidence that the method outperforms established baselines**: The paper claims "competitive performance" but the results in Table 4 show that on the smaller biomedical datasets (MayoSRS, Mini Mayo SRS), their method is outperformed by CHOI (which uses human-annotated claims codes). On larger datasets, their method performs similarly to the best baselines but without providing evidence that this is statistically significant. The paper fails to demonstrate that their method provides any meaningful improvement over existing approaches, especially considering the methodological flaws in the evaluation.

4. **Insufficient validation of claimed semantic properties**: The paper claims "concepts and their representative phrases are embedded close to one another," but Figure 3a shows that while concepts are similar to their average phrasal embeddings, Figure 3c indicates that concepts have cosine similarity near 0.5 to the average of words in their representative phrases. Figure 4a shows MAP scores for concepts and phrases are "skewed towards 0, with clear peaks at 0.5 and 1," which contradicts the claim that concepts and phrases are embedded close to one another. This is a fundamental flaw in the paper's core claim.

5. **Lack of downstream task validation**: The paper acknowledges the issues with similarity/relatedness tasks (citing Chiu et al. 2016b and Faruqui et al. 2016) but fails to provide evidence of performance on downstream NLP tasks. The authors suggest "applications such as entity linking be pursued in future work," which implies they have not validated their embeddings on any meaningful downstream task. This is a significant weakness given the paper's claims about semantic properties.

## Questions to Authors

1. How do you reconcile the claim that your method requires "no human annotations" with the fact that it relies on UMLS and YAGO, which are human-annotated knowledge bases? Is your method truly "weakly-supervised" as claimed, or is it more accurately described as "ontology-based"?

2. The paper acknowledges that hyperparameter tuning was done on the evaluation datasets. Given that this is a serious methodological flaw, how do you respond to this issue, and how would you revise your evaluation to properly separate training, validation, and test sets?

3. Figure 4a shows that concepts are not well clustered with their representative phrases (MAP scores near 0), which contradicts the paper's claim that "concepts and their representative phrases are embedded close to one another." Can you explain this discrepancy, and what evidence do you have that your embeddings actually preserve the ontological relationships between concepts and phrases?

## Overall Recommendation

This paper should be rejected. While the dataset is a potentially useful contribution, the methodological flaws in the evaluation, the mischaracterization of the approach as "weakly-supervised," and the lack of convincing evidence that the method provides meaningful improvements over existing approaches make the paper unsuitable for publication. The paper fails to meet the high standards of rigor expected for ACL, particularly in the evaluation methodology and in providing sufficient evidence for the claimed improvements over established baselines.

The paper would benefit from a complete re-evaluation of the methodology with proper train/validation/test separation, addressing the ontology dependency issue, and providing evidence of performance on downstream tasks beyond similarity/relatedness. Without these fundamental improvements, the paper does not make a sufficiently strong contribution to the field.
