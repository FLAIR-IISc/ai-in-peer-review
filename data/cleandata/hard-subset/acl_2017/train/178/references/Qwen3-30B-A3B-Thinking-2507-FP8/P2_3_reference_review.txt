

# Review of "A Weakly-Supervised Method for Jointly Embedding Concepts, Phrases, and Words"

## Summary and Main Contributions
This paper proposes a weakly-supervised method for jointly embedding concepts, phrases, and words into a shared vector space. The key insight is to use existing ontology mappings (from UMLS and YAGO) as distant supervision for training embeddings, eliminating the need for human annotations of concepts. The authors replace mapped phrases with unique identifiers in a corpus, then train a modified skip-gram model that jointly embeds concepts, phrases, and words. They evaluate their method on biomedical concept similarity tasks and introduce a new dataset for real-world entity similarity/relatedness.

Main contributions (in order of strength):
1. A novel weakly-supervised approach for concept embedding that requires no human annotations for concepts
2. A new dataset for evaluating similarity and relatedness of real-world entities (people, places, organizations)
3. Demonstration that their method achieves competitive performance with methods requiring human annotations

## Strengths

**1. Novel weakly-supervised approach with practical significance (primary strength)**  
The paper's most compelling contribution is its clever use of ontology mappings as distant supervision to avoid human annotation for concepts. This addresses a critical bottleneck in the field - the high cost of creating concept-level embeddings. The method is simple yet effective: replace phrases with unique identifiers in a corpus, then train a modified skip-gram model. The authors demonstrate that their approach achieves competitive performance on biomedical similarity tasks compared to methods requiring human annotations (e.g., CHOI), while being significantly more scalable. This is a practical contribution that could have broad impact across NLP applications.

**2. Introduction of a novel evaluation dataset (strong strength)**  
The paper introduces a well-constructed dataset of similarity/relatedness judgments for real-world entities (people, places, organizations), which fills a significant gap in the literature. Prior work has focused on words or biomedical concepts, but this dataset provides the first benchmark for general-domain entity embeddings. The dataset construction is thorough, with proper validation questions, crowdsourcing methodology, and detailed analysis of inter-annotator agreement (Fleiss' κ = 0.24, which the authors acknowledge as fair agreement). This dataset alone is a valuable contribution that will benefit future research.

**3. Comprehensive evaluation across multiple domains and datasets (strong strength)**  
The paper conducts thorough evaluations across biomedical and general-domain tasks using multiple corpora (PubMed, Clinical, WIKINYT) and ontologies (UMLS, YAGO). The ablation studies (e.g., Figure 3) provide useful insights into how the model preserves ontological relationships between concepts, phrases, and words. The comparison against multiple baselines (including prior work that required human annotations) demonstrates the method's effectiveness across different contexts.

## Weaknesses

**1. Questionable novelty of the "joint embedding" claim (primary weakness)**  
The paper's central claim is that it jointly embeds concepts, phrases, and words, but the evaluation doesn't convincingly demonstrate the value of this joint approach. The APPROXPHRco method (approximating concepts as the average of phrase embeddings) performs nearly identically to the full CONCEPTco model across most tasks (Table 4 and 5). For example, on the UMNSRS Similarity task, APPROXPHRco achieves 0.637 vs. CONCEPTco's 0.653, a negligible difference. This suggests the joint embedding doesn't provide substantial benefits over simpler approaches, weakening the paper's main contribution.

**2. Unfair comparison with human-annotated baselines (significant weakness)**  
The paper compares its weakly-supervised method to baselines like CHOI, which uses human-annotated medical codes (ICD9, etc.). This is an apples-to-oranges comparison since CHOI has access to significantly richer information (human-annotated clinical codes) than the distant supervision used in the proposed method. The paper doesn't adequately address this discrepancy, making the comparison misleading. A more valid comparison would be against methods using only ontology mappings, like Wang et al. (2014a), which the paper cites but doesn't compare against.

**3. Insufficient discussion of the ontology mismatch limitation (important weakness)**  
The paper notes a mismatch between UMLS (abstract biomedical concepts) and YAGO (concrete real-world entities), but doesn't thoroughly explore how this affects performance. The model is applied to both types of knowledge, yet the paper doesn't analyze whether performance differs between these two types of concepts. This is a significant limitation, as the model's effectiveness for abstract concepts versus concrete entities isn't clear, and the paper doesn't provide guidance on when this method would be most appropriate.

## Questions to Authors

1. The paper claims that "CONCEPTco" (jointly embedded concepts) outperforms APPROXPHRco (concept approximation), but the differences are marginal (e.g., 0.653 vs. 0.637 on UMNSRS Similarity). Could you elaborate on why the joint embedding provides any meaningful advantage over simpler phrase averaging, given these small performance differences?

2. The comparison with CHOI is problematic because CHOI uses human-annotated medical codes, while your method uses distant supervision. How would you compare against a more appropriate baseline that also uses only ontology mappings (like Wang et al., 2014a), and how would this affect your conclusions?

3. The paper mentions a mismatch between UMLS (abstract concepts) and YAGO (concrete entities), but doesn't analyze how this affects performance. Could you provide a breakdown of performance for abstract versus concrete concepts?

## Overall Assessment

This paper presents a practical and novel approach to concept embedding that avoids the need for human annotations, which is a significant contribution. The introduction of a new dataset for real-world entity similarity is also valuable. However, the paper's claim about the benefits of "joint embedding" is undermined by the minimal performance differences between the joint model and simpler phrase averaging approaches. The comparison with human-annotated baselines is also problematic.

The paper makes a useful contribution to the field by demonstrating that weak supervision from ontology mappings can achieve competitive performance, but the novelty of the joint embedding approach is overstated. With clearer justification for the joint embedding approach and a more appropriate comparison to baselines using similar data sources, this paper would be significantly stronger. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions addressing the concerns about the joint embedding claim and comparison methodology.
