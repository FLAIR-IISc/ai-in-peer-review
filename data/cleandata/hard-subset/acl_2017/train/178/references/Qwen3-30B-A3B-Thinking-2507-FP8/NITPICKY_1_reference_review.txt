

# ACL Review: "A Weakly-Supervised Method for Jointly Embedding Concepts, Phrases, and Words"

## Summary and Contributions

This paper proposes a weakly-supervised method for jointly embedding concepts, phrases, and words into a shared vector space using structured knowledge from ontologies (UMLS and YAGO) and an unannotated corpus. The method replaces phrases from the ontology with unique identifiers and adapts the skip-gram model to learn embeddings for concepts, phrases, and words simultaneously. The authors claim their approach can capture semantic properties comparable to prior methods that require human annotations.

As I see them, the paper's most significant contributions are:
1. A novel weakly-supervised approach to jointly embed concepts, phrases, and words without requiring human annotations
2. A new dataset of similarity and relatedness judgments for real-world entities (people, places, organizations)
3. An implementation called "cui2vec" that makes the method available to the community

## Strengths

1. **Novel Dataset for Entity Similarity/Relatedness**: The paper presents a new dataset of similarity and relatedness judgments for real-world entities (people, places, organizations), which is a significant contribution as there are no comparable datasets for entity-level similarity evaluation. The dataset was collected through Mechanical Turk with careful validation procedures.

2. **Practical Implementation**: The authors provide a software implementation called "cui2vec" that makes their method accessible to the community. This is a valuable contribution for reproducibility and further research.

3. **Appropriate Methodology for the Task**: The paper's approach to using distant supervision from ontologies is well-motivated and addresses a genuine problem in semantic modeling where multi-word expressions cannot be composed from their constituent words.

## Weaknesses

1. **Critical Typographical Errors in Mathematical Formulation**: The paper contains several critical typographical errors in its mathematical formulation that undermine the clarity and correctness of the proposed model. Specifically:
   - In Equation (4), "1 |Cs|" should be "1/|Cs|"
   - In Equation (7), there's a missing closing parenthesis: "η ( logσ(~c · Pc) )" should be "η ( logσ(~c · Pc) )"
   - In Figure 2, the caption states "the dashed ENS box is discarded after training" but ENS is a matrix used during training, not something that would be discarded. This is conceptually confusing.

2. **Inconsistent Terminology and Formatting**: The paper has numerous inconsistencies in terminology and formatting that make it difficult to follow:
   - Inconsistent use of "≥ 25" vs "25" for minimum frequency thresholds (Section 4.2 vs A.1)
   - Inconsistent capitalization of "Lou Gehrig's disease" (sometimes with a capital "D" in the text)
   - Table 1 has a value "7,191" for "Max Phrases" in the WIKINYT/YAGO row, which is implausibly high compared to other values (390, 160, etc.) and likely a typo
   - In Table 4, the column headers don't match the table structure, making it difficult to interpret the results

3. **Lack of Clear Explanation for Key Methodological Choices**: The paper introduces hyperparameters β and η to control compositionality, but fails to provide a clear explanation for why these specific values were chosen or how they should be interpreted. The justification for the specific values used in Table 3 is inadequate, with the paper simply stating "we used the results to tune optimal settings" without explaining the rationale for the particular settings chosen.

4. **Inadequate Justification for "Competitive" Performance Claims**: The paper claims "our embeddings are competitive with prior models that required human annotations," but the results in Table 4 show that on the smaller biomedical datasets, their method is significantly outperformed by CHOI (0.817 vs 0.593 on Mayo SRS). The paper does not adequately explain this discrepancy or provide a nuanced interpretation of what "competitive" means in this context.

5. **Insufficient Detail on Dataset Construction**: While the paper claims to present a "novel dataset of similarity and relatedness of real-world entities," it provides only minimal details about the dataset in the main paper and relies on the supplemental material for most details. The actual entity pairs used in the dataset are not provided in the main paper, which is a significant issue for a paper claiming to present a new dataset.

## Questions to Authors

1. In Equation (7), you state "η ( logσ(~c · Pc) )" but the closing parenthesis is missing. Was this intentional, or is it a typo? Please clarify and correct this in the final version.

2. The value "7,191" for "Max Phrases" in Table 1 for WIKINYT/YAGO seems implausibly high compared to other values (390, 160, etc.). Is this a typo? If not, please explain what "Max Phrases" represents in this context.

3. In Table 4, the column headers don't match the table structure (there are more columns than headers). Please clarify the structure of this table for the reader.

4. The paper claims "our embeddings are competitive with prior models that required human annotations," but CHOI outperforms your method on the Mayo SRS and Mini Mayo SRS datasets. Please provide a more nuanced interpretation of what "competitive" means in this context and explain why this discrepancy exists.

5. The paper states "we restrict ourselves to text forms in English, and entities with at least one text form that appears ≥ 25 times in our corpora" (Section 4.2), but in the supplemental material (A.1), it says "we restricted ourselves to a minimum frequency threshold for words and phrases of 25." Please clarify the exact threshold used and ensure consistency.

## Overall Recommendation

Reject

While the paper presents a potentially useful method for jointly embedding concepts, phrases, and words, the numerous technical and formatting inconsistencies, typographical errors in mathematical formulations, and insufficient justification for key claims significantly undermine the paper's credibility. As a perfectionist reviewer, I cannot recommend acceptance due to these substantial issues that need to be addressed before the paper can be considered for publication. The paper would benefit from a thorough re-examination and revision to correct these fundamental issues before resubmission.
