

# ACL Review

## Summary
This paper proposes a weakly-supervised method for jointly embedding concepts, phrases, and words into a shared vector space, using structured knowledge from ontologies (UMLS and YAGO) as distant supervision. The authors replace phrases in the training corpus with unique identifiers and train embeddings using a modified skip-gram model with negative sampling. The paper claims their approach achieves competitive performance with prior methods requiring human annotations, and introduces a novel dataset of similarity and relatedness for real-world entities.

## Main Contributions (as I see them)
1. A novel weakly-supervised approach to jointly embed concepts, phrases, and words using only distant supervision from ontologies
2. A novel dataset of similarity and relatedness for real-world entities
3. Demonstrated preservation of ontological structure in the embedding space

## Strengths
1. **Novel weakly-supervised approach**: The core idea of using phrase-to-concept mappings from ontologies as distant supervision is well-motivated and addresses a meaningful gap in the literature (no human annotations required for concept embeddings).

2. **Novel dataset contribution**: The authors provide a significant contribution with their new dataset of similarity and relatedness for real-world entities, which fills an important gap in the literature (there are no comparable datasets for real-world entities).

3. **Demonstrated semantic structure preservation**: The analysis showing that concepts and representative phrases cluster together, and concepts of the same semantic type cluster together, provides strong evidence that the embeddings capture meaningful semantic structure.

## Weaknesses

1. **Critical methodological errors in the model formulation**: The paper contains several mathematical errors that undermine the technical soundness of the proposed model. Specifically:
   - In Equation (4), the normalization by |Cs| (where Cs is "the set of concepts that p can represent") is incorrect. The denominator should be |Cp| (the set of concepts that phrase p can represent), not |Cs|.
   - In Equation (7), the denominator is written as |Cs|, but should be |Cp| (the set of phrases representing concept c).
   - The notation "c∈Cs" in Equations (4) and (7) is confusing and inconsistent with the definition of Cs earlier in the text. The authors should have used "c ∈ Cp" or similar.
   - These errors make it difficult to understand the actual training procedure and undermine the paper's technical credibility.

2. **Confusing terminology and inconsistent labeling**: The paper uses inconsistent terminology that makes the methodology difficult to follow:
   - In Section 3.1, the paper states "all occurrences of the mapped phrases in the training corpus are replaced with the unique identifiers, converting them to unigrams," but phrases are not unigrams (they're multi-word tokens). This is a fundamental terminology error.
   - In Section 5.1, the baselines "PHRASEco" and "CONCEPTco" are confusingly labeled. "PHRASEco" appears to be using the phrase embedding directly (not concept embedding), yet the "co" suffix implies jointly trained embeddings. This creates significant confusion about the baselines.
   - The paper states "our method is outperformed by CHOI" on smaller biomedical datasets, but Table 4 shows CHOI has higher scores (0.817 vs. 0.593 for Mayo SRS), which is the opposite of what the text claims. This inconsistency is confusing and undermines the paper's credibility.

3. **Poor quality of the novel dataset**: The authors report "Overall Fleiss' κ for our annotations was 0.24 for both datasets, indicating fair agreement between annotators." A Fleiss' κ of 0.24 is on the lower end of acceptable agreement (typically κ > 0.4 is considered moderate agreement). This raises serious concerns about the quality of the dataset they claim is a significant contribution. The paper fails to adequately address these agreement issues or provide justification for using such a low-agreement dataset.

4. **Lack of replicability information**: The paper lacks crucial details for replication:
   - The authors state "we trained for 10 iterations" but don't specify the batch size or number of epochs.
   - They don't provide details about the vocabulary size, minimum frequency threshold beyond "25" (which is mentioned in the supplemental material but not the main text), or how they handled out-of-vocabulary words.
   - The hyperparameter tuning details in the supplemental material are insufficient to replicate their results (e.g., they don't specify the exact split for their 5-fold cross-validation).

5. **Inconsistent results reporting**: The paper makes several inconsistent claims about their results:
   - They claim their method is "competitive with prior models that required human annotations," but the results in Table 4 show that CHOI outperforms their method on all biomedical datasets (with CHOI having the highest scores).
   - In Section 6, they state "our embeddings preserve several semantic properties comparably to recent methods that require human-annotated data," but the results don't support this claim, as CHOI consistently outperforms their method.

## Questions to Authors

1. In Equations (4) and (7), the denominator is written as |Cs|, but this appears to be a typo. Could you clarify what the correct denominator should be (|Cp| or |Cs|) and explain why?

2. The paper claims "our method is outperformed by CHOI" on smaller biomedical datasets, but Table 4 shows CHOI has higher scores (0.817 vs. 0.593 for Mayo SRS). Could you clarify this discrepancy in your results reporting?

3. The Fleiss' κ of 0.24 for your new dataset is on the lower end of acceptable agreement. How did you address this low agreement level in your data collection process, and why do you believe the dataset is still reliable for evaluation?

4. In Section 3.1, you state that phrases are "converted to unigrams" after replacement. This is technically incorrect, as unigrams refer to single words, not phrase tokens. Could you clarify what you mean by "unigrams" in this context?

5. Your paper states that "the source ontology structure is reflected in the organization of our embeddings," but Figure 4 shows MAP scores skewed toward 0, suggesting most concepts aren't especially near their representative phrases. How do you reconcile these seemingly contradictory findings?

## Recommendation
Reject

The paper contains critical methodological errors in the model formulation, inconsistent results reporting, and significant issues with the quality of their novel dataset. While the core idea of weakly-supervised concept embedding is interesting, the technical flaws and inconsistencies are substantial enough to undermine the paper's credibility. The lack of replicability information further compounds these issues. A paper with such fundamental technical errors would not be accepted at ACL, regardless of the novelty of the idea.
