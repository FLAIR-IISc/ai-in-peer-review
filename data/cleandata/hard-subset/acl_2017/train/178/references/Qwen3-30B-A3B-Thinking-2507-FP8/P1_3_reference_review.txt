

# Peer Review for ACL

## Summary and Main Contributions

This paper proposes a weakly-supervised method for jointly embedding concepts, phrases, and words into a shared vector space. The key insight is to use known phrases from an ontology (UMLS and YAGO) as distant supervision to train concept embeddings without requiring human annotations for concepts. The model replaces phrases in a corpus with unique identifiers, then trains embeddings using a modified skip-gram model with negative sampling. The authors evaluate their method on biomedical concepts and real-world entities, showing competitive performance with prior methods that require human annotations. They also introduce a novel dataset of similarity and relatedness for real-world entities (people, places, organizations).

My assessment of the main contributions (ordered by strength):

1. **The novel dataset of similarity and relatedness for real-world entities** - This fills a significant gap in the literature as there were no comparable datasets for real-world entities before.
2. **The weakly-supervised method that achieves competitive results without requiring human annotations for concepts** - This is a significant advantage over prior approaches that relied on human annotations.
3. **Experimental validation showing the model preserves ontological relationships between concepts and phrases** - Demonstrates that the method captures meaningful semantic structure.

## Strengths

1. **Novel dataset for real-world entity similarity/relatedness**: The paper provides a much-needed dataset for evaluating embeddings of real-world entities (people, places, organizations), which is a significant contribution to the field. The authors describe their data collection process thoroughly, including crowdsourcing details, validation procedures, and inter-annotator agreement metrics (Fleiss' κ = 0.24). This dataset fills an important gap in the literature as the authors correctly note that there are no comparable datasets for real-world entities.

2. **Competitive performance with prior methods requiring human annotation**: The authors demonstrate that their weakly-supervised method achieves competitive results on biomedical concept similarity and relatedness tasks compared to prior methods that require human annotations (e.g., CHOI, DEVINE, MENCIA). On the largest datasets (UMNSRS), their concept embeddings have the highest correlation with human judgments on the similarity task, and competitive performance on the relatedness task. This is a significant achievement as it shows that human annotations for concepts are not necessary for effective concept embeddings.

3. **Demonstration of the model's ability to preserve ontological structure**: The authors provide compelling evidence that their embeddings preserve the relationships between concepts and their representative phrases. Figures 3 and 4 show that concepts have high similarity to their average phrase embeddings and that concepts of the same semantic type cluster together. This demonstrates that the method successfully captures meaningful semantic relationships encoded in the ontology.

## Weaknesses

1. **Lack of clear advantage over simpler baseline methods**: While the authors show their method is competitive with prior work, the results in Table 4 show that APPROXPHRco (approximating concepts as average of their phrase embeddings) often performs nearly as well as the proposed CONCEPTco method (direct concept embeddings). In fact, on the real-world entity datasets (Table 5), the phrase-based approximation outperforms the direct concept embeddings. This raises questions about the necessity of the more complex joint embedding approach.

2. **Inadequate justification for hyperparameter choices**: The paper reports hyperparameter tuning but doesn't convincingly explain why certain settings were optimal. For example, Table 3 shows that β=0.75 for PUBMED but β=0.25 for CLINICAL, with no clear rationale for these differences. The authors claim "a non-zero η is only optimal in a single corpus setting" but don't explain why this is the case or how it impacts the model's performance. The hyperparameter tuning process is described but lacks depth in analysis.

3. **Insufficient comparison with recent methods**: The paper compares with several prior methods, but misses comparisons with more recent work in the field, such as BabelNet-based approaches (e.g., Camacho-Collados et al., 2015) which also embed concepts from ontologies. The paper mentions BabelNet in the conclusion but doesn't compare with it directly. The comparison with CHOI (which uses human-annotated ICD9 codes) is valid, but the paper doesn't address whether the authors' method would benefit from incorporating some limited human annotation.

## Questions to Authors

1. In Table 5, the phrase-based APPROXPHRco outperforms the direct concept embedding CONCEPTco on the real-world entity datasets. Why does the joint embedding approach fail to outperform the simpler phrase averaging approach for real-world entities, and what might be done to improve the concept embedding performance?

2. The paper claims to be the first to provide a dataset for real-world entity similarity/relatedness, but doesn't explain why existing word similarity datasets (like WordSim-353, SimLex999) weren't extended to entities. What specific challenges did the authors encounter when creating this new dataset that necessitated a new collection process?

3. The authors state that "the source ontology structure is reflected in the organization of our embeddings." Can they provide specific examples from the embeddings that demonstrate this structure, particularly for concepts that have multiple phrase representations (e.g., "Lou Gehrig's disease" and "amyotrophic lateral sclerosis")?

## Additional Comments

The paper is well-written and the methodology is clearly described. However, the authors should address the concerns about the lack of clear advantage of their joint embedding approach over simpler baselines. The evaluation of semantic type clustering (Table 6) shows mixed results, with the Choi et al. embeddings performing better overall - this should be discussed more thoroughly.

The paper makes a significant contribution with the new dataset, but the novelty of the embedding method itself is somewhat limited given that similar approaches have been proposed (e.g., Wang et al., 2014a). The authors should better distinguish their method from previous work that also jointly embeds concepts and words.

Overall, the paper presents a valuable dataset and demonstrates a viable weakly-supervised approach for concept embeddings, but the methodological contribution could be strengthened by addressing the weaknesses mentioned above. The work is suitable for ACL with minor revisions.
