

# Review: A Weakly-Supervised Method for Jointly Embedding Concepts, Phrases, and Words

## Summary and Main Contributions

This paper proposes a weakly-supervised method for jointly embedding concepts, phrases, and words into a shared vector space. The key innovation is using ontology mappings (UMLS or YAGO) as "distant supervision" to train concept embeddings without requiring human annotations for concepts. The authors introduce a novel dataset of similarity and relatedness for real-world entities and evaluate their embeddings on biomedical and general-domain tasks.

The main contributions, in order of strength, are:
1. A weakly-supervised approach to concept embedding using ontology mappings as distant supervision (no human annotation required for concepts)
2. A novel dataset of similarity/relatedness judgments for real-world entities
3. Comprehensive evaluation showing competitive performance with human-annotated baselines

## Strengths

1. **Novel approach to concept embedding**: The paper presents a clever solution to the problem of concept embedding without human annotation. By using ontology mappings as distant supervision (treating any occurrence of a phrase as evidence for its associated concepts), the method avoids the need for human-annotated concept labels. This is a significant contribution as most prior work on concept embeddings (e.g., DEVINE, CHOI, MENCIA) requires substantial human annotation.

2. **Comprehensive evaluation**: The authors evaluate their method on both biomedical and general-domain datasets, providing a thorough comparison against multiple strong baselines. Their introduction of a new dataset for real-world entity similarity/relatedness fills an important gap in the literature (as they note, no comparable datasets exist for real-world entities).

3. **Insightful analysis of embedding structure**: The paper provides valuable analysis of how well the ontological relationships are preserved in the embedding space. The results show concepts cluster near their representative phrases and concepts of the same semantic type cluster together, demonstrating that the method captures meaningful semantic relationships.

## Weaknesses

1. **Limited novelty of the core method**: The core method is essentially a straightforward extension of word2vec with minor modifications (introducing β and η hyperparameters to control compositionality). The authors cite Wang et al. (2014a) as a similar approach, but don't clearly articulate how their method is significantly different or better. The paper's claims of novelty are overstated - the method is simple and incremental rather than innovative.

2. **Questionable evaluation methodology**: The authors acknowledge the "lack of train/test splits" on similarity/relatedness tasks, which is a well-known problem in the field (as cited in Chiu et al., 2016b). Their 5-fold cross-validation is performed on the evaluation data, meaning the model is indirectly tuned to the evaluation metrics. This severely undermines the validity of the reported results, as the authors themselves note that "hyperparameter settings that improve performance on intrinsic evaluations may degrade performance in extrinsic downstream applications."

3. **Questionable performance claims**: The paper claims their embeddings are "competitive with prior models that required human annotations," but the results show their method is outperformed by CHOI (which uses human-annotated ICD9 codes) on smaller biomedical datasets. The authors don't adequately address this comparison. The CHOI method uses additional human-annotated data, while the authors' method only uses the ontology mappings (which themselves are human-annotated). The paper doesn't clarify whether the authors consider the ontology mappings as "human annotation" (they do, as they state "our method requires no human annotations: we use known phrases as distant supervision"), but this is a significant oversight in the comparison.

## Questions to Authors

1. The paper claims to avoid human annotation for concepts, but the ontology mappings (UMLS and YAGO) are human-annotated. How does this differ from prior work that uses human-annotated ontologies (e.g., Wang et al., 2014a), which also requires human annotation for the ontology itself? The paper's claim of "weakly-supervised" needs clarification.

2. The CHOI baseline uses human-annotated ICD9 codes in addition to the concept embeddings, while your method only uses the ontology mappings. How do you justify claiming your method is competitive with human-annotated baselines when your method requires less human annotation?

3. The Fleiss' κ for your human annotations is 0.24 ("fair agreement"), which is quite low for an evaluation dataset. How did you handle this low agreement in your evaluation, and how might it affect the reliability of your results?

## Overall Assessment

This paper presents an interesting and practical approach to concept embedding that avoids the need for human annotations for concepts. The method is simple, elegant, and shows promising results. However, the novelty claim is overstated, the evaluation methodology has serious flaws, and the performance claims are questionable. The paper would be significantly stronger if the authors could better justify the novelty of their approach, address the evaluation methodology issues, and provide a more balanced comparison with human-annotated baselines.

The paper has merit as a practical contribution to the field, but the claims of novelty and the evaluation methodology need substantial revision before it could be accepted. The introduction of a new dataset for real-world entity similarity/relatedness is a valuable contribution, but it's overshadowed by the methodological issues in the paper.

**Recommendation: Reject** (with possibility of acceptance upon major revision addressing the evaluation methodology and novelty claims)
