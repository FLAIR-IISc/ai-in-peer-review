

# Review for ACL

## Summary of the Paper and Main Contributions

This paper proposes a weakly-supervised method for jointly embedding concepts, phrases, and words into a shared vector space. The core insight is to use structured knowledge from ontologies (UMLS and YAGO) as distant supervision: phrases that refer to a concept are treated as evidence for that concept, and the model learns concept embeddings by considering the contexts of these phrases. The authors replace mapped phrases in a corpus with unique identifiers, then train a modified skip-gram model with negative sampling to learn embeddings for concepts, phrases, and words simultaneously. They evaluate their approach on biomedical concept similarity/relatedness tasks and introduce a new dataset of similarity/relatedness judgments for real-world entities (people, places, organizations).

**Main Contributions (as I see them):**

1. A novel weakly-supervised method for concept embedding that requires no human annotations for concepts, only using phrase mappings from ontologies.
2. A new dataset of similarity and relatedness judgments for real-world entities (people, places, organizations).
3. Experimental validation demonstrating that the approach can capture semantic relationships between concepts, phrases, and words comparable to prior methods that require human annotations.

## Strengths

1. **Practical significance of the approach**: The paper addresses a meaningful gap in the literature by providing a method that can learn concept embeddings at scale without requiring human annotations for concepts. This is particularly valuable in domains like biomedicine where manually annotating concepts for embedding is prohibitively expensive. The authors demonstrate the method's scalability by embedding over 700k entities from YAGO, which is a significant contribution to the field.

2. **Novel dataset creation**: The introduction of a new dataset for similarity and relatedness of real-world entities fills an important gap in the literature. Prior work has focused on word or biomedical concept similarity, but there are no established datasets for evaluating similarity between real-world entities (people, places, organizations). The authors' careful data collection process using Mechanical Turk with validation questions and multiple annotators adds significant value to the community.

3. **Clear demonstration of the method's viability**: The paper provides a thorough experimental evaluation showing that the approach performs competitively with prior methods on large-scale biomedical datasets (UMNSRS Similarity and Relatedness), achieving the highest correlation on the similarity task. The analysis showing that concepts and their representative phrases are embedded close to one another (Figures 3a and 4a) provides evidence that the method captures the intended semantic relationships.

## Weaknesses

1. **Limited evidence for the core contribution (joint embedding)**: While the paper claims to jointly embed concepts, phrases, and words, the evaluation primarily focuses on concept embeddings. The paper doesn't clearly demonstrate how the joint embedding specifically benefits the concept embeddings compared to simpler approaches (like using phrase embeddings directly). The comparison to APPROXPHRco (which uses phrase embeddings directly) shows minimal performance differences, suggesting the joint aspect might not be as impactful as claimed.

2. **Insufficient differentiation from prior work**: The paper cites Wang et al. (2014a) as the closest approach, but doesn't adequately differentiate their method. Wang et al. also jointly embed KB entities and words, but rely on binary relations between entities (which the authors acknowledge are "highly incomplete"). The key difference in the current approach is using phrase mappings instead of binary relations, but this distinction is not clearly articulated as a novel contribution. The paper should more thoroughly discuss how their approach improves upon Wang et al.

3. **Hyperparameter tuning on the evaluation data**: The paper states they tune hyperparameters using an adapted 5-fold cross-validation, but the description suggests they're tuning on the same data used for evaluation. Specifically, they mention "the average of those results was considered to be that setting's error for the target partition" and "the overall error of each setting is then the average of its 5 per-fold errors." This is a form of data leakage because they're using the same data for both tuning and evaluation. This undermines the validity of the reported results, as the model is effectively being optimized for the test set.

4. **Limited analysis of the embedding space**: The paper provides some interesting analysis about compositionality (Figures 3 and 4), but it doesn't sufficiently explore how the joint embedding affects the structure of the vector space. For example, they don't analyze whether concepts from the same semantic type cluster more closely in their embedding space compared to baselines, despite mentioning this in the introduction. The semantic type clustering results (Table 6) are presented but not deeply analyzed in relation to the joint embedding approach.

5. **Overstated novelty claims**: The paper claims their method is "novel" and "requires no human annotations," but as noted in the related work, there's significant prior work on embedding concepts from ontologies (e.g., Mencia et al., 2016; Choi et al., 2016b). The novelty is more in the weakly-supervised aspect using phrase mappings as distant supervision, but this is not clearly established as a significant departure from prior work.

## Questions to Authors

1. The paper claims that the joint embedding of concepts, phrases, and words is a key contribution, but the evaluation primarily compares concept embeddings. Could you provide more analysis showing how the joint embedding specifically improves concept representations compared to using phrase embeddings directly (APPROXPHRco)? For example, are there specific semantic relationships that the joint model captures better?

2. In Table 4, the authors' method (CONCEPTco) is outperformed by CHOI on the Mayo SRS and Mini Mayo SRS datasets, but performs competitively on the larger UMNSRS datasets. Could you elaborate on why this pattern emerges, and whether the method might be more suitable for larger datasets with more diverse examples?

3. The paper mentions that "our embeddings are competitive with prior models that required human annotations for concepts." However, CHOI (2016b) also uses human annotations (ICD9 codes), so it's not clear what "human annotations for concepts" means. Could you clarify the exact annotation requirements for the baselines (particularly CHOI) compared to your method?

4. The paper states that "the source ontology structure is reflected in the organization of our embeddings," but the analysis in Section 5.5 (Table 6) shows that the clustering performance is mixed, with some semantic types performing poorly (e.g., "sign or symptom"). Could you provide more insight into why certain semantic types cluster poorly, and whether this is related to the ontology structure or the embedding approach?

## Additional Comments

The paper would benefit from a more careful discussion of the relationship between the ontology structure and the resulting embeddings. For example, how do the clustering results (Table 6) relate to the specific structure of UMLS versus YAGO?

The authors should clarify the exact nature of the "human annotations for concepts" that their method avoids, as this is a key claim of their contribution. If prior methods also use some form of human annotation (e.g., for the ontology itself), this needs to be clearly distinguished.

The paper would be stronger with a more detailed analysis of when the joint embedding approach provides benefits versus when it's not necessary (e.g., when phrases are highly compositional vs. when they're not).

## Overall Assessment

This paper presents a practical method for learning concept embeddings at scale without requiring human annotations for concepts, which is valuable for applications in domains like biomedicine. The creation of a new dataset for real-world entity similarity is also a significant contribution. However, the paper's claims about the novelty and superiority of the joint embedding approach are not sufficiently supported by the evaluation. The hyperparameter tuning procedure appears to introduce data leakage, which undermines the reported results. The paper would need substantial revisions to address these concerns before it could be considered for publication.

**Recommendation: Rejection** (with strong encouragement to revise and resubmit after addressing the concerns above)
