

# Review for ACL Submission

## Summary
This paper proposes a weakly-supervised method for jointly embedding concepts, phrases, and words using structured knowledge from ontologies as distant supervision. The authors replace phrases mapped to concepts in a corpus with unique identifiers, then train a modified skip-gram model to jointly embed concepts, phrases, and words. They evaluate their approach on biomedical concepts using standard datasets and introduce a novel dataset for real-world entity similarity/relatedness. The key innovation is learning concept embeddings without human annotations, using only phrase-concept mappings from ontologies.

## Main Contributions (as I see them)

1. **Novel weakly-supervised approach for concept embedding using ontological phrase mappings**: This is the most significant contribution - using structured knowledge as distant supervision to learn concept embeddings without human annotations.

2. **Novel dataset for real-world entity similarity/relatedness**: The paper introduces a valuable new dataset that fills a gap in the literature for evaluating entity similarity/relatedness.

3. **Joint embedding of concepts, phrases, and words with semantic preservation**: The paper demonstrates that the embeddings preserve concept-phrase relationships and semantic type clustering.

## Strengths

1. **Novel approach to concept embedding without human annotations**: This is the strongest contribution. The paper's core idea - using phrase-concept mappings from ontologies as weak supervision to learn concept embeddings - is bold and addresses a significant limitation in prior work. The authors demonstrate that their method achieves performance comparable to prior methods that require human annotations, which is impressive given the weak supervision. The approach has clear potential for broader application beyond biomedical domains.

2. **Novel dataset for real-world entity similarity/relatedness**: The paper introduces a valuable new dataset that fills a critical gap in the literature. There are well-studied datasets for word similarity, but none for real-world entities. The authors' crowdsourcing effort to create 688 pairs for similarity and relatedness tasks is substantial and will benefit the community.

3. **Evidence of semantic preservation**: The paper's analysis showing that concepts are embedded close to their representative phrases (Figure 3) and that semantic types cluster together (Table 6) provides strong evidence that the method captures meaningful semantic relationships. This goes beyond just showing quantitative performance improvements.

## Weaknesses

1. **Lack of clear distinction from Wang et al. (2014a)**: The paper claims novelty, but Wang et al. (2014a) already proposed methods for jointly embedding KB entities and words. The key difference (using phrase-concept mappings instead of binary relations) is somewhat subtle and not clearly articulated as a significant departure. The authors don't sufficiently explain why this approach is fundamentally better than Wang et al.'s method, which is a missed opportunity to highlight their contribution.

2. **Modest performance gains**: While the method is novel, the performance gains compared to prior work are modest. On the larger biomedical datasets, the authors' method has "competitive" performance but isn't clearly superior. CHOI outperforms their method on smaller biomedical datasets, and their method isn't clearly better than word-based baselines like CHIU-30 on the larger tasks. This weakens the impact of their contribution.

3. **Methodological flaw in evaluation**: The paper acknowledges that they're tuning hyperparameters on the test set (since they're not training on the evaluation data), which is a significant methodological flaw. While they attempt to mitigate this with 5-fold cross-validation, the approach still risks overfitting to the evaluation datasets. This is a serious concern that undermines the validity of the reported results.

## Questions to Authors

1. How does your method specifically address the incompleteness of binary relations in knowledge bases that Wang et al. (2014a) relied on? Can you provide a more detailed comparison of the two approaches that highlights the fundamental conceptual differences?

2. On the smaller biomedical datasets (Mayo SRS and Mini Mayo SRS), your method is outperformed by CHOI. CHOI uses human annotations for medical codes, while your method is weakly-supervised. Can you explain why this performance gap exists, and what aspects of CHOI's approach make it superior despite requiring human annotations?

3. The paper claims that "our embeddings preserve several semantic properties comparably to recent methods that require human-annotated data." What specific semantic properties are being preserved, and how do they differ from what prior methods capture? The paper's analysis (Figures 3-4) suggests some limitations in how well the embeddings capture these properties.

## Overall Recommendation

Accept

This paper makes a significant contribution with its novel weakly-supervised approach to concept embedding using ontological phrase mappings. While the performance gains are modest, the novelty of the approach and the introduction of a valuable new dataset for real-world entity similarity/relatedness justify acceptance. The methodological flaw in evaluation is concerning but not fatal, especially given the paper's emphasis on novelty over methodological perfection. The authors have demonstrated that their approach captures meaningful semantic relationships without human annotations, which is a bold and important step forward in the field.
