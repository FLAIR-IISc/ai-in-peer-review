

# ACL Review: "A Weakly-Supervised Method for Jointly Embedding Concepts, Phrases, and Words"

## Summary and Main Contributions

The paper proposes a weakly-supervised method to jointly embed concepts, phrases, and words using distant supervision from ontologies (UMLS and YAGO). The key idea is to replace phrases in a corpus with unique identifiers and train embeddings using a modified skip-gram model that incorporates concept information. The authors claim their approach avoids the need for human annotations, which is a significant advantage over prior work.

The main contributions of this work are:
1. A weakly-supervised method for jointly embedding concepts, phrases, and words without requiring human annotations
2. A novel dataset of similarity and relatedness for real-world entities (people, places, organizations)
3. Demonstration that concept embeddings can capture semantic properties comparable to methods requiring human annotations

## Strengths

1. **Novel Evaluation Framework for Real-World Entities**: The paper's most significant contribution is the creation of a new dataset for evaluating similarity and relatedness of real-world entities (people, places, organizations), which is a notable gap in the literature. The authors provide detailed methodology for data collection via Mechanical Turk, including validation questions to ensure quality. This dataset will be valuable for future research in entity representation learning.

2. **Strong Baseline Comparisons on Biomedical Data**: The paper demonstrates competitive results on biomedical concept embeddings compared to several prior methods that require human annotations. The authors' approach (CONCEPTco) achieves the highest correlation with human judgments on the larger UMNSRS similarity dataset (0.653 vs. prior best of 0.653 for CHIU-30). This shows the method's effectiveness in a domain where human annotation is expensive and time-consuming.

3. **Theoretical Justification and Practical Implementation**: The paper provides a clear theoretical foundation for their approach, building on Mintz et al. (2009) and adapting the skip-gram model. The authors also provide a software implementation (cui2vec) and make their dataset publicly available, which is highly valuable for reproducibility and future research.

## Weaknesses

1. **Limited Novelty of Core Method**: The method is largely a straightforward adaptation of existing techniques (Wang et al., 2014a; Mintz et al., 2009) with some parameter tuning. The core idea of using distant supervision from ontologies to train embeddings is not new, and the modifications to the skip-gram model (introducing β and η parameters) are not sufficiently novel. The paper would benefit from a more thorough comparison of how their approach differs from Wang et al. (2014a), which is cited as the closest approach.

2. **Questionable Evaluation Methodology**: The authors tune hyperparameters using the same data they use for evaluation (Table 3), which is a serious methodological flaw. As Chiu et al. (2016b) have shown, this leads to over-optimistic results that don't generalize to downstream tasks. The authors acknowledge this issue in the discussion ("we are still tuning on the test set"), but they don't adequately address it. The paper should have used a proper train/validation/test split for hyperparameter tuning.

3. **Inconsistent Performance Across Datasets**: The method performs well on the larger UMNSRS datasets but is outperformed by CHOI on smaller biomedical datasets (MayoSRS and Mini Mayo SRS). The authors don't adequately explain this discrepancy, which weakens their claim that their method is "competitive with prior models that required human annotations." The performance difference on smaller datasets is particularly concerning, as these are often the most challenging cases.

4. **Lack of Analysis on What's Being Learned**: The paper demonstrates that their embeddings preserve some semantic relationships (Figures 3 and 4), but doesn't provide sufficient analysis of what specific semantic properties are being captured. For instance, the paper doesn't analyze whether the embeddings capture hierarchical relationships, or how well they handle polysemy. The claim that "the source ontology structure is reflected in the organization of our embeddings" is not sufficiently supported by the evidence provided.

## Questions to Authors

1. The paper states that "our embeddings are competitive with prior models that required human annotations," yet the baselines like CHOI include additional human-annotated data (ICD9 codes, etc.). How do you justify this comparison when the prior methods have access to additional human-annotated information that your method does not?

2. The paper uses the same data for hyperparameter tuning and evaluation, which is a serious methodological flaw. How would you address this in a revised version of the paper?

3. The authors claim that "the source ontology structure is reflected in the organization of our embeddings," but the evidence provided (Figures 3-4) is quite weak. Could you provide more specific evidence of how the embeddings capture the ontology structure?

## Overall Recommendation

This paper presents a useful method for weakly-supervised embedding of concepts, phrases, and words with a valuable new dataset for real-world entities. However, the core method lacks sufficient novelty compared to prior work, and the evaluation methodology contains significant flaws. The paper would benefit from stronger justification of novelty, more rigorous evaluation, and better analysis of what semantic properties are actually being captured by the embeddings.

While the contribution of the new dataset is significant, the method itself is not sufficiently novel to justify publication at ACL without substantial revisions addressing the evaluation methodology and novelty concerns. I recommend rejection but would be willing to reconsider with a major revision addressing these issues.
