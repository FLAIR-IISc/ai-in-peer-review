

# ACL Review

## Summary
This paper proposes a weakly-supervised method for jointly embedding concepts, phrases, and words using structured knowledge from ontologies and an unannotated corpus. The authors claim their approach requires no human annotations and can capture semantic relationships between concepts and their representative phrases. The method involves replacing phrases with unique identifiers in a corpus, then training embeddings using a modified skip-gram model with negative sampling, incorporating hyperparameters to control compositionality between phrases and words, and concepts and phrases.

## Main Contributions
1. A weakly-supervised approach for jointly embedding concepts, phrases, and words using only structured knowledge from ontologies (no human annotations required)
2. A novel dataset of similarity and relatedness judgments for real-world entities (people, places, organizations)
3. Experimental validation showing competitive performance on biomedical concept similarity and relatedness tasks compared to methods requiring human annotations

## Strengths
1. The paper addresses an important problem in semantic representation learning: capturing multi-word concepts with different textual forms. The approach of using phrase mappings from ontologies as distant supervision is clever and well-motivated.
2. The paper provides a thorough experimental evaluation across multiple biomedical and general-domain datasets, including a novel entity similarity dataset.
3. The analysis of compositionality (how well the model preserves relationships between concepts, phrases, and words) provides valuable insights into the model's behavior.

## Weaknesses

### 1. Critical Methodological Flaw in Loss Function (Major)
The most severe issue is a fundamental error in the loss function formulation. In Section 3.2.2, the authors define the phrase embedding loss as:

`p = (1−β)(logσ(~p · ~o)−∑n∈N logσ(~p · ~n)) + β(logσ(~p · Wp))`

This formulation is incorrect. The loss should be minimized, but the term `β(logσ(~p · Wp))` would increase the loss when the dot product (~p · Wp) increases, which is the opposite of what's intended. They should have a negative sign: `-β(logσ(~p · Wp))` to encourage high similarity between phrase embeddings and the average of their words.

This is a critical error that would fundamentally affect the model's training and results. Without this correction, the model would be optimizing in the wrong direction for the compositionality component.

### 2. Poor Quality of Novel Dataset (Major)
The paper claims to present "two novel datasets composed of human judgments of similarity and relatedness between pairs of people, places, and organizations." However, the inter-annotator agreement (Fleiss' κ) is reported as 0.24 for both datasets, which falls in the "fair" category (according to standard scales: 0.20-0.40 = fair). This is quite low for a dataset intended for evaluation purposes. 

A κ of 0.24 indicates low consistency among annotators, which undermines the reliability of the dataset. The paper should either improve the annotation process to achieve higher agreement or acknowledge this limitation more clearly. As it stands, the dataset's quality is questionable for use in evaluation.

### 3. Inconsistent and Confusing Notation (Major)
The paper contains several instances of inconsistent and confusing notation that would make the method difficult to implement and understand:

- The negative sampling matrix is defined as ENS = |W| × k, but k is later used as the context window size in the training objective (Table 3, "WS is the size of the context window"). This is highly confusing and would lead to implementation errors.
- The phrase "Max Phrases" in Table 1 is listed as 3.2 for WIKINYT/YAGO, but this should be an integer (likely 3). A non-integer value for a count of phrases is nonsensical.
- In the MAP calculation (Equation 9), the paper writes "|Cp|" instead of "1/|Cp|", which is a critical error in the formula.

### 4. Misleading Performance Claims (Major)
The paper claims "our embeddings are competitive with prior models that required human annotations for concepts." However, Table 4 shows that on the MayoSRS dataset (one of the smaller biomedical datasets), their best method (CONCEPTco) achieves a Spearman's ρ of 0.593, while CHOI achieves 0.817. This is not "competitive" but substantially worse. The claim is misleading and should be revised to reflect the actual performance differences.

### 5. Missing Reproducibility Information (Minor but Important)
The paper states "With this paper, we include cui2vec, a software implementation of our method, as well as our novel dataset of similarity and relatedness of real-world entities." However, no link or reference to the software implementation or dataset is provided. This is a major omission for reproducibility, which is critical in NLP research.

## Questions to Authors
1. Please clarify the exact form of the loss function. The current formulation would optimize in the wrong direction for the compositionality term. What was the intended formulation?
2. What steps did you take to improve inter-annotator agreement beyond the basic validation questions? Given the low κ of 0.24, how can readers trust the quality of your dataset?
3. Why did you choose a minimum frequency threshold of 25 for words and phrases? Is this a standard value or chosen for convenience?
4. The paper states "We only report PM+CL results here; PUBMED and CLINICAL are similar." Please provide evidence or justification for this claim, as the results for different corpora could be quite different.
5. The paper mentions Figure 1 and Figure 2 but they are not included in the text. Please confirm these figures are included in the final submission.

## Final Recommendation
I recommend rejection of this paper. While the core idea of weakly-supervised joint embedding of concepts, phrases, and words is interesting, the paper contains multiple critical flaws that undermine its credibility and reproducibility:

1. A fundamental error in the loss function that would affect the model's training
2. A novel dataset with low inter-annotator agreement (κ = 0.24)
3. Multiple instances of inconsistent and confusing notation
4. Misleading performance claims
5. Missing reproducibility information

These issues collectively prevent me from recommending acceptance. The paper would require substantial revision to address these flaws before it could be considered for publication.
