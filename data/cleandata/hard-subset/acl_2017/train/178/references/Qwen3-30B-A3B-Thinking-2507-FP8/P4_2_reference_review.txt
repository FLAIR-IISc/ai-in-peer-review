

# Review of "A Weakly-Supervised Method for Jointly Embedding Concepts, Phrases, and Words"

## Summary and Main Contributions
This paper proposes a weakly-supervised method for jointly embedding concepts, phrases, and words into a shared vector space. The approach uses structured knowledge from ontologies (UMLS and YAGO) as distant supervision to train embeddings without human annotations. The method replaces phrases from the ontology with unique identifiers in a corpus and trains embeddings using a modified skip-gram model that incorporates both phrase and concept information.

**Main Contributions (from my perspective):**
1. A novel weakly-supervised approach for jointly embedding concepts, phrases, and words using only distant supervision from ontologies (no human annotations required)
2. A new dataset of similarity and relatedness judgments for real-world entities (people, places, organizations), filling a gap in the literature
3. Experimental validation showing that their method achieves competitive performance with human-annotated methods on concept similarity and relatedness tasks, particularly on larger datasets

## Strengths
1. **Novel weakly-supervised approach**: The paper presents a technically sound method that addresses a significant practical limitation in the field (the need for human annotations for concept embedding). The use of ontology mappings as distant supervision is cleverly executed and well-justified. The approach is clearly described and the training procedure is logically motivated, making it reproducible and potentially applicable to other domains.

2. **Comprehensive empirical validation**: The authors provide thorough evaluation across multiple domains (biomedical and general) and multiple corpora (PubMed, Clinical, WIKINYT). The comparison with multiple baselines (including prior human-annotated methods) demonstrates that their approach is competitive, especially on larger datasets. The method's ability to scale to a much larger vocabulary without additional expense is a significant practical advantage.

3. **Valuable dataset contribution**: The creation of a novel dataset for real-world entity similarity and relatedness is a substantial contribution that fills a clear gap in the literature. The careful data collection process (using Mechanical Turk with validation questions) and detailed analysis of inter-annotator agreement (Fleiss' κ = 0.24) add credibility to this contribution. This dataset will be useful for future research in the field.

## Weaknesses
1. **Limited novelty compared to prior work**: The core idea of using structured knowledge for embedding is not entirely new. Wang et al. (2014a) proposed a similar approach for jointly embedding KB entities and words, though they relied on binary relations in the knowledge base. The authors acknowledge this but don't sufficiently distinguish their approach from Wang et al. The key difference (using phrase mappings instead of binary relations) is mentioned but not thoroughly justified as a significant contribution. This weakens the novelty claim.

2. **Insufficient evidence for method superiority**: While the results are competitive, the paper doesn't convincingly demonstrate that their method is significantly better than existing approaches. On some datasets (like Mayo SRS), their method is outperformed by CHOI, which uses human-annotated medical codes. The paper lacks ablation studies to show the contribution of each component (β and η parameters) to the final performance, making it difficult to determine what specifically makes their approach valuable.

3. **Problematic evaluation methodology**: The authors acknowledge the issues with using similarity/relatedness tasks for evaluation (citing Chiu et al., 2016b), but they don't provide strong evidence to overcome these issues. They tune hyperparameters on the evaluation data (despite using 5-fold cross-validation), which is problematic for evaluation methodology. The paper doesn't demonstrate performance on downstream tasks (like entity linking or relation extraction) that would provide stronger evidence of the embeddings' utility.

## Questions to Authors
1. How does your approach specifically differ from Wang et al. (2014a) in a way that provides significant novelty, beyond just using phrase mappings instead of binary relations? Could you clarify the key technical distinctions?

2. You mention that CHOI outperforms your method on smaller biomedical datasets. What specific aspects of CHOI's approach (beyond human annotations) contribute to its better performance, and how might your method be improved to match this?

3. Your paper claims that "our embeddings preserve the links between concepts of the same type and between concepts and their representative phrases," but Figure 4 shows that concept-phrase similarity is not high (scores skewed toward 0). Could you clarify this apparent contradiction?
