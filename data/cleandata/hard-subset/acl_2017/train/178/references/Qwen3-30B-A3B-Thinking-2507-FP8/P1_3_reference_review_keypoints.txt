- Proposes a weakly-supervised method that jointly embeds concepts, phrases, and words using ontology phrases as distant supervision and a modified skip-gram with negative sampling.  
- Replaces phrases in the corpus with unique concept identifiers to train concept embeddings without human annotations.  
- Evaluated on biomedical concepts and real-world entities, showing competitive performance with methods that require human annotation.  
- Introduces a novel dataset of similarity and relatedness for real-world entities (people, places, organizations).  

- Novel dataset fills a gap for real-world entity similarity/relatedness and includes detailed collection and validation procedures.  
- Method achieves competitive results on biomedical similarity/relatedness, matching or exceeding prior human-annotated approaches on some tasks (e.g., UMNSRS similarity).  
- Embeddings preserve ontological relationships: concepts align with representative phrases and cluster by semantic type.  

- Simpler baseline (approximating concepts by averaging phrase embeddings) often performs nearly as well or better than the proposed joint embedding, questioning necessity of complexity.  
- Hyperparameter choices lack clear justification and deeper analysis (e.g., differing β values across corpora and unexplained η behavior).  
- Insufficient comparison with recent related work (e.g., BabelNet-based approaches) and limited discussion of potential benefits from incorporating limited human annotation.  

- Why does APPROXPHRco outperform CONCEPTco on real-world entities, and how can concept embeddings be improved for these cases?  
- Why were existing word similarity datasets not extended to entities, and what challenges necessitated collecting a new entity dataset?  
- Can the authors provide concrete embedding examples showing ontology structure, especially for concepts with multiple phrase representations?  

- Paper is well-written with clear methodology but should better justify advantages over simple baselines and distinguish from prior joint-embedding work.  
- Evaluation of semantic type clustering is mixed and should be discussed more thoroughly, noting some prior methods perform better.  
- Overall: valuable dataset and viable weakly-supervised approach, but methodological novelty and comparisons need strengthening; suitable for ACL with minor revisions.
