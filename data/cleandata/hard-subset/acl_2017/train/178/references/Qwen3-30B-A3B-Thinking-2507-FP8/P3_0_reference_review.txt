

# Peer Review: A Weakly-Supervised Method for Jointly Embedding Concepts, Phrases, and Words

## Summary and Main Contributions

This paper proposes a weakly-supervised method to jointly embed concepts, phrases, and words into a shared vector space, using structured knowledge from ontologies (UMLS and YAGO) as distant supervision. The key innovation is training concept embeddings using phrases linked to concepts in an ontology without requiring human annotations for concepts themselves. The authors also introduce a novel dataset of similarity/relatedness judgments for real-world entities (people, places, organizations).

Main contributions (ordered by strength):
1. A weakly-supervised approach for concept embedding that requires no human annotation for concepts, only structured knowledge linking phrases to concepts
2. A new dataset of similarity and relatedness judgments for real-world entities, addressing a significant gap in the literature
3. Competitive performance on biomedical similarity/relatedness tasks compared to methods requiring human annotations, particularly on larger datasets

## Strengths

1. **Novel weakly-supervised approach**: The paper's core contribution is a clever use of distant supervision from ontology-structured phrases to train concept embeddings without human annotation. This addresses a genuine limitation in the field, as most prior work requires manual annotation for concepts. The method is particularly valuable for domains where manual annotation is prohibitively expensive, such as biomedical or large-scale general-domain applications.

2. **Significant dataset contribution**: The creation of a novel dataset for real-world entity similarity/relatedness is a substantial contribution. The paper correctly identifies the lack of comparable datasets for general real-world entities (as opposed to words or biomedical concepts), and the dataset will be valuable for future research in this area. The careful data collection process with validation questions and inter-annotator agreement analysis adds credibility.

3. **Competitive performance on larger datasets**: The paper demonstrates that on larger biomedical datasets (UMNSRS Similarity and Relatedness), their method achieves the highest correlation with human judgments for similarity tasks, outperforming many baselines. This shows the method's potential for scaling to large vocabularies without additional annotation costs.

## Weaknesses

1. **Lack of clear novelty**: The paper's core idea is not sufficiently differentiated from prior work. Wang et al. (2014a) already proposed methods for jointly embedding KB entities and words, and the authors acknowledge this but don't clearly articulate how their phrase-based distant supervision approach differs in a meaningful way. The paper doesn't sufficiently address why their method is better than Wang's, particularly since Wang's approach also claims to avoid manual annotation (though it uses Wikipedia links, which are still a form of annotation).

2. **Questionable significance of results**: The performance gains are marginal on the larger datasets and the method is outperformed by CHOI on the smaller MayoSRS datasets. The paper claims "competitive performance" but doesn't convincingly demonstrate that their approach is substantially better than existing methods. On the real-world entity dataset, the phrase-based approximation (APPROXPHRco) outperforms the direct concept embeddings (CONCEPTco), which undermines the paper's main claim about concept embeddings.

3. **Methodological flaw in evaluation**: The paper uses the test set for hyperparameter tuning (despite mentioning 5-fold cross-validation), which is a serious methodological error. As Chiu et al. (2016b) and Faruqui et al. (2016) have shown, this can lead to overly optimistic results and invalid evaluation. The paper acknowledges the issue with similarity/relatedness tasks but doesn't properly address it in their evaluation methodology.

4. **Overstated claims about semantic properties**: The paper claims "source ontology structure is reflected in the organization of our embeddings" based on Figures 3 and 4, but the evidence is weak. The high similarity between concepts and their average phrase embeddings (Figure 3a) is expected by design, and the MAP analysis (Figure 4) shows that most concepts aren't especially close to their representative phrases in the vector space, contradicting the claim of preserved ontology structure.

5. **Lack of analysis on when the method works best**: The paper doesn't provide a clear analysis of when the method is particularly advantageous. The hyperparameter tuning (Table 3) shows different optimal settings for different corpora, but the paper doesn't explain why this is the case or provide guidance on when to use this method versus alternatives.

## Questions to Authors

1. How does your method specifically differ from Wang et al. (2014a), which also jointly embeds KB entities and words? The paper claims Wang's method relies on binary relations, but your method uses phrases as distant supervision. Could you clarify this distinction more precisely and demonstrate why your approach is better?

2. Your method is outperformed by CHOI on smaller biomedical datasets but performs better on larger ones. Could you explain this discrepancy and provide guidance on when this method would be preferable to existing approaches?

3. You mention using 5-fold cross-validation for hyperparameter tuning, but the paper's evaluation process appears to use the test set for tuning (Table 3 shows "tuned hyperparameters for each corpus"). Could you clarify how you properly addressed the test set leakage in your evaluation?

4. The paper claims that "source ontology structure is reflected in the organization of our embeddings" but the evidence (Figures 3-4) doesn't strongly support this. Could you provide additional analysis or examples to better demonstrate how ontology structure is preserved in your embeddings?

## Overall Assessment

This paper makes a modest contribution to the field with a novel weakly-supervised approach for concept embedding and a valuable new dataset. However, the claimed novelty is limited, the performance gains are marginal, and the evaluation methodology contains a serious flaw. The paper would be significantly stronger with more rigorous comparison to prior work, clearer differentiation from Wang et al. (2014a), and proper evaluation methodology. While the dataset contribution is valuable, the main methodological contribution is not sufficiently novel or compelling.
