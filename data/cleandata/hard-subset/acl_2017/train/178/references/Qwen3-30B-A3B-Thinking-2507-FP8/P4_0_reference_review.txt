

# Review of "A Weakly-Supervised Method for Jointly Embedding Concepts, Phrases, and Words"

## Summary and Main Contributions

This paper proposes a weakly-supervised method for jointly embedding concepts, phrases, and words into a shared vector space. The approach uses structured knowledge from ontologies (UMLS and YAGO) as distant supervision, replacing phrases with unique identifiers in the training corpus and adapting the skip-gram model to train embeddings for words, phrases, and concepts simultaneously. The authors evaluate their method on biomedical concepts and real-world entities, presenting a new dataset of similarity and relatedness judgments for real-world entities.

**Main contributions (in order of strength):**

1. A novel weakly-supervised approach for jointly embedding concepts, phrases, and words using only structured knowledge from ontologies as distant supervision, without requiring human annotations for concepts.
2. A new dataset of human judgments for similarity and relatedness between real-world entities (people, places, organizations), which fills a significant gap in the literature as no comparable datasets existed previously.
3. Experimental validation showing that the concept embeddings achieve competitive performance with prior methods requiring human annotations, particularly on larger biomedical datasets (UMNSRS Similarity and Relatedness).

## Strengths

1. **Novel weakly-supervised approach with meaningful application:** The paper presents a genuinely novel method for concept embedding that doesn't require human annotations, which is a significant contribution to the field. The approach cleverly leverages the many-to-many relationship between concepts and phrases in ontologies as distant supervision, a technique that is both elegant and practical. The method is conceptually clear and well-motivated, addressing a real limitation in current semantic modeling approaches where words are treated as atomic units rather than being embedded with their conceptual representations.

2. **Strong empirical results on large-scale biomedical datasets:** The authors demonstrate that their method achieves competitive results on the larger biomedical similarity and relatedness datasets (UMNSRS), with their concept embeddings showing the highest correlation with human judgments on the similarity task. This is a significant strength as it demonstrates that the weakly-supervised approach can match or exceed supervised methods on meaningful evaluation tasks. The results on the real-world entity datasets (with the new dataset they created) are also strong, with their phrase-based concept approximation outperforming direct concept embeddings.

3. **High-quality novel dataset for real-world entities:** The creation of a new dataset of human judgments for similarity and relatedness between real-world entities is a valuable contribution to the field. The dataset construction process was thorough, with proper validation procedures and filtering for annotator quality. The authors clearly articulate the gap this dataset fills (lack of comparable datasets for real-world entities), and the dataset is well-suited to evaluate the embedding method's performance on concrete entities rather than just abstract concepts.

## Weaknesses

1. **Limited novelty compared to Wang et al. (2014a):** While the paper acknowledges Wang et al. as the closest approach, it doesn't sufficiently differentiate its method from theirs. Wang et al. also proposed methods for jointly embedding KB entities and words into the same space, but their approach relied on binary relations between entities in the knowledge base, which the authors claim are "highly incomplete." However, the paper doesn't provide a clear explanation of why their phrase-based approach is fundamentally better or more novel than Wang et al.'s method, which also used distant supervision from knowledge bases. The distinction between "binary relations" and "phrase mappings" is not sufficiently justified as a significant methodological advancement.

2. **Performance gap on smaller biomedical datasets:** On the smaller biomedical datasets (Mayo SRS and Mini Mayo SRS), the proposed method is outperformed by CHOI (which uses human-annotated medical codes). This is a significant weakness as it suggests the weakly-supervised approach might not be as effective for smaller-scale or more specialized tasks, which limits the scope of the method's applicability. The paper doesn't adequately address why the method performs better on larger datasets but worse on smaller ones, which is an important consideration for potential users of the method.

3. **Insufficient analysis of hyperparameter tradeoffs:** The paper introduces hyperparameters β and η to control the tradeoff between compositionality (phrases being composed of words, concepts being composed of phrases) and independence. However, the analysis of these parameters is limited. The paper states that "similarity and relatedness performance trend negative as β and η increase" but doesn't provide a detailed analysis of why this happens or how to optimally set these parameters. This weakens the claim of a meaningful tradeoff and leaves the reader without clear guidance for implementing the method in practice.

## Questions to Authors

1. The paper claims that their method is "the first to jointly embed concepts, phrases, and words without human annotations." However, Wang et al. (2014a) also proposed a method for jointly embedding KB entities and words. How does your method differ from Wang et al. (2014a) in terms of the supervision mechanism and the resulting embeddings? Why is your method considered more novel than Wang et al.'s approach?

2. On the smaller Mayo SRS and Mini Mayo SRS datasets, your method is outperformed by CHOI. Could you elaborate on why the weakly-supervised approach performs better on larger datasets but worse on smaller ones? Is this a limitation of the distant supervision approach, and if so, what are the implications for the method's applicability in different scenarios?

3. The paper states that "similarity and relatedness performance trend negative as β and η increase." Could you provide more detailed analysis of this trend, including specific examples of how the embeddings change with different β and η values? How should researchers set these hyperparameters for different applications, and what are the theoretical reasons for the negative performance trend?

4. The inter-annotator agreement (Fleiss' κ) for the real-world entity dataset is 0.24, which indicates "fair" agreement. How does this level of agreement affect the reliability of the dataset, and how does it compare to standard thresholds for annotation quality in similar studies? What steps could be taken to improve the agreement in future iterations of the dataset?
