- Proposes a weakly-supervised method that jointly embeds concepts, phrases, and words using ontology-based distant supervision (replace phrases with identifiers + modified skip-gram).

- Introduces a novel dataset of 688 pairs each for similarity and relatedness of real-world entities (people, places, organizations).

- Shows analysis that concepts and their representative phrases tend to cluster together in the embedding space.

- Novel and practical approach requiring only ontologies (UMLS, YAGO) as supervision, avoiding human annotations and scaling to large vocabularies.

- Method is simple and elegant (phrase replacement + standard word2vec training), potentially broadly applicable.

- The new evaluation dataset is carefully collected and documented, filling a gap for general-domain entity similarity/relatedness.

- Provides insightful analyses (compositionality, semantic-type clustering) that demonstrate meaningful semantic structure beyond standard metrics.

- Performance gains are marginal versus simpler baselines, and the method is outperformed on some datasets (e.g., MayoSRS vs. CHOI).

- Limited evaluation scope: mainly biomedical and YAGO entities, no tests on abstract concepts, standard word similarity datasets, or compositional benchmarks.

- Polysemy handling is inadequately addressed—treating all phrase mentions as all associated concepts may worsen ambiguity; hyperparameter choices lack clear justification.

- Open questions about concept–phrase proximity (high variance in MAP), coverage impact of the 25-occurrence phrase threshold, and why joint embeddings underperform simple phrase-based approximations on real-world entities.

- Overall: interesting and useful weakly-supervised contribution with a valuable dataset, but benefits over simpler methods and broader applicability are not convincingly demonstrated; recommends acceptance with minor revisions.
