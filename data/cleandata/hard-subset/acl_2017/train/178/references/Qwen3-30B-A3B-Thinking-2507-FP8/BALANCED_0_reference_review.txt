

# ACL Review: "A Weakly-Supervised Method for Jointly Embedding Concepts, Phrases, and Words"

## Summary and Main Contributions

This paper proposes a weakly-supervised method for jointly embedding concepts, phrases, and words into a shared vector space using structured knowledge from ontologies as distant supervision. The key innovation is using known phrases linked to concepts in an ontology (UMLS and YAGO) as distant supervision signals to train context-based representations, eliminating the need for human annotations. The authors introduce a novel dataset of similarity and relatedness judgments for real-world entities (people, places, organizations), and demonstrate competitive performance on biomedical concept similarity/relatedness tasks compared to methods requiring human annotations.

**Main contributions as I see them (in order of importance):**
1. A novel weakly-supervised approach for concept embedding using ontology-linked phrases as distant supervision, eliminating the need for human annotations
2. A novel dataset of similarity and relatedness judgments for real-world entities (people, places, organizations)
3. Competitive results on biomedical concept similarity/relatedness tasks compared to methods requiring human annotations

## Strengths

1. **Novel approach to concept embedding with significant practical advantages**: The paper addresses a genuine gap in the literature - the inability of standard word embeddings to represent concepts that aren't simply compositional from their constituent words (e.g., "Lou Gehrig's disease"). The method's use of ontology-linked phrases as distant supervision is clever and requires no human annotations, which is a major practical advantage over prior approaches that rely on manual concept annotation. This is particularly valuable in domains where manual annotation is expensive, such as biomedicine.

2. **Valuable new dataset for real-world entities**: The paper introduces a much-needed dataset for evaluating similarity and relatedness of real-world entities (people, places, organizations), which the authors correctly note is lacking in the literature. The dataset construction using Mechanical Turk with validation questions is well-described, and the resulting dataset size (688 pairs for each task) is substantial for this domain. This dataset will be a useful resource for future research in entity representation.

3. **Competitive performance on key evaluation tasks**: The method achieves competitive results on biomedical concept similarity and relatedness tasks, particularly on the larger UMNSRS datasets where it achieves the highest correlation with human judgments on the similarity task. This demonstrates that the method can capture meaningful semantic properties of concepts without requiring human annotations, which is a significant contribution to the field.

## Weaknesses

1. **Lack of clear novelty compared to prior work**: The paper doesn't sufficiently differentiate itself from Wang et al. (2014a), which the authors themselves cite as the closest approach. The authors claim their method "relies on the binary relations between entities in the knowledge base, which have been shown to be highly incomplete," but don't provide evidence for this claim or demonstrate how their approach avoids this issue. The core technique (using ontology to guide embedding training) is similar to Wang et al., and the paper fails to clearly articulate how this work advances beyond it.

2. **Limited evaluation on meaningful downstream tasks**: The paper only evaluates on similarity and relatedness tasks, which the authors themselves cite as problematic (referencing Chiu et al., 2016b; Faruqui et al., 2016). The paper acknowledges that "the lack of train/test splits on the similarity and relatedness tasks" is a concern, and the method is tuned on the test set. The authors admit that "applications to other intrinsic tasks such as analogy completion or downstream NLP tasks would provide additional strong evidence of these properties," but don't provide any such evidence. This significantly weakens the paper's contribution.

3. **Questionable significance of the "joint embedding" aspect**: The paper claims "jointly embedding concepts, phrases, and words" as a key contribution, but the experimental results suggest this may not be the main source of value. Table 5 shows that phrase-based embeddings (PHRASEco) consistently outperform the direct concept embeddings (CONCEPTco), and Figure 3 shows very high similarity between concepts and their phrase embeddings (mean cosine similarity ~0.5). This suggests the joint embedding doesn't provide much additional value beyond using phrase embeddings directly, contradicting the paper's main claim.

4. **Insufficient hyperparameter analysis and justification**: The paper reports optimal hyperparameters but doesn't provide clear justification for why certain settings work better. The authors state "the differences between best and worst-performing hyperparameter settings were relatively small" (max difference of .075 in Spearman's ρ), which raises questions about the robustness of the method. The paper also doesn't explain why the optimal β and η parameters differ across corpora (Table 3), which is a significant oversight.

5. **Lack of failure analysis**: The paper provides no examples of where the method fails or why it might fail. This is particularly important given the reliance on distant supervision, which can introduce noise from the ontology (e.g., incorrect phrase-to-concept mappings). A failure analysis would have strengthened the paper by showing the boundaries of the method's applicability.

## Questions to Authors

1. The paper claims "the source ontology structure is reflected in the organization of our embeddings," but doesn't provide concrete examples. Could you provide specific examples from the embedding space showing how ontology structure is preserved (e.g., concept clusters corresponding to semantic types)?

2. The paper states "the source ontology structure is reflected in the organization of our embeddings," but the semantic type clustering results (Table 6) show mixed performance, with some semantic types (like "sign or symptom") performing poorly. How do you explain the poor performance on certain semantic types, and what would be required to improve performance on those?

3. The paper mentions "a slight mismatch between the ontologies we used" (UMLS for biomedical concepts and YAGO for real-world entities), but doesn't explore how this might affect the embeddings. Could you provide examples of how this mismatch manifests in the embedding space, particularly for concepts that might have dual representations in both ontologies?

4. The paper states that "the differences between best and worst-performing hyperparameter settings were relatively small," yet still reports specific optimal settings. Given this, how confident are you that the reported performance differences (e.g., between CONCEPTco and PHRASEco) are not simply due to hyperparameter tuning noise rather than meaningful methodological differences?

## Overall Recommendation

I recommend rejection. While the paper has a clear motivation and addresses a genuine gap in the literature, the contribution lacks sufficient novelty compared to prior work, the evaluation is limited to a single type of intrinsic task with known limitations, and the claimed benefits of the joint embedding approach are not supported by the results. The method's practical advantage (no human annotation required) is valuable, but the paper doesn't demonstrate enough evidence that this advantage translates to meaningful improvements over simpler approaches (like using phrase embeddings directly).

## Suggestions for Improvement

1. **Clarify the novelty**: Conduct a more thorough comparison with Wang et al. (2014a) and other related work, clearly articulating how this method advances beyond prior approaches in terms of both methodology and performance.

2. **Expand the evaluation**: Include extrinsic evaluations on downstream tasks (e.g., entity linking, classification) to provide stronger evidence of the method's utility. This would address the authors' own concerns about the limitations of similarity/relatedness tasks.

3. **Reframe the contribution**: If the joint embedding aspect isn't the main contribution, reconsider the paper's framing. The core contribution appears to be the weakly-supervised method for concept embedding, not the joint embedding itself. The paper should focus on this contribution and provide clear evidence that it's superior to simpler alternatives.

4. **Include failure analysis**: Provide examples of where the method fails and analyze why, particularly focusing on cases where the ontology mappings might be incorrect or ambiguous.

5. **Improve hyperparameter analysis**: Provide more thorough analysis of the hyperparameter sensitivity, including why certain settings work better and how the results might generalize to other domains.
