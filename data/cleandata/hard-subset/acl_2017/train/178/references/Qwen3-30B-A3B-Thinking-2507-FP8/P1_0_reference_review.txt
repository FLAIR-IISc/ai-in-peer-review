

# Peer Review

## Summary and Main Contributions

This paper proposes a weakly-supervised method for jointly embedding concepts, phrases, and words into a shared vector space. The key insight is to use known phrases from ontologies (UMLS and YAGO) as distant supervision for training concept embeddings - whenever a phrase appears in a corpus, it's assumed to represent all concepts that phrase can refer to. The authors train embeddings for concepts, phrases, and words simultaneously using a modified skip-gram model with negative sampling. 

The main contributions, in order of strength, are:

1. A truly weakly-supervised method that requires no human annotations for concepts (only leveraging existing phrase-concept mappings in ontologies)
2. A novel dataset of similarity and relatedness for real-world entities (people, places, organizations), which fills a significant gap in the literature
3. Experimental validation showing competitive performance with prior methods that require human annotations for concepts

## Strengths

1. **Truly weakly-supervised approach**: The paper's most significant contribution is its weakly-supervised nature. Unlike prior methods (e.g., Choi et al. 2016b, Mencia et al. 2016) that require human annotations for concepts, this method only uses the existing phrase-concept mappings in ontologies. As stated in the paper, "our model requires no human annotations" - this is a substantial advantage for scalability across domains where such mappings exist but human annotations for concepts do not.

2. **Novel evaluation dataset**: The creation of a new dataset for evaluating similarity and relatedness of real-world entities (people, places, organizations) is a major contribution. The authors explicitly state that "there are well-studied datasets of word similarity and relatedness available... but we are not aware of comparable similarity or relatedness datasets for real-world entities." Their dataset of 688 pairs for both similarity and relatedness tasks will be valuable for future research in this area.

3. **Thorough analysis of embedding structure**: The paper provides a detailed analysis of how the embeddings preserve ontological relationships. Figures 3 and 4 demonstrate that concepts are well-represented by their representative phrases (high cosine similarity), and phrases are often well-represented by their constituent words. This analysis goes beyond standard evaluation metrics to provide insights into how the model actually organizes semantic information.

4. **Implementation availability**: The authors provide a software implementation (cui2vec) and the novel dataset, which significantly increases the paper's reproducibility and utility for the community.

5. **Cross-domain validation**: The method is validated on both biomedical concepts (using UMLS) and real-world entities (using YAGO), demonstrating its applicability across different domains.

## Weaknesses

1. **Performance on biomedical benchmarks**: The method's performance on biomedical similarity tasks is not as strong as some prior methods that require human annotations. As shown in Table 4, CHOI (which requires human annotations) outperforms the proposed method on the MayoSRS and Mini Mayo SRS datasets (0.817 vs 0.593 and 0.726 vs 0.591). The authors acknowledge this but don't sufficiently explain why their weakly-supervised approach underperforms, which is a significant limitation that needs addressing.

2. **Evaluation methodology flaw**: The paper uses a problematic evaluation methodology where hyperparameters are tuned on the test set. As noted in the paper, "we are still tuning on the test set" despite using 5-fold cross-validation. The authors acknowledge that "these optimized hyperparameters will likely change for downstream tasks," which is a serious methodological issue that undermines the validity of the reported results.

3. **Insufficient comparison to Wang et al. (2014a)**: The paper claims to be the closest to Wang et al. (2014a), but the comparison is limited. Wang et al. also jointly embeds KB entities and words, but the current paper doesn't provide a clear performance comparison or detailed analysis of how their approach differs in terms of effectiveness. The paper states Wang et al. "relied on binary relations between entities in the knowledge base, which have been shown to be highly incomplete," but doesn't demonstrate how their method overcomes this limitation in practice.

4. **Lack of analysis on phrase-concept mapping quality**: The paper doesn't sufficiently analyze the quality of the phrase-concept mappings used from ontologies. For instance, how many phrases map to each concept (as shown in Table 1, the average is 5.4 for UMLS), and how accurate these mappings are. The authors state they use mappings where phrases appear at least 25 times in the corpus, but don't discuss whether these mappings are accurate or if they contain errors that could affect the embeddings.

5. **Limited analysis of β and η hyperparameters**: The paper introduces β and η as hyperparameters to control compositionality, but the analysis is limited. The authors note "a non-zero η is only optimal in a single corpus setting," yet they don't provide a thorough analysis of how these parameters affect performance across different domains or how they should be selected in practice.

## Questions to Authors

1. Could you provide more details about the phrase-concept mappings used from UMLS and YAGO? Specifically, how many mappings are there per concept on average, and what is the quality of these mappings (e.g., how often do phrases correctly map to concepts)?

2. The paper states that CHOI outperforms the proposed method on the smaller biomedical datasets. Could you provide more insight into why this might be the case, and whether the weakly-supervised approach could be enhanced to better compete with these methods?

3. Given that the evaluation methodology uses test set tuning, could you provide results with proper train/test splits to validate the reported performance?

4. Could you elaborate on how your method handles ambiguous phrases that map to multiple concepts (e.g., "bank" that could refer to financial institution or river side)? How does this compare to approaches that explicitly model word sense disambiguation?

5. The paper mentions that "the source ontology structure is reflected in the organization of our embeddings." Could you provide specific examples of how the embedding space reflects the ontology structure beyond the general observations in Section 5.5?

## Overall Recommendation

The paper presents a valuable weakly-supervised approach for joint embedding of concepts, phrases, and words that requires no human annotations for concepts. The creation of a novel dataset for real-world entity similarity and relatedness is particularly noteworthy. However, the method's performance on biomedical benchmarks is not as strong as some prior methods that require human annotations, and the evaluation methodology has a significant flaw. With proper addressing of these weaknesses, particularly the evaluation methodology and performance comparison, this paper could be a strong contribution to the field. I recommend acceptance with major revisions.
