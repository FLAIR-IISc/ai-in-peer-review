

# Peer Review for ACL

## Summary and Main Contributions

This paper proposes a weakly-supervised method for jointly embedding concepts, phrases, and words into a shared vector space. The key insight is to leverage structured knowledge from ontologies (UMLS and YAGO) as distant supervision for training embeddings without human annotations. The approach replaces phrases in a corpus with unique identifiers and then trains embeddings for concepts, phrases, and words using a modified skip-gram model with negative sampling. The authors evaluate their method on biomedical concept similarity/relatedness tasks and introduce a novel dataset for real-world entity similarity/relatedness.

The main contributions, in order of strength as I see them:
1. **Novel dataset for real-world entity similarity/relatedness** - The authors create and make publicly available a dataset of human judgments for similarity and relatedness between people, places, and organizations, which fills a significant gap in the literature.
2. **Weakly-supervised joint embedding method** - The approach uses ontology mappings as distant supervision to train embeddings without human annotations, demonstrating competitive performance to methods requiring manual labeling.
3. **Comprehensive evaluation framework** - The paper provides thorough evaluation across multiple datasets, including both biomedical and general-domain tasks, with analysis of semantic type clustering and compositionality.

## Strengths

1. **Significant contribution of the novel dataset**: The creation of a large-scale dataset for evaluating similarity/relatedness between real-world entities (people, places, organizations) is a major contribution. Prior work has focused on word similarity or biomedical concepts, but there was no comparable resource for general-domain entities. The authors provide detailed methodology for data collection, annotation, and validation, and make the dataset publicly available. This will be a valuable resource for future work in semantic representation learning.

2. **Demonstrated effectiveness of weakly-supervised approach**: The paper shows that their method achieves competitive performance to human-annotated baselines on larger datasets (UMNSRS Similarity and Relatedness), which is significant given the lack of human annotation required. This addresses a key practical limitation in the field, as obtaining human annotations for concepts is expensive and time-consuming. The results on the larger biomedical datasets (UMNSRS) are particularly convincing.

3. **Comprehensive evaluation framework**: The paper evaluates the embeddings across multiple dimensions: similarity/relatedness tasks, compositionality analysis, and semantic type clustering. The analysis in section 5.4 (Figure 3) shows that concepts are well-approximated by their representative phrases, and Figure 4 demonstrates the preservation of ontological relationships. This multi-faceted evaluation provides a more complete picture than many prior works that rely solely on similarity/relatedness tasks.

## Weaknesses

1. **Limited novelty of the core method**: While the paper presents a technically sound approach, the core idea of using ontology mappings as distant supervision for training embeddings is not particularly novel. Similar approaches have been explored in prior work (e.g., Mintz et al., 2009; Wang et al., 2014a). The modifications to word2vec are largely incremental and don't address fundamental limitations of this approach. The paper doesn't sufficiently distinguish its method from these prior works, which weakens the contribution claim.

2. **Inconsistent performance claims**: The paper claims competitive performance to human-annotated methods, but on the smaller biomedical datasets (Mayo SRS and Mini Mayo SRS), the proposed method is outperformed by CHOI (which uses human annotations). The authors don't adequately explain this discrepancy or discuss why their method underperforms on smaller datasets despite being weakly-supervised. This undermines the main claim of the paper.

3. **Hyperparameter tuning concerns**: The paper uses a 5-fold cross-validation for hyperparameter tuning but admits they're tuning on the evaluation datasets (section 5.2). This creates a risk of data leakage and over-optimistic results. The authors should clarify how they avoid this issue, as it's a significant methodological flaw in the evaluation.

4. **Questionable value of the "joint embedding" aspect**: The paper claims to jointly embed concepts, phrases, and words, but the results (particularly in section 5.4) suggest that the concept embeddings are largely derived from phrase embeddings, which are themselves derived from word embeddings. The joint aspect doesn't seem to provide substantial additional benefit beyond simpler approaches like APPROXPHRco (which simply averages phrase embeddings). The paper doesn't convincingly demonstrate why joint embedding is necessary.

## Questions to Authors

1. The paper states that their method is competitive with human-annotated approaches, yet it's outperformed by CHOI on the smaller Mayo SRS and Mini Mayo SRS datasets. Could you elaborate on why this discrepancy exists, particularly given that CHOI uses human annotations while your method is weakly-supervised?

2. The hyperparameter tuning (section 5.2) appears to be done on the evaluation datasets themselves, which could lead to data leakage. How did you prevent this, and what evidence do you have that your results aren't inflated due to this practice?

3. In section 5.4, the compositionality analysis shows that concepts are well-approximated by their representative phrases (Figure 3a), but the concept embeddings don't seem to provide additional value beyond the phrase embeddings (Table 4 shows APPROXPHRco often outperforms CONCEPTco). How do you explain this, and why is the joint embedding approach necessary?

## Overall Recommendation

This paper makes a valuable contribution with its novel dataset for real-world entity similarity/relatedness, which will be useful for the community. The weakly-supervised approach demonstrates promising results on larger biomedical datasets, though the claimed advantages over human-annotated methods are somewhat undermined by the performance on smaller datasets. The paper's main weakness is the limited novelty of the core method, which is incremental rather than transformative. With appropriate revisions to address the concerns about novelty, performance claims, and hyperparameter tuning, this paper would be a strong contribution to ACL. As it stands, I recommend a borderline acceptance with major revisions.
