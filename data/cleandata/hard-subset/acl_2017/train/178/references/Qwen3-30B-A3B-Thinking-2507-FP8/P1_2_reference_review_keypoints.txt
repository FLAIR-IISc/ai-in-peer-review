- Proposes a weakly‑supervised method that replaces ontology phrases with IDs in raw text and trains a modified skip‑gram to jointly embed concepts, phrases, and words using distant supervision from UMLS/YAGO.  
- Contribution: a novel weakly‑supervised approach for joint concept/phrase/word embeddings without human annotations.  
- Contribution: a new dataset of human judgments for similarity and relatedness of real‑world entities (people, places, organizations).  
- Contribution: analyses showing embeddings preserve ontological relations and cluster by semantic type.  
- Strength: method is novel and clever, leveraging ontology phrase mappings as distant supervision and working without expensive annotations.  
- Strength: the new real‑world entity similarity/relatedness dataset fills an important gap and is carefully collected.  
- Strength: insightful analyses (compositionality, semantic type clustering) demonstrate meaningful embedding properties beyond accuracy numbers.  
- Weakness: novelty over prior work (e.g., Wang et al. 2014a, Mencia et al. 2016, Choi et al. 2016b) is not clearly distinguished or justified.  
- Weakness: evaluation methodology is questionable—hyperparameters appear tuned on evaluation sets and the adapted 5‑fold CV is insufficiently described, risking data leakage/overfitting.  
- Weakness: limited comparison to state‑of‑the‑art contextual methods (BERT/ELMo) and lack of downstream task evaluation to validate utility.  
- Weakness: insufficient analysis and practical guidance for the β and η hyperparameters (impact and choice unexplained).  
- Weakness: limited discussion of broader limitations, including handling of polysemy and concepts poorly represented in ontologies.  
- Question: how exactly does this method differ from Wang et al. (2014a) and other similar prior work?  
- Question: provide details on the adapted 5‑fold cross‑validation and how it avoids using evaluation data for tuning.  
- Question: why does the method outperform CHOI on larger datasets but underperform on smaller ones—methodological or dataset-specific reasons?  
- Question: what is the empirical effect of extreme hyperparameter settings (e.g., β=1 or η=1) and guidance for choosing them?  
- Question: how does the method handle polysemy or concepts not well represented in the ontology?  
- Overall recommendation: recommend rejection in current form due to methodological flaws and weak novelty claim, but endorse acceptance after major revisions addressing these issues.
