- Proposes a weakly-supervised method that jointly embeds concepts, phrases, and words using ontology mappings (UMLS/YAGO) as distant supervision.  
- Introduces a novel dataset of similarity/relatedness judgments for real-world entities.  
- Claims comprehensive evaluation showing competitive performance with human-annotated baselines across biomedical and general-domain tasks.  

- Strength: Novel practical solution to concept embedding without direct human concept labels by leveraging ontology mappings as distant supervision.  
- Strength: Thorough evaluation on multiple domains and baselines, filling a dataset gap for real-world entity similarity/relatedness.  
- Strength: Analysis shows embeddings preserve ontological structure—phrases cluster near their concepts and similar semantic types cluster together.  

- Weakness: Core method is a relatively simple extension of word2vec with minor hyperparameter additions, so claimed novelty is overstated.  
- Weakness: Evaluation methodology is problematic—no proper train/test splits and 5-fold CV on evaluation data risks tuning to intrinsic metrics and undermines validity.  
- Weakness: Performance claims are questionable; method is outperformed by CHOI on some biomedical tasks and the paper fails to clarify whether ontology mappings count as human annotation.  

- Question: How is the claim of avoiding human annotation justified given that UMLS/YAGO ontology mappings are human-annotated?  
- Question: How do you justify claiming competitiveness with human-annotated baselines when comparisons (e.g., CHOI) use different levels of human annotation?  
- Question: How was the low Fleiss' κ (0.24) handled in evaluation, and how does it affect result reliability?  

- Overall: Interesting and practical contribution with valuable dataset, but novelty claims, evaluation methodology, and performance comparisons need substantial clarification and revision.  
- Recommendation: Reject, but possibly acceptable after major revisions addressing evaluation and novelty concerns.
