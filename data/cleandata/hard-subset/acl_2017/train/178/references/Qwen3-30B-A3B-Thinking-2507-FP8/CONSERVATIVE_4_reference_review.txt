

# ACL Review: "A Weakly-Supervised Method for Jointly Embedding Concepts, Phrases, and Words"

## Summary
This paper proposes a weakly-supervised method for jointly embedding concepts, phrases, and words using distant supervision from ontologies (UMLS and YAGO). The authors replace phrases in a corpus with unique identifiers based on their ontology mappings, then train a modified skip-gram model to jointly embed concepts, phrases, and words. They claim their method can capture semantic relationships between concepts and their textual forms without requiring human annotations for concepts, and present experimental results showing competitive performance on biomedical similarity/relatedness tasks. They also introduce a novel dataset of similarity and relatedness for real-world entities.

## Main Contributions (as I see them)
1. The novel dataset of similarity and relatedness for real-world entities (people, places, organizations) is a valuable contribution that fills an important gap in the literature.
2. A straightforward adaptation of word2vec for weakly-supervised joint embedding of concepts, phrases, and words.
3. Experimental evidence that their method can capture some semantic relationships without human annotations, though the results are mixed.

## Strengths
1. **Novel dataset**: The creation of a new dataset for evaluating similarity and relatedness of real-world entities (people, places, organizations) is a significant contribution that addresses a clear gap in the literature. This dataset could be valuable for future research in semantic evaluation.

2. **Methodological simplicity**: The approach is a straightforward extension of established techniques (word2vec), which makes it methodologically sound and replicable. The authors build on the well-established distant supervision paradigm from Mintz et al. (2009), which is a strength in terms of methodological rigor.

3. **Comprehensive evaluation**: The paper evaluates on both biomedical concepts (using MayoSRS, Mini Mayo SRS, UMNSRS) and real-world entities, demonstrating the method's versatility across different domains.

## Weaknesses
1. **Overstated novelty and claims**: The paper claims their method is "competitive with prior models that required human annotations," but the results show their method is significantly outperformed by supervised methods on key datasets (e.g., CHOI achieves 0.817 on MayoSRS vs. their CONCEPTco's 0.593). This is a substantial discrepancy that undermines their main claim. The method is a straightforward adaptation of existing techniques rather than a significant advance.

2. **Serious methodological flaw**: The authors use the evaluation datasets (MayoSRS, Mini Mayo SRS, UMNSRS, and their novel real-world entity dataset) for hyperparameter tuning, which is a major methodological error. The paper mentions "an adapted 5-fold cross-validation," but the supplemental material does not provide evidence that this properly avoided data leakage. This invalidates the reported results, as the model is being optimized for the specific evaluation tasks.

3. **Inadequate evaluation of the "concept" embedding**: The paper's core contribution is embedding concepts, but the evaluation shows that their direct concept embedding (CONCEPTco) is consistently outperformed by approximating concepts as the average of their phrase embeddings (APPROXPHRco). This suggests the concept embedding component is not actually useful, yet the paper positions it as the key contribution.

4. **Lack of standard evaluation**: The paper does not evaluate on standard word similarity/relatedness datasets (e.g., WordSim-353, SimLex-999), which would provide a more comprehensive picture of their embeddings' quality. This omission makes it difficult to assess how their method compares to the broader field.

5. **Insufficient explanation of performance gaps**: The paper acknowledges their method underperforms compared to supervised baselines but doesn't adequately explain why. For example, they state "the extreme similarity observed in our compositionality analysis, and the high performance of concept approximations, suggests that some non-lexical information about concepts is not being captured," but they don't provide a compelling explanation for this limitation or how it might be addressed.

## Questions to Authors
1. How do you justify the novelty of your method given that it's a straightforward adaptation of word2vec to use ontology mappings as distant supervision, and the closest prior work (Wang et al., 2014a) is different but similar in spirit? The paper doesn't clearly distinguish your method from prior work.

2. Why does your method perform significantly worse than supervised methods (e.g., CHOI) on the MayoSRS dataset (0.593 vs. 0.817), contradicting your claim that your weakly-supervised method is "competitive" with supervised methods? How do you explain this gap?

3. Can you provide more details about your "adapted 5-fold cross-validation" for hyperparameter tuning? Specifically, how did you ensure that the evaluation datasets were not used for any part of the training or tuning process, to avoid data leakage?

4. Why do you not evaluate your embeddings on standard word similarity/relatedness datasets (e.g., WordSim-353, SimLex-999), which would provide a more comprehensive picture of their quality? This omission limits the interpretability of your results.

5. Given that APPROXPHRco (approximating concepts as the average of phrase embeddings) consistently outperforms CONCEPTco (direct concept embeddings), what is the actual utility of your concept embedding component? If your method works better without it, why include it?

## Additional Comments
The paper would benefit from a more critical analysis of why their method underperforms compared to supervised approaches, rather than simply claiming it's "competitive." The authors acknowledge the limitations of their approach in the discussion, but these limitations are significant enough to undermine the main contribution. The novel dataset is valuable, but the methodological flaws in the evaluation make it difficult to trust the main claims. The paper would need substantial revisions to address these concerns before it could be considered for publication.
