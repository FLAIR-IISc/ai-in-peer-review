

# Review of "A Weakly-Supervised Method for Jointly Embedding Concepts, Phrases, and Words"

## Summary and Contributions

This paper proposes a weakly-supervised method to jointly embed concepts, phrases, and words using distant supervision from ontologies (UMLS and YAGO) without requiring human annotations for the target task. The authors claim to demonstrate that their embeddings preserve semantic properties comparable to methods that require human annotations. The main contributions appear to be:
1. A novel weakly-supervised method for joint embedding of concepts, phrases, and words
2. A new dataset of similarity and relatedness for real-world entities (people, places, organizations)
3. Demonstration that concept embeddings can capture semantic properties without human annotations

## Strengths

1. **Novel dataset creation**: The creation of a new dataset for similarity and relatedness of real-world entities (people, places, organizations) is a valuable contribution, especially given the lack of comparable datasets in the general domain. This fills an important gap in the literature.

2. **Practical applicability**: The method's ability to scale to larger vocabularies without additional annotation cost is practically significant, particularly in domains like biomedicine where manual annotation is expensive.

3. **Conceptual contribution**: The idea of using distant supervision from ontologies to train concept embeddings is interesting and potentially useful for applications where human annotations are scarce.

## Weaknesses

1. **Methodological flaws in mathematical formulation**: The paper contains significant mathematical inconsistencies that undermine the credibility of the proposed method. Specifically:

   - In Equation (4), the loss is written as `[ logσ(~c · ~o)− ∑ n∈N logσ(~c · ~n) ] 1 |Cs|`, which is mathematically nonsensical. This should be interpreted as `[ logσ(~c · ~o)− ∑ n∈N logσ(~c · ~n) ] / |Cs|`, but the notation as written is incorrect and confusing.

   - The proposed loss function in Equations (6) and (7) introduces a contradiction. Equation (6) suggests that phrase embeddings are updated using both their own representation and the average word embeddings of the phrase (`logσ(~p · Wp)`), which contradicts the paper's stated goal of embedding phrases as atomic units rather than compositions of words.

   - The authors claim to jointly embed concepts, phrases, and words, but the mathematical formulation suggests that phrases are being treated as compositions of words in the loss function, which undermines the core contribution.

2. **Inconsistent terminology and evaluation**: The paper uses inconsistent terminology throughout, particularly regarding "CONCEPTco" versus "APPROXPHRco" (which is described as "approximating a concept's embedding as the average of the embeddings of its representative strings"). Table 4 shows that "APPROXPHRco" outperforms "CONCEPTco" on biomedical datasets, which contradicts the paper's claim that "CONCEPTco" represents the directly trained concept embeddings. This confusion makes the evaluation results difficult to interpret.

3. **Low inter-annotator agreement for the new dataset**: The authors report Fleiss' κ of 0.24 for both similarity and relatedness datasets, which indicates "fair" agreement between annotators (κ < 0.4 is generally considered "fair" agreement). This is quite low for a dataset intended for evaluation, and the authors don't provide sufficient justification for using this dataset in their evaluation. The low agreement suggests potential issues with the quality of the dataset, which undermines the validity of the evaluation results.

4. **Overstated claims about "no human annotations"**: The paper claims their method "requires no human annotations," but they rely on pre-existing ontologies (UMLS and YAGO) that required significant human curation. This is a common misconception in weakly-supervised learning, and the authors fail to acknowledge that their method depends on human effort to create the underlying ontologies.

5. **Lack of statistical significance**: The paper reports Spearman's ρ values but doesn't provide statistical significance tests (p-values or confidence intervals) for the differences between methods. Without this information, it's impossible to determine whether the reported performance differences are meaningful or could be due to random chance.

## Additional Issues

- **Inconsistent figure references**: The paper mentions "Figure 1" and "Figure 2" but doesn't include these figures in the provided text, making it difficult to understand the model architecture.

- **Inconsistent equation numbering**: In Section 3.2.1, the equations are numbered as (1), (2), (3), and then (4), but the text doesn't clearly indicate that (4) is the fourth equation.

- **Formatting inconsistencies in references**: The reference list contains inconsistent formatting (e.g., "Baroni and Lenci.,? 2011" with an extra question mark and punctuation).

## Recommendation

**Reject**

While the paper presents an interesting idea and creates a novel dataset, the methodological flaws in the mathematical formulation and inconsistent terminology undermine the credibility of the proposed method. The low inter-annotator agreement for the new dataset further weakens the evaluation. The paper makes several overstated claims without sufficient evidence, and the evaluation methodology has significant issues that prevent meaningful interpretation of the results.

The flaws in the mathematical formulation alone are substantial enough to warrant rejection, as they prevent the reader from understanding the actual method being proposed. A paper with such fundamental mathematical inconsistencies cannot be considered for publication in ACL, which requires rigorous technical presentation.

## Questions to Authors

1. In Equation (4), the loss is written as `[ logσ(~c · ~o)− ∑ n∈N logσ(~c · ~n) ] 1 |Cs|`. This appears to be a mathematical error. Can you clarify the intended mathematical expression?

2. The paper claims "CONCEPTco" represents directly trained concept embeddings, but Table 4 shows "APPROXPHRco" (approximation as average of phrase embeddings) outperforms "CONCEPTco" on biomedical datasets. Can you explain this discrepancy, especially since the paper claims "CONCEPTco" should be the optimal embedding?

3. The inter-annotator agreement (Fleiss' κ) for the new entity similarity/relatedness dataset is 0.24, which is considered "fair" agreement. Given this low agreement, what steps were taken to ensure the quality of the dataset, and how can the results be considered reliable?

4. The paper claims "requires no human annotations," but relies on UMLS and YAGO ontologies that required significant human curation. How does the paper address this limitation in the context of weakly-supervised learning?

5. The authors report Spearman's ρ values without statistical significance tests. Can you provide p-values or confidence intervals for the reported differences between methods?
