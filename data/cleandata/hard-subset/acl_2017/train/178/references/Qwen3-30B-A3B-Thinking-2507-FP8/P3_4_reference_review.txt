

# Peer Review: "A Weakly-Supervised Method for Jointly Embedding Concepts, Phrases, and Words"

## Summary
This paper proposes a weakly-supervised method for jointly embedding concepts, phrases, and words using structured knowledge from ontologies as distant supervision. The authors replace phrases in a corpus that correspond to concepts in an ontology with unique identifiers, then train a modified skip-gram model to jointly embed concepts, phrases, and words. They evaluate on biomedical concept similarity/relatedness tasks and present a new dataset of similarity/relatedness for real-world entities.

## Primary Contributions (as I see them)
1. A novel weakly-supervised approach to concept embedding that leverages ontology mappings as distant supervision
2. A new dataset of similarity and relatedness judgments for real-world entities
3. Demonstrating that concept embeddings can be learned without human annotation while preserving semantic relationships

## Strengths

1. **Novel Weakly-Supervised Approach**: The core contribution of using ontology mappings as distant supervision for concept embedding is genuinely novel and well-motivated. Unlike prior work that requires human annotation for concepts (e.g., CHOI), the authors demonstrate that concept embeddings can be learned using only structured knowledge from ontologies. This is particularly valuable in domains where ontology mappings exist but human annotation is expensive or impractical.

2. **Competitive Performance on Biomedical Tasks**: The method achieves strong results on biomedical similarity/relatedness tasks, outperforming word embeddings (CHIU-2, CHIU-30) and approaching or exceeding human-annotated baselines (CHOI, MENCIA) on larger datasets. This demonstrates that the approach can effectively capture semantic relationships without direct human annotation.

3. **Insightful Analysis of Embedding Structure**: The paper provides valuable analysis of how well the embeddings preserve ontological relationships. The compositionality analysis (Figures 3 and 4) reveals important insights about the relationship between concepts, phrases, and words in the embedding space, suggesting the model distributes component phrases around their parents rather than keeping them tightly clustered.

## Weaknesses

1. **Marginal Performance Gains Over Baselines**: While the method is novel, the performance gains over existing approaches are modest. On the smaller biomedical datasets (MayoSRS, Mini Mayo SRS), the method is outperformed by CHOI, which requires human annotation. On larger datasets, the improvements are marginal (e.g., +0.01-0.03 in Spearman's ρ). This weakens the argument that the approach is significantly better than prior work.

2. **Methodological Flaws in Evaluation**: The paper uses the same evaluation setup as prior work but doesn't address well-known issues with similarity/relatedness evaluation (Chiu et al., 2016b). Crucially, they perform hyperparameter tuning on the evaluation sets (as noted in Supplemental Material A.1), which constitutes a serious methodological flaw that invalidates the reported results. This is a critical flaw that needs to be addressed.

3. **Contradictory Results on Real-World Dataset**: The paper's main claim is that direct concept embeddings (CONCEPTco) are superior to approximations, yet on the real-world entity dataset, the phrase-based approximation (APPROXPHRco) significantly outperforms CONCEPTco (Table 5). This undermines the paper's central claim and suggests the method may not be as effective as claimed.

4. **Overstated Novelty**: The approach shares significant similarities with Wang et al. (2014a), which also jointly embeds entities and words using knowledge base structure. The paper doesn't adequately differentiate its method from this prior work, which is the closest related approach. The key difference (no human annotation) is somewhat undermined by the fact that ontology mappings themselves are human-curated.

## Questions to Authors

1. How does your method specifically differ from Wang et al. (2014a), which also jointly embeds entities and words using knowledge base structure? What makes your approach better in terms of requiring no human annotations?

2. The paper claims to require no human annotations, but the ontology mappings (UMLS and YAGO) are human-curated. How does this differ from other methods that require human annotations for concepts, and why is this a meaningful distinction?

3. Why does the phrase-based approximation (APPROXPHRco) outperform the direct concept embeddings (CONCEPTco) on the real-world entity dataset? This seems to contradict the main claim of the paper.

4. How do you address the methodological flaw of hyperparameter tuning on the evaluation sets, which invalidates the reported results? Would the results change if proper train/test splits were used?

## Overall Assessment

This paper presents a novel approach to concept embedding that leverages distant supervision from ontology mappings. While the method is interesting and the evaluation on biomedical tasks is promising, the paper's claims are overstated due to methodological flaws in evaluation and contradictory results on the real-world dataset. The marginal performance gains over baselines and the lack of clear advantage over the closest prior work (Wang et al., 2014a) weaken the contribution. 

The paper would be significantly strengthened by addressing the evaluation methodology issues, providing a more nuanced comparison with prior work, and explaining the contradictory results on the real-world dataset. As it stands, the paper's claims about the superiority of direct concept embeddings over approximations are not well-supported by the evidence presented.

The authors' contribution to the field is valuable for demonstrating that concept embeddings can be learned without human annotation, but the paper would benefit from more careful evaluation and a more critical assessment of its limitations. The new dataset for real-world entity similarity/relatedness is a valuable contribution that could be better leveraged with more thorough analysis.
