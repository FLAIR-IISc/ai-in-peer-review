

# Review of "A Weakly-Supervised Method for Jointly Embedding Concepts, Phrases, and Words"

## Summary and Main Contributions
This paper proposes a weakly-supervised method for jointly embedding concepts, phrases, and words into a shared vector space. The approach uses distant supervision from concept-phrase mappings in an ontology to train embeddings without requiring human annotations for concepts. The authors replace phrases in a corpus with unique identifiers, then train embeddings using a modified skip-gram model that incorporates the ontological relationships between concepts and phrases. They evaluate their method on biomedical concepts (using UMLS) and real-world entities (using YAGO), demonstrating competitive performance on similarity and relatedness tasks compared to methods requiring human annotations. The paper also introduces a novel dataset of similarity and relatedness judgments for real-world entities.

Main contributions (ordered by strength):
1. A weakly-supervised method for jointly embedding concepts, phrases, and words using only ontology-based phrase mappings as distant supervision, without requiring human annotations for concepts.
2. A novel dataset of similarity and relatedness judgments for real-world entities (people, places, organizations), which fills a significant gap in the literature.
3. Experimental validation showing that concept embeddings trained with this method preserve semantic properties (concept-phrase relationships, semantic type clustering) comparably to methods requiring human annotations.

## Strengths
1. **Addressing a meaningful gap in the literature**: The paper tackles a genuine problem in semantic representation learning—embedding concepts directly without human annotation. Prior work either treats words as atomic units or requires substantial human effort for concept annotation. The proposed method leverages existing ontology structure (which already contains concept-phrase mappings) as distant supervision, which is a practical and valuable contribution, especially for large-scale applications.

2. **Novel dataset creation**: The authors introduce a new dataset for evaluating similarity and relatedness of real-world entities, which is a significant contribution. The paper provides thorough documentation of the data collection process, including validation questions, annotation guidelines, and inter-annotator agreement metrics (Fleiss' κ = 0.24). This dataset fills a critical gap in the literature, as no comparable datasets existed for real-world entities prior to this work.

3. **Comprehensive evaluation**: The experimental setup is thorough, with evaluations across multiple corpora (biomedical and general domain), multiple ontologies (UMLS and YAGO), and multiple evaluation tasks (similarity and relatedness for biomedical concepts and real-world entities). The results demonstrate that the method achieves competitive performance with methods requiring human annotations, particularly on larger datasets (UMNSRS).

## Weaknesses
1. **Limited novelty compared to prior work**: The paper acknowledges Wang et al. (2014a) as the closest approach, which also proposed joint embedding of KB entities and words. The main difference appears to be using phrase mappings instead of binary relations between entities. However, the paper doesn't clearly articulate how this modification constitutes a significant advance over Wang et al. (2014a), which is a key concern for novelty. The approach seems to be a minor adaptation rather than a fundamentally new method.

2. **Inadequate justification for concept embeddings**: The results show that PHRASEco (using the learned embedding of a particular string directly) often outperforms CONCEPTco (direct concept embeddings) in the real-world entity tasks (Table 5). This suggests that the concept embeddings may not add significant value over simple phrase embeddings. The paper doesn't adequately explain why concept embeddings would be preferable in practice, undermining a core aspect of the proposed method.

3. **Limited evaluation beyond similarity/relatedness tasks**: The paper acknowledges issues with similarity/relatedness tasks (citing Chiu et al. 2016b), but the evaluation is restricted to these tasks. While the authors mention the need for evaluation on other tasks (e.g., analogy completion, downstream NLP tasks), they don't provide any evidence of their embeddings' utility on these more robust metrics. This limits the evidence supporting the claimed "semantic properties" of the embeddings.

4. **Insufficient analysis of distant supervision limitations**: The paper acknowledges "noisiness" in their distantly-supervised training but doesn't provide evidence of how this noise affects the embeddings or how their method mitigates these issues. The evaluation doesn't include a comparison with a baseline that uses the same ontology mappings but without the embedding method (e.g., simply averaging phrase embeddings), making it difficult to assess the specific value of their proposed approach.

## Questions to Authors
1. The paper claims the method "requires no human annotations," but it uses concept-phrase mappings from ontologies (which typically require substantial human effort to create). How do the authors consider the human effort required for ontology construction in their "no human annotations" claim, and how does this compare to the human effort required for other methods that require annotations (e.g., Choi et al. 2016b)?

2. The results show that PHRASEco often outperforms CONCEPTco in the real-world entity tasks. Can the authors provide more insight into why this is the case, and under what specific conditions would CONCEPTco be preferable to PHRASEco?

3. The paper notes that semantic type clustering performance is mixed, with "sign or symptom" being poorly clustered across all methods. Could the authors elaborate on why this particular semantic type is particularly challenging, and whether their method offers any specific advantages for this type?
