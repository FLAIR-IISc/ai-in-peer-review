Summary and claimed contributions
The paper proposes a weakly supervised method to jointly embed concepts, phrases, and words into a shared vector space by adapting skip-gram with negative sampling. It leverages ontologies (UMLS, YAGO) to map canonical concepts to multiple textual forms, replaces detected multiword phrases with unique identifiers in the corpus, and updates concept embeddings whenever any of their surface forms appear, distributing credit across possible concepts for ambiguous phrases. Two regularization terms encourage compositional consistency of phrases with their words (β) and concepts with their phrases (η). The approach requires no human annotation of concept mentions or knowledge-graph relations. Experiments cover biomedical and general-domain corpora; intrinsic evaluations on similarity/relatedness benchmarks are reported, alongside analyses of concept–phrase–word proximity and semantic type clustering. The paper also releases code (“cui2vec”) and a new entity similarity/relatedness dataset.

Contributions (as assessed)
1) A weakly supervised joint embedding framework that integrates concepts, phrases, and words without relying on human-annotated mentions or knowledge-graph relations, with simple compositionality regularizers (β, η).
2) An extensive intrinsic evaluation showing competitive performance with prior supervised or KB-structure-based concept embeddings, plus analyses demonstrating preservation of ontology links (concept–phrase, semantic type clustering).
3) Release of a new real-world entity similarity/relatedness dataset and software implementation.

Strengths
1) Minimal supervision and practical applicability. The method uses only ontology phrase mappings and raw text. It avoids manual mention annotation and knowledge-graph relation modeling, yet yields competitive performance on intrinsic similarity/relatedness. This lowers the barrier to building concept-level embeddings in domains where annotations are scarce.

2) Breadth of evaluation and strong baselines. Experiments span multiple corpora (PubMed, clinical notes, Wikipedia/NYT) and ontologies (UMLS, YAGO). Comparisons include established biomedical concept embeddings (De Vine; Choi; Mencia) and strong word-level baselines (Chiu with different windows). On larger biomedical datasets (UMNSRS Similarity), the joint concept embeddings achieve the highest Spearman ρ; on relatedness they are competitive. For general-domain entities, phrase-based approximations perform best, highlighting the model’s flexibility.

3) Insightful analyses of compositionality and ontology structure. The paper examines cosine similarity between concepts and the mean of their phrases, and between phrases and the mean of their words, as well as MAP distributions. Results show concepts are close to the average of their phrases, and phrases exhibit a bimodal relationship with constituent words, providing diagnostic insight. Semantic type clustering (MAP) shows competitive performance on several types.

4) Transparent methodology and reproducibility. The training objective is clearly specified; the distant supervision assumption and ambiguity handling are explicit (uniform credit across candidate concepts). Hyperparameter spaces and tuned settings are reported, and both code and a new evaluation dataset are released.

Weaknesses
1) Ambiguity handling may inject substantial noise. The distant supervision assumption credits all concepts for an ambiguous phrase occurrence, with uniform normalization. This can distort concept embeddings, especially for highly polysemous surface forms. No disambiguation or probabilistic weighting is attempted. The observed strength of phrase-based approximations relative to direct concept embeddings suggests this noise materially limits concept-specific signal.

2) Intrinsic-only evaluation and modest gains over simple baselines. The primary evaluations are similarity/relatedness correlations; no extrinsic tasks (e.g., entity linking, concept normalization, downstream clinical prediction) are reported. In the general domain, APPROXPHRco outperforms direct CONCEPTco, and in biomedical datasets CHOI is much stronger on MayoSRS. Overall, improvements are task-dependent and often small, raising questions about practical advantage beyond phrase-level modeling.

3) Hyperparameter tuning on test sets risks overfitting. Although an adapted cross-validation is described, tuning is still performed on the evaluation datasets without a clean train/dev/test split. Given known instability of intrinsic evaluations to hyperparameters, this setup may overstate performance. Reporting fold-wise held-out results or using separate development sets would strengthen claims.

4) Limited detail and minor inconsistencies in the technical formulation. The negative sampling/output embedding is described as ENS with dimensions |W| × k, which appears dimensionally inconsistent (should match embedding size, not number of negatives). The notation Cs appears where Cp was defined, and clarity on how the shared output/negative sampling matrix is used for phrases and concepts is limited. More precise specification would aid reproducibility.

5) New dataset quality and scope concerns. The real-world entity similarity/relatedness dataset has fair inter-annotator agreement (Fleiss’ κ=0.24) and modest size (688 pairs). Entity pair selection was partially manual by relatedness category, which may introduce bias. The dataset’s reliability for fine-grained model comparison is thus limited.

Questions to authors (optional)
- Ambiguity handling: Have you tried alternative credit assignment for ambiguous phrases (e.g., weighting by prior probabilities, EM-style re-estimation, or contextual similarity during training)? What is the impact on concept-level performance?

- Output/negative sampling matrix: Please clarify the dimensions and usage of ENS, and whether a single output matrix is shared across words, phrases, and concepts. Are separate output embeddings per type beneficial?

- Compositional regularizers: Can you provide ablations or learning curves showing sensitivity to β and η beyond the tuned optima, and per-task effects? Do adaptive or type-dependent β/η help?

- Phrase detection: How are overlapping/nested phrases handled beyond greedy longest-match? Is case normalization or lemmatization applied? What is the effect of the min-frequency threshold on coverage vs. performance?

- Extrinsic evaluation: Can you report results on a concept-centric downstream task (e.g., entity linking/normalization or clinical risk prediction) to assess utility beyond intrinsic correlations?

- Dataset: Could you provide per-pair IAA and release the selection protocol to assess potential biases? Are there plans to expand the dataset and/or establish standard splits?

Overall assessment
The paper is clearly written and technically straightforward. The core idea—weakly supervised joint embeddings of concepts, phrases, and words—has moderate novelty, and the empirical results are competitive on several intrinsic benchmarks, with useful analyses and released resources. The main limitations are noise from ambiguity, reliance on intrinsic evaluations with potential tuning leakage, and modest advantage over simpler baselines in some settings. These issues are addressable and, if strengthened, would increase the work’s impact.
