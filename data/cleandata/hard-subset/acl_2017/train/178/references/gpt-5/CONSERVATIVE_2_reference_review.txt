Summary and main contributions
This paper proposes a weakly supervised adaptation of skip-gram with negative sampling to jointly embed words, phrases (surface forms), and ontology concepts into a shared space. It leverages dictionaries from UMLS/YAGO to replace multi-word phrases with IDs in the corpus, and learns separate input embeddings for words, phrases, and concepts that share a common context/output matrix, with optional regularizers encouraging phrase≈average(words) and concept≈average(phrases). The approach requires no human annotations beyond the ontology lexicons. The authors evaluate on biomedical and general-domain similarity/relatedness datasets, introduce a small new entity similarity/relatedness dataset, and analyze compositionality and semantic-type clustering.

As I see it, the main contributions are:
1) A simple weakly supervised training setup to co-embed concepts, phrases, and words using only lexicon mappings, with compositionality regularizers (β, η).
2) Large-scale training on PubMed/clinical/Wikipedia+NYT with concept coverage, showing competitive intrinsic correlation on UMNSRS similarity and some analyses of the learned space.
3) A small, crowdsourced entity similarity/relatedness dataset (with low IAA) and release of code/data.

Strengths
- Practicality and simplicity: The method is a straightforward modification of SGNS, easy to implement and scale. It avoids costly manual annotations and uses widely available lexicons (UMLS/YAGO) with greedy phrase replacement, which is appealing for practitioners who want concept-level vectors without a heavy engineering stack.
- Coverage: The approach scales to large concept vocabularies under frequency constraints and handles many-to-many mappings between phrases and concepts. Reporting coverage stats across corpora and ontologies is useful.
- Competitive intrinsic performance in some settings: On UMNSRS Similarity, the concept embeddings match or slightly exceed strong word baselines (e.g., comparable to CHIU-30) and are competitive with prior concept embeddings on larger datasets. This suggests the weak supervision is not entirely swamped by noise.
- Joint space analysis: The compositionality and semantic-type clustering analyses are informative. Showing clustering by UMLS semantic types and proximity between concepts and their representative phrases helps readers gauge what the space captures.
- Resources: Releasing code (cui2vec) and the entity similarity/relatedness dataset increases potential impact and reproducibility.

Weaknesses
- Limited novelty over established methods: The core idea—using dictionary-based phrase identification with SGNS and jointly learning entity/word embeddings—has substantial precedent (e.g., Wang et al. 2014a; De Vine et al. 2014; various entity/KB/text joint models). The main technical additions (shared context/output matrix and simple β/η regularizers to means) are incremental and not clearly shown to be critical (no strong ablations or gains attributable to β/η).
- Evaluation relies almost entirely on intrinsic similarity/relatedness, with acknowledged limitations: The paper does not include extrinsic tasks (e.g., concept normalization/entity linking, downstream clinical prediction, semantic typing, KB completion) where concept embeddings are most impactful. Given well-known issues with intrinsic evaluations, stronger validation against established downstream baselines is needed to substantiate claims.
- Hyperparameter tuning on test sets and lack of statistical testing: The adapted 5-fold procedure still tunes on the same small datasets used for reporting, risking optimistic estimates. No confidence intervals or significance tests are provided, and improvements over strong baselines are often small or inconsistent. On Mayo SRS and Mini Mayo, a supervised baseline (CHOI) is much better; on UMNSRS Relatedness, CHIU-30 and phrase baselines are comparable or better.
- Ambiguity handling is weakly justified: The distant supervision assumption that any occurrence of a mapped phrase updates all its possible concepts equally is strong and likely introduces sense mixing. There is no comparison to simple heuristics (e.g., longest match + type constraints), priors (P(c|p)), or entity linking; no analysis of how ambiguity affects performance. The compositionality results suggest substantial variance and that non-lexical information is not well captured.
- New dataset quality is limited: The general-domain entity similarity/relatedness dataset has low inter-annotator agreement (κ=0.24), small size (~688 pairs), and manual pair selection with validation questions; this constrains its utility as an evaluation resource. Moreover, on this dataset, concept embeddings are often not better than phrase-based approximations, undercutting the necessity of explicit concept vectors.
- Missing or weak baselines in the general domain: There is no comparison to established entity embedding baselines from Wikipedia/YAGO graphs (e.g., TransE/ComplEx/DeepWalk/node2vec), to Wikipedia page embeddings or anchor-context entity vectors, or to retrofitting methods with semantic lexicons. Given YAGO availability, graph-based baselines should be included.

Questions to authors
- Ambiguity: How often do phrases map to multiple concepts in your training data, and what proportion of updates are ambiguous? Did you try weighting concept updates by priors P(c|p) (e.g., frequency of c among p’s mentions) or any simple disambiguation heuristics? How sensitive are results to this?
- Ablations: What is the effect of β and η separately and jointly beyond the coarse trends in the supplement? Can you quantify gains attributable to these regularizers versus the baseline joint SGNS with phrase replacement? What happens if you remove the shared output matrix or train with only TT or only TU?
- Statistical reliability: Can you report confidence intervals or significance tests for Spearman’s ρ across multiple random seeds/training runs? Some reported differences are small.
- Extrinsic validation: Can you include at least one downstream task where concept embeddings are directly applicable (e.g., UMLS concept normalization in clinical/biomedical text, semantic type prediction, ICD code prediction, entity linking with YAGO)? How do you compare against established supervised/weakly supervised systems there?
- General-domain baselines: Why were standard KB/text entity embedding baselines (TransE/ComplEx/DeepWalk/node2vec, Wikipedia page/anchor-context embeddings) omitted on the YAGO evaluation? Can you add these comparisons?
- Data coverage and OOV: What fraction of pairs in each evaluation dataset are covered by your vocabulary (concepts/phrases/words) after the ≥25 frequency threshold? How are missing items handled in the reported scores?
- Implementation details: How are negative samples drawn across the three input types? Is the context vocabulary purely words, or do phrases also serve as context? Please clarify the notation mismatch (Cs vs Cp) in the loss.
