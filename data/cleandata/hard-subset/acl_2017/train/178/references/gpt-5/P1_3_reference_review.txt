Summary
This paper proposes a weakly supervised method to jointly embed words, surface phrases, and ontology concepts into a shared vector space. Given an ontology mapping concepts to one or more textual forms, the method (i) greedily replaces matched phrases in a corpus with unique IDs to learn phrase embeddings, and (ii) updates concept embeddings whenever any of their surface forms occur, using a skip-gram with negative sampling objective over untagged word contexts. Two auxiliary regularizers control the similarity of phrases to the average of their words (β) and of concepts to the average of their phrases (η). The approach is evaluated on biomedical concept similarity/relatedness datasets (MayoSRS, UMNSRS), and on two new crowdsourced datasets of real-world entity similarity/relatedness. Analyses examine concept–phrase/phrase–word compositionality and semantic type clustering. Code (cui2vec) and the new entity datasets are released.

Main contributions (as I see them)
1) A simple, scalable weakly supervised framework to jointly learn embeddings for concepts, phrases, and words without relying on KB relational edges or manual concept annotations in text.
2) Empirical evaluation showing competitive performance on biomedical concept similarity (UMNSRS similarity) versus stronger baselines that use richer supervision, plus new real-world entity similarity/relatedness datasets.
3) Analysis demonstrating how concept–phrase–word relations and ontology semantic types are reflected in the learned space.

Strengths
- Practical, scalable weak supervision: The method needs only a phrase lexicon from an ontology and an unlabeled corpus, avoiding reliance on incomplete KB edges or expensive human annotations (e.g., CHOI). It scales to hundreds of thousands of concepts (Table 1) across multiple domains (UMLS and YAGO), and uses straightforward training (skip-gram NS).
- Competitive intrinsic results with minimal supervision: On UMNSRS similarity (the largest biomedical set), concept embeddings achieve the best or tied-best Spearman’s ρ (0.653; Table 4), on par with strong word baselines (CHIU-30) and above several concept baselines (De Vine, Mencia). In general-domain evaluations, concept/phrase-based methods perform strongly (Table 5), with APPROXPHRco at 0.810/0.766 and CONCEPTco close behind (0.789/0.750), demonstrating the approach generalizes beyond biomedicine.
- Resource contribution: The release of cui2vec and two new entity similarity/relatedness datasets fills a notable gap for real-world entity evaluation (word-centric datasets are abundant; entity-centric ones are scarce). Even with only “fair” agreement (κ=0.24), these datasets should be useful for benchmarking.
- Thoughtful analyses: The compositionality and MAP analyses (Figures 3 and 4) provide insight into how concepts relate to their phrases and how phrases relate to their words. Semantic type clustering (Table 6) shows structure emerging in the space and where it remains challenging (e.g., “sign or symptom”).
- Transparent hyperparameter exploration: The paper acknowledges sensitivity to hyperparameters and reports tuned settings per corpus (Table 3) with cross-validation in the supplement.

Weaknesses
- Limited novelty in modeling: The core idea—lexicon-based distant supervision by replacing phrases and updating a concept vector when any of its aliases appear—is intuitive and closely related to prior joint word–entity training from text (e.g., Wang et al., Toutanova et al.). The auxiliary regularizers (β, η) are simple dot-product attractions without negative terms, and best settings often set them to zero, suggesting limited added value.
- Ambiguity handling is naive and may inject noise: When a phrase is ambiguous, the model updates all candidate concepts uniformly (Eq. 4/7), with no disambiguation using context or priors. This weak assumption can significantly dilute gradient signal for frequent ambiguous mentions (e.g., “Washington”). The analyses suggest concept embeddings are often near the mean of phrases, and phrase-to-word proximity is bimodal, hinting at unresolved ambiguity/compositionality effects.
- Evaluation is mostly intrinsic and sometimes inconclusive: Results depend on the dataset; CHOI substantially outperforms on MayoSRS/Mini Mayo SRS (Table 4), while the proposed approach is strongest on UMNSRS similarity. Semantic type clustering lags the best prior work overall (Table 6). No extrinsic tasks (entity linking, retrieval, code prediction) are reported, limiting claims about practical utility. Statistical significance testing is not reported, and hyperparameter tuning is performed on the same evaluation sets (albeit with CV).
- Strong baselines sometimes match or beat concept embeddings: In general-domain evaluations, simple phrase-based approximations (APPROXPHRco) outperform learned concept vectors (Table 5). This raises concerns that the concept layer adds limited information beyond aggregated surface forms; similarly, compositionality regularization often prefers β=η=0.
- Methodological clarity gaps: The training architecture departs from standard skip-gram by introducing a separate negative sampling matrix ENS; the rationale and implications vs. standard input/output embeddings are not fully explained. Details on phrase matching (case-folding, morphology, overlapping matches, multi-mentions per sentence), coverage and bias from the ≥25 frequency threshold, and memory/training efficiency are limited. The crowdsourced datasets have low agreement (κ=0.24), which weakens conclusions; additional quality controls or larger annotator pools could help.

Questions to authors
- Can you incorporate simple disambiguation when updating concept vectors (e.g., context-similarity weighting over candidate concepts, or priors from alias frequency)? Do results improve with such weighting vs. uniform splitting?
- What is the empirical impact of the separate ENS matrix vs. standard output embeddings? Could you report an ablation?
- Did you perform statistical significance testing on Spearman’s ρ differences (e.g., Steiger test) across systems?
- How sensitive are results to the phrase frequency cutoff (≥25)? Does lowering it materially improve concept coverage without harming correlation?
- For the new entity datasets, can you provide per-pair variance or confidence intervals and release annotator-level scores? Any filtering by entity type pairs in evaluation?

Additional comments
- Consider an extrinsic evaluation (e.g., entity linking, biomedical IR re-ranking, or code prediction) to strengthen claims about utility.
- Comparing against joint word–entity embedding methods that exploit Wikipedia anchors/links would contextualize gains and trade-offs.
- Clarify phrase matching (tokenization, case, normalization) and overlapping match resolution, and report ambiguity rates.
- Some references need cleanup (typos, repeated entries).
