Summary and claimed contributions
This paper proposes a weakly supervised method to jointly embed ontology concepts, their surface phrases, and words in a shared space. The approach replaces mapped phrases in text with unique tokens, trains skip-gram with negative sampling on parallel tagged/untagged corpora, and optionally regularizes phrase embeddings toward the average of their words (β) and concept embeddings toward the average of their phrases (η). The method is evaluated on biomedical (UMLS) and general-domain (YAGO) concepts using similarity/relatedness datasets; the authors also release a new entity similarity/relatedness dataset and a software implementation (cui2vec). Additional analyses examine concept–phrase and phrase–word compositionality and semantic-type clustering.

As I see it, the main contributions (in order of strength) are:
1) An annotation-light, scalable training procedure to obtain concept-level embeddings directly from raw text using only an ontology’s alias dictionary, jointly embedding concepts, phrases, and words.
2) Empirical evaluation across domains (biomedical and general), including a new entity similarity/relatedness dataset, and competitive performance on larger biomedical similarity datasets.
3) Analyses probing compositionality links and semantic-type clustering, providing insight into how the space organizes concepts, phrases, and words.

Strengths
1) Practical and scalable weak supervision: The method uses only an alias mapping from existing ontologies and unannotated corpora, avoiding costly concept annotation or KB relation extraction. It scales to hundreds of thousands of concepts (e.g., 188k UMLS concepts; ~750k YAGO entities) and works across domains (PubMed/clinical; Wikipedia/NYT).
2) Competitive intrinsic performance with minimal supervision: On UMNSRS Similarity, the proposed concept embeddings outperform prior concept/word baselines, and are competitive on relatedness. In the general domain, jointly trained phrase-based approximations and concept embeddings achieve strong correlations, suggesting the approach transfers beyond biomedicine.
3) Thoughtful analyses: The compositionality and MAP analyses are informative: concepts are close to the mean of their phrases; phrases exhibit bimodal proximity to word averages; semantic-type clustering results are nuanced and identify hard types (e.g., sign or symptom). This helps diagnose where the model succeeds and where it falls short.
4) Resource contributions: Releasing cui2vec and a new entity similarity/relatedness dataset adds community value. The hyperparameter exploration (including β/η) and reported settings per corpus improve transparency and reproducibility.
5) Clear problem motivation: The case for modeling multiword concepts atomically (rather than compositional word averages) is well articulated, with realistic examples (“the Big Apple”, “ALS”).

Weaknesses
1) Incremental novelty and limited gains from “joint” training: Several results show that simple approximations (averaging embeddings of representative phrases) match or exceed the direct concept embeddings; β/η often tune to zero; and most benefits appear driven by high-quality phrase forms rather than the concept-learning mechanism itself. This weakens the core claim that the proposed joint objective substantively improves concept representations beyond strong baselines.
2) Evaluation scope and potential overfitting: The work relies almost entirely on intrinsic similarity/relatedness evaluations, and hyperparameters are tuned on the same test sets (albeit with a cross-validation scheme that may still leak signal). No downstream extrinsic tasks (e.g., concept normalization, entity linking, document classification, ICD coding assistance) are reported, limiting evidence that the learned concept embeddings capture usable semantics beyond correlation with human similarity ratings.
3) Noise from distant supervision and lack of disambiguation: The assumption that any occurrence of a phrase indicates any concept it might represent, with gradient updates divided uniformly across concepts, introduces substantial ambiguity. There is no context-based disambiguation or multi-instance learning to mitigate this, and no analysis stratified by phrase ambiguity to quantify the impact on performance.
4) New dataset quality and representativeness: The entity similarity/relatedness dataset has relatively low inter-annotator agreement (Fleiss’ κ=0.24), small size (688 pairs), and manual pair selection that may bias distributions. This limits the dataset’s utility for fine-grained comparison and may inflate apparent performance (e.g., by over-representing easy pairs).
5) Missing comparisons and ablations: For YAGO entities, comparisons to contemporary entity embedding methods that leverage Wikipedia (e.g., neural entity/linking-based embeddings) are absent. Ablations isolating the contributions of: (i) dual-corpus training (tagged vs untagged), (ii) the β/η regularizers, and (iii) sharing the negative sampling matrix are insufficient to establish where improvements arise.

Questions to authors
- What is the quantitative added value of the joint objective over using phrase embeddings (and their averages) alone? Please provide ablations showing performance deltas for: only tagged corpus, only untagged, no β/η, and no concept updates (phrases only).
- How ambiguous are phrase-to-concept mappings in practice (e.g., distribution of |Cp|)? Can you report performance stratified by phrase ambiguity and frequency?
- Clarify the negative sampling/objective: Do concepts/phrases predict only word contexts? Is the “shared negative sampling matrix” the only coupling across modalities when β=η=0? If so, can you justify why this is sufficient to align spaces?
- Could you evaluate on at least one downstream task (e.g., concept normalization to UMLS in clinical notes, or Wikipedia entity linking) to demonstrate extrinsic utility?
- For the new dataset, can you report per-pair variance or confidence intervals, and release detailed guidelines/instructions? Are there plans to expand it and/or create standardized train/dev/test splits?
- Ethical/IRB details for the clinical notes: were they de-identified and approved for this use?

Decision
Weak reject. The paper addresses an important problem with a practical, annotation-light approach, and reports competitive intrinsic results with useful analyses and resources. However, the central methodological contribution appears incremental, with limited evidence that the joint concept objective substantially improves over strong phrase-based baselines; evaluation is confined to intrinsic tasks with potential overfitting; and ambiguity handling is rudimentary. Strengthening the paper with clearer ablations, context-aware disambiguation or ontology-structure-aware training, and at least one downstream task would significantly improve its impact.
