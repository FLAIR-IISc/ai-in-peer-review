Summary and contributions
- Proposes a weakly supervised method to jointly embed ontology concepts, their surface phrases, and words into a shared vector space by adapting SGNS: replace recognized multiword phrases with IDs, learn separate input embeddings for words/phrases/concepts, and update concept vectors using contexts of any of its surface forms, without explicit sense disambiguation. Adds simple compositionality regularizers that encourage phrase≈mean(words) and concept≈mean(phrases).
- Provides a large-scale instantiation for UMLS (biomedical) and YAGO (general domain), and evaluates on biomedical similarity/relatedness (MayoSRS, UMNSRS) and a newly collected entity similarity/relatedness dataset.
- Offers analyses of compositionality and semantic-type clustering; releases code and a new dataset.

Strengths
- Practical, scalable weak supervision: The DS-style assumption (any mapped surface form triggers all candidate concepts) allows training concept embeddings without manual concept annotations or KB relation triples. This is attractive for domains where curated relation structure is incomplete or unavailable.
- Joint space across words, phrases, concepts: The design makes it easy to use any of the three as features and to compute cross-level similarities (e.g., phrase–concept), which is valuable for downstream concept normalization or entity linking.
- Competitive performance with minimal supervision: On UMNSRS Similarity, concept embeddings are on par with strong baselines that rely on curated labels (e.g., ICD9 sequences), and phrase-based approximations perform strongly across tasks. Results on the new general-domain dataset are also solid.
- Clear ablations against intuitive baselines: The comparisons to word-only, phrase-only, and approximations (averaging words or phrases) are appropriate and reveal an important empirical finding: approximating concepts via their phrases is often as strong as direct concept embeddings.
- Useful analyses and resources: The compositionality and clustering analyses provide insight into how information propagates across levels; releasing cui2vec and an entity similarity/relatedness dataset is a community contribution.

Weaknesses
- Limited novelty relative to prior joint text–KB embedding: Conceptually close to earlier joint models (e.g., Wang et al., 2014a; Toutanova et al., 2015), but drops KB relations and supervision, relying on DS to map text to entities. The main methodological novelty—equal updates to all concepts for an ambiguous phrase and a shared output (negative sampling) matrix—is a relatively small tweak to SGNS. A stronger positioning vs. prior joint text–entity models (including text-only components) is needed.
- No explicit disambiguation; strong DS assumption: Assigning every ambiguous mention equally to all candidate concepts is crude. While scalable, it injects substantial noise. There is no analysis of how ambiguity (polysemy of phrases, number of candidate concepts) affects performance. More principled alternatives (latent concept selection, context-conditioned soft attention over candidates, or priors from mention–concept frequency) could be feasible and likely beneficial.
- Regularization design is ad hoc and one-sided: The β, η terms use logσ(p·mean(words)) and logσ(c·mean(phrases)). This asymmetrically pushes phrases/concepts toward averages without reciprocal constraints or distance penalties. L2 regularization (||p−mean(w)||^2, ||c−mean(p)||^2), contrastive terms, or multi-view alignment could offer a clearer inductive bias. The paper notes performance often degrades as β/η increase, but lacks a systematic ablation table and discussion of why.
- Evaluation concerns and limited downstream evidence: Hyperparameters are tuned on the same intrinsic datasets (despite a cross-validation scheme), which risks overfitting. No downstream tasks (e.g., entity linking, concept normalization, relation extraction) are reported, despite the method being motivated by such use-cases. The entity similarity/relatedness dataset has low IAA (κ≈0.24), limiting conclusions in the general domain.
- Clarity and notation issues: The training description appears to conflate symbols (k is both window size and number of negative samples); ENS is described as |W|×k (likely |W|×d). It is not fully clear where context/output embeddings live (ENS vs a standard output matrix) and whether phrases/concepts ever serve as contexts. Implementation details (sampling strategy across TU/TT, handling overlaps, phrase boundary conditions) would benefit from more precision.

Questions to authors
- Ambiguity handling: How often are phrases ambiguous (distribution of |Cp|)? Can you report performance stratified by phrase ambiguity or concept lexicalization breadth? Have you tried weighting concept updates by priors (e.g., link probabilities or surface-form frequency per concept) or context-conditioned softmax over candidate concepts?
- Objective and coupling: Do phrases/concepts ever appear as context “o,” or is the output space strictly word contexts? If the latter, did you try a shared output matrix for words/phrases/concepts and letting phrases/concepts also predict neighboring words to tighten coupling? Please clarify the ENS dimensions and notation.
- β/η ablations: Can you provide a concise table showing the effect of β and η (0 vs non-zero) across datasets, and an alternative L2 penalty formulation? The current σ(dot) regularizers may be poorly calibrated.
- Fair baselines and data: For biomedical comparisons, how do coverage and domain match compare to De Vine, Choi, and Mencia (e.g., concept set overlap, corpus type)? Could you include a simple “phrase ID only” model (train only PHRASEco on TT) to isolate the gain from concept updates?
- Downstream tasks: Have you evaluated on concept normalization/entity linking (e.g., mapping mentions to CUIs) or semantic type prediction, where joint embeddings should help? Even small-scale evidence would strengthen the paper.

Additional comments and suggestions
- Consider a latent-variable DS setup (EM or noise-contrastive training) that samples a single concept per ambiguous mention proportional to p(c|context), or an attention mechanism over candidate concepts conditioned on context words.
- Explore using ontology structure as a lightweight additional signal (parent–child/type constraints) without relying on full KB relation triples; e.g., a regularizer encouraging proximity of a concept to its semantic type centroid.
- Provide an error analysis contrasting cases where CONCEPTco beats APPROXPHRco and vice versa; this would clarify when direct concept embeddings add value beyond averaging phrase vectors.
- Release detailed preprocessing (greedy longest-match, tokenization, min frequency) and mapping statistics to facilitate replication.

Overall: A practical and well-executed weakly supervised approach with useful resources and analyses. Methodological novelty is moderate and evaluation could be stronger/more controlled, but the paper is likely of interest to ACL given the scalability and cross-level embedding design.
