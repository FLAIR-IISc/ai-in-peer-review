Summary
The paper proposes a weakly supervised method to jointly embed concepts (ontology IDs), their surface phrases, and words into a shared vector space. The approach uses ontology-derived mappings between concepts and phrases as distant supervision and adapts skip-gram with negative sampling to update embeddings for words, phrases (phrases replaced as single tokens), and concepts (updated using contexts of their phrase occurrences). Two hyperparameters, β and η, regularize compositionality between phrases and their words and between concepts and their phrases. Experiments on biomedical corpora (UMLS with PubMed and clinical notes) and a general-domain corpus (YAGO with Wikipedia+NYT) evaluate similarity/relatedness via Spearman correlation, compare to prior concept and word embeddings, and analyze compositionality and semantic-type clustering. The authors also release a new crowdsourced dataset of similarity and relatedness for real-world entities.

Main contributions (as assessed)
1) A simple, scalable weakly supervised method to jointly learn embeddings for concepts, phrases, and words using only ontology mappings and raw text, with optional compositionality regularization (β, η). The formulation generalizes SGNS to support concept updates from phrase contexts without human concept annotations.
2) An empirical study across biomedical and general domains showing competitive performance on concept similarity (especially on larger biomedical datasets), plus analyses indicating that ontology structure (concept–phrase affinity and semantic-type clustering) is partially reflected in the learned space.
3) A new dataset of human judgments of similarity and relatedness between real-world entities (identified by Wikipedia pages and phrases).

Strengths
- Clear problem motivation and method design. The paper clearly articulates the need to model concepts beyond word-level representations and presents a straightforward training objective, preprocessing, and hyperparameterization. Equations and training details (e.g., phrase replacement, handling multiple concept mappings, normalization) are sufficiently described to understand and reimplement.

- Scalable and annotation-light. The method requires only ontology-derived phrase–concept mappings and raw text, avoiding manual concept annotation or reliance on knowledge-graph relation structure. It scales to large vocabularies (hundreds of thousands of concepts/phrases) across two domains.

- Competitive intrinsic performance. On larger biomedical similarity datasets (UMNSRS), jointly learned concept embeddings achieve top or near-top Spearman’s ρ; phrase-based approximations also perform strongly. Results are competitive with approaches that leverage human annotations (e.g., claims codes), indicating the effectiveness of distant supervision.

- Analyses that probe the learned space. The compositionality and concept–phrase proximity analyses, as well as semantic-type clustering, provide insight into what the model captures. The observed alignment between concepts and their phrases, and evidence of clustering by semantic type, support the claim that the embeddings encode ontology-consistent structure.

- Resource contribution. Releasing an implementation (cui2vec) and a new entity similarity/relatedness dataset increases the work’s practical value and potential impact.

Weaknesses
- Limited novelty relative to prior art. While the joint training of concepts, phrases, and words with weak supervision is useful, the core idea builds on established SGNS, distant supervision assumptions (Mintz et al.), and prior concept/entity embedding techniques (e.g., De Vine; Choi; Wang et al.). The compositionality regularizers are simple alignment terms. The contribution is more in combining known ideas effectively than introducing a fundamentally new modeling paradigm.

- Mixed empirical gains and limited scope of evaluation. The method does not consistently outperform strong baselines across all datasets (e.g., CHOI on smaller biomedical sets), and in the general domain the phrase-based approximation sometimes exceeds direct concept embeddings. There is no extrinsic evaluation (e.g., entity linking, concept normalization, retrieval), which would strengthen claims about usefulness beyond intrinsic correlation.

- Tuning and evaluation protocol. Hyperparameters are tuned on the same similarity/relatedness datasets used for reporting (via adapted cross-validation), raising concerns about overfitting and optimistic estimates. No results with held-out dev/test splits or statistical significance across random seeds are reported.

- Assumptions and noise handling. The assumption that any occurrence of a mapped phrase denotes all possible associated concepts (uniformly) introduces ambiguity noise. There is no weighting by concept priors or disambiguation mechanism. Phrase matching is greedy and frequency-thresholded, which may bias toward frequent surface forms and miss rare but salient ones. These choices may limit concept specificity and contribute to the high effectiveness of phrase-based approximations.

- Quality of the new entity dataset. The reported inter-annotator agreement is modest (κ=0.24, “fair”), which may limit the dataset’s discriminative power. Details on how entity ambiguity and aliasing were handled are provided, but the lack of gold splits and low agreement complicate benchmarking.

Questions to Authors (for rebuttal)
- Negative sampling and contexts: Are negative samples drawn only from the word vocabulary (ENS = |W| × k)? Do concept and phrase losses use only word contexts, or are phrase contexts considered beyond word-level? Please clarify how ENS is shared and whether you explored negative sampling from phrases/concepts.

- Ambiguity handling: For phrases mapped to multiple concepts, you normalize updates uniformly. Did you try weighting by concept priors (e.g., frequency of phrase–concept within ontology or corpus) or employing simple disambiguation heuristics? Can you report an ablation comparing uniform vs. weighted updates?

- Regularizer ablations: You note trends for β and η in the supplement. Can you provide systematic ablations (β=0 vs >0; η=0 vs >0) on the main tables to quantify their contribution and whether they help beyond phrase/word baselines?

- Robustness and significance: Do results hold across multiple random seeds? Are differences with baselines statistically significant (e.g., via bootstrap over pairs)?

- Extrinsic evaluation: Have you tested the embeddings on downstream tasks (e.g., biomedical concept normalization, entity linking, relation classification)? Even one extrinsic task would substantiate practical value.

- Phrase identification: How are overlapping or nested phrases resolved beyond greedy longest match? What proportion of phrase tokens are replaced in each corpus, and how sensitive are results to the minimum frequency threshold (25)?

- Entity dataset: Can you provide train/dev/test splits and guidance for future use? Are there steps to improve agreement (e.g., expert annotation subset), and can you report per-category agreement to assess where disagreement concentrates?

Overall, this is a clear and technically sound application of weak supervision for joint concept/phrase/word embeddings, with competitive intrinsic results and useful resources. The main limitations are modest novelty, mixed gains, and evaluation protocol concerns.
