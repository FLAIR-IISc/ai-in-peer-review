Summary and main contributions
- A weakly supervised method to jointly embed concepts (ontology nodes), their surface phrases, and words in a shared space by adapting skip-gram/negative sampling. The core idea is to use ontology-provided phrase-to-concept mappings as distant supervision over raw text, updating concept vectors whenever any of their surface forms occur in context.
- Practical, scalable instantiation across two domains (biomedical with UMLS; general domain with YAGO), with large corpora and vocabularies, minimal annotation, and two compositionality regularizers (β phrase-by-words, η concept-by-phrases) to balance independence vs. compositionality.
- Resources and evaluation: release of cui2vec implementation and a new human-labeled similarity/relatedness dataset for real-world entities; analyses showing concept–phrase proximity and clustering by semantic types; competitive performance on intrinsic similarity/relatedness vs. supervised/KB-driven baselines.

Strengths
- Novel and bold problem framing: unifying words, phrases, and ontology-level concepts in one SGNS training loop using only distant supervision from phrase–concept links. This bridges word-centric and KB-centric embeddings without relying on incomplete KB relations. The formulation is simple yet impactful: replace surface forms with IDs, run parallel tagged/untagged streams, and update concepts through phrase contexts.
- Annotation-free scalability and breadth: Trained on billions of tokens with >190k UMLS concepts and ~750k YAGO entities/phrases under a single framework. This is a practical contribution beyond many prior concept-embedding works that depend on human annotation (e.g., codes, links) or restricted concept sets.
- Competitive empirical results with minimal supervision: On the larger biomedical sets (UMNSRS), concept embeddings match or exceed strong baselines on similarity (ρ up to .653; CHIU-30 tied on similarity), and are close on relatedness. In the general domain, the jointly learned space yields strong correlations for phrases/concepts (APPROXPHRco up to .810/.766), demonstrating transferability beyond biomedicine.
- Useful analyses of the joint space: The paper probes compositionality (concept–phrase and phrase–word similarities), rank-based retrieval of child phrases/words, and semantic-type clustering. Findings (e.g., bimodality for phrase–word MAP, concept–phrase proximity, and partially successful type clustering) provide insights into what the model learns and where it falls short.
- Resource contribution: The new entity similarity/relatedness datasets and the cui2vec implementation are valuable to the community, which lacks entity-level similarity benchmarks. Even with modest agreement, having this resource is beneficial and encourages further work.

Weaknesses
- Limited novelty beyond prior joint text–KB work in some aspects: While the supervision source differs, the high-level goal resembles Wang et al. (2014a) and related methods that jointly embed words and entities. The compositionality regularizers (β, η) are incremental adaptations. Clarifying the conceptual advance over these lines (beyond not using KB relations) would strengthen the novelty claim.
- Heavy reliance on intrinsic evaluations with tuning on the test sets: Despite a cross-validation-style tuning protocol, the intrinsic metrics (Spearman ρ on similarity/relatedness) remain susceptible to overfitting and known criticisms. The absence of extrinsic tasks (e.g., entity linking, concept normalization, clinical prediction, retrieval) leaves unclear whether the joint space confers practical gains.
- Concept layer appears partially redundant: In general-domain experiments, phrase-based concept approximations often outperform direct concept vectors, and compositionality analyses show concepts are extremely similar to averages of their phrases/words. This suggests the model is not capturing additional non-lexical concept information; the authors themselves point to potentially integrating ontology structure, which is currently unused.
- Ambiguity and noise are not explicitly handled: Polysemous phrases mapped to multiple concepts are treated by averaging updates across candidates, which likely injects noise in both training and evaluation. There is no attempt at disambiguation (contextual routing) or uncertainty modeling, and the greedy phrase replacement may introduce tagging errors. Quantifying the impact of ambiguity would help.
- New dataset agreement is low (κ=0.24): While entity similarity is challenging, the modest agreement raises concerns about the reliability and difficulty of the benchmark. More details on calibration, rater training, and robust aggregation could improve confidence, as would reporting inter-rater reliability per relation type and conducting significance tests across systems.

Questions to authors
- Novelty/positioning: Can you more sharply differentiate your contributions from Wang et al. (2014a) and De Vine et al. (2014)? In particular, what unique capabilities emerge from your distant supervision setup and joint phrase–concept–word training that those cannot offer?
- Ambiguity handling: How often do phrases map to multiple concepts in your training data? Did you try context-based soft assignment or EM-style disambiguation instead of uniform normalization across concepts? Any ablation quantifying the effect on performance?
- Negative sampling architecture: ENS is described as |W| × k and “shared.” Please clarify how negative samples are drawn and parameterized for words/phrases/concepts. Are output embeddings shared across all three or only tied to words? How does this choice affect information sharing?
- β and η ablations: Could you report a clearer ablation grid showing how β and η affect both intrinsic metrics and the compositionality/cluster analyses? Do the optimal values differ systematically by domain or ontology properties?
- Extrinsic validation: Have you tested the joint space on entity linking/normalization, UMLS CUI prediction, or document-level tasks (e.g., clinical outcome prediction) to validate usefulness beyond intrinsic ranking? If not, could you outline which tasks you expect to benefit most and why?
- Resource details: Will you release the exact phrase–concept lexicons used, the preprocessed corpora statistics, and the general-domain embeddings? For the new datasets, can you provide per-item variance and category-wise agreement to aid interpretation?
