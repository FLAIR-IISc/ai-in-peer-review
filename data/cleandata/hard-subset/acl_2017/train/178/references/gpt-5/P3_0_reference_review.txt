Summary and main contributions
- The paper proposes a weakly supervised skip-gram-style method to jointly embed ontology concepts, their surface phrases, and words into a shared space. Occurrences of known phrases in raw text are used as distant supervision for concept updates; words and phrases predict word contexts, and concepts are updated from the contexts of their surface forms. Two regularizers (β, η) encourage phrase-word and concept-phrase compositionality.
- The approach is instantiated on UMLS (biomedical) and YAGO (general entities), trained on large corpora (PubMed, clinical notes, Wikipedia+NYT). Evaluations include standard biomedical similarity/relatedness datasets and a new crowd-sourced entity similarity/relatedness dataset. Additional analyses probe concept–phrase/phrase–word neighborhood structure and semantic type clustering.
- The authors release code (cui2vec) and a novel entity similarity/relatedness dataset.

Strengths
- Scalable, practical weak supervision: The method needs only a lexicon mapping phrases to concept IDs (no document-level concept annotations), yet can train large vocabularies from massive corpora. This fills a practical niche between purely structural KB embeddings and costly human-annotated concept corpora.
- Competitive intrinsic results in biomedicine: On UMNSRS Similarity (the largest biomedical set), the jointly trained concept embeddings achieve the top Spearman ρ among compared methods, while being competitive on relatedness versus strong word baselines (CHIU-30). This suggests the approach captures taxonomic/definitional similarity better than off-the-shelf word vectors and some prior concept embeddings trained on clinical codes or MeSH.
- Cross-domain applicability: The same training recipe works for UMLS and YAGO, indicating generality across abstract concepts and real-world entities. The analysis further shows some reflection of semantic type structure in the learned space.
- Careful, multifaceted analysis: Beyond headline ρ values, the paper studies compositionality via cosine to averaged children, retrieval of phrases/words given parents (MAP distributions), and semantic type clustering. These insights are valuable for diagnosing where concept- versus phrase-level supervision helps.
- Resource contribution: The new entity similarity/relatedness datasets (with details on collection and quality control) and code release increase the community value of the work.

Weaknesses
- Limited novelty relative to prior joint text–KB embedding: The core idea—use text contexts to tie entity/concept representations to words—overlaps with earlier work (e.g., Wang et al., 2014a; Toutanova et al., 2015), which jointly embed entities and words via textual co-occurrence while leveraging KB structure. This submission does not exploit KB triples/graph structure, relying only on alias mappings, which reduces methodological novelty. The compositionality regularizers (β,η) are simple dot-product proximity terms and, according to tuning, often best set to zero, further weakening the claimed methodological contribution.
- Concept embeddings add little beyond phrases: On general entities, APPROXPHRco (averaging phrase embeddings) outperforms direct concept embeddings; in biomedicine, phrase-based approximations are often very close to concept performance. The compositionality analysis similarly shows concepts are extremely close to the mean of their phrases. This suggests that the extra “concept layer” may not capture non-lexical knowledge beyond what phrase vectors already encode from context.
- No disambiguation of polysemous phrases: The distant supervision assumption treats each mention of a surface form as an occurrence of all its possible concepts (updates normalized across senses). This introduces substantial label noise, especially in YAGO, and the model provides no mechanism to resolve senses using local context (e.g., latent concept assignment, attention, or EM). An ablation on unambiguous phrases or a soft disambiguation variant would clarify the impact of this noise.
- Evaluation and tuning concerns: Hyperparameters are tuned on the same test sets via an “adapted 5-fold” scheme that still uses the full set for selection; the authors acknowledge this. This risks overfitting the intrinsic evaluations. Moreover, the new entity datasets have low agreement (Fleiss’ κ≈0.24) and lack comparisons to strong Wikipedia entity baselines (e.g., hyperlink-based entity embeddings, RDF2Vec, TransE/ComplEx on Wikidata/YAGO), limiting the conclusiveness of the general-domain results.
- Objective and training clarity: The paper states that information is shared “only via the shared negative sample embedding,” yet all three inputs (words/phrases/concepts) predict word contexts using a shared output space, which itself couples them. The parameterization (input vs output embeddings) and negative sampling vocabulary (words only?) should be clarified. The regularization terms logσ(p·Wp) and logσ(c·Pc) lack margins/negatives; it is unclear how they interact with the main SGNS loss and whether they risk collapse or length effects. Some notation issues (e.g., Eq. 4 Cs vs Cp) impede clarity.

Questions to authors
- Clarify parameterization: Do you maintain separate input and output (context) embeddings as in SGNS? Is ENS the output matrix for words only? When training phrases/concepts, are negatives sampled from the word vocabulary only?
- How frequent is phrase–concept ambiguity in your training lexicons (UMLS/YAGO) after frequency filtering? Can you report performance when restricting to unambiguous surface forms or when weighting concepts by prior (if available)?
- Why does APPROXPHRco outperform CONCEPTco on general entities? Did you examine whether concepts with many aliases are hurt by averaging noisy contexts via the DS assumption?
- Coverage/OOV: What fraction of pairs in each evaluation set are covered by your embeddings, and how are OOV cases handled? Are reported ρ computed on the intersection across systems or per-system coverage?
- Comparisons to entity embedding baselines: Can you include results with common Wikipedia/YAGO entity embeddings (e.g., RDF2Vec, TransE/ComplEx trained on YAGO/Wikidata, or hyperlink-based entity vectors) on your new datasets?
- Regularizers: Since best β,η are often zero, can you provide ablations showing when they help/hurt and whether alternative formulations (e.g., margin losses, temperature scaling, or length normalization) improve stability?
- Runtime/memory: What are the training time and memory footprints for the largest settings? How sensitive are results to the min-frequency threshold of 25?

Additional comments/suggestions
- Incorporate ontology structure (hierarchies, relations) as additional training signals (e.g., retrofitting, graph contrastive losses), which may inject non-lexical knowledge and reduce the reliance on noisy DS.
- Explore a latent sense assignment for ambiguous phrases (e.g., context-dependent soft attention over candidate concepts), or multi-instance learning to mitigate DS noise.
- Add extrinsic evaluations where concept/entity embeddings matter (e.g., biomedical coding similarity, entity linking, concept normalization), and avoid tuning on test-only intrinsic benchmarks.
