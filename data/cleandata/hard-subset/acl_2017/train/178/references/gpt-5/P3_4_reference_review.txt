Summary and contributions
This paper proposes a weakly supervised method to jointly embed words, phrases, and ontology concepts into a single vector space. The key idea is to use ontology-provided mappings from concepts to multiple representative strings (phrases) as distant supervision: occurrences of mapped phrases in a large corpus are treated as indirect mentions of all concepts they can realize, and concept embeddings are trained from their local word contexts. The model adapts skip-gram with negative sampling, uses separate input embeddings for words/phrases/concepts and a shared output (context) embedding matrix over words, and introduces two hyperparameters to softly enforce compositionality from words→phrases and phrases→concepts. The approach is evaluated on biomedical concept similarity/relatedness (UMLS) and on a new crowdsourced dataset of entity similarity/relatedness (YAGO), with analyses of compositionality and semantic-type clustering.

Contributions (as I see them)
1) A simple, scalable joint training procedure that learns concept embeddings without human mention annotation or KB relation structure, by leveraging only concept–phrase mappings and raw text.
2) A thorough intrinsic evaluation across domains (biomedical and general), including comparisons to strong biomedical baselines and analyses of compositionality and semantic-type clustering.
3) A new dataset of human similarity/relatedness judgments for real-world entities, and the release of software (cui2vec).

Strengths
- Practical, annotation-light joint embedding of words, phrases, and concepts: The distant supervision setup (greedy phrase replacement + shared context over words) is straightforward and scalable to very large ontologies (hundreds of thousands of concepts) and corpora. This addresses an important bottleneck in concept/entity representation where mention-level labels or KB relations are often required.

- Competitive biomedical performance at scale: On the larger UMNSRS Similarity set, concept embeddings outperform prior concept models (De Vine, Mencia) and are competitive with strong word baselines (CHIU-30). This is notable given no reliance on manually annotated codes or KB relations.

- Solid analytical insights: The compositionality analyses are informative (e.g., concepts are very close to the mean of their phrase embeddings; phrases show a bimodal relationship with their words), and the semantic-type clustering comparison offers a nuanced view (mixed results, with notable gaps for “sign or symptom”).

- Cross-domain coverage and resources: Demonstrating applicability to both UMLS and YAGO is valuable; the entity similarity/relatedness dataset and released tooling add community value.

- Clear articulation of modeling trade-offs: The explicit β and η knobs illuminate the tension between treating phrases/concepts as atomic vs. compositional. The observation that larger β/η often hurt similarity performance is an interesting, honest finding.

Weaknesses
- Limited novelty in modeling and weak coupling across levels: The core training is a modest adaptation of skip-gram; the “joint” aspect relies primarily on sharing the context (output) word matrix and having phrases/concepts predict word contexts. Except for β/η regularizers (which often were tuned to 0), there is little mechanism to align words, phrases, and concepts beyond co-occurrence with the same word contexts. Compared to prior joint models (e.g., Wang et al., Toutanova et al.), this is conceptually simpler but also less expressive.

- Distant supervision assumption is very coarse: Treating any occurrence of an ambiguous phrase as an occurrence of each possible concept, with uniform normalization, injects substantial noise. No attempt is made to estimate sense priors, perform simple heuristics (e.g., type constraints, domain filtering), or use EM/attention to infer latent concept assignments. This likely limits concept specificity and may explain why phrase-based approximations sometimes outperform direct concept embeddings.

- Evaluation design issues limit conclusiveness: The intrinsic evaluations rely on hyperparameter tuning over the same test sets; although an adapted 5-fold procedure is described, it still risks overfitting and does not provide clean held-out generalization. The new entity dataset has low agreement (κ=0.24) and lacks comparisons to strong general-domain entity baselines (e.g., KG embeddings like TransE/ComplEx trained on YAGO/Wikipedia, or entity-focused distributional methods). Consequently, it is hard to precisely quantify the benefit of the proposed approach for real-world entities.

- Objective design choices under-specified/atypical: The paper uses a single “negative sampling” output matrix over words for all prediction tasks. It is unclear whether the context vectors (~o) come from a dedicated output embedding space or are tied to input word embeddings. ENS is described as |W|×k (k overloaded), and equations conflate Cp/Cs notation. The β/η terms optimize logσ(dot with a mean), which can saturate and is not a symmetric proximity objective; more standard L2 or cosine penalties might behave better.

- Mixed and modest gains relative to strong baselines: On MayoSRS/Mini Mayo SRS, CHOI’s code-based embeddings substantially outperform the proposed method. In general-domain tasks, phrase-based approximations outperform direct concept embeddings, suggesting the method adds limited value beyond high-quality phrase representations.

Questions to authors
1) Clarify the output/context side: Are context word vectors separate from input word vectors (as in standard skip-gram)? Is ENS the output matrix? If so, why is it called “negative sampling matrix” and typed as |W|×k? Please reconcile notation (k vs d) and define where ~o resides.

2) How are negative samples drawn (unigram^3/4, domain-specific distributions)? Are they always words? Do phrases/concepts ever serve as contexts or negatives?

3) Phrase → concept ambiguity: Did you try non-uniform priors (e.g., proportional to phrase–concept frequency in the ontology, or learned via EM)? Any simple disambiguation heuristics (type filtering in local context) and their effects?

4) β/η regularization: Why use logσ(~p·Wp) instead of an explicit distance penalty between ~p and the mean of its words (and likewise for concepts)? Did you compare alternative penalties or annealing strategies?

5) Entity baselines: Can you include comparisons against KG-based entity embeddings trained on YAGO (TransE/DistMult/ComplEx) and/or Wikipedia entity linking-based embeddings? This would better contextualize general-domain results.

6) Coverage and preprocessing: What fraction of concepts have at least one surface form appearing ≥25 times, and how does the greedy longest-match phrase replacement affect overlapping mentions? Any statistics on ambiguity (avg |Cp|) and its correlation with performance?

7) Reproducibility and resources: Will the new entity similarity/relatedness dataset and trained embeddings be released? For the clinical corpus, can you share counts/statistics or synthetic surrogates to facilitate replication?

Overall, the paper targets an important practical problem with a simple, scalable solution and provides useful analyses and resources. However, the modeling novelty is limited, some design choices need clarification, and evaluations (especially in the general domain) could be strengthened.
