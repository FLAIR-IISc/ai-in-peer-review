Summary and main contributions
- Proposes a weakly supervised variant of SGNS to jointly embed words, phrases, and ontology concepts into a shared space by replacing matched ontology phrases in text with identifiers and updating concept vectors from the contexts of their surface forms. Introduces two hyperparameters to softly encourage compositionality (phrase≈avg(words), concept≈avg(phrases)).
- Provides large-scale training across PubMed abstracts, clinical notes, and Wikipedia+NYT without per-mention annotation; evaluates on biomedical similarity/relatedness datasets and introduces a small crowdsourced entity similarity/relatedness dataset.
- Presents analyses of compositionality, concept–phrase/phrase–word neighborhood structure, and semantic type clustering.

Strengths
- Scalable weak supervision: The distant-supervision formulation leverages ontology mappings without manual per-mention labels and scales across very large corpora and vocabularies (hundreds of thousands of concepts/phrases), which is practically valuable.
- Unified space for words–phrases–concepts: Learning all three in one space is appealing for applications that must bridge surface forms and canonical identifiers. The architecture allows both independent concept representations and optional compositional constraints (β, η).
- Empirical breadth: Multiple corpora (biomedical and general-domain) and multiple evaluation angles (intrinsic similarity/relatedness, semantic type clustering, compositionality diagnostics) provide a reasonably comprehensive picture.
- Competitive intrinsic results without human annotation: On UMNSRS Similarity the concept embeddings are on par with strong word baselines (e.g., CHIU-30) and better than some prior concept-only models trained with manual signals; the general-domain phrase-level approximations perform strongly.

Weaknesses
- Methodological and notation issues in the core model description:
  - The “negative sampling matrix” ENS is defined as |W|×k, where k is the number of negative samples. This is dimensionally incorrect; output/context embeddings must be |W|×d. It is unclear whether ~o and ~n are drawn from EW or an output matrix; the text says “shared negative sample embedding matrix” but does not cleanly specify input/output partitions.
  - Equation inconsistencies: Eq. (1) conditions on (w,c) yet the text defines o as the context word; Eq. (4) uses Cs (not defined previously; likely Cp); variable overloading for k (window size vs #negatives) and inconsistent symbols (o vs c) make the objective ambiguous.
  - Lack of essential training details for reproducibility: negative sampling distribution (e.g., unigram^3/4?), subsampling of frequent tokens, dynamic/context windowing, optimizer and schedule beyond a learning rate mentioned in the supplement, handling of OOV/UNK, casing/morphology, and whether phrase replacements are case-insensitive and tokenization-robust.
  - The only mechanism to “share information” across levels is the output/negative-sample side; with β,η mostly tuned to 0 (Table 3, supplement), the spaces are effectively decoupled. This weakens the core “joint” contribution.
- Ambiguous phrase handling is too naive and potentially harmful: Each mention of an ambiguous phrase p updates all concepts in Cp equally (uniform 1/|Cp|). This ignores strong priors over P(c|p) and context-dependent disambiguation, likely smearing concept vectors. There is no analysis of concept ambiguity rates, no ablations with non-uniform priors, and no measurement of how ambiguity affects performance.
- Limited and sometimes unfavorable gains over strong baselines:
  - On Mayo SRS/Mini Mayo SRS, prior work (CHOI) substantially outperforms; on UMNSRS Similarity, gains over CHIU-30 are essentially tied (0.653 vs 0.653). In the general domain, the best performance comes from APPROXPHRco rather than direct concept embeddings, undermining the benefit of learning explicit concept vectors.
  - Hyperparameters are tuned on the test sets via “adapted 5-fold CV,” and only PM+CL results are reported in Table 4 (“PUBMED and CLINICAL are similar” without numbers). This raises concerns about overfitting and selective reporting.
  - No significance testing across methods.
- Evaluation design concerns:
  - The new entity similarity/relatedness dataset is small (688 pairs), with low inter-annotator agreement (Fleiss’ κ=0.24). Pair selection was manual, and many annotators did only one HIT; this limits reliability. There is no public release statement or detailed quality checks beyond simple validation items.
  - Intrinsic tasks are known to be problematic proxies. No downstream evaluations (e.g., entity linking, concept normalization) where the proposed joint space should shine are provided.
- Clinical data/ethics and preprocessing details are missing:
  - Use of clinical notes requires explicit statements on de-identification and IRB approval; none are provided.
  - Greedy longest-match replacement is underspecified: handling of tokenization discrepancies, punctuation, case, overlapping/nested mentions, and multiword variants is unclear. The minimum frequency threshold of 25 excludes many medical concepts; no coverage analysis is provided.

Questions to Authors
- Please clarify the training objective precisely:
  - What are the input vs output embeddings for words, phrases, and concepts? Is there a distinct output matrix of size |W|×d for context words? If so, why is ENS described as |W|×k instead of |W|×d?
  - In Eqs. (1)–(7), which matrices are used to obtain ~o and ~n? Are words the only items with output vectors? Please correct the variable inconsistencies (o vs c; Cs vs Cp; k reused).
- What negative sampling distribution and subsampling strategy did you use? Fixed vs dynamic window? Any smoothing (e.g., 3/4 power) and down-weighting of very frequent words/phrases?
- How prevalent is phrase ambiguity (avg |Cp|, distribution tails)? Can you report performance when weighting concept updates by a prior P(c|p) (e.g., from surface form frequency or link statistics) or when filtering highly ambiguous phrases?
- Why do β and η tune mostly to zero? Do stronger compositional constraints help in low-data regimes or for specific concept types?
- The general-domain results show APPROXPHRco > CONCEPTco. Under what conditions are explicit concept vectors preferable to phrase-level approximations? Any diagnostic explaining the gap?
- Can you provide full results (including PUBMED-only and CLINICAL-only) and statistical significance tests? Also report coverage (#concepts evaluated) across datasets.
- For the clinical corpus, please state de-identification procedures, IRB approval, and data handling. 
- Will code, trained embeddings, and the new entity similarity/relatedness dataset be released to enable replication?
