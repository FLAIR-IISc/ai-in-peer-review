Summary and contributions
- Proposes a weakly supervised method to jointly embed words, phrases, and ontology concepts (UMLS/YAGO) using SGNS over unannotated text, where phrase mentions are used as distant supervision for their possible concepts; adds optional compositionality regularizers tying phrases to the average of their words (β) and concepts to the average of their phrases (η).
- Introduces a new human-annotated dataset for real-world entity similarity and relatedness (people/places/organizations).
- Provides analyses of concept–phrase/phrase–word compositionality and semantic type clustering.

Strengths
- Practicality and scalability: The approach uses only ontology phrase lists and large unannotated corpora, without manual concept annotations. It scales to hundreds of thousands of concepts (Table 1), and shows competitive performance with methods trained on costly annotations (Table 4).
- Joint space and resource release: A single vector space for words/phrases/concepts is useful for downstream tasks such as entity linking or query understanding. The commitment to release code (cui2vec) and a new entity similarity/relatedness dataset is valuable for the community.
- Competitive intrinsic results: On UMNSRS Similarity, CONCEPTco ties or beats strong baselines and approaches the high-window word baseline (CHIU-30). Phrase-based variants perform very well on the new entity datasets (Table 5).
- Cross-domain evaluation and analysis: Evaluations span biomedical and general domains, with additional analyses of compositionality (Figures 3,4) and semantic type clustering (Table 6), providing a reasonably rounded view of the learned spaces.

Weaknesses
- Serious notation and technical clarity issues that impede reproducibility.
  - Overloaded and inconsistent symbols: k is used for context window, number of negatives (NS in Table 3), and as a dimension in ENS = |W| × k. Cp vs Cs is inconsistent in Eq. 4/7; Pc denotes both a set and an averaged vector. TU/TT parallel iteration is not formalized.
  - Output vs input embeddings are unclear: SGNS typically uses separate input and output matrices; here ENS is introduced but its role and dimensionality are inconsistent (|W| × k is nonsensical; should be × d). Where do ~o and ~n come from (ENS or EW)? Are contexts for phrases/concepts always words? Figure 2 is referenced but not described sufficiently.
  - Loss definitions are incomplete: Eqs. (2–4) conflate shared vs separate output embeddings and do not specify negative sampling distribution (e.g., unigram^0.75), subsampling of frequent tokens, dynamic windowing, or handling of sentence boundaries. The per-mention normalization for polysemous phrases is asserted but not derived or justified (and uses Cs, undefined).
  - Compositionality regularizers (Eqs. 6–7) are ad hoc: only similarity terms logσ(~p·Wp) and logσ(~c·Pc) are added, with no corresponding negatives, margin, or norm control; gradients may be ill-scaled relative to context terms. No detail on whether Wp/Pc are stopped-gradient targets or updated jointly; no discussion of stability.
  - MAP definition (Eq. 8) appears incorrect: 1V̂<c is described as an indicator function but should be a count of relevant items up to rank V̂c (otherwise precision reduces to 0/1). This likely affects Figure 4 and Table 6 methodology.

- Evaluation design and tuning raise risks of overfitting and weak conclusions.
  - Hyperparameters are tuned on the test sets (authors acknowledge), with an “adapted” 5-fold approach that still uses the test items; no held-out development sets are used. No statistical significance testing across methods.
  - Key settings (PUBMED and CLINICAL results) are omitted in Table 4 (“similar”), limiting transparency; only PM+CL is shown.
  - No ablations: The incremental value of β and η, concept updates normalization, or phrase replacement is not isolated. Given that simple approximations (APPROXPHRco) often match or beat CONCEPTco (e.g., Table 5), the benefit of the “concept” layer is unclear.

- Quality and reporting of the new entity dataset are questionable.
  - Low agreement (Fleiss’ κ=0.24) and small, convenience pool (34 unique workers; some with many assignments) cast doubt on reliability. No per-type breakdown, no post-hoc filtering by variance, and no RA-quality controls beyond simple validation items. Unclear licensing/release details.
  - Construction is manual stratification by author judgment; no evidence of coverage or difficulty calibration. Results heavily favor phrase-based approximations; concept embeddings underperform, undermining the claimed benefit.

- Methodological gaps in preprocessing and supervision.
  - Greedy phrase matching over tokens with no disambiguation: phrases that map to multiple concepts credit all concepts equally, introducing noise; no exploration of alternatives (e.g., prior priors, context-based reweighting, or NIL handling).
  - No details on tokenization, case/punctuation normalization, phrase boundary conflicts, overlap resolution, and how ambiguous surface forms across concepts/ontologies are handled.
  - Clinical data usage lacks ethical/IRB and de-identification details.

- Novelty is incremental relative to prior art.
  - The core idea—distantly supervised co-occurrence training tying text mentions to ontology items—is close to prior work (e.g., Wang et al., 2014a; Toutanova et al., 2015; De Vine et al., 2014), with the main change being the tri-level embedding and simple regularizers. Given the modest gains and strong performance of simple approximations, the contribution seems more engineering than conceptual.

Questions to authors
1) Clarify the model: What are the exact embedding matrices (input/output) and their dimensions? Where do ~o and ~n come from? What is ENS precisely (shape, usage, discarded when)? Please provide full equations with unambiguous symbols and specify the negative sampling distribution and subsampling.
2) In Eqs. (6–7), are Wp and Pc treated as constants (stop-gradient) or do gradients flow into word/phrase embeddings through these averages? Did you try margins or L2 penalties instead of logσ similarity terms?
3) How are ambiguous phrases handled at training time beyond uniform normalization? Did you try posterior reweighting by context similarity, or pruning highly polysemous forms? What is the impact on results?
4) Please correct and restate the MAP computation. If it was implemented as written, how sensitive are Figures 4 and Table 6 to the correct definition?
5) Provide full results for PUBMED and CLINICAL across all settings, and significance tests versus baselines. Also include ablations for β, η, and “concept updates off.”
6) For the new entity dataset, provide per-relation-type breakdown, inter-annotator reliability per item (or filter by variance), and a data statement (licensing, intended use). Will raw annotations be released?
7) What are the exact phrase-matching rules (tokenization, case-folding, multi-match tie-breaking, overlap resolution)? How many phrases are ambiguous across multiple concepts, and how frequent are such mentions in each corpus?
8) Please state IRB/ethics for CLINICAL notes and de-identification procedures.

Additional comments
- Numerous formatting and reference issues: many citations are incomplete or malformed, stray text appears (e.g., “a) found that a learning rate…”), footnote markers misplaced, figures/tables minimally described, symbol reuse (k, Pc) is confusing. These must be fixed for camera-ready quality.
- Report training stability (random seeds, variance over runs) and computational budget. Provide trained vectors to facilitate verification.
- Consider adding an entity-linking or code-prediction extrinsic evaluation to substantiate utility beyond intrinsic rankings.

Overall recommendation: Reject. While the idea is practical and results are sometimes competitive, pervasive clarity/notation errors, limited and potentially overfit evaluation, weak new dataset quality, and the strong performance of simple approximations undercut the methodological contribution. A revised version addressing the technical and reporting deficiencies and adding rigorous ablations and extrinsic evaluations could be much stronger.
