Summary and main contributions
- A weakly supervised adaptation of skip-gram with negative sampling that jointly learns embeddings for words, phrases (surface forms), and ontology concepts by: (i) greedily replacing ontology-mapped phrases in text with unique tokens, (ii) updating phrase vectors from word contexts, and (iii) updating concept vectors from the contexts of any of their surface forms, with optional compositionality regularizers between words↔phrases (β) and phrases↔concepts (η).
- An intrinsic evaluation on biomedical similarity/relatedness datasets (MayoSRS, Mini, UMNSRS) and a new crowd-sourced entity similarity/relatedness dataset; plus analyses of concept–phrase proximity and semantic type clustering.
- Release of software (cui2vec) and a new general-domain entity similarity/relatedness dataset.

Strengths
- Simple, scalable recipe leveraging existing ontologies without manual annotation. Using distant supervision from ontology surface forms is practical; the approach scales to billions of tokens across PubMed, clinical notes, Wikipedia/NYT.
- Competitive intrinsic performance on larger biomedical datasets. On UMNSRS Similarity the proposed concept embeddings match or slightly exceed strong word baselines (CHIU-30) and outperform several concept baselines (De Vine, Mencia); relatedness is competitive.
- Joint space for words/phrases/concepts enables useful analyses. The paper provides empirical diagnostics (concept–phrase similarity distributions, MAP analyses, semantic type clustering) that shed light on what is captured by the representations.
- Release of resources. Open-source implementation and a new entity similarity/relatedness dataset are valuable for reproducibility and future benchmarking.

Weaknesses
- Limited novelty relative to established methods. The core is a small modification of SGNS with phrase replacement and concept updates from the same word-context objective. The “jointness” across levels is weak (shared output/negative-sampling matrix and optional β/η regularizers). Prior work has jointly embedded entities and words (e.g., Wang et al., 2014a; Toutanova et al., 2015) and embedded concepts from textual/graph context; differences here seem incremental.
- Heavy reliance on intrinsically weak evaluations, with tuning on test sets. The primary claims rest on word/concept similarity/relatedness, which are known to correlate poorly with downstream utility. Although an “adapted 5-fold” procedure is used, the same datasets serve for selection; no held-out dev set or external downstream task (entity linking, WSD, concept normalization, code prediction) is provided. This undermines the evidence for practical gains over standard baselines.
- Improvements are small, inconsistent, and sometimes favor trivial approximations. On several settings, approximating concepts by the average of their phrase embeddings (APPROXPHRco) or even word averages approaches or outperforms direct concept embeddings, and phrase embeddings outperform concept embeddings on the new entity dataset. The compositionality regularizers often hurt, and optimal η is frequently zero, weakening the case that the method meaningfully captures non-lexical concept information.
- Distant supervision assumptions introduce unquantified noise; ambiguity is not addressed. Any surface form is treated as an instance of all candidate concepts (uniformly normalized). No attempt is made to disambiguate, estimate ambiguity rates, or analyze performance as a function of surface-form polysemy. This is especially problematic in the general domain. The effect of this noise on learned concept vectors is not ablated.
- Methodological/specification gaps. The training objective is under-specified in places (e.g., the role and dimensionality of the “negative sampling matrix” ENS as the context/output embeddings; how contexts for phrases crossing token boundaries align with the untagged window; treatment of overlapping/nested surface forms; handling of very frequent phrases). No training-time, memory, or convergence diagnostics are reported. Hyperparameter choices vary widely by corpus without clear rationale beyond tuning on the evaluation set.
- New dataset has low agreement and limited utility. The MTurk entity similarity/relatedness dataset has κ≈0.24 (fair at best) and only ~688 pairs, with validation filtering but still considerable noise. Given this, it is a weak basis to claim general-domain benefits, particularly when phrase-based approximations outperform concept embeddings.

Questions to authors
- Clarify the objective: Is ENS the context/output embedding matrix for words (as in SGNS), shared across words/phrases/concepts on the input side? Please formalize input/output parameterization and gradients, and justify sharing only via ENS.
- How often do surface forms map to multiple concepts in your corpora? Provide statistics on ambiguity and an ablation comparing: (a) your uniform concept updates vs. (b) disambiguation by selecting the most likely concept (e.g., by context similarity) vs. (c) excluding highly ambiguous forms.
- Provide a stronger ablation: What happens if you remove concept updates entirely and represent concepts only by averaging their surface-form embeddings learned with phrase replacement? This would isolate the marginal value of EC.
- Overlap/greedy matching: How are nested/overlapping surface forms resolved across ontologies? What is the impact on coverage and noise? Any postprocessing for multi-token boundaries or punctuation?
- Can you report at least one downstream task (e.g., entity/concept normalization in clinical notes or PubMed, entity linking on Wikipedia) comparing against word-only and prior concept embeddings?
- Hyperparameter selection: Did you consider a single set of hyperparameters across corpora to test robustness? How sensitive are results to window sizes and frequency thresholds?
- Resource release: Will you release the trained embeddings for all corpora, the phrase-concept maps used, and detailed preprocessing scripts to reproduce greedy matching?

Overall, while the paper is thoughtfully executed and the idea is practical, the methodological contribution is incremental and the empirical evidence—limited to intrinsic metrics with tuning on the test sets and a noisy new dataset—falls short of demonstrating clear advantages over established baselines or simpler approximations.
