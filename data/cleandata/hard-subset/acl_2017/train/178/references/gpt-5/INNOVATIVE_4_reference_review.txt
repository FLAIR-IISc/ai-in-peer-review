Summary and main contributions
This paper proposes a weakly supervised method to jointly embed concepts (ontology IDs), phrases (surface forms), and words in a shared vector space by adapting skip-gram with negative sampling. The supervision comes solely from ontology-derived phrase-to-concept mappings: phrases are greedily detected in raw text and replaced with unique IDs; concept embeddings are trained via the contexts of their surface realizations, without using KB edges or human annotations. Two simple regularizers allow trading off compositionality between words→phrases (β) and phrases→concepts (η). The method is applied to UMLS (biomedical) and YAGO (general entities) with very large corpora. The authors evaluate on similarity/relatedness benchmarks, analyze compositionality and semantic-type clustering, release an implementation (cui2vec), and introduce a new human-annotated dataset of entity similarity/relatedness.

As I see it, the primary contributions are:
1) A conceptually clean, scalable, weakly supervised framework that jointly embeds concepts, phrases, and words without relying on KB relation structure or manual concept annotations.
2) Cross-domain empirical study (UMLS and YAGO) showing competitive intrinsic performance vs. methods that use costly supervision, plus analyses indicating ontology structure is reflected in the learned space.
3) Release of software and a new entity similarity/relatedness dataset.

Strengths
- Novel direction: concept–phrase–word unification via distant supervision only. Prior joint text–KB embedding work commonly relies on KB relational edges or annotated links; here, the authors show that phrase-level distant supervision plus distributional learning suffices to place concepts and phrases into a usable shared space, at large scale and across domains. This is a meaningful conceptual step that reduces annotation requirements and could simplify pipelines in entity-centric NLP.
- Scalability and practicality. The approach is simple, computationally tractable, and scales to hundreds of thousands of concepts and billions of tokens, using only a dictionary and raw text. This makes it attractive for domains where KB relations are incomplete or annotations are expensive (e.g., clinical text).
- Competitive performance with cheaper supervision. On UMNSRS Similarity (largest biomedical set), the method matches or outperforms strong word baselines (CHIU-30) and concept baselines (De Vine, Mencia) that require tagging or labeled codes; on relatedness it is competitive. In the general domain, the jointly-trained phrase/concept representations perform strongly. These results support the utility of the weak supervision signal.
- Informative analyses of structure and compositionality. The paper probes how concepts align with their phrases and how phrases align with words (cosine, MAP), and shows type-level clustering behavior. The findings (e.g., concepts near the mean of their phrases, variable phrase–word compositionality, mixed semantic type clustering) provide valuable insight for future modeling.
- Resources: code (cui2vec) and a new entity similarity/relatedness dataset. Even with modest IAA, the dataset fills a gap for evaluating entity-level semantics and can stimulate follow-up work.

Weaknesses
- Modest and inconsistent gains; limited novelty in modeling. The core model is essentially skip-gram with phrase replacement plus an extra concept matrix; the compositional regularizers are simple cosine-alignment terms, and in tuning, the optimal η,β are often zero. Improvements over strong baselines are uneven (e.g., CHOI remains best on smaller sets; CHIU-30 matches/exceeds on some metrics). This raises questions about how much the “joint” learning—beyond phrase gazetteering—contributes.
- No disambiguation of ambiguous phrases. The assumption that every occurrence of a surface form corresponds to each of its possible concepts, with uniform normalization, injects substantial noise. There is no attempt to disambiguate or to weight concepts by context during training, which likely limits downstream utility and may explain weaker concept–phrase proximity and the reliance on mean phrase approximations.
- Evaluation relies on intrinsic similarity/relatedness with tuning on test data. Although an adapted 5-fold procedure is used, the lack of held-out test sets and the known limitations of similarity/relatedness tasks for predicting extrinsic performance weaken claims. The absence of downstream evaluations (e.g., entity linking, concept normalization, biomedical IR, clinical prediction) is a missed opportunity to demonstrate impact.
- General-domain dataset design issues. The new entity similarity/relatedness sets are relatively small, curated, and have low agreement (κ=0.24). Details suggest potential selection bias; it is unclear how well they generalize. Stronger design (larger scale, better QA, public guidelines, multiple languages) would increase their value.
- Limited ablations and alternatives. There is no ablation showing the effect of: (i) phrase replacement alone vs. full joint concept training; (ii) different matching strategies (greedy vs. longest-match or disjointness constraints); (iii) using ontology structure (e.g., types, relations) as additional constraints or retrofitting. Given related work (e.g., Wang et al. 2014a; retrofitting), comparisons would clarify the unique benefit of the proposed supervision.

Questions to authors
- How often are phrases ambiguous (map to multiple concepts) in your corpora, and what is the empirical impact of this ambiguity? Have you tried context-weighted updates or EM-style disambiguation during training?
- Can you report a clean held-out evaluation (no hyperparameter tuning) on at least one biomedical and one general dataset, or provide train/dev/test splits for your new dataset?
- What is the effect of removing the concept component (i.e., only words+phrases) versus removing phrase replacement (concepts trained from raw text contexts around concept IDs only)? This would isolate where the gains come from.
- Have you explored adding lightweight ontology constraints (e.g., same-type closeness, parent-child proximity) to see if they complement your weak supervision without requiring relation triples?
- Any preliminary results on a downstream task (e.g., concept normalization, entity linking, biomedical retrieval) to corroborate intrinsic findings?

Overall, despite uneven empirical gains, the paper advances a clear, scalable idea—weakly supervised, joint concept–phrase–word embeddings that avoid KB edge dependence—and backs it with cross-domain experiments and analyses. I see this as a valuable direction and am inclined to support acceptance.
