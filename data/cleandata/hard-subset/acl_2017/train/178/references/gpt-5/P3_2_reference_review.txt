Summary and main contributions
This paper proposes a weakly supervised method to jointly embed words, phrases, and ontology concepts into a shared vector space by adapting SGNS. The key idea is to use ontology-provided mappings from phrases to canonical concepts as distant supervision: phrase occurrences in raw text are treated as potential mentions of any concept the phrase can denote, and concept embeddings are updated from the phrase’s observed word contexts. The model introduces two regularizers to control compositionality: β encourages phrase vectors toward the average of their word vectors; η encourages concept vectors toward the average of their phrase vectors. The approach requires no human annotation of concept mentions in text.

As I see it, the contributions are:
1) A practical framework for jointly embedding words, phrases, and ontology concepts from unannotated corpora using only phrase-to-concept dictionaries (UMLS, YAGO), scalable to hundreds of thousands of concepts.
2) Empirical evaluation showing competitive performance with annotation-heavy biomedical concept embeddings on intrinsic similarity/relatedness, plus analyses of compositionality and semantic type clustering.
3) A new crowdsourced dataset of real-world entity similarity/relatedness (688 pairs each), along with a software implementation (cui2vec).

Strengths
- Clear, pragmatic weak supervision setup with broad applicability: Using only canonical mappings (string → concept) to induce concept embeddings is a useful middle ground between fully text-only methods and annotation/KB-relation-driven approaches. The training design—using untagged words as contexts and updating words, phrases, and concepts simultaneously—cleanly exploits available supervision while remaining scalable.

- Competitive biomedical results without manual concept annotations: On UMNSRS Similarity (large), the concept embeddings match or outperform strong baselines (including CHIU-30 words), and are generally competitive on relatedness. This is noteworthy given that several baselines rely on human-curated codes or explicit KB relations.

- Careful comparisons and diagnostics: The paper compares against multiple baselines, including “approximate concept” constructions (averaging phrase or word vectors), and reports both word-only and jointly trained settings. The compositionality analysis and semantic type clustering give a nuanced view of what the model is (and is not) capturing beyond surface lexical statistics.

- Scale and domain breadth: Experiments span PubMed abstracts, clinical notes, and Wikipedia/NYT, with up to ~750k YAGO entities and ~190k UMLS concepts. This demonstrates practicality and suggests the method can be ported to other ontologies/domains.

- Resource contribution: The new entity similarity/relatedness datasets and code (cui2vec) are valuable for the community, given the relative scarcity of concept/entity-level intrinsic evaluations.

Weaknesses
- Limited novelty relative to prior weakly supervised/KB-aware embeddings: The core idea—treating concept mentions as tokens and learning from contexts—echoes prior biomedical work (e.g., De Vine et al., 2014, which trains over CUI sequences). The main technical additions here are (i) joint learning of words/phrases/concepts with a shared context (word) output space, and (ii) simple compositionality regularizers (β, η). While useful, these are incremental and the paper’s own tuning shows β, η are often optimal at 0, diminishing their practical impact.

- Ambiguity handling is crude and potentially harmful: All occurrences of an ambiguous phrase are assumed to denote all of its candidate concepts (uniformly normalized). This introduces substantial label noise. There is no attempt to downweight highly ambiguous phrases, disambiguate via context, or model a latent concept variable. The analyses would benefit from stratifying results by phrase ambiguity and frequency, and showing how much performance relies on monosemous phrases.

- Clarity issues and notational inconsistencies in the method: The paper overloads k for both window size and negative sampling (later NS/WS), and has notation typos (e.g., c∈Cs vs Cp). The role of the “negative sampling matrix” ENS is confusing: effectively, the output/context embeddings are for words only, which should be stated explicitly and cleanly (asymmetric SGNS). A more rigorous derivation with clear vocabularies for input vs output would improve reproducibility.

- Intrinsic-only evaluation with tuning on test data: Although the authors acknowledge the limitations of similarity/relatedness tasks and adopt a CV-like tuning, the lack of extrinsic tasks (e.g., entity linking/disambiguation, clinical coding, IR/QA) makes it hard to assess downstream utility. Given claims about entity/concept modeling, an entity linking or concept normalization benchmark would be very informative.

- New entity datasets have low inter-annotator agreement and limited baselines: The crowdsourced datasets report fair agreement (κ=0.24). The evaluation compares model variants but lacks external entity embedding baselines (e.g., entity embeddings from Wikipedia link graphs or KB-based TransE/ComplEx initialized with text), which would contextualize the difficulty and the utility of the proposed approach. Moreover, the best results on entities are achieved by phrase-based approximations rather than direct concept embeddings, suggesting the concept modeling adds limited value in this setting.

Questions to authors
1) Ambiguity: What proportion of phrases are ambiguous (map to >1 concept), and how does performance vary with ambiguity? Have you tried weighting concepts by priors (e.g., surface-form popularity) or using a latent concept assignment (EM/REINFORCE-style) rather than uniform averaging?

2) Output/context embedding: Please clarify precisely the parameterization. Are all outputs (contexts) words only? Are there any output vectors for phrases/concepts? If not, why introduce the ENS matrix as a separate construct rather than a single word-context matrix? A schematic with input/output vocabularies and losses would help.

3) Regularizers: Since β and η often tuned to 0, can you show performance curves as they vary, and an ablation where the regularizers are removed entirely? Are there settings/types (e.g., rare concepts) where η>0 consistently helps?

4) Phrase replacement: How do you resolve overlaps/conflicts among matched phrases (beyond longest-match)? Are matches case-sensitive, and do you normalize morphology? What fraction of tokens get replaced in each corpus, and how does this affect word embedding quality?

5) Ontology structure: You discuss incorporating ontology relations in future work. Did you observe any correlation between embedding distances and ontology graph distances (beyond semantic type clustering)? A simple rank-correlation analysis would strengthen the claim that structure is reflected in the space.

6) Clinical data: Please clarify IRB/consent/de-identification for the clinical notes and whether those resources or derived counts can be shared to facilitate reproducibility.

7) Entity datasets: Will the new datasets be released with worker-level scores? Can you report per-pair variance and analyze whether low-κ pairs drive the weaker conclusions?

Overall, this is a solid, well-executed engineering paper with useful resources, moderate methodological novelty, and convincing scale. Stronger handling of ambiguity and an extrinsic evaluation would significantly increase its impact.
