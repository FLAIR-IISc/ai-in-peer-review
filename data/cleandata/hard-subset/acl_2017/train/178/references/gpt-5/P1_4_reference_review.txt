Summary and main contributions
This paper proposes a weakly supervised method to jointly embed ontology concepts (canonical identifiers), their surface phrases, and words into a shared vector space. The approach adapts skip-gram with negative sampling to train on parallel untagged and phrase-tagged corpora; phrases are greedily matched from an ontology lexicon and collapsed to single tokens, and concept vectors are updated using the contexts of any of their matched phrase forms. Two auxiliary terms control compositionality between words→phrases (β) and phrases→concepts (η). The method requires no manual annotations beyond the ontology lexicon. Experiments cover biomedical (UMLS) and general-domain (YAGO) corpora and evaluate on standard biomedical similarity/relatedness datasets, plus a newly collected entity similarity/relatedness dataset; additional analyses examine concept–phrase/phrase–word consistency and semantic type clustering.

As I see it, the main contributions (strongest first):
1) A simple, scalable, weakly supervised procedure to jointly learn embeddings for concepts, phrases, and words using only an ontology’s phrase lexicon and raw text, with competitive performance on large biomedical similarity/relatedness benchmarks.
2) A cross-domain empirical study at large scale (hundreds of thousands of concepts) with systematic hyperparameter tuning and analyses (compositionality and semantic type clustering).
3) A new crowd-sourced evaluation set for entity similarity and relatedness (people/places/organizations), and release of code/data (cui2vec).

Strengths
- Practicality and scalability with minimal supervision: The approach needs only an ontology’s surface forms, not manual concept annotations or knowledge graph edges. It trains on very large corpora (e.g., 3.5B tokens; >700k YAGO entities) with simple preprocessing, making it attractive for domains where high-quality linking is expensive.
- Competitive performance on strong biomedical benchmarks: On UMNSRS Similarity (large, 566 pairs), the concept embeddings are on par with or better than strong baselines, including annotation-heavy methods (e.g., comparable to CHIU-30). This supports the claim that context-only distant supervision can capture key semantic properties of concepts.
- Thorough comparative evaluation and tuning: The paper contrasts multiple approximations (word/phrase averages vs direct concept vectors), compares to prior concept/word embeddings, and carefully tunes hyperparameters (including β/η), acknowledging the sensitivity of intrinsic evaluations.
- Insightful analyses of the learned space: The compositionality and MAP analyses provide a nuanced picture of how concepts relate to their phrases and phrases to their words (e.g., concepts close to average phrase embeddings but individual phrase neighbors vary; phrase–word proximity is bimodal). Semantic type clustering comparisons further probe whether ontology structure is reflected in the space.
- New evaluation resource: The Mechanical Turk datasets for real-world entities (similarity/relatedness, 688 pairs each) fill a gap where word-level datasets abound but entity-level ones are scarce; details of collection and agreement are reported.

Weaknesses
- Limited methodological novelty: The core idea—treat concept mentions via phrase lexicons and train SGNS on their contexts—is close to prior distant supervision and phrase-tagging approaches (e.g., De Vine et al. tag CUIs then train word2vec; Wang et al. 2014a joint embeddings). The main technical additions (β/η compositionality regularizers and a shared output/negative-sampling matrix) are incremental and, by the authors’ own tuning, often set to zero (η=0 in most best settings), suggesting limited impact.
- Handling of ambiguity is naive: When a phrase maps to multiple concepts, updates are uniformly split across concepts. UMLS/YAGO have substantial ambiguity; without disambiguation or priors P(c|p), this injects noise. The paper does not quantify ambiguity prevalence, analyze its effect, or compare against simple heuristics (e.g., frequency-based priors, context similarity selection).
- Evaluation limitations and potential overfitting: Intrinsic similarity/relatedness is the primary evaluation; performance on smaller, high-agreement biomedical sets (MayoSRS/Mini) lags far behind CHOI. There is no downstream task (e.g., entity linking, typing), despite the method being well-suited for that. Hyperparameters are tuned via cross-fold reuse of the test sets, which the authors acknowledge; significance testing and confidence intervals are missing, and several differences are small.
- Quality of the new entity datasets: Inter-annotator agreement is low (Fleiss’ κ=0.24), making reliability debatable. While this reflects task difficulty, the paper would benefit from stronger validation (e.g., gold sanity checks beyond simple validation items, or expert annotations).
- Clarity and technical details: Some notation/typos (e.g., ENS dimension; inconsistency Cp/Cs) reduce clarity. Important preprocessing details are underspecified: phrase matching (tokenization, case, overlap resolution beyond “greedy”), normalization (morphology/redirects), handling nested mentions, and frequency thresholds’ effect on coverage. The choice to share only the output (context) matrix across words/phrases/concepts is unconventional and its implications are not deeply analyzed.

Questions to authors
- Ambiguity: What fraction of phrase mentions are ambiguous (|Cp|>1) in each corpus, and how does performance change if you (a) drop highly ambiguous phrases, (b) weight concepts by priors (e.g., anchor/page frequencies), or (c) perform simple context-based disambiguation during training?
- β/η ablations: Beyond tuning, can you report controlled ablations showing their effect on core metrics and analyses? In which regimes do nonzero β/η help? Do they ever hurt concept–phrase alignment?
- ENS and parameter sharing: Please clarify the dimensions and role of ENS. Why restrict the output/context vocabulary to words only? Would sharing output embeddings for phrases/concepts, or tying input–output parameters, improve cross-type alignment?
- Preprocessing specifics: How are phrases normalized (case, punctuation, multiword variants, redirects)? How are overlapping/nested phrases resolved beyond “greedy longest”? Any de-duplication across ontologies? What is the impact of the ≥25 frequency cutoff on long-tail concept coverage?
- Statistics and robustness: Are differences w.r.t. baselines statistically significant (e.g., via bootstrap over pairs)? How stable are results across random seeds and across corpora splits?

Additional comments
- Consider integrating light-weight ontology structure (e.g., hypernym edges) or retrofitting to better capture non-lexical relations and improve semantic type clustering.
- A downstream evaluation (entity linking, entity typing, or code prediction) would substantially strengthen claims.
- Releasing the code, pretrained vectors, and the new datasets with detailed documentation would aid reproducibility.
