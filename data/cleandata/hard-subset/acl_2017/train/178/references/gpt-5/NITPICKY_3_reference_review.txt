Summary and Contributions
- The paper proposes a weakly supervised method to jointly embed ontology concepts, their surface phrases, and words in a shared SGNS-style space. Mentions of ontology-mapped phrases in text are treated as distant supervision signals for their candidate concepts; two hyperparameters (β, η) softly tie phrases to the average of their words and concepts to the average of their phrases.
- It evaluates on biomedical similarity/relatedness datasets (UMLS concepts) and introduces a new crowdsourced dataset of human similarity/relatedness judgments for real-world entities (YAGO), comparing against several prior biomedical concept/word embeddings.
- It includes qualitative analyses: compositionality diagnostics (concept/phrase/word similarity distributions) and semantic type clustering for UMLS types.

Strengths
- Practical weak supervision: The approach requires no human concept annotations, scaling to hundreds of thousands of concepts across large corpora. The distant supervision formulation is simple and deployable, with results competitive to some supervised/curated baselines on larger biomedical similarity tasks.
- Joint space for words/phrases/concepts: Training words, phrases, and concepts together enables direct comparison and analysis across levels of abstraction. The compositionality regularizers (β, η) are conceptually simple knobs to trade off independence vs. compositionality.
- Cross-domain evaluation: Demonstrates the method in two domains (biomedical and general entities). The biomedical experiments compare to multiple strong baselines; the entity experiments provide a new dataset and show that phrase-level approximations can be strong.
- Diagnostic analyses: The compositionality and semantic type clustering analyses go beyond single-number benchmarks and offer insight into how phrases and concepts are organized in the learned space.
- Resource contribution: Releasing cui2vec and the new entity similarity/relatedness dataset is potentially valuable for the community, subject to clarifications on licensing/ethics.

Weaknesses
- Limited novelty and unclear technical correctness: The core training is an SGNS variant with additional “input” embeddings for phrases and concepts and a shared “output” matrix over words. This resembles prior joint text–KB embedding frameworks and sense/entity embedding with distant supervision. The claimed “jointness” is weak: information is shared primarily via the shared negative-sampling/context matrix; the β/η terms are simple similarity pulls to averages. Moreover, there are technical inconsistencies:
  - ENS is defined as |W| × k (k earlier denotes context window or number of negative samples), but should be |W| × d. The equations never distinguish input vs. output/context embeddings, and it’s unclear whether ~o and ~n come from the same matrix and how they are updated.
  - In Eq. (4)/(7), the index Cs appears but Cp was defined; normalization by |Cs| likely a typo. Such issues call into question the exact training objective and reproducibility.
- Evaluation design and fairness concerns: 
  - Hyperparameters are tuned on the same evaluation datasets (even with the described “adapted 5-fold,” this is still optimizing on test judgments without a clean train/dev/test protocol). No significance tests are reported; several improvements are small.
  - Some baseline comparisons are not apples-to-apples (e.g., CHOI uses ICD codes and different concept inventories; MENCIA uses MeSH), making conclusions about superiority tenuous.
  - Crucially, several concept approximations (APPROXPHRco, PHRASEco) meet or beat direct concept embeddings (CONCEPTco), undermining the necessity of the concept-embedding machinery. A strong word2vec baseline with careful hyperparameters (e.g., CHIU-30) is competitive.
- Ambiguous mapping and noise not addressed: A phrase can map to multiple concepts; the method splits updates uniformly, introducing substantial label noise. There is no disambiguation or weighting by priors/context. The effect of polysemy/ambiguity is not quantified (e.g., performance vs. ambiguity degree), and no ablations explore alternative strategies (e.g., EM, marginalization, or using ontology priors).
- New dataset quality is weak: The entity similarity/relatedness dataset has low inter-annotator agreement (Fleiss’ κ=0.24, “fair”), only 24 HITs with 34 unique Turkers, and basic QA (4 validation pairs). The small number of participants who completed many HITs risks annotator bias. Without higher-quality labels or stronger reliability filtering, conclusions from this dataset are fragile.
- Missing critical details and ablations:
  - Negative sampling distribution, subsampling of frequent words, phrase matching details (case normalization, stemming, overlap resolution beyond “greedy longest”), tokenization, OOV handling, and learning schedules are insufficiently specified for faithful reproduction.
  - No ablation showing the effect of β and η beyond a short statement; no analysis of computational cost or convergence; no study of min-frequency threshold sensitivity (beyond a brief aside for CLINICAL).
  - No downstream task (e.g., entity linking, WSD, retrieval) to demonstrate utility beyond intrinsic correlations, which are known to be unreliable predictors of extrinsic value.
- Formatting and references are error-prone: Numerous typos and broken references (e.g., “scle- rosis,” “literau re,” dangling footnotes, inconsistent years with “?,” malformed entries like “Vine et al.”), missing figure callouts/context, and general copy-editing issues. This is well below ACL formatting standards and impedes comprehension.

Questions to Authors
- Clarify the model: What are the exact input and output embeddings? Is ENS the context/output matrix with shape |W| × d? How are ~o and ~n parameterized and updated? Do concepts/phrases have their own output/context vectors?
- Negative sampling: What distribution is used (unigram^0.75)? Are negatives drawn only from words (contexts) or also phrases/concepts? Is subsampling of frequent words applied?
- Phrase detection/mapping: How are overlaps resolved (leftmost-longest, case-insensitive)? Are variants (plurals, punctuation) normalized? How many phrases are ambiguous (|Cp|>1), and what fraction of training updates are ambiguous?
- Disambiguation: Did you try context-based disambiguation or priors from the ontology (e.g., popularity) instead of uniform averaging over Cp? Any ablations on ambiguous vs. unambiguous phrases?
- Tuning protocol: Please provide a clean dev/test split or cross-dataset tuning to avoid tuning on test. Report statistical significance for Table 4/5 comparisons.
- Dataset ethics and IRB: For CLINICAL notes, what de-identification/IRB approvals apply? Will embeddings be released without leaking PHI? For MTurk, provide more detail on annotator demographics, quality controls beyond 4 validation items, and license for release.
- Reproducibility: Release complete hyperparameters (including sampling, subsampling, LR schedules), code to recreate phrase replacement, and scripts to train/evaluate. Without this, results are hard to replicate.
- Why do approximations outperform direct concept embeddings on general entities? Can you provide diagnostics to show when CONCEPTco helps over APPROXPHRco?

Overall, while the idea is practical and the resources are promising, the accumulation of technical ambiguities, evaluation issues, dataset concerns, and numerous presentation errors presently outweigh the contributions.
