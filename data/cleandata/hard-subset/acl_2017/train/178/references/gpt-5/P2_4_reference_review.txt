Summary and main contributions
This paper proposes a weakly supervised method to jointly embed words, phrases, and ontology concepts into a shared vector space. The method adapts skip-gram with negative sampling (SGNS): (1) phrase mentions in raw text are greedily matched and replaced by unique phrase tokens, (2) word embeddings are trained on word contexts, (3) phrase embeddings are trained on word contexts surrounding phrase spans, and (4) concept embeddings are trained to predict word contexts of any of the phrase surface forms that can denote the concept, with distant supervision distributing credit equally across all candidate concepts for an ambiguous phrase. Two optional regularizers encourage compositionality of phrases by words (β) and concepts by phrases (η). Experiments cover biomedical (UMLS) and general-domain (YAGO) concepts and evaluate intrinsic similarity/relatedness, semantic-type clustering, and compositionality. The authors also release a new human-annotated dataset for entity similarity/relatedness.

As I see them, the main contributions are:
1) A simple, scalable, annotation-light approach to jointly embedding words, phrases, and concepts using only a lexicon mapping from ontologies, without relying on KB edges or manual concept annotations.
2) An empirical study across domains and corpora showing competitive intrinsic performance versus stronger-supervision concept embeddings, plus analyses of compositionality and semantic-type clustering.
3) A new crowdsourced dataset of similarity/relatedness for real-world entities, and promised code/embeddings release (cui2vec).

Strengths
- Simplicity and scalability with broad applicability: The approach requires only phrase-to-concept lexicons and unannotated text, avoiding costly concept annotations or KB relation triples. It is easy to reproduce concept embeddings for new domains with an ontology lexicon and supports very large vocabularies (hundreds of thousands of concepts).
- Competitive intrinsic results with weaker supervision: On UMNSRS Similarity, the method ties the best word baseline (CHIU-30) and is competitive on relatedness; on general-domain entity similarity/relatedness, jointly trained phrase/concept representations outperform or match strong word/phrase baselines, despite not using KB edges or manual annotations as in CHOI et al. This is notable given the distant supervision noise.
- Multi-level analysis: Beyond headline correlations, the paper examines concept–phrase and phrase–word relationships (cosine and MAP distributions) and semantic-type clustering across several UMLS types, providing insight into what the model learns (e.g., concept vectors lie close to the average of their phrases; phrases show bimodality relative to their words).
- Broad, careful experimental setup: Multiple corpora (PubMed, clinical notes, Wikipedia+NYT), explicit hyperparameter sweeps, and comparisons against a diverse set of baselines (word-only, phrase approximations, concept embeddings trained with stronger supervision). The limitations of intrinsic evaluations are acknowledged.
- Resources: The released entity similarity/relatedness dataset fills a gap for evaluating real-world entities rather than words, and code/embeddings (cui2vec) improve reproducibility and impact.

Weaknesses
- Limited novelty: The core modeling is a straightforward SGNS variant with distant supervision; directly predicting word contexts from concept and phrase embeddings is close in spirit to prior work that (a) trains concept/entity embeddings from text using auto-tagging or Wikipedia links (De Vine et al., Toutanova et al.), and (b) jointly embeds KB and text (Wang et al.). The proposed β/η compositionality terms are simple regularizers. The paper’s main novelty is the combination and the “no-annotation” pipeline, which, while practical, is not conceptually deep.
- Intrinsic-only evidence with small gains and ties: Improvements over strong word baselines are small or tied (e.g., UMNSRS Similarity equals CHIU-30; relatedness differences are negligible). Statistical significance is not reported. No extrinsic tasks (entity linking, concept normalization, clinical retrieval, downstream classification) are provided to demonstrate utility beyond intrinsic correlations, which are known to be weak predictors of downstream gains.
- Distant supervision assumption is very noisy and under-modeled: The model assumes any ambiguous phrase expresses all its candidate concepts equally and simply averages gradients across concepts. There is no attempt at context-aware disambiguation (e.g., soft attention/EM), priors, or using ontology type constraints during training. This likely contributes to η being tuned to 0 in most settings and to concept vectors collapsing to averages of phrase vectors, suggesting limited added signal beyond surface forms.
- Analysis suggests limited added value over phrase/word compositions: The compositionality analysis shows concept vectors are extremely close to the average of their phrase embeddings; phrase-based concept approximations often outperform the direct concept embeddings on general-domain tasks. This undermines the claimed benefit of learning concept embeddings per se, at least under the current objective.
- Clarity and minor technical issues: Some notational/typographical errors reduce clarity (e.g., ENS reported as |W|×k rather than |W|×d; inconsistent Cp/Cs; whether output embeddings are shared and how negatives are sampled is under-specified). Phrase matching details (case-folding, overlapping matches, normalization) are light. The entity dataset has low inter-annotator agreement (κ=0.24), and the selection protocol may induce biases; more validation and discussion would help.

Questions to authors
- Ambiguity modeling: Did you try context-based soft assignment of ambiguous phrase mentions to concepts (e.g., EM, attention over candidate concepts, or type-consistency constraints) instead of uniform averaging? How does this affect performance and η>0?
- Output/negative sampling details: Are the “output” embeddings shared across words/phrases/concepts or only words? Is ENS actually |W|×d? What unigram distribution and smoothing were used for negatives?
- Significance and robustness: Are the observed correlation differences statistically significant (bootstrap CIs)? How sensitive are results to the phrase frequency threshold (≥25) and greedy matching strategy?
- Extrinsic evaluation: Can you report at least a small-scale downstream task (e.g., entity linking to CUIs, concept normalization in clinical notes, or retrieval) to substantiate utility beyond intrinsic metrics?
- Resource release: Will you release trained embeddings for all corpora and the code to reproduce the preprocessing (phrase matching) and training? For the entity dataset, will you release per-item annotations and scripts?

Overall assessment
The paper presents a practical, scalable approach and valuable resources with careful analysis. However, technical novelty is modest, empirical gains over strong baselines are small/tied, and the evaluation is intrinsic-only. I view this as a borderline submission: strong on practicality and resource contribution, weaker on conceptual innovation and convincing empirical advantage.
