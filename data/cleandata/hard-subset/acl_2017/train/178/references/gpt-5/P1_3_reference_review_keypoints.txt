- Proposes a weakly supervised method that jointly embeds words, surface phrases, and ontology concepts by replacing matched phrases with IDs and updating concept vectors from alias occurrences using skip-gram with negative sampling.  
- Introduces two regularizers (β for phrase→word, η for concept→phrase) to encourage compositionality.  
- Evaluated on biomedical concept similarity (MayoSRS, UMNSRS) and two new crowdsourced real-world entity similarity/relatedness datasets; releases cui2vec and datasets.  

- Contribution: Scalable, simple framework to learn joint embeddings from a phrase lexicon and unlabeled text without KB edges or manual annotations.  
- Contribution: Competitive intrinsic performance on biomedical concept similarity (UMNSRS) and strong general-domain results, plus new entity evaluation datasets.  
- Contribution: Analyses showing concept–phrase–word relationships and semantic type clustering in the learned space.  

- Strength: Practical and scalable, requiring only a lexicon and unlabeled corpus and applicable to large ontologies (UMLS, YAGO).  
- Strength: Competitive intrinsic results on key biomedical benchmarks, matching or exceeding several stronger baselines.  
- Strength: Resource release (cui2vec + two entity datasets) fills an evaluation gap for entity-centric benchmarks.  
- Strength: Thoughtful analyses of compositionality and semantic type structure.  
- Strength: Transparent hyperparameter exploration and reporting.  

- Weakness: Limited modeling novelty; approach is similar to prior joint word–entity training and regularizers add little (often tuned to zero).  
- Weakness: Naive ambiguity handling—updates all candidate concepts uniformly—likely injects noise for ambiguous mentions.  
- Weakness: Evaluation is largely intrinsic and mixed; no extrinsic tasks, limited statistical testing, and some baselines outperform or match concept vectors.  
- Weakness: Simple baselines (APPROXPHRco) sometimes outperform learned concept vectors, questioning the value of the concept layer.  
- Weakness: Methodological details lacking (separate ENS matrix rationale, phrase-matching specifics, frequency cutoff effects, efficiency).  
- Weakness: Crowdsourced datasets show low annotator agreement (κ=0.24), weakening some conclusions.  

- Question: Can simple disambiguation (context-weighting or alias priors) improve concept updates versus uniform splitting?  
- Question: What is the impact of the separate ENS negative-sampling matrix—any ablation results?  
- Question: Were Spearman’s ρ differences tested for statistical significance?  
- Question: How sensitive are results to the phrase frequency cutoff (≥25) and does lowering it help coverage?  
- Question: For the new datasets, can you release per-pair variances, annotator-level scores, and any type-based filtering?  

- Suggestion: Add extrinsic evaluations (entity linking, IR re-ranking, code prediction) to demonstrate practical utility.  
- Suggestion: Compare to joint word–entity methods using Wikipedia anchors/links to contextualize trade-offs.  
- Suggestion: Clarify phrase matching/tokenization, overlapping match resolution, ambiguity rates, and dataset quality controls.  
- Suggestion: Fix reference formatting and minor typos.
