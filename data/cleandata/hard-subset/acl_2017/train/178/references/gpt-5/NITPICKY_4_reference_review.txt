Summary and contributions
- A weakly supervised method to jointly embed concepts (ontology nodes), their surface phrases, and words into a shared space by adapting skip-gram with negative sampling; concept vectors are learned from contexts of any of their surface forms in raw text.
- A new crowdsourced dataset of entity similarity/relatedness (people/places/organizations) and analyses of the learned space (compositionality, semantic-type clustering).
- An implementation (cui2vec) and large-scale training on PubMed, clinical notes, and Wikipedia/NYT.

Strengths
- Practical weak supervision at scale: The paper shows that simple distant supervision from ontology phrase mappings plus large unannotated corpora yields competitive performance, notably on UMNSRS Similarity (Table 4), without requiring manual concept annotations. This is an attractive engineering trade-off.
- Joint space for words/phrases/concepts: The model allows querying and analysis across granularities, enabling analyses like concept–phrase and phrase–word compositionality and semantic-type clustering (Figures 3–4; Table 6). This is a useful capability lacking in many prior approaches that treat entities separately.
- New evaluation resource: The entity similarity/relatedness dataset (688 pairs/task) fills a gap for real-world entities and is a valuable resource, despite modest agreement (κ=0.24). The data collection procedure and filtering are documented in the supplement.
- Broad empirical comparisons: The paper compares with strong baselines (De Vine, Choi, Mencia, Chiu-2/30) and examines multiple corpora/domains, with transparent hyperparameter tuning details.

Weaknesses
- Technical/notation inconsistencies that undermine clarity and reproducibility:
  - Overloaded k: used as context window size (“k untagged words”) and also as number of negative samples. Table 3 uses WS and NS, but Section 3.2 and 3.2.1 reuse k ambiguously.
  - ENS shape is given as |W|×k; this should be |W|×d if it is an output/context embedding matrix. Calling it a “negative sampling matrix” is also nonstandard and confusing (it’s the output embeddings).
  - Eq. (1) uses P(D=1|w,c) but the loss defines o as the observed context word; mismatch between c and o. Eq. (4) has c∈Cs and divides by |Cs|, but earlier Cp denotes concepts for phrase p; Cs is undefined. Similar inconsistencies for Pc vs Pc (average vs set).
  - The additional compositionality terms (Eqs. 6–7) use logσ(~p·Wp) and logσ(~c·Pc) with no negatives or norm control; this can drive norms arbitrarily high. No regularization or temperature is discussed.
  - The training pass description (“iterate over tagged and untagged versions in parallel”) does not specify how alignment is maintained when TT compresses multiword phrases into unigrams, nor how positions/offsets are tracked.
  - The negative sampling distribution, subsampling of frequent words, dynamic windowing, and whether two embedding matrices (input/output) are used for words are all unspecified.
- Limited novelty relative to prior work: The core idea—using KB/ontology-derived aliases to supervise entity/concept embeddings from text and embedding words jointly—has close precedents (e.g., Wang et al., 2014a; Toutanova et al., 2015; De Vine et al., 2014). The paper’s main novelty is the particular multi-granularity setup with optional compositionality terms, but the empirical gains are modest and sometimes the simpler approximations (APPROXPHRco) match or beat direct concept vectors (Tables 4–5).
- Evaluation design concerns:
  - Hyperparameter tuning is performed on the test sets via an “adapted 5-fold cross-validation” that still uses the evaluation items for model selection; this risks overfitting. No held-out dev sets or external tasks are used.
  - No statistical significance testing or confidence intervals; many differences are small (e.g., CHIU-30 equals or exceeds CONCEPTco on UMNSRS Similarity; Table 4).
  - Coverage/OOV not reported: It is unclear how many pairs in MayoSRS/Mini/UMNSRS were excluded or how missing concepts/phrases were handled, given the ≥25 frequency threshold.
  - The new entity dataset has low agreement (κ=0.24) and small per-HIT annotator counts, reducing reliability. There is also overlap between training sources (Wikipedia) and evaluation entities; potential leakage is not discussed.
- Methodological choices insufficiently justified:
  - Ambiguity handling: a phrase may map to multiple concepts; updates are “normalized by the number of concepts” (uniform). This is a crude assumption; no exploration of priors, context disambiguation, or weighting by ontology frequency/popularity is provided.
  - The compositionality hyperparameters η and β mostly hurt according to the supplement, yet no ablation plots or principled analysis are presented. The positive effect is claimed in one corpus only, but not shown.
  - The core result that concept vectors are extremely close to averages of their phrases/words (Figures 3–4) suggests the model adds little beyond averaging. This undercuts the motivation for dedicated concept vectors.

Questions to authors
- Please clarify the exact objective and matrix shapes: Do you have separate input and output embeddings for words? What is ENS precisely, and why is its shape |W|×k? Are negatives drawn from W only? How are phrase/concept vectors used as “inputs” vs “outputs”?
- How do you align TT and TU during training when phrases collapse to single tokens? Do you skip internal phrase words or include them from TU? What is the exact windowing procedure?
- How many pairs are excluded from each biomedical evaluation due to OOV concepts/phrases, and what is the exact mapping protocol from text forms/CUIs to your embeddings?
- Can you report results with fixed η=β=0 vs best η/β, and include significance tests for key comparisons in Tables 4–5?
- What negative sampling distribution do you use (e.g., unigram^3/4)? Do you apply subsampling of frequent tokens and dynamic windowing?
- For the new dataset, will you release the full item list, raw ratings, and guidelines? Can you analyze potential train/eval leakage (entities heavily represented in training corpora)?

Additional comments
- Numerous typos/formatting issues: “scle- rosis,” “many-tomany,” “litueraure,” “cooccurrence,” inconsistent capitalization (Onto), inconsistent notation (Cp/Cs, Pc/Pc), and equation variable mismatches (c vs o). Please proofread thoroughly.
- References contain many formatting errors (question marks, escaped characters, missing venues/volumes). Ensure ACL style compliance.
- Table/figure captions should be self-contained; specify corpora and settings explicitly. Provide significance markers where appropriate.
- Consider evaluating on a downstream task (e.g., entity linking or concept normalization) to substantiate utility beyond intrinsic similarity.
