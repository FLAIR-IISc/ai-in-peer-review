Summary and claimed contributions
This paper proposes a weakly supervised approach to jointly embed canonical concepts, their surface phrases, and words into a single vector space. The method uses an ontology (UMLS or YAGO) to map phrases to concepts, replaces phrase spans with unique identifiers in the corpus, and adapts skip-gram with negative sampling to update word, phrase, and concept embeddings. Two hyperparameters encourage compositionality of phrases from words and of concepts from phrases. The approach is evaluated on biomedical similarity/relatedness benchmarks and on a new crowdsourced dataset for general-domain entities.

As I see them, the main contributions (strongest first) are:
1) A scalable weakly supervised training scheme that learns concept embeddings from raw text using ontology-derived phrase mappings, without manual concept annotations in the training corpora.
2) An empirical evaluation showing competitive performance with more annotation-intensive baselines on biomedical similarity/relatedness tasks, plus cross-domain results and analyses (concept–phrase and phrase–word alignment; semantic type clustering).
3) A new dataset for entity-level similarity and relatedness (people/places/organizations).

Strengths
1) Practical weak supervision and scalability: The method leverages existing ontologies to harvest phrase–concept mappings and trains on large corpora without human annotation in context (PubMed, clinical notes, Wikipedia+NYT). The approach scales to hundreds of thousands of concepts/phrases (Table 1), which is valuable for domains like biomedicine where coverage matters.
2) Competitive intrinsic performance with reduced annotation cost: On UMNSRS Similarity (larger, harder dataset), CONCEPTco achieves top correlation (0.653), matching strong word baselines (CHIU-30) and outperforming prior concept embeddings trained on annotated signals in several cases (Table 4). In general domain, concept/phrase variants perform strongly (Table 5). This suggests the weakly supervised signal is sufficient to reach the neighborhood of state-of-the-art on intrinsic evaluations.
3) Careful comparative study and analyses: The paper compares multiple approximations (word- and phrase-based), strong published baselines (DeVine, Choi, Mencia; CHIU), and explores compositionality via cosine similarity distributions and MAP-based retrieval of child phrases/words (Figures 3–4). The semantic-type clustering analysis (Table 6) provides an orthogonal perspective, highlighting strengths/weaknesses across types.
4) Cross-domain demonstration and resource release: Showing the method applies to both abstract biomedical concepts and concrete entities is non-trivial. The release of code (cui2vec) and the new entity similarity/relatedness dataset increases the work’s potential impact and reproducibility.

Weaknesses
1) Limited novelty relative to prior joint embedding work: The core pipeline—phrase matching, treating phrases as unigrams, and context-based training—echoes established techniques. The closest prior (Wang et al., 2014a) and related sense/entity embedding methods are acknowledged, but the methodological delta here (shared negative-sample matrix; simple compositionality regularizers β, η) seems incremental. Moreover, ontology structure is not exploited beyond phrase–concept mappings.
2) Noisy supervision assumption and lack of disambiguation: The model assumes each occurrence of an ambiguous phrase equally indicates all its mapped concepts, which can be highly noisy in both UMLS and YAGO. Uniformly distributing updates across all candidate concepts ignores context and likely harms representation quality. There is no attempt at soft disambiguation (e.g., attention/EM) or weighting by priors/popularity; nor is there an analysis of performance as a function of phrase ambiguity.
3) Evaluation methodology and modest gains: Intrinsic tasks dominate; there are no downstream evaluations (e.g., entity linking, WSD, retrieval), and the paper explicitly acknowledges tuning on the test sets (albeit with adapted 5-fold CV). Reported improvements over strong baselines are small and inconsistent; in several cases the simple APPROXPHRco is as good as or better than direct concept embeddings (Table 5), calling into question the necessity of the concept layer as currently trained. On semantic type clustering, the best prior (Choi et al.) substantially outperforms overall (Table 6).
4) Quality and utility of the new dataset: The new entity similarity/relatedness datasets have low inter-annotator agreement (Fleiss’ κ = 0.24), and the selection/validation process may introduce biases. Without stronger reliability or scale, it is unclear how broadly useful these datasets will be for the community.
5) Missing details/ablations and clarity issues: Important training specifics are under-specified (negative sampling distribution, scheduling of (w,p,o) updates, phrase matching policy beyond greedy, case/normalization). Ablations isolating the contributions of β and η are limited and suggest higher values hurt performance; a deeper analysis (including removing concept training entirely while keeping phrases) would better justify design choices. Some references and presentation elements appear garbled, which affects clarity.

Questions to authors
- How frequent is phrase-to-multiple-concepts ambiguity in your mapped data? Can you report performance stratified by ambiguity level and by concept prior frequency?
- How are negative samples drawn (unigram distribution/exponent)? Is the ENS “shared output space” necessary for transfer, or would a standard input–output embedding suffice?
- Did you try context-sensitive disambiguation (e.g., attention over candidate concepts per phrase occurrence or an EM step)? Even a lightweight heuristic prior could reduce noise.
- Can you compare to more recent joint word–entity models that rely only on weak supervision from Wikipedia/YAGO (e.g., training with mention contexts but without explicit KB edges), to better position your contribution?
- Can you provide a downstream task (entity linking, UMLS CUI normalization, or biomedical IR) to confirm that intrinsic gains translate to utility?
- What is the impact of incorporating ontology structure (types/relations) as additional training signals? A controlled ablation would help quantify the benefit.

Recommendation
Weak reject. The paper addresses an important problem and presents a practical, scalable weakly supervised approach with competitive intrinsic results and useful resources. However, methodological novelty is limited, supervision is noisy without disambiguation, gains over strong baselines are modest and sometimes matched by simple approximations, and evaluation lacks downstream validation. Strengthening the disambiguation mechanism, leveraging ontology structure, adding robust ablations, and demonstrating downstream benefits would significantly improve the paper.
