Summary
This paper proposes a weakly supervised method to jointly embed words, surface phrases, and ontology concepts in a shared vector space. The approach uses an ontology (UMLS or YAGO) to map canonical concepts to multiple representative phrases, replaces phrase mentions in raw text with unique identifiers, and adapts skip-gram with negative sampling to learn embeddings for words, phrases, and concepts simultaneously. Two regularization terms control compositionality: β encourages phrase embeddings to be similar to the average of constituent word embeddings, and η encourages concept embeddings to be similar to the average of their mapped phrase embeddings. The model is trained on large unannotated corpora (PubMed abstracts, clinical notes, Wikipedia+Gigaword), and evaluated on intrinsic similarity/relatedness correlations for biomedical concepts (MayoSRS, UMNSRS) and on a newly collected relatedness/similarity dataset for real-world entities. Analyses of compositionality and semantic type clustering are also provided.

Main Contributions (as assessed)
1) A simple, scalable weakly supervised framework to jointly learn embeddings for ontology concepts, their surface forms, and words without manual concept annotations and without relying on KB relational structure. The compositionality hyperparameters provide a tunable bridge from words→phrases→concepts.

2) Empirical evidence that the learned concept embeddings are competitive with or better than prior work on larger biomedical similarity datasets (UMNSRS similarity) while using less supervision, plus extensive analysis of how phrases/words relate to concepts in the shared space.

3) A new human-annotated similarity/relatedness dataset for real-world entities and release of a software implementation (cui2vec).

Strengths
- Weak supervision and scalability: The method leverages only surface phrase-to-concept mappings from ontologies and large raw corpora, avoiding manual concept annotation or KB relation usage. This is practical and broadly applicable, especially in domains with incomplete KB relations.

- Competitive performance on large biomedical benchmarks: On UMNSRS similarity, the proposed concept embeddings achieve the highest Spearman correlation among compared methods, and are competitive on relatedness. This is notable given that some baselines (e.g., CHOI) rely on human-annotated codes or curated signals.

- Joint space and insightful analyses: The paper evaluates how concepts relate to their phrases and phrases to words (cosine and MAP analyses) and examines semantic type clustering. These analyses provide evidence that the learned space preserves some ontological structure and offers interpretable behavior (e.g., bimodality in phrase–word proximity).

- Breadth of experimental settings and transparent tuning: Multiple corpora (biomedical literature, clinical notes, general domain) and extensive hyperparameter exploration are reported, with reasonable efforts (5-fold-like scheme) to mitigate tuning on test data.

- Resource contribution: The release of a new real-world entity similarity/relatedness dataset and an implementation increases the paper’s utility to the community.

Weaknesses
- Limited novelty relative to prior joint embedding work: Joint embedding of words and entities has been explored (e.g., Wang et al., 2014a; concept/sense embeddings from KBs and text). The main novelty here is using only phrase-to-concept mappings as distant supervision plus explicit β/η compositionality terms, which is incremental.

- No disambiguation for polysemous phrases: The training assumes each mention of a mapped phrase is evidence for all concepts it can denote (weighted equally). This introduces label noise, especially in general-domain YAGO settings where ambiguity is common. The paper lacks quantitative analysis of phrase ambiguity rates and their impact.

- Concept embeddings not consistently superior to phrase approximations: On the general-domain evaluations, approximating concepts via average phrase embeddings outperforms the learned concept vectors. Even in biomedical settings, approximations perform close to the direct concept embeddings. This suggests the method may not capture concept-level information beyond surface forms.

- Evaluation limitations and dataset quality concerns: Intrinsic-only evaluation with tuning on the test sets (even with adapted cross-validation) limits the strength of conclusions; no extrinsic tasks (e.g., entity linking, concept normalization) are provided. The new entity datasets show only “fair” inter-annotator agreement (κ=0.24), which weakens conclusions for that part.

- Objective/methodological clarity gaps: The negative sampling matrix is defined over words only; it is unclear whether negative samples for phrase/concept updates also come solely from word vectors, and why that choice is theoretically justified. Details of computational cost, convergence, and sensitivity to the frequency threshold (≥25) are not fully explored.

Questions to Authors
- Negative sampling: Do negative samples for phrase and concept updates come only from the word vocabulary (ENS is |W|×k)? If so, why exclude phrases/concepts as negatives, and how does this choice affect learning dynamics?

- Ambiguity handling: What proportion of phrases in UMLS and YAGO are mapped to multiple concepts, and how frequent are their occurrences? Have you tried simple disambiguation heuristics (e.g., type or prior-based weighting) rather than uniform averaging across senses?

- Concept vs. approximation: Why do averaged phrase embeddings outperform direct concept embeddings on the general-domain datasets? Did you try stronger η or other regularizers to better align concepts with their phrase manifolds?

- Sensitivity to frequency threshold: How do performance and coverage change when lowering the minimum occurrence threshold (especially in clinical notes)? Any trade-offs in noise?

- Extrinsic evaluation: Can you report results on an entity linking or concept normalization task, where concept embeddings might be particularly impactful? Even a small-scale evaluation would strengthen the practical relevance.

Overall assessment
The paper presents a clear, technically sound, and scalable approach with careful analyses and useful resources. The novelty is moderate, and the lack of disambiguation plus intrinsic-only evaluation temper the impact. Nonetheless, the demonstrated competitiveness with minimal supervision and the joint embedding perspective are valuable. With clarifications and, ideally, an extrinsic evaluation or better handling of polysemy, this would be a strong contribution.
