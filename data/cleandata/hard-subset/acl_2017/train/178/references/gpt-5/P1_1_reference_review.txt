Summary and contributions
This paper proposes a weakly supervised method to jointly learn embeddings for words, surface phrases, and canonical ontology concepts by adapting skip-gram with negative sampling. Mentions of mapped phrases in an unlabeled corpus are collapsed to single tokens; concept vectors are updated whenever any of their representative phrases occur, with optional regularizers to encourage compositionality (phrase-by-words and concept-by-phrases). The approach is evaluated on biomedical (UMLS) and general-domain (YAGO) ontologies across similarity/relatedness tasks, clustering by semantic type, and structural analyses; the authors also release a new crowd-annotated similarity/relatedness dataset for real-world entities and a software implementation (cui2vec).

Main contributions (as I see them), in order of significance:
1) A simple, scalable, weakly supervised framework to jointly embed words, phrases, and ontology concepts in one space, requiring only phrase-to-concept dictionaries and raw text, yet achieving competitive intrinsic performance without manual concept annotations.
2) Systematic analyses of the joint space (compositionality, concept–phrase and phrase–word proximity, type clustering), providing insight into how ontology structure is reflected in the learned geometry.
3) A small new similarity/relatedness dataset for real-world entities and a publicly released implementation.

Strengths
- Practical, scalable weak supervision with competitive results: The method leverages only phrase-lexicon mappings and raw corpora, avoiding reliance on expensive manual concept annotations or complete KB relations. On UMNSRS Similarity (566 pairs), concept embeddings match the best word-only baseline (Spearman ρ ≈ 0.653) and are competitive on Relatedness, while vastly undercutting supervision compared to CHOI and MENCIA. This is valuable for settings with large ontologies and scarce annotations.
- Joint space for words, phrases, and concepts with interpretable analyses: The paper goes beyond reporting ρ by probing compositionality and hierarchy (e.g., concept similarity to average phrase embeddings, phrase–word bi-modality, and semantic type clustering). These analyses clarify what the model captures (e.g., concepts near the centroid of their phrases; phrases variably compositional), which is informative for downstream use.
- Broad coverage and cross-domain evaluation: Training on PubMed, clinical notes, Wikipedia+NYT, and combinations shows the approach is robust across domains and ontologies (UMLS and YAGO). This breadth is non-trivial and suggests generality.
- Transparent hyperparameter study and ablations: The β and η trade-offs are explored, with an adapted 5-fold procedure, and hyperparameters are tuned per corpus. The finding that heavier compositional constraints often hurt intrinsic performance is useful for practitioners.
- Resources: Releasing cui2vec and a real-world entity similarity/relatedness dataset (with collection details) can benefit the community.

Weaknesses
- Limited novelty relative to prior art: The core training is essentially skip-gram over a tokenized mix of words and collapsed phrases, plus concept updates using the same word contexts. This is close to distant supervision paradigms (Mintz) and prior concept/entity embedding from text/KB (De Vine; Wang et al.; Camacho-Collados), with only modest additions (two simple regularizers and a shared negative-sampling matrix). The methodological advance feels incremental rather than conceptually new.
- Intrinsic-only evaluation with tuning on test sets: The main claims rest on similarity/relatedness and clustering; there is no extrinsic evaluation (e.g., entity linking, concept normalization, clinical phenotyping) to demonstrate real utility. Although an adapted CV is used for hyperparameter selection, the lack of strict train/dev/test separation on intrinsic tasks weakens the evidence, especially given known issues with intrinsic metrics’ predictive power for downstream tasks.
- Noisy concept supervision and lack of disambiguation: The assumption that every occurrence of an ambiguous phrase signals all possible concepts, with uniform weighting, introduces systematic label noise. This is likely why phrase-based approximations sometimes outperform direct concept vectors (Table 5), calling into question the added value of explicit concept embeddings. There is no attempt to mitigate ambiguity (e.g., contextual disambiguation, priors over concept distributions, or type constraints).
- Underwhelming gains vs strong baselines: On biomedical tasks, CHOI (with explicit supervision) handily wins on MayoSRS; on UMNSRS Similarity the proposed method only ties the best word baseline (CHIU-30), and on Relatedness it underperforms CHIU-30. For YAGO, APPROXPHRco outperforms CONCEPTco, suggesting the explicit concept layer may not provide consistent advantages. Semantic type clustering is also weaker overall than CHOI.
- Some technical clarity gaps and design choices: The negative sampling matrix is defined over words only; for phrase/concept objectives, negatives seem to be words, which may bias training and limit alignment across modalities. The additional regularization terms lack negatives and may induce collapse without explicit normalization. A few notational issues (e.g., Cs vs Cp in Eq. 4) and missing details (negative sampling distribution; tokenization/matching policy; handling overlapping phrases; vocab thresholds per corpus) impede full reproducibility.

Questions to authors
- Ambiguity handling: How sensitive are results to the assumption of uniform updates across all candidate concepts for an ambiguous phrase? Did you try simple disambiguation heuristics (e.g., type filtering, context similarity, frequency priors) or soft attention over concepts per mention?
- Negative sampling: Are negative samples drawn only from words (ENS = |W|)? If so, did you try negatives from the union vocabulary (words+phrases+concepts) to better align spaces? What is the sampling distribution (unigram^0.75)?
- Regularization terms: The β and η terms optimize logσ(similarity) without negatives. Did you experiment with margin-based or symmetric contrastive losses to avoid trivial solutions? Any evidence of vector norm growth or collapse?
- Why do phrase-based approximations outperform concept embeddings on YAGO (Table 5)? Is this due to higher surface-form frequency/coverage or ambiguity in YAGO mappings? Any analysis by ambiguity level or frequency?
- Extrinsic evaluation: Can you report any preliminary results on an entity linking or concept normalization task (even small-scale), or at least show improved retrieval of gold concepts given mentions, to substantiate downstream value?
- Data/model details: Please clarify the greedy phrase matching procedure (tokenization, case-folding, handling overlaps), minimum frequency thresholds per corpus, and whether phrase tokens are included in the context vocabulary for word training.

Additional comments
- The new entity similarity/relatedness dataset is useful but small with fair IAA (κ=0.24). Consider releasing annotator-level scores and gold splits, and reporting inter-annotator distributional baselines.
- Incorporating ontology structure (types/relations/hierarchy) as additional training signals could address limitations observed in clustering and small-set evaluations.
- Despite modest novelty, the paper is carefully executed, broadly applicable, and provides useful resources.
