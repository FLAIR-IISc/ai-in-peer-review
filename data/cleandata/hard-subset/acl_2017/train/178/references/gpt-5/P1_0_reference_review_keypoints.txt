- Proposes a weakly supervised method to jointly embed words, phrases, and ontology concepts by replacing known phrase occurrences with phrase IDs during skip-gram training and using two regularizers (β, η) for compositionality.
- Main contributions: scalable joint embedding framework requiring only ontology lexicons and unlabeled text; empirical evidence of competitive biomedical performance and analyses of compositionality/semantic clustering; a new crowdsourced dataset for real-world entity similarity/relatedness (cui2vec release).

- Strength: Practical, scalable weak supervision using only concept–phrase dictionaries and large unlabeled corpora, enabling hundreds of thousands of concepts/phrases to be handled.
- Strength: Joint embedding of words, phrases, and concepts is useful for downstream tasks; analyses reveal when phrase and concept compositionality holds or fails.
- Strength: Competitive intrinsic performance on larger biomedical similarity sets (UMNSRS Similarity) despite minimal supervision, matching or slightly exceeding some strong baselines.
- Strength: Careful hyperparameter tuning and transparent β/η analysis highlighting sensitivity and when compositionality helps.
- Strength: New crowdsourced general-entity similarity/relatedness resource expands evaluation beyond words.

- Weakness: Methodological novelty is limited—mostly standard skip-gram with phrase replacement and averaging regularizers; does not leverage ontology graph structure beyond lexicon mapping.
- Weakness: Gains are inconsistent and modest; some smaller biomedical sets and relatedness benchmarks are outperformed by baselines or show minimal improvement.
- Weakness: Distant supervision treats phrase occurrences as signals for all mapped concepts uniformly, introducing ambiguity/noise without disambiguation or instance weighting.
- Weakness: Evaluation is primarily intrinsic (and tuned), with no downstream task experiments to demonstrate practical utility.
- Weakness: Baselines and comparisons may be unfair or incomplete; need ablations against simpler phrase-ID skip-gram and stronger competitors using similar supervision.

- Q: How many phrases map to multiple concepts and how often do ambiguous phrases occur in the corpora; can performance be stratified by ambiguous vs unambiguous phrases?
- Q: What fraction of ontology concepts pass the 25-occurrence threshold in each corpus, and how sensitive are results to this threshold?
- Q: Please clarify negative sampling and parameter sharing: are there shared/context matrices and separate input/output embeddings for words, phrases, and concepts?
- Q: Provide an ablation comparing β=η=0 versus β>0 and η>0 to quantify each regularizer’s contribution.
- Q: Will code, phrase-tagging scripts, and the new entity similarity datasets be released, with links, licenses, and training time/memory specs?
- Q: Any downstream task results (e.g., entity linking or concept normalization) to show practical benefits of concept-level embeddings?

- Overall: Practical, well-executed weakly supervised framework with useful analyses and resources, but moderate novelty and mixed intrinsic gains; adding disambiguation, ontology-structure use, and downstream validation would strengthen the work—reviewer leans borderline accept pending clarifications and resource release.
