Summary
This paper proposes a weakly supervised method to jointly embed words, surface phrases, and ontology concepts into a shared vector space. Given an ontology that maps concepts to multiple textual forms (possibly many-to-many), the authors: (i) greedily tag a large corpus with phrase identifiers, (ii) train skip-gram with negative sampling over parallel tagged/untagged streams, updating word, phrase, and concept vectors, and (iii) add two compositionality regularizers that pull phrase vectors toward the mean of their words (β) and concept vectors toward the mean of their phrases (η). They train on PubMed abstracts, clinical notes, and Wikipedia+NYT, and evaluate on biomedical similarity/relatedness datasets (MayoSRS, UMNSRS) and a new crowdsourced entity similarity/relatedness dataset. Results are competitive with several baselines that use human annotation; analysis shows some clustering by semantic type and alignment between concepts and their phrases.

Main contributions (as I see them)
1) A simple, scalable weakly supervised framework for jointly embedding words, phrases, and concepts via distant supervision from an ontology, without requiring manual concept annotations or knowledge-graph triples.
2) An intrinsic evaluation showing competitive performance with much more supervised systems in biomedical similarity (particularly on UMNSRS Similarity), plus qualitative analyses of concept–phrase and phrase–word alignment and semantic type clustering.
3) A new crowdsourced dataset for real-world entity similarity and relatedness, and a released implementation (cui2vec).

Strengths
- Problem importance and scope: The paper targets a practical gap—aligning multiword surface forms and canonical concepts across domains—where pure word-level embeddings fall short. The approach can leverage large unlabeled corpora and ontologies with minimal engineering, and scales to hundreds of thousands of concepts (UMLS, YAGO).

- Method simplicity and practicality: Adapting skip-gram to update distinct word/phrase/concept matrices, with a lightweight preprocessing step (greedy phrase substitution), is easy to implement, domain-agnostic, and avoids reliance on incomplete KB triples. The compositionality knobs (β, η) offer an interpretable way to balance atomicity vs. compositionality.

- Competitive results without manual concept labels: On UMNSRS Similarity, the proposed concept embeddings match or exceed the strongest word baselines (CHIU-30) and are in the same ballpark as supervised concept embeddings (e.g., Choi et al.), while training at much larger scale and without human-coded sequences. Phrase-based approximations also perform strongly on the new entity dataset.

- Analysis beyond headline numbers: The paper probes the learned space with concept–phrase and phrase–word similarity distributions and MAP analyses, as well as semantic type clustering. These provide insight into where the model succeeds (phrase–word bimodality) and where signal is diffuse (concept–phrase proximity and type clustering).

- Resource contribution: A new entity similarity/relatedness dataset and released code/data increase reproducibility and potential impact.

Weaknesses
- Limited novelty and somewhat ad hoc objective: The core idea—treat mapped phrases as unigrams and learn their contexts—is widely used; updating concept vectors whenever any of their (possibly ambiguous) surface forms appears is a straightforward distant supervision variant of Mintz et al. The coupling across levels via a shared “negative sample” matrix and the β/η regularizers feels ad hoc and under-motivated theoretically. The regularizers maximize log σ of inner products with means, without corresponding negatives; this can encourage collapse and the tuning results indeed suggest larger β/η hurt performance.

- Ambiguity handling is naive: For polysemous phrases mapped to multiple concepts, the model updates all candidate concepts uniformly (1/|Cp|) each time the phrase occurs. No attempt is made to disambiguate via context, EM, or attention. This introduces systematic noise and likely explains why phrase-based approximations sometimes beat direct concept embeddings (Table 5). An oracle or a learned disambiguator would likely help materially.

- Objective/notation clarity issues: The negative sampling/output embedding matrix ENS is given as |W| × k (k is the number of negatives), which appears dimensionally incorrect; it should be |W| × d. It is also unclear if outputs exist for phrases/concepts or only for words; all negatives appear to be words. Equations (1)–(7) mix symbols (w,c vs w,o; Cp vs Cs) and omit details of how (w,p,o) triples are generated from the parallel corpora. These issues hinder reproducibility and precise understanding of what is shared and where gradients flow.

- Evaluation scope and fairness: The main results are intrinsic similarity/relatedness; there is no downstream evaluation (e.g., concept normalization/entity linking, clinical prediction), where the purported benefits would be most convincing. Hyperparameters are tuned against the test datasets (albeit with a CV heuristic), which risks overfitting. Comparisons span different vocabularies, supervision levels, and corpora; coverage differences are not quantified. On semantic type clustering, the approach lags behind stronger supervised code-sequence embeddings (Choi et al.), indicating that structure not captured by text-only contexts matters.

- New dataset has low agreement and limited characterization: Fleiss’ κ≈0.24 (fair) suggests substantial annotator variance. The pair selection process is partly manual and constrained to a few high-level types, which may limit generality. More analysis of category-wise difficulty and inter-annotator correlation would strengthen claims.

Questions to authors
- Please clarify the output/negative sampling parameterization: what is the dimensionality of ENS, and are output/context embeddings defined for phrases and concepts or only words? Are negatives drawn only from words? If so, why exclude phrase/concept negatives?

- How exactly are (w,p,o) observations constructed from TU and TT (e.g., when phrases overlap, at window boundaries)? A small algorithmic description would help.

- Can you report coverage statistics (percentage of evaluation pairs for which both concepts/phrases were embedded) and sensitivity to the min frequency threshold of 25?

- Did you try any simple disambiguation heuristic (e.g., context similarity over phrase’s candidate concepts) or a soft attention/EM variant? An ablation could quantify how much ambiguity hurts.

- Could you provide downstream results (even small-scale) on entity linking or concept normalization to support utility beyond intrinsic tasks?

Additional comments
- Notation/typos: Cs vs Cp; Eq. (1) uses c as context but later o denotes context; ENS shape likely |W| × d, not ×k. Please correct for clarity.

- Table 4 shows your best UMNSRS Similarity equals CHIU-30 (0.653). Please temper the claim of “highest correlation” or add statistical testing.

- It would be informative to compare against retrofitting approaches (e.g., Faruqui et al.) that inject ontology structure post hoc, and to add a variant that directly regularizes with ontology relations/types (as you suggest in Discussion).
