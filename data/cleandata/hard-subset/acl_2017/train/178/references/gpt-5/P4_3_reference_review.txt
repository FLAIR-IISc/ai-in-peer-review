Summary
The paper proposes a weakly supervised method to jointly embed concepts (ontology identifiers), their surface phrases, and words into a shared vector space. The approach uses distant supervision from an ontology mapping concepts to phrases: occurrences of mapped phrases in a large unannotated corpus are replaced by unique identifiers, and skip-gram with negative sampling is adapted so that words, phrases, and concepts predict context words. Two optional regularization terms encourage phrase embeddings to align with averages of their constituent word embeddings (β) and concept embeddings to align with averages of their phrase embeddings (η). Experiments on biomedical similarity/relatedness benchmarks (MayoSRS, UMNSRS) and on a new real-world entity similarity/relatedness dataset show competitive performance versus prior work, some of which uses human annotations. Analyses examine alignment among concepts/phrases/words and clustering by semantic type. Software (cui2vec) and the new entity datasets are released.

Main contributions (as assessed)
1) A weakly supervised framework to jointly learn embeddings of concepts, phrases, and words using only phrase–concept lexicons and raw text, without relying on KB relation triples or human concept annotations during training.

2) Empirical evaluation showing competitive intrinsic performance on biomedical similarity/relatedness benchmarks compared to methods that require manual annotations, and scaling to large corpora and vocabularies.

3) A new intrinsic evaluation resource for real-world entities (similarity and relatedness) created via crowdsourcing, plus analyses of how ontology structure is reflected in the learned space.

Strengths
- Clear, simple training formulation that scales and avoids manual concept-level annotation. The distant supervision setup (phrase lexicon matching + SGNS predicting context words) is straightforward to implement and relies on widely available resources (e.g., UMLS, YAGO). The reported vocabularies (hundreds of thousands of concepts) and corpora (billions of tokens) demonstrate practical scalability.

- Competitive intrinsic results in the biomedical domain without curated labels. On UMNSRS Similarity (the larger dataset), concept embeddings achieve the best Spearman’s ρ among compared systems, and on relatedness are competitive with strong baselines (e.g., CHIU-30). This is notable given that some competing systems (e.g., CHOI) use human-annotated codes/training signals.

- Joint space enables cross-level analysis and interpretability. The concept–phrase–word analyses (cosine similarities and MAP retrieval) provide insight into compositionality and how surface forms relate to canonical concepts. The semantic type clustering evaluation shows that the learned space reflects some ontology structure, with competitive MAP on several types.

- Resource contribution. The release of cui2vec and a new entity similarity/relatedness dataset adds value for the community. The dataset covers people/places/organizations and offers both similarity and relatedness judgments.

Weaknesses
- Limited methodological novelty relative to prior joint embedding work. The core idea (using surface mentions to align words and entities/concepts into a shared space via context prediction) is close in spirit to existing approaches (e.g., joint text–KB embeddings, distant supervision for relation/entity learning). The main distinctiveness is not using KB relation triples and introducing β/η regularizers; however, the latter often tuned to zero and provide limited measurable benefit.

- Concept embeddings offer marginal gains over phrase-based approximations. In several settings, approximating a concept by averaging its phrase embeddings (APPROXPHRco) matches or outperforms direct concept embeddings (CONCEPTco), especially on real-world entity tasks. The compositionality analysis shows concepts are very close to their mean phrase embeddings, suggesting the concept layer largely mirrors aggregated phrase information rather than adding distinct semantics.

- Strong reliance on lexicon matching and a simplistic handling of ambiguity. The assumption that each occurrence of a phrase supports all concepts it can denote, with uniform normalization (1/|Cp|), injects substantial noise for ambiguous phrases. No disambiguation or weighting is learned from context, despite context prediction being available. This limits technical soundness where polysemy is common.

- Evaluation scope and tuning practices limit conclusions. Intrinsic evaluations (cosine-based similarity/relatedness, clustering) do not directly assess downstream utility; this is acknowledged, but no extrinsic tasks (e.g., entity linking, concept normalization) are reported. Hyperparameters are tuned via adapted cross-validation on the test benchmarks themselves, risking overfitting and inflating reported numbers. For clinical data, the corpus is not public, limiting replicability for that domain.

- New entity datasets have low inter-annotator agreement. Fleiss’ κ of 0.24 indicates only fair agreement, which weakens the reliability of absolute correlations and makes fine-grained comparisons less conclusive. More rigorous quality control or expert annotation would strengthen this contribution.

Questions to Authors
- Ambiguity handling: What fraction of phrase types map to multiple concepts in your training sets, and what proportion of total phrase occurrences are ambiguous? Can you report performance breakdowns for pairs composed only of unambiguous concepts/phrases versus those involving ambiguous ones?

- Negative sampling/output embeddings: You define a single negative sampling matrix ENS = |W| × k; typically SGNS uses an output embedding matrix of size |V| × d. Please clarify the parameterization (dimensions) and whether phrases/concepts always predict context words only (never phrases/concepts). Did you try sampling negatives from phrases/concepts as well?

- Phrase matching: How do you resolve overlapping phrase matches and nesting beyond greedy longest match? Any statistics on precision/recall of matches versus gold annotations (where available)? How sensitive are results to the minimum frequency threshold (≥25)?

- Regularizers (β, η): Since best settings are often β=η=0, can you provide ablations showing cases where non-zero values help, and why? Did you try alternative formulations (e.g., contrastive or reconstruction losses) to more strongly couple levels without hurting performance?

- Dataset construction: For the new entity datasets, can you provide per-category κ (by entity type pair and by similarity vs relatedness) and examples where annotators agreed/disagreed? Did you filter pairs with high lexical overlap to ensure the task probes conceptual, not surface-form, similarity?

- Ontology structure: Have you tried incorporating type/hierarchy edges as auxiliary objectives (e.g., regularizing same-type concepts to be closer) to capture non-lexical information? Any preliminary results?

- Downstream tasks: Did you evaluate on entity linking or concept normalization to test whether concept embeddings provide gains over phrase/word baselines in practical settings? If not, can you outline expected use cases and how to apply the embeddings?
