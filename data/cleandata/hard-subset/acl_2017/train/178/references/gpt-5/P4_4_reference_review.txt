Summary
The paper proposes a weakly supervised method to jointly embed concepts (ontology identifiers), their surface phrases, and words into a shared vector space. The approach adapts skip-gram with negative sampling and uses two parallel corpora: an untagged version and a version where ontology-linked phrases are greedily replaced with unique identifiers. Concept embeddings are updated from the contexts of their surface forms under a distant supervision assumption (each occurrence of a phrase is treated as an occurrence of every concept it may denote, with normalized updates). Two hyperparameters encourage similarity between phrases and the average of their constituent word embeddings (β), and between concepts and the average of their phrases (η). Experiments on UMLS (biomedical) and YAGO (general entities) evaluate similarity/relatedness (Spearman’s ρ), semantic-type clustering (MAP), and compositionality properties, alongside comparisons to prior word, phrase, and concept embedding baselines. The authors also introduce a new crowdsourced dataset of similarity/relatedness for real-world entities.

Main contributions (as I see them)
- A simple, scalable framework to jointly learn embeddings for concepts, phrases, and words using only lexicon/ontology mappings and unannotated text, with optional regularizers (β, η) to trade off compositionality vs. independence.
- An empirical study showing that distantly supervised concept embeddings can be competitive with resource-intensive baselines on larger biomedical similarity tasks (e.g., UMNSRS Similarity: 0.653, tying best prior word setting), and strong results for entity similarity/relatedness in the general domain; plus analyses of compositionality and semantic-type clustering.
- Release of resources: implementation (cui2vec) and a new entity similarity/relatedness dataset (688 pairs per task; κ≈0.24).

Strengths
- Scalable, annotation-light approach: The method requires only mappings from ontologies (UMLS/YAGO) to surface forms and large raw text, avoiding manual concept annotation and knowledge graph relation reliance. It leverages billions of tokens (e.g., 2.8B PM+CL; 3.5B WIKINYT), making it practical for large vocabularies (e.g., 193k UMLS concepts; 748k YAGO entities; Table 1).
- Competitive biomedical performance on larger datasets: On UMNSRS Similarity, the concept embeddings achieve ρ=0.653 (CONCEPTco, PM+CL), matching CHIU-30 (0.653) and outperforming several concept baselines (De Vine 0.455; Mencia 0.565). Relatedness scores are competitive as well (PHRASEco 0.591 vs CHIU-30 0.590; Table 4). This supports the claim that distant supervision can approach annotation-heavy alternatives at scale.
- Strong general-domain entity results: On the new dataset, phrase-based concept approximations achieve high correlations (APPROXPHRco 0.810 relatedness, 0.766 similarity), with direct concept embeddings close behind (CONCEPTco 0.789/0.750), outperforming word-only baselines (WORDco 0.645/0.636; Table 5).
- Clear method and thorough analyses: The training objective and preprocessing are explicit, including handling of polysemous phrases (normalized updates) and the compositionality regularizers (Eqs. 5–7). The paper conducts insightful analyses: (i) compositionality plots show concepts are very close to the mean of their phrases, while phrase-to-word similarity varies (Fig. 3); (ii) MAP distributions indicate how phrases/words distribute around parents (Fig. 4); (iii) semantic-type clustering shows mixed but reasonable performance across types (Table 6).
- Useful resources: The release of code and a new similarity/relatedness dataset for entities addresses a gap for evaluating real-world entity semantics.

Weaknesses
- Limited novelty relative to prior joint/text+KB embedding work: The core idea—replacing linked phrases with identifiers and training skip-gram-style embeddings, plus averaging-based regularization—is a relatively straightforward adaptation. Related methods have embedded entities/concepts with text and/or KB supervision (e.g., Wang et al., 2014a; De Vine et al., 2014; Choi et al., 2016b). The primary novelty is the specific distant-supervision formulation for concepts via phrase contexts and the joint learning of words/phrases/concepts, which is incremental.
- Evaluation limitations and potential overfitting: Hyperparameters are tuned on the same evaluation sets (no train/test split), which the paper acknowledges, risking optimistic estimates. Intrinsic-only evaluations dominate; there is no extrinsic validation (e.g., concept normalization, entity linking). The new MTurk datasets have modest agreement (Fleiss’ κ=0.24), which may limit reliability of conclusions in the general domain.
- Ambiguity handling introduces noise and is not ablated: The assumption that every ambiguous phrase occurrence signals all its possible concepts (with uniform normalization) induces label noise. No disambiguation is attempted and no sensitivity analysis quantifies its impact. Results (e.g., η often tuned to 0; Table 3) and analyses suggest that enforcing concept–phrase similarity can hurt, possibly due to noisy supervision.
- Marginal gains over simple approximations in several settings: In the general domain, approximating concept embeddings by averaging phrase embeddings outperforms learned concept embeddings (APPROXPHRco > CONCEPTco; Table 5). In biomedical similarity/relatedness, simple averaging of phrases (APPROXPHRco) often matches or is close to CONCEPTco (Table 4). This questions how much the concept-specific training adds beyond high-quality phrase embeddings.
- Some modeling/design choices under-specified or unintuitive: Negative samples appear to be only words (ENS size |W|×k), even when training phrase/concept models, potentially biasing objectives. The coupling among words/phrases/concepts is otherwise weak (shared negatives); the regularizers only attract to means (no repulsion), and η is often tuned to 0, limiting the “joint” nature. Details of phrase matching (case, overlapping mentions), context definition across TT/TU, and vocabulary selection for negatives could be clarified.

Questions to authors (optional)
- Negative sampling: Why restrict negative samples to words for all losses? Did you try sampling from phrases/concepts or having separate negative vocabularies, and how did that affect results?
- Ambiguity: Beyond uniform normalization when a phrase maps to multiple concepts, did you experiment with priors (e.g., frequency-based, ontology type compatibility) or simple disambiguation? How sensitive are results to ambiguity rates?
- Coupling strength: Given that η was often tuned to 0 and averaging-based approximations are strong, what evidence supports the benefit of learning separate concept tables versus computing concept vectors post hoc from phrases?
- Extrinsic validation: Can you report results on a concept normalization or entity linking task to assess practical utility?
- Dataset release and quality: Will the general-domain datasets and code be released? Did you analyze per-category agreement (e.g., person–person vs org–place) and consider strategies to improve IAA?
