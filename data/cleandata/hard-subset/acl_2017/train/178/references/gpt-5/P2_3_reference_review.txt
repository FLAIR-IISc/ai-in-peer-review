Summary
The paper proposes a weakly supervised approach to jointly embed words, surface phrases, and ontology concepts into a shared vector space. It uses a phrase dictionary from UMLS/YAGO to replace phrase mentions in raw text with phrase IDs, then trains skip-gram with negative sampling over parallel tagged/untagged corpora: words and phrases predict word contexts; concepts are updated whenever one of their phrases occurs (with uniform credit assignment across possible concepts). Two hyperparameters encourage compositionality (phrase≈average of words, concept≈average of phrases). The method requires no annotated concept mentions or KB relations. Evaluation includes biomedical similarity/relatedness datasets (UMLS concepts) and a new crowdsourced entity similarity/relatedness dataset (YAGO), plus analyses of compositionality and semantic type clustering.

Main contributions (as I see them)
1) A simple, scalable weakly supervised method to jointly learn embeddings for words, phrases, and ontology concepts using only a phrase lexicon and raw text (no entity linking or KB relations).
2) A broad empirical study across domains/corpora with competitive results on biomedical similarity/relatedness versus models using stronger supervision, and release of a new entity similarity/relatedness dataset.
3) Analyses showing how phrase/concept compositionality and semantic type structure are reflected in the learned space.

Strengths
- Practicality and scope: The approach is easy to implement, relies only on widely available resources (lexicon + raw text), and scales to large corpora and vocabularies. This is valuable in domains where manual concept annotation or high-quality entity linking is expensive.
- Competitive biomedical performance without strong supervision: On larger UMLS similarity/relatedness sets, the concept embeddings match or exceed strong baselines, including models trained on manually coded EHR sequences (CHOI) for some tasks, despite using weaker supervision. This supports the core claim that distant supervision via phrase dictionaries suffices for useful concept representations.
- Careful comparisons and ablations: The paper compares concept embeddings to multiple approximations (word averages, phrase averages) trained jointly and independently, and to several prior biomedical resources (De Vine, Choi, Mencia; Chiu word embeddings). The finding that APPROXPHRco and CONCEPTco are close is informative and honest about the method’s limits.
- Analyses beyond headline metrics: Compositionality diagnostics (concept↔phrase, phrase↔word) and semantic type clustering (MAP across types) provide insight into what the model captures and where it falls short (e.g., sign or symptom clustering is uniformly hard).
- Resource contribution: The new entity similarity/relatedness dataset fills a gap (concept/entity-level comparisons instead of words). The crowdsourcing protocol, validation design, and κ reporting are transparent.

Weaknesses
- Limited novelty: Concept and entity embeddings trained from distant supervision over phrase-tagged sequences are close to existing lines of work (e.g., dictionary-based tagging of CUIs then word2vec on CUI sequences; joint word-entity spaces). The main methodological differences are (i) training concept embeddings by predicting word contexts via phrase proxies and (ii) simple compositionality regularizers (β, η). These are useful but incremental.
- Intrinsic-only evaluation and modest gains: The evaluation is largely Spearman’s ρ on similarity/relatedness. There is no downstream task (e.g., entity linking, concept normalization, retrieval, clinical prediction) to demonstrate practical impact or generalization. Moreover, in the general domain, APPROXPHRco often outperforms direct concept embeddings, undercutting the case for explicitly learning concept vectors.
- Ambiguity handling is simplistic: The assumption “any occurrence of a phrase signifies all concepts it can represent” with uniform normalization can inject substantial noise for ambiguous phrases. No statistics are provided on ambiguity rates or its impact. Alternatives (e.g., EM-style soft assignment, priors from surface frequency, or leveraging local lexical cues) are not explored. This likely explains variance in performance and could be a key lever.
- Hyperparameter tuning on test sets: Although an “adapted 5-fold cross-validation” is used, there is no held-out development set. This can inflate performance and confound fair comparison, especially given known sensitivity of embedding methods to hyperparameters. The paper acknowledges this but still relies on the same test sets for selection and reporting.
- Dataset and analysis limitations: The new entity dataset has relatively low inter-annotator agreement (κ=0.24), and the total size (688 pairs) is modest. While the resource is welcome, reliability concerns make it harder to draw strong conclusions. In the biomedical clustering analysis, the method trails CHOI overall; the paper could more deeply analyze where the proposed approach helps/hurts by type, frequency, and ambiguity.
- Clarity/technical nits: Some notation glitches (e.g., c∈Cs vs Cp), and the role of the shared negative-sampling/output matrix could be clearer. It appears only word context vectors are used on the output side; discussing why phrase/concept contexts aren’t used and whether sharing more parameters helps would strengthen the presentation.

Questions to authors
- Ambiguity: What fraction of phrases are polysemous (map to multiple concepts), and how frequent are such mentions in the corpora? Can you report performance as a function of phrase ambiguity and show ablations with alternative weighting (e.g., frequency-based or prior-informed assignment)?
- Coverage and frequency: How sensitive are results to the min-frequency threshold of 25? Do low-frequency concepts degrade performance or clustering? Any back-off strategies considered?
- Regularizers: Do β and η help any downstream behavior beyond intrinsic ρ? Can you show ablations where only β or only η is active, and qualitative examples where they correct obvious composition errors?
- Ontology structure: Have you tried a simple retrofitting or relation-regularized variant (e.g., parent-child/type adjacency) to complement the distant supervision? This seems aligned with your discussion and may address the observed limitations.
- Reproducibility: Will you release code, trained embeddings (biomedical and general-domain), and the phrase lexicons used for mapping? For the clinical corpus, can you at least release training scripts and PubMed/Wiki-trained models?

Overall assessment
A solid, pragmatic contribution with careful experiments and insightful analyses; novelty is moderate and evaluation is primarily intrinsic. Strength lies in weak supervision and scalability; the paper would be substantially strengthened by addressing ambiguity, adding a downstream task, and/or incorporating lightweight ontology structure.
