Summary and contributions
This paper proposes a weakly supervised method to jointly embed ontology concepts, their textual phrases, and words by adapting skip-gram with negative sampling. Mentions of ontology-linked phrases in raw text are treated as distant supervision for concepts; phrases are collapsed to unigrams in a parallel tagged corpus, and concept vectors are trained from the contexts of their phrases. Two optional regularizers encourage phrase-word and concept-phrase compositionality. The approach is evaluated on intrinsic similarity/relatedness benchmarks in the biomedical and general domains and accompanied by a new crowdsourced entity similarity/relatedness dataset.

As I see it, the main contributions are:
1) A scalable distant-supervision formulation to learn concept embeddings jointly with phrases and words without manual annotations.
2) An empirical study across large corpora (biomedical and general) showing competitive intrinsic correlation on concept similarity (notably on UMNSRS Similarity), plus qualitative structure analyses.
3) Release of resources (implementation; a new entity similarity/relatedness dataset).

Strengths
1) Practical, scalable weak supervision: Leveraging ontology lexicons to tag phrases and train concept vectors from raw text is simple, scalable, and annotation-free. The ability to train on very large corpora (PubMed, clinical notes, Wikipedia+NYT) and to cover hundreds of thousands of concepts shows engineering merit and potential utility.

2) Competitive performance on established biomedical intrinsic tasks: On UMNSRS Similarity the proposed concept embeddings are at or near the best reported correlations and competitive on relatedness, despite not using curated code sequences or link annotations. This provides evidence that distant supervision can approach the performance of more annotation-heavy methods.

3) Joint space for words, phrases, and concepts: Training all three in a shared space is useful for applications bridging surface forms and canonical entities. The compositionality analyses (concept–phrase and phrase–word) and semantic type clustering provide informative diagnostics about how the space organizes lexical and ontological information.

4) New dataset and code release: Providing a general-domain entity similarity/relatedness dataset and an implementation encourages reproducibility and further work, even if the dataset has relatively low inter-annotator agreement.

Weaknesses
1) Limited novelty relative to established approaches: The core idea—using distant supervision from ontology-linked phrases to train concept/entity vectors from text—is close to prior lines of work (e.g., distant supervision for KB population; entity/word joint embeddings; biomedical concept embeddings from CUI/document or code co-occurrence). The proposed losses mainly adapt standard skip-gram with simple averaging regularizers. Given this, the paper would benefit from stronger empirical evidence or a more substantial methodological advance.

2) No disambiguation for ambiguous phrases; supervision is very noisy: Any occurrence of a phrase mapped to multiple concepts is treated as signaling all candidate concepts, with uniform normalization. This is a strong assumption and is likely to inject substantial noise, especially for highly ambiguous surface forms. There is no analysis of ambiguity rates, nor breakdowns showing robustness vs. number of candidate concepts. This weakens claims that the method reliably learns concept semantics.

3) Evaluation is mostly intrinsic and partly unreliable: The evidence is dominated by similarity/relatedness correlations. The new general-domain dataset has low agreement (Fleiss’ κ ≈ 0.24), which undermines the strength of those results. There are no extrinsic evaluations (e.g., entity linking/normalization, concept retrieval, relation extraction, downstream clinical tasks) to demonstrate that the learned representations are useful beyond intrinsic tasks—an important gap given known issues with intrinsic metrics’ external validity.

4) Strong baselines diminish the benefit of explicit concept training: In several settings, approximating a concept by averaging its phrase embeddings (APPROXPHRco) or using phrase embeddings directly (PHRASEco) matches or outperforms learned concept vectors (e.g., Table 5). This calls into question whether separate concept embeddings add value beyond high-quality phrase/word representations, especially in the general domain.

5) Methodological clarity and tuning concerns: 
- The “negative sampling matrix” description is unconventional and under-specified (negative samples drawn only from words? output embeddings? distribution?). 
- Contexts for phrases/concepts are only words; negative samples seemingly exclude concepts/phrases, potentially limiting cross-type coupling. 
- Hyperparameters are tuned on the test datasets via adapted cross-validation on intrinsic tasks; no held-out development tasks or significance testing are provided. 
- The clinical corpus is private; replicability of key results (PM+CL) is limited. 
- The replacement/tagging strategy is greedy exact matching with a frequency threshold ≥25; the impact of these choices is not ablated.

Questions to authors
1) Ambiguity: What fraction of phrase mentions are ambiguous (|Cp|>1), and how does performance vary with ambiguity? Can you provide results where you (a) drop highly ambiguous phrases, (b) weight concepts by priors or context similarity, or (c) perform a simple disambiguation step?

2) Negative sampling and training details: Please clarify the negative sampling setup. From which distribution are negatives drawn? Are negatives only words (not phrases/concepts)? Do you maintain separate input/output embeddings? How are subsampling and smoothing handled?

3) Significance and stability: Are the reported correlations significantly different from strong baselines (e.g., CHIU-30; APPROXPHRco)? Please provide significance tests and variance across multiple random initializations.

4) Extrinsic validation: Can you report at least one downstream task (e.g., UMLS concept normalization, entity linking on Wikipedia/YAGO, code prediction, or retrieval)? This would substantially strengthen the case for the method.

5) Dataset quality: Given κ=0.24, can you (a) report results on a higher-agreement subset, (b) increase annotators per item, or (c) provide calibration/aggregation details (e.g., MACE) to improve label reliability?

6) Ablations: Please include ablations for (i) removing β and η (beyond tuning anecdotes), (ii) replacing greedy matching with more conservative matching, and (iii) varying the frequency threshold.

7) Comparison to retrofitting and KB-aware objectives: How does your method compare to retrofitting phrase embeddings to ontology synonymy (Faruqui et al., 2015) or to text+KB joint objectives (e.g., Wang et al., 2014a) on the same corpora?

Overall, while the approach is practical and shows competitive intrinsic results with large-scale coverage, the novelty is incremental and the empirical validation is not yet strong enough to endorse over established baselines without more rigorous disambiguation, stronger extrinsic evidence, and clearer methodological details.
