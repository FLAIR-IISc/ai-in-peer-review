Summary and main contributions
This paper proposes a weakly supervised method to jointly embed ontology concepts, their surface phrases, and words into a single vector space. The approach uses dictionary-based phrase matching over large corpora to replace surface forms with phrase IDs, then adapts skip-gram with negative sampling to (i) train word embeddings on untagged words, (ii) train phrase embeddings on word contexts surrounding the phrase span, and (iii) train concept embeddings from the contexts of any of their surface forms (uniformly distributing updates for ambiguous phrases). Two regularizers control compositionality: β (phrases toward averaged word embeddings) and η (concepts toward averaged phrase embeddings). The method requires no manual annotations beyond an ontology mapping.

As I see it, the primary contributions are:
1) A scalable weakly supervised framework to jointly embed concepts, phrases, and words without labeled concept mentions or KB relations, applicable to biomedical CUIs and general-domain entities.
2) An empirical study across multiple corpora and ontologies, including analyses of compositionality and semantic type clustering, and comparisons to strong biomedical baselines.
3) A new crowdsourced dataset of similarity/relatedness for real-world entities (people/places/organizations).

Strengths
1) Practical, annotation-light formulation with broad coverage. The distant supervision via phrase lexicons is well-motivated: it avoids costly manual linking and known incompleteness of KB relations, yet scales to hundreds of thousands of concepts (e.g., ~750k YAGO concepts) and large corpora. This gives the work potential impact for domains where annotated entity mentions or relation triples are scarce (e.g., clinical text).

2) Joint space for words, phrases, and concepts with interpretable trade-offs. The training scheme naturally shares information via contexts while allowing control over compositionality (β, η). The analyses (e.g., cosine similarity of concepts to averaged phrase embeddings; MAP of concept–phrase and phrase–word neighborhoods) provide insight into when phrases are compositional vs. idiosyncratic and how ontology structure manifests in the geometry.

3) Competitive performance without supervision-heavy resources. On UMNSRS Similarity, the proposed concept embeddings achieve top correlation among compared methods and are competitive on Relatedness, despite not using human-coded concept sequences (unlike CHOI). In the general domain, the jointly trained representations perform strongly on the new entity datasets.

4) Resource contribution and transparent experimental setup. The release of cui2vec implementation and the new entity similarity/relatedness dataset (with details on MTurk protocol) is valuable. Hyperparameter tuning is systematic, and the paper is clear about trends (e.g., higher β/η often hurt intrinsic scores) and the variability across corpora.

Weaknesses
1) Limited novelty relative to prior joint embedding work and modest gains. Conceptually, the method is close to prior lines (e.g., Wang et al., 2014a; Toutanova et al., 2015; De Vine et al., 2014): mapping surface forms to canonical identifiers and exploiting text contexts to learn entity/concept embeddings. The main novelty is avoiding KB relation triples and introducing simple compositionality regularizers. However, the empirical improvements over strong baselines are mixed: concepts often do not clearly outperform simple approximations (averaging phrase embeddings), and in several biomedical evaluations, supervision-heavy CHOI remains best.

2) Ambiguous phrase handling is naive and likely suboptimal. The model assumes each occurrence of a polysemous phrase equally updates all its candidate concepts (uniform normalization by |Cp|). This can inject substantial noise, especially for frequent ambiguous phrases, and likely explains why averaged phrase approximations rival direct concept embeddings. No attempt is made to disambiguate mentions using context (even a soft attention over candidate concepts), which is feasible in this setup.

3) Evaluation relies heavily on intrinsic similarity/relatedness with tuning on test sets. The paper acknowledges concerns that intrinsic scores do not predict downstream performance and that there are no clean dev/test splits for these datasets. Although an “adapted 5-fold CV” is used for hyperparameter selection, the final reporting still risks overfitting to these intrinsic tasks. Absent are downstream evaluations where concept-level representations are useful (e.g., entity linking/normalization, clinical code prediction, relation extraction), which would strengthen claims of utility.

4) New entity dataset has low agreement and limited validation. While the dataset is useful, Fleiss’ κ≈0.24 (fair) suggests high annotator variance. The selection procedure relies on manual pairing and small per-HIT annotator pools. It would help to establish convergent validity (e.g., correlations with Wikipedia link-based relatedness, or performance of established word-level models) and to discuss limitations due to the low IAA.

5) Clarity and technical details have rough edges. There are notational inconsistencies/typos (e.g., Eq. 4 “Cs” vs “Cp”), and the role of the negative sampling matrix ENS (restricted to words) is under-explained. Details on phrase matching (greedy overlap resolution, case sensitivity, stemming/variants) and how overlapping phrases are handled affect supervision quality but are only briefly stated.

Questions to authors
- Ambiguity resolution: Did you try context-sensitive weighting over Cp (e.g., attention or a softmax over candidate concepts for each mention) instead of uniform updates? If so, how did it affect performance? If not, can you comment on feasibility and expected trade-offs?

- Negative sampling and parameterization: Why is there a single negative sampling matrix of size |W|×k rather than sharing the output space across words/phrases/concepts? Did you try sampling negatives from the union of words and phrases? Could this improve alignment?

- Phrase detection: How exactly are overlapping or nested phrases resolved in preprocessing? Are matches case-normalized? Are morphological variants included? What proportion of matched mentions are multi-mapped (|Cp|>1), and how frequent are high-ambiguity phrases?

- Hyperparameter tuning: Your “adapted 5-fold CV” is performed over the final test pairs. Can you provide results with a strictly held-out dev/test split (e.g., by reserving folds for final reporting only) to estimate generalization? Alternatively, report performance variability across folds.

- Resources: Will you release the learned embeddings (for UMLS/YAGO) alongside code and the entity dataset? For the clinical corpus, what are the privacy/IRB constraints and can you share counts/statistics enabling replication?

- Downstream tasks: Do concept/phrase embeddings confer gains in entity linking or clinical concept normalization compared to word-only models or retrofitting? Even a small-scale study would substantiate practical value.

Overall assessment
Originality: moderate; the setting is sensible but not fundamentally novel. Soundness: generally sound, but simplifying assumptions for ambiguity may limit effectiveness. Significance: moderate; scalable and practical, with mixed improvements. Clarity: good overall, with some technical details needing clarification. Resources: useful code/dataset contribution. I lean toward weak reject unless the authors can (i) address ambiguity handling and (ii) provide stronger evidence on downstream tasks or cleaner evaluation splits; otherwise, this is a solid resource-oriented paper with incremental methodological novelty.
