Summary and main contributions
- Proposes a simple, scalable, weakly supervised method to jointly embed words, surface phrases, and canonical ontology concepts into a shared space by training skip-gram over parallel tagged/untagged corpora and updating concept vectors via the contexts of their surface forms. Introduces two compositionality regularizers (β, η) to softly tie phrases to their words and concepts to their phrases.
- Demonstrates that such concept embeddings, learned without manual annotations or KB relation edges, are competitive on biomedical similarity/relatedness benchmarks and across a very large vocabulary. Releases cui2vec and a new MTurk dataset for real-world entity similarity/relatedness.
- Provides qualitative/quantitative analyses showing partial preservation of ontology structure (concept–phrase proximity and semantic type clustering).

Strengths
- Novel direction: Weakly supervised, context-only joint learning of words/phrases/concepts. Unlike most prior entity/concept embeddings that rely on KB triples, manual links, or human-coded sequences, this approach uses distant supervision from ontology string mappings and unannotated text. This is a bold, practical idea with broad applicability across domains and ontologies.
- Scalability and simplicity: The method uses a light modification of skip-gram with negative sampling and a straightforward tagging pipeline, enabling training over billions of tokens and hundreds of thousands of concepts (e.g., ~188k UMLS concepts; ~748k YAGO entities) without extra annotation cost. This lowers the barrier to producing concept-level embeddings in resource-constrained settings.
- Empirical evidence that concept embeddings can be learned without KB edges: On UMNSRS Similarity, CONCEPTco matches or exceeds strong word baselines (e.g., equals CHIU-30 at 0.653), and relatedness results are competitive with word/phrase variants. This challenges the assumption that KG edges or manual links are necessary to learn useful concept representations.
- Cross-domain evaluation and resources: The paper covers biomedical and general entities, and releases a new entity similarity/relatedness dataset and code (cui2vec). Even if some results are not SOTA across the board, the resources and setup provide a valuable foundation for future work on concept/entity semantics.
- Insightful analyses: The compositionality and clustering analyses reveal interesting structure (e.g., near-perfect alignment of concept vectors and mean of phrase vectors; mixed but nontrivial semantic type clustering), giving the community a clearer picture of what the model learns.

Weaknesses
- Concept disambiguation is too crude: The central distant-supervision assumption (every mention of a surface phrase contributes equally to all its candidate concepts) injects substantial noise. The update normalization by |Cp| is a weak proxy for sense disambiguation and likely blunts concept specificity. This design choice probably explains why concept vectors are often no better than phrase approximations and why β/η > 0 often hurts.
- Added value over simple approximations is unclear: APPROXPHRco sometimes outperforms CONCEPTco (e.g., on the new entity dataset) and concept vectors are extremely close to the average of their phrases. Together with the tuned β, η often being 0, this suggests the method mostly recovers phrase-level information aggregated to the concept, offering limited new signal beyond phrase averaging.
- Evaluation limitations and tuning concerns: The real-world entity dataset has relatively low agreement (κ ≈ 0.24) and modest size, which weakens conclusions. The hyperparameter search is effectively tuned against the same evaluation sets (albeit with an adapted cross-validation), raising risk of overfitting. No extrinsic evaluations (e.g., entity linking, code assignment, retrieval) are provided to establish downstream value.
- Missing comparisons/ablations: There is no ablation isolating the contribution of (a) joint training over tagged/untagged text; (b) the shared negative-sampling matrix; (c) the β/η regularizers, beyond noting optimal values. Also absent are comparisons to alternative weakly supervised disambiguation (contextual weighting of candidate concepts), or to retrofitting/sense-induction baselines that might narrow the gap without KB edges.
- Coverage and matching assumptions: Requiring ≥25 frequency and greedy longest-match string tagging may bias toward popular entities and brittle lexical forms. The approach does not address morphological variants, nested mentions, or noisy surface forms; the resulting concept coverage and errors are not analyzed.

Questions to authors
- Can you incorporate lightweight disambiguation during training (e.g., soft attention over Cp proportional to P(c|context) using current vectors) instead of uniform updates? Do you expect this to improve the gap between CONCEPTco and APPROXPHRco?
- What is the empirical contribution of the shared negative-sampling matrix versus standard separate input/output embeddings? An ablation here would clarify the “jointness.”
- β and η are often tuned to 0. Are there settings where the regularizers help substantially? Could curriculum or annealing (start with β/η high, decay to 0) improve stability?
- How sensitive are results to the phrase-matching pipeline (greedy longest match, frequency threshold 25)? Do lower thresholds or fuzzy matching meaningfully increase concept coverage without hurting quality?
- Can you report at least one extrinsic task (e.g., concept normalization, entity linking, concept-based retrieval) to validate downstream utility? Even small-scale would strengthen the case.

Overall evaluation
This paper pursues an ambitious and timely idea—learning concept-level semantics from raw text using only ontology string mappings—and shows it can work at scale, with competitive intrinsic results and useful analyses/resources. Despite methodological imperfections (notably the uniform concept updating and limited added value beyond phrase averaging), the approach is novel, bold, and practical. I lean toward acceptance, contingent on clarifying the role of joint training and providing a path (even in rebuttal) toward contextualized concept disambiguation or an extrinsic sanity check.
