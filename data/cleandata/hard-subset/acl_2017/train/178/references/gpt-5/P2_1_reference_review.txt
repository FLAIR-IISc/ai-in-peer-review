Summary and key contributions
- This paper proposes a weakly supervised method to jointly embed concepts (ontology identifiers), their surface phrases, and words in a shared space. It replaces ontology-mapped phrases in text with unique IDs and trains a skip-gram/negative-sampling model over parallel tagged/untagged corpora, with optional regularizers tying phrase embeddings to their constituent words (β) and concept embeddings to their phrases (η). Concept updates are assigned uniformly to all candidate concepts a phrase can denote.
- Contributions as I see them:
  1) A simple, scalable distant-supervision recipe for concept/phrase/word joint embeddings requiring no manually sense-annotated text, demonstrated on UMLS (biomedical) and YAGO (general entities).
  2) An empirical study showing competitive performance vs. annotation-intensive concept embeddings on intrinsic similarity/relatedness, plus analyses of compositionality and semantic-type clustering; release of code (cui2vec) and a new entity similarity/relatedness dataset.
  3) A practical formulation that integrates concepts, their phrases, and words, with tunable compositionality (β, η), and evidence that ontology structure is partially reflected in the learned space.

Strengths
- Practical, annotation-light approach with broad applicability. The distant supervision assumption (phrase occurrences signal concept occurrences) allows training at large scale without human linking. The method works across domains (PubMed/clinical vs. Wiki/NYT) and ontologies (UMLS/YAGO), addressing a real gap where multiword expressions and synonymy are not well handled by word-only models.
- Competitive results against strong baselines that use annotations. On UMNSRS Similarity (566 pairs), the concept embeddings achieve the top correlation (Table 4) and are competitive on relatedness; this is notable compared to CHOI and DEVINE, which rely on curated code sequences or concept-tagged corpora. In the general domain, performance is strong (Table 5), and phrase-based approximations are state-of-the-art within the compared set.
- Careful experimental setup and ablations. The paper compares multiple approximations (word/phrase averages vs. learned concept vectors), evaluates on multiple corpora, and tunes hyperparameters with cross-validation. Analyses probe the geometry: concepts are very close to the mean of their phrases (Fig. 3a), phrase–word compositionality is heterogeneous (Fig. 3b), and concept–phrase/phrase–word MAP distributions (Fig. 4) are informative about neighborhood structure. The semantic type clustering evaluation (Table 6) is a useful lens, showing where the method excels or lags.
- Resource release. The cui2vec implementation and a new MTurk-based similarity/relatedness dataset for entities fill a resource gap (there are well-known word datasets but fewer entity-focused ones), increasing the paper’s practical impact.

Weaknesses
- Limited novelty in modeling; key ideas are incremental. The training is a straightforward adaptation of skip-gram with phrase ID substitution and optional similarity regularizers. Related work already explores joint word–entity embeddings, KB-driven entity embeddings, and concept-ID sequence training. The main novelty over prior work (e.g., Wang et al. 2014a; De Vine et al. 2014) is the particular distant-supervision setup plus β/η regularization, which is conceptually simple.
- No disambiguation; uniformly crediting all candidate concepts likely injects substantial noise. Phrases often map to multiple concepts; distributing updates equally can blur concept vectors. This is plausible given (i) weaker performance on small datasets (Mayo, Mini-Mayo; Table 4) where noise is less likely to average out, and (ii) that phrase-based concept approximations often rival or beat learned concepts (Tables 4–5), suggesting the concept layer adds limited additional signal over phrases. There is no analysis of how ambiguous phrases affect quality or of alternatives (e.g., EM, context-sensitive gating, priors).
- Evaluation is almost entirely intrinsic and has known limitations. While the paper acknowledges the caveats, there are no extrinsic tasks (e.g., entity linking, concept normalization, clustering, or retrieval) to corroborate the utility of the embeddings. The new MTurk datasets have low agreement (κ=0.24), and there is no comparison to established entity relatedness benchmarks. The heavy reliance on tuned intrinsic correlations, even with cross-validation, leaves uncertainty about downstream impact.
- Technical clarity issues and missing details. There are notational inconsistencies (e.g., P(D=1|w,c) but use of o; Cp vs. Cs; concept loss indexing), and the negative sampling matrix is described as ENS=|W|×k, which is atypical (should be an output embedding of dimension d). Important training details are missing or brief: negative sampling distribution, subsampling of frequent tokens, handling overlapping/multiword phrase matching in context windows, and exact treatment of phrases with many candidate concepts. Ablations for β and η are only summarized; fuller curves would help interpretability.
- Comparisons and positioning. The general-domain evaluation lacks comparisons to widely used entity relatedness methods (e.g., hyperlink-based measures) or KB/text joint embedding baselines. The paper cites Toutanova et al. (2015) but does not compare. Given that Table 6 shows CHOI excels at semantic-type clustering, it would help to discuss scenarios where the proposed method clearly dominates or complements KB/annotation-based approaches.

Questions to authors
- Ambiguity handling: What fraction of phrase mentions map to multiple concepts in your datasets? How does excluding ambiguous phrases (or weighting concepts by priors/popularity) affect results? Did you try context-sensitive disambiguation (e.g., soft attention over candidate concepts)?
- Negative sampling/output embeddings: Please clarify the role and dimensionality of ENS. Are there separate output embeddings for words/phrases/concepts, or a shared output matrix? What distribution did you use for negative sampling?
- Preprocessing: How do you resolve overlapping phrase matches when replacing with IDs? Do you apply word subsampling (e.g., Mikolov’s 1e-3) in TU or TT? What is the exact context window when a multiword phrase is collapsed—are boundary words treated specially?
- β and η: Could you provide performance curves vs. β and η (beyond the single tuned point), and a qualitative analysis of when higher compositionality helps or hurts?
- Extrinsic validation: Can you report at least one downstream task (e.g., concept normalization in clinical notes, entity linking on Wikipedia) to demonstrate practical gains over word-only or phrase-only baselines?
- Ethics/data: Were the clinical notes de-identified and approved by IRB? For the MTurk dataset, could you provide annotation guidelines and per-category (Org/Place/Person) agreement and performance?
