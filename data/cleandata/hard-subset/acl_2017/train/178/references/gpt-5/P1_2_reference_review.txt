Summary and contributions
This paper proposes a weakly supervised method to jointly embed ontology concepts, their textual phrases, and words into a shared space. The approach replaces known ontology-linked phrases with unique identifiers in a large corpus, and adapts skip-gram with negative sampling to learn vectors for words (from untagged tokens), phrases (from surrounding words), and concepts (from the contexts of any of their phrases). Two regularizers encourage similarity between a phrase and the average of its words (β), and between a concept and the average of its phrases (η). The model requires no manual annotation beyond an ontology’s concept–phrase lexicon. The authors evaluate on biomedical similarity/relatedness datasets (UMLS concepts) and introduce a new crowdsourced similarity/relatedness dataset for real-world entities (YAGO). They add analyses of concept–phrase/phrase–word compositionality and semantic type clustering.

Contributions (as I see them), in order of importance:
1) A simple, scalable distant-supervision framework for learning joint concept/phrase/word embeddings using only ontology lexicons and raw text, without knowledge-graph relations or manual annotation.
2) An empirical study across biomedical and general domains, including the release of a new entity similarity/relatedness dataset, showing competitive intrinsic performance on larger datasets.
3) Diagnostic analyses describing how the learned space reflects ontology structure (concept–phrase proximity, clustering by semantic type) and the role of compositional regularizers.

Strengths
- Practical, scalable, and annotation-free modeling: The method leverages existing ontology lexicons and large corpora (PubMed, clinical notes, Wikipedia+Gigaword) to learn concept embeddings at large scale (e.g., ~750k YAGO entities; Table 1) without relying on incomplete KB relations or expensive human linking. This makes the approach broadly applicable when KB structure is sparse or unreliable.

- Competitive performance on intrinsic biomedical similarity: On UMNSRS Similarity (the larger dataset), concept embeddings achieve top or tied performance among reported baselines (e.g., CONCEPTco 0.653, CHIU-30 0.653; Table 4), while being competitive on relatedness. Given the weaker supervision and greater coverage compared to methods trained on human-coded sequences (e.g., CHOI), this is a positive result.

- Cross-domain evaluation and new dataset: The method is tested on both biomedical concepts and general-domain entities. The authors release a new entity similarity/relatedness dataset (688 pairs per task) with clear collection protocol and quality control (validation questions, minimum pay), which can benefit the community.

- Transparent analyses and ablations: The paper examines compositionality (Figures 3–4) and semantic type clustering (Table 6), providing insight into how the model organizes concepts, phrases, and words. The hyperparameter study (β, η, window size, dimension, negatives) is thorough and informative.

- Reproducibility: The adaptation to skip-gram is straightforward, and the paper promises a software release (cui2vec) and dataset, increasing impact and usability.

Weaknesses
- Limited novelty and weak “jointness”: Methodologically, the approach is a direct adaptation of skip-gram with phrase identification and simple regularizers. Information sharing across levels is minimal (primarily via shared contexts/negative sampling), and the learned concept vectors are often very close to the average of their phrases. Indeed, approximations (APPROXPHRco) frequently match or outperform direct concept vectors (Tables 4–5), which undermines the added value of the proposed “concept” training beyond averaging phrase embeddings.

- No disambiguation; noisy supervision: The model assumes any observed phrase denotes all its possible concepts and updates them equally (1/|Cp|). This is a strong assumption in both UMLS and YAGO where surface forms are often ambiguous. The paper does not quantify the extent of ambiguity, its impact on performance, or any mitigation (e.g., context-sensitive weighting). This likely injects significant noise and may explain the limited gains over phrase averaging.

- Evaluation limitations and potential tuning on test sets: The evaluation is entirely intrinsic (similarity/relatedness, clustering). There are no extrinsic tasks (e.g., entity linking, concept normalization, clinical coding) where concept embeddings are expected to shine. Moreover, hyperparameters are tuned on the evaluation datasets via an “adapted 5-fold CV,” which still risks overfitting given the small sizes and lack of train/test splits (an acknowledged issue). Results on the two small Mayo datasets lag the best prior (CHOI), and on relatedness the gains over strong word baselines are small.

- New dataset quality and baselines: The released entity similarity/relatedness datasets have only fair inter-annotator agreement (Fleiss’ κ=0.24) and modest size. The evaluation lacks comparisons to strong entity embedding baselines (e.g., Wikipedia entity embeddings or methods leveraging KB relations/links), making it hard to gauge the advantage of this approach in the general domain.

- Underuse of ontology structure; limited impact of β/η: The model does not exploit ontology relations (hierarchies, types) beyond surface mappings. The authors’ own analysis notes that enforcing concept–phrase or phrase–word similarity often hurts similarity/relatedness (non-zero η rarely optimal), suggesting that the regularizers contribute little. Incorporating graph structure (as in Wang et al., 2014a) could be crucial to capture non-lexical semantics.

Questions to authors
- Ambiguity handling: What proportion of phrases in UMLS and YAGO map to multiple concepts and with what frequency in the corpora? Did you try context-based weighting among candidate concepts (e.g., softmax over concept priors or context similarity) instead of uniform 1/|Cp| updates?

- Negative sampling details: ENS is |W|×k; are negative samples always drawn from words (not phrases/concepts)? Are phrases/concepts ever used as context targets? Please clarify the exact training updates for each level.

- Phrase matching: How do you handle overlapping/ nested phrases, casing, morphological variants, and tokenization differences between ontology strings and corpora? What fraction of ontology phrases are matched in practice (coverage), and how does the greedy matching affect precision/recall?

- Data leakage and splits: Given tuning on the evaluation datasets, can you provide results with a strict held-out split or using alternative intrinsic tasks (e.g., concept analogy-like probes) to reduce tuning bias?

- General-domain baselines: Can you compare against entity embeddings trained from Wikipedia hyperlinks/KB relations (e.g., TransE, Hyperplanes, or word+entity joint models) on your new dataset to contextualize performance?

- Extrinsic evaluations: Did you attempt entity linking or concept normalization to test the practical utility of the concept vectors compared to phrase or word embeddings? If not, what are the anticipated strengths/limitations?

Additional comments (suggestions)
- Consider incorporating ontology graph structure (types/relations) as an auxiliary objective and adding context-sensitive disambiguation of phrase mentions to reduce noise.
- Provide an ablation comparing: (a) concept training disabled (phrases only), (b) concept training with/without β/η, and (c) concept updates only for unambiguous phrases, to isolate where gains come from.
- Release code, embeddings, and detailed preprocessing scripts to enable replication at scale.
