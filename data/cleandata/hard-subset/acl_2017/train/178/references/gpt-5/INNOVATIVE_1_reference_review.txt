Summary and contributions
This paper proposes a weakly supervised framework to jointly embed ontology concepts, their surface forms (phrases), and words into a shared space by injecting ontology-derived phrase-to-concept mappings into skip-gram training over large unlabeled corpora. The key idea is to replace occurrences of mapped phrases with unique identifiers and use them as distant supervision for the concept embeddings, while also learning phrase and word vectors.

Main contributions (as I see them):
1) A scalable, annotation-free method for jointly learning concept, phrase, and word embeddings by leveraging only ontology lexicalizations, with simple extensions (β, η) to control compositionality between words→phrases and phrases→concepts.
2) Empirical evidence that the resulting concept embeddings are competitive with or superior to stronger baselines on large biomedical similarity tasks, and qualitative analyses showing the space reflects ontology structure; release of cui2vec.
3) Introduction of a new crowdsourced dataset for similarity and relatedness of real-world entities (people, places, organizations), and demonstration that the approach generalizes beyond biomedicine.

Strengths
- Conceptual novelty with strong simplicity. Turning ontology lexicalizations into weak supervision for concept embeddings, without relying on expensive entity linking or KB relation structure, is a compelling and pragmatic idea. The method is an elegant modification of skip-gram that many practitioners can adopt, and it unifies concepts, phrases, and words in one space.

- Scalability and breadth. The approach trains on billions of tokens and hundreds of thousands of concepts across domains (UMLS and YAGO), with minimal assumptions. This positions the technique as a general recipe for concept/entity embeddings when only a dictionary is available.

- Competitive intrinsic performance where scale matters. On UMNSRS Similarity (566 pairs), the jointly trained concept embeddings achieve the highest Spearman’s ρ (0.653), and on relatedness the best settings are competitive (PHRASEco near CHIU-30). On general-domain entities, APPROXPHRco and CONCEPTco perform strongly. These results are particularly appealing given the lack of manual annotations and the breadth of concepts covered.

- Useful resources for the community. Releasing an implementation (cui2vec) and a new general-domain entity similarity/relatedness dataset adds tangible value, independent of the exact modeling details.

- Insightful analyses of the learned space. The compositionality and MAP analyses provide a nuanced view: concepts cluster near the mean of their phrases (Fig. 3a), phrases show bi-modality with respect to their words (Fig. 4b), and semantic types partially cluster (Table 6). These observations support the claim that ontology structure is reflected in the geometry.

Weaknesses
- Limited modeling novelty and underutilization of ontology structure. The core training is a fairly straightforward skip-gram variant with phrase replacement and averaging-based regularizers. It does not leverage relations or hierarchy beyond lexicalizations, in contrast to prior joint text–KB methods (e.g., Wang et al., 2014a), which somewhat reduces the methodological originality. This is mitigated by scalability and practicality, but worth acknowledging.

- Ambiguity handling is crude. The assumption that any occurrence of a mapped phrase instantiates “each concept it can refer to,” with uniform update splitting, is strong and likely harmful for frequent ambiguous phrases. There is no attempt at weak disambiguation (e.g., type constraints, priors, or context-based gating), nor an analysis isolating its impact. This seems like low-hanging fruit and could materially improve concept specificity.

- Claims about compositionality control are not strongly supported. Best hyperparameters often set β=0 and η=0 (Table 3), suggesting that the proposed regularizers contribute little in practice. A systematic ablation across corpora to quantify the gains (or lack thereof) from non-zero β/η is missing.

- Evaluation is largely intrinsic and tuning risks overfitting. Results center on similarity/relatedness and clustering; no downstream evaluations (e.g., entity linking, concept normalization, code prediction) are provided. Although an adapted 5-fold procedure is used, hyperparameters are tuned on the same datasets being reported, and no statistical significance testing is presented.

- Crowdsourced dataset quality concerns. The new entity datasets are valuable, but inter-annotator agreement is modest (Fleiss’ κ=0.24), and per-HIT annotator counts are small. Moreover, on this dataset, the phrase-based approximation outperforms direct concept embeddings, which raises questions about the added value of concept vectors in the general domain.

Questions to authors
- Can you provide a thorough ablation of β and η across corpora and tasks, including settings where they help? In particular, are there scenarios where non-zero η consistently improves concept quality?

- How sensitive are results to the uniform sharing assumption for ambiguous phrases? Have you tried weighting by concept priors (e.g., frequency of lexicalization) or simple context-based disambiguation? A small-scale study on highly ambiguous phrases would be helpful.

- Could you report at least one downstream task (even small-scale), such as biomedical concept normalization or entity linking, to demonstrate practical utility beyond intrinsic scores?

- Please clarify phrase matching details: case sensitivity, handling of overlapping/nested phrases, multiword tokenization, and how greedy longest-match affects recall/precision of concept supervision.

- Will the trained YAGO-based embeddings and code be released alongside the biomedical ones?

Overall recommendation
This paper introduces a simple, scalable, and novel direction: using ontology lexicalizations as weak supervision to jointly learn concept/phrase/word embeddings without manual annotations. Despite some methodological modesty and evaluation gaps, the idea is impactful and broadly useful. I lean toward acceptance, provided the authors address ambiguity handling and strengthen the ablations and evaluation.
