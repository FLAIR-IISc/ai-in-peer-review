Summary
This paper proposes a weakly supervised method to jointly embed ontology concepts, their surface phrases, and words into a shared vector space. The approach replaces known ontology-linked phrases in a large unannotated corpus with unique tokens and adapts skip-gram with negative sampling: word embeddings are trained on untagged word contexts; phrase embeddings are trained using the contexts around the phrase span; and concept embeddings are updated whenever any of their phrases occur (weighted by phrase ambiguity). Two hyperparameters encourage compositionality between phrases and their constituent words (β) and between concepts and their phrases (η). The method is evaluated on biomedical similarity/relatedness datasets (MayoSRS, UMNSRS) and a new crowdsourced dataset of real-world entities (YAGO), and is analyzed for compositionality and semantic type clustering.

Main contributions (as I see them)
1) A practical weakly supervised framework to jointly learn embeddings for concepts, phrases, and words from raw text plus an ontology’s phrase-to-concept lexicon, requiring no manual concept annotations.
2) Empirical evaluation across two domains (biomedical and general entities), including analyses of compositionality and semantic clustering; release of code (cui2vec) and a new real-world entity similarity/relatedness dataset.
3) A simple compositionality regularization (β, η) connecting word–phrase–concept levels within a unified objective.

Strengths
- Clear, simple, and scalable modeling formulation: The adaptation of skip-gram to jointly learn words, phrases, and concepts with shared word contexts and a single negative-sampling matrix is elegant and easy to implement. The method leverages widely available ontologies (UMLS, YAGO) and unannotated corpora, scales to hundreds of thousands of concepts, and avoids reliance on costly human annotations or complete knowledge graphs.

- Competitive performance without manual concept labels: On large biomedical benchmarks, the approach matches or slightly outperforms strong word-based baselines (e.g., UMNSRS Similarity: CONCEPTco ≈ CHIU-30) and is close on relatedness, demonstrating that distant supervision can be sufficient for intrinsic semantic quality. On the general-domain datasets, phrase-based concept approximations are strong, suggesting robustness across domains.

- Joint space and interpretability analyses: The paper examines how concepts relate to their phrases and how phrases relate to words (cosine and MAP distributions), and evaluates semantic-type clustering. These analyses provide insight into what is (and is not) captured by the embeddings, beyond headline scores.

- Resource contribution: The new similarity/relatedness datasets for real-world entities and the release of cui2vec are valuable to the community. Even with only “fair” IAA, such resources are scarce for entities and can seed further work.

- Careful hyperparameter study: The authors tune β, η, window size, negatives, and dimensionality across corpora and report trends (e.g., high β/η often hurts), helping future practitioners.

Weaknesses
- Limited novelty relative to prior art: The central idea—using ontology-mapped phrases in text to learn concept embeddings—is close to earlier biomedical work (e.g., De Vine et al., sequences of CUIs; Choi et al., code sequences), and joint word–entity embeddings exist (Wang et al., Toutanova et al.). The key difference here is simultaneous word–phrase–concept training with weak supervision and simple compositionality regularization. While practical, the conceptual novelty over existing methods is incremental.

- Intrinsic-only evaluation and small margins: The paper focuses on similarity/relatedness and clustering. The gains over strong baselines are often small or inconsistent (e.g., UMNSRS Similarity essentially ties CHIU-30; relatedness differences are marginal). No extrinsic tasks (entity linking, concept normalization, downstream clinical IE) are presented, limiting claims about usefulness.

- Ambiguity handling is simplistic: Treating every ambiguous phrase occurrence as equally indicative of all its candidate concepts (uniform updates scaled by |Cs|) introduces substantial noise. There is no attempt at even lightweight disambiguation (e.g., context-based gating, EM-style soft assignments), which likely caps performance. The analyses suggest concept embeddings often collapse toward averages of phrase/word embeddings, indicating limited non-lexical signal capture.

- Evaluation protocol concerns: Hyperparameters are tuned on the test sets via adapted cross-validation on small datasets without fixed train/dev/test splits, which risks overfitting. The new entity dataset has low inter-annotator agreement (κ=0.24), making it a noisy target for intrinsic evaluation and hyperparameter selection.

- Comparisons and ablations could be stronger: Key comparisons are to pre-trained external embeddings with different corpora/coverage. A controlled re-implementation of “CUI-sequence” training (De Vine-style) on the same corpora would strengthen claims. Ablations isolating the impact of β/η, phrase replacement, and ambiguity weighting (and reporting variance) would clarify where gains come from.

Questions to Authors
- Negative sampling/output space: You use a single negative-sampling matrix of size |W|×k, implying all targets (words/phrases/concepts) score against word contexts only. Did you try sampling/learning negative “contexts” from phrases or concepts as well? How sensitive are results to restricting contexts to untagged words?

- Ambiguity: What fraction of phrase mentions are ambiguous in your corpora, and how does ambiguity level affect concept quality? Did you test alternatives to uniform splitting (e.g., context similarity to candidate concept/phrase prototypes, or learned attention weights)?

- β and η: Beyond the aggregate trend that larger β/η can hurt, can you provide ablations on specific datasets quantifying how much each term helps, and examples where compositionality clearly improves (or harms) semantics?

- Fairness of biomedical baselines: Can you train the De Vine-style “CUI sequence” baseline on the same PM+CL corpus with your dictionary to control for corpus/domain effects? Similarly, have you tried retrofitting or graph-regularizing your concept embeddings with UMLS relations to test the claim about missing non-lexical information?

- General-domain entity dataset: Given κ=0.24, how robust are system rankings across different annotator subsets or after MACE-style denoising? Will you release raw annotations and item-wise reliability to facilitate re-use?

Suggestions for improvement
- Incorporate light-weight disambiguation during training (contextual gating, posterior reweighting over candidate concepts), which should reduce noise from polysemy and likely boost both intrinsic and extrinsic performance.

- Add extrinsic evaluations aligned with the task motivation: concept normalization (UMLS linking), entity linking/typing, downstream clinical or biomedical IE. Even small-scale experiments would substantiate the practical value of the joint space.

- Strengthen ablations and controlled baselines: Reproduce CUI-sequence training and simple word/phrase averaging on the same corpora; quantify the marginal utility of joint training and of β/η; analyze sensitivity to phrase-matching heuristics (greedy longest match, minimum frequency thresholds).

- Explore injecting ontology structure (hierarchies, relations) as an auxiliary objective to capture non-lexical constraints, and report whether this addresses the observed tendency toward lexical averaging.

- Improve dataset analysis: Report category-wise IAA, difficulty strata, and replicate stability; consider adding expert-annotated subsets for calibration.

Overall recommendation: Borderline reject. The paper is well-executed and useful in practice, with solid analyses and resources, but the methodological novelty and empirical gains are modest, and the lack of extrinsic validation limits impact. With stronger disambiguation, controlled baselines, and downstream evaluations, this work would be significantly more compelling.
