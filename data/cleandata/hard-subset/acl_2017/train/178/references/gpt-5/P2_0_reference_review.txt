Summary and main contributions
- The paper proposes a weakly supervised method to jointly embed ontology concepts, their surface phrases, and words into a shared vector space. The approach replaces known multiword phrases with unique tokens in an unannotated corpus and adapts skip-gram with negative sampling to train three embedding matrices (words, phrases, concepts). Two hyperparameters encourage optional compositionality between words→phrases (β) and phrases→concepts (η).
- It evaluates biomedical concept embeddings (UMLS) on MayoSRS and UMNSRS similarity/relatedness, and releases and evaluates on a new Mechanical Turk dataset for real-world entities (YAGO). Analyses examine compositionality, preservation of concept–phrase/phrase–word links, and semantic-type clustering. Code and the new dataset are provided.

As I see it, the main contributions are:
1) A scalable, weakly supervised pipeline to learn concept embeddings from phrase lists without human annotation or KB relation usage, jointly embedding words/phrases/concepts.
2) Empirical evidence that these embeddings are competitive on larger biomedical similarity tasks and adequate on a new entity similarity/relatedness dataset; plus diagnostic analyses of compositionality and clustering.
3) Release of resources: implementation (cui2vec) and a new real-world entity similarity/relatedness dataset.

Strengths
- Practicality and scalability: The approach requires only an ontology-derived phrase list and raw text, avoiding manual annotations or KB relation triples. Replacing phrases with single tokens is simple and robust; the method scales to hundreds of thousands of concepts (Table 1). This is valuable for domains like biomedicine where human annotation is costly.
- Competitive performance without supervision: On UMNSRS Similarity, the joint concept embeddings match strong word-only baselines (CONCEPTco 0.653 vs CHIU-30 0.653) and outperform other concept embeddings that used manual annotations or code co-occurrence (e.g., DEVINE, MENCIA). On relatedness, performance is close to the best word baselines (PHRASEco 0.591 vs CHIU-30 0.590), suggesting the distant supervision is effective despite noise.
- Cross-domain applicability and resource release: The same method applies to real-world entities (YAGO), with reasonable correlations and the release of a new similarity/relatedness dataset to fill a documented gap (no widely used entity-level similarity sets). Open-sourcing the code further helps reproducibility and adoption.
- Careful analyses: The paper provides compositionality analyses (cosine and MAP distributions) showing when phrases are compositional and when they are not, and a semantic-type clustering evaluation adapted from prior work. The exploration of β and η, and 5-fold hyperparameter tuning, show attention to stability and robustness.

Weaknesses
- Limited novelty and weak “jointness”: Methodologically, the contribution is incremental. Replacing phrases with unigrams and training skip-gram variants is well known; prior work has jointly embedded entities and words (e.g., Wang et al., Toutanova et al.). The “joint” connection here largely occurs via a shared negative-sampling matrix and optional regularizers; there is no principled modeling of ontology structure or entity relations, and concept updates distribute uniformly across all possible concepts for an ambiguous phrase. This raises questions about the true added value of the concept matrix beyond phrase embeddings.
- Mixed empirical gains; concept matrix adds little: In the general-domain evaluation, approximating concepts by averaging their phrase embeddings outperforms direct concept embeddings (APPROXPHRco > CONCEPTco in Table 5), and in biomedicine, simple word baselines with larger windows tie or outperform (CHIU-30 ties best concept result on UMNSRS Similarity; CHOI strongly outperforms on MayoSRS/MiniMayo SRS). Analyses show very high cosine between a concept and the average of its phrases, suggesting EC may not encode much beyond surface forms. Moreover, increasing β/η tends to hurt performance, suggesting the touted compositionality regularization is not beneficial in practice.
- Evaluation limitations and dataset concerns: The work relies heavily on intrinsic similarity/relatedness, which are known to have limited predictive power for downstream tasks. Hyperparameters are tuned on these test sets (albeit with an adapted CV), further blurring generalization. The new entity dataset has low inter-annotator agreement (κ=0.24) and involves substantial manual preselection; without comparison to existing entity-centric evaluations (e.g., entity linking, KB completion, entity typing), it is hard to draw strong conclusions.
- Methodological details that impact reproducibility: Important training specifics are underspecified. For example: distribution for negative sampling; whether negatives come only from words (ENS is |W|×k), which seems asymmetric for phrases/concepts; precise scheduling of updates as TT/TU are traversed; handling of overlapping/ambiguous phrase matches; case/normalization; the effect of the 25-frequency threshold on coverage; and the computational cost across corpora. The ambiguity handling (uniformly updating all candidate concepts) is particularly strong and could bias learning in favor of frequent ambiguous phrases.
- Ontology structure underused and weak clustering: Despite the claim that ontology structure is reflected, semantic type clustering MAP scores are modest (overall 0.12–0.22) and significantly below Choi et al. for many types. No exploitation of KB relations or graph structure is attempted, even though that might directly address the non-lexical semantics that current analyses indicate are missing.

Questions to authors
- Clarify the negative sampling: Are negatives drawn only from the word vocabulary (as ENS suggests)? If so, why not also sample from phrases/concepts when training those components? Did you try a unified output space?
- Ambiguity handling: You uniformly divide updates across all candidate concepts for a phrase. Did you try simple heuristics (e.g., prior over concepts, context similarity, sense priors) to reduce noise? How sensitive are results to high-ambiguity phrases?
- Training regimen: When iterating over TT and TU “in parallel,” is the context window always over the untagged stream while targets include words and completed phrases? Please detail the sampling of (w, p, o) triples per token and per epoch.
- External validity: Can you report at least one extrinsic task (e.g., UMLS concept normalization, entity linking, biomedical IR, or clinical risk prediction) to corroborate the intrinsic gains? Even a small-scale study would strengthen the case.
- Ethical/reproducibility: Were the clinical notes de-identified under IRB approval, and are the trained embeddings (not the raw text) from CLINICAL/PM+CL sharable? This affects reproducibility.

Overall assessment
A solid, practical, and scalable approach with competitive results and useful resources, but with limited methodological novelty and mixed empirical advantages over strong baselines. Strengthening the experimental story with extrinsic tasks and clearer methodological details, or incorporating ontology graph structure, would significantly increase the impact.
