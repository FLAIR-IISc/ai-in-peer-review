Summary
The paper proposes a weakly supervised method to jointly embed words, phrases, and ontology concepts into a shared vector space. It replaces known ontology phrases in a corpus with unique IDs, trains skip-gram with negative sampling on both tagged (phrase-collapsed) and untagged text, and updates concept embeddings whenever any of their surface forms appear. Two hyperparameters (β, η) softly encourage phrase-from-words and concept-from-phrases compositionality. The approach is evaluated on biomedical similarity/relatedness datasets and on two new crowdsourced datasets of real-world entity similarity/relatedness. Analyses examine compositionality and semantic type clustering.

Main contributions (as I see them)
1) A simple, scalable weakly supervised joint embedding framework for concepts, phrases, and words using only an ontology and raw text (no manual concept annotations in corpus).
2) A new crowdsourced dataset for similarity/relatedness of real-world entities (people/places/organizations).
3) Empirical analysis of the learned space (concept–phrase/phrase–word alignment and semantic type clustering across multiple corpora).

Strengths
- Practicality and scalability: The approach leverages existing ontologies and raw corpora (PubMed, clinical notes, Wikipedia/NYT), and scales to hundreds of thousands of concepts and up to ~3.5B tokens. No manual in-corpus concept labeling is required.
- Competitive intrinsic results: On large biomedical similarity tasks (UMNSRS Similarity), concept embeddings are competitive with strong baselines and match CHIU-30 on one dataset; phrase-based settings are solid on relatedness. The general-domain experiments show strong performance of phrase-based approximations.
- Cross-domain validation and analyses: The method is tested in biomedical and open-domain settings, with additional analyses on compositionality and semantic type clustering, revealing meaningful structure (e.g., concepts close to averages of their surface forms).
- Resource release: The claim to release code (“cui2vec”) and the new real-world entity similarity/relatedness datasets is valuable to the community.

Weaknesses
- Methodological and notation issues that impede reproducibility and clarity:
  - The “negative sampling matrix” ENS is defined as |W| × k (k appears to be the number of negative samples), which is dimensionally incoherent; negative samples need embeddings of size d. It is unclear whether there is a separate context/output embedding matrix (standard in skip-gram) and how positives/negatives share it across words/phrases/concepts.
  - The model uses k both for “sliding context window of k untagged words” and as a count of negatives; Table 3 then uses WS for window size. Variable reuse is confusing.
  - Equation (4) uses Cs, which is undefined (likely Cp). The loss appears to both sum and average over concepts but the notation is wrong/ambiguous.
  - The β/η “compositionality” terms maximize logσ(p·Wp) and logσ(c·Pc) with no negatives or proper distance penalties; this is an unusual and weak pressure to align vectors and may explain why optimal β/η are often zero (Table 3), undermining the paper’s main modeling idea.
  - The claim that information is shared “only via the shared negative sample embedding matrix” is either incorrect or imprecise: positives also dot with the context “o” embedding, whose location is not clearly defined.
- Preprocessing and ambiguity handling are underspecified:
  - Phrase detection: case sensitivity, tokenization, longest-match-first, overlap resolution, sentence boundary issues, and alignment between TT and TU are not detailed.
  - Ambiguity: The assumption that any mention of a phrase expresses “each concept it can refer to,” with uniform normalization, injects large noise (especially for highly ambiguous names). There is no quantitative analysis of ambiguity prevalence or its impact (e.g., per-phrase polysemy vs. performance).
  - Sampling: No details on negative sampling distribution (e.g., unigram^0.75), subsampling of frequent words, dynamic windowing, or optimizer and schedules beyond α.
- Evaluation and claims:
  - Hyperparameters are tuned on the evaluation sets (no train/dev/test split), despite acknowledgment of this problem; the “adapted 5-fold CV” is not standard for Spearman rank correlation and likely optimistic.
  - Improvements vs. strong baselines are modest or tied (e.g., UMNSRS Similarity ties with CHIU-30), and in the open-domain experiments the best performance comes from approximating concepts via phrases (APPROXPHRco) rather than the learned concept vectors, weakening the added value of concept embeddings.
  - The new crowdsourced datasets have low IAA (κ=0.24), raising concerns about label reliability; details about entity disambiguation (exact YAGO/Wikipedia IDs presented to annotators) are not fully clear.
- Presentation quality:
  - Numerous typos and formatting issues (e.g., “literaure,” “many-tomany,” broken hyphenation “scle- rosis,” Table 4 line break missing, inconsistent notation).
  - Reference list contains many formatting errors and incomplete/incorrect entries (missing years, odd escaping “et al\.,?,” inconsistent author formatting, missing venues/pages).
- Novelty and positioning:
  - Closely related work (e.g., De Vine et al., Choi et al., Wang et al. 2014a) is not contrasted rigorously. The practical novelty vs. “embedding sequences of CUIs” or “joint text–KB embeddings” is incremental; the main advertised novelty (β/η joint compositional constraints) appears ineffective (often tuned to zero).

Questions to Authors
1) Please clarify the embedding architecture: Do you maintain a separate context/output embedding matrix shared across words/phrases/concepts? What exactly is ENS (dimensions, role, and how positives access context embeddings)?
2) Fix and restate the loss functions (especially Eq. 4–7): define all symbols, correct Cs vs. Cp, and justify the choice of logσ(dot) without negatives for β/η terms. Did you try L2 distance or KL penalties?
3) How do you perform phrase matching and alignment between TT and TU (tokenization, casing, longest-match-first, overlap, sentence boundaries)? How are multiword contexts extracted consistently?
4) How do you handle phrase–concept ambiguity quantitatively (distribution of |Cp|, ablation restricting to unambiguous phrases, impact on performance)? Any heuristic disambiguation (e.g., type constraints or P(C|p) priors)?
5) What is the negative sampling distribution, subsampling of frequent tokens, dynamic windowing, optimizer, batch size, and learning-rate schedule? Provide training time/hardware, vocabulary sizes retained, and memory footprint.
6) For the new datasets: What exact entity identifiers and surface forms were shown to annotators? Could you report bootstrap confidence intervals and statistical significance for differences in ρ? Can you release the data?
7) Given that β/η often tune to 0, what is the practical gain of the proposed compositionality mechanism? Can you show a task where η>0 helps over strong baselines?
8) Several claims seem overstated (e.g., “highest correlation” when tied; “no annotation” while relying on curated ontologies). Please recalibrate claims and fix references/typos.
