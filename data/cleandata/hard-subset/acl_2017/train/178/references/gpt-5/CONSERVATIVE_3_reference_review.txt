Summary and main contributions
- The paper proposes a weakly supervised method to jointly embed words, surface phrases, and ontology concepts into a shared SGNS-style vector space. Phrase mentions in raw text (greedily matched from ontology aliases) are replaced with unique IDs; concept embeddings are updated using the contexts of their phrase forms. Two regularizers (β, η) softly tie phrase embeddings to averages of their words and concept embeddings to averages of their phrases.
- The approach is evaluated intrinsically on biomedical (UMLS) and general-domain (YAGO) concept similarity/relatedness, plus semantic-type clustering. The authors also release a new crowdsourced dataset of entity similarity/relatedness and a software implementation (cui2vec).
- As I see it, the primary contributions are: (1) a simple distant-supervision pipeline for learning concept/phrase/word embeddings without manual concept annotation; (2) a cross-domain intrinsic evaluation with some analyses; (3) a new entity similarity/relatedness dataset.

Strengths
- Straightforward, scalable pipeline leveraging existing ontologies. The method requires no manual concept annotation and avoids training a concept tagger, using greedy alias matching and SGNS. This makes it easy to run at scale over large corpora (PubMed, Wikipedia/Gigaword).
- Competitive intrinsic results on larger biomedical datasets. On UMNSRS Similarity, the concept embeddings are at or near the best reported performance; relatedness is competitive with strong word baselines (e.g., CHIU-30). This suggests the weakly supervised signals are not entirely drowned by alias ambiguity.
- Useful resources. Releasing code/embeddings (cui2vec) and a new entity similarity/relatedness dataset is valuable for reproducibility and future benchmarking, even if the latter’s agreement is only fair.
- Empirical analyses beyond headline scores. The paper examines concept–phrase–word neighborhoods and semantic-type clustering, offering some insight into what the space captures and where it fails (e.g., “sign or symptom”).

Weaknesses
- Limited novelty relative to established joint text–KB embedding work. Prior methods embed entities and words jointly from text (e.g., Wang et al., 2014; Toutanova et al., 2015), and biomedical work has embedded CUIs via automatically tagged corpora (De Vine et al., 2014) or code sequences (Choi et al., 2016). The proposed training is a relatively incremental variant of SGNS with alias substitution and simple regularizers. The claim of “jointly embedding concepts, phrases, and words” is not sufficiently differentiated from these established approaches.
- Distant supervision assumption is very noisy and insufficiently analyzed. The model equally updates all concepts that an alias can represent (normalized by count), with no disambiguation. This is a strong assumption, especially in UMLS where alias ambiguity is common. The paper does not quantify ambiguity rates, nor ablate the impact of ambiguity on performance (e.g., filtering highly ambiguous phrases or weighting by priors). Without such analysis, it is hard to assess robustness or when the method will fail.
- Evaluation is almost entirely intrinsic and leverages datasets with known limitations. The reliance on similarity/relatedness and clustering—tasks with weak correlation to downstream utility—leaves the practical value unproven. No extrinsic tasks (entity linking/concept normalization, retrieval, coding, IE) are reported, despite being natural fits. Moreover, the new entity datasets have low inter-annotator agreement (Fleiss’ κ=0.24), which undermines their strength as an evaluation benchmark.
- Benefit of concept embeddings over simple approximations is unclear. In several settings, averaging phrase embeddings (APPROXPHRco) matches or outperforms direct concept embeddings (CONCEPTco), and the best general-domain results are attained by the phrase-based approximation. The compositionality analysis also suggests that concepts are very similar to the mean of their phrases but not close to individual phrases. This weakens the case that the proposed concept training adds substantial value beyond pooling phrase representations.
- Potential issues in experimental rigor and clarity. 
  - Hyperparameters are tuned on the same intrinsic datasets being reported (even with an “adapted” CV), risking overfitting; no statistical significance testing is provided for differences that are often small.
  - Baselines are not fully matched for data: CHIU word embeddings are trained on PubMed abstracts only, while the proposed method uses PM+CL; for general-domain, the method uses WIKINYT while baselines differ. It is unclear whether comparisons are strictly fair.
  - Methodological details are sometimes confusing (e.g., definition and shape of the negative sampling matrix ENS, which is nonstandard; unclear whether there are separate input/output embeddings). Notational inconsistencies in the loss (e.g., c∈Cs vs Cp) hinder reproducibility.
- Missing ablations and diagnostics. There is no ablation of the β/η regularizers (beyond tuning), no analysis of the impact of sharing only via negative samples, and no comparison to simpler alternatives (e.g., phrase-only SGNS, or retrofitting concepts into a word/phrase space). Runtime/memory and coverage trade-offs (min freq ≥25) are not explored.

Questions to authors
- Ambiguity: What proportion of phrases are mapped to multiple concepts (per corpus)? How sensitive are results to excluding highly ambiguous phrases or to weighting updates by alias–concept priors?
- Disambiguation: Did you try a simple disambiguation heuristic (e.g., vector similarity of context to candidate concept/phrase centroids) before updating concept embeddings? How does this affect performance?
- Regularizers: In the tuned settings, η is very often 0. Does η ever help materially? Can you provide ablations with β/η on/off and their impact on each task?
- Negative sampling and parameterization: Please clarify the architecture. Do you use separate input/output embeddings as in SGNS? What is ENS exactly (its shape, role), and is it the only shared component among words, phrases, and concepts?
- Baseline fairness: Can you provide baselines trained on the exact same corpora (e.g., CHIU-style word embeddings on PM+CL; phrase-only embeddings on WIKINYT) and report significance tests?
- Extrinsic tasks: Have you evaluated on concept normalization/entity linking (UMLS/YAGO), code prediction, or IR? Given the known limitations of similarity/relatedness tasks, at least one downstream evaluation would strengthen the paper markedly.
- Dataset reliability: Given κ=0.24, can you report per-pair variance, filter to higher-agreement subsets, or provide calibration analyses? Do conclusions hold on a high-agreement slice?
- Error analysis: Where does the method fail most (e.g., highly polysemous phrases, rare concepts, overlapping aliases)? Any qualitative examples would help.
- Resource release: Will you release the trained embeddings for all corpora and the exact phrase match lists used? For the clinical corpus (non-public), can you ensure reproducibility on public subsets?
- Ontology structure: Since you already use ontologies, did you attempt incorporating hierarchy/relations (as in Wang et al., 2014) to mitigate ambiguity and improve clustering? If not, why, and how do you expect it would interact with your method?
