Summary and main contributions
The paper proposes a weakly supervised method to jointly learn embeddings for words, phrases, and ontology concepts in a shared space. The key idea is to use an ontology-provided mapping from concepts to phrases as distant supervision: occurrences of known phrases in an unlabeled corpus are replaced with phrase IDs and used to update concept, phrase, and word embeddings within a skip-gram with negative sampling framework. Two regularizers control compositionality: β encourages phrase vectors to align with the average of their words; η encourages concept vectors to align with the average of their phrases. The approach is evaluated on biomedical (UMLS) and general-domain (YAGO) concepts using intrinsic similarity/relatedness benchmarks, provides analyses on compositionality and semantic type clustering, and introduces a new crowdsourced dataset for real-world entity similarity/relatedness. Code/datasets are said to be released (cui2vec).

Contributions (as I see them), ordered by significance:
1) A scalable, weakly supervised joint embedding framework that places ontology concepts, their lexical forms, and words in a single vector space without requiring manual sense annotations or KB relation triples.
2) Empirical evidence that such concept embeddings are competitive on biomedical similarity/relatedness (especially on larger sets), along with insightful analyses of compositionality and semantic type clustering.
3) A new crowdsourced dataset for similarity/relatedness of real-world entities (people, places, organizations).

Strengths
- Practical and scalable weak supervision: The method leverages only concept–phrase dictionaries from ontologies and unlabeled text. No sense annotations, KB links, or relation triples are required. This opens the door to large-scale concept embedding in domains where annotated corpora are scarce. The corpora used are large (PubMed, clinical notes, Wikipedia+NYT), and the method handles hundreds of thousands of concepts/phrases.
- Joint space for words, phrases, and concepts: Having all three levels co-embedded is useful for downstream applications (e.g., entity linking, normalization, query expansion). The analyses (Figures 3 and 4) provide a nuanced picture of how phrases relate to words and how concepts relate to phrases, revealing when compositionality holds and when it doesn’t.
- Competitive intrinsic performance with minimal supervision: On UMNSRS Similarity (the larger biomedical set), the concept embeddings match or slightly exceed strong baselines (e.g., CHIU-30 words), and they are competitive on relatedness. This is noteworthy given that some compared methods rely on manual coding (CHOI) or pre-annotated sequences (DEVINE).
- Careful hyperparameter study and transparency: The paper acknowledges sensitivity to hyperparameters, performs cross-validated tuning, and reports per-corpus optimal settings. The β/η study offers insight into when compositionality helps (generally small gains; higher values often hurt).
- New evaluation resource for general entities: The crowdsourced entity similarity/relatedness sets help evaluate beyond words and are a practical contribution for future work.

Weaknesses
- Limited novelty relative to prior embedding frameworks: The core method is largely standard skip-gram with (i) phrase replacement and (ii) averaged regularizers between words→phrases and phrases→concepts. Similar uses of phrase IDs, word averaging, and distant supervision exist in the literature. The model does not incorporate ontology graph structure (hierarchy/relations) beyond the lexicon mapping, which constrains its potential to capture non-lexical, conceptual knowledge (a point the authors also note).
- Inconsistent and modest gains on benchmarks: On smaller biomedical sets (MayoSRS, Mini Mayo), CHOI’s code embeddings substantially outperform the proposed method. On UMNSRS Relatedness, improvements are small and sometimes behind word-only baselines. On the new general-domain dataset, phrase-based approximations often outperform direct concept vectors, suggesting the learned concept layer may add limited value beyond lexical signals.
- Distant supervision introduces ambiguity and noise without disambiguation: The assumption that every occurrence of a mapped phrase signals all its possible concepts (with uniform updates) is a strong and noisy simplification, especially in the general domain. No attempt is made to disambiguate or weight likely concepts per instance, and the potential impact of polysemy/homonymy is not quantified. This could systematically bias concept vectors toward surface-level co-occurrence rather than conceptual meaning.
- Evaluation is mostly intrinsic, with tuning on test sets: Despite an adapted cross-validation, intrinsic correlation tasks remain susceptible to overfitting and are known to be weak predictors of downstream utility. No downstream tasks (e.g., entity linking, concept normalization, retrieval, clinical prediction) are reported to demonstrate practical benefits of joint concept embeddings.
- Fairness and completeness of baselines: Some baselines rely on different supervision or different corpora; others are word-only. It would be helpful to include stronger direct competitors that also use ontology mapping without KB relations (if available) and to normalize for corpus/domain differences where possible. An ablation showing gains over simpler phrase-ID skip-gram without concept updates would isolate the contribution of the concept layer and the β/η regularizers.

Questions to authors
- Ambiguity handling: How many phrases in each ontology are ambiguous (map to multiple concepts), and how often do such phrases occur in the corpora? Can you report performance stratified by unambiguous vs ambiguous phrase coverage? Did you try instance-level weighting (e.g., prior probabilities per concept) instead of uniform splitting?
- Ontology coverage and thresholds: The 25-occurrence threshold excludes long-tail concepts. What fraction of ontology concepts are covered under your thresholds in each corpus? How sensitive are results to this threshold?
- Shared negative sampling/context representations: You mention a single negative sampling matrix ENS of size |W| x k that is shared. Please clarify the architecture: Are phrases/concepts always predicting word-contexts only? Are there separate input/output embeddings for words vs phrases vs concepts? A schematic of parameter sharing would help.
- β and η effects: The supplement notes small or negative trends for larger β/η. Could you add an ablation showing performance with β=η=0 vs β>0, η>0, to quantify the independent contribution of each regularizer?
- Resource release and reproducibility: Will you release the new entity similarity/relatedness datasets and code/scripts for phrase tagging and training? Please provide links and licensing. Also, can you report training time/memory for the largest runs?
- Downstream utility: Do you have preliminary results on any downstream task (e.g., concept normalization/entity linking in biomedical abstracts or Wikipedia) to demonstrate that concept-level embeddings outperform word/phrase-only baselines in practice? If not, can you outline plans or expected use cases?

Overall: This is a practical and well-executed weakly supervised framework with solid analyses and useful resources. The methodological novelty is moderate and the intrinsic gains are mixed; adding disambiguation, leveraging ontology graph structure, and demonstrating downstream impact would considerably strengthen the paper. I lean toward borderline accept if resource release and clarity issues are addressed; otherwise borderline reject.
