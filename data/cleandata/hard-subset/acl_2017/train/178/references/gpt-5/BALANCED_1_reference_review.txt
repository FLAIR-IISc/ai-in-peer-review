Summary and contributions
This paper proposes a weakly supervised approach to jointly embed ontology concepts, their surface phrases, and words in a shared vector space. The method adapts skip-gram with negative sampling, using distant supervision from concept–phrase mappings (UMLS, YAGO): phrase mentions in text are treated as occurrences of all concepts that phrase can denote, and concept embeddings are updated using the surrounding word context. Two regularizers (β, η) softly encourage phrase ≈ average(word) and concept ≈ average(phrases). The model is trained on large corpora (biomedical abstracts and clinical notes; Wikipedia+NYT). Evaluation focuses on intrinsic similarity/relatedness correlations and analyses of concept–phrase/word alignment and semantic-type clustering. The authors also release cui2vec and a new MTurk dataset of entity similarity/relatedness.

Contributions (as I see them), in order of strength:
1) A scalable, annotation-free joint embedding method that integrates concepts, phrases, and words via distant supervision, with simple compositionality regularizers.
2) An empirical study showing competitive performance on biomedical similarity (UMNSRS similarity: ρ≈0.653 on PM+CL) and analyses indicating nontrivial structure (type clustering, concept–phrase proximity), plus a new general-domain entity similarity/relatedness dataset (688 pairs each, κ≈0.24).
3) Software and dataset release that can facilitate further research.

Strengths
- Practical and scalable weak supervision: The approach leverages existing ontologies and raw text without requiring manual concept annotations, enabling coverage of large vocabularies (e.g., 193k UMLS concepts; ~749k YAGO entities with frequency thresholding). This is a meaningful contribution for domains where annotation is costly.
- Competitive intrinsic performance where scale matters: On larger biomedical sets, concept embeddings perform on par with strong word baselines (UMNSRS similarity: CONCEPTco 0.653 ≈ CHIU-30 0.653) and better than several concept baselines (e.g., De Vine 0.455). In general-domain evaluation, concept/phrase-based settings (APPROXPHRco/CONCEPTco) achieve the top correlations (e.g., relatedness up to 0.810), suggesting the joint space is useful beyond biomedicine.
- Joint space analysis is thoughtful: The compositionality and MAP analyses provide insight into how concepts, phrases, and words are arranged. The observed clustering by semantic type (mixed but often competitive with prior work) gives evidence that the space encodes ontology structure to a degree despite noisy supervision.
- Method is simple, reproducible, and well-motivated: Adapting skip-gram with parallel tagged/untagged corpora and adding interpretable regularizers (β, η) is elegant. The paper discusses hyperparameter tuning across corpora and provides implementation details and resources (cui2vec), enhancing reproducibility and utility.

Weaknesses
- Limited novelty relative to prior joint/knowledge-aware embeddings: The core idea—jointly embedding entities/concepts and text using distant supervision—has strong precedents (e.g., Wang et al., 2014a; De Vine et al., 2014; sense/entity embeddings). The technical delta is modest: soft regularizers and a particular way of tying concept updates to phrase contexts without using KB relations. The method does not incorporate ontology graph structure (relations/types), which prior works show can add substantial signal.
- Intrinsic-only evaluation and tuning on test sets: The paper relies on similarity/relatedness correlations, with acknowledged pitfalls (e.g., Chiu et al., 2016b). No extrinsic tasks (entity linking/concept normalization, code assignment, retrieval, WSD) are reported, limiting claims about practical utility. Hyperparameters are tuned via an adapted cross-validation on the evaluation sets, which still risks overfitting; no statistical significance tests are provided for correlation differences that are often small.
- Weak handling of ambiguity in distant supervision: For polysemous phrases, concept updates are averaged uniformly over all candidate concepts. This is a strong assumption likely to inject noise and bias concepts toward surface form contexts. The analyses support this: concepts are very close to averages of their phrases, and phrases to averages of words; the method may capture lexicalized usage more than non-lexical conceptual properties. The clustering results (e.g., overall MAP below Choi et al.) and the need for low β/η to optimize correlations further suggest limited abstraction.
- Methodological clarity and design choices: The “single negative sampling matrix” tied to words (and not phrases/concepts) is unusual and under-justified. It is unclear why negatives are restricted to words only and how this impacts phrase/concept representations. Preprocessing choices (greedy phrase matching, handling overlaps/variants/case) are likely to matter but are only briefly described. Frequency thresholds (≥25 occurrences) and corpus mixing may advantage/disadvantage baselines with different coverage, and fairness of comparisons (e.g., supervised CHOI vs. weakly supervised) is not fully discussed.
- New entity dataset is small with low agreement: The general-domain dataset has fair agreement (κ≈0.24), limited size (688 pairs), and narrow entity types (persons, places, organizations). While useful, it is not a strong benchmark; comparisons to existing entity relatedness datasets are missing, and quality controls could be stronger.

Questions to authors
- How frequent is phrase–concept ambiguity in your training data? Did you experiment with non-uniform weighting (e.g., priors, context similarity, EM) instead of uniform averaging over concepts?
- How are overlapping/nested phrase matches resolved beyond “greedy over length”? Do you handle case, morphology, or punctuation variants in matching? What is the approximate precision of the phrase tagger?
- Why are negative samples drawn only from word outputs? Did you try sampling from phrases/concepts or a shared output space, and how did that affect learning?
- Did you assess statistical significance of correlation differences (e.g., via bootstrap)? Many gaps are small.
- Can you report any extrinsic results (e.g., UMLS concept normalization, biomedical IR, entity linking) or planned evaluations?
- Could you incorporate ontology edges/types as regularizers (e.g., TransE/retrofitting-style losses) and report gains, especially on clustering and small-set benchmarks?

Suggestions for improvement
- Add a lightweight disambiguation mechanism for ambiguous phrase mentions (contextual attention, EM over concept assignments, or use ontology priors), and compare to uniform averaging.
- Incorporate ontology structure (typed edges, hierarchy) as an auxiliary objective to enhance conceptual abstraction; report ablations with β/η and structural losses.
- Include extrinsic evaluations representative of target applications (entity/concept linking, code assignment, clinical outcome prediction using concept features).
- Strengthen evaluation rigor: held-out tuning, statistical testing, error analyses, and comparisons to existing entity relatedness benchmarks where applicable.
- Clarify preprocessing and release detailed scripts/resources enabling exact reproduction (phrase matching rules, vocabularies, mappings).

Overall, this is a solid, practical paper with useful resources, but the methodological novelty and evaluation strength are limited. Strengthening the ambiguity modeling, adding structural signals, and demonstrating extrinsic utility would significantly improve the contribution.
