- Proposes a weakly supervised method to jointly embed words, phrases, and ontology concepts by treating ontology phrase mappings as distant supervision and adapting skip-gram with negative sampling plus two compositionality hyperparameters.

- Evaluated on biomedical (UMLS) tasks and a new crowdsourced YAGO entity similarity/relatedness dataset, with analyses of compositionality and semantic-type clustering.

- Contribution: simple, scalable joint training that learns concept embeddings from concept–phrase mappings and raw text without mention annotations or KB relations.

- Contribution: thorough intrinsic evaluation across domains, comparisons to biomedical baselines, and analyses of compositionality and semantic-type clustering; released cui2vec software.

- Contribution: new human similarity/relatedness dataset for real-world entities and released resources.

- Strength: practical, annotation-light approach scalable to very large ontologies and corpora via greedy phrase replacement and shared context modeling.

- Strength: competitive biomedical performance at scale (outperforms some prior concept models on UMNSRS and is competitive with strong word baselines).

- Strength: informative analytical insights (concepts close to mean of phrase embeddings; bimodal phrase–word relations; mixed semantic-type clustering, notable gap for “sign or symptom”).

- Strength: cross-domain applicability and community value from released dataset and tooling.

- Strength: clear articulation of modeling trade-offs via β and η hyperparameters and honest reporting that larger β/η often harm performance.

- Weakness: limited novelty — jointness mainly via shared context/output matrix and co-occurrence; β/η regularizers often tuned to zero, making coupling weak compared to prior joint models.

- Weakness: coarse, noisy distant supervision that treats ambiguous phrase occurrences uniformly as all candidate concepts without sense priors, heuristics, or latent assignment inference.

- Weakness: evaluation design issues — hyperparameter tuning risks test-set overfitting, new dataset has low inter-annotator agreement (κ=0.24), and lacks comparisons to strong general-domain entity baselines.

- Weakness: objective and notation under-specified/atypical — unclear whether context vectors are separate, confusing ENS/|W|×k vs d notation, and β/η penalties use logσ(dot) rather than standard symmetric distance penalties.

- Weakness: mixed/modest gains vs strong baselines — clinical code-based embeddings outperformed on some sets and phrase approximations sometimes beat direct concept embeddings.

- Question: clarify whether context word vectors are separate from input embeddings, define ENS/output matrix, reconcile k vs d notation, and specify where ~o resides.

- Question: how are negative samples drawn (unigram^3/4?), are negatives always words, and do phrases/concepts ever serve as contexts or negatives?

- Question: did you try non-uniform phrase→concept priors (frequency-based) or EM/heuristic disambiguation (type filtering), and what were the effects?

- Question: why use logσ(~p·Wp) for β/η regularization instead of explicit distance/cosine/L2 penalties, and were alternatives or annealing strategies compared?

- Question: include KG-based entity baselines (TransE/DistMult/ComplEx) or Wikipedia entity-linking embeddings for YAGO to better contextualize general-domain results?

- Question: provide coverage/preprocessing statistics — fraction of concepts with surface forms ≥25 occurrences, effects of greedy longest-match replacement, average ambiguity |Cp|, and correlation with performance?

- Question: will the new entity dataset and trained embeddings be released, and can more clinical corpus statistics or surrogates be shared to aid reproducibility?

- Overall: a practical, scalable approach addressing an important problem with useful analyses and resources, but limited modeling novelty, noisy supervision, under-specified design choices, and evaluation gaps—especially in general-domain baselines and robustness.
