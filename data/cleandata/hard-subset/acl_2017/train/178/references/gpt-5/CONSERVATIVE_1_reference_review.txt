Summary
The paper proposes a weakly supervised method to jointly embed concepts, their surface phrases, and words into a shared vector space. It uses ontology-provided mappings from concepts to phrases as distant supervision: phrases in text are greedily identified and replaced with unique identifiers; concept vectors are trained by the contexts of all their mapped phrases using a skip-gram/negative sampling objective. Two additional hyperparameters (β, η) softly encourage phrase–word and concept–phrase compositionality. Experiments on UMLS/YAGO with PubMed, clinical notes, and Wikipedia/NYT report intrinsic evaluations (similarity/relatedness; a new entity similarity/relatedness dataset; semantic-type clustering). Results are competitive with some annotated baselines, and resources (code/models/dataset) are claimed to be released.

Contributions (as I see them)
1) A practical, scalable adaptation of skip-gram that learns joint word/phrase/concept embeddings using only phrase–concept dictionaries from ontologies (no manual annotations).
2) Resource contribution: large-scale trained embeddings (cui2vec) and a small human-rated entity similarity/relatedness dataset.
3) Empirical evidence that weakly supervised concept embeddings can be competitive with some methods trained on manually annotated signals on intrinsic evaluations, plus some analyses of compositionality and clustering.

Strengths
- Practicality and scalability: The method is simple, computationally feasible, and leverages established training (skip-gram with negative sampling). It can scale to hundreds of thousands of concepts with no manual annotation, which is valuable in domains like biomedicine where annotation is costly.
- Sensible problem framing: Treating concepts atomically (rather than relying purely on compositional phrase/word representations) is well-motivated for multiword expressions and synonymy.
- Competitive intrinsic results at scale: On UMNSRS Similarity, the proposed concept embeddings are among the strongest; on relatedness they are competitive with strong word baselines. This is notable given the weak supervision.
- Resource release: Making embeddings and a dataset public (caveat on clinical data) increases potential impact and reproducibility.
- Analysis beyond topline numbers: The compositionality analysis and semantic-type clustering provide some qualitative/diagnostic insight into what the model learns.

Weaknesses
- Limited novelty relative to prior joint embedding work: The approach is largely an adaptation of skip-gram with distant supervision from phrase–concept dictionaries. Related efforts such as Wang et al. (2014a), Toutanova et al. (2015), and several biomedical concept-embedding works already embed entities/concepts with text signals and/or KB structure. The main new elements (uniform concept updating for ambiguous phrases; β/η regularization) are incremental and not convincingly shown to be essential.
- Evaluation relies almost entirely on intrinsic metrics, with concerns: No extrinsic tasks (e.g., entity linking, concept normalization, WSD, clinical coding prediction) are reported, despite the authors acknowledging that intrinsic tasks may not predict downstream performance. The new entity similarity/relatedness dataset has low agreement (Fleiss’ κ = 0.24), limiting its reliability; moreover, the proposed concept embeddings are not superior to phrase-based approximations on these sets.
- Baseline coverage is incomplete for the general domain: There is no comparison against strong text+entity baselines that exploit Wikipedia anchors/links (e.g., joint word–entity embeddings from hyperlinks) or knowledge graph + text joint training (e.g., TransE/TransH variants augmented with text), despite these being highly relevant for YAGO/Wikipedia entities. In biomedicine, comparison to simple retrofitting (Faruqui et al.) or to concept-normalization pipelines is missing.
- Ambiguity/noise modeling is weak and unquantified: The method assumes that any occurrence of an ambiguous phrase contributes equally to all its possible concepts (uniform averaging). This is a strong assumption likely to inject substantial noise; the paper does not quantify the prevalence of ambiguity, its impact, or test alternative weightings (e.g., frequency-based priors, simple string-level WSD). No ablation isolates the effect of ambiguity handling.
- Risk of overfitting in hyperparameter tuning and under-specified training details: Hyperparameters are tuned on the same test sets via an adapted cross-validation protocol; given the small sizes of some datasets, the risk of optimistic bias is non-trivial. Some implementation choices are unclear: the “shared negative sampling matrix” is unusual (negatives apparently drawn only from words?), the negative sampling distribution is unspecified, and preprocessing (phrase matching policy, casing, overlapping/embedded phrases) is only briefly described. These choices can materially affect results and comparability.

Questions to Authors
1) Ambiguity: What proportion of phrases map to multiple concepts in your training sets? How much of the concept updates come from ambiguous phrases? Have you tried simple disambiguation heuristics (most frequent sense from ontology priors, context string overlap) or non-uniform weighting, and what is the impact?
2) Negative sampling: From which vocabulary are negatives drawn for word/phrase/concept updates (words only, phrases, concepts, or their union)? What distribution exponents are used (e.g., unigram^0.75)? Please clarify the role and necessity of the “shared negative sampling matrix.”
3) β/η effects: Please provide ablations demonstrating the gains from β and η beyond the base model, ideally on a held-out set. When do these terms help/hurt, and why?
4) External baselines: Can you compare against joint word–entity embeddings trained with Wikipedia anchors and/or KB+text models (e.g., TransE variants with textual mentions), especially for the YAGO/Wikipedia setting?
5) Extrinsic evaluation: Can you report results on at least one downstream task where concept/entity embeddings are directly useful (e.g., biomedical concept normalization on BC5CDR/NCBI Disease, medical code prediction, or standard entity linking like AIDA-CoNLL)?
6) Resource availability: Which corpora/models will you release? If clinical models cannot be shared, can you reproduce the main results using only public data?

Overall, while the approach is practical and shows competitive intrinsic results, the methodological novelty is limited and the evaluation does not yet provide sufficiently rigorous evidence (especially extrinsic) to justify acceptance. I would encourage strengthening the work with stronger baselines, ambiguity modeling, and downstream evaluations.
