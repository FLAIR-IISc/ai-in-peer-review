Summary
This paper proposes a weakly supervised method to jointly embed words, surface phrases, and ontology concepts into a shared vector space. The approach replaces occurrences of ontology-linked phrases in raw text with unique identifiers, then trains a skip-gram model with negative sampling over parallel tagged/untagged corpora. It introduces two hyperparameters to softly tie phrases to the average of their word vectors (β) and concepts to the average of their phrase vectors (η), thus trading off compositionality vs. independence. The method is evaluated on biomedical similarity/relatedness datasets (MayoSRS, MiniMayoSRS, UMNSRS) and on a new crowdsourced similarity/relatedness dataset for real-world entities. Additional analyses probe compositionality and semantic-type clustering. Code (cui2vec) and the new dataset are released.

Claimed/main contributions (as I see them; strongest first)
1) A simple, scalable weakly supervised framework that jointly embeds words, phrases, and ontology concepts without manual concept annotations, using only dictionary phrase mappings on unannotated corpora.
2) Empirical evaluation across biomedical and general domains, including a new crowdsourced similarity/relatedness dataset for entities, and analyses of compositionality and semantic-type clustering.
3) A practical regularization mechanism (β, η) to control phrase-by-words and concept-by-phrases compositionality during training.

Strengths
- Practicality and scalability with minimal supervision. The method leverages existing ontology phrase dictionaries and raw text, avoiding expensive manual concept annotations and KB relation triples. It scales to millions of documents (e.g., 2.8B tokens on PM+CL, 3.5B on WikiNYT) and hundreds of thousands of concepts (Table 1), which is valuable for domains like biomedicine.

- Competitive intrinsic performance with stronger baselines. On UMNSRS Similarity, CONCEPTco achieves the best Spearman ρ=0.653 (tied with CHIU-30), and is competitive on Relatedness (0.559; PHRASEco 0.591 ≈ CHIU-30 0.590). On the new general-domain dataset, concept/phrase-based models perform strongly (e.g., APPROXPHRco: 0.810 relatedness, 0.766 similarity; CONCEPTco: 0.789, 0.750), indicating the approach transfers beyond biomedicine (Tables 4–5).

- Thoughtful analyses of the shared embedding space. The compositionality analysis shows that concept vectors are close to the mean of their phrases (Fig. 3a) and that phrase-vs-word compositionality is variable (Fig. 3b/c). The MAP analysis (Fig. 4) provides a nuanced view of how phrases/words distribute around parents. The semantic-type clustering evaluation (Table 6) contextualizes performance relative to medical code embeddings.

- Resource contribution. Releasing cui2vec and a new entity similarity/relatedness dataset (with collection details and quality controls) is useful for the community, regardless of the final decision.

Weaknesses
- Limited methodological novelty. The core model is a straightforward adaptation of skip-gram with phrase replacement, plus two regularization terms. Prior work has jointly embedded entities and words (e.g., Wang et al., 2014a) or embedded concepts via distant supervision (De Vine et al., 2014), and distant supervision from surface forms is well-established. The paper’s main novelty lies in joint training of words/phrases/concepts with β/η controls, which feels incremental.

- Ambiguity handling is naive. Each ambiguous phrase occurrence uniformly updates all candidate concepts (Eq. 4/7), introducing substantial noise. No mention disambiguation is attempted (e.g., attention, context classifiers, EM). This likely caps performance (notably MayoSRS where CHOI’s human-annotated codes dominate) and undermines the claim that ontology structure is “reflected” in the embeddings.

- Evaluation is primarily intrinsic and tuning may conflate test and dev. The work relies on similarity/relatedness correlations and clustering; there are no downstream tasks such as entity linking, concept normalization, or clinical retrieval to demonstrate utility. The authors acknowledge tuning on the test sets and attempt an adapted 5-fold CV, but this is not a clean separation and may inflate reported numbers. No statistical significance is reported for Spearman differences.

- The new general-domain dataset has low agreement. Fleiss’ κ≈0.24 (“fair”) suggests noisy gold standards. While the collection protocol is described and filters applied, the low IAA limits the strength of conclusions from Tables 5. It would help to analyze performance conditioned on high-agreement subsets and to release clearer reliability diagnostics.

- Some design choices are under-justified or asymmetric. Negative samples are drawn only from words (ENS is |W|×k), which biases training toward predicting word contexts; phrases/concepts are never negatives for each other. This asymmetry, coupled with observations that concepts are very close to averages of phrases (Fig. 3a), suggests the model may not capture non-lexical conceptual information. Moreover, the claim that ontology structure is “reflected” is only weakly supported (Table 6 shows inferior overall MAP to CHOI).

Questions to authors
1) How sensitive are results to the ambiguous-phrase update rule? Have you tried context-weighted updates (e.g., softmax attention over candidate concepts) or EM-style hard assignments during training? Please include an ablation.
2) Why are negative samples drawn only from words? Did you try sampling from the union of words/phrases/concepts or type-specific negatives? How does this affect performance and the compositionality results?
3) Can you report statistical significance (e.g., bootstrap over pairs) for Spearman differences in Tables 4–5, and provide per-dataset dev/test splits to avoid tuning leakage?
4) What is the coverage of concepts with at least one matched phrase in the corpora, and how does coverage relate to performance (e.g., correlation between number of surface forms/mentions and accuracy)?
5) Could you add at least one extrinsic task (e.g., concept normalization/UMLS entity linking on i2b2/MedMentions, or Wikipedia entity linking) to demonstrate practical gains over word-only or phrase-only baselines?

Additional suggestions for improvement
- Incorporate ontology graph structure (IS-A, synonyms) into training via retrofitting or a multi-task loss to capture non-lexical semantics.
- Improve the MTurk dataset reliability (e.g., expert subsets, clearer instructions, pair re-annotation) and analyze results by agreement strata.
- Provide clearer training details (runtime, hardware, learning rate schedules per corpus), handle overlapping phrases explicitly, and release code to reproduce hyperparameter search.
- Add ablations on β and η across corpora to quantify the trade-offs and when compositionality helps/hurts.

Overall recommendation
Weak reject. The paper is well-executed, practical, and releases useful resources; intrinsic results are competitive. However, methodological novelty is limited, ambiguity handling is too simplistic, and the lack of extrinsic evaluation and statistical testing weakens the case for acceptance. Strengthening disambiguation, adding downstream tasks, and integrating ontology structure would substantially improve the contribution.
