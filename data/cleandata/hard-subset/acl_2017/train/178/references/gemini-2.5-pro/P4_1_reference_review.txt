### Summary and Contributions

This paper introduces a weakly-supervised method for creating a joint vector space for concepts, the multi-word phrases that represent them, and the individual words that compose those phrases. The method adapts the skip-gram with negative sampling architecture. Using an ontology (e.g., UMLS, YAGO), the system first identifies and replaces known phrases in a large, unannotated corpus with unique identifiers. It then trains three sets of embeddings (for concepts, phrases, and words) in parallel, using the textual context of phrase mentions to update both the phrase's embedding and the embeddings of the concepts it can represent. The model also includes hyperparameters to enforce similarity between a phrase and its constituent words, and between a concept and its representative phrases. The approach is evaluated on concept similarity and relatedness tasks in both biomedical and general domains, and on a semantic type clustering task.

The main contributions of this work are:
1.  A novel, weakly-supervised framework for jointly learning embeddings for abstract concepts, their multi-word textual representations (phrases), and words in a single, shared vector space. The model leverages distant supervision from an ontology, removing the need for manually annotated data.
2.  The creation and release of a new crowdsourced dataset for evaluating similarity and relatedness of general-domain entities (people, places, organizations), addressing a gap in available resources for this type of evaluation.
3.  A comprehensive empirical evaluation in both the biomedical and general-knowledge domains. The results demonstrate that the proposed method is competitive with, and in some cases surpasses, prior models that require more extensive supervision, particularly on larger datasets.

### Strengths

1.  **Methodological Soundness and Clarity:** The proposed method is a clear and logical extension of the well-established skip-gram model. The preprocessing step of replacing phrases with unique identifiers is straightforward but effective for treating multi-word expressions as atomic units during training. The objective function is broken down into intuitive components for words, phrases, and concepts, and the introduction of hyperparameters `β` and `η` to explicitly model compositionality is a well-motivated design choice. The overall approach is technically sound and is described with sufficient detail for reproducibility.
2.  **Novelty in Tackling the Concept-to-Text Gap:** The paper effectively addresses the challenging problem that a single abstract concept can be represented by multiple, lexically diverse phrases (e.g., "Lou Gehrig’s disease" and "amyotrophic lateral sclerosis"). By explicitly modeling concepts, phrases, and words in a shared space and using an ontology to link them, the model learns representations that bridge this lexical gap. This joint modeling approach is a novel contribution compared to methods that embed only words or only knowledge base entities.
3.  **Thorough Multi-Domain Evaluation and Analysis:** The authors conduct a rigorous evaluation across two distinct domains (biomedical and general) using multiple corpora and standard evaluation tasks. The comparison against a wide range of baselines, including both word-level approximations and concept embeddings from prior work (some using stronger supervision), provides a robust assessment of the model's performance. The results are strong, showing the method is highly competitive. Furthermore, the internal analyses of compositionality (Section 5.4) and semantic type clustering (Section 5.5) provide valuable insights into the geometric properties of the learned vector space.
4.  **Valuable Resource Contribution:** The creation of a new dataset for general-domain entity similarity and relatedness is a significant contribution to the community. Such resources are scarce, and this dataset will facilitate future research in entity representation learning. The authors provide a clear description of the data collection and quality control process, lending credibility to the resource.

### Weaknesses

1.  **Unclear Advantage of Direct Concept Embeddings over Baselines:** The experimental results do not consistently demonstrate a strong advantage for the directly learned concept embeddings (`CONCEPTco`) over the simpler baseline of averaging the embeddings of a concept's representative phrases (`APPROXPHRco`). In Tables 4 and 5, `APPROXPHRco` is often very close in performance to `CONCEPTco`, and even outperforms it on the newly introduced general-domain datasets. This raises questions about the added value and necessity of learning a separate concept embedding matrix (`EC`), as a strong representation can apparently be derived compositionally from the phrase embeddings alone.
2.  **Potential for High Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** The reported optimal hyperparameters in Table 3 vary dramatically across the different training corpora. For example, the vector dimensionality ranges from 100 to 600, and the compositionality parameters `β` and `η` are set to 0 for two of the four corpora, effectively disabling the explicit compositionality terms in the loss function for those cases. This suggests that the model's performance may be highly sensitive to these settings, which could limit its practical applicability and require extensive, dataset-specific tuning for new domains.
3.  **Limited Technical Novelty of the Core Architecture:** While the overall framework and task are novel, the core embedding mechanism is a direct adaptation of the skip-gram with negative sampling model. The primary innovations are the data preprocessing strategy and the formulation of three parallel loss terms derived from the standard skip-gram objective. As such, the work is more of an effective and novel application of existing techniques rather than the introduction of a fundamentally new embedding algorithm.

### Questions to Authors

1.  The `APPROXPHRco` baseline, which averages phrase embeddings to represent a concept, performs competitively with and sometimes better than the `CONCEPTco` embeddings. Could you elaborate on why the model's direct training of concept embeddings does not yield a more significant or consistent performance improvement over this baseline? Does this suggest that the contextual information available to phrases is sufficient and that the additional layer of abstraction for concepts provides limited new information under this training scheme?
2.  The optimal settings for the compositionality hyperparameters `β` and `η` were 0.0 for the PM+CL and WIKINYT corpora. This deactivates the explicit similarity enforcement between word/phrase and phrase/concept embeddings. What properties of these larger, combined corpora might explain why a non-compositional approach is favored? Does this finding imply that for sufficiently large and diverse corpora, the shared context signal is strong enough to align the embeddings without needing explicit compositional constraints?
3.  The preprocessing step uses a greedy, longest-match strategy to identify and replace phrases in the text. This could lead to ambiguity where phrases are nested (e.g., "New York" within "New York City"). Could you comment on how frequently such cases occur and how this greedy strategy might impact the learned embeddings for the nested versus the longer phrases?
