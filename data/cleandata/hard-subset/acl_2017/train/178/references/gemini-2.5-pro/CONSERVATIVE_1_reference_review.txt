### Summary and Contributions

This paper proposes a weakly-supervised method to jointly learn vector representations for concepts, phrases, and words. The core idea is to leverage an existing ontology (like UMLS or YAGO) to identify phrases in a large unannotated corpus that correspond to canonical concepts. The method extends the skip-gram with negative sampling model by treating phrases as single tokens and using the context of a phrase mention to update embeddings for the phrase itself, its constituent words, and the underlying concept(s) it represents. The main contributions of this work, as I see them, are:

1.  A thorough empirical evaluation of a weakly-supervised concept embedding method against several strong baselines, including those that rely on heavily annotated data. The results demonstrate that the proposed method can achieve competitive, though not consistently superior, performance on standard biomedical similarity and relatedness tasks.
2.  The creation and release of a new, crowdsourced dataset for evaluating similarity and relatedness of general-domain entities (people, places, organizations), which addresses a gap in available resources for this type of evaluation.
3.  The method itself, which is a straightforward and sensible adaptation of the well-established word2vec framework to incorporate concept-level information from an ontology via distant supervision.

### Strengths

1.  **Solid and Rigorous Evaluation:** The authors conduct a comprehensive set of experiments. They compare their model not only against internal ablations and compositional baselines but also against multiple strong, published methods in the biomedical domain (e.g., CHOI, MENCIA). This level of rigorous comparison against established work is commendable and provides a clear context for the model's performance.
2.  **Practicality of Weak Supervision:** The proposed method's main advantage is its use of distant supervision, relying only on a pre-existing ontology and an unannotated corpus. This contrasts sharply with competing methods like Choi et al. (2016b), which require sequences of human-annotated codes. This makes the proposed approach significantly more scalable and applicable to new domains where such heavily annotated data is unavailable.
3.  **Creation of a New Evaluation Resource:** The introduction of a new similarity and relatedness dataset for general-domain entities is a valuable contribution. The authors provide a clear description of their data collection methodology via crowdsourcing, including quality control measures. Such resources are essential for fostering further research in this area.
4.  **Detailed Analysis:** The paper goes beyond simple performance tables. The analyses in Sections 5.4 and 5.5, which examine the geometric relationships between concepts, phrases, and words, and the clustering of semantic types, provide valuable insights into the properties of the learned vector space, even if the results are mixed.

### Weaknesses

1.  **Lack of Consistent Empirical Superiority:** The primary weakness of this work is that the proposed method fails to demonstrate a clear and consistent advantage over established baselines. In the biomedical evaluation (Table 4), the `CONCEPTco` embeddings are outperformed by the `CHOI` baseline on the Mayo and Mini Mayo datasets. On the larger UMNSRS datasets, performance is competitive but not decisively better than the `CHIU-30` word-only baseline. A new method should provide more compelling evidence of its superiority to justify its adoption.
2.  **Methodological Novelty is Limited:** The proposed model is a relatively incremental extension of the standard skip-gram architecture. The core ideas—lexicalizing multi-word phrases and using a shared context to update multiple related embeddings—are adaptations of existing techniques. The introduction of the `β` and `η` hyperparameters is a simple linear interpolation in the loss function. While the application is novel, the underlying methodology does not represent a significant technical leap.
3.  **Key Claim Undermined by Results:** The central motivation of the paper is that learning dedicated embeddings for concepts (`CONCEPTco`) is beneficial. However, the results on the authors' own general-domain dataset (Table 5) contradict this. The best-performing model is `APPROXPHRco`, a simpler approach that approximates a concept embedding by averaging the embeddings of its phrases. If a compositional baseline using the model's own phrase embeddings outperforms the dedicated concept embeddings, it seriously questions the necessity and effectiveness of the proposed concept embedding procedure.
4.  **Over-reliance on Intrinsic Evaluation:** The evaluation relies entirely on similarity/relatedness tasks and semantic type clustering. As the authors themselves note, performance on these intrinsic tasks does not always correlate with performance on downstream, extrinsic applications (Chiu et al., 2016b; Faruqui et al., 2016). Without demonstrating utility on a task like entity linking, named entity disambiguation, or relation extraction, the practical value of these embeddings remains unproven.

### Questions to Authors

1.  The results in Table 5 show that `APPROXPHRco` outperforms `CONCEPTco` on your new general-domain dataset. This seems to undermine the primary motivation for learning separate concept embeddings. Could you please elaborate on why you believe this is the case? Does this suggest that for YAGO entities, the concept is little more than the average of its surface forms, or does it point to a limitation in how concept embeddings are trained in your model?
2.  In your analysis of phrase-word compositionality (Figure 4b), you note a "strikingly bi-modal" distribution of MAP scores. What is your hypothesis for this? Does this bimodality correlate with the semantic compositionality of the phrases (e.g., phrases like "high blood pressure" are near their component words, while idiomatic phrases like "Lou Gehrig's disease" are far from theirs)? A qualitative analysis of examples from both modes would strengthen this section considerably.
