**Summary of the paper and its main contributions**

This paper introduces a weakly-supervised method for jointly learning vector representations for concepts, multi-word phrases, and individual words in a shared semantic space. The core idea is to leverage an existing ontology (like UMLS or YAGO) that maps canonical concepts to their textual phrase forms. Using this mapping for distant supervision, the method identifies and replaces known phrases in a large, unannotated text corpus, creating a parallel corpus. It then adapts the skip-gram with negative sampling architecture to train embeddings for words, phrases, and their associated concepts based on their textual contexts. The model also includes hyperparameters to enforce similarity between a phrase/concept and its constituent parts. The authors evaluate their learned embeddings on biomedical and general-domain concept similarity and relatedness tasks, demonstrating competitive or state-of-the-art performance compared to several strong baselines.

As I see them, the main contributions are:

1.  A practical and scalable weakly-supervised framework for creating joint embeddings for concepts, phrases, and words. The method's strength lies in its ability to leverage existing knowledge bases without requiring any manually annotated text for training.
2.  The creation and release of a new, crowdsourced dataset for evaluating similarity and relatedness of real-world entities (people, places, organizations), filling a notable gap in available evaluation resources.
3.  A comprehensive empirical evaluation across multiple domains (biomedical, general) and corpora, including detailed hyperparameter tuning and intrinsic analyses of the learned embedding space's geometric properties (e.g., compositionality and semantic type clustering).

**Strengths**

1.  **Effective and elegant use of distant supervision.** The proposed method is conceptually simple yet powerful. By preprocessing the text to replace known multi-word phrases with unique identifiers, the authors cleverly adapt the well-understood word2vec framework to learn representations for entities that are not single words. This makes the approach highly practical and scalable to new domains, provided a suitable ontology or lexicon exists.
2.  **Strong empirical performance on intrinsic tasks.** The results in Table 4 are compelling. The proposed `CONCEPTco` embeddings achieve the best performance on the large UMNSRS Similarity dataset and are competitive on the relatedness task, outperforming several baselines that rely on more direct or even manual supervision (e.g., MENCIA, DEVINE). This provides strong evidence that the model successfully captures concept-level semantics from unstructured text.
3.  **Significant resource contribution.** The new general-domain entity similarity and relatedness dataset is a valuable contribution. The paper rightly points out the lack of such resources for entities as opposed to words. The authors are transparent about the data collection methodology, including quality control and inter-annotator agreement, making it a useful resource for future research in this area.
4.  **Insightful analysis of the embedding space.** The paper goes beyond just reporting Spearman's ρ. The analyses in Sections 5.4 and 5.5, which examine compositionality and the clustering of semantic types, provide valuable insights into what the model learns. For example, the bimodal distribution in Figure 4b suggests the model learns to distinguish between compositional and non-compositional phrases, embedding them differently relative to their constituent words, which is a desirable property.

**Weaknesses**

1.  **The contribution of the compositionality mechanism is unclear.** The model introduces two hyperparameters, β and η, to explicitly enforce compositionality (Sec 3.2.2). However, the hyperparameter tuning results (Table 3) show that the optimal values for these are often 0.0. The appendix further states that "performance trend negative as β and η increase." This suggests that these explicit compositionality terms, presented as a feature of the model, are not helpful for the main evaluation tasks and may even be detrimental. This weakens the contribution of this specific part of the model design. The good performance appears to stem primarily from the joint training objective itself, rather than these explicit constraints.
2.  **Simplistic handling of phrase ambiguity.** The model's core assumption is that "any occurrence of a known phrase signifies an occurrence of each concept it can refer to." When a phrase maps to multiple concepts, the context is used to update all of them. This is a very strong assumption that conflates distinct concepts. For example, the phrase "mercury" could refer to a chemical element, a planet, or a Roman god. Under this model, contexts about astronomy would be used to update the embedding for the chemical element, introducing significant noise. The paper lacks an analysis of how this ambiguity affects embedding quality.
3.  **The evaluation is limited to similarity and relatedness tasks.** The authors correctly acknowledge in their discussion that performance on intrinsic similarity tasks does not always generalize to downstream applications. While the semantic type clustering analysis is a step in the right direction, the paper would be significantly stronger if it demonstrated the utility of these novel concept embeddings on an extrinsic, task-based evaluation (e.g., entity linking, relation extraction, or concept normalization), where the benefit of having a unified concept-level representation could be more directly measured.
4.  **Potential for greedy matching errors in preprocessing.** The preprocessing step replaces phrases greedily based on the number of matched tokens. This heuristic could lead to incorrect tagging. For instance, a greedy match on "New York" would prevent a subsequent, more specific match on "New York University." While any matching strategy will have trade-offs, the paper does not discuss or analyze the potential impact of this design choice on the final results.

**Questions to Authors**

1.  Given that your hyperparameter search found that the optimal values for the compositionality parameters β and η were often zero, could you elaborate on their role in the model? Do they provide any qualitative benefits not captured by the similarity tasks, or would you recommend setting them to zero in most practical applications?
2.  Your model handles ambiguous phrases (those mapping to multiple concepts) by updating all possible concepts. How does this design choice affect concepts whose phrases are highly ambiguous versus those with unambiguous phrases? Could you provide any analysis correlating concept embedding quality with the degree of ambiguity of its surface forms in the ontology?
3.  The performance of `APPROXPHRco` is very strong, sometimes outperforming the explicitly learned `CONCEPTco` embeddings (e.g., on the new general entity datasets). What is your interpretation of this result? Does it suggest that, for some concepts, a distributed representation across its various surface forms is more effective than a single-point estimate for the abstract concept?
