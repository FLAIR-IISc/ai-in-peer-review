### **Summary and Contributions**

This paper presents a weakly-supervised method for learning vector representations for concepts, phrases, and words in a shared semantic space. The core idea is to leverage an existing ontology (like UMLS or YAGO) to identify multi-word phrases in a large, unannotated text corpus. The method adapts the skip-gram with negative sampling model by treating identified phrases as single tokens and adding an objective to learn embeddings for the canonical concepts that these phrases represent. The model also includes hyperparameters to control the degree to which phrase embeddings are compositional of their words and concept embeddings are compositional of their phrases. The authors evaluate their method on biomedical and general-domain concept similarity/relatedness tasks, demonstrating competitive performance against prior methods, including those that require stronger supervision. They also introduce and make available a new dataset for evaluating the similarity and relatedness of real-world entities.

In my view, the main contributions of this work are:

1.  **A new dataset for real-world entity similarity and relatedness.** This is a valuable resource for the community, as standard benchmarks for evaluating multi-word expressions and entity representations are scarce. The authors provide a detailed methodology for its crowdsourced creation.
2.  **An effective, simple, and scalable weakly-supervised method for learning concept embeddings.** While the underlying technique is an extension of a well-known model, its application to jointly embedding concepts, phrases, and words using only distant supervision from an ontology is well-executed and shown to be effective. The scalability of the approach is a significant advantage.
3.  **A comprehensive empirical evaluation and analysis.** The paper thoroughly evaluates the proposed method across different domains, corpora, and ontologies. The inclusion of insightful analyses on compositionality and semantic type clustering provides a deeper understanding of the properties of the learned embeddings.

### **Strengths**

1.  **Clear Motivation and Presentation.** The paper is well-motivated, addressing the important and practical problem of representing multi-word concepts that are not easily handled by standard word embedding models. The method is explained clearly and logically, making the paper easy to follow.

2.  **Valuable New Resource.** The creation of a new dataset for entity similarity and relatedness is a strong and tangible contribution. The lack of such resources has been a bottleneck for research in this area. The authors' transparency about their data collection and annotation process is commendable and increases the dataset's utility for future research.

3.  **Thorough and Rigorous Experiments.** The empirical evaluation is a key strength of this paper. The authors test their method on both biomedical and general-domain data, using multiple large-scale corpora. The comparison against a wide range of baselines is comprehensive, including both word-based approximations and dedicated concept embedding models from prior work. The results convincingly show that the proposed method is competitive with, and in several cases superior to, existing approaches.

4.  **Insightful Qualitative and Quantitative Analysis.** The authors go beyond simply reporting performance on similarity tasks. The analyses in Sections 5.4 and 5.5, which investigate the learned relationships between concepts and phrases and the clustering of concepts by semantic type, provide valuable insights. For example, the bimodal distribution in Figure 4b suggests the model can learn to distinguish between compositional and non-compositional phrases, which is a key part of the paper's motivation.

### **Weaknesses**

1.  **Limited Novelty of the Core Method.** The proposed model is a relatively straightforward extension of the skip-gram architecture. The primary modifications involve treating phrases as unique tokens and adding a third embedding matrix for concepts, whose updates are driven by the contexts of associated phrases. While effective, the technical contribution is incremental rather than a significant departure from existing embedding techniques. The main novelty lies in the problem formulation and the application of this framework.

2.  **Unclear Contribution of the Compositionality Parameters (β, η).** The paper introduces β and η to explicitly model the trade-off between compositional and independent representations. However, the hyperparameter tuning results (Table 3) show that for three of the four corpora, the optimal setting for η (concept-phrase compositionality) is 0, and for two of the four, the optimal setting for β (phrase-word compositionality) is also 0. This suggests that for the main evaluation tasks, the model performs best when concepts and phrases are learned independently from their constituents' embeddings, which undermines the motivation for including these parameters. A deeper discussion of why this might be the case is missing.

3.  **Potentially Unfavorable Comparison on General-Domain Task.** On the newly created general-domain dataset (Table 5), the `APPROXPHRco` baseline (averaging the embeddings of a concept's phrases) outperforms the directly learned `CONCEPTco` embedding. This is a surprising and counter-intuitive result, as the `CONCEPTco` vector is intended to be a more principled representation of the abstract concept. This finding raises questions about the effectiveness of the concept-level training objective, at least for general-domain entities, and this result is not sufficiently discussed.

4.  **Low Inter-Annotator Agreement for the New Dataset.** While the creation of the new dataset is a strength, the reported Fleiss' κ of 0.24 is on the lower end of what is considered "fair" agreement. This low agreement might indicate that the annotation task is inherently highly subjective or that the instructions were ambiguous, potentially limiting the dataset's reliability as a gold standard for evaluation.

### **Questions to Authors**

1.  The hyperparameter tuning results frequently favor setting the compositionality parameters β and η to 0, suggesting that learning independent representations is often optimal for similarity tasks. This seems to run counter to the motivation for introducing these parameters. Could you elaborate on why you believe the model often learns better representations without this explicit compositional signal? Does this imply that the context alone is a stronger signal than the internal structure of phrases/concepts for these tasks?

2.  In your general-domain evaluation (Table 5), the concept approximation `APPROXPHRco` outperforms the directly learned `CONCEPTco` embeddings. What is your interpretation of this result? Does it suggest that for YAGO entities, which may have more varied textual forms, averaging over these surface forms provides a more robust representation than trying to learn a single abstract vector via your proposed objective?

3.  Regarding your new dataset, a Fleiss' κ of 0.24 indicates only fair agreement. Could you provide more details on the instructions and examples provided to the annotators? Did you perform any analysis of the pairs with high annotator disagreement to better understand the sources of subjectivity in this task?
