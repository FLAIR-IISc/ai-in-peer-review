### Summary and Contributions

This paper introduces a novel, weakly-supervised method for jointly learning vector representations for abstract concepts, multi-word phrases, and individual words within a single, unified semantic space. The core idea is to leverage an existing ontology (like UMLS or YAGO) to identify known multi-word phrases in a large, unannotated text corpus. These phrases are then treated as atomic units during a modified skip-gram training process, allowing the model to learn distinct embeddings for the phrase (e.g., "the Big Apple") and its underlying concept (e.g., the entity NEW_YORK_CITY), in addition to the embeddings for its constituent words ("Big", "Apple").

In my view, the main contributions are:

1.  **A novel and ambitious modeling framework:** The primary contribution is the elegant formulation for jointly embedding three distinct levels of semantic representation (word, phrase, concept). This directly tackles the long-standing problem of non-compositionality in a principled way, moving beyond simple averaging or post-hoc composition techniques. The idea of using distant supervision from an ontology to drive this process in a unified model is a significant conceptual advance.
2.  **A new public dataset for entity similarity and relatedness:** The authors introduce a new, human-annotated dataset for evaluating the similarity and relatedness of real-world entities. This is a valuable resource for the community, addressing a notable gap in evaluation benchmarks for concept-level representations.
3.  **A scalable, weakly-supervised method:** The approach requires no direct human annotation for the embeddings themselves, relying only on an existing knowledge base and raw text. This makes the method highly scalable and applicable to diverse domains, as demonstrated by its application to both biomedical and general-domain corpora.

### Strengths

1.  **High Novelty and Conceptual Boldness:** The paper's core strength lies in its novel and ambitious goal. Instead of treating concepts as compositions of words, or learning them in a separate space from text, this work proposes to learn all three representations—concepts, their textual phrase forms, and the words within those phrases—simultaneously. This is a fresh and compelling direction for representation learning that directly addresses a fundamental limitation of word-centric models. The problem of representing concepts like "Lou Gehrig's disease" is not new, but this unified, weakly-supervised approach is a bold and interesting solution.
2.  **Impressive Empirical Performance with Weak Supervision:** The proposed method achieves results that are competitive with, and in some cases superior to, prior methods that rely on stronger forms of supervision, such as manually annotated medical codes (CHOI) or curated knowledge base relations. For a first attempt at such a joint model, demonstrating that it can outperform more heavily supervised techniques on large-scale similarity tasks (UMNSRS Similarity) is a very strong signal that the underlying idea is sound and powerful.
3.  **Creation of a Valuable Community Resource:** The development and release of a new dataset for real-world entity similarity is a significant contribution in its own right. Evaluation of concept and entity embeddings has been hampered by a lack of standard benchmarks outside of the biomedical domain, and this work helps to fill that void.
4.  **Scalability and Generality:** The reliance on distant supervision from ontologies makes the method highly practical. It can be applied to any domain where a concept-phrase mapping (even a noisy one) exists, alongside a large text corpus. The successful application to both the highly technical biomedical domain (UMLS) and the general domain (YAGO) effectively demonstrates this flexibility.

### Weaknesses

While I am enthusiastic about this paper's direction, there are some areas that could be strengthened. I view these as minor limitations in the context of the paper's novelty, rather than fatal flaws.

1.  **Limited Impact of Compositionality Hyperparameters:** The introduction of the β and η hyperparameters to explicitly model the trade-off between compositional and non-compositional semantics is a very interesting idea. However, the hyperparameter tuning results (Table 3 and Appendix A.1) show that the optimal setting for these is often 0.0, suggesting that this explicit similarity enforcement provides little to no benefit, and can even be harmful. This weakens the claim that this mechanism is an effective way to control compositionality and raises questions about whether the shared context and negative sampling matrix are doing all the heavy lifting.
2.  **Evaluation is Limited to Similarity Tasks:** The authors rightly note the criticisms surrounding similarity/relatedness tasks for evaluating embeddings. While their inclusion of a novel dataset and multiple existing ones provides a solid intrinsic evaluation, the paper would be more compelling if it demonstrated the utility of these unique, three-level embeddings in a downstream application. A task like entity linking, where one must map a textual phrase to a canonical concept ID, seems like a perfect testbed to show the practical advantage of having distinct, high-quality embeddings for both phrases and concepts.
3.  **Unclear Interpretation of Internal Analyses:** The analysis in Section 5.4 presents a somewhat confusing picture. While concepts are shown to be very similar to the *average* of their phrase embeddings (Fig 3a), the MAP analysis suggests they are often not particularly close to their *individual* representative phrases (Fig 4a). This is counter-intuitive and warrants a deeper explanation. It may suggest that the concept embedding is learning a centroid that is not necessarily close to any single realization, but this could be explored more thoroughly, perhaps with qualitative examples.

### Questions to Authors

1.  Regarding the compositionality parameters (β and η), given that their optimal values were frequently zero, could you offer more insight into this finding? Does this imply that explicitly enforcing similarity between a phrase and its words (or a concept and its phrases) is fundamentally the wrong approach, or could it be that the log-bilinear objective used is not the ideal way to implement this constraint?
2.  The MAP analysis in Section 5.4 is intriguing. Could you provide some intuition or a qualitative example for a concept whose embedding is close to the average of its phrase embeddings, but distant from each individual phrase embedding? What does this tell us about what the `CONCEPTco` vectors are capturing that the `PHRASEco` vectors are not?
