### Summary and Contributions

This paper introduces a weakly-supervised method for learning a joint vector space for concepts (from an ontology), phrases (their textual mentions), and words. The core idea is to leverage a knowledge base (like UMLS or YAGO) to identify phrases in a large unannotated corpus and treat them as tokens representing underlying concepts. The model is an extension of the skip-gram with negative sampling architecture, with separate embedding matrices for words, phrases, and concepts, which are updated based on the textual context in which they appear. The authors also introduce hyperparameters to enforce similarity between phrases and their constituent words, and between concepts and their constituent phrases. The method is evaluated on biomedical and general-domain entity similarity/relatedness tasks, showing competitive or state-of-the-art performance.

As I see them, the main contributions of this work are:

1.  **A Scalable Weakly-Supervised Model:** The primary contribution is the method itself, which cleverly uses distant supervision to train concept embeddings without requiring manually annotated data. By treating phrase mentions as concept mentions, the model can scale to very large corpora and ontologies, which is a significant practical advantage over methods that rely on curated annotations.

2.  **A New Evaluation Dataset:** The paper introduces a novel and valuable dataset for evaluating similarity and relatedness of general-domain entities (people, places, organizations). The lack of such resources is a known gap in the field, and this contribution will be useful for future research on entity representations.

3.  **Comprehensive Empirical Evaluation:** The authors conduct a thorough evaluation on multiple datasets (both biomedical and general-domain) and compare against a strong set of baselines, including both word-based compositional methods and dedicated concept embedding models. The additional analyses on compositionality and semantic type clustering provide useful insights into the properties of the learned representations.

### Strengths

1.  **Elegant and Effective Method:** The proposed method is a simple yet powerful extension of a well-understood model (word2vec). The idea of replacing known phrases with unique identifiers to learn embeddings for them and their associated concepts in a unified framework is elegant. The fact that this relatively straightforward approach achieves results competitive with, and in some cases superior to, more complex models or those requiring richer supervision (e.g., CHOI et al. 2016b, which uses manually annotated medical codes) highlights its effectiveness.

2.  **High Scalability and Broad Applicability:** The model's reliance on only an ontology (for concept-phrase mappings) and an unannotated text corpus makes it highly scalable and broadly applicable. It can be readily applied to any domain where a suitable knowledge base exists (e.g., biology, law, finance), without the prohibitive cost of manual annotation. The successful application to both the specialized biomedical domain (UMLS) and the general domain (YAGO) effectively demonstrates this flexibility.

3.  **Thorough and Insightful Analysis:** The paper goes beyond simply reporting performance on a single task. The comparison of different embedding types (CONCEPTco, APPROXPHRco, PHRASEco, etc.) is well-executed and reveals interesting nuances, such as APPROXPHRco outperforming CONCEPTco on the general-domain task. Furthermore, the analyses in Sections 5.4 (compositionality) and 5.5 (semantic type clustering) provide a deeper understanding of the learned vector space. The finding that phrases are often distant from their component words (Fig 4b) supports the paper's initial motivation that many concepts are non-compositional.

### Weaknesses

1.  **Limited Methodological Novelty and Underwhelming Compositionality Component:** While the overall framework is novel, the core technical components are direct adaptations of skip-gram. The main novel architectural addition—the `β` and `η` parameters for enforcing compositionality—appears to be ineffective. The hyperparameter tuning (Table 3) shows that the optimal setting for these is often zero, meaning the compositionality constraints are turned off for the best-performing models. This undermines a significant part of the proposed model's novelty and suggests that the simpler formulation without these terms is sufficient.

2.  **Exclusive Reliance on Intrinsic Evaluation:** The paper's evaluation is entirely based on intrinsic tasks like similarity, relatedness, and clustering. As the authors rightly acknowledge, it is well-documented in the literature (e.g., Chiu et al., 2016b; Faruqui et al., 2016) that performance on these tasks does not reliably predict performance on downstream, extrinsic NLP applications. The lack of even a single extrinsic task evaluation (e.g., entity linking, relation extraction, document classification using the concept embeddings) makes it difficult to assess the practical utility of the learned representations.

3.  **Oversimplified Preprocessing and Ambiguity Handling:** The preprocessing step relies on a greedy, longest-match strategy for identifying phrases. This approach does not account for nested or overlapping entities (e.g., "New York" within "New York City") and may lead to inconsistent phrase tokenization. Furthermore, when a phrase can map to multiple concepts, the model simply averages the update gradient across all possible concepts. This is a very basic way to handle polysemy and may wash out the semantic signal, especially for highly ambiguous phrases. A more sophisticated mechanism for disambiguation or attention could potentially yield better results.

4.  **Unclear Interpretation of Compositionality Analysis:** The analysis in Section 5.4 is interesting but the conclusion drawn is not fully supported. The bimodal distribution of MAP scores for phrase-word relationships (Figure 4b) is a key finding, suggesting that the model learns to treat some phrases as compositional and others as atomic units. However, the paper would be much stronger if it included qualitative examples to illustrate this. Showing which types of phrases fall into the high-MAP group (likely compositional ones like "high blood pressure") versus the low-MAP group (likely non-compositional ones like "the Big Apple") would provide much clearer evidence for the model's behavior.

### Questions to Authors

1.  The compositionality parameters `β` and `η` were often set to 0 in your best-performing models, particularly on the larger corpora. This seems to negate the utility of this component of your model. Could you elaborate on why you believe these explicit compositionality constraints did not help? Does this suggest that for this task, treating phrases and concepts as opaque, non-compositional units is a superior strategy?

2.  The inter-annotator agreement (Fleiss' κ = 0.24) for your new general-domain entity dataset is reported as "fair". This suggests the task is quite difficult and subjective. How might this level of noise in the ground truth affect the reliability of the evaluation results on this dataset? Did you observe any particular types of entity pairs (e.g., Organization-Place) that were especially difficult for annotators?

3.  Regarding the greedy phrase matching, could you discuss the potential impact of nested entities? For instance, if both "amyotrophic lateral sclerosis" and "lateral sclerosis" are in the ontology, your method would always match the longer phrase. How might this affect the embedding learned for the shorter, nested concept?
