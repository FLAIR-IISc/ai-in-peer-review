This paper proposes a weakly-supervised method for jointly embedding concepts, phrases, and words, leveraging distant supervision from an ontology and an unannotated corpus. The primary contribution is the development of a model that can learn embeddings for concepts even without direct annotations, by utilizing the contexts of their textual representations (phrases). A secondary contribution is the analysis demonstrating how the source ontology structure is reflected in the organization of the learned embeddings. Finally, the authors also provide a novel dataset for evaluating similarity and relatedness of real-world entities.

**Strengths:**

1. **Addressing a Relevant Problem:** The paper tackles a crucial problem in semantic modeling: representing concepts that are not simply compositional from their constituent words and that have multiple lexical forms. This is particularly important in specialized domains like biomedicine, where multi-word expressions and synonymous terms are prevalent.
2. **Weakly-Supervised Approach:** The proposed method is notable for its ability to learn concept embeddings without relying on expensive human annotations. This is a significant advantage, particularly for low-resource languages or specialized domains where annotation is costly and time-consuming.
3. **Empirical Validation & Novel Dataset:** The authors provide a comprehensive experimental evaluation on both biomedical and general-domain datasets. The creation and release of a new dataset for real-world entity similarity and relatedness is a valuable contribution to the research community. The results demonstrate competitive performance compared to prior models, particularly in certain settings.
4. **Insightful Analysis:** The analysis of embedding organization, demonstrating how the ontological structure is reflected in the learned embeddings, provides valuable insights into the behavior of the model and confirms its ability to capture semantic relationships.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Novelty of the Core Method:** While the application to concept embedding is valuable, the underlying technique of adapting word2vec with negative sampling is not fundamentally novel. The core idea builds heavily on existing methods for distributional semantics and knowledge base embedding, without introducing a significantly new algorithmic component.
2. **Hyperparameter Sensitivity & Tuning:** The reported results indicate significant sensitivity to hyperparameters, requiring careful tuning for each corpus. This suggests that the method may not be easily portable to new domains without substantial experimentation and optimization. The detailed description of the tuning process, while present in the supplement, would benefit from more discussion in the main text.
3. **Performance Compared to Strong Baselines:** While competitive, the performance of the proposed method is not consistently superior to strong baselines, particularly the knowledge-graph-based embeddings (CHOI) in the biomedical domain. The paper could benefit from a more thorough discussion of the limitations of the proposed approach and potential avenues for improvement to surpass these baselines.
4. **Fleiss’ Kappa for New Dataset:** The reported Fleiss' kappa of 0.24 for the new dataset indicates only *fair* agreement between annotators. This raises questions about the reliability and quality of the dataset and its suitability for robust evaluation.

**Questions to Authors:**

1.  Could you elaborate on the rationale behind choosing the specific hyperparameters reported in Table 3? What insights did you gain from the hyperparameter tuning process that informed these choices?
2. The paper mentions that the β and η parameters had a negative impact on performance when increased. Could you provide more details on *why* this might be the case? Is it possible that these parameters require different tuning strategies for different corpora or concepts?
3.  Given the relatively low inter-annotator agreement for the new dataset, what steps were taken to ensure the quality of the annotations and mitigate the impact of unreliable judgments? Would reporting individual annotator statistics alongside the aggregated results provide additional transparency?
4. The compositionality analysis in Figures 3 and 4 are very insightful. Does the model struggle to represent concepts that are truly non-compositional (i.e., where the meaning cannot be derived from its constituent words), and if so, are there potential modifications to address this limitation?



**Additional Comments:**

*   The paper is well-written and clearly explains the methodology and experimental setup.
*   The inclusion of a software implementation and the new dataset are valuable contributions to reproducibility and future research.
*   Consider briefly discussing the computational cost of the proposed method compared to existing approaches.
*   The references are generally relevant and up-to-date.
