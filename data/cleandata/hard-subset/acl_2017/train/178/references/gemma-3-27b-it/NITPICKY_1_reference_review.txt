This paper proposes a weakly-supervised method for jointly embedding concepts, phrases, and words, leveraging an ontology and distributional similarity training. The primary contribution is an attempt to learn concept representations without manual annotations by exploiting existing phrase-concept mappings as distant supervision. A secondary contribution is the introduction of hyperparameters to control the compositionality of phrases and concepts during embedding training. Finally, the authors provide a novel dataset for evaluating similarity and relatedness of real-world entities.

Despite the potentially useful idea, and the creation of a new dataset, I find this paper fundamentally flawed due to a multitude of minor (yet pervasive) issues that collectively undermine confidence in the results and the rigor of the work. The paper reads as a collection of loosely connected ideas rather than a cohesive research contribution. 

**Strengths:**

1. **Novel Dataset:** The creation of a dataset for evaluating real-world entity similarity/relatedness is a modest positive. While the Fleiss’ Kappa of 0.24 is quite low, the existence of *some* human-annotated data for this task is potentially valuable.
2. **Motivation:** The problem addressed – representing concepts beyond simple word compositions – is a valid and important one in NLP, particularly within biomedical domains.
3. **Concept-Phrase Joint Embedding:** The core idea of linking concepts and their textual representations through distributional similarity is logically sound.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Methodological Sloppiness & Lack of Justification:** The “greedy” phrase replacement during preprocessing (Section 3.1) is a troubling design choice. Why is a greedy approach used? What impact does this have on the subsequent embedding training? The authors provide no analysis.  Similarly, the choice of hyperparameters (Section 5.2) is presented as simply “tuned” without sufficient detail regarding the search space or the rationale behind the selected values. The hyperparameter search space appears arbitrarily defined.
2. **Evaluation Rigor & Reporting:** The experimental setup is deeply problematic. The cross-validation strategy, while acknowledged as potentially flawed (foot-note 4), is still used. The reliance on Spearman's rho alone provides an incomplete picture of performance. Presenting results without error bars or statistical significance tests renders comparisons difficult to interpret. The comparison against baselines is not comprehensive – crucial baselines are missing. For instance, simply averaging pre-trained word embeddings (e.g. GloVe, fastText) for concepts isn't included.
3. **Overstated Claims & Lack of Ablation:** The authors claim competitive performance with methods using human annotations, but the results (Table 4) are mixed at best. In several cases, their method is *outperformed* by stronger baselines.  Furthermore, there’s a complete lack of ablation studies to understand the contribution of β and η. Do these hyperparameters actually improve performance, or are they detrimental?  The effect of each hyperparameter is glossed over.
4. **Presentation & Clarity:** The writing is often imprecise and convoluted. For example, the explanation of the training objective (Section 3.2.1) is unnecessarily complex and difficult to follow. The numerous equations are presented without sufficient contextual explanation. The constant references to figures without properly guiding the reader to interpret them are a distraction.
5. **Inconsistent Ontology Usage:** The paper utilizes both UMLS and YAGO, yet doesn't fully explain *why* or *how* these different ontologies are integrated. This feels haphazard and undermines the rigor of the approach. The differing nature of the ontologies (abstract vs. concrete entities) isn’t adequately addressed.



**Questions to Authors:**

1.  What is the justification for the greedy phrase replacement strategy? What is the impact of this strategy on the learned embeddings? Please provide an analysis of the number of tokens and phrases altered in each corpus.
2.  Please provide a more detailed explanation of the hyperparameter tuning process, including the search space, evaluation metric, and rationale behind the selected values.
3.  Why were specific baselines (e.g., simple averaging of pre-trained word embeddings) not included in the experimental evaluation?
4.  Present the results with error bars or statistical significance tests to allow for a more rigorous comparison of the different methods.
5.  Provide an ablation study to analyze the impact of the β and η hyperparameters. How does performance change when these parameters are set to 0?
6.  What is the justification for using both UMLS and YAGO? How were the different ontologies integrated, and what were the challenges associated with this integration?




**Additional Comments:**

*   The notation is inconsistent throughout the paper.
*   The references are not consistently formatted.
*   The supplemental material seems incomplete and doesn’t fully address the claims made in the main text.



**Overall Assessment:**

I recommend **rejection**. While the core idea has merit, the pervasive methodological flaws, lack of rigor in the evaluation, and unclear presentation outweigh the potential contributions. The paper requires substantial revision and significantly more thorough experimentation to be considered acceptable for ACL. The multiple minor issues signal a lack of attention to detail that casts doubt on the reliability of the results.
