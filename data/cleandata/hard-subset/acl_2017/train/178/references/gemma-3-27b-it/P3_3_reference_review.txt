## Review for ACL Submission: "A Weakly-Supervised Method for Jointly Embedding Concepts, Phrases, and Words"

**Summary:** This paper presents a weakly-supervised method for jointly embedding concepts, phrases, and words by leveraging existing ontologies and distributional similarity training. The core idea is to use known phrases as distant supervision, training embeddings using the contexts of these phrases to represent underlying concepts. The authors validate their approach on biomedical and general-domain datasets, demonstrating competitive performance on concept similarity and relatedness tasks, and provide a novel dataset for real-world entity similarity.

**Main Contributions (as I see them):**

1. **A Practical Approach to Joint Embedding:** The paper offers a practical and scalable method for jointly embedding concepts and their textual realizations, addressing a key challenge in semantic representation where lexical forms often fail to capture the full meaning of concepts.  The weak supervision strategy avoids the need for costly manual annotations.
2. **Novel Dataset for Real-World Entity Similarity:** The authors provide a new dataset for evaluating similarity and relatedness of real-world entities, a task lacking robust benchmark resources. This is a valuable contribution to the community.
3. **Empirical Validation & Analysis:** The thorough empirical evaluation on multiple datasets, including ablation studies and analysis of embedding space organization, provides insights into the strengths and weaknesses of the proposed approach.

**Strengths:**

1. **Relevance and Importance:** The problem addressed – representing concepts beyond their lexical forms – is crucial for many NLP tasks, particularly in domains like biomedicine where non-compositional terms are prevalent. A solution enabling better concept representation is highly valuable.
2. **Sound Methodology:**  The adaptation of word2vec with distant supervision is a logical and well-explained approach. The introduction of β and η hyperparameters to control compositionality offers a valuable degree of flexibility, acknowledging that phrases and concepts aren’t uniformly compositional.
3. **Strong Empirical Results:** The results demonstrate competitive performance against strong baselines, including methods requiring manual annotations. The improvement over word-averaging baselines highlights the benefit of jointly embedding concepts. The results on the new dataset are particularly encouraging.
4. **Comprehensive Evaluation:** The authors go beyond simple similarity/relatedness scores, providing analysis of semantic type clustering and embedding space organization, which provides a more nuanced understanding of what the model learns.
5. **Availability of Resources:** Releasing the code and the new dataset fosters reproducibility and further research in this area.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Novelty of Core Method:** While the application to joint concept/phrase/word embedding is valuable, the underlying technique (adapting word2vec with distant supervision) is not fundamentally new. The novelty primarily lies in the specific application and the combined focus on concepts, phrases, and words. This diminishes the impact of the work.
2. **Reliance on Ontology Quality:** The performance heavily depends on the quality of the ontology used (UMLS, YAGO).  Errors or incompleteness in the ontology will directly impact the quality of the learned embeddings.  This limitation isn’t sufficiently discussed; a more detailed analysis of how ontology quality impacts results would be beneficial.
3. **Fleiss’ Kappa for the New Dataset:** A Fleiss’ Kappa of 0.24 for the new dataset indicates only *fair* agreement between annotators. While acknowledged, the potential impact of this relatively low agreement on the reliability of the dataset, and consequently the evaluation, requires further discussion. What are the main sources of disagreement, and how might this affect the interpretation of the results?
4. **Hyperparameter Sensitivity and Limited Generalizability:** The tuned hyperparameters vary significantly across corpora, suggesting the method may be sensitive to data characteristics.  More analysis of the impact of corpus properties on optimal hyperparameter settings would strengthen the findings. This sensitivity raises questions about the generalizability of the tuned hyperparameters to unseen datasets.
5. **Lack of Downstream Task Evaluation:** While the intrinsic evaluations are solid, the paper lacks evaluation on a *downstream* NLP task (e.g., entity linking, relation extraction). Demonstrating performance gains on a real-world application would significantly strengthen the impact of the work.



**Questions to Authors:**

1.  Regarding the relatively low Fleiss’ Kappa for the new dataset: Could you elaborate on the types of disagreements observed between annotators? Were there specific entity pairs that proved particularly challenging, and if so, why?
2.  The hyperparameter tuning revealed significant variations across corpora. Could you provide more insight into why the optimal hyperparameters differ so substantially? Is there a correlation between corpus characteristics (size, domain, etc.) and the optimal settings?
3.  You mention the potential for further improvement through integration of ontology structure directly into the training objective. Could you provide more details about how you envision implementing such an approach?
4.  While you provide an ablation study, it lacks a comparison to more recent and sophisticated joint embedding methods (e.g. those based on transformers). Why weren't these included, and how do you believe your approach compares to them qualitatively?
5.  Have you experimented with using different weighting schemes for concepts with varying numbers of representative phrases (e.g., inverse document frequency-like weighting)?  Could this address the issue of more frequently mentioned concepts dominating the embedding space?




**Additional Comments:**

*   The paper is well-written and clearly organized.
*   The inclusion of a supplemental material section is appreciated.
*   The code and dataset availability are a strong positive aspect of this submission.




**Overall Assessment:**

This is a solid and well-executed paper that addresses an important problem in semantic representation. While the core method lacks substantial novelty, the practical application, novel dataset, and thorough evaluation make it a valuable contribution.  Addressing the weaknesses raised above (especially concerning the dataset reliability and lack of downstream task evaluation) would significantly strengthen the submission. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions.
