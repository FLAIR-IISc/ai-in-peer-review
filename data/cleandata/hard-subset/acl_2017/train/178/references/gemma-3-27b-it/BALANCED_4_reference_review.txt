This paper proposes a weakly-supervised method for jointly embedding concepts, phrases, and words, leveraging distant supervision from an ontology and an unannotated corpus. The core idea is to train embeddings based on the contexts of known phrases, which are then used to represent their corresponding concepts. The primary contribution is a method for jointly learning embeddings of concepts, phrases, and words without requiring human annotations, relying instead on existing ontological knowledge and distributional semantics. A second, valuable contribution is the presented dataset of similarity and relatedness for real-world entities, identified by both Wikipedia pages and textual phrases. Finally, the analysis demonstrating reflection of ontology structure in the learned embeddings offers some insight into the model’s behavior.

**Strengths:**

1. **Novelty of the Approach:** The method addresses a significant challenge in semantic modeling: representing concepts that aren't easily compositional and can be expressed in multiple ways. The use of distant supervision and joint embedding offers a promising alternative to methods requiring extensive manual annotation. While the individual techniques (word embeddings, distant supervision) are not new, their combination within this specific framework, aiming for joint representation of concepts, phrases, and words, *is* novel.
2. **Valuable New Dataset:** The introduced dataset for real-world entity similarity and relatedness is a significant contribution. The lack of comparable datasets in the general domain is noted in the paper, and this fills a clear gap. Providing this resource benefits the broader NLP community. The methodological details regarding the Mechanical Turk data collection are sufficiently detailed.
3. **Insightful Analysis:** The analysis revealing that the ontology structure is partially reflected in the embedding space—concepts and phrases cluster together and concepts of the same type exhibit some regularity—provides evidence that the model is learning meaningful representations. The visualization of MAP scores is particularly helpful in understanding this relationship.
4. **Comprehensive Experimental Setup:** The authors conduct a thorough experimental evaluation, comparing their method to several baselines, including both approximation methods using existing embeddings and other contemporary approaches. The comparison across multiple corpora (PubMed, Clinical, WikiNYT) enhances the robustness of the findings. The inclusion of hyperparameter tuning adds to the rigor of the work.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Performance Gains:** While the method demonstrates competitive performance, it doesn't consistently outperform existing methods, particularly on the biomedical tasks. The performance is often comparable to, but not substantially better than, methods leveraging pre-trained word embeddings or more heavily supervised approaches (like Choi et al., 2016b). The justification for using this method compared to other existing methods isn't particularly strong, and the incremental benefit isn't sufficiently demonstrated.
2. **Dependence on Ontology Quality:** The quality of the learned embeddings is inherently tied to the quality of the underlying ontology. Inaccuracies or incompleteness in the ontology’s mapping between concepts and phrases will directly impact the model's performance. The authors acknowledge this, but a more detailed discussion of potential limitations related to ontology biases would be beneficial.
3. **Compositionality Trade-off (β and η):** The introduced hyperparameters (β and η) for controlling compositionality appear difficult to tune effectively. The results suggest that enforcing strong compositionality is detrimental, and the optimal settings are often zero. The rationale behind these hyperparameters is sound, but their practical impact seems limited, and their inclusion adds complexity without significant benefit. A more detailed ablation study on the impact of these hyperparameters is needed.
4. **Lack of Downstream Task Evaluation:** The evaluation is primarily focused on intrinsic tasks (similarity and relatedness). Evaluating the learned embeddings on more complex downstream NLP tasks (e.g., entity linking, relation extraction) would provide stronger evidence of their utility and generalizability.



**Questions to Authors:**

1. Could you elaborate on the specific challenges you encountered during hyperparameter tuning, particularly regarding the β and η parameters? Were there specific corpora or settings where these parameters showed more promise?
2. Given the dependence on ontology quality, how sensitive are your results to noise or incompleteness in the concept-phrase mappings? Have you experimented with techniques to mitigate the impact of inaccurate ontological information?
3. Do you have any preliminary results or plans for evaluating your embeddings on downstream NLP tasks? If so, could you share some insights into the expected performance gains?
4. The paper mentions an adapted 5-fold cross-validation, but doesn't detail *how* the splits were created. Was it a random split, or did you take into account any potential biases within the datasets?

**Additional Comments:**

*   The writing is clear and well-organized.
*   The related work section provides a good overview of the relevant literature.
*   The inclusion of a software implementation and the novel dataset is commendable.
*   Consider adding error bars to the tables showing Spearman's rho.



Overall, this is a solid paper with a promising approach and a valuable contribution in the form of a new dataset. However, the limited performance gains and the practical challenges associated with the compositionality trade-off raise concerns. I recommend rejection with encouragement to address the weaknesses identified and conduct further evaluation on downstream tasks. The authors should focus on demonstrating a more substantial advantage over existing methods and addressing the limitations related to ontology quality and hyperparameter tuning.
