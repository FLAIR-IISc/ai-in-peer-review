## Peer Review for "A Weakly-Supervised Method for Jointly Embedding Concepts, Phrases, and Words"

**Summary:**

This paper presents a weakly-supervised method for jointly embedding concepts, phrases, and words using distributional similarity training. The core idea is to leverage existing ontology mappings (concept-phrase pairs) as a form of distant supervision, training embeddings by treating occurrences of known phrases as indicators of the concepts they represent. The authors validate their approach on biomedical and general-domain datasets, demonstrating competitive performance on concept similarity and relatedness tasks. They also introduce a new dataset for evaluating real-world entity similarity and relatedness.

**Main Contributions (as I see them):**

1. **Novel Dataset:** The introduction of a new dataset for evaluating similarity and relatedness of real-world entities is a valuable contribution. Existing datasets are limited, and this provides a useful resource for the community.
2. **Practical Weakly-Supervised Approach:** The proposed method offers a practical way to learn concept embeddings without relying on expensive manual annotations. The distant supervision strategy, leveraging existing ontologies, is a sensible and potentially scalable approach.
3. **Joint Embedding Framework:** The framework for jointly embedding concepts, phrases, and words is well-defined and allows for capturing relationships between different levels of semantic granularity.

**Strengths:**

1. **Strong Experimental Validation:** The authors perform a comprehensive set of experiments, comparing their method against several baselines, including existing embedding models and pretrained embeddings. The results demonstrate that their approach is competitive and, in some cases, outperforms existing methods, especially on larger datasets.
2. **Clear Methodology & Implementation Details:** The paper provides a clear description of the proposed method, including the training objective and hyperparameters. The availability of the code ("cui2vec") is a significant positive, facilitating reproducibility and further research.
3. **Insightful Analysis:** The analysis of embedding structure, specifically the MAP calculations and compositional evaluations, provides valuable insights into how well the model captures semantic relationships between concepts, phrases, and words.  The observation that phrases are generally closer to their component words than concepts are to their phrases is particularly interesting.
4. **Relevance to Biomedical NLP:** The application of the method to biomedical concepts and the use of relevant datasets (UMLS, PubMed) highlights the potential of this work for improving biomedical NLP tasks.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Novelty of the Core Method:** While the application to this specific problem and the joint embedding framework are valuable, the underlying method is largely based on existing techniques (skip-gram with negative sampling). The core innovation is the *application* of this technique with distant supervision, rather than a fundamentally new algorithmic approach.
2. **Sensitivity to Ontology Quality:** The performance of the method is heavily dependent on the quality and completeness of the ontology used for distant supervision.  The paper acknowledges this, but a more thorough discussion of how ontology errors or ambiguities might impact the results would be beneficial.
3. **Suboptimal Performance on Smaller Biomedical Datasets:** While competitive on larger datasets, the method is outperformed by existing approaches (CHOI) on the smaller MayoSRS and Mini MayoSRS datasets. This suggests that the method may require a substantial amount of data to achieve optimal performance.
4. **IAA on the New Dataset is Moderate:** The Fleiss’ Kappa score of 0.24 for inter-annotator agreement on the newly introduced dataset suggests moderate agreement. While acceptable, a higher level of agreement would strengthen the validity of the dataset.  More details about the specific discrepancies and how they were addressed would also be helpful.

**Questions to Authors:**

1.  **Ontology Robustness:** How sensitive are the learned embeddings to noise or incompleteness in the ontology? Have you explored techniques for mitigating the impact of inaccurate or missing concept-phrase mappings?
2.  **Scalability to Larger Ontologies:**  How does the method scale to very large ontologies with millions of concepts?  Are there any computational bottlenecks that need to be addressed?
3. **Downstream Task Evaluation:** Beyond similarity and relatedness, have you evaluated the performance of the learned embeddings on any downstream NLP tasks (e.g., concept normalization, relation extraction)?
4. **Annotation Discrepancies:** Can you provide more details regarding the disagreements in the annotation of the new dataset, and what steps were taken to address those discrepancies? Specifically, were there particular entity pairs that proved consistently difficult for annotators to agree upon?
5.  **Hyperparameter Tuning Details:** Can you provide more information about the selection process of the hyperparameters described in Table 3, beyond noting that those settings yielded better performance?

**Additional Comments:**

* The writing quality is generally good, but some sentences are a bit dense and could be simplified for clarity.
* It would be helpful to include a brief discussion of the limitations of the proposed approach and potential avenues for future research.
