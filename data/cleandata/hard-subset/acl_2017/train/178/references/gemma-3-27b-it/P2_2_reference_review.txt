## Review for ACL Submission: "A Weakly-Supervised Method for Jointly Embedding Concepts, Phrases, and Words"

**Summary:** This paper presents a weakly-supervised method for learning joint embeddings of concepts, phrases, and words using distributional similarity training. The authors leverage existing ontologies (UMLS, YAGO) to distantly supervise the training process, using phrases as proxies for concepts. The method adapts word2vec to this joint embedding task and includes hyperparameters to control the compositionality of phrases and concepts.  Experiments on biomedical and general-domain datasets demonstrate competitive performance on concept similarity and relatedness tasks. A novel dataset of real-world entity similarity and relatedness is also introduced.

**Main Contributions (as I see them):**

1. **A practical approach to weakly-supervised concept embedding:** The core contribution is a relatively straightforward and scalable method for learning concept embeddings *without* relying on expensive human annotation. This is particularly valuable for domains like biomedicine where annotation is costly.
2. **Novel datasets for evaluating entity similarity/relatedness:**  The creation of the real-world entity similarity/relatedness dataset is a valuable addition to the community, filling a gap in available evaluation resources.
3. **Demonstration of the benefit of joint embedding:** The paper provides empirical evidence that jointly embedding concepts, phrases, and words can lead to improved performance, particularly in capturing relationships within ontologies.

**Strengths:**

1. **Clear Motivation and Problem Definition:** The paper clearly articulates the limitations of existing word embedding approaches when dealing with multi-word concepts and synonymy.  The motivation for a method that can represent concepts independently of their surface form is well-established.
2. **Sound Methodology:** The adaptation of the skip-gram model with negative sampling is a logical and effective approach. The inclusion of β and η hyperparameters to control compositionality provides flexibility and allows the model to adapt to different types of concepts. The preprocessing step of replacing phrases with unique identifiers is clever and allows for efficient training.
3. **Comprehensive Evaluation:** The authors evaluate their method on multiple datasets (biomedical and general-domain) and compare against a wide range of baselines, including both approximation methods and prior work. The inclusion of the novel dataset is a strong point.
4. **Analysis of Embedding Space:**  The analysis of the learned embeddings, particularly regarding the organization of concepts, phrases, and words, provides valuable insights into the model's behavior. The MAP analysis, while showing some limitations, demonstrates the model’s ability to capture relationships within the ontology.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Novelty of Core Method:**  While the combination of techniques is sound, the core method (adapting word2vec with distant supervision) isn't fundamentally novel. The primary innovation lies in *applying* this combination to the specific problem of concept embedding and doing so in a scalable way.  This doesn’t necessarily disqualify the paper, but weakens its impact.
2. **Performance is not consistently superior:**  The results show competitive, but not consistently superior, performance compared to existing methods.  On some datasets, the baselines (especially the strong ones like Choi et al., 2016b) outperform the proposed method. This raises questions about the practical benefits of the proposed approach. The gains seem more pronounced on the larger datasets, suggesting the method might benefit from more data.
3. **Evaluation Primarily Relies on Correlation:** The evaluation relies heavily on Spearman’s rank correlation.  While this is a standard metric, it doesn't provide a complete picture of the embedding quality.  Downstream task performance (e.g., entity linking, relation extraction) would provide stronger evidence of the usefulness of the learned embeddings.
4. **Fleiss’ Kappa for the New Dataset is Moderate:** The Fleiss’ Kappa of 0.24 for the inter-annotator agreement on the novel dataset indicates fair agreement, which is a concern. While usable, the dataset's reliability might be lower than ideal, potentially influencing the results.




**Questions to Authors:**

1.  Could you provide a more detailed analysis of *why* the proposed method performs worse than Choi et al. (2016b) on the smaller biomedical datasets? Is it due to the limited data, the specific choices made in Choi’s model, or other factors?
2.  Have you explored any downstream tasks to evaluate the usefulness of the learned embeddings? If not, do you have any plans to do so?
3.  Given the moderate Fleiss’ Kappa for the new dataset, could you elaborate on the steps taken to ensure data quality and mitigate the impact of low inter-annotator agreement? Specifically, what was the impact of removing participants who did not answer the validation questions correctly?
4.   The hyperparameter tuning section mentions that settings optimized for intrinsic evaluations might degrade performance in extrinsic applications.  Have you investigated this observation further?  Are there any specific hyperparameters that seem particularly sensitive to this trade-off?




**Additional Comments:**

*   The code and dataset release is a significant positive aspect of this submission.
*   The writing is generally clear and well-organized.



**Overall Assessment:**

I believe this paper represents a solid contribution to the field. The method is well-motivated, technically sound, and evaluated on a comprehensive set of datasets. While the core method isn't groundbreaking, the practical approach to weakly-supervised concept embedding, coupled with the release of a new dataset, makes this a valuable addition to the literature. I recommend **Accepting** the paper, with the caveat that the authors address the weaknesses and questions raised above.
