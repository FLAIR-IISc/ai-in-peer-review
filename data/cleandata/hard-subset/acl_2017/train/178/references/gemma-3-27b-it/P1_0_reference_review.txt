## Peer Review for "A Weakly-Supervised Method for Jointly Embedding Concepts, Phrases, and Words"

**Summary:**

This paper presents a weakly-supervised method for jointly embedding concepts, phrases, and words using distributional similarity training, leveraging distant supervision from existing ontologies (UMLS, YAGO). The core idea is to train embeddings by considering the contexts of phrases that represent a given concept. The authors validate their approach on biomedical and general-domain concept similarity and relatedness tasks, demonstrating competitive performance against both supervised and unsupervised baselines, and providing a new dataset for general-domain entity similarity/relatedness.

**Main Contributions (as I see them):**

1. **Novel Dataset:** The creation and release of a new dataset for evaluating similarity and relatedness of real-world entities (people, places, organizations) is a valuable contribution. Existing datasets in this area are limited.
2. **Practical Weakly-Supervised Approach:** The paper demonstrates a viable approach to learning concept embeddings without extensive human annotation, relying on readily available knowledge bases and unannotated text. This is particularly important for domains like biomedicine where annotation is expensive.
3. **Empirical Validation:** The comprehensive experimental evaluation, including comparisons to several baselines and ablation studies, provides solid evidence that the proposed method can achieve competitive performance.

**Strengths:**

1. **Strong Empirical Results:** The results consistently demonstrate that the proposed method performs competitively with, and in some cases outperforms, strong baselines, particularly on the larger datasets. The performance on the biomedical datasets is encouraging, showing the method's applicability to a challenging domain.
2. **Well-Motivated Approach:** The paper clearly articulates the limitations of word-centric embedding models when dealing with multi-word concepts and the need for a method that can capture semantic meaning independently of surface form. The approach directly addresses this challenge.
3. **Comprehensive Evaluation:** The authors perform a thorough evaluation using multiple datasets and comparison methods. The ablation study with the hyperparameters β and η provides insights into the impact of compositionality constraints. The inclusion of both similarity and relatedness tasks is also a strength.
4. **Clear and Reproducible Methodology:** The paper provides sufficient details about the methodology, including hyperparameter tuning, corpora, and ontologies used.  The authors also released the code and dataset, which greatly facilitates reproducibility.
5. **Interesting Analysis of Embedding Space:** The analysis showing how concepts, phrases, and words relate to each other within the embedding space (Figures 3 & 4) provides valuable insights into the learned representations and helps validate the approach.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Novelty of the Core Method:** While the application to jointly embedding concepts, phrases, and words is a reasonable extension, the core method is based on established techniques (skip-gram with negative sampling). The primary innovation lies in the distant supervision strategy and its application to this specific problem, rather than a fundamentally new embedding algorithm.
2. **Sensitivity to Ontology Quality:** The method heavily relies on the quality of the ontology used for mapping concepts to phrases. Inaccuracies or incompleteness in the ontology could significantly impact the performance.  The paper doesn’t adequately discuss the potential impact of ontology errors.
3. **Low Inter-Annotator Agreement in the New Dataset:**  The Fleiss’ Kappa of 0.24 for the newly created dataset is relatively low, suggesting considerable disagreement among annotators. While the authors mention this, it raises concerns about the reliability and validity of the dataset. More detailed analysis of the disagreements would be helpful.
4. **Hyperparameter Tuning Complexity:** The extensive hyperparameter tuning process, while thorough, adds complexity and might be difficult to replicate without significant computational resources. A more principled approach to hyperparameter optimization might be beneficial.
5. **Limited Discussion of Failure Cases:** The paper primarily focuses on positive results. A more in-depth discussion of instances where the method fails or produces suboptimal embeddings would provide a more balanced assessment.



**Questions to Authors:**

1.  How sensitive are the results to the quality and completeness of the underlying ontologies (UMLS, YAGO)? Could you provide examples of how ontology errors might affect the learned embeddings?
2.  Given the low inter-annotator agreement in the new dataset, could you provide more detail about the nature of the disagreements and potential biases in the annotations?  Did you explore any techniques to mitigate the impact of low agreement (e.g., filtering out unreliable annotators)?
3.  The MAP scores for semantic type clustering showed mixed results. Can you discuss potential reasons for the poor clustering of some semantic types (e.g., “sign or symptom”)?
4.  You mention the possibility of extending the work to downstream NLP tasks. Do you have any preliminary results or plans for applying the learned embeddings to tasks such as entity linking or relation extraction?
5.  Could you elaborate on the computational resources required for hyperparameter tuning and training the models?  Would the tuning process be feasible for researchers with limited computational resources?




**Additional Comments:**

*   The paper is well-written and clearly organized.
*   The supplemental material is helpful and provides valuable details about the experimental setup.
*   The code release is greatly appreciated and will facilitate further research in this area.



**Overall Assessment:**

This is a solid and well-executed paper that presents a practical and effective approach to learning concept embeddings in a weakly-supervised manner. While the core method may not be groundbreakingly novel, the combination of distant supervision, comprehensive evaluation, and the creation of a new dataset makes a valuable contribution to the field. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions, primarily addressing the weaknesses related to ontology quality, dataset reliability, and hyperparameter tuning complexity.
