This paper proposes a weakly-supervised method for jointly embedding concepts, phrases, and words, leveraging existing ontologies and distributional similarity training. The core idea is to use known phrases as distant supervision to train embeddings for related concepts, phrases, and words in an unannotated corpus. The primary contribution is the method itself, combining structured knowledge with word embedding techniques to learn context-based representations without manual annotations. A secondary contribution lies in the creation of a new dataset for evaluating similarity and relatedness of real-world entities.

**Strengths:**

1. **Addresses a meaningful problem:** The paper tackles the challenge of representing concepts that aren’t easily captured by word embeddings alone, particularly those with multiple textual forms or lacking direct lexical overlap. This is a common issue in many NLP applications, especially in specialized domains like biomedicine.
2. **Weakly-supervised approach is valuable:** The reliance on distant supervision significantly reduces the need for costly and time-consuming manual annotation. This makes the method more scalable and applicable to resource-constrained scenarios.
3. **Comprehensive evaluation:** The authors evaluate their approach on multiple datasets – both biomedical and general domain – using various metrics (Spearman’s rank correlation). The inclusion of a newly created dataset is particularly noteworthy, providing a valuable resource for the community.
4. **Detailed analysis of embedding space:** The analysis exploring the relationship between concepts, phrases, and their embeddings (Figures 3 and 4) provides insight into the behavior of the model and validates the preservation of ontological links. This contributes to understanding the learned representations.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited novelty of the core method:** While the combination of techniques is reasonable, the core approach of using distant supervision with word embeddings isn’t fundamentally novel. Similar strategies have been explored in prior work (mentioned in the related work section) – for example, Wang et al. (2014a). The authors need to more clearly articulate *how* their method differs from and improves upon existing approaches.
2. **Performance is not consistently superior:** The results in Table 4 show that the proposed method is not always competitive with strong baselines, particularly on the biomedical datasets, where Choi et al. (2016b) consistently outperforms the proposed method. The authors should analyze why their method underperforms in these cases.
3. **Hyperparameter tuning details are insufficient:** While the authors mention hyperparameter tuning, the description in the supplemental material is somewhat limited. Providing a more detailed justification for the chosen hyperparameters and exploring the impact of different settings could strengthen the results.
4. **Fleiss’ Kappa for the new dataset is relatively low:** The Fleiss’ Kappa value of 0.24 for the new dataset suggests only fair agreement between annotators. This raises concerns about the reliability and quality of the ground truth data used for evaluation.

**Questions to Authors:**

1.  Could you elaborate on the key differences between your method and Wang et al. (2014a)? Specifically, how does your approach address the limitations of relying on binary relations in knowledge bases that are mentioned in your related work section?
2.  Why does your method consistently underperform compared to Choi et al. (2016b) on the biomedical datasets? Could this be related to the specific characteristics of the training corpora or ontologies used?
3.  Given the relatively low Fleiss’ Kappa for your new dataset, what steps were taken to ensure the reliability of the annotations? Would a more rigorous annotation protocol or filtering process improve the quality of the dataset?
4.  The paper mentions that the hyperparameters tuned in the biomedical corpora are the same as used for the other corpora. Can you confirm that a parameter sweep was also conducted on the WIKINYT corpus? Or was that corpus only evaluated with the pre-set hyperparameters?

**Additional Comments:**

*   The notation used in Section 3.2.1 (specifically the loss function equations) could be clarified for better readability.
*   Consider adding a discussion of the computational complexity of the proposed method.
*   Ablation studies examining the contribution of the β and η parameters would be helpful to understand their impact on performance.
