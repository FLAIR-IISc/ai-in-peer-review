## Peer Review for "A Weakly-Supervised Method for Jointly Embedding Concepts, Phrases, and Words"

**Summary:**

This paper presents a weakly-supervised method for jointly embedding concepts, phrases, and words using distributional similarity training. The approach leverages existing ontology knowledge (UMLS & YAGO) to provide distant supervision, replacing known phrases in a corpus with unique identifiers and then training word embeddings using the skip-gram model, adapted to incorporate concept embeddings. The authors validate their method on biomedical and general-domain concept similarity and relatedness tasks, demonstrating competitive performance against existing methods, including those using human annotations. They also present a new dataset for evaluating real-world entity similarity and relatedness.

**Main Contributions (as I see them):**

1. **Novel Dataset:** The creation and release of a new dataset for evaluating similarity and relatedness of real-world entities is a significant contribution. Existing datasets in this area are limited, and this provides a valuable resource for the community.
2. **Practical Weakly-Supervised Approach:** The proposed method offers a practical approach to learning concept embeddings without requiring costly human annotation, which is particularly valuable in domains like biomedicine where annotation is expensive.
3. **Demonstration of Joint Embedding Benefit:** The work provides empirical evidence supporting the benefit of jointly embedding concepts, phrases, and words, particularly in capturing relationships between different textual representations of the same underlying concept.

**Strengths:**

1. **Strong Empirical Evaluation:** The paper includes a comprehensive empirical evaluation across multiple datasets (biomedical and general-domain) and compares against a diverse set of baselines, including strong, previously published methods. This lends credibility to the reported results.
2. **Detailed Methodology:** The method is described with sufficient detail, including clear explanations of the training objective and the hyperparameters involved. The inclusion of the software implementation (cui2vec) further strengthens reproducibility.
3. **Insightful Analysis:** The analysis of embedding organization (Figures 3 & 4) provides valuable insights into how the model captures relationships between concepts, phrases, and words.  The observation that concepts and their representing phrases are embedded close to one another is particularly encouraging.
4. **Addresses a Relevant Problem:** The problem of representing concepts that are not directly encoded in words is important for many NLP tasks.  This work provides a step toward better handling these types of knowledge representations.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Novelty in Method:** While the application to jointly embedding concepts, phrases, and words is reasonable, the core method (adapting word2vec with distant supervision) isn't groundbreaking. It builds heavily on established techniques. The novelty primarily lies in the application and the combined dataset/evaluation.
2. **Suboptimal Performance on General Domain Data:** The results on the newly created general-domain datasets show that the phrase-based concept approximation outperforms the direct concept embeddings. This suggests the current model doesn’t capture the nuances of real-world entities as effectively as it does biomedical concepts. This difference in performance requires further investigation.
3. **Concerns about Inter-Annotator Agreement:** The Fleiss’ kappa value of 0.24 for the new dataset is relatively low, indicating only "fair" agreement among annotators. While the authors address this with filtering, it raises concerns about the reliability of the dataset and the potential impact on the reported results. A more robust data collection process might be necessary.
4. **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** The extensive hyperparameter tuning highlights the sensitivity of the model. While thorough, it also suggests that the method may not generalize well to new datasets without significant tuning efforts.  Reporting the sensitivity analysis in an appendix or as supplementary material would be valuable.



**Questions to Authors:**

1. **Generalization to Other Domains:**  How do you anticipate the performance of your model on domains significantly different from biomedicine and general web data (e.g., legal text, financial reports)?
2. **Impact of Low IAA:** The Kappa score of 0.24 for the novel dataset is somewhat concerning. Could you elaborate on the types of disagreement observed among annotators, and how this might bias the evaluation results?
3. **Method for Phrase Replacement:** The greedy phrase replacement strategy could potentially introduce noise. Have you explored alternative phrase replacement methods (e.g., based on dependency parsing) to improve the quality of the training data?
4. **Computational Cost:** What is the computational cost (training time, memory requirements) of your method compared to the baseline methods, particularly for large-scale datasets?



**Additional Comments:**

*   The formatting of the tables could be improved for readability.
*   The supplemental material is crucial for understanding the hyperparameter tuning process. Make sure it's well-organized and accessible.
*   Consider discussing the limitations of distant supervision in more detail, including potential sources of noise and bias.
