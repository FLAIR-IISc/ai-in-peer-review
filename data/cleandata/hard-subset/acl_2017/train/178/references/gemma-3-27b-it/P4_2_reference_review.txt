This paper proposes a weakly-supervised method for jointly embedding concepts, phrases, and words, leveraging distant supervision from existing ontologies. The core idea is to train embeddings using the contexts of known phrases to represent underlying concepts, without requiring human annotations. The authors evaluate their approach on biomedical concepts and real-world entities, demonstrating competitive performance on similarity and relatedness tasks, and introducing a new dataset for the latter.

The primary contribution of this work is the development of a practical and effective method for jointly embedding concepts, phrases, and words using only weakly-supervised data. While joint embedding isn’t entirely novel, the specific approach of using phrase contexts as a signal for concept embeddings, combined with the introduced hyperparameters for balancing compositionality, offers a valuable addition to existing techniques. The method demonstrates a reasonable trade-off between leveraging structured knowledge and learning from distributional data. A second, significant contribution is the introduction of a novel dataset for evaluating similarity and relatedness of real-world entities. This dataset fills a gap in existing resources and provides a valuable benchmark for future research. Finally, the analysis revealing how the ontological structure is reflected in the organization of the learned embeddings provides useful insights into the model’s behavior.

**Strengths:**

1. **Practical Weakly-Supervised Approach:** The proposed method offers a compelling solution for learning concept embeddings without the need for expensive human annotations. This is particularly valuable in domains like biomedicine where annotation is costly and requires specialized expertise. The use of readily available ontologies as a source of weak supervision is a pragmatic and scalable approach.
2. **Novel Dataset:** The creation and release of the new dataset for evaluating the similarity and relatedness of real-world entities is a significant contribution. Existing datasets are often limited in scope or focus on words rather than entities. This dataset provides a more comprehensive and relevant benchmark for evaluating models in this area.
3. **Compositionality Control:** The introduction of the hyperparameters β and η, allowing control over the compositionality of phrases and concepts, is a thoughtful addition. This allows the model to adapt to different types of expressions, acknowledging that some phrases are compositional while others are not.
4. **Empirical Validation:** The authors provide a thorough empirical evaluation, comparing their method against several baselines and state-of-the-art approaches. The results demonstrate competitive performance on a variety of tasks and datasets.
5. **Analysis of Embedding Space:** The analysis of the learned embedding space, showing how ontological structures are reflected in the organization of the embeddings, provides valuable insights into the model’s behavior and confirms its ability to capture meaningful semantic relationships.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Novelty of Joint Embedding:** While the proposed method is effective, the concept of jointly embedding different linguistic units is not entirely new. The core contribution lies in the specific implementation and the application to weakly-supervised learning. More emphasis could be placed on differentiating this work from prior approaches in terms of specific algorithmic choices and the benefits gained from the proposed method.
2. **Sensitivity to Ontology Quality:** The performance of the method is inherently tied to the quality and completeness of the underlying ontology. Errors or inconsistencies in the ontology can propagate to the learned embeddings. This limitation should be explicitly acknowledged and discussed, potentially with suggestions for mitigating its impact.
3. **Fleiss’ Kappa for the New Dataset:** The reported Fleiss’ kappa of 0.24 for the new real-world entity dataset indicates only fair agreement between annotators. While acceptable, this relatively low agreement raises concerns about the reliability of the dataset and the potential for noise in the evaluation results. More details about the annotation process and the distribution of disagreements would be helpful.
4. **Hyperparameter Tuning Complexity:** The method introduces several hyperparameters (β, η, negative samples, dimensionality, window size), which require careful tuning. The paper provides some details about the tuning process, but a more systematic and comprehensive analysis of the impact of each hyperparameter would be beneficial.

**Questions to Authors:**

1. How sensitive is the performance of your method to the quality and completeness of the ontologies used? Have you experimented with using different ontologies or with incorporating external knowledge sources to improve ontology coverage?
2. Can you provide more details about the annotation process for the new real-world entity dataset? Specifically, what instructions were given to annotators, and what measures were taken to ensure data quality? Are there specific pairs of entities where disagreement was particularly high, and if so, why do you think that is the case?
3. Could you elaborate on the computational cost of the proposed method, particularly in terms of training time and memory requirements, compared to other approaches?
4. How do the learned embeddings perform on downstream tasks such as entity linking or relation extraction?



**Additional Comments:**

*   The notation used in the paper could be more consistent. For example, the definition of `Cs` in Section 3.2.1 is unclear.
*   The discussion of related work could be more focused on methods that are directly relevant to the proposed approach.
*   Consider including error analysis to better understand the limitations of the model and identify areas for improvement.
