## Peer Review for "A Weakly-Supervised Method for Jointly Embedding Concepts, Phrases, and Words"

**Summary:**

This paper presents a weakly-supervised method for jointly embedding concepts, phrases, and words using distributional similarity training. The authors leverage existing ontology knowledge to link concepts to phrases and then utilize these links as a form of distant supervision during embedding training. The core idea is to train embeddings for concepts by using the context of their corresponding phrases in an unannotated corpus. The method is evaluated on biomedical and general-domain concept similarity and relatedness tasks, demonstrating competitive performance against models requiring human annotations. A novel dataset for real-world entity similarity and relatedness is also presented.

**Main Contributions (as I see them):**

1. **Novel Dataset:** The creation and release of a new dataset for evaluating similarity and relatedness of real-world entities (people, places, organizations) is a valuable contribution to the field. Existing datasets are limited, and this provides a new benchmark for evaluating embedding models.
2. **Practical Weakly-Supervised Approach:** The proposed method offers a viable approach to learning concept embeddings without the need for expensive manual annotations, which is particularly beneficial in domains like biomedicine where annotation is costly.
3. **Demonstration of Joint Embedding Benefits:** The paper effectively demonstrates that incorporating concept information into the embedding process, even through weak supervision, improves the quality of representations and their ability to capture semantic relationships.

**Strengths:**

1. **Clear Motivation & Problem Definition:** The paper clearly articulates the limitations of purely word-based embedding models when dealing with multi-word expressions and the need for representing concepts independent of their surface forms.
2. **Solid Experimental Evaluation:** The authors perform a comprehensive evaluation across multiple datasets (biomedical and general domain) and compare their method against a range of baselines, including strong supervised approaches. The inclusion of ablation studies (comparing different approximations) helps to isolate the benefits of their approach.
3. **Insightful Analysis:** The analysis of embedding space organization (clustering of semantic types, relationship between concepts/phrases/words) provides valuable insights into the model’s behavior and its ability to capture semantic properties. The MAP evaluation provides concrete evidence supporting the model's learning.
4. **Reproducibility:** The release of code and the dataset significantly enhances the reproducibility of the research and allows other researchers to build upon this work.
5. **Well-Written and Organized:** The paper is clearly written and well-organized, making it easy to follow the authors' approach and understand the experimental results.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Novelty of the Core Method:** While the application to jointly embedding concepts, phrases, and words is well-executed, the underlying methodology (adapting word2vec with distant supervision) is not fundamentally novel. The core technical contribution is the specific application of this technique to this specific problem.
2. **Performance Gains are Modest:**  The performance gains achieved over strong baselines, particularly on the biomedical datasets, are often modest. While competitive, the results don’t represent a substantial leap forward.  The authors acknowledge this, but it raises questions about the practical impact of the approach in real-world applications.
3. **Sensitivity to Hyperparameter Tuning:** The sensitivity to hyperparameters, as described in the paper, is a concern. The process of identifying optimal hyperparameters could be challenging and time-consuming for users applying this method to new datasets.  While the authors detail the tuning process, it remains a barrier to entry.
4. **Fleiss' Kappa for New Dataset:** The Fleiss’ kappa score of 0.24 for the newly created dataset suggests only “fair” agreement between annotators. While acceptable, this raises concerns about the reliability and robustness of the dataset, and the potential impact on the evaluation results.



**Questions to Authors:**

1.  Could you elaborate on the potential computational cost of the hyperparameter tuning process for applying this method to a new, large-scale dataset? Would you suggest any strategies for reducing this cost (e.g., transfer learning from similar datasets)?
2.  The paper mentions that the learned embeddings can be used for entity linking. Could you provide more detail on how this would be implemented and what specific benefits this approach would offer over existing entity linking methods?
3.  Regarding the relatively low Fleiss’ kappa score for the new dataset, were any steps taken to improve inter-annotator agreement during data collection (e.g., clearer guidelines, training examples)? And how might this affect the reliability of your evaluation on the general domain tasks?
4.  The ablation studies show that phrase-based approximations perform well. How does the choice of approximation (APPROXPHRco vs. APPROXWORD) depend on the specific application or domain?

**Additional Comments:**

*   The authors should consider adding a discussion of the limitations of relying on pre-defined ontologies. The quality and coverage of the ontology can significantly impact the performance of the method.
*   It would be helpful to include a more detailed error analysis to understand the types of errors the model makes and identify potential areas for improvement.
*   A short example of a typical use case scenario, perhaps in the biomedical domain, would help illustrate the practical benefits of the approach.



Overall, this is a solid and well-executed paper that makes a valuable contribution to the field of semantic embeddings. While the core methodology may not be groundbreaking, the combination of a practical approach, a novel dataset, and insightful analysis makes it a worthy addition to the ACL conference. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions addressing the weaknesses and questions outlined above.
