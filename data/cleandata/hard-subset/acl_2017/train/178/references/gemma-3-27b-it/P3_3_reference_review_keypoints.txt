- Presents a weakly-supervised method to jointly embed concepts, phrases, and words using ontologies and distributional contexts.
- Uses known phrases as distant supervision to train concept embeddings from phrase contexts.
- Validates approach on biomedical and general-domain datasets and introduces a new real-world entity similarity dataset.

- Practical, scalable method for jointly embedding concepts and their textual realizations without costly manual annotation.
- Provides a novel dataset for evaluating real-world entity similarity, filling a benchmark gap.
- Thorough empirical evaluation with ablations and analysis of embedding space organization.

- Addresses an important problem of representing concepts beyond lexical forms, especially relevant in biomedicine.
- Adapts word2vec with distant supervision and introduces β and η hyperparameters to control compositionality.
- Shows competitive empirical results versus strong baselines and improvements over word-averaging.
- Offers comprehensive evaluation including semantic type clustering and embedding space analyses.
- Releases code and dataset to foster reproducibility.

- Core technique (word2vec adaptation with distant supervision) lacks strong novelty; novelty is mostly in application.
- Performance depends heavily on ontology quality (UMLS, YAGO), with limited discussion of this dependency.
- New dataset has low annotator agreement (Fleiss’ Kappa = 0.24), raising concerns about reliability.
- Hyperparameter sensitivity varies across corpora, questioning generalizability of tuned settings.
- Lacks evaluation on downstream tasks (e.g., entity linking, relation extraction) to demonstrate real-world impact.

- Please elaborate on types and sources of annotator disagreements in the new dataset and which entity pairs were challenging.
- Explain why optimal hyperparameters differ substantially across corpora and whether corpus properties correlate with settings.
- Describe envisioned approaches for integrating ontology structure directly into the training objective.
- Why weren’t recent transformer-based joint embedding methods included for comparison, and how does this method qualitatively compare?
- Have you tried weighting schemes (e.g., IDF-like) for concepts with differing numbers of representative phrases to prevent dominance by frequent concepts?

- Paper is well-written, clearly organized, and includes helpful supplemental material.
- Code and dataset availability are strong positives.

- Solid, well-executed paper addressing an important problem; novelty is limited but contributions and evaluation merit acceptance with minor revisions.
