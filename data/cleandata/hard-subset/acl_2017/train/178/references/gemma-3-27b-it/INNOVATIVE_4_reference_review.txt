This paper introduces a weakly-supervised method for jointly embedding concepts, phrases, and words, leveraging structured knowledge from ontologies and unannotated text. The core idea – using known phrases as distant supervision for training concept embeddings – is genuinely interesting and addresses a long-standing issue in semantic representation: the inability of standard word embeddings to adequately represent multi-word concepts and their various surface forms. The creation of the new dataset for real-world entity similarity/relatedness is also a valuable contribution, as such resources are lacking in the field.

**Strengths:**

1. **Novelty of Approach:** The paper tackles a significant problem with a relatively novel approach. Existing methods often rely on strong supervision or compositionality assumptions that don’t hold for many real-world concepts. This work sidesteps the need for extensive annotation by smartly utilizing existing ontological knowledge.
2. **Joint Embedding Space:**  The concept of creating a unified embedding space for concepts, phrases, and words is compelling. This allows for reasoning across different levels of abstraction and facilitates knowledge transfer. While not entirely new (some prior work exists in this space), the specific implementation leveraging distant supervision is a strong point.
3. **New Dataset:** The creation of a new dataset for evaluating real-world entity similarity and relatedness fills a gap in the existing resources. This dataset, despite the fair agreement among annotators, provides a valuable benchmark for evaluating methods in this area.
4. **Promising Results:** While not consistently outperforming all baselines, the results, particularly on the larger datasets, are competitive with methods requiring significantly more supervision. The analysis showing the reflection of ontological structure within the embedding space provides encouraging evidence of the method’s effectiveness.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Analysis of Failure Cases:** The paper presents results but doesn’t delve deeply into *why* the method sometimes underperforms. A more detailed analysis of failure cases – specific concepts or phrases where the embeddings are demonstrably poor – would be very valuable.
2. **Hyperparameter Sensitivity & Tuning:** While the authors perform hyperparameter tuning, the report lacks detail about the search space and the rationale behind the chosen settings. The dependence on corpus-specific tuning suggests a lack of generalizability and a potential brittleness to different domains.
3. **Weakness of Distant Supervision:**  The reliance on distant supervision introduces inherent noise. The assumption that every instance of a known phrase signifies the corresponding concept is a strong one, and likely introduces inaccuracies in the training data. The paper doesn’t fully address the potential impact of this noise.
4. **Evaluation Metric Limitations:** Spearman's rho, while common, might not be the most sensitive metric for evaluating the nuances of semantic relationships. Exploring other evaluation metrics that capture different aspects of semantic similarity would strengthen the analysis.



**Questions to Authors:**

1. Can you provide a more detailed analysis of the failure cases? Specifically, what types of concepts or phrases consistently yield poor embeddings, and why do you think this is happening?
2.  The hyperparameter tuning seems highly corpus-dependent. What steps have you taken to mitigate this dependence and ensure the model generalizes well to unseen data?
3. How sensitive are the results to the quality of the ontology (e.g., completeness of phrase mappings to concepts)? Have you investigated the impact of using different or incomplete ontologies?
4. Would you consider an ablation study to analyze the impact of the β and η parameters, perhaps by showing the performance change with different combinations of settings or by removing them altogether?

**Additional Comments:**

The code and dataset are made publicly available, which is excellent. The writing is generally clear and concise. The related work section is thorough, but could be slightly more focused on the most relevant prior art.



Overall, this is a promising paper with a novel and potentially impactful approach. While there are weaknesses, they are not fatal. The novelty of the idea, combined with the valuable contribution of the new dataset, tips the balance towards acceptance, even with the limitations in the analysis and evaluation. I believe this work has the potential to stimulate further research in this important area.
