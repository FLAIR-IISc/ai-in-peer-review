This paper proposes a weakly-supervised method for jointly embedding concepts, phrases, and words, leveraging structured knowledge from ontologies and distributional similarity training. The primary contribution is an attempt to bridge the gap between lexical forms and underlying concepts in semantic space, without relying on extensive human annotation. A secondary contribution is the creation of two novel datasets for evaluating similarity and relatedness of real-world entities.

**Strengths:**

1. **Addresses a Relevant Problem:** The issue of representing concepts independent of their lexical realization is a longstanding challenge in NLP. This work directly tackles this by attempting to learn concept embeddings grounded in both distributional information and ontological structure.
2. **Weakly-Supervised Approach:** The use of distant supervision via phrase mapping is a practical and scalable approach, particularly for domains where annotated data is scarce (like biomedicine). This is a significant advantage over methods requiring extensive manual annotation.
3. **Novel Datasets:** The introduction of the similarity and relatedness datasets for real-world entities fills a gap in existing evaluation resources and allows for a more comprehensive assessment of the model’s performance.
4. **Comprehensive Evaluation:** The paper provides a reasonably thorough evaluation, comparing the proposed model against several baselines, including strong pre-trained embeddings and approximation methods. The inclusion of multiple datasets (biomedical and general domain) is also commendable.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Suboptimal Hyperparameter Tuning:** The hyperparameter tuning process, while mentioned, feels insufficiently rigorous. The cross-validation scheme is acknowledged as not ideal and the description lacks details on how the best settings were selected. The limited range of parameters explored is concerning.
2. **Ambiguous Training Objective:** The introduction of β and η to control compositionality feels ad-hoc and lacks a strong theoretical justification. The effect of these parameters isn’t thoroughly analyzed, and the best settings appear corpus-dependent without clear explanation.
3. **Limited Performance Gains:** While the model achieves competitive results, the improvements over strong baselines (e.g., CHIU-2, pre-trained embeddings) are often marginal. This raises questions about the practical impact of the proposed method. The assertion of superior performance in certain tasks is sometimes overstated given the small margins.
4. **Dataset Quality Concerns:** The Fleiss’ Kappa value of 0.24 for the newly created datasets suggests only fair agreement among annotators. This raises concerns about the reliability and validity of the evaluation results. While acknowledged, the impact of this low agreement is not adequately addressed.
5. **Inconsistent Reporting and Detail:** Several minor inconsistencies and omissions exist. The description of the data preprocessing (phrase replacement) lacks clarity and the code availability is merely stated without providing a link. Details in the experimental setup are also sometimes vague, making reproducibility challenging.

**Questions to Authors:**

1. Can you provide a more detailed explanation of the hyperparameter tuning process, including the search space explored and the criteria used to select the final settings?
2. What is the theoretical motivation behind the introduction of β and η? Can you provide a more nuanced analysis of their impact on the learned embeddings and downstream performance?
3. How sensitive are your results to the quality of the ontology mapping (i.e., the mapping between concepts and phrases)? Have you explored alternative mapping strategies or assessed the impact of mapping errors?
4. Given the low inter-annotator agreement in the new datasets, can you provide evidence that the results are still meaningful and reliable? What steps were taken to mitigate the impact of annotator disagreement?
5. Please provide a link to the publicly available code and datasets.



**Additional Comments:**

* The writing quality could be improved. Several sentences are convoluted or awkwardly phrased.
* The related work section is extensive but could be more focused and critical.
* The use of abbreviations (e.g., “CUI”) should be consistent and defined upon first mention.
* The supplemental material is referenced but not attached to this review, making it impossible to verify some claims.



**Overall Assessment:**

Despite addressing a relevant problem and presenting a novel approach, the paper suffers from several weaknesses, particularly regarding hyperparameter tuning, dataset quality, and marginal performance gains. The inconsistencies and lack of detail further detract from the overall quality of the work. While the contribution of new datasets is valuable, it does not outweigh the shortcomings in the methodological rigor and empirical results. I lean towards **rejection**, as the presented work, while not *incorrect*, doesn't meet the standards for acceptance at a competitive conference like ACL. Significant revisions addressing the weaknesses outlined above would be necessary for reconsideration.
