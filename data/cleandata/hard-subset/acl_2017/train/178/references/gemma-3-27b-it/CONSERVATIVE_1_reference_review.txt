This paper proposes a weakly-supervised method for jointly embedding concepts, phrases, and words, leveraging structured knowledge from ontologies and distributional similarity training. The primary contribution is a method for creating concept embeddings without human annotation, using distant supervision from known phrases. A secondary contribution is the release of a novel dataset for evaluating similarity and relatedness of real-world entities. A third, less substantial, contribution is an analysis of the resulting embedding space, demonstrating some reflection of ontological structure.

**Strengths:**

1. **Addresses a relevant problem:** The challenge of representing concepts that aren't directly lexicalized, and handling synonymy, is a longstanding issue in NLP. The authors correctly identify the limitations of purely word-based embedding approaches.
2. **Sound methodological foundation:** The adaptation of the skip-gram model with negative sampling is a reasonable and well-established approach. The incorporation of β and η to control compositionality appears thoughtfully considered, even if the results aren't overwhelmingly positive (see weaknesses).
3. **Comprehensive experimental setup:** The paper performs experiments across multiple corpora (PubMed, Clinical, WikiNYT) and ontologies (UMLS, YAGO). The comparison to several baselines, including both approximation methods using existing embeddings and state-of-the-art biomedical concept embeddings, is commendable.
4. **Publicly available dataset:** The creation and release of a dataset for evaluating real-world entity similarity and relatedness is a valuable contribution to the community, as such resources are relatively scarce.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited novelty:** While the proposed method is a reasonable extension of existing techniques (word2vec, distant supervision), it lacks significant novelty. The core idea of leveraging known phrases as supervision for concept embeddings feels incremental, rather than groundbreaking. The improvements over strong baselines, particularly on the larger datasets, are modest.
2. **Insufficient evidence of substantial gains:** The results presented in Table 4 are mixed. While the method performs competitively, it is often outperformed by stronger baselines like Choi et al. (2016b), which *do* rely on some form of human annotation. The gains achieved through the proposed method aren’t substantial enough to justify the complexity added. The performance on the real-world entity datasets (Table 5) is even less convincing, with concept approximations consistently outperforming the jointly trained concept embeddings.
3. **Overly optimistic framing of the evaluation:** The authors state their results are competitive with prior models requiring human annotation. However, the annotation used in the baselines (e.g., Choi et al.) is often of a different *type* – focused on identifying instances within text, rather than generating broad semantic representations. This difference makes direct comparison somewhat misleading. The authors should acknowledge this distinction more explicitly.
4. **Fleiss’ Kappa for the new dataset is relatively low:** A Fleiss’ Kappa of 0.24 suggests only "fair agreement" among annotators for the newly created similarity/relatedness dataset. This raises concerns about the quality and reliability of the dataset itself, potentially impacting the validity of the reported results. Further analysis of disagreements and potential sources of bias would be beneficial.

**Questions to Authors:**

1. The β and η hyperparameters are described as controlling compositionality. However, the results suggest they often *hurt* performance. Can you provide a more in-depth analysis of how these parameters affect the learned embeddings, and why they don't consistently lead to improvements?
2. The performance of the proposed method is often lower than that of Choi et al. (2016b), which uses a different type of annotation. How do you account for this difference in annotation methodology when interpreting the results?
3. Can you provide more details about the distribution of disagreements among annotators for the new similarity/relatedness dataset? Were there specific entity pairs where agreement was particularly low, and if so, why do you think this was the case?
4. The paper states that the hyperparameter tuning process was performed independently for each corpus. Could you provide the results of a grid search conducted across all corpora simultaneously? This could reveal whether there is a single optimal hyperparameter setting that generalizes well.



**Additional Comments:**

*   The formatting in Table 3 is a bit awkward; it would be clearer to present the hyperparameters in a more concise format.
*   The discussion section briefly touches on the limitations of evaluating embeddings using similarity/relatedness tasks. Expanding on this point and discussing potential alternative evaluation strategies would be valuable.
*   The authors should provide a link to the code and the new dataset.



Overall, this paper presents a solid, but ultimately incremental, contribution. The method is well-motivated and technically sound, but the lack of substantial performance gains and the limitations of the evaluation prevent me from strongly recommending acceptance. While the new dataset is a valuable resource, it is not sufficient to outweigh the other weaknesses. I recommend rejection with the suggestion that the authors conduct further analysis and exploration of more novel techniques.
