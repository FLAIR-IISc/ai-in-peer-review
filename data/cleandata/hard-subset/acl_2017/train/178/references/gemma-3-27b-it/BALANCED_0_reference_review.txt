This paper proposes a weakly-supervised method for jointly embedding concepts, phrases, and words, leveraging distant supervision from existing ontologies. The core idea is to train embeddings by considering the contexts in which known phrases (representing concepts) appear. The authors evaluate their approach on biomedical concepts and real-world entities, demonstrating competitive performance on similarity and relatedness tasks. A novel dataset of real-world entity similarity and relatedness is also introduced.

**Contributions:**

1. **Joint Embedding Framework:** The paper presents a novel framework for jointly embedding concepts, phrases, and words, which addresses the limitations of traditional word embedding approaches that struggle with multi-word expressions and concept variations. This is a conceptually sound approach.
2. **Weakly-Supervised Approach:**  The use of distant supervision through ontology mappings is a practical and scalable method for learning concept embeddings without relying on expensive human annotations.  This is a significant practical contribution.
3. **Novel Dataset:** The introduction of a new dataset for evaluating similarity and relatedness of real-world entities is a valuable resource for the research community. This is a concrete contribution that facilitates future work.

**Strengths:**

1. **Clear Problem Statement and Motivation:** The paper clearly articulates the problem of representing concepts beyond simple word combinations and motivates the need for a more sophisticated approach. The examples provided (“the Big Apple”, “Lou Gehrig’s disease”) effectively illustrate the challenge.
2. **Sound Methodology:** The adaptation of the skip-gram model with negative sampling is a reasonable choice, and the inclusion of hyperparameters (β and η) to control compositionality is a thoughtful extension.  The approach of replacing phrases with unique identifiers is a clever way to enable training.
3. **Comprehensive Evaluation:** The authors evaluate their method on a variety of datasets (both biomedical and general-domain) and compare it to several baselines, including strong pre-trained embeddings. The inclusion of both similarity and relatedness tasks provides a more thorough assessment.
4. **Detailed Experimental Setup:** The paper provides a detailed description of the training corpora, ontologies used, and hyperparameter tuning process. This facilitates reproducibility and allows for a better understanding of the results.
5. **Analysis of Embedding Space:** The analysis of the embedding space, including the visualization of concept-phrase and phrase-word relationships, provides insights into the behavior of the model.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Performance Gains:** While the results are competitive, the performance gains over strong baselines (particularly the pre-trained embeddings) are often modest. This raises questions about the extent to which the proposed method truly advances the state-of-the-art.
2. **Impact of Hyperparameter Tuning:** The impact of the β and η hyperparameters is not consistently beneficial. The analysis suggests that they may even degrade performance in some cases. This suggests that the current implementation of these hyperparameters could be improved.
3. **Fleiss’ Kappa for Dataset Reliability:** The Fleiss’ Kappa score for the newly created dataset (0.24) indicates fair agreement between annotators, which is relatively low. This could raise concerns about the reliability of the dataset and its suitability for rigorous evaluation. More effort on dataset quality control could be beneficial.
4. **Lack of Ablation Studies:**  The paper would benefit from ablation studies to isolate the contribution of each component of the model. For example, removing the β and η hyperparameters or training without the concept embeddings would help to understand their individual effects.
5. **Limited Discussion of Error Analysis:** The paper lacks a detailed discussion of error analysis. Identifying specific cases where the model fails could provide valuable insights for future improvements.

**Questions to Authors:**

1.  The β and η hyperparameters didn't consistently improve performance. Could you elaborate on why you believe this is the case?  Are there alternative ways to incorporate compositionality that might be more effective?
2. Given the relatively low Fleiss’ Kappa for your new dataset, what steps were taken to ensure its quality and reliability? Would more stringent quality control measures improve the results?
3. Could you provide some examples of concept pairs where your method significantly outperforms the baselines, and conversely, examples where it performs poorly? What characteristics differentiate these cases?
4. How sensitive is the performance of your model to the quality of the ontology mappings (i.e., the accuracy of the phrase-to-concept associations)?



**Additional Comments:**

*   The paper is generally well-written and clearly organized.
*   The provided code and dataset would be highly valuable to the research community.
*   The reference list is comprehensive and up-to-date.
*   The discussion section could be expanded to include a more critical assessment of the limitations of the proposed approach and potential directions for future research.



**Overall:**

This paper presents a valuable contribution to the field of semantic representation learning. The proposed method is conceptually sound, and the evaluation is thorough. While the performance gains are modest, the approach is promising and the introduction of a new dataset is a significant asset. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions, specifically addressing the weaknesses outlined above and answering the questions posed.
