This paper proposes a weakly-supervised method for jointly embedding concepts, phrases, and words, leveraging structured knowledge from ontologies and distributional similarity training. The core idea is to use known phrases as distant supervision to train embeddings for concepts, phrases, and words, effectively propagating semantic information. The authors evaluate their approach on biomedical and general-domain datasets, demonstrating competitive performance on concept similarity and relatedness tasks and introducing a new dataset for real-world entity similarity.

The primary contribution is the demonstration that reasonable concept embeddings can be learned with minimal supervision, utilizing only phrase-concept mappings from existing ontologies and standard word embedding techniques. While the method itself isn’t groundbreaking, the ability to bypass the need for extensive manual annotation for concept representation is a potentially valuable outcome. A secondary contribution is the introduction of a new dataset for evaluating similarity and relatedness of real-world entities, which could be useful for future research. Finally, the analysis demonstrating that ontological structure is partially reflected in the organization of the embeddings provides some insight into the model’s behavior.

**Strengths:**

1. **Practicality of Weak Supervision:** The primary strength lies in the practical approach of leveraging readily available knowledge bases for supervision. The idea of using phrase-concept mappings to bootstrap concept embeddings without manual annotation is sensible and addresses a significant bottleneck in semantic representation learning.
2. **Competitive Performance:** The results, while not dramatically superior to existing methods, are competitive, particularly considering the lack of human annotation. Achieving comparable performance to supervised approaches is a positive indicator of the method’s effectiveness.
3. **Novel Dataset:** The introduction of a new dataset for evaluating real-world entity similarity is a welcome addition to the field. The lack of such datasets has hindered progress in evaluating semantic models for entities, and this contribution addresses that gap.
4. **Analysis of Embedding Space:** The analysis showing that the embedding space reflects the underlying ontology structure, with concepts and phrases clustering reasonably well, provides some evidence that the model is learning meaningful representations. This lends credibility to the approach.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Lack of Novelty in Method:** The core method – adapting word2vec with distant supervision – feels incremental. The underlying techniques are well-established, and the contribution is primarily in the application to concept embedding rather than a novel algorithmic innovation. The authors attempt to address compositionality with β and η, but the impact appears limited and the parameters themselves require significant tuning.
2. **Limited Evaluation:** The evaluation primarily focuses on similarity and relatedness tasks. While these are valuable, they don't fully demonstrate the utility of the learned concept embeddings in more complex downstream applications (e.g., knowledge base completion, information retrieval, question answering). Performance on these tasks would provide stronger evidence of the method's practical value. The cross-validation described in the supplement appears overly generous.
3. **Overstated Claims of Competitiveness:** While competitive, the paper sometimes overstates the level of performance. For example, the claim of being “competitive with prior models that required human annotations” is qualified by the fact that Choi et al. outperform the method on the smaller biomedical datasets. More nuanced language would be appropriate.
4. **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** The extensive hyperparameter tuning reported in the supplementary material suggests the method is sensitive to parameter settings, potentially limiting its generalizability and ease of use. A more robust method would ideally be less reliant on careful tuning. The choice of hyperparameters feels arbitrary in the absence of a more thorough ablation study.
5. **Fleiss’ Kappa for New Dataset:** A Fleiss’ kappa of 0.24 for the new dataset is fairly low, indicating only fair agreement between annotators. This raises concerns about the reliability and quality of the dataset itself.

**Questions to Authors:**

1. Could you provide results on more challenging downstream tasks to demonstrate the utility of the learned concept embeddings beyond similarity/relatedness? Specifically, results on a knowledge base completion or information retrieval task would be valuable.
2. How sensitive are the results to the choice of phrases used as distant supervision? If the mapping between concepts and phrases is incomplete or inaccurate, how does that impact performance?
3. Can you provide a more detailed ablation study of the β and η parameters, explaining their impact on performance and providing guidelines for their selection?
4. Given the low Fleiss’ Kappa for the new dataset, what steps were taken to ensure its quality and reliability? Did you consider excluding participants or pairs with low agreement?
