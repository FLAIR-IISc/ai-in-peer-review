This paper proposes a weakly-supervised method for jointly embedding concepts, phrases, and words, leveraging an ontology and distributional similarity training. The core idea is to use known phrases as distant supervision to train embeddings for corresponding concepts, effectively bridging the gap between lexical forms and abstract semantic entities. The authors validate their approach on biomedical and general-domain datasets, demonstrating competitive performance on concept similarity and relatedness tasks, and introduce a novel dataset for real-world entity similarity/relatedness. 

The primary contribution is the proposed method itself – a relatively straightforward adaptation of word2vec to handle concepts in addition to words and phrases. A secondary contribution lies in the introduction of a new dataset for evaluating entity similarity and relatedness, a resource that is arguably lacking in the general domain. 

**Strengths:**

1. **Sound Methodology:** The proposed method is built upon established techniques (word2vec) and principles (distant supervision). While not groundbreaking, it represents a sensible application of well-understood methods to a challenging problem. This adherence to established methodologies reduces the risk of fundamental flaws.
2. **Comprehensive Evaluation:** The authors perform a fairly extensive evaluation, comparing against a diverse set of baselines, including both approximation methods using existing embeddings and specialized concept embedding models.  The inclusion of both biomedical and general-domain datasets strengthens the claims of generalizability.
3. **Novel Dataset:** The creation of a new dataset for real-world entity similarity and relatedness is a valuable contribution to the community, providing a resource for evaluating and comparing different embedding approaches.
4. **Clear Presentation:** The paper is generally well-written and clearly explains the methodology and experimental setup. The inclusion of diagrams helps to visualize the model architecture.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Novelty:** The core method lacks substantial novelty. It’s a fairly direct application of existing techniques, and the reported gains over strong baselines are often marginal. The paper doesn’t convincingly demonstrate that the proposed approach offers a significant advancement over existing methods.  Simply adapting word2vec, even with distant supervision, doesn’t constitute a major breakthrough.
2. **Insufficient Justification for Hyperparameter Choices:** While hyperparameter tuning is mentioned, the rationale behind the selected settings isn’t adequately justified. The discussion of tuning feels somewhat superficial, and the reader is left wondering why specific values were chosen.  The fact that settings varied considerably across corpora suggests a lack of robustness or a deeper understanding of the parameter space.
3. **Weak Performance Gains:** Despite the comprehensive evaluation, the performance improvements are not consistently substantial. In many cases, the proposed method is outperformed by simpler baselines or existing specialized embeddings. This raises concerns about the practical impact of the proposed approach.  The performance on the new dataset, while reasonable, doesn’t demonstrate a clear advantage over existing methods when adapted to this new benchmark.
4. **Fleiss’ Kappa Value for Dataset:** The Fleiss’ kappa of 0.24 reported for the dataset suggests only 'fair agreement' among annotators. This calls into question the reliability of the dataset as a gold standard for evaluation, and suggests potential issues with the quality of the annotations.

**Questions to Authors:**

1.  Could you provide a more detailed explanation of the rationale behind the hyperparameter settings selected for each corpus? What specific analyses led to these choices?
2.  How sensitive are the results to the choice of the 25-token frequency threshold? Did you experiment with different thresholds and observe any significant changes in performance?
3.  Given the marginal performance gains in several experiments, can you provide a more in-depth error analysis to identify specific cases where the proposed method struggles and potential reasons for these failures?
4.  What steps were taken to ensure the quality and reliability of the annotations for the new real-world entity dataset, given the relatively low Fleiss’ kappa value?



**Additional Comments:**

The supplemental material is helpful in providing more details on the hyperparameter tuning process and the dataset creation. The authors should consider providing code for reproducibility. It would be beneficial to see results on more downstream tasks, beyond similarity and relatedness, to assess the practical utility of the learned embeddings.
