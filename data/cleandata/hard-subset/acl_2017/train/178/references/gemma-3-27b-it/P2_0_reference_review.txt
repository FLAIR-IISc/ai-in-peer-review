## Review for ACL: A Weakly-Supervised Method for Jointly Embedding Concepts, Phrases, and Words

**Summary:**

This paper proposes a weakly-supervised method to jointly embed concepts, phrases, and words. The approach leverages an existing ontology to link concepts to phrases and then utilizes these links as distant supervision for training embeddings using a modified skip-gram model. The authors evaluate their method on biomedical and general-domain datasets, demonstrating competitive performance on concept similarity and relatedness tasks, and provide a novel dataset for evaluating real-world entities.

**Main Contributions (as I see them):**

1. **Novel Dataset:** The creation and release of a new dataset for evaluating similarity and relatedness of real-world entities is a valuable contribution to the community. Existing datasets in this area are limited, and this provides a useful benchmark.
2. **Practical Weakly-Supervised Approach:** The paper demonstrates a viable method for learning concept embeddings without relying on extensive human annotation, a significant advantage in domains like biomedicine where annotation is expensive and time-consuming.
3. **Empirical Validation:**  The paper provides thorough empirical validation across multiple datasets and demonstrates competitive performance against existing methods, including those utilizing human-annotated data.



**Strengths:**

1. **Clear Motivation and Problem Definition:** The paper clearly articulates the limitations of purely word-based embedding models in representing concepts with non-compositional semantics and multiple lexical forms. The motivation is well-established and relevant to many NLP applications.
2. **Sound Methodology:** The core idea of using distant supervision based on ontology linkages is logical and well-implemented.  The modifications to the skip-gram model to incorporate concept embeddings and the hyperparameters for controlling compositionality are sensible and well-explained.
3. **Comprehensive Evaluation:** The authors evaluate their approach on a variety of datasets (biomedical and general domain) and compare against a wide range of baselines, including existing embedding methods and state-of-the-art approaches. This provides a strong empirical grounding for their claims.
4. **Insightful Analysis:** The analysis of embedding space organization (Figures 3 & 4) provides valuable insights into how the model is learning to represent concepts, phrases, and words, and demonstrates that the ontological relationships are partially preserved.  The discussion of the limitations regarding the semantic type clustering is also commendable.
5. **Code and Data Release:** Releasing the code and the novel dataset promotes reproducibility and further research in the field.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Novelty in the Core Method:** While the application to jointly embedding concepts, phrases, and words is well-executed, the core methodology (distant supervision + modified skip-gram) is not fundamentally novel. Existing work leverages similar techniques for related tasks (e.g., entity embedding, word sense disambiguation).  The contribution lies more in the specific application and thorough evaluation.
2. **Reliance on Ontology Quality:** The performance of the method is inherently dependent on the quality and completeness of the underlying ontology. The authors acknowledge this, but a more detailed discussion of the potential impact of ontology biases or errors would be beneficial.  How would errors in phrase-concept mappings affect the embeddings?
3. **Performance Gains are Moderate:** While competitive, the performance gains over strong baselines (especially on the biomedical tasks) are often modest.  The authors should better contextualize these gains—are they statistically significant? Do they translate to improvements in downstream tasks?  More convincing results on downstream tasks would strengthen the paper.
4. **Fleiss’ Kappa for the New Dataset:** The Fleiss' Kappa of 0.24 for the new dataset is considered "fair" agreement and relatively low.  This raises questions about the reliability and consistency of the annotations. More information on the quality control measures used during data collection (beyond discarding participants who failed validation questions) would be helpful.




**Questions to Authors:**

1.  Could you elaborate on the potential impact of errors or biases in the ontologies used on the learned embeddings? Have you explored any techniques to mitigate these issues (e.g., filtering noisy mappings)?
2.  While the ablation study on hyperparameters is appreciated, could you provide more insight into the specific impact of β and η on the learned embeddings and their downstream performance?  What types of concepts or phrases benefit most from adjusting these parameters?
3.  What are the computational costs associated with training the embeddings, particularly with larger datasets and higher dimensionalities?
4.  Have you investigated the performance of your embeddings on any downstream tasks (e.g., entity linking, biomedical relation extraction)?  If so, could you provide those results? If not, why not?




**Additional Comments:**

*   The formatting is generally good, but some of the figures are a little small and difficult to read.
*   Consider adding a discussion of related work on knowledge graph embedding techniques beyond those already mentioned.




**Overall Assessment:**

This is a solid and well-executed paper. While the core method isn't groundbreaking, the combination of a practical weakly-supervised approach, comprehensive evaluation, and the release of a valuable new dataset makes it a worthwhile contribution to the field. I recommend **acceptance** with minor revisions addressing the weaknesses outlined above.
