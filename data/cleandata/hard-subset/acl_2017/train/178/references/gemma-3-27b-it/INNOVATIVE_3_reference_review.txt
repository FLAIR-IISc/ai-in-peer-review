This paper presents a weakly-supervised method for jointly embedding concepts, phrases, and words, leveraging distant supervision from an ontology and an unannotated corpus. The primary contribution is the exploration of joint embedding in this manner, effectively bridging the gap between lexical forms and abstract concepts without relying on extensive human annotation. A secondary, valuable contribution is the novel dataset of similarity and relatedness for real-world entities, addressing a gap in existing evaluation resources.

**Strengths:**

1. **Novelty of Approach:** The core idea of jointly embedding concepts, phrases, and words using distant supervision is genuinely interesting and represents a potentially impactful direction. While embedding concepts isn’t new, the specific method of utilizing phrase occurrences as proxies for concept occurrences, and the joint embedding framework, are novel. The paper tackles a fundamental problem in semantic representation – handling non-compositionality and multiple lexicalizations of the same concept.
2. **Practicality & Scalability:** The weakly-supervised nature of the method is a significant strength. Avoiding the need for large-scale human annotation makes the approach much more practical and scalable to different domains and ontologies. This is particularly relevant for specialized domains like biomedicine where annotation is expensive.
3. **Evaluation & Datasets:** The experimental validation, while not groundbreaking in performance, is solid. The inclusion of a new dataset for real-world entity similarity/relatedness is a valuable contribution to the community and facilitates further research in this area. The authors demonstrate reasonable performance on established biomedical datasets too.
4. **Clear Presentation & Implementation:** The paper is well-written and clearly explains the method. Providing the code (cui2vec) is a commendable practice, enabling reproducibility and further development.
5. **Analysis of Embedding Space:** The analysis of the embedding space – demonstrating that concepts and representative phrases are close together and semantic types cluster – provides some insight into the effectiveness of the approach and suggests it’s learning meaningful representations.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Performance Gap:** While competitive, the performance on many tasks doesn’t substantially outperform strong baselines, particularly those utilizing more supervised approaches. The improvement is incremental, and the performance gain doesn't always justify the complexity of the proposed method. The authors acknowledge this but don’t fully address *why* the performance isn't more significant.
2. **Hyperparameter Sensitivity & Tuning:** The hyperparameter tuning section highlights the sensitivity of the model. The optimal hyperparameters vary considerably across corpora, suggesting a lack of robustness or a need for corpus-specific tuning, which diminishes the 'plug-and-play' appeal of the approach. The explanation of why certain hyperparameters are optimal is lacking.
3. **Limited Ablation Study:**  The ablation study focusing on the β and η parameters is not entirely convincing. The authors note that increasing these parameters generally hurts performance, but lack a more detailed understanding of *why* this is happening. A more thorough ablation study, potentially exploring different regularization techniques or architectural modifications, would strengthen the work.
4. **Evaluation Limitations:** The reliance on Spearman’s rank correlation, while standard, provides a limited view of the embedding quality. Exploring downstream tasks (e.g., entity linking, relation extraction) would offer a more compelling demonstration of the utility of the learned embeddings. The datasets used, while reasonable, could benefit from larger sizes and potentially more diverse entity types.
5. **Ontology Dependence:** The performance of the method is inherently tied to the quality and coverage of the underlying ontology. The authors briefly mention this, but a more thorough discussion of how ontology limitations might affect the results would be beneficial.



**Questions to Authors:**

1.  Can you provide a more in-depth analysis of why increasing β and η generally degrades performance? Is it due to overfitting, insufficient signal, or another factor?
2.  How sensitive is the method to the choice of the sliding window size? Did you experiment with different window sizes beyond those reported in the hyperparameter tuning section?
3.  Could you discuss the potential impact of different ontology selection (e.g., using a different granularity of concepts or a different ontology altogether) on the results?
4.  Have you explored any methods to mitigate the impact of noisy or ambiguous phrases in the training data?
5.  What are the computational costs associated with training the model, particularly for large-scale corpora and ontologies?



**Additional Comments:**

The authors should clarify the difference between “concept” and “entity” in their writing to avoid confusion. The supplementary material is helpful. The code release is greatly appreciated.



Overall, I find this paper to be a promising contribution, particularly due to its novelty and practical potential. While the performance gains are incremental, the approach is well-motivated and the inclusion of a new dataset is valuable. I recommend **acceptance** with minor revisions addressing the weaknesses outlined above. The boldness of the approach, attempting to learn concept embeddings without extensive supervision, outweighs the limitations in performance.
