## Review for "A Weakly-Supervised Method for Jointly Embedding Concepts, Phrases, and Words"

**Summary:**

This paper presents a weakly-supervised method for learning joint embeddings of concepts, phrases, and words, leveraging existing ontological knowledge (UMLS, YAGO) and distributional similarity training on unannotated corpora. The core idea is to use known phrases as distant supervision to train embeddings for their corresponding concepts, and then jointly learn representations for words, phrases, and concepts using a modified skip-gram model.  The authors evaluate their approach on biomedical concept similarity/relatedness tasks and introduce a new dataset for real-world entities.

**Main Contributions (as I see them):**

1. **Novel Weakly-Supervised Approach:** The core contribution is a practical approach to learning concept embeddings without relying on expensive human annotations.  The use of phrase occurrences as a signal for concept embeddings is a sensible and potentially scalable strategy.
2. **New Dataset for Real-World Entity Similarity/Relatedness:**  The authors provide a valuable resource for the community by creating a dataset for evaluating embeddings of real-world entities, a task for which publicly available datasets are scarce.
3. **Empirical Validation & Analysis:**  The paper provides a comprehensive evaluation of the method on multiple datasets (biomedical and general-domain) with thoughtful analysis of the learned embeddings, showing reflection of ontological structure and relationships.

**Strengths:**

1. **Practicality and Scalability:**  The method's reliance on distant supervision is a significant strength, making it applicable to scenarios where annotated data is limited. The scalability to large corpora is also appealing.
2. **Competitive Performance:** The results demonstrate that the proposed method achieves competitive performance on concept similarity/relatedness tasks, often rivaling or outperforming methods that *do* rely on human annotations.  The performance on the new real-world entity dataset is particularly noteworthy.
3. **Thorough Evaluation & Analysis:** The authors present a comprehensive evaluation using several baselines, and the analysis of embedding space structure (clustering based on semantic type, relationships between concepts, phrases, and words) provides useful insights into the method's behavior. The hyperparameter tuning is also appreciated.
4. **Clear Presentation & Implementation Availability:** The paper is well-written and clearly explains the proposed method. Providing the software implementation ("cui2vec") further enhances the reproducibility and impact of the work.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Novelty of Core Method:** While the application to concept embeddings is valuable, the underlying approach of adapting word2vec with distant supervision isn’t groundbreaking. The key innovation lies in *how* this adaptation is applied to the specific problem of jointly embedding concepts, phrases, and words, and the quality of the evaluation.  More discussion about how this approach differs significantly from existing weakly supervised learning techniques would be beneficial.
2. **Sensitivity to Ontology Quality:** The performance is heavily dependent on the quality and coverage of the underlying ontology (UMLS, YAGO). Errors or incompleteness in the ontology will directly impact the learned embeddings. This dependency isn't sufficiently discussed, and the authors should acknowledge this limitation more explicitly.
3. **Hyperparameter Sensitivity & Generalizability:** While the hyperparameter tuning is appreciated, the best hyperparameters vary significantly between corpora. This suggests a potential sensitivity to corpus characteristics and raises questions about the generalizability of the optimal settings to other domains.  The authors could explore methods for automatically adapting hyperparameters.



**Questions to Authors:**

1. **Ontology Dependency:**  How sensitive are your results to errors or incompleteness in the underlying ontologies (UMLS, YAGO)? Have you performed any experiments to assess the impact of noisy or incomplete ontology mappings on the learned embeddings?
2. **Hyperparameter Transfer:** Have you investigated the feasibility of transferring optimal hyperparameters between different corpora?  Is there a systematic way to predict or adapt hyperparameters based on corpus characteristics?
3. **Downstream Task Performance:**  While the similarity/relatedness tasks are useful for intrinsic evaluation, do the learned embeddings improve performance on more challenging downstream NLP tasks, such as relation extraction or question answering? If so, please provide results.
4. **Negative Sampling:** How crucial is the negative sampling component for performance? Have you tried other negative sampling strategies or explored the impact of the negative sample size?




**Additional Comments:**

*   The notation throughout the paper is generally clear, but could be made more consistent (e.g., use of subscripts).
*   The supplemental material is helpful and provides further details on the experimental setup and analysis.
*   Consider discussing the potential for applying this method to other languages or domains beyond biomedicine and general knowledge.



**Overall Assessment:**

I recommend **acceptance** with minor revisions. The paper presents a valuable contribution to the field of concept embedding, offering a practical and scalable approach with competitive performance. The creation of the new dataset for real-world entity similarity is a significant bonus. Addressing the weaknesses outlined above would further strengthen the paper and enhance its impact.
