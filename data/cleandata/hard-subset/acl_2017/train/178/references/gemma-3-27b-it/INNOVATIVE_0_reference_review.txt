This paper proposes a weakly-supervised method for jointly embedding concepts, phrases, and words, leveraging distant supervision from an ontology and an unannotated corpus. The core idea – training concept embeddings using the contexts of their phrase representations – is a sensible approach to bridging the gap between lexical forms and underlying semantic concepts. The primary contribution is the proposed model and its demonstration on biomedical and general-domain datasets. A secondary contribution is the release of a novel dataset for evaluating similarity and relatedness of real-world entities.

**Strengths:**

1. **Novelty of the approach:** The paper tackles a challenging problem – representing concepts independent of specific textual forms – with a relatively novel approach. While existing work explores word and phrase embeddings, directly linking concepts to contextualized embeddings via distant supervision is a strong conceptual advance. The idea of jointly embedding concepts, phrases, and words in a shared space is compelling and allows for leveraging the strengths of each representation type.
2. **Weakly-supervised learning:** The reliance on distant supervision, rather than expensive human annotations, is a major strength. This makes the method scalable and applicable to a wider range of scenarios where labeled data is scarce. The authors successfully demonstrate competitive performance compared to methods requiring manual annotation.
3. **Empirical validation with diverse datasets:** The evaluation across both biomedical and general-domain datasets (including a newly created one) strengthens the findings and demonstrates the versatility of the approach. The creation of the real-world entity similarity/relatedness dataset is a valuable contribution to the community.
4. **Analysis of embedding space:** The analysis investigating how well the ontological structure is reflected in the learned embeddings provides insights into the model's behavior and suggests that it is capturing meaningful semantic relationships. Observing the relationship between concepts, phrases, and words in the embedding space helps validate the approach.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited ablation study:** While hyperparameters are tuned, a more thorough ablation study investigating the contributions of individual components (e.g., the β and η parameters) would be beneficial. The current results don't provide a clear understanding of how these parameters influence performance and whether their effect is consistent across datasets.
2. **Performance gap on biomedical tasks:** While competitive, the method is outperformed by Choi et al. (2016b) on the smaller biomedical datasets. The authors acknowledge this but could provide more in-depth analysis of the reasons for this difference and potential avenues for improvement.
3. **Dependence on ontology quality:** The performance of the method is inherently tied to the quality and completeness of the underlying ontology. Errors or inconsistencies in the ontology could propagate to the learned embeddings. This limitation should be explicitly addressed.
4. **Fleiss' Kappa for crowdsourced dataset is low:** While a novel dataset, the Fleiss’ kappa of 0.24 indicates only fair agreement between annotators, raising concerns about the reliability of the ground truth. The authors acknowledge this and mention filtering, but further discussion of potential biases or limitations introduced by the annotation process would be helpful.

**Questions to Authors:**

1. Could you provide a more detailed explanation of the choice of hyperparameters and the reasoning behind the specific values used for each dataset?
2. How sensitive is the performance of the model to variations in the quality and completeness of the ontology? Have you experimented with different ontologies or explored techniques to mitigate the impact of ontology errors?
3. What are the computational costs associated with training and using the model, particularly for large-scale datasets?
4. Given the low Fleiss’ Kappa on your new real-world entity dataset, how confident are you that the results obtained using this dataset are reliable? Could you provide examples of cases where there was significant disagreement among annotators and discuss potential reasons for the disagreement?
