This paper proposes a weakly-supervised method for jointly embedding concepts, phrases, and words, leveraging existing ontologies and distributional similarity training. The core idea is to use known phrases as distant supervision to train embeddings for related concepts, effectively bridging the gap between lexical forms and underlying semantic meaning. A primary contribution is the exploration of this joint embedding space and demonstration that learned embeddings capture relationships reflective of the source ontology. A secondary, though valuable, contribution is the introduction of two new datasets for evaluating semantic similarity and relatedness of real-world entities.

**Strengths:**

1. **Addresses a Relevant Problem:** The paper tackles the important issue of representing concepts beyond their surface-level lexicalization. Many concepts have multiple expressions and are not simply compositional from their constituent words. This is particularly crucial in domains like biomedicine, as highlighted in the paper.
2. **Solid Experimental Setup:** The paper includes a comprehensive evaluation across multiple corpora (PubMed, Clinical notes, Wikipedia/NYT) and leverages both existing biomedical datasets (MayoSRS, UMNSRS) and introduces new general-domain datasets. The hyperparameter tuning is also described in detail.
3. **Demonstrated Ontology Reflection:** The analysis indicating the organization of embeddings reflecting the source ontology (concepts and phrases clustered together, concepts of the same semantic type clustering) is a significant finding, providing evidence that the model is learning meaningful representations.
4. **Novel Datasets:** The creation and release of the real-world entity similarity and relatedness datasets are a valuable contribution to the community, filling a gap in available evaluation resources.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Methodological Imprecision & Lack of Justification:** The "greedy" phrase replacement during preprocessing is problematic. The authors state it’s done “over the number of tokens matched” without justification for this criterion. This could introduce significant noise, especially with overlapping phrases. The impact of this on downstream results is not discussed.
2. **Hyperparameter Sensitivity and Reporting:** While hyperparameter tuning is described, the reported values (Table 3) seem somewhat arbitrary and lack a clear explanation. Different corpora have drastically different optimal settings, but this isn’t thoroughly investigated or justified beyond stating “tuned”. The cross-validation procedure is described in the supplemental material, but the overall tuning process seems ad-hoc.
3. **Limited Gains Over Strong Baselines:** While the method shows competitive performance, it rarely *outperforms* strong baselines, particularly Choi et al. (2016b) on biomedical tasks. The gains are often marginal, raising questions about the practical benefit of the proposed approach given its complexity. The authors acknowledge this but downplay its significance.
4. **Inconsistent Evaluation Metric Interpretation:** The discussion of Spearman's ρ lacks nuance. A score of 0.6 or 0.7 is presented as “good” without sufficient context regarding the inherent difficulty of the task and the baseline performance. A more critical assessment of the results is needed.
5. **Fleiss’ Kappa for New Datasets:** The reported Fleiss’ Kappa of 0.24 for the new datasets is fairly low, indicating only fair agreement among annotators. This casts doubt on the reliability of these datasets and, consequently, the evaluation results using them. A more rigorous quality control process for annotation would have been beneficial.



**Questions to Authors:**

1.  Please justify the choice of "number of tokens matched" as the criterion for the greedy phrase replacement. What alternatives were considered, and why was this approach chosen? How does this preprocessing step affect the learned embeddings?
2.  Provide a more detailed rationale for the selected hyperparameters (Table 3). What was the motivation behind these settings, and how were they chosen specifically for each corpus?
3.  Given the marginal gains over existing methods, what specific modifications to the model or training procedure could lead to more substantial improvements in performance?
4. What steps were taken to improve inter-annotator agreement for the novel datasets? Would increasing the number of annotations per pair increase the Fleiss’ Kappa to acceptable levels?
5. Could you provide an ablation study showing the contribution of the β and η hyperparameters? Specifically, how does performance change when these parameters are set to zero, and what is the optimal range for each?



**Additional Comments:**

*   The formatting of the equations is inconsistent.
*   The reference list is overly verbose with repetitive phrasing (e.g., “Proceedings of…” repeated for each conference).
*   The supplemental material contains useful details but could be better organized for clarity.



Overall, while the paper addresses a relevant problem and presents some interesting findings, the methodological concerns, limited gains over baselines, and issues with data quality and reporting make it difficult to recommend acceptance in its current form. Significant revisions addressing the weaknesses outlined above are necessary.
