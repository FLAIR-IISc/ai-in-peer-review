This paper presents a weakly-supervised method for jointly embedding concepts, phrases, and words, leveraging existing ontological knowledge and distributional semantics. The primary contribution is a conceptually appealing and relatively simple approach to bridging the gap between lexical forms and abstract concepts, enabling semantic representation even with non-compositional phrases. A secondary contribution is the release of a new dataset for evaluating similarity and relatedness of real-world entities.

**Strengths:**

1. **Novelty of Approach:** The core idea of using distant supervision with ontological mappings to jointly embed concepts, phrases, and words is genuinely novel and addresses a significant problem in semantic representation. Existing methods often treat concepts as atomic units or rely on expensive manual annotation. This work proposes a scalable and practical alternative.
2. **Potential for Broad Impact:** The ability to represent concepts independent of their lexical realization has broad implications for various NLP tasks, including information retrieval, knowledge base completion, and biomedical text mining. The approach isn't limited to a specific domain.
3. **New Dataset:** The creation of a dataset for evaluating real-world entity similarity and relatedness fills a gap in existing resources. While the inter-annotator agreement (Fleiss’ κ = 0.24) is fair rather than strong, the dataset itself is a valuable contribution to the community.
4. **Empirical Validation:** The results demonstrate competitive performance against strong baselines, including methods requiring human annotation. The experiments on both biomedical and general-domain data provide a reasonable level of validation. The analysis of embedding organization – concepts and phrases clustering – provides some insight into the learned representations.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Analysis of Negative Results:** While the paper shows positive results on several datasets, the analysis of *why* the model succeeds (or fails) is somewhat limited. The authors acknowledge some areas for improvement (e.g., needing to consider ontology structure more explicitly), but a more in-depth investigation would be beneficial. For example, a more detailed error analysis of the similarity/relatedness task would reveal patterns in the model’s mispredictions.
2. **Hyperparameter Sensitivity & Reporting:** The hyperparameter tuning section is dense and somewhat obscures the practical implications of the chosen settings. While the authors state tuned hyperparameters, the impact of each parameter on performance isn’t fully elucidated. Better visualization of the hyperparameter search space and the rationale behind the final choices would be helpful.
3. **Weak Inter-Annotator Agreement:** The new dataset's relatively low Fleiss' kappa score raises concerns about the reliability of the ground truth. The authors do acknowledge this, but the potential impact on the evaluation results should be carefully considered.
4. **Methodological Simplicity vs. Sophistication:** While simplicity is a strength, the method lacks some of the sophisticated techniques found in more recent embedding models (e.g., attention mechanisms, transformers). While not necessarily a flaw, it suggests that there’s room for further improvement.

**Questions to Authors:**

1. Can you provide a more detailed error analysis of the similarity/relatedness tasks, highlighting specific examples where the model fails and discussing potential reasons for these failures?
2. Could you elaborate on the rationale behind the chosen hyperparameters, particularly regarding the trade-off between β and η? Are there any theoretical or empirical guidelines that informed these choices?
3. Given the fair inter-annotator agreement on the new dataset, how confident are you in the reported results? Have you considered any alternative methods for evaluating the model on real-world entities, such as using existing knowledge bases or performing downstream tasks?
4. What is the computational cost associated with training and deploying the proposed model, and how does it compare to other state-of-the-art embedding methods?

**Additional Comments:**

The supplementary material is generally well-organized and provides helpful details about the experimental setup and hyperparameter tuning. It would be helpful to include more visualizations of the learned embeddings (e.g., t-SNE plots) to provide a more intuitive understanding of the model's behavior.



Despite some weaknesses, I believe this paper presents a significant contribution to the field. The novelty of the approach, the potential for broad impact, and the release of a new dataset outweigh the methodological limitations. I recommend **acceptance** with minor revisions, focusing on a more in-depth analysis of the results and addressing the questions raised above. The willingness to prioritize novel ideas over methodological perfection aligns well with my reviewing philosophy.
