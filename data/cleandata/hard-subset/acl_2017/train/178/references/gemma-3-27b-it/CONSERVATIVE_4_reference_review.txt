This paper proposes a weakly-supervised method for jointly embedding concepts, phrases, and words, leveraging existing ontologies and distributional similarity training. The primary contribution is a method to create concept embeddings without human annotation, utilizing distant supervision from phrase occurrences in a corpus. A secondary, though valuable, contribution is the release of a new dataset for evaluating similarity and relatedness of real-world entities.

**Strengths:**

1. **Addresses a relevant problem:** The task of representing concepts, especially those with multiple textual forms or non-compositional semantics, is a longstanding challenge in NLP. The authors correctly identify the limitations of purely word-based embedding approaches.
2. **Sound methodological foundation:** The approach builds upon established techniques like word2vec and adapts them in a reasonable way to incorporate concept information. The use of distant supervision is a sensible strategy given the difficulty of obtaining large-scale annotations for concepts.
3. **Comprehensive evaluation:** The authors evaluate their approach on multiple datasets, including both biomedical and general-domain tasks. The inclusion of a new dataset is particularly noteworthy and provides a valuable resource for the community.
4. **Detailed analysis:** The paper goes beyond simply presenting results and includes analysis of the embedding space, examining the relationships between concepts, phrases, and words, and investigating the preservation of ontological structure.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited novelty:** While the approach is well-motivated and technically sound, the core idea of combining distributional semantics with structured knowledge is not entirely new (see related work). The adaptation of word2vec, while effective, lacks a significant innovative leap. The primary innovation—distant supervision—is a well-established technique and its application here, while functional, doesn't represent a substantial methodological advance.
2. **Performance is not consistently superior:**  The results show that the proposed method is competitive with existing approaches, but it does not consistently outperform them across all datasets and tasks. The Choi et al. (2016b) method, relying on manually annotated codes, achieves superior performance on some biomedical datasets. This suggests that the quality of the distant supervision signal may be a limiting factor.
3. **Hyperparameter sensitivity and tuning complexity:** The extensive hyperparameter tuning reported suggests the model is sensitive to these settings. This sensitivity adds to the complexity of applying the method and raises concerns about generalizability. While the authors report optimal settings, the process itself is not sufficiently explained to facilitate replication.
4. **Fleiss’ Kappa for the new dataset is relatively low:** The reported Fleiss’ kappa of 0.24 for the new similarity/relatedness dataset indicates only fair agreement among annotators. This raises concerns about the reliability of the dataset and the validity of the evaluation results on that dataset. Greater scrutiny of the annotation process and potential sources of disagreement would be beneficial.

**Questions to Authors:**

1.  Could you provide a more detailed justification for the choice of hyperparameters, beyond simply reporting the tuned values? Specifically, what rationale guided the search process and what insights were gained about the interplay between hyperparameters?
2.  The paper mentions limitations of the distant supervision approach. What steps could be taken to improve the quality of the supervision signal, potentially by incorporating constraints or filtering based on ontological information?
3.  Regarding the relatively low inter-annotator agreement for the new dataset, could you elaborate on the potential sources of disagreement among annotators? Were specific entity pairs particularly challenging to judge?
4.  The introduction states that the model can handle phrases that are not compositionally derived from their words (e.g., "the Big Apple"). Can you provide examples of cases where the model successfully captures the meaning of such phrases, and how this contrasts with purely compositional methods?



**Additional Comments:**

The paper is generally well-written and clearly presented. The inclusion of code and the new dataset is a significant positive aspect.  The supplemental material is helpful. However, the limited novelty of the core method and the lack of consistently superior performance warrant a cautious assessment.
