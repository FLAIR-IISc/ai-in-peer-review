## Review for ACL Submission: "A Weakly-Supervised Method for Jointly Embedding Concepts, Phrases, and Words"

**Summary:**

This paper presents a weakly-supervised method for jointly embedding concepts, phrases, and words. The approach leverages existing ontologies (UMLS, YAGO) to map concepts to their textual representations and uses this mapping for training word embeddings via a modified skip-gram model. The key idea is to use the context of phrases as a proxy for the context of their corresponding concepts, enabling learning without manual annotation. The authors evaluate their method on biomedical concept similarity/relatedness tasks and introduce a new dataset for evaluating real-world entities.

**Main Contributions (as I see them):**

1. **Novel Dataset:** The creation and release of a new dataset for evaluating the similarity and relatedness of real-world entities, judged by human annotators, is a valuable contribution to the field. Existing datasets are limited, and this provides a useful benchmark.
2. **Practical Weakly-Supervised Approach:**  The method offers a practical way to generate concept embeddings without relying on costly human annotation, a significant advantage in domains like biomedicine where annotation is expensive and requires specialized expertise.
3. **Demonstration of Joint Embedding:** The paper demonstrates the feasibility of jointly learning embeddings for concepts, phrases, and words, showcasing the potential for richer semantic representations.

**Strengths:**

1. **Clear Motivation and Problem Definition:** The paper clearly articulates the limitations of relying solely on word embeddings for representing concepts, particularly when dealing with multi-word expressions and synonymous phrases. The motivation for a joint embedding approach is well-established.
2. **Thorough Experimental Evaluation:** The authors conduct a comprehensive experimental evaluation, comparing their method against several baselines, including strong pretrained embeddings and approximations of their approach.  They test across multiple corpora and datasets.
3. **Analysis of Embedding Space:** The analysis examining the organization of the learned embeddings (clustering of semantic types, relationship between concepts and phrases) provides valuable insights into the effectiveness of the proposed method and highlights some of its strengths. The MAP analysis is particularly insightful.
4. **Well-Written and Reproducible:** The paper is clearly written and well-structured.  The authors provide details about their implementation (cui2vec) and data, potentially facilitating reproducibility. The supplemental material adds further clarity.
5. **Competitive Performance:** While not always state-of-the-art, the results are competitive with existing methods, particularly considering the lack of human annotation used during training.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Novelty of the Core Method:** The underlying methodology – adapting word2vec with distant supervision – is not entirely novel.  Similar techniques have been explored in related work (particularly Wang et al., 2014a, which the authors acknowledge). The primary innovation lies in the application to this specific task and the combination with existing ontologies.
2. **Sensitivity to Ontology Quality:** The performance of the method is inherently tied to the quality and completeness of the underlying ontologies (UMLS, YAGO).  Errors or missing mappings in these ontologies can directly impact the learned embeddings. This dependence is not sufficiently discussed.
3. **Performance Gaps on Some Tasks:**  On the biomedical datasets, the proposed method is often outperformed by Choi et al. (2016b), which relies on human-annotated data. This suggests that the weakly-supervised approach struggles to achieve the same level of accuracy as methods with richer supervision. The improvement over baselines isn't consistently substantial.
4. **Fleiss’ Kappa for the New Dataset:** The Fleiss’ Kappa value of 0.24 for the newly created dataset is relatively low, indicating fair agreement between annotators. This raises concerns about the reliability of the dataset and the potential impact on the reported results.  More details about the annotation process and potential sources of disagreement would be helpful.




**Questions to Authors:**

1. **Ontology Coverage:** How sensitive is the performance of your method to the coverage of the ontologies? Did you investigate the impact of different coverage levels or perform ablation studies excluding concepts with limited phrase mappings?
2. **Error Analysis:** Can you provide a more detailed error analysis of the cases where your method performs poorly compared to supervised baselines (e.g., examples of concepts where the learned embeddings are inaccurate or misleading)?
3. **Kappa Disagreement:**  What steps were taken to ensure the quality of the new similarity/relatedness dataset despite the relatively low Fleiss’ Kappa? Were specific annotation guidelines provided to reduce ambiguity?  Can you share some examples of cases where annotators significantly disagreed?
4. **Hyperparameter Tuning Strategy:**  While you mention hyperparameter tuning, can you elaborate on the search space explored and the criteria used for selecting the optimal hyperparameters for each corpus? Did you use any automated hyperparameter optimization techniques?
5. **Computational Cost:** What is the computational cost (training time, memory usage) of your method compared to the baseline approaches?



**Additional Comments:**

*   The paper would benefit from a more nuanced discussion of the limitations of the distant supervision approach and the potential biases introduced by the ontologies.
*   It would be interesting to see how the performance of the method scales with the size of the training corpus and the number of concepts in the ontology.




**Overall Assessment:**

This is a solid paper that presents a practical and well-evaluated approach to learning concept embeddings. While the core methodology lacks significant novelty, the new dataset and the demonstration of a functional weakly-supervised method are valuable contributions. The weaknesses identified are not severe enough to warrant rejection, but addressing the questions raised above would strengthen the paper considerably. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions.
