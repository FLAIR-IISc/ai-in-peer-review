## Review for ACL Submission: "A Weakly-Supervised Method for Jointly Embedding Concepts, Phrases, and Words"

**Overall Assessment:** This paper presents a reasonable approach to embedding concepts, phrases, and words jointly using weak supervision. The method is well-motivated, and the experimental results demonstrate competitive performance on several biomedical and general-domain tasks. However, the novelty is incremental, and the paper lacks a thorough analysis of *why* the method works and doesn’t work, limiting its impact. I recommend **Accept with Minor Revisions**.

**Summary & Contributions (as I see them):**

1. **Weakly-Supervised Joint Embedding:** The primary contribution is a practical method for creating joint embeddings of concepts, phrases, and words using distant supervision from ontologies, without requiring manual annotations. This is valuable, as high-quality annotations are expensive and scarce.
2. **Novel Dataset:** The creation of a new dataset for evaluating similarity and relatedness of real-world entities is a positive addition. This addresses a gap in existing resources.
3. **Empirical Validation:** The extensive experiments on both biomedical and general-domain data, along with a thorough comparison against baselines, provides a solid empirical foundation for the proposed method.

**Strengths:**

1. **Practicality and Scalability:** The method is appealing due to its ability to leverage readily available ontologies and unannotated text, making it scalable to large datasets. This avoids the bottleneck of manual annotation.
2. **Competitive Performance:** The reported results show that the proposed method achieves competitive performance compared to existing methods, especially on biomedical tasks, and sometimes surpasses them. This demonstrates the effectiveness of the approach.
3. **Comprehensive Evaluation:** The authors conduct a thorough evaluation using established datasets and a novel dataset, covering both similarity and relatedness tasks. This rigorous evaluation strengthens the credibility of the findings.
4. **Insightful Analysis of Embedding Space:** The analysis of how concepts, phrases, and words are organized within the embedding space (through MAP scores and cosine similarity) provides valuable insights into the model's behavior. This helps to understand how well the ontological relationships are being captured.
5. **Code and Data Release:**  Releasing the code and the novel dataset is a significant contribution to reproducibility and further research.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Incremental Novelty:** The core idea of leveraging distant supervision for embedding knowledge base entities is not entirely new (see Wang et al. 2014a mentioned in the paper). While this work extends this idea to a joint embedding of concepts, phrases, and words, the novelty is incremental rather than groundbreaking.  A clearer positioning relative to prior work is needed.
2. **Limited Analysis of Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** While hyperparameter tuning is performed, the paper doesn’t offer much insight into *why* the chosen hyperparameters work best.  A more detailed analysis of how different parameters impact the embedding quality would be beneficial.  The observation that optimal settings vary by corpus suggests a deeper interaction that warrants investigation.
3. **Incomplete Error Analysis:** The results show performance differences across semantic types in the semantic type clustering experiment. However, the paper does not provide a detailed error analysis to understand *why* the method struggles with certain types (e.g., “sign or symptom”). Understanding the limitations is crucial for future improvements.
4. **Uneven Performance Between Domains:** Performance on the novel real-world entity dataset is somewhat disappointing compared to the biomedical tasks. This suggests the method may be more suitable for the structured knowledge found in biomedical ontologies than for the more ambiguous and nuanced relationships in the general domain. This discrepancy needs to be addressed.

**Questions to Authors:**

1.  **Novelty:** Could you explicitly highlight the key differences between your method and Wang et al. (2014a), focusing on the joint embedding aspect and the use of weak supervision from ontologies?
2. **Hyperparameter Analysis:**  Can you elaborate on the observation that the optimal hyperparameters vary significantly between the different corpora?  Are there any corpus-specific characteristics that explain these differences?
3. **Error Analysis:** What are your hypotheses for why the semantic type clustering performance is lower for certain types (e.g., “sign or symptom”)? Could you provide some examples of misclassifications to illustrate the challenges?
4. **General Domain Performance:** Why do you think your method performs comparatively worse on the general domain dataset? Are there any modifications you could make to improve performance in this setting?




**Additional Comments:**

*   The phrase "distant supervision" is used frequently, but a more precise definition in the context of this paper might be helpful.
*   A brief discussion of computational complexity would be valuable, particularly regarding the scalability of the method to very large ontologies and corpora.
*   The supplemental material is helpful but could be further organized for better readability.
