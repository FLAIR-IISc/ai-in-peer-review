This paper proposes a weakly-supervised method for jointly embedding concepts, phrases, and words, leveraging structured knowledge from ontologies and an unannotated corpus. The core idea is to use known phrases as distant supervision in distributional similarity training, effectively propagating concept information through their textual representations. The authors validate their approach on biomedical concepts and real-world entities, demonstrating competitive performance on similarity and relatedness tasks. A novel dataset of entity similarity and relatedness is also introduced.

**Contributions:**

1. **Joint Embedding Framework:** The primary contribution is a novel framework for jointly embedding concepts, phrases, and words, addressing the limitations of treating words as atomic semantic units and handling multi-word expressions with non-compositional semantics. This allows for representing underlying concepts regardless of specific textual forms.
2. **Weakly-Supervised Approach:**  The method effectively utilizes distant supervision from existing ontologies, eliminating the need for expensive human annotations. This is a significant advantage, particularly in specialized domains like biomedicine where annotation is costly and requires expertise.
3. **Novel Dataset:** The introduction of a new dataset for evaluating similarity and relatedness of real-world entities is valuable, as existing datasets are limited in this area. This allows for a more comprehensive evaluation of the proposed method and provides a resource for future research.

**Strengths:**

1. **Addressing a Key Problem:** The paper tackles a fundamental challenge in semantic representation – representing concepts that are not easily captured by word embeddings alone. The ability to handle non-compositional phrases is crucial for many NLP tasks.
2. **Strong Empirical Validation:** The authors provide thorough experimental results on multiple datasets (biomedical and general domain), demonstrating competitive performance compared to existing methods. The inclusion of both similarity and relatedness tasks strengthens the evaluation.
3. **Clear and Well-Structured Presentation:** The paper is well-written and clearly explains the methodology and experimental setup. The figures and tables are informative and contribute to the understanding of the approach.
4. **Practical Implementation and Data Release:** The authors release both the code and the new dataset, promoting reproducibility and enabling further research in this area. This is a commendable practice.
5. **Hyperparameter Tuning & Analysis:** The inclusion of a hyperparameter tuning section and results adds value, showing a considered approach to optimizing performance.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Gains over Strong Baselines:** While the results are competitive, the improvements over strong baselines (e.g., pre-trained word embeddings like CHIU-2, CHIU-30) are often modest, particularly on the general-domain dataset. This raises questions about the practical impact of the proposed method.
2. **Sensitivity to Ontology Quality:** The performance heavily relies on the quality and coverage of the underlying ontology. The authors acknowledge this but don't thoroughly investigate how performance varies with different ontologies or ontology completeness.
3. **MAP Interpretation:** The analysis of semantic type clustering using MAP, while interesting, is somewhat difficult to interpret. The reported MAP scores are relatively low, and the authors don’t provide a clear explanation of what constitutes a “good” MAP score in this context. A more intuitive analysis of the clustering results would be beneficial.
4. **Fleiss’ Kappa for the Dataset:** The Fleiss’ Kappa of 0.24 for the new dataset is considered 'fair agreement' and lower than desired. Although acknowledged, a more detailed discussion on the impact of this level of agreement on the dataset’s utility would be useful.
5. **Lack of Ablation Study:** A more detailed ablation study, specifically examining the contribution of the β and η parameters, would help to understand their individual impact on performance and guide future model development.

**Questions to Authors:**

1. How sensitive is the performance of your method to the quality and completeness of the underlying ontology? Could you provide results with different ontologies or perform an analysis on the impact of ontology coverage?
2. The MAP scores for semantic type clustering seem relatively low. Could you provide a more intuitive visualization or analysis of the clustering results to help us understand the extent to which concepts of the same type are clustered together?
3.  Can you elaborate on the choice of hyperparameters (β and η)? What is the practical guidance for selecting optimal values based on the characteristics of the specific domain or ontology being used?
4. You mention that the hyperparameters for the biomedical corpora and the general domain corpus are quite different. Can you speculate why this is the case? Does this point to the method being less generalizable?



**Additional Comments:**

* The paper would benefit from a discussion of the computational complexity of the proposed method compared to other approaches.
* It would be helpful to include a qualitative example demonstrating how the joint embedding captures the relationship between a concept and its different textual representations.
* Consider adding a section discussing the limitations of the method and potential directions for future research.

**Overall Assessment:**

This paper presents a valuable contribution to the field of semantic representation. The proposed method addresses a challenging problem, and the experimental results demonstrate its effectiveness. While the improvements over strong baselines are modest, the novelty of the approach, the release of a new dataset, and the clear presentation make this a worthy submission. Despite some weaknesses, I recommend **acceptance** with minor revisions, focusing on addressing the questions and suggestions outlined above.
