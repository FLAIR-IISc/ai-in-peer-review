- Presents a weakly-supervised method using ontologies (UMLS, YAGO) and a modified skip-gram to jointly embed concepts, phrases, and words.  
- Introduces and releases a novel human-annotated dataset for real-world entity similarity/relatedness.  
- Offers a practical approach to generate concept embeddings without costly human annotation, useful in domains like biomedicine.  
- Demonstrates feasibility of jointly learning embeddings for concepts, phrases, and words, producing richer semantic representations.  
- Clearly motivates the problem and limitations of word-only embeddings, especially for multi-word expressions and synonyms.  
- Conducts thorough experimental evaluation against strong baselines across multiple corpora and datasets.  
- Analyzes embedding space organization (semantic type clustering, concept-phrase relationships) with insightful MAP analysis.  
- Paper is well-written, reproducible, and supplemented with implementation/data details (cui2vec).  
- Results are competitive, though not always state-of-the-art, given the lack of human annotation in training.  
- Core method (distantly supervised word2vec adaptation) has limited novelty relative to prior work (e.g., Wang et al., 2014a).  
- Method performance depends on ontology quality and coverage, a dependency insufficiently discussed.  
- Weakly-supervised approach underperforms supervised baselines (e.g., Choi et al., 2016b) on several biomedical tasks.  
- New dataset has low inter-annotator agreement (Fleiss’ Kappa = 0.24), raising concerns about reliability and annotation details.  
- Requests analysis of ontology coverage sensitivity and ablation studies excluding sparsely-mapped concepts.  
- Asks for detailed error analysis comparing failures to supervised baselines with example cases.  
- Seeks clarification on measures taken to mitigate low Kappa, annotation guidelines, and examples of major disagreements.  
- Requests elaboration on hyperparameter tuning strategy, search space, selection criteria, and any automated optimization.  
- Asks for computational cost comparison (training time, memory) versus baselines.  
- Suggests a more nuanced discussion of limitations and ontology-induced biases.  
- Recommends exploring scalability with training corpus size and ontology concept count.  
- Overall assessment: solid, well-evaluated paper with useful dataset; minor revisions recommended before acceptance.
