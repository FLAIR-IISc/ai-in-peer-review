This paper introduces a weakly-supervised method for jointly embedding concepts, phrases, and words, leveraging distant supervision from ontologies to learn representations without manual annotations. The core idea – using phrase occurrences as a signal for concept embeddings – is a sensible and potentially powerful approach to bridging the gap between lexical forms and underlying semantic entities.

The primary contribution is the proposed model itself, and the demonstration that jointly embedding concepts, phrases, and words is feasible and competitive with supervised approaches. A secondary contribution is the novel dataset of real-world entity similarity and relatedness, which fills a gap in available resources. Finally, the analysis suggesting that the ontology structure is reflected in the embedding space is interesting, offering some insight into the model's behavior.

**Strengths:**

1. **Novelty of Approach:** The idea of using readily available ontological mappings for weak supervision in this manner is genuinely novel. Most related work relies heavily on annotated data or complex knowledge base reasoning. This approach sidesteps both, making it potentially scalable and adaptable to various domains.
2. **Competitive Performance:** The results, while not state-of-the-art across all tasks, are competitive with supervised methods, particularly considering the lack of manual annotation. Achieving parity with methods requiring significant annotation effort is a strong indication of the approach's merit.
3. **Practicality and Scalability:** The method's reliance on readily available resources (ontologies and unannotated corpora) makes it practically applicable to a wide range of scenarios. The computational cost appears reasonable, suggesting good scalability.
4. **Novel Dataset:** The new dataset for real-world entity similarity and relatedness is a valuable addition to the NLP community. Filling gaps in evaluation resources is always a positive contribution.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Analysis of Negative Results:** While the paper reports performance on several datasets, the analysis of *why* the method underperforms in certain cases (e.g., the semantic type clustering) is limited. A deeper investigation into the limitations would strengthen the paper.
2. **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** The tuning of hyperparameters, while described, feels somewhat ad-hoc. The explanation for the differences in optimal settings between corpora is superficial. More systematic analysis of the parameter space, and the impact of each parameter on performance, would be beneficial.
3. **Evaluation Focus:** The evaluation primarily relies on established datasets with existing benchmarks. While this allows for comparison, it doesn’t fully demonstrate the unique capabilities of the jointly embedded space. A task specifically designed to leverage the combined concept-phrase-word embeddings would be more compelling.
4. **Dataset Quality:** The Fleiss’ kappa score for the new real-world entity dataset is relatively low (0.24). While acceptable, it raises concerns about the reliability of the human judgments. More details on the data collection and quality control processes would be reassuring.

**Questions to Authors:**

1. Can you provide a more detailed analysis of the cases where the model performs poorly? Specifically, what characteristics of concepts or phrases seem to hinder performance?
2. You mention tuning hyperparameters, but the explanation for the chosen values is limited. Could you provide a more systematic exploration of the hyperparameter space, and justify the final settings?
3. Could you design and evaluate a task that explicitly leverages the jointly embedded concept-phrase-word representations, rather than relying solely on existing datasets?
4.  What steps were taken to ensure the quality of the annotations in the new real-world entity dataset, beyond the validation questions? Were there any checks for inter-annotator consistency beyond the reported Fleiss' kappa?
