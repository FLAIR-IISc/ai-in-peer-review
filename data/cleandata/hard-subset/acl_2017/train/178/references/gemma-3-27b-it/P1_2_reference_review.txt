## Peer Review for "A Weakly-Supervised Method for Jointly Embedding Concepts, Phrases, and Words"

**Summary:**

This paper presents a weakly-supervised method for learning joint embeddings of concepts, phrases, and words. The approach leverages existing ontology knowledge (UMLS and YAGO) and an unannotated corpus, using phrases as distant supervision to train embeddings. The core idea is to adapt the word2vec skip-gram model to simultaneously learn representations for words, phrases representing concepts, and the concepts themselves. The authors validate their method on biomedical and general-domain concept similarity and relatedness tasks, introducing a new dataset for real-world entity similarity/relatedness.

**Main Contributions (as I see them):**

1. **Novel Dataset:** The introduction of a new, human-annotated dataset for measuring similarity and relatedness of real-world entities (people, places, organizations) is a valuable contribution to the community, as such datasets are scarce.
2. **Weakly-Supervised Joint Embedding:** The proposed method offers a practical approach to learning concept embeddings without relying on expensive human annotations. This is particularly useful in domains like biomedicine where annotation is costly and requires specialized expertise.
3. **Empirical Validation & Analysis:** The comprehensive experimental evaluation on multiple datasets (including the new one) and the analysis of embedding structure (reflecting ontology and compositionality) provide insights into the effectiveness of the approach and its behavior.

**Strengths:**

1. **Practicality & Scalability:** The weakly-supervised nature of the method makes it highly practical and scalable to large datasets and ontologies, a significant advantage over methods relying on extensive annotation.  This is clearly demonstrated by the authors' application to both PubMed and Wikipedia corpora.
2. **Competitive Performance:** The results demonstrate competitive performance compared to existing methods, particularly those requiring human annotation. While not always state-of-the-art, the method achieves solid results, especially considering the lack of manual supervision.
3. **Thorough Evaluation & Ablation:** The authors perform a comprehensive evaluation, comparing against multiple baselines (including approximation methods and prior work) and conducting detailed hyperparameter tuning. The ablation studies (e.g., through β and η) provide valuable insights into the effects of different components of the model.
4. **Insightful Analysis:** The analysis of embedding space organization, demonstrating that concepts and phrases are embedded close to each other, and that semantic types cluster reasonably well, adds to the understanding of the learned representations and validates the method’s ability to capture semantic relationships.
5. **Clear Presentation & Reproducibility:** The paper is well-written and clearly explains the method and experiments. The authors have released the code and dataset, significantly enhancing the reproducibility of the work.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Novelty of Core Method:** While the application to jointly embedding concepts, phrases, and words *together* is a reasonable combination, the core methodology – adapting word2vec with distant supervision – isn’t fundamentally novel. The core innovation lies in the application and the dataset creation.
2. **Dependency on Ontology Quality:** The performance of the method is inherently tied to the quality of the underlying ontology (UMLS and YAGO). Errors or ambiguities in the ontology can propagate to the embeddings, potentially limiting the accuracy and robustness of the results. The paper acknowledges this, but a more detailed discussion of potential limitations stemming from ontology imperfections would be beneficial.
3. **Fleiss' Kappa for New Dataset:** The reported Fleiss’ Kappa of 0.24 for the inter-annotator agreement on the new similarity/relatedness dataset is relatively low, indicating fair agreement. This raises concerns about the reliability and validity of the dataset and its use as a primary evaluation metric.  While acknowledged in the paper, the implications for the experimental results should be discussed more thoroughly.
4. **Limited Downstream Task Evaluation:**  The evaluation focuses solely on similarity and relatedness tasks. Demonstrating the usefulness of these embeddings in downstream NLP tasks (e.g., entity linking, relation extraction, knowledge graph completion) would significantly strengthen the paper and highlight the practical impact of the work.



**Questions to Authors:**

1.  How sensitive is the performance of your method to the quality and completeness of the ontology? Did you explore strategies to mitigate the impact of errors or ambiguities in the ontology?
2.  Given the relatively low inter-annotator agreement on the new similarity/relatedness dataset, how confident are you in the reliability of the results obtained using this dataset?  Did you consider using more stringent quality control measures or alternative datasets?
3.  Could you elaborate on the computational cost of training the embeddings?  How does the training time scale with the size of the corpus and the complexity of the ontology?
4.  What is the practical impact of the β and η hyperparameters?  Can you provide guidelines on how to choose appropriate values for these parameters based on the specific application and corpus?
5.  Do you foresee issues when applying this method to lower-resource languages where high-quality ontologies and large corpora may not be readily available?

**Additional Comments:**

*   The Supplemental Material is helpful for understanding the hyperparameter tuning and data collection process.
*   The paper could benefit from a discussion of related work on knowledge graph embedding techniques, particularly those that utilize both relational structure and textual information.
*   Consider adding a brief error analysis, identifying common types of errors made by the model and discussing potential avenues for improvement.



Overall, this is a solid and valuable contribution to the field. The weaknesses are not critical and can be addressed with further work. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions addressing the questions and concerns raised above.
