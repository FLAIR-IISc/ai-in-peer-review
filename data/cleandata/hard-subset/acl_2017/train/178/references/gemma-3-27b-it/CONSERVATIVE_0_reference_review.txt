This paper proposes a weakly-supervised method for jointly embedding concepts, phrases, and words, leveraging distant supervision from existing ontologies. The core idea is to train embeddings by using the contexts of phrases as proxies for the underlying concepts they represent. The authors evaluate this approach on biomedical and general-domain datasets, introducing a new dataset for evaluating real-world entity similarity.

The primary contribution is the proposed method for jointly embedding concepts, phrases, and words using distant supervision. While the idea of leveraging existing knowledge for embedding creation is not novel, the specific formulation and implementation, particularly the weighting parameters (β and η) aiming to balance compositionality, represent a reasonable extension of existing techniques. A secondary contribution is the creation of a new dataset for evaluating real-world entity similarity and relatedness, which could be useful for future research.

**Strengths:**

1. **Sound Methodology:** The method builds upon well-established techniques (word2vec) and integrates them with a sensible approach to distant supervision. The use of known phrases to train concept embeddings is a logical starting point, and the incorporation of hyperparameters to control compositionality is a thoughtful addition.
2. **Comprehensive Evaluation:** The authors evaluate their approach on multiple datasets (biomedical and general-domain) and compare against a range of baselines, including existing embedding methods and approximations of their own method. This provides a reasonable assessment of the approach's performance.
3. **Novel Dataset:** The introduction of a new dataset for real-world entity similarity and relatedness is a valuable contribution. Datasets of this type are often lacking, and the effort to collect human judgments is commendable. 
4. **Clear Presentation:** The paper is well-written and clearly explains the proposed method, experimental setup, and results. The inclusion of diagrams illustrating the model architecture and training process is helpful.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Improvement Over Strong Baselines:** While the proposed method performs competitively, it does not consistently outperform strong baselines, particularly in the biomedical domain where existing, carefully tuned embeddings (e.g., Choi et al., 2016b) achieve superior results. The gains achieved do not seem substantial enough to warrant a significant shift in methodology.
2. **Weak Justification for Hyperparameter Settings:** The hyperparameter tuning process is described, but the rationale behind the selected values remains unclear. The authors mention that certain settings performed better, but do not provide a clear explanation of *why* these settings were chosen. The limited insight into the hyperparameter space is concerning.
3. **Reliance on Distant Supervision Noise:** The method is inherently susceptible to noise introduced by the distant supervision approach. The assumption that all occurrences of a phrase signify the underlying concept is a simplification that can lead to inaccurate embeddings. While the authors attempt to mitigate this with the weighting parameters, the impact of noise is not thoroughly analyzed.
4. **Lack of Ablation Study:** A more detailed ablation study is needed to understand the individual contributions of different components of the model. For example, it would be valuable to assess the impact of the β and η parameters independently and in combination, and to compare the performance of the model with and without the weighting mechanism.




**Questions to Authors:**

1. The performance gains are modest compared to existing, well-tuned baselines. Can you provide a more in-depth analysis of the specific cases where your method outperforms the baselines, and what characteristics of those cases might explain the improved performance?
2. What is the rationale behind the selected values for the β and η hyperparameters? Could you provide a more detailed analysis of the hyperparameter space and the impact of different settings on the results?
3. How sensitive is the performance of your method to the quality of the ontology and the mapping between concepts and phrases? Would using a more comprehensive or accurate ontology significantly improve the results?
4. You mention that the Fleiss’ kappa for the real-world entity datasets is “fair”. Could you elaborate on the potential impact of this relatively low agreement on the reliability of the dataset and the validity of your findings?



**Additional Comments:**

*   The paper references supplementary material but does not provide a clear link to it.
*   A discussion of the computational cost of training and using the embeddings would be helpful.
*   Consider adding a section discussing the limitations of the proposed method and potential avenues for future research.
