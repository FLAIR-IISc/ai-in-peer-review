This paper proposes a weakly-supervised method for jointly embedding concepts, phrases, and words, leveraging structured knowledge from an ontology and distributional similarity training. The core idea is to use known phrases as distant supervision, training embeddings for concepts using the contexts of their phrase forms. The authors evaluate on biomedical concepts and real-world entities, presenting a novel dataset for the latter.

**Contributions:**

1. **Weakly-Supervised Joint Embedding:** The primary contribution is a method for learning joint embeddings of concepts, phrases, and words without requiring human annotations. This is valuable as manually annotating concepts is expensive and time-consuming. The approach of using phrase occurrences as a signal for concept presence is a sensible and potentially scalable strategy.
2. **Novel Dataset for Real-World Entities:** The introduction of a dataset for evaluating similarity and relatedness of real-world entities (identified by both Wikipedia page and text phrase) is a significant contribution. Existing datasets are often limited in scope or focus on words rather than entities. This provides a valuable resource for the community.
3. **Analysis of Embedding Structure:** The analysis revealing that source ontology structure is reflected in the organization of the embeddings (concepts and phrases clustered together, concepts of the same type clustered) provides insight into the quality and interpretability of the learned representations.

**Strengths:**

1. **Addresses a Relevant Problem:** The paper tackles a well-motivated problem – representing concepts that aren't easily compositional and can be expressed through multiple phrasal forms. Existing word embedding techniques often struggle with these scenarios.
2. **Solid Experimental Setup:** The experiments are well-designed, utilizing established biomedical datasets (MayoSRS, UMNSRS) and a newly created dataset for general-domain entities. Comparisons are made against a variety of baselines, including both approximations using existing embeddings and state-of-the-art concept embedding methods.
3. **Clear and Well-Structured Presentation:** The paper is generally well-written and easy to follow. The method is clearly explained, and the experimental results are presented in a concise and organized manner.
4. **Practical Implementation:** The authors provide a software implementation (cui2vec) and the newly created dataset, promoting reproducibility and further research.
5. **Thorough Ablation Studies:** The hyperparameter tuning and subsequent analysis provide valuable insights into the model's behavior and the importance of different parameters.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Novelty in Core Method:** While the combination of techniques is reasonable, the core method of using distributional similarity with distant supervision is not entirely novel. Similar approaches have been explored in related areas (e.g., relation extraction, entity embedding). The key innovation lies in the specific application to jointly embedding concepts, phrases, and words.
2. **Performance Not Consistently Superior:** While competitive, the proposed method does not consistently outperform existing approaches across all datasets and tasks. In some cases (particularly on the smaller biomedical datasets), it is outperformed by methods like CHOI. This suggests that the approach may be more effective in certain scenarios than others.
3. **Sensitivity to Hyperparameter Tuning:** The performance of the model appears sensitive to hyperparameter settings, as evidenced by the extensive tuning process. This could make it challenging to apply the method to new domains or datasets without significant re-tuning.
4. **Fleiss’ Kappa for New Dataset is Moderate:** The Fleiss’ kappa value of 0.24 for the crowdsourced dataset indicates fair agreement, which is a concern. While acceptable, the relatively low agreement raises questions about the reliability of the dataset and the potential impact on the evaluation results.

**Questions to Authors:**

1.  Could you elaborate on the rationale behind the specific hyperparameters chosen for each corpus, beyond just stating that they were selected through tuning? What insights did you gain from the tuning process that informed these choices?
2.  Given the moderate Fleiss’ kappa for your new dataset, how robust are your conclusions regarding the performance of your method on general-domain entities? Have you considered alternative methods for assessing the quality of the dataset, or potentially collecting additional annotations?
3.  The results indicate that the concept approximation methods (APPROXPHRco, APPROXWORDco) perform well. How do these results inform your interpretation of the learned embeddings? Are the embeddings primarily capturing information about the phrases themselves, rather than the underlying concepts?
4. How computationally expensive is the proposed method compared to the baseline methods, particularly considering the hyperparameter tuning process?



**Additional Comments:**

*   The authors mention potential applications to entity linking. It would be valuable to see preliminary results or a discussion of the challenges involved in applying the method to this task.
*   The discussion of related work is comprehensive but could benefit from explicitly highlighting the limitations of existing approaches that motivate the proposed method.
* The formatting of table 3 could be improved for readability.
