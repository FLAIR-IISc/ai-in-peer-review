Summary and contributions
The paper proposes a weakly supervised method to jointly embed ontology concepts, their surface phrases, and words into a shared space by leveraging ontology-provided phrase inventories as distant supervision over unlabeled text. The model adapts skip-gram with negative sampling and introduces two hyperparameters to softly tie concepts to phrases and phrases to words. Trained on biomedical (PubMed, clinical notes) and general corpora (Wikipedia+NYT) with UMLS and YAGO, the approach reports competitive intrinsic performance (similarity/relatedness) while expanding coverage by over 3x without manual annotation, plus analyses of compositionality and semantic type structure.

Main contributions (as I see them)
1) A scalable, annotation-free pipeline for joint embeddings of concepts, phrases, and words that substantially increases concept coverage while remaining competitive on intrinsic evaluations.
2) An empirical study across domains and corpora, including release of software and a new human-annotated entity similarity/relatedness dataset.
3) Diagnostic analyses of compositionality and semantic type clustering illuminating what the model captures and where it falls short.

Strengths
- Practical impact via weak supervision and coverage: Replaces costly manual tagging with ontology phrase inventories; achieves >3x vocabulary coverage and handles both UMLS and YAGO at scale (millions/billions of tokens).
- Competitive intrinsic performance with stronger baselines: On UMNSRS Similarity the concept embeddings match the best word baseline (CHIU-30) and outperform other concept baselines; phrase-based concept approximations are strong on general-domain datasets.
- Careful, transparent experimentation: Cross-corpus hyperparameter tuning, comparisons to multiple prior biomedical embeddings, and ablations via β/η provide a thorough picture; results are consistent with known domain effects.
- Useful resources: A new entity similarity/relatedness dataset (688 pairs each) and a software implementation (cui2vec) support reproducibility and future work.
- Insightful analyses: Compositionality and semantic-type clustering analyses reveal how phrase/word evidence aggregates and where ontology structure emerges.

Weaknesses
- Limited methodological novelty: The core idea (phrase tagging + skip-gram) is close to prior distant supervision/entity-word joint embedding lines; the β/η regularization is a modest extension.
- Evaluation limitations: Intrinsic-only results with acknowledged tuning on test sets; the new dataset’s agreement (Fleiss’ κ=0.24) is low, weakening conclusions.
- Ambiguity and noise not rigorously quantified: The assumption that any phrase mention signals all linked concepts and greedy matching can inject substantial noise; no breakdown by phrase ambiguity or disambiguation strategy.
- Mixed gains and baselines parity: CHOI dominates small biomedical sets; CHIU-30 ties the best biomedical similarity; phrase-based approximations sometimes outperform direct concept embeddings, suggesting the concept layer adds limited additional signal.
- Underuse of ontology structure: The method ignores graph relations/types during training, leaving non-lexical information untapped.

Questions to authors (optional)
- Can you report performance stratified by phrase ambiguity (number of linked concepts) and show how η mitigates noise?
- Any downstream evaluations (e.g., entity linking, ICD/code prediction) to substantiate utility beyond intrinsic tasks?
- Will you release the code, embeddings, and the MTurk prompts/annotations, and provide clearer coverage statistics per corpus/ontology?
