- Proposes a weakly supervised method to jointly learn embeddings for ontology concepts, representative phrases, and words using greedy phrase tagging, SGNS-style training, and optional compositionality regularizers (β, η).

- Key contribution: scalable, annotation-free distant-supervision framework yielding competitive concept embeddings with much higher vocabulary coverage than prior annotated approaches.

- Key contribution: joint embedding of words, phrases, and concepts in a single space with optional compositionality regularizers enabling cross-level analysis.

- Key contribution: release of a small crowdsourced general-domain entity similarity dataset plus open-source code and embeddings (cui2vec).

- Strength: practical, annotation-free scaling with strong coverage (~3× vocabulary gains) useful for clinical/biomedical pipelines.

- Strength: competitive intrinsic performance on biomedical benchmarks (UMNSRS Similarity, MayoSRS) matching prior supervised methods without labels.

- Strength: cohesive joint space analysis showing concept vectors near phrase averages and bimodal phrase-word proximity, indicating capture of compositional and non-compositional phenomena.

- Strength: transparent comparisons against strong baselines and systematic evaluation of direct vs approximated concept vectors.

- Strength: reproducibility and resource release increases impact and enables follow-up work.

- Weakness: naive ambiguity handling—treating all candidate concepts equally (normalized by count) likely dilutes signals and could be improved with context-based disambiguation.

- Weakness: objective/output space underspecified and possibly inconsistent (ENS dimensionality and negative-sampling term differ from standard SGNS), requiring clarification and ablations (shared vs separate outputs).

- Weakness: limited novelty—approach is close in spirit to prior distant-supervision/entity-mention methods; main novelty is joint learning and simple regularizers, which is incremental.

- Weakness: evaluation relies mainly on intrinsic similarity/relatedness (limited downstream predictive value), with hyperparameter tuning on benchmarks and a new dataset with low inter-annotator agreement (κ≈0.24).

- Weakness: mixed results where phrase-based approximations often match or outperform direct concept vectors (especially on YAGO), questioning the utility of explicit concept embeddings under current training.

- Question: clarify output/negative-sampling parameterization—ENS dimensionality, whether negative term uses log σ(−u·n), and whether contexts are only words; provide ablations on shared vs independent outputs.

- Question: how sensitive are results to the 1/|Cp| ambiguity normalization, and were simple disambiguation heuristics (frequency priors, semantic type compatibility, softmax over candidates) tried?

- Question: can you provide extrinsic evaluations (concept normalization/linking on ShARe/CLEF, MIMIC, or Wikipedia/CoNLL) to validate practical utility beyond intrinsic correlations?

- Question: do concept vectors improve robustness to lexical variation (e.g., generalize to rare paraphrases unseen in training) compared to phrase-based approximations?

- Question: provide ablations on β and η (including zeroing one at a time) and training with only tagged vs tagged+untagged corpora to isolate sources of gains.

- Overall: solid, practical paper with strong coverage and competitive intrinsic results, but needs clarification of the objective, better ambiguity handling, and at least one extrinsic experiment to strengthen the contribution.
