Summary
This paper proposes a weakly supervised method to jointly learn embeddings for ontology concepts, their representative phrases, and words by adapting skip-gram with negative sampling. The key idea is to use ontology-derived phrase-to-concept mappings as distant supervision: replace phrase mentions in text with unique IDs, then train word, phrase, and concept embeddings from word contexts. Two regularizers control compositionality between words→phrases (β) and phrases→concepts (η). The approach is evaluated on UMLS (biomedical) and YAGO (general-domain), across multiple corpora (PubMed, clinical notes, Wikipedia+NYT), using similarity/relatedness benchmarks and analyses of concept–phrase proximity and semantic type clustering. Results are competitive with methods requiring human annotation, while covering far more concepts. The authors also contribute a small crowdsourced entity similarity/relatedness dataset and release code/embeddings (cui2vec).

Main contributions (as I see them)
1) A scalable weakly supervised framework to jointly embed concepts, phrases, and words without manual corpus annotation, using ontology phrase inventories as distant supervision. This enables large coverage (e.g., 193k UMLS concepts vs. 26–52k in prior concept-embedding baselines).
2) Empirical evidence that such embeddings reach competitive performance on biomedical similarity/relatedness (especially on larger datasets) and preserve some ontological/semantic structure (concept–phrase linkage, semantic type clustering).
3) A small new general-domain entity similarity/relatedness dataset and analysis across domains.

Strengths
- Scalability and practicality: The method eliminates expensive manual labeling and does not require KB relation triples; it only needs phrase inventories from ontologies and unlabeled text. Coverage gains are substantial (e.g., PM+CL: 193k UMLS concepts vs. 26–52k for MENCIA/De Vine), which is important for real applications where coverage gaps hinder utility.
- Competitive intrinsic performance with minimal supervision: On UMNSRS Similarity (the largest biomedical set), the concept embeddings match or beat strong word baselines (CONCEPTco 0.653 vs. CHIU-30 0.653) and outperform CUI embeddings trained on annotated sequences (e.g., De Vine 0.455). On relatedness, jointly trained phrases and approximated concepts are on par with tuned word baselines (PHRASEco 0.591 ~ CHIU-30 0.590). This suggests the distant supervision is sufficient to capture useful semantics.
- Thorough, transparent experimentation and ablations: The paper evaluates multiple configurations (CONCEPTco, PHRASEco, APPROXPHRco, APPROXWORD, WORDind/WORDco), varies corpora, and carefully tunes hyperparameters with cross-validated selection. Analyses of concept–phrase/phrase–word compositionality (cosine and MAP distributions) and semantic type clustering provide interpretability beyond headline numbers.
- Cross-domain evidence: Applying the same idea to YAGO with Wikipedia+NYT shows strong phrase-based performance on entity similarity/relatedness (e.g., APPROXPHRco 0.810/0.766), indicating the approach generalizes beyond biomedical text.
- Resource contribution: Public code/embeddings (cui2vec) and a new entity similarity/relatedness dataset, however modest, will likely be useful to the community.

Weaknesses
- Limited novelty in modeling: Methodologically, this is a solid but incremental adaptation of skip-gram with phrase replacement and simple regularization. Similar ideas exist for concept/entity embeddings from sequences (De Vine; Choi) and joint word–entity spaces (Wang et al., Toutanova et al.). The main advance is the specific joint training and weak supervision setup rather than a new model class.
- Heavy reliance on intrinsic evaluations and tuning on test sets: The core claims rest on similarity/relatedness correlations, which are known to have limited predictive value for downstream tasks. Although the authors use adapted 5-fold CV for hyperparameters, there is still tuning against these test targets and no extrinsic tasks (e.g., entity linking, concept normalization, clinical IR/coding). This limits evidence of real-world impact.
- Ambiguity and noise handling is rudimentary: The many-to-many phrase–concept mapping is handled by uniformly distributing updates when a phrase is ambiguous. There is no disambiguation, prior weighting, or noise-robust training (beyond negative sampling). The effect of ambiguous phrases on learning quality is not quantified, and there is no ablation by ambiguity rate.
- The compositionality regularizers rarely help: Best settings frequently set β=0 and η=0 (Table 3), undermining the practical contribution of the proposed similarity penalties. While the analysis notes a negative trend with larger β,η, the paper does not explore why or propose alternatives, leaving the “joint” aspect largely realized via shared negatives and co-training rather than the explicit coupling.
- Comparisons are not entirely apples-to-apples: Baselines differ in ontologies (MeSH vs. UMLS), supervision (manual code sequences vs. distant), and corpora, complicating head-to-head claims. For example, CHOI’s superior performance on Mayo SRS/Mini Mayo reflects different supervision and smaller but higher-quality code sequences; the paper acknowledges this but could calibrate conclusions more carefully.
- Quality and size of the new entity dataset: The dataset is small (688 pairs) with fair agreement (Fleiss’ κ=0.24), which limits its value for benchmarking. Details imply some annotators did many HITs, and validation pairs were simplistic; thus the dataset should be framed as preliminary.

Questions to Authors
- How sensitive are results to phrase ambiguity? Can you report performance separately for pairs involving concepts whose phrases are highly ambiguous vs. unambiguous? Any ablation weighting phrase–concept updates by estimated priors/frequencies?
- Could you include at least one downstream task (even small-scale) such as entity linking or concept normalization, to substantiate utility beyond intrinsic metrics?
- Why do β and η mostly tune to zero? Did you try alternative coupling (e.g., contrastive losses, tied parameters, or retrofitting-style postprocessing) that might better encourage phrase–word or concept–phrase alignment?
- How exactly are negative samples drawn for phrase and concept updates if ENS is word-only? Did you try sampling negatives from the corresponding space (phrases for phrases, concepts for concepts), and how did that affect convergence/performance?
- What proportion of training phrase mentions are ambiguous, and what's the distribution of number of concepts per phrase? Any evidence that greedy longest-match replacement introduces boundary errors or biases?

Overall assessment
Originality: moderate. Soundness: generally sound; distant supervision assumption plausible but under-analyzed for ambiguity. Clarity: good; method and experiments are well described. Significance: good in terms of scalability and practicality; limited by absence of extrinsic validation. I lean toward weak accept if the authors can address ambiguity handling and add at least one downstream evaluation; otherwise borderline.
