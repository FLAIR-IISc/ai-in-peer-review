Summary and Contributions
This paper proposes a weakly supervised method to jointly learn embeddings for ontology concepts (UMLS, YAGO), their representative phrases, and words by (i) greedily tagging known phrases in a large corpus, (ii) training a skip-gram-with-negative-sampling-style objective where words serve as contexts and phrases/concepts act as inputs, and (iii) optionally regularizing phrase-word and concept-phrase compositionality via β and η. Evaluation spans intrinsic similarity/relatedness for biomedical concepts (MayoSRS, UMNSRS) and a new small general-domain entity dataset; analyses of compositionality and semantic type clustering are provided.

Contributions as I see them (in order of strength):
1) A simple, scalable distant-supervision framework that yields competitive concept embeddings without human annotation, with substantially higher coverage than prior annotated approaches.
2) Joint embedding of words, phrases, and concepts in a single space with optional compositionality regularizers, enabling analysis across levels (word→phrase→concept).
3) A small crowdsourced dataset for general-domain entity similarity/relatedness; plus open-source release of code/embeddings.

Strengths
- Practical, annotation-free scaling with strong coverage: Mapping ontology phrases into raw text and training on untagged word contexts is computationally straightforward and avoids costly human annotation or KB links. The coverage gains (e.g., ~3x vocabulary vs prior concept-embedding work) are compelling for downstream use in clinical/biomedical pipelines that need broad normalization.

- Competitive intrinsic performance where prior work required labels: On UMNSRS Similarity, the proposed concept embeddings match or outperform prior methods (including CHIU-30 words and De Vine’s CUI embeddings), and are close on relatedness. Given no manual coding and no KB relational supervision, this is a notable result.

- Cohesive joint space and insightful analysis: The paper convincingly shows that concept embeddings sit near the average of their phrases, and that phrase-word proximity is bimodal—evidence that the model captures both non-compositional phrases and truly compositional ones. The semantic type clustering analysis, while not always best, indicates that structure from UMLS/YAGO leaks into the space.

- Transparent comparisons and reasonable baselines: The authors systematically compare direct concept vectors vs approximations from phrases/words, and against strong published baselines (De Vine, Choi, Mencia; CHIU word embeddings), helping position the method’s value and limitations.

- Reproducibility and resources: Releasing cui2vec and the general-domain dataset increases impact and facilitates follow-up work.

Weaknesses
- Ambiguity handling is overly naive; no disambiguation: The central assumption that any mention of a mapped phrase uniformly updates all its candidate concepts (normalized by count) is very weak. In medicine and entities, phrase ambiguity is common and skewed; treating all senses equally likely dilutes learning signals and may explain why concept vectors are often close to phrase averages and why η>0 generally hurts. Even simple context-based soft disambiguation (e.g., type priors, EM, or attention over candidate concepts) could materially improve quality.

- Output space and objective design are underspecified/possibly inconsistent: The model uses a single negative-sampling “matrix” ENS tied to words as contexts. Equations and shapes appear inconsistent (ENS = |W| × k is not dimensionally compatible; it should be |W| × d), and the negative sampling term is written as −∑ log σ(u·n), which differs from log σ(−u·n) used in standard SGNS. It is unclear whether this is a notational slip or a true deviation; either way, this needs clarification, and ablations (e.g., shared vs separate output embeddings; sampling from union of words/phrases) would strengthen claims about “jointness.”

- Limited novelty relative to prior distant/weak supervision: Replacing ontology phrases in text and training distributional embeddings of the resulting tokens is close in spirit to De Vine (CUI sequences), and to work that uses entity mentions as supervision. The main novelty is simultaneous learning of words, phrases, and concepts on untagged contexts plus simple compositionality regularizers. This is useful but somewhat incremental; stronger contributions would include principled sense disambiguation or integrating ontology graph structure during training.

- Intrinsic-only evaluation and tuning on test sets: The main claims rest on similarity/relatedness correlations (known to have limited predictive value for downstream tasks) and the new dataset has low inter-annotator agreement (κ≈0.24). Moreover, hyperparameters are tuned on the same benchmarks via cross-fold re-ranking; although the authors attempt to mitigate overfitting, a clean dev/test split or unseen evaluation would be preferable. Missing extrinsic evaluations (e.g., concept normalization, ICD coding, entity linking, clustering/retrieval) weaken the practical impact.

- Mixed results undermine the need for explicit concept vectors: On YAGO, phrase-based concept approximations often outperform direct concept embeddings; in biomed, concept vectors are often close to phrase averages. This suggests the EC parameters provide limited additional signal beyond phrases/words under the current training scheme. A more compelling case would require scenarios where EC clearly outperforms approximations (e.g., robust to lexical variation, better transfer to normalization tasks).

Questions to Authors
1) Can you clarify the output/negative-sampling parameterization? Specifically: the dimensionality of ENS, whether the negative term is implemented as log σ(−u·n) or as written, and whether contexts are always words (never phrases/concepts). Any ablation on “shared output” vs independent outputs?

2) How sensitive are results to the ambiguity normalization (1/|Cp|)? Did you try simple disambiguation heuristics (e.g., frequency priors, semantic type compatibility, or a softmax over candidate concepts using local context)?

3) Could you report extrinsic evaluations (even small-scale): concept normalization/linking on a standard corpus (e.g., ShARe/CLEF eHealth, MIMIC concept normalization), or entity linking on Wikipedia/CoNLL? This would help validate the practical utility beyond intrinsic correlations.

4) Do concept vectors confer robustness to lexical variation beyond phrase-level approximations? For example, for rare paraphrases unseen in training, does EC generalize better than EP-based approximations?

5) Can you provide ablations on β and η (including zeroing one at a time) and on training with only tagged vs tagged+untagged corpora, to isolate where gains come from?

Overall
This is a solid, practical paper with convincing coverage and competitive intrinsic results, but with limited novelty and some modeling/evaluation gaps. Addressing the ambiguity assumption, clarifying the objective, and adding at least one extrinsic experiment would significantly strengthen the contribution.
