Summary
The paper proposes a weakly supervised approach to jointly learn embeddings for ontology concepts, their surface phrases, and words from unlabeled text. It uses lexicons from UMLS and YAGO as distant supervision: phrases mapped to concepts are greedily identified in text, replaced by unique phrase IDs, and concept embeddings are updated using contexts of their phrase mentions. The base model is skip-gram with negative sampling; additional compositionality regularizers tie phrase vectors to the average of their word vectors (β) and concept vectors to the average of their phrase vectors (η). Experiments train on biomedical corpora (PubMed, clinical notes, combined) and general-domain corpora (Wikipedia+NYT), and evaluate on biomedical concept similarity/relatedness datasets and a new crowdsourced entity similarity/relatedness dataset. Results show competitive intrinsic performance to prior methods requiring human annotations, with substantially greater vocabulary coverage. Analyses examine alignment between concepts/phrases/words and clustering by semantic type.

Main contributions (as assessed)
- A general weakly supervised framework to learn joint embeddings of concepts, phrases, and words using only ontology phrase lists and unlabeled corpora, without manual corpus annotation or KB relation usage.
- Empirical evaluation across domains showing competitive intrinsic performance (Spearman’s ρ on similarity/relatedness) with much larger coverage than annotation-dependent baselines, plus analyses of compositionality and semantic type clustering.
- A new dataset of human judgments for similarity and relatedness of real-world entities and release of software (cui2vec).

Strengths
- Low-cost, scalable supervision strategy with broad coverage: By leveraging ontology phrase inventories and greedy phrase matching, the method avoids manual annotation and knowledge graph relation reliance. The vocabulary coverage claim (>3x) is supported by Table 1 (e.g., ~188k–193k UMLS concepts vs 15k–52k in prior biomedical concept embeddings), enabling evaluation on larger concept sets.
- Competitive intrinsic performance on larger biomedical datasets: On UMNSRS Similarity (566 pairs), the joint concept embeddings achieve the top correlation (ρ=0.653), on par with strong word baselines (CHIU-30: 0.653) and outperform specialized concept embeddings (e.g., De Vine: 0.455, Mencia: 0.565). On UMNSRS Relatedness, results are competitive (CONCEPTco 0.559; PHRASEco/CHIU-30 ~0.590), despite requiring no human annotation.
- Cross-domain generality: The approach is applied to UMLS and YAGO with different corpora. On the new general-domain datasets, phrase-level approximations (APPROXPHRco ρ=0.810 relatedness, 0.766 similarity) and concept embeddings (0.789/0.750) perform well relative to word/phrase baselines, indicating the method’s applicability beyond biomedicine.
- Joint space enables multi-level analysis: The compositionality and alignment analyses (Figures 3 and 4) provide evidence about how phrases relate to words and concepts, and semantic type clustering (Table 6) shows meaningful structure (e.g., competitive MAP for “pharmacologic substance” and “neoplastic process”), adding interpretability beyond single-level embeddings.
- Clear articulation of training objective and regularization: The use of β and η to trade off independence vs compositionality is well-motivated, and hyperparameter exploration shows their effects, adding transparency about when compositional constraints help.

Weaknesses
- Limited novelty relative to closely related work: The core idea (using lexicon-matched spans to tie entities/concepts to distributional contexts) is close to prior approaches that auto-tag concepts (De Vine et al., Choi et al.) or jointly embed entities and words (Wang et al. 2014a), differing mainly by not using KB relations and by introducing simple compositionality penalties. The technical contribution is incremental (SGNS with additional averaging-based similarity terms).
- Evaluation is largely intrinsic and tuning risks overfitting: The primary evidence is similarity/relatedness correlations; extrinsic evaluations (e.g., entity linking, WSD, downstream clinical tasks) are absent. Hyperparameters are tuned on the same datasets used for reporting, with an “adapted 5-fold” approach that still uses the test items for model selection; this weakens claims about generalization. The authors themselves note intrinsic–extrinsic disconnects.
- Ambiguity handling may inject substantial noise: The distant supervision assumes every matched phrase signifies any of its possible concepts, and concept updates are averaged over senses. No disambiguation or probabilistic weighting is used, which can dilute concept representations, especially for highly polysemous phrases. While acceptable as weak supervision, a quantitative analysis of polysemy prevalence and its impact is missing.
- Compositionality regularizers provide limited gains: Optimal β and η are often 0 (Table 3 and supplemental), suggesting the proposed similarity-enforcing terms are not crucial. An ablation comparing the full model to training only phrase embeddings plus post-hoc concept averaging would sharpen the empirical contribution.
- Clarity and technical details: There are minor notation issues (e.g., concept loss uses c∈Cs with Cs undefined; likely Cp). The negative sampling matrix dimensions and sharing across word/phrase/concept updates are briefly described but could be clearer (e.g., whether negative contexts draw only from words, and how this affects concept/phrase updates). Preprocessing choices (greedy longest match, case sensitivity, overlap resolution) and their error rates are not quantified.

Questions to Authors
- Loss and sampling details: In Eq. (4)/(7), should Cp (concepts of phrase p) replace Cs? Are negative samples always words from W? If so, what is the motivation for not sampling phrases/concepts as negatives for phrase/concept updates?
- Update weighting: For each token, how are word, phrase, and concept updates interleaved and weighted? Is there a coefficient controlling the relative contribution of each to the total loss per step?
- Ambiguity impact: What fraction of phrases are mapped to multiple concepts in your training sets, and how often do these ambiguous phrases occur? Can you report performance as a function of phrase ambiguity or after filtering highly ambiguous phrases?
- Phrase detection: How are overlapping/multi-span matches handled beyond greedy longest-match? Is matching case-sensitive and does it use tokenization normalization (e.g., punctuation, possessives)?
- Generalization: Can you provide an extrinsic task evaluation (e.g., clinical concept normalization, entity linking) or a small-scale experiment showing that concept embeddings improve over word/phrase baselines?
- Data splits: Can you release train/dev/test splits for the similarity/relatedness datasets (both biomedical and your new YAGO-based sets) and re-report with tuning on dev only to address overfitting concerns?
- Coverage claims: The “>3x coverage” statement—relative to which baseline and under what frequency thresholds? Please quantify coverage and overlap with evaluation datasets.
