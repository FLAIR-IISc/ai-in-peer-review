Summary and contributions
This paper proposes a weakly supervised method to jointly learn embeddings for ontology concepts, their surface phrases, and words by: (i) greedily replacing known concept phrases in a raw corpus with unique phrase IDs, (ii) training a modified skip-gram with negative sampling over parallel tagged/untagged corpora, and (iii) coupling the three spaces (words, phrases, concepts) via shared negative-sample embeddings and optional compositionality regularizers that align phrases with the average of their words (β) and concepts with the average of their phrases (η). The approach uses UMLS and YAGO phrase inventories as distant supervision without manual concept annotation. The authors evaluate on biomedical and newly collected general-domain entity similarity/relatedness benchmarks, claim competitive performance with substantially larger coverage, and provide analyses of compositionality and semantic-type clustering.

Main contributions (as I see them; strongest first):
1) A practical, scalable weakly supervised framework for jointly embedding concepts, phrases, and words that avoids manual concept annotations while achieving competitive intrinsic performance and >3x vocabulary coverage over prior biomedical concept embeddings.
2) Construction and release of a new general-domain entity similarity and relatedness dataset (688 pairs each) and software (cui2vec), broadening evaluation resources beyond word-level datasets.
3) Empirical analyses showing how joint training preserves concept–phrase proximity and induces semantic-type clustering, with interpretable regularization to trade off independence vs compositionality.

Strengths
- Scalable distant supervision with strong coverage: The method leverages existing ontology phrase inventories to learn >190k UMLS concept embeddings (PM+CL) and ~750k YAGO entities (WIKINYT) without manual tagging. Compared to De Vine (52k CUIs) and Mencia (26k MeSH), coverage is substantially larger, addressing a core bottleneck for concept-level embedding resources.

- Competitive intrinsic performance without manual labels: On UMNSRS Similarity, CONCEPTco achieves 0.653 Spearman’s ρ, matching CHIU-30 (0.653) and outperforming other concept baselines that require stronger supervision (e.g., De Vine, Mencia). On relatedness, jointly trained words/phrases are competitive with strong word baselines (PHRASEco 0.591 vs CHIU-30 0.590), showing the approach’s robustness despite label noise.

- Cross-domain generality and new evaluation resource: The approach transfers to general-domain entities using YAGO and WIKINYT, with high correlations on the new datasets (e.g., APPROXPHRco 0.810 relatedness, 0.766 similarity). Releasing these datasets addresses a notable gap for entity-level intrinsic evaluation beyond words.

- Transparent modeling and useful analyses: The paper clearly describes the training pipeline, the many-to-many phrase–concept mapping, and the design choice to share a negative-sample matrix. The β/η regularizers are intuitive knobs to encourage compositionality. The compositionality and mean average precision analyses provide insight into when phrases are decomposable and how concepts relate to their phrases/words. The semantic-type clustering comparison situates the model relative to clinically curated code embeddings.

- Thorough hyperparameter exploration: The authors perform cross-validated hyperparameter tuning and report per-corpus optimal settings, acknowledging task sensitivity and providing reproducible details (learning rates, thresholds, windows).

Weaknesses
- Limited novelty relative to prior joint text–KB embedding work: While the engineering is elegant, the conceptual contributions are incremental to SGNS with distant supervision. Prior work (e.g., Wang et al., 2014a; Toutanova et al., 2015) has jointly embedded entities and text; this paper’s main novelty is the specific weakly supervised phrase-to-concept coupling and the shared negative-sample matrix. The method does not exploit ontology graph structure (relations, types) beyond phrase inventories, missing an opportunity to model non-lexical knowledge.

- Ambiguity and noise in distant supervision not adequately addressed: The assumption that any occurrence of a phrase signifies any of its candidate concepts (uniformly normalized) introduces substantial label noise, especially for polysemous phrases and many-to-many mappings. There is no disambiguation, context-aware weighting, or prior over concepts. The analyses suggest concept vectors often reduce to averages of phrases/words, indicating limited ability to separate senses or capture non-lexical semantics.

- Evaluation focus and fairness: Intrinsic evaluations are limited to similarity/relatedness; no downstream tasks (e.g., entity linking, concept normalization, clinical prediction) are reported, so practical utility remains uncertain. The new entity datasets have low inter-annotator agreement (κ=0.24), weakening conclusions. The authors also acknowledge tuning on the test data (albeit with adapted CV), which can inflate reported correlations. Some baselines use different corpora/supervision, complicating fairness.

- Mixed results and limited gains of concept embeddings: On MayoSRS/Mini Mayo SRS, CHOI (with human-annotated code sequences) substantially outperforms (0.817/0.726 vs 0.593/0.591). On UMNSRS relatedness, word/phrase models rival concept embeddings (PHRASEco 0.591 vs CONCEPTco 0.559). In general domain, approximated concept embeddings via phrases outperform direct concept vectors, suggesting the concept layer adds limited value beyond phrase aggregation.

- Modeling choices under-explored: The coupling between spaces is only via shared negative-sample embeddings (plus optional β/η penalties). Alternative couplings (shared context matrices, multi-task objectives, retrofitting with ontology edges) are not examined. There is little ablation isolating β vs η effects beyond tuning, and no error analysis showing where concepts diverge from phrase averages.

Questions to authors
- How large is the many-to-many ambiguity in practice (distribution of |Cp| per phrase), and how does performance vary with ambiguity? Any results with context-based disambiguation or non-uniform priors over concepts?

- What is the negative sampling distribution (unigram^0.75 over words only)? Why restrict negatives to words, and how sensitive are results to sampling from phrases/concepts or sharing/tieing other embedding parameters?

- Can you provide ablations quantifying the effect of the shared negative-sample matrix vs fully separate spaces? Similarly, targeted β/η ablations on subsets of compositional phrases vs non-compositional MWEs?

- How does coverage and performance change with lower frequency thresholds (e.g., min count < 25), especially for clinical corpora?

- For the new datasets, can you report per-pair variance and robustness to annotator filtering, or provide higher-agreement subsets? Any comparison to existing entity-relatedness datasets (e.g., KG-based or Wikipedia anchor-based proxies)?

- Do concept embeddings help downstream tasks beyond similarity/relatedness (e.g., concept normalization, entity linking, clinical phenotyping)? Even small-scale experiments would strengthen claims.

- Could incorporating ontology edges (e.g., UMLS relations, YAGO graph) during training improve the observed tendency for concept vectors to collapse toward phrase/word averages? Any preliminary results?
