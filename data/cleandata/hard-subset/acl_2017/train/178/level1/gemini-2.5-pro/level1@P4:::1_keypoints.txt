- Summary: Weakly-supervised method uses ontology-based distant supervision with an adapted skip-gram to jointly learn concept, phrase, and word embeddings from raw text; evaluated on biomedical and general corpora and a new released entity similarity dataset.

- Contribution: Proposes a practical, scalable distant-supervision model for joint concept/phrase/word embeddings that avoids manual annotation.

- Contribution: Provides comprehensive empirical evaluation across biomedical (UMLS) and general-domain (YAGO) corpora against many baselines.

- Contribution: Releases a new crowdsourced dataset for general-domain entity similarity and relatedness.

- Strength: Highly scalable and practical, learning many more concepts (e.g., >190k UMLS) than prior supervised approaches.

- Strength: Technically sound extension of skip-gram with intuitive updates and compositionality hyperparameters (β, η).

- Strength: Thorough cross-domain experiments showing competitive performance, particularly on large evaluation sets like UMNSRS.

- Strength: New evaluation resource is well-documented and useful for future research.

- Weakness: Direct concept embeddings (CONCEPTco) do not consistently outperform phrase-approximation (APPROXPHRco), questioning the need for the extra concept matrix.

- Weakness: Compositionality parameters β and η often tune to 0.0 and lack detailed ablation/analysis to show their benefit.

- Weakness: Greedy longest-match phrase tagging ignores nested/overlapping phrases and can cause information loss for shorter sub-phrases.

- Weakness: Evaluation is limited to intrinsic similarity/relatedness tasks and lacks extrinsic downstream validation.

- Question: Why do CONCEPTco vectors underperform APPROXPHRco on general-domain data — are aliases sufficient to capture entity meaning?

- Question: Have you tried alternative compositionality penalties (e.g., direct L2 distance to component-average) instead of skip-gram–style regularizers?

- Question: How prevalent are nested/overlapping phrases in your corpora and does greedy matching lead to sparsity for shorter phrases?
