- Introduces a weakly-supervised method that jointly embeds concepts, phrases, and words using ontology-based distant supervision and an adapted skip-gram, with no manual annotation and a released dataset.

Contributions:
- Novel, practical weakly-supervised framework combining distributional statistics with ontologies to learn high-coverage concept/phrase/word embeddings.
- Comprehensive empirical validation showing competitive performance across biomedical and general domains and much larger vocabulary coverage.
- Releases a new general-domain entity similarity/relatedness dataset and software (cui2vec).

Strengths:
- High novelty and scalability: distant supervision, large concept counts (e.g., 193k UMLS, 748k YAGO), and easy implementation via word2vec adaptations.
- Thorough, rigorous evaluation across domains, corpora, and strong baselines demonstrating competitive results.
- Valuable resource contribution: shared code and a new entity-level benchmark.
- Insightful analyses on compositionality and semantic-type clustering that illuminate embedding geometry.

Weaknesses:
- Limited technical novelty in the objective: mainly a straightforward skip-gram adaptation with ad-hoc cosine compositionality terms.
- Compositionality hyperparameters (β, η) often optimal at zero and tend to degrade performance, questioning their utility.
- Empirical contradiction: simple averaged phrase embeddings (APPROXPHRco) can outperform learned concept vectors (CONCEPTco) on general-domain data, undermining the motivation for atomic concept embeddings.
- Evaluation restricted to intrinsic similarity/relatedness tasks with no extrinsic downstream evaluation to confirm practical utility.

Questions to authors:
- Why do β and η typically hurt performance — does enforcing similarity to component averages add noise rather than useful signal?
- Why does APPROXPHRco beat CONCEPTco on the general-domain task — could ontology differences (YAGO vs UMLS) or entity types explain this?
- How does the greedy longest-match preprocessing handle nested entities (e.g., "New York" vs. "New York City") and could it introduce biases compared to alternative phrase identification methods?
