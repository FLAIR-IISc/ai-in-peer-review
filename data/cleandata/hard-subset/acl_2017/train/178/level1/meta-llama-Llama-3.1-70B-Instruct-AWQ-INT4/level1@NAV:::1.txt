**Summary of the Paper**

The paper proposes a novel method for jointly learning concept, phrase, and word embeddings from an unlabeled text corpus using representative phrases for ontology concepts as distant supervision. The authors adapt the skip-gram with negative sampling variant of word2vec for joint embedding of concepts, phrases, and words. They evaluate their method on biomedical and general-domain datasets and show that their embeddings are competitive with existing methods on concept similarity and relatedness tasks, while requiring no human corpus annotation.

**Main Contributions**

1. **Joint embedding of concepts, phrases, and words**: The authors propose a novel method for jointly embedding concepts, phrases, and words into a shared vector space, which allows for the capture of semantic relationships between them.
2. **Distant supervision using ontology concepts**: The authors use representative phrases for ontology concepts as distant supervision to train their model, which eliminates the need for human annotation.
3. **Competitive performance on similarity and relatedness tasks**: The authors show that their embeddings are competitive with existing methods on concept similarity and relatedness tasks, while requiring no human corpus annotation.

**Strengths**

1. **Novel approach to joint embedding**: The authors propose a novel approach to jointly embedding concepts, phrases, and words, which allows for the capture of semantic relationships between them.
2. **Effective use of distant supervision**: The authors effectively use representative phrases for ontology concepts as distant supervision to train their model, which eliminates the need for human annotation.
3. **Competitive performance on similarity and relatedness tasks**: The authors show that their embeddings are competitive with existing methods on concept similarity and relatedness tasks, while requiring no human corpus annotation.

**Weaknesses**

1. **Limited evaluation on downstream tasks**: The authors only evaluate their method on similarity and relatedness tasks, and do not provide a comprehensive evaluation on downstream tasks such as entity linking or question answering.
2. **Lack of comparison with other joint embedding methods**: The authors do not compare their method with other joint embedding methods, which makes it difficult to assess the novelty and effectiveness of their approach.
3. **Limited analysis of hyperparameter tuning**: The authors do not provide a detailed analysis of hyperparameter tuning, which makes it difficult to understand the impact of different hyperparameters on the performance of their method.

**Questions to Authors**

1. How do you plan to extend your method to other downstream tasks such as entity linking or question answering?
2. Can you provide a more detailed analysis of hyperparameter tuning and its impact on the performance of your method?
3. How do you plan to address the issue of polysemy in your method, where a single word or phrase can have multiple meanings?
