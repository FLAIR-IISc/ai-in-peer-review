**Summary**

The paper proposes a novel method for jointly learning concept, phrase, and word embeddings from an unlabeled text corpus using representative phrases for ontology concepts as distant supervision. The authors adapt the skip-gram with negative sampling variant of word2vec to train embeddings for medical concepts in the Unified Medical Language System and general-domain concepts in YAGO. The method requires no human annotations and demonstrates competitive performance with existing methods on concept similarity and relatedness tasks.

**Contributions**

1. **Joint embedding of concepts, phrases, and words**: The paper proposes a novel method for jointly embedding concepts, phrases, and words into a shared vector space, which allows for the capture of semantic relationships between these entities.
2. **Distant supervision using ontology concepts**: The authors use representative phrases for ontology concepts as distant supervision to train the embeddings, which eliminates the need for human annotations.
3. **Competitive performance on similarity and relatedness tasks**: The method demonstrates competitive performance with existing methods on concept similarity and relatedness tasks, despite requiring no human annotations.

**Strengths**

1. **Novel approach to concept embedding**: The paper proposes a novel approach to concept embedding that leverages distant supervision from ontology concepts, which is a significant contribution to the field.
2. **Competitive performance**: The method demonstrates competitive performance with existing methods on concept similarity and relatedness tasks, which is a significant achievement given the lack of human annotations.
3. **Scalability**: The method can scale to a large vocabulary without additional expense, which is a significant advantage over existing methods that require human annotations.

**Weaknesses**

1. **Limited evaluation**: The paper only evaluates the method on a limited set of datasets, which may not be representative of the broader range of applications for concept embeddings.
2. **Lack of comparison to state-of-the-art methods**: The paper does not compare the method to state-of-the-art methods for concept embedding, which makes it difficult to assess the method's performance in relation to existing approaches.
3. **Limited analysis of hyperparameter tuning**: The paper does not provide a detailed analysis of the hyperparameter tuning process, which makes it difficult to understand the impact of different hyperparameters on the method's performance.

**Questions to Authors**

1. How do the authors plan to extend the method to handle polysemy and homophony in concept embeddings?
2. Can the authors provide more details on the hyperparameter tuning process and the impact of different hyperparameters on the method's performance?
3. How do the authors plan to evaluate the method on a broader range of datasets and applications to demonstrate its generalizability?
