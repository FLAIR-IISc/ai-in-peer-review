**Summary of the Paper**

This paper proposes a novel method for jointly learning concept, phrase, and word embeddings from an unlabeled text corpus, using the representative phrases for ontology concepts as distant supervision. The authors adapt the skip-gram with negative sampling variant of word2vec to train embeddings for medical concepts in the Unified Medical Language System (UMLS) and general-domain concepts in YAGO. The method requires no human annotations and demonstrates competitive performance with existing methods on concept similarity and relatedness tasks, while covering a larger vocabulary size.

**Main Contributions**

1. **Joint Embedding Method**: The authors propose a novel method for jointly embedding concepts, phrases, and words into a shared vector space, using distant supervision from phrases linked to concepts in an ontology.
2. **Competitive Performance**: The method demonstrates competitive performance with existing methods on concept similarity and relatedness tasks, while requiring no human annotations and covering a larger vocabulary size.
3. **Novel Dataset**: The authors present two novel datasets of similarity and relatedness of real-world entities, identified both by Wikipedia page and text phrase.

**Strengths**

1. **Effective Use of Distant Supervision**: The authors effectively use distant supervision from phrases linked to concepts in an ontology to train the embeddings, eliminating the need for human annotations.
2. **Competitive Performance**: The method demonstrates competitive performance with existing methods on concept similarity and relatedness tasks, indicating the effectiveness of the proposed approach.
3. **Scalability**: The method can scale to a larger vocabulary size without additional expense, making it a promising approach for large-scale applications.

**Weaknesses**

1. **Limited Evaluation**: The authors primarily evaluate the method on biomedical datasets, with limited evaluation on general-domain datasets. Further evaluation on diverse datasets is necessary to fully assess the method's performance.
2. **Lack of Hyperparameter Analysis**: While the authors perform hyperparameter tuning, they do not provide a detailed analysis of the impact of hyperparameters on the method's performance.
3. **Limited Comparison to Baselines**: The authors primarily compare their method to word-based baselines, with limited comparison to other concept-based methods.

**Questions to Authors**

1. How do the authors plan to extend the method to handle more abstract concepts in the general domain?
2. Can the authors provide a more detailed analysis of the impact of hyperparameters on the method's performance?
3. How do the authors plan to address the issue of polysemy in the embeddings, as mentioned in the discussion section?

**Additional Comments**

The authors' use of distant supervision is a promising approach for reducing the need for human annotations in concept embedding methods. However, further evaluation on diverse datasets and a more detailed analysis of hyperparameters are necessary to fully assess the method's performance. Additionally, the authors may consider exploring other applications of the method, such as entity linking, to further demonstrate its effectiveness.
