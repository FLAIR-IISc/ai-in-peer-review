**Summary of the Paper**

The paper proposes a novel method for jointly learning concept, phrase, and word embeddings from an unlabeled text corpus using distant supervision from an ontology. The method, called cui2vec, uses representative phrases for ontology concepts as distant supervision and adapts the skip-gram with negative sampling variant of word2vec for joint embedding. The authors evaluate their method on biomedical and general-domain concept similarity and relatedness tasks, achieving competitive performance with existing methods that require human-annotated data.

**Main Contributions**

1. **Novel method for jointly learning concept, phrase, and word embeddings**: The authors propose a method that uses distant supervision from an ontology to learn concept, phrase, and word embeddings in a shared vector space.
2. **Competitive performance on biomedical and general-domain concept similarity and relatedness tasks**: The authors evaluate their method on several datasets and achieve competitive performance with existing methods that require human-annotated data.
3. **Novel dataset of similarity and relatedness of real-world entities**: The authors create a new dataset of human judgments of similarity and relatedness between pairs of people, places, and organizations.

**Strengths**

1. **Effective use of distant supervision**: The authors demonstrate the effectiveness of using distant supervision from an ontology to learn concept, phrase, and word embeddings.
2. **Competitive performance on biomedical and general-domain tasks**: The authors show that their method achieves competitive performance on several datasets, including biomedical and general-domain concept similarity and relatedness tasks.
3. **Scalability to large vocabulary**: The authors demonstrate that their method can scale to a large vocabulary without additional expense.

**Weaknesses**

1. **Limited evaluation on downstream tasks**: The authors primarily evaluate their method on intrinsic tasks, such as concept similarity and relatedness, and do not provide extensive evaluation on downstream tasks.
2. **Lack of clear trends in hyperparameter tuning**: The authors find that hyperparameter tuning does not yield clear trends, and the optimal settings vary across corpora.
3. **Potential issues with ontology mismatch**: The authors note a slight mismatch between the ontologies used, which may affect the performance of their method.

**Questions to Authors**

1. How do the authors plan to extend their method to more abstract data in the general domain, similar to previous work with BabelNet?
2. Can the authors provide more details on the hyperparameter tuning process and the optimal settings for each corpus?
3. How do the authors plan to address the potential issues with ontology mismatch and its impact on the performance of their method?
