

# Review for ACL 2017

## Summary and Contributions
This paper proposes a weakly-supervised method for jointly embedding concepts, phrases, and words using representative phrases from an ontology as distant supervision. The main contributions are: (1) a novel approach requiring no human annotation that achieves competitive performance with methods requiring manual annotation; (2) a new dataset for evaluating similarity and relatedness of real-world entities (people, places, organizations), filling a significant gap in the literature; and (3) demonstration of significantly larger vocabulary coverage (more than 3x) compared to existing methods.

## Strengths

1. **Practical significance of weak supervision**: The paper addresses a critical problem in the field - the need for concept embeddings without expensive human annotation. The authors convincingly demonstrate that their method achieves performance competitive with existing methods that require human annotation (e.g., CHOI, MENCIA) on large-scale biomedical datasets (UMNSRS), while eliminating the annotation bottleneck. This is a major practical contribution with clear applicability to domains where manual annotation is prohibitively expensive.

2. **Novel dataset creation**: The paper's creation of a new dataset for evaluating similarity and relatedness of real-world entities is a significant contribution. As the authors note, while word-level similarity datasets exist (WordSim-353, SimLex999), comparable datasets for real-world entities (people, places, organizations) were lacking. The authors' careful data collection process using Mechanical Turk with validation questions and quality control measures produces a valuable resource for future research.

3. **Vocabulary scale advantage**: The paper demonstrates more than 3x vocabulary coverage compared to existing methods that require human annotation. This is a substantial practical advantage, as the authors note: "our embeddings show performance competitive with existing methods on concept similarity and relatedness tasks, while requiring no human corpus annotation and demonstrating more than 3x coverage in the vocabulary size." This scale advantage is particularly valuable for real-world applications where broad coverage is essential.

## Weaknesses

1. **Limited evaluation beyond similarity/relatedness tasks**: The paper relies primarily on similarity and relatedness tasks for evaluation, despite acknowledging concerns raised by Chiu et al. (2016b, 2016a) and Faruqui et al. (2016) about the limitations of these tasks for evaluating semantic embeddings. The authors claim their embeddings capture "some semantic properties," but the paper lacks evidence from other intrinsic tasks (e.g., analogy completion) or extrinsic NLP tasks (e.g., entity linking). This is a significant limitation in demonstrating the true value of their embeddings.

2. **Performance gap on smaller datasets**: The results in Table 4 show that the proposed method is outperformed by CHOI on the two smaller biomedical datasets (Mayo SRS and Mini Mayo SRS), despite CHOI requiring manual annotations. The paper doesn't adequately explain why their method can be competitive on larger datasets while falling behind on smaller ones, which weakens the claim of "competitive performance" overall.

3. **Insufficient analysis of ontology dependency**: The method's performance is highly dependent on the quality of the ontology's phrase-to-concept mappings. The paper doesn't analyze how sensitive the method is to the quality of these mappings or how it performs with ontologies that have different mapping characteristics. For example, if an ontology has many concepts with no representative phrases, the method would fail to embed those concepts. The paper should address this limitation and provide sensitivity analysis.

## Questions to Authors

1. The paper claims "more than 3x coverage in the vocabulary size" but doesn't specify what the baseline vocabulary size is. Can you clarify what the baseline is (e.g., compared to which existing method's vocabulary size), and provide the exact numbers?

2. Given the limitations of similarity/relatedness tasks for evaluating semantic embeddings (as acknowledged in Section 6), have you evaluated your embeddings on other intrinsic tasks (e.g., analogy completion) or downstream NLP tasks (e.g., entity linking)? If not, how would you justify the claim that your embeddings "preserve several semantic properties"?

3. The paper states that "the source ontology structure is reflected in the organization of our embeddings" but doesn't provide sufficient evidence. How do the authors measure the degree to which the ontology structure is preserved in the embedding space, beyond the MAP analysis in Figure 4?

## Overall Assessment

This paper presents a valuable contribution to the field with a practical weakly-supervised method for learning concept embeddings that avoids the annotation bottleneck. The creation of a new dataset for real-world entity similarity/relatedness is particularly noteworthy. However, the paper's reliance on similarity/relatedness tasks without additional evidence from other evaluation methods is a significant weakness. The performance gap on smaller biomedical datasets compared to CHOI also needs better explanation.

The paper has strong potential for impact due to its practical approach to concept embedding. With additional evaluation on other tasks and better analysis of the ontology dependency, this work could be significantly strengthened. As it stands, it makes a meaningful contribution to the field, but the evaluation framework needs to be more robust to fully support the claims. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions to address the evaluation concerns and provide more detailed analysis of the method's limitations.
