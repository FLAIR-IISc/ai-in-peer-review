

# Peer Review for ACL 2017

## Summary and Contributions

This paper proposes a weakly-supervised method for jointly embedding concepts, phrases, and words using representative phrases from an ontology as distant supervision. The approach requires no human annotation of the corpus, only leveraging the existing mappings between concepts and phrases in ontologies (UMLS for biomedical concepts, YAGO for general entities). The authors evaluate their embeddings on concept similarity and relatedness tasks, demonstrating competitive performance with supervised methods while achieving significantly greater vocabulary coverage.

The main contributions, as I see them, are:

1. A novel method for jointly embedding concepts, phrases, and words using distant supervision from ontology mappings, requiring no human corpus annotation
2. A novel dataset for evaluating similarity and relatedness of real-world entities (people, places, organizations), which fills an important gap in the literature
3. Demonstration that concept embeddings trained without human annotation can capture semantic relationships comparable to supervised methods

## Strengths

1. **Significant gap addressed**: The paper tackles a fundamental problem in semantic representation learning - embedding concepts rather than just words - without requiring the expensive human annotation needed by prior approaches. While methods like Wang et al. (2014a) rely on knowledge base relations (which are often incomplete), this approach uses the more abundant phrase-concept mappings in ontologies. This is a meaningful contribution to the field.

2. **Novel evaluation dataset**: The creation of a new dataset for evaluating similarity/relatedness of real-world entities (people, places, organizations) is a valuable contribution. As the authors note, existing similarity datasets primarily focus on words, not real-world entities, making this dataset particularly useful for future work.

3. **Practical scalability**: The method achieves "more than 3x coverage in the vocabulary size" compared to supervised approaches. This is a substantial practical advantage, as it allows embedding of a much larger number of concepts without additional annotation cost, which is particularly valuable in domains like biomedicine where ontologies are large.

4. **Clear methodology**: The approach builds on well-established word2vec techniques in a straightforward way, making it easy to implement and understand. The model architecture and training procedure are well-described and logically motivated.

## Weaknesses

1. **Critical evaluation methodology flaw**: The paper tunes hyperparameters using 5-fold cross-validation on the evaluation datasets (as stated in the supplementary material). This is a major methodological error in NLP evaluation, as it constitutes data leakage from the test set into the training process. The authors acknowledge this issue but fail to provide a proper solution, which significantly undermines the validity of their results. This is a critical weakness that must be addressed.

2. **Misleading claims about performance**: The paper claims "competitive performance" with supervised methods, but on the smaller MayoSRS and Mini Mayo SRS datasets, their method is clearly outperformed by CHOI (a supervised method). Only on the larger UMNSRS datasets do they achieve better or competitive results. The paper does not sufficiently qualify this nuance, creating an overstatement of their contribution.

3. **Weak theoretical justification for design**: The authors introduce hyperparameters β and η to control compositionality, but provide only weak theoretical justification. The analysis in Section 5.4 (Figure 4) shows that concepts are not well-clustered with their representative phrases (MAP scores skewed toward 0), which contradicts the rationale for the η parameter. This undermines the paper's claim that the method captures "the source ontology structure" as suggested in the abstract.

4. **Vague claim about vocabulary coverage**: The paper states "demonstrating more than 3x coverage in the vocabulary size" without specifying what this is compared to. It's unclear whether this refers to the number of concepts covered or the overall vocabulary size, and without a clear baseline for comparison, this claim lacks impact.

## Questions to Authors

1. How did you handle the critical issue of hyperparameter tuning on the evaluation datasets? The paper states you used 5-fold cross-validation on the evaluation datasets, which constitutes data leakage. Please clarify how you avoided this in your final evaluation.

2. Why does your method underperform CHOI on the smaller MayoSRS and Mini Mayo SRS datasets, despite being competitive on larger datasets? Is this due to the smaller dataset size or a fundamental limitation of the weakly-supervised approach?

3. In your compositionality analysis (Section 5.4), you show high similarity between concepts and their average phrase embeddings (Figure 3a), but low MAP scores for concept-phrase relationships (Figure 4a). How do you reconcile these apparently contradictory findings?

4. The paper claims "more than 3x coverage in the vocabulary size" - compared to what specific baseline? Please clarify the baseline for this claim to make it meaningful.
