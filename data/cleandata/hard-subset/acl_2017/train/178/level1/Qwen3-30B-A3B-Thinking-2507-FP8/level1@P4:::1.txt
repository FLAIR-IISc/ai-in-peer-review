

# Review for ACL 2017

## Summary
This paper proposes a weakly-supervised method for jointly embedding concepts, phrases, and words using representative phrases from an ontology as distant supervision. The authors train embeddings for medical concepts (UMLS) and general-domain concepts (YAGO) from unlabeled corpora, demonstrating competitive performance on concept similarity and relatedness tasks compared to methods requiring human annotation. The main contributions are: (1) a novel weakly-supervised approach to jointly embed concepts, phrases, and words without human annotation, (2) empirical validation of the method on biomedical and general-domain concepts showing competitive performance, and (3) creation of a novel dataset for evaluating similarity and relatedness of real-world entities.

## Strengths
1. **Novel approach to concept embedding with minimal supervision**: The method's key strength is its use of representative phrases from ontologies as distant supervision, eliminating the need for human-annotated corpus data. This is a significant practical contribution as it enables scaling to much larger vocabularies (3x coverage) while maintaining competitive performance. The approach effectively bridges structured knowledge (ontologies) with distributional semantics, addressing a gap in the literature.

2. **Comprehensive empirical evaluation**: The paper provides thorough evaluation across multiple domains (biomedical and general), multiple corpora (PubMed, Clinical, WIKINYT), and multiple evaluation metrics (similarity, relatedness, semantic type clustering). The comparison against multiple baselines (including both concept-based and word-based approaches) provides strong evidence of the method's efficacy.

3. **Creation of a valuable new dataset**: The authors present two novel datasets of similarity and relatedness for real-world entities, addressing a clear gap in the literature. The dataset collection methodology (using Mechanical Turk with validation questions) is well-described, and the resulting datasets (688 pairs each for similarity and relatedness) provide a much-needed resource for future research.

## Weaknesses
1. **Limited novelty compared to prior work**: While the authors cite Wang et al. (2014a) as the closest approach, their method differs primarily in using phrase-based supervision rather than binary relations from the knowledge base. However, this distinction may not be sufficiently novel, as similar approaches have been explored in related work (e.g., Mintz et al., 2009 for relation extraction). The paper does not sufficiently establish how their approach fundamentally differs from or improves upon prior work.

2. **Modest performance gains**: The results show competitive performance but not significant improvements over existing methods. On the largest biomedical dataset (UMNSRS Similarity), the proposed method achieves 0.653 Spearman's ρ, while CHIU-30 (a word embedding baseline) achieves 0.653. This suggests the method may not provide substantial advantages over simpler approaches. The paper also doesn't demonstrate clear advantages for downstream NLP tasks beyond similarity/relatedness.

3. **Inadequate analysis of limitations**: The paper acknowledges the "noisiness" of their distant supervision approach but doesn't thoroughly analyze how this noise impacts the embeddings. There's limited discussion of cases where the method fails or where the phrase-concept mappings are problematic. The authors also don't adequately address how their method would handle concepts with no representative phrases or with ambiguous phrases.

4. **Evaluation methodology concerns**: The paper uses 5-fold cross-validation for hyperparameter tuning but doesn't specify how they handle the evaluation datasets. This risks data leakage, as they're tuning on the same data they're evaluating on. The paper also doesn't provide sufficient detail on how they handle concept polysemy, which is a known challenge in concept embedding.

## Questions to Authors
1. The paper claims "more than 3x coverage in the vocabulary size" compared to prior work. Please provide specific numbers for the vocabulary sizes of your embeddings versus prior work to clarify this claim.

2. The paper reports that concept embeddings (CONCEPTco) perform better than phrase-based approximations (PHRASEco) on biomedical datasets but worse on general-domain datasets. Can you explain why this difference exists, and what factors might predict which approach will perform better for a given domain?

3. The paper mentions that "the source ontology structure is reflected in the organization of our embeddings," but doesn't provide specific evidence for this claim. Please elaborate on how the ontology structure is reflected in the embedding space (e.g., through specific examples or quantitative measures).
