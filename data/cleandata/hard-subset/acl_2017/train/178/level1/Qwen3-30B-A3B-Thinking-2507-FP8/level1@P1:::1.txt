

# Peer Review

## Summary and Main Contributions

This paper proposes a weakly-supervised method for jointly embedding concepts, phrases, and words using distant supervision from ontology mappings. The authors replace phrases representing concepts (from UMLS and YAGO ontologies) with unique identifiers in unlabeled text corpora, then train a modified skip-gram model to jointly embed concepts, phrases, and words. The key contributions, as I see them, are:

1. A novel weakly-supervised approach for jointly embedding concepts, phrases, and words that avoids expensive manual annotation by using ontology phrase mappings as distant supervision signals.
2. A new dataset of human judgments for similarity and relatedness between real-world entities (people, places, organizations), addressing a gap in evaluation resources.
3. Demonstrated competitive performance on concept similarity/relatedness tasks without human annotation, with significantly larger vocabulary coverage (3x+) compared to existing methods.

## Strengths

1. **Novel weakly-supervised approach**: The core contribution is genuinely novel in using ontology phrase mappings as distant supervision signals to train concept embeddings without manual annotation. This addresses a significant limitation in prior work that required either expensive manual annotation or ignored textual context. The method's simplicity and effectiveness in leveraging existing structured knowledge (ontologies) to train embeddings from unannotated text is a strong technical contribution.

2. **Novel evaluation dataset**: The creation of a new dataset for similarity/relatedness evaluation of real-world entities (688 pairs for each task) fills a clear gap in the literature. Existing evaluation resources primarily focus on words or biomedical concepts, not general real-world entities. This dataset is valuable for future research in the field.

3. **Demonstrated scalability and coverage**: The paper convincingly shows that their method achieves significantly larger vocabulary coverage (more than 3x) compared to existing methods while maintaining competitive performance on similarity/relatedness tasks. This scalability without additional annotation cost is a major practical advantage.

4. **Insightful analysis of semantic structure**: The analysis of concept-phrase proximity, phrase-word compositionality, and semantic type clustering provides valuable insights into what the embeddings capture. The finding that concepts cluster by semantic type (albeit with mixed performance) demonstrates that the embeddings preserve meaningful semantic structure.

## Weaknesses

1. **Methodological flaw in evaluation**: The paper has a significant methodological flaw: hyperparameters were tuned on the test sets (as revealed in the supplemental material), which violates standard evaluation practices. The authors state they used "adapted 5-fold cross-validation" but acknowledge that "the validation numbers skew worse than the overall numbers, as the smaller dataset size in the cross-fold evaluations penalizes ranking errors more highly." This undermines the validity of their results, as the tuning process is effectively using the test data to optimize performance.

2. **Inconsistent performance on biomedical datasets**: The method is outperformed by CHOI (a human-annotated method) on the two smaller biomedical datasets (Mayo SRS and Mini Mayo SRS), which are often considered more reliable for evaluation due to higher inter-rater agreement. The paper claims "competitive performance" but doesn't adequately address why their method underperforms on these more reliable datasets.

3. **Lack of clear comparison with most relevant prior work**: The paper mentions Wang et al. (2014a) as the closest approach but doesn't sufficiently explain how their method differs or why it's superior. Wang et al. also jointly embeds entities and words but relies on knowledge base relations (which the authors acknowledge are incomplete). The paper doesn't clearly demonstrate why their distant supervision approach is better than Wang et al.'s method.

4. **Limited analysis of the method's limitations**: The paper doesn't adequately address the limitations of their approach. For example, Figure 3 shows that the compositionality between concepts and phrases is limited (mean similarity near 0.5), suggesting the embeddings might not capture meaningful semantic relationships. The authors note this but don't explore why this occurs or how it might be improved.

5. **Ambiguous "3x coverage" claim**: The paper claims "more than 3x coverage in the vocabulary size" but doesn't clearly define what this means or how it's measured. Without a clear comparison to other methods' vocabulary sizes, this claim lacks sufficient evidence.

## Questions to Authors

1. The paper states that "CHIU-2 (words only) achieved the highest correlation on the similarity task," but the results table shows CHIU-30 as the highest performer. Please clarify which word embedding model was actually used for this claim.

2. The hyperparameter tuning process appears to have used the test data (as per supplemental material), which is a serious methodological flaw. Can you confirm whether the tuning was done on the test set and how this impacts the validity of your results?

3. The paper claims "competitive performance" compared to methods that require human annotation, yet CHOI (a human-annotated method) outperforms your approach on the smaller biomedical datasets. How do you reconcile this with your claim of "competitive performance"?

4. In Figure 3, the mean cosine similarity between concepts and their average phrase embeddings is near 0.5, which suggests the embeddings don't strongly preserve the concept-phrase relationship. How do you explain this finding, and what implications does it have for the model's effectiveness?
