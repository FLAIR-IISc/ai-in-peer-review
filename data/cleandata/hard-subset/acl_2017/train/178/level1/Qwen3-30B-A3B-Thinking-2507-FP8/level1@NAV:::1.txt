

This paper proposes a weakly-supervised method for jointly embedding concepts, phrases, and words using representative phrases from ontologies as distant supervision. The main contributions, as I see them, are: (1) the creation of a novel dataset for similarity and relatedness evaluation of real-world entities (people, places, organizations), which fills a significant gap in the literature; (2) the practical demonstration that concept embeddings can be learned without human annotation while maintaining competitive performance on similarity tasks; and (3) the evidence that the model preserves ontological relationships between concepts and phrases.

Strengths: First, the dataset creation is a substantial contribution that will benefit the field, as there are no comparable resources for real-world entity similarity/relatedness. Second, the method's ability to achieve competitive performance without manual annotation is practically valuable, especially in resource-constrained domains like biomedicine. Third, the analysis showing concepts cluster with their representative phrases provides meaningful insights about the embedding space organization.

Weaknesses: First, the core method is not particularly novel—it's a straightforward adaptation of word2vec with distant supervision, similar to Wang et al. (2014a) and Mintz et al. (2009), with only minor modifications (β and η parameters). Second, the performance gains over strong baselines like CHOI are modest (only 0.05-0.07 Spearman's ρ on larger datasets), and the method is outperformed on smaller biomedical datasets. Third, the evaluation relies on the same similarity/relatedness tasks that have been criticized for poor correlation with downstream performance, and the authors acknowledge this limitation. Fourth, the hyperparameter sensitivity (e.g., η=0.0 being optimal for most corpora) suggests the method may not be robust across different settings.

Questions to Authors: How do you justify the choice of β and η values given their high sensitivity to corpus type and the lack of clear trends in their impact on performance? Could you provide additional analysis on how your embeddings perform on downstream tasks beyond similarity/relatedness evaluation?
