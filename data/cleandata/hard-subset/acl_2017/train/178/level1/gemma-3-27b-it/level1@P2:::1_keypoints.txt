- Joint embedding framework: Proposes a method to jointly learn embeddings for concepts, phrases, and words using distant supervision from representative phrases.  
- Weakly-supervised learning: Demonstrates meaningful embeddings can be learned without manual annotation, useful for specialized domains like biomedicine.  
- Novel evaluation dataset: Introduces a new general-domain entity similarity/relatedness dataset to fill gaps in existing resources.  

- Relevance and motivation: Clearly motivates the need for multi-word concept representations and variation handling.  
- Solid experimental setup: Evaluates on multiple corpora (PubMed, clinical notes, WikiNYT) and ontologies (UMLS, YAGO) with strong baselines.  
- Clear methodology: Presents the approach clearly with helpful diagrams and compositionality hyperparameters (β, η).  
- Competitive performance: Achieves strong results on similarity/relatedness tasks, especially on larger datasets (UMNSRS, new entity dataset).  
- Embedding-space analysis: Provides analyses of concept/phrase/word relationships and semantic type clustering.  

- Limited novelty: Core technique (distributional similarity with distant supervision) builds on prior work (e.g., word2vec, Mintz et al.) and novelty beyond joint embedding is not strongly emphasized.  
- Hyperparameter sensitivity: Performance appears sensitive to β, η, window size, and dimensionality without thorough sensitivity analysis.  
- Low IAA for new dataset: Fleiss’ Kappa ≈ 0.24 raises concerns about annotation reliability and evaluation robustness.  
- No downstream evaluation: Lacks experiments on downstream NLP tasks (e.g., entity linking, relation extraction) to validate practical utility.  

- What is specifically novel: Request to clarify which aspects of the framework provide novel insights beyond existing distributional semantics work.  
- Hyperparameter sensitivity details: Ask for more analysis on how β and η affect performance and in which scenarios they matter most.  
- Improving IAA: Question whether alternative annotation strategies (better instructions, pilot studies) were tried and how low IAA limits dataset use.  
- Downstream evaluation plans: Ask if the authors plan to evaluate embeddings on downstream tasks and which tasks/results they anticipate.  

- Paper quality: Well-written and clearly organized.  
- Reproducibility: Inclusion of code and dataset is commendable.  
- Bias discussion: Recommend discussing potential training-data biases and their impact on learned embeddings.
