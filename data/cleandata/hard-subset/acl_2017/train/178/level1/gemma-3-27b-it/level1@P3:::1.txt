This paper proposes a weakly-supervised method for jointly embedding concepts, phrases, and words, leveraging distant supervision from ontology phrases. The core idea is sound: utilizing existing knowledge bases (UMLS, YAGO) and unannotated text to learn meaningful representations without costly human annotation. The authors demonstrate competitive performance on concept similarity/relatedness tasks and present a novel evaluation dataset.

**Main Contributions:**

1. **Weakly-Supervised Joint Embedding Framework:** The most significant contribution is the framework itself. The method effectively bridges the gap between structured knowledge (ontologies) and distributional semantics (word embeddings) using a distant supervision approach. This is a valuable approach for domains like biomedicine where annotation is expensive.
2. **Novel Evaluation Dataset:** The creation of a new dataset for evaluating similarity and relatedness of real-world entities is a strong contribution. Existing datasets are limited, and this dataset provides a valuable resource for the community. The described data collection and filtering process appears reasonable.
3. **Empirical Validation & Analysis:** The extensive experimental evaluation on multiple corpora and ontologies, along with the analysis of embedding organization (concepts near phrases, semantic type clustering), provides strong evidence for the effectiveness of the proposed method. The ablation studies with hyperparameters offer useful insights.

**Strengths:**

1. **Addresses a Key Challenge:** The paper tackles a critical problem in NLP – representing concepts that are not easily captured by word-level embeddings – and does so without relying on expensive manual annotation.
2. **Strong Baseline Comparison:** The comparison against a wide range of existing methods, including those utilizing human annotation (De Vine, Choi, Mencia), demonstrates the competitiveness of the proposed approach.
3. **Comprehensive Evaluation:** The evaluation is well-structured, utilizing standard datasets *and* a newly created one, and covers both biomedical and general-domain scenarios.
4. **Insightful Analysis:** The analyses of embedding space organization, and the effects of hyperparameters, provide valuable insights into the model's behavior and suggest potential directions for improvement.
5. **Code Release:** The release of the cui2vec software and the new dataset enhances reproducibility and encourages further research.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Performance Gains:** While competitive, the performance gains over strong baselines (e.g., Choi et al. 2016b on biomedical datasets) are often marginal. A deeper analysis of *why* the proposed method doesn’t consistently outperform these baselines would be valuable.
2. **Greedy Phrase Matching:** The greedy phrase matching approach during preprocessing seems potentially suboptimal. It may lead to incorrect associations or miss important context. The authors acknowledge this, but a discussion of its impact on results and potential alternatives would strengthen the paper.
3. **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** The substantial hyperparameter tuning process suggests that the model is sensitive to parameter settings. A more principled method for hyperparameter selection (e.g., Bayesian optimization) might yield more robust results. The reported limited impact of hyperparameter variation, contrasted with the extensive tuning process, is slightly concerning.
4. **Kappa Scores for Dataset Reliability:** The Fleiss’ kappa scores of 0.24 for the newly created dataset are relatively low, indicating fair agreement among annotators. This raises questions about the reliability and potential biases in the dataset. Further discussion about the variability and potential limitations of the dataset would be helpful.
5. **Lack of Downstream Task Evaluation:** The evaluation focuses solely on intrinsic tasks (similarity/relatedness). Evaluating the embeddings on downstream tasks (e.g., relation extraction, entity linking) would provide a more convincing demonstration of their practical utility.

**Questions to Authors:**

1.  Could you elaborate on the limitations of the greedy phrase matching approach and discuss potential alternative methods for identifying concept mentions in the corpus?
2.  Given the substantial hyperparameter tuning process, could you provide more insight into the sensitivity of the model to different parameter settings? Were there specific parameters that had a disproportionate impact on performance?
3.  The Fleiss’ kappa scores for the new dataset are relatively low. Could you discuss the potential sources of disagreement among annotators and how this might affect the validity of the results?
4.  Do you have plans to evaluate the embeddings on downstream tasks, such as relation extraction or entity linking? If not, why?
5.  The paper mentions that the hyperparameter settings were tuned for each corpus. Was there any attempt to learn a more generalizable set of hyperparameters that could perform well across multiple corpora?

**Additional Comments:**

*   The notation throughout the paper is generally clear, but some equations could benefit from more detailed explanations.
*   The related work section is comprehensive but could be slightly more focused on directly comparable approaches.
*   The discussion of the benefits of joint embedding could be strengthened by providing examples of scenarios where it is particularly advantageous.
