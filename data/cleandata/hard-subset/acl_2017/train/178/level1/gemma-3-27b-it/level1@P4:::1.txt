This paper proposes a weakly-supervised method for jointly learning concept, phrase, and word embeddings using distant supervision from representative phrases in ontologies. The core idea is to leverage the co-occurrence of phrases representing concepts in a corpus to infer relationships between concepts, phrases and words, without relying on manual annotation.

The primary contribution is the proposed method for jointly embedding concepts, phrases, and words using distant supervision. While the idea of using ontologies for embedding is not entirely novel, the specific approach of leveraging phrase occurrences as a form of weak supervision and the joint embedding framework represent a worthwhile contribution. A secondary contribution lies in the novel datasets of similarity and relatedness for real-world entities, which could be valuable to the community. Finally, the demonstration that the learned embeddings capture some structural information from the source ontologies is a notable finding.

**Strengths:**

1. **Addressing a key challenge:** The paper tackles the challenge of learning embeddings for concepts that often lack direct lexical representations, and avoids the need for expensive manual annotation. This is a significant practical advantage, especially in domains like biomedicine where annotation is costly.
2. **Solid experimental evaluation:** The authors conduct a thorough set of experiments on both biomedical and general-domain tasks, comparing their method against several baselines, including strong existing embedding models. The inclusion of novel datasets adds to the value of the evaluation. The ablations with β and η are also well-motivated and insightful.
3. **Demonstrated effectiveness:** The results show competitive performance on concept similarity and relatedness tasks, particularly in the larger datasets. This validates the effectiveness of the proposed method. The observation that phrase approximations perform well highlights the practical utility of the approach.
4. **Analysis of embedding space:** The analysis showing that concepts and their representative phrases are embedded close to each other, and that concepts of the same semantic type cluster together, provides evidence that the embeddings capture meaningful semantic relationships.
5. **Code and data release:** The release of the code and novel datasets is commendable and will facilitate further research in this area.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited novelty of core idea:** While the specific implementation is reasonable, the core idea of leveraging existing knowledge bases (ontologies) for embedding learning is not entirely new. Related work mentions similar approaches (e.g., Wang et al., 2014a), and the paper doesn’t strongly differentiate itself in the broader landscape of knowledge-enhanced embedding methods.
2. **Sensitivity to Ontology Quality:** The method’s performance is heavily reliant on the quality of the ontology and the mappings between concepts and phrases. Errors or incompleteness in the ontology will directly impact the learned embeddings. This limitation should be more thoroughly discussed.
3. **Evaluation limitations:** Despite a comprehensive evaluation, the reliance on Spearman's rank correlation might not fully capture the nuances of semantic similarity. More sophisticated evaluation metrics, or downstream task performance, would strengthen the conclusions. The Fleiss' Kappa for the new datasets is relatively low, raising concerns about the reliability of those evaluations.
4. **Hyperparameter tuning complexity:** The hyperparameter tuning process seems somewhat ad-hoc, and the reported settings are specific to each corpus. A more systematic exploration of the hyperparameter space, or a method for automatically adapting hyperparameters to different corpora, would be valuable.
5. **Lack of error analysis:** The paper lacks a detailed error analysis. Identifying specific cases where the method fails, and analyzing the reasons for those failures, would provide insights for future improvements.

**Questions to Authors:**

1. How sensitive is the method to the quality and completeness of the ontology? Have you investigated the impact of noisy or incomplete mappings between concepts and phrases?
2. The Fleiss’ Kappa for the new datasets is 0.24. Do you believe this is sufficient for reliable evaluation? What steps were taken to ensure data quality and reliability?
3. Could you provide a more detailed explanation of the hyperparameter tuning process? Was a grid search or other optimization method used?
4. Can you provide examples of concept pairs where the method performs poorly, and explain why?
5.  How does the performance of the method change when applied to different languages or domains?
