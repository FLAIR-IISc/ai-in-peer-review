- Core idea: Train joint embeddings for concepts, phrases, and words using representative phrases from ontologies (UMLS, YAGO) as distant supervision in a modified skip-gram model.

- Primary contribution: Demonstrates practical learning of effective concept embeddings without expensive human annotation by leveraging distant supervision.

- Secondary contribution: Introduces new datasets for evaluating semantic similarity and relatedness of real-world entities.

- Strength: Practicality and scalability—avoids manual annotation and scales to much larger vocabularies (reported 3x coverage).

- Strength: Competitive performance—achieves results on concept similarity/relatedness comparable to methods using human annotation.

- Strength: Novel dataset contribution—fills a gap in evaluation resources for general-domain entity similarity/relatedness.

- Strength: Thorough experimentation—evaluated across multiple corpora/ontologies with solid ablation and hyperparameter studies.

- Weakness: Limited novelty—the core skip-gram with negative sampling is well-established; novelty is mainly the distant supervision application.

- Weakness: Dependence on ontology quality—embedding quality is tied to ontology completeness and correctness, which can limit performance.

- Weakness: Compositionality analysis unclear—the interpretation of MAP scores and conclusions about capturing compositionality need clarification.

- Question: How sensitive is performance to the choice/selection strategy of representative phrases for a concept?

- Question: Does the greedy phrase replacement during preprocessing introduce biases or ambiguities, and can the authors elaborate on its limitations?

- Question: What measures can mitigate the impact of noisy or incomplete ontologies on the learned embeddings?
