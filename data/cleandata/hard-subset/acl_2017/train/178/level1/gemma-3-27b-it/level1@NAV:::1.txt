## Review of "A Weakly-Supervised Method for Jointly Embedding Concepts, Phrases, and Words"

This paper proposes a weakly-supervised method to learn joint embeddings for concepts, phrases, and words, leveraging representative phrases as distant supervision from ontologies like UMLS and YAGO. The core idea is to train embeddings by considering occurrences of known phrases as indicators of associated concepts, utilizing a modified skip-gram model.

The primary contribution of this work lies in the practical demonstration of learning reasonably effective concept embeddings *without* requiring expensive human annotation. While the core embedding technique itself isn't entirely novel, the application to jointly embedding concepts and phrases using distant supervision, and achieving competitive performance against manually annotated baselines, is a significant step. A second, valuable contribution is the introduction of new datasets for evaluating semantic similarity and relatedness of real-world entities, filling a gap in the available evaluation resources.

**Strengths:**

1. **Practicality and Scalability:** The proposed method avoids the bottleneck of manual annotation, enabling scaling to large vocabularies (3x coverage reported) which is crucial for real-world applications. This is a clear advantage over methods relying on human-labeled data.
2. **Competitive Performance:** The method achieves competitive results on concept similarity and relatedness tasks compared to existing approaches, particularly those requiring human annotation. This indicates the effectiveness of the distant supervision approach.
3. **Novel Dataset Contribution:** The inclusion of newly created datasets for evaluating general-domain entity similarity/relatedness addresses a significant lack in the field and provides valuable resources for future research. 
4. **Thorough Experimentation:** The authors conduct experiments on multiple corpora and ontologies, demonstrating the robustness of their approach and providing insights into the impact of different settings. The ablation studies with hyperparameters are also well performed.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Novelty of Core Technique:** The underlying skip-gram model with negative sampling is well-established. The primary novelty lies in the application to this specific problem and the distant supervision strategy, but the fundamental algorithmic contribution is incremental.
2. **Dependence on Ontology Quality:** The performance is inherently tied to the quality and coverage of the chosen ontologies (UMLS, YAGO). Errors or incompleteness in these ontologies will directly impact embedding quality. This limitation should be explicitly discussed.
3. **Compositionality Analysis Needs Clarification:** While the analysis of embedding similarity and compositionality is interesting, the interpretation of results (e.g., MAP scores) could be more nuanced and connected to specific linguistic phenomena. It's unclear what conclusions can be drawn about the model's ability to capture concept compositionality. 

**Questions to Authors:**

1.  How sensitive is the performance to the choice of representative phrases for a concept? Have you explored different selection strategies for these phrases and their impact on the learned embeddings?
2.  Could you elaborate on the potential limitations introduced by the "greedy" phrase replacement during preprocessing? Does this introduce any biases or ambiguities?
3.  Given the reliance on ontology quality, what steps could be taken to mitigate the impact of noisy or incomplete ontologies on the learned embeddings?
