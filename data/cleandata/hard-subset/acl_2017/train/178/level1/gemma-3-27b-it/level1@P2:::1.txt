This paper proposes a weakly-supervised method for jointly embedding concepts, phrases, and words, leveraging representative phrases for ontology concepts as distant supervision. The core idea – using co-occurrence statistics of concept phrases in unlabeled text to learn embeddings – is sound and addresses a relevant problem in semantic representation learning, particularly in domains like biomedicine where comprehensive annotation is expensive.

**Contributions:**

1. **Joint Embedding Framework:** The most significant contribution is the framework itself for simultaneously learning embeddings for concepts, phrases, and words using distant supervision. This is a valuable approach, as it inherently links different levels of granularity in semantic representation.
2. **Weakly-Supervised Learning:** The method successfully demonstrates that meaningful embeddings can be learned *without* manual annotation. This is particularly impactful given the cost of annotating concepts in specialized domains like medicine.
3. **Novel Evaluation Dataset:** The creation of a new dataset for evaluating general-domain entity similarity and relatedness is a worthwhile addition, as existing datasets are limited in this area.

**Strengths:**

1. **Relevance and Motivation:** The problem addressed is well-motivated. The need for semantic representations that can handle multi-word concepts and variations in expression is clear, and the paper effectively establishes this need in the introduction.
2. **Solid Experimental Setup:** The authors conduct experiments on multiple corpora (PubMed, Clinical notes, WikiNYT) and ontologies (UMLS, YAGO), demonstrating the generality of their approach. They also compare against several strong baselines, including both existing embedding methods and state-of-the-art biomedical concept embedding approaches.
3. **Clear Methodology:** The method is presented in a clear and understandable manner, with a helpful diagram illustrating the relationships between the different embedding matrices. The inclusion of hyperparameters (β and η) for controlling compositionality is a sensible addition.
4. **Competitive Performance:** The method achieves competitive performance on concept similarity and relatedness tasks, especially considering the lack of manual annotation. The results on the larger datasets (UMNSRS, the new entity dataset) are particularly encouraging.
5. **Analysis of Embedding Space:** The analysis of the embedding space (e.g., examining the relationship between concepts, phrases, and words; assessing semantic type clustering) provides valuable insights into the learned representations.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Novelty of Core Technique:** While the *combination* of techniques is reasonable, the core idea of using distributional similarity with distant supervision isn't entirely novel. The method builds on established techniques like word2vec and Mintz et al. (2009).  The paper doesn't sufficiently highlight what is truly novel beyond the specific application and joint embedding aspect.
2. **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** The tuning of hyperparameters (β, η, window size, dimensionality) seems crucial for performance. The paper acknowledges this, but a more detailed analysis of the sensitivity to these parameters and the rationale behind the chosen settings would be beneficial.
3. **IAA of New Dataset:** The Fleiss' Kappa for the newly created similarity/relatedness dataset is relatively low (0.24), indicating fair agreement between annotators. While the authors discuss the filtering process, the potential impact of low inter-annotator agreement on the reliability of the evaluation results should be further addressed.  A more robust evaluation with higher agreement is desirable.
4. **Lack of Downstream Task Evaluation:** The evaluation is limited to intrinsic tasks (similarity/relatedness). Demonstrating the effectiveness of the learned embeddings on a downstream NLP task (e.g., entity linking, relation extraction) would provide stronger evidence of their quality.




**Questions to Authors:**

1.  Given that the core idea of distant supervision is not new, can you elaborate on *specifically* what aspects of your framework contribute novel insights or advancements compared to existing work in distributional semantics?
2.  Could you provide more detail on the sensitivity analysis of the hyperparameters β and η? Were there specific scenarios where these parameters significantly impacted performance, and why?
3.   Regarding the new dataset, did you explore alternative methods for improving inter-annotator agreement (e.g., more detailed instructions, pilot studies)? Could you discuss the potential limitations of using this dataset for evaluating the embeddings, given the relatively low IAA?
4.  Do you have any plans to evaluate the learned embeddings on a downstream NLP task? If so, which task(s) and what results do you anticipate?



**Additional Comments:**

*   The paper is well-written and clearly organized.
*   The inclusion of the code and dataset is commendable.
*   Consider adding a discussion of potential biases in the training data and how they might affect the learned embeddings.
