This paper proposes a weakly-supervised method for jointly embedding concepts, phrases, and words by leveraging representative phrases for ontology concepts as distant supervision. The core idea is to train embeddings using the contexts of concept phrases in an unlabeled corpus, effectively bridging the gap between lexical forms and abstract concepts. The authors evaluate their approach on biomedical and general-domain datasets, demonstrating competitive performance on concept similarity and relatedness tasks.

**Main Contributions:**

1. **Weakly-Supervised Joint Embedding:** The most significant contribution is the development of a method for jointly learning embeddings for concepts, phrases, and words without relying on manual annotations. This is achieved by cleverly utilizing existing ontology mappings to create distant supervision signals from unlabeled text.
2. **Scalability and Vocabulary Coverage:** The approach exhibits scalability, allowing the creation of embeddings for a significantly larger vocabulary compared to methods requiring manual annotation (3x coverage reported). This is a practical benefit, particularly for large-scale knowledge bases.
3. **Empirical Validation:** The authors provide a comprehensive empirical evaluation on both biomedical and general-domain tasks, demonstrating that the proposed method achieves competitive performance against existing approaches, including those relying on human annotation. The creation of a novel dataset for general-domain entity similarity and relatedness is also valuable.

**Strengths:**

1. **Novelty of the Approach:**  The method of using representative phrases as distant supervision for concept embeddings is a creative and well-motivated approach. It addresses a key limitation of prior work that required expensive annotation or ignored contextual information.
2. **Strong Empirical Results:** The experimental results on established biomedical benchmarks (MayoSRS, UMNSRS) demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed method. Achieving competitive performance without manual labels is a significant achievement. The results on the newly created general-domain entity datasets are also encouraging.
3. **Practical Significance:** The scalability of the approach and the ability to handle large vocabularies make it practical for real-world applications involving extensive knowledge bases. This is a significant advantage over methods limited by annotation costs.
4. **Thorough Evaluation:** The authors conduct a thorough evaluation, comparing their method against several baselines and ablations, and analyzing the characteristics of the learned embeddings (e.g., semantic type clustering).  The hyperparameter tuning is also well-described.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Dependence on Ontology Quality:** The performance of the method heavily relies on the quality and completeness of the ontology used for mapping concepts to phrases. If the ontology has limited coverage or inaccurate mappings, the resulting embeddings will be suboptimal.  This dependency is not fully addressed in the paper.
2. **Limited Analysis of Negative Cases:** While the authors show that concepts and phrases are often close in the embedding space, they do not provide much analysis of *why* the method fails in certain cases. Understanding the types of errors made could provide insights for improving the approach.
3. **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** The results show that performance is sensitive to hyperparameter settings. While the authors conduct hyperparameter tuning, the optimal settings vary across corpora, suggesting the method may not generalize easily without careful tuning for each specific domain.
4. **Lack of Downstream Task Evaluation:** The evaluation focuses on intrinsic tasks (similarity and relatedness).  Demonstrating the benefits of the learned embeddings on downstream NLP tasks (e.g., entity linking, relation extraction) would further strengthen the paper.



**Questions to Authors:**

1. How robust is the method to noisy or incomplete ontology mappings? Could you provide a sensitivity analysis to show the impact of different levels of ontology quality on the resulting embeddings?
2. Can you provide examples of concept-phrase pairs where the method performs poorly and discuss the potential reasons for these failures? 
3.  The β and η parameters seem crucial for performance. Can you elaborate on the intuition behind these parameters and provide guidelines for selecting appropriate values for different datasets?
4.  Have you explored alternative strategies for handling the many-to-many relationship between concepts and phrases beyond simply averaging embeddings? For example, could you use attention mechanisms to weigh different phrases based on their relevance to a given concept?

**Additional Comments:**

*   The notation and mathematical formulation are clear and well-presented.
*   The related work section provides a good overview of the existing literature.
*   The inclusion of the code and the novel dataset is a valuable contribution to the community.
*   The phrase "distant supervision" is used throughout. It might be beneficial to briefly define this term for readers less familiar with the technique.
