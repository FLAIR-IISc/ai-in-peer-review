## Review of "A Weakly-Supervised Method for Jointly Embedding Concepts, Phrases, and Words"

This paper presents a weakly-supervised method for learning joint embeddings of concepts, phrases, and words, leveraging representative phrases from ontologies as distant supervision. The core idea of utilizing phrase occurrences to train concept embeddings without manual annotation is promising, particularly given the scalability benefits. 

The primary contribution of this work lies in the proposed training methodology. By adapting the skip-gram model with negative sampling and introducing hyperparameters to control compositionality, the authors demonstrate a viable approach to learning concept embeddings from unlabeled text. The experimental validation on biomedical and general-domain datasets, showing competitive performance against existing methods (some requiring human annotation), is a significant achievement. A secondary contribution is the release of a novel dataset for evaluating similarity and relatedness of real-world entities, which addresses a gap in existing resources.

**Strengths:**

1. **Novelty of the Approach:** The method of using representative phrases as distant supervision for concept embedding is a clever and practical solution to the annotation bottleneck. It allows for scaling to larger vocabularies and ontologies without the cost of manual labeling.
2. **Competitive Performance:** The results demonstrate that the proposed method achieves performance comparable to, and in some cases exceeding, existing methods, even those relying on human annotations. This is particularly noteworthy given the weakly-supervised nature of the approach.
3. **Scalability & Vocabulary Coverage:** The method boasts significantly larger vocabulary coverage (3x) compared to existing methods, highlighting its potential for broader applications. This is a crucial advantage for real-world scenarios with extensive ontologies.
4. **New Dataset:** The introduction of a new dataset for evaluating real-world entity similarity and relatedness is a valuable contribution to the field, providing a benchmark for future research.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Analysis of Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** While the paper mentions hyperparameter tuning, the analysis lacks depth. A more detailed discussion of the impact of β and η on performance, and the rationale behind the chosen values, would strengthen the findings.
2. **Evaluation Primarily on Correlation:** The reliance on Spearman’s rank correlation as the primary evaluation metric is limiting. While informative, it doesn’t fully capture the nuances of semantic relationships. Exploring other metrics, such as precision/recall or downstream task performance, would provide a more comprehensive assessment.
3. **Potential for Bias from Ontology Quality:** The quality of the representative phrases within the ontologies directly impacts the learned embeddings. The paper doesn’t sufficiently address the potential for bias introduced by imperfect or ambiguous phrase mappings.

**Questions to Authors:**

1. How sensitive are the results to the choice of representative phrases within the ontologies? Did you explore different strategies for selecting representative phrases, and if so, what impact did they have on performance?
2. Could you elaborate on the rationale behind the chosen values for the β and η hyperparameters? Were any ablation studies performed to assess the individual contribution of each parameter?
3. Do you foresee any challenges in applying this method to languages other than English, and if so, how might those challenges be addressed?
