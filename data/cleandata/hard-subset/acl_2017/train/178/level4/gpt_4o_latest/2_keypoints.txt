- Proposes a model embedding words, atomic phrases, and concepts using an ontology linking concepts to phrases and a skip-gram variant for training.  
- Evaluated on concept similarity and relatedness tasks using UMLS and Yago ontologies.  
- Addresses the important problem that semantically similar phrases may not be lexically similar and that many phrases are non-compositional.  
- Presents a reasonable approach for training phrase embeddings with reported competitive or superior performance on some concept-similarity tasks.  
- Provides software that could be useful for biomedical NLP researchers.  
- Main limitation: limited novelty—model is essentially a modified skip-gram.  
- Full model does not consistently beat baselines; Choi baseline outperforms on Mayo datasets and the model is only competitive with simpler prior methods (e.g., Chiu) on UMNSRS.  
- Calling phrase linkage "distant supervision" is misleading because training appears to use phrase occurrences as contexts rather than true supervised concept prediction.  
- Notation introduced (e.g., E_W) is unused elsewhere, reducing clarity and utility.  
- Using a single global parameter β to control phrase compositionality is questionable; cross-validated β values of zero for some corpora contradict the paper’s premise.  
- Experimental setup for Table 4 is unclear—how phrase/word vectors are used for similarity when concept↔phrase mapping is many-to-many needs clarification.  
- The approximate-phr method (averaging phrase vectors) performs best in Table 5, calling into question the necessity of the concept ontology versus starting from a seed phrase set.
