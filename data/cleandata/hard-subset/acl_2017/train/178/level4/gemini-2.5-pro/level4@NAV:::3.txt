This paper introduces a method for creating joint vector representations for words, phrases, and concepts. The approach leverages both plain text and a hand-crafted ontology where concepts are defined by a set of phrases. The authors demonstrate their model in two domains: the medical field, using the UMLS ontology, and a general domain, with the YAGO ontology. For evaluation, the model is compared against baseline methods and previous work, primarily on intrinsic similarity and relatedness tasks. While existing medical benchmarks are used, the authors also contribute a new general-domain concept similarity dataset, created via crowdsourcing, which they plan to release. The reported performance is on par with that of prior methods.

Strengths:
- The proposed joint embedding model is intuitive and well-motivated. Its primary strength lies in its ability to find a configurable balance between treating phrases as single, atomic units and as compositional structures derived from their constituent words. This same principle is effectively extended to concepts, which are modeled as compositions of their various representative phrases.
- The manuscript represents a substantial body of work. This includes the core model development, the creation and contribution of a new evaluation resource, and the execution of a comprehensive set of experiments and analyses.

Weaknesses:
- The evaluation is confined entirely to intrinsic tasks, such as word and concept similarity benchmarks. It is widely acknowledged that performance on these tasks has limited correlation with an embedding's utility in downstream applications. Consequently, it has become standard practice to include at least one extrinsic task when evaluating new embedding models, an element which is missing here.
- The similarity and relatedness datasets are framed as capturing human judgments on *concept* similarity. However, my understanding is that the annotators were presented with *phrases*. If so, these datasets measure phrase similarity, not concept similarity, and the analysis should reflect this distinction.
- The medical evaluation benchmarks have significant limitations. The 'mini MayoSRS' dataset is exceptionally small (29 pairs), while its larger counterpart 'MayoSRS' (101 pairs) is also small and has reported issues with low inter-annotator agreement. The 'UMNSRS' dataset, while larger, is restricted to single-word concepts presented as such to annotators. This fact, which should be explicitly stated, calls into question the dataset's relevance for evaluating the representation of multi-word phrases and complex concepts central to this work.
- A critical methodological issue is that the authors extensively tune their model's hyperparameters on the same datasets used for reporting final results and for comparison with prior work. This practice invalidates the reported results and analyses.
- The authors claim superiority over prior work by achieving comparable results with less manual annotation. This argument is not compelling because the proposed method itself depends on large, manually constructed ontologies. Furthermore, the annotation in the cited prior work was derived from existing clinical records and did not require a dedicated annotation effort.
- The paper lacks deeper analysis and insight into the model's behavior. The relationship between a phrase and its words is treated analogously to that between a concept and its phrases, yet these are fundamentally different compositional types that merit separate investigation. For example, the analysis around line 588 would be more insightful with a linguistic examination of phrase composition (e.g., the role of headwords). The work would also be significantly strengthened by an analysis of how the hyperparameters that control the tradeoff between atomic and compositional representations influence the resulting embeddings.

General Discussion:
In light of the significant weaknesses detailed above, particularly those concerning the evaluation datasets and methodology, I recommend rejection. I encourage the authors to thoroughly revise their evaluation framework before considering a resubmission.
