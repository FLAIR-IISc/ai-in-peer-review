This paper presents an extension of word embedding methods designed to generate representations for multi-word phrases and the concepts they represent. The proposed method works by first assigning a unique identifier to a set of words and phrases that denote the same concept, with these sets being sourced from an ontology. It then replaces all occurrences of these words and phrases in a training corpus with their corresponding identifier to create a "tagged" corpus. This tagged corpus is then appended to the original corpus, and the combined text is used to train the embeddings. The experiments are situated in the biomedical domain, utilizing relevant corpora and ontologies. The authors also report the creation of a novel test dataset for evaluating similarity and relatedness between real-world entities, which is a valuable contribution.

Overall, the paper is well-written. The proposed technique is intuitive and straightforward, though its technical contribution is arguably modest. The scope of the findings is also limited by the evaluation being confined to the biomedical domain. The paper could be strengthened by a more thorough discussion of the newly generated test resource, as this dataset may prove to be the most significant contribution of this work. I have identified one minor technical issue, which likely pertains to the mathematical formulation rather than the implementation itself.

**Technical problem:**
In Equation 8, the authors aim to define the Mean Average Precision (MAP) calculation. While using MAP is a reasonable choice, I suggest that a natural cut-off could be used for the ranking instead of considering the entire vocabulary. More importantly, the formula presented in Equation 8 does not define a probability distribution. It can be easily shown that the expression does not sum to one. Therefore, the authors should revise the explanation to remove the claim that this equation represents a probability.
