This paper presents a method for extending word embeddings to represent not only words but also the phrases and concepts they denote. The approach leverages an ontology to identify groups of words and phrases that refer to the same concept. Each group is assigned a unique identifier, which then replaces all corresponding word and phrase occurrences in the training corpus to create a "tagged" version. This tagged corpus is appended to the original, and an embedding model is trained on the combined text. The method is applied to the biomedical domain, using relevant corpora and ontologies. The authors also report on the creation of a novel test dataset for evaluating similarity and relatedness for real-world entities.

Overall, the paper is well-written and the proposed technique is intuitive. However, the technical contribution appears somewhat incremental. The scope of the contribution is also constrained by its evaluation, which is focused exclusively on the biomedical domain.

The new test resource is a valuable contribution, and it would be beneficial to include more discussion about its generation and potential uses. Indeed, this resource may represent the paper's most significant and lasting contribution.

There is one minor technical issue, which likely pertains to the mathematical formulation rather than the implementation itself.

**Technical Problem:**
Regarding Eq. 8, which defines the MAP calculation: while evaluating against the entire vocabulary is one approach, a natural cut-off could be considered instead. More importantly, Equation 8, as written, does not define a valid probability distribution. This can be readily demonstrated, even for an infinite vocabulary. Consequently, the accompanying explanation should be revised to remove any claims that this equation represents a probability.
