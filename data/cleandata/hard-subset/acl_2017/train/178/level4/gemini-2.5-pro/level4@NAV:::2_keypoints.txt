- Proposes embeddings for words, phrases, and concepts using a concept ontology linking concepts to phrase sets treated as atomic units.  
- Trains embeddings with a method similar to skip-gram by using identified phrases in corpora as indivisible tokens.  
- Evaluates on concept similarity/relatedness tasks using UMLS and Yago ontologies.  
- Strength: Addresses non-compositional phrases and semantically related phrases lacking lexical overlap.  
- Strength: Produces competitive or superior embeddings on concept similarity tasks.  
- Strength: Releases software useful for biomedical NLP researchers.  
- Weakness: Limited novelty—model is largely an adaptation of skip-gram.  
- Weakness: Does not consistently achieve top performance; Choi baseline outperforms on Mayo datasets and rivals on UMNSRS.  
- Terminology concern: Calling known phrases “distant supervision” is unclear/misleading since training appears unsupervised rather than a predictive supervised task.  
- Presentation issue: Notation introduced in Section 3.2 (e.g., E_W) is unused later and seems superfluous.  
- Methodological concern: Global hyperparameter β for phrase compositionality is unrealistic, and cross-validated β values (often zero) are counterintuitive.  
- Experimental clarity: Table 4 setup is unclear given many-to-many concept–phrase mappings and how phrase/word vectors contribute to concept similarity.  
- Concept necessity questioned: Table 5’s best result from averaging phrase vectors ('approximate phr') suggests comparable results might be obtained without the concept ontology, using seed phrases instead.
