### **Summary**

This paper presents a method for jointly learning vector space embeddings for words, phrases, and concepts. The core idea is to leverage an ontology that maps concepts to a set of corresponding phrases. Occurrences of these phrases are identified in a large text corpus and treated as single, atomic tokens. The model then employs an extension of the skip-gram architecture to train embeddings for words, these atomic phrases, and the concepts they represent. The proposed approach is evaluated on standard concept similarity and relatedness tasks, using UMLS and Yago as the source ontologies.

### **Strengths**

1.  The paper addresses the important and challenging problem of learning representations for multi-word phrases. It correctly identifies that many phrases are non-compositional and that semantically related phrases may not be lexically similar. The proposed method of leveraging an ontology to identify and embed such phrases as atomic units is an intuitive and plausible approach to this problem.

2.  The empirical results show that the learned embeddings are competitive with, and in some cases superior to, previous methods on the task of measuring concept similarity.

3.  The authors' decision to release their software is a welcome contribution to the community, and it could be particularly useful for researchers in the biomedical NLP domain who frequently work with ontologies like UMLS.

### **Weaknesses**

1.  A significant weakness is that the full proposed model does not consistently outperform simpler baselines in the paper's own experiments. For instance, in Table 4, the Choi et al. baseline achieves substantially better results on both Mayo datasets. A similar trend is visible in Table 6, where the proposed model is, at best, competitive with the simpler Chiu et al. model on the larger UMNSRS datasets. This raises questions about the added value of the model's complexity.

2.  The technical novelty of the model is limited. The method is presented as a direct extension of the skip-gram architecture, adapting it to handle pre-identified phrases and concepts. While the application is novel, the core learning mechanism is well-established.

3.  The results in Table 5 seem to undermine the rationale for the proposed concept embedding method. The best-performing variant ("approx phr") is a simpler approach that approximates concept vectors by averaging the embeddings of their constituent phrases. This suggests that one might achieve comparable or better results simply by identifying a seed set of phrases and ignoring the concept-level modeling during training, which questions the necessity of the joint concept learning framework.

4.  The modeling of phrase compositionality via a single global hyperparameter, β, is a questionable design choice, as it assumes a uniform degree of compositionality across all phrases. More problematically, the cross-validated results in Table 3 show that the optimal value for β is often zero (for PM+CL and WikiNYT). This effectively discards the compositional component of the model, contradicting one of the paper's key motivations.

### **Detailed Comments and Questions for the Authors**

1.  **Clarity on "Distant Supervision":** The paper repeatedly refers to its use of known phrases as "distant supervision." This terminology could be misleading. In NLP, distant supervision typically refers to the process of creating noisy labeled data for a supervised predictive task (e.g., relation extraction). Here, the ontology appears to be used to define tokens and provide a shared context for what is fundamentally an unsupervised embedding algorithm. Please clarify the reasoning for this terminological choice or consider using a more precise description.

2.  **Experimental Setup for Table 4:** The evaluation protocol for concept similarity in Table 4 needs further explanation. Given the many-to-many mapping between concepts and phrases, it is unclear how the word and phrase embeddings are used to calculate the similarity between two concepts. For instance, is a concept's similarity score derived from its dedicated concept vector, or is it an aggregation (e.g., average) of the vectors of its associated phrases? A more detailed description of this step is necessary to fully interpret the results.

3.  **Unused Notation:** The notation introduced at the beginning of Section 3.2 (e.g., E_W, V_W) does not appear to be used in the subsequent equations or text. For clarity and consistency, this notation should either be used or removed.
