**Summary**

This paper proposes a model for jointly embedding words, phrases, and concepts into a common vector space. The method leverages an ontology that maps concepts to a set of corresponding phrases. These phrases are then identified in a text corpus and treated as single atomic units. Using this framework, the model employs an approach analogous to the skip-gram method to train embeddings for words, the newly atomic phrases, and their associated concepts. The proposed model is evaluated on concept similarity and relatedness tasks, using UMLS and Yago as the source ontologies.

**Strengths**

The paper addresses the important and challenging problem that semantically similar phrases are not always lexically similar, and that many phrases are non-compositional. The model proposed to learn phrase embeddings is a plausible approach to tackling this issue. On the task of scoring concept similarity, the resulting embeddings are shown to be competitive with, and in some cases superior to, previous methods.

The accompanying software release could be a useful resource for the biomedical NLP community.

**Weaknesses**

The primary weakness of this work is the model's limited novelty, as it is largely an adaptation of the skip-gram method.

Furthermore, the full model proposed by the authors does not achieve the best performance in their own experiments. In Table 4, for instance, the Choi et al. baseline is substantially better on both Mayo datasets. A similar trend is observable in Table 6. On the larger UMNSRS dataset, the proposed model is, at best, competitive with simpler prior models like that of Chiu et al.

**General Discussion**

*   The paper describes its method as using "distant supervision," but this terminology is potentially misleading. As I understand the model, it uses the co-occurrence of a concept's associated phrases with context words to train embeddings. This is not supervision in the traditional machine learning sense (e.g., for a predictive task like classifying concepts in text), which makes the choice of term confusing.

*   The notation introduced in Section 3.2 (e.g., E_W) is not subsequently used in the paper, making its inclusion superfluous.

*   The use of a single global hyperparameter, β, to control the compositionality of all phrases is a questionable modeling choice, as it implies that all phrases share the same degree of compositionality. More surprisingly, the optimal values for β found via cross-validation are zero for two of the three corpora (Table 3). A β value of zero forces the model to treat all phrases as entirely non-compositional, which seems to contradict the paper's motivation for including a compositional component.

*   The experimental setup for Table 4 requires clarification. The task is to compute similarity between concepts, yet several of the compared models only produce word and phrase embeddings. Given the many-to-many mapping between concepts and phrases, it is unclear how these word or phrase vectors are aggregated to produce a single similarity score for a concept pair. It would seem that only models which produce explicit concept vectors can be directly applied to this task.

*   In Table 5, the best-performing method is "approximate phr," which represents a concept vector as the average of its associated phrase embeddings. This result raises a significant question about the overall contribution. If a simple average of phrase vectors performs best, it undermines the necessity of jointly training explicit concept vectors using the ontology. This suggests that one might achieve similar or better results by starting with a curated list of phrases and forgoing the ontological structure altogether.
