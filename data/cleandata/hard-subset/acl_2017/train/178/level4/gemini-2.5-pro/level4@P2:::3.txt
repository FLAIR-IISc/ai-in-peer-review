**Summary**

This paper proposes a weakly-supervised method to jointly learn embeddings for words, multi-word phrases, and ontology concepts within a single vector space. The model leverages an existing ontology (mapping concepts to their textual phrases) to provide distant supervision, applying the skip-gram objective to an unlabeled text corpus. The method is evaluated in the biomedical domain using the UMLS ontology and in the general domain using YAGO. The authors report that their embeddings achieve competitive performance on concept similarity and relatedness tasks compared to prior methods, some of which require more direct supervision.

As I see them, the main contributions of this work are:

1.  A straightforward and adaptable model for jointly learning representations for concepts, phrases, and words from unannotated text. A key feature is the introduction of hyperparameters that explicitly control the trade-off between treating phrases/concepts as atomic units versus compositions of their constituent parts (words/phrases).
2.  The creation and release of a new crowdsourced dataset for evaluating the similarity and relatedness of general-domain entities (people, places, organizations). This is a valuable resource for future research in concept and entity representation.

**Strengths**

1.  The proposed model offers an elegant and principled approach to a challenging problem. By representing concepts, phrases, and words in a shared space, it can capture semantic relationships that word-only models miss. The use of hyperparameters (β and η) to balance compositional and non-compositional semantics is a thoughtful design choice, allowing the model to be adapted to different types of concepts and phrases (e.g., idiomatic vs. literal).
2.  The paper demonstrates a substantial amount of work. The authors apply their method to two distinct domains, use multiple large-scale corpora, conduct a series of intrinsic evaluations, and contribute a new evaluation dataset. The inclusion of various baselines and analyses of the resulting vector space adds to the paper's thoroughness.

**Weaknesses**

1.  **Flawed Evaluation Methodology:** The most critical weakness is that the hyperparameters were tuned directly on the test sets. While the authors use a cross-validation scheme (Appendix A.1), they acknowledge this is performed on the full evaluation datasets, stating "we are still tuning on the test set." This practice invalidates the reported results and makes the comparisons with prior work unreliable, as the model's performance is likely overestimated. For the results to be credible, tuning should be performed on a separate development set.
2.  **Lack of Extrinsic Evaluation:** The evaluation is confined to intrinsic similarity and relatedness tasks. As the authors themselves note, and as has been widely discussed in the community (e.g., Chiu et al., 2016b; Faruqui et al., 2016), performance on these tasks often fails to predict a model's utility in downstream NLP applications (e.g., entity linking, relation extraction, document classification). The absence of even one extrinsic task makes it difficult to assess the practical value of the proposed embeddings.
3.  **Limitations of the Evaluation Datasets:** The datasets used for evaluation have several issues that weaken the conclusions drawn.
    *   The primary medical datasets (MayoSRS and its subset) are very small (101 and 29 pairs, respectively), and the larger set is known to have low inter-annotator agreement. Drawing strong conclusions from such limited data is problematic.
    *   The UMNSRS datasets, while larger, consist only of single-word concepts. Using these to evaluate a model whose main novelty lies in handling multi-word phrases and concepts is a significant mismatch.
    *   There is a potential mismatch in what is being evaluated. The tasks are framed as "concept similarity," but human judgments were collected by presenting annotators with textual phrases. It is more accurate to consider these as "phrase similarity" tasks, and the analysis should reflect this distinction.
4.  **Overstated Contribution Regarding Supervision:** The paper claims an advantage over prior work by avoiding expensive manual annotation. However, this argument is not entirely convincing. First, the proposed method relies heavily on large, manually-constructed ontologies (UMLS, YAGO), which represent a significant human effort. Second, the "manually annotated" data used by a key baseline (Choi et al., 2016b) comes from existing clinical billing codes, which were not created specifically for the purpose of training embeddings. Therefore, the claim of being significantly "cheaper" is debatable.
5.  **Superficial Analysis:** The paper presents several analyses (e.g., compositionality, semantic type clustering), but these lack depth and fail to provide strong insights. For instance, the analysis of phrase-word compositionality (Figure 4b) reports a bimodal distribution but offers no linguistic explanation for why some phrases are close to their component words while others are not. A deeper dive, perhaps examining the effect of headwords or idiomaticity, would be more illuminating. Similarly, the paper misses an opportunity to analyze the impact of the key hyperparameters (β and η) on the model's behavior beyond their effect on the final similarity score.

Given these significant weaknesses, particularly the flawed evaluation methodology, I recommend rejecting this submission. The core idea is promising, but the work requires a more rigorous evaluation and deeper analysis before it is ready for publication.

**Questions to Authors**

1.  The preprocessing step (Section 3.1) mentions that phrase replacement is done "greedily over the number of tokens matched." Could you clarify how this handles nested or overlapping phrases (e.g., "New York" within "New York City") and discuss the potential impact of this strategy on the learned representations?
2.  In Section 3.2.1, the loss calculation is described with respect to "the observed phrase just completed." This phrasing is slightly ambiguous. For word embedding training, is the loss calculated for every word in the corpus (as in standard word2vec), or only for words immediately preceding/following a recognized phrase?
3.  For hyperparameter tuning (Appendix A.1), which specific similarity or relatedness dataset/score was used as the optimization objective? Was it an average across all evaluation datasets for a given corpus, or a single specific one?

**Additional Comments**

*   In Section 1, the phrase "representations for contexts, phrases, and words" appears to contain a typo. Given the model description, it should likely be "concepts, phrases, and words."
*   The terminology for the context embedding matrix, ENS, as the "negative sampling matrix" is confusing, since these embeddings are also used for positive context words. Standard terminology like "context" or "output" matrix would be clearer.
*   The notation in Equation 1, `P(D=1|w, c)`, uses `c` for the context word, but the surrounding text and subsequent equations use `o`. This should be made consistent.
*   In Section 5.1, the term "representative strings" is used, while "phrases" is used elsewhere. Using consistent terminology would improve clarity.
*   There is a missing reference for Pedersen et al., 2007.
*   Table 5 is discussed in the text at the end of Section 5.3, but it is not explicitly referenced.
