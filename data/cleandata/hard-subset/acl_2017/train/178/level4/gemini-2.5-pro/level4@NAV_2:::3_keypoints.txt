- Paper proposes a weakly-supervised joint embedding method that uses an ontology as distant supervision to learn word, phrase, and concept vectors from unannotated text and shows competitive similarity/relatedness results on UMLS and YAGO.
- Contribution: a configurable joint embedding model with hyperparameters that trade off treating phrases/concepts as atomic vs. compositional.
- Contribution: a new crowdsourced general-domain similarity/relatedness evaluation dataset.
- Contribution: extensive experiments across four corpora and multiple baselines demonstrating competitive performance without manual concept annotations.
- Strength: the model is intuitive and well-motivated, offering a flexible middle ground between atomic and compositional representations for phrases and concepts.
- Strength: substantial engineering and evaluation effort, including a new resource and comprehensive analyses over multiple datasets.
- Weakness: hyperparameters were tuned on test sets, an invalid procedure that risks overfitting and undermines result reliability.
- Weakness: evaluation is limited to intrinsic similarity/relatedness tasks and lacks extrinsic downstream task evaluation, limiting practical assessment.
- Weakness: mismatch between claimed concept evaluation and datasets elicited on phrases; medical datasets used are small or restricted to single-word concepts and have annotation quality issues.
- Weakness: claim of requiring less manual annotation is overstated because the method depends on large, manually constructed ontologies (UMLS, YAGO); some prior work used readily available clinical records rather than new annotations.
- Weakness: analysis is shallow—phrase-word and concept-phrase relations are treated similarly despite being different, and more linguistic investigation and hyperparameter effect analysis are needed.
- Question: which specific similarity/relatedness task was used for hyperparameter tuning, and will the authors re-run key experiments with a held-out development set?
- Question: how are overlapping phrases handled during preprocessing (e.g., "New York" vs "New York City")—is longest match used?
- Question: clarify the ENS/negative sampling matrix dimensions and naming (typo and misleading use for positive samples).
- Question: clarify training updates for constituent words when a multi-word phrase is observed—are constituent words updated or skipped?
- Question: notation confusion in Equation 1 where `c` denotes both context word and concept—please clarify.
- Question: rationale for using a 0–100 annotation scale for the new dataset rather than a coarser scale (e.g., 1–7).
