This paper presents a method for jointly embedding words, phrases, and concepts, leveraging both plain text corpora and a manually constructed ontology in which concepts are defined by one or more representative phrases. The method is applied in the medical domain using the UMLS ontology and in the general domain using YAGO. To evaluate their approach, the authors compare it to simpler baselines and prior work, primarily on intrinsic similarity and relatedness benchmarks. They utilize existing benchmarks for the medical domain and create a new general-domain concept similarity and relatedness dataset via Mechanical Turk, which they intend to release. The reported results are comparable to prior work.

### Strengths

*   The proposed joint embedding model is straightforward and conceptually sound. Its primary value lies in its ability to find a configurable middle ground between treating phrases as atomic units and modeling their compositionality. This same approach is also applied to concepts, which are treated as compositions of their representative phrases.
*   The paper represents a substantial amount of work, including model development, the creation of a new evaluation dataset, and a variety of experiments and analyses.

### Weaknesses

*   The evaluation is limited to intrinsic tasks, focusing on similarity and relatedness datasets. It is well-established that performance on such tasks often correlates poorly with the utility of embeddings in downstream, extrinsic applications. Consequently, it has become standard practice to include at least one extrinsic task when evaluating new embedding models.
*   The paper presents its evaluation datasets as measuring human judgments of *concept* similarity. However, it appears the human annotators were actually presented with *phrases*. If this is the case, the datasets should be considered and analyzed as measures of *phrase* similarity, not concept similarity.
*   The evaluation on medical concepts suffers from dataset limitations. The 'mini MayoSRS' dataset is extremely small (29 pairs), while its superset, 'MayoSRS', is only slightly larger (101 pairs) and has been reported to have low inter-annotator agreement. The other medical dataset, 'UMNSRS', is more reasonably sized but is limited to concepts represented by single words, which were presented as such to annotators. This limitation should be explicitly stated, as it calls into question the dataset's relevance for evaluating representations of multi-word phrases and complex concepts.
*   The authors state that they extensively tuned their model's hyperparameters on the same datasets used for reporting final results. This methodology invalidates the direct comparison to prior work and makes the reported results and subsequent analyses unreliable.
*   The authors claim an advantage over prior work by achieving comparable results with less manual annotation. This argument is not compelling for two reasons: first, the proposed method itself relies on large, manually-constructed ontologies. Second, the manually annotated data used by prior work was derived from existing clinical records, not from a dedicated annotation effort for that specific task.
*   The paper lacks deeper qualitative analysis to provide insight into the model's behavior beyond the quantitative results. The model treats the relationship between a phrase and its component words as analogous to the relationship between a concept and its constituent phrases. These are fundamentally different types of composition, and each deserves a dedicated analysis. For example, the analysis around line 588, which discusses the relationship between phrases and their component words, would benefit from a more linguistic perspective (e.g., examining the role of headwords). Furthermore, the work would be strengthened by an investigation into the hyperparameters that control the trade-off between the atomic and compositional representations of phrases and concepts.

### General Discussion

Given the significant weaknesses outlined above, particularly concerning the evaluation methodology, I recommend rejecting this submission. I encourage the authors to address these issues, especially by strengthening their evaluation datasets and methodology, before resubmitting their work.

### Minor Comments

*   **Line 69:** "contexts" should likely be "concepts".
*   **Line 202:** How are overlapping phrases (e.g., "New York" and "New York City") handled during training?
*   **Line 220:** The dimensions of the context matrix appear to be |W| x d. Also, the term "negative sampling matrix" is potentially misleading, as these embeddings also represent contexts in positive instances. A more general term like "context matrix" or "output matrix" might be clearer.
*   **Line 250:** The phrase "the observed phrase just completed" is unclear regarding how individual words are trained in the joint model. It could imply that only the final word of a phrase is used as a target, which seems unlikely. Please clarify the update procedure for word embeddings within phrases.
*   **Equation 1:** The notation is confusing. The variable 'c' is used for the context word, but 'o' is used in the objective function subscript. Using consistent notation would improve clarity.
*   **Line 361:** The citation Pedersen et al. (2007) is missing from the bibliography.
*   **Line 388:** A 1-100 scale for human similarity judgments is unusually fine-grained. Could the authors comment on the motivation for this choice and its effect on annotator reliability?
*   **Line 430:** The introduction of the term "strings" here is confusing. It would be clearer to consistently use the term "phrases".
*   **Line 496:** Please specify which dataset or task was used as the development set for hyperparameter tuning. This crucial information appears to be missing from the main text and the appendix.
*   **Table 3:** It is difficult to discern trends from this table. For instance, the combination PM+CL behaves quite differently from PM or CL in isolation. Visualizing the effect of these hyperparameters on a development set would be more insightful.
*   **Line 535:** A reference to Table 5 appears to be missing.
