- Proposes a weakly-supervised method that jointly learns embeddings for words, phrases, and abstract concepts using an ontology to identify phrase-to-concept mentions in raw text.  
- Adapts skip-gram with negative sampling to train separate embedding matrices for words, phrases, and concepts from contextual co-occurrences.  
- Evaluates embeddings on concept similarity and relatedness in biomedical and general domains, showing competitive performance against baselines.  
- Main contribution: a scalable, weakly-supervised model that uses an ontology for distant supervision to learn high-coverage concept/phrase embeddings without manual annotation.  
- Main contribution: releases a new general-domain entity similarity/relatedness dataset and open-source implementation (cui2vec).  
- Strength: well-motivated solution for representing non-compositional multi-word concepts and lexical variants using ontology-driven phrase identification.  
- Strength: thorough experimental evaluation across domains, corpora, baselines, and ablations.  
- Strength: valuable community resources (crowdsourced dataset + code) that facilitate replication and further research.  
- Weakness: limited technical novelty—method is a direct extension of skip-gram to three embedding matrices rather than a new learning paradigm.  
- Weakness: inconsistent empirical gains—structured CHOI baseline outperforms on MayoSRS, CHIU-30 is competitive on UMNSRS, and APPROXPHRco (averaging phrase vectors) sometimes beats direct concept vectors (CONCEPTco).  
- Weakness: oversimplified compositionality control via global hyperparameters β and η, which often tune to zero, suggesting little benefit over treating phrases atomically.  
- Question: clarify what constitutes the “distant supervision” signal—is it simply the ontology mapping of text spans to concept identifiers?  
- Question: how were models that lack concept vectors (PHRASEco, WORDco) scored on concept-pair datasets—by averaging phrase embeddings or choosing a representative phrase?  
- Question: do the authors agree that the ontology’s main benefit may be providing a high-quality gazetteer of synonyms (explaining APPROXPHRco’s success), and could a simpler phrase+word-only model suffice?
