This paper presents a method for extending word embedding models to generate representations for multi-word phrases and concepts, in addition to individual words. The proposed approach leverages an ontology to identify sets of words and phrases that denote the same underlying concept. Each of these sets is assigned a unique identifier. The authors then create a "tagged" version of the training corpus by replacing all occurrences of these words and phrases with their corresponding concept identifier. This tagged corpus is then appended to the original corpus, and the combined text is used to train the final embeddings. The authors apply this method to the biomedical domain, utilizing domain-specific corpora and ontologies.

A notable contribution of this work is the creation and release of a new test dataset for evaluating word similarity and relatedness, focused on real-world biomedical entities.

Overall, the paper is well-written and the proposed technique is intuitive and clearly explained. While the method itself is a straightforward extension of existing embedding techniques, its practical application is demonstrated effectively. However, the primary contribution may be perceived as incremental. Furthermore, the scope of the findings is constrained by the evaluation, which is focused exclusively on the biomedical domain.

The creation of the new test resource is a significant strength of this paper and may represent its most durable contribution. The authors could strengthen the paper by providing a more detailed discussion and analysis of this resource, as it is likely to be of high value to the research community.

**Technical Issue**

A minor technical issue appears in the presentation of Equation 8, which is intended to define the Mean Average Precision (MAP) calculation. The text incorrectly refers to this equation as defining a probability. However, the expression in Equation 8 does not constitute a valid probability distribution, as its sum over the vocabulary does not equal one. The authors should revise the description to remove the reference to probability. Additionally, regarding the MAP calculation itself, it may be more practical and standard to define a cut-off point (e.g., MAP@k) rather than ranking the entire vocabulary, which can be computationally expensive and noisy.
