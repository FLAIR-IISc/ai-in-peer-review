### Summary of the Paper

This paper introduces a weakly-supervised method for jointly learning vector space embeddings for words, multi-word phrases, and abstract concepts. The model leverages an existing ontology (e.g., UMLS or YAGO) to identify phrases in a large, unannotated text corpus that correspond to known concepts. It then adapts the skip-gram with negative sampling architecture to learn three distinct sets of embeddings simultaneously: one for words, one for phrases (treated as atomic units), and one for the concepts themselves. The training process uses the textual context surrounding a phrase to update the embeddings for the phrase itself, the words within that context, and any concepts the phrase represents. The authors evaluate their learned embeddings on concept similarity and relatedness tasks in both the biomedical and general domains, demonstrating competitive performance against several baselines.

### Main Contributions

1.  **A Novel Weakly-Supervised Model:** The primary contribution is a novel and scalable method for jointly embedding concepts, phrases, and words without requiring any manually annotated text for supervision. By using an ontology as a source of distant supervision to identify concept mentions, the model can be trained on massive unannotated corpora, achieving high vocabulary coverage.

2.  **A New Evaluation Dataset and Open-Source Software:** The authors contribute a new, publicly available dataset for evaluating general-domain entity similarity and relatedness, which fills a gap in existing resources. Additionally, the release of their software implementation (`cui2vec`) is a valuable contribution that will facilitate replication and further research in the biomedical NLP community and beyond.

### Strengths

1.  **Well-Motivated Problem and Elegant Solution:** The paper effectively addresses the important challenge of representing multi-word concepts. These concepts are often non-compositional (e.g., "the Big Apple") and can have multiple, lexically distinct surface forms (e.g., "Lou Gehrig’s disease" and "amyotrophic lateral sclerosis"). The proposed method of using an ontology to identify such phrases and learn embeddings for them and their underlying concepts from context is a practical and scalable approach.

2.  **Thorough Experimental Evaluation:** The evaluation is comprehensive, spanning two different domains (biomedical and general knowledge), multiple corpora of varying types (scientific literature, clinical notes, web text), and several standard evaluation datasets. The authors compare their model against a wide range of baselines, including methods based on word embeddings, other concept embedding techniques, and several ablations of their own model. This thoroughness provides a clear picture of the model's performance characteristics.

3.  **Valuable Community Resources:** The creation and release of a new crowdsourced dataset for entity similarity and relatedness is a significant strength. Such resources are crucial for advancing research. Paired with the release of the source code, this submission provides tangible assets that will be of immediate use to other researchers.

### Weaknesses

1.  **Limited Technical Novelty:** The core learning algorithm is a direct adaptation of the well-established skip-gram with negative sampling model. While its application to the joint learning of concepts, phrases, and words is novel, the underlying mechanism does not introduce a fundamentally new machine learning paradigm. The model essentially extends the skip-gram objective to three different embedding matrices that are updated based on shared contexts.

2.  **Inconsistent Empirical Performance:** The results do not consistently establish the superiority of the full proposed model (`CONCEPTco`). In Table 4, the `CHOI` baseline, which relies on more structured, manually annotated data, significantly outperforms the proposed method on the two MayoSRS datasets. On the larger UMNSRS datasets, the proposed concept embeddings are competitive but not definitively better than a strong word-based baseline (`CHIU-30`). More strikingly, in Table 5, the `APPROXPHRco` model (averaging phrase embeddings) outperforms the direct `CONCEPTco` embeddings, which questions the utility of learning the explicit concept vectors (`EC`) in the general domain.

3.  **Oversimplified Compositionality Mechanism:** The use of global hyperparameters `β` and `η` to control compositionality is a major simplification, as it assumes a single, uniform degree of compositionality across all phrases and concepts. The hyperparameter tuning results in Table 3, where the optimal values for `β` and `η` are often zero, suggest that this mechanism provides little benefit and that treating phrases as non-compositional atomic units is frequently the best strategy within this framework.

### Questions to Authors

1.  The term "distant supervision" is used to describe the method. Could you clarify what exactly constitutes the "supervision" signal here? Since the model learns from co-occurrence statistics in a way that is very similar to standard unsupervised word embedding models, the use of this term could be made more precise. Is it that the ontology provides the "supervision" for mapping specific text spans (phrases) to concept identifiers?

2.  In Table 4, you evaluate several models that do not produce concept vectors (e.g., `PHRASEco`, `WORDco`) on datasets of concept pairs (identified by UMLS CUIs). How was the similarity score calculated for these models? For a given concept pair, did you average the embeddings of all its associated phrases, or was a single representative phrase chosen for the comparison?

3.  The results in Table 5 show that the `APPROXPHRco` model (approximating a concept vector by averaging its phrase vectors) is the best-performing method. This seems to suggest that the main benefit of the ontology is to provide a high-quality gazetteer of synonymous phrases, rather than enabling the learning of superior abstract concept representations via the `EC` matrix. Do you agree with this interpretation? If so, could a simpler model that only learns phrase and word embeddings achieve similar or better results?
