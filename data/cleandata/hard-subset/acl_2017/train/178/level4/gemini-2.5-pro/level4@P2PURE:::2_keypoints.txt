- Proposal: joint embedding of words, phrases, and concepts into a common vector space using an ontology to map concepts to phrases, treating phrases as atomic units and training with a skip-gram–style approach; evaluated on UMLS and Yago concept similarity/relatedness tasks.

- Strength: tackles the important issue that semantically similar phrases can be lexically dissimilar and non-compositional, using a plausible phrase-embedding approach.

- Strength: achieves competitive or superior performance on some concept similarity tasks and provides a software release useful to biomedical NLP.

- Weakness: limited novelty, being largely an adaptation of the skip-gram method.

- Weakness: the full proposed model does not deliver the best empirical performance; simpler baselines (e.g., Choi et al., Chiu et al.) often match or outperform it (Tables 4 and 6, UMNSRS).

- Terminology concern: calling the method “distant supervision” is misleading because the model uses co-occurrence for embedding training rather than supervision for a predictive task.

- Presentation issue: introduced notation (e.g., E_W) is not used later and appears superfluous.

- Modeling concern: using a single global hyperparameter β for phrase compositionality is questionable because it forces all phrases to share one compositionality degree; cross-validation often sets β=0 for two corpora, contradicting the paper’s motivation.

- Experimental clarity: it is unclear how word- or phrase-only models are converted into single concept vectors for the concept similarity task given many-to-many concept↔phrase mappings.

- Contribution questioned: in Table 5, representing a concept as the average of its phrase embeddings (“approximate phr”) performs best, suggesting simple averaging of curated phrases might obviate the need for jointly trained ontology-based concept vectors.
