**Summary:** This work introduces a method for generating vector space embeddings for words, phrases, and concepts. The approach leverages a concept ontology where each concept is linked to a set of corresponding phrases. These phrases, identified within text corpora, are treated as single, indivisible units. Building on this foundation, the authors employ a technique fundamentally similar to the skip-gram model to learn embeddings for individual words, the aforementioned atomic phrases, and their associated concepts. The model's performance is assessed on concept similarity and relatedness tasks, utilizing the UMLS and Yago ontologies for evaluation.

**Strengths:**
A central contribution of this paper is its focus on the challenge that semantically related phrases may lack lexical overlap, and that many phrases are non-compositional. To address this, the authors put forward a credible model for learning phrase embeddings. The resulting embeddings demonstrate competitive or superior performance on the task of measuring concept similarity.

The accompanying software release is a valuable resource for researchers in the biomedical NLP community.

**Weaknesses:**
The main limitation of this work lies in its limited novelty. The proposed model is largely an adaptation of the existing skip-gram architecture.

Additionally, the complete model as proposed does not appear to achieve the top performance in the presented results (Table 4). Specifically, on the two Mayo datasets, the Choi baseline performs significantly better, and a similar pattern is observable in Table 6. On the larger UMNSRS dataset, the proposed method's performance is, at best, on par with earlier, less complex models like Chiu's.

**General Discussion:**
The authors describe their use of known phrases as a form of "distant supervision" for training embeddings. However, the nature of this supervision is unclear. As I interpret the method, each instance of a concept-linked phrase serves as context for training word vectors. This does not align with the conventional understanding of supervision, which typically involves a predictive task such as concept identification. This choice of terminology is potentially misleading.

The notation established in Section 3.2 (e.g., E_W) is not utilized in subsequent sections of the paper, making its introduction superfluous.

The introduction of a hyperparameter, β, to globally control phrase compositionality is unexpected. This approach implies that a single, universal constant determines the degree of compositionality for all phrases. More surprisingly, the optimal β values selected via cross-validation (Table 3) are counterintuitive. For the PM+CL and WikiNYT corpora, the value is zero, effectively negating the role of compositionality entirely.

The experimental design for Table 4 requires clarification. The text states that the dataset provides similarity labels for concepts/entities. Given the many-to-many mapping between concepts and phrases, it is unclear how phrase or word vectors contribute to the similarity calculation; it would seem that only concept vectors are applicable.

In Table 5, the top-performing approach is the 'approximate phr' method, where concepts are represented by the average of their constituent phrase vectors. This result raises questions about the necessity of the concept ontology itself, as a similar outcome might be achievable by starting directly with a seed set of phrases.
