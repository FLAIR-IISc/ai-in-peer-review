- Summary: Proposes jointly learning word, phrase, and concept embeddings by using an ontology to map concepts to phrases, treating matched phrases as atomic tokens and training an extended skip-gram model; evaluated on UMLS and Yago.

- Strength: Tackles multi-word phrase representation by leveraging ontologies to capture non-compositional and lexically dissimilar phrases as atomic units.

- Strength: Learned embeddings are competitive with, and sometimes outperform, prior methods on concept similarity tasks.

- Strength: Authors release their software, which is valuable for biomedical NLP researchers working with ontologies like UMLS.

- Weakness: The full model does not consistently beat simpler baselines (e.g., Choi/Chiu), calling into question the benefit of added complexity.

- Weakness: Limited technical novelty—the approach is mainly a direct extension of skip-gram to pre-identified phrases and concepts.

- Weakness: The best-performing variant ("approx phr") averages phrase embeddings and outperforms or matches the joint model, suggesting concept-level training may be unnecessary.

- Weakness: Modeling phrase compositionality with a single global hyperparameter β is problematic; cross-validation often selects β=0, negating the compositional component.

- Comment/Question: The term "distant supervision" is misleading here—clarify or use more precise terminology since the ontology defines tokens for an unsupervised embedding task.

- Comment/Question: Experimental protocol for Table 4 is unclear—explain how concept similarity is computed given many-to-many concept–phrase mappings (dedicated concept vectors vs. aggregated phrase vectors).

- Comment/Question: Remove or use unused notation (e.g., E_W, V_W in Section 3.2) for clarity and consistency.
