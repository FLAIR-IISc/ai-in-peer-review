This paper introduces a method for jointly embedding words, phrases, and concepts using a combination of plain text corpora and a manually constructed ontology, where concepts are defined by one or more representative phrases. The method is demonstrated in two domains: the medical domain, using the UMLS ontology, and the general domain, using YAGO. To evaluate their embeddings, the authors perform intrinsic similarity and relatedness tasks, comparing their model against several baselines and prior work. They utilize existing medical-domain benchmarks and introduce a new general-domain concept similarity dataset, created via crowdsourcing, which they plan to release. The reported results are generally comparable to those of previous methods.

**Strengths**

*   The proposed joint embedding model is intuitive and well-motivated. A key contribution is its ability to find a configurable middle ground between treating phrases as atomic units and modeling them compositionally from their constituent words. This same compositional approach is extended to concepts, which are viewed as being composed of their various representative phrases.
*   The scope of the work is commendable, encompassing model development, the creation of a new evaluation dataset, and a range of experiments and analyses.

**Weaknesses**

*   The evaluation is confined to intrinsic tasks, primarily similarity and relatedness benchmarks. While informative, such evaluations are known to be poor predictors of an embedding model's utility in downstream applications. The current standard in the field is to include at least one extrinsic task to demonstrate practical value, a component that is missing here.
*   There appears to be a mismatch in the evaluation protocol. The datasets are framed as measuring *concept* similarity, but the human annotators were reportedly presented with *phrases*. Consequently, these benchmarks are more accurately described as measuring phrase similarity. This distinction is critical and should be addressed in the analysis, as judging the similarity of "heart attack" and "myocardial infarction" is different from judging the similarity of the abstract concepts they represent.
*   The medical domain evaluation relies on datasets with significant limitations. The 'mini MayoSRS' dataset is extremely small (29 pairs), while its superset 'MayoSRS' (101 pairs) has been reported to have low inter-annotator agreement. The 'UMNSRS' dataset, while larger, is restricted to single-word concepts. This limitation makes it a questionable benchmark for a model specifically designed to handle multi-word phrases and complex concepts.
*   A major methodological flaw is that hyperparameters were tuned on the same datasets used for reporting final results and comparing against prior work. This practice invalidates the reported results and comparisons, as it constitutes testing on the development set. The performance is likely an overestimation of the model's true effectiveness.
*   The authors claim an advantage over prior work by achieving comparable results with less manual annotation effort. This argument is unconvincing for two reasons. First, the proposed method itself relies on large, manually-constructed ontologies (UMLS, YAGO), which represent a significant annotation effort. Second, the prior work cited used annotations derived from existing clinical records, which did not require a dedicated annotation project for that specific task.
*   The paper lacks a deeper analysis and interpretation of the results. The model treats the word-to-phrase and phrase-to-concept relationships as analogous compositional problems. However, these are fundamentally different relationships that warrant separate investigation. For instance, an analysis of the word-phrase compositionality could explore the role of headwords or other linguistic phenomena. Furthermore, the paper would be significantly strengthened by an analysis of the hyperparameters that control the tradeoff between the atomic and compositional representations, providing insight into when each view is more beneficial.

**General Discussion**

In light of the significant weaknesses outlined above, particularly the lack of extrinsic evaluation, the questionable evaluation methodology (hyperparameter tuning on the test set), and the reliance on limited datasets, I recommend rejecting this submission. I believe the work has potential, but it requires a more rigorous evaluation framework. I encourage the authors to address these issues, especially by incorporating extrinsic tasks and adopting a sound cross-validation protocol, before resubmission.

**Minor Comments**

*   Line 069: "contexts" should likely be "concepts".
*   Line 202: How are overlapping phrases (e.g., "New York" and "New York City") handled during training?
*   Line 220: The dimensions of the negative sampling matrix should be |W| x d, not |C| x d. Additionally, the term "negative sampling matrix" is potentially confusing, as these embeddings are also used for positive context words.
*   Line 250: The training process for words within the joint model is unclear. The phrase "the observed phrase just completed" suggests that only the final words of phrases are trained as targets, which seems unlikely. Please clarify.
*   Equation 1: The notation is confusing. `c` is used for the context word, but `o` is used in the objective function `J(o)`. Please ensure consistency.
*   Line 361: The reference Pedersen et al. (2007) is cited but missing from the bibliography.
*   Line 388: Using a 1-100 scale for human similarity judgments is unusually fine-grained and may introduce noise. Was there a specific motivation for this choice over a more standard Likert scale?
*   Line 430: The term "strings" is introduced here. For consistency, it would be clearer to continue using the term "phrases".
*   Line 496: It is critical to specify which dataset/task was used for hyperparameter tuning. This information appears to be missing from the main text and the appendix.
*   Table 3: It is difficult to discern clear trends from this table. For example, the behavior of PM+CL is not a simple combination of PM and CL. Visualizing development set performance as a function of these hyperparameters would be more insightful.
*   Line 535: A reference to Table 5 appears to be missing.
