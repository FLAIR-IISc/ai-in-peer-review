### Summary and Contributions

This paper introduces a weakly-supervised method for jointly learning vector space embeddings for words, phrases, and concepts. The core idea is to leverage an existing ontology (e.g., UMLS, YAGO) that maps canonical concepts to their various textual phrases. By identifying these phrases in a large, unannotated text corpus and treating them as atomic tokens, the model adapts the skip-gram with negative sampling architecture to learn embeddings for all three unit types in a shared space. The efficacy of the resulting embeddings is evaluated on concept similarity and relatedness tasks.

As I see them, the main contributions of this work are:

1.  **A Novel Weakly-Supervised Model:** The primary contribution is a method for learning concept embeddings directly from raw text, using only an ontology as a source of distant supervision. This approach avoids the need for expensive, manually annotated corpora, which is a significant advantage over some prior work.
2.  **New Evaluation Datasets:** The authors have created and released two new datasets for evaluating the similarity and relatedness of general-domain entities (people, places, organizations). Given the scarcity of such resources for entities beyond the word level, this is a valuable contribution to the community.
3.  **Comprehensive Empirical Analysis:** The paper provides a thorough evaluation in both the biomedical and general domains, comparing the proposed model against several strong baselines. The analysis demonstrates that the method produces competitive results, particularly on larger-scale similarity tasks.

### Strengths

1.  **Addresses an Important and Well-Motivated Problem:** The paper effectively tackles the challenge that concepts are often expressed through lexically diverse or non-compositional phrases (e.g., "Lou Gehrig's disease" vs. "amyotrophic lateral sclerosis"). The proposed model offers an elegant solution by learning a single vector for the underlying concept that is informed by the contextual usage of all its phrasal forms. This is a more principled approach than simply averaging word vectors, especially for non-compositional expressions.
2.  **Scalable and Annotation-Free Method:** The weakly-supervised nature of the model is a major strength. By relying on an ontology and an unannotated corpus, the method can be easily applied to new domains and scaled to embed a very large number of concepts without incurring the high cost of manual annotation. The paper demonstrates this by learning embeddings for over 190k medical concepts and 740k general-domain concepts.
3.  **Valuable Publicly Released Resources:** The authors are releasing the software implementation (`cui2vec`) and their new general-domain entity similarity datasets. These resources are a significant asset for the research community, particularly for those working in biomedical NLP, and will facilitate future research and replication.

### Weaknesses

1.  **Limited Technical Novelty of the Model:** The core learning algorithm is a direct and relatively minor modification of the standard skip-gram with negative sampling model. While the application to jointly embedding concepts, phrases, and words is new, the underlying mechanism itself does not represent a significant technical advance in representation learning.
2.  **Empirical Results Are Not Conclusively Superior:** The experimental results, while positive, do not consistently demonstrate the superiority of the full `CONCEPTco` model.
    *   In Table 4, the `CHOI` baseline is substantially better on the two MayoSRS datasets. On the larger UMNSRS datasets, the proposed model's performance is at best competitive with, and not clearly superior to, the `CHIU-30` word embedding baseline on the relatedness task and the `APPROXPHRco` approximation on the similarity task.
    *   Similarly, in Table 6, the `CHOI` embeddings show much stronger clustering for several semantic types, achieving a significantly higher overall MAP score. This raises questions about the practical advantages gained by the proposed method over existing approaches.
3.  **The Necessity of the Concept Layer is Questionable:** In the general-domain experiments (Table 5), the best results are achieved by `APPROXPHRco`, a method that simply averages the embeddings of a concept's representative phrases. The fact that this approximation outperforms the explicitly learned `CONCEPTco` embeddings undermines the central motivation of the model. It suggests that a simpler approach—learning only phrase embeddings and averaging them when needed—might be sufficient or even superior, questioning the added value of the dedicated concept embedding matrix `EC`.
4.  **Oversimplified Modeling of Compositionality:** The model uses single global hyperparameters (`β` and `η`) to control the degree of compositionality for all phrases and concepts. This is a strong and unrealistic assumption, as compositionality varies greatly between phrases (e.g., "high blood pressure" is largely compositional, while "the Big Apple" is not). The surprising finding that the optimal value for `β` is often zero (Table 3) suggests this component is not helpful and that treating all phrases as atomic units is empirically better within this framework.

### Questions to Authors

1.  The term "distant supervision" is used to describe the training methodology. Typically, this term implies using a knowledge base to generate noisy labels for a supervised predictive task (e.g., relation extraction). Here, the model seems to use the ontology to structure the input for an unsupervised learning algorithm. Could you clarify your use of this term and how it aligns with its conventional meaning?
2.  In Table 4, several baselines and model variants produce embeddings for words or phrases, not concepts (e.g., `WORDco`, `PHRASEco`, `CHIU-30`). How was the similarity for a concept pair calculated using these embeddings? Since a concept can map to multiple phrases (and thus multiple phrase embeddings), was a specific aggregation method used (e.g., averaging similarity scores across all phrase pairs)?
3.  Given that the `APPROXPHRco` model (averaging phrase embeddings) outperforms the `CONCEPTco` model on the general-domain tasks in Table 5, what is the justification for including the separate concept embedding layer (`EC`) in the model? Do you have evidence of tasks or scenarios where the dedicated concept vectors provide a clear advantage over this simpler and more effective approximation?
