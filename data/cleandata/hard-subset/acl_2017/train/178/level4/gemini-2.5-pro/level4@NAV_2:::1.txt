### Summary and Contributions

This paper presents a weakly-supervised method for learning vector space representations for words, multi-word phrases, and ontology concepts in a joint manner. The approach leverages an existing ontology to provide distant supervision; it identifies known phrases for concepts within a large, unannotated text corpus, replaces them with unique identifiers to create a "tagged" version of the corpus, and then trains a modified skip-gram model on both the original and tagged corpora. The method is evaluated on biomedical and general-domain ontologies (UMLS and YAGO).

The main contributions of this work, in order of significance, are:
1.  **A new evaluation dataset:** The authors have created and are releasing a novel dataset for measuring the similarity and relatedness of real-world entities. This is a valuable resource that addresses a gap in current evaluation benchmarks, which have historically focused on single words.
2.  **A weakly-supervised joint embedding method:** The paper proposes an intuitive method to extend word embedding models to create representations for concepts and phrases without requiring manual annotation of concepts in the training corpus.

### Strengths

1.  **Introduction of a valuable new resource:** The most significant strength of this work is the creation of a new dataset for entity similarity and relatedness. Given the community's focus on moving beyond single-word representations, this dataset is a timely and potentially impactful contribution that will facilitate future research in concept and entity representation.
2.  **Well-written and clear presentation:** The paper is clearly written, logically structured, and easy to follow. The method is described in sufficient detail, and the experimental setup is well-documented.
3.  **Simple and effective method:** The proposed technique is a natural and straightforward extension of the widely-used skip-gram model. Despite its simplicity, the method achieves competitive results on similarity and relatedness tasks, particularly when compared to other methods that require more extensive supervision or annotation.

### Weaknesses

1.  **Limited technical novelty:** The core method is an adaptation of the standard skip-gram model. While the application is novel, the modification itself (replacing phrases with IDs and adding new embedding matrices) is an incremental contribution from a technical standpoint. The main innovation lies in the problem formulation and the use of distant supervision rather than in a new learning algorithm.
2.  **Narrow evaluation focus:** Although experiments are performed on both biomedical and general-domain data, the evaluation and analysis are heavily weighted towards the biomedical domain (UMLS). The paper would be stronger if it included a more comprehensive analysis of the embeddings learned from the general-domain corpus (WIKINYT) using the YAGO ontology, especially given that the new evaluation dataset is in the general domain.
3.  **Inaccuracy in a key evaluation metric's description:** The formulation for calculating Mean Average Precision (MAP) contains a technical error. Equation (8) defines the precision for a child term `c`, but it is described as a probability, `Pr(c|Cp, V)`. The formula provided is the standard definition for precision-at-k (where k is the rank of term `c`), not a probability. This should be corrected for clarity and accuracy.

### Questions to Authors

1.  Regarding the calculation of MAP in Section 5.4, could you please clarify Equation (8)? The text refers to this value as a probability, but the formula appears to be the standard definition of precision at a given rank. Is the terminology intentional, or should the description be updated to reflect that this is a precision score?
2.  The new entity similarity and relatedness dataset is an excellent contribution. Could you elaborate on its properties? For instance, considering the Fleiss' κ of 0.24 indicates only "fair" agreement, were there specific types of entity pairs that annotators found particularly ambiguous or difficult to rate? More discussion on the resource itself would strengthen the paper.
