**Summary and Contributions**

This paper introduces a weakly-supervised method to jointly learn vector space embeddings for words, multi-word phrases, and ontology concepts. The approach leverages an existing ontology (e.g., UMLS, YAGO) to identify known phrases in a large, unannotated text corpus. These phrases are then treated as single tokens, and an adaptation of the skip-gram with negative sampling algorithm is used to train embeddings for words, phrases, and the concepts they represent, all within a shared semantic space. The resulting embeddings are evaluated on concept similarity and relatedness tasks in both the biomedical and general domains, demonstrating competitive performance.

In my view, the primary contributions of this work are:
1.  A novel, weakly-supervised framework for jointly embedding concepts, phrases, and words. The method cleverly uses an ontology as a source of distant supervision to learn representations from unannotated text, bypassing the need for expensive manual annotation.
2.  A comprehensive empirical evaluation showing that the learned embeddings achieve competitive or state-of-the-art performance on several concept similarity and relatedness benchmarks compared to previous methods, some of which require more intensive supervision.
3.  The introduction of a new crowdsourced dataset for evaluating similarity and relatedness between general-domain entities, which is a valuable resource for the community. The release of the associated software also enhances the paper's impact and reproducibility.

**Strengths**

1.  The paper effectively addresses the important and challenging problem of representing multi-word concepts. It acknowledges that many concepts are non-compositional (e.g., "Lou Gehrig's disease") or have multiple, lexically diverse surface forms (e.g., "the Big Apple," "New York City"). The proposed approach of identifying phrases from an ontology and learning their representations directly from textual context is a well-motivated and plausible solution to this problem.
2.  The experimental results are strong and demonstrate the viability of the proposed method. On the larger biomedical datasets (UMNSRS, Table 4) and the new general-domain dataset (Table 5), the embeddings produced by the model are highly competitive with, and in some cases superior to, a range of strong baselines. This shows that meaningful concept representations can be learned without manually annotated corpora.
3.  The work contributes valuable resources to the research community. The release of a new dataset for general-domain entity similarity and relatedness fills a notable gap in evaluation resources. Furthermore, the public release of the `cui2vec` software package is a significant contribution that facilitates reproducibility and enables other researchers to apply this method to their own data and ontologies.

**Weaknesses**

1.  The methodological novelty of the proposed model is somewhat limited. The core training algorithm is a straightforward adaptation of the well-established skip-gram with negative sampling model. The primary innovation lies in the preprocessing step of identifying and replacing phrases based on an ontology and applying the objective function to three distinct entity types (words, phrases, concepts). While the application is novel and the results are good, the underlying technique is not a significant departure from existing methods.
2.  The empirical results do not consistently demonstrate the superiority of the proposed model's concept embeddings (`CONCEPTco`). In several key comparisons, existing baselines perform better. For example, in Table 4, the Choi et al. (2016b) model is substantially better on the two MayoSRS datasets. In Table 6, the same baseline shows superior overall performance on the semantic type clustering task. On the UMNSRS datasets, the proposed method is only marginally better than a word-embedding baseline (Chiu-30). This suggests that while the method is effective, it does not represent a definitive improvement across all conditions.
3.  The results cast some doubt on the necessity of explicitly learning concept embeddings. In the general-domain evaluation (Table 5), the top-performing method is `APPROXPHRco`, which simply averages the embeddings of a concept's constituent phrases. This raises the question of whether the additional machinery for learning `CONCEPTco` vectors is truly beneficial, or if the model's main strength is simply in learning high-quality phrase embeddings.

**Questions to Authors**

1.  Could you clarify the use of the term "distant supervision"? Typically, this term implies generating noisy labels for a supervised task (e.g., relation extraction). Here, the ontology is used to define the items to be embedded from text, but the training itself appears to be a standard unsupervised distributional learning process. How does your framing align with the conventional understanding of this term?
2.  The hyperparameters β and η were introduced to balance distributional and compositional information. However, the hyperparameter search (Table 3) often selected values of 0.0 for these parameters, effectively turning off the compositional component. This seems counter-intuitive, especially for β, as many phrases are at least partially compositional. Could you elaborate on why a purely distributional signal appears to be optimal in these cases and what this implies about the model's design?
3.  Given that the `APPROXPHRco` method (averaging phrase embeddings) was the best-performing model on the general-domain tasks (Table 5) and also performed well on biomedical tasks (Table 4), what is the clear, demonstrated advantage of learning the `CONCEPTco` embeddings directly? Do these results suggest that the primary value of the framework lies in learning phrase representations, rather than joint concept representations?
