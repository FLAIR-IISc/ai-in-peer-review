- Paper proposes jointly learning embeddings for words, phrases, and concepts by mapping ontology concepts to representative phrases, treating phrases as single tokens, and adapting skip-gram with negative sampling.  
- Evaluated on concept similarity and relatedness using UMLS and YAGO.

- Addresses the important problem of representing multi-word and non-compositional concepts and is well-motivated.  
- Resulting embeddings are competitive and sometimes superior on several benchmarks, notably larger UMNSRS datasets.  
- Release of the cui2vec software is a useful resource for biomedical NLP researchers.

- Limited novelty: method is a relatively minor modification of standard skip-gram where phrases are just treated as tokens and concept vectors updated from phrase contexts.  
- Experimental results do not consistently show CONCEPTco’s superiority: Choi et al. (2016b) outperforms on MayoSRS/Mini MayoSRS; on UMNSRS the model is at best on par with simpler baselines like Chiu et al. (2016a).  
- CONCEPTco is not the top performer in semantic type clustering (Table 6).

- Terminology: request to clarify the use of “distant supervision,” which may be misleading compared to standard unsupervised distributional learning.  
- Clarify or correct unused notation for embedding matrices (E_W, E_P, E_C) introduced in Section 3.2.  
- Question the use of a single global compositionality hyperparameter β for all phrases and the finding that β=0 was optimal for large corpora, which undermines the model’s compositionality motivation.  
- Provide more experimental details for Tables 4 and 5, specifically how similarity scores are derived for methods (PHRASEco, WORDco) when concepts map to multiple phrases.  
- Challenge the added value of dedicated concept embeddings since APPROXPHRco (averaging phrase embeddings) outperforms CONCEPTco, suggesting phrase-averaging may suffice without concept-level training.
