**Summary**

This paper presents a model for learning vector space embeddings for words, phrases, and concepts. The model leverages an ontology that provides mappings between concepts and their corresponding phrases. These phrases are identified in a text corpus and treated as single, atomic units during training. Building on this, the paper employs a method analogous to the skip-gram model to jointly train embeddings for words, these atomic phrases, and their associated concepts. The proposed model is evaluated on concept similarity and relatedness tasks using the UMLS and Yago ontologies.

**Strengths**

The paper tackles the important challenge of modeling phrase semantics, recognizing that phrases can be semantically similar without being lexically related and that many phrases are non-compositional. The proposed model offers a plausible approach for learning phrase embeddings that addresses these issues. The resulting embeddings are shown to be competitive with, and in some cases superior to, existing methods on concept similarity tasks.

The public release of the software is a welcome contribution, particularly for researchers in biomedical NLP.

**Weaknesses**

The primary weakness of the paper is that the novelty of the proposed model is limited; it is largely an adaptation of the skip-gram architecture.

Furthermore, the full model presented by the paper is not the top-performing model in the authors' own experiments (Table 4). On the two Mayo datasets, the Choi et al. (2016) baseline is substantially better. A similar trend is observable in Table 6. On the larger UMNSRS dataset, the proposed model is at best competitive with simpler previous models (e.g., Chiu et al., 2016).

**General Discussion**

The authors describe their method as using known phrases for "distant supervision," but this terminology is potentially misleading. As I understand it, each occurrence of a concept-linked phrase simply provides a context for the skip-gram objective. This differs from the conventional use of "supervision," which typically involves training a model for a specific predictive task, such as classifying concept mentions in text. This choice of terminology may cause confusion.

The notation introduced in Section 3.2 (e.g., E_W) appears to be unused in the remainder of the paper, which makes its inclusion superfluous.

The use of a single hyperparameter, β, to globally control the compositionality of all phrases is a surprising modeling choice. This approach implies that a single constant determines the degree of compositionality for every phrase in the vocabulary. What is particularly noteworthy are the optimal values for β found via cross-validation (Table 3). For the PM+CL and WikiNYT corpora, the optimal value is zero. This effectively discards the compositional component of the model entirely, treating all phrases as non-compositional and seemingly arguing against a key motivation for the model.

The experimental setup for Table 4 requires further clarification. The paper states that the evaluation datasets measure the similarity or relatedness of concepts. However, given the many-to-many mapping between concepts and phrases, it is unclear how phrase or word vectors are used to compute these concept-level similarity scores. It appears that only the dedicated concept vectors could be used for this task, but the paper does not explicitly confirm this.

Finally, in Table 5, the "approximate phr" baseline—which represents concepts by averaging the embeddings of their associated phrases—is the top-performing method. This result raises a question about the necessity of learning distinct concept vectors. It suggests that one might achieve similar or better results simply by starting with a seed set of phrases and their groupings, without needing to explicitly model concept embeddings via the ontology.
