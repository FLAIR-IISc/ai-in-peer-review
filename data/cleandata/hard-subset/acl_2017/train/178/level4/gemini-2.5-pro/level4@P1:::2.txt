### **Summary**

This paper introduces a method for jointly learning vector space embeddings for words, phrases, and concepts. The approach leverages an ontology that maps concepts to a set of representative phrases. By identifying these phrases in a large, unannotated text corpus and treating them as single tokens, the model adapts the skip-gram with negative sampling algorithm to train embeddings for words, the identified phrases, and the concepts associated with them. The proposed method is evaluated on concept similarity and relatedness tasks using the UMLS and YAGO ontologies.

### **Strengths**

1.  The paper addresses the important and challenging problem of representing multi-word concepts. It correctly acknowledges that many phrases are non-compositional (e.g., "the Big Apple") and that a single concept can be represented by multiple, lexically dissimilar phrases (e.g., "Lou Gehrig’s disease" and "amyotrophic lateral sclerosis"). The proposed method is a well-motivated approach to this problem.

2.  The resulting embeddings are shown to be competitive with, and in some cases superior to, existing methods on several concept similarity and relatedness benchmarks, particularly on the larger UMNSRS datasets.

3.  The release of the `cui2vec` software is a welcome contribution that could be valuable for researchers in the biomedical NLP community.

### **Weaknesses**

1.  The primary weakness of the work is its limited novelty. The proposed model is a relatively minor modification of the standard skip-gram with negative sampling algorithm, where multi-word phrases are simply treated as single tokens during training, and concept vectors are updated based on the contexts of their associated phrases.

2.  The experimental results do not consistently demonstrate the superiority of the proposed `CONCEPTco` model over established baselines.
    *   In Table 4, the Choi et al. (2016b) baseline significantly outperforms the proposed method on both the MayoSRS and Mini MayoSRS datasets.
    *   On the larger UMNSRS datasets, the model's performance is, at best, on par with simpler baselines like the word embeddings from Chiu et al. (2016a).
    *   A similar trend is observable in Table 6, where the proposed model is not the top performer in semantic type clustering.

### **Questions to Authors**

1.  The paper describes its method as using "distant supervision." Could the authors clarify this terminology? My understanding is that the contexts of known phrases are used to update concept embeddings. This seems more analogous to standard unsupervised distributional learning rather than supervision in the traditional sense (e.g., predicting a label). The term may be confusing.

2.  The notation for the embedding matrices (`E_W`, `E_P`, `E_C`) is introduced in Section 3.2 but appears to be unused in the subsequent equations and text. Please clarify or correct this.

3.  The use of a single, global hyperparameter `β` to control the compositionality of all phrases is questionable, as it implies that all phrases in the vocabulary share the same degree of compositionality. More surprisingly, the optimal value for `β` found via cross-validation was 0 for the two largest corpora (PM+CL and WIKINYT), which effectively turns off the compositionality term. This seems to contradict one of the core motivations of the model. Could the authors comment on this finding?

4.  Could the authors provide more detail on the experimental setup for Tables 4 and 5? The tasks evaluate concept similarity, but several of the methods evaluated (e.g., `PHRASEco`, `WORDco`) are not concept-level representations. If a concept maps to multiple phrases, how is a single similarity score derived from phrase or word embeddings for the comparison?

5.  In Table 5, the best-performing method is `APPROXPHRco`, which represents a concept as the average of its constituent phrase embeddings. This result raises a critical question: what is the added value of learning dedicated concept embeddings (`CONCEPTco`) if a simpler phrase-averaging approach performs better? It suggests that one might achieve comparable or superior results simply by starting with a curated list of phrases and their embeddings, without needing the full concept-level ontology for training the concept vectors themselves.
