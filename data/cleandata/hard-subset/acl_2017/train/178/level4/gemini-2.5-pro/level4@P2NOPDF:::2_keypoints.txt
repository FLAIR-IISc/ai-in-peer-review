- Proposes a skip-gram–based model that learns embeddings for words, atomized phrases, and ontology-linked concepts by treating known phrases as atomic units in text.
- Evaluates the model on concept similarity and relatedness tasks using UMLS and Yago ontologies.
- Addresses the challenge that semantically related phrases can be lexically dissimilar and non-compositional, proposing a knowledge-guided phrase embedding approach.
- Presents a plausible model that can learn phrase embeddings directly from text and shows competitive or superior performance on some concept-similarity benchmarks.
- Releases software implementation, which is a useful resource for biomedical NLP researchers.
- Main novelty is limited—model is a direct extension of skip-gram.
- Experimental results do not consistently show the full model outperforming baselines; some baselines (e.g., Choi et al.) outperform it on several datasets.
- On larger datasets (e.g., UMNSRS) the model is at best competitive with simpler existing methods, questioning the benefit of added complexity.
- The term “distant supervision” is used ambiguously and should be clarified or replaced, since the method identifies contexts rather than providing labeled predictive examples.
- Introduced embedding-matrix notation (e.g., E_W) appears unused and could be removed for clarity.
- Using a single global compositionality hyperparameter β for all phrases is a strong assumption; cross-validation often selects β=0, undermining the need for a compositional component.
- The procedure for computing concept similarity (Table 4) is unclear given many-to-many concept–phrase mappings; need to clarify how word/phrase embeddings contribute to concept similarity.
- The “approximate phr” baseline (averaging phrase embeddings) outperforms learned concept embeddings in Table 5, raising doubts about the value of explicitly learning concept vectors versus aggregating phrase embeddings or even forgoing the ontology.
