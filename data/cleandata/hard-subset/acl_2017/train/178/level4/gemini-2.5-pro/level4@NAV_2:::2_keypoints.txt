- Proposes a weakly-supervised method that uses an ontology to identify phrases in raw text and jointly embeds words, phrases, and ontology concepts in a shared vector space.  
- Main contributions: a novel ontology-guided embedding framework, comprehensive empirical evaluation showing competitive performance, and a new crowdsourced general-domain similarity dataset plus released software.  
- Effectively tackles multi-word and non-compositional concept representation by treating ontology-identified phrases as single tokens for learning.  
- Strong experimental results on biomedical (UMNSRS) and new general-domain benchmarks showing competitiveness with or superiority to several baselines.  
- Provides valuable community resources: a new dataset for entity similarity/relatedness and the cui2vec software package for reproducibility.  
- Limited methodological novelty since the core training is an adaptation of skip-gram with negative sampling and the main innovation is preprocessing and applying the objective to three entity types.  
- Empirical gains are inconsistent: several baselines outperform the proposed CONCEPTco embeddings on key datasets (e.g., MayoSRS, semantic type clustering).  
- Raises doubt about the necessity of explicit concept embeddings because simple phrase-averaging (APPROXPHRco) often matches or outperforms learned CONCEPTco vectors.  
- Question on the term "distant supervision": how does using an ontology to select items align with the conventional notion of distant supervision as noisy labeling?  
- Question on hyperparameters β and η: why did tuning often set them to 0, effectively disabling compositional signals, and what does this imply about model design?  
- Question on the utility of CONCEPTco: given APPROXPHRco’s strong performance, what clear advantage do learned concept vectors provide over high-quality phrase embeddings?
