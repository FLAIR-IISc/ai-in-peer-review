The authors present a weakly-supervised method for jointly embedding words, phrases, and concepts. The model is trained on a text corpus and uses an ontology, where concepts are linked to one or more representative phrases, to provide distant supervision. The authors demonstrate their method in both the medical domain, using the UMLS ontology, and the general domain, using YAGO. The evaluation compares the proposed model against several baselines and prior work, primarily on intrinsic similarity and relatedness tasks. They evaluate on existing medical benchmarks and also introduce and release a new general-domain dataset for concept similarity and relatedness, collected via crowdsourcing. The reported results are competitive with prior work.

### Strengths
- The proposed joint embedding model is intuitive and straightforward. A key strength of the model is its ability to find a configurable middle ground between treating phrases as atomic units and learning compositional representations from their constituent words. This same principle is extended to concepts, which are treated as being 'composed' of their various representative phrases.

- The paper represents a substantial amount of work, including model development, the creation of a new evaluation dataset, and a variety of experiments and analyses.

### Weaknesses
- The evaluation is limited to intrinsic tasks, focusing primarily on similarity and relatedness datasets. As the authors themselves acknowledge, performance on such intrinsic tasks often correlates poorly with performance on downstream, extrinsic tasks. It is now standard practice to include at least one extrinsic evaluation to demonstrate the practical utility of a new embedding model.

- The paper presents its evaluation datasets as measuring similarity between *concepts*. However, if I understand correctly, human annotators were shown *phrases* representing those concepts. The datasets should therefore be considered measures of phrase similarity, not concept similarity, and the analysis should reflect this distinction.

- Several of the medical evaluation datasets have significant limitations. The 'mini MayoSRS' dataset is extremely small (29 pairs), while its superset, 'MayoSRS' (101 pairs), is known to have relatively low inter-annotator agreement. The UMNSRS dataset, while more reasonably sized, is limited to concepts represented by single words, which is how they were presented to annotators. This limitation should be explicitly stated, as it calls into question the dataset's relevance for evaluating embeddings of multi-word phrases.

- The authors state that they performed extensive hyperparameter tuning on their test sets. This is a significant methodological flaw. Tuning on the test set invalidates the reported results and comparisons to prior work, as it does not reflect how the model would perform on unseen data.

- The authors argue that their method is superior to prior work because it achieves comparable results without requiring the same level of manual annotation. I find this argument unconvincing. First, the proposed method itself relies on large, manually-constructed ontologies (UMLS, YAGO). Second, the manual annotations used by some prior work (e.g., Choi et al., 2016b) were derived from existing clinical billing codes, not from a dedicated annotation effort for the purpose of training embeddings.

- More broadly, the analysis lacks deep insights into the model's behavior. The paper treats two distinct relationships—the one between a phrase and its component words, and the one between a concept and its representative phrases—as analogous forms of compositionality. These relationships are fundamentally different and merit separate, dedicated analyses. For instance, the analysis of phrase compositionality would be much stronger with a linguistic analysis. Is the model's behavior explained by factors like the dominance of headwords within phrases? Similarly, the paper misses an opportunity to investigate the effect of the hyperparameters (β and η) that explicitly control the tradeoff between atomic and compositional representations.

### General Discussion
Given the weaknesses outlined above, particularly those concerning the evaluation methodology, I recommend rejecting this submission. I encourage the authors to strengthen their evaluation before resubmission.

### Minor Comments
- **Line 69:** "contexts" should likely be "concepts".
- **Line 202:** How are overlapping phrases handled during the preprocessing step? The greedy matching strategy is mentioned, but what happens if a shorter phrase is contained within a longer one?
- **Line 220:** The dimensions for the matrix `ENS` should be |W| × d. Also, the term "negative sampling matrix" is misleading, as these are the context embeddings used for positive examples as well. A more standard term would be "context embedding matrix".
- **Line 250:** The phrase "the observed phrase just completed" is unclear. How are individual word embeddings trained when they are part of a multi-word phrase?
- **Equation 1:** The notation is confusing. The probability is defined for a word-context pair (w, c), but the text refers to the context word as 'o'. Using 'o' instead of 'c' in the equation would improve consistency.
- **Line 361:** The citation Pedersen et al. (2007) is missing from the bibliography.
- **Line 388:** A 0-100 scale for crowdsourced similarity judgments is very fine-grained. Did the authors consider that this might introduce noise?
- **Line 430:** The term "representative strings" is introduced here. For consistency, it would be better to continue using the term "phrases".
- **Line 496 & Appendix A.1:** It is not specified which of the similarity/relatedness tasks was used as the development set for hyperparameter tuning. This is a critical detail.
- **Table 3:** The optimal hyperparameters vary significantly across corpora, and it is difficult to discern any clear trends. For instance, the optimal setting for the combined PM+CL corpus is very different from the individual PM or CL corpora. An analysis of performance on a development set across different hyperparameter values would be insightful.
- **Line 535:** There appears to be a missing reference to Table 5, which contains the results for the general-domain datasets.
