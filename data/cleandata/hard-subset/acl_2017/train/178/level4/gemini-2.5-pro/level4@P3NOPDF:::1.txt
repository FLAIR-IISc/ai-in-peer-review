### **Summary**

This paper proposes a method for learning vector representations for phrases and concepts, extending standard word embedding techniques. The core idea is to leverage an existing ontology to identify sets of words and phrases that refer to the same concept. Each concept is assigned a unique identifier, which is then used to replace all corresponding word and phrase occurrences in the training corpus, creating a "tagged" version of the text. This tagged corpus is appended to the original corpus, and a word embedding model is trained on the combined dataset. The approach is demonstrated and evaluated in the biomedical domain, using relevant corpora and ontologies. A key secondary contribution of this work is the creation and release of a new test dataset for measuring similarity and relatedness for real-world entities.

### **Strengths**

1.  **Clarity and Presentation:** The paper is well-written and easy to follow. The proposed method is explained clearly, and the motivation is well-established.

2.  **Intuitive Method:** The technique of creating concept-tagged text and combining it with the original corpus is intuitive, straightforward, and appears effective for the stated task of learning concept embeddings.

3.  **Valuable New Resource:** A significant strength of this work is the introduction of a new, manually curated test set for real-world entity similarity and relatedness. This resource is a valuable contribution to the community, potentially even more impactful than the proposed method itself, and could facilitate future research in this domain.

### **Weaknesses and Areas for Improvement**

1.  **Novelty and Significance:** While the proposed method is sensible, its novelty is somewhat limited. It can be seen as a straightforward extension of existing word embedding techniques, applying a form of entity normalization at the corpus level before training. The overall contribution feels more incremental than groundbreaking.

2.  **Limited Scope of Evaluation:** The evaluation is confined to the biomedical domain. While this is a valuable and challenging domain, the paper would be stronger if it included a discussion on the generalizability of the approach. For instance, how would it perform in other domains with rich ontologies (e.g., finance, law), or with noisier, less-structured knowledge sources like DBpedia or Wikidata?

3.  **Discussion of the Test Resource:** Given the potential value of the new test dataset, the paper would benefit from a more detailed discussion of its creation, characteristics, and potential applications. As it stands, this valuable resource feels somewhat undersold, and a more thorough treatment could elevate the paper's impact.

### **Technical Comments and Minor Corrections**

1.  **Equation 8 (MAP Calculation):** There is a small but important technical issue in the description of Equation 8 for the MAP calculation. The authors describe this formula as defining a probability, which is incorrect. It is straightforward to show that the expression does not represent a valid probability distribution (i.e., it does not sum to 1). The authors should revise the text to remove any references to it being a probability and instead describe it accurately as a scoring function used for ranking.

    Furthermore, while ranking the entire vocabulary is one approach, the authors might consider discussing the rationale for this choice. A more standard approach for MAP in information retrieval tasks involves evaluating a ranked list up to a certain cut-off point 'k'. A brief justification for ranking the full vocabulary would strengthen this section.

2.  **Minor Grammatical Correction:**
    *   Line 556: "most concepts has" should be corrected to "most concepts have".
