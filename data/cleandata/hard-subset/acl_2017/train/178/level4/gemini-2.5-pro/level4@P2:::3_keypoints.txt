- Summary: Proposes a weakly-supervised model to jointly learn embeddings for words, multi-word phrases, and ontology concepts using skip-gram with distant supervision from ontologies (UMLS, YAGO); reports competitive intrinsic results.
- Contribution 1: Presents an adaptable model with hyperparameters (β, η) to trade off atomic vs. compositional representations for concepts/phrases.
- Contribution 2: Creates and releases a new crowdsourced general-domain entity similarity/relatedness dataset.
- Strength 1: Elegant shared-space approach capturing semantic relations beyond word-only models; thoughtful hyperparameter design for compositionality.
- Strength 2: Extensive experimental effort across two domains, multiple corpora, intrinsic evaluations, baselines, and a released dataset.
- Weakness 1: Flawed evaluation methodology—hyperparameters tuned on full test sets (admitted by authors), invalidating reported results and comparisons.
- Weakness 2: No extrinsic evaluation; reliance solely on intrinsic similarity/relatedness limits assessment of practical utility.
- Weakness 3a: Medical evaluation sets are very small and have low inter-annotator agreement, undermining strong conclusions.
- Weakness 3b: UMNSRS datasets contain only single-word concepts, mismatching the model’s novelty in multi-word handling.
- Weakness 3c: Evaluation conflates concept vs. phrase similarity—human judgments were collected on textual phrases, so tasks are better framed as phrase similarity.
- Weakness 4: Overstates supervision advantage—method depends on large manually-constructed ontologies and comparison baselines use existing non-task-specific annotated sources.
- Weakness 5: Analyses are superficial (e.g., compositionality and hyperparameter effects) and lack deeper linguistic or behavioral insights.
- Recommendation: Reject due to significant evaluation and analysis flaws despite a promising core idea.
- Question 1: Clarify greedy phrase replacement handling for nested/overlapping phrases (e.g., "New York" vs. "New York City") and its impact on representations.
- Question 2: Clarify loss computation—whether loss is calculated for every word as in standard word2vec or only around recognized phrases.
- Question 3: Specify which dataset/score was used to optimize hyperparameters (single dataset vs. average across evaluations).
- Additional comment: Typo in Section 1 ("contexts, phrases, and words" should likely be "concepts, phrases, and words").
- Additional comment: Terminology ENS as "negative sampling matrix" is confusing; recommend standard "context" or "output" matrix.
- Additional comment: Notation inconsistency in Equation 1 (uses c) vs. surrounding text (uses o) should be made consistent.
- Additional comment: Inconsistent use of "representative strings" vs. "phrases"; unify terminology.
- Additional comment: Missing reference for Pedersen et al., 2007.
