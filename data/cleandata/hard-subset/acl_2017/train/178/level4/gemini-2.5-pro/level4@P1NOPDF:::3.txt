The authors present a method for jointly embedding words, phrases, and concepts using a plain text corpus and a manually-constructed ontology in which concepts are defined by one or more phrases. They apply their method in the medical domain using the UMLS ontology and in the general domain using the YAGO ontology. The authors evaluate their approach against simpler baselines and prior work, primarily on intrinsic similarity and relatedness benchmarks. They use existing benchmarks in the medical domain and create a new general-domain concept similarity dataset via Mechanical Turk, which they plan to release. Their results are comparable to those of prior work.

**Strengths:**
- The proposed joint embedding model is straightforward and intuitive. Its primary contribution, in my view, is in finding a configurable middle ground between treating phrases as atomic units and modeling their compositionality. This approach is similarly applied to concepts, which are modeled as compositions of their representative phrases.
- The paper represents a substantial amount of work, including model development, the creation of a new evaluation dataset, and extensive evaluations and analyses.

**Weaknesses:**
- The evaluation is limited to intrinsic tasks, focusing on similarity and relatedness datasets. As the authors themselves acknowledge, such evaluations are known to be poor predictors of performance on downstream, extrinsic tasks. It has become standard practice to include at least one extrinsic task when evaluating embedding models.
- The similarity/relatedness datasets are presented as capturing human judgments of *concept* similarity. However, if I understand correctly, annotators were shown *phrases* representing these concepts. The datasets should therefore be considered and analyzed as measures of *phrase* similarity, not concept similarity.
- The medical evaluation datasets have significant limitations. The 'mini MayoSRS' dataset is extremely small (29 pairs), while its superset, 'MayoSRS', is only slightly larger (101 pairs) and has been reported to have low inter-annotator agreement. The other medical dataset, 'UMNSRS', is more reasonably sized, but it is limited to concepts that can be represented by single words, and these single-word terms were what was shown to annotators. This limitation should be explicitly stated, as it calls into question the dataset's relevance for evaluating representations of multi-word phrases and complex concepts.
- The authors note that they extensively tune hyperparameters on the same test sets where they report final results and compare against prior work. This evaluation methodology is flawed and calls the validity of all reported results and analyses into question.
- The authors claim their method is superior to prior work because it achieves comparable results with less manual annotation. This argument is unconvincing for two reasons: first, the proposed method itself relies on large, manually-constructed ontologies. Second, the manually annotated data used in the prior work cited was derived from existing clinical records and did not require a separate, dedicated annotation effort.
- In general, the paper lacks a deeper analysis of the results to provide insight into the model's behavior. The model treats the relationship between a phrase and its component words as analogous to the relationship between a concept and its representative phrases. However, these two types of composition are fundamentally different and each warrants its own dedicated analysis. For instance, the analysis around line 588, which discusses the relationship between phrases and their component words, would benefit from a more linguistic perspective. The observed behavior might be explained by factors such as the dominance of phrase headwords, which is not explored. The paper would also be stronger if it included an investigation into the hyperparameters that control the trade-off between the atomic and compositional representations of phrases and concepts.

**General Discussion:**
Due to the significant weaknesses outlined above, particularly concerning the evaluation methodology, I recommend rejecting this paper. I encourage the authors to address these issues, especially by strengthening their evaluation datasets and methodology, before resubmitting their work.
