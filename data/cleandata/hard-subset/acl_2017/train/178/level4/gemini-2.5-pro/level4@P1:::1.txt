### Summary

This paper presents a weakly-supervised method for jointly learning vector space representations for words, phrases, and concepts. The approach leverages an existing ontology (e.g., UMLS or YAGO) to identify textual phrases that represent canonical concepts. It then creates a "tagged" version of a corpus by replacing these phrases with unique identifiers. By training on both the original and tagged versions of the corpus in parallel, the model, an extension of skip-gram with negative sampling, learns embeddings for all three types of units in a shared semantic space. The method is evaluated on biomedical and general-domain corpora, with experiments on concept similarity, relatedness, and semantic type clustering.

In my view, the main contributions of this work are:

1.  **A new dataset for general-domain entity similarity and relatedness.** The authors have created and released a new, human-annotated evaluation benchmark for real-world entities. This is a valuable resource that addresses a notable gap, as most existing benchmarks focus on single-word similarity.
2.  **A practical, weakly-supervised method for learning concept embeddings.** The proposed method does not require manually annotated training data. It is shown to be competitive with more heavily supervised approaches, particularly on larger-scale evaluation datasets, while offering significantly greater vocabulary coverage.

### Strengths

1.  **Valuable New Resource:** The most significant contribution of this paper may be the new evaluation dataset. Existing popular datasets like WordSim-353 and SimLex-999 focus on single words, whereas this work provides a much-needed benchmark for evaluating representations of entities (people, places, organizations). This resource will be valuable for future research on concept and entity embedding. The paper also provides a clear description of the data collection methodology.

2.  **Effective Use of Distant Supervision:** The proposed model is simple and elegant, effectively using distant supervision from an ontology to learn concept embeddings from unannotated text. The results demonstrate that this approach is competitive with prior methods that rely on expensive, human-annotated resources, such as manually-coded clinical data or corpora tagged with concept identifiers. The method's scalability and reduced reliance on manual annotation are significant practical advantages.

3.  **Comprehensive Experiments and Analysis:** The authors conduct a thorough evaluation across two distinct domains (biomedical and general), using multiple standard datasets as well as their own. The analysis is not limited to similarity and relatedness tasks; the investigations into compositionality (Figures 3 and 4) and semantic type clustering (Table 6) provide deeper insights into the properties of the learned embedding space and the relationships between words, phrases, and concepts.

### Weaknesses

1.  **Limited Technical Novelty:** While effective, the core method is a fairly straightforward and natural extension of the standard skip-gram model. It combines the well-established ideas of distant supervision for entity learning with the word2vec training objective. While the application is novel and the implementation is solid, the underlying technique does not represent a substantial methodological leap, which may limit the paper's impact from a purely technical standpoint.

2.  **Biomedical Focus in Evaluation:** Although the paper includes experiments on a general-domain corpus using the new dataset, the evaluation is still heavily weighted towards the biomedical domain (three out of four corpora and multiple established medical datasets, versus one new general-domain dataset). This makes it more difficult to fully assess the generalizability of the findings. Broadening the evaluation to other domains or discussing the potential challenges of applying this method to ontologies with different structures would strengthen the paper's claims.

3.  **Incorrect Formulation in Analysis:** The paper's analysis of how well the embeddings preserve ontological links contains a minor but important technical error. In Section 5.4, Equation 8 defines the precision for a given item in a ranked list but misleadingly labels it as a probability (`Pr(...)`). Precision is a ratio, not a probability distribution, and this notation is mathematically incorrect. While this likely does not affect the implementation or the final MAP results, it should be corrected for clarity and accuracy.

### Questions to Authors

1.  Regarding Equation 8: As noted in the weaknesses, the use of `Pr(...)` to denote precision is incorrect. Can you please correct this notation and the corresponding explanation in the text? Furthermore, for the MAP calculation, did you consider using a standard cut-off (e.g., MAP@k) instead of ranking against the entire vocabulary? The latter can be computationally intensive and sensitive to noise from irrelevant items ranked very low.

### Additional Comments

The paper is generally well-written and easy to follow. I noted one minor grammatical error: "most concepts has" should be "most concepts have".
