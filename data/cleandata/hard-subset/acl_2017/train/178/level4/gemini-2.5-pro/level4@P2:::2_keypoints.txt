- Proposes a weakly-supervised method that jointly learns word, phrase, and concept embeddings by using an ontology to treat phrases as atomic tokens in a skip-gram with negative sampling framework.  
- Releases two new general-domain entity similarity/relatedness datasets (people, places, organizations).  
- Provides broad empirical evaluation in biomedical and general domains showing competitive performance on larger-scale similarity tasks.  

- Tackles lexical diversity and non-compositional phrases by learning single concept vectors informed by all phrasal forms.  
- Scalable, annotation-free approach that can embed very large concept sets (∼190k medical, ∼740k general-domain concepts).  
- Public release of code (cui2vec) and datasets supports reproducibility and community use.  

- Limited technical novelty: model is a relatively small modification of standard skip-gram rather than a major methodological advance.  
- Empirical results are not consistently superior; several baselines (CHOI, CHIU-30, APPROXPHRco) match or outperform the proposed model on key datasets.  
- The added concept layer EC may be unnecessary: APPROXPHRco (averaging phrase embeddings) outperforms CONCEPTco on general-domain tasks.  
- Compositionality is oversimplified via global hyperparameters (β, η), and the fact that optimal β is often zero suggests that component is not helpful.  

- Clarify the use of the term “distant supervision” and how it aligns with its conventional meaning.  
- Explain how similarity was computed for baselines that produce only word/phrase embeddings when concepts map to multiple phrases (aggregation method used).  
- Justify the inclusion of the separate concept embedding layer EC and provide evidence or tasks where dedicated concept vectors outperform simple phrase-averaging.
