### Summary

This paper presents a model for learning vector space embeddings for words, phrases, and concepts. The model leverages an ontology where each concept is mapped to a set of phrases. These phrases are identified in a text corpus and treated as single atomic units. Building on this foundation, the paper adapts the skip-gram method to simultaneously learn embeddings for words, these atomic phrases, and their associated concepts. The proposed model is evaluated on concept similarity and relatedness tasks using the UMLS and Yago ontologies.

### Strengths

The paper addresses the important challenge that many semantically related phrases are not lexically similar, and that not all phrases are compositional. To address this, the paper proposes a plausible model for learning phrase embeddings directly from text, guided by an external knowledge base. The resulting embeddings are shown to be competitive with, and in some cases superior to, previous methods on the task of measuring concept similarity.

Additionally, the release of the software implementation is a valuable contribution that could be particularly useful for researchers in the biomedical NLP community.

### Weaknesses

The primary weakness of the paper is the limited novelty of the proposed model, which is a direct extension of the skip-gram architecture.

Furthermore, the experimental results do not consistently support the superiority of the full proposed model over the baselines. For instance, in Table 4, the Choi et al. baseline performs substantially better on the two Mayo datasets. A similar trend is observable in Table 6. On the larger UMNSRS dataset, the proposed model is at best competitive with simpler, existing models (Chiu et al.). This suggests that the additional complexity of the model does not always translate to improved performance.

### General Discussion

**Clarity on "Distant Supervision":** The authors describe their method as using "distant supervision." This terminology is potentially misleading. As I understand it, the known phrases are used to identify contexts for training embeddings, not to provide labeled examples for a predictive task (e.g., classifying concepts in text). This is different from the conventional use of the term. Could the authors clarify their use of this term or consider a more precise alternative to avoid confusion?

**Unused Notation:** The notation for embedding matrices (e.g., E_W) introduced in Section 3.2 appears to be unused in the remainder of the paper. This could be removed for clarity.

**The Compositionality Parameter β:** The use of a single global hyperparameter, β, to control the compositionality of all phrases is a strong and perhaps counter-intuitive assumption, as it implies that all phrases share the same degree of compositionality. More surprisingly, the results of cross-validation (Table 3) show that the optimal value for β is zero for two of the three corpora (PM+CL and WikiNYT). This result suggests that a purely non-compositional model is optimal for these datasets, which seems to undermine the primary motivation for introducing a compositional component in the first place.

**Experimental Setup for Table 4:** The experimental setup for Table 4 requires clarification. The task is to measure the similarity between concepts, but the model produces embeddings for words, phrases, and concepts. Given the many-to-many mapping between concepts and phrases, it is unclear how the word and phrase embeddings are used to compute concept similarity. It would seem that only the concept vectors are directly applicable. Please clarify this procedure.

**Implications of Table 5 Results:** In Table 5, the "approximate phr" method, which represents a concept simply as the average of its associated phrase embeddings, achieves the best performance. This result raises a critical question: if a simple aggregation of phrase embeddings outperforms the explicitly learned concept embeddings, what is the added value of learning the concept embeddings directly? It suggests that one might achieve similar or better results by starting with a curated set of phrases and forgoing the use of the concept ontology altogether, which challenges a central premise of the work.
