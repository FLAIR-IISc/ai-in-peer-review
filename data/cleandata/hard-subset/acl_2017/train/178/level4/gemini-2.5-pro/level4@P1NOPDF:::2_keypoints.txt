- Paper proposes joint embeddings for words, atomic phrases, and concepts using an ontology and a skip-gram–style objective, evaluated on UMLS and Yago tasks.
- Treats concept-linked phrases as atomic units identified in corpus text for training.
- Addresses phrase semantics and non-compositionality, offering a plausible method for phrase embeddings.
- Resulting embeddings are competitive with, and sometimes superior to, existing methods on concept similarity tasks.
- Public release of the software is a valuable contribution for biomedical NLP researchers.
- Novelty is limited—model is primarily an adaptation of the skip-gram architecture.
- The full proposed model is not the best-performing variant in authors’ experiments; baselines (e.g., Choi et al., 2016) outperform it on some datasets.
- Describing the method as "distant supervision" is misleading; occurrences are used as skip-gram contexts rather than task-specific supervision.
- Introduced notation (e.g., E_W) appears unused and superfluous.
- Using a single global β to control compositionality is problematic; cross-validation often selects β=0, discarding the compositional component.
- Experimental details are unclear on how concept-level similarity is computed given many-to-many phrase–concept mappings.
- The "approximate phr" baseline (averaging phrase embeddings) outperforms concept vectors, questioning the need to learn separate concept embeddings.
