### **Summary**

This paper presents a model for jointly embedding words, phrases, and concepts into a shared vector space. The model leverages an external ontology where concepts are mapped to a set of corresponding phrases. These phrases are identified in a text corpus and treated as atomic units. Using this framework, the authors adapt the skip-gram method to train embeddings for words, these atomic phrases, and the concepts they are associated with. The proposed approach is evaluated on concept similarity and relatedness tasks using the UMLS and Yago ontologies.

### **Strengths**

1.  The paper addresses the important challenge that semantically related phrases are not always lexically similar, and that many phrases are non-compositional. The proposed model offers a plausible approach to learning phrase embeddings that accounts for these phenomena.
2.  The resulting embeddings are shown to be competitive with, and in some cases superior to, previous methods on the task of identifying concept similarity.
3.  The accompanying software release could be a valuable resource for researchers in biomedical NLP.

### **Weaknesses**

1.  The primary weakness of the paper is the limited novelty of the proposed model, which is largely an incremental modification of the skip-gram architecture.
2.  The full model presented by the authors does not consistently outperform the baselines in the experiments. As shown in Table 4, the Choi et al. baseline is substantially better on the two Mayo datasets. A similar trend is also observed in Table 6. On the larger UMNSRS dataset, the proposed model is, at best, competitive with simpler prior models (e.g., Chiu et al.).

### **General Discussion**

1.  **Use of "Distant Supervision":** The paper describes its use of known phrases as a form of "distant supervision." This terminology is potentially confusing. As I understand the method, each occurrence of an ontology-linked phrase provides a context for training word and concept embeddings. This differs from the typical use of distant supervision for creating labeled examples for a predictive task (e.g., relation extraction). The authors should clarify their use of this term.
2.  **Unused Notation:** The notation introduced in Section 3.2 (e.g., E_W) does not appear to be used in the remainder of the paper, making it superfluous.
3.  **The Compositionality Parameter β:** The use of a single global hyperparameter, β, to control the compositionality of all phrases is a strong and perhaps overly simplistic assumption, as it implies that all phrases share the same degree of compositionality. More surprising are the optimal values for β found via cross-validation (Table 3). For the PM+CL and WikiNYT datasets, the optimal β is zero, which effectively discards the compositional component entirely and seems to undermine one of the motivations for the model.
4.  **Clarity on Experimental Setup (Table 4):** The experimental setup for Table 4 requires clarification. The task involves measuring the similarity or relatedness of concepts. Given that the mapping from concepts to phrases can be many-to-many, it is unclear how phrase and word vectors are used to compute a final similarity score between two concepts. It seems that only the learned concept vectors could be used for this, but the paper does not explicitly state this.
5.  **Necessity of the Concept Ontology (Table 5):** In Table 5, the best-performing method is "approximate phr," which represents a concept vector as the average of its associated phrase vectors. This strong result raises a critical question about the necessity of the proposed concept embedding methodology. If a simple post-hoc averaging of phrase vectors is superior, it suggests that one might achieve similar or better results by simply identifying a seed set of phrases, forgoing the use of a concept ontology and the joint training framework altogether.
