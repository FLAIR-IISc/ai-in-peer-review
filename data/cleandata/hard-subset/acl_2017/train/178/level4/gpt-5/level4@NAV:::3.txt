Review- The authors introduce a method for jointly embedding words, phrases, and concepts using plain text corpora alongside a manually curated ontology, where each concept is linked to one or more phrases. They demonstrate the approach in the medical domain with UMLS and in the general domain with YAGO. For evaluation, they compare against simpler baselines and prior work, focusing primarily on intrinsic similarity and relatedness benchmarks. They employ existing medical-domain benchmarks and use Mechanical Turk to create a new general-domain concept similarity/relatedness dataset, which they plan to release. Their reported performance is on par with prior work.

Strengths:
- The proposed joint embedding framework is simple and intuitively reasonable. In my view, its chief merit is offering a tunable middle ground between modeling phrases as indivisible units and modeling their compositional structure. The same idea is extended to concepts, which are treated as being “composed” from multiple representative phrases.
- The paper reports a substantial amount of work, including model development, a new evaluation resource, and several evaluations and analyses.

Weaknesses:
- The evaluation is limited to intrinsic assessments, mostly similarity/relatedness datasets. As the authors acknowledge, such tests are weak predictors of downstream utility. It has recently become standard to include at least one or two extrinsic tasks when assessing embedding models.
- The similarity/relatedness datasets are framed as reflecting human judgments over concepts, but if I understand correctly, annotators were shown phrases. Thus these are effectively phrase similarity datasets and should be presented and analyzed accordingly.
- The ‘mini MayoSRS’ medical concept dataset is extremely small (29 pairs), and ‘MayoSRS’ is only slightly larger (101 pairs) with relatively low inter-annotator agreement. The ‘UMNSRS’ set is a more reasonable size, but it includes only concepts expressible as single words and was presented as such to annotators. This should be clearly stated, and it raises concerns about its relevance to phrase and general concept representations.
- The authors appear to tune hyperparameters extensively on the same datasets used for reporting final results and comparisons to prior work. This practice undermines the validity of the reported outcomes and analyses.
- The claim that the method outperforms prior work because it achieves comparable results with less manual annotation is not compelling. The approach also relies on large manually constructed ontologies, and the annotated dataset used in prior work was derived from existing clinical notes without bespoke annotation.
- I found the analysis lacking in explanatory insight beyond the reported numbers. The paper treats the relationship between a phrase and its constituent words, and between a concept and its alternative phrases, as analogous compositional relations. These are fundamentally different and merit separate analyses. For example, around line 588, I would expect an NLP-focused analysis specifically on phrase–word composition (e.g., headword effects). It would also strengthen the work to analyze how hyperparameters mediate the trade-off between atomic and compositional representations of phrases and concepts.

General Discussion:
Given the above weaknesses, I recommend rejection. I encourage the authors to strengthen their evaluation datasets and methodology before resubmission.
