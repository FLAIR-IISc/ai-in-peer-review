- Paper proposes a weakly supervised joint embedding of words, phrases, and ontology concepts using ontology-linked phrases as distant supervision and skip-gram extension with compositionality controls (β, η).

- Primary contributions: joint embedding framework with tunable compositionality, a new general-domain real-world entity similarity dataset, and empirical study showing competitive intrinsic performance and improved vocabulary coverage.

- Strength: Clear, interpretable extension of skip-gram with configurable β and η to trade off atomic vs. compositional representations.

- Strength: Leverages large ontologies and unlabeled corpora for distant supervision, achieving substantially larger concept vocabulary coverage (>3×).

- Strength: Competitive intrinsic similarity/relatedness results on biomedical (UMNSRS) and new general-domain datasets without manual corpus annotation.

- Strength: Analyses provided on compositionality, concept-phrase proximity, and semantic-type clustering indicate ontological structure is reflected in the learned space.

- Strength: New dataset fills a gap by providing human similarity/relatedness judgments for real-world entities; collection method and agreement stats documented.

- Weakness: Evaluation relies solely on intrinsic similarity/relatedness tasks, lacking extrinsic downstream evaluations to demonstrate practical usefulness.

- Weakness: Evaluations use surface phrases for judgments, conflating phrase and concept evaluation and making it hard to assess true concept representation quality.

- Weakness: Biomedical benchmarks are small or constrained (Mini MayoSRS, MayoSRS small/low IAA; UMNSRS limited to single-word items), limiting robustness of conclusions.

- Weakness: Hyperparameters (β, η, etc.) appear tuned on test sets, risking overfitting and undermining comparative validity.

- Weakness: Claim of reduced supervision is overstated given reliance on large curated ontologies (UMLS/YAGO) and some baselines using existing annotations.

- Weakness: Analysis of compositionality is insufficiently granular; phrase-word vs. concept-phrase relations need more targeted experiments (e.g., idiomaticity, headword effects).

- Question: How are overlapping or nested phrase matches handled during preprocessing (longest-first, tie resolution)?

- Question: Clarify skip-gram mechanics—do all words in a phrase serve as targets with surrounding contexts or only the last word?

- Question: Explain why a single |W|×k negative sampling matrix is used for both positive contexts and negatives and how phrase/concept contexts are projected.

- Question: Which tasks/datasets were used to select hyperparameters and were dev splits used; can fixed-hyperparameter results be provided?

- Question: Were annotators shown phrases, concept IDs, or both; can evaluations isolating concept-to-concept comparisons be provided?

- Question: Can the authors provide β and η sensitivity analyses for compositional vs. idiomatic phrases and concepts with varying numbers of surface forms?

- Question: Will the new datasets include standard train/dev/test splits to avoid tuning on test sets?

- Minor: Multiple textual/notation issues to fix (typos, unclear phrasing about targets, matrix dimensions, notation consistency, missing ref).

- Minor: Suggest justifying the 1–100 rating scale or reporting effects of binning given observed IAA.

- Minor: Table and plotting suggestions—plot dev-set performance vs. β, η, window, dimension to clarify trends.

- Overall recommendation: Reject in current form; strengthen by adding extrinsic tasks, clarifying training/evaluation protocols, avoiding tuning on test sets, and deepening compositionality analyses.
