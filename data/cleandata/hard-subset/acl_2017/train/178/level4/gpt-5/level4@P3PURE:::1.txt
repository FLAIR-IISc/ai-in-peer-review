This paper extends word-embedding methods to produce representations not only for words but also for phrases and concepts corresponding to those words. The approach assigns a single identifier to each set of phrases, words, and the underlying concept they denote; replaces occurrences of those phrases and words with the identifier to create a “tagged” corpus; and then appends this tagged corpus to the original corpus for training. The concept/phrase/word sets are drawn from an ontology. Because the application domain is biomedical, the work relies on biomedical corpora and ontologies. The paper also introduces a new test dataset for word similarity and relatedness for real-world entities, which is a novel contribution.

Overall, the paper is clearly written. The technique is natural, though not a particularly substantial contribution. The scope is limited by the evaluation being confined to the biomedical domain. More discussion of the newly generated test resource would strengthen the paper; indeed, this resource may be the most interesting contribution.

There is one small technical issue, likely more about mathematical presentation than implementation. In Equation 8, the authors aim to define a MAP calculation. This is a good idea, though a natural cut-off could be used instead of ranking the entire vocabulary. However, Equation 8 does not define a probability; this can be shown even if the size of the vocabulary is infinite. The explanation should be adjusted accordingly—remove language that frames the expression as a probability.
