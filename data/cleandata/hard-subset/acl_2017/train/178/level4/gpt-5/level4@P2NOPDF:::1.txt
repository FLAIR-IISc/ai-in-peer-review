This paper extends word embeddings to also provide representations for multiword phrases and ontology-defined concepts. The method assigns a single identifier to each set of synonymous phrases, words, and the underlying concept; occurrences of these items in the training corpus are replaced with that identifier to produce a tagged corpus, which is then concatenated with the original corpus for training. The concept sets are sourced from a biomedical ontology, consistent with the application domain. The authors also introduce a new evaluation dataset for similarity and relatedness among real-world entities, which is a notable contribution.

Overall, the paper is clearly written. The technique is sensible and straightforward, but it feels incremental rather than substantial. The empirical scope is limited by the focus on the biomedical domain, which makes it difficult to assess broader applicability.

The newly created evaluation resource may be the most interesting contribution. Providing more detail about its design, coverage, annotation protocol, inter-annotator agreement, and intended use cases would significantly strengthen the paper. If feasible, discussing plans for public release and maintenance would also help establish its value to the community.

Technical issue:
- Equation 8: The intent to define a MAP objective is good. However, ranking the entire vocabulary seems unnecessary; a natural cutoff (e.g., top-K or threshold-based negatives) could be used instead. More importantly, as written, Eq. 8 does not define a probability distribution. This remains true regardless of whether the vocabulary is finite or infinite. Please revise the explanation to avoid framing it as a probability and clarify what is being optimized.
