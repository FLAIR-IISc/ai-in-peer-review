Summary:
This paper proposes a method to embed words, multiword phrases, and ontology concepts into a shared vector space. It leverages an ontology mapping concepts to surface phrases, treats these phrases as atomic tokens in text corpora, and trains embeddings with a Skip-gram–style objective. The approach is evaluated on concept similarity and relatedness tasks, using UMLS and YAGO as underlying ontologies.

Strengths:
- The paper directly addresses the issue that lexically dissimilar phrases can be semantically close and that many phrases are non-compositional. The proposed approach to phrase-level embeddings is reasonable for this setting.
- The learned embeddings perform competitively, and sometimes better, on concept similarity tasks.
- The released software could be valuable for biomedical NLP researchers.

Weaknesses:
- The methodological novelty is limited; the approach is largely a variant of Skip-gram.
- The full proposed model is not consistently superior in the experiments. In Table 4, the Choi baseline substantially outperforms it on the two Mayo datasets, and Table 6 shows a similar pattern. On the larger UMNSRS dataset, the model is at best on par with simpler prior methods (e.g., Chiu).

General Discussion:
- The paper frames the use of known phrases as distant supervision, but the supervision signal is unclear. As I understand it, each occurrence of a phrase linked to a concept provides contexts for training embeddings. However, this is not supervision in the usual sense of labeled predictive tasks (e.g., concept identification). The terminology is therefore confusing and would benefit from clarification.
- The notation introduced in Section 3.2 (e.g., E_W, etc.) is never used subsequently and could be streamlined or integrated into later sections for coherence.
- The use of a single global beta parameter to control the compositionality of phrases is surprising. This implies a uniform degree of compositionality across all phrases, which seems overly restrictive. The cross-validated beta values in Table 3 amplify this concern: for PM+CL and WikiNYT, beta is zero, effectively arguing against compositionality in those settings.
- The experimental setup for Table 4 needs clarification. The datasets annotate similarity/relatedness between concepts (entities). Given the many-to-many mapping between concepts and phrases, how are word or phrase vectors used to compute these similarities? It seems only concept vectors can be used directly; if phrase or word vectors are involved, the procedure should be explicitly described.
- In Table 5, the approximate phr variant—representing a concept by averaging its phrase vectors—performs best. This raises the question of whether the explicit concept ontology is necessary for the reported gains. Could similar performance be achieved by starting from a seed set of phrases without relying on the ontology mappings?
