Summary and contributions:
This paper proposes a weakly supervised approach to jointly learn embeddings for concepts, phrases, and words by leveraging ontology mappings (UMLS, YAGO) and unlabeled text. Phrases that denote ontology concepts are treated as atomic tokens in a skip-gram–style model, and concept vectors are trained from the contexts of their representative phrases. The method is evaluated on concept similarity and relatedness in biomedical and general domains and is released as open-source software.

As I see it, the main contributions (ordered by strength) are:
1) A scalable training scheme that produces concept, phrase, and word embeddings without manual corpus annotation, achieving broad coverage and competitive performance on larger similarity/relatedness benchmarks.
2) Release of software (cui2vec) and a new crowdsourced dataset for similarity/relatedness among real-world entities.
3) An empirical study comparing direct concept embeddings to several approximations (phrase and word averages) across multiple corpora and ontologies.

Strengths:
- Scalable, annotation-free setup with strong coverage: The use of ontology phrase lists as distant supervision enables training from raw text without hand-labeling, yielding substantially larger coverage than prior concept-embedding methods. This is valuable for domains like biomedicine where manual annotation is costly.
- Competitive performance on larger datasets: On UMNSRS, the jointly learned concept/phrase embeddings match or outperform strong baselines on similarity and are competitive on relatedness, despite requiring less supervision than methods such as CHOI that rely on manually annotated codes. This supports the claim that the approach is practically useful at scale.
- Unified space for words, phrases, and concepts: The model produces a shared space that supports cross-level comparisons (e.g., phrase-to-word, concept-to-phrase), enabling analyses such as compositionality and type clustering and potentially supporting downstream tasks like entity linking.
- Useful resources for the community: The released implementation and the new general-domain similarity/relatedness datasets should be valuable to researchers, particularly in biomedical NLP where data resources are comparatively scarce.

Weaknesses:
- Limited methodological novelty: Conceptually, the approach extends skip-gram with negative sampling in a relatively straightforward way, treating mapped phrases as atomic tokens and averaging contexts for concepts. The compositionality regularizers (β, η) are simple global terms. The core modeling contribution appears incremental relative to prior word/phrase/entity embedding work.
- Mixed empirical gains vs. strong baselines: On the Mayo datasets (Table 4), CHOI clearly outperforms the proposed method, and Table 6 shows that CHOI also has superior semantic type clustering overall. Even on UMNSRS, improvements are modest and sometimes equaled by simpler word-based baselines (e.g., CHIU-30). This weakens the case that the proposed learning of concept vectors offers consistent advantages over existing approaches.
- Concept vectors sometimes underperform phrase-based approximations: In the general-domain experiments (Table 5), approximating concepts by averaging their phrase vectors outperforms direct concept embeddings. This raises the question of whether explicit concept-level parameters are necessary, especially when phrase-level modeling suffices or excels.
- Ambiguity around “distant supervision” and training signals: The paper frames the use of phrase matches as “distant supervision,” but no supervised labels are exploited beyond phrase–concept mappings; occurrences are treated as positive contexts rather than supervised targets. The terminology is potentially misleading, and the supervision signal for concepts (especially in cases of polysemous phrases) would benefit from clearer justification and analysis.
- Modeling choices and notation clarity: The global β and η implicitly assume a uniform degree of phrase- and concept-level compositionality across the vocabulary. Cross-validated choices often set β=0 (e.g., PM+CL, WIKINYT), suggesting the model learns best with no phrase–word compositionality term, which is surprising. Moreover, the matrices introduced in Section 3.2 (e.g., EW, EP, EC) are not subsequently referenced in the derivations, which could be streamlined for clarity.

Questions to authors:
- Clarify “distant supervision”: In what sense is the training signal supervisory beyond phrase lexicons? Is there any disambiguation of phrases that map to multiple concepts, or are all candidate concept embeddings updated equally? Have you measured how this ambiguity affects performance?
- Similarity/relatedness computation: For datasets that specify concepts/entities, how exactly are phrase or word vectors used when the mapping is many-to-many? Do you average over all phrases per concept, select canonical phrases, or always use concept vectors? Please detail the protocol for each row in Tables 4 and 5.
- Necessity of concept embeddings: Given that APPROXPHR (averaging phrase vectors) often outperforms direct concept vectors, what are the practical benefits of maintaining separate concept parameters? Could a simpler pipeline (phrase-only training + aggregation) achieve similar results with fewer parameters?
- Compositionality hyperparameters: What motivated global β and η rather than adapting them per phrase/concept or per semantic type? Do you observe performance gains if β/η are learned or conditioned on frequency, ambiguity, or phrase length?
- Evaluation splits and tuning: You mention adapted 5-fold cross-validation for hyperparameter tuning on intrinsic tasks. Could you share per-fold variance and confirm that no test labels from the held-out fold influenced tuning? Any extrinsic evaluations (e.g., entity linking) to corroborate the intrinsic results?

Overall assessment:
The paper offers a practical and well-executed approach to learning large-coverage concept/phrase/word embeddings without manual annotation and provides useful resources. However, the modeling novelty is limited, and the empirical gains over strong baselines are inconsistent, with phrase-average approximations often rivaling or exceeding direct concept vectors. Clarifying the supervision framing, evaluation protocols, and the role of concept parameters would strengthen the contribution.
