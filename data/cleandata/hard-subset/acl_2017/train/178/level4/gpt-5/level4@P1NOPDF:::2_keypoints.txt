- Proposal: embed words, phrases, and concepts in a shared vector space by treating ontology-linked phrases as atomic tokens and training a skip-gram-style model; evaluated with UMLS and YAGO.

- Strength: Addresses non-compositional and lexically dissimilar phrases by modeling phrases as atomic units.

- Strength: Treating ontology-linked phrases as atomic provides a plausible method for learning phrase embeddings.

- Strength: Learned embeddings are competitive and sometimes superior on concept similarity tasks.

- Strength: Released software could benefit biomedical NLP researchers.

- Weakness: Limited novelty — essentially a variant of skip-gram.

- Weakness: Full proposed model is not consistently best; Choi baseline outperforms on Mayo datasets and similar declines appear elsewhere.

- Weakness: On larger UMNSRS dataset the method is at best competitive with simpler prior models (e.g., Chiu).

- Critique: Calling phrase occurrences "distant supervision" is misleading, since they primarily provide context rather than predictive supervision.

- Critique: Introduced notation (e.g., E_W) is unused later in the paper.

- Critique: Using a single global beta for compositionality is unrealistic; cross-validated zeros (Table 3) are puzzling and argue against compositionality.

- Issue: Experimental setup for Table 4 is unclear — how are many-to-many concept–phrase mappings handled when computing concept similarity?

- Concern: Table 5 shows averaging phrase vectors (approximate phr) performs best, raising the question of whether the explicit ontology is necessary versus starting from a seed set of phrases.
