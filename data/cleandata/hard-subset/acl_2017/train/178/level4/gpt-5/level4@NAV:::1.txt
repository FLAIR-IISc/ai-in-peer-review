The paper extends standard word embedding techniques to also learn representations for phrases and the underlying concepts that the words denote. The approach assigns a single identifier to each set containing a concept and all phrases/words referring to it, replaces occurrences of those phrases/words with the identifier to produce a “tagged” corpus, and then concatenates this tagged corpus with the original corpus for training. The concept/phrase/word groupings are derived from an ontology. Given the biomedical focus, the corresponding corpora and ontologies from this domain are employed. The authors also introduce a new evaluation set for word similarity and relatedness involving real-world entities, which is novel.

Overall, the paper is well written. The technique is fairly natural, though not a particularly substantial advance. The contribution’s scope is limited due to the evaluation being confined to the biomedical domain. More discussion of the newly created test resource would be valuable. That resource may, in fact, be the most compelling contribution of the paper.

There is one minor technical issue, likely a matter of mathematical presentation rather than implementation.

Technical problem:
Eq. 8: The authors aim to define the MAP calculation. This is a good idea, though I believe a natural cutoff could be used rather than ranking the entire vocabulary. Equation 8 does not define a probability; this is straightforward to show, even in the case of an infinite vocabulary. The explanation should therefore be revised to avoid referring to it as a probability.
