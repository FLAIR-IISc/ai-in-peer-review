Summary and contributions
This paper proposes a weakly supervised method to jointly embed ontology concepts, their representative phrases, and words into a shared vector space. The key idea is to use ontology-derived phrase-to-concept mappings as distant supervision: occurrences of mapped phrases in an unlabeled corpus are replaced with unique identifiers in a “tagged” version of the corpus, and embeddings are trained over both the original and tagged text. The authors evaluate on biomedical corpora (UMLS concepts with PubMed and clinical notes) and on a general-domain corpus (YAGO entities), and they introduce a new human-annotated dataset for similarity and relatedness between real-world entities.

As I see them, the main contributions are:
1) A simple, weakly supervised framework for jointly learning embeddings of concepts, phrases, and words without manual corpus annotation.
2) A new evaluation dataset for entity-level similarity and relatedness in the general domain.
3) Empirical evidence that the approach achieves competitive performance on concept similarity/relatedness with substantially larger coverage than prior concept-embedding methods.

Strengths
- Clear and practical method: The approach is straightforward to implement, requires no manual annotation of the training corpus, and leverages widely available ontologies (UMLS, YAGO). Training on parallel original and phrase-tagged corpora is an intuitive way to tie concepts and phrases to contextual evidence.
- Competitive performance with broad coverage: Results on standard biomedical similarity/relatedness benchmarks are broadly competitive with prior work that relies on human annotations. The reported coverage (more than 3× larger vocabulary) is a tangible practical advantage for downstream applications in domains with long-tail terminology.
- Cross-domain evidence and a new dataset: While the modeling focus is biomedical, the authors also train on a general-domain corpus and release a new entity similarity/relatedness dataset. This resource could be valuable to the community, where entity-level intrinsic evaluations are relatively scarce.

Weaknesses
- Incremental novelty: The technique, while sensible, feels like a natural extension of skip-gram training with distant supervision from known phrase-to-concept mappings. The methodological contribution is modest rather than conceptually substantial.
- Narrow empirical scope: Most evaluation centers on biomedical datasets; the general-domain experiments are primarily used to motivate the new dataset rather than to stress-test the method at scale across diverse entity types and relations. The paper would benefit from broader analysis outside the biomedical setting.
- Underdeveloped discussion of the new resource: The entity similarity/relatedness dataset is potentially the most impactful contribution, yet its description and analysis are comparatively brief. More detail on construction choices, quality control, and recommended usage (e.g., splits, known pitfalls, reliability by category) would strengthen the paper.
- Technical issue in Eq. 8 (MAP computation): The expression labeled as a “probability” is not a probability. It is an average-precision component derived from ranked retrieval, not a normalized probability distribution; the current wording is mathematically inaccurate. Additionally, while ranking against the entire vocabulary is defensible, a natural cutoff (e.g., top-k) could be considered to reduce noise and align with practical retrieval scenarios.

Questions to authors (optional)
- Could you clarify the intended interpretation of Eq. 8 and revise the “probability” terminology accordingly? If you keep full-vocabulary ranking, why is that preferable to a top-k cutoff for evaluating concept–phrase and phrase–word neighborhood fidelity?
- For the new entity similarity/relatedness dataset, can you provide more details on annotation protocols, recommended train/dev/test splits, and known areas of low annotator agreement (e.g., by entity type or relation)?
