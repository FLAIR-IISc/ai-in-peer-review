The paper extends word embedding methods to provide representations not only for words but also for phrases and the concepts they denote. The approach assigns a single identifier to each set comprising an ontology concept and its synonymous surface forms (words and phrases). All occurrences of these forms in the training corpus are replaced with this identifier, producing a “tagged” corpus, which is then concatenated with the original corpus for training. The concept/phrase/word sets are drawn from a biomedical ontology, and the corpora used are from the biomedical domain. The authors also introduce a new test dataset for word similarity and relatedness for real-world entities, which is a novel contribution.

Overall, the paper is clearly written. The technique is natural and practical, though the methodological contribution is not very substantial. The scope is limited by the focus on the biomedical domain and the corresponding evaluation. More discussion of the new test resource would be valuable; this resource may in fact be the paper’s most interesting contribution.

There is one small technical issue, likely a matter of mathematical exposition rather than implementation. Equation 8 aims to define a MAP calculation, which is a good idea; however, a natural cutoff could be used instead of ranking the entire vocabulary. As written, Equation 8 does not define a probability; it is straightforward to show this, even if the size of the vocabulary is infinite. Please revise the explanation to avoid referring to it as a probability.
