Summary
This paper presents a weakly supervised method to jointly learn embeddings for ontology concepts, their surface phrases, and words. The approach replaces occurrences of ontology-linked phrases in an unlabeled corpus with unique phrase identifiers to form a tagged corpus, and trains a skip-gram model with negative sampling over both the original and tagged corpora. Concept vectors are updated via the contexts of their surface forms, with optional regularizers encouraging phrase-by-word and concept-by-phrase compositionality (controlled by β and η). The method is evaluated primarily in the biomedical domain (UMLS, PubMed, and clinical notes), and secondarily on general-domain entities (YAGO over Wikipedia+NYT). The paper reports competitive intrinsic results on concept similarity and relatedness, substantially larger vocabulary coverage than prior medical concept embeddings, qualitative analyses of concept–phrase–word compositional structure, and a new crowdsourced dataset for entity similarity and relatedness.

Contributions (as I see them)
1) A simple, scalable weakly supervised training scheme that jointly embeds concepts, phrases, and words without manual corpus annotation, by leveraging ontology phrase forms as distant supervision.
2) An empirical study across multiple corpora showing competitive performance on intrinsic concept similarity/relatedness tasks and markedly higher coverage compared to prior biomedical concept embeddings, along with analyses of compositionality and semantic type clustering.
3) A new general-domain dataset of human judgments for entity similarity and relatedness.

Strengths
- Practical, scalable approach with no manual annotation: The method leverages existing ontologies and unlabeled text, yielding more than 3x vocabulary coverage relative to prior biomedical concept embeddings. This is a meaningful practical benefit for applications requiring broad concept coverage.
- Competitive intrinsic performance: On larger biomedical datasets (UMNSRS Similarity/Relatedness), jointly trained concept/phrase embeddings achieve correlations on par with or exceeding strong baselines, while not relying on manually coded sequences (e.g., ICD9). For general-domain entities, the phrase-based approximations also perform strongly.
- Joint embedding and analyses: The paper evaluates how learned vectors preserve concept–phrase and phrase–word relationships and examines semantic type clustering. The observed proximity of concepts to their phrase means and the mixed, but sometimes strong, clustering by semantic type provide informative diagnostics about what the model learns.
- Clear experimental setup and comparisons: The paper compares to multiple published biomedical embeddings (e.g., De Vine, Choi, Mencia) and to word-level baselines trained under reasonable hyperparameters, and it reports hyperparameter sensitivity and tuned settings.
- New resource: The crowdsourced entity similarity/relatedness dataset fills a gap for evaluating embeddings at the entity level beyond words and phrases.

Weaknesses
- Limited novelty in modeling: Conceptually, the approach is a natural extension of skip-gram with distant supervision via phrase replacement. While useful, the methodological contribution is incremental. The β/η compositionality terms are straightforward, and the paper itself notes they often do not help and can harm performance.
- Evaluation scope and reliance on intrinsic tasks: Results are largely intrinsic and biomedical; downstream tasks (e.g., entity linking, concept normalization, retrieval, or clinical prediction) are not evaluated. This limits evidence of practical utility and generalization beyond similarity/relatedness.
- Assumptions about surface-form mentions and ambiguity: The distant supervision assumes any occurrence of a mapped phrase denotes any of its possible concepts (uniformly). This can introduce substantial noise, especially for ambiguous forms and domain shift. Although the model normalizes updates by the number of candidate concepts, the impact of ambiguity is not quantified (e.g., performance versus ambiguity level).
- Hyperparameter tuning on test sets: The paper acknowledges tuning hyperparameters on the same datasets used for evaluation (albeit with an adapted 5-fold), which risks optimistic bias. A clean development/test split or external validation would strengthen claims.
- General-domain dataset quality and utility: Fleiss’ κ = 0.24 indicates only fair agreement, which constrains the reliability of conclusions drawn from these data. Moreover, the finding that phrase-based concept approximations outperform direct concept embeddings suggests the concept-level signal beyond surface forms may be limited in this setting.
- Analyses reveal heavy reliance on surface forms: The high similarity between concepts and the average of their phrases, and mixed phrase–word proximity, suggest the model captures surface-form co-occurrence more than non-lexical concept information. Incorporating ontology structure (hierarchies/relations) could address this but is not attempted.

Questions to Authors
- Ambiguity handling: How often do phrases map to multiple concepts in your training sets, and what is the observed effect on learned vectors and downstream correlations? Have you tried non-uniform weighting across candidate concepts (e.g., by prior frequency or context cues)?
- Tuning and splits: Can you provide results with a strict development/test split (no cross-fold reuse of test pairs for tuning) to rule out overfitting? Alternatively, report results with fixed default hyperparameters.
- Role of β and η: In which conditions (corpus/tasks) did β or η meaningfully help? Could you provide ablations and intuition (e.g., for highly compositional phrases) where these regularizers are beneficial?
- Ontology structure: Have you explored adding ontology edges (e.g., parent–child, related-to) as an auxiliary objective, as in joint KG–text models, to capture non-lexical concept relations?
- Dataset release: Will the new entity similarity/relatedness datasets and code be publicly released, including annotation instructions and quality-control details?

Additional comments and minor issues
- Equation 8: The quantity labeled Pr(c|Cp, V) is not a probability. It is a precision-at-rank term used in average precision. Please remove probabilistic language and define it as precision at the rank of c within the ranked list; then define MAP in the standard way as the mean of average precision over queries.
- Consider adding a cut-off analysis (e.g., precision@k) rather than ranking the entire vocabulary for the MAP-style evaluations, which better reflects practical retrieval scenarios.
- Typo: “most concepts has” → “most concepts have.”
