Summary:
This paper extends word embedding methods to jointly learn representations for words, multiword phrases, and the underlying concepts they denote. The approach maps synonymous words and phrases to a shared concept identifier derived from an ontology, replaces their occurrences with that identifier to produce a “tagged” corpus, and appends this tagged corpus to the original text for training. The work focuses on the biomedical domain, leveraging domain-specific corpora and ontologies. The authors also introduce a new evaluation dataset for entity-level similarity and relatedness, which is novel and potentially valuable.

Strengths:
- Clear, well-written presentation overall.
- Simple, practical method that leverages existing ontological resources with minimal engineering overhead.
- Concept-level embeddings are useful for downstream tasks that require entity normalization and synonym handling.
- The new evaluation resource for real-world entities addresses a gap in current benchmarks.

Limitations and concerns:
- The core technique is natural and incremental rather than a substantial methodological advance.
- The evaluation is limited to the biomedical domain, leaving generality and portability to other domains unclear.
- The new test resource appears under-discussed; more details are needed to assess its construction quality, reliability, and scope.

Technical comment (Equation 8):
- The attempt to define MAP over a ranked list is sensible for evaluation, but ranking the entire vocabulary may be less realistic than adopting a cutoff (e.g., MAP@k), which better matches retrieval scenarios and reduces noise from the long tail.
- As written, Equation 8 does not define a probability distribution. Please remove any language suggesting that it is a probability. If a probabilistic interpretation is intended, the expression would need to be reformulated accordingly; otherwise, present it strictly as an evaluation metric without probabilistic claims.

Suggestions for improvement:
- Provide a more detailed description of the new evaluation resource, including:
  - Construction protocol (source selection, concept mapping, and sampling),
  - Annotation guidelines and scale (similarity vs. relatedness),
  - Quality control (inter-annotator agreement, adjudication),
  - Coverage statistics and concept type distribution,
  - Licensing and availability to facilitate reuse.
- Include ablations to clarify where the gains come from:
  - Original corpus only vs. tagged corpus only vs. the combined (appended) corpus,
  - Sensitivity to the proportion/weight of tagged data relative to original text,
  - Impact of ontology coverage and noise (e.g., handling polysemy or ambiguous mappings).
- Discuss failure cases where collapsing different surface forms to a single concept identifier could be harmful (e.g., near-synonyms with important distinctions, context-dependent senses).
- Strengthen comparisons against alternative ways to inject semantic knowledge, such as retrofitting, joint text-graph embeddings, phrase- or subword-level models, and concept normalization pipelines, to position the contribution more clearly.
- If feasible, add a small cross-domain experiment or analysis (even a pilot) to indicate how portable the approach might be beyond biomedicine.

Clarity and presentation:
- The paper is generally easy to follow. It would help to define key terms (concept, entity, CUI/identifier) early and use them consistently throughout.
- Provide implementation details that aid reproducibility: the mapping dictionary used, preprocessing/normalization steps, training settings, and how the tagged and original corpora are combined (e.g., simple concatenation vs. weighting).

Minor corrections:
- Line 556: “most concepts has” → “most concepts have”.

Overall assessment:
The paper presents a practical and well-executed idea with promising utility, particularly for domains rich in ontological knowledge. While the methodological novelty is modest, the concept-level perspective and the new evaluation resource are valuable. Expanding the discussion and documentation of the dataset, clarifying the evaluation metric in Equation 8, and strengthening analysis and baselines would significantly improve the paper’s impact and credibility.
