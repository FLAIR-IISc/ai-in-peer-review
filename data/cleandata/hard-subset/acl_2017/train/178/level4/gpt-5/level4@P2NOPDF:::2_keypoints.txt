- Proposes embedding words, multiword phrases, and ontology concepts into a shared vector space by mapping ontology concepts to surface phrases, treating phrases as atomic tokens, and training with a Skip-gram–style objective, evaluated on UMLS and YAGO concept similarity/relatedness tasks.

- Strength: Directly addresses lexically dissimilar and non-compositional phrases by learning phrase-level embeddings, which is a reasonable approach for this problem.

- Strength: Learned embeddings perform competitively and sometimes outperform baselines on concept similarity tasks.

- Strength: Released software could be valuable for biomedical NLP researchers.

- Weakness: Limited methodological novelty — the approach is largely a variant of Skip-gram.

- Weakness: The full model is not consistently superior; Choi baseline substantially outperforms it on the Mayo datasets and similar patterns appear in other results; on UMNSRS it is at best comparable to simpler methods like Chiu.

- Terminology/confusion: Framing the use of known phrases as “distant supervision” is unclear — occurrences provide context but not supervision in the usual labeled predictive-task sense, so this should be clarified.

- Exposition issue: Notation introduced in Section 3.2 (e.g., E_W) is never used again and should be streamlined or integrated for coherence.

- Modeling concern: Using a single global beta parameter to control phrase compositionality is surprising and likely overly restrictive; cross-validated beta values of zero on some corpora amplify this concern.

- Experimental clarity needed: Table 4’s setup is unclear — given many-to-many mappings between concepts and phrases, the procedure for computing concept similarity from word/phrase vectors must be explicitly described.

- Question about necessity of ontology: Table 5’s best-performing “approximate phr” (averaging phrase vectors) suggests the explicit concept ontology may not be necessary; similar gains might be achievable from a seed phrase set without ontology mappings.
