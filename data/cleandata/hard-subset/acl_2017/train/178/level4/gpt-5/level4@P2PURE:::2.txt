Summary:
This paper proposes a model to embed words, phrases, and concepts in a shared vector space. It relies on an ontology of concepts, each mapped to phrases found in text corpora and treated as atomic tokens. Using this setup, the approach essentially applies the skip-gram method to train embeddings for words, for these atomic phrases, and for their associated concepts. The model is evaluated on concept similarity and relatedness tasks, using UMLS and Yago as the underlying ontologies.

Strengths:
- The paper tackles the problem that lexically dissimilar phrases can be semantically similar and that many phrases are non-compositional. It proposes a plausible approach to learning phrase embeddings, and the resulting embeddings are competitive or better at identifying concept similarity.
- The released software could be useful for biomedical NLP researchers.

Weaknesses:
- The main weakness is limited novelty; the model is largely a tweak of skip-gram.
- The full model is not consistently the best performer. In Table 4, on the two Mayo datasets, the Choi baseline is substantially better, and a similar trend appears in Table 6. On the larger UMNSRS dataset, the proposed model is at best competitive with simpler prior models (e.g., Chiu).

General Discussion:
- The paper claims to use known phrases as distant supervision for training embeddings, but it is unclear what constitutes “supervision” here. If my understanding is correct, each occurrence of a phrase associated with a concept provides context for training word embeddings. This is not supervision in the usual sense (e.g., for concept identification or other predictive tasks), so the terminology is confusing.
- The notation introduced in Section 3.2 (e.g., E_W) is never used elsewhere in the paper.
- The use of a single global beta to control phrase compositionality is surprising, as it implies a universal constant determines how compositional any phrase should be. Moreover, the cross-validated beta values in Table 3 are puzzling: for PM+CL and WikiNYT, beta is zero, effectively arguing against compositionality.
- The experimental setup for Table 4 requires clarification. The datasets label similarity/relatedness of concepts (entities). If the concept–phrase mapping is many-to-many, how are word/phrase vectors used to compute similarities? It appears only concept vectors can be used.
- In Table 5, the approximate phr method (which approximates a concept by averaging its phrase vectors) performs best. This calls into question the necessity of the concept ontology; starting with a seed set of phrases might yield the same results.
