Summary:
This paper presents a model for embedding words, phrases, and concepts into vector spaces. It leverages an ontology of concepts, each mapped to multiple phrases. These phrases are identified in text corpora and treated as atomic symbols. Using this setup, the paper applies an approach essentially equivalent to skip-gram to train embeddings for words, the atomic phrases, and their associated concepts. The work is evaluated on concept similarity and relatedness tasks, using UMLS and YAGO as the backing ontologies.

Strengths:
- The paper addresses the important observation that phrases that are not lexically similar can be semantically close, and that many phrases are not compositional. It proposes a plausible model to train phrase embeddings under this assumption.
- The resulting embeddings are shown to be competitive or better for identifying similarity between concepts in some settings.
- The released software could be useful for biomedical NLP researchers.

Weaknesses:
- The model is not particularly novel; it is essentially a tweak to skip-gram.
- The full proposed model does not appear to perform best in the reported results (Table 4). On the two Mayo datasets, the Choi baseline is substantially stronger. A similar trend seems to hold in Table 6. On the larger UMNSRS data, the proposed model is at best competitive with simpler prior models (Chiu).

General Discussion:
- The paper describes using known phrases as distant supervision to train embeddings, but it is unclear what constitutes the “supervision.” If every occurrence of a phrase associated with a concept merely provides context for training embeddings, that does not amount to supervision in the traditional sense (e.g., for predicting the concept in text). The terminology is therefore confusing and should be clarified.
- The notation introduced in Section 3.2 (e.g., E_W) is not used subsequently in the paper.
- The use of a single global β to control the compositionality of phrases by words is surprising. This implies a universal constant governing how compositional any phrase should be. Moreover, the cross-validated β values in Table 3 are unexpected: for PM+CL and WikiNYT, β is zero, which effectively argues against compositionality in those settings.
- The experimental setup for Table 4 requires clarification. The datasets label similarity/relatedness of concepts (or entities). If the concept–phrase mapping is many-to-many, how are phrase and word vectors used to compute similarities? It seems that only concept vectors would be applicable, but the paper does not make this explicit.
- In Table 5, the approximate phr method (which approximates a concept by averaging the embeddings of its phrases) performs best. This calls into question the necessity of the concept ontology in the proposed approach; starting with a seed set of phrases might achieve similar results without the ontology.
