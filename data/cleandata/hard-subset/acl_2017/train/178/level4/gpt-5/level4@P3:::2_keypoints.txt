- Proposed method: weakly supervised pipeline that replaces matched phrases with IDs and jointly trains word, phrase, and concept embeddings via adapted skip-gram with negative sampling.
- Two global hyperparameters (β, η) control compositionality between words→phrases and phrases→concepts.
- Evaluations: tested on biomedical and general-domain similarity/relatedness benchmarks, introduced a new human-annotated entity dataset, and released code (cui2vec).
- Contribution: scalable, annotation-free approach using ontology phrase lists to achieve much larger vocabulary coverage than prior concept-embedding work.
- Contribution: resource release (software and new similarity/relatedness dataset) and broad experiments across multiple corpora and ontologies.
- Contribution: analyses of compositionality and semantic-type clustering showing ontology structure partly emerges in embedding geometry.
- Strength: high scalability and coverage without manual annotation, practical for domains with expensive labeling (e.g., clinical notes).
- Strength: competitive or state-of-the-art performance on some benchmarks (e.g., UMNSRS Similarity) and reasonable general-domain results.
- Strength: joint embedding space enables cross-granularity comparisons and probing analyses (concept–phrase, phrase–word).
- Strength: comprehensive experimental breadth and reproducibility via released resources.
- Strength: thoughtful analyses of hyperparameters, compositionality, and semantic-type clustering beyond headline metrics.
- Weakness: limited methodological novelty — mainly an engineering adaptation of skip-gram plus two global regularizers.
- Weakness: mixed empirical gains; some baselines (e.g., CHOI, APPROXPHRco) outperform or match the proposed concept vectors in several settings.
- Weakness: vague framing of “distant supervision”—training is effectively phrase detection plus context-based updates, not explicit labeled supervision.
- Weakness: single global β/η is a coarse assumption given varied compositionality; tuned values are often zero, reducing their impact.
- Weakness: clarity and evaluation issues — inconsistent notation, under-specified similarity scoring with many-to-many mappings, hyperparameter tuning protocol, lack of downstream tasks, and missing ablations (e.g., shared negative-sampling matrix).
- Question: clarify exact supervision signal, how ambiguous phrases are handled during training, and whether candidate concepts are weighted or treated uniformly.
- Question: specify the exact procedure for computing similarity/relatedness when concepts map to multiple phrases (canonical choice, average, max, etc.).
- Question: explain when learned concept embeddings outperform phrase averages and whether the ontology mainly serves as a phrase inventory.
- Question: did you try phrase- or concept-specific compositionality parameters (learned gates/attention) instead of global β/η?
- Question: have you considered regularizing concept embeddings with ontology graph structure (hierarchy/type constraints) to improve non-lexical knowledge capture?
- Question: provide extrinsic downstream evaluations (e.g., entity linking, normalization, retrieval) to validate utility beyond intrinsic correlations.
- Question: report dataset annotation quality details (per-pair variances, confidence intervals) and whether results hold when filtering for higher agreement.
- Overall assessment: solid, well-executed systems paper with practical value and useful resources, but limited novelty, mixed advantages over strong baselines, and several methodological and evaluation clarifications needed.
