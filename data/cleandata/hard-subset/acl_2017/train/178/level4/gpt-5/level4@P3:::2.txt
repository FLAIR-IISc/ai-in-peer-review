Summary
The paper proposes a weakly supervised method to jointly learn embeddings for concepts, phrases, and words by leveraging mappings from ontologies (UMLS and YAGO) to representative phrases. The approach replaces matched phrases in raw text with unique identifiers and adapts skip-gram with negative sampling to train three embedding matrices in parallel: words, phrases, and concepts. Two hyperparameters control the degree of compositionality between words→phrases and phrases→concepts. The authors evaluate on biomedical and general-domain concept similarity/relatedness benchmarks, present a new human-annotated dataset for real-world entities, and release software.

Contributions (as I see them)
1) Scalable, annotation-free pipeline for jointly embedding concepts, phrases, and words using ontology-derived distant supervision, yielding competitive performance with substantially larger coverage than prior concept-embedding work that requires human annotation.
2) Resources: release of software (cui2vec) and a new similarity/relatedness dataset for real-world entities; comprehensive experiments across multiple corpora and ontologies.
3) Analyses of compositionality and semantic-type clustering that shed light on how phrase and concept structure is reflected in the learned spaces.

Strengths
- Scalability and coverage without manual annotation. The method uses only ontology phrase lists and raw text, yielding over 3x vocabulary coverage while avoiding costly concept annotation. This makes the approach practical for domains where annotation is expensive (e.g., clinical notes).
- Competitive performance on larger, more reliable benchmarks. On UMNSRS Similarity (566 pairs), concept embeddings are state-of-the-art or on par with strong baselines; on relatedness tasks the results are generally competitive with word-based systems. The approach also works in the general domain (Wikipedia+NYT) with reasonable correlations.
- Joint space across words, phrases, and concepts. Learning a shared space facilitates cross-granularity comparisons and probing analyses (e.g., concept–phrase proximity, phrase–word compositionality). The analysis suggests ontology structure partially emerges in the embedding geometry (semantic type clustering, concept–phrase closeness).
- Resource contributions and experimental breadth. The release of a new entity similarity/relatedness dataset and code increases reproducibility and utility. Training on diverse corpora (PubMed, clinical notes, Wikipedia/NYT) and two ontologies (UMLS, YAGO) demonstrates generality and robustness to domain variation.
- Thoughtful analysis. The paper examines how compositionality behaves, how hyperparameters affect performance, and how semantic types cluster, offering insight beyond headline scores.

Weaknesses
- Limited methodological novelty. The core is a straightforward adaptation of skip-gram with phrase substitution and two global regularization terms (β and η). While the engineering is sound and the idea useful, the modeling advances are incremental relative to existing word/phrase/entity embedding literature.
- Inconsistent empirical gains versus strong baselines. On the Mayo datasets, CHOI substantially outperforms the proposed method. In semantic-type clustering, the medical code embeddings from CHOI are often stronger overall. Even on general-domain evaluations, approximating concepts as averages of their phrases (APPROXPHRco) sometimes outperforms learned concept vectors, suggesting the explicit concept embedding does not always add value beyond phrase averages.
- Ambiguity around “distant supervision” and training signal. The paper states it uses known phrases as distant supervision, but the “supervision” is effectively phrase detection followed by context-based training. It is not supervision in a predictive sense (e.g., no labeled concept mentions), and the text should clarify precisely what is supervised (the assumption that each phrase occurrence implicates all of its candidate concepts, with uniform normalization).
- Global, static compositionality parameters. Using a single β (phrase-by-words) and η (concepts-by-phrases) across the vocabulary is a strong assumption given varied compositionality (e.g., “amyotrophic lateral sclerosis” vs. “Big Apple”). The tuned values are often zero (Table 3), implying the regularizers bring little benefit as implemented. This undercuts the model’s claim of jointly controlling compositionality.
- Clarity and evaluation details. a) The notation introduced for embedding matrices is not used consistently thereafter; b) The experimental setup for computing similarity/relatedness with many-to-many concept–phrase mappings is under-specified—when scoring “phrase” or “word” settings, how are multiple surface forms per concept resolved? c) Hyperparameter tuning is performed on the test sets (albeit with a cross-validation adaptation), and all evaluations are intrinsic; no downstream tasks are used to corroborate semantic utility. d) The shared negative-sampling matrix is presented as the sole cross-level parameter coupling; an ablation of this choice would help.

Questions to authors
- Supervision signal: Please specify exactly what is “supervised” in training. Is the only supervision the ontology-derived phrase list used to tag text and to distribute updates to all candidate concepts for a phrase mention? How are ambiguous phrases handled at training time (uniformly over candidate concepts?), and did you consider posterior weighting?
- Evaluation mapping: For Tables 4 and 5, when computing similarity/relatedness for a concept pair with multiple phrases each, how are phrase-based scores obtained? Are you selecting a canonical phrase, averaging across all phrases, or taking a max over phrase combinations? Please provide the precise procedure.
- Concept vs. phrase value: In Table 5, APPROXPHRco is best. Under what conditions do concept embeddings outperform phrase averages, and why? Could the ontology still be useful primarily as a phrase inventory (without explicit concept vectors)?
- Compositionality parameters: Did you try concept- or phrase-specific β/η (e.g., learned gates or attention over component embeddings)? The global parameters may be too coarse; this might explain why their optimal values are frequently zero.
- Ontology structure: Have you tried regularizing concept embeddings with ontology graph structure (e.g., hierarchy edges or type constraints) in addition to contexts, as in joint text–graph methods? This could address cases where non-lexical knowledge matters and improve semantic-type clustering.
- Downstream validation: Can you report extrinsic evaluations (e.g., entity linking, ICD/CUI normalization, biomedical retrieval) to test whether the joint space delivers gains beyond intrinsic correlation metrics?
- Dataset quality: The new general-domain datasets have Fleiss’ kappa ≈ 0.24 (fair). Can you share per-pair variance or confidence intervals, and do your conclusions hold if you filter to pairs with higher agreement?

Overall assessment
This is a solid, well-executed systems paper with clear practical value: it demonstrates that ontology-guided, weakly supervised joint training can deliver competitive concept embeddings at scale without manual annotation and with broad coverage. However, the methodological novelty is limited, gains over strong baselines are mixed, and several presentation and evaluation details need clarification. With stronger justification for the learning choices (esp. compositionality), clearer evaluation protocols, and at least one downstream task, this would be a stronger candidate for acceptance. As it stands, I view the primary contribution as a scalable, useful resource and empirical study rather than a novel method.
