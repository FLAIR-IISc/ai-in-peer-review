Review- Summary: This paper introduces a framework for embedding words, phrases, and concepts into vector spaces. It employs a concept ontology that maps concepts to phrases, which are identified in text corpora and treated as atomic units. Building on this, the approach effectively uses the skip-gram objective to train embeddings for words, these atomic phrases, and their associated concepts. The method is evaluated on concept similarity and relatedness tasks, using UMLS and YAGO as the underlying ontologies.
Strengths:
The paper addresses the issue that lexically dissimilar phrases can be semantically close and that many phrases are non-compositional. It proposes a plausible model for learning phrase embeddings. The resulting embeddings are shown to be competitive or superior for identifying concept similarity.
The released software could be valuable to biomedical NLP researchers.
- Weaknesses:
The main weakness is limited novelty: the model is essentially a small modification of skip-gram.
In addition, the full model does not appear to be the best performer in the results (Table 4). On the two Mayo datasets, the Choi baseline is substantially better. A similar trend is evident in Table 6 as well. On the larger UMNSRS dataset, the proposed model is at best competitive with earlier, simpler models (Chiu).
- General Discussion:
The paper states that it uses known phrases as distant supervision to train embeddings, but it is unclear what the “supervision” entails. If my understanding is correct, each occurrence of a phrase linked to a concept provides context for training word embeddings. However, this is not supervision in the usual sense (e.g., for concept identification or other predictive tasks), making the terminology confusing.
The notation introduced in Section 3.2 (E_W, etc.) is not used elsewhere in the paper.
The use of β to control phrase compositionality is surprising. This amounts to a single global constant determining “how compositional” any phrase should be. The selected β values in Table 3 are also puzzling: for PM+CL and WikiNYT, β is zero, which effectively argues against compositionality.
The experimental setup for Table 4 needs clarification. The datasets annotate similarity/relatedness between concepts (or entities). If the concept–phrase mapping is many-to-many, how are phrase/word vectors used to compute similarities? It seems only concept vectors can be used.
In Table 5, the approximate phr method (which approximates concepts by averaging their phrase vectors) performs best. This calls into question the need for the concept ontology; starting with a seed set of phrases might yield the same results.
