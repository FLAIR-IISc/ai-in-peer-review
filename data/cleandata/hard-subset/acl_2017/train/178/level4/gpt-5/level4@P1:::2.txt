Summary and contributions:
This paper proposes a method for jointly embedding words, phrases, and ontology concepts into a shared vector space. Concepts are linked to multiple representative phrases from an ontology (UMLS or YAGO), and these phrases are identified in unlabeled corpora and treated as atomic tokens. A skip-gram–style objective is then used to train embeddings for words, the phrase unigrams, and the associated concepts. The approach is evaluated on concept similarity and relatedness tasks.

As I see it, the main contributions are:
1) A practical training setup that uses ontology phrase lists as distant supervision to embed concepts, phrases, and words jointly without manual corpus annotation.
2) An empirical evaluation on biomedical (UMLS) and general-domain (YAGO) concepts showing competitive performance on similarity/relatedness benchmarks, alongside analyses of compositionality and semantic type clustering.
3) Software release that could be useful to biomedical NLP researchers.

Strengths:
- Addresses non-compositionality and lexical variation: The paper targets an important gap: many concepts are multiword, non-compositional, and have multiple surface forms (e.g., “the Big Apple” vs. “New York City”). Treating phrases as atomic and linking them to concepts is a reasonable and practically impactful design choice.
- Simple, scalable training with no manual annotations: The method leverages existing ontologies for distant supervision and trains on unannotated corpora. This yields broad coverage (the paper claims >3x vocabulary coverage) and avoids expensive annotation, which is valuable for biomedical NLP where labeled data are scarce.
- Competitive performance on larger benchmarks: On the larger UMNSRS similarity dataset, the proposed concept embeddings achieve the highest correlation (Table 4), and relatedness is competitive with strong word-based baselines (e.g., CHIU-2/30). This suggests the approach can match or exceed simpler baselines at scale.
- Useful software release: The accompanying implementation lowers barriers for adoption in biomedical applications and replication of the reported results.

Weaknesses:
- Limited novelty of the modeling: Methodologically, this is largely a straightforward adaptation of skip-gram with negative sampling, plus two global trade-off hyperparameters (β for phrase-by-word and η for concept-by-phrase compositionality). The training pipeline relies on phrase substitution and averaging, which are standard. As such, the modeling contribution feels incremental.
- Proposed “full model” is not consistently best: On the two Mayo datasets, the CHOI baseline substantially outperforms the proposed method (Table 4), and Table 6 shows that Choi et al.’s medical code embeddings also lead in semantic type clustering. Even on UMNSRS, gains over simpler word-based models (Chiu) are modest. The paper would be stronger with clearer, consistent advantages of the joint concept embeddings over established baselines.
- Ambiguity around “distant supervision”: The paper states it uses “known phrases as distant supervision,” but the supervision signal is not clearly defined. If each occurrence of a mapped phrase simply provides standard skip-gram contexts for training embeddings, that is not supervision in the sense of labeled prediction. The terminology is potentially confusing without a clearer explanation of how the ontology constraints function as supervisory signals beyond token substitution.
- Global compositionality control seems mismatched and often zeroed: Using a single global β to control phrase-by-word compositionality is surprising, since compositionality varies widely across phrases. Cross-validated β values in Table 3 are zero for PM+CL and WIKINYT, implying the best settings eschew compositionality entirely. This both questions the utility of β and suggests the model often reduces to non-compositional phrase treatment.
- Clarity and notation issues: The matrices introduced in Section 3.2 (e.g., EW, EP, EC) and the negative sampling setup are introduced but not consistently referenced later. The notation increases cognitive load without clear payoff. In addition, more detail is needed on how concept updates are distributed when phrases map to multiple concepts.
- Experimental setup for Table 4 needs clarification: The datasets label concept/entity pairs. Given a many-to-many concept–phrase mapping, how exactly are phrase- or word-based representations used to compute similarities for concept pairs? The current description suggests concept vectors should be used, but the table includes phrase/word approximations whose computation in the many-to-many setting is not fully explained.
- Value of concept embeddings vs. phrase approximations: In Table 5 (general domain), the APPROXPHRco method (averaging the embeddings of a concept’s phrases) outperforms direct concept embeddings. This raises the question of whether the ontology-level concept embeddings add value beyond a seed set of phrases, especially when approximations are superior.

Questions to authors:
- Please clarify what you mean by “distant supervision.” What is the supervisory signal beyond identifying phrase spans from ontology lists? How does this concretely differ from training on a phrase-substituted corpus without ontology labels?
- How are similarities computed in Table 4 when concepts have many-to-many mappings with phrases? For phrase/word settings, do you average over all representative phrases/words per concept, or select a canonical form? How do you handle ambiguity where a phrase maps to multiple concepts?
- Can you provide ablations or per-phrase analyses for β and η? Given that optimal β and η are often zero, under what conditions do these terms help? Would per-phrase (or per-type) compositionality weights outperform a single global scalar?
- Given that APPROXPHRco is often the best on the general-domain datasets (Table 5), what is the practical benefit of learning explicit concept embeddings? Are there tasks where the concept vectors outperform phrase averages?
- Could you elaborate on why the proposed method lags on the Mayo datasets and on semantic type clustering (Table 6)? Is this due to data sparsity, differences in supervision (manual codes vs. phrase matches), or limitations of the training objective?
- The notation introduced in Section 3.2 (e.g., EW, EP, EC) is not reused consistently. Could you streamline or align the notation with the loss definitions to improve clarity?
