The paper extends word embedding methods to also produce representations for phrases and for concepts corresponding to words. The approach assigns a shared identifier to sets comprising phrases, words, and the underlying concept they denote; occurrences of these phrases and words are then replaced with this identifier to create a tagged corpus, which is appended to the original corpus for training. These concept/phrase/word sets are drawn from an ontology. Because the application domain is biomedical, the authors use biomedical corpora and ontologies. The paper also introduces a new test dataset for word similarity and relatedness among real-world entities, which is novel.

Overall, the paper is clearly written. The technique is natural but not a particularly substantial contribution. The scope of the work is limited by its evaluation being confined to the biomedical domain. More discussion of the new test resource would be helpful; this resource may be the paper’s most interesting contribution.

There is one small technical issue, likely a matter of mathematical expression rather than implementation. Equation 8 aims to define the MAP calculation, which is a good idea, though a natural cutoff could be used instead of ranking the entire vocabulary. However, Equation 8 does not define a probability; it is straightforward to show this, even if the vocabulary size were infinite. The explanation should be revised to remove references to probability.
