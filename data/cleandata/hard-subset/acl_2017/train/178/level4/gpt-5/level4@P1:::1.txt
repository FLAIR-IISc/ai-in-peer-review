Summary and contributions
The paper proposes a weakly supervised method to jointly learn embeddings for concepts, phrases, and words by leveraging ontology-provided mappings from concepts to representative phrases. The authors create a tagged version of the corpus by greedily replacing occurrences of mapped phrases with unique identifiers, and then train on both the original and tagged corpora using a skip-gram with negative sampling objective. Concept embeddings are updated using the contexts of their phrase realizations. The work is evaluated primarily in the biomedical domain (UMLS), with an additional general-domain dataset (YAGO).

As I see them, the main contributions are:
1) A novel crowdsourced dataset of similarity and relatedness judgments for real-world entities (people, places, organizations). This resource appears broadly useful and may be the most interesting contribution.
2) A simple, natural weakly supervised method to jointly embed concepts, phrases, and words without manual corpus annotation, using ontology phrase mappings as distant supervision.
3) An empirical evaluation in the biomedical domain showing competitive performance against prior methods that rely on manual annotations, while offering broader vocabulary coverage.

Strengths
- Clear, straightforward idea and presentation: The paper is well written and easy to follow. The approach—tagging ontology-linked phrases in text and jointly training embeddings for words, phrases, and concepts—is intuitive and technically sound.
- No manual annotation required: By using ontology phrase mappings as distant supervision, the method avoids expensive human concept tagging. This is a practical advantage in domains such as biomedicine where annotation is costly.
- New evaluation resource: The crowdsourced entity similarity/relatedness dataset fills a gap for entity-level evaluation (as opposed to word-level), and could be valuable for the community beyond this paper’s specific method.

Weaknesses
- Limited novelty of the core technique: While sensible, the proposed training scheme feels like a natural extension of existing word/phrase embedding approaches rather than a substantial algorithmic contribution.
- Narrow scope of empirical evaluation: Most results focus on the biomedical domain. This limits claims about generality, especially since performance and hyperparameter behavior can be domain-dependent.
- Insufficient discussion of the new dataset: The entity similarity/relatedness resource could be the most impactful contribution, but the paper provides relatively limited discussion in the main text about its construction, selection criteria, quality control, and intended use (e.g., train/test splits).
- Technical issue with Eq. 8: The expression presented as “Pr(c|Cp, V)” is not a probability. Even with an infinite vocabulary, it is easy to see this cannot be normalized as a probability distribution. Please revise the explanation to avoid probabilistic terminology. Also, consider reporting MAP over a natural cutoff (e.g., top-k neighborhood) rather than ranking over the entire vocabulary, which may be both more interpretable and more stable.
- Minor writing issue: Line 556: “most concepts has” should be “most concepts have.”

Questions to authors (optional)
- Dataset details: Please expand on the construction of the new entity similarity/relatedness dataset in the main paper (currently relegated to the supplement). Specifically:
  - How were entity pairs selected across types and relatedness bins?
  - What quality-control procedures were used beyond the validation pairs (e.g., annotator filtering, aggregation methods)?
  - Are there recommended train/dev/test splits to avoid tuning on test data?
  - What is the expected use scenario (intrinsic vs. extrinsic tasks), and how should users interpret the relatively low Fleiss’ κ?
- Generalization beyond biomedicine: Can you provide more discussion or preliminary results on non-biomedical corpora to support claims of generality? For example, how sensitive are β and η to corpus domain, and do the observed trends (e.g., performance declining as β/η increase) hold outside biomedicine?
- Objective and implementation details:
  - Eq. 8: Please clarify the intent behind this expression and remove probability language. If you adopt a top-k neighborhood, how do MAP scores change?
  - Phrase identification: The paper states you replace occurrences of mapped phrases with unique identifiers greedily. Are words ever replaced directly, or only multiword phrases? How do you handle overlapping or nested phrase matches and ambiguity across concepts?
  - Negative sampling: You share a single negative-sampling matrix ENS. Did you try separate negative-sampling distributions for words vs. phrases, or frequency-adjusted sampling for tagged tokens?

Overall assessment
This is a clearly presented paper with a pragmatic, weakly supervised approach that avoids manual labeling and yields reasonable concept representations, especially in the biomedical domain. However, the methodological novelty is modest, and the empirical scope is narrow. The new entity similarity/relatedness dataset is a valuable contribution; expanding its description and providing clearer usage guidelines could substantially strengthen the paper. The minor technical issue around Eq. 8 should be corrected.
