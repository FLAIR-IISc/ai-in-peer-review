- Proposes a weakly supervised method that jointly embeds ontology concepts, representative phrases, and words by replacing mapped phrases with concept identifiers in a tagged corpus and training on both original and tagged text.
- Main contributions: (1) a simple framework for joint concept/phrase/word embeddings without manual annotation, (2) a new general-domain entity similarity/relatedness dataset, (3) empirical results showing competitive performance with much larger vocabulary coverage than prior methods.
- Clear and practical method: easy to implement, leverages existing ontologies (UMLS, YAGO), and ties concepts/phrases to context via parallel original and tagged corpora.
- Competitive performance with broad coverage on biomedical benchmarks, offering over 3× larger vocabulary useful for long-tail terminology in downstream tasks.
- Cross-domain training and release of a new entity similarity/relatedness dataset address a gap in entity-level intrinsic evaluations.
- Incremental novelty: method is a natural extension of skip-gram with distant supervision, so methodological contribution is modest.
- Narrow empirical scope: evaluations focus mainly on biomedical data; general-domain experiments are limited and not a thorough stress-test across diverse entity types/relations.
- Underdeveloped description of the new dataset: construction, quality control, recommended splits, and per-category reliability are insufficiently detailed.
- Technical/math issue: Eq. 8 is labeled as a “probability” but represents an average-precision ranking component, not a normalized probability; evaluation choices (full-vocab vs. top-k) need justification.
- Questions for authors: clarify Eq. 8 terminology and rationale for full-vocabulary ranking vs. top-k; provide annotation protocols, recommended train/dev/test splits, and known low-agreement areas for the new dataset.
- Minor comment: fix typo “most concepts has” → “most concepts have.”
