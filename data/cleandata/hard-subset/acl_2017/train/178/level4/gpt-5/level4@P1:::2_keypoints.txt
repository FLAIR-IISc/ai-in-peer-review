- Joint method: embeds words, phrases, and ontology concepts into a shared vector space using ontology phrase lists and skip-gram training on unlabeled corpora.  
- Contribution: practical training pipeline that uses ontology phrase lists as distant supervision to jointly learn word/phrase/concept embeddings without manual annotation.  
- Contribution: empirical evaluation on biomedical (UMLS) and general-domain (YAGO) concepts with competitive results on similarity/relatedness and analyses of compositionality and type clustering.  
- Contribution: software release to facilitate biomedical NLP use and replication.  
- Strength: addresses non-compositionality and lexical variation by treating multiword phrases as atomic tokens linked to concepts.  
- Strength: simple, scalable training leveraging ontologies and unlabeled text, increasing vocabulary coverage and avoiding costly labels.  
- Strength: strong performance on larger benchmarks (UMNSRS highest correlation; relatedness competitive with strong baselines).  
- Strength: useful implementation release for adoption and reproducibility.  
- Weakness: limited methodological novelty—largely a straightforward adaptation of skip-gram with two global hyperparameters and standard averaging/substitution.  
- Weakness: proposed full model is not consistently superior; baselines (e.g., Choi) outperform on Mayo datasets and semantic-type clustering.  
- Weakness: ambiguous use of “distant supervision”; the ontology signal is not clearly distinct from simple phrase-substituted training.  
- Weakness: single global compositionality weight β is coarse and often selected as zero, questioning its utility and suggesting per-phrase variability.  
- Weakness: clarity and notation issues—matrices (EW, EP, EC) and negative sampling are introduced but not consistently used; concept update mechanics for multi-mapped phrases are unclear.  
- Weakness: experimental procedure for Table 4 is underspecified—how similarities are computed under many-to-many concept–phrase mappings and ambiguous phrase-to-concept mappings.  
- Weakness: concept embeddings may add limited value since APPROXPHRco (averaging phrase embeddings) sometimes outperforms direct concept vectors.  
- Question: clarify what is meant by “distant supervision” and how ontology constraints provide supervision beyond phrase substitution.  
- Question: detail how similarities in Table 4 are computed for concepts with many-to-many phrase mappings and how ambiguity is resolved.  
- Question: provide ablations or per-phrase analyses for β and η; when do these terms help and would per-phrase weights improve results?  
- Question: explain the practical benefit of explicit concept embeddings versus phrase-average approximations and identify tasks where concept vectors outperform.  
- Question: elaborate on why the method lags on Mayo datasets and semantic type clustering—data sparsity, supervision differences, or objective limitations?  
- Question: streamline and consistently apply notation in Section 3.2 and align it with loss definitions to improve clarity.
