- Paper proposes joint embeddings of words, phrases, and concepts using plain text plus a manual ontology (UMLS, YAGO) and evaluates mainly on intrinsic similarity/relatedness tasks.

- Strength: Joint embedding model is straightforward, intuitive, and offers a configurable middle ground between atomic phrase treatment and compositional modeling.

- Strength: Substantial amount of work — model development, a new general-domain concept similarity dataset, and several evaluations/analyses.

- Weakness: Evaluation is limited to intrinsic similarity/relatedness tasks; lacks extrinsic (downstream) evaluations that better demonstrate utility.

- Weakness: Annotators judged displayed phrases, so the datasets measure phrase similarity rather than true concept similarity and should be treated/analysed as such.

- Weakness: Medical concept datasets are weak — miniMayoSRS is tiny (29 pairs), MayoSRS is small (101 pairs) with low agreement, and UMNSRS contains only single-word concepts shown as single words, reducing relevance for phrase/concept representation claims.

- Weakness: Extensive hyperparameter tuning appears to have been done on the same datasets used for reporting comparisons, calling reported results and analyses into question.

- Weakness: Claim of matching prior work with less manual annotation is unconvincing because this approach relies on large hand-built ontologies and prior annotated dataset came from existing clinical records without dedicated annotation.

- Weakness: Paper lacks deeper, targeted analyses distinguishing phrase–word compositionality from concept–phrase compositionality; these relations differ and merit separate study (e.g., role of dominant head words).

- Weakness/Suggestion: Missing analysis of how hyperparameters control the trade-off between atomic and compositional views for both phrases and concepts.

- Recommendation: Reviewer recommends rejection; suggests improving evaluation methodology, adding extrinsic tasks, better datasets, and targeted analyses before resubmission.
