Summary: The paper proposes a model for embedding words, phrases, and concepts in a shared vector space. It leverages an ontology in which concepts are linked to phrases; occurrences of these phrases in text are treated as atomic tokens. Building on this representation, the model effectively applies a skip-gram-style objective to learn embeddings for words, the atomic phrases, and their associated concepts. The approach is evaluated on concept similarity and relatedness tasks, using UMLS and YAGO as the underlying ontologies.

Strengths:
- The paper tackles an important issue: lexically dissimilar phrases can be semantically close, and many phrases are non-compositional.
- Treating ontology-linked phrases as atomic units provides a plausible way to learn phrase embeddings.
- The learned embeddings are competitive, and in some cases superior, on concept similarity evaluations.
- The released software could be useful for biomedical NLP researchers.

Weaknesses:
- The model’s novelty is limited; it is essentially a variant of skip-gram.
- The full proposed model is not consistently the best performer. In Table 4, the Choi baseline substantially outperforms it on the two Mayo datasets; a similar trend appears in Table 6. On the larger UMNSRS dataset, the method is at best competitive with simpler prior models (e.g., Chiu).

General Discussion:
- The paper states that it uses known phrases as distant supervision to train embeddings, but it is unclear what constitutes the “supervision.” If I understand correctly, each occurrence of a phrase associated with a concept simply provides context for training word embeddings. This is not supervision in the usual sense (e.g., for concept identification or other predictive tasks), so the terminology is confusing.
- The notation introduced in Section 3.2 (E_W, etc.) is not used later in the paper.
- The use of a single global beta parameter to control how compositional phrases are is surprising. This implies a universal degree of compositionality across all phrases. Moreover, the cross-validated values in Table 3 are puzzling: for PM+CL and WikiNYT, beta is zero, which effectively argues against compositionality.
- The experimental setup for Table 4 needs clarification. The evaluation labels similarity/relatedness of concepts (or entities). If the concept–phrase mapping is many-to-many, how are phrase or word vectors used to compute these similarities? It seems that only concept vectors should be applicable.
- In Table 5, the approximate phr method (approximating a concept by averaging its phrase vectors) performs best. This raises the question of whether the explicit concept ontology is necessary; starting with a seed set of phrases might yield the same results.
