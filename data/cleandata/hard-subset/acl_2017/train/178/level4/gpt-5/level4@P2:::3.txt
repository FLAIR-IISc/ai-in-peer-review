Summary and contributions
The paper proposes a method to jointly embed words, phrases, and ontology concepts using unlabeled text and a manually curated ontology that maps concepts to representative phrases. The approach is evaluated in the biomedical domain (UMLS) and a general-domain setting (YAGO). Comparisons are made against simpler approximations and prior work, primarily on intrinsic similarity/relatedness benchmarks. A new crowdsourced dataset for real-world entity similarity and relatedness is introduced for the general domain. The main contributions, as I see them:
1) A joint embedding framework with tunable parameters that balances treating phrases/concepts as atomic units versus modeling their compositional structure.
2) A new crowdsourced evaluation set for general-domain entity similarity/relatedness (with plans to release).
3) An empirical study across domains comparing the method to baselines and prior work, reporting comparable results.

Strengths
- Conceptually straightforward joint model with a clear motivation. The configurable trade-off (for both phrase-by-word and concept-by-phrase links) is a practical way to interpolate between atomic and compositional representations.
- Substantial execution effort: model design, multi-corpus training (biomedical and general), creation of a new evaluation dataset, and several analyses of the learned space (e.g., compositionality, semantic type clustering).
- The approach scales to large vocabularies without manual corpus annotation, leveraging distant supervision from ontologies, and achieves performance competitive with stronger baselines on several intrinsic benchmarks.
- The paper’s cross-domain scope (UMLS and YAGO) suggests potential applicability beyond a single ontology or domain.

Weaknesses
- Evaluation is limited to intrinsic tasks (similarity/relatedness), whose predictive power for downstream utility is known to be weak. Current practice commonly includes at least one or two extrinsic tasks (e.g., entity linking, retrieval, or clinical prediction) to demonstrate practical gains.
- The datasets are framed as concept-level judgments, but annotators saw phrases. This makes them phrase-level similarity/relatedness datasets in practice; results and analysis should reflect that. In the biomedical setting, Mini MayoSRS is very small (29 pairs) and MayoSRS is also small (101 pairs) with reported low agreement. UMNSRS is larger but limited to single-word concepts presented to annotators as such, reducing its relevance for evaluating phrase and general concept representations.
- Extensive hyperparameter tuning is performed directly on the test sets used for reporting and comparison, which undermines the validity of the results and any claimed improvements. Even the adapted cross-validation described in the supplement does not fully resolve this issue since final test metrics still reflect tuned settings on the same data.
- The claim of superiority over prior work due to reduced reliance on human annotation is overstated. The method depends on large, manually constructed ontologies, and prior work often leverages existing annotations from clinical workflows rather than bespoke labeling.
- The paper conflates two different types of “composition”: phrase-by-word and concept-by-phrase. These relations differ linguistically and semantically and merit separate, deeper analyses. For instance, headword effects and lexicalized MWEs may explain the observed bimodality in phrase–word neighborhoods. Similarly, the effects of the β and η hyperparameters (controlling compositionality) on performance and structure of the space are not thoroughly investigated.

Questions to authors (for rebuttal)
- How exactly were overlapping phrase matches resolved during preprocessing (greedy by length, other tie-breaking rules)?
- In the joint model, are all words within multiword phrases used as targets for word updates, or only the final token? Please clarify how word updates are interleaved with phrase/concept updates.
- Which specific datasets and metrics were used for hyperparameter tuning for each corpus? Were any held-out development sets used that were not part of the reported test results?
- For the general-domain crowdsourced datasets, were annotators shown canonical concepts or surface phrases? Please clarify instructions and whether ambiguity/synonymy was addressed.
- Do you have any preliminary extrinsic results (e.g., entity linking, retrieval, or clinical coding) that demonstrate practical benefits of the joint space?

Recommendation
Reject. The method is sensible and the scope of work is notable, but the evaluation relies exclusively on intrinsic tasks with tuning on test sets, and the datasets raise construct-validity concerns. Strengthening the evaluation (clear dev/test separation, extrinsic tasks) and deepening the analyses of compositionality would significantly improve the paper.
