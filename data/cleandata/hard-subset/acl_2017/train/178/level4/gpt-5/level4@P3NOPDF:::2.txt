Summary
This paper proposes a method to jointly embed words, phrases, and concepts into a shared vector space. The approach leverages an ontology (e.g., UMLS, YAGO) that maps concepts to sets of phrases. Phrases are treated as atomic units in the corpus, and a skip-gram–style objective is used to learn embeddings for words, phrase tokens, and their associated concepts. The method is evaluated on concept similarity and relatedness benchmarks using biomedical (UMLS) and general-domain (YAGO) ontologies.

Strengths
- Clear motivation: phrases that are not lexically similar can be semantically close, and many phrases are non-compositional. The model directly addresses this by treating known phrases as atomic units.
- Empirical performance: learned embeddings are competitive and sometimes better than prior methods on concept similarity/relatedness benchmarks.
- Practical utility: released software may benefit biomedical NLP researchers, where high-quality concept embeddings are useful and ontologies are rich.

Weaknesses
- Limited novelty: the method is essentially a modification of skip-gram with ontology-informed phrase/concept tokens; the core learning framework is standard.
- Results do not consistently favor the full model: on the two Mayo datasets in Table 4, the Choi baseline substantially outperforms the proposed approach, and a similar trend appears in Table 6. On UMNSRS, the proposed model is at best competitive with simpler baselines (e.g., Chiu).
- Concept vs phrase utility: In Table 5, the “approximate phr” method (approximating concept embeddings by averaging their phrase embeddings) performs best, casting doubt on whether explicit concept embeddings and the ontology provide clear added value over strong phrase-only baselines.
- Global compositionality parameter: The use of a single global β to trade off compositional vs atomic phrase representations is overly coarse. Cross-validated β values of 0 for PM+CL and WikiNYT imply that a purely non-compositional treatment works best there, which is surprising and suggests the current modeling of compositionality may be inadequate.
- Clarity gaps: 
  - “Distant supervision” is used somewhat loosely. If supervision is limited to recognizing known phrases and associating them with concepts, the term may be misleading; the approach looks more like weakly supervised or self-supervised learning with ontology-informed tokenization.
  - Notation introduced in Section 3.2 (e.g., E_W) is not referenced later and could be streamlined or tied explicitly to the training objective and experiments.
  - The evaluation protocol in Table 4 is under-specified, especially given the many-to-many concept–phrase mapping: it is unclear when concept vectors vs phrase/word vectors are used to compute similarities and how ambiguity is handled.

Questions and requests for clarification
- Supervision signal: What exactly constitutes the “distant supervision”? Are phrase occurrences labeled with a unique concept ID during training, or are concepts only attached to phrase types, with no disambiguation in context?
- Many-to-many mappings in evaluation (Table 4): 
  - For concept similarity datasets, are similarities computed using concept embeddings only? If phrase embeddings are involved, how are multiple phrases per concept handled (e.g., averaging, max, learned aggregator)?
  - How are polysemous phrases linked to multiple concepts treated at training and test time?
- Table 5 vs ontology value: Since averaging phrase embeddings yields the best performance, in which settings do explicit concept embeddings outperform phrase-based proxies (e.g., rare concepts, long-tail phrases, cross-ontology alignment)? Please provide targeted analyses.
- β parameterization: Why is a single global β appropriate? Were per-phrase or per-type β, or a learned gating mechanism, considered? Can you provide ablations showing sensitivity to β and whether performance improves with finer-grained control?
- Corpus and preprocessing details: Please clarify corpus sizes, phrase identification procedure, frequency thresholds, handling of overlapping phrases, and negative sampling or other skip-gram hyperparameters. Are results averaged over multiple runs with variance reported?

Suggestions for improvement
- Strengthen the case for concepts:
  - Add ablations contrasting: (i) word-only, (ii) phrase-only (atomic), (iii) phrase compositional, (iv) concept-only, and (v) full model. Highlight scenarios where concepts help beyond phrase averaging.
  - Provide breakdowns by concept frequency, phrase ambiguity, and phrase length to show where concept embeddings are most beneficial.
- Improve compositionality modeling:
  - Replace the global β with a learned per-phrase gate or attention-based composition that decides, from data, when to favor atomic vs compositional representations.
  - Consider regularizing β toward nontrivial values or tying it to phrase statistics (e.g., frequency, PMI, lexical variability).
- Clarify and tighten terminology:
  - Reframe “distant supervision” as ontology-guided tokenization and concept attachment, unless there is explicit instance-level supervision. If concept disambiguation is performed, describe it clearly.
  - Ensure all introduced notation is used consistently in the objective and experiments.
- Evaluation and reporting:
  - Provide statistical significance tests and confidence intervals.
  - Detail how similarities are computed in all cases (concept vs phrase vs word vectors) and how many-to-many mappings are resolved.
  - Include additional baselines, such as retrofitting or post-hoc concept projection, to better isolate the benefits of joint training.
  - Extend analysis to downstream tasks (e.g., entity linking, relation extraction) where concept-level semantics may matter more than surface similarity.
- Reproducibility:
  - Document preprocessing, ontology alignment, and hyperparameters comprehensively. If possible, release the phrase/concept vocabularies used and mapping tables to facilitate exact replication.

Overall assessment
The paper presents a well-motivated application of ontology-informed phrase and concept embeddings using a largely standard training framework. While results are solid, the contribution feels incremental, and the strongest baselines sometimes outperform the full model. The paper would benefit from clearer exposition of the supervision signal and evaluation protocol, stronger ablations isolating the impact of concepts vs phrases, and a more nuanced treatment of compositionality. With these improvements and deeper analysis demonstrating when and why concepts add value beyond phrase embeddings, the work would be considerably strengthened.
