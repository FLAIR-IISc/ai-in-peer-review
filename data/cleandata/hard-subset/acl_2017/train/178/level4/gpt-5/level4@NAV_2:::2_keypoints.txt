- Summary: Proposes a framework that maps phrases to ontology concepts, replaces matched phrases with unique tokens, and trains shared embeddings for words, phrase tokens, and concepts via a skip-gram objective, evaluated on UMLS and YAGO.

- Strength: Addresses lexical mismatch and non-compositional phrases with a reasonable phrase-training setup, achieving competitive or improved concept similarity/relatedness vectors.

- Strength: Released software is likely to be practically useful for biomedical NLP practitioners.

- Weakness: Limited methodological novelty—largely a straightforward modification of skip-gram.

- Weakness: The complete model does not consistently outperform prior work (e.g., Choi baseline on Mayo datasets; similar patterns in other tables).

- Weakness: On larger UMNSRS benchmarks the method is at best on par with simpler prior baselines (e.g., Chiu).

- Discussion: Calling known-phrase usage “distant supervision” is confusing since occurrences merely provide context rather than predictive labels in the usual supervised sense.

- Discussion: Notation breaks continuity—matrices introduced in Section 3.2 (e.g., E_W) are not referenced later.

- Discussion: Using a single global β for compositionality is problematic, implying uniform compositionality across phrases; cross-validated β values (β=0 for some corpora) are puzzling.

- Discussion: Table 4 setup is unclear—dataset labels are at the concept level, so it’s unclear how phrase/word vectors are used for similarity given many-to-many phrase–concept mappings.

- Discussion: Table 5 shows APPROXPHR (averaging phrase embeddings) performs best, raising the question of whether the ontology is necessary versus starting from a seed list of phrases.
