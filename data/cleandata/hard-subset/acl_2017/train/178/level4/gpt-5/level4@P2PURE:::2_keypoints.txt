- Proposal: embed words, phrases, and ontology-mapped concepts in a shared vector space by treating concept-linked phrases as atomic tokens and training with skip-gram; evaluated on UMLS and Yago tasks.
- Strength: addresses non-compositional and lexically dissimilar phrase semantics with a plausible approach that yields competitive or superior concept similarity embeddings.
- Strength: released software that could benefit biomedical NLP researchers.
- Weakness: limited novelty—model is largely a tweak of skip-gram.
- Weakness: full model is not consistently best; Choi baseline outperforms on Mayo datasets and the model is only competitive with simpler prior methods (e.g., Chiu) on larger datasets.
- Terminology concern: calling the use of known phrases “distant supervision” is misleading—mapping phrase occurrences to contexts is not supervision in the usual predictive-task sense.
- Presentation issue: notation introduced (e.g., E_W) is never used elsewhere in the paper.
- Modeling concern: using a single global beta to control phrase compositionality is surprising and implausible; cross-validated beta values of zero for some corpora argue against compositionality.
- Experimental clarity: Table 4’s setup is unclear—if concept–phrase mapping is many-to-many, it’s not explained how word/phrase vectors are used to compute concept similarities (seems only concept vectors are applicable).
- Necessity question: Table 5 shows averaging phrase vectors (approx phr) performs best, calling into question the need for the concept ontology versus starting from a seed set of phrases.
