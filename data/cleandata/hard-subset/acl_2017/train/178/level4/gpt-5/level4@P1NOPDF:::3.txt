The authors present a method to jointly embed words, phrases, and concepts from plain text corpora and a manually constructed ontology in which concepts are represented by one or more phrases. They apply the method in the medical domain using UMLS and in the general domain using YAGO. For evaluation, they compare against simpler baselines and prior work, primarily on intrinsic similarity and relatedness benchmarks. They use existing datasets in the medical domain and collect a new general-domain concept similarity and relatedness dataset via Mechanical Turk, which they intend to release. They report results comparable to prior work.

Strengths:
- The joint embedding model is straightforward and reasonable. Its main value, in my view, is that it offers a configurable middle ground between treating phrases as atomic units and modeling their compositionality. The same principle is applied to concepts “composed” of several representative phrases.
- The paper reports a substantial amount of work: model development, creation of a new evaluation dataset, and multiple evaluations and analyses.

Weaknesses:
- The evaluation covers only intrinsic tasks, mainly similarity/relatedness datasets. As the authors note, such evaluations have limited power to predict performance on extrinsic tasks. It has become common to include at least one or two extrinsic tasks when evaluating embedding models.
- The similarity/relatedness datasets are presented as reflecting human judgments over concepts. However, if I understand correctly, annotators were shown phrases, not abstract concepts. These should therefore be considered phrase similarity datasets and analyzed as such.
- The medical concept dataset “mini MayoSRS” is extremely small (29 pairs), and its superset “MayoSRS” is only slightly larger (101 pairs) and has relatively low inter-annotator agreement. The other medical dataset, “UMNSRS,” is more reasonable in size but includes only concepts representable as single words and was presented to annotators as such. This should be stated in the paper and raises questions about its relevance for evaluating phrase and general concept representations.
- The authors extensively tune hyperparameters on the same datasets used for reporting results and for comparison to prior work. This makes the reported results and analyses difficult to trust.
- The paper argues that the method is superior to prior work because it achieves comparable results while prior work required more manual annotation. I find this unconvincing: the proposed approach also relies on large manually constructed ontologies, and the manually annotated dataset used by prior work was derived from existing clinical records rather than purpose-built annotations.
- I was missing deeper insights behind the reported numbers. The paper treats (a) the relation between a phrase and its component words and (b) the relation between a concept and its alternative phrases as analogous compositional relations. These differ in nature and merit separate analysis. For example, around line 588, I would expect an NLP analysis specific to phrase–word relations (e.g., headword effects) to explain the observed behavior. Another missing aspect that could strengthen the work is an investigation of the hyperparameters governing the trade-off between atomic and compositional views of phrases and concepts.

General Discussion:
Given the above weaknesses, I recommend rejection. I encourage the authors to improve their evaluation datasets and methodology—especially by adding extrinsic tasks, clarifying the nature of the human judgments (phrase vs. concept), avoiding tuning on test sets, and providing more targeted analyses—before resubmitting.
