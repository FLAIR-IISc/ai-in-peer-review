Summary and contributions
The paper proposes a weakly supervised method to jointly embed words, phrases, and ontology concepts in a shared vector space. Using distant supervision from mappings of concepts to representative phrases (from UMLS and YAGO), the model extends skip-gram with negative sampling to learn embeddings for words, phrases, and concepts, with hyperparameters that control the degree of compositionality between levels (phrase-by-word and concept-by-phrase). The authors evaluate on biomedical and general-domain similarity/relatedness datasets and introduce a new crowdsourced dataset for real-world entities.

As I see it, the primary contributions are:
1) A joint embedding framework that uses ontology-linked phrases as distant supervision to learn concept, phrase, and word embeddings with configurable compositionality constraints (β, η).
2) A new general-domain dataset of human similarity and relatedness judgments for real-world entities, intended for public release.
3) An empirical study across multiple corpora and ontologies, demonstrating competitive intrinsic performance without manual corpus annotation and improved vocabulary coverage.

Strengths
- Clear, sensible modeling idea with configurable compositionality: The architecture is a straightforward and interpretable extension of skip-gram that jointly learns at three levels. The β and η parameters provide a tunable middle ground between treating phrases/concepts as atomic units and enforcing compositional ties to their constituents. This is a useful design that can accommodate both idiomatic expressions and compositional phrases.

- Distant supervision at scale with broad coverage: By leveraging large ontologies and unlabeled corpora, the approach avoids costly corpus annotation while training concept embeddings at substantially larger vocabulary scales (reported >3x coverage). This addresses a practical bottleneck in concept representation learning and is supported by the coverage statistics provided.

- Competitive intrinsic performance across domains: On larger biomedical datasets (UMNSRS), the method is competitive or superior to prior concept representations that rely on manual annotations, and the model performs well on the new general-domain datasets. The results suggest that the joint space preserves meaningful similarity/relatedness relations among concepts, phrases, and words.

- Useful analyses of the joint space: The paper includes examinations of compositionality (phrase-word, concept-phrase), proximity between concept and phrase embeddings, and semantic-type clustering. While some analyses could go further, they provide evidence that ontological structure and phrase-linking are reflected in the learned space.

- Dataset contribution: The new real-world entity similarity/relatedness dataset fills a gap in commonly used evaluation resources focused largely on words rather than entities. The collection methodology and basic agreement statistics are documented.

Weaknesses
- Evaluation limited to intrinsic tasks: The paper relies solely on intrinsic similarity/relatedness evaluations, which are known to be weak predictors of downstream performance. Given current standards, at least one or two extrinsic tasks (e.g., entity linking, concept normalization, clinical coding, IR/reranking) would substantially strengthen the claim that the learned concept embeddings are useful.

- Mismatch between “concept” framing and phrase-based judgments: Both the biomedical and new general-domain evaluations appear to use surface phrases for human judgments, effectively making them phrase similarity datasets. This conflates phrase and concept evaluation and makes it difficult to isolate whether the method improves concept representations per se. The issue is more acute for UMNSRS, which is restricted to single-word concepts.

- Small or constrained biomedical benchmarks: Mini MayoSRS (29 pairs) and MayoSRS (101 pairs, with reported low IAA) are too small to draw robust conclusions. UMNSRS is larger but limited to single-word items, which weakens its relevance to evaluating multi-word phrases and concepts—the central focus of the paper. These limitations should be explicitly acknowledged and their implications discussed.

- Hyperparameter tuning on test sets: The paper reports tuning β, η, and other hyperparameters on the same datasets used for final reporting, which risks overfitting and undermines the comparative validity of the results. While the appendix mentions a cross-validation-like procedure, the main text still presents tuned performance on test-only resources without a held-out development split or a fixed evaluation protocol for prior work.

- Supervision and comparison framing: The claim of significantly less supervision than prior work is somewhat overstated. The approach depends on large, manually curated ontologies (UMLS/YAGO), and some compared baselines also leverage existing annotations from clinical records rather than bespoke labeling. A more nuanced comparison of supervision, data sources, and coverage would be appropriate.

- Insufficiently granular analysis of compositionality: While the paper introduces β and η to trade off atomic vs. compositional views, the analyses treat phrase-word and concept-phrase relations similarly. These relations differ in nature (linguistic composition vs. synonymy/denotation). More targeted analyses (e.g., headword effects, idiomaticity, ambiguity, sensitivity curves for β and η) would yield sharper insights into when and why the joint training helps.

Questions to authors
- Phrase detection and overlaps: How are overlapping or nested phrase matches handled during preprocessing? Is matching longest-first and are ties resolved deterministically?

- Training targets: In Section 3.2, do all words within a phrase serve as targets with their surrounding word contexts, or only the last word? Please clarify the skip-gram mechanics for words vs. phrases vs. concepts in the parallel corpora.

- Negative sampling matrix: Why is there a single |W| × k matrix used for both positive contexts and negatives? Are phrase/concept contexts projected via the word context space? Clarify the motivation and implications.

- Hyperparameter tuning: On which task(s) were the hyperparameters selected for each corpus? Were dev splits used? Can you provide results with fixed hyperparameters across datasets to assess robustness?

- Evaluation modality: For the biomedical and new general datasets, were annotators shown phrases, concept IDs, or both? If phrases, can you separate evaluations that directly test concept embeddings (e.g., via CUI-to-CUI comparisons independent of phrase choices)?

- β and η sensitivity: Can you provide ablations or sensitivity plots isolating the impact of β and η, particularly for highly compositional vs. idiomatic phrases and for concepts with many vs. few surface forms?

- Dataset splits: For the new datasets, can you release standard train/dev/test splits to avoid tuning on test sets and to support consistent future comparisons?

Additional comments and minor issues
- Line 69: “contexts” → “concepts.”
- Line 202: Please clarify how overlapping phrase matches are handled.
- Line 220: The dimensions should be |W| × d for EW. The term “negative sampling matrix” is confusing, since those embeddings are also used for positive context representations.
- Line 250: “the observed phrase just completed” is unclear. It reads as if only the last token of a phrase is a target. Please clarify how word-level training proceeds in the joint model.
- Equation (1): Notation is confusing; using c where o is expected. Please standardize symbols.
- Line 361: Pedersen et al., 2007 is missing in the references.
- Line 388: A 1–100 rating scale seems unnecessarily fine-grained given observed IAA. Please justify or report effects of binning.
- Line 430: Use “phrases” consistently instead of introducing “strings.”
- Line 496: Specify exactly which tasks drove hyperparameter tuning for each corpus; this is important for interpreting the reported results (even if details are in the appendix).
- Table 3: Trends are hard to interpret across corpora (e.g., PM+CL behaving differently from PM or CL). Consider plotting development-set performance vs. β, η, window size, and dimension.
- Line 535: Missing reference to Table 5.
- Claims about superior supervision efficiency should be tempered to reflect reliance on large curated ontologies.

Overall recommendation
While the method is well-motivated and the paper includes meaningful analyses and a valuable dataset contribution, the exclusive reliance on intrinsic evaluations, tuning on test sets, and the ambiguity around concept vs. phrase evaluation weaken the empirical claims. I recommend rejection in the current form. Strengthening the evaluation with at least one extrinsic task, clarifying the training/evaluation protocols, and deepening the compositionality analyses would significantly improve the paper.
