Summary
The paper proposes a weakly supervised method to jointly embed words, phrases, and ontology concepts in a shared vector space. Concepts are linked to one or more representative phrases from a manually constructed ontology (UMLS for biomedical concepts and YAGO for general-domain entities). The method treats phrase occurrences in raw text as distant supervision for concept contexts, and introduces hyperparameters to balance atomic treatment versus compositionality of phrases and concepts. The authors evaluate primarily on intrinsic similarity/relatedness benchmarks: standard biomedical datasets and a new crowdsourced dataset for real-world entities. Results are broadly comparable to prior work, with substantially larger vocabulary coverage and no corpus-level manual annotation.

Main contributions (as I see them)
- A joint embedding model for words, phrases, and concepts using distant supervision from ontology phrase mappings, with tunable trade-offs between atomic and compositional representations.
- A new crowdsourced dataset of similarity and relatedness judgments for people, places, and organizations (intended for release).
- Empirical evaluation and analyses across multiple corpora and ontologies, including compositionality and clustering by semantic type.

Strengths
- The proposed model is simple, sensible, and configurable. The ability to interpolate between treating phrases as atomic units and modeling their compositionality, and analogously between concepts and their representative phrases, is a clear practical advantage.
- The paper represents a substantial amount of work: model development, construction of a new evaluation dataset, and multiple evaluations and analyses across domains.
- The method avoids corpus-level manual annotation while achieving competitive performance and high vocabulary coverage, which is attractive from a scalability perspective.

Weaknesses
- The evaluation is limited to intrinsic tasks (mainly similarity/relatedness). As the authors note, such evaluations are weak predictors of downstream utility. It is now common to include at least one or two extrinsic tasks when assessing embedding models; their absence here limits the paper’s impact.
- The “concept” similarity/relatedness datasets are effectively phrase-based, since humans saw phrases during annotation. They should be treated and analyzed as phrase similarity datasets.
- The MayoSRS datasets are very small (Mini MayoSRS: 29 pairs; MayoSRS: 101 pairs) with reported low inter-annotator agreement. UMNSRS is larger, but restricted to concepts that can be represented as single words and were displayed as such to annotators. This limitation should be clearly stated, and it weakens the relevance of UMNSRS to phrase and general concept representations.
- Hyperparameters are extensively tuned on the same datasets used for reporting and comparison, which calls the reported results and analyses into question.
- The claim of superiority over prior work on the grounds of reduced manual annotation is not compelling. This work still depends on large manually constructed ontologies, and the “manual” datasets used in prior work come from existing clinical records rather than bespoke annotation.
- The analysis does not provide sufficient insight into the different natures of phrase–word versus concept–phrase relations. Treating both as analogous compositional relations is an oversimplification; each warrants targeted analysis. For example, around line 588, an NLP-oriented analysis (e.g., headword dominance) could help explain the observed behavior. Similarly, a deeper investigation of the η and β hyperparameters that control the atomic/compositional trade-offs would strengthen the contribution.

Questions to authors (optional)
- None beyond the minor clarifications below; however, addressing the evaluation-design concerns and the role of η/β would be valuable in the response.

Recommendation
Given the above weaknesses—especially the reliance on intrinsic evaluation, tuning on test sets, and limited insight into phrase/concept compositionality—I recommend rejection. I encourage the authors to improve the evaluation methodology and analyses before resubmission.
