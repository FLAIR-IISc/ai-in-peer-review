The authors present a method to jointly embed words, phrases, and concepts from plain-text corpora and a manually constructed ontology, where concepts are represented by one or more phrases. They apply the method in the medical domain using UMLS and in the general domain using YAGO. For evaluation, they compare against simpler baselines and prior work, focusing mostly on intrinsic similarity and relatedness benchmarks. They use existing medical-domain benchmarks and collect a new general-domain concept similarity and relatedness dataset via Mechanical Turk, which they intend to release. They report results comparable to prior work.

Strengths:
- The proposed joint embedding model is straightforward and reasonable. Its main value, in my view, is that it provides a configurable middle ground between treating phrases as atomic units and modeling their compositionality. The same idea is applied to concepts “composed” of multiple representative phrases.
- The paper reports a substantial amount of work, including model development, creation of a new evaluation dataset, and several evaluations and analyses.

Weaknesses:
- The evaluation includes only intrinsic tasks, mainly similarity/relatedness datasets. As the authors note, such evaluations have limited power to predict the utility of embeddings in extrinsic tasks. Accordingly, it has recently become common to include at least one or two extrinsic tasks when evaluating embedding models.
- The similarity/relatedness datasets are presented as capturing human judgments over concepts. However, if I understand correctly, annotators were shown phrases; therefore these should be considered phrase similarity datasets and analyzed as such.
- The medical concept dataset mini MayoSRS is extremely small (29 pairs), and its larger superset MayoSRS is only slightly larger (101 pairs) and was reported to have relatively low human annotator agreement. The other medical dataset, UMNSRS, is more reasonable in size, but includes only concepts that can be represented as single words and were presented as such to annotators. This should be stated, and it raises questions about the dataset’s relevance to phrase and general concept representations.
- As the authors themselves note, they extensively fine-tune hyperparameters on the very same datasets used to report results and compare with prior work. This makes the reported results and analyses questionable.
- The claim that the method is superior to prior work because it attains comparable results with less manual annotation is not very convincing. The method also relies on large manually constructed ontologies, and the manually annotated dataset used in prior work came from existing clinical records that did not require dedicated annotations.
- I was missing more informative insights beyond the reported numbers. The paper treats (i) the relation between a phrase and its component words and (ii) the relation between a concept and its alternative phrases as analogous compositional relations. However, these differ in nature and, in my view, each deserves its own analysis. For instance, around line 588, I would expect an NLP analysis specific to phrases vs. component words (e.g., the role of dominant headwords). Another useful addition would be an investigation of how the hyperparameters control the trade-off between atomic and compositional views of phrases and concepts.

General Discussion:
Given the above weaknesses, I recommend rejection. I encourage the authors to improve their evaluation datasets and methodology before resubmission.
