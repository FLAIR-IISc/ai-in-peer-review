- Paper proposes embedding words, phrases, and concepts by treating ontology-mapped phrases as atomic symbols and training with a skip-gram–style objective, evaluated on UMLS and YAGO.
- Addresses the important issue that many phrases are non-compositional and lexically dissimilar to semantically related phrases.
- Proposes a plausible model for training phrase embeddings under non-compositionality assumptions.
- Resulting embeddings are competitive or better for concept similarity in some settings.
- Released software could be valuable for biomedical NLP researchers.
- Model lacks novelty—essentially a tweak to skip-gram.
- The full proposed model often does not perform best; Choi baseline outperforms on Mayo datasets and similar trends appear elsewhere.
- On larger UMNSRS data the proposed method is at best competitive with simpler prior models (e.g., Chiu).
- The claim of using known phrases as “distant supervision” is unclear and misleading if occurrences only provide context rather than label prediction.
- Notation introduced in Section 3.2 (e.g., E_W) is unused later and should be cleaned up.
- Using a single global β to control compositionality is questionable; cross-validated β=0 for some corpora is surprising and suggests issues.
- Experimental setup for Table 4 is unclear about how phrase/word vectors are combined for many-to-many concept–phrase mappings.
- Table 5 shows averaging phrase embeddings (approximate phr) performs best, calling into question the necessity of the concept ontology versus starting from seed phrases.
