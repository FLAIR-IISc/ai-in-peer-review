This paper proposes a method to jointly embed words, phrases, and concepts using plain-text corpora and a manually constructed ontology in which concepts are represented by one or more phrases. The approach is instantiated in the medical domain with the UMLS ontology and in the general domain with YAGO. The authors evaluate primarily on intrinsic similarity and relatedness benchmarks, using existing datasets in the medical domain and a new general-domain concept similarity/relatedness dataset they collected with Mechanical Turk and intend to release. They report results comparable to prior work.

Strengths:
- The joint embedding model is straightforward and intuitive. Its main value is in offering a configurable middle ground between treating phrases as atomic units and modeling their compositionality. The same principle is applied to concepts that are “composed” of multiple representative phrases.
- The paper reports a substantial amount of work: model development, the creation of a new evaluation dataset, and several evaluations and analyses.

Weaknesses:
- The evaluation covers only intrinsic tasks, mainly similarity/relatedness datasets. As the authors note, such evaluations have limited predictive power for downstream utility. It has become standard to include at least one or two extrinsic tasks when evaluating embedding models.
- The datasets are presented as measuring similarity between concepts, but (if I understand correctly) annotators judged similarity based on displayed phrases. These should therefore be treated and analyzed as phrase similarity datasets.
- The medical concept datasets are weak: miniMayoSRS is extremely small (29 pairs); MayoSRS is only slightly larger (101 pairs) and has relatively low inter-annotator agreement. UMNSRS is larger but includes only concepts representable as single words, and these were shown as single words to annotators. This should be stated explicitly and makes the dataset’s relevance to phrase and general concept representations questionable.
- The authors extensively tune hyperparameters on the same datasets used for reporting and comparison with prior work, rendering the reported results and analyses questionable.
- The claim of superiority to prior work because similar results are achieved with less manual annotation is not compelling. This work also relies on large, manually constructed ontologies, and the manually annotated dataset used in the prior work is derived from existing clinical records that did not require dedicated annotation.
- I would have liked deeper analysis beyond the headline numbers. The paper treats (a) the relation between a phrase and its component words and (b) the relation between a concept and its alternative phrases as analogous types of compositionality, but these relations differ in nature and merit separate analyses. For example, around line 588, an NLP-focused analysis specific to phrase–word relations would be appropriate (e.g., the role of dominant head words). Another missing analysis that could strengthen the paper is a study of how hyperparameters control the trade-off between atomic and compositional views for both phrases and concepts.

General discussion and recommendation:
Given the above weaknesses, I recommend rejection at this time. I encourage the authors to improve their evaluation methodology and datasets, include extrinsic tasks, and provide targeted analyses of the two distinct compositional relations before resubmission.
