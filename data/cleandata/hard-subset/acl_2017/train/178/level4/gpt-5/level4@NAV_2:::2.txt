Summary:
This paper introduces a framework for embedding words, phrases, and ontology concepts into a shared vector space. The approach leverages an ontology mapping from concepts to phrases, replaces matched phrases in text with unique tokens, and then trains embeddings for words, these phrase tokens, and their associated concepts via a skip-gram–style objective. The method is evaluated on concept similarity and relatedness benchmarks using UMLS and YAGO as the underlying ontologies.

Strengths:
- The paper tackles the important issue that lexically dissimilar phrases can denote the same concept and that many expressions are non-compositional. The proposed training setup for phrase embeddings is reasonable, and the resulting vectors are shown to be competitive or better on concept similarity/relatedness.
- The released software is likely to be practically useful for biomedical NLP practitioners.

Weaknesses:
- The methodological novelty is limited; the approach is largely a straightforward modification of skip-gram.
- The complete model does not consistently outperform prior art in the reported results (Table 4): on the two Mayo datasets, the Choi baseline is markedly stronger. A similar pattern appears in Table 6 as well.
- On the larger UMNSRS benchmarks, the proposed approach is at best on par with earlier, simpler baselines (e.g., Chiu).

General Discussion:
- The paper frames the approach as using known phrases as distant supervision, but it is unclear what constitutes “supervision” here. If each occurrence of a phrase mapped to a concept simply contributes context for training embeddings, this does not align with supervision in the usual predictive sense (e.g., labeling concepts in text), which makes the terminology confusing.
- The matrices introduced in Section 3.2 (e.g., E_W, etc.) are not subsequently referenced, which breaks the continuity of the notation.
- Using a single global parameter β to modulate phrase-by-word compositionality is unexpected, as it implies a uniform degree of compositionality across all phrases. Moreover, the cross-validated β values in Table 3 are puzzling: for PM+CL and WikiNYT, β is 0, effectively negating compositionality.
- The setup behind Table 4 requires clarification. The labels in the datasets are at the concept/entity level. Given the many-to-many mapping between concepts and phrases, how are phrase/word vectors utilized for the similarity computations? It would seem only concept vectors should be applicable.
- In Table 5, the APPROXPHR method (approximating concepts by averaging their phrase embeddings) performs best. This raises the question of whether the ontology is necessary for this evaluation; starting from a seed list of phrases might achieve similar performance without explicit concept embeddings.
