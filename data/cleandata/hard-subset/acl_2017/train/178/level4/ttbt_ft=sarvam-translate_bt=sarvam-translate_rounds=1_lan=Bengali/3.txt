The authors have presented a method for embedding words, phrases, and concepts based on a plain text corpora and a manually created ontology, where concepts are represented through one or more phrases. They apply their method in the medical field using the UMLS ontology and in the general field using the YAGO ontology. To evaluate their method, the authors compared it to simple baselines and previous work, most of which were based on underlying similarity and relationship benchmarks. They used existing benchmarks in the medical field and used mechanical Turing to create a new general-domain concept similarity and relationship dataset that they wanted to publish. They reported results that were comparable to previous work.

Strengths:
- The proposed joint embedding model seems simple and reasonable to me. In my opinion, its main value lies in finding a (configurable) middle ground between considering phrases as atomic units on the one hand and considering their compositionality on the other. The same method is applied to concepts, which are composed of several representative phrases.
- This paper describes a suitable volume of work, including model creation, additional contributions in the form of a new evaluation dataset, and several evaluations and analyses.

Weaknesses:
- The evaluations mentioned in this paper only include underlying tasks, mainly similarity/relationship datasets. As the authors note, such evaluations are known to have very limited ability to predict the usefulness of embedding in extrinsic tasks. Accordingly, it has recently become much more common to include at least one or two extrinsic tasks as part of the evaluation of the embedding model.
- The similarity/relationship evaluation datasets used in the paper are presented as datasets that record people's judgments of similarity between concepts. However, if I understand correctly, the actual judgments were made based on the presentation of phrases to human annotators, and therefore these should be considered phrase similarity datasets and analyzed accordingly.
- The medical concept evaluation dataset, 'Mini MyOSRSS', is very small (29 pairs), and its larger superset 'MyOSRSS' is only slightly larger (101 pairs) and has been reported to have relatively low agreement among human annotators. Another medical concept evaluation dataset, 'UNSRSS', is more reasonable in size, but it is only based on concepts that can be presented as single words and were presented in that way to human annotators. This should be mentioned in the paper, and the relevance of this dataset in the presentation of phrases and general concepts is questionable.
- The authors themselves have mentioned that they (quite extensively) fine-tuned the hyperparameters of the datasets they used to report their results and compare them with previous work. This raises questions about all reported results and analyses.
- The authors believe that their method is much better than previous work because it achieved comparable results, whereas previous work required much more manual annotation. I don't think this argument is very strong, because the authors use a large manually created ontology and the manually annotated dataset used in previous work comes from existing clinical records, where dedicated annotation was not required.
- In general, I have not found more useful insights into the reasons behind the reported numbers. The authors try to consider the relationship between a phrase and its component words on the one hand and a concept and its alternative phrases on the other as similar compositional relationships. However, they are different in nature, and in my opinion, each requires a dedicated analysis. For example, near line 588, I would expect a specific NLP analysis for the relationship between phrases and their component words. Perhaps the reason for the reported behavior is dominant phrase headwords, etc. Another aspect that was missing but could have strengthened the work is an investigation into the impact of hyperparameters that control the compromise between the atomic and compositional views of phrases and concepts.

General Discussion:
Due to the aforementioned weaknesses, I advise rejecting this submission. I encourage the authors to improve their evaluation datasets and methods before resubmitting this paper.
