The authors have presented a method for embedding words, phrases, and concepts based on an ontology created manually and using a plain text corpus, where concepts are represented through one or more phrases. They apply their method in the medical field using UMLS ontology and in the general field using Yago ontology. To evaluate their method, the authors compared it to common baselines and previous work, most of which were based on implicit similarity and relation benchmarks. They used existing benchmarks in the medical field and used mechanical Turing to create a new general domain concept similarity and relation dataset that they wanted to publish. They reported results that are comparable to previous work.

Strengths:
- The proposed joint embedding model seems simple and reasonable to me. In my opinion, its main value lies in finding a (configurable) middle ground between considering phrases as atomic units on the one hand and considering their compositivity on the other. The same method is applied to concepts, which consist of several representative phrases.
- This paper describes a reasonable amount of work, including model creation, additional contributions in the form of a new evaluation dataset, and several evaluations and analyses.

Weaknesses:
- The evaluations mentioned in this paper only include implicit tasks, mainly similarity/relation datasets. As the authors note, such evaluations are known to have very limited capacity to estimate the usefulness of embedding in external tasks. Accordingly, it has recently become much more common to include at least one or two external tasks as part of the evaluation of the embedding model.
- The similarity/relation evaluation datasets used in the paper are presented as datasets that record human judgments about similarity between concepts. However, if I understand correctly, the actual judgments were based on the presentation of phrases to human annotations, and therefore should be considered phrase similarity datasets and analyzed accordingly.
- The medical concept evaluation dataset, 'Mini MIOSRS' is very small (29 pairs) and its larger superset 'MIOSRS' is only slightly larger (101 pairs) and relatively fewer agreements between human annotations have been reported. Another medical concept evaluation dataset, 'UNSRS' is more reasonable in size, but it is only based on concepts that can be presented as single words and was therefore presented in human annotations. This should be mentioned in the paper, and the relevance of this dataset in the presentation of phrases and general concepts is questionable.
- The authors themselves have noted that they have (quite extensively) fine-tuned the hyperparameters of the datasets they used to report their results and compare them with previous work. This raises questions about all reported results and analyses.
- The authors believe that their method is much better than previous work because it achieved comparable results, whereas previous work required much more manual annotation. I don't think this argument is very strong, because the authors use a large manually created ontology and the manually annotated datasets used in previous work come from existing clinical records, where dedicated annotation was not required.
- In general, I did not find more useful insights behind the numbers reported. The authors try to consider the relationship between a phrase and its component words on the one hand, and the relationship between a concept and its alternative phrases on the other hand, as similar compositional relationships. However, they are different in nature and, in my opinion, require a dedicated analysis for each. For example, near line 588, I would expect a specific NLP analysis for the relationship between phrases and their component words. Perhaps the reason for the reported behavior is the dominant phrase headword, etc. Another aspect that was missing but could have strengthened the work is the investigation of the effect of hyperparameters that control the compromise between the atomic and compositional perspectives of phrases and concepts.

General Discussion:
Due to the above weaknesses, I recommend rejecting this submission. I encourage the authors to improve their evaluation datasets and methods before resubmitting this paper.
