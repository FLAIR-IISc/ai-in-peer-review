The authors present a method for jointly embedding words, phrases, and concepts based on corpus and manually constructed ontology in plain text, where concepts are represented by one or more phrases. They apply their method in the medical field using UMLS ontology and in the general field using YAGO ontology. To evaluate their approach, the authors compare it to a simple baseline and prior work, which is mostly based on internal similarity and relatedness benchmarks. They use existing benchmarks in the medical field and a mechanical turner to generate a new general-domain concept similarity and relatedness dataset, which they also intend to release. They report results that are comparable to prior work.

**Strengths:**

*   The proposed joint embedding model is straightforward and seems reasonable to me. In my opinion, its main value lies in reaching a (configurable) middle ground between treating phrases as atomic units on one hand and considering their totality on the other. This approach applies to concepts that are 'built' from multiple representative phrases.
*   The paper describes a good amount of work, including model development, an additional contribution in the form of a new evaluation dataset, and several evaluations and analyses.

**Weaknesses:**

*   The evaluation described in this paper only includes internal work, mainly on the similarity/relatedness dataset. As the authors note, such an evaluation has very limited power in predicting the usefulness of embedding in external tasks. Accordingly, including at least one or two external tasks as part of the evaluation of the embedding model has recently become very common.
*   The similarity/relatedness evaluation dataset used in the paper is presented as a dataset recording human judgments of similarity between concepts. However, if I understand correctly, the actual judgments were made based on presenting phrases to human annotators, and therefore they should be considered as phrase similarity datasets, and analyzed in this way.
*   The medical concept evaluation dataset, 'Mini MayoSRS', is very small (29 pairs), and its larger superset 'MayoSRS' is only slightly larger (101 pairs) and reported to have relatively low human annotator consensus. Other medical concept evaluation datasets, 'UNSRS', are more appropriate in size, but it is based only on concepts that can be represented as single words, and they were represented to human annotators in this way. This should be mentioned in the paper and makes the relevance of this dataset in relation to the representation of phrases and general concepts questionable.
*   As the authors themselves note (in considerable detail), they fix their hyperparameters on the same datasets for which they report their results and compare them to prior work. This makes all reported results and analyses questionable.
*   The authors suggest that their method is better than prior work, as it achieved comparable results while prior work required much more manual annotation. I don't think this argument is very strong because the authors also use large manually constructed ontologies, and also because the manually annotated dataset used in prior work comes from existing clinical records that did not require dedicated annotation.
*   In general, I was not getting much useful information about what is going on behind the numbers reported. The authors try to consider the relationship between a phrase and its constituent words on one hand, and the relationship between a concept and its alternative phrases on the other hand, as a similar type of constructive relationship. However, they are different in nature and in my opinion each deserves a dedicated analysis. For example, around line 588, I would expect a specific NLP analysis for the relationship between phrases and their constituent words. Perhaps the reason for the behavior reported is the major phrase headword, etc. Another aspect that was absent but could have strengthened the work is the investigation of the effect of hyperparameters that control the tradeoff between the atomic and constructive approaches of phrases and concepts.

**General Discussion:**

Due to the weaknesses mentioned above, I advise rejecting this presentation. I encourage the authors to consider improving their evaluation datasets and methodology before re-submitting this paper.
