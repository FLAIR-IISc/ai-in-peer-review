- Addresses the gap between lexical similarity and semantic relatedness, especially for non-compositional phrases.  
- Proposes a plausible method to learn phrase embeddings by mapping ontology concepts to atomic phrases in text.  
- Adaptation of skip-gram jointly trains embeddings for words, phrases, and concepts, yielding embeddings that capture semantic relationships.  
- Experimental results show competitive or superior concept similarity performance in some cases.  
- Publicly released software is a useful resource for biomedical NLP researchers.  

- Novelty is incremental: method is essentially a modification of skip-gram rather than a new architecture.  
- Performance is underwhelming against simpler baselines (e.g., Choi) on Mayo datasets and only competitive on UMNSRS, questioning added complexity.  

- The use of the term “distant supervision” is unclear and should be clarified, as the training signal differs from conventional supervised tasks.  
- Introduced notation (e.g., E_W) is unused later and thus unnecessary/confusing.  
- A global compositionality constant β is restrictive and counterintuitive; cross-validation results (β=0) suggest odd conclusions against compositionality.  
- Experimental procedure for computing concept similarities is unclear given many-to-many concept↔phrase mappings; it seems similarities may rely solely on concept vectors.  
- The “approximate phr” baseline (averaging associated phrases) achieves the best performance in one table, calling into question the necessity of using an explicit ontology.  

- Overall: solid, well-executed application with limited novelty and mixed empirical gains; recommend acceptance with minor revisions to clarify supervision, β, and interpretations of key results.
