This paper presents an extension to standard word embedding techniques, aiming to create unified representations for words, phrases, and concepts that share a common meaning. The core idea is to leverage an existing ontology to identify sets of synonymous or related terms (words, phrases, and concepts). These terms are then replaced with a single identifier within the training corpus, creating a “tagged” corpus. This tagged corpus is subsequently combined with the original corpus and used to train the embeddings. The approach is applied specifically within the biomedical domain, utilizing relevant corpora and ontologies. 

A noteworthy contribution of this work is the creation of a new test dataset for evaluating word similarity and relatedness, focused on real-world biomedical entities. This dataset represents a valuable resource for the community. 

Overall, the paper is well-written and the proposed technique is intuitively sound. However, the contribution, while potentially useful, feels incremental. The evaluation is limited in scope, focusing exclusively on the biomedical domain, which restricts the generalizability of the findings. Expanding the discussion around the novel test dataset would strengthen the paper considerably; in fact, this resource may ultimately be the most significant outcome of this research.

There is a minor technical issue in Equation 8, which attempts to define a calculation analogous to Mean Average Precision (MAP). While the intention is commendable, the formulation does not represent a true probability, even considering an infinite vocabulary size. The authors should revise the explanation to remove probabilistic interpretations and clarify the metric being used.  Furthermore, a natural cutoff point for ranking should be considered instead of ranking the entire vocabulary.

Finally, a minor editorial correction is needed on line 556: "most concepts has" should be "most concepts have."
