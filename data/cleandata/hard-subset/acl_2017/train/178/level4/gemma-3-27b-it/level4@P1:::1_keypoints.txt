- Paper extends word embeddings to produce representations for phrases and concepts by assigning unique identifiers to sets of synonymous phrases/words/concepts.
- Method creates a “tagged” corpus by replacing occurrences with identifiers and trains on both original and tagged corpora.
- Concept/phrase sets are derived from an ontology and the work focuses on biomedical corpora and ontologies.
- A new test dataset for evaluating similarity and relatedness between real-world entities is created and presented as a novel contribution.
- Paper is generally well-written and the technique is intuitive but offers limited methodological novelty.
- Primary contribution is the new dataset rather than a significant algorithmic advance.
- The weakly-supervised approach is practical and scalable, avoiding costly human annotation.
- Empirical results show competitive performance on existing biomedical concept similarity and relatedness tasks.
- Scope is limited by heavy focus on the biomedical domain, reducing generalizability of findings.
- The analysis of the newly generated dataset’s characteristics and potential biases is insufficient and needs expansion.
- Mathematical imprecision in Equation 8: MAP formulation is incorrect as written and should not be described as a probability; consider defining a cutoff.
- Minor typographical correction: Line 556 “most concepts has” → “most concepts have”.
- Strength: clear presentation and detailed explanation of methodology and experimental setup.
- Weakness: core embedding technique is a straightforward extension of existing methods (limited novelty).
- Question: please provide more details on dataset characteristics, entity-type distributions, and annotation biases.
- Question: how sensitive are results to hyperparameters (e.g., β and η) and were extensive ablation studies performed?
- Question: how does this method compare to recent transformer-based embeddings (e.g., BERT) not specifically designed for concept embedding?
