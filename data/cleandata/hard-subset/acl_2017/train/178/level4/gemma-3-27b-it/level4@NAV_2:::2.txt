This paper presents a model for embedding words, phrases and concepts into vector spaces. To do so, it uses an ontology of concepts, each of which is mapped to phrases. These phrases are found in text corpora and treated as atomic symbols. Using this, the paper uses what is essentially the skip-gram method to train embeddings for words, the now atomic phrases and also the concepts associated with them. The proposed work is evaluated on the task of concept similarity and relatedness using UMLS and Yago to act as the backing ontologies.

Strengths:
The key question addressed by the paper is that phrases that are not lexically similar can be semantically close and, furthermore, not all phrases are compositional in nature. To this end, the paper proposes a plausible model to train phrase embeddings. The trained embeddings are shown to be competitive or better at identifying similarity between concepts.
The software released with the paper could be useful for biomedical NLP researchers. 
- 
Weaknesses:
The primary weakness of the paper is that the model exhibits limited novelty, representing largely a refinement of the skip-gram method. 
Furthermore, the full model presented does not consistently achieve state-of-the-art performance; existing baselines, such as the Choi model, significantly outperform it on the Mayo datasets (Table 4), and a similar trend is observable in Table 6. On the larger UMNSRS dataset, the proposed model demonstrates, at best, competitive performance with simpler, pre-existing models (Chiu).
- 
General Discussion:
The paper frames its approach as utilizing “distant supervision” from phrases linked to concepts. However, the nature of this “supervision” is unclear. The paper appears to utilize every occurrence of a concept-associated phrase to provide context for training word embeddings, which differs from traditional supervised learning paradigms (e.g., concept identification). The terminology, therefore, warrants clarification.
The notation introduced in Section 3.2 (E_W, etc.) is not subsequently utilized within the remainder of the paper.
The introduction of β to control phrase compositionality is unexpected. Implementing a single global constant to determine the “compositionality” of all phrases is a simplifying assumption. The cross-validation results (Table 3) reveal that β is frequently set to zero for the PM+CL and WikiNYT corpora, effectively negating compositionality. 
The experimental setup for Table 4 requires further explanation. The paper asserts that the data represents similarity/relatedness of concepts (or entities). Given the many-to-many mapping between concepts and phrases, the method for utilizing phrase/word vectors to compute similarities remains unclear; it appears limited to employing only the concept vectors.
In Table 5, the approximate phrase method – which approximates concepts using the average of their constituent phrases – achieves the highest performance. This raises questions about the necessity of the concept ontology itself. It appears that equivalent results could be obtained by initiating with a seed set of phrases.

Questions to Authors:
1. Could the authors clarify the nature of the “supervision” provided by the phrase-concept associations, and how it differs from traditional supervised learning paradigms?
2. The parameter β seems to have a limited impact on performance. Could the authors provide intuition about why a global β parameter is sufficient, and whether more nuanced strategies for incorporating compositionality might be explored?
3. In the evaluation using concept similarity/relatedness, how are the phrase/word vectors utilized given the many-to-many mapping between concepts and phrases?
4. Given the performance of the approximate phrase method in Table 5, what is the primary justification for incorporating the explicit concept ontology into the embedding process?

Additional Comments:
- The experimental evaluation could be strengthened by including additional downstream tasks beyond concept similarity and relatedness.
- A more thorough ablation study could help identify the individual contributions of different components of the proposed model.
