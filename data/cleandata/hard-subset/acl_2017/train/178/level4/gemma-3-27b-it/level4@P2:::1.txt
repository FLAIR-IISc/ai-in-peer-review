This paper introduces a method for jointly learning embeddings of concepts, phrases, and words from unlabeled text, leveraging representative phrases from ontologies as a form of distant supervision. The core idea is to replace concept phrases in a corpus with unique identifiers, creating a tagged corpus, and then train embeddings on a combination of the original and tagged corpora. The authors evaluate their approach on biomedical and general-domain data, demonstrating competitive performance on concept similarity and relatedness tasks, and notably achieving broader vocabulary coverage than existing methods. A novel dataset for evaluating similarity and relatedness of real-world entities is also presented.

The primary contribution of this work is the proposed method for jointly embedding concepts, phrases, and words using distant supervision. While the underlying techniques (word embeddings, negative sampling) are established, the application to concept embedding without human annotation, and the integration of ontological knowledge via phrase substitution, represent a valuable contribution. The second notable contribution is the creation of a new dataset for evaluating the similarity and relatedness of real-world entities, which addresses a gap in existing resources.

**Strengths:**

1. **Novelty of Approach:** The method of using representative phrases as distant supervision for learning concept embeddings is a creative and practical solution to the challenge of obtaining labeled data for ontology concepts. This allows the creation of embeddings for a broader range of concepts than methods relying on manual annotation.
2. **Competitive Performance:** The results demonstrate that the learned embeddings achieve performance comparable to existing methods that *do* rely on human annotation. This suggests the proposed method effectively captures semantic relationships, despite the inherent noise of distant supervision.
3. **New Dataset:** The creation of a dataset for evaluating the similarity and relatedness of real-world entities fills a demonstrable gap in the available resources for this task. This dataset has the potential to be a valuable resource for the broader research community. 
4. **Scalability:** The method allows for scaling to larger vocabularies without the cost of human annotation, a significant practical advantage.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Scope of Evaluation:** The primary evaluation focuses on the biomedical domain. While understandable given the data used, the generalizability of the method to other domains remains unclear and should be addressed. More experimentation with general-domain corpora and tasks would strengthen the paper.
2. **Overstated Probabilistic Interpretation:** Equation 8 presents a calculation that is not a probability. While the authors intend to define MAP, the formulation lacks probabilistic grounding and should be clarified. The accompanying text should remove language referencing probability.
3. **Modest Methodological Contribution:** The core idea, while clever, builds heavily on existing word embedding techniques. The novelty lies primarily in the application and adaptation of these techniques to the specific problem of concept embedding, rather than a fundamentally new algorithmic development.
4. **Insufficient Discussion of Dataset Details:** While the new dataset is a valuable contribution, the paper could benefit from a more in-depth discussion of the dataset creation process, including the rationale for entity pair selection and a more detailed analysis of inter-annotator agreement beyond Fleiss’ kappa. 

**Questions to Authors:**

1. How sensitive are the results to the choice of representative phrases for the ontology concepts? Could the performance be improved by incorporating multiple phrases or using a more sophisticated phrase selection strategy?
2. Can you elaborate on the computational cost of the phrase replacement step? Is this step scalable to very large corpora?
3. How do the hyperparameters (β, η, etc.) influence the performance on different tasks and datasets? Can you provide more insight into the optimal settings for various scenarios?
4.  What are the potential benefits of incorporating additional sources of knowledge, such as concept hierarchies or relationships between concepts, into the training process?

**Additional Comments:**

* Consider including a more detailed ablation study to assess the contribution of the different components of the proposed method (e.g., joint embedding, distant supervision).
* Provide an analysis of the computational resources required for training the embeddings.
