The authors present a method to jointly embed words, phrases and concepts, based on plain text corpora and a manually-constructed ontology, in which concepts are represented by one or more phrases. They apply their method in the medical domain using the UMLS ontology, and in the general domain using the YAGO ontology. To evaluate their approach, the authors compare it to simpler baselines and prior work, mostly on intrinsic similarity and relatedness benchmarks. They use existing benchmarks in the medical domain, and generate a new general-domain concept similarity and relatedness dataset using mechanical turkers, which they also intend to release. They report results that are comparable to prior work.

**Strengths:**

- The proposed joint embedding model is straightforward and makes reasonable sense. Its primary value, in my view, is in reaching a configurable balance between treating phrases as atomic units and accounting for their compositionality. The same approach is applied to concepts being ‘composed’ of several representative phrases.
- The paper describes a substantial amount of work, including model development, a novel evaluation dataset as a contribution, and several evaluations and analyses performed.

**Weaknesses:**

- The evaluation relies solely on intrinsic tasks, primarily similarity/relatedness datasets. As the authors acknowledge, these evaluations have limited predictive power for the utility of embeddings in extrinsic tasks. Including at least one or two extrinsic tasks would strengthen the evaluation.
- The similarity/relatedness evaluation datasets are presented as judgments of concept similarity; however, the annotations were based on phrases, making them phrase similarity datasets requiring analysis as such.
- The medical concept evaluation datasets ('mini MayoSRS' and 'MayoSRS') are small (29 and 101 pairs, respectively) and have relatively low inter-annotator agreement. ‘UMNSRS’ is more reasonable in size but focuses on concepts representable as single words, which is relevant to representations of phrases and general concepts. This limitation should be discussed.
- The authors extensively fine-tune hyperparameters on the same datasets used for evaluation and comparison, raising concerns about the validity of the reported results and analyses.
- The argument that the method surpasses prior work due to reduced manual annotation is weak, given the reliance on large, manually constructed ontologies and the use of pre-existing, readily available clinical records for prior work.
- The paper lacks in-depth analysis of the model's inner workings. While the authors attempt to treat the relationships between phrases and their components and between concepts and their representative phrases similarly, these relationships differ fundamentally. A dedicated analysis of each is warranted. For instance, around line 588, an NLP analysis specific to the relationship between phrases and their component words would be valuable. Investigating the impact of the hyperparameters controlling the trade-off between atomic and compositional representations would also strengthen the work.

**Questions to Authors:**

- Can you provide a rationale for selecting a similarity scale of 0-100 for the human annotation task?
- What task was used for hyperparameter tuning, and can you present development set trends for these hyperparameters?
- How are phrase overlaps handled during the preprocessing stage?
- Regarding equation 1, why is 'c' used instead of 'o'? This causes confusion.
- Line 220 states a negative sampling matrix with dimensions |W| x k, but the described matrices suggest dimensions |W| x d. Could you clarify this discrepancy? Additionally, the term “negative sampling matrix” is confusing, as these embeddings are also utilized in positive instances.
- Regarding "the observed phrase just completed" (line 250), could you clarify how words are trained in the joint model? The current wording suggests only the last word of a phrase is considered, which is unlikely.

**Additional Comments:**

- Line 069: Change “contexts” to “concepts”.
- Line 202: Please clarify how phrase overlaps are handled.
- Line 361: The Pedersen et al. (2007) reference is missing.
- Line 430: The term “strings” is confusing; continue using “phrases”.
- Line 496: Specify which task was used for hyperparameter tuning.
- Table 3: The differing behavior of PM+CL compared to PM or CL alone warrants further investigation and discussion.

Due to the aforementioned weaknesses, I recommend rejecting this submission. I encourage the authors to address the evaluation methodology and datasets before resubmitting.
