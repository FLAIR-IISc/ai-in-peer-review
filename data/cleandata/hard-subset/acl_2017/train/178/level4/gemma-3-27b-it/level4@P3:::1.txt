This paper proposes a weakly-supervised method for jointly embedding concepts, phrases, and words, leveraging representative phrases for ontology concepts as a form of distant supervision. The core idea is to replace concept phrases in a corpus with unique identifiers and then train embeddings on this modified corpus alongside the original text. The method is evaluated on biomedical and general-domain tasks, demonstrating competitive performance on concept similarity and relatedness, and introduces a novel dataset for evaluating real-world entity similarity.

The primary contribution of this work lies in the practical demonstration of learning concept embeddings without relying on expensive human annotations. While the core technique builds upon established word embedding methods, the adaptation to incorporate concepts and phrases via distant supervision is a valuable contribution, particularly in domains like biomedicine where manual annotation is costly. A secondary contribution is the introduction of a new dataset for evaluating the similarity and relatedness of real-world entities, addressing a gap in existing resources. The authors also provide a thorough empirical evaluation across diverse corpora and ontologies.

**Strengths:**

1. **Practicality and Scalability:** The method’s key strength is its ability to generate concept embeddings from unlabeled text, removing a significant bottleneck in many NLP applications. This is particularly important for specialized domains like biomedicine where expert annotation is expensive and time-consuming. The reported 3x coverage in vocabulary size compared to existing methods highlights the potential for broader knowledge representation.
2. **Competitive Performance:** The embeddings achieve performance competitive with existing methods on established concept similarity and relatedness tasks, despite not requiring human annotation. This demonstrates the effectiveness of the distant supervision approach and validates the model's ability to capture semantic relationships.
3. **Novel Dataset:** The creation and release of a new dataset for evaluating the similarity and relatedness of real-world entities is a valuable contribution to the community. This resource will facilitate further research in entity representation and evaluation.
4. **Thorough Evaluation:** The authors conduct a comprehensive evaluation across multiple corpora (PubMed, Clinical notes, Wikipedia/NYT) and ontologies (UMLS, YAGO), providing a robust assessment of the method’s generalizability and performance. The ablation studies and hyperparameter tuning further strengthen the experimental analysis.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Novelty of Core Technique:** While the application to concept embedding is useful, the underlying technique of distant supervision and joint embedding is not fundamentally novel. The core innovation lies in the specific adaptation and application to this problem, rather than a breakthrough in embedding methodology itself.
2. **Dependence on Ontology Quality:** The performance of the method is inherently tied to the quality and coverage of the underlying ontology. Errors or incompleteness in the ontology could propagate to the learned embeddings, limiting their effectiveness. The paper acknowledges this but could benefit from a more in-depth discussion of the potential impact of ontology limitations.
3. **MAP Calculation Issue:** The authors' MAP calculation (Eq. 8) does not represent a probability, as indicated in the paper. While this is a technical detail, it undermines the theoretical foundation of that specific analysis and requires clarification. The suggestion to use a natural cutoff instead of ranking the entire vocabulary is reasonable.
4. **Limited Analysis of Error Cases:** The paper presents a broad overview of performance, but lacks a deeper dive into specific error cases. Analyzing examples where the embeddings fail to capture semantic relationships could provide valuable insights for future improvement.

**Questions to Authors:**

1. How sensitive is the performance of the method to the choice of representative phrases for each concept? Are there strategies for automatically selecting the most informative phrases?
2. Could you elaborate on the potential impact of noisy or inaccurate mappings within the ontologies on the quality of the learned embeddings?
3. How does the performance of your method compare to other recent approaches that leverage knowledge graphs or external resources for concept embedding, even if those approaches require some form of supervision?
4. Regarding the MAP calculation, will you revise the explanation to reflect that it is not a probability, and consider using a more appropriate metric for evaluating the neighborhood structure of the embeddings?

**Additional Comments:**

* The paper could benefit from a more detailed discussion of the computational resources required for training and evaluating the embeddings, particularly for large-scale corpora.
* Consider including a qualitative example of learned embeddings – perhaps visualizing the embedding space and highlighting the proximity of related concepts and phrases.
