- Paper proposes a joint embedding model that maps words, phrases, and ontology concepts into a shared vector space using phrases as atomic symbols in the corpus.  
- Method adapts the skip-gram framework and uses representative phrases as distant supervision to train concept embeddings.  
- Evaluation uses concept similarity and relatedness benchmarks constructed from UMLS and YAGO, including new datasets introduced by the authors.  

Contributions:
- Joint embedding framework: unified approach to represent words, phrases, and concepts, addressing non-compositional and multi-form concepts.  
- Distant supervision with phrases: practical, scalable strategy that reduces reliance on manual annotation.  
- Novel evaluation datasets: new resources for measuring similarity and relatedness of real-world entities.  

Strengths:
- Tackles a relevant problem of representing concepts with multiple textual forms and non-compositional semantics.  
- Achieves competitive performance on concept similarity and relatedness tasks compared to strong baselines.  
- Practical and scalable approach with released code, avoiding expensive annotation.  
- Thorough experimental evaluation with multiple baselines and embedding analyses.  

Weaknesses:
- Limited methodological novelty: mainly an adaptation of skip-gram with distant supervision.  
- Inconsistent results: several datasets show baselines (e.g., Choi) outperforming the proposed model.  
- Loose use of the term “distant supervision,” lacking a clear traditional supervisory signal.  
- Introduces notation (e.g., E_W) that is unused later, adding unnecessary complexity.  
- Simplistic compositionality control via a single global parameter β, which often is tuned to zero.  
- Unclear procedure for computing concept similarity when concepts map to multiple phrases.  

Questions for authors:
- Why does cross-validation frequently select β = 0; is this due to corpus properties or issues with β’s implementation?  
- How exactly is concept similarity computed in Table 4 given many-to-many concept–phrase mappings?  
- Given the strong APPPROXPHR baseline, what added value does the ontology provide beyond a seed phrase set?  
- Can you explain why hyperparameter settings vary substantially across different corpora?  

Additional comments:
- Introduction should more clearly state limitations of prior work and the paper’s specific contributions.  
- Add discussion of potential downstream applications of the learned embeddings beyond similarity/relatedness.  
- Provide deeper analysis of semantic properties captured by embeddings beyond clustering.
