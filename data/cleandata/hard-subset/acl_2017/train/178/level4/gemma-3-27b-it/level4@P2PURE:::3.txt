The authors present a method for jointly embedding words, phrases, and concepts, leveraging plain text corpora and a manually-constructed ontology where concepts are represented by one or more phrases. They apply this method to the medical domain using the UMLS ontology and to the general domain using the YAGO ontology. The approach is evaluated by comparison to baselines and prior work, primarily on intrinsic similarity and relatedness benchmarks. The authors utilize existing medical domain benchmarks and introduce a novel general-domain concept similarity and relatedness dataset, created using Mechanical Turk, which they plan to release. Reported results are comparable to those of previous studies.

Strengths:
- The proposed joint embedding model is straightforward and well-motivated. Its key strength lies in finding a configurable balance between treating phrases as atomic units and acknowledging their compositionality, a similar principle applied to concepts and their representative phrases.
- The paper details a substantial body of work, encompassing model development, the creation of a new evaluation dataset, and several evaluations and analyses.

Weaknesses:
- The evaluation is limited to intrinsic tasks, focusing largely on similarity/relatedness datasets. Given the known limitations of intrinsic evaluations in predicting performance on extrinsic tasks, it is now increasingly common practice to include at least one or two extrinsic tasks in embedding model evaluations.
- The similarity/relatedness datasets are presented as evaluations of concept similarity, however, the underlying judgements were based on phrases. Consequently, these datasets should be considered as phrase similarity datasets and analyzed accordingly.
- The medical concept evaluation dataset, ‘mini MayoSRS’, is very small (29 pairs), and its larger counterpart, ‘MayoSRS’ (101 pairs), suffers from reported low human annotator agreement. The ‘UMNSRS’ dataset, while larger, is restricted to concepts expressible as single words, presented as such to annotators. This limitation should be explicitly discussed in the paper and casts doubt on its relevance for evaluating representations of phrases and general concepts.
- The authors extensively fine-tune hyperparameters using the same datasets on which they report results and compare their approach to prior work. This practice undermines the validity of the reported results and analyses.
- The authors argue their method is superior to prior work because it achieves comparable results with less manual annotation. This argument is not compelling, as their approach relies on large, manually-constructed ontologies and prior work often utilized clinical records requiring no dedicated annotation effort.
- I found a lack of in-depth analysis to explain the reported results. The paper attempts to equate the relationship between a phrase and its component words with that between a concept and its representative phrases as compositional relationships. However, these relationships differ in nature and warrant separate, detailed analysis. For example, around line 588, an NLP-specific analysis of the relation between phrases and their component words would be valuable, potentially exploring the influence of dominant phrase headwords. Furthermore, an investigation into the effect of hyperparameters governing the trade-off between atomic and compositional representations of phrases and concepts is absent, but could strengthen the work. 

General Discussion:
Given the aforementioned weaknesses, I recommend rejection of this submission. I encourage the authors to address the evaluation datasets and methodology before resubmission.
