Summary: This paper presents a model for embedding words, phrases, and concepts into vector spaces. It leverages an ontology of concepts, mapping each concept to corresponding phrases, which are then identified in text corpora and treated as atomic symbols. The paper then employs a method essentially based on skip-gram to train embeddings for words, these atomic phrases, and the associated concepts. The proposed approach is evaluated on the tasks of concept similarity and relatedness, using UMLS and Yago as backing ontologies.

Strengths:
The paper addresses the important point that semantically close phrases need not be lexically similar, and that not all phrases are compositional. To address this, the paper proposes a reasonable model for training phrase embeddings. The resulting embeddings demonstrate competitive or superior performance in identifying similarity between concepts. 

The software released alongside the paper is a valuable resource for biomedical NLP researchers.

Weaknesses:
The main limitation of this paper is that the model is not particularly novel; it constitutes a modification of the skip-gram method. 

Furthermore, the results (Table 4) suggest that the full model presented does not consistently achieve the best performance. On the two Mayo datasets, the Choi baseline significantly outperforms the proposed model. A similar trend appears in Table 6. On the larger UMNSRS dataset, the proposed model is, at best, competitive with simpler, existing models (Chiu).

General Discussion:
The paper claims to use known phrases as a form of distant supervision for training embeddings. However, the nature of this “supervision” is unclear. As I understand it, each occurrence of a phrase associated with a concept provides contextual information for training word embeddings. This is not supervision in the traditional sense – for example, for identifying the concept within text or for other predictive tasks. Therefore, the terminology is somewhat confusing. 

The notation introduced in Section 3.2 (E_W, etc.) is not used elsewhere in the paper.

The use of β to globally control the compositionality of phrases based on their constituent words is surprising. This effectively posits a single, global constant determining the degree to which any phrase should be compositional. The values of β chosen through cross-validation (Table 3) are also unusual. For PM+CL and WikiNYT, β is zero, effectively arguing against compositionality altogether.

The experimental setup for Table 4 requires further explanation. The paper states that the data labels similarity/relatedness of concepts (or entities). However, given the many-to-many mapping between concepts and phrases, it is unclear how phrase/word vectors are used to compute these similarities. It appears that only concept vectors are utilized.

In Table 5, the approximate phrase method – which approximates concepts as the average of their constituent phrases – performs best. This raises the question of why the concept ontology is necessary. It appears that similar results could be achieved by starting directly with a seed set of phrases, bypassing the ontology altogether.
