This paper presents an extension to standard word embedding techniques to create representations not only for words, but also for phrases and concepts linked to those words via an ontology. The core idea is to consolidate references to the same underlying concept – whether expressed as a word, phrase, or formal concept identifier – into a single identifier within the training corpus. This is achieved by creating a “tagged” corpus where instances of synonymous terms are replaced by the common identifier, which is then appended to the original corpus for embedding training. The authors leverage biomedical corpora and ontologies for their experiments, and notably contribute a new test dataset for assessing word similarity and relatedness specifically tailored to real-world biomedical entities.

Overall, the paper is well-written and the proposed technique is intuitively sound, though the contribution feels incremental rather than transformative. The focus on the biomedical domain, while understandable given the authors’ expertise and available resources, limits the generalizability and perceived impact of the work. A more detailed discussion of the newly generated test resource would be valuable; indeed, this resource may represent the most significant contribution of the paper. 

My primary concern lies with the mathematical formulation presented in Equation 8, which attempts to define a Mean Average Precision (MAP) calculation. While the intent to capture a notion of ranking quality is clear, the equation does *not* define a probability, even abstracting away the practical challenges of an infinite vocabulary. The resulting value is a count or a ratio, and framing it as a probability is misleading. The authors should revise the explanation to accurately reflect the metric being calculated, perhaps focusing on a rank-based assessment or a thresholded precision. A more natural cut-off point for ranking, instead of considering the entire vocabulary, could also be considered.

Beyond this technical point, the paper is largely clear and logically structured. I identified one minor typographical error: on line 556, “most concepts has” should be corrected to “most concepts have.” 

Specifically, I would encourage the authors to:

*   **Expand the discussion of the new test dataset.** Describe the creation process in greater detail, including any inter-annotator agreement measures. Analyze the characteristics of the dataset and its potential use cases beyond the immediate experiments presented. Consider making the dataset publicly available to benefit the wider research community.
*   **Re-evaluate Equation 8.**  Remove the probabilistic interpretation and clarify the intended meaning of the MAP calculation. Consider alternative ranking metrics more appropriate for this context.
*   **Address limitations regarding domain specificity.** Discuss how the proposed method might be adapted to other domains and the potential challenges encountered when using different ontologies and corpora.
*   **Strengthen the motivation.** While the motivation is clear, a more compelling argument for why existing embedding methods are insufficient for handling concepts and phrases in the biomedical domain would strengthen the paper.



In summary, this paper presents a reasonable, though not groundbreaking, extension to word embedding techniques. The creation of a new biomedical similarity/relatedness dataset is a valuable contribution, and the authors should emphasize this aspect of their work. Addressing the technical concern regarding Equation 8 and providing a more thorough discussion of the dataset’s creation and potential applications would significantly improve the paper.
