- Paper proposes embedding words, phrases, and concepts by mapping ontology concepts to phrases, treating phrases as atomic tokens, and training skip-gram–style embeddings; evaluated on concept similarity/relatedness with UMLS and Yago.

- Addresses the important issue that semantically close phrases may not be lexically similar and that many phrases are non-compositional.

- Proposes a reasonable phrase-embedding training approach that yields competitive or superior concept-similarity performance in some settings.

- Released software is a valuable resource for biomedical NLP researchers.

- Methodological novelty is limited—essentially a modification of skip-gram.

- Full model does not consistently outperform baselines; Choi baseline substantially outperforms the proposed model on the Mayo datasets.

- On larger UMNSRS, the proposed model is at best competitive with simpler existing models (e.g., Chiu).

- The characterization of “distant supervision” is misleading—phrase occurrences provide context, not traditional supervision for concept identification or predictive tasks.

- Notation introduced in Section 3.2 (e.g., E_W) is unused elsewhere and unnecessary.

- Using a single global β to control phrase compositionality is surprising and implausible; cross-validated β values (including zeros) are counterintuitive.

- Experimental setup for Table 4 is unclear given many-to-many concept–phrase mappings; it’s not explained how phrase/word vectors are aggregated or whether only concept vectors are used.

- The approximate-phrase baseline (averaging constituent phrase vectors) performs best in Table 5, calling into question the necessity of the ontology versus starting from a seed phrase set.
