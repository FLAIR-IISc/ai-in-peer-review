Summary: This paper introduces a model for embedding words, phrases, and concepts into vector spaces. The approach leverages an ontology of concepts, each mapped to multiple phrases, which are then identified within text corpora and treated as atomic symbols. The model employs a skip-gram-based method to train embeddings for words, these atomic phrases, and their associated concepts. Evaluation is performed on concept similarity and relatedness tasks, utilizing UMLS and Yago as underlying ontologies.

Strengths:
The paper addresses the important observation that semantically similar phrases are not always lexically related, and that not all phrases exhibit compositional semantics. The proposed model offers a reasonable approach to learning phrase embeddings under these conditions. Results demonstrate that the trained embeddings achieve competitive or superior performance in identifying concept similarity. 
The accompanying software release is a valuable resource for biomedical NLP researchers.

Weaknesses:
A primary limitation of this work is its incremental novelty; the model represents a modification of the skip-gram architecture.
Furthermore, the complete model, as presented, does not consistently achieve the best results reported (Table 4). On the two Mayo datasets, the Choi baseline significantly outperforms the proposed model, and a similar trend is apparent in Table 6. On the larger UMNSRS dataset, the model performs at best comparably to simpler, existing models (Chiu).
- General Discussion:
The paper frames the use of known phrases as distant supervision for embedding training. However, the nature of this “supervision” is unclear. As I understand the methodology, each occurrence of a concept-associated phrase provides contextual information for training word embeddings, but this differs from traditional supervised learning paradigms (e.g., concept identification). Therefore, the terminology is somewhat misleading. 
The notation introduced in Section 3.2 (E_W, etc.) is not subsequently used within the paper.
The use of a single, global parameter β to control phrase compositionality is notable. This parameter effectively dictates the degree to which any phrase should be considered compositional. The cross-validation-derived β values (Table 3) are unexpectedly low; specifically, values of zero for PM+CL and WikiNYT suggest a rejection of compositionality for those datasets.
The experimental setup for Table 4 requires further clarification. While the data labels concept (or entity) similarity/relatedness, the many-to-many mapping between concepts and phrases raises questions about how phrase/word vectors are used in the similarity computation. It appears that only concept vectors are utilized.
In Table 5, the approach that approximates concepts with the average of their constituent phrases achieves the best performance. Consequently, the necessity of the concept ontology is unclear; the same results could potentially be obtained by starting directly with a seed set of phrases.
