The authors present a method for jointly embedding words, phrases, and concepts, leveraging plain text corpora and a manually constructed ontology where concepts are defined by one or more phrases. This method is applied to both the medical domain, utilizing the UMLS ontology, and the general domain with YAGO. The evaluation compares the approach to baselines and existing work, primarily through intrinsic similarity and relatedness benchmarks. New, mechanically-turk generated datasets for general-domain concept similarity and relatedness are introduced and slated for public release. Reported results achieve performance comparable to prior methods.

**Strengths:**

- The proposed joint embedding model is logically sound and offers a valuable middle ground between treating phrases as atomic units and accounting for their compositionality. This same principle is extended to concepts represented by multiple phrases.
- The work encompasses significant effort, including model development, the creation of a new evaluation dataset, and comprehensive evaluation and analysis.

**Weaknesses:**

- The evaluation is limited to intrinsic tasks, focusing on similarity/relatedness datasets. Given the acknowledged limitations of intrinsic evaluations in predicting performance on extrinsic tasks, inclusion of at least one or two extrinsic tasks would be beneficial.
- The similarity/relatedness datasets are presented as measures of concept similarity, but the underlying judgments were elicited based on phrases presented to annotators. Therefore, these should be interpreted as phrase similarity datasets and analyzed accordingly.
- The medical concept evaluation datasets, ‘mini MayoSRS’ (29 pairs) and ‘MayoSRS’ (101 pairs), are small and have reported low inter-annotator agreement. The ‘UMNSRS’ dataset focuses solely on single-word concepts, which limits its relevance to representations of phrases and general concepts, and this limitation should be explicitly acknowledged.
- Hyperparameter tuning was performed extensively on the same datasets used for evaluation and comparison, raising concerns about the validity of the reported results and analyses.
- The claim of superiority to prior work based on reduced manual annotation effort is not compelling, considering the reliance on large, manually constructed ontologies and the use of existing clinical records for annotation in prior studies. 
- I found a lack of in-depth analysis to explain the reported results. The paper draws parallels between the relationship of a phrase and its constituent words, and a concept and its alternative phrases, but these relationships differ fundamentally and warrant separate investigation. For instance, analysis of phrase-word relationships around line 588, specifically regarding the potential influence of headwords, would be valuable. Additionally, a study of the impact of hyperparameters controlling the trade-off between atomic and compositional representations of phrases and concepts is missing.

**General Discussion:**

Due to the identified weaknesses, I recommend rejection of this submission. I encourage the authors to refine their evaluation datasets and methodology before considering resubmission.
