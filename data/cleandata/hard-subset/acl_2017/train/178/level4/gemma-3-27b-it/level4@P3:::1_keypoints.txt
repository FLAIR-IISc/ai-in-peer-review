- Proposes a weakly-supervised method that replaces concept phrases with unique IDs in text and trains joint embeddings of concepts, phrases, and words via distant supervision.
- Main contribution: demonstrates practical learning of concept embeddings without costly human annotations, especially useful in biomedicine.
- Secondary contribution: introduces and releases a new dataset for evaluating real-world entity similarity and relatedness.
- Conducts thorough empirical evaluation across multiple corpora (PubMed, clinical notes, Wikipedia/NYT) and ontologies (UMLS, YAGO), including ablations and hyperparameter tuning.
- Strength: practical and scalable approach, reporting ~3x vocabulary coverage over existing methods.
- Strength: competitive performance on established concept similarity and relatedness tasks despite no human supervision.
- Strength: the new dataset fills a gap and will aid future research on entity representation.
- Strength: comprehensive experiments support the method’s generalizability.
- Weakness: the core technique (distant supervision + joint embedding) is not fundamentally novel—innovation is mainly in the adaptation.
- Weakness: method performance depends on ontology quality; errors/incompleteness may propagate to embeddings.
- Weakness: MAP calculation (Eq. 8) is incorrect as a probability and needs clarification or a more appropriate cutoff/metric.
- Weakness: lacks detailed error analysis and examples of failure cases to guide improvements.
- Q: How sensitive is performance to the choice of representative phrases and can these be selected automatically?
- Q: What is the impact of noisy or inaccurate ontology mappings on learned embedding quality?
- Q: How does this method compare to recent approaches that use knowledge graphs or external resources for concept embedding?
- Q: Will the MAP explanation be revised and will a better metric for neighborhood structure be used?
