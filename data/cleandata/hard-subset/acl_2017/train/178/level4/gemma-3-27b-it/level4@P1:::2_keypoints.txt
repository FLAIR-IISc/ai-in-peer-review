- Addresses embedding words, phrases, and concepts by mapping ontology concepts to phrases and treating phrases as atomic symbols in a modified skip-gram training.  
- Evaluated on concept similarity/relatedness using UMLS and YAGO ontologies.  
- Well-motivated problem: semantically close phrases may not be lexically similar and phrases may be non-compositional.  
- Proposed model is a plausible method for training phrase embeddings under non-compositional conditions.  
- Resulting embeddings show competitive or superior performance on some conceptual similarity tasks.  
- Software release is a valuable resource for biomedical NLP researchers.  
- Main weakness: limited novelty—an incremental modification of skip-gram.  
- Full model does not consistently outperform baselines; Choi baseline outperforms on Mayo datasets and trends in Table 6 are similar.  
- On larger UMNSRS dataset, the model is at best competitive with simpler models like Chiu.  
- The notion of “distant supervision” is unclear and differs from conventional supervised scenarios; terminology is confusing.  
- Notation introduced in Section 3.2 (E_W, etc.) is unused later in the paper.  
- Using a single global β to control phrase compositionality is unexpected and appears ineffective (β often near zero).  
- Experimental setup for Table 4 is unclear given many-to-many concept–phrase mappings and ambiguous use of phrase/word vectors vs. concept vectors.  
- Phrase approximation (averaging associated phrases) achieves best performance in Table 5, calling into question the necessity of the formal concept ontology.  
- Questions for authors: clarify the distant supervision, explain unused notation, discuss limited impact of β and alternatives, detail how similarity scores are computed with many-to-many mappings, and justify the benefit of including a formal ontology versus phrase seeds.
