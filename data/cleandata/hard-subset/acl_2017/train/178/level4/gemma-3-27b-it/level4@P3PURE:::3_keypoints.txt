- Paper proposes a joint embedding method for words, phrases, and concepts using text corpora plus a manually-constructed ontology (UMLS and YAGO).
- Concepts are represented by one or more representative phrases; model balances treating phrases as atomic vs. compositional units.
- Evaluations are primarily intrinsic similarity and relatedness benchmarks; authors also created a new general-domain concept similarity/relatedness dataset via Mechanical Turk.
- Reported results are comparable to prior work.
- Strength: Model is straightforward, reasonable, and configurable for atomic vs. compositional phrase treatment.
- Strength: Substantial amount of work including model development, a new evaluation dataset, and multiple analyses.
- Weakness: Evaluation is limited to intrinsic tasks, lacking extrinsic downstream task evaluation to demonstrate practical utility.
- Weakness: Human judgments were elicited on phrases but presented as concept similarity, so datasets should be treated/analyzed as phrase similarity.
- Weakness: Medical evaluation datasets are very small (mini MayoSRS: 29 pairs; MayoSRS: 101 pairs) and have low annotator agreement, limiting reliability.
- Weakness: UMNSRS dataset is limited to single-word concepts presented as such to annotators, reducing relevance to phrase/concept representations.
- Weakness: Hyperparameters are extensively fine-tuned on the same datasets used for evaluation, calling reported comparisons into question.
- Weakness: Claim of requiring less manual annotation is weak because the method depends on large, manually-constructed ontologies and prior work used existing clinical records.
- Weakness: Analysis lacks deeper insight into model behavior, especially differences between phrase–word and concept–phrase compositional relations.
- Weakness: No investigation of hyperparameter effects on the atomic vs. compositional tradeoff, which would strengthen the paper.
- Recommendation: Reject; authors should improve evaluation datasets and methodology before resubmission.
