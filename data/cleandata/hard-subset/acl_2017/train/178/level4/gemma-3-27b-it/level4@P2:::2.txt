This paper introduces a method for jointly embedding concepts, phrases, and words into vector spaces, leveraging an ontology to map concepts to representative phrases found within unlabeled text corpora. The approach adapts the skip-gram model to learn embeddings for words, phrases (treated as atomic symbols), and their associated concepts. Evaluation focuses on concept similarity and relatedness tasks using UMLS and YAGO ontologies.

**Contributions:**

1.  **Joint Embedding Framework:** The paper proposes a method for jointly learning embeddings of concepts, phrases, and words, addressing the limitations of solely relying on word-level representations for complex semantic units and the challenges of multi-word expressions with non-compositional semantics.
2.  **Distant Supervision via Phrase Mapping:** The use of ontology-derived phrase mappings as a form of distant supervision for training concept embeddings is a key aspect of this work. This allows for learning from unannotated corpora, overcoming the need for expensive manual annotation.
3.  **Empirical Validation & Datasets:** The paper provides empirical validation of the approach on concept similarity and relatedness tasks, demonstrating competitive performance with existing methods. Furthermore, the authors release a novel dataset for evaluating similarity and relatedness of real-world entities.

**Strengths:**

1.  **Addresses a Relevant Problem:** The paper tackles the important issue of representing concepts beyond their lexical forms and acknowledging the non-compositionality of certain phrases. This is a significant challenge in semantic modeling.
2.  **Competitive Performance:** The reported results demonstrate that the proposed method achieves performance comparable to or exceeding that of existing methods, particularly on larger datasets. This suggests the effectiveness of the joint embedding and distant supervision approach.
3.  **Resource Availability:** The release of both the software implementation and a new dataset for evaluating real-world entity similarity/relatedness contributes to the reproducibility and wider applicability of the research.

**Weaknesses:**

1.  **Limited Novelty of Core Method:** While the application to concept embeddings is valuable, the underlying technique is primarily an adaptation of the skip-gram model, lacking substantial algorithmic novelty. The core contribution resides more in the specific application and distant supervision strategy.
2.  **Suboptimal Performance of Full Model:** The full model presented (incorporating β and η hyperparameters) does not consistently outperform simpler baselines (e.g., Choi's method on Mayo datasets, or Chiu on UMNSRS). This raises questions about the effectiveness of these added parameters and the overall model complexity.
3.  **Unclear Definition of “Supervision”:** The description of distant supervision feels imprecise. Simply using phrase occurrences as context for training doesn’t align with typical definitions of supervision. Clarifying this terminology is crucial.
4.  **Unused Notation:** The introduced notation in Section 3.2 (EW, EP, EC) is never used in subsequent analysis, creating unnecessary complexity and potentially confusing readers.
5.  **Counterintuitive Hyperparameter Values:** The cross-validation results reveal surprisingly low or zero values for the compositionality parameter β in certain corpora. This suggests either an issue with the parameterization or that compositionality is not effectively modeled within the current framework.
6.  **Evaluation Methodology Ambiguity:** The process of computing similarity/relatedness when concepts have multiple associated phrases is unclear. It's unclear if the concept vectors alone are used, potentially obscuring the contributions of the phrase embeddings.
7.  **Questionable Utility of Ontology:** The finding that approximating concepts with average phrase embeddings yields the best results (Table 5) questions the necessity of the concept ontology itself. If phrases alone suffice, the added complexity of the ontology may be unnecessary.

**Questions to Authors:**

1.  Could you elaborate on the motivation behind using β and η as *global* hyperparameters controlling compositionality? Is there a justification for assuming a uniform compositionality factor across all phrases and concepts?
2.  Regarding the evaluation, how are similarity scores computed when a concept has multiple associated phrases? Are only concept vectors used, or are phrase embeddings incorporated into the calculation?
3.  Given the superior performance of the phrase-based concept approximation (APPROXPHRco), what is the primary justification for including explicit concept embeddings in the model? What specific advantages do concept embeddings offer that are not captured by phrase-based approximations?
4.  Could you clarify the precise nature of the "supervision" signal derived from the phrase mappings? Is it simply co-occurrence within a context window, or is there a more structured learning objective involved?
