- Paper proposes learning embeddings for words/phrases/concepts by mapping ontology concepts to phrases and training a skip-gram model over both phrases and concepts.
- Evaluated on concept similarity/relatedness using UMLS and Yago ontologies.
- Addresses an important problem: lexical similarity ≠ semantic similarity and non-compositional phrases.
- Proposed approach is reasonable and shows competitive or superior performance in some conceptual similarity tasks.
- Publicly released software is a useful resource for biomedical NLP researchers.
- Contributions are incremental, mainly modifying skip-gram rather than introducing a novel architecture.
- Empirical results raise concerns: Choi baseline outperforms the proposed model on Mayo datasets and similar trends appear elsewhere.
- On the larger UMNSRS dataset the model is at best competitive with simpler baselines like Chiu’s.
- The term “distant supervision” is poorly defined here; using known phrases in training may not constitute supervision in the conventional sense.
- Notation introduced in Section 3.2 (e.g., E_W) is unused elsewhere and should be removed.
- Using a single global parameter β for phrase compositionality is restrictive; cross-validation showing β=0 suggests compositionality is ignored by the model on some datasets.
- Experimental details for Table 4 are unclear—how phrase/word vectors map to concept-level similarity needs explicit explanation.
- The “approximate phr” baseline (averaging constituent phrase vectors) achieves the best results in Table 5, calling into question the added value of using an ontology.
