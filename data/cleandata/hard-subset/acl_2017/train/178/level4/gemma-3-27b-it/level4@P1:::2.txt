This paper presents a model for embedding words, phrases, and concepts into vector spaces. To do so, it leverages an ontology of concepts, each of which is mapped to phrases. These phrases are identified within text corpora and treated as atomic symbols. Utilizing this approach, the paper employs a modified skip-gram method to train embeddings for words, phrases, and associated concepts. The proposed work is evaluated on concept similarity and relatedness tasks, using UMLS and YAGO as the underlying ontologies.

**Strengths:**

The central problem addressed by the paper—that semantically close phrases may not be lexically similar, and not all phrases are compositional—is well-motivated. The proposed model offers a plausible approach to training phrase embeddings under these conditions. The resulting embeddings demonstrate competitive or superior performance in identifying conceptual similarity.

The accompanying software release could be a valuable resource for biomedical NLP researchers.

**Weaknesses:**

The primary weakness of the paper is the limited novelty of the model. It represents an incremental modification to the skip-gram algorithm. 

Furthermore, the reported results (Table 4) suggest that the full model does not consistently outperform existing baselines. The Choi baseline significantly outperforms the proposed model on the two Mayo datasets, and a similar trend is observed in Table 6. On the larger UMNSRS dataset, the proposed model is at best competitive with simpler, previously established models (Chiu).

**General Discussion:**

The paper frames its approach as utilizing phrases as a form of distant supervision for training embeddings. However, the nature of this “supervision” is unclear. As I understand it, each occurrence of a phrase associated with a concept provides contextual information for training word embeddings. This differs from traditional supervised learning scenarios (e.g., concept identification within text) and the terminology is somewhat confusing. 

The notation introduced in Section 3.2 (E<sub>W</sub>, etc.) is not subsequently used in the remainder of the paper.

The use of β to control phrase compositionality is unexpected. Effectively, this imposes a single, global constant governing “how compositional” any phrase should be. The cross-validation results (Table 3) reveal surprisingly low values for β (often zero for the PM+CL and WikiNYT corpora), which suggests limited benefit from enforcing compositionality.

The experimental setup for Table 4 requires further explanation. The paper states that the data labels similarity/relatedness of concepts (or entities). However, given the many-to-many mapping between concepts and phrases, it is unclear how phrase/word vectors are used in the similarity calculation. It appears the calculations primarily rely on concept vectors. 

In Table 5, the phrase approximation method (approximating concepts as the average of their associated phrases) achieves the best performance. This raises the question of whether the concept ontology is essential; similar results might be obtainable by simply starting with a seed set of phrases.

**Questions to Authors:**

1.  Could you clarify the nature of the “distant supervision” used in your method, and how it differs from more conventional supervised learning approaches?
2.  Why is the notation introduced in Section 3.2 not utilized elsewhere in the paper?
3.  The β parameter appears to have a limited impact on performance. Could you discuss the potential reasons for this, and whether alternative approaches to controlling compositionality might be more effective?
4.  Given the many-to-many mapping between concepts and phrases, how are similarity scores calculated in the experiments presented in Table 4? Specifically, how are phrase and word embeddings integrated into the calculation?
5.  If a phrase-based approximation (as seen in Table 5) achieves comparable or superior performance, what is the primary benefit of incorporating a formal concept ontology into the model?
