- Introduces a skip-gram-based model that embeds words, phrases (treated as atomic), and ontology-linked concepts using mappings from ontologies like UMLS and Yago.
- Evaluated on concept similarity and relatedness tasks using biomedical and general-domain ontologies.
- Strength: tackles non-lexical semantic similarity and non-compositional phrases with a reasonable phrase-embedding approach.
- Strength: Trained embeddings achieve competitive or superior results on some concept similarity benchmarks.
- Strength: Public software release is a useful resource for biomedical NLP researchers.
- Weakness: Novelty is incremental—model is a modification of the skip-gram architecture.
- Weakness: Full model does not consistently outperform baselines; Choi baseline beats it on Mayo datasets and it is only comparable to Chiu on UMNSRS.
- Critique: Calling the method “distant supervision” is misleading because occurrences of concept phrases provide contextual signals rather than true supervised labels.
- Critique: Notation introduced in Section 3.2 (e.g., E_W) is unused later in the paper.
- Concern: A single global β parameter governs phrase compositionality, and cross-validated β values are unexpectedly low (zeros for some datasets), implying a blanket rejection of compositionality.
- Concern: Experimental details for Table 4 are unclear given many-to-many concept↔phrase mappings—unclear how phrase/word vectors are combined for similarity scores.
- Observation: Simple averaging of constituent phrase vectors (Table 5) outperforms other variants, calling into question the added value of the concept ontology versus starting from a seed phrase set.
