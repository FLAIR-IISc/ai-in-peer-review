- Introduces an ontology-based method that identifies concept-linked phrases in corpora and trains word/phrase/concept embeddings using a skip-gram-like approach, evaluated on UMLS and Yago tasks.  
- Addresses the important problem that semantic similarity ≠ lexical similarity and that many phrases are non-compositional.  
- Method is reasonable and yields competitive or superior concept-similarity embeddings; released software is a useful biomedical NLP resource.  
- Novelty is limited—approach is an incremental modification of skip-gram rather than a fundamentally new model.  
- Full model does not consistently beat baselines: Choi outperforms on Mayo datasets and similar patterns appear elsewhere; UMNSRS results are only competitive with simpler models.  
- The paper’s framing of “distant supervision” is unclear and not well differentiated from standard unsupervised context training.  
- Introduced notation (e.g., E_W) is not used later in the paper, indicating presentation/consistency issues.  
- Using a single global parameter β to control phrase compositionality is unexpected and potentially inappropriate.  
- Cross-validation yields peculiar β values (zero for PM+CL and WikiNYT), which effectively removes compositionality and requires explanation.  
- Experimental setup for Table 4 lacks clarity—how many-to-many concept↔phrase mappings are handled and whether similarity uses phrase/word vectors or only concept vectors is unclear.  
- The approximate phrase method (averaging phrase embeddings) outperforms the ontology-based concept method (Table 5), calling into question the necessity of the concept ontology versus a seed-phrase approach.
