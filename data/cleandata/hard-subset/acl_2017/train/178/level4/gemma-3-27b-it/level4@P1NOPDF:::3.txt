The authors present a method to jointly embed words, phrases, and concepts, based on plain text corpora and a manually-constructed ontology, where concepts are represented by one or more phrases. They apply their method in the medical domain using the UMLS ontology, and in the general domain using the YAGO ontology. To evaluate their approach, the authors compare it to simpler baselines and prior work, primarily on intrinsic similarity and relatedness benchmarks. They utilize existing benchmarks in the medical domain and employ Mechanical Turk workers to generate a new general-domain concept similarity and relatedness dataset, which they also intend to release. They report results comparable to those of prior work.

**Strengths:**

- The proposed joint embedding model is straightforward and reasonable. Its main advantage, in my opinion, lies in finding a configurable balance between treating phrases as atomic units and considering their compositionality. The same approach is applied to concepts, which are represented as compositions of several representative phrases.
- The paper describes a substantial body of work, encompassing model development, the creation of a new evaluation dataset, and a series of evaluations and analyses.

**Weaknesses:**

- The evaluation focuses solely on intrinsic tasks, mainly similarity/relatedness datasets. As the authors acknowledge, such evaluations have limited predictive power regarding the utility of embeddings in extrinsic tasks. Consequently, including at least one or two extrinsic tasks has become increasingly common in the evaluation of embedding models.
- The similarity/relatedness evaluation datasets are presented as recordings of human judgments of concept similarity. However, if I understand correctly, the judgments were actually based on phrases presented to the annotators; therefore, these datasets should be considered phrase similarity datasets and analyzed accordingly.
- The medical concept evaluation dataset, ‘mini MayoSRS’, is extremely small (29 pairs), and its larger superset, ‘MayoSRS’, is only moderately larger (101 pairs) and reportedly has low human annotator agreement. The other medical concept dataset, ‘UMNSRS’, is more reasonable in size, but focuses exclusively on concepts representable as single words, which were also presented as such to annotators. This limitation should be discussed in the paper and raises questions about its relevance to representations of phrases and general concepts.
- The authors extensively fine-tune their hyperparameters on the very datasets used for reporting results and comparison with prior work. This practice casts doubt on the validity of the reported results and analyses.
- The authors argue that their method surpasses prior work because it achieves comparable results with less manual annotation. However, this argument is not particularly compelling, as they also leverage large, manually-constructed ontologies. Furthermore, the manually annotated datasets used in prior work often originate from existing clinical records that did not require dedicated annotation efforts.
- I found a lack of insightful analysis to explain the reported numerical results. The authors attempt to treat the relationship between a phrase and its constituent words similarly to the relationship between a concept and its representative phrases. However, these relationships differ in nature, and each, in my view, merits a dedicated analysis. For example, around line 588, I would expect NLP-specific analysis of the relation between phrases and their component words, perhaps exploring the influence of dominant phrase headwords. Additionally, an investigation into the effect of the hyperparameters controlling the tradeoff between the atomic and compositional views of phrases and concepts is missing, which could strengthen the work.

**General Discussion:**

Due to the aforementioned weaknesses, I recommend rejecting this submission. I encourage the authors to improve their evaluation datasets and methodology before resubmitting this paper.
