This paper introduces a model for generating vector space embeddings of words, phrases, and concepts, leveraging an ontology to map concepts to their associated phrases. These phrases are identified within text corpora and treated as fundamental units for training embeddings, employing a method largely based on skip-gram. The model’s performance is evaluated on concept similarity and relatedness tasks using the UMLS and Yago ontologies as reference standards.

The work addresses an important issue: that semantic similarity doesn't always align with lexical similarity, and that not all phrases exhibit compositional semantics. The proposed model provides a reasonable approach to learning phrase embeddings, and the resulting embeddings demonstrate competitive or superior performance in identifying conceptual similarity. The released software is a valuable resource for researchers in biomedical natural language processing.

However, the model’s novelty is limited, representing an incremental modification of the skip-gram method. Furthermore, the full model architecture doesn’t consistently achieve the best results; the Choi baseline outperforms it on the two Mayo datasets (Table 4), and a similar pattern is observed in Table 6. On the UMNSRS dataset, the proposed model is only competitive with simpler, existing models like Chiu’s.

The paper describes the use of known phrases as a form of distant supervision, but the nature of this “supervision” isn't clearly defined. While phrase occurrences linked to concepts provide context for training word embeddings, this differs from traditional supervised learning paradigms. The notation introduced in Section 3.2 (E_W, etc.) remains unused throughout the remainder of the paper. The approach of using a single global parameter, β, to govern phrase compositionality is unexpected. The cross-validation results (Table 3) yield peculiar values for β—specifically, zero for PM+CL and WikiNYT, effectively negating compositionality. The experimental setup for Table 4 requires further clarification. Given the many-to-many mapping between concepts and phrases, it’s unclear how phrase or word vectors contribute to calculating concept similarity; it appears reliance is solely on concept vectors. Finally, the superior performance of the approximate phrase method (averaging phrase embeddings to represent concepts) in Table 5 raises questions about the necessity of the concept ontology, suggesting that a direct approach using a seed set of phrases could yield comparable results.
