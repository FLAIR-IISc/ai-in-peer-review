The authors present a method for jointly embedding words, phrases, and concepts, leveraging plain text corpora and a manually-constructed ontology where concepts are defined by one or more phrases. This method is evaluated in the medical domain using the UMLS ontology and in the general domain using YAGO. The evaluation compares the proposed embedding approach against baselines and prior work, primarily using intrinsic similarity and relatedness benchmarks. A new general-domain concept similarity and relatedness dataset was created using Mechanical Turk and is intended for public release. Results demonstrate performance comparable to existing methods.

**Strengths:**

- The proposed joint embedding model is well-motivated and logically sound. Its strength lies in finding a configurable balance between treating phrases as atomic units and considering their compositional structure, extending this principle to concepts composed of multiple representative phrases.
- The work is substantial, encompassing model development, the creation of a new evaluation dataset, and thorough evaluations and analyses.

**Weaknesses:**

- The evaluation relies exclusively on intrinsic tasks—similarity and relatedness—which, as the authors acknowledge, offer limited insight into the practical utility of the embeddings in downstream applications. Including at least one or two extrinsic tasks would strengthen the evaluation.
- The presented datasets are described as reflecting human judgments of concept similarity; however, the actual annotations were based on phrases, suggesting these are more accurately characterized as phrase similarity datasets and should be analyzed accordingly.
- The medical concept evaluation datasets, ‘mini MayoSRS’ (29 pairs) and ‘MayoSRS’ (101 pairs), are small and, in the case of ‘MayoSRS’, suffer from low inter-annotator agreement. The ‘UMNSRS’ dataset, while larger, focuses solely on concepts representable as single words, potentially limiting its relevance to evaluating phrase and general concept representations. This limitation should be discussed.
- The extensive hyperparameter tuning performed on the evaluation datasets raises concerns about the validity of the reported results and comparisons.
- The argument that the method surpasses prior work due to reduced manual annotation effort is unconvincing, given the authors’ reliance on large, manually-constructed ontologies and the fact that prior work often used clinical records requiring less dedicated annotation.
- The paper lacks in-depth analysis of the underlying factors driving the observed results. The parallel treatment of the relationship between a phrase and its constituent words, and a concept and its alternative phrases, as equivalent compositional relationships may be a simplification. Each deserves dedicated investigation. For example, a specific NLP analysis of the phrase-word relationship (around line 588) could reveal insights into the influence of headwords. Furthermore, an exploration of the impact of hyperparameters controlling the atomic versus compositional tradeoff would be beneficial.

**General Discussion:**

Due to the aforementioned weaknesses, I recommend rejecting this submission. I encourage the authors to address the evaluation concerns and refine their methodology before resubmitting.
