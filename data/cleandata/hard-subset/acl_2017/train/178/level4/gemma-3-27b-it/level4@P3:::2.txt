This paper presents a model for embedding words, phrases, and concepts into vector spaces. It leverages an ontology of concepts, each mapped to phrases, treating these phrases as atomic symbols within a text corpus. The model adapts the skip-gram method to train embeddings for words, phrases, and associated concepts. Evaluation focuses on concept similarity and relatedness using UMLS and YAGO as ontologies.

**Contributions:**

1. **Joint Embedding Framework:** The paper proposes a method for jointly learning embeddings of concepts, phrases, and words, addressing the limitations of solely relying on word-level representations for complex semantic units and the lack of lexical overlap between synonymous phrases. This is the strongest contribution, offering a unified approach to representing semantic knowledge.
2. **Distant Supervision with Phrases:** The use of representative phrases as distant supervision for concept embedding is a practical approach, reducing the need for expensive manual annotation. This allows for scaling to larger vocabularies and corpora.
3. **Novel Evaluation Datasets:** The inclusion of new datasets for evaluating similarity and relatedness of real-world entities contributes to the field by providing resources for assessing the performance of embedding models in a less-explored domain.

**Strengths:**

1. **Addresses a Relevant Problem:** The paper tackles the important challenge of representing concepts with multiple textual forms and non-compositional semantics, a crucial limitation of traditional word embedding approaches.
2. **Competitive Performance:** The proposed model achieves competitive results on concept similarity and relatedness tasks, demonstrating its effectiveness in capturing semantic relationships. While not consistently superior, its performance is comparable to strong baselines.
3. **Practicality and Scalability:** The distant supervision approach avoids the cost of manual annotation, enabling the method to scale to larger datasets and vocabularies – a significant advantage for real-world applications. The release of code further enhances its usability.
4. **Thorough Experimental Evaluation:** The paper presents a comprehensive evaluation, including comparisons against multiple baselines and analyses of the learned embeddings, providing evidence of their quality and characteristics.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Novelty:** The core method relies heavily on adapting the skip-gram model, and the novelty lies primarily in the application of distant supervision within this framework. The model architecture itself is not groundbreaking.
2. **Inconsistent Results & Baseline Performance:** The presented results are not uniformly strong. In several cases (Mayo datasets, Table 4), the Choi baseline significantly outperforms the proposed model. This raises concerns about the practical advantage of the proposed approach in certain scenarios. The best-performing configurations are also not always intuitive (e.g., β=0).
3. **Ambiguous “Supervision” Definition:** The term “distant supervision” is used loosely. While the method leverages phrase occurrences, it lacks a clear supervisory signal in the traditional sense (e.g., predicting concept presence). This can be misleading.
4. **Unused Notation:** The notation introduced in Section 3.2 (E<sub>W</sub>, etc.) is not used throughout the remainder of the paper, creating unnecessary complexity and potentially confusing readers.
5. **Questionable Compositionality Control:** The use of a single global parameter (β) to control phrase compositionality seems overly simplistic. The cross-validation results suggest that compositionality may not always be beneficial, as β is often set to zero.
6. **Evaluation Clarification Needed:** The method for computing concept similarity when concepts have multiple associated phrases (Table 4) requires further clarification. It's unclear how the phrase/word vectors are utilized in this scenario.

**Questions to Authors:**

1.  Could you elaborate on the rationale behind the chosen value of β being frequently zero during cross-validation? Does this indicate that the chosen corpora are not well-suited for compositional modeling, or does the implementation of β need to be re-evaluated?
2.  How is concept similarity computed in Table 4, given the many-to-many mapping between concepts and phrases? Are only the concept embeddings used, or are the phrase embeddings also considered in the similarity calculation? If so, how?
3.  Given the strong performance of the "APPROXPHR" baseline in Table 5, what is the primary justification for incorporating the concept ontology into the model? Could comparable results be achieved with just a seed set of phrases?
4. The paper mentions corpus choice influences results. Could you provide more intuition on why the hyperparameter settings vary so much between corpora?

**Additional Comments:**

*   The introduction could benefit from a more focused discussion of the limitations of existing methods and a clearer articulation of the specific contributions of this work.
*   Consider adding a discussion of potential applications of the learned embeddings beyond similarity and relatedness tasks.
*   A more detailed analysis of the semantic properties captured by the embeddings (beyond the clustering analysis) would strengthen the paper.
