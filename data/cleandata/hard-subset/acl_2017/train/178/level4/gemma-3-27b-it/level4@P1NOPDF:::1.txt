The paper describes an extension of word embedding methods to provide representations for phrases and concepts alongside their corresponding words. The method works by assigning a unique identifier to groups of phrases, words, and concepts that all denote the same entity, then replacing occurrences of these phrases and words with this identifier in the training corpus, effectively creating a “tagged” corpus. This tagged corpus is then appended to the original corpus for training. The concept/phrase/word sets are sourced from an existing ontology, with the researchers leveraging biomedical corpora and ontologies given the domain of application. Notably, the researchers also report the creation of a novel test dataset for evaluating word similarity and relatedness specifically for real-world biomedical entities.

In general, the paper is well-written. The technique presented is intuitive, though perhaps not a particularly substantial contribution. The scope of the work is somewhat limited by its focused evaluation within the biomedical domain. More discussion regarding the generated test resource would be beneficial; in fact, this resource may represent the most interesting contribution of the paper. 

There is a minor technical issue regarding the MAP calculation. 

Specifically, in Eq. 8, the authors attempt to define the MAP calculation. While this is a reasonable goal, I believe a natural cut-off could be defined, rather than ranking the entire vocabulary. Furthermore, Equation 8 does not actually define a probability; it is straightforward to demonstrate this, even with an infinite vocabulary size. Therefore, the explanation requires revision to remove references to probability.

Finally, there are a few small corrections needed:

Line 556: “most concepts has” should be “most concepts have”.
