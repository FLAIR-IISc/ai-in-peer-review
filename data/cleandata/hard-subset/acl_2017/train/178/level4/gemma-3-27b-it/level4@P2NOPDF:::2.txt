This paper introduces a model for generating vector space embeddings of words, phrases, and concepts, leveraging an ontology to map concepts to their corresponding phrases. These phrases are treated as atomic symbols extracted from text corpora, and embeddings are trained using a skip-gram approach extended to include both words/phrases and the concepts they represent. The model is evaluated on concept similarity and relatedness tasks using the UMLS and Yago ontologies.

The paper addresses an important issue: that semantic similarity doesn't always correlate with lexical similarity, and that not all phrases exhibit compositional semantics. The proposed approach offers a reasonable strategy for learning phrase embeddings that can capture these nuances, and the results demonstrate competitive or superior performance in identifying conceptual similarity compared to existing methods. Furthermore, the publicly released software will likely be a valuable resource for researchers in the biomedical Natural Language Processing (NLP) community.

However, the paper’s contributions are somewhat incremental. The core of the model relies heavily on the skip-gram method, representing a modification rather than a fundamentally novel architecture. More significantly, the presented results raise concerns about the effectiveness of the full model. Specifically, Table 4 shows that the Choi baseline consistently outperforms the proposed model on the two Mayo datasets, and a similar trend is apparent in Table 6. On the larger UMNSRS dataset, the proposed model achieves, at best, competitive performance with simpler, existing models like Chiu’s. 

The framing of the model’s use of distant supervision is unclear. While the paper claims to use known phrases as distant supervision, the nature of this "supervision" isn't well-defined. If every instance of a concept-associated phrase contributes to the training of word embeddings, it's not clear this qualifies as supervision in the traditional sense of a predictive task (e.g., concept identification). This terminology should be clarified. Additionally, the notation introduced in Section 3.2 (E<sub>W</sub>, etc.) is not referenced elsewhere in the paper and can be removed.

The use of a single, global parameter β to control phrase compositionality is conceptually surprising.  This effectively assumes a uniform degree of compositionality across all phrases, which seems restrictive.  The cross-validation results in Table 3 further support this concern; the optimal β value of zero for both PM+CL and WikiNYT suggests that, according to this model, compositionality plays no role in these datasets.

The experimental setup for Table 4 requires further explanation. The paper states that the data provides similarity/relatedness labels for *concepts*. However, given the many-to-many mapping between concepts and phrases, it’s unclear how phrase or word vectors are used to compute these similarities. It appears that only concept vectors are utilized, which should be explicitly stated.

Finally, the best performance in Table 5 is achieved by the “approximate phr” method, which represents concepts as the average of their constituent phrases. This result weakens the justification for employing a dedicated concept ontology. The same results could potentially have been achieved by simply starting with a seed set of phrases, raising the question of whether the ontology adds significant value.
