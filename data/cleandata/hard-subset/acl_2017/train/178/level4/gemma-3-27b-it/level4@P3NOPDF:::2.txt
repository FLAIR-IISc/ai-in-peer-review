## Review for ACL 2017

This paper presents a model for learning vector representations of words, phrases, and concepts by leveraging an existing ontology. The approach maps concepts to phrases found in text corpora, treating these phrases as atomic symbols. The model then adapts the skip-gram method to jointly train embeddings for words, atomic phrases, and their associated concepts. Evaluation focuses on concept similarity and relatedness tasks, utilizing UMLS and Yago as backing ontologies.

**Strengths:**

The paper addresses a critical issue in semantic representation: the disconnect between lexical similarity and semantic relatedness, particularly for non-compositional phrases. The proposed model offers a plausible approach to learning phrase embeddings that aims to overcome this limitation. Experimental results demonstrate that the learned embeddings achieve competitive or superior performance in identifying concept similarity, suggesting the model effectively captures semantic relationships. Furthermore, the publicly released software represents a valuable resource for researchers in the biomedical NLP community.

**Weaknesses:**

The primary limitation of this work is its incremental novelty. The proposed model is essentially a modification of the established skip-gram framework. While effective, the core contribution lies in the application to concept embeddings rather than a fundamentally new architectural component. 

A more significant concern arises from the experimental results presented. Table 4 reveals that the proposed model is often outperformed by the Choi baseline on the Mayo datasets, and a similar trend is observable in Table 6. On the larger UMNSRS dataset, the model achieves, at best, competitive performance with simpler, previously published models such as Chiu’s. This raises questions about the substantial benefits gained from the added complexity of the proposed approach.

**General Discussion:**

The paper frames its approach as utilizing known phrases for “distant supervision.” However, the nature of this supervision remains unclear. While occurrences of phrases associated with a concept provide contextual information for training word embeddings, this differs from traditional supervised learning paradigms involving predictive tasks like concept identification. The term “supervision” in this context feels imprecise and could benefit from clarification.

The notation introduced in Section 3.2 (e.g., E<sub>W</sub>) is not subsequently utilized throughout the remainder of the paper, rendering its inclusion unnecessary and potentially confusing.

The use of a global constant β to regulate phrase compositionality is somewhat surprising. This parameter effectively dictates a uniform degree of compositionality for all phrases, which seems restrictive. The cross-validation results in Table 3 yield odd values for β (specifically, zero for PM+CL and WikiNYT), indicating a strong argument *against* compositionality for those datasets, which feels counterintuitive given the motivation of the work.

The experimental setup for Table 4 warrants further explanation. The paper states that the data provides similarity/relatedness judgements for concepts. However, given the many-to-many mapping between concepts and phrases, it's unclear how phrase/word vectors are leveraged to compute these similarities. It appears the similarities are ultimately derived from the concept vectors alone. 

Finally, the best performance in Table 5 is achieved by the “approximate phr” method, which represents concepts as the average of their associated phrases. This result raises the question of the necessity of the concept ontology itself. One could argue that comparable results could be obtained by simply starting with a seed set of phrases, bypassing the need for an explicit ontology.



**Overall Assessment:**

The paper presents a solid, well-executed application of established embedding techniques to the problem of concept representation. However, the lack of substantial novelty and the underwhelming performance relative to simpler baselines limit its overall impact. While the released software is a valuable contribution, the paper would benefit from a more critical discussion of its limitations and a clearer justification for the architectural choices made. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions addressing the points raised above, particularly clarification regarding the "distant supervision" terminology, the role of β, and the interpretation of the results in Table 4 and 5.
