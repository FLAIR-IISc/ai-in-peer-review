This paper presents a method for jointly embedding words, phrases, and concepts, leveraging both plain text corpora and a manually-constructed ontology where concepts are defined by one or more phrases. The approach is applied to both the medical (UMLS) and general (YAGO) domains. The authors evaluate their method against baselines and existing work using intrinsic similarity and relatedness benchmarks, including a novel dataset created via Mechanical Turk for the general domain, which they plan to release. The reported results are comparable to prior work.

**Strengths:**

*   The proposed joint embedding model is conceptually sound and offers a valuable approach by bridging the gap between treating phrases as atomic units and acknowledging their compositional nature. This extends to concepts represented by multiple phrases.
*   The paper demonstrates a significant amount of work, encompassing model development, the creation of a new evaluation dataset, and a range of evaluations and analyses.

**Weaknesses:**

*   The evaluation relies solely on intrinsic tasks (similarity/relatedness), which are known to be weak predictors of performance in real-world applications. Including at least one or two extrinsic tasks would significantly strengthen the evaluation.
*   The similarity/relatedness datasets are presented as concept similarity benchmarks, but the underlying human judgements were elicited based on *phrases*, not concepts. This fundamentally defines them as phrase similarity datasets and necessitates analysis accordingly.
*   The medical concept evaluation datasets, ‘mini MayoSRS’ (29 pairs) and ‘MayoSRS’ (101 pairs), are small and suffer from reported low inter-annotator agreement. ‘UMNSRS’, while larger, focuses exclusively on single-word concepts, limiting its relevance to a model designed for phrases and general concepts. This limitation should be explicitly discussed.
*   The extensive hyperparameter tuning performed on the same datasets used for evaluation raises serious concerns about the validity of the reported results and analyses. This introduces a risk of overfitting to the evaluation metrics.
*   The authors’ claim of superiority over prior work based on reduced manual annotation effort is unconvincing. Their method still relies on large, manually-constructed ontologies, and prior work often utilized existing clinical records that didn't require dedicated annotation efforts.
*   The paper lacks in-depth analysis of the underlying mechanisms driving the observed results. While the authors attempt to draw parallels between phrase-word and concept-phrase relationships, these are distinct and warrant individual investigation. For example, a linguistic analysis around line 588 exploring the influence of headwords within phrases could provide valuable insights. Similarly, a systematic investigation of the hyperparameters controlling the tradeoff between atomic and compositional representations is missing.

**General Discussion:**

Due to the significant weaknesses outlined above, I recommend rejecting this submission. The evaluation methodology and potential for overfitting are major concerns. I encourage the authors to address these issues – specifically, by including extrinsic evaluations, clarifying the nature of their datasets, justifying their hyperparameter tuning strategy, and providing more detailed analyses – before resubmitting.
