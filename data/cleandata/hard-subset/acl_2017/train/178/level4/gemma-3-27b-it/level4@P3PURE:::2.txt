Summary: This paper presents a model for embedding words, phrases, and concepts into vector spaces. It leverages an ontology of concepts, each mapped to multiple phrases, which are then identified within text corpora and treated as atomic symbols. The authors employ a modified skip-gram approach to train embeddings for words, these atomic phrases, and their associated concepts. The proposed model is evaluated on concept similarity and relatedness tasks using UMLS and Yago as underlying ontologies.

Strengths:
The paper addresses the important issue that semantically close phrases are not always lexically similar, and that not all phrases exhibit compositional semantics. To this end, the authors propose a plausible model for training phrase embeddings that accounts for these phenomena. The resulting embeddings demonstrate competitive or superior performance in identifying concept similarity. 
The software released alongside the paper is a valuable resource for biomedical NLP researchers.

Weaknesses:
The primary weakness of the paper is the limited novelty of the proposed model, which represents a refinement of the skip-gram method. 
Furthermore, the full model presented does not consistently achieve the best results reported (Table 4). On the two Mayo datasets, the Choi baseline significantly outperforms the proposed approach. A similar trend is evident in Table 6. On the larger UMNSRS dataset, the proposed model performs at best competitively with previously established, simpler models like Chiu's. 
- General Discussion:
The paper frames its use of known phrases as distant supervision for embedding training. However, the nature of this "supervision" remains unclear. As I understand the method, each occurrence of a phrase associated with a concept contributes to the context used for training word embeddings. This differs from traditional supervised learning paradigms (e.g., concept identification within text) and the terminology is therefore potentially misleading.
The notation introduced in Section 3.2 (E_W, etc.) is not utilized elsewhere in the paper, rendering it unnecessary.
The use of a single global parameter, β, to control the compositionality of phrases is surprising. This effectively imposes a uniform constraint on the compositional nature of all phrases. The values of β selected through cross-validation (Table 3) are also noteworthy; a value of zero for PM+CL and WikiNYT suggests a rejection of compositionality in those cases.
The experimental setup for Table 4 requires further clarification. The paper states that the data provides similarity/relatedness labels for concepts. However, given the many-to-many mapping between concepts and phrases, it is unclear how phrase/word vectors are used to compute these similarities – it appears only concept vectors are employed.
In Table 5, the approximate phrase method, which represents concepts as the average of their constituent phrases, yields the best performance. This raises the question of why the concept ontology is necessary; the same results could potentially be achieved by directly utilizing a seed set of phrases.
