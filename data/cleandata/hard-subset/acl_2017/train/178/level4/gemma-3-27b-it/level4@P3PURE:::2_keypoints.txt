- Presents a model that embeds words, phrases, and concepts by mapping ontology concepts to multiple phrases, treating phrases as atomic tokens, and training with a modified skip-gram on UMLS and Yago.  
- Addresses the problem that semantically similar phrases may not be lexically similar and that many phrases are non-compositional.  
- Proposes a plausible phrase-embedding method that shows competitive or superior concept-similarity performance.  
- Releases useful software that benefits biomedical NLP researchers.  
- Limited novelty: the model is mainly a refinement of the skip-gram approach.  
- The full model does not consistently outperform baselines—Choi beats it on Mayo datasets, and on UMNSRS it is only competitive with simpler methods.  
- Calling the approach “distant supervision” is misleading because the method simply uses phrase occurrences as context rather than true supervised labels.  
- Introduces notation (e.g., E_W) that is not used elsewhere and is therefore unnecessary.  
- Using a single global β to control phrase compositionality is surprising and enforces a uniform constraint; cross-validated β values (including zeros) raise questions.  
- Experimental details for Table 4 are unclear—how similarities are computed given many-to-many concept↔phrase mappings is not explained.  
- Table 5 shows the simple average-of-phrases representation outperforms others, calling into question the necessity of the concept ontology versus using a seed phrase set.
