This paper proposes a method to jointly embed words, phrases, and concepts, leveraging text corpora and a manually-constructed ontology where concepts are represented by one or more phrases. The method is applied to the medical domain (UMLS ontology) and the general domain (YAGO ontology). Evaluation involves comparisons to baselines and prior work, primarily on intrinsic similarity and relatedness benchmarks, including a newly created general-domain concept similarity/relatedness dataset collected via Mechanical Turk. Results are reported as comparable to existing approaches.

**Contributions (as I see them):**

1. **A practical approach to jointly embedding concepts, phrases, and words using distant supervision.** The method offers a potentially scalable way to learn concept embeddings without extensive manual annotation, bridging the gap between word-level and concept-level semantic representations. The idea of using representative phrases as a form of distant supervision is a reasonable and relatively straightforward technique.
2. **A novel evaluation dataset for general-domain concept similarity and relatedness.** The creation of a new dataset, despite limitations discussed below, is a valuable contribution, providing a resource for future research in this area.
3. **An exploration of the trade-off between atomic and compositional representations of phrases and concepts.** The introduction of β and η hyperparameters to control the influence of compositionality is a thoughtful addition, allowing for flexibility in modeling different types of phrases and concepts.

**Strengths:**

*   **Intuitive and well-motivated approach:** The core idea of leveraging existing ontological knowledge through distant supervision is conceptually sound and addresses a clear need in semantic representation learning.
*   **Comprehensive experimentation:** The authors conduct a substantial set of experiments, evaluating the model across different corpora, ontologies, and baselines. The inclusion of a newly created dataset demonstrates commitment to thorough evaluation.
*   **Clear presentation of the method:** The model is described clearly and logically, with a helpful diagram illustrating the relationships between the different embedding matrices.
*   **Consideration of compositionality:** The hyperparameters β and η offer a mechanism to balance atomic and compositional representations, adding flexibility to the model.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Limited evaluation scope:** The reliance on intrinsic evaluation tasks (similarity/relatedness) is a significant limitation. The lack of extrinsic evaluations hinders the assessment of the practical utility of the learned embeddings in downstream NLP tasks.
*   **Questionable relevance of phrase-based similarity as concept similarity:** The evaluation relies on human judgments of *phrase* similarity, rather than *concept* similarity, potentially biasing the results. The evaluation datasets should be clearly framed as phrase similarity benchmarks.
*   **Small and potentially unreliable medical datasets:** The 'mini MayoSRS' dataset is exceptionally small and the larger 'MayoSRS' dataset suffers from questionable inter-annotator agreement. The UMNSRS dataset, focused on word-representable concepts, is of limited relevance to evaluating phrase and concept embeddings.
*   **Hyperparameter tuning on the test set:** The extensive hyperparameter tuning performed on the evaluation datasets introduces a significant risk of overfitting and inflates the reported performance gains, making comparisons to prior work unreliable.
*   **Weak claim of superiority based on annotation effort:** The argument that the method outperforms prior work due to reduced annotation effort is unconvincing. Prior work often leverages readily available, pre-existing data (e.g., clinical records), rather than requiring dedicated annotation campaigns.
*   **Insufficient analysis of compositionality effects:** While the hyperparameters for compositionality are introduced, the analysis of their impact is superficial. A deeper investigation into how these parameters affect the learned embeddings and their performance on different tasks is needed. The relationship between phrase structure and embedding space could also be explored further.

**Questions to Authors:**

1.  **Extrinsic Evaluation:** Could you provide results on at least one extrinsic downstream task (e.g., entity linking, relation extraction) to demonstrate the practical utility of your embeddings?
2.  **Phrase vs. Concept Similarity:** Do you agree that the evaluation primarily assesses phrase similarity, rather than concept similarity? If so, how does this impact the interpretation of your results?
3.  **Hyperparameter Tuning:** How would the results change if a separate development set were used for hyperparameter tuning?
4.  **Compositionality Analysis:** Can you provide a more detailed analysis of the impact of β and η on the learned embeddings? For instance, how do these parameters affect the proximity of phrases and their component words in the embedding space?
5.  **Error Analysis:** What are some examples of cases where your method performs poorly, and what might be the underlying reasons?

While the paper presents a reasonable approach and a valuable new dataset, the limitations in evaluation and analysis, coupled with the questionable hyperparameter tuning strategy, undermine the confidence in the reported results. I encourage the authors to address these weaknesses before resubmitting.
