The paper describes an extension of word embedding methods to also provide representations for phrases and concepts that correspond to words. The method works by assigning an identifier for groups of phrases, words, and the concept they all denote, replacing occurrences of these phrases and words with this identifier in the training corpus to create a “tagged” corpus, and then training on a combination of the original and tagged corpora. The concept/phrase/word sets are derived from existing ontologies. Focused on the biomedical domain, the researchers utilize related corpora and ontologies for their experiments and report on the creation of a novel test dataset for assessing word similarity and relatedness for real-world entities.

The paper is well-written and the technique presented is intuitive, though its contribution is not substantial. The scope is somewhat limited by the focus on biomedical applications. More detailed discussion of the newly generated test resource would be valuable, as it may represent the most significant contribution of the work. 

There is a minor technical issue in the mathematical formulation. Specifically, Equation 8 does not define a probability, even if the vocabulary size is infinite. The explanation should be revised to reflect this. Additionally, a minor grammatical correction is needed: line 556 should read “most concepts have” instead of “most concepts has”.

The primary contribution of this work is the creation of a new dataset for evaluating similarity and relatedness of real-world entities. While the method for jointly embedding concepts, phrases, and words is reasonable, it builds on existing word embedding techniques and does not present a significant algorithmic advancement. The empirical results, while competitive, do not definitively demonstrate a substantial improvement over existing methods. 

Weaknesses:
1. **Limited Novelty of Method:** The core idea of leveraging phrase occurrences as distant supervision for concept embeddings is relatively straightforward and builds directly upon established techniques like word2vec and distant supervision for relation extraction. The primary innovation lies in the application to the joint embedding of concepts, phrases, and words, but the fundamental approach lacks significant novelty.
2. **Domain Specificity:** The evaluation is heavily focused on the biomedical domain. While this is a valid application area, it limits the generalizability and impact of the work. It is unclear whether the observed performance improvements would translate to other domains.
3. **Technical Imprecision in Equation 8:**  As noted, Equation 8 does not represent a probability distribution. This is a minor point, but it indicates a lack of carefulness in the mathematical formulation.

Questions to Authors:
1. Could you elaborate on the potential applications of this method beyond the biomedical domain?
2. What steps were taken to ensure the quality and representativeness of the newly generated dataset? Please provide more details on the Mechanical Turk setup and filtering criteria.
3. Could you discuss the computational cost of this method compared to existing approaches, particularly in terms of training time and memory requirements?
