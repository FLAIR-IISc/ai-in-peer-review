The authors present a method to jointly embed words, phrases, and concepts, based on plain text corpora and a manually-constructed ontology, where concepts are represented by one or more phrases. They apply their method in the medical domain using the UMLS ontology, and in the general domain using the YAGO ontology. To evaluate their approach, the authors compare it to simpler baselines and prior work, primarily on intrinsic similarity and relatedness benchmarks. They utilize existing benchmarks in the medical domain and generate a new general-domain concept similarity and relatedness dataset using mechanical turkers, which they intend to release. They report results comparable to prior work.

**Strengths:**

- The proposed joint embedding model is straightforward and reasonable. Its main value, in my opinion, lies in achieving a (configurable) balance between treating phrases as atomic units and considering their compositionality. This same approach is applied to concepts being ‘composed’ of several representative phrases.
- The paper describes a substantial amount of work, including model development, the creation of a new evaluation dataset, and several evaluations and analyses.

**Weaknesses:**

- The evaluation reported focuses solely on intrinsic tasks—primarily similarity/relatedness datasets. As the authors acknowledge, such evaluations have limited predictive power regarding the utility of embeddings in extrinsic tasks. Consequently, including at least one or two extrinsic tasks has become increasingly common in evaluating embedding models.
- The similarity/relatedness evaluation datasets are presented as recordings of human judgments of concept similarity. However, if I understand correctly, the judgments were based on presenting *phrases* to human annotators. Therefore, these should be considered phrase similarity datasets and analyzed accordingly.
- The medical concept evaluation dataset, ‘mini MayoSRS,’ is extremely small (29 pairs), and its larger superset, ‘MayoSRS,’ is only slightly larger (101 pairs) and was reported to have relatively low human annotator agreement. The other medical concept evaluation dataset, ‘UMNSRS,’ is more reasonable in size but is based only on concepts representable as single words, presented as such to the human annotators. This limitation should be discussed in the paper and casts doubt on its relevance to representations of phrases and general concepts.
- The authors extensively fine-tune their hyperparameters on the very datasets used for reporting results and comparison with prior work. This practice renders the reported results and analyses questionable.
- The authors argue their method is superior to prior work due to achieving comparable results with less manual annotation. This argument is weak, as they also rely on large, manually-constructed ontologies, and the manually annotated datasets used in prior work often come from existing clinical records that did not require dedicated annotations.
- I found the analysis lacking in useful insights into the underlying behavior of the model. The authors attempt to equate the relationship between a phrase and its component words with that between a concept and its representative phrases as similar compositional relations. However, these relationships differ in nature and, in my view, each warrants dedicated analysis. For example, around line 588, I would expect NLP-specific analysis concerning the relation between phrases and their component words, perhaps investigating the role of dominant phrase headwords. Furthermore, an investigation of the effect of the hyperparameters controlling the tradeoff between atomic and compositional views of phrases and concepts is absent but would strengthen the work.

**General Discussion:**

Due to the aforementioned weaknesses, I recommend rejecting this submission. I encourage the authors to improve their evaluation datasets and methodology before resubmitting this paper.
