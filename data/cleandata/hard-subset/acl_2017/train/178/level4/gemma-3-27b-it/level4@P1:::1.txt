The paper describes an extension of word embedding methods to also provide representations for phrases and concepts. The method works by assigning a unique identifier to sets of phrases, words, and concepts that all denote the same entity, replacing occurrences of these phrases and words with the identifier in the training corpus to create a “tagged” corpus, and then training on a combination of the original and tagged corpora. The concept/phrase/word sets are derived from an ontology. Focusing on the biomedical domain, the researchers utilize relevant corpora and ontologies. Notably, they also report the creation of a new test dataset for evaluating similarity and relatedness between real-world entities, which is a novel contribution.

In general, the paper is well-written and the technique is intuitive, though its contribution is not substantial. The scope is somewhat limited by the focus on the biomedical domain. More detailed discussion of the generated test resource would be beneficial, as it represents the potentially most interesting contribution of the paper.

There is a minor technical point regarding the mathematical formulation. In Equation 8, the authors aim to define the Mean Average Precision (MAP) calculation, which is a good idea. However, a natural cutoff point could be defined rather than ranking the entire vocabulary. As written, Equation 8 does not define a probability; even with an infinite vocabulary size, it is easy to demonstrate this. Therefore, the explanation should be revised to remove references to probability.

**Contributions:**

I see the primary contribution of this work as the creation of a new dataset for evaluating the similarity and relatedness of real-world entities. While the embedding method itself is a reasonable extension of existing techniques, it does not represent a significant methodological advance. The empirical evaluation demonstrates that the embeddings achieve competitive performance, but this largely relies on established datasets and techniques.

**Strengths:**

1. **Novel Dataset:** The newly generated dataset for evaluating real-world entity similarity and relatedness fills a gap in the existing resources and provides a valuable asset to the research community. The authors detail the data collection process, including quality control measures, which strengthens the reliability of the resource.
2. **Practical Approach:** The weakly-supervised method offers a practical way to generate concept embeddings without relying on costly human annotation. This makes it scalable to larger ontologies and corpora.
3. **Competitive Performance:** The embeddings achieve competitive results on existing biomedical concept similarity and relatedness tasks, demonstrating the effectiveness of the proposed approach.
4. **Clear Presentation:** The paper is generally well-written and clearly explains the methodology and experimental setup.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Novelty:** The core embedding technique is a relatively straightforward extension of existing methods (e.g., word2vec) and does not introduce significant algorithmic innovation.
2. **Domain Specificity:** The evaluation is heavily focused on the biomedical domain, which limits the generalizability of the findings. While the authors create a new dataset for general entities, the primary validation remains within a specific domain.
3. **Mathematical Imprecision:** The description of Equation 8 is mathematically inaccurate, claiming to define a probability when it does not. While this is a minor issue, it impacts the clarity of the technical explanation.
4. **Limited Analysis of Dataset:** While the authors present the new dataset, the analysis of its characteristics and potential biases is somewhat limited. A more in-depth discussion of the dataset’s properties and limitations would be valuable.

**Questions to Authors:**

1. Could you provide more details about the characteristics of the new dataset, such as the distribution of entity types and the potential sources of bias in the annotations?
2. How sensitive are the results to the choice of hyperparameters, particularly β and η? Did you explore a wider range of values and perform more extensive ablation studies?
3. How does the performance of your method compare to recent state-of-the-art embedding techniques that are *not* specifically designed for concept embedding, such as those based on transformers (e.g., BERT)?
