The paper describes an extension of word embedding methods to provide representations for phrases and concepts in addition to words. The method functions by assigning a unique identifier to groups of phrases, words, and concepts that all denote the same entity, replacing occurrences of these phrases and words with the identifier in the training corpus – creating a “tagged” corpus – and then training embeddings on a combined corpus of the original and tagged data. The concept/phrase/word sets are sourced from an existing ontology; in this work, biomedical corpora and ontologies are utilized. The researchers also present a novel test dataset for evaluating word similarity and relatedness specifically for real-world biomedical entities. 

Overall, the paper is well-written and the technique is intuitive, though the contribution feels incremental. The scope of the evaluation is limited by its focus on the biomedical domain. Further discussion of the newly generated test resource would be beneficial; indeed, this resource may represent the most significant contribution of the paper. 

There is one minor technical concern regarding the mathematical formulation. Specifically, Eq. 8 aims to define a Mean Average Precision (MAP) calculation. While the intent is sound, defining a natural cut-off point would be more appropriate than ranking the entire vocabulary. Furthermore, Eq. 8 does not represent a probability distribution, even with an infinite vocabulary size. Therefore, the explanation should be revised to remove any probabilistic interpretation.
