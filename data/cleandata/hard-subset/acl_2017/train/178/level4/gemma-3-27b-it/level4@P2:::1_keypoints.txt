- Introduces a method to jointly learn embeddings for concepts, phrases, and words using representative ontology phrases as distant supervision via phrase replacement and training on tagged + original corpora.  
- Demonstrates competitive performance on biomedical and general-domain similarity/relatedness tasks and achieves broader vocabulary coverage than prior methods.  
- Presents a new dataset for evaluating similarity and relatedness of real-world entities.  

Strengths:
- Novel use of representative phrases as distant supervision to generate concept embeddings without human annotation.  
- Achieves performance comparable to methods that rely on human-labeled data despite noisy distant supervision.  
- New dataset fills a gap for evaluating real-world entity similarity/relatedness.  
- Scalable approach enabling larger vocabulary coverage without annotation costs.  

Weaknesses:
- Evaluation is primarily biomedical, leaving general-domain generalizability unclear.  
- Equation 8 is presented with a probabilistic interpretation despite not being a true probability; language should be clarified.  
- Methodological novelty is modest, largely adapting existing embedding techniques rather than introducing new algorithms.  
- Dataset creation and annotation details (entity selection rationale, deeper inter-annotator analysis) are insufficiently discussed.  

Questions for authors:
- How sensitive are results to the choice of representative phrases, and would using multiple/more sophisticated phrase selection improve performance?  
- What is the computational cost and scalability of the phrase-replacement preprocessing on very large corpora?  
- How do hyperparameters (β, η, etc.) affect performance across tasks and datasets, and what are recommended settings?  
- Would incorporating additional knowledge (hierarchies, concept relations) into training provide measurable benefits?  

Additional comments:

- Provide analysis of computational resources required for training.
