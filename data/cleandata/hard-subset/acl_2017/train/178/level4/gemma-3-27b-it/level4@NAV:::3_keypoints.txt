- Paper proposes a joint embedding of words, phrases, and concepts using raw text plus a phrase-defined ontology (evaluated on UMLS and YAGO) and introduces new Mechanical-Turk datasets; results are comparable to prior work.  
- Model is logically sound, providing a middle ground between atomic-phrase and compositional representations, and extends to concepts with multiple phrase aliases.  
- Significant effort: model development, new evaluation dataset creation, and comprehensive experiments and analyses.  
- Evaluation is limited to intrinsic similarity/relatedness tasks; no extrinsic downstream tasks to validate practical utility.  
- The similarity/relatedness annotations were elicited on phrases, so datasets should be treated and analyzed as phrase-similarity rather than true concept-similarity benchmarks.  
- Medical evaluation sets (mini MayoSRS: 29 pairs; MayoSRS: 101 pairs) are small with low inter-annotator agreement; UMNSRS uses only single-word concepts, limiting relevance to phrase/concept representations.  
- Extensive hyperparameter tuning was performed on the same datasets used for evaluation, risking overfitting and undermining validity of reported comparisons.  
- The claim of reduced manual annotation effort is weak given reliance on large manually curated ontologies and that prior work used clinical records for annotations.  
- Analysis is insufficient: phrase-word vs concept-phrase relationships are treated as analogous but differ fundamentally and need separate investigation (e.g., headword effects, hyperparameter trade-offs between atomic and compositional representations).  
- Recommendation: reject in current form; authors should improve evaluation methodology and dataset analyses before resubmission.  
- Multiple presentation and clarity issues: typos (e.g., “contexts”→“concepts”), confusing terminology (introducing “strings” vs “phrases”), and unclear explanations of training details (word training, phrase overlaps).  
- Notational and referencing problems: confusing notation in Eq.1 (c vs o), likely incorrect matrix dimensions, missing Pedersen et al. 2007 in refs, and missing reference to Table 5.  
- Experimental reporting gaps: which task was used for hyperparameter tuning is not specified, and Table 3 trends are hard to interpret without development-set analyses.  
- Unusual annotation choices: the fine-grained (1–100) similarity scale for human annotations is atypical and should be justified.
