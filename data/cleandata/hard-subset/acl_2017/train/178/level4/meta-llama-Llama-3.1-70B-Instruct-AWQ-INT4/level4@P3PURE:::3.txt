This paper proposes a method for jointly embedding words, phrases, and concepts based on plain text corpora and a manually-constructed ontology, where concepts are represented by one or more phrases. The authors apply their method in the medical domain using the UMLS ontology and in the general domain using the YAGO ontology. To evaluate their approach, they compare it to simpler baselines and prior work, primarily on intrinsic similarity and relatedness benchmarks. They utilize existing benchmarks in the medical domain and create a new general-domain concept similarity and relatedness dataset through Mechanical Turk, which they intend to release. The reported results are comparable to those of prior work.

Strengths:
- The proposed joint embedding model is straightforward and reasonable, offering a configurable middle ground between treating phrases as atomic units and considering their compositionality. The same approach is applied to concepts composed of several representative phrases.
- The paper presents a substantial volume of work, including model development, an additional contribution in the form of a new evaluation dataset, and several evaluations and analyses.

Weaknesses:
- The evaluation reported in this paper is limited to intrinsic tasks, mainly on similarity/relatedness datasets. As the authors acknowledge, such evaluations have limited power in predicting the utility of embeddings in extrinsic tasks. It has become increasingly common to include at least one or two extrinsic tasks in the evaluation of embedding models.
- The similarity/relatedness evaluation datasets used in the paper are presented as recording human judgments of similarity between concepts. However, it appears that the actual judgments were made based on presenting phrases to human annotators, making them phrase similarity datasets that should be analyzed as such.
- The medical concept evaluation dataset, 'mini MayoSRS,' is extremely small (29 pairs), and its larger superset 'MayoSRS' is only slightly larger (101 pairs) with relatively low human annotator agreement. The other medical concept evaluation dataset, 'UMNSRS,' is more reasonable in size but is limited to concepts that can be represented as single words and were presented as such to human annotators. This limitation should be noted in the paper, as it makes the relevance of this dataset questionable with respect to representations of phrases and general concepts.
- As the authors themselves note, they extensively fine-tune their hyperparameters on the same datasets for which they report results and compare them to prior work. This makes all reported results and analyses questionable.
- The authors argue that their method is superior to prior work because it achieves comparable results while requiring less manual annotation. However, this argument is not strong, as the authors also use large manually-constructed ontologies, and the manually annotated dataset used in prior work comes from existing clinical records that did not require dedicated annotations.
- In general, more insightful analysis into the reported numbers is lacking. The authors treat the relation between a phrase and its component words and a concept and its alternative phrases as similar types of compositional relations. However, these relations differ in nature and deserve dedicated analyses. For example, an NLP analysis specific to the relation between phrases and their component words, such as the impact of dominant phrase headwords, would be beneficial. Additionally, investigating the effect of hyperparameters controlling the tradeoff between atomic and compositional views of phrases and concepts could strengthen the work.

General Discussion:
Due to the above-mentioned weaknesses, I recommend rejecting this submission. I encourage the authors to consider improving their evaluation datasets and methodology before re-submitting this paper.
