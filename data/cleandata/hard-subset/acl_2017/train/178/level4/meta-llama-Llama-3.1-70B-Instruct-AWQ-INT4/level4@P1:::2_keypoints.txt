Main contributions:
- Joint method to learn concept, phrase, and word embeddings using ontology representative phrases as distant supervision.
- Adaptation of skip-gram with negative sampling to jointly embed concepts, phrases, and words.
- Evaluation on concept similarity/relatedness (UMLS, YAGO) showing competitive results.

Strengths:
- Tackles semantic closeness of lexically dissimilar and non-compositional phrases.
- Shows competitive or superior performance on concept similarity.
- Released software useful for biomedical NLP researchers.

Weaknesses:
- Limited novelty — essentially a tweak to skip-gram.
- Full model often underperforms simpler baselines (Choi) on Mayo datasets and is only competitive on larger UMNSRS.
- “Distant supervision” terminology is misleading relative to traditional supervised concept identification.
- Introduces notation (e.g., E_W) that is never used elsewhere in the paper.
- Single global β for phrase compositionality is questionable; cross-validated β values (zeros) contradict compositionality claims.
- Experimental details for Table 4 are unclear on handling many-to-many concept–phrase mappings when computing similarities.
- Approximate phr (averaging phrase vectors) outperforms the model in Table 5, raising doubt about the necessity of the concept ontology.

Questions to authors:
- Please clarify the use of the term “distant supervision.”
- How do you respond to the critique that the method lacks novelty?
- Explain the experimental setup for Table 4, specifically how phrase/word vectors are used for similarity with many-to-many mappings.
- Justify using a single global β for compositionality and interpret the cross-validated β results.
- Discuss implications of approximate phr’s strong performance and whether the concept ontology is necessary.
