This paper proposes a model for embedding words, phrases, and concepts into vector spaces by leveraging an ontology of concepts, each mapped to phrases. These phrases are extracted from text corpora and treated as atomic symbols. The model utilizes an approach akin to the skip-gram method to train embeddings for words, phrases, and their associated concepts. The proposed model is evaluated on the task of concept similarity and relatedness, with UMLS and Yago serving as the backing ontologies.

The paper addresses the key question of how to capture semantic similarities between phrases that are not lexically similar and may not be compositional in nature. To this end, the proposed model provides a plausible approach to training phrase embeddings, which are shown to be competitive or superior in identifying concept similarities. Additionally, the software released with the paper has the potential to be useful for biomedical NLP researchers.

However, the primary weakness of the paper lies in the model's lack of novelty, as it is essentially a tweak to the skip-gram method. Furthermore, the full model presented in the paper does not consistently yield the best results. For instance, in Table 4, the Choi baseline outperforms the proposed model on the two Mayo datasets, and a similar trend is observed in Table 6. On the larger UMNSRS data, the proposed model is at best competitive with previous, simpler models (Chiu).

The paper's claim of using known phrases as distant supervision to train embeddings is somewhat misleading, as it does not constitute traditional supervision (e.g., identifying concepts in text or other predictive tasks). Instead, every occurrence of a phrase associated with a concept provides context for training word embeddings. The notation introduced in Section 3.2 (E_W, etc.) is not utilized in the rest of the paper, which may cause confusion.

The use of β to control for phrase compositionality is surprising, as it implies a single global constant determining the compositionality of any phrase. The values of β chosen through cross-validation in Table 3 are also unexpected, with PM+CL and WikiNYT yielding a value of zero, which argues against compositionality.

The experimental setup for Table 4 requires further explanation, particularly regarding how phrase/word vectors are used to compute similarities when the concept-phrases mapping is many-to-many. It appears that only concept vectors can be used in this scenario.

The results in Table 5 suggest that the approximate phr method, which approximates concepts with the average of their phrases, yields the best performance. This raises the question of whether the concept ontology is necessary, as similar results could be achieved by starting with a seed set of phrases.
