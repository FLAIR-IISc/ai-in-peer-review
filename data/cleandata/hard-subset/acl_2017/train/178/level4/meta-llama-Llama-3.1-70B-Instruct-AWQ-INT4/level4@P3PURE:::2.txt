This paper proposes a model for embedding words, phrases, and concepts into vector spaces. The approach utilizes an ontology of concepts, each mapped to phrases that are identified in text corpora and treated as atomic symbols. The model then employs a variant of the skip-gram method to train embeddings for words, phrases, and their associated concepts. The proposed work is evaluated on the task of concept similarity and relatedness, leveraging UMLS and Yago as the backing ontologies.

Strengths:
The paper addresses a crucial question regarding phrases that are not lexically similar but semantically close, as well as phrases that are non-compositional in nature. To this end, the authors propose a plausible model for training phrase embeddings, which are shown to be competitive or superior in identifying similarity between concepts. Furthermore, the software released with the paper has the potential to be useful for biomedical NLP researchers.

Weaknesses:
The primary weakness of the paper lies in the model's lack of novelty, as it is essentially a modification of the skip-gram method. Moreover, the full model presented in the paper does not appear to be the best-performing one in the results (as seen in Table 4). On the two Mayo datasets, the Choi baseline substantially outperforms the proposed model, a trend that also dominates Table 6. On the larger UMNSRS data, the proposed model is at best competitive with previous, simpler models (Chiu).

General Discussion:
The paper claims to utilize known phrases as distant supervision to train embeddings. However, the notion of "supervision" in this context is unclear. As understood from the paper, every occurrence of a phrase associated with a concept provides context for training word embeddings, but this does not constitute supervision in the classical sense (e.g., identifying concepts in text or other predictive tasks). Therefore, the terminology may be misleading. Additionally, the notation introduced in Section 3.2 (E_W, etc.) is not used in the rest of the paper.

The use of β to control for compositionality of phrases by words is unexpected. This approach is equivalent to assuming a single global constant that determines the compositionality of any phrase. The chosen values of β, as determined by cross-validation in Table 3, are also surprising, with PM+CL and WikiNYT having a value of zero, which essentially argues against compositionality.

The experimental setup for Table 4 requires further explanation. The paper states that the data labels similarity/relatedness of concepts (or entities), but if the concepts-phrases mapping is indeed many-to-many, it is unclear how phrase/word vectors are used to compute similarities. It appears that only concept vectors can be utilized in this case.

In Table 5, the approximate phr method (which approximates concepts with the average of the phrases in them) achieves the best performance. This raises the question of why the concept ontology is necessary, as similar results could be obtained by starting with a seed set of phrases.
