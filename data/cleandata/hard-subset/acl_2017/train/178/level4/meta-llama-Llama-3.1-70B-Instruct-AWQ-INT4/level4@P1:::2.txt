This paper presents a model for embedding words, phrases, and concepts into vector spaces. To do so, it uses an ontology of concepts, each of which is mapped to phrases. These phrases are found in text corpora and treated as atomic symbols. Using this, the paper uses what is essentially the skip-gram method to train embeddings for words, the now atomic phrases, and also the concepts associated with them. The proposed work is evaluated on the task of concept similarity and relatedness using UMLS and Yago to act as the backing ontologies.

**Main Contributions:**

1. A novel method for jointly learning concept, phrase, and word embeddings from an unlabeled text corpus, using representative phrases for ontology concepts as distant supervision.
2. A model that adapts the skip-gram with negative sampling variant of word2vec for joint embedding of concepts, phrases, and words.
3. An evaluation of the proposed method on concept similarity and relatedness tasks, showing competitive performance with existing methods.

**Strengths:**

* The key question addressed by the paper is that phrases that are not lexically similar can be semantically close, and, furthermore, not all phrases are compositional in nature.
* The proposed model is shown to be competitive or better at identifying similarity between concepts.
* The software released with the paper could be useful for biomedical NLP researchers.

**Weaknesses:**

* The primary weakness of the paper is that the model is not too novel, being essentially a tweak to skip-gram.
* The full model presented by the paper doesn't seem to be the best one in the results (in Table 4). On the two Mayo datasets, the Choi baseline is substantially better. A similar trend seems to dominate Table 6 too. On the larger UMNSRS data, the proposed model is at best competitive with previous simpler models (Chiu).
* The paper's use of terminology, such as "distant supervision," is somewhat confusing, as it does not involve traditional supervision in the sense of identifying concepts in text or other predictive tasks.
* The notation introduced in Section 3.2 (E_W, etc.) is never used in the rest of the paper.
* The use of β to control for compositionality of phrases by words is surprising, as it implies a single global constant deciding "how compositional" any phrase should be. The actual values of β chosen by cross-validation from Table 3 are also odd, with PM+CL and WikiNYT having a value of zero, which argues against compositionality.
* The experimental setup for Table 4 needs some explanation, particularly regarding how phrase/word vectors are used to compute similarities when the concepts-phrases mapping is many-to-many.
* In Table 5, the approximate phr method (which approximates concepts with the average of the phrases in them) is the best performing. This raises the question of whether the concept ontology is necessary, as similar results could be achieved by starting with a seed set of phrases.

**Questions to Authors:**

* Can you clarify the terminology used in the paper, particularly regarding "distant supervision"?
* How do you respond to the criticism that the model is not too novel, being essentially a tweak to skip-gram?
* Can you explain the experimental setup for Table 4, particularly regarding how phrase/word vectors are used to compute similarities?
* How do you justify the use of β to control for compositionality of phrases by words, given its surprising implications?
* Can you discuss the implications of the approximate phr method being the best performing in Table 5, and whether the concept ontology is necessary?
