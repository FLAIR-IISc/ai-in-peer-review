The paper proposes a novel method for jointly learning concept, phrase, and word embeddings from an unlabeled text corpus, using representative phrases for ontology concepts as distant supervision. The authors adapt the skip-gram with negative sampling variant of word2vec for joint embedding of concepts, phrases, and words, and introduce two hyperparameters to control the compositionality of phrases by words and concepts by phrases.

**Summary of the Paper**

The paper describes a method for jointly embedding concepts, phrases, and words into a real-valued space, using structured knowledge from an ontology and an unannotated training corpus. The authors use a variant of word2vec to train embeddings for medical concepts in the Unified Medical Language System and general-domain concepts in YAGO, using a variety of corpora.

**Main Contributions**

1. The authors propose a novel method for jointly learning concept, phrase, and word embeddings from an unlabeled text corpus, using representative phrases for ontology concepts as distant supervision.
2. The method adapts the skip-gram with negative sampling variant of word2vec for joint embedding of concepts, phrases, and words, and introduces two hyperparameters to control the compositionality of phrases by words and concepts by phrases.
3. The authors evaluate their method on biomedical similarity and relatedness tasks, and compare it with several approximations using embeddings learned on their corpora.

**Strengths**

1. The method is able to capture certain semantic characteristics of ontology concepts nearly as well as prior methods that require significantly more annotation.
2. The method can scale to a much larger vocabulary without additional expense.
3. The authors provide a novel dataset of similarity and relatedness of real-world entities, which is a valuable contribution to the field.

**Weaknesses**

1. The method relies on a simple replacement of phrases and words with their corresponding identifiers in the training corpus, which may not capture the full complexity of the relationships between concepts, phrases, and words.
2. The evaluation of the method is limited to biomedical similarity and relatedness tasks, and it is unclear how well the method would perform on other tasks or in other domains.
3. The authors do not provide a detailed analysis of the hyperparameters and their impact on the performance of the method.

**Questions to Authors**

1. How do the authors plan to extend their method to other domains or tasks, and what challenges do they anticipate in doing so?
2. Can the authors provide more details on the hyperparameters and their impact on the performance of the method?
3. How do the authors plan to address the issue of polysemy in their method, and what strategies do they propose for handling ambiguous words or phrases?

**Additional Comments**

1. The paper could benefit from a more detailed analysis of the relationships between concepts, phrases, and words, and how these relationships are captured by the method.
2. The authors could provide more details on the corpora used in the evaluation, and how they were preprocessed and prepared for training.
3. The paper could benefit from a more detailed discussion of the implications of the method for natural language processing and other applications.
