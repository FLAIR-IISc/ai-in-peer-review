The authors present a method to jointly embed words, phrases, and concepts based on plain text corpora and a manually-constructed ontology, in which concepts are represented by one or more phrases. They apply their method in the medical domain using the UMLS ontology and in the general domain using the YAGO ontology. To evaluate their approach, the authors compare it to simpler baselines and prior work, mostly on intrinsic similarity and relatedness benchmarks. They use existing benchmarks in the medical domain and use mechanical turkers to generate a new general-domain concept similarity and relatedness dataset, which they also intend to release. They report results that are comparable to prior work.

**Summary and Contributions:**

The paper proposes a novel method for jointly learning concept, phrase, and word embeddings from an unlabeled text corpus using representative phrases for ontology concepts as distant supervision. The main contributions of this work are:

1. A weakly-supervised method for jointly embedding concepts, phrases, and words into a real-valued space.
2. A new evaluation dataset for general-domain concept similarity and relatedness, which will be released.
3. Experimental results that demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed method in capturing semantic properties of ontology concepts.

**Strengths:**

1. The proposed joint embedding model is straightforward and makes reasonable sense. Its main value lies in reaching a configurable middle ground between treating phrases as atomic units and considering their compositionality.
2. The paper describes a decent volume of work, including model development, an additional contribution in the form of a new evaluation dataset, and several evaluations and analyses performed.

**Weaknesses:**

1. The evaluation reported in this paper includes only intrinsic tasks, mainly on similarity/relatedness datasets, which have limited power in predicting the utility of embeddings in extrinsic tasks.
2. The similarity/relatedness evaluation datasets used in the paper are presented as datasets recording human judgments of similarity between concepts, but they should be considered as phrase similarity datasets and analyzed as such.
3. The medical concept evaluation datasets used in the paper have limitations, such as small size and low human annotator agreement.
4. The authors extensively fine-tune their hyperparameters on the same datasets for which they report their results and compare them with prior work, making the reported results and analyses questionable.
5. The authors' argument that their method is superior to prior work because it achieved comparable results without requiring manual annotation is not strong, as they also use large manually-constructed ontologies.

**Questions to Authors:**

1. How do you plan to address the limitations of the evaluation datasets and methodology?
2. Can you provide more insights into what is going on behind the reported numbers, such as an NLP analysis specific to the relation between phrases and their component words?
3. How do you plan to investigate the effect of the hyperparameters that control the tradeoff between the atomic and compositional views of phrases and concepts?
