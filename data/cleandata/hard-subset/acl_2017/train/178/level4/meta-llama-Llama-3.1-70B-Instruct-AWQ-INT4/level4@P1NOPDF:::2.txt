Summary: This paper presents a model for embedding words, phrases, and concepts into vector spaces. To achieve this, it leverages an ontology of concepts, each mapped to phrases, which are found in text corpora and treated as atomic symbols. The paper then employs a method similar to the skip-gram approach to train embeddings for words, the now atomic phrases, and the concepts associated with them. The proposed work is evaluated on the task of concept similarity and relatedness using UMLS and Yago as the backing ontologies.

Strengths:
The key question addressed by the paper is that phrases lacking lexical similarity can be semantically close, and not all phrases are compositional in nature. To this end, the paper proposes a plausible model for training phrase embeddings. The trained embeddings are shown to be competitive or better at identifying similarity between concepts. Additionally, the software released with the paper could be useful for biomedical NLP researchers.

Weaknesses:
The primary weakness of the paper is that the model is not particularly novel, being essentially a tweak to the skip-gram method. Furthermore, the full model presented by the paper does not appear to be the best-performing one in the results (in Table 4). On the two Mayo datasets, the Choi baseline substantially outperforms the proposed model. A similar trend seems to dominate Table 6 as well. On the larger UMNSRS data, the proposed model is at best competitive with previous, simpler models (Chiu).

General Discussion:
The paper claims to use known phrases as distant supervision to train embeddings. However, the notion of "supervision" here is unclear. If understood correctly, every occurrence of a phrase associated with a concept provides context to train word embeddings. This does not constitute supervision in the traditional sense, such as identifying the concept in the text or other predictive tasks. Therefore, the terminology is somewhat confusing. The notation introduced in Section 3.2 (E_W, etc.) is never used in the rest of the paper.

The use of β to control for the compositionality of phrases by words is quite surprising. Essentially, this is equivalent to saying that there is a single global constant that decides "how compositional" any phrase should be. The surprising part here is that the actual values of β chosen by cross-validation from Table 3 are odd. For PM+CL and WikiNYT, it is zero, which basically argues against compositionality.

The experimental setup for Table 4 needs some explanation. The paper states that the data labels similarity/relatedness of concepts (or entities). However, if the concepts-phrases mapping is indeed many-to-many, then how are the phrase/word vectors used to compute the similarities? It seems that we can only use the concept vectors.

In Table 5, the approximate phr method (which approximates concepts with the average of the phrases in them) is the best-performing. So, it is not clear why we need the concept ontology. Instead, we could have just started with a seed set of phrases to get the same results.
