- Proposes a weakly-supervised method that jointly embeds concepts, phrases, and words by replacing representative phrases with a concept identifier and appending a tagged corpus to the original for training.  
- Uses ontology-derived representative phrases as distant supervision to learn embeddings without human corpus annotation.  
- Introduces a new biomedical test dataset for word similarity and relatedness of real-world entities.  
- Method captures many semantic characteristics of ontology concepts nearly as well as more heavily annotated prior methods.  
- Approach scales to a much larger vocabulary with little extra cost.  
- Provides a thorough evaluation including comparisons to several embedding-based approximations.  
- Evaluation is narrowly focused on the biomedical domain, limiting the scope of demonstrated contribution.  
- Method may fail to capture non-lexical concept information, as indicated by extreme similarity in compositionality analysis.  
- Slight mismatches between the ontologies used could negatively affect performance.  
- Requests more discussion of the generated test resource, including applications and limitations.  
- Asks how the authors will address the method’s inability to capture all non-lexical concept information.  
- Suggests evaluating the method on other domains or tasks to demonstrate generalizability.  
- Paper is well-written and the technique is intuitive and easy to understand.  
- Notes the unusual choice of learning rate (α = 0.05) for biomedical corpora and requests justification.  
- Recommends a deeper analysis of semantic type clustering, explaining why some types cluster better than others.
