This paper proposes a vector space model for embedding words, phrases, and concepts by leveraging an ontology that maps concepts to phrases. These phrases, derived from text corpora, serve as atomic symbols and are used to train embeddings for words, phrases, and their associated concepts via a skip-gram-like approach. The model's performance is evaluated on concept similarity and relatedness tasks using UMLS and Yago as the underlying ontologies.

The paper's primary contribution lies in addressing the challenge of phrases with dissimilar lexical structures yet similar semantic meanings and the non-compositional nature of certain phrases. The proposed model provides a viable solution for training phrase embeddings and demonstrates competitive or superior performance in identifying concept similarities.

The accompanying software has the potential to benefit biomedical NLP researchers.

However, the model's novelty is limited, as it is essentially an adaptation of the skip-gram method. Furthermore, the full model presented in the paper does not consistently outperform other approaches, as evidenced by the results in Tables 4 and 6. On the Mayo datasets, the Choi baseline yields substantially better results, while on the larger UMNSRS dataset, the proposed model is at best competitive with simpler models like Chiu.

The paper's discussion of using known phrases as distant supervision for training embeddings is somewhat misleading, as it does not conform to the traditional understanding of supervision in predictive tasks. Instead, the context provided by phrase occurrences associated with concepts is used to train word embeddings.

Additionally, the notation introduced in Section 3.2 (E_W, etc.) is not utilized elsewhere in the paper. The use of β to control for phrase compositionality by words is unexpected, as it implies a single global constant determining the compositionality of any phrase. The chosen values of β, as reported in Table 3, are also surprising, with PM+CL and WikiNYT having a value of zero, which suggests a lack of compositionality.

The experimental setup for Table 4 requires clarification, particularly regarding the use of phrase/word vectors to compute similarities in a many-to-many concepts-phrases mapping. It appears that only concept vectors can be used for this purpose.

Finally, the approximate phr method, which approximates concepts with the average of their phrases, yields the best performance in Table 5. This raises questions about the necessity of the concept ontology, as similar results could be achieved by starting with a seed set of phrases.
