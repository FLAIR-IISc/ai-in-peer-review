The authors present a method for jointly embedding words, phrases, and concepts using plain text corpora and a manually constructed ontology. This ontology represents concepts with one or more phrases. The proposed approach is applied to the medical domain using the UMLS ontology and to the general domain using the YAGO ontology. To evaluate their method, the authors compare it to simpler baselines and prior work, primarily using intrinsic similarity and relatedness benchmarks. They utilize existing benchmarks in the medical domain and create a new general-domain concept similarity and relatedness dataset through Mechanical Turk, which they also plan to release. The reported results are comparable to those of prior work.

Strengths:
- The proposed joint embedding model is straightforward and reasonable. Its primary value lies in finding a configurable middle ground between treating phrases as atomic units and considering their compositionality. The same approach is applied to concepts composed of several representative phrases.
- The paper describes a substantial volume of work, including model development, an additional contribution in the form of a new evaluation dataset, and several evaluations and analyses performed.

Weaknesses:
- The evaluation reported in this paper only includes intrinsic tasks, mainly on similarity and relatedness datasets. As the authors note, such evaluations have limited power in predicting the utility of embeddings in extrinsic tasks. Recently, it has become more common to include at least one or two extrinsic tasks as part of the evaluation of embedding models.
- The similarity and relatedness evaluation datasets used in the paper are presented as datasets recording human judgments of similarity between concepts. However, the actual judgments were made based on presenting phrases to human annotators, and therefore, they should be considered as phrase similarity datasets and analyzed as such.
- The medical concept evaluation dataset, 'mini MayoSRS,' is extremely small (29 pairs), and its larger superset 'MayoSRS' is only slightly larger (101 pairs) and had relatively low human annotator agreement. The other medical concept evaluation dataset, 'UMNSRS,' is more reasonable in size but is based only on concepts that can be represented as single words and were represented as such to human annotators. This should be mentioned in the paper, making the relevance of this dataset questionable with respect to representations of phrases and general concepts.
- The authors extensively fine-tune their hyperparameters on the same datasets for which they report their results and compare them with prior work. This makes all the reported results and analyses questionable.
- The authors argue that their method is superior to prior work because it achieved comparable results while prior work required much more manual annotation. However, this argument is not strong, as the authors also use large manually constructed ontologies, and the manually annotated dataset used in prior work comes from existing clinical records that did not require dedicated annotations.
- The paper lacks useful insights into what is happening behind the reported numbers. The authors treat the relation between a phrase and its component words and a concept and its alternative phrases as similar types of compositional relations. However, these relations are different in nature and deserve dedicated analyses. For example, an NLP analysis specific to the relation between phrases and their component words could be added. Perhaps the reason for the reported behavior is dominant phrase headwords, etc. An investigation of the effect of the hyperparameters that control the tradeoff between the atomic and compositional views of phrases and concepts could also strengthen the work.

General Discussion:
Due to the above-mentioned weaknesses, I recommend rejecting this submission. I encourage the authors to improve their evaluation datasets and methodology before re-submitting this paper.
