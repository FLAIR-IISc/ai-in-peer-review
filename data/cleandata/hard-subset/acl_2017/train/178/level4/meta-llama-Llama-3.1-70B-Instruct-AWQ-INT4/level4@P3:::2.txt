This paper presents a model for jointly embedding words, phrases, and concepts into vector spaces. The authors use an ontology of concepts, each of which is mapped to phrases, to train embeddings for words, phrases, and concepts. The proposed work is evaluated on the task of concept similarity and relatedness using UMLS and Yago to act as the backing ontologies.

**Contributions:**

1. A novel method for jointly learning concept, phrase, and word embeddings from an unlabeled text corpus using representative phrases for ontology concepts as distant supervision.
2. The proposed method shows competitive performance with existing methods on concept similarity and relatedness tasks without requiring human corpus annotation.
3. The authors release a software implementation of their method, cui2vec, and a novel dataset of similarity and relatedness of real-world entities.

**Strengths:**

1. The paper addresses the issue of phrases that are not lexically similar but are semantically close, and proposes a plausible model to train phrase embeddings.
2. The trained embeddings are shown to be competitive or better at identifying similarity between concepts.
3. The software released with the paper could be useful for biomedical NLP researchers.

**Weaknesses:**

1. The primary weakness of the paper is that the model is not too novel, being essentially a tweak to skip-gram.
2. The full model presented by the paper doesn't seem to be the best one in the results, with the Choi baseline being substantially better on the two Mayo datasets, and the proposed model being at best competitive with previous simpler models on the larger UMNSRS data.
3. The terminology used in the paper is sometimes confusing, such as the use of "distant supervision" to describe the method, which is not traditional supervision.

**Questions to Authors:**

1. Can you clarify what you mean by "distant supervision" in the context of your method, and how it differs from traditional supervision?
2. How do you respond to the criticism that the model is not novel, and that the full model presented in the paper is not the best one in the results?
3. Can you provide more explanation for the experimental setup in Table 4, particularly with regards to how the phrase/word vectors are used to compute similarities?
4. Why do you think the approximate phr method, which approximates concepts with the average of the phrases in them, performs better than the full model in Table 5, and whether this suggests that the concept ontology is necessary for the method.
