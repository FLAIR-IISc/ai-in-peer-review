The paper proposes an extension of word embedding methods to also provide representations for phrases and concepts that correspond to words. The method works by fixing an identifier for groups of phrases, words, and the concept that all denote this concept, replacing the occurrences of the phrases and words by this identifier in the training corpus, creating a "tagged" corpus, and then appending the tagged corpus to the original corpus for training. The concept/phrase/word sets are taken from an ontology. Since the domain of application is biomedical, the related corpora and ontologies are used. The researchers also report on the generation of a new test dataset for word similarity and relatedness for real-world entities, which is novel.

**Summary of the paper:**
The paper describes a weakly-supervised method for jointly embedding concepts, phrases, and words into a real-valued space, using structured knowledge from an ontology and an unannotated training corpus. The method leverages representative phrases for ontology concepts as distant supervision and requires no human corpus annotation.

**Main contributions:**

1. A novel method for jointly learning concept, phrase, and word embeddings from an unlabeled text corpus, using representative phrases for ontology concepts as distant supervision.
2. The creation of a new test dataset for word similarity and relatedness for real-world entities, which is a valuable contribution to the field.

**Strengths:**

1. The method is able to capture certain semantic characteristics of ontology concepts nearly as well as prior methods that require significantly more annotation.
2. The approach is able to scale to a much larger vocabulary without additional expense.
3. The paper provides a thorough evaluation of the method, including comparisons with several approximations using embeddings learned on the corpora.

**Weaknesses:**

1. The scope of the contribution is limited, as the evaluation is focused within the biomedical domain.
2. The method may not capture all non-lexical information about concepts, as suggested by the extreme similarity observed in the compositionality analysis.
3. There is a slight mismatch between the ontologies used, which may affect the performance of the method.

**Questions to authors:**

1. Can you provide more discussion on the generated test resource, including its potential applications and limitations?
2. How do you plan to address the issue of not capturing all non-lexical information about concepts?
3. Have you considered evaluating the method on other domains or tasks to demonstrate its generalizability?

**Additional comments:**

1. The paper is well-written, and the technique is natural and easy to understand.
2. The use of a learning rate of α = 0.05 in the biomedical corpora is an interesting choice, and it would be useful to see a discussion on why this value was chosen.
3. The paper could benefit from a more detailed analysis of the semantic type clustering results, including a discussion on why certain types are better clustered than others.
