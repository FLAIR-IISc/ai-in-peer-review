This paper proposes a model for embedding words, phrases, and concepts into vector spaces, leveraging an ontology of concepts mapped to phrases. These phrases, found in text corpora, are treated as atomic symbols and used to train embeddings for words, phrases, and associated concepts via a method akin to skip-gram. The proposed work is evaluated on concept similarity and relatedness tasks, utilizing UMLS and Yago as backing ontologies.

The paper addresses a crucial question: phrases lacking lexical similarity can be semantically close, and not all phrases are compositional in nature. To this end, the proposed model offers a plausible approach to training phrase embeddings, which demonstrate competitiveness or superiority in identifying concept similarities. Additionally, the accompanying software release may prove useful for biomedical NLP researchers.

However, the primary weakness of the paper lies in the model's lack of novelty, being essentially a tweak to skip-gram. Furthermore, the full model does not consistently outperform other approaches in the results (Tables 4 and 6). On the Mayo datasets, the Choi baseline substantially outperforms the proposed model, while on the larger UMNSRS data, the proposed model is at best competitive with previous, simpler models (Chiu).

The paper's claim of using known phrases as distant supervision to train embeddings is somewhat misleading, as the "supervision" is not in the traditional sense. Instead, every occurrence of a phrase associated with a concept provides context for training word embeddings. This terminology may cause confusion. Moreover, the notation introduced in Section 3.2 (E_W, etc.) is not utilized in the rest of the paper.

The use of β to control for compositionality of phrases by words is unexpected, implying a single global constant decides the compositionality of any phrase. The chosen values of β, as determined by cross-validation (Table 3), are also surprising, with values of zero for PM+CL and WikiNYT arguing against compositionality.

The experimental setup for Table 4 requires clarification, particularly regarding the computation of similarities using phrase/word vectors in a many-to-many concepts-phrases mapping. It appears that only concept vectors can be used for this purpose.

In Table 5, the approximate phr method, which approximates concepts with the average of the phrases in them, achieves the best performance. This raises questions about the necessity of the concept ontology, as similar results could be obtained by starting with a seed set of phrases.
