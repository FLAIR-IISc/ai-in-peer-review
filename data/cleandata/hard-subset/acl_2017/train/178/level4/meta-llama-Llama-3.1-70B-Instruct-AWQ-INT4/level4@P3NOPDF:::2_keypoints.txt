- Proposes embeddings for words, phrases, and concepts by leveraging an ontology that maps concepts to phrases treated as atomic symbols.
- Uses a skip-gram variant to train embeddings and evaluates on concept similarity/relatedness with UMLS and Yago.
- Addresses the important problem of non-lexically similar but semantically close and non-compositional phrases.
- Shows competitive or improved performance on some concept similarity tasks and releases software useful for biomedical NLP.
- Main novelty is limited: the model is essentially a tweak to skip-gram rather than a fundamentally new approach.
- The full proposed model is not the best-performing variant in the results (Table 4).
- On two Mayo datasets the Choi baseline is substantially better, and similar trends appear in Table 6.
- On the larger UMNSRS dataset the proposed model is at best competitive with simpler baselines (Chiu).
- Calling known phrases “distant supervision” is misleading—no traditional supervision or labeling is provided; phrase occurrences merely provide context.
- Notation introduced in Section 3.2 (e.g., E_W) is unused elsewhere in the paper.
- Using a single global β to control phrase compositionality is surprising and unrealistic; chosen β values (zeros for some corpora) undermine compositionality claims.
- The experimental setup for Table 4 is unclear: with many-to-many concept↔phrase mappings, how are phrase/word vectors used to compute concept similarities?
- The approximate phr method (averaging phrase vectors) is the best performer (Table 5), calling into question the necessity of the ontology versus starting from a seed phrase set.
