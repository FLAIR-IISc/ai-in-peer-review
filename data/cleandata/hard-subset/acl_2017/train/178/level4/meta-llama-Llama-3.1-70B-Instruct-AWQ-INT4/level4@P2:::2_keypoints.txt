- Proposes joint learning of concept, phrase, and word embeddings using ontology-derived representative phrases as distant supervision.  
- Uses a skip-gram–style model to train embeddings for words, atomic phrases, and associated concepts from unlabeled text.  
- Evaluated on concept similarity and relatedness tasks using UMLS and Yago, showing competitive performance and released useful software for biomedical NLP.

- Addresses handling non-compositional or lexically dissimilar but semantically close phrases.  
- Presents a plausible phrase-embedding training approach with competitive similarity-identification results.

- Model lacks strong novelty, largely a tweak to skip-gram.  
- Full model is not consistently best in results (Choi baseline outperforms on Mayo datasets; Chiu competitive on UMNSRS).

- Clarify what is meant by “distant supervision” and how it differs from standard supervision when using phrase occurrences to train embeddings.  
- Explain the choice and interpretation of the β hyperparameter controlling phrase compositionality and why chosen values seem odd.  
- Provide details on the experimental procedure for Table 4, especially how phrase/word vectors are combined to compute concept/entity similarities.

- Notation introduced in Section 3.2 (e.g., E_W) is unused elsewhere.  
- A single global β controlling compositionality is surprising and potentially unrealistic.  
- Table 5 shows the approximate-phr method (averaging phrases) performs best, calling into question the added value of the concept ontology versus starting from seed phrases.
