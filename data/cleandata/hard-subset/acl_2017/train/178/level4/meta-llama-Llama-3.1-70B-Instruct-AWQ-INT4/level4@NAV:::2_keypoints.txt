- Proposes a vector space model embedding words, phrases, and concepts using an ontology that maps concepts to corpus-derived phrases and trains embeddings via a skip-gram-like approach.  
- Main contribution: addresses phrases with dissimilar lexical forms and non-compositional phrases, offering a viable method for phrase embeddings.  
- Evaluated on concept similarity/relatedness using UMLS and Yago; shows competitive or superior performance in some cases.  
- Accompanying software could be useful for biomedical NLP researchers.  
- Novelty is limited—method is essentially an adaptation of skip-gram rather than a fundamentally new approach.  
- Full model does not consistently outperform baselines: Choi is substantially better on Mayo datasets and Chiu is competitive on UMNSRS.  
- The paper’s claim of using known phrases as “distant supervision” is misleading; it uses phrase-context occurrences to train embeddings, not traditional supervision.  
- Notation introduced in Section 3.2 (e.g., E_W) is not used elsewhere in the paper.  
- Using a single global β to control phrase compositionality is questionable, and reported β values (zeros for PM+CL and WikiNYT) are surprising and suggest lack of compositionality.  
- Experimental setup for Table 4 needs clarification—how similarities are computed given many-to-many concept↔phrase mappings and whether only concept vectors are used.  
- The approximate phr method (averaging phrase vectors for concepts) performs best in Table 5, calling into question the necessity of the concept ontology versus starting from a seed set of phrases.
