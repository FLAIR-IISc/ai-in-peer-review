- Joint embedding model is straightforward and offers a configurable middle ground between treating phrases as atomic and modeling compositionality; applied similarly to concepts composed of multiple phrases.
- Paper presents substantial work: model development, a new evaluation dataset, and multiple evaluations/analyses.
- Evaluation is limited to intrinsic similarity/relatedness tasks and lacks extrinsic downstream evaluations to demonstrate utility.
- Datasets presented as concept-similarity are actually based on phrase judgments and should be treated/analyzed as phrase similarity datasets.
- Medical evaluation datasets are problematic: mini MayoSRS is tiny (29 pairs), MayoSRS is small (101 pairs) with low annotator agreement, and UMNSRS is limited to single-word concepts—reducing relevance for phrase/concept representations.
- Extensive hyperparameter tuning on the same datasets used for evaluation makes the reported results and comparisons questionable.
- Claim of superiority over prior work is unconvincing because the authors rely on large manually-constructed ontologies and prior annotations came from existing clinical records.
- Paper lacks deeper analysis of the different compositional relations (phrase↔words vs. concept↔phrases); needs NLP-specific analyses (e.g., headword effects) and exploration of hyperparameter tradeoffs between atomic and compositional views.
- Reviewer recommends rejection and asks for improved evaluation datasets and methodology before resubmission.
