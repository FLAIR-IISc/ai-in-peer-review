The authors propose a novel approach for jointly embedding words, phrases, and concepts using plain text corpora and a manually constructed ontology. This ontology represents concepts through one or more phrases. They apply their method to the medical domain using the UMLS ontology and the general domain using the YAGO ontology. To assess their approach, the authors compare it to simpler baselines and prior work, primarily focusing on intrinsic similarity and relatedness benchmarks. They utilize existing benchmarks in the medical domain and create a new general-domain concept similarity and relatedness dataset through mechanical turkers, which they plan to release. The authors report results comparable to prior work.

Strengths:
- The proposed joint embedding model is intuitive and reasonable, offering a configurable middle ground between treating phrases as atomic units and considering their compositionality. This approach is also applied to concepts composed of multiple representative phrases.
- The paper presents a substantial volume of work, including model development, an additional contribution in the form of a new evaluation dataset, and several evaluations and analyses.

Weaknesses:
- The evaluation in this paper is limited to intrinsic tasks, mainly on similarity and relatedness datasets. As acknowledged by the authors, such evaluations have limited predictive power for the utility of embeddings in extrinsic tasks. It has become increasingly common to include at least one or two extrinsic tasks in the evaluation of embedding models.
- The similarity and relatedness evaluation datasets presented in the paper record human judgments of similarity between concepts. However, the actual judgments were made based on presenting phrases to human annotators, making them phrase similarity datasets that should be analyzed as such.
- The medical concept evaluation dataset, 'mini MayoSRS,' is extremely small (29 pairs), and its larger superset, 'MayoSRS,' is only slightly larger (101 pairs) with relatively low human annotator agreement. The other medical concept evaluation dataset, 'UMNSRS,' is more reasonable in size but is based on concepts represented as single words, which may question its relevance to phrase and general concept representations.
- The authors extensively fine-tune their hyperparameters on the same datasets for which they report results and compare them to prior work. This makes the reported results and analyses questionable.
- The authors argue that their method is superior to prior work due to comparable results with less manual annotation. However, this argument is weakened by the use of large manually constructed ontologies and the origin of manually annotated datasets from existing clinical records.
- The paper lacks insightful analysis into the reported numbers. The authors treat the relation between a phrase and its component words and a concept and its alternative phrases as similar compositional relations. However, these relations differ in nature and deserve dedicated analysis. For instance, an NLP analysis specific to the relation between phrases and their component words could provide valuable insights.

General Discussion:
Given the mentioned weaknesses, I recommend rejecting this submission. I encourage the authors to improve their evaluation datasets and methodology before re-submitting the paper.
