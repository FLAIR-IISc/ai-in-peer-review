The authors present a method for jointly embedding words, phrases, and concepts based on plain text corpora and a manually constructed ontology. This ontology represents concepts using one or more phrases. The proposed approach is applied in both the medical domain, utilizing the UMLS ontology, and the general domain, utilizing the YAGO ontology. To evaluate their method, the authors compare it to simpler baselines and prior work, primarily focusing on intrinsic similarity and relatedness benchmarks. They leverage existing benchmarks in the medical domain and create a new general-domain concept similarity and relatedness dataset through Mechanical Turk, which they plan to release. The reported results are comparable to those of prior work.

Strengths:

* The proposed joint embedding model is straightforward and reasonable, offering a configurable middle ground between treating phrases as atomic units and considering their compositionality. This approach is also applied to concepts composed of multiple representative phrases.
* The paper presents a substantial volume of work, including model development, an additional contribution in the form of a new evaluation dataset, and several evaluations and analyses.

Weaknesses:

* The evaluation in this paper only includes intrinsic tasks, mainly similarity and relatedness datasets. As the authors acknowledge, such evaluations have limited power in predicting the utility of embeddings in extrinsic tasks. It has become increasingly common to include at least one or two extrinsic tasks in the evaluation of embedding models.
* The similarity and relatedness evaluation datasets used in the paper are presented as recording human judgments of similarity between concepts. However, the actual judgments were made based on presenting phrases to human annotators, and therefore, they should be considered phrase similarity datasets and analyzed as such.
* The medical concept evaluation dataset, 'mini MayoSRS,' is extremely small (29 pairs), and its larger superset 'MayoSRS' is only slightly larger (101 pairs) with relatively low human annotator agreement. The other medical concept evaluation dataset, 'UMNSRS,' is more reasonable in size but is based only on concepts represented as single words and presented as such to human annotators. This should be mentioned in the paper, making the relevance of this dataset questionable for representations of phrases and general concepts.
* The authors extensively fine-tune their hyperparameters on the same datasets for which they report results and compare them with prior work. This makes all the reported results and analyses questionable.
* The authors argue that their method is superior to prior work, as it achieved comparable results with less manual annotation. However, this argument is not strong, as the authors also use large manually constructed ontologies, and the manually annotated dataset used in prior work comes from existing clinical records that did not require dedicated annotations.
* The paper lacks useful insights into what is happening behind the reported numbers. The authors treat the relation between a phrase and its component words and a concept and its alternative phrases as similar compositional relations. However, these relations are different in nature and deserve dedicated analyses. For example, an NLP analysis specific to the relation between phrases and their component words could provide more insight. The investigation of the effect of hyperparameters controlling the tradeoff between atomic and compositional views of phrases and concepts could also strengthen the work.

General Discussion:

Due to the mentioned weaknesses, I recommend rejecting this submission. I encourage the authors to consider improving their evaluation datasets and methodology before re-submitting this paper.
