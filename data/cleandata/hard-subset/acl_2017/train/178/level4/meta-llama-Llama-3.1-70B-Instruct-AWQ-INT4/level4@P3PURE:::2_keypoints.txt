- Proposes embedding words, phrases, and concepts by mapping ontology concepts to phrases treated as atomic symbols and training a skip-gram variant.  
- Evaluated on concept similarity and relatedness using UMLS and Yago.  
- Strength: Addresses semantically close but lexically different phrases and non-compositional phrases with a plausible phrase embedding model.  
- Strength: Model is competitive or superior on some similarity tasks; released software is potentially useful for biomedical NLP.  
- Weakness: Lacks novelty—essentially a modification of skip-gram.  
- Weakness: The full proposed model is not the best-performing variant (Table 4).  
- Weakness: Choi baseline substantially outperforms the proposed model on the two Mayo datasets; on UMNSRS the model is only competitive with simpler models (Chiu).  
- Terminology concern: Calling phrase occurrences “distant supervision” is misleading since they provide context but not classical supervised signals like concept labeling.  
- Presentation issue: Notation introduced in Section 3.2 (e.g., E_W) is not used elsewhere.  
- Modeling concern: Using a single global β to control phrase compositionality is unexpected and unrealistic; cross-validated β=0 for some corpora contradicts compositionality assumptions.  
- Experimental clarity issue: Table 4’s setup is unclear—if concept↔phrase mapping is many-to-many, it’s unspecified how word/phrase vectors are used to compute concept similarities (seems only concept vectors can be used).  
- Ontology necessity questioned: Table 5’s best performance from the approximate-phr (average-of-phrases) method suggests the ontology may be unnecessary—similar results might be obtained from a seed set of phrases.
