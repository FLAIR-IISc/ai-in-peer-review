The paper presents an extension of word embedding methods to provide representations for phrases and concepts that correspond to words. The method works by assigning a fixed identifier to groups of phrases, words, and concepts that denote the same concept, replacing the occurrences of these phrases and words with this identifier in the training corpus to create a "tagged" corpus, and then appending the tagged corpus to the original corpus for training. The concept/phrase/word sets are derived from an ontology, specifically utilizing biomedical corpora and ontologies given the domain of application. Additionally, the researchers report on the generation of a new test dataset for word similarity and relatedness for real-world entities, a novel contribution.

In general, the paper is well-written. The technique, while natural, is not a particularly substantial contribution. The scope of the contribution is limited due to the focused evaluation within the biomedical domain. A more in-depth discussion of the generated test resource could be useful, as it may be the most interesting contribution of the paper.

One minor technical issue arises, likely due to a mathematical expression rather than implementation. In Eq. 8, the authors aim to define the MAP calculation, a good idea, but one that could be improved by defining a natural cut-off rather than ranking the entire vocabulary. A closer examination of Eq. 8 reveals that it does not define a probability, a fact that can be easily demonstrated, even considering an infinite vocabulary size. Therefore, the explanation should be revised to remove references to probability.

Minor corrections are also needed: on line 556, "most concepts has" should be corrected to "most concepts have".
