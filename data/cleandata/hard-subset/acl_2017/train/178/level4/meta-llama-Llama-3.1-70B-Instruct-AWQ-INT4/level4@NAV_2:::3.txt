The authors present a method to jointly embed words, phrases, and concepts based on plain text corpora and a manually-constructed ontology, in which concepts are represented by one or more phrases. They apply their method in the medical domain using the UMLS ontology and in the general domain using the YAGO ontology. To evaluate their approach, the authors compare it to simpler baselines and prior work, mostly on intrinsic similarity and relatedness benchmarks. They use existing benchmarks in the medical domain and use mechanical turkers to generate a new general-domain concept similarity and relatedness dataset, which they also intend to release. They report results that are comparable to prior work.

**Summary of the paper and main contributions:**

The authors propose a novel method for jointly learning concept, phrase, and word embeddings from an unlabeled text corpus using representative phrases for ontology concepts as distant supervision. The main contributions of this work are:

1. A joint embedding model that can capture the relationships between concepts, phrases, and words.
2. A method for learning concept embeddings without requiring manual annotation.
3. A new evaluation dataset for general-domain concept similarity and relatedness.

**Strengths:**

- The proposed joint embedding model is straightforward and makes reasonable sense.
- The paper describes a decent volume of work, including model development, an additional contribution in the form of a new evaluation dataset, and several evaluations and analyses performed.

**Weaknesses:**

- The evaluation reported in this paper includes only intrinsic tasks, mainly on similarity/relatedness datasets.
- The similarity/relatedness evaluation datasets used in the paper are presented as datasets recording human judgments of similarity between concepts, but they should be considered as phrase similarity datasets.
- The medical concept evaluation dataset is small and has low human annotator agreement.
- The authors fine-tune their hyperparameters on the same datasets for which they report their results and compare them with prior work.
- The authors' argument that their method is superior to prior work because it achieved comparable results while prior work required much more manual annotation is not strong.

**Questions to Authors:**

- Can you provide more insights into what is going on behind the reported numbers, specifically regarding the relation between phrases and their component words?
- How do you handle phrase overlaps in your model?
- Can you clarify how words are trained in the joint model?
- Can you provide more information about the hyperparameter tuning process, including which task was used for tuning and what trends were observed in the development set?
