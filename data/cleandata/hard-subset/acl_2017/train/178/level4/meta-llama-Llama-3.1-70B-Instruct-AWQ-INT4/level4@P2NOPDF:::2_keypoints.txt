- Proposes embedding words, phrases, and concepts by treating mapped phrases as atomic symbols and training via a skip-gram–like method, evaluated on UMLS and Yago concept similarity/relatedness tasks.

- Tackles important issue that semantically close phrases may lack lexical overlap and that many phrases are non-compositional.

- Shows a plausible phrase-embedding approach with competitive or superior performance on some concept-similarity benchmarks and releases software valuable for biomedical NLP.

- Main weakness: methodological novelty is limited — the model is essentially a tweak to skip-gram.

- Empirical results are inconsistent: the full model does not always beat baselines (Choi does much better on Mayo; on UMNSRS the model is at best competitive with simpler methods like Chiu).

- Calling the use of known phrases “distant supervision” is misleading — phrase occurrences provide context for training rather than true supervision, which can confuse readers.

- Notation introduced in Section 3.2 (e.g., E_W) is unused elsewhere and unnecessary.

- The use of a single global β to control phrase compositionality is conceptually odd; cross-validated β values (including zeros) are surprising and counterintuitive.

- Experimental details for Table 4 are unclear, particularly how similarities are computed given many-to-many concept–phrase mappings (it seems only concept vectors can be used).

- The approximate phr baseline (averaging phrase vectors) performs best in Table 5, calling into question the necessity of the ontology since similar results might come from a seed phrase set.
