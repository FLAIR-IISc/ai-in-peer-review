This paper proposes a model for embedding words, phrases, and concepts into vector spaces by leveraging an ontology of concepts, each mapped to phrases. These phrases, found in text corpora, are treated as atomic symbols. The model utilizes a variant of the skip-gram method to train embeddings for words, phrases, and associated concepts. The proposed work is evaluated on the task of concept similarity and relatedness using UMLS and Yago as backing ontologies.

The paper addresses a key question regarding phrases that are not lexically similar but can be semantically close, and not all phrases are compositional in nature. To this end, the paper proposes a plausible model to train phrase embeddings, which are shown to be competitive or better at identifying similarity between concepts. Furthermore, the software released with the paper could be useful for biomedical NLP researchers.

However, the primary weakness of the paper is that the model is not particularly novel, as it is essentially a tweak to the skip-gram method. Moreover, the full model presented by the paper does not seem to be the best-performing one in the results (in Table 4). On the two Mayo datasets, the Choi baseline is substantially better, and a similar trend dominates Table 6. On the larger UMNSRS data, the proposed model is at best competitive with previous simpler models (Chiu).

The paper's use of known phrases as "distant supervision" to train embeddings is unclear, as it does not provide traditional supervision (e.g., identifying concepts in text or predictive tasks). Instead, every occurrence of a phrase associated with a concept provides context to train word embeddings. This terminology is somewhat confusing.

Additionally, the notation introduced in Section 3.2 (E_W, etc.) is not used in the rest of the paper. The use of β to control for compositionality of phrases by words is also surprising, as it implies a single global constant deciding the compositionality of any phrase. The chosen values of β from Table 3 are odd, with PM+CL and WikiNYT having a value of zero, which argues against compositionality.

The experimental setup for Table 4 requires further explanation. The paper states that the data labels similarity/relatedness of concepts (or entities), but if the concepts-phrases mapping is many-to-many, it is unclear how phrase/word vectors are used to compute similarities. It seems that only concept vectors can be used.

In Table 5, the approximate phr method (which approximates concepts with the average of the phrases in them) is the best-performing approach. This raises questions about the necessity of the concept ontology, as similar results could be achieved by starting with a seed set of phrases.
