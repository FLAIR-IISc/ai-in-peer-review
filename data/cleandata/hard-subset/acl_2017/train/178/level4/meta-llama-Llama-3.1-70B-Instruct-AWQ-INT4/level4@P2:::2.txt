This paper presents a model for embedding words, phrases, and concepts into vector spaces. To do so, it uses an ontology of concepts, each of which is mapped to phrases. These phrases are found in text corpora and treated as atomic symbols. Using this, the paper uses what is essentially the skip-gram method to train embeddings for words, the now atomic phrases, and also the concepts associated with them. The proposed work is evaluated on the task of concept similarity and relatedness using UMLS and Yago to act as the backing ontologies.

**Main Contributions:**

1. The paper proposes a novel method for jointly learning concept, phrase, and word embeddings from an unlabeled text corpus using representative phrases for ontology concepts as distant supervision.
2. The model is shown to be competitive or better at identifying similarity between concepts on several datasets, including biomedical and general-domain entities.
3. The software released with the paper could be useful for biomedical NLP researchers.

**Strengths:**

1. The paper addresses the key question of how to handle phrases that are not lexically similar but semantically close, and not all phrases are compositional in nature.
2. The proposed model is a plausible approach to train phrase embeddings, and the trained embeddings are shown to be competitive or better at identifying similarity between concepts.

**Weaknesses:**

1. The primary weakness of the paper is that the model is not too novel, as it is essentially a tweak to the skip-gram method.
2. The full model presented by the paper doesn't seem to be the best one in the results (in Table 4). On the two Mayo datasets, the Choi baseline is substantially better. A similar trend seems to dominate Table 6 too. On the larger UMNSRS data, the proposed model is at best competitive with previous simpler models (Chiu).

**Questions to Authors:**

1. Can you clarify the notion of "distant supervision" used in the paper? If every occurrence of a phrase associated with a concept provides the context to train word embeddings, how is this different from traditional supervision methods?
2. Can you explain the choice of values for the hyperparameter β, which controls the compositionality of phrases by words? Why are the chosen values odd, and what implications does this have for the model?
3. Can you provide more details on the experimental setup for Table 4, particularly on how the phrase/word vectors are used to compute similarities between concepts or entities?

**Additional Comments:**

1. The notation introduced in Section 3.2 (E_W, etc.) is never used in the rest of the paper.
2. The use of β to control for compositionality of phrases by words is quite surprising, as it implies a single global constant that decides "how compositional" any phrase should be.
3. The results in Table 5 suggest that the approximate phr method (which approximates concepts with the average of the phrases in them) is best performing. Can you explain why the concept ontology is necessary in this case, and why starting with a seed set of phrases would not yield the same results?
