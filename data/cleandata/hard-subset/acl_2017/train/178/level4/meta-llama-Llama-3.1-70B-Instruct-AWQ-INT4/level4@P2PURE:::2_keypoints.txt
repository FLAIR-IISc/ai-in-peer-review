- Proposes ontology-guided embeddings for words, phrases, and concepts by treating corpus-extracted phrases as atomic and training skip-gram-style embeddings; evaluated with UMLS and Yago.

- Addresses semantic similarity for lexically dissimilar/noncompositional phrases; approach is plausible and shows competitive or superior performance; released software may benefit biomedical NLP.

- Main weakness: limited novelty—method is essentially a tweak to skip-gram.

- Full model not consistently superior: Choi baseline outperforms on Mayo datasets and similar trends in Table 6; on UMNSRS the model is only competitive with simpler methods like Chiu.

- Claim of using known phrases as "distant supervision" is misleading—phrase occurrences only provide context, not traditional supervised signals like concept identification.

- Notation introduced in Section 3.2 (e.g., E_W) is not used elsewhere and may confuse readers.

- Using a single global β to control phrase compositionality is surprising and questionable; cross-validated β values (including zeros) are unexpected and argue against compositionality.

- Experimental setup for Table 4 is unclear about computing similarities under many-to-many concept–phrase mappings; it seems only concept vectors may be usable.

- Table 5 shows the approximate method (averaging phrase vectors) performs best, calling into question the necessity of the concept ontology versus starting from a seed phrase set.
