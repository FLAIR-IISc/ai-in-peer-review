The authors present a method for jointly embedding words, phrases, and concepts based on plain text corpora and a manually constructed ontology, where concepts are represented by one or more phrases. They apply this method in the medical domain using the UMLS ontology and in the general domain using the YAGO ontology. To evaluate their approach, the authors compare it to simpler baselines and prior work, primarily on intrinsic similarity and relatedness benchmarks. They utilize existing benchmarks in the medical domain and employ mechanical turkers to generate a new general-domain concept similarity and relatedness dataset, which they also intend to release. The reported results are comparable to those of prior work.

Strengths:
- The proposed joint embedding model is straightforward and reasonable. Its primary value lies in achieving a configurable middle ground between treating phrases as atomic units and considering their compositionality, as well as applying the same approach to concepts composed of several representative phrases.
- The paper presents a substantial volume of work, including model development, the creation of a new evaluation dataset, and several evaluations and analyses.

Weaknesses:
- The evaluation in this paper is limited to intrinsic tasks, mainly on similarity and relatedness datasets. As the authors acknowledge, such evaluations have limited power in predicting the utility of embeddings in extrinsic tasks. Recently, it has become more common to include at least one or two extrinsic tasks in the evaluation of embedding models.
- The similarity and relatedness evaluation datasets used in the paper are presented as recording human judgments of similarity between concepts. However, it appears that the actual judgments were made based on presenting phrases to human annotators, and therefore these datasets should be considered as phrase similarity datasets and analyzed accordingly.
- The medical concept evaluation dataset, 'mini MayoSRS', is extremely small (29 pairs), and its larger superset 'MayoSRS' is only slightly larger (101 pairs) with a relatively low human annotator agreement. The other medical concept evaluation dataset, 'UMNSRS', is more reasonable in size but is limited to concepts represented as single words, which were presented as such to human annotators. This limitation should be mentioned in the paper, as it makes the relevance of this dataset questionable with respect to representations of phrases and general concepts.
- The authors extensively fine-tune their hyperparameters on the same datasets for which they report results and compare them to prior work. This makes all the reported results and analyses questionable.
- The authors argue that their method is superior to prior work because it achieved comparable results with less manual annotation. However, this argument is not strong, as the authors also utilize large manually constructed ontologies, and the manually annotated dataset used in prior work comes from existing clinical records that did not require dedicated annotations.
- The paper lacks insightful analysis into the reported numbers. The authors treat the relation between a phrase and its component words, and a concept and its alternative phrases, as similar types of compositional relations. However, these relations are different in nature and deserve dedicated analysis. For example, an NLP analysis specific to the relation between phrases and their component words could provide valuable insights.

General Discussion:
Due to the above-mentioned weaknesses, I recommend rejecting this submission. I encourage the authors to improve their evaluation datasets and methodology before re-submitting this paper.
