

Summary: This paper introduces a method for jointly embedding concepts, phrases, and words using distant supervision from ontology mappings. The approach replaces representative phrases (from ontologies like UMLS and YAGO) with unique identifiers in unlabeled text corpora, then applies a modified skip-gram model to train embeddings for all three types of units. The authors demonstrate competitive performance on concept similarity and relatedness tasks while achieving significantly larger vocabulary coverage than existing methods.

Strengths:
1. The paper addresses a meaningful gap in semantic representation: capturing non-compositional phrases (e.g., "Lou Gehrig's disease") that cannot be decomposed into their constituent words. The authors effectively demonstrate that their approach preserves semantic relationships between concepts and their multiple textual forms, which is particularly valuable for biomedical and real-world entity representation.

2. The method requires no human annotation, making it significantly more scalable than prior approaches that rely on manual concept tagging or ontology structure. The authors show their embeddings achieve over 3x vocabulary coverage compared to existing methods while maintaining competitive performance on standard similarity and relatedness tasks.

3. The authors contribute two novel datasets for evaluating similarity and relatedness between real-world entities, which fills a significant gap in the literature. Their careful crowdsourcing methodology and analysis of inter-annotator agreement (Fleiss' κ = 0.24) provide valuable resources for future work in this area.

Weaknesses:
1. The core contribution lacks sufficient novelty. The approach is essentially a minor adaptation of the skip-gram model with negative sampling (word2vec), and the authors fail to clearly articulate how their method differs from Wang et al. (2014a) or other prior work that jointly embeds entities and words. The claimed novelty is overstated relative to the technical contribution.

2. The experimental results undermine the paper's main claim of superiority. On the MayoSRS and Mini Mayo SRS datasets (Table 4), the Choi baseline (which requires human annotations) substantially outperforms the proposed method. On the larger UMNSRS datasets, the authors' method is at best competitive with simpler alternatives like CHIU-2, and the concept embeddings are consistently outperformed by phrase-based approximations (APPROXPHRco) in Table 5.

3. The paper's terminology is misleading regarding "distant supervision." The approach doesn't involve traditional distant supervision (where a phrase labels a concept), but rather uses phrase occurrences as context for training word embeddings. This misrepresentation of the methodology confuses the contribution.

4. The hyperparameter settings in Table 3 reveal a fundamental issue with the proposed model: the β and η parameters (which control compositionality) are set to 0 in most cases. This suggests the authors' method doesn't actually leverage the compositional aspects of phrases, contradicting their stated goal and undermining their core claim about handling non-compositional phrases.

Questions to Authors:
1. The paper claims the model jointly embeds concepts, phrases, and words, yet Table 5 shows that APPROXPHRco (which uses phrase embeddings to approximate concepts) outperforms the direct concept embeddings (CONCEPTco). How does this align with your claim that concept embeddings preserve semantic relationships? Why would a simpler approximation outperform the more complex model?

2. Your method requires mapping between concepts and phrases from ontologies, yet Table 5 demonstrates that phrase-based approximations (APPROXPHRco) perform best. Could you clarify why the concept embeddings are less effective than the phrase embeddings for similarity tasks, and why the concept ontology doesn't provide additional value beyond the phrase mappings?

3. The paper describes β as controlling "compositionality of phrases by words," but the optimal values (Table 3) are 0 for most corpora. This suggests the model doesn't actually use compositional information. How do you reconcile this with your claim that the model handles both compositional and non-compositional phrases?
