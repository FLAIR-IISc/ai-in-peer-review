

Summary: The paper proposes a weakly-supervised method for jointly embedding concepts, phrases, and words using ontology-mapped phrases as distant supervision, eliminating the need for manual concept annotation. The main contributions are: (1) A novel framework that jointly embeds concepts, phrases, and words without requiring manual annotation of concepts; (2) A new dataset of human judgments for similarity and relatedness between real-world entities (people, places, organizations); and (3) Experimental validation demonstrating competitive performance on concept similarity and relatedness tasks compared to prior methods requiring human annotation.

Strengths:
1. The model provides a meaningful middle ground between treating phrases as atomic units versus fully decomposing them into component words. This configurable approach to handling phrase compositionality is a valuable contribution to the field.
2. The work demonstrates substantial effort with a well-developed model, a new evaluation dataset, and thorough analysis of embedding properties, including compositionality and semantic clustering.
3. The method achieves competitive performance while offering significant vocabulary coverage (more than 3x larger than prior approaches) without requiring additional manual annotation.

Weaknesses:
1. The evaluation is limited exclusively to intrinsic similarity/relatedness tasks, which have known limitations in predicting performance on downstream extrinsic applications. The field increasingly expects at least one extrinsic task to validate embedding utility, which is notably absent here.
2. The evaluation datasets should be interpreted as phrase similarity rather than concept similarity, since human judgments were based on textual phrases rather than abstract concepts. This misalignment undermines the claimed evaluation of concept embeddings.
3. The medical evaluation datasets (MayoSRS with 101 pairs and Mini MayoSRS with 29 pairs) are too small and exhibit low inter-annotator agreement (Fleiss' κ = 0.24), making them unreliable for evaluation. The UMNSRS dataset is problematic as it evaluates only single-word representations of concepts, not phrase-based representations.
4. Hyperparameters are tuned on the very same evaluation datasets used for reporting results, which invalidates the evaluation as it constitutes test set leakage. The paper fails to properly separate training, development, and test sets.
5. The claim that the method "requires no human corpus annotation" is misleading. While it avoids manual concept annotation, the approach relies on manually-constructed ontologies (UMLS and YAGO), and prior work used existing clinical records that required no additional annotation effort.

Questions to Authors:
1. How do you justify the claim that your method requires "no human corpus annotation" when the ontologies (UMLS and YAGO) used in your approach are themselves manually constructed?
2. Could you provide details on specific extrinsic tasks that would better validate the utility of your embeddings in downstream applications?
3. Given the small size and low agreement of the MayoSRS datasets, how would you address the evaluation limitations in future work?

Additional Comments:
- Line 202: Clarify how phrase overlaps are handled during preprocessing.
- Line 220: The terminology "negative sampling matrix" is confusing as these embeddings are used for both positive and negative instances.
- Line 250: The description of "the observed phrase just completed" is unclear regarding how words are trained in the joint model.
- Table 3: The PM+CL hyperparameter settings differ significantly from PM and CL alone, suggesting the combined corpus requires different configuration. This deserves more analysis.
- Line 496: Specify which task was used for hyperparameter tuning (this is critical for reproducibility).

The paper makes a modest contribution to the field of semantic embeddings with a reasonable approach, but the evaluation methodology severely undermines the significance of the results. The claimed advantages over prior work are not supported by the evidence provided, particularly regarding the annotation effort. The paper would benefit significantly from addressing the evaluation limitations and including extrinsic tasks before reconsideration.
