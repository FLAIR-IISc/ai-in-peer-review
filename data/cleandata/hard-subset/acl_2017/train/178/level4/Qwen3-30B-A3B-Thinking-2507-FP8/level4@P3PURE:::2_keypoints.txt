- Paper proposes mapping concepts to associated phrases, treating phrases as atomic symbols and training word/phrase/concept embeddings via an adapted skip-gram; evaluated on UMLS and Yago.  
- Addresses non-compositional phrases and presents a plausible phrase-embedding approach.  
- Results are competitive or superior on some concept similarity tasks; released software is useful for biomedical NLP.  
- Methodological novelty is limited — essentially a minor adaptation of skip-gram.  
- Full model does not consistently outperform simpler baselines; Choi baseline beats it on Mayo datasets and it is only competitive on UMNSRS.  
- Calling phrase occurrences “distant supervision” is misleading since phrases are used as context, not true supervisory labels.  
- Notation introduced in Section 3.2 (e.g., E_W) is unused elsewhere, indicating presentation issues.  
- Using a single global β to control compositionality is problematic; cross-validated β values are inconsistent and β=0 for some corpora negates compositionality.  
- Experimental details for Table 4 are unclear: how concept similarity is computed from phrase/word vectors given many-to-many concept-phrase mappings.  
- Table 5 shows approximating concepts by phrase averages outperforms the model, questioning the necessity of the ontology (seed phrases alone might suffice).
