

Summary: This paper introduces a model for embedding words, phrases, and concepts into vector spaces. It employs an ontology of concepts, each mapped to associated phrases extracted from text corpora and treated as atomic units. The model then applies an adaptation of the skip-gram method to train embeddings for words, these atomic phrases, and their associated concepts. The approach is evaluated on concept similarity and relatedness tasks using UMLS and Yago as ontological resources.

Strengths:  
The paper addresses the key challenge that non-lexically similar phrases may exhibit semantic proximity and that not all phrases are compositional. It proposes a plausible framework for training phrase embeddings, which demonstrate competitive or superior performance in identifying concept similarity compared to existing methods. The released software holds potential utility for biomedical NLP researchers.

Weaknesses:  
The model lacks significant novelty, amounting to a minor adaptation of skip-gram. Furthermore, the full model does not consistently outperform simpler baselines in the results: on the Mayo datasets (Table 4), the Choi baseline is significantly superior, and a similar pattern persists in Table 6. On the larger UMNSRS dataset, the proposed model is at best competitive with prior simpler models (Chiu).

Major Concerns:  
The paper claims to use known phrases as "distant supervision" for embedding training, but this terminology is misleading. The method treats phrase occurrences as context for word embeddings, not as traditional supervision (e.g., for concept identification). The notation introduced in Section 3.2 (e.g., \(E_W\)) is never utilized subsequently.  
The use of \(\beta\) to control phrase compositionality is problematic. It implies a single global constant dictates compositionality for all phrases, yet cross-validated \(\beta\) values in Table 3 are inconsistent: \(\beta = 0\) for PM+CL and WikiNYT, effectively negating compositionality.  
The experimental setup for Table 4 requires clarification. The paper states labels indicate concept similarity/relatedness, but with a many-to-many concept-phrases mapping, it is unclear how phrase/word vectors (not concept vectors) are used to compute similarities.  
In Table 5, the phrase-approximation method (approximating concepts via phrase averages) achieves the best results, raising the question of why the concept ontology is necessary. A seed set of phrases could yield equivalent outcomes without the ontology.
