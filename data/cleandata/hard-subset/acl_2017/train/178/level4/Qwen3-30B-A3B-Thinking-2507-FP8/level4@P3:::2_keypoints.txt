- Summary: Proposes joint embeddings of concepts, phrases, and words via ontology mappings as distant supervision, replacing phrase instances with IDs and training a modified skip-gram; evaluated on UMLS and YAGO claiming competitive results without human annotation.

- Contribution: Practical method to learn concept embeddings from unlabeled text that avoids human annotation.

- Contribution: New dataset of similarity and relatedness judgments for real-world entities.

- Contribution: Software release (cui2vec) useful for biomedical NLP.

- Strength: Addresses representing non-compositional concept phrases and preserves concept–phrase semantic relationships better than simple phrase averaging.

- Strength: Requires no human annotation and achieves competitive similarity/relatedness performance with >3x vocabulary coverage versus prior methods.

- Strength: Provides useful resources (dataset and cui2vec) that fill gaps for real-world entity similarity evaluation.

- Weakness: Method is mainly a minor skip-gram adaptation (β and η tweaks), not a fundamentally novel model; simpler approximations often match or beat it.

- Weakness: Experimental results are inconsistent with claims — baselines (e.g., Choi) outperform on small datasets and proposed method is only competitive on larger ones.

- Weakness: Calling the approach “distant supervision” is misleading since training uses phrase occurrences as contextual signals rather than traditional supervision.

- Weakness: Experimental inconsistencies (APPROXPHRco outperforming CONCEPTco; β=0 for most corpora) cast doubt on the need for a concept embedding layer and the emphasis on compositionality.

- Question: Why learn concept embeddings when APPROXPHRco (phrase-averaged approximations) consistently outperforms your concept embeddings — what advantage does the concept layer provide?

- Question: Please clarify the supervision signal and justify labeling the method as “distant supervision” versus standard context-based training.

- Question: Why does your method underperform on smaller Mayo datasets but perform competitively on larger UMNSRS datasets — is it mainly suited to large-scale corpora?

- Question: The paper claims ontology structure is reflected in embeddings, but figures show inconsistent proximity of concepts to representative phrases — can you clarify this relationship?
