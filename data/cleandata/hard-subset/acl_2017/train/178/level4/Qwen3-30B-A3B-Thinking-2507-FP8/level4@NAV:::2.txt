

Review Summary: This paper introduces a model for embedding words, phrases, and concepts into vector spaces by leveraging an ontology where each concept maps to associated phrases. These phrases, identified in text corpora and treated as atomic units, serve as the basis for training embeddings using an approach essentially derived from skip-gram. The embeddings cover words, phrases, and their associated concepts. Evaluation focuses on concept similarity and relatedness tasks using UMLS and Yago as ontological backbones.

Strengths:
The paper addresses the critical challenge that non-lexically similar phrases may exhibit semantic proximity, and not all phrases are compositional. It proposes a plausible model for training phrase embeddings, with results demonstrating competitive or superior performance in concept similarity identification. The accompanying software release holds potential utility for biomedical NLP researchers.

Weaknesses:
The model lacks significant novelty, representing primarily a minor adaptation of skip-gram. Furthermore, the full model presented does not consistently yield the best results; on the Mayo datasets (Table 4), the Choi baseline substantially outperforms it, with a similar trend observed in Table 6. On the larger UMNSRS dataset, the proposed model is at best comparable to simpler prior methods (Chiu).

General Discussion:
The paper describes using known phrases as "distant supervision" for embedding training, but the terminology is misleading. The process involves phrase occurrences providing context for word embedding training, which does not constitute traditional supervision (e.g., for concept identification or predictive tasks). The notation introduced in Section 3.2 (e.g., E_W) is unused thereafter. The parameter β, used to control phrase compositionality, is unexpected: cross-validated values in Table 3 are unusually low (zero for PM+CL and WikiNYT), effectively rejecting compositionality for those datasets. The experimental setup for Table 4 requires clarification—given the many-to-many concept-phrases mapping, it is unclear how phrase/word vectors are employed to compute concept similarities, as concept vectors should be the direct basis. In Table 5, the approximate phr method (approximating concepts via phrase averages) achieves the highest performance, undermining the necessity of the ontology, as a seed set of phrases alone could yield equivalent results.
