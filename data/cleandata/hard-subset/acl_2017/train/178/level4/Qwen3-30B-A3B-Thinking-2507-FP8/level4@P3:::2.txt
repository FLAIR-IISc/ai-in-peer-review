

Summary: This paper proposes a method for jointly embedding concepts, phrases, and words using ontology mappings as distant supervision. The authors replace phrase instances in unlabeled corpora with unique identifiers and train embeddings using a modified skip-gram model. They evaluate their approach on biomedical concepts (UMLS) and general-domain entities (YAGO), claiming competitive performance on concept similarity and relatedness tasks without human annotation.

Main contributions (as assessed by reviewer):
1. A practical approach for learning concept embeddings from unlabeled text that avoids expensive human annotation
2. A novel dataset of similarity and relatedness judgments for real-world entities
3. A software implementation (cui2vec) that could benefit biomedical NLP research

Strengths:
1. The paper addresses a meaningful problem: representing concepts with multiple non-compositional textual forms (e.g., "the Big Apple" and "New York City" both refer to the same location). The authors demonstrate that their approach preserves semantic relationships between concepts and their representative phrases better than simple phrase averaging, which is a significant contribution to the field.

2. The method requires no human annotation, which is a major advantage over prior work that relies on manually tagged corpora. The authors show their embeddings achieve competitive performance on concept similarity and relatedness tasks while demonstrating more than 3x vocabulary coverage compared to prior methods, making it particularly valuable for large-scale applications.

3. The authors provide a useful software implementation (cui2vec) and a novel dataset of similarity and relatedness judgments for real-world entities, both of which will benefit the community. The dataset fills an important gap in the literature, as the authors correctly note there are no comparable similarity or relatedness datasets for real-world entities.

Weaknesses:
1. The core method is a minor adaptation of skip-gram, not a fundamentally novel contribution. The authors introduce β and η parameters to control compositionality, but this is essentially a tweak to an existing model rather than a novel approach. The most competitive results (Tables 4-5) are achieved by simpler approximations like APPROXPHRco (concept embeddings approximated by average of phrase embeddings), suggesting the joint embedding framework may not be necessary.

2. The experimental results are inconsistent with the paper's claims. On the Mayo SRS and Mini Mayo SRS datasets (smaller biomedical datasets), the Choi baseline significantly outperforms the proposed method (Table 4). On the larger UMNSRS datasets, the proposed method is at best competitive with simpler approaches like CHIU-2, not demonstrably superior. This undermines the main claim of the paper.

3. The "distant supervision" terminology is misleading. The approach doesn't involve supervision in the traditional sense (e.g., predicting concept labels from context), but rather uses phrase occurrences as contextual signals. The authors use the phrase occurrences to train embeddings, which is not supervision but rather a form of contextual training. This terminology confusion weakens the paper's theoretical contribution.

4. The experimental setup has inconsistencies. The paper claims to evaluate concept embeddings, but Table 5 shows that APPROXPHRco (using phrase embeddings to approximate concepts) outperforms CONCEPTco (the proposed concept embeddings). This raises questions about the necessity of the concept embedding layer. Additionally, the hyperparameters in Table 3 show β=0 for most corpora, suggesting compositionality isn't important, yet the paper emphasizes it as a key contribution.

Questions to Authors:
1. Given that APPROXPHRco (using phrase embeddings to approximate concepts) consistently outperforms the proposed concept embeddings (Tables 4-5), what is the specific advantage of learning concept embeddings instead of simply using phrase embeddings? Why not skip the concept embedding layer entirely?

2. The paper claims to use "distant supervision," but the training procedure is essentially a modified skip-gram model. Can you clarify the supervision signal in your approach and why it's considered "distant supervision" rather than standard context-based training?

3. In Table 4, the Choi baseline significantly outperforms your method on the Mayo datasets. Could you provide more analysis of why your method performs worse on these smaller datasets while being competitive on larger ones? Does this suggest your approach is better suited for large-scale applications?

4. The paper states that "the source ontology structure is reflected in the organization of our embeddings" (Section 1). However, Figures 3-4 suggest that concepts are not consistently closer to their representative phrases than to other phrases. Can you clarify the relationship between ontology structure and embedding organization?
