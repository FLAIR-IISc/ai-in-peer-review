

The paper proposes a method for jointly embedding concepts, phrases, and words using distant supervision from ontology mappings. The approach replaces phrases corresponding to concepts in the training corpus with unique identifiers, creating a "tagged" version of the corpus that's used alongside the original for training. The authors evaluate their method on biomedical concepts from UMLS and general-domain entities from YAGO, using both biomedical and general-domain corpora.

While the technical approach is sound and well-executed, the novelty is limited. The method is essentially an adaptation of word2vec to incorporate structured knowledge from ontologies, which is not particularly innovative. The evaluation is confined to the biomedical domain, which restricts the broader applicability of the findings.

The most significant contribution appears to be the novel dataset for evaluating similarity and relatedness of real-world entities, which the authors created using Mechanical Turk. This dataset could be a valuable resource for future work, but the paper doesn't sufficiently discuss its potential value or how it might be used in other contexts.

There's a minor technical issue with Equation 8, which defines the MAP calculation. The authors incorrectly frame this as a probability, but the equation actually computes precision at a given position in the ranking. The explanation should be revised to remove the probability reference.

Additionally, there's a grammatical error on line 556: "most concepts has" should be "most concepts have."

Overall, the paper is well-written, but the contribution is modest. The technique itself is a natural extension of existing work, and the results are competitive with but not clearly superior to prior methods that require human annotation. The creation of the new dataset represents the most promising aspect of the work, and with more discussion of its potential value, this could be a more substantial contribution. The authors should consider emphasizing this resource more prominently in their presentation.
