

**Summary**  
This paper proposes a weakly-supervised method for jointly learning embeddings for concepts, phrases, and words from unlabeled text corpora, using representative phrases for ontology concepts as distant supervision. The authors apply their approach to biomedical concepts (UMLS) and general-domain entities (YAGO), demonstrating competitive performance on concept similarity and relatedness tasks without human annotation. They also introduce a novel dataset of similarity and relatedness judgments for real-world entities (people, places, organizations), which is a significant contribution to the field.

**Main Contributions**  
1. The most valuable contribution is the novel dataset of human-annotated similarity and relatedness scores for real-world entities, filling a critical gap in evaluation resources for entity-level semantic modeling.  
2. The method effectively leverages ontology structure to enable weakly-supervised joint embedding of concepts, phrases, and words, eliminating the need for manual corpus annotation.  
3. The experimental demonstration that this approach achieves competitive performance with existing methods requiring extensive human annotation, while scaling to substantially larger vocabularies.

**Strengths**  
1. The paper makes a substantial contribution through its novel evaluation dataset for entity-level similarity and relatedness. This resource addresses a clear gap in the literature, as prior work has focused primarily on word-level semantic evaluation. The dataset's creation process is well-documented, with appropriate validation procedures and inter-annotator agreement metrics. This dataset alone would be a valuable addition to the field.  
2. The method is well-motivated and effectively leverages existing techniques (word2vec) with a reasonable extension to incorporate structured knowledge. The approach to using phrase mappings as distant supervision is natural and avoids the need for expensive human annotation, which is particularly valuable for large-scale applications.  
3. The evaluation is thorough, with comprehensive comparisons against multiple baselines across biomedical and general-domain settings. The authors provide detailed analysis of embedding compositionality and semantic clustering, which supports their claim that their method captures meaningful semantic relationships.

**Weaknesses**  
1. The core technical contribution is not sufficiently novel. The method essentially adapts word2vec for joint embedding with minor modifications (e.g., using phrase identifiers as tokens and concept averaging), building directly on existing approaches like Wang et al. (2014a) and others. The authors fail to clearly articulate how their method differs from or improves upon prior work that also jointly embeds knowledge base entities and words.  
2. The evaluation scope is limited to biomedical and general-domain entities, with insufficient discussion of broader applicability. The paper does not explore whether the method would work effectively for other domains or ontologies, which limits the perceived significance of the contribution.  
3. The paper overstates the novelty of their approach by claiming to "jointly embed" concepts, phrases, and words as a primary contribution, while the actual innovation is minimal compared to existing work. The evaluation does not convincingly demonstrate that this joint embedding approach yields substantially better results than simpler alternatives like averaging phrase embeddings (APPROXPHRco), which outperforms their direct concept embeddings on the entity similarity task.

**Questions to Authors**  
1. How does your approach specifically differ from Wang et al. (2014a), which also jointly embeds knowledge base entities and words? What advantages does your method offer over theirs, particularly given their use of knowledge graph structure?  
2. Your results show that APPROXPHRco (averaging phrase embeddings) outperforms your direct concept embeddings (CONCEPTco) on the real-world entity datasets. Why does this occur, and how does it impact your claim that joint embedding is beneficial?  
3. The paper mentions a slight mismatch between UMLS (abstract biomedical concepts) and YAGO (concrete entities), but does not explore how this affects performance. Could you provide more insight into how the method handles this distinction, and whether it would work well for more abstract concepts in general domains?

**Additional Comments**  
The draft contains grammatical errors (e.g., "most concepts has" should be "most concepts have" on line 556) and the technical explanation of Equation 8 should be revised to clarify that it defines a precision metric, not a probability. The paper's presentation is generally clear, but the novelty claims require more careful justification to align with the actual technical contribution. The resource contribution (novel dataset) is substantial and would be a valuable addition to the field even if the method itself were less novel.
