

Summary: This paper introduces a method for jointly embedding concepts, phrases, and words into a shared vector space. The approach utilizes an ontology where each concept is mapped to multiple representative phrases, which are then treated as atomic units within an unlabeled text corpus. The authors adapt the skip-gram model with negative sampling to simultaneously learn embeddings for words, phrases, and concepts, using the phrases as distant supervision signals. The method is evaluated on concept similarity and relatedness tasks using UMLS for biomedical concepts and YAGO for general-domain entities.

Strengths: The paper addresses an important gap in semantic modeling by recognizing that semantically related concepts may not share lexical overlap and that not all phrases are compositional. The proposed model provides a practical solution for learning embeddings without requiring manual annotations. The results demonstrate competitive performance on similarity and relatedness tasks, showing that the method can effectively capture semantic relationships between concepts. The release of the cui2vec software implementation with the paper will be valuable for researchers in biomedical NLP.

Weaknesses: The core contribution of the model is largely incremental, as it represents a minor adaptation of the standard skip-gram approach rather than a fundamentally novel technique. The full model with concept embeddings does not consistently outperform simpler alternatives, as evidenced by Table 4 where the Choi baseline substantially outperforms the proposed method on the MayoSRS and Mini Mayo SRS datasets. The model's performance on larger datasets (UMNSRS) is only competitive with simpler baselines like CHIU-2, suggesting the added complexity may not be justified. The authors' claims about the importance of concept embeddings are weakened by the fact that the APPROXPHRco method (approximating concepts through phrase embeddings) achieves the highest performance across most datasets.

Questions to Authors: The paper describes using phrases as distant supervision, but it's unclear what the supervisory signal actually is. If phrases are simply treated as tokens in the training corpus, this doesn't constitute traditional supervision for concept identification. Could the authors clarify the role of "supervision" in this context? The notation introduced in Section 3.2 (EW, EP, EC) is not used elsewhere in the paper—could the authors explain why this notation was introduced? The β hyperparameter values in Table 3 (0 for PM+CL and WikiNYT) suggest that compositionality is not beneficial, yet the model incorporates this parameter—why include it if it's consistently set to zero? In Table 5, the APPROXPHRco method (using phrase embeddings to approximate concepts) achieves the best results, raising questions about the necessity of concept embeddings versus simply using phrase embeddings directly.
