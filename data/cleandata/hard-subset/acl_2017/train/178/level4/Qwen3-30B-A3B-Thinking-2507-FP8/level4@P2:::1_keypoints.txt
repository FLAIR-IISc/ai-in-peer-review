- Proposes a weakly-supervised method to jointly learn embeddings for concepts, phrases, and words using representative phrases as distant supervision.  
- Applies the method to biomedical (UMLS) and general-domain (YAGO) concepts and shows competitive performance without human annotation.  
- Introduces a novel human-annotated dataset of similarity and relatedness judgments for real-world entities (people, places, organizations).  

- Key contribution: the new entity-level similarity/relatedness dataset filling an evaluation gap.  
- Key contribution: leveraging ontology structure to enable weak supervision for joint embeddings.  
- Key contribution: competitive results at scale compared to methods requiring human annotation.  

- Strength: the dataset is well-documented with validation and inter-annotator agreement, and is a valuable resource.  
- Strength: method is well-motivated, extends word2vec naturally, and avoids expensive manual annotation using phrase mappings.  
- Strength: thorough evaluation with multiple baselines and detailed analyses of compositionality and clustering.  

- Weakness: technical novelty is limited—method largely adapts word2vec with minor modifications and overlaps with prior work (e.g., Wang et al. 2014a).  
- Weakness: limited evaluation scope (only biomedical and general-domain), with little discussion of broader applicability to other ontologies/domains.  
- Weakness: novelty claims are overstated; simpler baselines (APPROXPHRco) sometimes outperform the proposed direct concept embeddings (CONCEPTco).  

- Question: how does this approach specifically differ from Wang et al. (2014a) and what advantages does it offer?  
- Question: why does APPROXPHRco outperform CONCEPTco on real-world entity datasets, and what does that imply about joint embedding benefits?  
- Question: how does the method handle the abstract UMLS concepts versus concrete YAGO entities, and would it work for abstract concepts in other domains?  

- Additional: paper contains grammatical errors that need correction.  
- Additional: Equation 8 should be clarified as a precision metric rather than a probability.  
- Additional: presentation is clear but novelty claims need stronger justification; the dataset remains a substantial contribution.
