

The authors present a method for jointly embedding words, phrases, and concepts using plain text corpora and a manually-constructed ontology where concepts are represented by one or more phrases. They apply this method in the medical domain (using UMLS ontology) and general domain (using YAGO ontology). Evaluation compares against simpler baselines and prior work, primarily on intrinsic similarity and relatedness benchmarks. For the medical domain, they use existing benchmarks; for the general domain, they generate a new human-annotated concept similarity/relatedness dataset via Mechanical Turk (to be released). Results are comparable to prior work.  

Strengths:  
- The joint embedding model is straightforward and logically bridges treating phrases as atomic units versus considering their compositionality, offering a configurable middle ground. The same principle applies to concepts represented by multiple phrases.  
- The paper demonstrates substantial work, including model development, a new evaluation dataset, and comprehensive evaluations/analyses.  

Weaknesses:  
- Evaluation is limited to intrinsic tasks (similarity/relatedness), which have limited predictive power for extrinsic task utility—a standard practice now requires at least one extrinsic task.  
- The similarity/relatedness datasets record human judgments based on phrases presented to annotators, making them phrase similarity datasets, not concept similarity datasets.  
- The medical evaluation dataset "mini MayoSRS" is extremely small (29 pairs), and its superset "MayoSRS" is only slightly larger (101 pairs) with low inter-annotator agreement. The "UMNSRS" dataset, while larger, evaluates single-word concepts (not phrases/concepts), undermining its relevance to the paper’s focus.  
- Hyperparameters are extensively fine-tuned on the evaluation datasets themselves, invalidating reported results and comparisons.  
- The claim that their method avoids manual annotation is weak: they rely on large manually-constructed ontologies, and prior work used existing clinical records (no dedicated annotation).  
- The paper lacks deeper insights into the model’s behavior. The compositional relationship between phrases and their component words differs fundamentally from concepts and their representative phrases, warranting dedicated analysis (e.g., phrase headwords’ role). Investigation of hyperparameters controlling the atomic/compositional tradeoff is also missing.  

General Discussion:  
Due to these critical weaknesses, I recommend rejection. Authors should substantially improve evaluation methodology and datasets before resubmission.
