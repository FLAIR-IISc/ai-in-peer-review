

Summary: The authors propose a method for jointly embedding words, phrases, and concepts using unlabeled text corpora and manually constructed ontologies that map concepts to multiple representative phrases. The approach is evaluated in the medical domain (UMLS) and general domain (YAGO), with comparisons against simpler baselines and prior work using intrinsic similarity and relatedness benchmarks. The authors also create a new general-domain evaluation dataset through crowdsourcing.

Main Contributions:
- The proposed model provides a principled approach for jointly embedding concepts, phrases, and words by leveraging the relationship between concepts and their representative phrases. Its key innovation is a configurable tradeoff between treating phrases as atomic units versus their compositional structure, which is similarly applied to concepts represented by multiple phrases.
- The work demonstrates substantial effort through comprehensive model development, creation of a novel evaluation dataset for general-domain entity similarity and relatedness, and thorough evaluation across multiple domains and tasks.

Strengths:
- The method's core innovation lies in its configurable tradeoff between atomic and compositional views of phrases and concepts, which is well-motivated and implemented.
- The paper makes a valuable contribution through the creation of a new general-domain entity similarity and relatedness dataset, which fills an important gap in the literature.

Weaknesses:
- The evaluation relies exclusively on intrinsic similarity and relatedness tasks, which have limited predictive power for extrinsic downstream applications. Modern embedding evaluation standards increasingly require at least one extrinsic task to validate practical utility.
- The evaluation datasets are presented as concept similarity benchmarks, but the human judgments were based on phrases rather than concepts, making them more appropriately classified as phrase similarity datasets. This misalignment needs clarification.
- The medical domain evaluation includes extremely small datasets (mini MayoSRS with 29 pairs and MayoSRS with 101 pairs), both with low inter-annotator agreement. The UMNSRS dataset, while larger, is limited to concepts representable as single words, undermining its relevance for phrase-based representations.
- The authors extensively tune hyperparameters on the evaluation datasets themselves, creating a significant risk of overfitting and invalidating the reported performance comparisons.
- The claim that their method requires less manual annotation than prior work is misleading, as it relies on substantial manually constructed ontologies (UMLS and YAGO), and prior work's annotated data comes from existing clinical records without dedicated annotation efforts.
- The paper lacks deeper analysis of the compositional relationships between phrases and words versus concepts and phrases, which are fundamentally different relationships. A more thorough investigation of the hyperparameters controlling the tradeoff between atomic and compositional views would strengthen the work.

Questions to Authors:
- Please clarify how phrase overlaps are handled during preprocessing (Line 202).
- Specify which task was used for hyperparameter tuning (Line 496).
- Explain the training procedure for words in the joint model (Line 250).
