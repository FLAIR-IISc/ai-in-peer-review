

The paper proposes an extension to word embedding techniques that incorporates phrase and concept representations by leveraging ontology-based grouping. Specifically, the method assigns a unique identifier to all phrases, words, and concepts denoting the same entity within a biomedical ontology. It then replaces these instances with the identifier in the training corpus to create a "tagged" corpus, which is appended to the original corpus for training. The approach is applied within the biomedical domain, utilizing domain-specific corpora and ontologies. A notable contribution is the creation of a new test dataset for evaluating word similarity and relatedness among real-world biomedical entities, which appears novel.

The paper is well-written and the technique is conceptually straightforward. However, the core contribution is modest: the method extends existing embedding approaches without introducing significant theoretical or empirical innovations. The evaluation scope is limited to the biomedical domain, which restricts broader applicability and generalizability. While the domain focus is reasonable for the application, the paper would benefit from more discussion on how the method could be adapted to other domains or why the biomedical context is uniquely suited to this approach.

The most significant potential contribution lies in the newly generated test dataset for entity similarity evaluation. This resource could be valuable for the community, yet the paper treats it as a secondary outcome rather than a central contribution. A more detailed description of the dataset's construction, coverage, and potential utility—alongside comparative analysis with existing benchmarks—would substantially strengthen the paper. As currently presented, the dataset's novelty and value are underemphasized.

Regarding technical details, Equation 8 (MAP calculation) requires clarification. The authors aim to define a mean average precision metric, but the formulation as presented does not constitute a probability. The expression sums over the entire vocabulary, which is mathematically infeasible for infinite vocabularies and misrepresents the metric. The authors should revise the explanation to clarify that this is a ranking-based score (e.g., "the normalized score for the target word") rather than a probability, and consider introducing a practical cutoff (e.g., top-k ranking) to align with standard MAP implementations.

In summary, while the paper presents a technically sound but incremental method with a promising dataset, its impact is constrained by narrow evaluation scope and insufficient emphasis on the dataset's potential value. The technical clarification for Equation 8 is necessary for precision. With stronger discussion of the dataset's significance and minor revisions to the mathematical formulation, the paper could make a more compelling contribution. As is, the work is adequate but not transformative for the field.
