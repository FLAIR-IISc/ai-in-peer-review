

## Review for "A Weakly-Supervised Method for Jointly Embedding Concepts, Phrases, and Words"

**Summary and Contributions:**  
This paper introduces a weakly-supervised approach for jointly embedding concepts, phrases, and words using ontology-based distant supervision. The primary contribution is a method that leverages representative phrases from ontologies (UMLS and YAGO) as distant supervision to train embeddings without human annotation, achieving competitive performance on concept similarity and relatedness tasks while offering 3x larger vocabulary coverage than prior methods. The secondary contribution is the creation of a novel dataset for evaluating similarity and relatedness of real-world entities (people, places, organizations), addressing a gap in the literature. The tertiary contribution is demonstrating the method's scalability to large vocabularies while maintaining semantic coherence.

**Strengths:**  
1. **Practical weak supervision approach**: The method cleverly replaces phrases representing concepts with unique identifiers in the training corpus, enabling joint embedding without human annotation. This is a significant practical contribution that removes a major bottleneck in prior work requiring costly manual annotation. The authors demonstrate that their approach achieves competitive performance on standard evaluation tasks despite this weak supervision.

2. **Novel entity similarity dataset**: The creation of a new dataset for evaluating similarity and relatedness of real-world entities (with 688 pairs for both tasks) fills an important gap in the literature. Prior work has focused on words or biomedical concepts, but no comparable dataset exists for general-domain entities. This dataset represents a valuable resource for future research.

3. **Scalability and efficiency**: The method achieves 3x larger vocabulary coverage than prior approaches without additional annotation cost. The results on larger datasets (UMNSRS) show that their concept embeddings outperform most baselines, with only marginal differences from the top-performing CHOI method. This demonstrates clear practical value for applications requiring broad coverage.

**Weaknesses:**  
1. **Limited scope of evaluation**: The evaluation is confined to biomedical concepts (UMLS) and general-domain entities (YAGO), but the authors provide insufficient analysis of how the method would perform on more abstract concepts in the general domain (e.g., as addressed in BabelNet work). The paper claims the method "can model both kinds of information," but lacks evidence for abstract concepts.

2. **Marginal performance gains**: While the method outperforms most baselines on larger datasets, the performance gains are minimal (e.g., 0.01-0.03 Spearman's ρ on UMNSRS Similarity). On smaller biomedical datasets (Mayo SRS), it underperforms compared to CHOI. This suggests the contribution may be incremental rather than transformative.

3. **Insufficient discussion of limitations**: The paper claims the method "preserves the links between concepts and their representative phrases," but the evidence (Figures 3-4) shows high variance in these relationships. The authors do not adequately address why their method fails to capture semantic richness comparable to approaches that incorporate ontology structure directly (e.g., Wang et al., 2014a).

**Questions to Authors:**  
1. The paper claims the method "can model both abstract biomedical concepts and concrete real-world entities," but the evaluation focuses on concrete entities in YAGO and biomedical concepts in UMLS. How would the authors expect their approach to perform on more abstract concepts (e.g., "justice" or "freedom") in the general domain, and what modifications would be needed?

2. The authors state that "the source ontology structure is reflected in the organization of our embeddings" (Section 6), but the evidence (Figures 3-4) shows considerable variance in how concepts relate to phrases and words. Can the authors provide additional evidence or analysis to substantiate this claim beyond the marginal MAP scores in Table 6?

3. While the paper claims to require "no human corpus annotation," it relies on ontology mappings between concepts and phrases. How does this differ from Wang et al. (2014a), which also uses ontology mappings but requires human annotation via Wikipedia links? The paper should clarify this distinction.

**Additional Comments:**  
The paper contains minor grammatical errors (e.g., "most concepts has" on line 556 should be "most concepts have"). The technical explanation of the MAP calculation in Equation 8 is problematic as written; the authors should clarify that this is a ranking metric rather than a probability. The hyperparameter tuning process (Section 5.2) is well-executed, but the paper could better justify why certain settings (e.g., β=0.75 for PUBMED) are optimal without providing more detailed analysis.
