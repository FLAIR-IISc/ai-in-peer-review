

Summary: This paper proposes a model for embedding words, phrases, and concepts into vector spaces using an ontology where concepts are mapped to phrases extracted from text corpora. These phrases serve as atomic units, and the model adapts the skip-gram method to jointly train embeddings for words, phrases, and their associated concepts. Evaluation focuses on concept similarity and relatedness tasks using UMLS and Yago as ontologies.

Strengths:  
The paper addresses a meaningful gap—non-compositional phrases that are semantically related despite lexical dissimilarity—by proposing a well-motivated approach to phrase embedding. The resulting embeddings demonstrate competitive or superior performance in concept similarity tasks. Additionally, the released software holds clear utility for biomedical NLP research.

Weaknesses:  
1. **Lack of novelty**: The core model constitutes a minor adaptation of skip-gram, offering limited conceptual innovation.  
2. **Suboptimal results**: The full model (Section 3) underperforms compared to baselines in key experiments. On the Mayo datasets (Table 4), the Choi baseline significantly outperforms the proposed method. A similar trend appears in Table 6. On the larger UMNSRS dataset (Table 4), the model is merely competitive with simpler baselines (e.g., Chiu).  
3. **Terminology confusion**: The paper claims to use "distant supervision" via known phrases, but the mechanism lacks traditional supervision. Each phrase occurrence provides contextual training data for word embeddings, not concept labeling—rendering the term misleading.  
4. **Unjustified parameters**: The use of a global β to control phrase compositionality is problematic. Cross-validated β values (Table 3) are unexpected: β=0 for PM+CL and WikiNYT, effectively negating compositionality. This undermines the parameter’s proposed rationale.  
5. **Ontology redundancy**: The best-performing method in Table 5 (approximate phr) approximates concepts by averaging phrase embeddings, making the ontology unnecessary. If this approach suffices, the core contribution—using concept-phrase mappings—loses justification.  
6. **Methodological gaps**: The paper states concept similarity labels are used, but with many-to-many concept-phrase mappings (Section 4.1), it is unclear how phrase/word vectors (not concept vectors) compute similarity. The notation introduced in Section 3.2 (e.g., E_W) is never reused, adding confusion.

General Discussion:  
The work’s central claim—that phrases provide "distant supervision" for concept embeddings—is misaligned with standard definitions. Supervision typically implies predictive tasks (e.g., concept classification), yet the method solely uses phrases as contextual tokens. This misnomer weakens the paper’s theoretical framing. Furthermore, the β parameter’s role is poorly justified: a single global constant cannot reasonably govern the compositionality of all phrases, especially when cross-validation yields β=0 for key datasets. Critically, the superior performance of the ontology-agnostic approximate phr method (Table 5) directly challenges the necessity of the proposed ontology framework. If phrase averaging alone achieves the best results, the core innovation—mapping concepts to phrases—becomes superfluous. The experimental design (e.g., Table 4’s similarity computation) requires clearer justification to reconcile the ontology’s role with the results.  

Overall, while the approach addresses a valid problem and yields useful embeddings for biomedical NLP, the lack of novelty, inconsistent results, and fundamental flaws in the ontology’s necessity significantly diminish the contribution. The paper would benefit from a stronger theoretical foundation, clearer terminology, and experiments that directly validate the ontology’s utility.
