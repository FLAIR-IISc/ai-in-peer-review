- Paper proposes embedding words, phrases, and concepts by mapping ontology concepts to associated phrases, treating phrases as atomic units, and training embeddings with a skip-gram–derived approach; evaluated on UMLS and YAGO concept similarity/relatedness.

- Addresses that non-lexically similar phrases can be semantically close and not all phrases are compositional.

- Proposes a plausible phrase-embedding training method with competitive or superior results on some concept similarity tasks.

- Provides a software release that could be useful for biomedical NLP researchers.

- Lacks significant novelty, largely a minor adaptation of skip-gram.

- Full model does not consistently outperform baselines; Choi baseline substantially outperforms it on Mayo datasets and similar trends appear elsewhere.

- On the larger UMNSRS dataset, the proposed model is only comparable to simpler prior methods (e.g., Chiu).

- Calling the method "distant supervision" is misleading because phrase occurrences provide context rather than true supervision for predictive tasks.

- Notation introduced in Section 3.2 (e.g., E_W) is unused afterwards, suggesting presentation issues.

- The compositionality parameter β is unexpected and often cross-validates to zero for some datasets, effectively rejecting compositionality and raising questions about its role.

- Experimental setup for Table 4 is unclear given many-to-many concept–phrase mappings and how concept similarities are computed from phrase/word vectors.

- In Table 5, the approximate concept method (averaging phrase vectors) achieves the best performance, which undermines the necessity of the ontology since a seed phrase set alone might suffice.
