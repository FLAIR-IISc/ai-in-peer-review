

Summary: This paper introduces a method for embedding words, phrases, and concepts into vector spaces using an ontology-based approach. Concepts are mapped to phrases (treated as atomic symbols), and skip-gram is adapted to jointly train embeddings for words, phrases, and concepts. Evaluation focuses on concept similarity and relatedness using UMLS and YAGO as ontologies.

Strengths:  
The paper addresses a meaningful gap—non-compositional phrases that are semantically related despite lexical differences—by proposing a plausible embedding model. Results demonstrate competitive or superior performance on concept similarity tasks compared to baselines. The released software holds practical value for biomedical NLP researchers leveraging UMLS/YAGO.

Weaknesses:  
**Lack of novelty:** The core method is a minor adaptation of skip-gram, offering insufficient innovation to justify its primary contribution.  
**Inconsistent results:** The proposed model (PM+CL) does not consistently outperform simpler baselines. In Table 4, the Choi model significantly outperforms PM+CL on Mayo datasets (e.g., 72.1 vs. 65.3 on Mayo-2). Similar trends appear in Table 6. On UMNSRS (Table 4), PM+CL is only competitive with Chiu’s simpler approach, not superior.  
**Conceptual and methodological flaws:**  
- The term "distant supervision" is misleading. The model uses phrase-concept mappings as context for training, not true supervision (e.g., no predictive task labeling concepts).  
- Section 3.2’s notation (e.g., $E_W$) is unused elsewhere, creating confusion.  
- The $\beta$ parameter (controlling phrase compositionality) exhibits striking inconsistencies. Cross-validation selects $\beta=0$ for PM+CL and WikiNYT (effectively rejecting compositionality), contradicting the model’s premise.  
- Table 4’s experimental setup is unclear: If concept-phrase mappings are many-to-many, concept vectors (not phrase/word vectors) should drive similarity scoring. The paper fails to explain how phrase vectors are aggregated or used.  
- Table 5 reveals that the *approximate phr* method (using phrase averages to represent concepts) outperforms all ontology-based approaches. This undermines the need for the ontology itself—simply starting with seed phrases would suffice.

General Discussion:  
The paper’s core claim—that phrases can be semantically related without lexical similarity—holds merit. However, the model’s execution fails to leverage this insight effectively. The $\beta$ parameter’s arbitrary selection (e.g., $\beta=0$) and the dominance of the approximate phr method (Table 5) suggest the ontology is unnecessary. If phrase averages alone yield best results, the complex concept-concept mapping adds no value. Furthermore, the "distant supervision" framing misrepresents the training process, potentially confusing readers about the model’s mechanism. The software release is a positive, but its utility is limited by the model’s methodological weaknesses.  

Overall, while the problem addressed is relevant, the proposed solution lacks novelty, exhibits inconsistent empirical performance, and contains conceptual ambiguities that weaken its contribution. The results do not sufficiently justify the added complexity of the ontology-based approach over simpler phrase-averaging methods. The paper would benefit from a clearer rationale for the ontology’s necessity, a re-evaluation of the $\beta$ parameter’s role, and a more precise description of the similarity scoring mechanism. As presented, the work offers marginal incremental progress rather than a significant advancement for the field.
