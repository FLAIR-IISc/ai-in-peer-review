

Summary: The paper proposes a weakly-supervised method for jointly embedding concepts, phrases, and words using ontology-based phrase mappings as distant supervision. The approach replaces phrase occurrences with unique identifiers in the training corpus, then trains embeddings using a modified skip-gram model. The authors evaluate on biomedical concepts from UMLS and general-domain entities from YAGO, demonstrating competitive performance on similarity and relatedness tasks with significantly larger vocabulary coverage than prior methods.

Main contributions: 
1. A novel weakly-supervised framework for jointly embedding concepts, phrases, and words without manual annotation, using ontology phrase mappings as distant supervision.
2. A new dataset of similarity and relatedness judgments for real-world entities, which addresses a clear gap in evaluation resources for entity-level semantic relationships.
3. Empirical demonstration that concept embeddings trained with this method preserve semantic relationships comparable to methods requiring manual annotation, while achieving 3x greater vocabulary coverage.

Strengths: 
1. The method provides a practical solution for concept embedding without requiring human annotation, which is a significant advantage over previous approaches that rely on expensive manual processes for concept identification.
2. The vocabulary coverage (3x larger than prior methods) is a substantial practical benefit, particularly for applications requiring comprehensive semantic representation across diverse domains.
3. The introduction of a novel dataset for real-world entity similarity and relatedness is a valuable contribution to the field, addressing a clear gap in existing evaluation resources.

Weaknesses: 
1. The evaluation is limited to the biomedical domain, which restricts the generalizability of the findings and makes it difficult to assess the method's applicability to other domains beyond medical concepts.
2. The contribution of the method itself is not particularly novel, as it extends existing word embedding techniques with a straightforward modification, and the paper doesn't sufficiently differentiate its approach from prior work like Wang et al. (2014a).
3. The technical description of the model contains a problematic definition in Equation 8, which presents a non-probabilistic measure as a probability, potentially causing confusion for readers.

Questions to Authors:
1. How does the method handle concepts that have multiple phrase representations with different semantic nuances? Could this lead to ambiguous embeddings that might impact downstream applications?
2. The paper claims 3x vocabulary coverage but doesn't provide a clear comparison to prior work in terms of absolute vocabulary size. Could the authors clarify this comparison and provide specific numbers for the vocabulary sizes of their method versus prior approaches?
3. The paper mentions "a slight mismatch between the ontologies" (UMLS vs YAGO) but doesn't discuss how this might affect the embeddings. Could the authors elaborate on this potential limitation and its implications for the model's performance?
