

Summary: This paper proposes a model for embedding words, phrases, and concepts into vector spaces using an ontology where each concept is mapped to associated phrases. These phrases, extracted from text corpora and treated as atomic symbols, are used to train embeddings via an adaptation of the skip-gram method, generating embeddings for words, phrases, and their associated concepts. The model is evaluated on concept similarity and relatedness tasks using UMLS and Yago as ontologies.  

Strengths:  
The paper addresses a key challenge: phrases lacking lexical similarity may exhibit semantic proximity, and not all phrases are compositional. It presents a plausible approach for training phrase embeddings, with results demonstrating competitive or superior performance in identifying concept similarity compared to baselines. The released software holds potential utility for biomedical NLP research.  

Weaknesses:  
The model lacks significant novelty, amounting to a minor adaptation of skip-gram. Crucially, the full model presented does not consistently outperform simpler baselines. As shown in Table 4, the Choi baseline substantially outperforms the proposed method on the Mayo datasets, with a similar trend observed in Table 6. On the larger UMNSRS dataset (Table 4), the proposed model is at best competitive with simpler prior approaches (e.g., Chiu).  

General Discussion:  
The paper claims to use "known phrases as distant supervision" to train embeddings, but the terminology is misleading. The model leverages phrase occurrences as context for word embedding training, which does not constitute supervision in the traditional sense (e.g., for concept identification or predictive tasks). The notation introduced in Section 3.2 (e.g., $E_W$) is unused elsewhere in the paper.  

The use of $\beta$ to control phrase compositionality via word-level weighting is problematic. The approach assumes a single global constant dictates compositionality for all phrases, yet cross-validated $\beta$ values in Table 3 are inconsistent: $\beta = 0$ for PM+CL and WikiNYT, effectively negating compositionality.  

The experimental setup for Table 4 requires clarification. The paper states that data labels concept similarity/relatedness, but if the concept-phrases mapping is many-to-many, it remains unclear how phrase/word vectors—not concept vectors—are used to compute similarities.  

Finally, Table 5 shows that the approximate phr method (approximating concepts via phrase averages) achieves the best performance. This undermines the necessity of the concept ontology; a seed set of phrases alone could yield equivalent results.
