

**Summary and Contributions**  
This paper proposes a weakly-supervised method for jointly embedding concepts, phrases, and words using ontology representative phrases as distant supervision. The main contributions are: (1) a novel joint embedding model that flexibly balances phrase compositionality and atomicity via hyperparameters β and η; (2) a new dataset for evaluating similarity and relatedness of real-world entities (collected via Mechanical Turk); and (3) competitive performance on concept similarity/relatedness tasks without requiring human corpus annotation. The method is validated on biomedical concepts (UMLS) and general-domain entities (YAGO).

**Strengths**  
1. The model design is conceptually sound and addresses a meaningful gap in embedding methods. By introducing β and η to control compositionality, the approach provides a principled tradeoff between treating phrases as atomic units and modeling their internal structure—a valuable contribution to the field.  
2. The new dataset for real-world entity similarity/relatedness fills a critical gap in the literature, as no such benchmark existed prior to this work. The careful data collection process (with validation questions, filtering for annotator quality) adds significant value.  
3. The method demonstrates competitive performance on established benchmarks while avoiding the need for manual annotation of training corpora. This is particularly valuable for domains where manual annotation is expensive or impractical.

**Weaknesses**  
1. The evaluation relies solely on intrinsic similarity/relatedness tasks, which are known to poorly predict performance on extrinsic downstream applications. The absence of even one extrinsic task (e.g., entity linking, relation extraction) significantly weakens the claim about the practical utility of the embeddings.  
2. The evaluation datasets mischaracterize the evaluation target. Human judgments were based on phrases (e.g., "New York City" and "Big Apple"), so the datasets should be considered *phrase* similarity datasets, not *concept* similarity. This mischaracterization invalidates the interpretation of results for concept-level embeddings.  
3. Hyperparameter tuning was performed directly on the evaluation datasets (e.g., tuning β and η on MayoSRS to maximize Spearman’s ρ), making all reported results questionable. This violates standard evaluation practices and undermines the validity of comparisons with prior work.  
4. The medical evaluation datasets suffer from fundamental issues: Mini MayoSRS is extremely small (29 pairs) and has low inter-rater agreement; UMNSRS uses single-word representations for concepts, which is inconsistent with the paper’s focus on multi-word phrases.  
5. The claim that the method "requires no human corpus annotation" is misleading. While it avoids annotating training text, it relies on manually-constructed ontologies (UMLS/YAGO), which required substantial human effort to create.

**Questions to Authors**  
1. How does the model handle overlapping phrases (e.g., "New York" and "New York City" both referring to the same concept)? The paper notes "greedy" phrase replacement but does not address this edge case.  
2. The paper states that hyperparameters were tuned on "the evaluation datasets" (Section 5.2), but it is unclear which specific tasks were used for tuning. Please clarify this for reproducibility.  
3. The model assumes phrases represent concepts, but the paper does not investigate whether phrase-level compositionality (e.g., "high blood pressure") improves concept-level embeddings. What insights did you gain about this relationship?

**Additional Comments**  
- Line 220: The negative sampling matrix should be |W| × d, not |W| × k (as stated in the paper).  
- Line 250: The description of training "the observed phrase just completed" is confusing. Clarify how words are trained in the joint model.  
- Line 388: Using a 0-100 scale for similarity judgments is unnecessarily fine-grained; a more standard 0-10 scale would be preferable.  
- Table 3: The PM+CL hyperparameters (β=0.0, η=0.0) differ significantly from those for PM or CL alone. Please explain this discrepancy.  
- Line 535: Missing reference to Table 5.  

This paper presents a promising approach with valuable contributions, but the evaluation methodology contains critical flaws that must be addressed. Without meaningful extrinsic evaluation and proper validation of the experimental setup, the claims about the method's utility remain unsubstantiated. The authors should revise the paper to address these issues before resubmission.
