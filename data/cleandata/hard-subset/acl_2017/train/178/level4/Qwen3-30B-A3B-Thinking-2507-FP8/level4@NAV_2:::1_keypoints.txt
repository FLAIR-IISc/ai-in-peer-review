- Summary: Proposes weakly-supervised joint embeddings of concepts, phrases, and words using ontology-based phrase mappings and a modified skip-gram, evaluated on UMLS and YAGO with strong similarity/relatedness performance and much larger vocabulary coverage.
- Contribution 1: Introduces a weakly-supervised framework using ontology phrase mappings as distant supervision to jointly embed concepts, phrases, and words without manual annotation.
- Contribution 2: Releases a new dataset of similarity and relatedness judgments for real-world entities to fill an evaluation gap.
- Contribution 3: Shows empirically that embeddings preserve semantic relationships comparable to supervised methods while achieving ~3x greater vocabulary coverage.
- Strength 1: Eliminates the need for costly human annotation for concept identification, making concept embedding more practical.
- Strength 2: Achieves substantially larger vocabulary coverage (3x), beneficial for broad-domain semantic applications.
- Strength 3: Provides a valuable new dataset for entity-level similarity and relatedness evaluation.
- Weakness 1: Evaluation is primarily biomedical, limiting claims about generalizability to other domains.
- Weakness 2: Methodological novelty is limited—essentially a straightforward extension of existing embedding techniques and not clearly differentiated from prior work (e.g., Wang et al. 2014a).
- Weakness 3: Technical issue in Equation 8 presenting a non-probabilistic measure as a probability, which may confuse readers.
- Question 1: How are concepts with multiple phrase variants and differing semantic nuances handled to avoid ambiguous embeddings?
- Question 2: Can the authors provide absolute vocabulary size numbers to clarify the claimed 3x coverage compared to prior work?
- Question 3: How does ontology mismatch (e.g., UMLS vs YAGO) affect embeddings and model performance, and what are the implications?
