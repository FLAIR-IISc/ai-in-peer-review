- Paper proposes jointly embedding words, phrases, and concepts by mapping ontology concepts to associated phrases and adapting skip-gram to train atomic phrase tokens.  
- Evaluation targets concept similarity and relatedness using UMLS and YAGO.

- Strength: Addresses the gap that non-lexically-similar or non-compositional phrases can be semantically close.  
- Strength: Proposed method is a plausible approach for phrase embeddings and shows competitive or superior performance on some concept similarity tasks.  
- Strength: Released software would be useful for biomedical NLP researchers.

- Weakness: Core idea is a minor adaptation of skip-gram and lacks strong novelty.  
- Weakness: Full model does not consistently beat baselines; Choi et al. outperforms the proposed method on Mayo datasets and similar trends appear elsewhere.  
- Weakness: On larger UMNSRS dataset the model is only marginally competitive with simpler baselines like Chiu et al.

- Terminology issue: Calling the approach "distant supervision" is misleading since phrase occurrences are used as context, not true supervised labels for concept prediction.  
- Clarity issue: Notation (e.g., E_W) is introduced but not used later, reducing clarity.  
- Modeling issue: The global β parameter for phrase compositionality is counterintuitive; cross-validation yielding β=0 for some datasets undermines the compositionality claim.  
- Experimental ambiguity: Table 4’s setup is unclear about how phrase/word vectors are combined for similarity when concept–phrase mappings are many-to-many, making phrase embeddings’ role ambiguous.  
- Justification issue: Table 5 shows simple phrase-averaging works best, raising the question why an ontology is needed and whether the model’s added complexity is justified.
