- Summary: Proposes ontology-based embeddings mapping concepts to phrases (atomic symbols) and adapts skip-gram to jointly train words, phrases, and concepts; evaluated on UMLS and YAGO.

- Strength: Addresses a meaningful gap—non-compositional phrases semantically related despite lexical differences—with a plausible embedding model.

- Strength: Shows competitive or superior performance on some concept similarity tasks compared to baselines.

- Strength: Released software is practically useful for biomedical NLP researchers.

- Weakness: Lack of novelty—method is a minor adaptation of skip-gram and offers limited innovation.

- Weakness: Inconsistent empirical results—PM+CL often fails to outperform simpler baselines (e.g., Choi model on Mayo datasets; mixed results on UMNSRS).

- Weakness: The term “distant supervision” is misleading; training uses phrase-concept mappings as context rather than true supervision.

- Weakness: Presentation issues—notation (e.g., E_W) is introduced but unused, causing confusion.

- Weakness: The β parameter (controls phrase compositionality) shows inconsistent selection (often β=0), contradicting the model’s premise.

- Weakness: Experimental setup unclear—paper does not explain how many-to-many concept-phrase mappings are aggregated or how similarity is scored.

- Weakness: Simple phrase-average (“approximate phr”) outperforms ontology-based approaches, calling into question the necessity of the ontology.

- General: Core claim is valid but the model fails to leverage it effectively; ontology-based complexity appears unjustified given simpler methods’ performance.

- Recommendation: Paper needs clearer rationale for the ontology, re-evaluation of β’s role, and precise description of similarity scoring; current contribution is marginal.
