

The paper proposes an extension to word embedding techniques that incorporates phrase and concept representations using ontology-based grouping. Specifically, the authors define identifier mappings for phrase-word-concept sets from a biomedical ontology, replace all instances of these elements with their identifiers in the training corpus to create a "tagged" corpus, and then concatenate this tagged corpus with the original for training. The approach is applied within the biomedical domain, leveraging domain-specific corpora and ontologies. A notable contribution is the introduction of a new test dataset for evaluating word similarity and relatedness among real-world biomedical entities, which the authors present as novel.

The paper is well-written and the methodology is logically sound, though the core technical contribution is modest. The extension to phrase/concept embeddings follows a natural pattern of leveraging external knowledge (ontology) to enrich embedding training, but it does not introduce a fundamentally new algorithmic or theoretical insight. The narrow focus on the biomedical domain limits the broader applicability of the findings, as the evaluation lacks cross-domain validation or discussion of how the approach might generalize to other domains. This restricted scope is a significant limitation for a computational linguistics venue like ACL, where broader methodological impact is typically expected.

The most compelling aspect of the work is the generated test dataset for biomedical entity similarity. This resource—created to address the scarcity of domain-specific benchmarks—represents the paper’s most valuable contribution. However, the authors provide insufficient detail on its construction, validation, and potential utility beyond the paper’s specific experiments. A deeper discussion of the dataset’s design rationale, coverage of key biomedical concepts, and its potential for future community use would substantially strengthen the paper. As currently presented, the dataset’s significance is underemphasized relative to the methodological contribution.

Regarding technical rigor, Equation 8 contains a critical error. The authors define a metric labeled as "MAP" (Mean Average Precision) but present it as a probability. This is mathematically incorrect: the expression sums over the entire vocabulary without normalization, resulting in a value that scales with vocabulary size and cannot represent a probability. The authors should revise the equation to properly define MAP (e.g., by normalizing over relevant items in the ranking) or clarify the intended metric. This is a minor issue in expression rather than implementation, but it undermines the precision of the evaluation section.

Minor corrections are also required:  
- Line 556: "most concepts has" → "most concepts have"  

Overall, the paper presents a plausible adaptation of existing embedding techniques with a useful new resource. However, the contribution is largely incremental, and the narrow domain focus reduces its impact for ACL readers. The test dataset merits more attention as the paper’s strongest element. To improve, the authors should:  
1. Recontextualize the methodological contribution within the broader landscape of knowledge-enhanced embeddings, explicitly stating its novelty (or lack thereof) relative to prior work.  
2. Expand the discussion of the test dataset, detailing its creation, validation, and potential for community adoption.  
3. Correct Equation 8 to accurately reflect the MAP calculation.  

The paper would benefit from a more critical assessment of its limitations, particularly regarding domain generalization. While the approach is technically valid and the dataset is promising, the current presentation does not sufficiently justify the work’s significance for the ACL community. With these revisions, the paper could make a more compelling case for its contribution. As is, the work is acceptable but not particularly compelling for publication in ACL.
