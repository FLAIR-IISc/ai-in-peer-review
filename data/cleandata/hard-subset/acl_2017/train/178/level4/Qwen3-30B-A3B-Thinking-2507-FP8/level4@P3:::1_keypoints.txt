- Weakly-supervised method: replaces representative phrases with unique ontology IDs to jointly embed concepts, phrases, and words without human annotation.  
- Performance and coverage: achieves competitive concept similarity/relatedness results and ~3x larger vocabulary coverage than prior methods.  
- New dataset: introduces a 688-pair general-domain entity similarity/relatedness dataset (people, places, organizations).  
- Scalability: demonstrates scalability to large vocabularies while maintaining semantic coherence.  

- Strength: practical weak supervision removes need for costly manual annotation and yields competitive results.  
- Strength: the novel entity similarity dataset fills a gap for general-domain evaluation.  
- Strength: shows clear practical value through larger coverage and strong performance on larger biomedical datasets.  

- Weakness: evaluation scope is limited—insufficient evidence for modeling abstract general-domain concepts (e.g., justice).  
- Weakness: marginal performance gains over top baselines (small Spearman ρ improvements; underperforms on some smaller datasets).  
- Weakness: insufficient discussion/analysis of limitations and variance in preserving concept–phrase links compared to ontology-structure methods.  

- Q1: How would the approach handle abstract general-domain concepts and what modifications would be needed?  
- Q2: Can the authors provide stronger evidence that ontology structure is reflected in the embeddings beyond marginal MAP scores and Figures 3–4?  
- Q3: Clarify the distinction between “no human corpus annotation” and prior work (e.g., Wang et al., 2014a) that uses ontology mappings and human-annotated Wikipedia links.  

- Additional: contains minor grammatical errors; Equation 8’s MAP explanation is misleading and should be clarified as a ranking metric; hyperparameter choices (e.g., β=0.75) need better justification.
