Summary: Introduces a method that replaces ontology phrases with IDs in corpora and trains joint embeddings for concepts, phrases, and words via a modified skip-gram, achieving larger vocabulary coverage and competitive similarity/relatedness performance.

Strength 1: Effectively captures non-compositional phrases and preserves relationships between concepts and their multiple textual forms, useful for biomedical and entity representation.

Strength 2: Requires no human annotation, is highly scalable, and achieves over 3x vocabulary coverage compared to prior methods while maintaining competitive task performance.

Strength 3: Provides two novel, carefully crowdsourced datasets for entity similarity/relatedness with documented inter-annotator agreement (Fleiss' κ = 0.24).

Weakness 1: Technical novelty is limited—method is a minor adaptation of skip-gram and the distinction from prior joint entity-word embedding work (e.g., Wang et al.) is not clearly articulated.

Weakness 2: Experimental results are mixed or weaker than claimed—human-annotated baselines and simpler phrase-approximation methods often outperform the proposed concept embeddings.

Weakness 3: The term "distant supervision" is misleading; the method uses phrase occurrences as embedding context rather than true distant supervision labeling, which confuses the methodological claim.

Weakness 4: Key compositionality hyperparameters (β and η) are often zero, implying the model does not leverage compositional phrase information and contradicting claims about handling compositional vs non-compositional phrases.

Question 1: Why do phrase-based approximations (APPROXPHRco) outperform direct concept embeddings if the model is supposed to preserve concept semantics?

Question 2: If ontology mappings are required, why do concept embeddings provide less value than phrase embeddings—what extra benefit does the ontology bring?

Question 3: Given β is zero in most settings, how do you justify claims that the model controls or leverages phrase compositionality?
