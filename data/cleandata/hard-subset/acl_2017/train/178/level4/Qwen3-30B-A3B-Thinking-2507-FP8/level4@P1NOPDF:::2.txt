

Summary: This paper introduces a model for embedding words, phrases, and concepts into vector spaces using an ontology where each concept maps to associated phrases. These phrases, treated as atomic tokens, are extracted from text corpora. The approach adapts the skip-gram method to jointly train embeddings for words, phrases, and their associated concepts. Evaluation focuses on concept similarity and relatedness tasks using UMLS and YAGO as ontological backbones.

Strengths:  
The paper addresses a meaningful gap: phrases lacking lexical similarity may exhibit semantic closeness, and not all phrases are compositional. The proposed model offers a plausible solution for training phrase embeddings, with results demonstrating competitive or superior performance in concept similarity tasks. The accompanying software would be valuable for biomedical NLP researchers.

Weaknesses:  
The core contribution lacks novelty—it is a minor adaptation of skip-gram. More critically, the full model does not consistently outperform baselines. As shown in Table 4, Choi et al.’s baseline significantly outperforms the proposed method on the Mayo datasets, with a similar trend in Table 6. On the larger UMNSRS dataset (Table 4), the model is only marginally competitive with simpler baselines like Chiu et al.’s approach.

General Discussion:  
The paper claims to use "distant supervision" via known phrases to train embeddings. However, this terminology is misleading. The method treats phrase occurrences as context for word embedding training—not as supervision for concept identification or prediction. The term "supervision" thus creates confusion.  

Section 3.2 introduces notation (e.g., E_W) that remains unused elsewhere, undermining clarity. The parameter β, which controls phrase compositionality via word vectors, is problematic. It implies a single global constant dictates compositionality for all phrases—a counterintuitive assumption. Cross-validation results (Table 3) reveal unexpected β values: β=0 for PM+CL and WikiNYT, effectively negating compositionality. This undermines the model’s theoretical foundation.  

Table 4’s experimental setup requires clarification. The paper states that data labels concept similarity/relatedness, but if the concept-phrase mapping is many-to-many, how are phrase/word vectors used to compute similarity? Concept vectors alone would suffice, making the phrase embeddings’ role ambiguous.  

Table 5 shows that the approximate phrase method (using phrase averages to represent concepts) achieves the best results. This raises a key question: why employ a concept ontology at all? A seed set of phrases alone could yield comparable outcomes, rendering the ontology unnecessary. The model’s complexity appears unjustified by its performance.
