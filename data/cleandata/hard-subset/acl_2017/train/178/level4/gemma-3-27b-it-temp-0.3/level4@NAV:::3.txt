The authors present a method for jointly embedding words, phrases, and concepts, leveraging plain text corpora and a manually-constructed ontology where concepts are represented by one or more phrases. The method is applied to both the medical domain, utilizing the UMLS ontology, and the general domain, using YAGO. Evaluation involves comparison to baselines and existing work, primarily on intrinsic similarity and relatedness benchmarks. The authors contribute a new general-domain concept similarity and relatedness dataset, created using mechanical turkers, which they plan to release, alongside utilizing existing medical domain benchmarks. Reported results are comparable to prior work.

**Strengths:**

- The proposed joint embedding model is conceptually sound and offers a valuable approach by bridging the gap between treating phrases as atomic units and acknowledging their compositional nature. This same principle extends to representing concepts as compositions of representative phrases.
- The work is substantial, encompassing model development, the creation of a new evaluation dataset, and a range of evaluations and analyses.

**Weaknesses:**

- The evaluation is limited to intrinsic tasks focused on similarity and relatedness. Given the known limitations of intrinsic evaluations in predicting real-world performance, the inclusion of at least one or two extrinsic tasks would significantly strengthen the evaluation.
- The similarity/relatedness datasets are presented as concept-level, but the underlying human judgements were based on phrases. Therefore, they should be interpreted and analyzed as phrase similarity datasets.
- The medical concept evaluation datasets, ‘mini MayoSRS’ (29 pairs) and ‘MayoSRS’ (101 pairs), are small and have reported low inter-annotator agreement. The ‘UMNSRS’ dataset, while larger, focuses solely on single-word concepts, which limits its relevance to evaluating representations of phrases and general concepts; this limitation should be acknowledged.
- The authors extensively fine-tune hyperparameters on the same datasets used for reporting results and comparison, raising concerns about the validity of the reported performance and analyses.
- The argument that the method surpasses prior work due to reduced manual annotation requirements is weak, as the approach still relies on large, manually-constructed ontologies and existing clinical records were used for annotation in prior work.
- The paper lacks in-depth analysis of the underlying mechanisms driving the observed results. The treatment of the relationship between a phrase and its component words, and a concept and its alternative phrases, as analogous compositional relationships warrants dedicated investigation. For instance, an NLP analysis around line 588 could explore the role of phrase headwords. Furthermore, an investigation into the impact of hyperparameters controlling the tradeoff between atomic and compositional representations would be beneficial.

**General Discussion:**

Due to the outlined weaknesses, I recommend rejecting this submission. I encourage the authors to refine their evaluation datasets and methodology before resubmission.
