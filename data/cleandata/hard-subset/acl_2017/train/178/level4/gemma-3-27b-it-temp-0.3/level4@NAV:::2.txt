This paper introduces a model for generating vector space embeddings of words, phrases, and concepts, leveraging an ontology to map concepts to phrases which are then identified within text corpora and treated as atomic symbols. The model then employs a skip-gram approach to train embeddings for words, these atomic phrases, and their associated concepts. Evaluation focuses on concept similarity and relatedness tasks, utilizing UMLS and Yago as underlying ontologies.

The paper addresses an important issue: that semantic similarity doesn’t always correlate with lexical similarity, and that phrases aren’t always compositional. The proposed model offers a reasonable approach to learning phrase embeddings, demonstrating competitive or superior performance in identifying conceptual similarity. Furthermore, the accompanying software release has the potential to be a valuable resource for biomedical NLP researchers.

However, the model’s novelty is limited, representing primarily an adaptation of the skip-gram method. A key concern arises from the experimental results; the complete model detailed in the paper does not consistently achieve the best performance. Specifically, in Table 4, the Choi baseline significantly outperforms the proposed model on the two Mayo datasets, and a similar pattern is observed in Table 6. On the larger UMNSRS dataset, the model achieves only comparable results to simpler, existing models like Chiu.

The paper frames its approach as utilizing phrases for distant supervision during embedding training, but the nature of this “supervision” remains unclear. While phrase occurrences linked to concepts provide context for word embedding training, this differs from traditional supervised learning paradigms (e.g., concept identification). The terminology is therefore somewhat ambiguous. Additionally, the notation introduced in Section 3.2 (E_W, etc.) is not subsequently used. 

The use of a single global parameter, \beta, to control phrase compositionality is also questionable. The cross-validation results presented in Table 3 reveal surprisingly low values for \beta (zero for PM+CL and WikiNYT), effectively negating compositionality. The experimental setup for Table 4 also requires further clarification. Given the many-to-many mapping between concepts and phrases, it’s unclear how phrase/word vectors are used to calculate concept similarity – it appears only concept vectors are utilized. Finally, the superior performance of the approximate phrase method in Table 5 (using the average of phrase vectors to represent concepts) raises questions about the necessity of the concept ontology itself; a seed set of phrases could potentially yield equivalent results.
