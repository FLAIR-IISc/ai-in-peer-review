- Summary: Proposes a weakly-supervised method that replaces representative phrases with unique IDs and trains joint concept/phrase/word embeddings via a modified skip-gram, evaluated on UMLS and YAGO showing competitive similarity/relatedness performance and much higher vocabulary coverage.

- Contribution 1: Novel distant-supervision method for jointly embedding concepts, phrases, and words using ontology concept-phrase mappings.

- Contribution 2: Shows competitive concept similarity and relatedness performance without human-annotated training data.

- Contribution 3: Introduces a new dataset for evaluating similarity and relatedness of real-world entities.

- Strength 1: Eliminates need for human annotation while maintaining competitive performance, improving scalability.

- Strength 2: Empirical evidence that embeddings preserve semantic relationships between concepts and their representative phrases (high cosine similarity).

- Strength 3: New real-world entity similarity/relatedness dataset fills an important gap for future research.

- Strength 4: Achieves over 3x vocabulary coverage compared to existing approaches, enabling broader domain coverage.

- Weakness 1: No clear explanation for underperformance relative to CHOI on smaller biomedical datasets (Mayo SRS, Mini Mayo SRS).

- Weakness 2: Hyperparameters tuned on evaluation data risks inflated performance and potential overfitting despite cross-validation.

- Weakness 3: Limited analysis of failure modes for concepts with sparse or ambiguous textual representations; compositionality results show high variance.

- Weakness 4: Inconsistent hyperparameter settings across corpora imply effort is needed to adapt the method to different domains.

- Weakness 5: Missing direct comparisons to recent relevant concept embedding work (e.g., Mencia et al. 2016; Choi et al. 2016a).

- Question 1: How do authors reconcile similar performance of APPROXPHRco (average phrase embeddings) and CONCEPTco (direct concept embeddings) with the claim that concept embeddings better capture semantics?

- Question 2: Which semantic types showed best and worst clustering performance, and what explains those differences?

- Question 3: Can authors provide concrete examples demonstrating how ontology structure is preserved in the embedding space?
