

Summary: The paper proposes a weakly-supervised method for jointly embedding concepts, phrases, and words using representative phrases from ontologies as distant supervision signals. The authors train embeddings on biomedical concepts (UMLS) and general-domain entities (YAGO) from unlabeled text corpora. They demonstrate competitive performance on concept similarity and relatedness tasks compared to methods requiring human annotation, while achieving higher vocabulary coverage. The paper also introduces a novel dataset of similarity and relatedness judgments for real-world entities.

Main Contributions:
1. A novel weakly-supervised approach for jointly embedding concepts, phrases, and words using distant supervision from ontology phrases, eliminating the need for human annotation.
2. Experimental validation showing competitive performance on concept similarity and relatedness tasks compared to supervised methods, with significantly larger vocabulary coverage.
3. A new dataset of human judgments for similarity and relatedness of real-world entities (people, places, organizations), filling an important gap in the literature.

Strengths:
1. The paper addresses a meaningful gap in the field by providing a method that doesn't require human annotation for concept embeddings, which is a significant practical advantage over existing approaches. The authors demonstrate this through convincing results on multiple datasets.
2. The method's ability to scale to much larger vocabulary sizes (over 3x coverage) without additional annotation cost is a substantial practical contribution that merits attention.
3. The introduction of a new dataset for real-world entity similarity/relatedness is valuable and fills an important gap in the evaluation resources available for this domain.

Weaknesses:
1. The paper doesn't sufficiently address the limitations of distant supervision, particularly how noise from the phrase-concept mappings affects performance. For instance, when a single concept has multiple phrases with varying semantic nuances (e.g., "New York City" vs. "The Big Apple"), the model might learn inconsistent representations. The authors should discuss how they handle such cases.
2. The comparison to prior work is somewhat misleading. The authors compare their weakly-supervised method to supervised approaches (e.g., CHOI, DEVINE) without acknowledging the significant human annotation cost required for those methods. A more balanced comparison would include a cost-benefit analysis.
3. The analysis of why the method works well is incomplete. The results show that phrase-based concept approximations (PHRASEco) outperform direct concept embeddings (CONCEPTco) on the general-domain dataset (Table 5), yet the authors don't explore this discrepancy or provide insight into the model's behavior.

Questions to Authors:
1. How do you handle cases where a concept has multiple representative phrases with different semantic nuances (e.g., "Lou Gehrig's disease" vs. "amyotrophic lateral sclerosis")? Does the model learn different embeddings for different phrases representing the same concept, or does it average them?
2. In Table 4, CHOI outperforms your method on the smaller MayoSRS and Mini Mayo SRS datasets. Given that CHOI uses manual annotations (which your method avoids), how do you explain this performance difference, and what might be the specific advantages of their manual annotation approach?
3. Your method achieves higher coverage but potentially lower precision (as seen in the smaller datasets). Could you provide a more detailed analysis of the trade-off between coverage and precision across different corpus sizes and ontologies?

Additional Comments:
The paper is well-written and the methodology is clearly presented. The supplemental material provides sufficient detail about the hyperparameter tuning and dataset construction. However, the authors should strengthen their discussion of the limitations of their approach, particularly regarding the quality of the phrase-concept mappings from the ontologies and how that impacts the final embeddings. The claim that "our embeddings show performance competitive with existing methods" would be stronger if the authors provided a more nuanced comparison that acknowledges both the advantages and disadvantages of their weakly-supervised approach.
