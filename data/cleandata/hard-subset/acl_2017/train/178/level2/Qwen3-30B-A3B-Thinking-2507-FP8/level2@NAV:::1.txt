

**Review**

Summary: This paper proposes a weakly-supervised method for jointly embedding concepts, phrases, and words using ontology phrases as distant supervision. The main contributions are: 1) a novel method requiring no human annotation for concept embeddings, 2) competitive performance on concept similarity/relatedness tasks compared to methods requiring manual annotation, and 3) significantly larger vocabulary coverage (more than 3x) than prior approaches.

Strengths:
1. The method addresses a meaningful problem in semantic representation learning by eliminating the need for expensive manual annotation. The use of ontology phrases as distant supervision is well-motivated and appropriately framed.
2. The experimental results are compelling, showing competitive performance on standard biomedical similarity/relatedness datasets (UMNSRS, MayoSRS) while achieving substantially larger vocabulary coverage. The authors also provide a valuable new dataset for evaluating real-world entity similarity.
3. The analysis of embedding compositionality (Figures 3-4) provides useful insights into how the model preserves ontological relationships between concepts, phrases, and words.

Weaknesses:
1. The paper lacks sufficient detail on avoiding test set leakage during hyperparameter tuning. While they mention "adapted 5-fold cross-validation," they don't clarify how this prevented data leakage in their evaluation, which is a serious methodological flaw given the known issues with similarity/relatedness evaluation tasks.
2. The analysis of the β and η hyperparameters is superficial. The paper shows non-zero values can help in some cases but doesn't provide a thorough analysis of when and why these parameters matter, or how to select them optimally.
3. The authors fail to address recent critiques of similarity/relatedness tasks for evaluating embeddings (Chiu et al., 2016b), which undermines the validity of their performance claims as these tasks have known issues with generalizing to downstream applications.

Questions to Authors:
1. Please clarify exactly how you implemented your 5-fold cross-validation to prevent test set leakage during hyperparameter tuning.
2. Can you provide a more detailed analysis of the β and η parameters, particularly identifying specific scenarios where they provide significant benefits?
3. How do you plan to address the limitations of similarity/relatedness tasks for embedding evaluation in future work?
