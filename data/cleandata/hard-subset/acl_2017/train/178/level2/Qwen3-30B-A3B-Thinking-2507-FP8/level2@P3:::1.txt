

Summary: The paper proposes a weakly-supervised method for jointly embedding concepts, phrases, and words using distant supervision from ontology concept-phrase mappings. The approach replaces representative phrases in an unlabeled corpus with unique identifiers and trains embeddings using a modified skip-gram model. The authors evaluate their method on biomedical concepts (UMLS) and general-domain entities (YAGO), demonstrating competitive performance on concept similarity and relatedness tasks while achieving significantly higher vocabulary coverage than existing methods.

Main Contributions: The paper makes three key contributions: (1) a novel method for jointly embedding concepts, phrases, and words using distant supervision from ontology mappings, (2) demonstration of competitive performance on concept similarity and relatedness tasks without human annotation, and (3) introduction of a novel dataset for evaluating similarity and relatedness of real-world entities.

Strengths: 
1. The method addresses a significant limitation in prior work by eliminating the need for human annotation of the training corpus while still achieving competitive performance on concept similarity and relatedness tasks. This represents a meaningful contribution to scalability of concept embedding methods.

2. The paper provides valuable empirical evidence that the method preserves semantic relationships between concepts and their representative phrases, as demonstrated by high cosine similarity between concept embeddings and their average phrase embeddings. This indicates the model successfully captures the ontology structure.

3. The introduction of a new dataset for real-world entity similarity and relatedness fills an important gap in the literature, as the authors correctly note that comparable datasets are not available for entities. This will be a valuable resource for future research.

4. The method achieves more than 3x vocabulary coverage compared to existing approaches, which is a substantial practical advantage for applications requiring comprehensive coverage of domain-specific concepts.

Weaknesses:
1. The paper does not sufficiently address why the method underperforms CHOI on smaller biomedical datasets (Mayo SRS and Mini Mayo SRS), while performing competitively on larger datasets (UMNSRS). This discrepancy needs clearer explanation, particularly given that CHOI relies on manual annotation while the proposed method does not.

2. The evaluation methodology involves tuning hyperparameters on the same data used for evaluation (as acknowledged in the discussion section), which potentially inflates performance metrics. While the authors attempt to mitigate this with cross-validation, the evaluation remains vulnerable to overfitting to the test set.

3. The paper fails to thoroughly explore the limitations of the method, particularly for concepts with limited or ambiguous textual representations. The analysis of compositionality (Figure 3) shows significant variance in phrase-word similarity, suggesting the method may not consistently capture the semantic relationships between phrases and their component words.

4. The hyperparameter tuning results are inconsistent across corpora (Table 3), with different optimal settings for each corpus. This suggests the method may require significant effort to adapt to different domains, potentially limiting its general applicability.

5. The paper does not compare against the most recent relevant work, such as the concept embedding methods proposed by Mencia et al. (2016) and Choi et al. (2016a), which are mentioned in the related work but not directly compared in the evaluation.

Questions to Authors:
1. The paper shows that APPROXPHRco (approximating concepts as average phrase embeddings) performs similarly to CONCEPTco (direct concept embeddings) on the biomedical datasets. How does this finding align with the authors' claim that concept embeddings better capture semantic relationships than phrase embeddings? 

2. The authors note that "the semantic type clustering performance is mixed among the semantic types, with our embeddings clustering competitively with prior work." Which semantic types performed best and worst, and what might explain these differences?

3. The paper mentions that "the source ontology structure is reflected in the organization of our embeddings" but does not provide concrete examples. Could the authors provide specific examples of how ontology structure is preserved in the embedding space?
