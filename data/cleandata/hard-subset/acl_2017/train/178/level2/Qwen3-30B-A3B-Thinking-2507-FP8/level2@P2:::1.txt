

Summary: This paper proposes a weakly-supervised method for jointly embedding concepts, phrases, and words using distant supervision from ontology mappings (phrases linked to concepts). The main contributions are: (1) a novel approach to learn concept embeddings without human corpus annotation, (2) competitive performance on concept similarity/relatedness tasks compared to methods requiring human annotation, and (3) a novel dataset for evaluating similarity/relatedness of real-world entities.

Strengths: 
1. The method's use of distant supervision from ontology mappings (phrases associated with concepts) to train concept embeddings without human corpus annotation is a significant contribution. This addresses a key limitation of prior work that requires expensive manual annotation, as evidenced by the 3x vocabulary coverage increase compared to previous methods.

2. The comprehensive evaluation across both biomedical and general-domain datasets, including the creation of a novel real-world entity similarity/relatedness dataset, provides valuable resources for the community. The dataset construction process is well-documented with appropriate crowd-sourcing methodology.

3. The analysis of semantic type clustering and compositionality provides useful insights into how the model organizes concepts, showing that concepts cluster by semantic type and that phrase-word compositionality is preserved to some degree.

Weaknesses:
1. The main claim of "competitive performance" is undermined by the results: on the Mayo SRS dataset (the smallest biomedical dataset), the CHOI method (which requires manual annotation) significantly outperforms the proposed method (0.817 vs. 0.593). The paper does not adequately explain why their method underperforms on smaller datasets while performing better on larger ones, which weakens the central claim of the paper.

2. The hyperparameter tuning procedure is problematic as it uses the test set (via 5-fold cross-validation), which violates standard evaluation practices. The authors acknowledge this as a concern but do not properly address it by using a proper train/val/test split for hyperparameter selection. This is a serious methodological flaw that affects the validity of the reported results.

3. The comparison to Wang et al. (2014a), the closest related work, is inadequate. Wang et al. uses relation information from the knowledge base (which the authors acknowledge is "highly incomplete"), but the authors don't clearly demonstrate why their method is better or how it addresses this limitation. The paper also fails to explain why their method doesn't outperform Wang et al. on the larger datasets.

4. The claim that "no human corpus annotation" is required is misleading, as the ontology mappings (which are human-constructed) and Wikipedia links for YAGO are used. The paper doesn't sufficiently clarify this distinction or explain why this isn't considered human annotation.

5. The paper lacks evidence beyond cosine similarity on specific datasets that the embeddings capture meaningful semantic relationships. While the authors acknowledge that "analogy completion" at the concept level is limited, they don't provide any strong evidence through other intrinsic or extrinsic tasks that would demonstrate the semantic properties of their embeddings.

Questions to Authors: How does the performance difference between small and large datasets relate to the training corpora used (PUBMED, CLINICAL, PM+CL)? Does the performance gap on smaller datasets correlate with the number of concept occurrences in the training corpus?
