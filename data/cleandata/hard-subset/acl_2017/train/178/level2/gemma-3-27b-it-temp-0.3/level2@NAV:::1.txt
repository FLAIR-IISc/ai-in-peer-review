## Review of "A Weakly-Supervised Method for Jointly Embedding Concepts, Phrases, and Words"

This paper presents a novel weakly-supervised method for learning joint embeddings of concepts, phrases, and words, leveraging representative phrases from ontologies as distant supervision. The core idea – utilizing existing knowledge bases to bypass the need for expensive manual annotation – is promising and addresses a significant challenge in semantic representation learning. The authors demonstrate competitive performance on concept similarity and relatedness tasks, while achieving substantial vocabulary coverage.

The primary contribution of this work is the proposed method for jointly embedding concepts, phrases, and words using distant supervision. This approach effectively leverages readily available ontological information to create semantic representations without requiring costly human annotation. A secondary contribution is the creation and release of a novel dataset for evaluating similarity and relatedness of real-world entities, which fills a gap in existing evaluation resources. Finally, the analysis demonstrating that the source ontology structure is reflected in the organization of the learned embeddings provides valuable insight into the method’s behavior.

The strengths of this paper lie in its practical utility and solid experimental evaluation. The competitive performance against existing methods, particularly those requiring manual annotation, is compelling. The extensive experimentation across different corpora and ontologies demonstrates the robustness of the approach. The inclusion of a new dataset is a valuable addition to the community. The ablation study with hyperparameters provides a good understanding of the model’s sensitivity.

However, the paper has several weaknesses. The improvement over strong baselines, particularly on the general-domain datasets, is modest. The analysis of the embedding space, while insightful, could be expanded to include visualizations and more quantitative measures. The discussion section acknowledges limitations but could benefit from a more detailed exploration of potential avenues for future work, such as incorporating more sophisticated knowledge graph embedding techniques. The reliance on distant supervision introduces noise, and a more thorough analysis of the impact of this noise on the learned embeddings would be beneficial.

**Questions to Authors:**

1.  Could you elaborate on the impact of the phrase selection process from the ontologies on the performance of your method? Are there biases introduced by the choice of representative phrases?
2.  The paper mentions that the hyperparameter tuning was performed on a separate validation set. Could you provide more details on the cross-validation strategy used and the rationale behind the chosen hyperparameters?
3.  How does the performance of your method degrade as the quality or completeness of the underlying ontology decreases?



**Additional Comments:**

The paper is well-written and clearly presented. The code and dataset release are commendable and will facilitate further research in this area. Despite the modest improvements, the approach is promising and warrants further investigation. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions addressing the weaknesses outlined above.
