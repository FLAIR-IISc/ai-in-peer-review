- Introduces a weakly-supervised, ontology-driven method that jointly learns embeddings for concepts, phrases, and words by adapting the skip-gram model and using distant supervision from canonical phrase mappings.  
- Proposes a scalable approach that avoids manual annotation and covers a much larger vocabulary of concepts than prior supervised methods.  
- Releases a new general-domain entity similarity/relatedness dataset for people, places, and organizations to fill an evaluation gap beyond the word level.  
- Provides comprehensive empirical evaluation on biomedical and general-domain tasks, showing competitive performance with more costly supervised baselines.  
- Practical and scalable: leverages existing ontologies to learn embeddings for large concept sets (e.g., 190k medical concepts) and plans to release code.  
- Strong empirical validation across multiple corpora and domains, with weakly-supervised embeddings matching or exceeding supervised baselines on tasks like UMNSRS.  
- Valuable community resource: the new entity similarity dataset and documented collection/validation process offer a useful benchmark.  
- Insightful analysis: investigates compositionality and semantic-type clustering and transparently discusses limitations.  
- Evaluation limited to intrinsic similarity/relatedness tasks, lacking extrinsic downstream evaluations (e.g., entity linking or relation extraction).  
- Benefit of direct concept embeddings is unclear since a simple averaged-phrase baseline outperformed learned concept embeddings on the new dataset.  
- New dataset has low inter-annotator agreement (Fleiss’ κ = 0.24), indicating possible label noise or task ambiguity that undermines reliability.
