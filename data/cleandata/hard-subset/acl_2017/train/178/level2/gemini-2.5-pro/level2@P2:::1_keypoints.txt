- Proposes a weakly-supervised method that uses ontology phrase mappings and skip-gram-style training to jointly learn embeddings for concepts, phrases, and words from unlabeled text.  
- Scales to large vocabularies without manual annotations by leveraging existing ontologies (e.g., UMLS, YAGO).  
- Demonstrates competitive performance on biomedical and general-domain concept similarity tasks and provides thorough empirical comparisons and ablations.  
- Releases a new crowdsourced dataset for general-domain entity similarity/relatedness, with documented quality control and inter-annotator agreement.  
- Offers analyses showing compositional properties of embeddings and semantic-type clustering, noting phrases can be distant from their component words.  
- Practical and scalable approach suited to domains with ontologies but limited annotated data.  
- Comprehensive evaluation across domains and corpora, including multiple baselines and ablation studies.  
- New dataset fills a gap for entity-level similarity resources and is transparently documented.  
- Results are mixed: simpler baselines (phrase-averaging) sometimes outperform the learned concept embeddings, questioning the necessity of a dedicated concept matrix.  
- Methodological novelty is limited—main changes are parallel embedding matrices and an adapted skip-gram objective.  
- Evaluation is entirely intrinsic (similarity/relatedness), lacking extrinsic downstream tasks to demonstrate practical utility.  
- Question: Why does phrase-averaging (APPROXPHRco) outperform direct concept embeddings (CONCEPTco) in some cases—might phrase-averaging be sufficient for some domains?  
- Question: Please clarify the training loop and loss computation for center items—how is the loss handled when the center is a word versus a phrase given preprocessing that merges phrases?
