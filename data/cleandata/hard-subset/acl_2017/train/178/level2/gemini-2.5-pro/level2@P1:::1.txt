**Summary and Contributions**

This paper presents a novel, weakly-supervised method for jointly learning vector representations for concepts, multi-word phrases, and individual words in a shared semantic space. The key idea is to leverage an existing ontology (e.g., UMLS, YAGO) as a source of distant supervision. The method identifies textual phrases in a large, unannotated corpus that correspond to known concepts in the ontology. It then adapts the skip-gram with negative sampling architecture to update embeddings for words, the phrases they appear in, and the underlying concepts that these phrases represent. The authors evaluate their method in both the biomedical and general-domain, showing competitive performance on concept similarity and relatedness tasks compared to prior methods, some of which require expensive manual annotations.

In my view, the main contributions of this work are:

1.  A simple and effective weakly-supervised method for jointly embedding concepts, phrases, and words. The approach cleverly extends the word2vec framework to incorporate structured knowledge from ontologies without requiring manually annotated text, making it highly scalable.
2.  The introduction and evaluation of a new benchmark dataset for general-domain entity similarity and relatedness. This is a valuable resource for the community, as such datasets for entities (as opposed to words) are scarce.
3.  A thorough empirical evaluation across two different domains (biomedical and general), demonstrating the method's versatility. The paper includes extensive comparisons with multiple baselines and provides insightful analyses into the properties of the learned embedding space.

**Strengths**

1.  **Elegant and Practical Method**: The proposed method is a well-motivated and elegant extension of the highly successful word2vec model. By treating ontology-linked phrases as single tokens and jointly training embeddings for concepts, phrases, and words, the model provides a principled way to represent non-atomic semantic units. The use of distant supervision from ontologies instead of manually annotated data is a significant practical advantage, allowing the method to be applied to vast corpora and concept vocabularies with minimal human effort.

2.  **Comprehensive Experiments**: The authors have conducted a strong and convincing set of experiments. The evaluation across both the biomedical and general-knowledge domains, using different corpora and ontologies, effectively demonstrates the general applicability of the approach. The comparison to a wide variety of baselines—including word-level embeddings, concept embeddings from prior work, and several compositional approximations—is thorough and helps to situate the contribution of the proposed model clearly.

3.  **Valuable New Resource and Analysis**: The paper contributes a new, carefully constructed dataset for general-domain entity similarity and relatedness, which will be useful for future research in this area. Furthermore, the authors go beyond simple performance metrics on extrinsic tasks. The analyses of compositionality (Figures 3, 4) and semantic type clustering (Table 6) provide valuable insights into the geometric properties of the learned space and the relationships between concepts, their phrasal forms, and their constituent words.

**Weaknesses**

1.  **Unclear Benefit of Direct Concept Embeddings**: A central goal of the paper is to learn explicit concept embeddings (`CONCEPTco`). However, the results suggest that a simpler baseline—approximating a concept by averaging the embeddings of its constituent phrases (`APPROXPHRco`)—often performs as well as or even better than the directly learned concept embedding (e.g., on the new general-domain datasets in Table 5). This raises a question about the added value of the concept embedding matrix `EC`. If a simple post-hoc averaging of phrase embeddings is more effective, the justification for the additional complexity of learning `EC` directly becomes less clear. The paper touches on this in the discussion but could benefit from a deeper analysis of why this might be the case.

2.  **Simplistic Phrase Identification**: The preprocessing method relies on a greedy, longest-match strategy to identify and replace phrases in the text. This heuristic may introduce noise and errors, particularly in cases of nested or overlapping phrases (e.g., "New York" vs. "New York City"). The potential impact of this design choice on the quality of the training signals and, consequently, the final embeddings is not discussed or evaluated. It would be valuable to understand the sensitivity of the model to this preprocessing step.

3.  **Limited Interpretation of Hyperparameter Choices**: The hyperparameter tuning (Table 3) reveals interesting differences across corpora. For instance, the combined PM+CL corpus uses `β=0` and `η=0`, effectively disabling the explicit compositionality constraints, whereas the CLINICAL corpus benefits from non-zero values. The paper does not provide much intuition or analysis for these choices. A discussion on how corpus characteristics (e.g., domain, style, size) might influence the optimal degree of compositionality would strengthen the paper's conclusions and provide better guidance for applying this method to new domains.

**Questions to Authors**

1.  Could you further elaborate on the role of the concept embedding matrix `EC` during training, especially in light of the strong performance of the `APPROXPHRco` baseline? Is it possible that the main contribution of `EC` is to act as a regularizer, pushing embeddings of synonymous phrases closer together, and that averaging these regularized phrase vectors is a more robust final representation than the learned concept vector itself?

2.  The greedy phrase matching in preprocessing seems like a potential source of ambiguity and error. Did you explore or consider the effects of this simplification? For example, did you perform any error analysis on how nested phrases are handled, and do you have any intuition on whether a more sophisticated phrase disambiguation technique might improve performance?
