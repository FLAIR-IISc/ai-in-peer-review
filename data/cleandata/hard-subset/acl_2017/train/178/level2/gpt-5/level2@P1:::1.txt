Summary and contributions
This paper proposes a weakly supervised method to jointly embed ontology concepts, their representative phrases, and words into a single vector space. The key idea is to use phrase-to-concept mappings from an ontology (UMLS, YAGO) as distant supervision: phrases in raw text are detected, collapsed to tokens, and their contexts are used to train phrase and concept embeddings with a skip-gram negative-sampling objective. Two hyperparameters (β, η) encourage compositionality between words→phrases and phrases→concepts. The approach requires no human annotation of the training corpus.

As I see them, the main contributions are:
1) A scalable, annotation-free method for learning concept, phrase, and word embeddings jointly from raw text using ontology phrase mappings (primary).
2) An empirical study demonstrating competitive performance on biomedical similarity/relatedness benchmarks with substantially larger coverage than prior concept-embedding work, plus analyses of concept–phrase and phrase–word structure in the learned space (secondary).
3) A new general-domain entity similarity/relatedness dataset and release of software/embeddings (cui2vec) (tertiary).

Strengths
- Scalable weak supervision without manual corpus annotation: The method piggybacks on existing ontology phrase lists and unlabeled corpora, avoiding expensive concept annotation. It yields very large vocabularies (e.g., 193k UMLS concepts in PM+CL vs 15–52k in prior concept embeddings), which is practically valuable.
- Competitive intrinsic performance in biomedicine: On UMNSRS Similarity, CONCEPTco achieves state-of-the-art or tied performance (ρ=0.653 on PM+CL, matching CHIU-30), and is competitive on UMNSRS Relatedness and Mayo sets, despite no manual annotation. This supports the claim that contexts of phrase mentions carry sufficient signal to learn useful concept embeddings.
- Useful joint space and analyses: The joint space enables assessing alignment between concepts, phrases, and words. The compositionality and MAP analyses are informative: concepts cluster near the average of their phrases, and phrases show a bimodal relation to component words. The semantic type clustering results provide additional insight into which UMLS semantic types are easier to capture.
- Breadth of domains and resources: The method is applied to both UMLS and YAGO, across multiple corpora (PubMed, clinical notes, Wikipedia+NYT). The release of code/embeddings and the new entity similarity/relatedness dataset increases potential impact and reproducibility.
- Simple training pipeline and clear objective: Adapting skip-gram with negative sampling, with explicit β and η to tune compositionality, is straightforward and likely easy for others to adopt.

Weaknesses
- Ambiguity and distant supervision noise not explicitly modeled: The assumption that any occurrence of a mapped phrase signifies an occurrence of each candidate concept is very strong. The only mitigation is uniform normalization by the number of concepts a phrase can represent. There is no disambiguation using local context, priors, or ontology constraints. This likely contributes to the gap vs CHOI on Mayo sets and to the observation that phrase-based approximations sometimes outperform concept embeddings (Table 5).
- Limited use of ontology structure: Beyond mapping phrases to concepts, the model does not exploit the ontology graph (relations, hierarchy, semantic types) that many KB embedding methods leverage. Given the compositionality analysis indicating that non-lexical concept information may be under-captured, incorporating structure (as a regularizer or additional training signal) could be important.
- Evaluation is largely intrinsic and tuning risks overfitting: The main evaluations are similarity/relatedness correlations; while common, they have known limitations and weak predictiveness for downstream tasks. The hyperparameter tuning uses adapted cross-validation on the test sets, which can still bias results. No statistical significance tests are reported across settings, and there is no downstream evaluation (e.g., entity linking/concept normalization, ICD/CUI coding, WSD) to demonstrate practical gains from joint concept embeddings.
- General-domain dataset quality and comparisons: The new entity similarity/relatedness datasets have low inter-annotator agreement (Fleiss’ κ≈0.24), which limits interpretability. The sets are relatively small (688 pairs), and the selection process, while described, may introduce sampling bias. Comparisons to established entity-centric resources or tasks (e.g., Wikipedia link-based relatedness baselines, EL benchmarks) are missing.
- Some methodological clarity and ablation gaps: A few details would benefit from clarification: the use of a single negative-sample embedding matrix shared across words/phrases/concepts (rationale and effect); the greedy phrase matching and its impact on boundary errors; handling of overlapping/ambiguous phrases; training efficiency (time/memory) vs baselines. Ablations isolating the effect of β and η and of phrase collapsing (TT vs TU) on final results are limited; Table 3 shows tuned values, but we do not see controlled comparisons on a fixed setting.

Questions to authors
- Ambiguity handling: Did you try context-based disambiguation of phrases to concepts during training (e.g., EM, posterior regularization, or sampling a single concept per mention using the context)? How would such a mechanism affect performance and training stability?
- Ontology signal: Can you quantify the effect of adding simple ontology-structure regularizers (e.g., pulling parent/child or same-type concepts together) on similarity/relatedness and type clustering? Any preliminary experiments?
- β and η: Can you provide ablations on a fixed corpus showing the incremental impact of β>0 and η>0 on concept and phrase performance, and on the compositionality/MAP diagnostics? In WIKINYT, η tuned to 0; do you have intuition for why concept–phrase compositionality hurt?
- Negative sampling matrix: Why use a single ENS shared across W/P/C rather than separate output matrices or a standard input-output parameterization (as in word2vec)? Did you observe measurable differences when using shared vs separate output parameters?
- Dataset release and utility: Will the general-domain similarity/relatedness datasets and detailed annotation guidelines be released? Can you report per-category agreement (person/person vs org/place, etc.) and whether removing low-agreement pairs changes the model rankings?
- Practicality: What are training time and memory footprints for PM+CL and WIKINYT? How sensitive are results to the 25-frequency threshold, especially in the clinical domain where rare concepts are important?

Additional comments
- Consider evaluating on an extrinsic task where concept-level representations matter (e.g., clinical concept normalization, biomedical entity linking, or medical code prediction), and comparing to both concept embeddings and word-only baselines.
- Reporting statistical significance of differences in ρ and providing confidence intervals would strengthen claims.
- A brief error analysis of pairs where CONCEPTco underperforms APPROXPHRco could illuminate failure modes (ambiguity, rare phrases, domain mismatch).
