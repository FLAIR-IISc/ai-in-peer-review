Summary
The paper proposes a weakly supervised method to jointly embed ontology concepts, their surface phrases, and words by replacing known concept-mention phrases in raw text and training a skip-gram model with additional compositionality regularizers. The approach is applied to UMLS (biomedical) and YAGO (general entities), reporting competitive intrinsic performance on concept similarity/relatedness without manual annotation, substantially larger vocabulary coverage, and qualitative analyses of concept–phrase coherence and semantic-type clustering. The authors also release code (cui2vec) and a small crowdsourced entity similarity/relatedness dataset.

Main contributions (as I see them)
1) A scalable, annotation-free pipeline for joint concept–phrase–word embeddings using distant supervision, achieving competitive intrinsic evaluation with >3x coverage versus prior concept-embedding resources.
2) Careful intrinsic evaluation across multiple corpora and ontologies, including analyses of compositionality and semantic-type clustering; plus a new general-domain entity similarity/relatedness dataset.
3) Practical release of code and resources that facilitate reproducibility.

Strengths
- Scalability and practicality: The distant supervision setup avoids costly manual annotations yet covers 188k–748k concepts, demonstrating a clear utility advantage over prior resources with 15k–52k concepts.
- Competitive performance: On UMNSRS Similarity, CONCEPTco matches or exceeds strong word baselines (e.g., CHIU-30) and compares favorably to prior concept embeddings; performance on relatedness is competitive and often close to the best word baselines.
- Joint space enables analysis and potential downstream use: The unified embedding space supports compositionality and type-clustering analyses, indicating learned structure beyond isolated word models.
- Reproducibility and transparency: Release of code, datasets, and detailed hyperparameter sweeps (with cross-validation) increases credibility and reusability.

Weaknesses
- Ambiguity in supervision: The assumption that any phrase occurrence updates all possible concepts uniformly (no disambiguation) introduces substantial noise; its effect is not quantified and likely explains weaker results on smaller datasets.
- Limited evaluation scope: The work relies on intrinsic correlation metrics without statistical significance testing or extrinsic tasks (e.g., entity linking, concept normalization), limiting claims of practical impact.
- Novelty is incremental: Related methods jointly embed words/entities (e.g., Wang et al.) or learn concept embeddings from sequences (De Vine; Choi). The compositionality regularizers (β, η) often help little (optimal values near zero), weakening the methodological novelty.
- Crowdsourced dataset quality: The new entity dataset has low agreement (Fleiss’ κ≈0.24) and modest size, which constrains its reliability as an evaluation benchmark.

Questions to authors
- Can you quantify the impact of phrase-to-multiple-concept ambiguity (e.g., ablations with heuristic disambiguation or priors over senses)?
- Did you test statistical significance between top systems on UMNSRS and MayoSRS? If not, please provide.
- Can you report extrinsic results (e.g., concept normalization, entity linking) to substantiate usefulness?
- How does performance vary with the ≥25-frequency threshold; does lowering it improve coverage without degrading quality too much?
