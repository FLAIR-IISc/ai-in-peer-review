Summary and claimed contributions
The paper proposes a weakly supervised method to jointly learn embeddings for ontology concepts, their representative phrases, and words. The key idea is to use ontology-provided phrase lists as distant supervision: phrases in raw text are greedily detected and replaced with unique identifiers, and a skip-gram-with-negative-sampling objective is adapted to learn three embedding matrices (words, phrases, concepts) from parallel tagged/untagged corpora. Two regularizers control compositionality between words→phrases (β) and phrases→concepts (η). The approach is evaluated on biomedical (UMLS) and general-domain (YAGO) concepts across PubMed abstracts, clinical notes, and Wikipedia+NYT.

As seen here, the main contributions are:
1) A scalable, annotation-free framework for jointly embedding concepts, phrases, and words using distant supervision from ontology phrase lists, with competitive performance on concept similarity/relatedness tasks and substantial coverage gains.
2) A new human-annotated dataset for similarity and relatedness of general-domain entities (688 pairs per task), plus release of software (cui2vec).
3) Analyses of compositionality and semantic-type clustering that probe how concepts, phrases, and words are organized in the learned space.

Strengths
- Practical, scalable alternative to annotation-heavy concept embeddings. In contrast to approaches relying on manually labeled mentions or knowledge-graph relations, this method trains on raw text using only ontology string inventories. Coverage is substantially higher: e.g., PM+CL models include ~193k UMLS concepts versus 52k in De Vine and 15k in Choi, more than 3x in vocabulary size without human annotation.
- Competitive intrinsic performance where it matters most (larger biomedical datasets). On UMNSRS Similarity, CONCEPTco reaches 0.653 Spearman, matching the best word baseline (CHIU-30, 0.653) and outperforming prior concept embeddings (e.g., DEVINE 0.455, MENCIA 0.565). On UMNSRS Relatedness, performance is competitive (CONCEPTco 0.559; PHRASEco 0.591 ~ CHIU-30 0.590).
- Cross-domain generalization. The method transfers from biomedical to general entities. On the new YAGO-based datasets, the jointly trained representations perform well (e.g., CONCEPTco 0.789 relatedness, 0.750 similarity; APPROXPHRco even higher at 0.810/0.766), indicating robustness beyond a single ontology/domain.
- Careful baselines and ablations clarifying where gains arise. Comparisons across WORDind/WORDco, PHRASEco, concept approximations, and multiple prior biomedical concept embeddings help isolate the effect of joint training and distant supervision. The observation that APPROXPHRco is often strong is informative for practitioners.
- Useful analyses and resources. The compositionality and neighborhood MAP analyses provide insight into how phrase-word and concept-phrase relationships are reflected. Semantic-type clustering (Table 6) is competitive in several types and highlights remaining challenges (e.g., “sign or symptom”). Release of software and the entity similarity/relatedness dataset aids reproducibility and future research.

Weaknesses
- Limited use of ontology structure beyond phrase inventories. The method ignores relations/types except indirectly via phrase lists. Prior work that integrates graph structure (e.g., Wang et al., 2014a; Hu et al., 2015) leverages additional non-lexical information. The paper itself notes that non-lexical concept information may be under-captured.
- Reliance on intrinsic evaluations and potential tuning-on-test. Most evidence comes from similarity/relatedness tasks; Table 3 hyperparameters are tuned on these very datasets (mitigated by adapted cross-validation, but still a limitation). No downstream tasks (e.g., entity linking, concept normalization, code prediction) are presented to demonstrate utility beyond intrinsic correlation.
- Distant supervision ambiguity may dilute concept signals. The assumption that any phrase occurrence signals all its candidate concepts is strong. While updates are normalized by 1/|Cp|, the impact of polysemous phrases on concept quality is not quantified (e.g., frequency of multi-mapping, ablation without normalization). The concept–phrase MAP distributions (Figure 4a skewed low) suggest limited proximity between concepts and their individual phrases.
- Compositionality regularizers seem underutilized. Tuned β, η are frequently zero (Table 3), and the supplementary analysis reports performance trending negative as these increase. This weakens the case that the joint learning with explicit compositionality constraints is a key driver of gains; strong performance of concept approximations reinforces this concern.
- New entity datasets have fair agreement only. Fleiss’ κ ≈ 0.24 indicates notable annotator noise, and dataset size is moderate (688 pairs). While valuable as first steps, claims about general-domain relatedness should be tempered, or complemented with stricter quality control or expert annotations.

Questions to authors
- How frequent are phrases that map to multiple concepts in each corpus, and how sensitive are results to this ambiguity? Please report the distribution of |Cp| and an ablation where multi-mapping phrases are excluded or downweighted differently.
- How does the greedy phrase matching handle overlapping/nested phrases, and what is the measured precision/recall of phrase detection against a standard tagger on a small annotated subset?
- Could concept/type information from the ontology (e.g., hierarchical parents) be incorporated via a simple auxiliary loss, and would that change the observed concept–phrase MAP distribution?
- For the general-domain datasets, can inter-annotator reliability be improved (e.g., stricter qualification, more annotators per item), and do results hold under alternative aggregation (e.g., MACE)?

Suggestions for improvement
- Add at least one downstream evaluation where concept-level semantics matter (e.g., clinical concept normalization, entity linking, concept-based retrieval). Even a small, controlled study would strengthen the case that intrinsic gains translate to applications.
- Incorporate lightweight ontology-structure signals (parent-child proximity, sibling separation, relation-based negative sampling) as an auxiliary objective. This is feasible within the current training loop and may capture non-lexical properties missing from context-only training.
- Provide quantitative analysis of the distant supervision noise: fraction of ambiguous phrases, their contribution to updates, and error analyses where concept approximations outperform direct concept embeddings.
- Report dev/test splits or a leave-dataset-out protocol for hyperparameter selection to avoid tuning on test items in intrinsic tasks.
- Explore multi-sense phrase embeddings or context-dependent disambiguation for highly polysemous phrases, which could reduce concept signal dilution without requiring manual annotation.

Overall evaluation
This is a solid, well-executed contribution that demonstrates a practical, scalable path to concept embeddings with competitive quality and markedly higher coverage, accompanied by useful analyses and resources. Addressing evaluation breadth (downstream tasks) and modestly integrating ontology structure would significantly strengthen the work.
