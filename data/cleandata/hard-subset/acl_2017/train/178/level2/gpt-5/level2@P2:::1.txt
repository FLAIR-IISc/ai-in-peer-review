Summary and contributions
This paper proposes a weakly supervised method to jointly learn embeddings for ontology concepts, their representative phrases, and words from unannotated text. The approach replaces occurrences of mapped phrases with unique identifiers, learns skip-gram embeddings over parallel tagged/untagged corpora, and updates concept vectors using the contexts of their phrases. Optional regularizers encourage phrase-word and concept-phrase compositionality. The method is evaluated on biomedical (UMLS) and general-domain (YAGO) concepts, showing competitive intrinsic similarity/relatedness performance to methods requiring manual annotations, while scaling to much larger vocabularies. The authors also release embeddings, code (cui2vec), and a new entity similarity/relatedness dataset.

As I see them, the main contributions are:
1) A simple, scalable weakly supervised framework for jointly embedding concepts, phrases, and words without manual corpus annotation, with competitive intrinsic performance and substantially larger coverage.
2) An empirical study across multiple corpora and domains, including analyses of compositionality and semantic type clustering that illuminate what the model captures and where it falls short.
3) A new general-domain entity similarity/relatedness dataset and resource release.

Strengths
- Practical, annotation-light approach with strong coverage: The distant supervision from ontology phrase mappings removes the need for manual corpus annotation or high-quality entity linking, yet yields concept embeddings competitive with prior biomedical methods (e.g., on UMNSRS Similarity) and greatly expands coverage (3x+) compared to code-based embeddings. This addresses a real bottleneck in concept embedding work.
- Joint space for concepts, phrases, and words: Learning all three levels in one space is useful for bridging text to canonical concepts. The analyses (e.g., concept–phrase similarity, phrase–word bimodality) provide insight into when multiword expressions are compositional and when they are not.
- Thorough empirical comparisons and sensitivity checks: The paper compares against strong baselines (word-only, concept-only, and prior biomedical concept embeddings), explores multiple corpora, and reports tuned hyperparameters with trends (e.g., higher β/η can hurt). This makes the claims more credible.
- Cross-domain applicability: Demonstrating results on both UMLS and YAGO suggests the approach is not domain-specific and can generalize to real-world entities.
- Resource contribution: Releasing code, embeddings, and an entity similarity/relatedness dataset adds practical value to the community.

Weaknesses
- Limited novelty in modeling and weak handling of ambiguity: The core method is a straightforward adaptation of skip-gram with additional embedding matrices; phrases are greedily matched and any ambiguous phrase occurrence is assumed to instantiate all its candidate concepts uniformly. This introduces substantial noise, especially in YAGO, and likely suppresses potential gains; no context-aware disambiguation or probabilistic assignment is attempted.
- Intrinsic-only evaluation and tuning on the test sets: The main evidence is similarity/relatedness correlations. While the authors acknowledge issues with intrinsic evaluations, there are no downstream tasks (e.g., concept normalization, entity linking, retrieval) to demonstrate utility. Moreover, hyperparameters are tuned via adapted cross-validation on these same datasets, which can inflate reported correlations despite attempts to mitigate.
- New dataset reliability: The Mechanical Turk entity similarity/relatedness datasets have low inter-annotator agreement (κ=0.24). This weakens conclusions in the general domain and suggests the task design or guidelines may need refinement. Also, the selection process (manual seeding by relatedness categories) may bias distributions.
- Mixed evidence that concept embeddings add beyond phrase approximations: In several settings, APPROXPHRco performs on par with or better than CONCEPTco (e.g., YAGO entity tasks), and the optimal β/η are often near zero, suggesting the joint regularizers and the concept layer may contribute less than intended. The concept–phrase approximations being extremely similar indicates the method may not capture non-lexical ontological information.
- Comparability and fairness across baselines: Some strong baselines (e.g., CHOI) rely on different data and supervision; however, claims of coverage and performance would benefit from analyses restricted to shared concept subsets and clearer reporting of overlap. Details like the single negative sampling matrix tied to words (not concepts/phrases) could bias learning and merit justification.

Questions to authors
- How sensitive are results to the assumption of uniform concept assignment for ambiguous phrases? Could you report performance when restricting to unambiguous phrases, or when weighting concepts by simple priors (e.g., frequency/popularity)?
- What is the negative sampling distribution and why is the negative matrix indexed only by words? Have you tried drawing negatives from the union of words/phrases/concepts?
- Can you provide results restricted to the intersection of concepts covered by your embeddings and each baseline (e.g., CHOI, De Vine) to clarify performance vs. coverage trade-offs?
- Do you observe consistent gains on any downstream task (e.g., clinical concept normalization, entity linking) using your concept/phrase embeddings versus word-only?

Additional comments and suggestions
- Consider integrating ontology structure (hierarchy, relations) as an auxiliary loss or via retrofitting to capture non-lexical semantics and improve semantic type clustering.
- Explore lightweight disambiguation (e.g., attention over candidate concepts conditioned on context, EM over sense assignments) to reduce distant supervision noise.
- Clarify notation and implementation details: Equation (4) uses Cs vs. Cp; please fix typos. Specify phrase-matching strategy for overlaps, case normalization, and tokenization. Report training time/memory and frequency threshold effects.
- For the new dataset, provide annotation guidelines, more examples, and a higher-agreement subset. If possible, add expert annotations or adjudication, and release raw judgments with recommended splits to avoid tuning on test data.
- Add qualitative case studies (nearest neighbors, error analyses) and an ablation isolating the effect of β and η, as well as using only TT vs. TU contexts.

Overall, this is a solid, pragmatic contribution with strong coverage and competitive intrinsic results, but the methodological novelty is modest and the main empirical evidence rests on intrinsic metrics and a low-IAA dataset. Strengthening disambiguation, leveraging ontology structure, and adding downstream evaluations would significantly improve the paper.
