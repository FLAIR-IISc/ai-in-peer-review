**Paper Review**

**Summary of the Paper**

The paper proposes a novel method for jointly learning concept, phrase, and word embeddings from an unlabeled text corpus using distant supervision from phrases linked to concepts in an ontology. The authors evaluate their method on biomedical and general-domain concepts, demonstrating competitive performance with existing methods that require human-annotated data.

**Main Contributions**

1. **Joint Embedding of Concepts, Phrases, and Words**: The authors propose a method to jointly embed concepts, phrases, and words into a real-valued space, using structured knowledge from an ontology and an unannotated training corpus.
2. **Distant Supervision**: The authors use representative phrases for ontology concepts as distant supervision to train the embeddings, eliminating the need for human-annotated data.
3. **Competitive Performance**: The authors demonstrate that their method achieves competitive performance with existing methods on concept similarity and relatedness tasks, while requiring no human corpus annotation and demonstrating more than 3x coverage in the vocabulary size.

**Strengths**

1. **Novel Method**: The authors propose a novel method for jointly learning concept, phrase, and word embeddings, which is a significant contribution to the field of natural language processing.
2. **Competitive Performance**: The authors demonstrate that their method achieves competitive performance with existing methods, which is a significant achievement given the lack of human-annotated data.
3. **Scalability**: The authors demonstrate that their method can scale to a much larger vocabulary without additional expense, which is a significant advantage over existing methods.

**Weaknesses**

1. **Lack of Human-annotated Data**: While the authors demonstrate that their method can achieve competitive performance without human-annotated data, the lack of human-annotated data may still be a limitation in certain applications.
2. **Dependence on Ontology**: The authors' method relies on the quality of the ontology used to provide distant supervision, which may be a limitation in certain applications.
3. **Hyperparameter Tuning**: The authors' method requires hyperparameter tuning, which may be time-consuming and require significant expertise.

**Questions to Authors**

1. How do the authors plan to address the limitation of relying on the quality of the ontology used to provide distant supervision?
2. How do the authors plan to extend their method to other applications, such as entity linking or question answering?
3. Can the authors provide more details on the hyperparameter tuning process and how they selected the optimal hyperparameters for each corpus?

**Additional Comments**

The authors' method is a significant contribution to the field of natural language processing, and their demonstration of competitive performance without human-annotated data is a significant achievement. However, the authors should consider addressing the limitations of their method, such as the reliance on the quality of the ontology and the need for hyperparameter tuning.
