**Summary of the Paper**

This paper proposes a novel method for jointly learning concept, phrase, and word embeddings from an unlabeled text corpus using representative phrases for ontology concepts as distant supervision. The authors demonstrate that their embeddings show performance competitive with existing methods on concept similarity and relatedness tasks, while requiring no human corpus annotation and demonstrating more than 3x coverage in the vocabulary size.

**Main Contributions**

1. A weakly-supervised method for jointly embedding concepts, phrases, and words into a real-valued space using structured knowledge from an ontology and an unannotated training corpus.
2. A novel approach to training concept embeddings using the contexts of their phrase forms, without requiring human annotations.
3. Experimental evaluation on biomedical and general-domain concepts, demonstrating competitive performance with existing methods that require human-annotated data.

**Strengths**

1. The proposed method is able to capture semantic characteristics of ontology concepts nearly as well as prior methods that require significantly more annotation.
2. The approach is scalable to a much larger vocabulary without additional expense.
3. The authors provide a thorough evaluation of their method, including comparisons with existing methods and analysis of the embedding space.

**Weaknesses**

1. The method relies on the quality of the ontology and the representative phrases, which may not always be accurate or comprehensive.
2. The authors note that some non-lexical information about concepts is not being captured in their method, and suggest that leveraging an approach similar to Wang et al. (2014a) may be one way to model some of this additional information.
3. The evaluation is limited to similarity and relatedness tasks, and the authors acknowledge that the method may not generalize well to other downstream applications.

**Questions to Authors**

1. Can you provide more details on how you selected the representative phrases for the ontology concepts, and how you handled cases where multiple phrases were available for a single concept?
2. How did you determine the optimal hyperparameter settings for your method, and are there any plans to explore more advanced hyperparameter tuning techniques?
3. Have you considered applying your method to other domains or ontologies, and if so, what challenges or opportunities do you foresee?

**Additional Comments**

The paper is well-written and clearly presents the proposed method and its evaluation. The authors provide a thorough discussion of the strengths and limitations of their approach, and suggest avenues for future work. The inclusion of a novel dataset of similarity and relatedness of real-world entities is a valuable contribution to the field. Overall, the paper makes a strong contribution to the area of concept embeddings and weakly-supervised learning.
