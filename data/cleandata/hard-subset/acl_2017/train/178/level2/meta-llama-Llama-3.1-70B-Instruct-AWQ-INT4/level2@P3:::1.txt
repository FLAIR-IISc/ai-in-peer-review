**Summary of the Paper**

This paper proposes a novel method for jointly embedding concepts, phrases, and words into a shared vector space using distant supervision from phrases linked to concepts in an ontology. The method, called cui2vec, is evaluated on various biomedical and general-domain datasets and shows competitive performance with state-of-the-art methods that require human-annotated data.

**Main Contributions**

1. **Joint Embedding of Concepts, Phrases, and Words**: The paper proposes a novel method for jointly embedding concepts, phrases, and words into a shared vector space, which is a significant contribution to the field of natural language processing.
2. **Distant Supervision**: The method uses distant supervision from phrases linked to concepts in an ontology, which eliminates the need for human-annotated data.
3. **Competitive Performance**: The method shows competitive performance with state-of-the-art methods on various biomedical and general-domain datasets.

**Strengths**

1. **Novel Method**: The paper proposes a novel method for jointly embedding concepts, phrases, and words, which is a significant contribution to the field of natural language processing.
2. **Effective Use of Distant Supervision**: The method effectively uses distant supervision from phrases linked to concepts in an ontology, which eliminates the need for human-annotated data.
3. **Competitive Performance**: The method shows competitive performance with state-of-the-art methods on various biomedical and general-domain datasets.

**Weaknesses**

1. **Limited Evaluation**: The paper only evaluates the method on a limited number of datasets, which may not be representative of all possible use cases.
2. **Lack of Theoretical Analysis**: The paper does not provide a theoretical analysis of the method, which may make it difficult to understand why it works and how it can be improved.
3. **Hyperparameter Tuning**: The paper uses hyperparameter tuning to optimize the performance of the method, which may not be practical for all use cases.

**Questions to Authors**

1. **How does the method handle polysemy and homophony in concepts and phrases?**
2. **Can the method be extended to handle more complex relationships between concepts and phrases, such as hierarchical relationships?**
3. **How does the method compare to other state-of-the-art methods in terms of computational efficiency and scalability?**

**Additional Comments**

The paper is well-written and easy to follow, with clear explanations of the method and its evaluation. The use of figures and tables to illustrate the results is also helpful. However, the paper could benefit from a more detailed theoretical analysis of the method and a more comprehensive evaluation on a wider range of datasets.
