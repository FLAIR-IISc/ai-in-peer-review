**Summary of the Paper**

This paper proposes a novel method for jointly learning concept, phrase, and word embeddings from an unlabeled text corpus using representative phrases for ontology concepts as distant supervision. The authors adapt the skip-gram with negative sampling variant of word2vec to train embeddings for medical concepts in the Unified Medical Language System and general-domain concepts in YAGO. The method requires no human annotations and demonstrates competitive performance with existing methods on concept similarity and relatedness tasks.

**Main Contributions**

1. **Joint Embedding of Concepts, Phrases, and Words**: The authors propose a novel method for jointly embedding concepts, phrases, and words into a shared vector space, which allows for the capture of semantic relationships between these entities.
2. **Distant Supervision**: The method uses representative phrases for ontology concepts as distant supervision, eliminating the need for human annotations.
3. **Competitive Performance**: The authors demonstrate that their method achieves competitive performance with existing methods on concept similarity and relatedness tasks.

**Strengths**

1. **Novel Approach**: The authors propose a novel approach to jointly embedding concepts, phrases, and words, which is a significant contribution to the field of natural language processing.
2. **Effective Use of Distant Supervision**: The method effectively uses representative phrases for ontology concepts as distant supervision, which reduces the need for human annotations.
3. **Competitive Performance**: The authors demonstrate that their method achieves competitive performance with existing methods on concept similarity and relatedness tasks.

**Weaknesses**

1. **Limited Evaluation**: The authors only evaluate their method on a limited set of datasets, which may not be representative of the broader range of applications.
2. **Lack of Comparison to Other Methods**: The authors do not compare their method to other joint embedding methods, which makes it difficult to assess the relative strengths and weaknesses of their approach.
3. **Limited Analysis of Hyperparameters**: The authors do not provide a detailed analysis of the hyperparameters used in their method, which may impact the performance of the model.

**Questions to Authors**

1. How do the authors plan to extend their method to other domains and applications?
2. Can the authors provide a more detailed analysis of the hyperparameters used in their method and their impact on performance?
3. How do the authors plan to address the issue of polysemy in their method, where a single word or phrase can have multiple meanings?

**Additional Comments**

The authors provide a clear and well-written paper that effectively communicates their novel approach to jointly embedding concepts, phrases, and words. The use of distant supervision is a significant contribution to the field, and the competitive performance of their method is impressive. However, the authors could benefit from a more detailed analysis of the hyperparameters used in their method and a comparison to other joint embedding methods.
