## Review of "A Weakly-Supervised Method for Jointly Embedding Concepts, Phrases, and Words"

This paper proposes a novel weakly-supervised method for jointly learning embeddings of concepts, phrases, and words, leveraging representative phrases for ontology concepts as distant supervision. The core idea of utilizing phrase occurrences to train concept embeddings, without requiring manual annotation, is promising. The authors evaluate their approach on biomedical and general-domain datasets, demonstrating competitive performance on concept similarity and relatedness tasks, while significantly increasing vocabulary coverage.

The primary contribution of this work is the proposed weakly-supervised method itself. The approach of using phrases as a bridge between concepts and words, coupled with the tunable parameters (β and η) to control compositionality, is a valuable contribution. A second, important contribution is the novel dataset of similarity and relatedness of real-world entities, which fills a gap in available resources for evaluating such models. Finally, the analysis demonstrating that the source ontology structure is reflected in the learned embeddings – concepts and phrases clustering together – is insightful. 

The strengths of this paper lie in its practical approach to a challenging problem, its solid experimental evaluation, and the introduction of a new dataset. The results on existing datasets are competitive, and the significant increase in vocabulary size achieved by the method is notable. The ablation studies, while not extensive, demonstrate the benefit of the proposed approach over baseline approximations. The detailed description of the experimental setup and data preprocessing is commendable.

However, the paper has several weaknesses. The performance gains over strong baselines (e.g., Choi et al., 2016b) are not always substantial and can be dataset-dependent. The analysis of the learned embeddings, while interesting, could be further expanded – a visualization of the embedding space would be beneficial. The impact of the hyperparameters β and η is not fully explained, and their optimal values seem highly dependent on the specific corpus. The discussion of related work, while thorough, does not adequately contextualize the novelty of the proposed approach beyond stating that it doesn’t require manual annotations. A more in-depth comparison to existing knowledge graph embedding techniques would be beneficial.

**Questions to Authors:**

1.  Could you provide a visual representation (e.g., t-SNE plot) of the learned embeddings to demonstrate the clustering of concepts and phrases?
2.  Can you elaborate on the sensitivity of the results to the choice of hyperparameters, particularly β and η? Are there any guidelines for setting these parameters based on the characteristics of the corpus?
3.  How does this approach compare to existing knowledge graph embedding techniques (e.g., TransE, RESCAL) in terms of performance and scalability?

**Additional Comments:**

The paper is well-written and generally easy to follow. The inclusion of a software implementation and the new dataset are valuable additions to the research community. Despite some weaknesses, the paper presents a promising approach to learning concept embeddings and is worth considering for acceptance. I would encourage the authors to address the weaknesses and questions raised in the review through revisions.
