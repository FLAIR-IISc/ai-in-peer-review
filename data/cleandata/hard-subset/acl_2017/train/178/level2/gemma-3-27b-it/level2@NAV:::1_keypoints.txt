- Proposes a weakly-supervised method to jointly learn embeddings of concepts, phrases, and words using representative phrases as distant supervision (no manual annotation).  
- Core idea: train concept embeddings from phrase occurrences with tunable parameters β and η to control compositionality.  
- Evaluated on biomedical and general-domain datasets, showing competitive concept similarity/relatedness performance and much larger vocabulary coverage.  
- Main contribution: the weakly-supervised embedding method itself.  
- Secondary contribution: a novel dataset of real-world entity similarity and relatedness.  
- Analysis shows learned embeddings reflect ontology structure, with concepts and phrases clustering together.  
- Strength: practical approach with solid experimental evaluation and notable vocabulary increase.  
- Strength: ablation studies indicate benefit over baseline approximations; experimental setup and preprocessing are well-documented.  
- Weakness: performance gains over strong baselines (e.g., Choi et al., 2016b) are modest and dataset-dependent.  
- Weakness: embedding analysis could be deeper; lacks visualization (e.g., t-SNE) to illustrate clustering.  
- Weakness: impact and optimal settings of hyperparameters β and η are not fully explained and appear corpus-dependent.  
- Weakness: related work discussion doesn’t fully contextualize novelty; missing in-depth comparison to knowledge graph embedding methods.  
- Question: provide a visual representation (e.g., t-SNE) of learned embeddings to demonstrate clustering.  
- Question: elaborate on sensitivity and guidelines for choosing β and η based on corpus characteristics.  
- Question: compare the approach to knowledge graph embedding techniques (e.g., TransE, RESCAL) in performance and scalability.  
- Additional comment: paper is well-written; provided software and dataset are valuable; authors should address noted weaknesses in revision.
