This paper proposes a weakly-supervised method for jointly embedding concepts, phrases, and words, leveraging representative phrases for ontology concepts as a form of distant supervision. The core idea is to train embeddings using the contexts of phrase forms of concepts, without requiring manual annotation. The authors evaluate their approach on biomedical and general-domain datasets, demonstrating competitive performance on concept similarity and relatedness tasks.

**Contributions:**

1. **Joint Embedding Framework:** The primary contribution is a novel framework for jointly learning embeddings of concepts, phrases, and words using distant supervision. This allows for representing concepts regardless of their specific textual form, addressing a limitation of solely word-based semantic models.
2. **Weakly-Supervised Approach:** The method eliminates the need for expensive manual annotation of concepts, making it scalable to large ontologies and corpora. This is a significant advantage over previous work that relied on human-annotated data.
3. **Evaluation Datasets & Analysis:** The paper includes a new dataset for evaluating similarity and relatedness of real-world entities, alongside comprehensive analyses of embedding quality, including an examination of how ontological structure is reflected in the embedding space and compositionality analysis.

**Strengths:**

1. **Practicality and Scalability:** The weakly-supervised nature of the method is a major strength.  Avoiding manual annotation drastically reduces the cost and effort required to build concept embeddings, particularly for large and complex ontologies like UMLS.
2. **Competitive Performance:** The results demonstrate that the proposed method achieves performance comparable to existing methods that *do* require human annotation. This is particularly impressive given the reliance on distant supervision.  The performance on the larger datasets (UMNSRS, the newly created general-domain datasets) is particularly noteworthy.
3. **Comprehensive Evaluation:** The authors go beyond standard similarity/relatedness tasks and provide insightful analyses of the learned embeddings. The assessment of ontological structure preservation and phrase/word compositionality provides valuable understanding of the model’s behavior.
4. **Novel Dataset:** The creation and release of a new dataset for evaluating the similarity and relatedness of real-world entities is a valuable contribution to the community. It addresses a gap in available resources for evaluating this type of embedding.
5. **Clear Presentation and Implementation:** The paper is well-written and the method is clearly explained. The authors also provide a software implementation of their method, facilitating reproducibility and further research.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Sensitivity to Phrase Mapping:** The performance of the method heavily relies on the quality and coverage of the mapping between concepts and phrases in the ontology. A poor or incomplete mapping could introduce noise and limit the effectiveness of the distant supervision.  The paper does mention restricting to phrases appearing >= 25 times, but a more detailed discussion of the impact of this threshold, and potential strategies for handling less frequent phrases, would be beneficial.
2. **Limited Compositionality Gains:** While the authors introduce hyperparameters (β and η) to control compositionality, the results suggest limited gains in performance from leveraging this feature. Further investigation into more sophisticated compositionality models may be warranted.
3. **Negative Sampling Strategy:** The paper doesn't explore alternative negative sampling strategies beyond the standard approach. Given the unique challenges of embedding concepts, phrases, and words together, investigating more informed negative sampling techniques could potentially improve performance.
4. **Hyperparameter Tuning Granularity:** While the authors perform hyperparameter tuning, the space explored appears somewhat limited. A more exhaustive search, potentially using automated hyperparameter optimization techniques, could uncover better settings.
5. **Dataset Bias:** The evaluation on the real-world entity dataset relies on Mechanical Turk.  The potential for bias in crowdsourced data (e.g., demographic skew, subjective interpretations) should be acknowledged and potentially mitigated through careful data analysis and participant filtering.

**Questions to Authors:**

1. How sensitive is the performance of the method to the quality and completeness of the concept-phrase mapping in the ontology? Could you provide a quantitative analysis of this sensitivity (e.g., by evaluating on subsets of the data with varying mapping coverage)?
2. The compositionality hyperparameters (β and η) seem to have limited impact.  Could you elaborate on potential reasons for this and discuss possible avenues for improving compositionality modeling within the proposed framework?
3. Have you considered exploring more sophisticated negative sampling strategies tailored to the joint embedding of concepts, phrases, and words?
4. What steps were taken to address potential biases in the Mechanical Turk dataset used for evaluating real-world entity similarity and relatedness?

**Additional Comments:**

* It would be helpful to include a more detailed discussion of the computational complexity of the proposed method, especially in comparison to existing approaches.
* The paper could benefit from a more in-depth comparison to related work, specifically highlighting the unique advantages of the proposed method over existing knowledge graph embedding techniques.
* While the inclusion of the software implementation is appreciated, providing example usage and more detailed documentation would further enhance its accessibility.
