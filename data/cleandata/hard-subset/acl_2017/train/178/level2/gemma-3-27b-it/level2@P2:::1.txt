This paper proposes a weakly-supervised method for jointly embedding concepts, phrases, and words, leveraging representative phrases for ontology concepts as distant supervision. The core idea is to adapt word2vec to simultaneously learn embeddings for concepts, phrases, and words from unlabeled text, using ontology knowledge to link concepts to their textual representations. The authors evaluate their approach on biomedical and general-domain concept similarity and relatedness tasks, demonstrating competitive performance with existing methods while avoiding costly human annotation.

**Contributions:**

1. **Joint Embedding Framework:** The primary contribution is the proposed framework for jointly embedding concepts, phrases, and words. This allows for capturing semantic relationships across different levels of granularity without relying on manual annotations.  The adaptation of word2vec for this purpose is a straightforward yet effective approach.
2. **Distant Supervision with Ontology Knowledge:**  Using representative phrases from ontologies (UMLS, YAGO) as distant supervision is a valuable technique. This bridges the gap between structured knowledge and distributional semantics, enabling the learning of concept embeddings from unlabeled text.
3. **Novel Evaluation Datasets:** The creation and release of novel datasets for evaluating the similarity and relatedness of real-world entities is a positive contribution.  These datasets fill a gap in the existing evaluation landscape.

**Strengths:**

1. **Competitive Performance:** The experimental results demonstrate that the proposed method achieves performance comparable to state-of-the-art methods on concept similarity and relatedness tasks, despite not requiring any human annotations. This is a significant achievement, particularly in domains like biomedicine where annotation is expensive.
2. **Scalability:** The method’s reliance on distant supervision makes it highly scalable to large ontologies and corpora. The reported coverage in vocabulary size (3x compared to existing methods) highlights this advantage.
3. **Clear Presentation:** The paper is well-written and clearly explains the methodology and experimental setup. The figures and tables are informative and easy to understand. The connection between the method and existing work is thoroughly discussed.
4. **Reproducibility:**  The authors explicitly state they are releasing code and datasets, facilitating reproducibility and further research.
5. **Insightful Analysis:** The analysis of concept/phrase/word relationships and the semantic type clustering provides valuable insights into the learned embeddings and validates the approach's ability to capture ontological structure.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Novelty of the Core Technique:** While the application to concept embedding is well-executed, the core technique of adapting word2vec with distant supervision is not entirely novel. Existing work (Wang et al., 2014a) has explored similar ideas. The paper would benefit from a more nuanced discussion of the differences and improvements offered by their approach.
2. **Sensitivity to Phrase Mapping Quality:** The performance of the method is heavily reliant on the quality of the mapping between concepts and phrases in the ontologies.  The authors acknowledge this, but a more in-depth analysis of the impact of imperfect mappings would be beneficial. Do some concepts have poor representative phrases, and how does this affect embedding quality?
3. **Fleiss' Kappa Value:**  The Fleiss' kappa value for the new datasets (0.24) indicates only fair agreement between annotators. While acceptable, a discussion of potential sources of disagreement and their impact on the results is warranted.  Was there ambiguity in the entities or the task instructions?
4. **Hyperparameter Tuning & Generalization:** While the authors perform hyperparameter tuning, the tuned hyperparameters appear to be corpus-specific.  There's limited discussion of how robust the performance is across different corpora or if transfer learning is possible.
5. **Modest Improvements:** While competitive, the performance gains over strong baselines are sometimes modest. A deeper analysis of the cases where the proposed method outperforms baselines could reveal valuable insights.

**Questions to Authors:**

1. How sensitive is the performance of the method to the quality and completeness of the concept-phrase mappings in the ontologies?  Could you provide an ablation study or analysis of concepts with poor phrase mappings?
2. Could you discuss the potential for transfer learning across corpora?  For example, can embeddings trained on PubMed be effectively used for tasks in the clinical domain?
3. What steps were taken to mitigate potential biases in the Mechanical Turk annotation process for the new datasets?  Were any quality control measures implemented beyond the validation questions?
4. The introduction of β and η parameters seems promising. Can you provide more intuition as to why setting these to 0 generally yields better results? Were there particular datasets/concept types where these parameters showed more benefit?



**Additional Comments:**

*  Consider adding a discussion of potential applications of the learned embeddings beyond similarity/relatedness tasks, such as concept prediction or relation extraction.
*  The phrase "concept" is used somewhat ambiguously. Explicitly defining the scope of "concept" in the context of the paper (e.g., referring specifically to ontology concepts) would enhance clarity.
*  The reference to Chiu et al., 2016b is missing from the bibliography.
