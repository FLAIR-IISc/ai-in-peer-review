This paper proposes a weakly-supervised method for jointly learning embeddings of concepts, phrases, and words, leveraging representative phrases for ontology concepts as distant supervision. The core idea is to adapt the skip-gram model with negative sampling to train embeddings for concepts, phrases, and words simultaneously, utilizing an unannotated corpus. The primary contributions are a novel training method utilizing distant supervision from ontology phrases, demonstration of competitive performance on concept similarity/relatedness tasks without human annotation, and a 3x increase in vocabulary coverage compared to existing methods.

**Strengths:**

1.  **Novelty of Approach:** The proposed method of utilizing known phrases as distant supervision for concept embedding is a reasonable approach to address the limitations of existing methods relying on expensive annotation or incomplete automated tagging. Adapting word embedding techniques for this purpose provides a practical solution.
2.  **Competitive Performance:** The reported results on concept similarity and relatedness tasks are competitive with existing methods, especially considering the absence of human annotation. This is a significant achievement, demonstrating the effectiveness of the proposed approach. The inclusion of results across multiple corpora and ontologies adds robustness to the findings.
3.  **Increased Vocabulary Coverage:** The reported 3x increase in vocabulary size demonstrates a key advantage of the method. Expanding coverage is crucial for practical applications and addresses a limitation of many existing approaches.
4.  **Comprehensive Evaluation:** The project presents a detailed experimental setup, including various baseline comparisons, hyperparameter tuning, and evaluation metrics. The creation and utilization of a new dataset for general-domain entity similarity and relatedness further strengthens the evaluation.
5.  **Availability of Resources:** The release of both the implementation and the novel dataset is a valuable contribution to the research community, fostering reproducibility and further research in this area.

**Weaknesses:**

1.  **Limited Analysis of Negative Impact of Noisy Supervision:** While the paper acknowledges the noisiness of distant supervision, it lacks a thorough analysis of how this noise affects the resulting embeddings. A more in-depth investigation into the impact of incorrect phrase-concept mappings would strengthen the validity of the findings. Specifically, analyzing how the tuning of hyperparameters addresses this is missing.
2.  **Modest Gains over Strong Baselines:** While competitive, the performance gains over strong baselines, particularly the existing biomedical embedding methods (e.g., Choi et al. 2016b), are relatively small on some datasets. A clearer articulation of the specific scenarios where the proposed method significantly outperforms others would be beneficial.
3.  **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** The paper mentions the importance of hyperparameter tuning, and presents the tuned hyperparameters in a table, but doesn’t adequately discuss the sensitivity of the model to these parameters. The reported results depend heavily on proper tuning, and more insight into the stability and robustness of the method across different hyperparameter settings would be valuable.
4.  **Concept-Phrase Mapping Ambiguity:** The paper assumes a many-to-many relationship between concepts and phrases. The choice of representative phrases and the potential ambiguity in this mapping could introduce noise into the embeddings. A discussion of how different phrase selection strategies might impact performance would be useful.
5.  **Fleiss’ Kappa on New Dataset:** The reported Fleiss’ kappa of 0.24 for the new dataset suggests only "fair agreement" between annotators, raising potential concerns about the reliability of the ground truth labels. A more thorough analysis of the annotation process and potential sources of disagreement would be beneficial.

**Questions to Authors:**

1.  Can you provide a more detailed analysis of the impact of noisy supervision on the quality of the embeddings? Have you explored methods to mitigate the effects of incorrect phrase-concept mappings?
2.  In what specific scenarios or datasets does the proposed method demonstrate a significant advantage over existing biomedical embedding techniques?
3.  How sensitive are the results to the choice of hyperparameters? Have you conducted an ablation study to assess the contribution of the β and η parameters?
4.  Could you elaborate on the criteria used for selecting representative phrases for each concept? How might different phrase selection strategies affect the performance of the model?
5.  What steps were taken to ensure the reliability of the annotations in the new dataset, given the relatively low Fleiss’ kappa value?

**Additional Comments:**

*   The writing is clear and well-organized.
*   The related work section provides a good overview of existing approaches.
*   The inclusion of a publicly available implementation and dataset is a significant contribution.
*   Consider adding visualizations of the learned embeddings to provide further insight into the model’s behavior. For example, t-SNE or PCA plots of concepts and their corresponding phrases.
