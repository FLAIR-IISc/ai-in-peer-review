## Review of "A Weakly-Supervised Method for Jointly Embedding Concepts, Phrases, and Words"

This paper presents a method for jointly embedding concepts, phrases, and words by replacing phrase occurrences with concept identifiers in a corpus and then training embeddings. The approach leverages distant supervision from ontologies (UMLS and YAGO) to avoid the need for manual annotation, a significant advantage. The application is focused on the biomedical domain, and the authors introduce a new dataset for evaluating concept similarity and relatedness. 

The primary contribution of this work, in my view, is the creation of the novel dataset for evaluating real-world entity similarity and relatedness. While the embedding method itself is conceptually straightforward and builds upon existing techniques (word2vec), the lack of comparable datasets for evaluating entity embeddings makes this a valuable resource for the community. The method is well-written and logically presented, and the experimental setup is reasonably thorough.

**Strengths:**

*   **Novel Dataset:** The introduced dataset addresses a clear gap in the literature and provides a valuable resource for evaluating entity embeddings. The process of data collection and filtering is well-described.
*   **Sound Methodology:** The core idea of replacing phrases with concept identifiers for training is a natural and intuitive approach to incorporating structured knowledge into distributional semantics.
*   **Clear Presentation:** The paper is well-organized, clearly written, and easy to follow. The experimental setup and results are presented in a concise and understandable manner.
*   **Biomedical Relevance:** The application to the biomedical domain is well-motivated, given the importance of concepts and ontologies in this field.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Limited Technical Novelty:** The embedding method itself is an extension of existing techniques and doesn’t represent a major technical breakthrough. The core idea is relatively simple and doesn’t introduce significant algorithmic innovation.
*   **Narrow Evaluation Scope:** The evaluation is heavily focused on biomedical data, limiting the generalizability of the findings. While the biomedical application is valuable, it’s unclear how well the method would perform on other domains.
*   **MAP Calculation Issue:** As noted by my supervisor, Eq. 8 (MAP calculation) is incorrectly described as a probability and should not rank the entire vocabulary. A cutoff or correction is needed.
*   **Dataset Discussion:** While the dataset is a strong contribution, the paper could benefit from a more in-depth discussion of its characteristics, limitations, and potential biases.

**Questions to Authors:**

1.  Could you clarify the rationale behind the chosen hyperparameters (β and η) and provide a more detailed analysis of their impact on performance?
2.  The MAP calculation in Eq. 8 appears to be flawed. Could you please correct this equation or provide a justification for the current formulation?
3.  What are the potential biases in your newly created dataset, and how might these biases affect the evaluation results?



**Additional Comments:**

*   Please correct the minor editorial error on line 556 (“most concepts has” → “most concepts have”).
*   It would be beneficial to compare the performance of your method to more recent and state-of-the-art embedding techniques.
