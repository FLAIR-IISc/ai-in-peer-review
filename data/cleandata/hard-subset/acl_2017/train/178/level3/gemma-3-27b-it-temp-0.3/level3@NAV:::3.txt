## Review of "A Weakly-Supervised Method for Jointly Embedding Concepts, Phrases, and Words"

This paper presents a method for jointly embedding concepts, phrases, and words, leveraging distant supervision from ontology phrases within an unlabeled corpus. The authors aim to address the limitations of existing approaches that rely on expensive annotation or ignore textual context. They introduce a model with tunable parameters controlling compositionality and evaluate it on biomedical and general-domain datasets, including a newly created dataset for general-domain entity similarity.

The primary contribution of this work lies in the creation of the new dataset for evaluating similarity and relatedness of real-world entities. While the model itself is relatively straightforward, the dataset fills a gap in available resources and provides a valuable benchmark for future research. A secondary contribution is the exploration of a tunable parameter balancing atomic vs. compositional representation of phrases and concepts, though the analysis of this tradeoff remains limited.

However, I have significant concerns regarding the validity of the results and recommend rejection in its current form, with encouragement for resubmission after substantial revisions. 

**Weaknesses:**

The most critical weakness is the heavy reliance on hyperparameter tuning performed *on* the evaluation datasets themselves. This fundamentally undermines the objectivity of the reported performance gains. The claim of outperforming prior work is therefore weak, as the method is effectively optimized for these specific datasets. 

Furthermore, the evaluation is limited to intrinsic similarity/relatedness tasks. The lack of extrinsic evaluation on downstream tasks prevents assessing the practical utility of the learned embeddings. The authors acknowledge this limitation but do not address it.

The interpretation of the datasets as representing “concept-similarity” is questionable, given that human judgments were elicited on *phrases*. This necessitates treating the datasets as phrase-similarity tasks, which alters the framing of the results. 

The medical evaluation datasets are also problematic. The MayoSRS dataset has low inter-annotator agreement, and the UMNSRS dataset only includes single-word concepts, limiting its relevance to the multi-word concept embedding task.

Finally, the paper lacks deeper analysis. Exploring the interplay between phrase-word compositionality and concept-phrase relations (e.g., headword effects) would provide valuable insights. A more thorough investigation of the atomic vs. compositional hyperparameter tradeoff is also needed.

**Questions to Authors:**

1.  What was the specific methodology used for hyperparameter tuning, and can you provide results on a held-out development set *separate* from the evaluation datasets?
2.  Do you have plans to evaluate the embeddings on downstream tasks to demonstrate their practical utility?
3.  Given that human judgments were elicited on phrases, how does this affect the interpretation of the evaluation results, and how would you reframe the findings accordingly?
