## Review of "A Weakly-Supervised Method for Jointly Embedding Concepts, Phrases, and Words"

This paper proposes a method for jointly learning embeddings of concepts, phrases, and words by leveraging ontology mappings as a form of distant supervision within a skip-gram framework. The core idea – utilizing known phrases to train concept embeddings – is a reasonable approach to address the lack of labeled data for ontology concepts. The authors evaluate their method on concept similarity and relatedness tasks using the UMLS and YAGO ontologies, and release their software, which is a valuable contribution to the biomedical NLP community.

The primary contribution of this work, in my view, is the creation and release of a publicly available implementation of a weakly-supervised concept embedding method, alongside the novel datasets for general-domain entity similarity and relatedness. This will likely be the most impactful aspect of the paper for other researchers. A secondary contribution is the demonstration that a relatively simple adaptation of existing word embedding techniques can achieve competitive performance on concept similarity tasks, particularly in certain settings.

**Strengths:**

*   **Practical Contribution:** The released software (cui2vec) is a significant benefit to the biomedical NLP community, providing a readily available tool for exploring concept embeddings.
*   **Competitive Performance:** The model achieves competitive or superior results on some concept similarity tasks, demonstrating the viability of the approach.
*   **Novel Datasets:** The creation of new datasets for evaluating similarity and relatedness of real-world entities fills a gap in the existing evaluation resources.
*   **Addresses a Key Problem:** The paper tackles the challenge of learning representations for concepts without relying on expensive manual annotation.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Limited Novelty:** The method is largely an adaptation of the skip-gram model and doesn't introduce substantial algorithmic innovation. The core idea of using ontology mappings for distant supervision is not entirely new.
*   **Inconsistent Performance:** The model does not consistently outperform strong baselines. Specifically, the Choi et al. (2016a) baseline significantly outperforms the proposed method on the Mayo datasets, and performs similarly on the UMNSRS dataset. This raises concerns about the generalizability and robustness of the approach.
*   **Questionable Compositionality Control:** The use of a single global β parameter to control compositionality seems overly simplistic. The cross-validation results suggest that compositionality may not be beneficial for all datasets, and a more nuanced approach may be required.
*   **Misleading Terminology:** Describing phrase occurrences as "distant supervision" is inaccurate. While phrases provide context, they don't represent traditional supervised signals.

**Questions to Authors:**

1.  The experimental setup for Table 4 is unclear. How are similarities computed when a concept maps to multiple phrases, and vice versa? Is it an average, maximum, or other aggregation method?
2.  The results in Table 5 suggest that simply averaging phrase vectors (APPROXPHR) performs best. What does this imply about the utility of the concept ontology and the joint embedding process? Is the ontology primarily serving as a source of seed phrases?
3.  Could you elaborate on the rationale behind using a single global β parameter for controlling compositionality? What motivated this design choice, and what are the limitations of this approach?
