Contributions:
- New dataset for evaluating similarity/relatedness of real-world biomedical entities, addressing limitations of word-focused benchmarks.
- Weakly-supervised embedding method that replaces phrases with concept IDs to jointly embed concepts, phrases, and words using ontologies.
- Comprehensive empirical evaluation on established biomedical benchmarks and the newly introduced dataset, demonstrating competitive performance.

Strengths:
- Clear, well-written paper with logical organization and motivation.
- Simple, practical approach requiring no manual annotation and easily adaptable to other domains.
- Thorough evaluation across multiple datasets, including the released dataset.
- Competitive results on biomedical tasks.
- Public release of the dataset and code (“cui2vec”) supports reproducibility.

Weaknesses:
- Contribution is incremental: core idea (phrase→concept ID) builds on existing techniques without a major technical advance.
- Evaluation is heavily biomedical, limiting demonstrated generalizability to other domains.
- Dataset description lacks depth; low Fleiss’ Kappa (0.24) raises concerns about annotation quality and potential biases.
- Technical error in MAP calculation (Eq. 8): MAP is mischaracterized and ranking the entire vocabulary is not a meaningful metric without a cutoff or k-NN formulation.
- Limited analysis of negative results and limited explanation for why certain approximations perform better or worse.

Questions to authors:
- What factors led to the low Fleiss’ Kappa and what quality-control steps were taken for annotations?
- Please clarify and correct the MAP calculation and specify the intended cutoff/k-NN procedure.
- Have you evaluated these embeddings on downstream biomedical NLP tasks (e.g., NER, relation extraction), and if so, what were the results?
- Was incorporating relational information from knowledge bases considered, and if not, why was it not pursued?
