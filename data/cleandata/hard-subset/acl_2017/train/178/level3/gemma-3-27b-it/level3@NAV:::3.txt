## Review of “A Weakly-Supervised Method for Jointly Embedding Concepts, Phrases, and Words”

This paper proposes a method for jointly embedding concepts, phrases, and words by leveraging representative phrases as distant supervision from ontologies like UMLS and YAGO. The approach aims to overcome the limitations of existing methods that rely on expensive annotation or ignore textual context. The authors present a model, a new evaluation dataset for general-domain entities, and a series of experiments demonstrating competitive performance on similarity/relatedness tasks. 

The primary contribution of this work, in my view, is the creation of the new dataset for evaluating similarity and relatedness between general-domain entities. Existing datasets are limited, and having a resource for evaluating on this task is valuable for the community. A secondary contribution lies in the overall framework for jointly embedding concepts, phrases, and words, despite its conceptual simplicity. The exploration of the atomic vs. compositional tradeoff, while limited, is a positive step.

However, this submission suffers from several critical weaknesses that currently preclude acceptance. The most significant is the lack of extrinsic task evaluation. Assessing performance solely on intrinsic similarity/relatedness tasks provides limited insight into the utility of the learned embeddings in real-world applications. Furthermore, the evaluation treats the datasets as phrase-similarity rather than concept-similarity, given that human judgments were elicited on phrases. This fundamentally impacts the interpretation of the results.

The medical evaluation datasets used are also problematic. The MayoSRS dataset is very small, and the reported low agreement on MayoSRS raises concerns about its reliability. The UMNSRS dataset only contains single-word concepts, which doesn't align with the paper’s focus on multi-word concepts. Moreover, the extensive hyperparameter tuning performed on the *same* datasets used for evaluation severely compromises the validity of the reported results. The claim of outperforming prior work is weakened by the continued reliance on large, manually curated ontologies and pre-existing clinical record annotations.

Finally, the analysis lacks depth. There's a missed opportunity to separate phrase-word compositionality from concept-phrase relationships (e.g., exploring headword effects) and to more thoroughly investigate the impact of the atomic vs. compositional hyperparameter settings.

**Recommendation: Reject.** The authors should significantly improve the evaluation methodology – including at least one extrinsic task – and address the concerns regarding dataset validity and hyperparameter tuning before resubmission.



**Questions to Authors:**

1.  What is the rationale for choosing the specific hyperparameters (β, η) during tuning, and how sensitive are the results to their selection?
2.  Could you provide a discussion on the potential biases introduced by using human judgments on phrases when evaluating concept similarity?
3. What experiments have been run, or are planned, to assess the performance of these embeddings on downstream tasks?
