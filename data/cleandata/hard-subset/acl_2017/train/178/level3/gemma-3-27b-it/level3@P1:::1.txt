This paper proposes a weakly-supervised method for jointly embedding concepts, phrases, and words, leveraging representative phrases for ontology concepts as a form of distant supervision. The core idea is to replace phrases in a corpus with unique concept identifiers, creating a parallel corpus used to train embeddings with a modified skip-gram model. The method is applied to biomedical concepts from the Unified Medical Language System (UMLS) and general-domain concepts from YAGO, demonstrating competitive performance on concept similarity and relatedness tasks compared to existing methods, while requiring no human annotation and achieving a wider vocabulary coverage. A novel dataset for evaluating similarity and relatedness of real-world entities is also introduced.

The primary contribution of this work lies in the practical demonstration of a simple yet effective technique for bridging the gap between lexical and conceptual representations without relying on expensive manual annotation. While the core idea of using distant supervision is not novel, the application to jointly embedding concepts, phrases, and words, coupled with the scaling to large ontologies and corpora, constitutes a valuable contribution. The second noteworthy contribution is the introduction of a new dataset for evaluating similarity and relatedness between real-world entities, filling a gap in existing resources. Finally, the extensive experimental evaluation across various corpora and ontologies provides a solid empirical basis for the proposed approach.

**Strengths:**

1. **Practicality and Scalability:** The method is conceptually straightforward and computationally efficient, making it practical for large-scale applications. The authors successfully apply their method to large ontologies (UMLS, YAGO) and corpora (PubMed, clinical narratives, Wikipedia), demonstrating its scalability. This is a significant advantage over methods that rely on extensive manual annotation.
2. **Competitive Performance:** The proposed method achieves performance comparable to state-of-the-art methods on concept similarity and relatedness tasks, despite not requiring any human annotation. This demonstrates the effectiveness of the distant supervision approach. The results suggest that leveraging existing ontological resources can yield high-quality conceptual representations.
3. **Novel Dataset:** The introduction of a new dataset for evaluating the similarity and relatedness of real-world entities is a valuable contribution to the field. This dataset addresses a gap in existing resources and provides a benchmark for evaluating and comparing different embedding methods. The process of collecting and validating the data is clearly described.
4. **Thorough Evaluation:** The paper presents a comprehensive evaluation of the proposed method, including comparisons to various baselines and ablation studies. The results are presented in a clear and organized manner, allowing for easy interpretation and comparison. The hyperparameter tuning process is also described in detail.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Novelty:** While the combination of techniques is effective, the underlying principles (distant supervision, word embeddings) are well-established. The core technical innovation is relatively incremental and does not represent a major breakthrough. The method builds upon existing techniques rather than proposing a fundamentally new approach.
2. **Narrow Focus of Evaluation:** The primary evaluation is focused on biomedical data. While this is a relevant domain, the generalizability of the findings to other domains is not fully established. Further evaluation on more diverse datasets would strengthen the claims of the paper.
3. **Dataset Discussion:** While the new dataset is a valuable contribution, the paper lacks a deeper discussion of its characteristics and potential biases. More analysis of the dataset, such as the distribution of similarity scores and the types of entities included, would be beneficial. The Fleiss' kappa value of 0.24 indicates only fair agreement between annotators; this should be discussed in relation to the reliability of the dataset.
4. **Technical Imprecision:** Equation 8, detailing the Mean Average Precision (MAP) calculation, is incorrectly framed as a probability and lacks a natural cutoff. The formulation should be clarified or revised to ensure a proper ranking is calculated.
5. **Minor Editorial Issues:** The paper contains minor grammatical errors (e.g., line 556: “most concepts has” should be “most concepts have”).

**Questions to Authors:**

1.  Could you provide more details about the rationale behind the choice of hyperparameters, particularly β and η? How sensitive are the results to changes in these parameters?
2.  How does the performance of the proposed method compare to more recent and advanced word embedding techniques, such as BERT or ELMo, when fine-tuned on the same biomedical datasets?
3.  What are the limitations of the distant supervision approach used in this work? Are there any cases where the automatically generated labels are likely to be inaccurate or misleading?
4.  Can you elaborate on the potential biases in the newly created dataset and how these biases might affect the evaluation results?

**Additional Comments:**

*   The supplementary material provides valuable details about the hyperparameter tuning process and dataset creation.
*   The inclusion of the software implementation (cui2vec) is a commendable effort and will facilitate reproducibility and further research.
*   Consider exploring alternative methods for integrating structured knowledge into the embedding process, such as graph neural networks.
