## Review of "A Weakly-Supervised Method for Jointly Embedding Concepts, Phrases, and Words"

This paper proposes a method for jointly learning embeddings of concepts, phrases, and words by leveraging ontology information as a form of “distant supervision” during skip-gram training. The authors evaluate their approach on concept similarity and relatedness tasks using UMLS and YAGO ontologies, demonstrating competitive results with existing methods. A key contribution is the release of software and a novel dataset for real-world entity similarity, which could be valuable to the biomedical NLP community.

The core idea – adapting skip-gram to incorporate concept information – isn’t particularly novel, being a relatively straightforward extension of established embedding techniques. However, the paper's practical implementation and evaluation are its strengths. The competitive performance on several datasets, particularly given the lack of manual annotation, is a notable achievement.  Furthermore, providing code and a new dataset significantly increases the paper's impact and potential for follow-up research.  The analysis revealing the interplay between compositional structure (controlled by β and η) and observed embeddings provides some interesting, though not entirely conclusive, insights.

Despite these strengths, several weaknesses warrant attention. Firstly, the "distant supervision" framing is misleading. While phrases provide context, they aren't labeled examples in the traditional supervised learning sense. Secondly, the model doesn’t consistently outperform baselines; the Choi et al. baseline notably exceeds its performance on the Mayo datasets, and exhibits a similar trend in other evaluations. On the larger UMNSRS dataset, the model’s results are only competitive with simpler baselines like Chiu et al. (2016b). 

The introduction of β and η to control compositionality is interesting, but the consistent finding of near-zero optimal values for these parameters (particularly for PM+CL and WikiNYT) is counterintuitive and requires further investigation. The experimental setup for Table 4 also lacks clarity; the many-to-many mapping between concepts and phrases raises questions about how similarity scores were computed. Finally, the finding that averaging phrase vectors (APPROXPHR) yields the best results in Table 5 questions the utility of the concept ontology itself – perhaps starting directly from seed phrases would be sufficient.

**Questions to Authors:**

1.  Can you clarify the method used to compute similarity scores in Table 4, given the many-to-many mapping between concepts and phrases?
2.  The optimal β and η values are often near zero. What explanations do you have for this finding, and how does it influence your interpretation of the model’s behavior?
3.  Given the performance of the “APPROXPHR” baseline, what is the added value of explicitly learning concept embeddings using the proposed method?
