This paper proposes a weakly-supervised method for jointly embedding concepts, phrases, and words, leveraging representative phrases for ontology concepts as distant supervision. The core idea is to replace phrases in a corpus with unique concept identifiers, creating a parallel corpus used to train embeddings via a modified skip-gram model. The method is applied to biomedical (UMLS) and general-domain (YAGO) ontologies, and evaluated on concept similarity and relatedness tasks. A novel dataset for evaluating similarity and relatedness of real-world entities is also presented.

The primary contribution of this work, as I see it, is the creation and release of a new dataset for evaluating the semantic similarity and relatedness of real-world entities, along with a baseline evaluation using the proposed method. While the embedding method itself is a reasonable extension of existing techniques, it doesn't represent a significant conceptual breakthrough. The second contribution is a demonstration that reasonably good concept embeddings can be learned without human annotation, relying on distant supervision from existing ontologies. Finally, the empirical validation on biomedical concept similarity and relatedness tasks, while not state-of-the-art, demonstrates competitive performance given the weakly-supervised setting.

**Strengths:**

1. **Novel Dataset:** The creation of the dataset for real-world entity similarity and relatedness is a valuable contribution. Existing datasets often focus on words, not entities, and this resource provides a much-needed benchmark for evaluating embeddings in this space. The authors provide details on the data collection process, including quality control measures, which increases the dataset's reliability and usability.

2. **Weakly-Supervised Approach:** The proposed method addresses the challenge of creating concept embeddings without the expensive and time-consuming process of manual annotation.  This is particularly valuable in domains like biomedicine where expert annotation is scarce and costly. The distant supervision approach is well-motivated and reasonably implemented. 

3. **Comprehensive Evaluation:** The paper presents a thorough empirical evaluation, comparing the proposed method against several baselines, including approximations of concept embeddings based on word embeddings and previously published concept embeddings.  The use of multiple datasets, including both biomedical and general-domain datasets, strengthens the conclusions.

4. **Clear Presentation:** The paper is well-written and clearly explains the proposed method, the experimental setup, and the results. The inclusion of a diagram illustrating the model architecture is helpful.  The authors also acknowledge limitations and potential areas for improvement.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Novelty of Method:** While the proposed method is a logical extension of existing embedding techniques, it lacks significant novelty. The core idea of using distant supervision is not new, and the modifications to the standard skip-gram model are relatively minor. The improvement over existing methods is incremental rather than substantial.

2. **Narrow Focus on Biomedical Domain:** The majority of the experiments and analysis focus on the biomedical domain. While this is a valid application area, it limits the generalizability of the findings.  Further evaluation on other domains would be beneficial to demonstrate the broader applicability of the proposed method.

3. **Potential Issues with Distant Supervision:** The reliance on distant supervision introduces noise into the training data. The assumption that all occurrences of a phrase signify the corresponding concept may not always hold true, potentially leading to inaccurate embeddings. The authors acknowledge this limitation but do not fully explore its impact.

4. **Incorrect MAP Calculation:** Equation 8 and its discussion contain an error. Calculating precision by ranking *the entire vocabulary* is impractical and meaningless. A natural cutoff or a more focused ranking scheme (e.g., based on a similarity threshold) is needed. This impacts the analysis of ontological structure and requires clarification.

5. **Limited Analysis of Dataset Quality:** While the authors discuss data collection for their new dataset, a deeper analysis of its characteristics, such as inter-annotator agreement beyond Fleiss' kappa, and the distribution of similarity scores, would be valuable. More information about the types of entities included in the dataset would also be beneficial.



**Questions to Authors:**

1.  Could you provide a more detailed discussion of the potential impact of noise in the distant supervision process, and explore techniques for mitigating this noise (e.g., filtering based on context or confidence scores)?
2.  Please clarify the intended interpretation and correct the calculation of the Mean Average Precision (MAP) in Equation 8. How is the ranking list restricted to a manageable size?
3.  Can you provide more details about the characteristics of the newly created dataset, including a distribution of similarity/relatedness scores, and a breakdown of the entity types included?
4.  How sensitive are the results to the choice of hyperparameters, particularly β and η?  Have you explored alternative strategies for tuning these parameters beyond the grid search described in the supplemental material?

**Additional Comments:**

* Line 556: “most concepts has” should be “most concepts have.”
* The supplemental material is helpful and provides additional details on the experimental setup and hyperparameter tuning.
* Consider discussing the computational cost of the proposed method compared to other embedding techniques.
