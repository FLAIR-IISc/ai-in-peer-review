- Proposal: weakly-supervised adaptation of skip-gram to jointly learn embeddings for words, phrases, and ontology concepts using representative phrases as distant supervision.  
- Evaluation: tested on biomedical concepts (UMLS) and general entities (YAGO) using concept similarity and relatedness tasks.  
- Main contribution: bridges lexical representations and structured knowledge without manual annotations, and releases cui2vec software plus new similarity/relatedness datasets.  
- Additional contribution: explores incorporating structured knowledge into distributional models via a compositionality hyperparameter (β).  
- Strength: directly embeds concepts to handle non-compositional phrases and multiword expressions.  
- Strength: competitive (and sometimes superior) performance on some concept similarity benchmarks without manual labeling.  
- Strength: software release enhances reproducibility and community use.  
- Weakness: method is an incremental modification of skip-gram rather than a major methodological advance.  
- Weakness: inconsistent experimental results — outperformed by CHOI on MayoSRS and only marginal gains over CHIU-2 on UMNSRS.  
- Weakness: on new real-world entity datasets, approximate phrase embeddings (APPROXPHRco) often outperform direct concept embeddings.  
- Weakness: labeling phrase occurrences as “distant supervision” is debatable — more akin to leveraging contextual signals and can be noisy.  
- Weakness: presentation issues — inconsistent notation in Section 3.2 and questionable use of a single global β for compositionality.  
- Missing detail: unclear how cosine similarity is computed given many-to-many concept–phrase mappings (random choice vs. averaging?).  
- Open questions requested: explain inconsistent dataset performance and CHOI’s strength on MayoSRS; justify single global β vs. concept/corpus-specific β; clarify similarity calculation for multi-phrase concepts; justify benefits of direct concept embeddings given strong APPROXPHRco baseline.
