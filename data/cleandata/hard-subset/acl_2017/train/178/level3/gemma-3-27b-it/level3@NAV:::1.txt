## Review of "A Weakly-Supervised Method for Jointly Embedding Concepts, Phrases, and Words"

This paper proposes a method to extend word embeddings to concepts by replacing phrase/word occurrences with a fixed concept identifier in a tagged corpus and training embeddings jointly. The application is in the biomedical domain, leveraging existing corpora and ontologies like UMLS and YAGO. The authors also introduce a new dataset for evaluating word/concept similarity and relatedness, a potentially valuable contribution. The paper is well-written and the proposed methodology is intuitive and naturally extends existing embedding techniques.

However, the core technical contribution feels incremental. The idea of substituting phrases with concept IDs for embedding learning is a relatively straightforward extension of existing techniques, and the performance gains demonstrated are not substantial, particularly when compared to existing, strongly supervised methods. The evaluation is heavily focused on biomedical data, which limits the generalizability of the findings. While this is a reasonable starting point given the domain expertise, broader evaluation on more diverse datasets would strengthen the work.

The most significant contribution of this paper is likely the novel test dataset for word/concept similarity. The creation of such resources is valuable to the community, but the paper could benefit from a more in-depth discussion of the dataset creation process, including details on crowd-sourcing quality control and potential biases. A more thorough analysis of the dataset’s characteristics would justify its value and encourage its adoption by other researchers. 

A technical issue exists in Equation 8, where the MAP calculation is incorrectly described as a probability and doesn’t include a natural cutoff for ranking (it appears to rank the entire vocabulary). This needs to be corrected, or a justification for the chosen approach provided. There’s also a minor editorial correction needed on line 556 ("most concepts has" → "most concepts have").

**Strengths:**

*   **Novel Dataset:** The creation of a new dataset for evaluating concept similarity is a valuable contribution to the field.
*   **Clear Writing:** The paper is well-written and easy to understand, effectively communicating the proposed method.
*   **Intuitive Methodology:** The extension of word embeddings to concepts via identifier substitution is a natural and logical approach.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Limited Technical Novelty:** The core method is a relatively incremental extension of existing embedding techniques.
*   **Narrow Evaluation:** The evaluation is primarily focused on biomedical data, limiting the generalizability of the results.
*   **Insufficient Dataset Discussion:** The description of the novel test dataset lacks sufficient detail regarding its creation and characteristics.
*   **Technical Error:** The MAP calculation in Eq. 8 is incorrectly described.



**Questions to Authors:**

1.  Could you provide a more detailed discussion of the dataset creation process, including the specific criteria used for selecting entity pairs and the measures taken to ensure data quality?
2.  What is the rationale for ranking the entire vocabulary when calculating MAP? Would applying a cutoff based on similarity scores improve the metric's relevance?
3.  Do you have any plans to evaluate your method on datasets outside of the biomedical domain to assess its generalizability?
4.   Given the relative performance compared to existing methods, what specific scenarios or downstream tasks would benefit most from the proposed approach?

**Additional Comments:**

The supplementary material regarding hyperparameter tuning would be helpful to include in the main body of the paper, or at least summarized more concisely.
