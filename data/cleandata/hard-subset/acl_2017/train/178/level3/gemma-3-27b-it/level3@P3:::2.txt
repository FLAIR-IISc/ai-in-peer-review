This paper proposes a weakly-supervised method for jointly embedding concepts, phrases, and words, leveraging ontology information and an unannotated corpus. The core idea is to use representative phrases for ontology concepts as “distant supervision” signals during training with a skip-gram model. The authors evaluate their approach on concept similarity and relatedness tasks using UMLS and YAGO ontologies.

**Main Contributions (as I see them, ordered by significance):**

1. **Evaluation Framework & Datasets:** The provision of a novel dataset for real-world entity similarity and relatedness (though with fair, not strong, inter-annotator agreement) is a valuable contribution to the field. This, combined with the use of established biomedical datasets, provides a solid evaluation foundation for concept embedding methods.
2. **Scalable Concept Embedding:** The method demonstrates the ability to generate embeddings for a significantly larger vocabulary of concepts than methods requiring manual annotation, offering a practical advantage.
3. **Joint Embedding Exploration:** While not entirely novel in its mechanism, the paper explicitly explores the joint embedding of concepts, phrases, and words within a single vector space, offering insights into how these different linguistic units relate to one another.

**Strengths:**

1. **Addressing Non-Compositionality:** The paper directly tackles the challenge of representing concepts that are not compositional, such as “Lou Gehrig’s disease,” which is a critical problem in biomedical NLP and beyond. The approach of leveraging ontology-defined phrases as anchors is a plausible and intuitive method for capturing such concepts.
2. **Competitive Performance:** The embeddings achieve competitive, and sometimes superior, performance on concept similarity tasks, particularly on certain datasets. This demonstrates the potential effectiveness of the weakly-supervised approach.
3. **Software Release:** The release of the “cui2vec” software and the novel dataset are highly valuable resources for the biomedical NLP community, fostering further research in this area.
4. **Comprehensive Evaluation:** The authors present a comprehensive evaluation, including comparisons against several baselines, including strong baselines like Choi et al. (2016a,b), and thorough hyperparameter tuning.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Novelty:** The core method is essentially a tweak to the standard skip-gram model, lacking significant algorithmic innovation. The addition of β and η parameters for controlling compositionality is a reasonable extension, but does not represent a breakthrough.
2. **Inconsistent Performance:** The model does not consistently outperform baselines. The substantial underperformance on the Mayo datasets (where Choi et al. consistently achieve superior results) is a major concern. The observation in Table 6 indicates a strong bias towards the performance of Choi et al.
3. **Competitive, Not State-of-the-Art:** On the larger UMNSRS dataset, the model performs at best competitively with simpler baselines (Chiu et al., 2016a). This suggests that the gains from the proposed method are limited in scale and may not generalize well.
4. **Misleading "Distant Supervision" Label:** Describing phrase occurrences as “distant supervision” is a misnomer. While phrase occurrences provide contextual information, they do not constitute the kind of labeled signals traditionally associated with distant supervision. This framing can be misleading.

**Questions to Authors:**

1. **Mayo Dataset Performance:** The significant performance gap on the Mayo datasets compared to Choi et al. (2016b) is concerning. Could you elaborate on the potential reasons for this discrepancy? What architectural or training differences might account for this result?
2. **β and η Interpretation:** The cross-validation results for β and η often suggest zero values (particularly for PM+CL and WikiNYT), seemingly contradicting the idea that compositionality is beneficial. Can you provide a more nuanced interpretation of these results and explain why compositionality might be detrimental in these specific settings?
3. **Table 4 Mapping Ambiguity:** In Table 4, concepts can map to multiple phrases. How are cosine similarities computed in this many-to-many scenario? Are the similarities averaged, or is a different aggregation method used? Please clarify the procedure.
4. **Averaging vs. Concept Embeddings:** Table 5 indicates that simply averaging phrase vectors ("approximate phr") consistently outperforms using the learned concept embeddings ("CONCEPTco"). What does this suggest about the true value of incorporating the concept ontology into the training process? Would starting directly from seed phrases potentially achieve similar or better results?

**Additional Comments:**

* The notation introduced in Section 3.2 (e.g., E_W) is never used later in the paper, making it unnecessary and potentially confusing.
* The supplemental material provides valuable details about the data collection process for the novel datasets.
* The discussion section acknowledges several limitations and areas for future work, demonstrating a critical self-awareness.
