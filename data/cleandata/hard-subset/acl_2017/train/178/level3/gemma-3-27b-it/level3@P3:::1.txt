This paper proposes a weakly-supervised method for jointly embedding concepts, phrases, and words, leveraging an ontology to map phrases to concepts and then treating concept identifiers as unigrams within the training corpus. The approach is applied to biomedical data, utilizing UMLS and YAGO ontologies and evaluating on standard biomedical similarity/relatedness datasets, alongside a newly introduced dataset for real-world entities. The writing is clear and the method itself is conceptually straightforward. However, while well-executed, the contribution feels incremental and lacks a substantial technical leap.

**Contributions (as I see them):**

1. **Novel Dataset:** The primary contribution lies in the creation and release of a new dataset for evaluating similarity and relatedness of real-world entities. Existing datasets are largely word-focused, and this provides a valuable resource for evaluating embeddings in a more knowledge-grounded setting.
2. **Weakly-Supervised Embedding Method:** The approach of replacing phrases with concept identifiers for training is a sensible and practical application of distant supervision. While not entirely novel in concept, its specific implementation and demonstration within the biomedical domain are valuable.
3. **Empirical Evaluation on Biomedical Data:** The comprehensive evaluation on established biomedical datasets provides a reasonable benchmark for the proposed method and demonstrates competitive performance.

**Strengths:**

1. **Clear and Well-Written:** The paper is exceptionally well-written, clearly explaining the methodology and experimental setup. The organization is logical, and the motivation is well-articulated.
2. **Practical Approach:** The method is conceptually simple and practical, requiring no manual annotation and leveraging readily available ontologies. This makes it easily adaptable to other domains.
3. **Comprehensive Evaluation:** The authors evaluate their approach on a variety of datasets, including both existing benchmarks and their newly created dataset, demonstrating its performance across different tasks.
4. **Demonstrated Competitiveness:** The proposed method achieves competitive results compared to existing approaches, particularly on biomedical datasets, indicating its effectiveness in this domain.
5. **Resource Release:** The public release of the novel dataset and the software implementation (“cui2vec”) facilitates further research in this area.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Incremental Contribution:** The core technical innovation – replacing phrases with concept IDs – is relatively minor. It builds upon established techniques (word2vec, distant supervision) without introducing a fundamentally new approach to embedding concepts.
2. **Domain Specificity:** The evaluation is heavily focused on biomedical data. While this is a valuable application, it limits the generalizability of the findings. It’s unclear how well the method would perform on more diverse domains without significant adaptation.
3. **Dataset Discussion:** While the new dataset is a key strength, its description lacks depth. More details regarding the annotation process (inter-annotator agreement beyond Fleiss’ Kappa, participant demographics, potential biases) and the characteristics of the entity pairs would be beneficial. The relatively low Fleiss' Kappa (0.24) is a concern and warrants further discussion.
4. **Technical Error in MAP Calculation:** The description of the MAP calculation (Eq. 8 and surrounding text) is incorrect. It states MAP is a probability, which is inaccurate. More importantly, the calculation as presented ranks the *entire* vocabulary, which is not a meaningful metric in this context. A cutoff based on a similarity threshold or k-nearest neighbors should be defined to make the MAP calculation interpretable.
5. **Limited Analysis of Negative Results:** While the authors present results, a more in-depth discussion of *why* certain approaches (e.g., approximations) perform better or worse could provide valuable insights.

**Questions to Authors:**

1. Could you elaborate on the factors that influenced the relatively low Fleiss’ Kappa value for the new dataset? What steps were taken to ensure the quality of the annotations despite this?
2. Please clarify the MAP calculation. As it stands, ranking the entire vocabulary is not meaningful. How was this metric intended to be used, and could you provide a corrected equation or explanation?
3.  You mention that optimizing hyperparameters for downstream tasks may differ. Have you explored applying your embeddings to any specific downstream biomedical NLP tasks (e.g., named entity recognition, relation extraction)? If so, what were the results?
4.  The introduction mentions the limitation of relying on binary relations in existing KBs. However, the method doesn't explicitly incorporate any additional relational information beyond the phrase-concept mapping. Was exploring incorporating relational knowledge considered, and if so, why was it not pursued?

**Additional Comments:**

* Line 556: “most concepts has” should be “most concepts have.”
* The supplemental material provides valuable details regarding hyperparameter tuning and dataset creation. Consider incorporating some of this information directly into the main paper for increased clarity.
