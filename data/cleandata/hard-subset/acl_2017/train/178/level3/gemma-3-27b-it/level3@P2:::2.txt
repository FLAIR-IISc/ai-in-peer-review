This paper proposes a weakly-supervised method for jointly embedding concepts, phrases, and words, leveraging representative phrases for ontology concepts as a form of distant supervision. The authors train embeddings using a skip-gram model, adapting it to handle concepts alongside words and phrases, and evaluate on concept similarity and relatedness tasks using both biomedical (UMLS) and general-domain (YAGO) ontologies. The core contribution lies in the attempt to learn embeddings for concepts directly from unlabeled text, circumventing the need for expensive manual annotation. A secondary contribution is the release of both the software implementation and a novel dataset for evaluating real-world entity similarity and relatedness.

**Strengths:**

The primary strength of this work is its attempt to address the challenge of representing non-compositional phrases and concepts within a distributed semantic model. The approach of using ontology-linked phrases as a signal for concept embedding is a sensible strategy, particularly in domains like biomedicine where multi-word expressions are common and often lack compositional semantics.  This addresses a known limitation of standard word embedding techniques.

A significant strength is the competitive performance of the proposed model on several concept similarity and relatedness tasks. While not consistently outperforming all baselines, the results in Table 4 demonstrate that the learned concept embeddings achieve comparable or superior performance to existing methods, particularly on the larger datasets. This suggests that the joint embedding approach is capable of capturing meaningful semantic relationships between concepts.

Finally, the release of the software implementation ("cui2vec") and the newly created dataset for evaluating real-world entity similarity and relatedness are valuable contributions to the research community.  These resources can facilitate further research in the areas of concept embedding and knowledge representation.

**Weaknesses:**

The most significant weakness is the limited novelty of the proposed method. The core technique relies heavily on adapting the standard skip-gram model with negative sampling, a well-established approach. The modifications—incorporating concepts and phrases—while conceptually sound, do not represent a fundamental departure from existing embedding techniques.  The methodological contribution feels incremental rather than groundbreaking.

A critical weakness is the inconsistent performance relative to strong baselines. While competitive on some datasets, the Choi et al. (2016b) baseline consistently outperforms the proposed method on the smaller MayoSRS datasets, and exhibits similar trends in Table 6.  This raises concerns about the robustness and generalizability of the proposed approach.  On the larger UMNSRS dataset, the model is only competitive with simpler baselines like Chiu et al. (2016a).

Furthermore, the choice of a single, global β parameter for controlling phrase compositionality appears questionable. The cross-validation results (Table 3) suggest that setting β to zero often yields the best performance, effectively disabling compositionality. This is counterintuitive and raises doubts about the effectiveness of the compositionality control mechanism.  A more nuanced approach, potentially allowing β to vary based on concept or phrase characteristics, might be more fruitful.

**Questions to Authors:**

1.  The experimental setup for Table 4 is unclear. Given the many-to-many mapping between concepts and phrases, how is the cosine similarity calculated for a pair of concepts? Is it the maximum similarity between any phrase representing each concept, or an average across all possible phrase pairings?
2.  The notation introduced in Section 3.2 (e.g., E_W) is never used in subsequent sections. Was this notation intended to be used, and if so, why was it omitted?
3.  The authors state that using phrases as "distant supervision" is analogous to Mintz et al. (2009). However, distant supervision typically involves automatic labeling based on knowledge base triples. The current approach leverages contextual co-occurrence, which is closer to distributional semantics. Could you clarify why the term “distant supervision” is used, and acknowledge the distinction from traditional distant supervision methods?
4.  The results in Table 5 suggest that simply averaging phrase vectors (“APPROXPHRco”) often performs best. This questions the added value of explicitly modeling concepts within the embedding space. Could you discuss this observation and elaborate on the potential benefits of a dedicated concept embedding versus phrase-based approximations?

**Additional Comments:**

- The use of Fleiss’ kappa for evaluating the agreement of the created dataset is not optimal, given the number of annotators. Other metrics, such as Krippendorff’s alpha, may be more appropriate.
- While the ablation study is helpful, a more thorough analysis of the impact of different hyperparameters, particularly β and η, would be beneficial.
- The discussion section could be expanded to address the limitations of the proposed method more explicitly and outline potential directions for future research.
