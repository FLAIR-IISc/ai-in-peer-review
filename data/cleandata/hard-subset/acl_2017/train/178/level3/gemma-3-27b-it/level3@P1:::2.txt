This paper proposes a weakly-supervised method for jointly learning embeddings of concepts, phrases, and words, leveraging representative phrases for ontology concepts as a form of distant supervision. The core idea is to adapt the skip-gram model to simultaneously train embeddings for words, phrases, and their corresponding concepts, using an unlabeled corpus. The method is evaluated on biomedical concepts from UMLS and general-domain entities from YAGO, with performance assessed on concept similarity and relatedness tasks.

The primary contribution of this work lies in its attempt to bridge the gap between lexical representations (words and phrases) and structured knowledge (ontology concepts) without relying on expensive manual annotations. While existing approaches often require human-labeled data or automated tagging methods that ignore contextual information, this work utilizes the inherent relationship between concepts and their textual representations within an ontology to generate a training signal. Another notable contribution is the release of both the software implementation of the proposed method (cui2vec) and the novel datasets of similarity and relatedness for real-world entities, providing valuable resources for the research community. Finally, the exploration of how to incorporate structured knowledge into distributional semantic models, through the use of hyperparameters controlling compositionality, is a worthwhile contribution, though its ultimate impact is debatable given the results.

Several strengths support the potential acceptance of this submission. First, the proposed method addresses a significant challenge in semantic representation learning: handling non-compositional phrases and multi-word expressions that do not easily map to their constituent words. By directly embedding concepts, the model attempts to capture semantic meaning beyond simple word combinations. Second, the experimental results demonstrate that the learned embeddings achieve competitive, and in some cases superior, performance on concept similarity tasks compared to existing methods, especially considering the lack of manual annotation. This suggests that the distant supervision approach is effective in capturing meaningful semantic relationships. The availability of the software implementation further enhances the value of this work by enabling other researchers to reproduce and extend the findings.

However, several weaknesses raise concerns about the novelty and overall impact of the submission. The proposed method, at its core, is a modification of the skip-gram model – a well-established technique – with a relatively straightforward adaptation for concept embeddings. While the integration of ontology information is a valuable addition, the methodological innovation appears incremental rather than groundbreaking. More importantly, the experimental results are inconsistent. While competitive on some datasets, the model is demonstrably outperformed by the CHOI baseline on the MayoSRS datasets and shows only marginal improvements over simpler baselines (like CHIU-2) on the larger UMNSRS dataset. This casts doubt on the practical benefits of the proposed method, particularly in the biomedical domain. Furthermore, the performance on the newly created real-world entity datasets favors the approximate phrase embedding (APPROXPHRco), suggesting that directly embedding concepts might not be the most effective approach.

Another point of concern is the framing of phrase occurrences as "distant supervision." While the method leverages the co-occurrence of phrases to infer concept representations, this is more accurately described as utilizing contextual information rather than employing a true supervised learning signal. This distinction is important as it highlights the inherent limitations and potential noise associated with the approach. Additionally, there are minor issues with the presentation of the model. The notation introduced in Section 3.2 is not consistently used throughout the paper, potentially confusing readers. The use of a single, global β parameter to control compositionality is also questionable, given the cross-validation results suggesting that compositionality is not universally beneficial. 

To clarify the experimental setup, the authors should detail how cosine similarities are computed given the many-to-many mapping between concepts and phrases. Is a single phrase chosen randomly, or is some averaging performed? Finally, the fact that simply averaging phrase vectors (APPROXPHRco) consistently performs well raises the question of whether the complex ontology integration is truly necessary or if starting from seed phrases is sufficient.

I recommend the authors address the following questions:

1.  Can you provide a more detailed explanation for the inconsistent performance of the model across different datasets? Why does the CHOI baseline perform so well on the MayoSRS datasets?
2.  What is the rationale behind using a single, global β parameter? Have you considered exploring concept-specific or corpus-specific values?
3.  Please clarify how concept similarity is calculated when a concept has multiple associated phrases.
4.  Given the strong performance of APPROXPHRco, can you elaborate on the advantages of directly embedding concepts instead of relying solely on phrase representations?
