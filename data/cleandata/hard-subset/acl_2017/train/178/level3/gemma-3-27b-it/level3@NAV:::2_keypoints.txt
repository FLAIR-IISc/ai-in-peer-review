- Proposed method: jointly learn embeddings for concepts, phrases, and words by incorporating ontology information as distant supervision into skip-gram training.  
- Evaluation: tested on concept similarity and relatedness tasks using UMLS and YAGO, showing competitive results.  
- Contributions: releases software and a novel real-world entity similarity dataset, useful for biomedical NLP.  
- Novelty concern: core idea is a straightforward extension of established embedding methods and not particularly novel.  
- Practical strength: solid implementation and evaluations; achieves competitive performance without manual annotation.  
- Analysis: study of compositionality controls (β and η) offers interesting but inconclusive insights into embedding behavior.  
- Framing issue: calling the approach “distant supervision” is misleading because phrases act as context rather than labeled examples.  
- Inconsistent gains: the model does not consistently outperform baselines (Choi et al. outperforms on Mayo; only competitive on UMNSRS).  
- Parameter concern: optimal β and η values are often near zero, a counterintuitive finding that needs explanation.  
- Experimental clarity: Table 4 setup is unclear—many-to-many concept–phrase mappings leave similarity-score computation ambiguous.  
- Utility question: baseline APPROXPHR (averaging phrase vectors) often performs best, questioning the added value of learning explicit concept embeddings.  
- Questions for authors: clarify Table 4 similarity computation, explain near-zero β/η and their implications, and justify the benefit of learned concept embeddings over seed-phrase baselines.
