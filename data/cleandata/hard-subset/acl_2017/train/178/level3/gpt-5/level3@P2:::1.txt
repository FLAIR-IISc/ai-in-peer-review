Summary and contributions
This paper proposes a weakly supervised method to jointly learn embeddings for ontology concepts, their representative phrases, and words. The core idea is to use an ontology’s phrase-to-concept mappings as distant supervision: occurrences of mapped phrases in an unlabeled corpus are greedily replaced by phrase IDs and used, alongside the original text, to train a skip-gram-with-negative-sampling model. Two hyperparameters tie the spaces: β to encourage phrase–word compositionality and η to encourage concept–phrase compositionality. The authors train on biomedical and general-domain corpora (UMLS and YAGO) and evaluate on similarity/relatedness benchmarks, also introducing a new crowdsourced entity similarity/relatedness dataset.

As I see them, the main contributions (in decreasing order of importance) are:
1) A new human-judgment dataset for similarity and relatedness between real-world entities (people/places/organizations), filling a gap in intrinsic evaluation resources for concepts/entities.
2) A simple, scalable weakly supervised procedure to jointly embed concepts, phrases, and words from unannotated corpora using ontology phrase mappings, requiring no manual corpus annotation.
3) An empirical study showing competitive intrinsic performance (notably on UMNSRS Similarity) with substantially larger concept coverage (>3x) than prior concept-embedding work, plus qualitative analyses (compositionality and semantic-type clustering).

Strengths
- Practical, annotation-free methodology with broad coverage
  The distant-supervision setup requires only an ontology’s phrase mappings and raw text, avoiding costly manual corpus tagging. The approach scales to substantially larger vocabularies than prior biomedical concept embeddings (reported >3x coverage), an important practical advantage in domains with long-tail concepts.

- Competitive performance on intrinsic evaluations
  On UMNSRS Similarity (the larger dataset), the concept embeddings outperform prior methods; relatedness is competitive with strong word baselines and jointly trained phrases. On the new general-domain entity datasets, phrase-based approximations and direct concept embeddings perform strongly. These results support the claim that the proposed training signals capture meaningful semantics despite weak supervision.

- Clean, natural modeling and thorough analyses
  Extending skip-gram to include phrase and concept tokens and adding explicit regularization terms (β, η) is straightforward and easy to reproduce. The paper includes helpful analyses: concept–phrase and phrase–word compositionality, neighborhood precision, and semantic-type clustering. These shed light on when phrases compose and how concept–phrase links are reflected in the space.

- Resource contribution with potential community value
  The new entity similarity/relatedness dataset addresses a recognized gap for evaluating concept/entity semantics beyond words. Even with fair agreement (Fleiss’ κ ≈ 0.24), such a resource can catalyze further work and benchmarking.

Weaknesses
- Limited novelty of the core method
  The approach is a relatively modest extension of skip-gram with distant supervision via ontology phrase matches, a well-trodden idea in related contexts. The main technical elements (greedy phrase collapsing, shared training, and simple regularizers) are natural but not a substantial methodological advance. The contribution is more in engineering/scaling and demonstrating practicality than in new modeling ideas.

- Ambiguity handling is crude and likely injects noise
  For polysemous phrases, the model updates all candidate concepts equally (normalized by count), effectively assuming each mention signifies each possible concept. This ignores context for disambiguation and may conflate distinct concepts. The paper would be stronger with an analysis of how ambiguity affects performance (e.g., restricting to unambiguous phrases, or weighting concepts via context).

- Evaluation scope and risk of overfitting in intrinsic tuning
  Despite including a general-domain corpus, most empirical focus and claims derive from biomedical datasets. The new general-domain evaluation is relatively small and has low agreement (κ ≈ 0.24), limiting the strength of conclusions. Moreover, hyperparameters are tuned on the test collections (albeit with an adapted cross-validation), which can inflate intrinsic scores and does not demonstrate transfer to downstream tasks. There are no extrinsic evaluations (e.g., entity linking, code prediction), where concept embeddings are most impactful.

- The new dataset needs deeper discussion and may be the primary contribution
  The dataset design, selection protocol, licensing/release plan, and reliability analysis warrant more space. With low IAA, understanding category balance, annotator demographics, and quality-control details is essential. As the methodological novelty is limited, the dataset’s clarity and robustness are crucial for the paper’s overall contribution.

- Technical issue in the MAP/probability exposition (Eq. 8) and ranking protocol
  Eq. 8 is described as a probability but is actually a precision-at-rank term; calling it a probability is misleading. More importantly, ranking each parent against the entire vocabulary to compute MAP can be uninformative and unstable. A local neighborhood (e.g., k-NN) or a frequency-controlled candidate set would be more appropriate. The section around Eq. 8 should be corrected and the evaluation re-run or at least reinterpreted with a justified cutoff.

Questions to authors
- Ambiguity: What fraction of phrase mentions are ambiguous (map to multiple concepts)? Can you report performance when training with only unambiguous phrases, or with ambiguity-weighted updates based on local context (e.g., soft attention over candidate concepts)?
- Regularization: How sensitive are results to β and η across corpora? Do you observe consistent gains from nonzero β/η on any tasks beyond the single corpus that favored η > 0?
- Negative sampling/tie-ins: Why share only the negative-sampling matrix across spaces rather than tying or initializing phrase/concept embeddings from word/phrase averages? Did you try stronger coupling (e.g., retrofitting) and how did it affect performance?
- Dataset: Will the entity similarity/relatedness datasets and code be publicly released under a permissive license? Please provide more details on pair selection, annotator demographics, and how you will package gold scores.

Additional comments
- Editorial: line 556 “most concepts has” → “most concepts have.”
- Please correct the description of Eq. 8 (remove “probability” phrasing), and consider re-evaluating MAP with a neighborhood-based cutoff (e.g., k ∈ {50, 100, 500}) and/or frequency-matched decoys.
- Consider adding at least one extrinsic task to substantiate usefulness beyond intrinsic rankings, or provide stronger evidence that intrinsic gains transfer.
