Summary
The paper proposes a weakly supervised method to jointly embed ontology concepts, their representative phrases, and words into one space by adapting skip-gram with negative sampling. The approach uses ontology-provided phrase–concept mappings as distant supervision, adds two hyperparameters (β, η) to balance compositionality (words→phrases, phrases→concepts), trains on biomedical and general-domain corpora, and evaluates on intrinsic similarity/relatedness tasks. The authors also release a small crowdsourced dataset of entity similarity/relatedness and provide several analyses (compositionality, concept–phrase proximity, semantic type clustering).

Contributions (as I see them; strongest first)
1) A simple and sensible joint embedding framework that balances atomic and compositional modeling via β (words→phrases) and η (phrases→concepts).
2) A set of analyses and ablations demonstrating coverage and some properties of the learned space (e.g., concept–phrase closeness, semantic type clustering).
3) A small general-domain crowdsourced dataset of entity pair similarity/relatedness.

Strengths
- Sensible modeling tradeoff: The joint objective and the β/η mechanism explicitly target the core tension between atomic concept representations and compositional phrase/word information. This is a clear, easy-to-implement extension of SGNS.
- Scope and effort: The paper includes model development, two ontologies (UMLS, YAGO), multiple corpora, construction of a new evaluation set, and several analyses (compositionality, clustering). This is substantial work.
- Coverage and practicality: The approach scales without manual corpus annotation and yields much broader concept coverage than some prior biomedical concept-embedding baselines.
- Joint space utility: Embedding words, phrases, and concepts together enables direct comparisons across units, potentially useful for applications like concept normalization or entity linking.
- Competitive intrinsic results: On larger biomedical sets and on the new entity dataset, results are competitive with strong baselines and sometimes best for similarity.

Weaknesses
- Evaluation is only intrinsic; no extrinsic tasks: The paper relies exclusively on similarity/relatedness correlations. Given known limitations of intrinsic evaluations, evidence of utility on downstream tasks (e.g., clinical concept normalization, entity linking, coding prediction) is necessary. This is the main reason I cannot recommend acceptance.
- Medical evaluation data are limited/inappropriate: Mini MayoSRS (29 pairs) and MayoSRS (101 pairs) are tiny; MayoSRS has low agreement; UMNSRS contains single-word concepts only, mismatching the paper’s multi-word focus. Conclusions from these sets are fragile.
- Human judgments are phrase-based; treat and analyze accordingly: The paper’s own general-domain dataset elicited judgments on phrases (surface forms). Some biomedical sets include text forms alongside CUIs. Results should be explicitly analyzed as phrase-similarity (and compared against concept-level CUI-based scoring) to avoid conflating concept similarity with surface-form similarity.
- Hyperparameter tuning leakage: β/η and other hyperparameters are “heavily tuned” on the same datasets used for reporting final results. The adapted 5-fold process still uses the final test items repeatedly during model selection. This undermines validity of the reported correlations; a clean development split or separate development datasets are needed.
- Weakness of “outperforming prior work” claim: The method still relies on large manually curated ontologies (UMLS, YAGO) and existing clinical annotations underpinning those resources. Claims of superiority over methods using manual annotations should be tempered and normalized by the supervision signal each method actually leverages.
- Insufficient analysis of atomic vs compositional tradeoff: β and η are introduced but not deeply probed. There is no systematic sweep analyzing headword effects, phrase overlap/polysemy, or how performance varies with phrase compositionality. Table 3 reports tuned settings but not trends.
- Interpretation and task mismatch: The new entity dataset has low IAA (κ=0.24) and a 0–100 scale, which is likely too fine-grained. Without stronger agreement or calibration, small correlation differences are hard to interpret.

Questions to Authors (to be addressed in rebuttal)
- Phrase matching and overlaps (Line 202): How are overlapping/embedded phrases handled in greedy replacement, especially when overlaps map to different concepts? Provide examples and tie-breaking rules.
- Hyperparameter tuning (Line 496): Which specific task(s) (similarity vs relatedness; which dataset) were used to pick β, η, window, negatives, and dimension for each corpus? Provide a clean dev/test separation and report test-only results.
- Joint training details (Line 250): Precisely how are word embeddings updated in the joint model when phrases are collapsed to unigram tokens in TT? Does word training use TU only, TT only, or both? The “phrase-completion” wording is confusing—please clarify the update schedule.
- Negative sampling matrix (Line 220): What is the “negative sampling matrix”? Is this the output/context embedding table for SGNS? If so, why a separate name, and how is it shared across words/phrases/concepts?
- Concept vs phrase evaluation: For datasets that provide both CUIs and surface forms (MayoSRS/UMNSRS), can you report correlations separately for (a) concept–concept (via CUIs) and (b) phrase–phrase (via strings), to quantify the impact of surface form?
- β/η exploration: Please provide performance curves vs β and η and analyses stratified by phrase compositionality (e.g., head-modifier transparency) and ambiguity (#concepts per phrase).
- Generalization: Can you provide at least one extrinsic task (e.g., concept normalization on clinical text, entity linking on Wikipedia/News) to demonstrate downstream value?

- Dataset framing: For your new dataset, label it clearly as entity-phrase similarity/relatedness; provide examples of annotation prompts and quality checks; consider releasing a higher-IAA subset.

Recommendation
Reject. The modeling idea is solid and the implementation thorough, but the paper relies entirely on intrinsic evaluations with limited or mismatched datasets, includes hyperparameter tuning on test data, and lacks deeper analyses of the core atomic vs compositional tradeoff. I encourage resubmission after: (1) adding extrinsic evaluations, (2) using stronger and larger medical evaluation sets or providing clean dev/test splits, and (3) expanding analyses of β/η, compositionality, and phrase/word/concept interactions.

Reviewer confidence: High.
