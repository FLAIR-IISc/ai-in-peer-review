- Proposes a weakly supervised method that collapses ontology-matched phrases to tokens and jointly learns word, phrase, and concept embeddings with skip-gram/negative sampling and two global compositionality terms (β, η).  
- Evaluated on biomedical (UMLS) and general-domain (YAGO) datasets, with analyses of compositionality and semantic type clustering; code and a new general-domain dataset released.

Contributions:
- Shows large-coverage concept embeddings learned without manual corpus annotation that are competitive on UMNSRS similarity and cover more concepts than annotated baselines.  
- Releases practical resources: an implementation (cui2vec) and a new general-domain entity similarity/relatedness dataset.  
- Provides analyses probing concept–phrase/phrase–word compositionality and semantic type clustering.

Strengths:
- Scales without human corpus annotation to hundreds of thousands of concepts/phrases across domains, improving vocabulary coverage >3x compared to some annotated methods.  
- Achieves competitive or state-of-the-art results on UMNSRS Similarity and strong general-domain entity similarity when aggregating phrase representations.  
- Treats phrases as atomic units to capture non-compositional semantics while allowing optional compositional signals via β and η.  
- Releases code and dataset and performs careful hyperparameter tuning and additional analyses.  
- Demonstrates the pipeline across multiple corpora (clinical notes, PubMed, Wikipedia, news), indicating cross-domain applicability.

Weaknesses:
- Limited methodological novelty: mainly an engineering adaptation of skip-gram with phrase collapsing and regularization, without leveraging ontology structure.  
- Inconsistent improvements versus strong baselines: some benchmarks (Mayo SRS, Mini Mayo SRS, semantic type clustering) favor competitors like CHOI, and best general-domain results often come from phrase averaging rather than concept vectors.  
- Calling phrase matches “distant supervision” is misleading since there is no context-level concept labeling or sense disambiguation.  
- Notation and clarity problems (unused symbols, undefined Cs, unclear sharing of embeddings and negative sampling across matrices).  
- Using single global β and η is crude; cross-validation often sets them to zero, undermining the compositionality motivation.  
- Ambiguity handling is simplistic: ambiguous phrases are uniformly split across concepts without sense priors or learned weighting.

Questions for authors:
- How are similarities computed when concepts have multiple phrases—do you always compare concept vectors or aggregate phrase vectors, and how for PHRASEco/APPROXPHRco?  
- Did you try sense priors or learned attention for ambiguous phrase→concept mappings instead of uniform normalization by |Cp|?  
- What negative sampling distribution and subsampling strategies were used, and are phrases exempt from subsampling?  
- Did you try per-phrase β or per-concept η (or learned weights) to better model compositionality, and are there ablations?  
- For Table 4 tuning, provide per-fold variance and confirm no data leakage; any extrinsic evaluations (e.g., entity linking) performed?  
- Why does APPROXPHRco outperform CONCEPTco in general domain—what concrete utility do learned concept vectors add?

Additional comments / requests:
- Tighten and fix notation (define Cs or use Cp consistently) and explicitly state interaction of EW/EP/EC and whether input/output embeddings are tied.  
- Clarify preprocessing: phrase matching algorithm details, tokenization, case-folding, min-frequency thresholds, and overlap resolution.  
- Consider integrating ontology structure (hierarchy/types/relations) as auxiliary objectives to improve small-dataset and clustering performance.  
- Report rater counts, score distributions, and annotation guidelines for the new crowdsourced dataset given low agreement (κ≈0.24).  
- Provide implementation details for negative sampling/subsampling and deterministic phrase-matching rules to aid reproducibility.  
- Adding one extrinsic downstream task would strengthen the paper’s impact.
