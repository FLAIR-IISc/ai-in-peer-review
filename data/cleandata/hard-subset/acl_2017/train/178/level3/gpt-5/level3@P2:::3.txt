Summary and Contributions
This paper proposes a weakly supervised method to jointly learn embeddings for ontology concepts, their surface phrases, and words. The approach replaces occurrences of ontology phrases in raw text with phrase IDs and trains a skip-gram model that updates word, phrase, and concept embeddings, with optional compositionality constraints controlled by two hyperparameters (β for phrase-by-words, η for concept-by-phrases). The authors train on large biomedical and general-domain corpora for UMLS and YAGO, evaluate on similarity/relatedness benchmarks, release a new general-domain entity similarity/relatedness dataset, and analyze concept–phrase and semantic-type structure.

As I see them, the main contributions are:
1) A simple, scalable joint embedding framework leveraging distant supervision from ontology phrase lists, with interpretable controls for atomic vs compositional modeling.
2) A new human-annotated dataset for general-domain entity similarity and relatedness.
3) Empirical analyses across domains/corpora of concept–phrase–word relationships and semantic-type clustering.

Strengths
- Sensible, scalable modeling framework: The method is straightforward to implement, scales to large corpora, and cleanly balances atomic concept representations against compositional signals via β and η. The distant-supervision setup avoids manual corpus annotation and achieves broad coverage.
- Thorough engineering and comparisons: The paper trains on multiple realistic corpora (PubMed, clinical notes, Wikipedia+NYT) and two ontologies (UMLS, YAGO), compares to strong biomedical baselines (De Vine, Choi, Mencia; Chiu word vectors), and reports results across multiple datasets.
- New evaluation resource: The general-domain entity similarity/relatedness dataset fills a gap for entity-level evaluation beyond word-only datasets. The collection protocol, filtering, and IAA reporting (Fleiss’ κ) are documented and the resource could be valuable to the community.
- Analyses beyond headline scores: The concept–phrase/phrase–word compositionality analyses and semantic-type clustering provide insight into what the model learns, and help validate that some ontology structure is reflected in the embedding space.

Weaknesses
- Intrinsic-only evaluation and limited generality: The paper evaluates exclusively on intrinsic similarity/relatedness correlations, which are known to be weak predictors of downstream utility, especially in biomedical NLP. There is no extrinsic evaluation (e.g., entity linking/normalization, ICD code suggestion, concept retrieval), leaving practical impact unclear.
- Hyperparameter tuning on test sets: Despite an adapted cross-validation procedure, the same test datasets are used for hyperparameter selection and final reporting. This risks optimistic bias, especially on small benchmarks (e.g., Mini MayoSRS with 29 pairs). A clean dev/test split per domain or external dev sets are needed to support strong claims.
- Biomedical benchmarks are limited/inappropriate: MayoSRS and Mini MayoSRS are small and have known agreement issues; UMNSRS contains mostly single-word terms, limiting its relevance to multiword concept modeling. Heavy reliance on these datasets weakens conclusions about concept-level performance, particularly for phrases.
- Overstated “no-annotation” claim and fairness of comparisons: While no manual corpus annotation is used, the method crucially depends on large, curated ontologies (UMLS, YAGO) and their manually vetted phrase inventories. Coverage gains likely come from leveraging many surface forms and frequency thresholds rather than modeling alone. Comparisons to methods trained on different supervision signals (e.g., billing codes) are not strictly apples-to-apples; claims of outperforming prior work should be softened.
- Missing deeper analyses of atomic vs compositional tradeoff: The paper introduces β and η but does not systematically dissect their effects (e.g., performance vs β/η across datasets; behavior by phrase length, ambiguity, headword presence, or semantic type). Ambiguity handling (uniformly splitting updates across candidate concepts) likely injects noise; no ablation on unambiguous phrases or posterior weighting is provided.

Recommendation
Reject. The modeling contribution and dataset are promising, but the evaluation methodology (intrinsic-only, test-set tuning, small medical benchmarks) and missing analyses limit the strength of the claims. With improved evaluation (clean dev/test splits, extrinsic tasks) and deeper analysis of the atomic/compositional tradeoff and ambiguity, this work could be a strong resubmission.

Questions to Authors (for rebuttal)
- Phrase detection and overlap: How are overlapping/nested phrases handled exactly (longest-match tie-breaking, case, punctuation)? What proportion of replaced tokens are ambiguous phrases?
- Ambiguity and concept updates: When a phrase maps to multiple concepts, updates are uniformly split. Did you try weighting by phrase–concept priors or restricting to unambiguous mentions? How sensitive are results to the ambiguity rate?
- Training details: Do word updates use contexts from TU, TT, or both? Please clarify “phrase completion” and how o is selected for phrases in TT. Why a single negative-sampling matrix shared across types?
- β and η: Please report performance curves as β and η vary, stratified by phrase length, headword presence, and ambiguity. Do settings that favor compositionality help phrases like “high blood pressure” while hurting idioms?
- General-domain dataset: Since annotators rate surface forms, do you consider these phrase-level judgments? Please report phrase-only baselines and interpret results accordingly. Can you provide per-category IAA and distribution of pair types?
