Summary
The paper proposes a weakly supervised approach to jointly learn embeddings for ontology concepts, their surface phrases, and words. The method greedily replaces occurrences of ontology-mapped phrases in a large corpus with fixed concept identifiers and appends this tagged stream to the original text for skip-gram training, optionally nudging phrase and concept vectors toward averages of their components. The main applications are biomedical (UMLS over PubMed/clinical notes), with an additional YAGO experiment. The authors also introduce a new similarity/relatedness dataset for real-world entities (people, places, organizations). Results show competitive intrinsic performance with prior concept-embedding baselines, strong vocabulary coverage, and qualitative structure (semantic type clustering), with no manual annotation.

Contributions (as I see them; ordered by importance)
1) A new evaluation resource for entity-level similarity and relatedness in the general domain (people/places/organizations), with details on crowdsourcing and filtering.
2) A simple, natural weakly supervised pipeline to jointly embed concepts, phrases, and words by concept-tagging text and training with skip-gram; no manual corpus annotation required; scalable and broadly applicable whenever an ontology with surface forms exists.
3) Empirical analysis indicating decent preservation of concept-phrase links and partial semantic-type clustering, and competitive intrinsic results with 3x vocabulary coverage vs. some prior concept-embedding work.

Strengths
- Practical and scalable method with minimal supervision: The distant supervision via ontology surface forms avoids expensive annotation yet reaches strong coverage (e.g., >180k UMLS concepts, hundreds of thousands of YAGO entities). This is a compelling engineering contribution for domains with ontologies but limited labeled text.
- Competitive intrinsic performance with broader coverage: On UMNSRS Similarity, the joint concept embeddings match or beat strong baselines (e.g., CHIU-30) and are competitive on relatedness; in several cases approximated concept vectors via phrases perform quite well. This shows that the simple pipeline is effective despite noise in tagging.
- Joint space for concepts, phrases, and words: Training a single space enables flexible use (e.g., mapping between surface forms and canonical IDs, compositional approximations when needed), and the analysis shows that concept and phrase neighborhoods often align, which is useful in downstream entity linking/normalization.
- New entity similarity/relatedness dataset: The paper fills a gap for entity-level evaluation beyond word similarity. Even with modest agreement (κ≈0.24), the datasets provide a starting point for assessing entity semantics and will likely be reused.
- Clear writing and natural framing: The paper is well structured, the motivation is intuitive, and the method is easy to reproduce (cui2vec).

Weaknesses
- Limited novelty/technical depth: The core idea—replace ontology-mapped phrases by IDs and train skip-gram—feels like a natural extension rather than a substantial algorithmic advance. The β and η “compositionality” regularizers are straightforward and, by the paper’s own tuning results, often set to zero.
- Narrow evaluation and limited generality: Most experiments are biomedical intrinsic evaluations; there are no downstream tasks (e.g., entity linking, concept normalization, IR, or clinical prediction) to validate utility. The general-domain YAGO results hinge on the new dataset; broader benchmarks (e.g., entity linking on Wikipedia) would strengthen claims of generality.
- New evaluation resource needs deeper validation: The crowdsourced dataset has fair agreement (κ=0.24) and small per-HIT sizes; more detailed reliability analysis, clearer category balance, and comparisons to alternative protocols would help. As presented, the dataset may be the most lasting contribution, but it requires more discussion of limitations and recommended usage.
- Technical issue in MAP/precision analysis (Eq. 8/9): The expression Pr(c|Cp, V) is described as a probability but is actually precision at the rank of c. Moreover, ranking against the entire vocabulary V is not an appropriate retrieval setting for measuring parent–child cohesion; it inflates denominators and mixes many irrelevant candidates. The authors should (a) rename Pr to precision-at-rank, (b) compute AP/MAP over a natural candidate set (e.g., top-k nearest neighbors, or a filtered vocabulary such as phrases sharing headwords/semantic type), and (c) report MAP@k (e.g., k=50/100) or R-precision. As written, the metric interpretation and conclusions (e.g., Figure 4) are hard to trust.
- Risk of tuning on test data and missing controls: Hyperparameters are tuned on the evaluation tasks without a clean dev/test split, and the adapted cross-validation still uses the same items repeatedly across folds. This raises concerns about overfitting. Also, ambiguity handling is simplistic (greedy longest match, uniform credit across senses), with no analysis of its impact.

Questions to Authors (for rebuttal)
- MAP/precision: Please correct Eq. 8/9 and clarify the candidate set used. If you rank over the full vocabulary, can you also report MAP@k and R-precision, and provide sensitivity to k? Do your qualitative conclusions in §5.4 hold under these corrected measures?
- Ambiguity and tagging: How do you handle polysemous phrases mapped to multiple concepts in practice (beyond uniform normalization)? Did you try disambiguation heuristics (e.g., semantic-type constraints, local lexical priors), and how sensitive are results to these choices?
- Dataset details: Will the entity similarity/relatedness datasets, rating guidelines, and per-item agreement be released? Can you provide recommended splits and robust evaluation protocols (e.g., bootstrapping CIs) to mitigate low κ?
- Downstream tasks: Do you have preliminary results on an entity linking or concept normalization benchmark to demonstrate practical benefits of the joint space?

Additional Comments
- Consider adding more general-domain evaluations (e.g., entity linking on Wikipedia/YAGO) and at least one downstream biomedical task to support claims of utility.
- Clarify symbol overloading in Eq. 9 (|P| denotes what exactly in the MAP aggregation? Parents? Phrases?).
- Minor editorial correction: line 556 “most concepts has” → “most concepts have.”
