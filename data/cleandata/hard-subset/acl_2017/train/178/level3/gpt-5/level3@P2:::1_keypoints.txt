- Proposes a weakly supervised method that jointly learns embeddings for ontology concepts, representative phrases, and words using greedy phrase-to-concept collapsing and skip-gram training.  
- Uses two regularizers (β for phrase–word compositionality, η for concept–phrase compositionality) to tie embedding spaces.  
- Trains on biomedical (UMLS) and general-domain (YAGO) corpora and introduces a new crowdsourced entity similarity/relatedness dataset.  
- Main contributions: new human-judgment dataset for entity similarity/relatedness.  
- Main contributions: a scalable, annotation-free procedure to jointly embed concepts, phrases, and words from raw text using ontology mappings.  
- Main contributions: empirical results showing competitive intrinsic performance and >3x concept coverage versus prior work, with qualitative analyses.  
- Strength: practical, annotation-free method that scales to much larger vocabularies and long-tail concepts.  
- Strength: competitive intrinsic results (notably UMNSRS Similarity) and strong performance on new general-domain benchmarks.  
- Strength: simple, reproducible modeling extension of skip-gram with thorough analyses (compositionality, neighborhood precision, clustering).  
- Strength: new dataset fills evaluation gap for concept/entity semantics and may benefit the community despite modest inter-annotator agreement.  
- Weakness: limited methodological novelty—modest extension of existing skip-gram + distant supervision ideas.  
- Weakness: crude ambiguity handling that updates all candidate concepts equally, likely injecting noise and conflating senses.  
- Weakness: evaluation scope skewed toward biomedical data; general-domain set is small with low agreement and hyperparameters tuned on test collections.  
- Weakness: no extrinsic evaluations provided to show utility in downstream tasks where concept embeddings matter.  
- Weakness: dataset needs more detail on design, selection, license, reliability, and annotator demographics given its centrality.  
- Weakness: technical/metric issue—Eq. 8 mischaracterized as a probability and MAP evaluation against entire vocabulary is unstable; recommend k-NN or frequency-matched decoys.  
- Question: what fraction of phrase mentions are ambiguous, and how does training on only unambiguous or context-weighted updates affect performance?  
- Question: how sensitive are results to β and η across corpora, and do they yield consistent gains beyond the single favorable corpus?  
- Question: why share only the negative-sampling matrix instead of stronger parameter tying or initialization from word/phrase averages, and were such couplings tried?  
- Question: will the datasets and code be publicly released under a permissive license, and please provide pair selection, annotator demographics, and packaging details.  
- Additional: fix editorial error “most concepts has” → “most concepts have.”  
- Additional: correct Eq. 8 wording (remove “probability”) and consider re-running MAP with neighborhood cutoffs (k ∈ {50,100,500}) or frequency-controlled decoys.  
- Additional: consider adding at least one extrinsic task or stronger evidence that intrinsic improvements transfer to downstream use cases.
