- Proposes a weakly supervised method that maps ontology concepts to phrases, substitutes mentions in text, and trains joint word/phrase/concept embeddings with a skip-gram objective plus compositionality regularizers; evaluated on UMLS and YAGO.

- Contribution: a scalable, annotation-free training recipe with released software (cui2vec) and broad biomedical coverage.

- Contribution: empirical study showing competitive performance on multiple similarity/relatedness benchmarks, especially for non-compositional phrases, and enabling cross-granularity comparisons.

- Contribution: new general-domain entity similarity/relatedness dataset (moderate IAA) extending evaluation beyond words.

- Strength: Practical impact — >3x vocabulary coverage without manual annotation and usable vectors for biomedical practitioners.

- Strength: Competitive results — tied or strong performance on UMNSRS and YAGO benchmarks, with phrase-based approximations performing well.

- Strength: Addresses non-compositionality by training phrase embeddings directly, beneficial for expressions like “Lou Gehrig’s disease.”

- Strength: Joint embedding space of words, phrases, and concepts supports downstream tasks needing cross-granularity operations.

- Weakness: Limited methodological novelty — essentially a small tweak to skip-gram with phrase substitution and optional regularizers.

- Weakness: Inconsistent superiority — other methods (e.g., CHOI) substantially outperform on some benchmarks and clustering.

- Weakness: “Distant supervision” claim is questionable since phrase occurrences are unlabeled context and polysemy is not resolved.

- Weakness: Compositionality control underspecified — a single global β is surprising and cross-validation often sets β=0, undermining claimed benefits of β/η.

- Weakness: Empirical signal sometimes favors averaging phrase vectors (APPROXPHRco), calling into question the added value of explicit concept embeddings and the η term.

- Question: How are similarities computed when concept↔phrase mappings are many-to-many — aggregation or disambiguation?

- Question: Why label the signal “distant supervision” — what supervision does the ontology provide beyond lexicon construction?

- Question: Did you try per-phrase or per-type β/η, gating by ambiguity, or ablations isolating the concept objective vs. starting from phrase embeddings?

- Question: Can you explain why CHOI wins on Mayo and clustering and provide error analysis where your method fails?
