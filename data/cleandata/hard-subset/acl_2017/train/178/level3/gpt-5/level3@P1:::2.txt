Summary
This paper proposes a joint embedding approach that learns vectors for words, phrases, and ontology concepts using only unlabeled corpora plus mappings from concepts to representative phrases (UMLS/YAGO). The training adapts skip-gram with negative sampling over a phrase-tagged corpus: occurrences of known phrases are collapsed to single tokens and used to update phrase vectors and, via an equal-sharing update, all concepts those phrases can denote. Two compositionality regularizers encourage similarity between phrases and the average of their words (β) and between concepts and the average of their phrases (η). The models are trained on PubMed abstracts, clinical notes, their concatenation, and Wiki+NYT. Evaluation covers biomedical and general-domain concept similarity/relatedness, type clustering, and compositionality analyses. Results are competitive with prior work on larger biomedical sets and strong on a new entity similarity/relatedness benchmark; coverage is substantially larger than supervised concept-embedding baselines. Software and a new general-domain evaluation set are released.

Main contributions (as I see them)
1) A practical, scalable procedure to jointly embed words, phrases, and ontology concepts without manual corpus annotation, achieving competitive performance on similarity/relatedness while increasing vocabulary coverage (>3x) over supervised concept-embedding baselines.
2) A new general-domain entity similarity/relatedness dataset (people/places/organizations) and an open-source implementation (cui2vec) likely to benefit the community.
3) Empirical analysis of compositionality and semantic type structure in the learned space, showing phrase–word and concept–phrase interactions and partial clustering by semantic type.

Strengths
- Addresses non-compositionality and synonymy in a simple, corpus-scale way: Collapsing multiword mentions to atomic phrase tokens and separating phrase and concept vectors allows the model to avoid erroneous word-level composition for idiomatic expressions (e.g., “Lou Gehrig’s disease,” “Big Apple”). This is an intuitively appealing fit to the problem and easy to adopt in other domains.
- Competitive intrinsic performance with no manual annotation: On UMNSRS Similarity, concept embeddings achieve the top correlation (ρ=0.653), matching strong word baselines (CHIU-30). On relatedness, jointly trained words/phrases are competitive. Given the absence of human concept annotations and the broader coverage, these results are notable.
- Broad coverage and cross-corpus evaluation: Training on multiple corpora (PubMed, clinical notes, Wiki+NYT) demonstrates robustness across domains and shows that coverage scales with corpus diversity, a practical advantage over code-based embeddings trained on limited labeled sources.
- Released resources: The implementation and the new general-domain entity relatedness/similarity dataset fill a gap (few entity-level benchmarks), enabling reproducibility and future work.
- Thoughtful analyses: The compositionality and semantic-type clustering analyses provide insight into what the model learns (e.g., concepts are close to the mean of their phrases; type clustering is moderate and varies by type), moving beyond single-number benchmarks.

Weaknesses
- Limited methodological novelty: The core is a straightforward adaptation of skip-gram with phrase collapsing and uniform updates to all candidate concepts for an ambiguous phrase. The β/η terms are simple cosine-alignment regularizers. Compared to prior joint text–KB methods or sense embeddings, the learning objective itself is a light tweak.
- Inconsistent empirical wins vs. strong baselines: On Mayo SRS/Mini Mayo SRS, CHOI (claims-code-based) substantially outperforms this method (e.g., 0.817 vs. 0.593), and in semantic type clustering (Table 6) CHOI is best overall. On larger UMNSRS, the gains are modest and often comparable to strong word baselines. This weakens the claim that concept-level modeling—as instantiated here—consistently outperforms simpler alternatives.
- “Distant supervision” framing is misleading for ambiguity: Treating each ambiguous phrase occurrence as an occurrence of all its possible concepts, with uniform credit 1/|Cs|, is not supervision about concept identity; it injects systematic label noise. The term “distant supervision” suggests noisy labels of specific targets, whereas here the model receives no disambiguation signal and may conflate senses.
- Questionable utility of explicit concept vectors in general-domain results: On Wiki+NYT (Table 5), averaging phrase embeddings (APPROXPHRco) outperforms direct concept embeddings on both relatedness and similarity. This suggests that the ontology layer adds limited extra value beyond well-trained phrase vectors and raises the question of when concept vectors are necessary.
- Compositionality control seems globally mis-specified: The use of a single global β and η is coarse; tuned values are often zero (PM+CL and WikiNYT), arguing against enforcing compositionality and undermining the motivational narrative. It would be more plausible to learn phrase- or type-specific weights, or to condition on ambiguity/compositionality diagnostics.
- Clarity and notation issues: The embedding matrices EW, EP, EC are introduced but the subsequent loss is expressed only in vector form; it is unclear how the shared negative-sampling matrix interacts with the separate spaces beyond standard SGNS. More importantly, the experimental description for Table 4 lacks detail on how cosine similarities are computed for many-to-many concept–phrase mappings (e.g., when evaluation lists both CUIs and textual forms).
- Evaluation limitations: The new entity dataset has low IAA (κ=0.24), and hyperparameters are tuned on the same test sets (albeit with a cross-validated procedure), which can inflate intrinsic scores. No downstream tasks (e.g., entity linking, concept normalization) are reported to demonstrate practical gains.

Questions to authors
- Disambiguation: For phrases that map to multiple concepts, do you ever attempt sense disambiguation (e.g., via context priors, type constraints, or EM)? How sensitive are results to the uniform split of updates among candidate concepts?
- Concept–phrase similarity at test time: When a dataset provides both CUIs and surface forms, precisely how do you compute the score? Do you (a) use CUI vectors only, (b) average over mapped phrases, or (c) take the best among phrase/word/concept representations? Please clarify for each line in Tables 4–5.
- Regularization weights: Did you explore learning β and η per phrase or per semantic type (e.g., via auxiliary predictors or gating)? Given that tuned global values are often 0, do you have evidence that nonzero, adaptive weights help for compositional phrases while not hurting idiomatic ones?
- Ontology structure: Have you tried adding ontology graph edges (e.g., is-a, synonyms) as additional training signals (retrofitting or joint loss)? This could address the weak type clustering and the limited gains of concept vectors over phrase averages.
- Dataset and downstream tasks: Will you release train/test splits for the entity datasets? Have you evaluated on an extrinsic task (e.g., UMLS concept normalization, entity linking) where concept vectors should help over phrase-only models?
