Summary
The paper proposes a weakly supervised approach to jointly embed words, surface phrases, and ontology concepts in a single vector space. The core idea is to use ontology-provided representative phrases as distant supervision: phrases are greedily identified in text and replaced with unique identifiers; training alternates over the tagged and untagged corpora with a skip-gram objective, updating word, phrase, and concept embeddings. Two compositionality trade-off terms (β for phrase↔words, η for concept↔phrases) optionally encourage alignment across levels. The method is instantiated on UMLS (biomedical) and YAGO (general entities) with PubMed, clinical notes, Wikipedia, and Gigaword. Evaluation focuses on intrinsic similarity/relatedness tasks, plus analysis of alignment across levels and semantic-type clustering. The authors also introduce a new human-annotated dataset for similarity/relatedness of real-world entities.

Contributions (as assessed, strongest first)
1) A practical, scalable weakly supervised pipeline for jointly embedding concepts, phrases, and words using only an ontology and raw text, with strong coverage and no manual corpus annotation.
2) A new evaluation resource for real-world entity similarity and relatedness, along with baseline results and analysis.
3) A thorough empirical study on biomedical similarity/relatedness and type clustering, showing competitive intrinsic performance and 3x vocabulary coverage versus prior concept-embedding methods.

Strengths
- Practicality and scalability of supervision: The method requires no human annotation over the training corpora and leverages existing ontologies to supervise concept mentions through surface phrases. This makes it easy to port to new domains where ontologies exist but annotated text is scarce. The reported >3x concept coverage compared to prior work that relies on human-coded sequences (e.g., medical codes) is compelling.
- Competitive intrinsic performance with minimal annotation: On larger biomedical datasets (UMNSRS), the method achieves top or near-top Spearman correlations for similarity (e.g., matching or exceeding strong word baselines like CHIU-30) and competitive relatedness (phrases slightly surpass words). While CHOI dominates on small coder-centric datasets (MayoSRS), the proposed approach closes the gap substantially while avoiding manual supervision.
- Unified space and analyses across levels: Jointly embedding concepts, phrases, and words enables coherence checks and diagnostics not available to word-only or graph-only methods. The compositionality analyses (concept↔phrases, phrase↔words) and semantic-type clustering offer insight into how much lexical vs. conceptual information is being captured.
- Resource contribution and transparency: The new entity similarity/relatedness dataset fills a gap for evaluating entity-level semantics (distinct from word similarity). The paper is generally clear and well written, provides implementation (cui2vec), and documents hyperparameter tuning and corpora/ontology choices carefully.

Weaknesses
- Limited technical novelty: The core training is a straightforward adaptation of skip-gram with negative sampling on a corpus augmented with phrase identifiers, with simple regularization terms β and η to encourage compositionality. While useful, this is a natural extension rather than a substantial methodological advance; no new learning objective beyond standard SGNS and simple alignment is introduced, and ontology structure beyond surface mappings is not exploited.
- Narrow evaluation and lack of downstream tasks: Intrinsic similarity/relatedness and clustering are informative but insufficient to demonstrate utility in real applications. There are no evaluations on entity linking, concept normalization, retrieval, or clinical prediction tasks where concept embeddings could plausibly shine. Additionally, the biomedical focus dominates; the general-domain evaluation is limited to the new dataset rather than established entity-centric tasks.
- New dataset needs stronger validation: The entity similarity/relatedness resource is small (688 pairs per task), has only fair agreement (Fleiss’ κ=0.24), and the quality-control/validation setup appears modest. The paper would benefit from deeper analysis of disagreement, category balance, demographics, and reliability, and a clearer argument for the dataset’s target use-cases. Given the method’s incremental novelty, this resource may be the primary lasting contribution and deserves more thorough discussion.
- Methodological ambiguity and a technical issue in evaluation: The MAP formulation in Section 5.4/Eq. 8 is described as a probability and computed over the full vocabulary, which is atypical and potentially misleading. Standard AP/MAP is defined over a finite candidate set; ranking the entire vocabulary penalizes recall in a way that conflates signal with noise. A more appropriate choice would be AP at k (e.g., k in {10, 50, 100}) or AP restricted to a controlled candidate pool. The text should correct the probability wording and justify the ranking scope or adopt a cutoff.
- Distant-supervision noise and ambiguity handling: The approach assumes any occurrence of a mapped phrase signals an occurrence of each mapped concept (with normalization), which injects sense ambiguity and domain drift. While the large corpora may drown some noise, there is little analysis of ambiguity resolution, phrase matching errors (greedy longest match, casing, morphology), or how η/β interact with ambiguity. The compositionality terms reportedly often hurt performance when increased, suggesting limited ability to leverage lexical forms to disambiguate concepts.

Questions to Authors
- Eq. 8 and MAP: Please clarify the intended candidate set for ranking and whether you can report AP@k variants. Also, remove the “probability” wording and align with standard IR definitions.
- Ambiguity/noise: How frequent are multi-concept phrases in your data, and what is their effect on learned vectors? Did you try weighting by prior probabilities (e.g., concept popularity) or context-based disambiguation during training?
- Downstream tasks: Can you report results on an entity linking or concept normalization benchmark (biomedical and/or Wikipedia) to demonstrate practical utility?
- Ontology structure: Did you experiment with incorporating graph relations (e.g., parent-child, synonymy) as additional training signals, or retrofitting/posthoc alignment, and if so, how did results change?
- Dataset details: Will the entity similarity/relatedness dataset be publicly released with full annotation guidelines, demographics, and item-level agreement? Any plans to expand it or provide domain-adapted subsets?
