Summary
The paper proposes a weakly supervised method to jointly embed ontology concepts, their representative phrases, and words into a shared vector space. Phrases found in an unlabeled corpus are used as distant supervision for concepts, and a modified skip-gram objective learns embeddings for words, phrases, and concepts. Two hyperparameters control how much phrases are tied to averages of their words (β) and how much concepts are tied to averages of their phrases (η), enabling a tunable trade-off between atomic and compositional modeling. The method is evaluated on biomedical and general-domain corpora (PubMed, clinical notes, Wikipedia+NYT), using standard similarity/relatedness datasets (MayoSRS, UMNSRS) and a newly collected Mechanical Turk dataset for entities. The model attains competitive correlations to human judgments while offering much broader coverage, and the authors present qualitative analyses (e.g., semantic type clustering, concept–phrase proximity).

Main contributions (as I see them; ordered by strength)
1) A practical and scalable joint embedding framework for concepts, phrases, and words with a tunable atomic/compositional trade-off. The formulation is simple, implementable, and yields broad coverage without manual corpus annotation.
2) A new general-domain similarity/relatedness dataset for real-world entities and release of resources (implementation and data), which will likely be useful for the community.
3) Empirical analyses indicating some preservation of ontology structure (concept–phrase proximity and semantic-type clustering), though with caveats noted below.

Strengths
- Sensible, general approach with a clear atomic/compositional trade-off. The β and η mechanisms are a clean way to encode when phrases should behave as units versus be composed from words, and when concepts should cohere around their surface forms. The model fits naturally atop skip-gram and negative sampling, keeping complexity low.
- Scale and coverage. Training across large corpora with distant supervision from UMLS/YAGO yields substantially larger vocabulary coverage than methods relying on concept-tagged corpora or code sequences. The reported >3x coverage gains are meaningful for downstream applicability.
- Competitive intrinsic performance with no manual corpus annotation. Despite noise from distant supervision, concept and phrase embeddings achieve correlations close to or better than some stronger baselines on the larger biomedical datasets and are strong on the new entity datasets. This speaks to the utility of the approach in low-annotation settings.
- New evaluation resource and release. The Mechanical Turk dataset for entity similarity/relatedness fills a gap in concept/entity-level intrinsic evaluation, and the release of cui2vec/code is valuable for reproducibility and reuse.
- Diagnostic analyses beyond headline scores. The paper probes concept–phrase and phrase–word relationships (compositionality, proximity distributions) and semantic-type clustering, offering insight into what the model captures and where it struggles (e.g., sign/symptom type).

Weaknesses
- Evaluation is limited to intrinsic similarity/relatedness tasks; no extrinsic validation. Without downstream tasks (e.g., entity linking/normalization, concept-based retrieval, clinical risk prediction, KB completion), it is hard to assess whether the joint embedding and the β/η trade-offs yield practical gains. This is especially important given well-known limitations of similarity benchmarks for predicting extrinsic utility.
- Heavy hyperparameter tuning on the same evaluation datasets undermines conclusions. Although an “adapted 5-fold CV” is used, the similarity/relatedness sets serve as both tuning and reporting targets, risking overfitting and optimistic correlations. A clean development/test separation (or tuning on one dataset and reporting on held-out others) is needed.
- General-domain dataset conflates phrases and concepts. Human judgments appear elicited on phrase strings (not necessarily disambiguated concepts), yet the model learns both phrase and concept embeddings. This mismatch complicates the interpretation of results (e.g., whether higher scores reflect phrase-level semantics rather than concept-level generalization). The paper should analyze these separately and report how often phrases are ambiguous.
- Biomedical intrinsic datasets are small or mismatched. Mini MayoSRS (29 pairs) and MayoSRS (101 pairs, low agreement) are too small/noisy for robust tuning or claims. The UMNSRS setting is also limited (many single-word forms), which does not stress multiword compositionality or concept normalization—key motivations of the paper. Stronger or more diverse biomedical evaluations are needed.
- Overstated novelty/annotation savings. While the method avoids manual corpus annotation, it crucially depends on large, manually curated ontologies (UMLS/YAGO) for distant supervision, and comparisons lean on baselines that themselves leverage annotations. Claims of outperforming prior work should be tempered to reflect reliance on rich human-curated resources.
- Limited analysis of the atomic vs compositional trade-off. The paper introduces β and η but does not deeply analyze their effects: e.g., per-corpus or per-phrase-type sensitivity, headword effects, or conditions under which stronger compositionality helps/hurts. Given this is core to the method’s motivation, a more granular ablation would strengthen the contribution.

Questions to authors (for rebuttal)
- How are overlapping/embedded phrases resolved during greedy matching? Please specify deterministic tie-breaking and show how often overlaps occur and with what effect on training.
- What was the exact development set used for hyperparameter selection per corpus? Can you provide results with a fixed dev set and a held-out test set to mitigate overfitting concerns?
- For the new entity dataset: were annotators presented with disambiguating links (e.g., Wikipedia pages) or only surface strings? Please report the proportion of ambiguous phrases and analyze performance separately for ambiguous vs unambiguous cases.
- Can you provide at least one extrinsic task evaluation (even in supplementary): e.g., entity linking (phrase→concept), or concept similarity for ICD code grouping?
- β and η often tuned to 0 in Table 3: does this imply limited benefit from compositionality? Please include curves showing performance vs β/η and qualitative examples where nonzero settings help/hurt.

Additional comments and minor issues
- Clarify/typos:
  - “contexts” → “concepts” (around line 69).
  - Explain phrase overlap handling (around line 202).
  - Matrix shapes and terminology (around line 220): the “negative sampling matrix” description is confusing; confirm dimensions (|W| x d) for output embeddings and avoid implying a separate matrix of size |W| x k.
  - Training description (around line 250): clarify how word updates are performed in the joint model and what “phrase just completed” means operationally in the windowing.
  - Equation 1 uses c where o is expected; unify notation.
  - Pedersen et al. 2007 should be fully and correctly cited in references.
  - The 1–100 similarity scale may be too fine-grained; justify and report inter-annotator variance by bin.
  - Prefer “phrases” to “strings” for clarity (around line 430).
  - Specify which tasks/datasets were used for hyperparameter tuning (around line 496) and add development-set trends (especially for PM+CL) so readers can see sensitivity.
  - Add a reference to Table 5 where first discussed (around line 535).
- Reporting: Consider reporting confidence intervals for Spearman’s ρ and per-category breakdowns (e.g., Person–Person vs Organization–Place) on the new dataset.

Overall recommendation
Reject. The method is promising and well-executed, and the resources are valuable, but the evaluation methodology (intrinsic-only, tuning on test sets, small/noisy biomedical datasets, phrase vs concept mismatch) weakens the empirical claims. Adding clean dev/test splits, extrinsic tasks, and deeper analysis of the β/η trade-off would make a strong resubmission.
