- Summary: Proposes weakly supervised joint embeddings for words, phrases, and ontology concepts by replacing representative phrases with atomic tokens and training a skip-gram–style model with global compositionality hyperparameters β and η; evaluated on biomedical and new general-domain datasets and releases cui2vec and datasets.

- Contribution 1: Released software and trained embeddings that substantially increase biomedical concept coverage and new general-domain entity similarity/relatedness datasets.

- Contribution 2: Thorough empirical comparison of word-, phrase-, and concept-level embeddings and approximations across corpora and tasks, showing coverage–performance trade-offs.

- Contribution 3: Practical method: simple adaptation of skip-gram that updates concept vectors from contexts of mapped phrases with optional global compositionality regularizers.

- Strength: Practical impact and coverage gain (>3x vocabulary coverage) for biomedical NLP without manual annotation.

- Strength: Simple, pragmatic training recipe—treating phrases as atomic tokens integrates smoothly with skip-gram/negative sampling.

- Strength: Competitive intrinsic results in several settings; phrase/approximation methods often perform well.

- Strength: Explicitly addresses non-compositional multiword expressions and provides a compositionality control (β).

- Strength: Public release of embeddings and datasets benefits the community.

- Weakness: Limited methodological novelty—incremental adaptation of existing skip-gram ideas and simple regularizers.

- Weakness: Inconsistent gains versus prior baselines; some baselines (e.g., CHOI) outperform their concept model on key benchmarks.

- Weakness: Evidence suggests global β/η are often zero in cross-validation, undermining the usefulness of the compositionality terms and suggesting the need for instance-specific gating.

- Weakness: Approximate phrase-averaging (APPROXPHRco) often matches or outperforms learned concept vectors, questioning added value of concept parameters/ontology mapping.

- Weakness: “Distant supervision” framing is misleading because mapped phrase occurrences provide distributional context rather than labeled supervision; many-to-many mappings create noisy updates.

- Weakness: Notation and clarity issues (inconsistent matrix usage, undefined Cs, unclear ENS description) hinder reproducibility.

- Weakness: Evaluation ambiguities—unclear handling of many-to-many mappings, disambiguation, and possible tuning on test sets weaken intrinsic claims.

- Question 1: How are similarities computed when concepts have multiple representative phrases—averaging, best match, or CUI-only—and how are ambiguous phrases mapped to multiple CUIs handled?

- Question 2: What specific signal do concept parameters capture beyond averaged phrase vectors—can you quantify cases where concept vectors help and analyze failures?

- Question 3: Why use global β and η instead of per-phrase/per-concept or data-driven gates (e.g., learned attention, PMI-based scores), and did you try simple heterogeneous variants?

- Question 4: Please justify calling the method “distant supervision” and clarify how assuming a phrase occurrence signals all mapped concepts affects noise and polysemy in practice.

- Question 5: Clarify the negative sampling matrix ENS (dimensions/purpose) and ensure consistent use/definitions of matrices and Cs in equations to improve reproducibility.

- Question 6: Do you have analysis for underperformance versus CHOI on MayoSRS/Mini MayoSRS and type clustering—is it domain mismatch, coverage, or hyperparameter choice?

- Additional comment: The resource release is the main strength; to strengthen acceptance, add downstream task evaluations or explicit use of ontology structure to capture non-lexical semantics.
