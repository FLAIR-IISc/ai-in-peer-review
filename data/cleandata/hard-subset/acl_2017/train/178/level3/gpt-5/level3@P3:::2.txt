Summary
This paper proposes a weakly supervised approach to jointly learn embeddings for words, surface phrases, and ontology concepts. The core idea is to map ontology concepts (UMLS and YAGO) to sets of representative phrases, replace those phrases with atomic tokens in text, and train a skip-gram–style model where concepts get updated from the contexts of any of their mapped phrases. Two global hyperparameters, β and η, softly tie phrase embeddings to the average of their component word embeddings and concept embeddings to the average of their phrase embeddings, respectively. The model is evaluated on concept similarity and relatedness tasks in the biomedical domain (MayoSRS, Mini MayoSRS, UMNSRS) and a new general-domain entity similarity/relatedness dataset. The authors release software (cui2vec) and the new general-domain datasets.

Main contributions (as I see them; strongest first)
1) Resource contribution: released software and trained embeddings that substantially increase coverage of biomedical concepts with no manual annotation, plus new general-domain entity similarity/relatedness datasets.
2) Empirical study: a thorough comparison of word-, phrase-, and concept-level embeddings and their approximations across corpora and tasks, showing competitive performance and useful coverage trade-offs.
3) Method: a practical tweak to skip-gram that jointly trains word, phrase, and concept embeddings by treating phrase occurrences as weak signals for their concepts and optionally enforcing global compositionality via β and η.

Strengths
- Practical impact and coverage: The approach leverages ontologies to target non-compositional phrases (e.g., “Lou Gehrig’s disease”) and produces embeddings for words, phrases, and concepts in a unified space without manual corpus annotation. The reported >3x vocabulary coverage increase is compelling for biomedical NLP, where coverage gaps are costly.
- Plausible and simple training recipe: Turning representative phrases into atomic tokens and updating concept vectors from their surrounding word contexts is a pragmatic way to train phrase and concept embeddings. The formulation integrates smoothly with standard skip-gram/negative sampling.
- Competitive intrinsic performance in several settings: On UMNSRS Similarity, CONCEPTco equals the strong CHIU-30 baseline (ρ=0.653), and on UMNSRS Relatedness the jointly trained representations (phrases/words) are competitive. On general-domain datasets, concept/phrase representations perform well, with APPROXPHRco often strongest.
- Addresses non-compositionality: The model explicitly treats multiword expressions as atomic when appropriate and provides a knob (β) to temper compositionality, matching the motivation.
- Released software/datasets: cui2vec and the general-domain entity similarity/relatedness datasets can benefit the community, particularly biomedical NLP researchers who need higher-coverage concept embeddings.

Weaknesses
- Limited methodological novelty: The core is a straightforward adaptation of skip-gram with phrase replacement and concept updates; the compositionality terms are simple regularizers. As a modeling contribution, this is incremental.
- Inconsistent gains vs. baselines: The full model does not consistently outperform prior work. CHOI’s medical code embeddings substantially outperform on MayoSRS/Mini MayoSRS (e.g., 0.817/0.726 vs. 0.593/0.591 for CONCEPTco) and lead in semantic type clustering overall (Table 6). On UMNSRS Relatedness, CHIU-30 (0.590) beats CONCEPTco (0.559).
- Evidence against the added compositionality: A single global β/η is surprising for heterogeneous phrases/concepts. Cross-validated values of β=0 for PM+CL and WIKINYT suggest that enforcing phrase-word compositionality hurts; η is also often 0. This undermines the utility of the added compositionality mechanism and suggests a need for instance-specific or data-driven gating.
- Approximations often match or beat concept vectors: APPROXPHRco (averaging phrase vectors) outperforms direct concept embeddings in several settings (e.g., MayoSRS/Mini MayoSRS and general-domain Table 5). This raises the question of whether the concept-level parameters and ontology mapping add enough beyond strong phrase-level baselines.
- Framing as “distant supervision” is potentially misleading: Occurrences of mapped phrases provide distributional context, not labeled relations or traditional supervision signals. Given many-to-many phrase↔concept mappings, treating each phrase mention as evidence for every mapped concept is a weak and noisy assumption; the term “weak supervision” or “weakly labeled contexts” might be clearer.
- Notation and clarity issues: The matrices introduced in Section 3.2 (e.g., EW) are not consistently used in the subsequent equations, and Eq. (4) mixes notation (c∈Cs) without defining Cs in that subsection. ENS as a |W|×k matrix is unusual and under-explained. These issues hinder reproducibility.
- Evaluation setup ambiguities: For Table 4, datasets provide both CUIDs and text forms and allow many-to-many mappings. It is unclear exactly how similarities are computed when a concept has multiple phrases (or vice versa), and how disambiguation is handled. Additionally, tuning on test sets—even with adapted cross-validation—limits the strength of intrinsic claims.

Questions to authors
1) Many-to-many mappings in evaluation: In Table 4, when a concept has multiple representative phrases and the datasets provide both CUI and text forms, how exactly do you compute a single similarity score for a pair? Do you average across phrases, select the best match, or use only CUI-based vectors? How do you handle ambiguous phrases mapped to multiple CUIs?
2) Role of the concept parameters: Given that APPROXPHRco often outperforms CONCEPTco (Tables 4–5), what specific signal do the concept parameters capture beyond averaging phrase vectors? Can you quantify cases where concept vectors help and analyze failure modes?
3) Compositionality control: Why choose global β and η rather than per-phrase/per-concept parameters or data-driven gates (e.g., learned attention over words/phrases, PMI-based compositionality scores)? Did you try simple heterogeneity (e.g., type-specific β/η) and does it help?
4) “Distant supervision” terminology: Could you justify this term in the context of your training signal and clarify how your assumption “a phrase occurrence signifies an occurrence of each concept it can represent” affects concept updates in practice (e.g., degree of noise, impact on polysemous phrases)?
5) Notation and ENS: Please clarify the purpose and dimensions of the negative sampling matrix ENS and how it couples the three spaces in practice. Also, ensure consistent use of the matrix notation introduced in 3.2 and define Cs in Eq. (4).
6) Discrepancy with CHOI/Mayo and Table 6: Do you have an analysis for why the method underperforms CHOI on MayoSRS/Mini MayoSRS and in overall type clustering? Is this primarily due to domain mismatch (claims code sequences vs. text), coverage, or hyperparameters?

Additional comments
- The resource contribution (software + higher coverage) is the main reason to consider acceptance; the method is practical but not novel, and intrinsic results are mixed. A stronger case would include downstream tasks (e.g., entity linking, clinical phenotyping) or direct incorporation of ontology structure to capture non-lexical semantics.
