Summary: The paper proposes a weakly supervised approach to jointly learn embeddings for words, phrases, and ontology concepts by replacing matched phrase mentions in a corpus with fixed concept identifiers, and training a skip-gram style model on both the tagged and original text. Experiments focus on biomedical concepts (UMLS) and general-domain entities (YAGO). The authors also introduce a new similarity/relatedness dataset for real-world entities.

Contributions (as I see them, strongest first):
1) A new evaluation resource of entity-level similarity/relatedness judgments, potentially useful to the community beyond this method.
2) A simple, scalable pipeline for jointly embedding concepts, phrases, and words via distant supervision from ontologies.
3) An empirical study demonstrating competitive intrinsic performance and high coverage on biomedical corpora.

Strengths:
- Practicality and simplicity: The distant-supervision tagging plus skip-gram training is natural, low-cost, and easy to reproduce and scale, achieving >3x coverage over some prior concept-embedding resources.
- Joint space: Embedding words, phrases, and concepts together is valuable for downstream tasks (e.g., entity linking, concept normalization), and the analysis shows reasonable alignment between concepts and their phrases.
- Empirical competitiveness: The method attains strong correlations on larger biomedical similarity datasets and reasonable performance on relatedness, often matching or approaching methods that require costly annotations.
- Resource contribution: The introduced entity similarity/relatedness dataset fills a gap in evaluation resources for real-world entities.

Weaknesses:
- Limited novelty: Methodologically, this is an incremental extension of established word2vec training with distant supervision; the technical contribution is modest.
- Narrow evaluation: Results are mostly intrinsic and largely biomedical; broader domains and downstream tasks (entity linking, retrieval, coding) would increase impact and test generality.
- Technical flaw in evaluation: Equation 8 (MAP) is incorrectly described as a probability and ranks the entire vocabulary, which is unnatural; the authors should correct the definition and/or introduce a principled cutoff or candidate set.
- Dataset discussion is thin: More detail is needed on annotator instructions, quality control, reliability (κ≈0.24 is only fair), demographics, licensing/availability, and recommended splits; this resource may be the paper’s primary contribution and deserves deeper treatment.
- Minor editorial issue: line 556 “most concepts has” → “most concepts have.”

Questions to Authors (optional):
- How are phrases mapping to multiple concepts handled beyond uniform normalization? Have you tried priors or ambiguity-aware weighting?
- Can you provide downstream evaluations where concept embeddings matter (e.g., entity linking or clinical coding)?
- Will you release the new dataset with clear license and standard train/dev/test splits?
- Please clarify and correct the MAP formulation and the ranking scope (full vocab vs. candidate sets).
