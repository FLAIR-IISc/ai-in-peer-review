Summary and contributions
The paper proposes a weakly supervised approach that maps ontology concepts to their representative phrases, replaces phrase mentions in text, and trains joint word/phrase/concept embeddings with a skip-gram–style objective augmented by compositionality terms. Evaluations span biomedical (UMLS) and general-domain (YAGO) concept similarity/relatedness. In my view, the primary contributions are:
1) Resource and practicality: a scalable, annotation-free training recipe with released software (cui2vec) and broad coverage that can directly benefit biomedical NLP.
2) Empirical study: competitive performance on multiple similarity/relatedness benchmarks, especially for non-compositional phrases, with joint embeddings enabling cross-granularity comparisons.
3) Dataset contribution: a new general-domain entity similarity/relatedness set (moderate IAA), extending evaluation beyond words.

Strengths
- Practical impact and coverage: The approach attains >3x vocabulary coverage without manual annotation and ships software, which is valuable for biomedical practitioners who need concept-level vectors but lack labeled corpora.
- Competitive results in several settings: On UMNSRS similarity, CONCEPTco/CHIU-30 are tied for best, and on relatedness the method is competitive with strong word baselines; in YAGO evaluations, phrase-based approximations (APPROXPHRco) are strong (Table 5).
- Addresses non-compositionality: Training phrase embeddings directly, rather than relying solely on word composition, is a plausible and useful design for expressions like “Lou Gehrig’s disease,” and the analysis indicates reasonable alignment of concepts and their phrases.
- Joint space utility: Co-embedding words, phrases, and concepts can facilitate downstream tasks (e.g., linking, retrieval) that require moving across granularities.

Weaknesses
- Limited methodological novelty: The core is a minor tweak to skip-gram with phrase substitution and optional regularizers; Section 3.2 introduces notation (e.g., EW) that is not used later, reinforcing the impression of incrementalism.
- Inconsistent superiority: CHOI substantially outperforms on MayoSRS/Mini (Table 4) and dominates semantic-type clustering (Table 6); on UMNSRS the model is at best competitive with simpler word baselines (CHIU).
- Questionable “distant supervision” claim: Phrase occurrences provide unlabeled context, not supervised signals; polysemy is unresolved, and the approach effectively treats all matches as positive for all linked concepts.
- Compositionality control is underspecified: A single global β is surprising; cross-validated β=0 for PM+CL and WikiNYT argues against enforcing compositionality, undercutting the purported benefit of the β/η terms.
- Empirical signal undermines concept objective: Table 5 shows averaging phrase vectors (APPROXPHRco) often best, calling into question the added value of explicit concept embeddings and the η term.

Questions to authors (optional)
- How are similarities computed in Table 4 given many-to-many concept↔phrase mappings? Do you aggregate over phrases or disambiguate mentions?
- Why call the signal “distant supervision”? Can you clarify what supervision the ontology provides beyond lexicon construction?
- Did you try per-phrase or per-type β/η, or gating them by ambiguity? Any ablations isolating the benefit of the concept objective vs. starting from phrase embeddings?
- Can you comment on why CHOI wins on Mayo and clustering, and provide error analysis on where your method fails?
