Summary
The paper proposes a weakly supervised approach to jointly learning embeddings for concepts, phrases, and words. Given an ontology (UMLS, YAGO), the authors map representative phrases to concept IDs, collapse matched phrases to single tokens in a corpus, and train a skip-gram/negative-sampling model that (a) learns word and phrase vectors from local word contexts and (b) updates concept vectors when any of their representative phrases appear. Two auxiliary terms with global weights β and η softly tie phrase vectors to the average of their words and concept vectors to the average of their phrases, respectively. The approach is evaluated on biomedical and general-domain similarity/relatedness datasets, with analyses of concept–phrase/phrase–word compositionality and semantic type clustering. Code and a new general-entity similarity/relatedness dataset are released.

Contributions (as I see them)
1) A large-coverage empirical study showing that concept-level vectors learned without manual corpus annotation can be competitive with prior concept embeddings on UMNSRS similarity and comparable on relatedness, while covering more concepts than annotated baselines.
2) Practical resources: an implementation (cui2vec) and a new general-domain entity similarity/relatedness dataset, which can benefit follow-up work.
3) Analyses that probe compositionality and semantic type clustering across concepts, phrases, and words.

Strengths
- Practicality and coverage: The approach requires no human corpus annotation and scales to hundreds of thousands of concepts and phrases across two domains (UMLS, YAGO). This addresses an important bottleneck in medical NLP and entity-centric modeling and yields >3x vocabulary coverage compared to some annotated alternatives.
- Competitive performance in several settings: On UMNSRS Similarity (the larger biomedical benchmark), CONCEPTco achieves the highest ρ among concept embeddings reported, and phrase-based variants are competitive on relatedness. In the general domain, the approach yields strong performance for entity similarity/relatedness, especially when aggregating phrase representations.
- Addresses non-compositionality: Treating phrases as atomic units is well-motivated for biomedical and entity-centric semantics. The method makes it easy to capture non-compositional phrases (e.g., disease names), while still allowing optional compositional signals via β and η.
- Resource release and careful experimentation: The release of software and a new entity similarity/relatedness dataset is valuable. The paper also performs hyperparameter exploration per corpus, reports tuned settings, and includes additional analyses (concept–phrase proximity, semantic type clustering).
- Cross-domain demonstration: Showing the same pipeline on UMLS and YAGO, and on clinical notes, PubMed, Wikipedia, and news, strengthens claims of generality.

Weaknesses
- Limited methodological novelty: The core training is a straightforward adaptation of skip-gram/negative sampling with phrase collapsing and a global regularization towards averages. There is no use of ontology structure (types/relations) beyond the phrase–concept mapping. As such, the main novelty is the application/engineering and the joint objective rather than a new learning principle.
- Inconsistent gains versus strong baselines: On Mayo SRS and Mini Mayo SRS, CHOI substantially outperforms the proposed method. In semantic type clustering (Table 6), CHOI achieves the best overall MAP, and the proposed embeddings are mixed across types. Even on general-domain entity tasks, the best result often comes from averaging phrase vectors (APPROXPHRco), not the learned concept vectors, questioning the added value of the concept-level training.
- Claims around “distant supervision” and supervision signal: Referring to phrase occurrences as “distant supervision” is somewhat misleading. The supervision here is indistinguishable from standard distributional context learning, with the ontology only providing surface forms. There is no concept-level labeling in context or sense disambiguation; ambiguous phrases are uniformly split across concepts.
- Clarity and notation issues: The notation introduced in Section 3.2 (e.g., EW, EP, EC) is not used later. Equation (4) uses c ∈ Cs, but Cs isn’t defined; earlier Cp is used for the concept set of phrase p. The paper would benefit from tighter, consistent notation and a clearer statement of how negative sampling and contexts are shared across the three matrices.
- Design of β and η and empirical findings: Using a single global β/η for all phrases/concepts is a crude way to control compositionality. Cross-validated settings often pick β=η=0 (e.g., PM+CL, WIKINYT), implying that the auxiliary compositionality signals do not help; this undercuts the motivation for the joint objective terms. A more nuanced, data-driven approach (e.g., per-phrase weights, ambiguity-aware weighting) might be needed.

Questions to Authors
- Many-to-many mappings: How exactly are similarities computed for a concept pair when each concept has multiple representative phrases? Do you always compare concept vectors (EC), or do you sometimes aggregate phrase vectors? For PHRASEco/APPROXPHRco, how are multiple phrases per concept aggregated at test time?
- Ambiguity handling: When a phrase maps to multiple concepts, you normalize updates by |Cp|. Did you try sense priors (e.g., frequency-based) or learned attention over concepts to reduce noise from ambiguous phrases?
- Negative sampling and subsampling: What is the negative sampling distribution and word/phrase subsampling strategy? Are phrases exempt from subsampling? These can materially affect results and reproducibility.
- Compositionality terms: Did you try per-phrase β (e.g., higher for compositional phrases, lower for proper names) or per-concept η (e.g., based on phrase diversity/ambiguity)? Any ablations where β/η are learned?
- Evaluation protocol: For Table 4 tuning, you mention adapted 5-fold CV on the test sets. Can you provide the per-fold variance and confirm no leakage across folds (e.g., via shared hyperparameters)? Any evaluation on extrinsic tasks (e.g., entity linking, code prediction) to address known limitations of similarity/relatedness metrics?
- Concept utility in the general domain: In Table 5, APPROXPHRco outperforms CONCEPTco. What do you see as the added value of concept vectors in this setting? Are there error analyses showing cases where concept vectors help over phrase averages?

Additional Comments
- Please tighten Section 3’s notation (e.g., define Cs or replace with Cp in Eq. 4/7), and explicitly state how EW/EP/EC interact during training and inference. Clarify whether ENS is tied/shared with output embeddings and whether separate input/output embeddings are used.
- Clarify preprocessing: phrase matching (tokenization, case-folding, greedy longest-match specifics), minimum frequency thresholds for phrases/words, and whether multiword overlap conflicts are resolved deterministically.
- Consider integrating ontology structure (hierarchy/types/relations) as an auxiliary objective; this may address the mixed performance on small datasets and the semantic type clustering.
- The new crowdsourced datasets have low agreement (κ≈0.24). Please report rater counts per item, score distributions, and provide guidelines or exemplars used to prime raters.
- The paper convincingly shows scalability and decent intrinsic quality; adding one extrinsic task would significantly strengthen the impact.
