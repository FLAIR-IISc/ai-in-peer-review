

This paper proposes a weakly-supervised method for jointly embedding concepts, phrases, and words using ontology concepts as distant supervision. The main contributions are: (1) a novel approach to jointly embed concepts, phrases, and words using a skip-gram model adapted to use representative phrases as supervision; (2) demonstration of competitive performance on concept similarity and relatedness tasks without human annotation; and (3) release of a novel dataset for evaluating similarity and relatedness of real-world entities.

Strengths: The paper addresses an important gap in semantic modeling by handling non-compositional phrases that cannot be represented as combinations of their constituent words. The authors demonstrate that their method can learn embeddings for concepts that capture semantic relationships without requiring human annotation of concepts in text, which is a significant practical advantage. The evaluation on the UMNSRS datasets shows their concept embeddings achieve the highest correlation with human judgments on similarity tasks (0.653), outperforming all other methods including those that require manual annotation. The release of cui2vec software and the novel entity similarity dataset will benefit the biomedical NLP community.

The method's ability to scale to larger vocabulary sizes (over 3x coverage compared to existing methods) without additional annotation cost is a significant practical contribution. The paper also provides valuable insights into how the ontology structure influences the embedding space, showing that concepts and their representative phrases are embedded close to one another, and concepts of the same semantic type cluster together.

Weaknesses: The core method is not sufficiently novel—it's essentially a minor modification to the skip-gram model with negative sampling. The authors use the term "distant supervision" to describe their approach, but this is misleading as the phrase occurrences provide context rather than the traditional supervised signal. The paper would be stronger if it clearly distinguished their method from existing approaches like Wang et al. (2014a).

The experimental results are inconsistent with the claimed novelty. The full model does not consistently outperform simpler baselines—Choi et al. (2016b) substantially outperforms their method on the Mayo SRS and Mini Mayo SRS datasets (0.817 vs 0.593 and 0.726 vs 0.591). On the larger UMNSRS datasets, their method is at best competitive with the simpler Chiu-30 baseline (0.653 vs 0.653 for similarity), suggesting the additional complexity of their model may not be justified.

The paper's hyperparameter tuning is questionable. The authors use a single global β to control compositionality, but Table 3 shows β=0 for the largest datasets (PM+CL and WIKINYT), suggesting compositionality is not beneficial for these corpora. This undermines their claim that their model can effectively balance compositionality and independence.

The experimental setup for concept similarity evaluation is unclear. With many-to-many concept-phrase mappings, it's unclear how the authors compute the similarity between concepts in Table 4. The paper states they use the learned concept embeddings, but the methodology for handling multiple phrases per concept is not detailed.

The most concerning result is shown in Table 5, where the phrase-based concept approximation (APPROXPHRco) outperforms the direct concept embeddings (CONCEPTco) on the real-world entity datasets (0.810 vs 0.789 for relatedness). This suggests the concept ontology may not add significant value beyond starting with the phrase representations, questioning the core premise of the paper.

Questions to authors: 
1. How exactly do you compute concept similarities when a concept has multiple representative phrases (as in the many-to-many mapping)? 
2. Why do the hyperparameters show β=0 for the largest datasets (PM+CL and WIKINYT), contradicting your claim that compositionality is beneficial? 
3. Given that APPROXPHRco consistently outperforms CONCEPTco (Tables 4 and 5), what specific value does the concept embedding layer provide beyond phrase embeddings? 
4. How would you modify your approach to better leverage the ontology structure, as suggested by the authors' own analysis of semantic type clustering?
