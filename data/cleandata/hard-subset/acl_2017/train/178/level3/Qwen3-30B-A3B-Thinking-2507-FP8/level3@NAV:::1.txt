

This paper proposes a weakly-supervised method for jointly embedding concepts, phrases, and words by replacing phrase occurrences with ontology concept identifiers in a corpus, then training embeddings using a modified skip-gram model. The authors evaluate their approach on biomedical concepts from UMLS and real-world entities from YAGO, introducing a novel dataset of human-rated similarity/relatedness judgments for real-world entities.

The primary contribution of this work is the novel test dataset for real-world entity similarity/relatedness, which fills a significant gap in the literature. The method itself, while well-executed and natural, represents only a subtle extension of existing word embedding techniques rather than a major technical advance. The paper demonstrates competitive performance on biomedical similarity tasks compared to methods requiring manual annotation, but the evaluation is narrowly focused on biomedical data, limiting generalizability.

Strengths include the high-quality, well-written paper, the practical weakly-supervised approach that avoids expensive manual annotation, and the valuable new dataset for real-world entity similarity/relatedness. The method's simplicity and effectiveness in capturing semantic relationships between concepts and their representative phrases are also notable.

Weaknesses include the limited technical novelty, as the approach builds directly on word2vec without introducing substantial innovations. The evaluation's narrow biomedical focus prevents broader claims about general applicability. The MAP calculation in Equation 8 is incorrectly described as a probability and should not rank the entire vocabulary, which requires correction. Additionally, the paper doesn't sufficiently discuss the significance and potential impact of their novel dataset, which appears to be its most valuable contribution.

The authors should clarify the relationship between their method and prior work (e.g., Wang et al. 2014a), address the technical issue with Equation 8, and provide more substantial discussion of the test dataset's value. The minor editorial error (line 556) should be corrected. While the method is sound and the dataset is valuable, the contribution does not rise to the level of a major technical advance expected at this conference.
