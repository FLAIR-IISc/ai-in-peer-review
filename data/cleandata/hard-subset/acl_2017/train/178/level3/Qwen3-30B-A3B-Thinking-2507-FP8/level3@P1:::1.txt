

This paper proposes a weakly-supervised method for jointly embedding concepts, phrases, and words using distant supervision from ontology mappings. The core idea involves replacing phrases in a corpus with unique concept identifiers from an ontology (UMLS for biomedical concepts, YAGO for general entities), then training embeddings on the modified corpus. The authors claim to achieve competitive performance on concept similarity and relatedness tasks without human annotation, with 3x larger vocabulary coverage than prior methods.

The main contributions of this work are:
1. A novel dataset for evaluating similarity and relatedness of real-world entities (people, places, organizations), addressing a significant gap in the literature.
2. A straightforward method for jointly embedding concepts, phrases, and words that requires no human annotation.
3. Comprehensive evaluation of concept embeddings on biomedical and general-domain datasets.

Strengths:
1. The novel entity similarity/relatedness dataset is a significant contribution that fills an important gap in the literature. The paper provides detailed methodology for its creation, including crowdsourcing procedures, validation questions, and inter-annotator agreement metrics. This dataset will be valuable for future work in semantic evaluation.
2. The method is well-motivated and conceptually simple, making it easy to understand and implement. The authors provide a clear technical description of the model architecture and training procedure, and the approach leverages established techniques (word2vec) in a natural way.
3. The evaluation is thorough for the biomedical domain, with multiple datasets (MayoSRS, Mini Mayo SRS, UMNSRS) and comprehensive comparisons against state-of-the-art baselines. The results demonstrate competitive performance with methods that require human annotation.

Weaknesses:
1. The method itself represents a subtle extension of existing techniques rather than a major technical advance. The core approach (replacing phrases with concept identifiers and training word embeddings) is a straightforward application of distant supervision, similar to Mintz et al. (2009), and does not introduce novel learning algorithms or significant theoretical insights.
2. The evaluation is narrowly focused on biomedical data, with limited exploration of the method's applicability to other domains. While the authors do include YAGO for general-domain concepts, the primary evaluation and most detailed analysis focus on biomedical concepts, limiting the generalizability of the claims.
3. The MAP calculation in Eq. 8 is incorrectly described as a probability and should not rank the entire vocabulary. The authors state it as "the precision of c in Cp, V" but don't explain how the ranking is determined or what cutoff should be used. This needs clarification or correction.
4. The paper does not sufficiently discuss the potential limitations and broader impact of their novel dataset. The authors could better explain how this dataset differs from existing word similarity datasets, its limitations, and how it might be used in future research.

Questions to Authors:
1. The paper claims "more than 3x coverage in the vocabulary size" compared to prior methods, but doesn't specify what baseline they're comparing against. Could you clarify which prior methods you're comparing to and how the vocabulary size was measured?
2. The MAP calculation in Eq. 8 is described as a probability but should not rank the entire vocabulary. Could you clarify how the ranking is determined and whether a natural cutoff should be applied?
3. The authors mention that their method "demonstrates more than 3x coverage in the vocabulary size" but don't discuss how this impacts downstream tasks. Could you provide examples of specific NLP applications where this increased coverage would be particularly beneficial?

Additional Comments:
The paper is well-written and the method is natural, but the contribution is limited in terms of technical novelty. The most significant contribution appears to be the novel dataset, which deserves more emphasis. The minor editorial error at line 556 ("most concepts has" should be "most concepts have") should be corrected.

Overall, while the method is straightforward and the dataset is valuable, the paper doesn't present a major technical advance. The contribution is incremental rather than transformative, and the evaluation is limited to biomedical domains. The novel dataset is a strong point, but the paper would benefit from more thorough discussion of its potential impact and limitations. The technical issues with the MAP calculation also need to be addressed.
