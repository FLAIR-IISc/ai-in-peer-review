- Proposes joint embedding of concepts, phrases, and words by mapping ontology concepts to representative phrases and training with a skip-gram style model.  
- Evaluated on concept similarity and relatedness tasks using UMLS and YAGO, claiming competitive performance without manual annotation.  
- Main contributions claimed: method for handling non-compositional phrases, released cui2vec software, and a new similarity/relatedness dataset for real-world entities.  
- Strength: practical utility of the released software for biomedical researchers.  
- Strength: method can handle non-compositional phrases and shows competitive performance on the larger UMNSRS dataset.  
- Strength: achieves over 3x vocabulary coverage compared to prior work and tackles embedding concepts without expensive manual annotation.  
- Weakness: method is essentially a minor modification to skip-gram and lacks clear methodological novelty.  
- Weakness: full model does not consistently outperform simpler baselines; Choi baseline substantially outperforms on Mayo datasets.  
- Weakness: on UMNSRS the authors’ approach is at best competitive with simpler baselines, not clearly superior.  
- Weakness: the term “distant supervision” is misleading because phrase occurrences provide contextual information rather than true supervised signals.  
- Weakness: experimental setup for Table 4 is unclear, especially how similarities are computed with many-to-many concept↔phrase mappings.  
- Major concern: Table 5 shows simple averaging of phrase vectors (approximate phr) performs best, undermining the value of using the concept ontology.  
- Overall: software and dataset are valuable, but methodological novelty and experimental support for the main claims are weak; clarify relation to simpler alternatives and the “distant supervision” framing.
