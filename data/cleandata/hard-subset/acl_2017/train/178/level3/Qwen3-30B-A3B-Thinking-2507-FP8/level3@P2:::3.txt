

# Review for ACL 2017

## Summary and Main Contributions
The paper proposes a weakly-supervised method for jointly embedding concepts, phrases, and words by using representative phrases from ontology concepts as distant supervision. The authors train embeddings for medical concepts (UMLS) and general-domain entities (YAGO) from unlabeled text corpora, claiming competitive performance on concept similarity/relatedness tasks with significantly higher vocabulary coverage than prior methods. 

The main contributions of this work, as I see them, are:
1. A novel weakly-supervised framework for jointly embedding concepts, phrases, and words using ontology mappings as distant supervision, which requires no human corpus annotation.
2. The creation of a new dataset for evaluating similarity and relatedness of real-world entities, addressing a gap in the literature.
3. Thorough empirical analysis demonstrating that concept embeddings can preserve semantic properties comparable to supervised methods without manual corpus annotation.

## Strengths
1. The method is conceptually sound and well-executed, with a clear framework for handling the many-to-many relationship between concepts and phrases. The proposed model sensibly balances treating phrases/concepts as atomic units versus compositional, which is a significant contribution to the field.
2. The authors have created a valuable new dataset for evaluating similarity/relatedness of real-world entities, which fills an important gap in the literature. This dataset is well-constructed with appropriate validation procedures and provides a useful resource for future work.
3. The paper provides thorough empirical analysis of the embedding space, including compositionality evaluations and semantic type clustering, which demonstrates that the model successfully captures certain semantic relationships between concepts and phrases.

## Weaknesses
1. The evaluation relies solely on intrinsic similarity/relatedness tasks without any extrinsic task evaluation, which is a significant limitation for a paper claiming to provide useful embeddings. Without showing performance on downstream NLP tasks, the practical utility of the embeddings remains unclear.
2. The medical evaluation datasets are extremely limited in size (MayoSRS: 101 pairs, Mini MayoSRS: 29 pairs) and inappropriate for the task (UMNSRS uses single-word concepts only), undermining the claimed competitive performance. The small size of these datasets makes the reported results statistically questionable.
3. Hyperparameters are heavily tuned on the same datasets used for reporting results, which invalidates the reported performance metrics. The paper states they use 5-fold cross-validation but doesn't specify which task was used for tuning, and the results show only a small improvement (0.075 Spearman's ρ) between best and worst hyperparameters, suggesting the tuning may not be meaningful.
4. The authors still rely on large manual ontologies (UMLS, YAGO) and prior annotations from clinical records (e.g., in DEVINE and CHOI baselines), which contradicts their claim of "no human corpus annotation." The ontology mappings themselves require substantial human curation, making the method not truly weakly-supervised.
5. The paper lacks deeper analysis of the tradeoff between atomic and compositional modeling, particularly regarding how headwords affect phrase embeddings. The authors present results for different β and η values but don't thoroughly analyze why certain settings work better than others.

## Questions to Authors
1. How would you respond to the criticism that your evaluation lacks extrinsic tasks to validate the utility of the embeddings for downstream applications?
2. Given the small size of your medical evaluation datasets (MayoSRS: 101 pairs, Mini MayoSRS: 29 pairs), how confident are you that your results generalize beyond these specific datasets?
3. Could you clarify how your method differs from prior work that uses distant supervision (e.g., Mintz et al., 2009), particularly in terms of the supervision signal used for concept embedding?
4. How did you handle phrase overlaps in the corpus (e.g., "New York" vs "New York City") when replacing phrases with unique identifiers?
