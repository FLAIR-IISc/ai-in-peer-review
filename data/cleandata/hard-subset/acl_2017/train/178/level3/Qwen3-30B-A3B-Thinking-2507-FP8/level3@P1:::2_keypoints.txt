- Proposes a weakly-supervised skip-gram adaptation to jointly embed concepts, phrases, and words using ontology-derived representative phrases as supervision.  
- Claims contributions: novel joint embedding approach, competitive performance without human annotation, and release of a new entity similarity dataset.  

- Strength: Handles non-compositional phrases that cannot be derived from constituent word vectors.  
- Strength: Learns concept embeddings without manual annotation of concepts in text, offering practical advantages.  
- Strength: Achieves highest correlation on UMNSRS similarity (0.653), outperforming methods requiring manual annotation.  
- Strength: Releases cui2vec software and a novel real-world entity similarity dataset for the biomedical NLP community.  
- Strength: Scales to much larger vocabulary coverage (3x) without extra annotation cost.  
- Strength: Shows ontology structure influences embedding space—representative phrases near concepts and semantic type clustering.  

- Weakness: Core method offers limited novelty, essentially a small modification to skip-gram with negative sampling.  
- Weakness: Calling the approach “distant supervision” is misleading because phrase occurrences act as context rather than traditional supervision.  
- Weakness: Experimental results do not consistently justify claimed novelty—simpler baselines sometimes outperform the full model.  
- Weakness: Choi et al. (2016b) substantially outperforms the paper on Mayo SRS and Mini Mayo SRS, questioning efficacy.  
- Weakness: On UMNSRS, the full model is only competitive with the simpler Chiu-30 baseline, undermining added complexity.  
- Weakness: Hyperparameter tuning is questionable—global β often set to 0 on large corpora, contradicting claims about balancing compositionality.  
- Weakness: Evaluation methodology unclear for many-to-many concept-phrase mappings—how similarities in Table 4 are computed is not detailed.  
- Weakness: Phrase-based approximation (APPROXPHRco) outperforming direct concept embeddings (CONCEPTco) suggests the ontology adds little beyond phrase representations.  

- Question: How are concept similarities computed when concepts have multiple representative phrases (many-to-many mappings)?  
- Question: Why do hyperparameters show β=0 for large datasets, contradicting the claimed benefit of compositionality?  
- Question: Given APPROXPHRco outperforms CONCEPTco, what additional value does the concept embedding layer provide beyond phrase embeddings?  
- Question: How would you modify the approach to better leverage ontology structure, per the observed semantic type clustering?
