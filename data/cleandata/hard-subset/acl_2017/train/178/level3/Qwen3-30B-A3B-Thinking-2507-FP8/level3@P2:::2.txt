

# Review for ACL 2017

## Summary and Main Contributions
The paper proposes a weakly-supervised method for jointly embedding concepts, phrases, and words using ontology concept-to-phrase mappings as "distant supervision" over unlabeled text. The approach replaces phrases with unique identifiers in the corpus and trains embeddings using a modified skip-gram model. The authors evaluate on biomedical concepts (UMLS) and general-domain concepts (YAGO), showing competitive performance on concept similarity and relatedness tasks without requiring human annotation.

As I see them, the main contributions are:
1. A practical method for learning concept embeddings without manual annotation, using existing ontology mappings as weak supervision.
2. A novel dataset of similarity and relatedness judgments for real-world entities.
3. Demonstrated effectiveness of the approach on concept similarity tasks, showing competitive performance to methods that require human annotation.

## Strengths
1. The paper addresses a meaningful problem in NLP: learning embeddings for non-compositional concepts (e.g., "Lou Gehrig's disease") that cannot be represented as simple combinations of words. The method avoids the need for expensive manual annotation, which is valuable for domains like biomedicine where such annotation is scarce and costly.

2. The evaluation demonstrates that the proposed embeddings can achieve competitive performance to state-of-the-art methods that require human annotation (e.g., CHOI), particularly on the larger UMNSRS dataset. The authors show that their method scales to a vocabulary size over 3x larger than existing approaches without additional annotation costs.

3. The release of the cui2vec software implementation and the novel entity similarity/relatedness dataset provide tangible value to the biomedical NLP community, as these resources are currently limited and would benefit many researchers working in this domain.

## Weaknesses
1. The method is not substantially novel - it's essentially a minor modification of the standard skip-gram model with phrase replacement. The core contribution is marginal compared to the existing body of work on word embeddings and concept embeddings, as the approach doesn't introduce fundamentally new techniques.

2. The method does not consistently outperform simpler baselines. On the smaller MayoSRS and Mini Mayo SRS datasets, the Choi baseline substantially outperforms the proposed method (e.g., 0.817 vs 0.593 on MayoSRS), and on the larger UMNSRS dataset, the simpler CHIU-2 baseline performs competitively with the proposed method (0.653 vs 0.653). This raises questions about the added value of the full model.

3. The description of the approach as "distant supervision" is misleading. Traditional distant supervision involves using distant labels to train a classifier, but here the phrase mappings are used to create a different training signal (replacing phrases with identifiers), not to provide labels. This mischaracterization undermines the paper's positioning.

4. The paper's design choice of using a global β parameter to control compositionality is questionable. Cross-validated values show β=0 for most corpora (PM+CL and WIKINYT), suggesting compositionality isn't important, which contradicts the motivation for introducing this parameter. The authors report that "enforcing some small amount of concept-phrase or phrase-word compositionality can be helpful," yet their own hyperparameter tuning shows this is not the case.

5. The paper fails to clearly explain how similarity is computed for concept pairs given the many-to-many mapping between concepts and phrases (Section 5.3). This makes it difficult to assess whether the reported results truly reflect the model's performance, as the evaluation methodology is ambiguous.

## Questions to Authors
1. How do you compute the similarity between two concepts given the many-to-many mapping between concepts and phrases? This is critical for understanding the evaluation methodology.

2. Why does the proposed method not consistently outperform simpler baselines (e.g., APPROXPHRco or CHIU-2) across all datasets? Can you provide more insight into why this happens?

3. The paper claims to use "distant supervision" but the approach is more like a phrase replacement technique. Could you clarify the distinction and why "distant supervision" is an appropriate term here?
