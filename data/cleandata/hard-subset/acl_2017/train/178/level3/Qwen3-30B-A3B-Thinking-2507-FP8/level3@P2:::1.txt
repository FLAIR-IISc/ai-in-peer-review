

# Review for ACL 2017

## Summary and Contributions

This paper proposes a weakly-supervised method for jointly embedding concepts, phrases, and words using ontology information as distant supervision. The authors replace phrases from an ontology (UMLS or YAGO) with unique identifiers in a corpus, then train embeddings using a modified skip-gram model. The main contributions, ranked by strength, are:

1. A simple yet effective method for concept embedding that requires no human annotation, achieving competitive performance on similarity and relatedness tasks with 3x vocabulary coverage compared to prior methods.
2. A novel dataset of similarity and relatedness judgments for real-world entities (people, places, organizations), addressing a significant gap in the literature.
3. Demonstration that concept embeddings can be trained effectively using only ontology mappings and unannotated text, without requiring manual concept annotation.

## Strengths

1. **Novel dataset for entity similarity/relatedness**: The authors introduce a new dataset of 688 entity pairs for similarity and relatedness evaluation, which is a valuable contribution to the field. This dataset fills a critical gap, as existing datasets focus primarily on word-level semantics rather than real-world entities. The dataset is well-constructed with proper validation questions and inter-annotator agreement metrics.

2. **Competitive performance with no human annotation**: The method achieves results competitive with prior approaches that require significant human annotation (e.g., Choi et al., 2016b). The authors demonstrate that concept embeddings trained using their method perform well on similarity and relatedness tasks, particularly on larger datasets (UMNSRS), with the best results outperforming most baselines.

3. **Scalability and practicality**: The method scales to much larger vocabulary sizes (3x coverage) without additional annotation costs. This is a significant practical advantage for real-world applications where manual annotation is prohibitively expensive, particularly in biomedical domains.

## Weaknesses

1. **Subtle technical contribution**: The core method is a straightforward adaptation of word2vec to handle ontology mappings, which represents a subtle rather than major technical advance. The modifications (β and η parameters) don't introduce significant new modeling insights, and the approach is closely related to Wang et al. (2014a), which also jointly embeds entities and words.

2. **Limited evaluation scope**: The evaluation is heavily focused on biomedical data, with only limited analysis of the general-domain (YAGO) results. The real-world entity dataset is introduced but not thoroughly analyzed - for example, the authors report that phrase-based concept approximations outperform direct concept embeddings, but don't explore why this is the case.

3. **Technical error in MAP calculation**: The MAP calculation in Section 5.4 (Equation 8) is incorrectly described as a probability and the explanation doesn't account for how the vocabulary is ranked. The authors claim to rank the entire phrase vocabulary, but this would produce values that don't make sense as precision scores. This needs clarification or correction.

## Questions to Authors

1. The paper claims "more than 3x coverage in the vocabulary size" but doesn't clarify what this is compared to. Could you specify what baseline method and vocabulary size you're comparing against?

2. In Equation 8, the precision calculation is described as "1V̂<c/V̂c" but this doesn't properly account for ranking. Could you clarify how the ranking of the vocabulary is handled, and whether a natural cutoff (e.g., top k words/phrases) should be used?

3. The paper states that "concept embeddings maintain similarity and relatedness" but the results in Table 4 show that CHOI (which uses manual annotations) outperforms the proposed method on smaller datasets (Mayo SRS and Mini Mayo SRS). Could you provide more insight into why this discrepancy occurs?

## Additional Comments

The paper is well-written and the method is natural, but the contribution is somewhat incremental. The technical issue with the MAP calculation (Equation 8) needs to be addressed. There's also a minor editorial error on line 556: "most concepts has" should be "most concepts have."

The authors have made a useful contribution with the new entity dataset, which is likely to be valuable for future work. However, the method itself doesn't represent a major advance in the field, and the evaluation doesn't fully explore the potential of the approach beyond the biomedical domain. The paper would be strengthened by more thorough analysis of the YAGO results and a clearer explanation of the vocabulary coverage metric.
