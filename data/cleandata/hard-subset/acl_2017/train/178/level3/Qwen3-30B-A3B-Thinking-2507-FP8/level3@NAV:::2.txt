

This paper proposes a method for jointly embedding concepts, phrases, and words by mapping ontology concepts to representative phrases and training with a skip-gram style model. The authors evaluate their approach on concept similarity and relatedness tasks using UMLS and YAGO, claiming competitive performance without requiring manual annotation.

The main contributions as I see them are: (1) a method for handling non-compositional phrases that could benefit biomedical NLP; (2) a released software tool (cui2vec) for biomedical researchers; and (3) a novel dataset of similarity/relatedness judgments for real-world entities.

Strengths include the practical utility of the released software and the method's ability to handle non-compositional phrases. The embeddings demonstrate competitive performance on the larger UMNSRS dataset, showing more than 3x vocabulary coverage compared to prior work. The paper also addresses an important problem in biomedical NLP: embedding concepts without requiring expensive manual annotation.

However, the work has several significant weaknesses. The method is essentially a minor modification to skip-gram, lacking novelty. More critically, the full model does not consistently outperform simpler baselines - the Choi baseline substantially outperforms on the Mayo datasets, and the authors' approach is at best competitive with simpler baselines on UMNSRS. The description of "distant supervision" is misleading, as phrase occurrences provide contextual information but not traditional supervised signals. The experimental setup for Table 4 is unclear, particularly regarding how similarities are computed with many-to-many concept↔phrase mappings. Most troublingly, Table 5 shows that simply averaging phrase vectors (approximate phr) performs best, which undermines the core contribution of using the concept ontology.

The paper's claims about the value of their concept embeddings are weakened by these results. While the software release and new dataset are valuable contributions, the methodological novelty is questionable and the experimental results do not strongly support the main thesis. The authors should clarify the relationship between their method and simpler alternatives and address the concerns about the "distant supervision" framing.
