- Proposes a weakly-supervised method that uses ontology mappings (UMLS, YAGO) to replace phrases with concept IDs and trains a modified skip-gram to jointly embed concepts, phrases, and words.  
- Claims: no human-annotated corpus required, larger vocabulary coverage, and better concept–text semantic capture.  
- Contribution: practical method to generate concept embeddings using existing ontology mappings.  
- Contribution: competitive performance on concept similarity/relatedness tasks versus methods needing human annotation.  
- Contribution: releases a real-world entity similarity/relatedness dataset and the cui2vec implementation.  
- Strength: targets non-compositional phrases (e.g., named diseases) that standard compositional models mishandle.  
- Strength: achieves top correlation on larger UMNSRS similarity dataset and competitive relatedness results.  
- Strength: practical utility for biomedical NLP practitioners through the released embeddings/software.  
- Weakness: limited novelty—method is a minor modification of skip-gram and not clearly distinguished from prior work (e.g., Wang 2014, Choi 2016).  
- Weakness: inconsistent gains—full model often does not outperform simpler baselines and is beaten on some datasets.  
- Weakness: misleading characterization of “distant supervision,” since phrase occurrences act as context rather than true weak labels.  
- Weakness: compositionality control parameters (β, η) appear unnecessary (often cross-validated to zero), questioning that modeling choice.  
- Question: how are similarities computed when concepts map to many phrases—how are multiple phrase embeddings combined for evaluation?  
- Question: why does averaging phrase vectors (APPROXPHRco) sometimes outperform the full concept model—does the ontology add value?  
- Question: the claim of “no human annotation” is misleading—how do required ontology mappings compare to prior manual annotations in effort and supervision?  
- Overall: useful practical contribution with valuable released resources, but limited novelty, mixed empirical gains, and theoretical/claim issues—better suited to an applied venue.
