

Summary: The paper proposes a method for jointly embedding concepts, phrases, and words using distant supervision from ontology concepts, requiring no human annotation. The main contributions are: (1) a novel model for jointly embedding concepts, phrases, and words by using representative phrases as distant supervision; (2) a new dataset for evaluating similarity and relatedness of real-world entities; and (3) comprehensive evaluation showing competitive performance on concept similarity and relatedness tasks with significantly larger vocabulary coverage than prior methods.

Strengths: The model design sensibly balances treating phrases/concepts as atomic versus compositional units through the β and η hyperparameters, which is a thoughtful approach to a challenging problem. The paper presents substantial work with thorough model development, a new evaluation dataset, and multiple complementary analyses (compositionality, semantic type clustering, etc.) that provide valuable insights into how the embeddings organize semantic information. The method achieves impressive vocabulary coverage (more than 3x larger than prior approaches) without requiring any human annotation, demonstrating practical scalability for real-world applications.

Weaknesses: The evaluation is limited to intrinsic similarity and relatedness tasks without any extrinsic task evaluation, which significantly undermines the claimed practical utility of the embeddings. The medical evaluation datasets are severely limited in size and quality: MayoSRS (101 pairs, low inter-rater agreement), Mini MayoSRS (29 pairs), and UMNSRS (single-word concepts only, despite the paper's focus on multi-word concepts), making the performance claims questionable. Hyperparameters were heavily tuned on the same datasets used for reporting results, which creates a serious risk of overfitting and invalidates the performance comparisons. The claim of outperforming prior work is weakened because the method still relies on large manual ontologies (UMLS, YAGO) and prior annotations from clinical records (for the DEVINE and CHOI baselines), contradicting the paper's emphasis on avoiding human annotation. The paper lacks deeper analysis of the compositionality tradeoff, particularly failing to separate phrase-word compositionality from concept-phrase relations (e.g., by examining headword effects), which would strengthen the theoretical contribution.

Questions to Authors: 
1. How would you address the criticism that your evaluation lacks extrinsic task validation, which is essential for demonstrating practical utility?
2. Could you provide justification for using UMNSRS (which evaluates single-word concepts) as a benchmark for your multi-word concept embeddings?
3. Please clarify which specific task was used for hyperparameter tuning, as this is not specified in the paper.
4. How do you account for the fact that your method still relies on manual ontologies (UMLS, YAGO), which contradict your claim of "no human corpus annotation"?
5. Could you provide additional analysis of the compositionality tradeoff, particularly separating phrase-word compositionality from concept-phrase relations?
