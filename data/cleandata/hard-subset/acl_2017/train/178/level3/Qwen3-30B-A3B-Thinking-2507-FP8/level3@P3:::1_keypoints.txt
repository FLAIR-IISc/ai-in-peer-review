- Proposes a weakly-supervised method that replaces phrase occurrences with ontology concept IDs to jointly embed concepts, phrases, and words.

- Evaluated on biomedical (UMLS) and real-world entity (YAGO) data and introduces a new dataset of similarity/relatedness judgments for real-world entities.

- Contribution: novel dataset for evaluating similarity/relatedness of real-world entities.

- Contribution: a distant-supervision approach for jointly embedding concepts, phrases, and words using an ontology.

- Contribution: competitive similarity/relatedness performance with no human annotation and claimed 3x vocabulary coverage.

- Strength: the new evaluation resource fills a gap and was collected with validation and inter-annotator agreement.

- Strength: method is natural and well-motivated, building on established word embedding practices.

- Strength: method is scalable (no human annotations) and offers practical vocabulary coverage gains, especially for domains like biomedicine.

- Weakness: limited technical novelty—essentially an adaptation of word2vec and similar prior work (e.g., Wang et al. 2014a).

- Weakness: narrow evaluation scope (only UMLS and YAGO), limiting claims about generalizability.

- Weakness: technical error in Equation 8—MAP is misdescribed and should be computed over relevant subsets, not the entire vocabulary.

- Weakness: the dataset, the paper’s most significant contribution, is insufficiently emphasized in the main text.

- Question: why claim the method is "novel" given its close similarity to prior extensions of word2vec?

- Question: what exact baselines/methods support the "more than 3x coverage" vocabulary comparison?

- Question: please clarify the correct MAP interpretation and calculation for Equation 8.

- Question: why wasn’t the new real-world entities dataset highlighted as the primary contribution in the title or abstract?
