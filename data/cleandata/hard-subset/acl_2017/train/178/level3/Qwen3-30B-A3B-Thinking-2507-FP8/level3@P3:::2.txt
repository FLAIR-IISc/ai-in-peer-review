

# Review of "A Weakly-Supervised Method for Jointly Embedding Concepts, Phrases, and Words"

## Summary and Contributions
The paper proposes a weakly-supervised method to jointly embed concepts, phrases, and words by leveraging ontology mappings as "distant supervision" for training. It maps concepts to their representative phrases in ontologies (UMLS for biomedical concepts, YAGO for general entities), replaces these phrases with unique identifiers in a corpus, and trains a modified skip-gram model to jointly learn embeddings for concepts, phrases, and words. The authors claim this approach requires no human annotation of the corpus, offers larger vocabulary coverage, and captures semantic relationships between concepts and their textual representations.

The primary contributions, as I see them, are:
1. A practical method for generating concept embeddings without requiring human-annotated corpora (only using existing ontology mappings).
2. Competitive performance on concept similarity/relatedness tasks compared to prior methods that require human annotation.
3. A novel dataset of real-world entity similarity/relatedness and the release of a software implementation (cui2vec).

## Strengths

1. **Targeting non-compositional phrases**: The paper addresses an important gap in the literature by focusing on phrases that cannot be decomposed into their word components (e.g., "Lou Gehrig's disease" vs. "amyotrophic lateral sclerosis"). This is a meaningful contribution to the literature on phrase embeddings, as most prior work assumes compositional phrases.

2. **Competitive performance on larger datasets**: The method achieves the highest correlation with human judgments on the larger UMNSRS similarity dataset (0.653) and competitive performance on relatedness (0.559), outperforming most baselines. This demonstrates the method's viability for concept representation at scale.

3. **Practical utility**: The release of cui2vec software provides a valuable tool for biomedical NLP researchers who need concept embeddings without access to expensive human annotations. The paper's focus on biomedical concepts (UMLS) makes this particularly relevant for a significant research community.

## Weaknesses

1. **Lack of novelty**: The proposed method is essentially a minor modification of the skip-gram model with negative sampling (word2vec), adding concept embeddings and using ontology mappings as "distant supervision." The core technique isn't novel, and the paper doesn't sufficiently distinguish itself from prior work like Wang et al. (2014a) or Choi et al. (2016b).

2. **Inconsistent performance**: The full model (with compositionality control) does not consistently outperform simpler baselines. The Choi baseline substantially outperforms the proposed method on Mayo datasets (Table 4), and on UMNSRS, the simpler CHIU-30 baseline performs comparably to the proposed method. This undermines the paper's claim that the proposed approach is superior.

3. **Misleading "distant supervision" claim**: The paper describes using phrase occurrences as "distant supervision," but this is inaccurate. Distant supervision typically involves using a weakly labeled training set to generate training examples, whereas here, phrase occurrences provide context but not supervised signals about concept relationships. This mischaracterization weakens the paper's theoretical foundation.

4. **Questionable compositionality control**: The introduction of β and η to control compositionality is problematic. Cross-validated β values are zero for PM+CL and WikiNYT (Table 3), which contradicts the rationale for introducing these parameters. This suggests the compositionality assumption is unfounded, and the authors should explain why the model performs best with no compositionality.

## Questions to Authors

1. The experimental setup for Table 4 is unclear: How are similarities computed given the many-to-many concept↔phrase mappings? When a concept has multiple phrases, how are the embeddings combined for evaluation?

2. Table 5 shows that APPROXPHRco (averaging phrase vectors) outperforms the full model (CONCEPTco) on the real-world entity datasets. This suggests the concept ontology may not be adding significant value over simply using phrase embeddings. How do you explain this result?

3. The paper states that the model "requires no human annotation," but it does require ontology mappings (e.g., UMLS mappings). How do these mappings compare to the human annotation required by prior methods (e.g., Choi's manual medical code annotation)? Is this truly a "no human annotation" approach?

## Overall Recommendation
The paper makes a practical contribution to biomedical NLP by providing a method to generate concept embeddings without human-annotated corpora, and the released software will benefit researchers. However, the core technique lacks novelty, the performance claims are undermined by consistent outperformance by simpler baselines, and the theoretical foundation is questionable. While the paper has some value, the novelty and significance are limited, and it would be better suited for a more applied venue than ACL.
