- Summary: Proposes weakly-supervised joint embeddings of concepts, phrases, and words by replacing phrases with ontology-derived identifiers and training a modified skip-gram; evaluated on UMLS and YAGO.

- Contribution: Practical method to learn concept embeddings without manual annotation using existing ontology concept-to-phrase mappings as weak supervision.

- Contribution: Novel dataset of similarity and relatedness judgments for real-world entities.

- Contribution: Shows competitive performance on concept similarity tasks versus methods that require human annotation.

- Strength: Addresses learning embeddings for non-compositional concepts without costly manual annotation, valuable for domains like biomedicine.

- Strength: Empirical evaluation shows competitive performance and scalability to much larger vocabularies (3x) without extra annotation.

- Strength: Release of cui2vec implementation and the new entity similarity/relatedness dataset benefits the biomedical NLP community.

- Weakness: Methodological novelty is limited—essentially a minor modification of skip-gram with phrase replacement.

- Weakness: Does not consistently outperform simpler baselines (e.g., CHOI, CHIU-2) across datasets, raising questions about added value.

- Weakness: Characterizing the approach as “distant supervision” is misleading given it uses phrase replacement rather than distant labels for classifier training.

- Weakness: Global β compositionality parameter is questionable—cross-validation often yields β=0, contradicting the motivation for compositional modeling.

- Weakness: Evaluation methodology is unclear—paper fails to explain how similarity is computed for concept pairs given many-to-many concept-phrase mappings.

- Question: How is similarity computed between two concepts given the many-to-many mapping between concepts and phrases?

- Question: Why does the proposed method not consistently outperform simpler baselines across all datasets—can you explain when and why it fails?

- Question: Can you clarify the use of the term “distant supervision” versus describing the approach as phrase replacement?
