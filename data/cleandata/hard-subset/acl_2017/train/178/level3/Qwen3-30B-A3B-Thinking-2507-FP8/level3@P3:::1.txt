

# Review for ACL 2017 Paper: "A Weakly-Supervised Method for Jointly Embedding Concepts, Phrases, and Words"

## Summary
This paper proposes a weakly-supervised method to jointly embed concepts, phrases, and words into a shared vector space using an ontology as distant supervision. The approach replaces phrase occurrences in a corpus with unique concept identifiers (from an ontology) and trains embeddings on this modified corpus. The authors evaluate their method on biomedical concepts (using UMLS) and real-world entities (using YAGO), demonstrating competitive performance on similarity/relatedness tasks without requiring human annotations. The paper also introduces a novel dataset of similarity and relatedness judgments for real-world entities.

## Main Contributions
1. The novel dataset of similarity and relatedness judgments for real-world entities (Wikipedia pages and text phrases), which fills a significant gap in the literature.
2. A natural and well-motivated approach for jointly embedding concepts, phrases, and words using distant supervision from an ontology.
3. Competitive performance on similarity/relatedness tasks compared to methods requiring human annotation, with 3x vocabulary coverage.

## Strengths
1. **Novel Evaluation Resource**: The most significant contribution is the introduction of a novel dataset for evaluating similarity and relatedness of real-world entities. This addresses a critical gap in the literature, as existing datasets focus on words rather than real-world entities. The careful data collection process, including validation questions and inter-annotator agreement analysis, adds credibility to this contribution.

2. **Natural Methodology**: The approach is well-motivated and builds logically on existing word embedding techniques. The core idea of using ontology-mapped phrases as distant supervision for concept embedding is intuitive and aligns with established practices in weakly-supervised learning.

3. **Practical Impact**: The method requires no human annotation, making it significantly more scalable than competing approaches. The demonstrated 3x vocabulary coverage advantage over existing methods is practically meaningful, especially in domains like biomedicine where vocabulary size is critical.

## Weaknesses
1. **Limited Technical Contribution**: The core method is a straightforward adaptation of existing word embedding techniques (word2vec) to incorporate concept identifiers. The novelty is subtle rather than substantial, as the approach builds directly on prior work by Wang et al. (2014a) and others. The authors' claim of "novel" method is overstated given the minimal technical innovation.

2. **Narrow Evaluation Scope**: The evaluation is restricted to biomedical data (UMLS) and a single general-domain ontology (YAGO), with no testing on more diverse or challenging domains. This severely limits the generalizability of the claims and makes it difficult to assess the method's broader applicability.

3. **Technical Error in Equation 8**: The paper incorrectly describes the MAP calculation (Equation 8) as a probability that ranks the entire vocabulary. This is inaccurate - MAP should be calculated only over the relevant subset of terms (the child concepts/words), not the entire vocabulary. The paper needs to clarify this or define a natural cutoff for the ranking.

4. **Insufficient Discussion of Primary Contribution**: The paper's most significant contribution (the new dataset) receives insufficient attention in the main text. The authors present it as an add-on rather than as the paper's primary contribution, which is a missed opportunity to highlight this valuable resource.

## Questions to Authors
1. Could you elaborate on why the authors consider the method a "novel" contribution when it's a straightforward extension of existing word2vec techniques? How does this differ substantially from Wang et al. (2014a) or other prior work?

2. The paper claims "more than 3x coverage in the vocabulary size" compared to prior methods. Could you clarify the exact comparison points and which specific methods you're comparing against for this claim?

3. The technical error in Equation 8 appears to be a significant oversight. Could you provide clarification on the correct interpretation and calculation of MAP in this context?

4. Given that the dataset for real-world entities appears to be the paper's most valuable contribution, why wasn't it highlighted as the primary contribution in the paper's title or abstract?
