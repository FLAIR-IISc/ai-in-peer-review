- Proposes a weakly‑supervised method that replaces ontology phrases with IDs and trains a modified skip‑gram to jointly embed concepts, phrases, and words.  
- Claims three main contributions: annotation‑free concept embeddings with competitive performance and ~3x vocabulary coverage, a new real‑world entity similarity/relatedness dataset, and showing ontology mappings + raw text suffice to train concept embeddings.  
- Introduces a novel dataset of 688 entity pairs for entity similarity/relatedness with validation and inter‑annotator agreement.  
- Demonstrates competitive performance versus annotation‑heavy baselines on similarity/relatedness tasks, especially on larger biomedical datasets.  
- Method scales to much larger vocabularies (claimed ~3x coverage) without extra annotation cost, useful for real‑world/biomedical applications.  
- Technical contribution is relatively subtle—essentially an adaptation of word2vec with β and η parameters and closely related to prior joint embedding work (Wang et al., 2014a).  
- Evaluation is narrowly focused on biomedical data; YAGO/general‑domain analysis is limited and the new entity dataset is not deeply analyzed (e.g., why phrase approximations beat direct concept embeddings).  
- There is a technical error/unclear description in the MAP calculation (Equation 8) regarding probability interpretation and how vocabulary ranking is handled.  
- Request to specify what baseline/method the “3x coverage” claim is compared to and the baseline vocabulary sizes.  
- Ask for clarification of Equation 8’s ranking procedure and whether a top‑k cutoff or other ranking scheme was used.  
- Request explanation for why CHOI (manual annotations) outperforms the proposed method on smaller datasets (Mayo SRS, Mini Mayo SRS).  
- Paper is well written but incremental; fix the MAP equation, correct minor editorial errors (e.g., “most concepts has” → “have”), and strengthen YAGO analysis and vocabulary coverage explanation.
