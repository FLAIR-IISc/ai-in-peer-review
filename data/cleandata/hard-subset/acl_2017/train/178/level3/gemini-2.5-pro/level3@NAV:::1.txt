**Summary**

This paper presents a weakly-supervised method for jointly learning vector representations for concepts, phrases, and words. The approach leverages an ontology to identify phrases corresponding to concepts within a large text corpus. These phrases are replaced with a unique concept identifier, creating a "tagged" version of the corpus, which is then appended to the original text for training a skip-gram model. The method is evaluated primarily on biomedical concept similarity and relatedness benchmarks. A key component of the work is the introduction of a new, crowdsourced dataset for evaluating similarity and relatedness of general-domain, real-world entities.

**Contributions**

1.  The paper's primary contribution is the introduction and release of a novel dataset for evaluating the similarity and relatedness of real-world entities. This resource addresses a clear gap in the field and provides a valuable benchmark for future work on concept and entity representations.
2.  The work introduces a simple, scalable, and intuitive method for learning concept embeddings using distant supervision from an ontology, demonstrating competitive performance on biomedical tasks without requiring expensive, manually annotated data.

**Strengths**

1.  The creation of the new evaluation dataset for general-domain entities is a significant and welcome contribution. Resources for evaluating representations beyond the word level are scarce, and this dataset will be valuable to the community. The authors have diligently documented its creation process.
2.  The proposed method is well-motivated, straightforward, and easily reproducible. Its simplicity is a strength, offering a practical way to extend word embedding techniques to the concept level by leveraging existing structured knowledge resources like ontologies.
3.  The paper is very well-written and easy to follow. The experiments are thorough within their chosen domain, comparing against multiple relevant baselines and providing useful qualitative analysis regarding compositionality and semantic clustering.

**Weaknesses**

1.  The methodological novelty is limited. The approach is an elegant but subtle extension of existing embedding techniques, essentially treating concept identifiers as special tokens. It does not represent a major technical advance in representation learning itself.
2.  The evaluation of the proposed method is narrowly focused on the biomedical domain, which limits the claims of generality. Although the model is trained on a general-domain corpus, its performance is not evaluated against competitors on the new general-domain dataset the paper introduces.
3.  The evaluation contains a technical issue in the Mean Average Precision (MAP) calculation (Section 5.4, Eq. 8). The formula is described as a probability, and its application by ranking against the entire vocabulary is unconventional and potentially problematic without a defined cutoff. This description should be corrected to clarify the evaluation procedure and ensure the validity of the reported results. A minor grammatical error was also noted (line 556: "most concepts has").
