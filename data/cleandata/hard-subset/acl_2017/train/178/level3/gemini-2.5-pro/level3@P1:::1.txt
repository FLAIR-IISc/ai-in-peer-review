### Summary of the Paper

This paper proposes a weakly-supervised method to jointly learn vector representations for concepts, phrases, and words. The core idea is to leverage an existing ontology (like UMLS or YAGO) that maps concepts to their textual phrase representations. The authors create a tagged version of a text corpus by replacing known phrases with unique identifiers. They then train embeddings in parallel on both the original and the tagged corpora using an adapted skip-gram with negative sampling model. This allows the model to learn representations for words, phrases (as unigrams in the tagged corpus), and concepts (updated using the contexts of their associated phrases) within a shared vector space. The method is evaluated on biomedical and general-domain concept similarity and relatedness tasks, demonstrating performance competitive with methods that require more intensive manual annotation.

The main contributions of this work, as I see them, are:

1.  **A novel dataset for general-domain entity similarity and relatedness.** The authors create and use a new evaluation resource consisting of 688 entity pairs (people, places, organizations) with human-annotated similarity and relatedness scores. This is a valuable contribution to the community, as such resources for entities (as opposed to words) are scarce.
2.  **A simple and effective weakly-supervised method for joint embedding.** The proposed technique is an intuitive extension of the widely-used word2vec framework. By using an ontology for distant supervision, it successfully learns meaningful concept representations without requiring expensive, manually annotated training corpora.

### Strengths

1.  **Creation of a valuable new evaluation resource.** The paper's most significant contribution may be the new general-domain entity similarity and relatedness dataset. The authors clearly document their collection methodology using Mechanical Turk, including details on quality control and inter-annotator agreement (Fleiss’ κ of 0.24). While the agreement is only "fair," the transparency is commendable. Such datasets are crucial for evaluating the growing body of work on entity and knowledge base embeddings, and this resource helps fill an important gap.
2.  **The method is elegant, practical, and well-motivated.** The approach of creating a parallel tagged corpus and adapting the skip-gram objective is a natural and straightforward way to inject structured knowledge into distributional training. The key advantage is its practicality: it sidesteps the need for expensive human annotation by using an existing ontology as a source of distant supervision. The results demonstrate that this simple method can achieve performance on par with or exceeding more complex models that rely on manually tagged data (e.g., MENCIA) or curated concept sequences (e.g., DEVINE).
3.  **The paper is exceptionally well-written and easy to follow.** The motivation is clearly articulated in the introduction, the method is described with sufficient detail, and the experiments are presented logically. The authors do a good job of situating their work within the related literature and conducting a thorough hyperparameter search and a reasonable set of analyses.

### Weaknesses

1.  **Limited technical novelty.** The core methodology is an incremental adaptation of the skip-gram with negative sampling model. While the application to jointly learning concept, phrase, and word embeddings is effective, the technical contribution itself is somewhat subtle. The modifications to the training objective are straightforward extensions, and the approach does not represent a major technical advance in representation learning. The contribution lies more in the novel application and framing of the problem rather than in a new underlying algorithm.
2.  **The evaluation is narrowly focused, limiting claims of generality.** While the authors use both biomedical and general-domain corpora, the most detailed analyses (e.g., semantic type clustering in Section 5.5) are performed only on the biomedical data. The general-domain evaluation is conducted solely on the authors' newly created dataset. While this new dataset is a strength, its use as the only general-domain evaluation makes it difficult to compare the model's performance against prior work on established benchmarks and to fully assess the generality of the method's effectiveness.
3.  **The MAP calculation for structural analysis is questionable.** The Mean Average Precision (MAP) evaluation in Section 5.4, used to assess how well ontological links are preserved, has a methodological flaw. First, Equation 8 defines `Pr(c|Cp, V)` as a precision score, not a probability as the notation suggests. More critically, the text states that the ranking is performed against the entire phrase or word vocabulary `V`. For large vocabularies, this is computationally expensive and makes the resulting MAP scores extremely sensitive to the distribution of items in the full vocabulary, likely yielding artificially low scores that are difficult to interpret. A more standard approach would be to calculate MAP@k for a reasonable value of k (e.g., k=100 or k=1000) to measure precision within a local neighborhood. This makes the conclusions drawn from Figures 4a and 4b less reliable.

### Questions to Authors

1.  Regarding the MAP calculation in Section 5.4: Could you clarify the motivation for ranking against the entire vocabulary `V` rather than using a fixed-size neighborhood (e.g., MAP@k)? This choice makes the reported MAP scores in Table 6 and Figure 4 very low and hard to compare against other potential methods. Additionally, the text describes the inner term of the MAP calculation as a probability, which appears to be incorrect terminology for a precision score.
2.  The new entity similarity/relatedness dataset is a highlight of the paper. Given that its creation is a significant contribution in its own right, have you considered positioning it more centrally in the paper's narrative? Also, the reported Fleiss' kappa of 0.24 indicates only "fair" agreement. Could you comment on how this level of annotator disagreement might impact the reliability of the evaluation results on this dataset?
