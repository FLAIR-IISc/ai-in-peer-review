### Summary of the paper

This paper presents a method for jointly learning vector space representations for words, multi-word phrases, and ontology concepts from an unannotated text corpus. The approach uses an ontology (UMLS for the medical domain, YAGO for the general domain) to identify known phrases in the text. These phrases are then treated as atomic units in a modified skip-gram with negative sampling model. The model trains three distinct embedding matrices for words, phrases, and concepts, which are updated based on the textual contexts in which words and phrases appear. The authors evaluate their learned embeddings on standard biomedical concept similarity and relatedness tasks, and also introduce a new crowdsourced dataset for evaluating general-domain entity similarity.

### Contributions

1.  **A new dataset for general-domain entity similarity and relatedness.** The authors create and release a dataset of 688 entity pairs (people, places, organizations) with human judgments for both similarity and relatedness. Given the lack of such resources for entities/concepts (as opposed to words), this is a valuable contribution to the community for evaluating future work in this area.
2.  **A weakly-supervised method for learning concept and phrase embeddings from text.** The paper proposes a plausible, dictionary-based method to learn embeddings for non-compositional phrases and the abstract concepts they represent, without requiring manually annotated training data. The release of the associated software (`cui2vec`) is a practical asset that will benefit other researchers, particularly in the biomedical domain.

### Strengths

1.  **The work is well-motivated and addresses an important limitation of word-based models.** The paper clearly articulates the problem of representing multi-word expressions that are not simple compositions of their constituent words (e.g., "Lou Gehrig’s disease"). The proposed approach of identifying such phrases from an ontology and learning atomic representations for them from context is an intuitive and sensible strategy.
2.  **The method demonstrates competitive performance on large-scale similarity tasks.** On the larger UMNSRS biomedical datasets, the concept embeddings (`CONCEPTco`) achieve the best performance on the similarity task, and the phrase embeddings (`PHRASEco`) are competitive on the relatedness task when compared to strong baselines like Chiu et al. (2016a).
3.  **The release of code and a new dataset enhances the paper's impact.** By providing an implementation of their method and a new evaluation resource, the authors facilitate reproducibility and enable further research in both biomedical NLP and general-domain concept representation, making this a useful contribution to the field.

### Weaknesses

1.  **The proposed `CONCEPTco` model is frequently outperformed by simpler baselines and prior work.** The central claim of the paper is a method for learning concept embeddings, yet the `CONCEPTco` model is often not the best-performing one. In Table 4, the Choi et al. (2016b) baseline is substantially better on both MayoSRS datasets. More importantly, in Table 5, a simpler approximation based on averaging the embeddings of phrases (`APPROXPHRco`) outperforms the direct concept embeddings (`CONCEPTco`). This raises significant questions about the utility of the dedicated concept embedding layer, suggesting that the model's primary value may lie in learning good phrase embeddings rather than concept embeddings.
2.  **The methodological novelty is somewhat limited.** The core technique is an extension of the standard skip-gram with negative sampling model. The main adaptations involve (1) pre-processing text to replace known phrases with unique identifiers—a common technique—and (2) training three separate embedding matrices using shared context information. While this joint training framework is sound, it is an incremental modification of a widely-used architecture rather than a fundamentally new approach.
3.  **The motivation for the compositionality parameters (`β`, `η`) is undermined by the hyperparameter tuning results.** The model introduces parameters to enforce similarity between concepts and their phrases, and between phrases and their words. However, the optimally tuned models for the PM+CL and WIKINYT corpora set these parameters to zero (Table 3). This indicates that for half the experimental settings, enforcing compositionality provided no benefit, which contradicts the motivation for introducing this model component in Section 3.2.2.
4.  The term **"distant supervision"** seems slightly misplaced. The method uses an ontology as a dictionary to find mentions of concepts in text. This is different from the typical use of the term (e.g., Mintz et al., 2009), which involves using KB facts to generate noisy labels for relation extraction from sentences where two entities co-occur. A more precise description might be "ontology-guided" or "dictionary-based" learning.

### Questions to Authors

1.  The strongest results on the new general-domain dataset (Table 5) and very competitive results on the medical datasets (Table 4) are achieved by `APPROXPHRco` (averaging phrase embeddings). This suggests that the model's main strength is in producing high-quality phrase embeddings, and that the additional concept embedding layer may not be adding value. Could you comment on the utility of the `CONCEPTco` embeddings in light of these results? What is the advantage of learning them directly if a simpler approximation of phrase vectors works better?
2.  Could you please clarify the experimental protocol for handling the many-to-many mapping between concepts and phrases in the similarity/relatedness evaluation? For the `PHRASEco` baseline, if a concept has multiple phrases in the ontology, which phrase's embedding is used for the comparison?
3.  The optimal values for the compositionality parameters `β` and `η` were tuned to zero for two of the four corpora. Does this suggest that encouraging compositionality is not actually beneficial for these tasks and corpora? This result seems to weaken the motivation for including these parameters in the model.
