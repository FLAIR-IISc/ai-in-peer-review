- Paper summary: joint learning of word, phrase, and concept embeddings by treating ontology-identified phrases as atomic tokens in a modified skip-gram model, evaluated on biomedical benchmarks and a new general-domain entity similarity dataset.

- Contribution: releases a new 688-pair general-domain entity similarity/relatedness dataset for people/places/organizations.

- Contribution: proposes a weakly-supervised, dictionary-based method to learn phrase and concept embeddings and releases code/cui2vec for reproducibility.

- Strength: well-motivated solution to representing non-compositional multi-word expressions using ontology-identified atomic units.

- Strength: competitive empirical performance—CONCEPTco best on UMNSRS similarity and PHRASEco competitive on relatedness versus strong baselines.

- Strength: code and dataset release increases impact and facilitates further research.

- Weakness: CONCEPTco is frequently outperformed by simpler baselines (e.g., Choi et al. 2016b) and by APPROXPHRco (averaging phrase embeddings), questioning the utility of a dedicated concept layer.

- Weakness: methodological novelty is limited—mainly preprocessing to replace phrases and training three embedding matrices within standard skip-gram/negative sampling.

- Weakness: compositionality parameters (β, η) often tune to zero, undermining the motivation for enforcing phrase–word and phrase–concept similarity.

- Weakness: use of the term "distant supervision" is misleading; the approach is better described as ontology-guided or dictionary-based.

- Question: given APPROXPHRco's strong results, what is the added value of learning CONCEPTco embeddings directly?

- Question: how is the many-to-many mapping between concepts and phrases handled in evaluation—which phrase embedding is used for PHRASEco when multiple phrases map to one concept?

- Question: do zero optimal values for β and η imply that encouraging compositionality is not beneficial for these corpora/tasks, and how does that affect the model rationale?
