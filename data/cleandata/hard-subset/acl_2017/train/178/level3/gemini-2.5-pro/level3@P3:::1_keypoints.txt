- Paper proposes a weakly-supervised method that uses an ontology to tag multi-word phrases in a corpus, concatenates original and tagged corpora, and trains skip-gram embeddings for words, phrases, and concepts in a shared space.

- Evaluated mainly in biomedicine, achieving competitive concept similarity/relatedness results and releasing a new crowdsourced entity similarity/relatedness dataset.

- Contribution: a new human-annotated dataset of 688 real-world entity pairs for similarity/relatedness evaluation.

- Contribution: a simple, scalable weakly-supervised embedding method that links phrases to ontology concept IDs without manual annotation.

- Strength: the new dataset fills a gap for entity- and concept-level evaluation beyond word-level benchmarks.

- Strength: methodological simplicity and scalability, easily implemented as an extension of word2vec by treating concept-linked phrases as tokens.

- Strength: strong empirical performance on biomedical benchmarks despite no direct supervision.

- Weakness: limited technical novelty—mostly an engineering extension of existing n-gram/tokenization ideas with ontology linking.

- Weakness: narrow evaluation focused on intrinsic similarity/relatedness tasks, lacking extrinsic downstream task validation.

- Weakness: flawed MAP evaluation in Sec. 5.4—Equation 8 is mischaracterized and ranking against the entire vocabulary makes results hard to interpret; MAP@k or curated negatives would be better.

- Question: clarify rationale for ranking against the full vocabulary in MAP evaluation, interpretation of bimodal distributions in Figure 4, and consider MAP@k or negative-sample ranking.

- Question: provide more analysis of the new dataset in the main paper (score distribution and types of semantic relations reflected).

- Minor editorial note: fix grammar "most concepts has" → "most concepts have."
