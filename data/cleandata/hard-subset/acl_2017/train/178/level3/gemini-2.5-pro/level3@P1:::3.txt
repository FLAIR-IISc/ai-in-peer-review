**Summary of the paper**

This paper introduces a method to jointly learn vector space embeddings for concepts, the multi-word phrases that represent them, and the individual words that compose those phrases. The model extends the skip-gram with negative sampling architecture. It uses a distant supervision approach, leveraging an existing ontology (UMLS or YAGO) to identify phrases in a large, unannotated text corpus and associate them with their canonical concepts. The model treats words, phrases, and concepts as distinct entities to be embedded and introduces hyperparameters to control the degree to which a phrase embedding is influenced by its constituent word embeddings, and a concept embedding by its constituent phrase embeddings. This allows for a flexible tradeoff between atomic and compositional representations. The authors evaluate the learned embeddings on biomedical and general-domain concept similarity and relatedness tasks. As part of this work, they also introduce and release a new dataset of human-annotated similarity and relatedness judgments for general-domain entities.

From my perspective, the main contributions are:
1.  The creation and release of a novel, human-annotated dataset for evaluating the similarity and relatedness of general-domain entities (people, places, organizations). This addresses a notable gap in available resources.
2.  The development of a straightforward and sensible model for jointly embedding concepts, phrases, and words, which includes an intuitive mechanism for balancing compositional and non-compositional semantics.

**Strengths**

1.  **Creation of a valuable new evaluation resource.** The paper's most significant contribution is the new dataset for general-domain entity similarity and relatedness. There is a scarcity of such resources compared to word-level datasets. By creating, annotating, and analyzing this dataset of 688 entity pairs, the authors have provided a valuable resource that will benefit future research in concept and entity representation learning.
2.  **Sensible and well-motivated model.** The proposed method is an intuitive extension of the widely-used word2vec framework. The problem of representing multi-word expressions and abstract concepts that are not simply the sum of their parts is a well-known challenge. The introduction of the β and η hyperparameters to explicitly model the tradeoff between atomic and compositional representations is a clear and logical approach to this problem.
3.  **Substantial body of work.** The authors present a substantial piece of work, including model development, training on four large and distinct corpora (biomedical literature, clinical notes, and general-domain text), extensive comparisons against multiple baselines, and several analyses of the resulting embedding spaces. This demonstrates a thorough effort in both implementation and experimentation.

**Weaknesses**

1.  **Critically flawed evaluation methodology.** The validity of the main experimental results is undermined by the hyperparameter tuning procedure. The authors state they use an "adapted 5-fold cross-validation" where settings are evaluated on partitions of the test set itself. This constitutes tuning on the test set, a practice which can lead to overfitting and optimistic reporting of results. Without evaluation on a held-out test set after tuning on a separate development set, the claims of achieving competitive or state-of-the-art performance (e.g., in Table 4) are not reliably supported.
2.  **Exclusive reliance on limited intrinsic evaluation tasks.** The paper evaluates embeddings solely on similarity and relatedness tasks. While common, this form of intrinsic evaluation is known to be a poor predictor of performance on downstream, extrinsic tasks. Demonstrating the utility of these embeddings in an application like entity linking or relation extraction would provide much stronger evidence of their quality. Furthermore, the specific biomedical datasets used are problematic: MayoSRS and Mini MayoSRS are very small (101 and 29 pairs), making Spearman's ρ results potentially unstable. The UMNSRS datasets primarily consist of single-word concepts, which is not an ideal benchmark for a model designed to handle multi-word phrases.
3.  **Overstated claims of weak supervision.** The paper frames the method as requiring "no human corpus annotation" and using "distant supervision." While true for the training corpus, this framing downplays the massive human annotation effort that produced the underlying ontologies (UMLS and YAGO), which are essential to the method. The claim that the method outperforms prior work that requires more annotation (e.g., Choi et al. 2016b) is weakened by the fact that both rely on vast, pre-existing sources of human-curated knowledge or annotation (ontologies vs. manually assigned clinical codes).
4.  **Lack of deep analysis.** The analysis of the compositionality hyperparameters (β and η) is shallow. Table 3 shows the final optimal values, but there is no analysis of how performance on a development set changes as these values are swept, which would provide insight into the model's behavior. For the PM+CL corpus, the optimal values were 0.0 for both, suggesting the compositionality mechanism provided no benefit, a surprising result that warrants further investigation. Similarly, the compositionality analysis in Section 5.4 could be deepened by examining how factors like phrase length, syntactic structure, or semantic type affect the proximity of phrases to their words and concepts.

**Questions to Authors**

1.  Could you please clarify your "adapted 5-fold cross-validation" tuning procedure? As described, it appears to perform tuning directly on the test data. Can you justify this approach over the standard practice of using separate development and test sets, and address the concern that this may inflate the reported performance metrics?
2.  Your new general-domain dataset was created by showing human annotators textual phrases (e.g., "the Big Apple"). Why should this be considered a *concept* similarity dataset rather than a *phrase* similarity dataset? This distinction seems crucial given that a central motivation for your work is to separate the representation of a concept from its various phrasal forms.
3.  The UMNSRS datasets were developed for evaluating single-word medical terms. How suitable are they for evaluating a model whose primary novelty is its handling of multi-word concepts and phrases? What proportion of the concept pairs in your UMNSRS evaluation involve at least one multi-word phrase?

**Additional Comments**

-   Line 69: Typo, "contexts of their phrase forms" should probably be "concepts of their phrase forms".
-   Line 202: When replacing phrases with unique identifiers, how are overlapping phrases handled (e.g., "lung disease" vs. "obstructive lung disease")? The paper mentions a greedy match, but please clarify if it is the longest match.
-   Line 220: The dimensions for the embedding matrices appear to be listed as `d x |V|`, but are conventionally `|V| x d`. Also, calling ENS the "negative sampling matrix" is non-standard; it is typically known as the context or output embedding matrix.
-   Equation 1 uses `~c` to denote the context vector, while the surrounding text and subsequent equations use `~o`. Please make the notation consistent.
-   The reference "Pedersen et al., 2007" is cited on line 361 but is missing from the bibliography.
-   The text does not explicitly refer to Table 5 when discussing the results on the general-domain entity datasets.
