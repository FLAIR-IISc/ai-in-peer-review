**Summary**
This paper proposes a method to jointly learn vector space embeddings for words, multi-word phrases, and ontology concepts. The core idea is to leverage an existing ontology (e.g., UMLS, YAGO) to identify known phrases in a large, unannotated text corpus. These phrases are then treated as atomic units. The model adapts the skip-gram architecture to train three separate embedding matrices for words, phrases, and concepts simultaneously, using the contexts surrounding words and phrases. A key assumption is that an occurrence of a phrase implies an occurrence of its associated concept(s), providing a "distantly supervised" signal to update the concept embeddings. The authors also introduce hyperparameters to enforce similarity between a phrase and its constituent words, and between a concept and its constituent phrases. The method is evaluated on biomedical and general-domain concept similarity and relatedness tasks, showing competitive performance against several baselines.

**Contributions**
1.  **A scalable, weakly-supervised method for learning concept and phrase embeddings.** The primary contribution is a practical approach that learns representations for concepts and their varied textual forms without requiring manually annotated data. By leveraging existing ontologies to identify phrases in raw text, the method provides a way to directly model non-compositional phrases (e.g., "Lou Gehrig's disease") and handle synonymy (e.g., "the Big Apple" and "New York City"), which are known limitations of purely word-based models.

2.  **New general-domain entity similarity and relatedness datasets.** The authors introduce two new datasets for evaluating the similarity and relatedness of real-world entities (people, places, organizations) derived from YAGO. Given the scarcity of such resources for entities/concepts compared to words, this is a valuable contribution to the community for future evaluations.

3.  **A publicly available software implementation.** The release of the `cui2vec` software provides a useful tool for researchers, particularly in the biomedical domain, to reproduce the results and apply the method to their own data and ontologies.

**Strengths**
1.  **Effective handling of non-compositional and synonymous phrases.** The core motivation of the paper is well-founded. Standard compositional methods often fail on idiomatic or technical multi-word expressions. By identifying phrases from an ontology and embedding them as atomic units, the model provides a principled way to learn their meanings directly from context, which is a significant advantage over simple word averaging.

2.  **Strong performance on larger-scale similarity tasks.** The proposed method demonstrates state-of-the-art or highly competitive performance on the larger biomedical evaluation datasets. Specifically, on the UMNSRS Similarity task (Table 4), the `CONCEPTco` embeddings achieve the highest Spearman's ρ (0.653), outperforming prior concept embedding methods and strong word-level baselines. This suggests that for larger, more comprehensive datasets, the model is effective at capturing the semantic properties required for similarity judgments.

3.  **Comprehensive empirical evaluation.** The authors perform a thorough evaluation across multiple domains (biomedical, general), corpora (PubMed, clinical notes, Wikipedia), and tasks (similarity, relatedness, semantic type clustering). The comparison against a wide range of baselines—including word-level models, phrase-level approximations, and other concept embedding techniques—provides a clear context for interpreting the model's performance.

**Weaknesses**
1.  **Inconsistent performance and strong baselines.** The model's performance is not uniformly strong across all datasets. On the two MayoSRS datasets (Table 4), the Choi et al. (2016b) baseline substantially outperforms the proposed method (e.g., 0.817 vs. 0.593 on MayoSRS). On the larger UMNSRS datasets, the model is at best on par with the Chiu-30 word embedding baseline. This inconsistency raises questions about the model's robustness and suggests that its advantages may be dataset-dependent.

2.  **The utility of the concept embeddings is questionable.** A key finding in the authors' own experiments undermines the central idea of learning a separate embedding for the canonical concept. On the new general-domain datasets (Table 5), the best performing method is `APPROXPHRco`—simply averaging the embeddings of a concept's representative phrases. This approach outperforms the directly learned `CONCEPTco` embeddings. This suggests that the complex mechanism for learning concept embeddings may not be adding value beyond the simpler and more direct process of identifying and embedding phrases from their contexts.

3.  **The proposed compositionality mechanism appears ineffective.** The authors introduce hyperparameters β and η to enforce similarity between phrases/words and concepts/phrases, respectively. However, the hyperparameter tuning (Table 3) shows that for the two largest corpora (PM+CL and WIKINYT), the optimal setting is β=0 and η=0. This effectively disables the compositionality constraints entirely, meaning the model performs best when these novel components are removed. This finding significantly weakens the contribution of this aspect of the model.

4.  **Misleading terminology and minor clarity issues.** Describing the phrase occurrences as "distant supervision" is potentially misleading. While the ontology provides links, the contexts themselves do not provide a direct supervised signal in the traditional sense (e.g., a label). It is more of an anchoring mechanism. Additionally, the notation for the embedding matrices (EW, EP, EC) is introduced in Section 3.2 but never used in the subsequent loss function equations, which slightly hinders readability.

**Questions to Authors**
1.  The evaluation datasets (e.g., MayoSRS) provide concept pairs identified by both a canonical identifier (CUI) and a specific text form. For the `PHRASEco` results in Table 4, if a concept has multiple phrasal forms in the training corpus, which specific phrase embedding do you use for the cosine similarity calculation? Is it the one provided in the dataset, or an average?

2.  As noted in the weaknesses, the `APPROXPHRco` method outperforms the `CONCEPTco` method on your new general-domain datasets (Table 5). This seems to suggest that the main benefit of your approach comes from identifying and embedding phrases, rather than the joint learning of a separate concept vector. Could you please comment on this interpretation? What specific value is the learned concept vector intended to capture that an aggregation of phrase vectors cannot?

3.  The optimal settings for your compositionality parameters β and η were often zero, especially on larger corpora. This suggests that enforcing compositionality hurts performance. Does this imply that most concepts in these domains are represented by non-compositional phrases, or could it point to a sub-optimality in how the compositionality constraint is formulated and applied (e.g., using a single global β for all phrases)?
