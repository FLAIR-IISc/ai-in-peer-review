- Proposes a weakly-supervised method that uses an ontology to tag phrases in a corpus and jointly learns embeddings for words, phrases, and concepts via parallel skip-gram training.  
- Evaluated mainly on biomedical concept similarity/relatedness, showing competitive performance with lighter supervision.  
- Contribution: introduces a new 688-pair dataset for general-domain entity similarity and relatedness (people, places, organizations).  
- Contribution: offers a practical, scalable weakly-supervised approach leveraging ontology aliases to learn concept embeddings without manual annotation.  
- Contribution: provides an extensive empirical evaluation in the biomedical domain showing strong results on standard similarity tasks.  
- Strength: the new entity-level evaluation resource fills a gap beyond word-level intrinsic tasks.  
- Strength: effective use of distant supervision yields high vocabulary coverage and domain portability given an ontology.  
- Strength: paper is well-written, clearly explained, and compares thoroughly against relevant baselines.  
- Strength: strong empirical performance on larger biomedical datasets (UMNSRS), especially on similarity.  
- Weakness: limited technical novelty—method is a straightforward adaptation of skip-gram with parallel corpora and shared context matrices.  
- Weakness: empirical focus is narrow (mostly biomedical); limited evaluation on general-domain tasks like entity linking or relation extraction.  
- Weakness: the new dataset has low inter-annotator agreement (Fleiss’ κ = 0.24) and needs deeper analysis of its impact on reliability.  
- Weakness: MAP/compositionality evaluation is unclear or possibly flawed (nonstandard Equation 8 and ambiguous ranking over entire vocabulary or unspecified cutoff).  
- Question: clarify the MAP calculation in Section 5.4, the exact formula, whether ranking is over the full vocabulary, and the computational cost or cutoff used.  
- Question: explain the low Fleiss’ κ (0.24) for the new dataset and discuss how this agreement level affects evaluation reliability and potential upper bounds on system performance.
