- Proposes a weakly-supervised method to jointly learn embeddings for concepts, phrases, and words using an ontology and a parallel tagged corpus with an adapted skip-gram negative sampling objective.
- Evaluated on biomedical and general-domain concept similarity/relatedness tasks, showing competitive performance versus methods requiring manual annotation.
- Contribution: new general-domain entity similarity/relatedness dataset of 688 entity pairs with human-annotated similarity and relatedness scores.
- Contribution: a simple, practical weakly-supervised joint-embedding technique that leverages existing ontologies for distant supervision.
- Strength: dataset creation is well-documented (Mechanical Turk, quality control), though inter-annotator agreement is only Fleiss’ κ = 0.24.
- Strength: method is elegant and effective, matching or exceeding more complex, manually-tagged approaches (e.g., MENCIA, DEVINE).
- Strength: paper is clearly written, well-motivated, and includes thorough hyperparameter search and analyses.
- Weakness: limited technical novelty—primarily an incremental extension of skip-gram rather than a major algorithmic advance.
- Weakness: evaluation scope is narrow; detailed analyses focus on biomedical data while general-domain evaluation relies solely on the new dataset.
- Weakness: MAP calculation (Section 5.4) is methodologically problematic—uses precision-like notation, ranks against entire vocabulary, yielding sensitive and hard-to-interpret scores; MAP@k would be more appropriate.
- Question: please clarify why ranking was done against the entire vocabulary instead of using MAP@k, and correct the probability/precision terminology in the MAP formulation.
- Question: consider positioning the new dataset more centrally and discuss how the low Fleiss’ κ (0.24) impacts the reliability of evaluation results.
- Minor correction: line 556 typo — change "most concepts has cosine similarity" to "most concepts have cosine similarity".
