### Summary of the Paper

This paper proposes a weakly-supervised method for jointly learning vector space embeddings for words, multi-word phrases, and ontology concepts. The core idea is to leverage an existing ontology (like UMLS or Yago) to identify canonical phrases within a large, unlabeled text corpus. These phrases are then treated as atomic tokens during training with a model based on the skip-gram with negative sampling architecture. The model learns three separate embedding matrices for words, phrases, and concepts, with information shared through shared context words. The authors also introduce hyperparameters to explicitly model the compositionality between words and phrases, and between phrases and concepts. The resulting embeddings are evaluated on concept similarity and relatedness tasks in both biomedical and general-domain settings, where they demonstrate performance that is competitive with, and in some cases superior to, previous methods.

### Main Contributions

1.  **A Practical Method for Embedding Non-Compositional Phrases and Concepts:** The primary contribution is a simple and effective method for learning representations for multi-word expressions and the abstract concepts they represent. By identifying phrases from an ontology and treating them as single units, the model can learn their meanings from context, which is particularly crucial for non-compositional phrases (e.g., "Lou Gehrig's disease") where meaning cannot be derived from constituent words. This approach is highly scalable and does not require manually annotated text.
2.  **A Strong Empirical Case for the Method in the Biomedical Domain:** The paper presents a thorough evaluation on several biomedical datasets. While not uniformly outperforming all baselines, the proposed model achieves state-of-the-art or competitive results on the larger UMNSRS datasets. This demonstrates the viability of using "distant supervision" from ontologies as a practical alternative to methods that rely on expensive human annotations, especially for specialized domains. The release of the `cui2vec` software further enhances this contribution for the biomedical NLP community.

### Strengths

1.  **Effectively Addresses the Challenge of Multi-Word Expressions:** The paper tackles the important and well-known problem of representing multi-word phrases whose meanings are not a simple composition of their parts. The proposed approach of linking text mentions to an ontology and training phrase-level embeddings is a plausible and well-motivated strategy to address this. It provides a direct way to learn embeddings for complex terms based on their actual usage in context.
2.  **Competitive Performance on Large-Scale Evaluation:** The empirical results are a key strength. On the larger UMNSRS similarity and relatedness datasets (Table 4), the `CONCEPTco` embeddings are the best-performing model on the similarity task and are highly competitive on the relatedness task, outperforming a number of baselines. This suggests that the model is effective and scales well with the size of the evaluation set.
3.  **Valuable Resources for the Community:** The authors are releasing their software implementation and a new crowdsourced dataset for general-domain entity similarity and relatedness. This is a commendable contribution, particularly for the biomedical NLP community, who will benefit from an accessible tool to create high-quality concept embeddings from their own corpora.

### Weaknesses

1.  **Inconsistent Performance and Strong Baselines:** The proposed `CONCEPTco` model does not consistently outperform other methods. In Table 4, the Choi et al. (2016b) baseline, which uses manually annotated codes, is substantially better on both MayoSRS datasets. On the larger UMNSRS dataset, the gains over the simpler Chiu-30 word-averaging baseline are marginal. More concerningly, on the general-domain task (Table 5), simply averaging the learned phrase vectors (`APPROXPHRco`) outperforms the full `CONCEPTco` model, which calls into question the utility of learning the final concept embedding layer.
2.  **Limited Methodological Novelty:** The core method is largely a clever application of the existing skip-gram model on a pre-processed corpus where phrases have been replaced by unique identifiers. The proposed extensions for modeling compositionality (controlled by β and η) appear to have limited impact. As shown in Table 3, the cross-validated β and η values are often tuned to zero for the larger corpora (PM+CL, WikiNYT), suggesting the model learned to completely ignore this component. This weakens the claim of a novel modeling contribution.
3.  **Misleading Framing of "Distant Supervision":** The paper describes its approach as using "distant supervision," which may be misleading. In the typical formulation (e.g., Mintz et al., 2009), a knowledge base provides noisy *labels* for instances in text (e.g., labeling a sentence as expressing a specific relation). Here, the ontology is used as a lexicon to identify phrase occurrences. The learning signal remains entirely unsupervised and distributional, derived from co-occurrence statistics in the corpus. While the ontology guides the process, it doesn't provide the type of supervised signal that "distant supervision" usually implies.
4.  **Unclear Benefit of the Concept Layer:** Several results suggest that the main benefit of the model comes from learning good phrase embeddings, rather than the joint learning of concept embeddings. The superior performance of `APPROXPHRco` in Table 5 is a strong indicator. This raises the question of whether a simpler model that only learns word and phrase embeddings would be just as effective, without the additional complexity of the concept embedding matrix (`EC`).

### Questions to Authors

1.  Could you please clarify the experimental setup for the similarity and relatedness evaluations in Table 4? Given the many-to-many mapping between concepts and phrases, how is the similarity score between a pair of concepts calculated? Is it simply the cosine similarity between their two `CONCEPTco` vectors, or is there a more complex aggregation involving their constituent phrase vectors?
2.  The hyperparameter tuning results (Table 3) show that the compositionality parameter β was set to 0.0 for the PM+CL and WikiNYT corpora. This means the model found it optimal to completely ignore the connection between a phrase's embedding and its constituent word embeddings. Why do you believe this is the case? Does this suggest that the proposed compositionality mechanism is not beneficial in practice, or that a single global β parameter is too simplistic for diverse corpora?
3.  Given that averaging phrase vectors (`APPROXPHRco`) performs better than the direct concept embeddings (`CONCEPTco`) on the general-domain tasks (Table 5), what is the added value of learning the concept embedding matrix `EC`? Could one achieve similar or better performance with a simpler model that stops at learning phrase embeddings from an ontology-derived lexicon?
