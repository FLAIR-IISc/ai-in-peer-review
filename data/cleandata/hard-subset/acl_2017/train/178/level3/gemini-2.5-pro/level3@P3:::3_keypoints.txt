- Proposes a weakly-supervised joint embedding model for concepts, phrases, and words using ontologies (UMLS/YAGO) and an adapted skip-gram with negative sampling; introduces a new general-domain entity similarity dataset.
- Contribution: releases a 688-pair human-annotated dataset for general-domain entity similarity and relatedness.
- Contribution: presents a joint embedding model that trains separate word, phrase, and concept embeddings with hyperparameters (β, η) controlling compositionality.
- Strength: model is a sensible, transparent extension of word2vec that explicitly trades off atomic vs. compositional phrase representations.
- Strength: substantial and thorough work including dataset creation, validation, and extensive experiments across biomedical and general corpora with many baselines.
- Weakness: evaluation is limited to intrinsic similarity/relatedness tasks and lacks extrinsic downstream evaluations to demonstrate real-world utility.
- Weakness: biomedical evaluation uses inadequate or problematic datasets (very small or low inter-annotator agreement) and UMNSRS may be inappropriate for concept-level claims.
- Weakness: new dataset is mischaracterized as a concept similarity benchmark even though annotations were collected on specific phrases, conflating phrase and concept similarity.
- Weakness: improper hyperparameter tuning—authors tune on the same datasets used for final evaluation (no held-out dev set), risking overly optimistic results.
- Weakness: claims of “less supervision” are overstated because the method depends heavily on large, manually curated ontologies, making supervision comparisons misleading.
- Weakness: lacks deeper analysis—no disentangling of compositionality vs. lexical/headword effects and no exploration of how β and η affect performance.
- Recommendation: reject due to flawed evaluation, dataset issues, improper tuning, overstated claims, and superficial analysis.
