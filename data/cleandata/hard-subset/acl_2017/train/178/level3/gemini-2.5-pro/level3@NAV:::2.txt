This paper proposes a weakly-supervised method to jointly learn vector representations for concepts, phrases, and words. The core idea is to use an ontology (e.g., UMLS, Yago) to map concepts to their representative phrases, identify these phrases in an unlabeled corpus, and then train embeddings for all three unit types using a modified skip-gram model. While the authors present the method as a key contribution, I see the main contributions as the thorough application of existing techniques to the important problem of representing non-compositional concepts and the release of a practical software tool for the biomedical NLP community.

**Strengths**

1.  The primary strength is the paper's focus on learning representations for non-compositional multi-word expressions and abstract concepts, a well-known limitation of standard compositional models. The approach of using an ontology to provide distant supervision for phrases is a plausible and scalable method to tackle this problem.
2.  The resulting embeddings are empirically strong, demonstrating performance that is competitive with, and in some cases superior to, strong baselines on large-scale concept similarity tasks (e.g., UMNSRS Similarity in Table 4). This shows the potential of the approach.
3.  The public release of the `cui2vec` software is a valuable practical contribution that can facilitate further research and applications, particularly for researchers in biomedical NLP.

**Weaknesses**

1.  The most significant weakness is the inconsistent performance of the full model, which fails to make a conclusive case for its superiority. The Choi et al. baseline substantially outperforms the proposed method on the Mayo datasets. More critically, on the general-domain datasets (Table 5), a simpler baseline of averaging phrase vectors (`APPROXPHRco`) achieves the best results, calling into question the added utility of the specific concept-embedding mechanism.
2.  The methodological novelty is limited. The model is a relatively straightforward adaptation of the well-established skip-gram with negative sampling architecture, rather than a fundamentally new approach.
3.  Certain modeling choices and experimental details raise concerns. The use of a single global hyperparameter (`β`) to control compositionality is surprising, and the cross-validated optimal value of zero for the PM+CL and WikiNYT corpora argues against the utility of this component. Furthermore, the paper lacks clarity on how similarities were computed in Table 4 given the many-to-many mappings between concepts and phrases.
