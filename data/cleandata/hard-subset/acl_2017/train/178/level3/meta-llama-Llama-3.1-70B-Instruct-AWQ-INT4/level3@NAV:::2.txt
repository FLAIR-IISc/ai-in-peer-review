**Summary of the Paper**

This paper proposes a novel method for jointly learning concept, phrase, and word embeddings from an unlabeled text corpus using a weakly-supervised approach. The method leverages structured knowledge from an ontology and an unannotated training corpus to train context-based representations for concepts, phrases, and words. The authors evaluate their approach on concept similarity and relatedness tasks using biomedical and general-domain datasets and demonstrate competitive performance with prior methods that require human-annotated data.

**Main Contributions**

1. **Novel method for jointly embedding concepts, phrases, and words**: The authors propose a weakly-supervised method for learning concept, phrase, and word embeddings from an unlabeled text corpus, leveraging structured knowledge from an ontology.
2. **Competitive performance on concept similarity and relatedness tasks**: The authors demonstrate competitive performance with prior methods that require human-annotated data on biomedical and general-domain datasets.
3. **Release of software implementation**: The authors release a software implementation of their method, cui2vec, which can benefit biomedical NLP researchers.

**Strengths**

1. **Targets non-compositional phrases**: The authors' method targets non-compositional phrases, offering a plausible method for training phrase embeddings.
2. **Trained embeddings are competitive or superior**: The authors' embeddings show competitive or superior performance on concept similarity and relatedness tasks in some settings.
3. **Released software could benefit biomedical NLP researchers**: The authors' software implementation, cui2vec, can benefit biomedical NLP researchers.

**Weaknesses**

1. **Methodology is not very novel**: The authors' method is essentially a tweak to skip-gram, which may not be considered novel by some reviewers.
2. **Full model does not consistently perform best**: The authors' full model does not consistently outperform simpler baselines, such as Choi, on some datasets.
3. **On the larger UMNSRS dataset, the model is at best competitive with simpler baselines**: The authors' model is at best competitive with simpler baselines, such as Chiu, on the larger UMNSRS dataset.

**Questions to Authors**

1. Can you provide more details on the experimental setup for Table 4, specifically how similarities are computed given many-to-many concept↔phrase mappings?
2. How do you justify using a single global β to control compositionality, given that cross-validated β values (zero for PM+CL and WikiNYT) counterintuitively argue against compositionality?
3. Can you provide more insights into the results of Table 5, specifically why averaging phrase vectors (approximate phr) performs best, and what implications this has for the utility of the concept ontology versus starting from seed phrases?
