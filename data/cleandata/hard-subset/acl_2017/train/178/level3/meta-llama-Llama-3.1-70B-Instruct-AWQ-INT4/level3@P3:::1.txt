This paper proposes a novel method for jointly learning concept, phrase, and word embeddings from an unlabeled text corpus, using representative phrases for ontology concepts as distant supervision. The authors evaluate their approach by learning embeddings for biomedical concepts and real-world entities, and demonstrate competitive performance with prior models that required human annotations for concepts.

**Summary of the Paper**

The paper presents a method for jointly embedding concepts, phrases, and words into a real-valued space, using structured knowledge from an ontology and an unannotated training corpus. The authors replace phrase/word occurrences with a fixed concept identifier in a tagged corpus and append it to the original corpus for training. They evaluate their approach on biomedical and general-domain datasets, and present a novel dataset of similarity and relatedness of real-world entities.

**Main Contributions**

1. A novel method for jointly learning concept, phrase, and word embeddings from an unlabeled text corpus.
2. A new dataset of similarity and relatedness of real-world entities, which can be used for evaluating embeddings.
3. Competitive performance with prior models that required human annotations for concepts.

**Strengths**

1. The paper presents a well-written and natural method for jointly learning concept, phrase, and word embeddings.
2. The authors provide a thorough evaluation of their approach on biomedical and general-domain datasets.
3. The novel dataset of similarity and relatedness of real-world entities is a valuable contribution to the field.

**Weaknesses**

1. The contribution of the paper is limited and not a major technical advance.
2. The evaluation is narrowly focused on biomedical data, limiting the generality of the approach.
3. The generated test resource needs more discussion and may be the paper's primary contribution.
4. There is a technical issue with Eq. 8 (MAP calculation) that is incorrectly described as a probability and should not rank the entire vocabulary.
5. Minor editorial correction: line 556 “most concepts has” → “most concepts have.”

**Questions to Authors**

1. Can you provide more discussion on the generated test resource and its potential applications?
2. How do you plan to address the limitation of the evaluation being narrowly focused on biomedical data?
3. Can you clarify the technical issue with Eq. 8 (MAP calculation) and provide a corrected explanation?
4. How do you plan to extend the approach to other domains and datasets?

**Additional Comments**

The paper is well-written and easy to follow. The authors provide a thorough evaluation of their approach and present a valuable contribution to the field. However, the contribution is limited, and the evaluation is narrowly focused on biomedical data. The generated test resource needs more discussion, and the technical issue with Eq. 8 (MAP calculation) should be addressed.
