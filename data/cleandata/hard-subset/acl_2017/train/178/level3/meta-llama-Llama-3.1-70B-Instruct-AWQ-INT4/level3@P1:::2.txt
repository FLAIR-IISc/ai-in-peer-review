This paper proposes a novel method for jointly learning concept, phrase, and word embeddings from an unlabeled text corpus, using representative phrases for ontology concepts as distant supervision. The method adapts the skip-gram with negative sampling variant of word2vec for joint embedding of concepts, phrases, and words. The authors evaluate their approach by learning embeddings for biomedical concepts and real-world entities, and demonstrate competitive performance with prior models that required human annotations for concepts.

**Main Contributions:**

1. A novel method for jointly learning concept, phrase, and word embeddings from an unlabeled text corpus.
2. The use of representative phrases for ontology concepts as distant supervision, eliminating the need for human annotations.
3. Competitive performance with prior models on concept similarity and relatedness tasks.

**Strengths:**

1. The method targets non-compositional phrases and offers a plausible method for training phrase embeddings.
2. The trained embeddings are competitive or superior at identifying concept similarity in some settings.
3. The released software could benefit biomedical NLP researchers.

**Weaknesses:**

1. The methodology is not very novel—essentially a tweak to skip-gram.
2. The full model does not consistently perform best; Choi baseline substantially outperforms on the Mayo datasets and trends similarly in Table 6.
3. On the larger UMNSRS dataset, the model is at best competitive with simpler baselines (Chiu).

**Questions to Authors:**

1. Can you clarify the experimental setup for Table 4, specifically how similarities are computed given many-to-many concept↔phrase mappings?
2. How do you respond to the concern that using a single global β to control compositionality is surprising, and that cross-validated β values (zero for PM+CL and WikiNYT) counterintuitively argue against compositionality?
3. Can you provide more insight into why averaging phrase vectors (approximate phr) performs best, and how this questions the utility of the concept ontology versus starting from seed phrases?

**Additional Comments:**

1. The notation introduced in Section 3.2 (e.g., E_W) is never used later in the paper.
2. Describing phrase occurrences as “distant supervision” is misleading—occurrences provide context but not traditional supervised signals.
3. The paper could benefit from more discussion on the implications of the results, particularly in the context of biomedical NLP research.
