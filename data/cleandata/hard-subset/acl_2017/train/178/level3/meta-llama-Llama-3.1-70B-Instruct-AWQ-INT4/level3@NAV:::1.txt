**Summary of the Paper**

The paper proposes a novel method for jointly learning concept, phrase, and word embeddings from an unlabeled text corpus. The method uses representative phrases for ontology concepts as distant supervision, replacing phrase occurrences with a fixed concept identifier in a tagged corpus and appending it to the original corpus for training. The authors evaluate their approach on biomedical and general-domain concepts, demonstrating competitive performance with existing methods on concept similarity and relatedness tasks.

**Main Contributions**

1. **Novel method for jointly embedding concepts, phrases, and words**: The authors propose a method that extends word embeddings to produce representations for phrases and concepts by leveraging structured knowledge from an ontology and an unannotated training corpus.
2. **Application to biomedical and general-domain concepts**: The authors demonstrate the effectiveness of their approach on both biomedical and general-domain concepts, using related corpora and ontologies.
3. **Introduction of a novel test dataset for word similarity/relatedness**: The authors introduce a novel dataset of similarity and relatedness of real-world entities, identified both by Wikipedia page and text phrase.

**Strengths**

1. **Competitive performance with existing methods**: The authors demonstrate that their method achieves competitive performance with existing methods on concept similarity and relatedness tasks.
2. **Ability to scale to a larger vocabulary**: The authors show that their method can scale to a much larger vocabulary without additional expense.
3. **Novel dataset for word similarity/relatedness**: The authors introduce a novel dataset that can be used to evaluate the performance of word embeddings on real-world entities.

**Weaknesses**

1. **Limited contribution**: The authors' contribution is limited/subtle and not a major technical advance.
2. **Narrow evaluation**: The evaluation is narrowly focused on biomedical data, limiting generality.
3. **Technical issue with Eq. 8**: The equation is incorrectly described as a probability and should not rank the entire vocabulary.
4. **Minor editorial correction**: Line 556 "most concepts has" should be corrected to "most concepts have."

**Questions to Authors**

1. Can you provide more details on the hyperparameter tuning process and how the optimal settings were determined?
2. How do you plan to address the technical issue with Eq. 8 and the minor editorial correction?
3. Can you provide more insights on the performance of your method on general-domain concepts and how it compares to existing methods?
4. How do you plan to extend your method to other domains and applications?
