This paper proposes a novel method for jointly learning concept, phrase, and word embeddings from an unlabeled text corpus. The method relies on the representative phrases for ontology concepts as distant supervision. The main contributions of this work are:

1. A method for jointly embedding concepts, phrases, and words into a shared vector space, which allows for the capture of semantic relationships between these entities.
2. An evaluation of the proposed method on biomedical and general-domain concept similarity and relatedness tasks, which demonstrates its competitiveness with existing methods that require human annotation.
3. The release of a software implementation of the method, cui2vec, which can benefit biomedical NLP researchers.

The strengths of this submission are:

1. The method targets non-compositional phrases and offers a plausible way for training phrase embeddings, which is a challenging task in NLP.
2. The trained embeddings are competitive or superior at identifying concept similarity in some settings, which demonstrates the effectiveness of the method.
3. The release of the cui2vec software can benefit biomedical NLP researchers, who can use it to train their own concept embeddings.

The weaknesses of this submission are:

1. The methodology is not very novel, as it is essentially a tweak to the skip-gram model.
2. The full model does not consistently perform best, as the Choi baseline substantially outperforms on the Mayo datasets and trends similarly in Table 6.
3. On the larger UMNSRS dataset, the model is at best competitive with simpler baselines (Chiu).
4. The description of phrase occurrences as "distant supervision" is misleading, as occurrences provide context but not traditional supervised signals.
5. The notation introduced in Section 3.2 (e.g., E_W) is never used later in the paper.
6. The use of a single global β to control compositionality is surprising, as cross-validated β values (zero for PM+CL and WikiNYT) counterintuitively argue against compositionality.

Questions to Authors:

1. Can you clarify the experimental setup for Table 4, specifically how similarities are computed given the many-to-many concept↔phrase mappings?
2. Can you explain why the full model does not consistently perform best, and why the Choi baseline substantially outperforms on the Mayo datasets?
3. Can you discuss the implications of using a single global β to control compositionality, and why cross-validated β values argue against compositionality?
4. Can you provide more details on the cui2vec software, such as its architecture and training procedure?

Additional Comments:

1. The paper could benefit from a more detailed discussion of the related work, specifically the differences between the proposed method and existing methods for concept embedding.
2. The evaluation section could be improved by providing more details on the experimental setup and the metrics used for evaluation.
3. The paper could benefit from more visualizations, such as plots of the embeddings or heatmaps of the similarity matrices, to help illustrate the results.
