This paper proposes a novel method for jointly learning concept, phrase, and word embeddings from an unlabeled text corpus, using the representative phrases for ontology concepts as distant supervision. The authors claim that their method outperforms existing methods on concept similarity and relatedness tasks, while requiring no human corpus annotation and demonstrating more than 3x coverage in the vocabulary size.

The main contributions of this work are:

1. A novel method for jointly learning concept, phrase, and word embeddings from an unlabeled text corpus, using the representative phrases for ontology concepts as distant supervision.
2. A new evaluation dataset of similarity and relatedness of real-world entities, identified both by Wikipedia page and text phrase.
3. An experimental evaluation of the proposed method on biomedical and general-domain concepts, demonstrating competitive performance with existing methods.

The strengths of this submission are:

1. The proposed method is straightforward and sensibly balances treating phrases/concepts as atomic vs compositional.
2. The paper presents substantial work, including model development, a new evaluation dataset, and multiple evaluations/analyses.
3. The authors provide a detailed description of their method and experiments, making it easy to follow and reproduce their work.

However, there are also several weaknesses:

1. The evaluation uses only intrinsic similarity/relatedness tasks and lacks extrinsic task evaluation, which is a major limitation of the paper.
2. Human judgments were elicited on phrases, so datasets should be treated/analyzed as phrase-similarity rather than concept-similarity, which may affect the validity of the results.
3. The medical evaluation datasets are very limited or inappropriate, which may not provide a comprehensive evaluation of the proposed method.
4. Hyperparameters are heavily tuned on the same datasets used for reporting results, which undermines the validity of the results.
5. The claim that the method outperforms prior work is weak because the authors still rely on large manual ontologies and prior annotations from clinical records.

To address these weaknesses, the authors could:

1. Conduct extrinsic task evaluations to demonstrate the effectiveness of their method in real-world applications.
2. Clarify how phrase overlaps are handled and provide a more detailed analysis of the atomic vs compositional hyperparameter tradeoff.
3. Develop more comprehensive evaluation datasets, including more diverse and larger-scale datasets.
4. Use a more robust hyperparameter tuning method, such as cross-validation, to ensure the validity of the results.
