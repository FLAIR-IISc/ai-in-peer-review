This paper proposes a novel method for jointly learning concept, phrase, and word embeddings from an unlabeled text corpus, using the representative phrases for ontology concepts as distant supervision. The authors claim that their method outperforms existing methods on concept similarity and relatedness tasks, while requiring no human corpus annotation and demonstrating more than 3x coverage in the vocabulary size.

**Summary of the Paper**

The paper introduces a weakly-supervised method for jointly embedding concepts, phrases, and words into a real-valued space. The method uses structured knowledge from an ontology and an unannotated training corpus. The authors experiment with learning embeddings for medical concepts in the Unified Medical Language System and general-domain concepts in YAGO, using a variety of corpora. The proposed model adapts the skip-gram with negative sampling variant of word2vec for joint embedding of concepts, phrases, and words. The authors evaluate their method on biomedical similarity and relatedness tasks, as well as on a novel dataset of similarity and relatedness of real-world entities.

**Contributions**

1. A novel method for jointly learning concept, phrase, and word embeddings from an unlabeled text corpus.
2. The use of representative phrases for ontology concepts as distant supervision, which eliminates the need for human corpus annotation.
3. A comprehensive evaluation of the proposed method on biomedical similarity and relatedness tasks, as well as on a novel dataset of similarity and relatedness of real-world entities.

**Strengths**

1. The proposed method is straightforward and sensibly balances treating phrases/concepts as atomic vs compositional.
2. The paper presents substantial work, including model development, a new evaluation dataset, and multiple evaluations/analyses.
3. The method demonstrates competitive performance on biomedical similarity and relatedness tasks, while requiring no human corpus annotation.

**Weaknesses**

1. The evaluation uses only intrinsic similarity/relatedness tasks and lacks extrinsic task evaluation.
2. Human judgments were elicited on phrases, so datasets should be treated/analyzed as phrase-similarity rather than concept-similarity.
3. Medical evaluation datasets are very limited or inappropriate: mini MayoSRS (29 pairs), MayoSRS (101 pairs, low agreement), UMNSRS (single-word concepts only).
4. Hyperparameters are heavily tuned on the same datasets used for reporting results, undermining result validity.
5. The claim that the method outperforms prior work is weak because authors still rely on large manual ontologies and prior annotations from clinical records.

**Questions to Authors**

1. How do you plan to address the limitations of the current evaluation datasets and methodology?
2. Can you provide more details on how phrase overlaps are handled in the proposed method?
3. How do you plan to explore the atomic vs compositional hyperparameter tradeoff in future work?
4. Can you provide more insights on why the method performs well on biomedical similarity and relatedness tasks, despite the limitations of the evaluation datasets?
