This paper proposes a novel method for jointly learning concept, phrase, and word embeddings from an unlabeled text corpus, leveraging structured knowledge from an ontology. The authors adapt the skip-gram with negative sampling variant of word2vec to train embeddings for medical concepts in the Unified Medical Language System and general-domain concepts in YAGO. The method uses representative phrases for ontology concepts as distant supervision, requiring no human corpus annotation.

The main contributions of this work are:

1. A novel approach to jointly embedding concepts, phrases, and words into a shared vector space, leveraging structured knowledge from an ontology.
2. A method for training concept embeddings using distant supervision from phrases linked to concepts in an ontology.
3. The release of cui2vec, a software implementation of the proposed method, and a novel dataset of similarity and relatedness of real-world entities.

The strengths of this paper are:

1. The proposed method targets non-compositional phrases and offers a plausible approach for training phrase embeddings.
2. The trained embeddings are competitive or superior at identifying concept similarity in some settings.
3. The released software could benefit biomedical NLP researchers.
4. The method demonstrates the ability to model both abstract biomedical concepts and concrete, real-world entities.

The weaknesses of this paper are:

1. The methodology is not very novel, essentially a tweak to skip-gram.
2. The full model does not consistently perform best; the Choi baseline substantially outperforms on the Mayo datasets and trends similarly in Table 6.
3. On the larger UMNSRS dataset, the model is at best competitive with simpler baselines (Chiu).
4. The description of phrase occurrences as “distant supervision” is misleading, as occurrences provide context but not traditional supervised signals.
5. The notation introduced in Section 3.2 (e.g., E_W) is never used later in the paper.
6. The use of a single global β to control compositionality is surprising, and cross-validated β values (zero for PM+CL and WikiNYT) counterintuitively argue against compositionality.

Questions to Authors:

1. Can you clarify the experimental setup for Table 4, specifically how similarities are computed given many-to-many concept↔phrase mappings?
2. How do you respond to the criticism that the methodology is not very novel, and that the full model does not consistently perform best?
3. Can you provide more insight into the choice of hyperparameters, particularly the use of a single global β to control compositionality?
4. How do you plan to address the issue of polysemy in concept embeddings, as mentioned in the discussion section?

Additional Comments:

* The paper would benefit from a more detailed discussion of the related work, particularly in the context of biomedical NLP.
* The use of Mechanical Turk for data collection is a good approach, but the authors could provide more information on the quality control measures taken to ensure reliable annotations.
* The paper could benefit from a more detailed analysis of the errors made by the model, particularly in cases where it performs poorly.
