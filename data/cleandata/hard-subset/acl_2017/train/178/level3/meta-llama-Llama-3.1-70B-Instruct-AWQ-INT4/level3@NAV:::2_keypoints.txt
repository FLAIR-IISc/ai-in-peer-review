- Summary: Proposes a weakly-supervised method that uses an ontology plus unlabeled text to jointly learn concept, phrase, and word embeddings, evaluated on biomedical and general datasets with competitive results.

- Contribution 1: Novel weakly-supervised approach to jointly embed concepts, phrases, and words leveraging structured ontology knowledge.

- Contribution 2: Demonstrates competitive performance on concept similarity and relatedness tasks, matching some supervised approaches.

- Contribution 3: Releases a software implementation (cui2vec) useful for biomedical NLP researchers.

- Strength 1: Specifically addresses non-compositional phrases, offering a plausible phrase-embedding method.

- Strength 2: Learned embeddings are competitive or superior on several similarity/relatedness benchmarks.

- Strength 3: Public release of code/embeddings aids reproducibility and adoption in biomedical NLP.

- Weakness 1: Method is largely a tweak to skip-gram and may lack strong novelty.

- Weakness 2: Full model does not consistently outperform simpler baselines across datasets.

- Weakness 3: On larger UMNSRS dataset, performance is at best competitive with simpler baselines like Chiu.

- Question 1: How were similarities computed in Table 4 given many-to-many concept↔phrase mappings?

- Question 2: Why use a single global β for compositionality control when cross-validation suggests β=0 for some datasets?

- Question 3: Why does averaging phrase vectors (approximate phr) perform best in Table 5, and what does this imply about the value of the concept ontology versus seed phrases?
