- Novel method for jointly learning concept, phrase, and word embeddings from unlabeled text.  
- Uses representative phrases for ontology concepts as distant supervision, removing need for human annotations.  
- Demonstrates competitive performance with prior models on concept similarity and relatedness tasks.  

- Targets non-compositional phrases and provides a plausible training approach for phrase embeddings.  
- Trained embeddings are competitive or superior on some concept similarity settings.  
- Released software could aid biomedical NLP researchers.  

- Methodological novelty is limited—essentially a tweak to skip-gram.  
- Full model underperforms baselines (Choi) on Mayo datasets and trends worse in Table 6.  
- On the larger UMNSRS dataset, performance is at best competitive with simpler baselines (Chiu).  

- Clarify experimental setup for Table 4, specifically how similarities are computed with many-to-many concept↔phrase mappings.  
- Explain why a single global β controlling compositionality is appropriate, given cross-validated β values that suggest non-compositionality.  
- Explain why averaging phrase vectors (approximate phr) performs best and whether the concept ontology adds value over starting from seed phrases.  

- Notation introduced in Section 3.2 (e.g., E_W) is unused later in the paper.  
- Calling phrase occurrences “distant supervision” is misleading since they provide context rather than traditional supervised signals.  
- Paper needs more discussion of result implications, especially for biomedical NLP research.
