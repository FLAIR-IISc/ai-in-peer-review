- Summary: Proposes ontology-supervised skip-gram for joint word/phrase/concept embeddings, shows competitive biomedical results, and releases cui2vec plus a new dataset.  
- Strength: Addresses representation of semantically similar but lexically dissimilar and non-compositional phrases, showing competitive performance on large datasets.  
- Strength: Practical utility from releasing the cui2vec software and a real-world entity similarity/relatedness dataset.  
- Strength: Scales to much larger vocabularies than prior human-annotated approaches.  
- Weakness: Limited methodological novelty—mostly a minor adaptation of skip-gram with ontology supervision.  
- Weakness: Underperforms on key datasets compared to simpler baselines (e.g., Choi and Chiu), raising practical benefit concerns.  
- Weakness: Ambiguous use of "distant supervision" and unclear explanation of how phrase/word vectors are used to compute concept similarities (Table 4).  
- Weakness: Ontology may be redundant—phrase-average approximations sometimes outperform ontology-based concepts (Table 5).  
- Weakness: Unused notation (e.g., E_W) and surprising/global compositionality parameter choices (β often cross-validates to 0), reducing clarity.  
- Question: Clarify specific advantages of the ontology-based approach over simpler phrase-average baselines.  
- Question: Explain the intended meaning of "distant supervision" and whether a different term is more accurate.  
- Question: Provide details on how phrase/word vectors are combined to compute concept similarities (Table 4).  
- Question: Given poorer performance on smaller datasets, how applicable is the method in low-data real-world scenarios?  
- Recommendation: Major revisions required due to limited novelty, performance issues, and experimental ambiguities before acceptance.
