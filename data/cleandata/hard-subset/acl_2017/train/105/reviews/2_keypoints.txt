- Strength: Proposes a novel encoder-decoder with explicit hard monotonic attention.  
- Strength: Well-written and mostly clear presentation of a novel idea for morphology.  
- Weakness: Model may be equivalent to an ordinary BiRNN with alignments decoupled.  
- Weakness: Evaluation limited to morphology; no other monotone seq2seq tasks tested.  
- Motivation: Monotonicity is appropriate for many seq2seq tasks and should be enforced rather than using general encoder-decoder.  
- Approach: Decouples alignment and transduction by first producing monotone alignments, then training to generate outputs consistent with them.  
- Major question: Unclear what alignment type is used—1-to-many, many-to-1, or many-to-many.  
- Baseline concern: A simple 1-to-many alignment + BiLSTM sequence tagger baseline (e.g., Schnober et al.) is not included or compared.  
- Clarification requested: How does the proposed method differ from that simple align-then-tag approach, and why was it omitted?  
- Dataset/generalization concern: Should evaluate on other monotone string-translation tasks to demonstrate broader advantage.  
- Reporting wording: “on par or better” is imprecise; results should be reported more accurately.  
- Feature confound: Linguistic features (e.g., POS) are not well described—need sources and analysis of whether features, not monotonicity, drive improvements.  
- Minor issues: formatting/notation fixes (NN in eqn (3), lowercase “where”, proper ellipsis spacing x1,...,xn), typo at l.437, and unnecessary Cyrillic font in Fig.1.  
- After author response: Alignments appear mixed (1-0, 0-1, 1-1) and later combined into many-to-many—this needs clarification.  
- After author response / reiteration: Reviewer again suggests using 1-x alignments and a neural tagger baseline to assess how much the complex model adds.  
- Greedy alignment concern: Does using greedy alignments lose performance compared to methods that sum over alignments (Rastogi et al., 2016)?  
- Related work / AC note: Relevant prior work includes Cohn et al. (2016) and connections to Stack LSTM–style action-based architectures should be discussed.
