- Proposed hard-attention neural model designed to exploit near-monotonic character alignments for morphological inflection.
- Achieves state-of-the-art results on CELEX, Wiktionary, and SIGMORPHON2016, with especially strong performance on small datasets.
- Provides a comparative analysis of learned representations between hard and soft attention models.

- Strong empirical results, notably outperforming a latent-variable WFST on the small CELEX benchmark.
- Hard-attention design is well-motivated by the nearly-monotonic nature of morphological alignment.
- Evaluation is comprehensive across diverse datasets and morphological complexities.
- The paper gives a clear, detailed explanation of architecture, training, and experiments (including a helpful diagram).
- The representation analysis offers useful insights into how hard vs. soft attention encode positional and character information.

- The hard-attention mechanism itself is not novel; the novelty is mainly its application to this task.
- Training depends on pre-learned character-level alignments, and robustness to alignment errors is not thoroughly examined.
- SIGMORPHON results show soft attention slightly outperforming overall; the explanation is somewhat post-hoc and lacks deeper per-language/morphology analysis.
- Lacks ablation studies to quantify the contribution of individual model components (e.g., encoder, decoder feedback).

- How sensitive is model performance to the quality or noise level of pre-learned alignments?
- Can the authors provide examples or analyses showing where soft attention captures long-range or non-monotonic dependencies better?
- Could the learned representations be visualized (e.g., t-SNE) and specific encoded features discussed?
- What is the computational cost comparison between hard and soft attention, particularly during inference?

- Paper references a GitHub repository but does not provide a link.
- Clarify the term "step action" in the decoder—does it mean moving the attention pointer forward?
- The introduction could be made slightly more concise.
