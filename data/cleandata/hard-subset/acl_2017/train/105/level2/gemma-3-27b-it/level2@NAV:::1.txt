## Review of “Morphological Inflection Generation with Hard Monotonic Attention”

This paper presents a novel neural model for morphological inflection generation leveraging a hard attention mechanism designed to exploit the near-monotonic alignment typically found in inflectional morphology. The authors evaluate their model on three standard datasets, demonstrating state-of-the-art performance and particularly strong results in low-resource scenarios. A comparative analysis with a soft attention model provides insights into the learned representations.

The primary contribution of this work is the introduction of a hard attention mechanism specifically tailored for morphological inflection. While attention mechanisms are common in sequence-to-sequence models, the constraint of hard, monotonic alignment is a key innovation and appears well-motivated by the characteristics of the task. A second significant contribution is the strong empirical performance, achieving state-of-the-art results on multiple datasets, especially notably surpassing previous models on the challenging CELEX dataset with limited training data. Finally, the analysis comparing the hard and soft attention models offers valuable insights into the differing learned representations, shedding light on the benefits of explicitly modeling alignment.

The strengths of this paper lie in its clear motivation, rigorous experimental evaluation, and insightful analysis. The improvement in low-resource settings is particularly compelling, addressing a key limitation of existing neural approaches. The ablation studies and comparison to soft attention demonstrate a solid understanding of the model's behavior. The writing is clear and the paper is well-structured.

However, several weaknesses should be addressed. The paper lacks a more in-depth discussion of the limitations of the hard attention approach, particularly for languages with more complex, non-monotonic morphology. While the SIGMORPHON results are presented, the authors only briefly acknowledge the relative underperformance on certain language families. A more detailed error analysis might reveal areas for improvement. Additionally, the experimental setup details, while present in the supplementary material, could benefit from greater clarity within the main body of the paper, specifically regarding hyperparameter tuning. The dependency on pre-trained alignments also feels slightly limiting; exploring end-to-end training even with modifications to the hard attention scheme would be beneficial.

**Questions to Authors:**

1.  Could you elaborate on the potential challenges of applying this hard attention mechanism to languages exhibiting significant non-monotonic morphological processes, and discuss possible extensions to address those challenges?
2.  For the languages where the hard attention model underperforms on SIGMORPHON, could you provide a more detailed error analysis to identify the types of morphological patterns the model struggles with?
3.  What impact does the quality of the pre-trained alignments have on the overall performance of the model? Have you experimented with different alignment methods or explored end-to-end training approaches?
