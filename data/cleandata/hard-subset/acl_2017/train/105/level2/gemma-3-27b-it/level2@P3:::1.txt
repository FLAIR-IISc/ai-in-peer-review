The paper presents a neural model for morphological inflection generation employing a hard attention mechanism. The core idea is to model the nearly-monotonic alignment commonly found between input and output characters in morphological inflection, offering a controllable attention mechanism. The work evaluates the model on three datasets (CELEX, Wiktionary, and SIGMORPHON2016) and demonstrates state-of-the-art results, alongside an analysis of the learned representations. The primary contributions are a novel hard attention model tailored for morphological inflection, strong empirical results establishing a new state-of-the-art on several datasets, and an analysis comparing the model to soft attention approaches offering insights into learned features.

Strengths:

1. **Strong Empirical Performance:** The model demonstrably achieves state-of-the-art or competitive results across all three datasets used in the evaluation. The particularly strong performance on the CELEX dataset, surpassing previous models even with limited training data, is a significant achievement. This highlights the model’s effectiveness in low-resource scenarios, a crucial benefit for morphologically rich languages.
2. **Novel Hard Attention Mechanism:** The proposed hard attention mechanism, directly modeling monotonic alignment, is a well-motivated and potentially valuable contribution. The design choices, specifically the decoupling of alignment and transduction and the use of a control mechanism, are clearly explained and appear to effectively address the challenges of the task. The contrast with soft attention is also compelling.
3. **Comprehensive Evaluation and Analysis:** The paper provides a thorough evaluation of the model against a range of baselines, including both neural and non-neural approaches. The ablation-style experiments (comparing against soft attention) and the analysis of learned representations contribute significantly to understanding the model's behavior and capabilities. The inclusion of SIGMORPHON provides results on a diverse set of languages.

Weaknesses:

1. **Dependence on Pre-trained Alignments:** The reliance on independently learned character-level alignments for training appears to be a potential limitation. While the authors justify this approach, it introduces an external dependency. The performance is thus partly attributable to the quality of the alignment model (Sudoh et al., 2013) and may not fully reflect the intrinsic capabilities of the hard attention mechanism. A more end-to-end approach, even with modifications to handle monotonicity, could potentially yield better results.
2. **Limited Discussion of Alignment Model:** While the pre-trained alignment model is cited, the paper lacks a detailed discussion of its limitations or potential biases. Understanding how the quality of these alignments impacts the downstream performance is important. Were any alignment errors analyzed? How sensitive is the model to noise in the pre-trained alignments?
3. **Presentation of Results:** The presentation of results in the tables, while comprehensive, could be improved. Adding percentage improvements over baselines would provide a clearer and more intuitive understanding of the model's gains.  Furthermore, statistical significance testing is absent. Establishing statistical significance for the reported improvements would strengthen the claims of superiority.



Questions to Authors:

1. How sensitive is the model’s performance to the quality of the pre-trained alignments? Could you provide some analysis of alignment errors and their impact on the final results?
2. Were any experiments conducted to explore potential end-to-end training strategies, perhaps incorporating a regularization term to encourage monotonicity during training?
3. Can you provide statistical significance testing for the results reported in the tables?

Additional Comments:

* The supplementary material should include details on the implementation of the soft attention baseline for reproducibility.
* The discussion of related work could be expanded to include more recent advancements in sequence transduction and attention mechanisms.
* The connection between the “step” action and the concepts of transition systems is interesting, but could be further elaborated.
