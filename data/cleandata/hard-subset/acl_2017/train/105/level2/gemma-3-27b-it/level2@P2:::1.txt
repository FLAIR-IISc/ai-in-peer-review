This paper presents a novel neural model for morphological inflection generation that leverages a hard attention mechanism to model the nearly-monotonic alignment between input and output character sequences. The model is evaluated on three datasets (CELEX, Wiktionary, and SIGMORPHON2016), achieving state-of-the-art results and demonstrating particularly strong performance on small datasets. The authors also provide an analysis comparing the learned representations of their hard attention model with a soft attention model.

**Contributions:**

1. **Hard Attention Model for Morphological Inflection:** The core contribution is the proposed hard attention model, specifically designed to exploit the inherent monotonic alignment often found in morphological inflection. This is a reasonable and potentially impactful approach given the task characteristics.
2. **State-of-the-Art Results:** The model achieves new state-of-the-art results on three established morphological inflection datasets, showcasing its effectiveness compared to both neural and non-neural baselines. This demonstrates practical benefit and validity of the proposed approach.
3. **Analysis of Learned Representations:** The comparative analysis between the hard and soft attention models, particularly regarding the learned representations, provides valuable insights into how each model approaches the task and what features they encode. This helps to understand the strengths of the proposed method.

**Strengths:**

1. **Strong Empirical Results:** The paper convincingly demonstrates the superiority of the proposed model on the CELEX dataset, a traditionally challenging benchmark due to its small size. Outperforming the latent variable WFST model on this dataset is a significant achievement, as it highlights the model's ability to learn effectively from limited data.
2. **Well-Motivated Design:** The hard attention mechanism is well-motivated by the observation of nearly-monotonic alignment in morphological inflection. The authors clearly articulate the intuition behind the design choice and how it addresses limitations of previous approaches.
3. **Comprehensive Evaluation:** The model is evaluated on a diverse set of datasets, including varying language families and morphological complexities (as seen in SIGMORPHON2016). This lends confidence to the generalizability of the approach.
4. **Clear and Detailed Explanation:** The paper provides a clear and detailed explanation of the model architecture, training procedure, and experimental setup. The inclusion of the network architecture diagram (Figure 1) is helpful for understanding the model’s components.
5. **Insightful Analysis:** The analysis of learned representations, although not extremely detailed, offers a valuable comparison between the hard and soft attention models and sheds light on how each model captures positional and character-level information.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Novelty of Hard Attention:** While the application of hard attention to morphological inflection is a reasonable idea, the hard attention mechanism itself is not novel. The core innovation lies in *how* it's applied to this specific problem, but the paper could benefit from a more nuanced discussion of existing hard attention mechanisms and how this approach differs.
2. **Dependency on Pre-learned Alignments:** The training procedure relies on independently learned character-level alignments. While this simplifies training, it introduces a dependency on the performance of the alignment model. The robustness of the system to errors in the pre-learned alignments is not thoroughly discussed. What happens when the automatic alignment is wrong? How sensitive is performance to alignment errors?
3. **SIGMORPHON Results – Nuance Needed:** The results on SIGMORPHON2016, while competitive, show that the soft attention model slightly outperforms the hard attention model overall. The authors attribute this to the morphological characteristics of certain languages. This explanation feels somewhat *post-hoc* and would be strengthened by a more in-depth analysis of the types of morphological processes that favor each model.  A more detailed examination of performance on individual languages and morphological features would be useful.
4. **Lack of Ablation Studies:**  The paper would benefit from ablation studies to understand the contribution of different components of the model (e.g., the bi-directional encoder, the recurrent decoder with feedback connection). This would help to pinpoint the most important design choices.

**Questions to Authors:**

1.  How sensitive is the performance of the model to the quality of the pre-learned character-level alignments?  Could you provide results with varying levels of alignment noise or error?
2.  The paper mentions that the soft attention model might be better at capturing long-range dependencies. Could you elaborate on this and potentially provide examples where the soft attention model excels due to its ability to handle non-monotonic relations?
3.  Could you provide further details on the analysis of the learned representations? For instance, could you visualize the representations using t-SNE or other dimensionality reduction techniques and discuss specific features that are encoded?
4.  How does the computational cost of the hard attention mechanism compare to the soft attention mechanism, particularly during inference?

**Additional Comments:**

* The paper refers to a github repository but does not provide a link.
* Consider clarifying the terminology "step action" in the decoder. Is this simply moving the attention pointer forward?
* The introduction could be slightly more concise.
