This paper proposes a neural model for morphological inflection generation that utilizes a hard attention mechanism, motivated by the nearly-monotonic alignment often found in morphological processes. The model is evaluated on three standard datasets (CELEX, Wiktionary, and SIGMORPHON2016) and achieves state-of-the-art results, particularly on smaller datasets where it outperforms previous approaches. The authors also provide an analysis comparing the learned representations of their hard attention model to those of a soft attention model.

**Contributions:**

1. **Novel Hard Attention Mechanism:** The core contribution is the introduction of a hard attention mechanism specifically tailored for morphological inflection, enforcing a near-monotonic alignment between input and output sequences. This is a well-motivated design choice given the nature of morphological processes.
2. **State-of-the-Art Performance:** The model achieves state-of-the-art results on three commonly used datasets for morphological inflection generation, demonstrating its effectiveness in a practical setting. The improvement on the CELEX dataset, specifically surpassing the latent variable WFST model, is a notable achievement.
3. **Analysis of Learned Representations:** The comparative analysis of the learned representations between the hard and soft attention models provides valuable insights into how these different attention mechanisms capture morphological information.

**Strengths:**

1. **Strong Empirical Results:** The consistently strong performance across three diverse datasets is a compelling argument for the effectiveness of the proposed model. The results are well-presented and clearly demonstrate an improvement over previous state-of-the-art methods.
2. **Well-Motivated Design:** The use of hard attention is logically justified by the characteristics of morphological inflection. The authors effectively argue that enforcing a near-monotonic alignment is beneficial for this task.
3. **Thorough Evaluation:** The paper includes a comprehensive evaluation of the model, including comparisons to a variety of baselines and an analysis of the learned representations. The experiments cover datasets of varying sizes and complexities.
4. **Clear and Concise Writing:** The paper is generally well-written and easy to follow. The technical details of the model are clearly explained, and the experimental setup is well-documented.
5. **Ablation via Comparison to Soft Attention:** Comparing to a soft attention baseline is a good way to isolate the effect of the hard attention mechanism. The analysis is insightful and contributes to understanding the strengths of each approach.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Dependence on Pre-learned Alignments:** The model relies on pre-learned character-level alignments for training. While the authors justify this choice, it introduces an external dependency and might limit the model's ability to learn alignments directly from the data. The impact of the quality of these pre-learned alignments is not fully explored.
2. **Limited Discussion of Error Analysis:** While the paper presents overall performance metrics, a more detailed error analysis would be beneficial. Understanding the types of errors the model makes could provide insights for future improvements. For example, are there specific morphological processes where the model struggles?
3. **SIGMORPHON Results Nuance:** The authors state their model is slightly less accurate than their soft attention implementation on SIGMORPHON. While they explain this in terms of language typology, a more in-depth investigation of *which* languages contribute to this difference could be insightful.
4. **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** The paper doesn't discuss the sensitivity of the model to different hyperparameter settings. Providing some information about this would increase the reproducibility and robustness of the findings.



**Questions to Authors:**

1. How sensitive is the model’s performance to the quality of the pre-learned alignments? Did you experiment with different alignment tools or methods for generating the alignments, and if so, what was the impact on performance?
2. Can you provide a more detailed error analysis, perhaps focusing on specific types of morphological errors that the model frequently makes?
3. For the SIGMORPHON dataset, can you specify which languages contribute most to the performance difference between the hard and soft attention models?
4. What is the computational cost associated with the hard attention mechanism compared to soft attention? Is there a significant difference in training or inference time?

**Additional Comments:**

*  The reference list is extensive and well-formatted.
*  The discussion of related work is comprehensive and provides good context for the proposed model.
*  Consider making the source code publicly available (if not already done) to facilitate reproducibility and further research.
* In the introduction, it would be helpful to provide a more intuitive explanation of *why* a hard attention mechanism is expected to be better suited for morphological inflection. A simple example illustrating the monotonic alignment would be beneficial.

Overall, this is a strong paper that presents a novel and effective approach to morphological inflection generation. The authors have made a significant contribution to the field, and the paper is well-written and thoroughly evaluated. Addressing the weaknesses mentioned above would further strengthen the paper and increase its impact. I recommend acceptance.
