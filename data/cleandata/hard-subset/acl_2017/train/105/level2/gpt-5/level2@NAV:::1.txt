Summary and contributions
The paper proposes a sequence-to-sequence model for morphological inflection that uses a hard, nearly monotonic attention implemented as step and write actions trained from external character alignments. Across CELEX, Wiktionary, and SIGMORPHON-2016, the model achieves strong results, notably outperforming prior neural and non-neural systems in low-resource settings. The paper also analyzes alignments and encoder representations for both hard and soft attention models.

Contributions (as I see them; strongest first):
1) A simple, externally-aligned hard monotonic attention transducer that trains with cross-entropy and generalizes well in low-resource regimes.
2) Comprehensive empirical evaluation showing new SOTA on CELEX and competitive or best results on several Wiktionary settings and suffixing languages in SIGMORPHON.
3) Comparative analysis of learned alignments/representations that explains when hard vs soft attention is advantageous.

Strengths
- Strong low-resource performance and generalization: On CELEX, the model achieves the best average accuracy (Table 1), surpassing Dreyer et al.’s latent-variable WFST and recent neural baselines. Learning curves (Fig. 2) substantiate reduced overfitting relative to soft attention.
- Broad, careful evaluation: Results span three standard benchmarks, multiple languages and typologies, and include competitive neural and non-neural baselines (Faruqui, MED, Yu, NWFST), with consistent gains on German verbs, Finnish nouns/adjectives, and Dutch verbs (Table 2).
- Simple, interpretable control mechanism: The step/write transition system with externally provided alignments yields stable training via cross-entropy, clearer alignment behavior (Fig. 3), and practical utility for tasks with monotonic alignments.
- Insightful analysis: SVD projections (Fig. 4) and alignment visualizations illuminate why hard attention overfits less (positional info handled by control rather than encoder), lending credibility to the modeling choices.

Weaknesses
- Limited generality across typologies: On languages with strong non-local dependencies (e.g., vowel/consonant harmony), soft attention slightly outperforms (Table 3), indicating the hard monotonic assumption can be restrictive.
- Dependence on external aligner: No ablations on aligner choice/quality or robustness to alignment errors; comparisons to joint training/marginalization (e.g., CTC/RNN-T, Yu et al.) lack a controlled study of the alignment signal.
- Best-derivation approximation and decoding details: The paper does not clarify search (greedy vs beam) or quantify the impact of not marginalizing over derivations on accuracy/runtime.
- Statistical and fairness concerns: No significance tests; some baselines (e.g., MED trained for 20 epochs vs 100 here) may be under-optimized, potentially inflating comparative gains.
- Reproducibility details are pushed to supplementary; core hyperparameters and training settings could be summarized more fully in the main text.

Questions to authors
- How sensitive are results to the alignment model and its hyperparameters? Please provide an ablation (different aligners or noisy alignments).
- What decoding strategy was used (beam size, runtime)? Does beam search materially change accuracy?
- Could a hybrid with limited-context soft attention or learned monotonic attention mitigate the deficits on harmony-heavy languages?
- Are results stable across random seeds, and are improvements statistically significant on CELEX/SIGMORPHON?
