Summary and contributions
This paper proposes a neural model for morphological inflection generation that uses a hard, nearly monotonic attention mechanism. The decoder emits either a WRITE(symbol) action or a STEP action that advances a single attention pointer over a biLSTM-encoded input. Training uses externally induced character alignments to derive gold action sequences, enabling simple cross-entropy training without learning alignments jointly. The model is evaluated on CELEX (low-resource), Wiktionary (large), and SIGMORPHON 2016 (ten languages), showing strong results, and includes an analysis comparing learned alignments and representations to a soft-attention baseline.

As I see them, the main contributions are:
1) A simple and effective hard monotonic attention model trained from external alignments that achieves state-of-the-art or competitive results, particularly in low-resource settings (primary).
2) A thorough empirical study across three standard datasets with diverse typologies, including careful comparisons to neural and non-neural baselines (secondary).
3) An analysis of learned alignments and encoder representations, shedding light on what features (character identity and position) are captured by hard vs. soft attention (tertiary).

Strengths
- Strong accuracy in low-resource conditions with a plausible inductive bias: On CELEX, the hard-attention model achieves the best average (89.44%) and is the first neural approach to surpass the tailored latent-variable WFST (88.3%). Learning curves show less overfitting than soft attention, supporting the claim that a monotonic bias helps generalization with scarce data.
- Competitive or superior results on large datasets: On Wiktionary, the model attains the best overall average (96.55%), with wins on German verbs, Finnish nouns/adjectives, and Dutch verbs. This demonstrates robustness beyond the low-resource regime and an advantage over encoder-decoder without attention and over a joint alignment/transduction neural transducer.
- Clear modeling and training simplicity: Reducing decoding to STEP/WRITE actions and using external alignments allows straightforward cross-entropy training without forward-backward or latent alignment marginalization. The transition-system view (ADVANCE/WRITE) is intuitive and facilitates reproducibility and analysis.
- Insightful qualitative analysis: The paper compares alignments predicted by hard vs. soft attention, showing both tend toward monotonicity, and visualizes encoder representations. The position-sensitive clustering differences between models are interesting and help explain generalization differences.
- Broad and fair evaluation: The study covers three established datasets, includes both neural and non-neural baselines (MED, NWFST, LAT, Faruqui et al., Yu et al.), and provides typology-aware interpretation of results on SIGMORPHON (hard attention excels for suffixing + stem-change languages).

Weaknesses
- Reliance on external alignments and best-derivation decoding: The approach depends on an external character aligner (CRP-based), and training optimizes the best-derivation objective (Eq. 2) rather than marginalizing over derivations that yield the same output (Eq. 1). While effective in practice, this introduces potential brittleness to alignment noise and a train-test mismatch. An ablation showing sensitivity to alignment quality (e.g., alternative aligners or noise) and a comparison to marginalization/beam search over derivations would strengthen the claim.
- Limited handling of non-monotonic or long-distance phenomena: Performance lags soft attention on several SIGMORPHON languages with vowel/consonant harmony, templatic morphology, or circumfixing. The model’s strictly forward pointer with multiple writes per position is elegant but may be insufficient where non-local dependencies dictate output. Discussing extensions (e.g., limited backward moves, segmental decisions, or explicit harmony features) or reporting hybrid results would be valuable.
- Incomplete methodological details and ablations: Decoding details (greedy vs. beam, beam size, termination conditions, handling of end-of-input and deletions) are not fully specified. The role of feature embeddings (size, ablation without features) and model capacity vs. soft attention are not systematically explored. Parameter count and runtime comparisons (training/inference) would help practitioners.
- Fairness/consistency of baselines: The paper notes differences in training schedules (e.g., 100 epochs for the soft baseline vs. 20 in MED) and that code is anonymized. While this is common, additional care to align hyperparameters or provide a single unified soft-attention implementation trained under the same protocol would reduce confounds. Statistical significance testing on key improvements (CELEX averages, Wiktionary averages) is also missing.

Questions to authors
- How is decoding performed at test time (greedy vs. beam)? If beam is used, what size, and how sensitive are results to beam width?
- What is the termination criterion for decoding? How do you prevent stepping past the end of the input, and how are deletions at the end handled?
- How sensitive is the model to the choice/quality of the external aligner? Can you report results with an alternative aligner or with noisy alignments?
- Have you tried marginalizing or approximating over derivations that yield the same string (e.g., lattice rescoring over STEP/WRITE) to bridge the Eq. 1 vs. Eq. 2 gap?
- Can you provide ablations for (a) removing morpho-syntactic feature embeddings, (b) varying encoder/decoder sizes, and (c) removing the si−1 feedback?
- On SIGMORPHON languages where soft attention outperforms hard, what are the dominant error types? Are they attributable to harmony or templatic constraints?
- What are the typical action sequence lengths relative to output length (e.g., ratio of steps to writes), and do these correlate with error rates or typological properties?

Overall recommendation
This is a solid and timely contribution with a simple, well-motivated model, strong empirical results—especially in low-resource settings—and thoughtful analysis. Addressing the noted concerns (alignment dependence, decoding details, ablations, and significance) would further strengthen the paper. I lean toward acceptance.
