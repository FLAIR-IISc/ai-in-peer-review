Summary and contributions
This paper proposes a neural encoder–decoder model for morphological inflection generation with a hard monotonic attention mechanism. The decoder emits WRITE(symbol) or STEP actions over a biLSTM-encoded input, guided by externally learned character alignments; training reduces to cross-entropy over oracle action sequences. The model is evaluated on CELEX (low-resource), Wiktionary (large), and SIGMORPHON 2016 (multilingual). Analyses compare alignments and encoder representations w.r.t. a soft-attention baseline.

Contributions (as I see them), in order of significance:
1) A simple, effective hard-attention transduction model that leverages monotonic alignments and external alignment supervision, achieving strong results, especially in low-resource settings where prior neural models underperform.
2) Comprehensive empirical evaluation across three standard benchmarks with clear gains on CELEX and competitive or best average performance on Wiktionary; nuanced results across typologically diverse languages in SIGMORPHON 2016.
3) Qualitative analyses of learned alignments and representations, contrasting hard vs. soft attention and offering insights into positional and symbol-type encoding.

Strengths
- Strong performance in low-resource setting with clear motivation: On CELEX the model outperforms prior neural approaches and surpasses Dreyer et al.’s latent-variable WFST, a notable result given past neural underperformance on this dataset. The learning-curve analysis supports the claim that hard monotonic control regularizes effectively under data scarcity.

- Conceptual clarity and simplicity: Framing inflection generation as a monotonic transition system with STEP/WRITE is natural for this task and aligns well with classic FST intuitions while retaining neural flexibility. Training via external alignments and cross-entropy is straightforward, avoiding forward–backward complexity and improving stability.

- Broad and fair empirical coverage: The paper evaluates against strong neural and non-neural baselines on CELEX, multiple Wiktionary language/pos sets, and SIGMORPHON 2016. The results show robustness: best or on par in several conditions, and competitive averages on large-scale data.

- Insightful analysis: The alignment visualizations show both models learn mostly monotonic alignments; the hard model reflects linguistically plausible one-to-many and deletion operations. The representation study suggests different encoding emphases (positional vs. symbol identity), plausibly explaining generalization differences.

Weaknesses
- Reliance on external alignments and best-derivation approximation: Training depends on a CRP-based character aligner and optimizes the best-derivation rather than marginalizing over derivations that yield the same string. While effective empirically, this introduces potential error propagation from misalignments and a train–test objective mismatch. The paper acknowledges this but does not quantify sensitivity to alignment quality or compare with marginalization-based training under controlled conditions.

- Incomplete fairness and rigor in comparisons: For SIGMORPHON, the authors’ soft-attention implementation outperforms MED and the paper attributes this to longer training. This undermines fairness in claiming relative advantages: either re-train MED comparably or report both configurations for both models. Also, there is no report of statistical significance testing, and Wiktionary comparisons mix systems of different capacities without standardizing preprocessing or hyperparameter tuning.

- Limited generality beyond monotonic settings: Results degrade relative to soft attention on languages with non-concatenative or long-distance phenomena (templatic Arabic, harmony). The model design is inherently monotonic, so the approach may be less suitable for broader morphological typologies or tasks with reorderings. Suggestions for hybridization (e.g., local non-monotonic moves or learned jump actions) are not explored.

- Evaluation breadth and search details: The paper reports exact-match accuracy only; edit distance or paradigm-level metrics could shed light on near-miss behavior and error profiles. Decoding strategy and search (greedy vs. beam) are not detailed; since STEP/WRITE sequences can be long, search could matter. Efficiency claims (if any) are not quantified (training speed, memory), which would be valuable given the model’s simplicity.

Questions to authors
- How sensitive is performance to alignment errors? Can you report an ablation with weaker/perturbed alignments, or compare with joint training that refines alignments during learning?

- What decoding strategy is used (greedy/beam)? Does beam search improve accuracy, particularly on languages with more complex morphophonology?

- Can you provide edit-distance metrics or error typologies (e.g., suffix errors vs. stem changes vs. deletions), especially on SIGMORPHON languages where the hard model underperforms?

- On SIGMORPHON, could you re-run MED or your soft baseline with the same training regimen (epochs, early stopping) to ensure a fully controlled comparison? Likewise, on Wiktionary, were hyperparameters tuned per language?

- Do you anticipate gains from marginalizing over derivations (e.g., via dynamic programming) or from training with expected risk? Any preliminary results?

Additional comments and suggestions
- Consider a hybrid controller: primarily monotonic (STEP/WRITE) with a limited set of learned non-monotone actions (e.g., JUMP-k or COPY-block) to better handle templatic or harmony-driven systems.

- Provide runtime and parameter counts to demonstrate the simplicity advantage quantitatively.

- Include an ablation of feature embeddings (morphosyntactic tags) and the explicit previous-symbol feedback to clarify their contributions.

- Discuss applicability to related tasks (transliteration, G2P) with evidence or small-scale experiments, since these also exhibit near-monotonic alignments.

- Minor: clarify preprocessing (character normalization, diacritics handling), and release the alignment scripts and hyperparameters to aid reproducibility.

Overall, this is a well-motivated and empirically solid paper. Despite some concerns about reliance on external alignments and comparison fairness, the contribution—showing that a simple hard monotonic controller trained on external alignments can excel in inflection generation, especially with limited data—is significant and timely. I lean toward acceptance after addressing fairness and analysis concerns.
