- Proposes a neural morphological inflection model using hard, nearly monotonic attention with STEP/WRITE actions and biLSTM encoder.  
- Trains from externally induced character alignments to produce gold action sequences, allowing simple cross-entropy training without learning alignments.  
- Evaluates on CELEX (low-resource), Wiktionary (large), and SIGMORPHON 2016 (ten languages) and analyzes comparisons to a soft-attention baseline.  

Contributions:
- Simple, effective hard monotonic attention model trained from external alignments achieving SOTA or competitive results, especially in low-resource settings.  
- Thorough empirical study across three datasets with diverse typologies and careful comparisons to neural and non-neural baselines.  
- Analysis of learned alignments and encoder representations showing differences in character identity and position sensitivity between hard and soft attention.  

Strengths:
- Strong low-resource accuracy (CELEX best average 89.44%), showing less overfitting than soft attention due to monotonic inductive bias.  
- Competitive or superior performance on large datasets (Wiktionary average 96.55%) with wins on several language/pos subsets.  
- Modeling and training simplicity via STEP/WRITE actions and external alignments enabling straightforward cross-entropy optimization.  
- Insightful qualitative analysis comparing hard vs. soft alignment monotonicity and encoder representation clustering.  
- Broad and fair evaluation including multiple baselines and typology-aware interpretation (hard attention excels for suffixing + stem-change languages).  

Weaknesses:
- Reliance on an external character aligner and best-derivation training (Eq. 2) introduces potential brittleness to alignment noise and train-test mismatch.  
- Limited handling of non-monotonic or long-distance phenomena (vowel/consonant harmony, templatic morphology, circumfixing) where soft attention sometimes wins.  
- Missing methodological/ablation details: decoding specifics, feature embedding roles, model capacity comparisons, parameter counts, and runtime.  
- Baseline fairness concerns: differing training schedules, anonymized code, and lack of statistical significance testing on key improvements.  

Questions for authors:
- Is test-time decoding greedy or beam search; if beam, what size and sensitivity to beam width?  
- What is the decoding termination criterion; how are steps past input and end-of-input deletions handled?  
- How sensitive are results to the choice/quality of the external aligner; any alternative-aligner or noisy-alignment experiments?  
- Have you tried marginalizing/approximating over derivations that yield the same string (to address Eq. 1 vs. Eq. 2)?  
- Can you provide ablations for removing morpho-syntactic feature embeddings, varying encoder/decoder sizes, and removing si−1 feedback?  
- For SIGMORPHON languages where soft attention outperforms hard, what are the dominant error types and are they due to harmony/templatic effects?  
- What are typical action sequence lengths relative to output length (steps:writes ratio), and do they correlate with error rates or typology?  

Overall recommendation:
- Solid, timely contribution with strong empirical results and analysis; leans toward acceptance pending clarifications on alignment dependence, decoding details, ablations, and significance testing.
