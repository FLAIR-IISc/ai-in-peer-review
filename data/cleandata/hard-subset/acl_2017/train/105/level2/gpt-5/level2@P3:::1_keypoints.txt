- Proposes a neural encoder–decoder for morphological inflection with hard, nearly-monotonic attention using explicit step/write control and oracle action sequences from external alignments.
- Contribution: simple, effective hard monotonic attention architecture that achieves strong performance, especially in low-resource settings (CELEX improvements).
- Contribution: shows externally supervised hard alignment can outperform soft attention and WFST-style models across datasets (Wiktionary, SIGMORPHON).
- Contribution: comparative analysis indicating both hard and soft attention learn monotonic alignments but exhibit different inductive biases in encoder representations.
- Strength: strong low-resource performance with reduced overfitting thanks to monotonic inductive bias (evidence from CELEX and learning curves).
- Strength: competitive results across datasets and typologies, best reported average on Wiktionary and strong performance on monotonic languages in SIGMORPHON.
- Strength: conceptually simple training via step/write actions and external alignments enabling cross-entropy training instead of marginalization.
- Strength: informative analyses (alignment visualizations, SVD projections) showing encoding of character identity and position and differing reliance on positional info.
- Strength: clear framing and relevance for morphology in MT, evaluated on multiple languages and datasets.
- Weakness: relies on external alignments without robustness analysis or comparison of aligners; sensitivity to alignment quality not quantified.
- Weakness: trains on a single oracle derivation without marginalizing equivalent derivations; effect of this approximation is unmeasured.
- Weakness: insufficient decoding/search details (greedy vs. beam, termination, impact on accuracy/latency).
- Weakness: limited ablations—bundled design choices (biLSTM, tag embeddings, prev-output feedback, hard control) not individually evaluated.
- Weakness: fairness concerns in baseline comparisons and training-budget control, and limited exploration of non-monotonic phenomena where performance drops.
- Question: is inference greedy or beam-based, what beam sizes were tried, and how does beam size affect accuracy and speed?
- Question: how sensitive are results to the choice or noise in the external aligner?
- Question: how is the oracle chosen when multiple monotonic derivations exist, and have multiple/randomized oracles been tried?
- Question: are training budgets and hyperparameters matched between hard and soft attention baselines in SIGMORPHON comparisons?
- Question: what are training/inference times and memory usage compared to soft attention and WFST models?
- Suggestion (essential): clarify decoding/search/termination, report accuracy vs. beam size, and add small ablations (hard vs. soft under identical training, remove prev-output feedback, alignment perturbation checks).
- Suggestion (optional): explore lightweight non-monotonic extensions (e.g., limited jump-back) and compare alternate alignment sources or hybrid fine-tuning of alignments.
- Overall: a well-motivated, simple, and effective hard-attention transduction model with strong empirical results; needs clearer decoding details, modest ablations, and alignment robustness checks to strengthen the contribution.
