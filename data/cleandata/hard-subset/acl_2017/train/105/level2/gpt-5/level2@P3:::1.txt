Summary and contributions
This paper proposes a neural encoder–decoder model for morphological inflection generation that uses a hard, nearly-monotonic attention mechanism with an explicit step/write control. Training leverages externally induced character alignments to derive oracle action sequences, enabling straightforward cross-entropy training without marginalizing over alignments. The model is evaluated on CELEX, Wiktionary, and SIGMORPHON 2016, and includes an analysis comparing hard vs. soft attention representations and alignments.

Perceived contributions (in order of significance):
1) A simple and effective hard monotonic attention architecture for character-level transduction that achieves strong performance, especially in low-resource settings (e.g., CELEX average 89.44 vs. 88.3 for LAT and 87.62–87.65 for recent neural baselines).
2) An empirical demonstration that externally supervised hard alignment can outperform soft attention and WFST-style models across multiple datasets, including competitive or state-of-the-art results on Wiktionary (average 96.55) and targeted strengths on suffixing + stem-change languages in SIGMORPHON 2016.
3) A comparative analysis of learned alignments and encoder representations showing that both hard and soft attention learn monotonic alignments and encode character identity and positional information, with distinct inductive biases.

Strengths
- Strong low-resource performance with clear evidence: On CELEX, the model surpasses both soft-attention and WFST-based neural/non-neural baselines (Avg. 89.44 vs. 88.3 for Dreyer et al. 2008 LAT; vs. 87.6–87.7 for MED/NWFST). The learning-curve analysis indicates reduced overfitting relative to soft attention, aligning with the model’s monotonic inductive bias.
- Competitive breadth across datasets and typologies: On Wiktionary, the approach attains the best reported average (96.55), outperforming prior neural (Faruqui et al. 2016; Yu et al. 2016) and non-neural systems. On SIGMORPHON 2016, it excels on languages whose morphology is close to the monotonic assumption (Russian, German, Spanish), and remains competitive elsewhere.
- Conceptual simplicity with effective supervision: Reducing decoding to step/write actions with a single attention pointer is elegant and practical. Using external character alignments to derive oracle action sequences simplifies training (cross-entropy vs. forward–backward) and appears advantageous compared to joint-alignment models (Yu et al., 2016), as supported by Wiktionary results.
- Informative analysis: The alignment visualizations show that soft attention also tends to learn monotonic behaviors on this task, supporting the modeling assumption. The SVD projection analyses clarify that both models encode character identity and position, with hard attention relying less on positional encoding—consistent with explicit step control and potentially explaining improved data efficiency.
- Clear task framing and relevance: The work addresses a core component for handling morphology in MT and related tasks, with evidence across ten languages and multiple datasets.

Weaknesses
- Reliance on external alignments without robustness analysis: The approach depends on CRP-based alignments (Sudoh et al., 2013) or shared-task alignments. The paper does not quantify sensitivity to alignment quality or compare aligners. An ablation testing alignment noise or alternative aligners would strengthen the claim that external supervision is a key advantage.
- Best-derivation approximation and decoding details: The training objective optimizes a single oracle derivation; alternative equivalent derivations are not marginalized. The effect of this approximation on accuracy is not measured. Inference/search details (greedy vs. beam, termination conditions) and their impact on accuracy/latency are insufficiently specified.
- Limited ablations: The model bundles several design choices (biLSTM encoder, tag embeddings, previous-output feedback, hard step control). There is no ablation to quantify the contributions of: (a) hard attention vs. soft on identical training regimes, (b) using external alignments vs. learned alignments, (c) conditioning on previous outputs or tag embeddings.
- Fairness of training protocol comparisons: The discussion notes that the soft attention baseline may have been undertrained in prior work (e.g., MED trained 20 epochs vs. 100 here). While the paper includes its own soft-attention implementation on SIGMORPHON, more systematic control of training budgets and early stopping across baselines would improve fairness.
- Coverage of non-monotonic phenomena: Performance drops relative to soft attention on languages with vowel/consonant harmony or templatic morphology (e.g., Turkish, Arabic, Navajo). The limitations and potential adaptations (e.g., limited non-monotonic moves, lookahead) are acknowledged in results discussion but not explored experimentally.

Questions to authors (for rebuttal)
- Decoding: Is inference greedy or beam-based? What beam sizes were tried, and what is the effect on accuracy and speed? How is termination handled, especially once the pointer reaches the final input position?
- Robustness to alignment quality: How sensitive is performance to the external aligner? Would alignments from a different tool or with injected noise materially affect results?
- Oracle derivation choice: When multiple monotonic derivations exist, how is the oracle selected? Has training with multiple or randomized oracles been tried, and if so, what is the impact?
- SIGMORPHON soft-attention baseline: Are training budgets and hyperparameters matched between the hard and soft attention models in that comparison (epochs, early stopping, regularization)?
- Computational profile: What are training/inference times compared to soft attention and to WFST models? Does hard attention offer practical speed or memory advantages at test time?

Suggestions for improvement
Essential (feasible within rebuttal/camera-ready):
- Clarify decoding/search, termination, and algorithmic complexity. Report accuracy vs. beam size trade-offs if applicable.
- Add small ablations: (a) hard vs. soft attention under identical training protocols on one dataset; (b) effect of removing previous-output feedback; (c) brief robustness check to alignment perturbations on a subset.
- Provide statistical significance over folds for CELEX and macro-averaged comparisons for Wiktionary/SIGMORPHON.

Optional (nice-to-have):
- Explore lightweight extensions for limited non-monotonicity (e.g., a limited “jump-back” action) and report on a language with harmony/templatic patterns.
- Compare alternate alignment sources or jointly fine-tune alignments after initialization to test whether a hybrid supervision strategy helps.

Overall assessment
The work presents a well-motivated, simple, and effective hard-attention transduction model with strong empirical results, notably in low-resource settings, and an insightful analysis. With added clarity on decoding, modest ablations, and a brief robustness check to alignment quality, this would be a solid contribution to ACL.
