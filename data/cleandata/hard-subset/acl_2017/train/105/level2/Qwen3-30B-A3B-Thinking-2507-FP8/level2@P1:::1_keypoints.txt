- Summary: Proposes a hard-attention neural model for nearly-monotonic morphological inflection; evaluated on CELEX, Wiktionary, SIGMORPHON2016 with SOTA results, especially on small CELEX; includes analysis vs soft attention.

- Contribution: Introduces a hard attention mechanism tailored for nearly-monotonic seq2seq learning in morphological inflection.

- Contribution: Reports state-of-the-art performance on three inflection datasets, with notable gains on small-data CELEX.

- Contribution: Analyzes learned representations, showing hard vs soft attention differences in encoding positional information.

- Strength: Well-motivated approach that leverages monotonic alignment, demonstrating robustness on low-resource data.

- Strength: Strong representational analysis with clear visualizations illustrating positional encoding differences.

- Strength: Clear, well-structured paper with thorough comparisons and appropriate experimental design.

- Weakness: Insufficiently clarifies novelty relative to prior monotonic seq2seq work (e.g., RNN Transducer, Neural Transducer).

- Weakness: Lacks detailed explanation or evidence for why hard attention improves performance beyond enforcing monotonicity.

- Weakness: Dependence on precomputed character-level alignments is not examined for sensitivity or robustness to imperfect alignments.

- Question: How sensitive is performance to the quality of character-level alignments and to different/noisy alignment algorithms?

- Question: Can you provide concrete alignment examples from the hard-attention vs soft-attention models on CELEX, showing differing choices?

- Question: Why does the model excel on suffixing+stem-change languages but underperform on languages with vowel/consonant harmony; which linguistic features affect applicability?
