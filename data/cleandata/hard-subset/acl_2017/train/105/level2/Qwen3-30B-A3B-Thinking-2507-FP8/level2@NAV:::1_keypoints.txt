- Summary: Proposes a neural morphological inflection model with hard attention for nearly-monotonic character alignments; evaluated on CELEX, Wiktionary, SIGMORPHON2016 and claims state-of-the-art, especially on small data.

Main Contributions:
- Introduces a hard attention mechanism that explicitly models monotonic alignments, improving performance on small datasets.
- Provides comprehensive empirical evaluation showing superiority over prior neural and non-neural methods, notably surpassing prior SOTA on CELEX.
- Analyzes learned alignments and representations to shed light on attention behavior for morphological inflection.

Strengths:
- Strong performance on the small CELEX dataset demonstrates effectiveness under data scarcity.
- Comparative analysis of hard vs. soft attention yields useful linguistic and modeling insights.
- Thorough empirical validation across multiple datasets and languages with clear baseline comparisons.

Weaknesses:
- Insufficient explanation for why hard attention outperforms others on monotonic morphological systems; lacks deeper linguistic justification.
- Comparison to the neural WFST model (Rastogi et al., 2016) is underdeveloped and lacks detail.
- Limitations section is brief and does not adequately address performance on highly non-monotonic morphological languages.

Questions to Authors:
- Please elaborate on the character-level alignment training process, especially handling ambiguous alignments in CELEX.
- How would the model perform on languages with highly irregular or non-monotonic morphology?
- What are the training and inference computational costs compared to soft attention models for larger datasets?
