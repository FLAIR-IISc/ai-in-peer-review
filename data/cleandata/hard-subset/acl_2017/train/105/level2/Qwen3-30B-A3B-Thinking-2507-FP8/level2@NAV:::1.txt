

Summary: This paper proposes a neural model for morphological inflection generation that employs a hard attention mechanism to explicitly model nearly-monotonic character alignments between input and output sequences. The authors evaluate their model on three standard datasets (CELEX, Wiktionary, SIGMORPHON2016) and claim it achieves state-of-the-art results, particularly with small training sets.

Main Contributions (as I see them):
1. A hard attention mechanism that explicitly models monotonic alignments, enabling better performance on small datasets where neural models typically struggle.
2. Comprehensive evaluation demonstrating the model's superiority over previous neural and non-neural approaches, especially on the CELEX dataset where it surpasses the previous state-of-the-art.
3. Analysis of learned alignments and representations that provides valuable insights into how attention mechanisms function for this specific task.

Strengths:
1. The model's performance on the small CELEX dataset is compelling evidence that the approach effectively addresses data scarcity issues that plague previous neural models.
2. The analysis comparing hard and soft attention mechanisms reveals meaningful insights about how these models encode linguistic features, which is valuable for the field.
3. The paper provides thorough empirical validation across multiple datasets and languages, with clear comparisons to relevant baselines.

Weaknesses:
1. The paper doesn't sufficiently explain why the hard attention mechanism works better for monotonic morphological systems beyond simply stating it's "inspired by nearly-monotonic alignment." More linguistic analysis would strengthen this claim.
2. The comparison to the neural WFST model (Rastogi et al., 2016) is underdeveloped - the authors state it "suffered from the same issue" on CELEX but don't provide sufficient detail on the comparison.
3. The limitations section is too brief; the paper doesn't thoroughly discuss how the model would perform on languages with highly non-monotonic morphology beyond the brief mention in the SIGMORPHON results.

Questions to Authors:
1. Could you elaborate on the character-level alignment process used for training, especially regarding how it handles ambiguous alignments in the small CELEX dataset?
2. How would the model perform on languages with highly irregular morphology where the monotonic assumption breaks down completely?
3. What are the computational costs of training and inference compared to soft attention models, particularly for larger datasets?
