- Proposes a neural morphological inflection model with hard attention for nearly-monotonic character alignments, achieving SOTA on three datasets and strong CELEX performance.  
- Main contribution: a hard-attention model that leverages monotonic alignments and outperforms prior neural approaches on small datasets.  
- Main contribution: thorough evaluation across three established datasets, establishing new SOTA on CELEX and robustness on larger datasets.  
- Main contribution: analysis of alignments and learned representations, offering insights into how the model captures morphology.  
- Strength: Compelling evidence that hard attention helps on small-data settings, with CELEX results surpassing a latent-variable WFST using less data.  
- Strength: Alignment and representation analyses reveal that hard-attention encodings rely less on position information, explaining robustness to scarce data.  
- Strength: Demonstrates versatility across languages and phenomena, excelling on suffixing+stem-change languages.  
- Weakness: No thorough comparison of computational complexity or training efficiency versus soft-attention baselines.  
- Weakness: Insufficient analysis of why the model outperforms prior neural models on CELEX beyond the monotonic-alignment claim.  
- Weakness: Representation analysis lacks deeper linguistic interpretation linking encoding differences to specific morphological phenomena.  
- Weakness: Claims to be the first neural model to beat the WFST on CELEX but does not explain why previous neural models failed while this one succeeds.  
- Weakness: Does not adequately address limitations—performs worse on vowel-harmony languages and lacks exploration of mitigations or extensions.  
- Question: How do training time and computational resources for the hard-attention model compare to soft-attention models on the same hardware?  
- Question: Why does the model perform better on suffixing morphology but worse on vowel harmony, and can it be adapted to handle such cases?  
- Question: Can the authors provide more linguistic interpretation of the position-encoding differences observed in Figure 4?
