

Summary:
The paper proposes a neural model for morphological inflection generation that employs a hard attention mechanism designed to model the nearly-monotonic alignment between input and output character sequences. The authors evaluate their model on three datasets (CELEX, Wiktionary, and SIGMORPHON2016), demonstrating state-of-the-art results, particularly on the small CELEX dataset where their model outperforms previous neural and non-neural approaches. They also provide an analysis of the representations learned by their model compared to soft attention models.

Main Contributions:
1. A hard attention mechanism for nearly-monotonic sequence-to-sequence learning, specifically designed for morphological inflection generation.
2. State-of-the-art results on three morphological inflection datasets, with significant improvements on the small CELEX dataset.
3. An insightful analysis of the representations learned by the hard attention model compared to soft attention models, revealing differences in how they encode positional information.

Strengths:
1. The paper addresses an important practical problem with a well-motivated approach. The nearly-monotonic alignment between input and output character sequences is a key characteristic of morphological inflection in many languages, and the authors correctly identify that modeling this directly could improve performance, especially with limited training data. Their model's ability to outperform previous approaches on the small CELEX dataset is a significant empirical contribution, as it demonstrates robustness to data scarcity - a critical issue in morphological processing for low-resource languages.

2. The analysis of the learned representations is particularly strong. The authors provide clear visualizations of 2D projections of character representations, colored by character identity and position, which effectively illustrate how the hard attention model encodes positional information differently than the soft attention model. This analysis provides valuable theoretical insight into how neural models process morphological information.

3. The paper is well-structured and clearly written, with a logical flow from problem statement to model description to experiments and analysis. The comparisons with previous work are thorough and well-justified, and the experimental design is appropriate for the research questions.

Weaknesses:
1. The novelty of the hard attention mechanism compared to previous work on monotonic sequence-to-sequence models (e.g., RNN Transducer, Neural Transducer) is not sufficiently clarified. The paper mentions these works in the related work section, but doesn't clearly explain how their approach differs or why it's better. This is important because the paper claims to be the first neural model to surpass the latent variable model on CELEX with small training sets.

2. The paper lacks sufficient explanation of why the hard attention mechanism leads to better performance, especially on small datasets. While the authors mention that the hard attention model "enforces the monotonic assumption" while soft attention doesn't, this is not sufficiently supported with evidence beyond the performance numbers. A more detailed analysis of the alignments learned by the two models would strengthen this claim.

3. The reliance on independently learned character-level alignments for training is a potential limitation that isn't adequately addressed. The authors don't discuss how sensitive their model is to the quality of these alignments or how they would handle cases where alignment might be imperfect. This could be a significant limitation for real-world applications where alignment might not be perfect.

Questions to Authors:
1. How sensitive is your model to the quality of the character-level alignments used for training? Have you tested the impact of using different alignment algorithms or imperfect alignments on model performance?

2. Could you provide more specific examples of the alignments predicted by your hard attention model versus the soft attention model on the CELEX dataset, particularly highlighting cases where the hard attention model makes a different alignment choice?

3. The paper notes that your model performs better on suffixing+stem-change languages but worse on languages with vowel/consonant harmony. Could you elaborate on why this might be the case, and whether there are specific linguistic features that make certain languages more amenable to your approach?
