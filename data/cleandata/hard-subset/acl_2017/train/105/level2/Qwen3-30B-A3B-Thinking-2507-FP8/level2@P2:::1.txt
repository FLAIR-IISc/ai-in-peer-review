

Summary: The paper proposes a hard attention mechanism for morphological inflection generation that directly models the nearly-monotonic alignment between input and output character sequences. The main contributions as I see them are: (1) a hard attention mechanism that explicitly models monotonic alignment for morphological inflection, (2) state-of-the-art results on three morphological inflection datasets, particularly excelling on the small CELEX dataset, and (3) an insightful analysis of representations learned by both hard and soft attention models.

Strengths:
1. The paper addresses a significant limitation in previous neural approaches to morphological inflection: their poor performance on small datasets. The hard attention model significantly outperforms previous neural models on the CELEX dataset (89.44% vs. 87.65% for the next best neural model), surpassing even the specialized latent variable FST model (88.3% vs. 89.44%). This is a substantial contribution for low-resource morphological processing.

2. The analysis of the learned representations provides valuable insights into how neural models encode linguistic features. The SVD visualization showing how hard attention encodes position information differently than soft attention (Figures 4c-4d) is particularly compelling and contributes to understanding the models' behavior.

3. The thorough evaluation across three different datasets (CELEX, Wiktionary, SIGMORPHON) demonstrates the robustness of the approach across varying morphological systems and dataset sizes, with the model achieving state-of-the-art results on two of the three datasets.

Weaknesses:
1. The novelty claim is overstated. The hard attention mechanism builds directly on Graves (2012) and Jaitly et al. (2015), which also use hard alignment with explicit control mechanisms. The paper fails to sufficiently distinguish its approach from these prior works, particularly in the related work section. The key innovation (using precomputed alignments) is not clearly articulated as a novel contribution.

2. The theoretical justification for the monotonic alignment assumption is weak. The paper states it's "commonly found" but provides no evidence of this across different languages or morphological systems. This is crucial for claiming the approach is generally applicable, especially since the results on SIGMORPHON show the model performs worse on languages with non-monotonic morphology (e.g., Hungarian, Navajo).

3. The comparison with soft attention models lacks depth. The paper doesn't include ablation studies to isolate the contribution of the hard attention mechanism versus other aspects of the model (e.g., the bi-directional encoder). The performance gains could be partially due to the different training procedure rather than the hard attention itself.

4. The paper fails to adequately address the limitations of the approach. It acknowledges that the model performs worse on languages with non-monotonic morphology (e.g., vowel harmony) but doesn't discuss how to handle these cases or whether the model could be extended to address them. This is a significant oversight given the paper's claim of being a general approach.

Questions to Authors:
1. The paper claims the hard attention model is the first neural model to surpass the latent variable FST model on CELEX, but the Rastogi et al. (2016) neural WFST model was reported to outperform the latent variable model when given twice as much data. How does your model compare to Rastogi et al.'s when trained on the same amount of data?

2. The paper states that "the sequence-to-sequence models require large training sets in order to perform well" but doesn't provide evidence of this beyond the CELEX results. Could you provide a more comprehensive analysis of how model performance scales with training set size across the different datasets?

3. The analysis shows the hard attention model learns representations that are less sensitive to character position than the soft attention model. How does this affect the model's ability to handle cases where position information is critical (e.g., vowel harmony in Hungarian)?
