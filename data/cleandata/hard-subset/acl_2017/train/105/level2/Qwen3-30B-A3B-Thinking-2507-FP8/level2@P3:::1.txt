

Summary: The paper proposes a neural model for morphological inflection generation that employs a hard attention mechanism designed to capture nearly-monotonic alignments between input and output character sequences. The work demonstrates state-of-the-art results on three morphological inflection datasets, with particular strength on the small CELEX dataset where previous neural models struggled. The paper also provides an analysis of the representations learned by the hard attention model compared to soft attention models.

Main contributions as observed:
1. A hard attention model that explicitly leverages nearly-monotonic alignments found in morphological inflection, showing superior performance on small datasets compared to previous neural approaches.
2. A thorough evaluation across three established datasets, establishing new state-of-the-art results on the CELEX dataset and demonstrating robustness on larger datasets.
3. An analysis of the alignments and representations learned by the model, providing insights into how neural models capture morphological features.

Strengths:
1. The paper makes a strong case for the utility of hard attention in morphological inflection, particularly on small datasets where previous neural models underperformed. The CELEX results (Table 1) are compelling, showing the model surpasses the latent variable WFST model of Dreyer et al. (2008) with significantly less training data, which is a meaningful contribution to the field.
2. The analysis of alignments (Figure 3) and representations (Figure 4) provides valuable insights into how the model operates. The observation that the hard attention model's representations encode position information less strongly than soft attention models is particularly insightful and helps explain the model's robustness to data scarcity.
3. The paper effectively demonstrates the model's versatility across multiple languages and morphological phenomena (Table 3), with strong performance on suffixing+stem-change languages while maintaining reasonable results on other morphological types.

Weaknesses:
1. The paper lacks a thorough comparison of computational complexity and training efficiency between the hard attention model and soft attention baselines. Given the claim that the model is simpler, it would be valuable to understand whether this simplicity translates to faster training or lower resource requirements.
2. The explanation for why the model outperforms previous neural approaches on the CELEX dataset is somewhat superficial. While the monotonic alignment assumption is well-motivated, the paper doesn't provide sufficient analysis of what specific aspects of the model (beyond the attention mechanism) contribute to this improvement on small data.
3. The analysis of the learned representations (Figure 4) is intriguing but lacks deeper linguistic interpretation. The paper notes the models encode position information differently but doesn't explore how this relates to specific morphological phenomena or linguistic theory.
4. The paper claims the model is the first neural model to surpass the latent variable WFST on CELEX, but doesn't provide a clear explanation of why previous neural models failed on this dataset while this one succeeds. The connection between the monotonic alignment assumption and the specific challenges of the CELEX dataset needs further elaboration.
5. The paper doesn't sufficiently address the limitations of the approach. While the model works well for suffixing morphology, the results on languages with vowel harmony (Table 3) show it performs worse than soft attention models, but the paper doesn't explore whether these limitations could be mitigated or whether the approach could be extended to handle more complex morphological phenomena.

Questions to Authors:
1. How does the training time and computational resources required for the hard attention model compare to the soft attention models on the same hardware? The paper mentions the training procedure is simpler but doesn't provide evidence of efficiency gains.
2. The paper notes the model performs better on languages with suffixing morphology but worse on languages with vowel harmony. Could the authors elaborate on why this is the case and whether the model could be adapted to handle these cases more effectively?
3. The analysis in Figure 4 shows different patterns in position encoding between the models. Could the authors provide more linguistic interpretation of what these patterns mean for morphological processing?
