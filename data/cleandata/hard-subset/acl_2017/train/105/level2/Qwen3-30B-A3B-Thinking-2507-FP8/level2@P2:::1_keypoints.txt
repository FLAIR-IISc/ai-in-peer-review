- Proposes a hard attention mechanism that explicitly models nearly-monotonic alignment for morphological inflection generation.  
- Claims state-of-the-art results on three morphological inflection datasets, notably strong on low-resource CELEX.  
- Provides analysis comparing representations learned by hard vs. soft attention models.

Strengths:
- Addresses poor neural performance on small datasets; hard attention outperforms prior neural and latent-variable FST models on CELEX.  
- Analysis (SVD visualization) offers insight into how hard vs. soft attention encode position and other features.  
- Thorough evaluation across CELEX, Wiktionary, and SIGMORPHON showing robustness and SOTA on two datasets.

Weaknesses:
- Novelty is overstated; builds on Graves (2012) and Jaitly et al. (2015) and does not clearly distinguish its contributions (e.g., precomputed alignments).  
- Weak theoretical justification for assuming monotonic alignment; lacks cross-linguistic evidence and struggles on non-monotonic languages.  
- Comparisons with soft attention lack ablations to isolate the effect of hard attention versus other model/training differences.  
- Fails to adequately address limitations or propose extensions for languages with non-monotonic morphology (e.g., vowel harmony).

Questions for authors:
- How does your model compare to Rastogi et al. (2016) when trained on the same amount of data, given their WFST neural model reportedly outperformed the latent-variable model with more data?  
- Can you provide systematic analysis of how performance scales with training set size across datasets to support the claim that seq2seq models need large datasets?  
- Given hard attention representations are less sensitive to character position, how does that impact handling phenomena where position is critical (e.g., Hungarian vowel harmony)?
