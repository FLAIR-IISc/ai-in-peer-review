**Summary of the Paper**

The paper proposes a neural model for morphological inflection generation that employs a hard attention mechanism. The model is designed to handle the nearly-monotonic alignment commonly found between the characters in a word and its inflection. The authors evaluate the model on three previously studied morphological inflection generation datasets and show that it provides state-of-the-art results in various setups compared to previous neural and non-neural approaches.

**Contributions of the Work**

The main contributions of this work are:

1. The authors propose a hard attention model for nearly-monotonic sequence to sequence learning, which is particularly suited for the morphological inflection generation task.
2. They evaluate the model on three previously studied morphological inflection generation datasets and establish a new state-of-the-art on these datasets.
3. They perform an analysis and comparison of their model and the soft-attention model, shedding light on the features such models extract for the inflection generation task.

**Strengths of the Submission**

1. The paper presents a novel approach to morphological inflection generation that outperforms previous state-of-the-art models on three datasets.
2. The authors provide a thorough analysis of the model's performance, including a comparison with the soft-attention model and an examination of the learned alignments and representations.
3. The paper is well-written and easy to follow, with clear explanations of the model and its components.

**Weaknesses of the Submission**

1. The paper relies heavily on the assumption of nearly-monotonic alignment, which may not hold for all languages or morphological phenomena.
2. The authors do not provide a detailed comparison of their model with other recent neural approaches to morphological inflection generation, such as the work of Kann et al. (2016).
3. The paper could benefit from a more in-depth discussion of the implications of the hard attention mechanism for the model's performance and the limitations of this approach.

**Questions to Authors**

1. Can you provide more details on the character level alignment process used to guide the training of the encoder-decoder network?
2. How do you plan to extend the model to handle more complex morphological phenomena, such as vowel harmony or consonant harmony?
3. Can you provide a more detailed comparison of your model with other recent neural approaches to morphological inflection generation?

**Additional Comments**

The paper presents a significant contribution to the field of morphological inflection generation, and the authors' analysis and comparison of the hard attention model with the soft-attention model provide valuable insights into the strengths and limitations of each approach. However, the paper could benefit from a more detailed discussion of the implications of the hard attention mechanism and a more thorough comparison with other recent neural approaches.
