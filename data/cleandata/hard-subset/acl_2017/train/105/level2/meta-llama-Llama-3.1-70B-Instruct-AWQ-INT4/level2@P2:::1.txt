**Summary of the Paper**

This paper presents a neural model for morphological inflection generation that employs a hard attention mechanism, inspired by the nearly-monotonic alignment commonly found between the characters in a word and its inflection. The model is evaluated on three previously studied morphological inflection generation datasets and achieves state-of-the-art results in various setups compared to previous neural and non-neural approaches. The authors also perform an analysis of the continuous representations learned by both the hard and soft attention models for the task, shedding light on the features such models extract.

**Main Contributions**

1. A hard attention model for nearly-monotonic sequence-to-sequence learning, specifically designed for morphological inflection generation.
2. State-of-the-art results on three previously studied morphological inflection generation datasets, outperforming previous neural and non-neural approaches.
3. An analysis of the continuous representations learned by both the hard and soft attention models, providing insights into the features extracted by these models.

**Strengths**

1. **Effective handling of small training sets**: The hard attention model performs significantly better than previous neural models on the small CELEX dataset, demonstrating its ability to handle low-resource settings.
2. **Improved performance on large training sets**: The model also achieves state-of-the-art results on the larger Wiktionary dataset, showcasing its robustness and ability to leverage large amounts of training data.
3. **Insightful analysis of learned representations**: The authors provide a thorough analysis of the continuous representations learned by both the hard and soft attention models, offering valuable insights into the features extracted by these models.

**Weaknesses**

1. **Limited linguistic typology**: The paper primarily focuses on languages with suffixing morphology, and the results may not generalize to languages with more complex morphological phenomena.
2. **Comparison to soft attention model**: While the authors compare their hard attention model to a soft attention model, the soft attention model is not the most recent or advanced version, which may affect the validity of the comparison.
3. **Lack of error analysis**: The paper does not provide a detailed error analysis, which would be helpful in understanding the limitations of the model and identifying areas for improvement.

**Questions to Authors**

1. How do you plan to extend the model to handle more complex morphological phenomena, such as vowel harmony and consonant harmony?
2. Can you provide more details on the character-level alignment process used to derive the gold transduction+control sequences?
3. How do you think the model's performance would be affected if the soft attention model were replaced with a more recent or advanced version?

**Additional Comments**

The paper presents a well-designed and effective model for morphological inflection generation, and the authors provide a thorough analysis of the learned representations. However, there are some limitations and areas for improvement, such as extending the model to handle more complex morphological phenomena and providing a more detailed error analysis. Overall, the paper makes a valuable contribution to the field of natural language processing and morphological inflection generation.
