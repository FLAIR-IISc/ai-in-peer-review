**Summary**

This paper proposes a neural encoder-decoder model for morphological inflection generation that utilizes a hard, monotonic attention mechanism. The core idea is motivated by the observation that character-level transductions in this task are often nearly monotonic. The model formalizes transduction as a sequence of actions: either writing an output symbol or stepping forward to the next input character representation. A key aspect of the proposed method is its training procedure, which decouples alignment from transduction. An external character aligner is first used to generate gold-standard alignments for the training data, which are then converted into deterministic sequences of write/step actions. The model is trained via standard cross-entropy loss to predict these action sequences.

The primary contributions of this work are:
1.  The introduction of a simple yet highly effective hard monotonic attention architecture that achieves state-of-the-art results on several standard benchmarks, most notably in a low-resource setting where previous neural models have underperformed.
2.  A convincing demonstration that incorporating a strong monotonic prior is beneficial for this task, leading to better generalization and reduced overfitting on small datasets compared to more flexible soft-attention models.
3.  An insightful comparative analysis of the representations and alignments learned by both hard and soft attention models, which provides a plausible explanation for the proposed model's strong performance.

**Strengths**

1.  **Strong Empirical Performance, Especially in Low-Resource Setting:** The work presents compelling results across three widely-used datasets. The performance on the small CELEX dataset is particularly noteworthy, as the proposed model is the first neural approach reported to outperform the highly specialized latent-variable WFST model of Dreyer et al. (2008). This result strongly validates the architectural choices for settings with limited training data. The model also remains competitive or superior on larger datasets like Wiktionary and SIGMORPHON, demonstrating its robustness.

2.  **Well-Motivated and Elegant Model Design:** The model's architecture is grounded in a clear linguistic intuition about the task—that inflection generation is largely a monotonic process. The hard attention mechanism, implemented as a choice between `WRITE` and `STEP` actions, is a direct and elegant formalization of this prior. The learning curves in Figure 2 provide strong evidence that this constraint acts as an effective regularizer, preventing the overfitting that affects the more flexible soft-attention baseline.

3.  **Effective and Simple Training Paradigm:** By decoupling the alignment and transduction steps, the work sidesteps the complexities often associated with training hard attention models (e.g., reinforcement learning) or latent alignment models (e.g., marginalization over alignments). Using pre-computed alignments to create a gold action sequence allows for stable and efficient training with a standard cross-entropy loss, providing a strong and unambiguous learning signal from the outset.

4.  **Insightful Analysis:** The analysis in Section 5 significantly strengthens the paper. The qualitative inspection of learned alignments confirms that they are linguistically plausible. More importantly, the SVD analysis of the encoder representations offers a compelling explanation for the model's success. It suggests that by explicitly handling position with the `STEP` action, the hard-attention model frees the encoder from needing to embed positional information as strongly as the soft-attention model, a task that likely requires more data to learn effectively.

**Weaknesses**

1.  **Limited Generality for Non-Monotonic Phenomena:** The model's primary strength—its strict monotonic assumption—is also its main limitation. The results on the SIGMORPHON dataset confirm this, showing that the model is outperformed by soft attention on languages with significant non-monotonic processes like templatic morphology (Arabic) or circumfixing (Georgian). The paper would be more complete with a more in-depth discussion of this limitation. While the current model is clearly effective for a large class of languages, considering potential extensions to handle a controlled degree of non-monotonicity (e.g., by adding a limited `STEP_BACK` or `JUMP` action) would be a valuable direction for future work and worth mentioning.

2.  **Dependence on External Aligner:** The model's performance is contingent on the quality of the alignments produced by the external alignment tool (Sudoh et al., 2013). The work does not investigate how sensitive the final transduction accuracy is to the quality of these pre-computed alignments. An analysis exploring this dependency would strengthen the paper. For instance, how would the model perform if a simpler aligner (e.g., based on Levenshtein distance) were used to generate the oracle? A brief discussion or experiment on this point would help clarify whether the performance gains stem primarily from the novel architecture or from the high quality of the alignment oracle.

3.  **Positioning Relative to Other Hard Attention Training Methods:** The related work section is thorough, but the paper could better position its training approach relative to alternative methods for training hard attention, such as those using reinforcement learning (RL). The proposed method of training on a pre-computed oracle is simpler and likely more stable, but an end-to-end RL approach would remove the dependency on an external aligner. A brief discussion contrasting the trade-offs between these two paradigms for training hard attention models would help to contextualize the work's contribution more clearly.
