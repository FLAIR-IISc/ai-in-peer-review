**Summary of the paper**

This paper introduces a neural encoder-decoder model for morphological inflection generation that utilizes a "hard monotonic attention" mechanism. The motivation stems from the observation that standard sequence-to-sequence models with soft attention tend to overfit in low-resource settings, failing to surpass specialized non-neural models. The proposed model is tailored to the nearly monotonic character alignments common in morphological inflection. It recasts the transduction problem as learning a sequence of actions, specifically `WRITE(character)` and `STEP` (to advance an attention pointer across the input). A key aspect of the method is that it does not jointly learn alignments; instead, it uses an external character aligner to pre-compute alignments for the training data, from which a gold sequence of actions is deterministically derived for training via standard cross-entropy loss. The authors conduct extensive experiments on three benchmarks (CELEX, Wiktionary, SIGMORPHON) and show that their model achieves state-of-the-art results, particularly in the low-resource CELEX setting. The paper concludes with an insightful analysis comparing the learned alignments and internal representations of their hard attention model against a soft attention baseline, suggesting why their method is more data-efficient.

**Contributions**

1.  The primary contribution is the proposal of a simple and effective hard monotonic attention model that incorporates a strong, appropriate inductive bias for morphological inflection. A key element of this contribution is the strategy of decoupling the alignment and transduction steps, using pre-computed alignments to simplify the training process into a straightforward supervised learning problem.
2.  A second significant contribution is the strong and comprehensive empirical validation. The paper establishes a new state-of-the-art on several standard datasets, and crucially, is the first neural model demonstrated to outperform the highly specialized latent-variable WFST model of Dreyer et al. (2008) in the low-data regime of the CELEX dataset, a notable achievement.
3.  The paper also provides a valuable analysis of the model's behavior. By visualizing and comparing the learned representations of the hard and soft attention models, the authors offer a compelling hypothesis for their model's superior data efficiency: the explicit `STEP` action relieves the encoder from needing to implicitly learn positional information, which is a harder task for the more general soft attention model.

**Strengths**

1.  **Well-Motivated and Elegant Model:** The central idea of enforcing a hard monotonic attention is an excellent fit for the problem of morphological inflection. This provides a strong inductive bias that helps the model generalize from small amounts of data. The formulation of the problem as a sequence of `WRITE` and `STEP` actions is intuitive and elegantly implemented within the encoder-decoder framework.
2.  **Impressive Empirical Results:** The experiments are thorough, covering low-resource, high-resource, and typologically diverse settings. The model's performance on the CELEX dataset is a standout result, as it directly addresses a well-known weakness of neural sequence-to-sequence models. Achieving state-of-the-art results across the board demonstrates the robustness and effectiveness of the proposed approach.
3.  **High-Quality Analysis:** The analysis in Section 5 significantly strengthens the paper. It moves beyond simply reporting accuracy scores to investigate *why* the model works. The comparison of learned alignments (Figure 3) and the SVD visualization of encoder states (Figure 4) provide concrete evidence supporting the authors' hypotheses about how the hard and soft attention models differ in their encoding of positional information. This kind of analysis is incredibly valuable to the community.
4.  **Clarity of Presentation:** The paper is exceptionally well-written. The model, experiments, and analysis are described clearly and logically, making the paper easy to understand and appreciate. The inclusion of Figure 1 provides a helpful visual aid for the model's mechanism.

**Weaknesses**

1.  **Reliance on an External Aligner:** The model's performance is dependent on the quality of an external alignment tool used during training. This introduces a potential pipeline dependency and raises questions about the model's robustness. How would the model perform if a high-quality aligner is not available for a given language pair, or if the aligner produces systematic errors? The paper presents the decoupling of alignment and transduction as a strength, but it would be beneficial to discuss the potential downsides and the model's sensitivity to alignment quality.
2.  **Limited Applicability for Non-Monotonic Phenomena:** The model is explicitly designed for tasks with "nearly-monotonic" alignment. While effective for the suffixing and stem-changing languages it excels on, its applicability to languages with more complex, non-concatenative morphology is limited. The results on the SIGMORPHON dataset already suggest this, as the soft-attention model performs better on languages with phenomena like vowel harmony or templatic morphology. A more direct discussion of the model's limitations and expected failure modes would strengthen the paper's claims.
3.  **Framing of SIGMORPHON Results:** The authors report that their own implementation of a soft attention model outperforms their proposed hard attention model on average across the SIGMORPHON languages. While they offer a good explanation for this, it does slightly temper the claim of the hard attention model's general superiority. This finding suggests a trade-off: the hard attention model is better when its strong inductive bias matches the data, while the more flexible soft attention model is better otherwise. This trade-off could be highlighted more explicitly.

**Questions to Authors**

1.  Regarding the reliance on an external aligner: Could you comment on the sensitivity of your model to the quality of the pre-computed alignments? For instance, have you considered ablations where noise is introduced into the gold alignments, or have you tried a different alignment tool to see how performance is affected?
2.  The gold action sequence `s1:q` is inferred deterministically from the character alignment. Are there any ambiguous cases in the alignment-to-action-sequence conversion (e.g., in many-to-many alignments) and, if so, how is a single gold sequence chosen?
3.  Your analysis showing that the soft attention model encodes position more strongly in its representations is very insightful. The learning curves on CELEX (Figure 2) are great evidence for better generalization in the low-data regime. Would it be possible to show similar learning curves on subsets of the larger Wiktionary dataset to further substantiate the claim that your model is more data-efficient in general?
