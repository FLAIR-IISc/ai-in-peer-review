- Uses a neural seq2seq model with hard monotonic attention where the decoder only `write`s or `step`s, trained on gold action sequences from pre-computed character alignments.
- Decouples alignment from transduction by deriving oracle action sequences from an external aligner.
- Empirically shows a constrained hard monotonic model is highly effective for morphological inflection, especially in low-resource settings.
- First neural approach reported to outperform a strong specialized non-neural baseline on the small CELEX dataset.
- Provides a comparative analysis of learned representations and alignments vs. a standard soft attention baseline, yielding insights into positional and character encoding.
- Strong performance in low-data regimes with clear learning-curve evidence of improved generalization.
- Well-motivated inductive bias: near-monotonicity simplifies learning and improves sample efficiency for many languages.
- Architecture is simple and interpretable; `step`/`write` actions produce explicit alignments unlike distributed soft attention weights.
- Main limitation: strict monotonicity reduces applicability to non-concatenative or long-range phenomena (e.g., Arabic, vowel harmony).
- Critical dependency on an external character aligner; the paper does not analyze sensitivity to aligner quality or noisy alignments.
- Question: How robust is the model to the quality of pre-computed alignments and have different aligners or alignment noise been tested?
- Question: Have the authors considered a hybrid model that can dynamically switch between hard monotonic `step` and flexible soft attention?
