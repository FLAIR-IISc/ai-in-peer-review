**Summary of the Paper and Main Contributions**

This paper presents a neural sequence-to-sequence model for morphological inflection generation that uses a hard, monotonic attention mechanism. Instead of learning a soft alignment, the model’s decoder is restricted to two actions at each step: `write` an output symbol or `step` to the next input character. A key aspect of the method is that it decouples alignment from transduction; the model is trained on gold action sequences derived from a pre-computed character alignment of the training data.

The main contributions of this work are:
1.  A strong empirical demonstration that a constrained, hard monotonic attention model is highly effective for morphological inflection, especially in low-resource settings. The model is the first neural approach to outperform a strong, specialized non-neural baseline on the small CELEX dataset.
2.  A compelling analysis comparing the learned representations and alignments of the proposed hard attention model against a standard soft attention baseline, providing valuable insights into how each model encodes positional and character-level information.

**Strengths**

1.  **Excellent Performance in Low-Data Regime:** The model’s strongest feature is its performance on the CELEX dataset. It significantly outperforms prior neural models and the latent-variable WFST, addressing a major weakness of sequence-to-sequence models that tend to overfit on small training sets. The learning curves in Figure 2 provide clear evidence for this improved generalization.
2.  **Well-Motivated Inductive Bias:** The assumption of near-monotonicity is a powerful and appropriate inductive bias for the task of morphological inflection in many languages. By hard-coding this assumption, the model simplifies the learning problem, allowing it to focus on the transduction task itself, which leads to better sample efficiency.
3.  **Simplicity and Interpretability:** The proposed architecture is simpler than models that jointly learn alignments. The `step`/`write` mechanism is intuitive, and the resulting alignments (as shown in Figure 3) are explicit and interpretable, unlike the distributed weights of a soft attention model.

**Weaknesses**

1.  **Limited Applicability to Non-Monotonic Phenomena:** The model's primary strength—its strict monotonicity—is also its main limitation. As the authors acknowledge in their SIGMORPHON results analysis, it underperforms the more flexible soft attention model on languages with non-concatenative morphology (e.g., Arabic) or complex long-range dependencies (e.g., vowel harmony). This narrows the model’s applicability.
2.  **Dependence on External Aligner:** The model's training process is entirely dependent on an external character alignment tool to generate the oracle action sequences. The paper does not investigate the model's sensitivity to the quality of this aligner. Poor or noisy alignments could directly lead to a poorly trained transduction model, making this a critical and un-analyzed dependency.

**Questions to Authors**

1.  How robust is the model to the quality of the pre-computed alignments? Have you experimented with different aligners or simulated the effect of alignment noise on final accuracy?
2.  Given that soft attention excels at non-monotonic phenomena while your model excels in low-resource monotonic settings, have you considered a hybrid approach? For example, a model that could learn to dynamically switch between a hard monotonic `step` and a more flexible soft attention lookup.
