- Summary: Proposes a neural encoder–decoder with hard monotonic attention that outputs WRITE(character) and STEP actions, using external character alignments to train via cross-entropy, achieving strong results on CELEX, Wiktionary, and SIGMORPHON.  
- Contribution 1: Introduces a simple, effective hard monotonic attention model that decouples alignment and transduction by using pre-computed alignments.  
- Contribution 2: Provides strong empirical validation, setting new state-of-the-art results and outperforming a specialized WFST model on low-resource CELEX.  
- Contribution 3: Offers analysis comparing hard vs. soft attention representations, arguing that explicit STEP relieves encoders from learning positional info and improves data efficiency.  
- Strength 1: Model is well-motivated and elegant—monotonic attention and WRITE/STEP formulation match morphological inflection tasks.  
- Strength 2: Thorough experiments across low/high-resource and typologically diverse settings, with standout low-data performance.  
- Strength 3: High-quality analysis (alignment visualizations, SVD of encoder states) supports the proposed explanations for improved efficiency.  
- Strength 4: Clear, well-written presentation with helpful figures (e.g., Figure 1).  
- Weakness 1: Performance depends on an external aligner, introducing pipeline dependency and potential sensitivity to alignment quality.  
- Weakness 2: Model is limited to nearly-monotonic phenomena and may struggle on non-concatenative or complex morphologies.  
- Weakness 3: SIGMORPHON results show the soft-attention baseline sometimes outperforms hard attention, indicating a trade-off based on data/morphology.  
- Question 1: How sensitive is performance to alignment quality (e.g., ablations with noisy alignments or different aligners)?  
- Question 2: Are there ambiguous cases in converting character alignments to gold action sequences (many-to-many), and how is a single gold sequence chosen?  
- Question 3: Can you provide learning curves on subsets of larger datasets (e.g., Wiktionary) to further demonstrate data-efficiency advantages?
