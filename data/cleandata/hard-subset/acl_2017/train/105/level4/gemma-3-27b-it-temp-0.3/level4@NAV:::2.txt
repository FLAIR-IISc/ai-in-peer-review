## Review

**Strengths:** This paper introduces a novel encoder-decoder model explicitly designed to incorporate monotonicity constraints.

**Weaknesses:** The proposed model may be functionally similar to a standard BiRNN with decoupled alignments. The evaluation is limited to morphology tasks, lacking assessment on other suitable monotone sequence-to-sequence tasks.

**General Discussion:**

The authors present a new encoder-decoder neural network architecture featuring “hard monotonic attention” and evaluate its performance on three morphology datasets. This is a well-written and largely clear paper that proposes a valuable idea: explicitly modeling monotonicity in morphology tasks. The rationale for this approach is sound – many sequence-to-sequence tasks exhibit inherent monotonicity, suggesting that general encoder-decoder models may not be the optimal choice. The fact that these general models perform reasonably well already highlights the power of neural techniques, and this work aims to further improve performance by explicitly enforcing monotonic output generation through decoupling alignment and transduction, followed by training to align with the monotonic alignments. 

However, certain aspects of the approach require clarification. Specifically:

1) What is the nature of the learned alignments? The running example (Fig. 1) suggests a 1-to-many alignment scheme, where a single input character can map to zero, one, or multiple output characters. This appears to contrast with the description in lines 311-312, which implies multiple input characters aligning to a single output character. Is the alignment scheme 1-to-many, many-to-1, or many-to-many?

2) A relatively straightforward approach to monotone Seq2Seq involves first performing a monotonic alignment of input and output characters with a 1-to-many constraint (using an existing monotone aligner like that of Jiampojamarn and Kondrak). Subsequently, a standard sequence tagger could be trained to predict the aligned output sequence. For example, aligning “flog” to “fliege” as “f-l-o-g / f-l-ie-ge” and then training a tagger (potentially an LSTM) to predict “f-l-ie-ge” from “f-l-o-g”. This approach has been suggested in prior work (e.g., [*, Section 4.2]). 

   2a) How does the proposed approach differ from this simpler strategy?
   2b) Why was this baseline not included in the experimental evaluation?

Further considerations:

3) The exclusive focus on morphology is a limitation. Evaluating the system on other monotonic sequence-to-sequence tasks would demonstrate its broader applicability and potential superiority, given the explicit modeling of monotonicity (cf. [*]).

4) The phrasing “on par or better” (l.791) appears subject to cognitive bias, with a tendency to interpret worse performance as “on par.” This wording should be revised for greater accuracy. Otherwise, the experimental results are acceptable.

5) The description of linguistic features is limited. Figure 1 suggests the inclusion of POS tags, etc. 

   5a) What is the source of these features?
   5b) Could these features, rather than the monotonicity constraints, be responsible for the observed performance improvements in some cases?

**Minor Points:**

6) Equation (3): Please rewrite $NN$ as $\text{NN}$ or similar.
7) l.231: “Where” should be lowercase.
8) l.237 and elsewhere: Use $x_1, \ldots, x_n$ or $x_1 \cdots x_n$ for clarity; avoid dots on the same level as surrounding symbols.
9) Figure 1: The use of Cyrillic font hinders accessibility and prevents detailed examination of the example.
10) l.437: Should be “these”.

**After Author Response:**

The author’s clarification regarding alignments suggests a process of initially establishing 1-0, 0-1, and 1-1 alignments, followed by the creation of many-to-many alignments. While the comparison to Nicolai, Cherry, and Kondrak (2015) is noted, the question remains: why not employ 1-x (where x is 0, 1, or 2) alignments as in Schnober et al. and subsequently train a neural tagger on these alignments (e.g., a BiLSTM)? It would be valuable to understand how the results would differ from this relatively simple baseline. (A tagger inherently enforces monotonicity, and given the monotonic alignments, the output remains monotone. In contrast, this work begins with a more general model and then imposes hard monotonicity constraints.)

**Additional Notes (from AC):**

Cohn et al. (2016) is also highly relevant: http://www.aclweb.org/anthology/N16-1102. The architecture’s relationship to methods like the Stack LSTM, which also predicts a sequence of actions to modify or annotate input, should also be considered. Finally, the potential drawbacks of using a greedy alignment, in contrast to Rastogi et al. (2016), which sums over all alignments, warrant discussion.
