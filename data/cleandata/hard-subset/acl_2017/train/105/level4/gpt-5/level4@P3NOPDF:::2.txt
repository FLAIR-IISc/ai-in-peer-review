Summary
The paper proposes an encoder–decoder architecture with hard monotonic attention that explicitly encodes a monotonicity bias for sequence transduction. The model decouples alignment from transduction: it first produces monotone alignments and then conditions generation on these alignments. Experiments on three morphological datasets show competitive to improved performance. The paper is clearly written and addresses an important gap: many seq2seq tasks are inherently monotone, yet standard attention models are designed for non-monotone phenomena.

Strengths
- Clear motivation for monotonicity in character-level transduction and a straightforward, implementable architecture.
- Solid empirical results on morphological inflection/lemmatization tasks.
- Generally well-written and easy to follow.

Weaknesses
- Conceptual novelty is somewhat limited: the approach is close to a BiRNN transducer with externally imposed, decoupled alignments and hard attention.
- Evaluation is restricted to morphology; no evidence is provided for other monotone seq2seq tasks.
- Key modeling choices and baselines are under-explained, especially regarding the alignment representation and the absence of a simple sequence-tagging baseline that enforces monotonicity by design.
- Limited analysis of the contribution of linguistic features vs. the monotonicity constraint.

Requests for clarification
1) Alignment types and representation:
- At several points, the paper appears to mix 1-to-many and many-to-1 alignments. The running example suggests 1-to-many (an input character mapping to zero, one, or several output characters), while lines 311–312 suggest many-to-1. Please clearly specify:
  - What elementary alignment links are permitted (e.g., 1–0, 0–1, 1–1)?
  - Whether training operates on a 1-to-many, many-to-1, or many-to-many representation (and how one is derived from the other).
  - Whether the model ever needs to split or merge input positions during decoding, and how that is encoded in the action space.

2) Baseline with monotone sequence tagging:
- There is a straightforward alternative: compute monotone 1-to-many alignments (e.g., with the Jiampojamarn–Kondrak aligner), then train a sequence tagger to predict output edit/generation actions per input position. For example, flog → fliege can be aligned as f-l-o-g / f-l-ie-ge; a tagger (e.g., BiLSTM) then predicts per-character outputs/actions to produce the target. See Schnober et al. (2016, Sec. 4.2) for a related setup.
  - How does your approach differ substantively from this pipeline?
  - Why is this not included as a baseline? Given that tagging is inherently monotone, this seems essential to assess the value of your hard-attention mechanism.

3) Scope of evaluation:
- It is unfortunate that the evaluation is limited to morphology. Other monotone seq2seq tasks (OCR post-correction, grapheme-to-phoneme, transliteration, monotone string transduction benchmarks as in Schnober et al., 2016) would strengthen the case that hard monotonic attention provides consistent gains beyond morphology.

4) Reporting language:
- The manuscript claims results are “on par or better.” Please avoid this umbrella phrasing; specify where the model wins, ties, or loses and by what margins (with statistical significance where applicable).

5) Linguistic features:
- Figure 1 suggests the use of features such as POS or morphological tags.
  - Where are these features sourced from?
  - Could the observed gains stem primarily from these features rather than from the monotonicity constraint? Ablations would be helpful.

Relation to prior work and architecture discussion
- Please discuss connections to:
  - Schnober et al. (2016): comprehensive comparison of traditional monotone transducers and encoder–decoders on monotone string translation; also a source for the tagging-style baseline.
  - Nicolai, Cherry, and Kondrak (2015): already compared in part, but clarify differences in alignment supervision and decoding.
  - Cohn et al. (2016): introduces structural alignment biases in attention; how does your hard constraint compare to their soft bias in practice?
  - Stack LSTM–style transition systems: your decoder predicts action sequences over a monotone traversal of the input, which resembles transition-based parsing/segmentation models. Discuss similarities and differences, especially in how actions modify or annotate the input and how alignment is encoded.
  - Rastogi et al. (2016): hard monotonic attention with marginalization over all alignments. Your approach appears to rely on greedy alignment selection. What do you gain in efficiency, and what do you lose in modeling capacity and robustness? Can you estimate the impact of summing over alignments vs. committing to one (e.g., via oracle vs. 1-best alignments, or n-best/marginalization approximations)?

After author response
Thank you for the clarifications. It seems the intended elementary links are 1–0, 0–1, and 1–1, which are then composed into many-to-many spans. Please ensure the camera-ready version unambiguously states this and aligns the examples with the formal description. My main concern remains the lack of a tagging-style monotone baseline using 1–x (x ∈ {0,1,2,...}) alignments (as in Schnober et al.). Because a tagger enforces monotonicity by construction, this baseline is crucial to isolate the benefit of your hard-attention encoder–decoder over simpler monotone models.

Overall assessment
A timely and well-motivated paper with clear writing and promising results. To strengthen the contribution and clarify novelty, I recommend: (i) adding or carefully discussing a monotone tagging baseline; (ii) expanding evaluation to at least one additional monotone seq2seq task; (iii) clarifying the alignment representation and the effect of greedy alignment vs. marginalization; and (iv) providing ablations for linguistic features. With these improvements, the work would make a solid contribution to monotone sequence transduction.
