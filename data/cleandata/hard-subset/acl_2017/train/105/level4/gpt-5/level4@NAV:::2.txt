Review-
- Strengths: The paper introduces a new encoder-decoder architecture that explicitly incorporates monotonicity.
- Weaknesses: The approach may effectively reduce to a standard BiRNN with alignments handled separately. Evaluation is limited to morphology and does not cover other monotone Seq2Seq tasks.
- General Discussion:
The authors present a novel encoder-decoder neural model with “hard monotonic attention,” evaluated on three morphological datasets. The paper is competently written, largely clear, and advances a fresh idea: explicitly modeling monotonicity for morphology tasks.

The motivation is straightforward: many seq2seq problems are monotone (unlike general machine translation), so generic encoder-decoder models may be ill-suited by default. Their decent performance nonetheless speaks to the strength of neural methods overall. This work enforces monotonic output character generation by decoupling alignment and transduction: first obtain a monotone input–output alignment, then train the model to generate outputs consistent with that alignment.

However, some details remain unclear. I have several questions:
1) What is the exact alignment scheme? The running example (Fig. 1) suggests 1-to-many (one input character aligned to zero, one, or multiple output characters), but lines 311–312 imply multiple input characters aligning to a single output. Are alignments 1-to-many, many-to-1, or many-to-many?
2) There is a simple baseline for monotone Seq2Seq: first perform a monotone 1-to-many character alignment (e.g., using a monotone aligner such as the Jiampojamarn and Kondrak toolkit). Then train a standard sequence tagger to reproduce this 1-to-many mapping. For instance, for flog->fliege (your example at l.613): align “f-l-o-g / f-l-ie-ge” and train a tagger (e.g., an LSTM) to map “f-l-o-g” (length 4) to “f-l-ie-ge” (length 4). Variants of this have been proposed before; see [*, Section 4.2].
My questions are:
2a) In what key respects does your method differ from this straightforward approach?
2b) Why was this not included as a baseline?

Further issues:
3) It is unfortunate that the evaluation is restricted to morphology, given the many other monotone seq2seq tasks where your explicit modeling of monotonicity could highlight advantages (cf. [*]).
4) You claim to perform “on par or better” (l.791). There is a common tendency in NLP to label worse results as “on par” and the rest as “better.” I suggest revising this phrasing, though I find the experimental results otherwise acceptable.
5) You provide limited information about the linguistic features. From Fig. 1, it appears they include POS, etc.
5a) What are the sources of these features?
5b) Could these features, rather than the monotonicity constraints, be driving some of the improvements?

Minor points:
6) Equation (3): please typeset NN as \text{NN} (or similar).
7) l.231: “Where” should be lowercase.
8) l.237 and elsewhere: x1 … xn. Conventionally, write x1, …, xn with commas, or x1 ⋯ xn with centered dots; the dots should align with adjacent symbols.
9) Figure 1: Is Cyrillic font necessary? I cannot view the example due to missing fonts.
10) l.437: should read “these”.

Thanks for the clarifications. It seems the alignment description in your response became muddled (perhaps a rendering issue), but my understanding is that you allow 1-0, 0-1, and 1-1 alignments, and then compose these into many-to-many alignments.

I realize you compare against Nicolai, Cherry, and Kondrak (2015), but my question was rather: why not adopt 1–x (x in {0,1,2}) alignments as in Schnober et al., and then train a neural tagger (e.g., a BiLSTM) on these? I’m curious how your results would compare to such a simple baseline. A tagger is monotone by design given monotone alignments; in contrast, you begin with a more general model and then impose hard monotonicity constraints.
