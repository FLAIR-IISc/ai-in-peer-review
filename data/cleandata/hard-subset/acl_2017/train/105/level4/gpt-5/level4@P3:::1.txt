Summary and contributions
This paper proposes a neural sequence-to-sequence model for morphological inflection generation that uses hard, monotonic attention over a bi-LSTM-encoded input. The decoder alternates between two actions: ADVANCE (step the attention pointer) and WRITE(character), with action sequences derived from externally learned character alignments. Training is standard cross-entropy on these oracle action sequences.

As I see it, the primary contributions are:
1) A simple, effective hard monotonic attention architecture for character-level transduction that decouples alignment learning from decoding and exploits the near-monotonicity of many inflectional processes.
2) A thorough empirical evaluation across CELEX, Wiktionary, and SIGMORPHON-2016, establishing state-of-the-art or competitive results, notably strong performance in low-resource settings and in suffixing/stem-change languages.
3) An analysis comparing learned alignments and encoder representations of hard vs. soft attention models, offering insight into what features these architectures encode.

Strengths
- Strong performance in low-resource settings, with clear evidence of better generalization. On CELEX, the model achieves the best average accuracy (89.44) and is, to my knowledge, the first neural model to surpass Dreyer et al.’s latent-variable WFST (88.3). It also outperforms recent neural baselines (MED 87.62; NWFST 87.65). Learning curves show similar training accuracy but substantially better dev accuracy than soft attention, indicating reduced overfitting due to the monotonic control.
- Competitive and often state-of-the-art results on larger datasets. On Wiktionary, the model attains the highest reported average accuracy (96.55) among the compared methods and leads on German verbs, Finnish nouns/adjectives, and Dutch verbs. On SIGMORPHON-2016, it outperforms soft attention for suffixing + stem-change languages (Russian, German, Spanish) and remains close elsewhere, demonstrating robustness and the value of monotonic control where appropriate.
- Simple training with interpretable control. By using external character alignments to derive oracle action sequences, the model avoids learning alignment and transduction jointly, enabling straightforward cross-entropy training and fast convergence. The explicit ADVANCE/WRITE sequence makes the model’s behavior interpretable, and the action space aligns naturally with edit operations (insertions, substitutions, deletions via consecutive ADVANCEs/WRITEs).
- Insightful analysis of model behavior. The qualitative alignment comparison shows that soft attention also tends toward monotonicity on this task, validating the modeling assumption. The encoder visualization suggests hard attention encodes character identity more distinctly and relies less on positional encoding than soft attention, consistent with hard attention’s explicit pointer mechanism. This analysis helps delineate when each approach should be preferred.
- Clear presentation and comprehensive evaluation. The paper covers three established benchmarks, compares against strong neural and non-neural baselines, reports exact-match accuracy consistently, and provides qualitative illustrations of predicted alignments and learned representations.

Weaknesses
- Mixed results across morphological typologies. On SIGMORPHON, the hard attention model underperforms soft attention for languages with strong long-distance dependencies and non-concatenative or harmony processes (e.g., Finnish, Turkish, Hungarian, Arabic, Navajo). While the gap is often small, the overall average trails a strong soft-attention baseline. This limits the method’s generality and suggests the model is best suited to near-monotonic, suffixing transformations.
- Dependence on external alignments without sensitivity analysis. The approach relies on a specific CRP-based character aligner to derive oracle action sequences. The paper does not quantify how alignment noise affects performance, nor compare aligners or investigate alternatives (e.g., learned or jointly refined alignments). This raises concerns about robustness and portability to domains/languages where alignment quality may vary.
- Objective mismatch and derivation ambiguity. The model optimizes for the best derivation rather than marginalizing over derivations that yield the same output. The paper acknowledges this but does not estimate the frequency or impact of derivational ambiguity, nor explore approximate marginalization or constrained dynamic programming. The practical effect on decoding and calibration is unclear.
- Unclear fairness and tuning parity with soft-attention baselines. For SIGMORPHON, the paper notes training the soft attention model longer (100 epochs) than MED (20 epochs), which complicates baseline comparability. A controlled comparison with identical training budgets and early stopping on dev across methods would strengthen the claims.
- Missing analysis of efficiency and resource use. While the method is simpler conceptually, the paper does not report training/inference time, memory footprint, or parameter counts relative to soft attention. Since decoding involves many action steps, empirical speed/latency comparisons would help practitioners weigh the practical benefits.

Questions to authors
- Alignment sensitivity: How sensitive is performance to the choice and quality of the external aligner? Have you tried different aligners or injected noise into alignments to measure robustness? Can the model fine-tune alignments during training (e.g., via auxiliary loss)?
- Decoding details and efficiency: Do you decode greedily or with a beam? What beam width was used, and how much does it help? Please report training/inference speed and memory vs. soft attention on representative datasets.
- Handling non-monotonic phenomena: Can the pointer move backward, or is the model strictly monotone? How does it cope with circumfixation, templatic patterns, or reordering beyond local edits? Would allowing limited backward moves improve results on non-concatenative languages?
- Best-derivation approximation: How often are multiple derivations possible for the same output in your data? Did you attempt any marginalization over derivations (e.g., constrained forward–backward) or regularization to mitigate derivation bias?
- Ablations: What is the impact of removing feature embeddings, the feedback connection (previous output symbol), or using a unidirectional encoder? An ablation study would clarify which components drive the gains.

Additional comments
- The paper is well written and easy to follow. Please include exact parameter counts, training schedules, and early-stopping criteria for all models to facilitate reproducibility.
- Clarify whether statistical significance tests were performed for key comparisons.
- The qualitative alignment examples are helpful; adding a small, systematic error analysis by transformation type (insertions, deletions, stem changes, harmonies) would further illuminate strengths and failure modes.

Overall assessment
This is a solid, well-executed contribution. The hard monotonic attention approach is conceptually clean, empirically strong in low-resource and near-monotonic settings, and accompanied by useful analysis. Addressing alignment sensitivity, efficiency, and fairness of baseline tuning would strengthen the paper, but I lean positive given its clarity, novelty relative to soft attention, and practical value for inflection tasks where monotonicity holds.
