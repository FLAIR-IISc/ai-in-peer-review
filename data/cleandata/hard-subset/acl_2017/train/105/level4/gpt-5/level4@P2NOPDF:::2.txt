- Strengths: Proposes a new encoder–decoder architecture with hard monotonic attention that explicitly models monotonicity.

- Weaknesses: May reduce to a standard BiRNN with a decoupled alignment component. Evaluation is limited to morphological inflection; no other monotone seq2seq tasks are considered.

- General discussion:
The paper introduces a neural encoder–decoder model with hard monotonic attention and evaluates it on three morphological datasets. The paper is well written and mostly clear, and incorporating explicit monotonicity for morphology is a novel and sensible idea. Many seq2seq problems are inherently monotone, unlike MT; that generic encoder–decoders still perform reasonably well on these tasks is itself an argument in favor of neural approaches. Here, the authors enforce monotonic character generation by decoupling alignment and transduction: they first obtain monotone input–output alignments, then train the decoder to generate outputs consistent with these alignments. However, key details are unclear. I have several questions and suggestions:

1) Alignment structure: Please clarify the allowed alignment types. Figure 1 suggests 1-to-many (one input character maps to zero, one, or multiple output characters). Lines 311–312, however, suggest many-to-1 (several input characters aligning to one output character). Are your primitive alignments 1-to-0/1/2+, many-to-1, or many-to-many? What constraints are enforced during training and decoding?

2) A simple monotone baseline: A straightforward approach is to (i) compute monotone 1-to-many character alignments (e.g., with Jiampojamarn and Kondrak’s toolkit), then (ii) train a standard sequence tagger to predict those 1-to-many expansions. For example, flog → fliege (your example at l.613): align “f-l-o-g” to “f-l-ie-ge” under a 1-to-many monotone constraint, then train a tagger (e.g., a BiLSTM) to map a length-4 input to a length-4 sequence of output symbols or edit actions. This style of approach has been discussed before (see, e.g., Schnober et al., 2016, COLING).
  2a) How does your method differ substantively from this baseline?
  2b) Why did you not include such a tagger-based monotone model as a baseline?

3) Scope of evaluation: It is unfortunate that experiments are restricted to morphology. There are several other monotone seq2seq tasks (e.g., transliteration, OCR post-correction, spelling normalization, phoneme–grapheme conversion) where your explicit monotonicity bias should shine (cf. Schnober et al., 2016). Including at least one such task would strengthen the empirical case.

4) Claims about performance: The phrase “on par or better” (l.791) can be misleading; please report where you are better and where you are worse without collapsing the two. The current results are otherwise fine.

5) Linguistic features: Figure 1 suggests using POS and other features.
  5a) What is the source of these features?
  5b) Could these features, rather than monotonic attention per se, explain gains in some settings? Please analyze or ablate.

- Minor points:
6) Equation (3): typeset NN as a math operator (e.g., \text{NN}).
7) l.231: “Where” → “where.”
8) l.237 and elsewhere: prefer x1, …, xn or x1 ⋯ xn, with ellipses at the same vertical level as surrounding symbols.
9) Figure 1: Avoid Cyrillic or nonstandard fonts; the example is hard to read without the font.
10) l.437: “these,” not “this.”

AFTER AUTHOR RESPONSE
Thanks for the clarifications. It seems there may have been confusion in the rebuttal (perhaps due to a formatting/coding issue), but my understanding is that you produce primitive 1–0, 0–1, and 1–1 alignments and later combine them into many-to-many segments. My question remains: why not use constrained 1–x alignments (x ∈ {0,1,2}) as in Schnober et al. and train a neural tagger (e.g., a BiLSTM) to predict them? Such a tagger is inherently monotone, ensuring the model respects the monotonicity constraint end-to-end, whereas your approach starts with a more general encoder–decoder and then imposes hard monotonicity. I would like to know how your results would compare to this simpler baseline.

NOTES FROM AC AND RELATED WORK
- Please discuss the relevance of Cohn et al. (2016) and how your method compares.
- Your architecture also seems related to action-based models such as the Stack LSTM, which predict sequences of edit or alignment actions over the input. Can you clarify similarities and differences (e.g., action space, training objective, treatment of alignment uncertainty)?
- You use greedy alignment. Do you lose accuracy compared to approaches like Rastogi et al. (2016), which also enforce hard monotonic attention but marginalize over all monotone alignments? Can your training objective incorporate summing over alignments, or is there a practical reason you avoid it?

Overall: a well-written paper with a promising idea—explicit monotonic attention for monotone seq2seq tasks. The main concerns are (i) clarity about alignment structure, (ii) omission of a strong, simple monotone tagger baseline, and (iii) narrow empirical scope. Addressing these would significantly strengthen the work.
