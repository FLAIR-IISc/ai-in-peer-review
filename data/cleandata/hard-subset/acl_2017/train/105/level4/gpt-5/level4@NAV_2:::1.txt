- Strengths:
The introduction of a hard, monotonic attention mechanism is original and meaningfully distinct from prior approaches.

- Weaknesses:
The empirical outcomes on morphological inflection are somewhat mixed. The proposed model performs particularly well with limited training data (e.g., CELEX) and when alignments are predominantly monotonic with weaker context sensitivity (e.g., Russian, German, Spanish).

- General Discussion:
The paper presents a new neural model for morphological inflection that employs “hard attention,” relying on externally induced character alignments obtained via a Bayesian transliteration-style aligner. This contrasts sharply with prior state-of-the-art neural methods based on “soft attention,” in which alignment and transduction are learned jointly within a single probabilistic framework. The idea is novel and well-motivated; the paper is clearly written; and the experimental evaluation is thorough. My main reservation is that the approach does not consistently achieve state-of-the-art results across all settings; it excels in regimes with mostly monotonic alignments and limited context-sensitive phenomena. The work would be more compelling if it articulated practical benefits—such as implementation simplicity and computational efficiency—in more concrete terms.
