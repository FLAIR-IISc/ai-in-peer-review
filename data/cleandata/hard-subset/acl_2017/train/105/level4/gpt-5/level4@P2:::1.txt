Summary and contributions:
- The paper introduces a neural encoder–decoder model for morphological inflection that uses a hard, nearly monotonic attention mechanism. The decoder alternates between WRITE(symbol) and STEP actions over a biLSTM-encoded input, with action supervision derived from externally learned character alignments.
- The model achieves strong results across three benchmarks (CELEX, Wiktionary, SIGMORPHON 2016), notably excelling in low-resource settings and on languages with largely monotonic, suffixing morphology. On CELEX, it surpasses a specialized latent-variable WFST.
- The authors analyze the learned representations and alignments, comparing hard and soft attention models and highlighting how positional and character-type information are encoded.

Strengths:
- Clear modeling idea with genuine novelty: The hard monotonic attention with explicit STEP/WRITE control and supervision from external alignments is a clean departure from standard soft-attention seq2seq. It directly matches the near-monotonic nature of many inflection processes while keeping decoding simple and interpretable.
- Strong low-resource performance: On CELEX (very small training sets), the model outperforms both soft-attention seq2seq and neural/WFST baselines, and is, to my knowledge, the first neural approach to surpass the tailored latent-variable WFST on this dataset. The learning-curve analysis convincingly suggests reduced overfitting relative to soft attention.
- Competitive to state of the art on larger data: On the Wiktionary benchmarks, the model attains the best average accuracy across inflection types, outperforming both non-neural and neural baselines (including non-attentional encoder–decoder and alignment–transduction models).
- Sound training procedure and simplicity: By using externally computed character alignments to derive oracle action sequences, the model can be trained with straightforward cross-entropy, avoiding complex marginalization over alignments and simplifying implementation compared to transducer models that learn alignments jointly.
- Useful analysis: The alignment visualizations show that both soft and hard attention learn (near-)monotone alignments, supporting the modeling assumption. The representation analysis offers insight into how character identity and position are encoded, and why hard attention may generalize better in low data regimes.

Weaknesses:
- Mixed results across morphological types: On SIGMORPHON, the hard attention model underperforms strong soft-attention baselines for languages with more context-sensitive or non-monotonic phenomena (e.g., vowel/consonant harmony, templatic morphology). This limits its generality and suggests the modeling bias can be detrimental outside monotonic regimes.
- Dependence on external alignments: Relying on a separate Bayesian character alignment step introduces a pipeline dependency and potential error propagation. The paper does not quantify sensitivity to alignment quality or compare alignment methods, leaving open how robust the approach is when alignments are noisy or scarce.
- Best-derivation approximation: Decoding optimizes the most likely action sequence rather than marginalizing over derivations that yield the same output. The paper acknowledges this but does not measure its impact (e.g., via beam search vs. greedy, or by comparing derivation vs. sequence-level objectives).
- Limited discussion of efficiency and practicality: While the method seems simpler to train than models with joint alignment learning, the paper does not report training/inference time, memory footprint, or alignment-preprocessing cost. These practical aspects would strengthen the case for adoption.
- Ablations and fairness of comparisons: Some comparisons attribute gains to training schedules (e.g., more epochs for soft attention in the authors’ implementation vs. MED’s default). A systematic ablation (epochs, model size, feature embeddings, use of dev data) would clarify how much improvement stems from the hard attention mechanism versus configuration differences.

Questions to authors:
- How sensitive is performance to the choice and quality of the external aligner? Have you tried alternative aligners or noisy alignments, and can you report performance deltas?
- What decoding strategy is used at test time (greedy vs. beam)? If beam search is used, what width, and how does it affect accuracy and speed? Any evidence that the best-derivation approximation hurts performance?
- Can you provide training and inference runtimes and memory usage, including the one-time alignment cost, relative to soft-attention seq2seq baselines?
- How does the model handle truly non-monotonic operations (e.g., templatic patterns) beyond what positional context in the encoder provides? Would adding limited reordering actions help?
- Did you conduct ablations on the feature embeddings (morpho-syntactic tags), decoder feedback of previous outputs, or encoder size to isolate which components drive gains on CELEX?

Overall assessment:
This is a well-written, novel, and carefully evaluated paper. The hard monotonic attention mechanism is an elegant fit for inflection generation and yields clear benefits in low-resource and mostly monotonic settings. The main limitations concern generality to non-monotonic phenomena and reliance on external alignments, alongside missing practical efficiency metrics. With clarifications on robustness and efficiency, this would be a strong contribution.
