- Strengths: The paper introduces a new encoder–decoder model that explicitly accounts for monotonicity.

- Weaknesses: The approach may reduce to a standard BiRNN with decoupled alignments. It is evaluated only on morphological tasks, with no experiments on other monotonic Seq2Seq problems.

- General Discussion:
The authors present a novel encoder–decoder architecture with “hard monotonic attention,” evaluated on three morphological datasets. This is a challenging paper to assess. On the one hand, it is well written, mostly clear, and proposes a genuinely new idea: explicitly modeling monotonicity for morphological tasks.

The motivation for enforcing monotonicity is straightforward: unlike machine translation, many Seq2Seq problems are monotonic, so general encoder–decoder models may not be the best fit. The fact that generic models still perform reasonably well is, in itself, a strong testament to neural approaches. This paper’s contribution is to explicitly enforce monotonic character generation by decoupling alignment and transduction: first, produce a monotonic alignment between input and output sequences; then, train a model to generate outputs consistent with these alignments.

However, several points remain unclear:
1) What is the exact form of your alignments? In the running example (Fig. 1), the alignments appear to be 1-to-many: one input character can map to zero, one, or multiple output characters. This seems to conflict with the description in lines 311–312, where multiple input characters are aligned to a single output character. Are your alignments 1-to-many, many-to-1, or many-to-many?

2) There is a straightforward baseline for monotone Seq2Seq: first, monotonically align input and output characters with a 1-to-many constraint (using any monotone aligner, e.g., Jiampojamarn and Kondrak’s toolkit). Then train a standard sequence tagger to predict these 1-to-many alignments. For instance, for flog → fliege (your example at l.613): first align “f-l-o-g / f-l-ie-ge.” Then train any tagger (e.g., an LSTM) to predict “f-l-ie-ge” (length 4) from “f-l-o-g” (length 4). Variants of this approach have been suggested before; see [*, Section 4.2].
My questions:
2a) How does your method differ from this simple baseline?
2b) Why was this baseline not included?

Further issues:
3) It is unfortunate that you evaluated only on morphology. There are many other monotonic Seq2Seq tasks where your method, which explicitly models monotonicity (cf. also [*]), could demonstrate broader advantages.

4) You claim performance “on par or better” (l.791). In NLP, there’s a common tendency to label worse results as “on par” and the rest as “better.” I suggest revising this wording, though I find the empirical results otherwise acceptable.

5) You provide little detail on your linguistic features. From Fig. 1, I infer they include POS, etc.
5a) What is the source of these features?
5b) Could these features, rather than monotonicity constraints, explain your gains in some cases?

Minor points:
6) Equation (3): please rewrite $NN$ as $\text{NN}$ or similar.
7) l.231: “Where” should be lowercase.
8) l.237 and elsewhere: $x1\ldots xn$. As I understand it, the mathematical convention is to write $x1,\ldots,xn$ for lists and $x1\cdots xn$ for products; the dots should align with surrounding symbols.
9) Figure 1: Is the Cyrillic font necessary? I cannot inspect your example without the required fonts.
10) l.437: should be “these.”
