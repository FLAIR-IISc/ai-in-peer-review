Summary and perceived contributions
- The paper proposes an encoder–decoder model with hard monotonic attention for character-level sequence transduction. It uses externally learned character alignments and a simple control mechanism with two actions—STEP and WRITE—to enforce near-monotone generation.
- The model achieves strong results on three morphological inflection datasets (CELEX, Wiktionary, SIGMORPHON), notably excelling in low-resource settings.
- The authors analyze learned alignments and encoder representations for both hard and soft attention, offering insight into what these models encode.

Contributions as I see them (in order of strength)
1) A practical hard-attention architecture that explicitly exploits monotonicity via precomputed alignments and a STEP/WRITE controller, enabling simple cross-entropy training.
2) Strong empirical performance, particularly on CELEX, where the model surpasses a specialized latent-variable WFST, and competitive results on larger datasets.
3) An informative qualitative analysis of alignments and representations in hard vs. soft attention models.

Strengths
- Leverages monotonicity effectively: The design aligns well with the inductive bias of morphological inflection (largely monotone with local edits), which appears to reduce overfitting and improve generalization in low-resource regimes (e.g., CELEX).
- Strong empirical showing: The model sets or matches state of the art across multiple benchmarks, and notably outperforms prior neural and non-neural approaches on CELEX.
- Simpler training: Decoupling alignment learning from sequence prediction allows for straightforward supervised training without marginalizing alignments, simplifying optimization relative to models that sum over alignments.
- Clear exposition and useful analysis: The qualitative alignment and representation analyses help demystify what the model learns and support the modeling choice.

Weaknesses
- Novelty relative to simpler baselines: The approach is close to a standard biLSTM encoder–decoder trained on externally provided monotone alignments, raising the question of how much the hard-attention controller adds beyond a simpler monotone tagging/transduction baseline that predicts 1-to-x outputs per input character (as in traditional monotone string translation setups).
- Limited task scope: Evaluation is restricted to morphological inflection. Other monotone transduction tasks (e.g., transliteration, grapheme–phoneme conversion, certain summarization variants) would more convincingly demonstrate the benefit of hard monotonic attention.
- Alignment specification is unclear: The text appears to suggest both many-to-one and one-to-many character alignments at different points. A precise description of allowed alignment cardinalities and how they are converted into STEP/WRITE sequences is needed.
- Potential confound from linguistic features: The paper does not clearly describe the source and content of morpho-syntactic features (e.g., POS, attributes). It is therefore hard to attribute gains strictly to monotonicity versus richer feature conditioning.
- Objective approximation: The model optimizes the best-derivation path rather than marginalizing over derivations that yield the same output, unlike approaches that sum over alignments. The empirical trade-off versus marginalizing approaches (e.g., Rastogi et al., 2016) is not fully explored.

Questions to authors
1) Alignment format: Do you use 1-to-0/1/2+ (one input to zero/one/many outputs), many-to-one, or many-to-many alignments? How are these converted deterministically into STEP/WRITE sequences?
2) Baseline design: How does your approach differ in practice from a simpler pipeline that (i) computes monotone 1-to-x character alignments and (ii) trains a neural tagger (e.g., biLSTM) to predict the aligned output sequence per input position? Why was such a baseline not included?
3) Feature details: What is the exact set of morpho-syntactic features provided to the model, and from which resources are they derived? Did you test ablations to quantify their contribution relative to hard monotonic attention?
4) Greedy derivation vs. marginalization: What performance trade-offs did you observe between your best-derivation training and approaches that marginalize over alignments (as in Rastogi et al., 2016)? Can you quantify any loss due to the approximation?
5) Architectural relations: How does your action-based controller relate to transition-based models such as Stack-LSTMs and to segmental/monotonic attention models (e.g., Cohn et al., 2016)? What advantages or limitations do you see?

Additional comments
- The phrase “on par or better” is overused in NLP to gloss over degradations. Please report where your model is better and where it is worse, and avoid umbrella phrasing.
- It would strengthen the paper to include at least one additional monotone task outside morphology (e.g., transliteration or G2P).

Minor points
- Equation and typography: In Eq. (3), set NN as text (e.g., “NN”). Use consistent list/dot notation (x1, …, xn vs. x1…xn); ensure dot placement matches standard practice.
- Capitalization: “Where” at the start of a sentence after an equation should be lowercase.
- Figure fonts: Avoid Cyrillic-only examples or provide Latin transliterations to ensure readability.
- Typos: “these” instead of “those” at the cited location.

After author response
- Thanks for the clarifications. My understanding is that you consider 1–0, 0–1, and 1–1 alignments at the character level and aggregate them into many-to-many relations when forming action sequences; if that is incorrect, please specify precisely.
- My main remaining concern is the absence of a simple monotone tagging baseline built on 1–x alignments (cf. Schnober et al., 2016). This would help isolate the value added by the hard-attention controller versus a simpler monotone neural model.
- Please also discuss connections to Stack-LSTM–style transition systems and to monotonic/segmental attention (e.g., Cohn et al., 2016), and comment on the implications of using a fixed alignment (greedy best derivation) compared with summing over alignments.
