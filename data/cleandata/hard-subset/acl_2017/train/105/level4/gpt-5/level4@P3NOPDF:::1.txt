Summary
This paper proposes a neural model for morphological inflection generation that uses hard, monotonic attention guided by character alignments obtained from a Bayesian transliteration aligner. Unlike prior soft-attention approaches that jointly learn alignment and transduction, the proposed model decouples these steps and enforces monotonicity during decoding.

Strengths
- Novelty: The use of hard monotonic attention for this task is a clear and substantial departure from standard soft-attention models.
- Soundness and clarity: The methodology is well motivated and clearly described.
- Empirical scope: Experiments are comprehensive and include multiple languages.
- Effectiveness in specific regimes: The model performs well when training data are limited (e.g., CELEX) and for languages where alignments are largely monotonic and less context-sensitive (e.g., Russian, German, Spanish).

Weaknesses
- Mixed results: The approach is not consistently state of the art across all conditions, especially when alignments are less monotonic or morphological processes are more context-sensitive.
- Dependence on external alignments: Relying on a separate Bayesian aligner may propagate alignment errors, yet the paper provides limited analysis of sensitivity to alignment quality.
- Practical aspects underexplored: The paper does not adequately discuss implementation complexity, computational cost, or decoding speed relative to soft-attention baselines.
- Limited analysis of non-monotonic phenomena: It remains unclear how well the model handles insertions, deletions, or longer-distance dependencies that violate strict monotonicity.

Comments and Suggestions
- Quantify practical merits:
  - Report training and inference time, memory usage, and search complexity compared with soft-attention baselines (e.g., wall-clock time, FLOPs, beam width).
  - Discuss ease of implementation and any constraints or simplifications introduced by monotonic decoding.
- Analyze alignment sensitivity:
  - Provide results with multiple aligners or alignment settings; include an ablation that varies alignment noise.
  - If possible, compare against a joint-learning variant to isolate the benefit of precomputed alignments.
- Characterize when the method excels:
  - Include learning curves across data sizes to show the regime where hard attention is most beneficial.
  - Offer per-language and per-phenomenon error analyses (e.g., context-dependent alternations, templatic morphology, and non-monotonic edits).
- Clarify handling of non-monotonicity:
  - Describe the action space (e.g., skip/emit) and its capacity to represent insertions/deletions or local reorderings.
  - State whether any back-off to soft attention or relaxed constraints is supported when strictly monotonic alignment is violated.
- Strengthen empirical rigor:
  - Report statistical significance, confidence intervals, and variance across random seeds for key comparisons.
  - Ensure comparisons use strong, tuned baselines and consistent data preprocessing.
- Provide qualitative insights:
  - Include alignment visualizations and example success/failure cases to illustrate interpretability and typical error modes.

Overall Assessment
This paper introduces a novel and well-argued approach to morphological inflection generation via hard monotonic attention with externally obtained alignments. The idea is distinct from prior soft-attention methods and shows clear benefits in low-resource settings and languages with predominantly monotonic alignments. However, the empirical gains are mixed across conditions, and the paper would be strengthened by a deeper analysis of when and why the method helps, as well as by a more thorough treatment of computational and implementation trade-offs. With additional evidence on efficiency, robustness to alignment quality, and behavior on non-monotonic phenomena, the contribution would be compelling. As it stands, I view the paper as promising and borderline; I lean toward acceptance if the authors can address the practical and analytical gaps outlined above.
