Summary and contributions
The paper proposes a neural sequence-to-sequence model for morphological inflection generation that enforces hard, nearly monotonic attention. Alignments are learned externally at the character level, and the decoder predicts a sequence of WRITE and STEP actions consistent with these alignments. The model achieves strong results on CELEX, Wiktionary, and SIGMORPHON 2016 datasets, and includes an analysis of representations learned by hard vs. soft attention.

Contributions as I see them:
1) A simple and effective hard-attention transduction model trained from external character alignments, with a best-derivation objective and straightforward cross-entropy training.
2) Strong empirical performance, especially in low-resource settings (CELEX), and competitive or better results on larger datasets.
3) An informative analysis comparing learned alignments and encoder representations under hard vs. soft attention.

Strengths
- Strong low-resource performance with clear motivation: On CELEX, the model outperforms both recent neural approaches (e.g., MED, NWFST) and the latent-variable WFST tailored to morphology (Dreyer et al., 2008). The hard attention control appears to regularize toward monotonic mappings, reducing overfitting relative to soft attention (as supported by learning curves).
- Competitive results at scale: On the larger Wiktionary datasets, the model attains best average accuracy across languages/inflection types among prior neural and non-neural baselines considered, and improves over encoder-decoder without attention (Faruqui et al., 2016) and over Yu et al. (2016) who couple alignment and transduction.
- Clear, modular training: Decoupling alignment from transduction enables a simple loss, stable training, and interpretable action sequences. This contrasts with forward-backward training that marginalizes alignments and can be harder to optimize.
- Insightful analysis: The comparison of hard vs. soft attention shows both induce largely monotonic alignments, but differ in how strongly positional information is encoded, plausibly explaining the hard model’s robustness on small data.
- Well written and easy to follow; code release is valuable for reproducibility.

Weaknesses
- Limited novelty relative to existing monotonic transduction frameworks: The model is closely related to transition-based derivation systems and to prior monotonic attention/transduction approaches (e.g., RNN-Transducer, Neural Transducer, Yu et al., 2016). It would benefit from a deeper comparison to work on hard/monotonic attention in NMT and speech (e.g., Cohn et al., 2016; location-aware attention), and to transition-based models such as Stack-LSTM-style action prediction. The paper’s main novelty is the specific instantiation and training from external alignments, which is valuable but incremental.
- Evaluation scope: All experiments are on morphology. Since the key assumption is monotonicity, evaluation on additional monotone string-to-string tasks (e.g., transliteration, G2P, acronym expansion, monotone MT variants) would strengthen the generality claim.
- Missing baseline: A simple and strong baseline is to perform monotone 1-to-many alignment and then train a neural sequence tagger (e.g., BiLSTM) to predict output segments per input position (as suggested in Schnober et al., 2016). This preserves monotonicity by design and may rival the proposed approach. The absence of such a baseline makes it harder to assess the value of the action-based decoder vs. tagging.
- Alignment and objective: The model optimizes the best-derivation rather than marginalizing over derivations that yield the same string, potentially losing probability mass. Rastogi et al. (2016) and related work sum over alignments; a discussion or experiment on whether the best-derivation approximation costs accuracy would be useful. Also, clarity on the exact alignment types used (1–0, 0–1, 1–1 and how these compose to many-to-many) is essential; the paper text and figures occasionally conflict.
- Feature ablations and dependencies: The role of morpho-syntactic features is not fully specified. It remains unclear whether gains on some datasets are driven by the hard attention mechanism vs. feature usage. Ablations (with/without features; learned vs. gold features) would clarify attribution.

Questions to authors
- Alignment details: Please precisely specify the alignment inventory (allowed links and constraints), how 1–0/0–1/1–1 links are composed into many-to-many alignments, and how deletion/insertion and one-to-many outputs are handled in action sequences.
- Best-derivation vs. marginalization: Did you try marginalizing over derivations that produce the same output (e.g., via dynamic programming), or approximate alternatives? Any empirical evidence on the impact of best-derivation training?
- Decoding: Are you using greedy decoding or beam search? How sensitive are results to beam size?
- Baselines: Why not include a monotone alignment + BiLSTM tagging baseline (predicting output substrings per input character) as in Schnober et al. (2016)? How do you expect it to fare relative to your model?
- Features: What is the source of the morpho-syntactic features for each dataset, and how are they embedded/concatenated? Could features alone explain some gains? Please provide ablations.
- Generality: Have you tested the model on transliteration or other monotone tasks? If not, what obstacles do you foresee?
- Relation to prior work: Please discuss connections and differences to Cohn et al. (2016) monotonic attention and to transition-based architectures (e.g., Stack-LSTM), and to Rastogi et al. (2016) who sum over alignments.

Additional comments
- Wording: Replace “on par or better” with precise statements; where your model is worse, state it plainly.
- Equation/notation: In Eq. (3), typeset NN as text; ensure consistent x1,…,xn notation with commas and centered dots where appropriate; lowercase “where” mid-sentence; fix “these” at l.437. Consider avoiding Cyrillic fonts in Figure 1 to improve readability.
- Clarity: Provide a small, concrete example mapping input→actions→output for 1-to-many and deletion cases, and include a brief complexity comparison vs. soft attention.

Overall, this is a solid and well-executed paper with a clear idea and strong empirical results, especially in low-resource settings. Addressing the baseline gap, clarifying alignment details, and broadening evaluation or discussion to related monotonic attention/transduction literature would substantially strengthen the contribution.
