- Proposes an encoder–decoder with hard, nearly-monotonic attention that uses externally induced character-level alignments and a STEP/WRITE transduction controller.
- Evaluated on CELEX, Wiktionary, and SIGMORPHON 2016, showing competitive or state-of-the-art accuracy, especially in low-resource settings.
- Decouples alignment and transduction by obtaining alignments externally and training a decoder with biLSTM-encoded inputs.
- Contribution: explicit hard monotonic attention model enabling straightforward cross-entropy training via an action sequence controller.
- Contribution: strong empirical results across three morphology benchmarks, notably outperforming prior systems on low-resource CELEX and excelling on suffixing+stem-change languages.
- Contribution: analysis of learned alignments and encoder representations comparing hard attention to soft attention.
- Strength: alignment bias matches task structure and reduces overfitting in low-data regimes (clear learning-curve evidence).
- Strength: simple, transparent control and training by predicting a single action sequence rather than joint alignment/transduction.
- Strength: broad, careful evaluation across multiple datasets and typologically diverse languages.
- Strength: informative analysis showing both models capture symbol identity/position, with soft attention encoding position more strongly.
- Weakness: limited novelty relative to existing monotone transduction paradigms and related models (RNN-Transducer, WFSTs, transition-based models).
- Weakness: missing a simple monotone-tagger baseline (biLSTM tagger on induced 1–x alignments) to isolate the controller’s benefit.
- Weakness: scope restricted to morphology; evaluation on other monotone string tasks (e.g., transliteration) would strengthen generality.
- Weakness: unclear/ inconsistent description of allowed alignment types and how many-to-many alignments are formed; needs precise definition.
- Weakness: objective optimizes a single best derivation rather than marginalizing over alignments; lack of comparison to marginalization approaches.
- Questions: clarify whether atomic links are 1–0, 0–1, 1–1 composed into many-to-many or if 1–x alignments are induced directly.
- Questions: please compare to a biLSTM monotone-tagging baseline (e.g., Schnober et al., 2016) to justify the STEP/WRITE controller.
- Questions: specify the source of morpho-syntactic features per dataset and report ablations on feature conditioning vs hard-attention bias.
- Questions: relate the action prediction to transition-based models (e.g., Stack-LSTMs) and discuss expressivity/training implications.
- Questions: provide evidence on whether using a single greedy alignment is sufficient versus training that marginalizes over alignments.
- Minor comments: temper wording like “on par or better,” fix notation/typography (Equation 3, variable dots, capitalization), and improve figure fonts.
- After rebuttal: author clarified atomic links compose into many-to-many; the main remaining concern is the absent 1–x monotone tagger baseline and discussion of greedy alignment vs marginalization.
