Summary and contributions
The paper proposes a neural encoder–decoder model with hard monotonic attention for morphological inflection generation. The core idea is to leverage the near-monotone alignment between input and output character sequences by introducing a discrete control mechanism with two actions (WRITE(symbol) and STEP), trained using externally induced character alignments. The authors evaluate on CELEX, Wiktionary, and SIGMORPHON 2016 datasets, and analyze learned alignments and representations relative to soft attention.

As I see them, the main contributions are:
1) A hard monotonic attention architecture that decouples alignment and transduction, enabling simple cross-entropy training and exploiting monotonicity common in inflection.
2) Strong empirical performance, especially in low-resource settings (CELEX), where it surpasses a tailored latent-variable WFST baseline, while remaining competitive on larger datasets.
3) An informative analysis comparing hard vs. soft attention alignments and encoder representations, shedding light on positional encoding and character clustering.

Strengths
- Task-appropriate inductive bias: The architecture directly encodes monotonic alignments via STEP/WRITE, matching the dominant structure of inflectional transformations (suffixation and local stem changes). This is well motivated and supported by the alignment visualizations, which show both models converge to largely monotone alignments but the hard attention mechanism enforces this structure more explicitly.
- Strong low-resource results: On CELEX, the model not only outperforms recent neural approaches but also surpasses the latent-variable WFST specifically designed for morphological transduction. The learning curves further suggest reduced overfitting versus soft attention, consistent with the monotonic inductive bias.
- Competitive large-scale performance: On Wiktionary, the model achieves the best average accuracy across settings and improves particularly on German verbs, Finnish NA, and Dutch verbs. This demonstrates robustness beyond very small datasets and clear gains over a non-attentional encoder–decoder baseline.
- Insightful analysis: The comparison of learned alignments and encoder embeddings is valuable. The findings that soft attention encodes positional information more strongly, while hard attention can externalize position via STEP, provide a plausible explanation for the hard attention model’s better data efficiency.

Weaknesses
- Novelty and relation to prior work: Conceptually, the model can be viewed as a standard biRNN encoder with a decoder that predicts a sequence of actions conditioned on precomputed (greedy) monotone alignments. The paper would benefit from a crisper positioning relative to (i) hard/monotonic attention and transducer models (e.g., Graves’ RNN Transducer; Yu et al., 2016), (ii) neural WFSTs that marginalize over alignments (Rastogi et al., 2016), (iii) transition-based models that predict action sequences (e.g., Stack LSTMs), and (iv) Cohn et al. (2016) and related methods that inject structural bias into attention.
- Missing simple monotone baseline: A very simple alternative is to induce monotone 1-to-x (x∈{0,1,2,…}) alignments and then train a tagger over input characters to predict the aligned output substrings (e.g., flog→fliege aligned as f-l-o-g / f-l-ie-ge; then tag each input position with its output substring). A BiLSTM tagger would maintain monotonicity by construction. This baseline has been explored in related monotone string translation work (e.g., Schnober et al., 2016, Sec. 4.2). Omitting it makes it harder to isolate the value of the proposed control mechanism versus simpler monotone modeling.
- Scope of evaluation: The model is only evaluated on morphology. Since the paper’s motivation is broadly about monotone seq2seq, evaluation on other monotone tasks (e.g., transliteration, G2P, OCR post-correction) would strengthen the claim of generality.
- Alignment training objective: The model optimizes for the best derivation given a single external alignment rather than marginalizing over alignments. This could lose probability mass and robustness compared to models that sum over monotone paths (e.g., NWFST, RNN-T style training). The empirical trade-off versus marginalization is not analyzed.
- Feature confounds and reporting: The model conditions on morpho-syntactic features (e.g., POS, gender). It is unclear (i) precisely which features are used and from where they are obtained, and (ii) to what extent performance gains derive from these features rather than the hard monotone mechanism. More ablations or clearer reporting would help. Also, the phrasing “on par or better” overstates some mixed results; more precise language is preferable.

Questions to authors
1) Alignment scheme: Do you induce 1-to-many, many-to-1, and/or many-to-many character alignments? The paper text and figures suggest both 1-to-many (e.g., o→ie) and cases implying many-to-1. Please clarify the exact alignment model and constraints and how WRITE/STEP sequences are deterministically derived from them.
2) Simple monotone tagging baseline: How does your approach differ in practice from training a BiLSTM tagger over input characters to predict aligned output substrings under monotone 1-to-x alignments (as above; cf. Schnober et al., 2016)? Why was this not included as a baseline, and how do you expect it would compare?
3) Relation to prior architectures: How does your method relate to Stack LSTM-style transition systems that predict action sequences over inputs? How does it compare to Cohn et al. (2016) and other attention mechanisms with monotonicity or positional bias?
4) Alignment marginalization: What do you lose by training on a single external alignment (best-derivation) versus marginalizing over all monotone alignments/pathways as in Rastogi et al. (2016) or RNN-T? Did you observe cases where errors stem from alignment choice?
5) Feature usage: Which morpho-syntactic features are used, and what is their source in each dataset? Could feature availability explain some gains? Have you tried ablating features to quantify the contribution of the hard attention mechanism alone?
