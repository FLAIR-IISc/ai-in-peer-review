- Strengths:
The concept of hard monotonic attention is novel and markedly distinct from prior approaches.

- Weaknesses:
Experimental results on morphological inflection generation are somewhat mixed. The proposed model performs well when training data are limited (e.g., CELEX). It is also effective when alignments are largely monotonic and less context-sensitive (e.g., Russian, German, and Spanish).

- General Discussion:
The authors present a novel neural model for morphological inflection generation that uses “hard attention,” relying on character alignments obtained separately via a Bayesian transliteration method. This approach differs substantially from prior state-of-the-art neural models that use “soft attention,” where character alignment and conversion are learned jointly within a single probabilistic framework. The idea is sound and novel; the paper is clearly written, and the experiments are comprehensive. My main concern is that the proposed method is not consistently state-of-the-art across all conditions. It is best suited to tasks with predominantly monotonic alignments and fewer context-sensitive phenomena. The paper would be more convincing if it discussed practical merits of the approach, such as ease of implementation and computational cost.
