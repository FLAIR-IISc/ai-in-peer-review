- Strengths: Proposes a new encoder–decoder model that explicitly enforces monotonicity via hard monotonic attention.

- Weaknesses: The model may effectively reduce to a standard BiRNN with decoupled alignments. Evaluation is restricted to morphology; no other monotone seq2seq tasks are considered.

- General discussion:
The paper introduces a novel encoder–decoder architecture with hard monotonic attention and evaluates it on three morphological datasets. It is generally well written and clear, and the central idea—explicitly incorporating monotonicity for morphology—is appealing. The motivation is sound: unlike machine translation, many seq2seq tasks are monotone and arguably should not rely on fully general attention. The fact that generic encoder–decoder models still perform well is already a testament to neural methods; the present work instead enforces monotonic character generation by decoupling alignment and transduction, first deriving monotone input–output alignments and then training the model to generate outputs consistent with them.

However, several points remain unclear or underexplored:

1) Alignment structure: How exactly do your alignments look? In the running example (Fig. 1), the pattern appears 1-to-many: one input character can align to zero, one, or several output characters. Yet lines 311–312 suggest multiple input characters aligning to one output character. Do you use 1-to-many, many-to-1, or many-to-many alignments (and at which stage)?

2) A simple monotone baseline: A straightforward approach is to first produce monotone 1-to-many alignments (e.g., with the Jiampojamarn & Kondrak toolkit), then train a standard sequence tagger to predict these. For instance, for flog → fliege (l. 613), align f-l-o-g with f-l-ie-ge (both length 4 after expansion), then train a tagger (e.g., a BiLSTM) to map the input sequence to the aligned output sequence. This setup has been discussed before (see [*, Sec. 4.2]).
   2a) How does your approach differ substantively from this baseline?
   2b) Why did you not include such a tagger-based monotone model as a baseline?

Further issues:
3) Limitation to morphology: It is unfortunate that evaluation is only on morphological inflection. Many other monotone seq2seq tasks exist; given that you model monotonicity explicitly, broader evaluation (cf. [*]) could better demonstrate the benefits.

4) Reporting phrasing: You state “on par or better” (l. 791). There is a common tendency in NLP to label slight deficits as “on par” and improvements as “better.” I recommend more neutral wording. That said, I am otherwise satisfied with the reported results.

5) Linguistic features: Fig. 1 suggests you use linguistic features (e.g., POS).
   5a) Where do these features come from?
   5b) Could they account for some observed gains, rather than the monotonicity constraints per se?

Minor points:
6) Equation (3): please typeset NN in upright text (e.g., \text{NN}) rather than as an italic math variable.
7) l. 231: “Where” should be lowercase.
8) l. 237 and elsewhere: for sequences like x1…xn, use x1, …, xn or x1 ⋯ xn so that the dots align with surrounding symbols.
9) Fig. 1: Is the Cyrillic font necessary? The provided example is unreadable without the specific fonts.
10) l. 437: should be “these.”

AFTER AUTHOR RESPONSE
Thank you for the clarifications. I believe the alignment description may have been garbled (perhaps a coding issue), but my understanding is that you first allow 1–0, 0–1, and 1–1 links and subsequently compose these into many-to-many alignments.

I realize you compare to Nicolai, Cherry, and Kondrak (2015), but my main question was why not adopt 1–x (x ∈ {0,1,2}) monotone alignments as in Schnober et al., and then train a neural tagger (e.g., a BiLSTM) on these? I am curious how your results would compare to such a simple, strong baseline. A tagger is inherently monotone, and with monotone alignments the entire pipeline remains monotone. In contrast, your approach begins with a more general encoder–decoder and then imposes hard monotonicity constraints.
