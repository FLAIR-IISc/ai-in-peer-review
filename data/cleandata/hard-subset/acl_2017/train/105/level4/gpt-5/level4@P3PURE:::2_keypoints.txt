- Summary: Proposes an encoder–decoder with hard monotonic attention, evaluated on three morphology datasets, explicitly modeling monotonicity for monotone seq2seq tasks.
- Strength: Introduces a new model that explicitly enforces monotonicity in alignment and generation.
- Strength: Clear motivation that many seq2seq tasks are monotone, making monotonic constraints appropriate.
- Strength: Decouples alignment and transduction by aligning input–output sequences monotonically before training the transducer.
- Weakness: Approach may effectively reduce to an ordinary BiRNN with decoupled alignments, questioning novelty.
- Weakness: Evaluation is limited to morphology; no experiments on other monotone seq2seq tasks to demonstrate generality.
- Clarification needed: Unclear alignment specification — does the model use 1-to-many, many-to-1, or many-to-many alignments?
- Baseline concern: A simple baseline (monotone 1-to-many aligner + BiLSTM tagger) is missing; authors should explain differences and why it was not included.
- Scope concern: Paper should evaluate on additional monotone tasks beyond morphology to strengthen empirical claims.
- Result wording: Rephrase “on par or better” to avoid biasing interpretation of results.
- Feature confound: Linguistic features (e.g., POS) are not well described — authors should state sources and whether gains come from features rather than monotonicity.
- Minor edits: Fix typesetting of NN in Eq.(3), capitalization at l.231, notation x1,…,xn, Cyrillic font in Fig.1, and typo at l.437.
- After response: Authors clarified using 1–0, 0–1, and 1–1 steps to derive many-to-many alignments, but baseline comparison to 1–x aligner + neural tagger remains unresolved.
- AC suggestions: Cite Cohn et al. (2016), discuss relation to Stack LSTM–style action-prediction models, and address greedy alignment vs. marginalization (Rastogi et al. 2016).
- Overall assessment: Promising and well-presented work, but needs clearer alignment specification, inclusion/comparison to a simple monotone tagging baseline, broader evaluation, and discussion of related architectures and alignment strategies.
