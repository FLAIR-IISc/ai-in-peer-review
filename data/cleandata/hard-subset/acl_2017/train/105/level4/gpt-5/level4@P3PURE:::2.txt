Summary
The paper proposes a novel encoder–decoder architecture with hard monotonic attention, explicitly enforcing monotonicity in output character generation. The model is evaluated on three morphology datasets. The paper is generally well written, mostly clear, and presents an interesting and relevant idea: explicitly modeling monotonicity for morphology and other monotone sequence-to-sequence tasks.

Strengths
- Introduces a new encoder–decoder model that explicitly handles monotonicity.
- Clear motivation: many seq2seq tasks (unlike machine translation) are monotone, making monotonic constraints natural.
- Decouples alignment and transduction, aligning input–output sequences monotonically before training the transducer.

Weaknesses
- The proposed approach may reduce to an ordinary BiRNN with decoupled alignments.
- Evaluation is limited to morphology; no experiments on other monotone seq2seq tasks.

Detailed comments and questions
- Method and clarity of alignments
  1) Alignment structure: The paper seems to mix alignment types. The running example (Fig. 1) suggests 1-to-many alignments (one input character aligned to zero, one, or several output characters). However, lines 311–312 suggest several input characters aligned to one output character (many-to-1). Please clarify precisely: do you use 1-to-many, many-to-1, or many-to-many alignments?

- Baseline with simple monotone seq2seq via tagging
  2) A simple and natural baseline for monotone seq2seq is:
     - First, compute monotone 1-to-many alignments between input and output (e.g., using the Jiampojamarn and Kondrak toolkit or any monotone aligner).
     - Then train a standard sequence tagger to predict the aligned output sequence from the input sequence, positionwise. For example, for flog → fliege (your example on l.613), align “f-l-o-g / f-l-ie-ge,” and train a tagger (e.g., an LSTM/BiLSTM) to map “f-l-o-g” (length 4) to “f-l-ie-ge” (length 4). This idea has appeared in prior work; see, for instance, [*, Section 4.2].
     2a) How does your approach differ substantively from this baseline?
     2b) Why was this baseline not included in the experiments?

- Scope of evaluation
  3) It is a pity that experiments are limited to morphology, given that many other interesting monotone seq2seq tasks exist. Since your method explicitly models monotonicity, evaluating on additional monotone tasks could strengthen the empirical case (cf. also [*]).

- Result interpretation
  4) The phrasing “on par or better” (l.791) should be reconsidered. There is a general tendency in the literature to cast worse performance as “on par,” and the rest as “better.” I recommend revising the wording. Otherwise, I am fine with the reported results.

- Linguistic features
  5) The linguistic features are not well described. From Fig. 1, I infer they include POS, etc.
     5a) Where do these features come from?
     5b) Could these features be responsible for improvements in some cases, rather than the hard monotonicity constraints per se?

Minor comments
  6) Equation (3): please typeset NN as \text{NN} (or similar).
  7) l.231: “Where” should be lower case.
  8) l.237 and many more: x1…xn. Conventionally, write x1, …, xn when separating items, but x1⋯xn for multiplication. The dots should be on the same baseline as surrounding symbols.
  9) Figure 1: Is the Cyrillic font necessary? The example text is difficult to read without the specific fonts.
  10) l.437: should be “these.”

After author response
Thank you for the clarifications. It seems the alignment description in your response may have been scrambled (possibly due to a coding issue). My understanding is that you align with 1–0, 0–1, and 1–1 steps, and then derive many-to-many alignments from these.

I am aware that you compare to Nicolai, Cherry, and Kondrak (2015). However, my question is specifically about the baseline using 1–x (x ∈ {0,1,2}) monotone alignments as in Schnober et al., followed by training a neural tagger (e.g., a BiLSTM) directly on these alignments. I am curious how your results would compare to such a simple and very natural baseline. A tagger is already a monotone model, and given monotone alignments, the entire pipeline remains monotone. By contrast, your approach starts with a more general seq2seq model and then imposes hard monotonicity constraints on it.

Notes from AC
- Cohn et al. (2016) is also quite relevant: http://www.aclweb.org/anthology/N16-1102.
- Isn’t your architecture related to methods like the Stack LSTM, which similarly predicts a sequence of actions that modify or annotate an input? A discussion of similarities/differences would be helpful.
- Do you lose anything by using a greedy alignment procedure, in contrast to Rastogi et al. (2016), which also uses hard monotonic attention but marginalizes over all alignments? A comparison or analysis here would strengthen the paper.

Overall assessment
This is a well-presented paper with a clear motivation and a promising idea. The main concerns are (i) unclear alignment specification, (ii) the absence of a strong and simple monotone tagging baseline built on 1-to-many alignments, and (iii) limited evaluation beyond morphology. Addressing these points and clarifying the relationship to closely related architectures and alignment strategies would substantially improve the work.
