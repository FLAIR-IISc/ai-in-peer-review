- Proposes a seq2seq morphological inflection model with hard, nearly monotonic attention using external character-level alignments and WRITE/STEP actions.
- Trained with a best-derivation objective and simple cross-entropy loss derived from external alignments.
- Strong empirical results on CELEX, Wiktionary, and SIGMORPHON 2016, especially in low-resource settings.
- Analysis comparing representations learned by hard vs. soft attention is included.

- Strength: Excels in low-resource morphology (CELEX), outperforming recent neural and WFST baselines.
- Strength: Competitive or best average accuracy on larger Wiktionary datasets versus prior neural and non-neural baselines.
- Strength: Decoupling alignment from transduction yields simple, stable training and interpretable action sequences.
- Strength: Insightful comparison shows hard vs. soft attention produce monotonic alignments but differ in positional encoding, explaining robustness.
- Strength: Well written, reproducible code release.

- Weakness: Limited novelty—closely related to prior monotonic/transduction frameworks and transition-based systems; contribution is incremental.
- Weakness: Evaluation restricted to morphology; lacks experiments on other monotonic string-to-string tasks to show generality.
- Weakness: Missing baseline of monotone 1-to-many alignment + neural sequence tagging (e.g., BiLSTM) to compare against action-based decoder.
- Weakness: Uses best-derivation instead of marginalizing over derivations, possibly losing probability mass—no empirical assessment of this choice.
- Weakness: Ambiguities in alignment inventory and how simple links compose into many-to-many; text and figures sometimes conflict.
- Weakness: Unclear role of morpho-syntactic features—no feature ablations to attribute gains to hard attention vs. features.

- Q: Precisely specify allowed alignment links, composition rules, and handling of deletions/insertions and one-to-many outputs.
- Q: Did you try marginalizing over derivations or alternatives, and what is the empirical impact of best-derivation training?
- Q: Decoding details—greedy vs. beam search, and sensitivity to beam size?
- Q: Why not include a monotone alignment + BiLSTM tagging baseline (Schnober et al., 2016), and how would it compare?
- Q: What are the sources and embedding/usage of morpho-syntactic features; provide ablations with/without features.
- Q: Have you evaluated on transliteration or other monotone tasks; if not, what obstacles exist?
- Q: Clarify relations to Cohn et al. (2016), transition-based architectures (Stack-LSTM), and Rastogi et al. (2016) who sum over alignments.

- Minor: Make claims precise (avoid “on par or better”), fix notation/typos (Eq.3, commas, fonts), and avoid Cyrillic in figures.
- Minor: Include a concrete example mapping input→actions→output for 1-to-many and deletion cases and a brief complexity comparison vs. soft attention.

- Overall: Solid, well-executed paper with strong empirical results and useful analysis; addressing baseline gap, alignment clarity, and broader evaluation would strengthen impact.
