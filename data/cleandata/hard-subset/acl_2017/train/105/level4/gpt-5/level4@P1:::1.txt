Summary and contributions:
- The paper proposes a neural model for morphological inflection generation that uses hard monotonic attention. Unlike prior soft-attention encoder–decoders that learn alignment and transduction jointly, this model decouples them: it relies on externally learned character alignments (via a Bayesian transliteration aligner) to supervise a simple step/write control mechanism over a biLSTM encoder–decoder.
- Empirically, the model achieves strong results on multiple datasets. It is particularly effective in low-resource settings (e.g., CELEX) and on languages where alignments are mostly monotonic and less context sensitive (e.g., Russian, German, Spanish). Overall, the approach is novel and substantially different from prior neural models for the task.

Strengths:
- Novel and sound modeling idea: The hard monotonic attention mechanism, trained using externally obtained character alignments, is a clear departure from standard soft attention. The control scheme (step vs. write) matches the near-monotonic character alignments common in inflection, making the inductive bias well aligned with the task.
- Strong performance where monotonicity and data scarcity matter: The model performs best in low-resource scenarios such as CELEX and on suffixing languages with limited long-range dependencies (e.g., Russian, German, Spanish). This is where soft attention often overfits or fails to exploit monotonic structure; the proposed model’s bias is beneficial.
- Clear presentation and comprehensive experiments: The paper is clearly written and evaluates on three established datasets with competitive baselines (neural and non-neural). The comparisons highlight both the advantages in low-resource and monotonic settings and where the model is less effective, giving a balanced view.

Weaknesses:
- Mixed empirical results across languages/phenomena: While the model excels on CELEX and several suffixing languages, it is not state of the art across all conditions. On languages exhibiting more context-sensitive processes (e.g., vowel/consonant harmony), soft attention tends to perform better. This limits the method’s universal applicability.
- Benefit conditioned on data size and alignment type: The gains are most pronounced with small training sets and nearly monotonic alignments. In settings with ample data or strong non-monotonic dependencies, the advantage diminishes or reverses, suggesting the inductive bias can become restrictive.
- Limited discussion of practical merits: The paper would be more persuasive if it detailed practical considerations such as ease of implementation, training/inference efficiency, and computational cost compared to soft-attention models. Given the added alignment step and the simplified decoding objective, concrete runtime and resource comparisons would help.

Questions to authors (for rebuttal):
- Can you provide training and inference time, memory usage, and convergence behavior compared to a strong soft-attention baseline on the same hardware? How does the added alignment step affect end-to-end wall-clock time?
- How simple is the model to implement in practice relative to soft attention? For example, what additional engineering is required to integrate the external alignment tool, and how does this impact reproducibility?
- Do you have ablations quantifying the sensitivity of performance to the quality of the external alignments? For instance, how robust is the model to noisy or imperfect alignments?

Overall assessment:
This is a well-written paper with a genuinely novel hard-attention formulation tailored to the near-monotonic nature of morphological inflection. The empirical results are strong in the regimes and languages where the inductive bias matches the data (small training sets; mostly monotonic transformations), but the approach is not uniformly superior across all morphological phenomena. Adding a clear account of implementation simplicity and computational trade-offs would strengthen the case for adoption.
