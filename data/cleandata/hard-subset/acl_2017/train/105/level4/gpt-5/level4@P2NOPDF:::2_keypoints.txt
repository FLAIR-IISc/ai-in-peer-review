- Strength: Proposes a new encoder–decoder architecture with hard monotonic attention that explicitly models monotonicity.

- Weakness: Model may effectively reduce to a standard BiRNN with a decoupled alignment component.

- Weakness: Evaluation is limited to morphological inflection only; no other monotone seq2seq tasks considered.

- Contribution: Introduces model evaluated on three morphological datasets; idea of enforcing explicit monotonicity for morphology is novel and sensible.

- Clarity issue: Unclear what primitive alignment types are (1-to-0/1/2+, many-to-1, or many-to-many) and what constraints are enforced during training and decoding.

- Baseline omission: Reviewer proposes a simple monotone baseline (compute 1-to-many character alignments then train a sequence tagger) and asks why this tagger-based monotone model was not included.

- Request for comparison: Ask how the authors’ method differs substantively from the straightforward 1-to-many alignment + tagger baseline and why it wasn’t evaluated.

- Scope concern: Recommend adding other monotone tasks (transliteration, OCR post-correction, spelling normalization, phoneme–grapheme conversion) to strengthen empirical claims.

- Reporting clarity: “On par or better” is misleading—report per-dataset where the method is better or worse rather than collapsing results.

- Feature confound: Figure 1 implies POS/other features—ask for the source of these features and whether gains could be due to features rather than monotonic attention; request analysis/ablation.

- Minor formatting/editing issues: Eq.(3) typesetting of NN, capitalization (“Where”→“where”), ellipses formatting, Figure 1 font/readability, and wording (“these” vs “this” at l.437).

- After-response clarification: Authors appear to produce 1–0, 0–1, 1–1 primitives combined into many-to-many; reviewer still asks why not use constrained 1–x alignments with a neural tagger that enforces monotonicity end-to-end.

- Related-work requests: Discuss relevance of Cohn et al. (2016) and clarify comparisons to action-based models (e.g., Stack LSTM) in terms of action space, objective, and alignment uncertainty.

- Alignment-marginalization concern: You use greedy alignment—ask whether this loses accuracy compared to methods that marginalize over all monotone alignments (e.g., Rastogi et al., 2016) and whether training could sum over alignments.

- Overall verdict: Promising, well-written paper but main concerns are alignment clarity, omission of a strong simple monotone tagger baseline, and narrow empirical scope; addressing these would significantly strengthen the work.
