- Proposes a neural morphological inflection model using hard monotonic attention supervised by externally learned character alignments rather than joint soft-attention learning.  
- Empirically strong on multiple datasets, especially in low-resource settings and languages with mostly monotonic alignments (e.g., CELEX, Russian, German, Spanish).  
- Novel modeling idea: hard monotonic attention with a step/write control matches near-monotonic inflectional alignments.  
- Performs best where monotonicity and data scarcity matter, avoiding soft-attention overfitting in those regimes.  
- Clear presentation and comprehensive experiments across three established datasets with competitive baselines.  
- Not state-of-the-art across all languages; soft attention outperforms on context-sensitive phenomena like vowel/consonant harmony.  
- Benefits are conditional on small data and near-monotonic alignments; advantage diminishes or reverses with abundant data or non-monotonic dependencies.  
- Limited discussion of practical merits such as implementation complexity, runtime, memory, and training/inference efficiency.  
- Question: provide training/inference time, memory usage, and convergence comparisons to soft-attention baselines on the same hardware.  
- Question: clarify implementation complexity and engineering required to integrate the external alignment tool and its impact on reproducibility.  
- Question: include ablations on sensitivity to alignment quality and robustness to noisy or imperfect alignments.  
- Overall: well-written, genuinely novel hard-attention approach that excels when its inductive bias matches the data but is not universally superior; stronger practical/computational analysis would aid adoption.
