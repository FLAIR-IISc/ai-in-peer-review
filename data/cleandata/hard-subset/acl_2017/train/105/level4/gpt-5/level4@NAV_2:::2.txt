Summary
The paper proposes an encoder–decoder architecture with hard, nearly monotonic attention for morphological inflection generation. The model decouples alignment and transduction: it first obtains character-level monotone alignments externally and then trains a decoder that alternates between WRITE and STEP actions over a biLSTM-encoded input. The approach is evaluated on CELEX, Wiktionary, and SIGMORPHON 2016 datasets, achieving competitive or state-of-the-art accuracy, with particularly strong gains in low-resource settings. The authors also analyze learned alignments and encoder representations, comparing to soft attention, and argue that the hard-attention model learns similarly monotone alignments while being simpler and less prone to overfitting.

Contributions (as I see them)
1) An explicit hard monotonic attention encoder–decoder that uses externally induced character alignments to control a simple STEP/WRITE transduction process, enabling straightforward cross-entropy training.
2) Strong empirical performance across three morphology benchmarks, notably surpassing prior neural and non-neural systems on low-resource CELEX and being competitive on larger datasets (with advantages in suffixing+stem-change languages).
3) An analysis of learned alignments and encodings that sheds light on positional and symbol-type information and highlights similarities and differences relative to soft attention.

Strengths
- Alignment bias matches task structure and improves generalization in low-resource settings. On CELEX, the model substantially reduces overfitting relative to soft attention (dev > train gap) and outperforms the morphology-specific latent-variable WFST (Hard avg 89.44 vs LAT 88.30), as well as recent neural baselines. The learning-curve evidence supports the claim that enforcing monotonicity helps when data are scarce.
- Simple, transparent control and training. By learning alignments externally and predicting a single action sequence, the model avoids the complexity of joint alignment-and-transduction (e.g., Yu et al., 2016) while achieving higher accuracy on Wiktionary and matching or exceeding soft attention on several SIGMORPHON languages. The action space directly captures operations such as deletion and one-to-many expansions, leading to interpretable derivations (e.g., legte→lege via consecutive STEPs; flog→fliege via multiple WRITEs).
- Broad and careful evaluation for morphology. Results cover three established datasets with multiple languages and morphological phenomena, including typologically diverse settings in SIGMORPHON. The pattern that hard attention excels in suffixing+stem-change languages is plausible and well-argued.
- Informative analysis. The comparison of encoder representations indicates both models capture symbol identity and position; soft attention appears to encode position more strongly, consistent with its attention mechanism. This helps contextualize when hard attention may have an advantage.

Weaknesses
- Limited novelty relative to existing monotone transduction paradigms. Conceptually, the method can be seen as a standard biRNN encoder with a decoupled, externally supervised monotone control signal. The relationship to RNN-Transducer/Neural Transducer/online segment-to-segment models, WFST approaches with neural scoring (Rastogi et al., 2016), and transition-based models could be articulated more sharply to clarify the incremental contribution.
- Missing simple monotone-tagger baseline. A straightforward baseline is to induce 1–x (x∈{0,1,2}) monotone alignments (e.g., as in Schnober et al., 2016, Sec. 4.2) and train a (biLSTM) sequence tagger to predict the aligned outputs position-wise. This would test whether the proposed action-based controller is necessary beyond a simpler monotone predictor; its absence makes it harder to attribute gains specifically to the hard attention mechanism.
- Scope restricted to morphology. Given the explicit modeling of monotonicity, evaluation on other monotone string transduction tasks (e.g., transliteration, monotone string translation) would strengthen the generality claims.
- Clarity on alignment assumptions and supervision. The paper alternately describes aligning “several input characters to one output character” and showing examples with 1-to-many output expansions. A precise, consistent statement of the alignment class used (allowable 0–1/1–0/1–1, and how many-to-many are derived) is needed.
- Objective approximates best derivation. The model optimizes the best-derivation path rather than marginalizing over derivations for a given output string. A discussion or ablation comparing to marginalization (as in Rastogi et al., 2016, which sums over alignments) would help assess what is lost or gained by the greedy alignment supervision.

Questions to Authors (to be addressed in rebuttal)
- Alignment scheme: Do you permit 1–0, 0–1, and 1–1 atomic links and then deterministically compose them into many-to-many? Or do you directly induce 1–x (x≥0) alignments? Please provide a precise definition and reconcile the apparent tension between many-to-one vs one-to-many descriptions.
- Simple monotone tagger baseline: How does your approach differ in practice from training a biLSTM tagger on induced 1–x monotone alignments (e.g., Schnober et al., 2016, Sec. 4.2)? Can you include such a baseline to quantify the added value of the explicit STEP/WRITE controller?
- Features: What is the exact source of the morpho-syntactic features (POS, gender, tense, etc.) for each dataset? Have you run ablations to determine whether feature conditioning (vs. the hard-attention bias) explains your gains in some settings?
- Relation to transition-based models: How is your action prediction related to Stack-LSTMs and other transition systems that predict sequences of actions over an input? Are there advantages or limitations in expressivity or training stability?
- Greedy alignments vs marginalization: Do you have evidence that using a single alignment per pair is sufficient? Would training with forward–backward over alignments (as in Yu et al., 2016; Rastogi et al., 2016) change outcomes?

After author response
Thank you for the clarifications. My understanding is that atomic links include 1–0, 0–1, and 1–1, which you then compose into many-to-many alignments to derive the oracle action sequence. My primary remaining concern is the missing baseline using induced 1–x monotone alignments with a neural tagger (e.g., biLSTM). A tagger is inherently monotone given such supervision; in contrast, your model begins with a more general encoder–decoder and imposes hard monotonicity via actions. Including this comparison would better isolate the benefit of the proposed control mechanism. Also relevant to situate your work are Cohn et al. (2016) on structural alignment biases and transition-based architectures (e.g., Stack LSTM). Finally, please discuss whether using a single greedy alignment (rather than summing over alignments) might miss useful uncertainty, in contrast to Rastogi et al. (2016).
