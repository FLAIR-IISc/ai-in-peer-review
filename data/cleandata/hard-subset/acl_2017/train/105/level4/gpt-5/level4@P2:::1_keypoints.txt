- Proposes a neural encoder–decoder for morphological inflection using hard, nearly monotonic attention with WRITE/STEP decoder actions supervised by external character alignments.
- Demonstrates strong performance on CELEX, Wiktionary, and SIGMORPHON 2016, notably excelling in low-resource and largely suffixing languages and outperforming a tailored WFST on CELEX.
- Analyzes learned representations and alignments, comparing hard vs. soft attention and showing how position and character-type are encoded.

Strengths:
- Novel, clear modeling idea: hard monotonic attention with explicit STEP/WRITE control that matches near-monotonic inflection processes.
- Strong low-resource performance: outperforms soft-attention and WFST baselines on very small datasets with less overfitting.
- Competitive on larger datasets: best average accuracy on Wiktionary benchmarks across inflection types.
- Simple, sound training: uses external alignments to derive oracle actions and trains with standard cross-entropy, avoiding complex marginalization.
- Useful analyses: alignment visualizations and representation probes support modeling assumptions and explain generalization benefits.

Weaknesses:
- Mixed results by morphology: underperforms soft-attention on languages with non-monotonic or context-sensitive phenomena (harmony, templatic morphology).
- Reliance on external alignments: pipeline dependency risks error propagation; sensitivity to alignment quality is not quantified.
- Best-derivation approximation: decoding maximizes a single action sequence rather than marginalizing over derivations, and its impact is not measured.
- Missing efficiency metrics: no reporting of training/inference time, memory footprint, or alignment preprocessing cost.
- Incomplete ablations/fairness checks: comparisons may conflate modeling gains with training schedule or configuration differences.

Questions for authors:
- How sensitive are results to the choice and quality of the external aligner; have alternative or noisy alignments been tested?
- What decoding strategy is used (greedy vs. beam), what beam widths, and does beam search or the best-derivation approximation affect accuracy/speed?
- What are training and inference runtimes and memory usage, including one-time alignment costs, relative to soft-attention baselines?
- How does the model handle truly non-monotonic operations (e.g., templatic patterns); would limited reordering actions help?
- Were ablations performed on feature embeddings, decoder feedback, or encoder size to isolate sources of gains on CELEX?

Overall assessment:
- Well-written and novel work that fits monotonic inflection tasks and yields clear low-resource benefits, but generality and robustness concerns plus missing efficiency analyses should be addressed for stronger impact.
