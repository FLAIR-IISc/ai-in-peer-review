- Proposes an encoder–decoder with hard monotonic attention using externally learned character alignments and a STEP/WRITE controller.  
- Achieves strong empirical results on CELEX, Wiktionary, and SIGMORPHON, especially in low-resource settings.  
- Provides qualitative analyses of learned alignments and encoder representations for hard vs. soft attention.

Contributions:
- Practical hard-attention architecture that enforces monotonicity via precomputed alignments and a STEP/WRITE controller.  
- Strong empirical performance, beating or matching prior work (notably on CELEX).  
- Informative qualitative analysis comparing hard and soft attention.

Strengths:
- Effectively leverages monotonicity, improving generalization and reducing overfitting in low-resource regimes.  
- Sets or matches state-of-the-art on multiple benchmarks.  
- Simpler training by decoupling alignment learning from sequence prediction (no marginalization).  
- Clear exposition and useful analyses that support modeling choices.

Weaknesses:
- Novelty unclear relative to simpler monotone baselines (e.g., biLSTM tagger on 1-to-x alignments).  
- Evaluation limited to morphology; other monotone tasks (transliteration, G2P) are missing.  
- Alignment specification is ambiguous regarding allowed cardinalities and conversion to STEP/WRITE.  
- Unclear source and content of morpho-syntactic features, risking confound with feature conditioning.  
- Training optimizes a best-derivation path instead of marginalizing over alignments; trade-offs not fully explored.

Questions to authors:
- What exact alignment formats (1–0/1/2+, many-to-one, many-to-many) are used and how are they deterministically converted to STEP/WRITE?  
- How does the method differ in practice from a pipeline that computes monotone 1-to-x alignments and trains a neural tagger; why no such baseline?  
- What morpho-syntactic features were provided, what are their sources, and were ablations run to measure their contribution?  
- Any empirical comparison between best-derivation training and marginalizing-over-alignments approaches (e.g., Rastogi et al., 2016)?  
- How does the action-based controller relate to transition/Stack-LSTM models and to segmental/monotonic attention methods (e.g., Cohn et al., 2016)?

Additional comments:
- Avoid vague phrasing like “on par or better”; report specific wins and losses.  
- Paper would be stronger with at least one additional monotone task (e.g., transliteration or G2P).
