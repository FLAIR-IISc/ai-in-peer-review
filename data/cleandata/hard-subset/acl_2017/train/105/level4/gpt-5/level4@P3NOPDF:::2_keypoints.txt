- Summary: Proposes an encoder–decoder with hard monotonic attention that decouples monotone alignments from generation; shows competitive/improved results on three morphological datasets.
- Strength: Clear motivation and straightforward, implementable architecture for character-level monotone transduction.
- Strength: Solid empirical performance on morphological inflection and lemmatization tasks.
- Strength: Paper is well written and easy to follow.
- Weakness: Limited conceptual novelty—similar to a BiRNN transducer with externally imposed, decoupled alignments and hard attention.
- Weakness: Evaluation restricted to morphology; no experiments on other monotone seq2seq tasks (e.g., G2P, transliteration, OCR correction).
- Weakness: Key modeling choices and baselines under-explained, particularly alignment representation and missing monotone sequence-tagging baseline.
- Weakness: Limited analysis separating gains from linguistic features vs. monotonicity constraint (need ablations).
- Clarification request: Specify allowed elementary alignment links (e.g., 1–0, 0–1, 1–1) and whether training uses 1-to-many, many-to-1, or many-to-many representations.
- Clarification request: Explain whether decoding requires splitting/merging input positions and how that maps to the action space.
- Clarification request / baseline: Compare against a monotone sequence-tagging pipeline (1-to-many alignment + per-input tagging) and justify its absence as a baseline.
- Clarification request: Broaden evaluation to additional monotone seq2seq tasks to demonstrate generality beyond morphology.
- Reporting request: Replace vague “on par or better” phrasing with explicit wins/ties/losses and margins, with statistical significance where applicable.
- Clarification request: Report sources of linguistic features (e.g., POS/morph tags) and run ablations to test their contribution vs. monotonicity.
- Related work: Discuss connections/comparisons to Schnober et al. (2016), Nicolai et al. (2015), Cohn et al. (2016), stack LSTM/transition systems, and Rastogi et al. (2016) regarding soft vs. hard biases and marginalization vs. greedy alignments.
- Specific concern: Quantify trade-offs of greedy single-alignment commitment vs. marginalization over alignments (efficiency vs. modeling capacity/robustness).
- After author response: Authors appear to permit 1–0, 0–1, 1–1 composed into spans—camera-ready should state this unambiguously; main remaining concern is lack of tagging-style monotone baseline.
- Minor comments: Fix equation typesetting, capitalization, ellipses formatting, figure fonts, small wording issues, and provide dataset/feature details for reproducibility.
- Overall assessment: Timely, well-motivated work with promising results; needs a monotone tagging baseline, broader evaluation, clearer alignment description, and feature ablations to strengthen contribution.
