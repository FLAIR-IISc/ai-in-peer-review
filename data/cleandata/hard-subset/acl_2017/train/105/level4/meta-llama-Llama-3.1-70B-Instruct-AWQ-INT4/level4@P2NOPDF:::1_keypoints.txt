- Novelty: proposes a "hard monotonic attention" mechanism that is substantially different and offers a fresh perspective for morphological inflection generation.  
- Strength in low-data/monotonic settings: model is effective with limited training data (e.g., CELEX) and when alignments are predominantly monotonic and less context-sensitive.  
- Mixed empirical performance: experimental results are mixed; the method is not consistently superior across all languages and conditions.  
- Language-specific success: shows strong performance on languages/tasks with monotonic alignments (e.g., Russian, German, Spanish).  
- Methodological difference: uses hard attention with Bayesian-derived character alignments for transliteration, unlike state-of-the-art soft-attention models that jointly align and convert.  
- Paper quality: idea is well-conceived, clearly written, and evaluated with comprehensive experiments.  
- Practical evaluation gap: lacks detailed discussion of practical advantages such as ease of implementation and computational cost.  
- Applicability limitation: best suited to monotonic, less context-sensitive phenomena and may not represent universal state-of-the-art.
