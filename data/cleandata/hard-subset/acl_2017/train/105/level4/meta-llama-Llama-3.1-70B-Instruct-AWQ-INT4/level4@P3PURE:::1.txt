Strengths:
The proposed hard monotonic attention mechanism is a novel and substantially different approach from existing methods, offering a fresh perspective on morphological inflection generation.

Weaknesses:
The experimental results on morphological inflection generation are mixed. While the proposed model is effective in certain scenarios, such as when the amount of training data is limited (e.g., CELEX) or when the alignment is mostly monotonic and less context-sensitive (e.g., Russian, German, and Spanish), its performance is not consistently superior across all conditions.

General Discussion:
The authors introduce a novel neural model for morphological inflection generation, leveraging "hard attention" and character alignments obtained through a Bayesian method for transliteration. This approach deviates significantly from previous state-of-the-art neural models, which rely on "soft attention" and jointly solve character alignment and conversion within a probabilistic framework.

The idea is sound, and the paper is well-written. The experimental evaluation is comprehensive, covering various aspects of the proposed method. However, a notable concern is that the proposed approach may not necessarily represent the state-of-the-art in all scenarios. Its suitability appears to be limited to tasks with mostly monotonic alignments and less context-sensitive phenomena. To strengthen the paper, the authors could provide a more detailed discussion on the practical merits of their proposed method, including its ease of implementation and computational cost.
