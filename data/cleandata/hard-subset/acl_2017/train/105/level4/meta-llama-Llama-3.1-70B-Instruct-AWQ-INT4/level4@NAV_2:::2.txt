**Summary of the Paper**

This paper proposes a neural model for morphological inflection generation that employs a hard attention mechanism, inspired by the nearly-monotonic alignment commonly found between the characters in a word and the characters in its inflection. The model is evaluated on three previously studied morphological inflection generation datasets and shows state-of-the-art results in various setups compared to previous neural and non-neural approaches.

**Main Contributions**

1. The authors propose a hard attention model for nearly-monotonic sequence to sequence learning, as common in the morphological inflection setting.
2. The model is evaluated on the task of morphological inflection generation, establishing a new state of the art on three previously studied datasets for the task.
3. The authors perform an analysis and comparison of their model and the soft-attention model, shedding light on the features such models extract for the inflection generation task.

**Strengths**

1. The authors propose a novel encoder-decoder neural network architecture with "hard monotonic attention" that explicitly takes into account monotonicity, which is a key aspect of morphological inflection generation.
2. The model performs on par or better than previous neural and non-neural approaches on various morphological inflection generation datasets.
3. The authors provide a thorough analysis and comparison of their model and the soft-attention model, which helps to understand the strengths and weaknesses of each approach.

**Weaknesses**

1. The model is only evaluated on morphology datasets, and it would be interesting to see how it performs on other monotonic sequence-to-sequence tasks.
2. The authors do not provide a clear explanation of how their approach differs from a simpler approach of using a standard sequence tagger to predict 1-to-many alignments.
3. The authors do not provide enough information about their linguistic features and how they were obtained.

**Questions to Authors**

1. How do your alignments look like? Do you use 1-to-many, many-to-1, or many-to-many alignments?
2. How does your approach differ from a simpler approach of using a standard sequence tagger to predict 1-to-many alignments?
3. Why did you not include this simpler approach as a baseline?
4. Can you provide more information about your linguistic features and how they were obtained?
5. Is it possible that these features are responsible for your better performance in some cases, rather than the monotonicity constraints?
