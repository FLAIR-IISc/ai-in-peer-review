The authors propose a novel encoder-decoder neural network architecture that incorporates "hard monotonic attention" to explicitly model monotonicity in sequence-to-sequence tasks. This paper is well-written and presents a clear idea, which is to enforce monotonic output character generation by decoupling alignment and transduction. The authors first align input-output sequences monotonically and then train the model to generate outputs in agreement with these monotone alignments.

However, there are several concerns and questions regarding the proposed approach. Firstly, the authors' description of the alignment process is unclear. The alignments seem to be of the 1-to-many type, where one input character can be aligned with zero, one, or several output characters. However, this contradicts the description in lines 311-312, which suggests that several input characters are aligned to one output character. It would be helpful to clarify whether the authors use 1-to-many, many-to-1, or many-to-many alignments.

Furthermore, a simpler approach to monotone sequence-to-sequence tasks is to use a monotone aligner to align input and output characters with a 1-to-many constraint, and then train a standard sequence tagger to predict these alignments. This approach may have been suggested in previous papers, such as Schnober et al. (2016). The authors' approach differs from this simple idea, but it is unclear why they did not include it as a baseline.

Additionally, the authors only evaluate their model on morphology tasks, which is a limitation. There are many other interesting monotonic sequence-to-sequence tasks that could have been used to demonstrate the superiority of their approach.

The experimental results show that the authors' model performs "on par or better" than other models. However, this wording is misleading, as it implies that the authors' model is always better or equal to other models. A more accurate description would be that the authors' model performs better in some cases and worse in others.

The authors also do not provide sufficient information about their linguistic features. From Figure 1, it can be inferred that they include part-of-speech tags, but it is unclear where these features come from and whether they contribute to the authors' better performance in some cases.

Minor points include the use of "NN" instead of "NN" or similar in Equation (3), and the inconsistent use of dots in mathematical expressions.

After the author response, it is clear that the authors' alignments are more complex than initially described, involving 1-0, 0-1, 1-1, and many-to-many alignments. However, the question remains why the authors did not use a simpler approach, such as 1-x alignments and a neural tagger, as a baseline.

The authors' architecture is also related to other methods, such as the Stack LSTM, which predicts a sequence of actions that modify or annotate an input. Additionally, the use of a greedy alignment may be a limitation, as it does not sum over all possible alignments like Rastogi et al. (2016).

Strengths: The authors propose a novel encoder-decoder neural network architecture that explicitly models monotonicity.

Weaknesses: The authors' description of the alignment process is unclear, and the model is only evaluated on morphology tasks. The authors do not provide sufficient information about their linguistic features, and the experimental results are misleading.

General Discussion: The authors' approach is a good start, but it requires further clarification and evaluation on other monotonic sequence-to-sequence tasks. The use of a simpler baseline and more accurate description of the experimental results would also improve the paper.
