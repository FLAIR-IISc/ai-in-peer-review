**Strengths and Weaknesses**

The paper proposes a novel encoder-decoder neural network architecture that explicitly incorporates monotonicity, a desirable property in many sequence-to-sequence tasks. However, a potential weakness of the model is that it may be equivalent to an ordinary BiRNN with decoupled alignments.

**General Discussion**

The authors present a well-written and clear paper that introduces a new idea: incorporating monotonicity into morphology tasks using "hard monotonic attention." They evaluate their approach on three morphology datasets. The motivation for including monotonicity is clear: many sequence-to-sequence tasks exhibit monotonicity, and general encoder-decoder models may not be the best choice for these tasks.

The authors' approach involves decoupling alignment and transduction, first aligning input-output sequences monotonically and then training to generate outputs in agreement with the monotone alignments. However, some aspects of their approach require clarification.

**Questions and Concerns**

1. The authors' description of their alignment process is unclear. Specifically, it is unclear whether they use 1-to-many, many-to-1, or many-to-many alignments. The running example in Figure 1 suggests 1-to-many alignments, but the text in lines 311-312 describes many-to-1 alignments.

2. A simpler approach to monotone sequence-to-sequence tasks involves using a monotone aligner to align input and output characters and then training a standard sequence tagger to predict these alignments. This approach may have been suggested in previous work, such as [Schnober et al., 2016, Section 4.2]. Two questions arise: (a) How does the authors' approach differ from this simpler idea? (b) Why did the authors not include this approach as a baseline?

**Additional Issues**

3. The authors only evaluate their approach on morphology tasks, despite the existence of other interesting monotonic sequence-to-sequence tasks. Evaluating their approach on these tasks could have demonstrated its superiority.

4. The authors' claim that their approach performs "on par or better" than other methods is problematic. This wording may reflect a cognitive bias among NLP researchers to map instances where they perform worse to "on par" and all other instances to "better."

5. The authors provide limited information about their linguistic features, which appear to include POS tags. Two questions arise: (a) Where did the authors obtain these features? (b) Is it possible that these features, rather than the monotonicity constraints, are responsible for the authors' better performance in some cases?

**Minor Points**

6. Equation (3) should be rewritten to use a more standard notation, such as $\text{NN}$.

7. The text in line 231 should be rewritten to use a lowercase "where."

8. The notation $x1\ldots xn$ should be rewritten to use a more standard notation, such as $x1,\ldots,xn$ or $x1\cdots xn$.

9. The use of Cyrillic font in Figure 1 is unnecessary and may make it difficult for readers to understand the example.

10. The text in line 437 should be rewritten to use the correct pronoun "these."

**After Author Response**

The authors' response clarifies some aspects of their approach, but raises additional questions. Specifically, the authors' alignments appear to be 1-0, 0-1, 1-1, and later many-to-many alignments. However, the authors do not address the question of why they did not use a simpler approach, such as training a neural tagger on monotone alignments. This approach would have provided a more direct comparison to the authors' method.

**Notes from AC**

The authors' architecture is also related to methods like the Stack LSTM, which predicts a sequence of actions that modify or annotate an input. Additionally, the authors' use of greedy alignment may be a limitation, as Rastogi et al. (2016) also use hard monotonic attention but sum over all alignments.
