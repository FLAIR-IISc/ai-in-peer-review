**Summary of the Paper**

This paper proposes a novel neural model for morphological inflection generation that employs a hard attention mechanism. The model is designed to capture the nearly-monotonic alignment between input and output character sequences commonly found in morphological inflection generation tasks. The authors evaluate their model on three previously studied datasets and show that it provides state-of-the-art results in various setups compared to previous neural and non-neural approaches.

**Main Contributions**

1. The authors propose a hard attention model for nearly-monotonic sequence-to-sequence learning, which is particularly well-suited for morphological inflection generation tasks.
2. They evaluate their model on three previously studied morphological inflection generation datasets and establish a new state-of-the-art on these datasets.
3. They perform an analysis and comparison of their model and the soft-attention model, shedding light on the features such models extract for the inflection generation task.

**Strengths**

1. The paper proposes a novel and well-motivated approach to morphological inflection generation that takes into account the monotonicity of the task.
2. The authors provide a thorough evaluation of their model on three previously studied datasets and compare it to several state-of-the-art baselines.
3. The paper includes a detailed analysis of the learned alignments and representations, which provides insights into how the model works and why it is effective.

**Weaknesses**

1. The authors do not provide a clear explanation of how their approach differs from a simple sequence tagger approach that uses monotone alignments.
2. The paper only evaluates the model on morphology datasets and does not explore its applicability to other monotonic sequence-to-sequence tasks.
3. The authors do not provide a detailed comparison of their model to other monotonic sequence-to-sequence models, such as the Stack LSTM.

**Questions to Authors**

1. Can you provide a clearer explanation of how your approach differs from a simple sequence tagger approach that uses monotone alignments?
2. Why did you not include a simple sequence tagger approach as a baseline in your evaluation?
3. How do you think your model would perform on other monotonic sequence-to-sequence tasks, such as abstractive summarization or machine translation?
4. Can you provide more details on how you obtained the linguistic features used in your model, and whether these features are responsible for the model's better performance in some cases?
5. Do you think your model loses anything by using a greedy alignment, in contrast to Rastogi et al. (2016), which also has hard monotonic attention but sums over all alignments?
