**Strengths:** The authors propose a novel encoder-decoder model that explicitly incorporates monotonicity, a valuable contribution to the field of sequence-to-sequence tasks.

**Weaknesses:** One potential concern is that the model may be equivalent to an ordinary bidirectional RNN with decoupled alignments. Furthermore, the evaluation is limited to morphology tasks, neglecting other monotonic sequence-to-sequence tasks.

**General Discussion:**
The authors introduce a new encoder-decoder neural network architecture featuring "hard monotonic attention." They evaluate this architecture on three morphology datasets, presenting a well-written and clear paper with a novel idea. The inclusion of monotonicity is justified, as many sequence-to-sequence tasks exhibit monotonicity, and general encoder-decoder models may not be the best fit. The authors' approach explicitly enforces monotonic output character generation by decoupling alignment and transduction, first aligning input-output sequences monotonically and then training to generate outputs in agreement with these alignments.

However, the authors' description of their alignment process is unclear, prompting several questions:

1. What is the nature of the alignments used in the model? The running example (Fig. 1) suggests 1-to-many alignments, whereas the description in lines 311-312 implies many-to-1 alignments.
2. A simpler approach to monotonic sequence-to-sequence tasks involves first aligning input and output characters monotonically with a 1-to-many constraint using a monotone aligner, and then training a standard sequence tagger to predict these alignments. This approach may have been suggested in previous work, such as [Schnober et al., 2016, Section 4.2]. Two questions arise:
   a. How does the authors' approach differ from this simpler idea?
   b. Why was this approach not included as a baseline?

Additional concerns and suggestions:

3. It is unfortunate that the evaluation is limited to morphology tasks, as there are many other interesting monotonic sequence-to-sequence tasks that could have been used to demonstrate the system's superiority.
4. The claim that the system performs "on par or better" (line 791) may be misleading, as it may mask instances where the system performs worse. It would be more accurate to report the results without this mapping.
5. The authors provide limited information about their linguistic features, which appear to include POS tags (inferred from Fig. 1).
   a. Where did the authors obtain these features?
   b. Is it possible that these features, rather than the monotonicity constraints, are responsible for the better performance in some cases?

Minor points:

6. Equation (3) should be rewritten with "NN" in a consistent font.
7. Line 231 should start with a lowercase "where."
8. The math notation in lines 237 and elsewhere should follow the recommended format, with dots on the same level as surrounding symbols.
9. The use of Cyrillic font in Figure 1 is unnecessary and may cause issues for some readers.
10. Line 437 should read "these" instead of "these".

**Reference:**
Schnober, Carsten, et al. "Still not there? Comparing Traditional Sequence-to-Sequence Models to Encoder-Decoder Neural Networks on Monotone String Translation Tasks." Proceedings of COLING 2016, the 26th International Conference on Computational Linguistics: Technical Papers, 2016.

**AFTER AUTHOR RESPONSE:**
The authors' response clarifies some points, but also raises new questions. The alignments seem to be a mix of 1-0, 0-1, 1-1, and many-to-many alignments. The reviewer wonders why the authors did not use 1-x (x in 0,1,2) alignments, as in Schnober et al., and train a neural tagger on these alignments. This approach would be a more straightforward baseline, and it would be interesting to see how the results would differ.

**NOTES FROM AC:**
Other relevant work includes Cohn et al. (2016) and Nicolai et al. (2015). The architecture is also related to methods like the Stack LSTM, which predicts a sequence of actions that modify or annotate an input. The use of greedy alignment, as opposed to summing over all alignments, may be a limitation of the approach.
