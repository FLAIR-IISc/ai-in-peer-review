- Strengths: The authors propose a novel encoder-decoder model that explicitly incorporates monotonicity, offering a unique approach to sequence-to-sequence tasks.

- Weaknesses: The model's novelty may be disputed, as it could be perceived as a standard BiRNN with decoupled alignments. Furthermore, the evaluation is limited to morphology tasks, neglecting other monotonic sequence-to-sequence tasks.

- General Discussion:

The authors introduce a new encoder-decoder neural network architecture with "hard monotonic attention," which they evaluate on three morphology datasets. This paper presents a well-written and clear idea, incorporating monotonicity in morphology tasks. The inclusion of monotonicity is justified, as many sequence-to-sequence tasks exhibit monotonic behavior, making general encoder-decoder models less suitable.

However, the authors' approach to enforcing monotonic output character generation by decoupling alignment and transduction is unclear. I have several questions and concerns:

1. The nature of the alignments used in the model is ambiguous. The running example (Fig. 1) suggests 1-to-many alignments, whereas the description in lines 311-312 implies many-to-1 alignments. Could the authors clarify whether they use 1-to-many, many-to-1, or many-to-many alignments?

2. A simpler approach to monotone sequence-to-sequence tasks involves a two-stage process: first, align input and output characters monotonically using a 1-to-many constraint, and then train a standard sequence tagger to predict these alignments. This approach may have been suggested in previous papers, such as [Schnober et al., Section 4.2]. I have two questions regarding this:

   a. How does the proposed approach differ from this simpler idea?

   b. Why was this approach not included as a baseline for comparison?

Further issues and minor points:

3. The evaluation is limited to morphology tasks, overlooking other interesting monotonic sequence-to-sequence tasks that could have demonstrated the system's superiority.

4. The claim of performing "on par or better" (line 791) may be misleading, as it seems to be a common bias in NLP research to map instances of worse performance to "on par" and all others to "better." It would be more accurate to report the results without this wording.

5. The authors provide little information about their linguistic features, which appear to include POS tags, among others.

   a. Where did the authors obtain these features?

   b. Is it possible that these features, rather than the monotonicity constraints, are responsible for the better performance in some cases?

Minor points:

6. Equation (3) should be rewritten as $\text{NN}$ or similar for clarity.

7. Line 231 should be lowercase "where" instead of uppercase.

8. The notation $x1\ldots xn$ should be replaced with $x1,\ldots,xn$ or $x1\cdots xn$ for consistency with mathematical conventions.

9. The use of Cyrillic font in Figure 1 is unnecessary and may cause difficulties for readers without the required fonts.

10. Line 437 should read "these" instead of the current wording.

Thank you for the clarifications. However, I believe the alignments may have been mixed up in the response. It seems that the authors align 1-0, 0-1, 1-1, and later create many-to-many alignments from these.

I would like to reiterate my previous question: why not use 1-x (x in 0,1,2) alignments, as in Schnober et al., and then train a neural tagger on these (e.g., BiLSTM)? I wonder how much the results would have differed from such a simple baseline. A tagger is a monotone model to start with, and given the monotone alignments, everything remains monotone. In contrast, the authors start with a more general model and then apply hard monotonicity constraints.
