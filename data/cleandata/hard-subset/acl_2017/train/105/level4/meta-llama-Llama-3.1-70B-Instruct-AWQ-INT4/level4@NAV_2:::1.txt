The paper presents a novel neural model for morphological inflection generation that employs a hard attention mechanism, inspired by the nearly-monotonic alignment commonly found between characters in a word and its inflection. The authors' main contributions are three-fold: (1) they present a hard attention model for nearly-monotonic sequence-to-sequence learning, (2) they evaluate the model on the task of morphological inflection generation, establishing a new state-of-the-art on three previously studied datasets, and (3) they perform an analysis and comparison of their model and the soft-attention model, shedding light on the features such models extract for the inflection generation task.

The primary contributions of this work are the hard attention model and its evaluation on morphological inflection generation datasets. The authors' approach to directly modeling an almost monotonic alignment between input and output character sequences is innovative and well-suited for the task. The use of a hard attention mechanism, which allows the network to jointly align and transduce while using a focused representation at each step, is a significant improvement over previous sequence-to-sequence work.

Strengths:

1. The idea of hard monotonic attention is novel and substantially different from others, offering a fresh perspective on sequence-to-sequence learning.
2. The authors provide a comprehensive evaluation of their model on three previously studied morphological inflection generation datasets, demonstrating its effectiveness in various settings.
3. The analysis and comparison of the hard attention model and the soft-attention model provide valuable insights into the features such models extract for the inflection generation task.

Weaknesses:

1. The experiment results on morphological inflection generation are somewhat mixed, with the proposed model being effective only in certain conditions (e.g., small training datasets or mostly monotonic alignments).
2. The authors could further discuss the practical merits of their proposed method, such as its ease of implementation and computational cost, to make a stronger case for its adoption.

Questions to Authors:

1. How do the authors plan to address the limitations of their model, particularly in cases where the alignment is not mostly monotonic or context-sensitive phenomena are present?
2. Can the authors provide more details on the computational cost and ease of implementation of their proposed method compared to existing state-of-the-art models?
3. How do the authors envision their hard attention model being applied to other nearly-monotonic align-and-transduce tasks, such as abstractive summarization or machine translation?
