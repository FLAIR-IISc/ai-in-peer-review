**Strengths:** The proposed encoder-decoder model is notable for its incorporation of monotonicity, offering a novel approach to sequence-to-sequence tasks.

**Weaknesses:** One potential concern is that the model may essentially be a standard BiRNN with decoupled alignments, raising questions about its distinctiveness. Additionally, the model's evaluation is limited to morphology tasks, without consideration of other monotonic sequence-to-sequence tasks.

**General Discussion:**
The authors introduce a novel neural network architecture featuring "hard monotonic attention" and evaluate its performance on three morphology datasets. This paper presents a challenging yet intriguing idea, namely the explicit enforcement of monotonicity in sequence-to-sequence tasks. The motivation for incorporating monotonicity is clear, given the inherent monotonicity of many such tasks. However, the authors' approach to achieving this through the decoupling of alignment and transduction raises several questions.

**Clarification Requests:**

1. The nature of the alignments used in the model is unclear. Specifically, it is uncertain whether the alignments are 1-to-many, many-to-1, or many-to-many.
2. A simpler approach to monotonic sequence-to-sequence tasks involves aligning input and output characters monotonically with a 1-to-many constraint, followed by training a standard sequence tagger to predict these alignments. This approach may have been suggested in prior work, such as [Schnober et al., 2016, Section 4.2]. Two questions arise from this:
   a. How does the proposed approach differ from this simpler idea?
   b. Why was this simpler approach not included as a baseline for comparison?

**Additional Concerns:**

3. The evaluation of the model is limited to morphology tasks, which is unfortunate given the potential applicability of the approach to other monotonic sequence-to-sequence tasks.
4. The claim of "on par or better" performance is problematic, as it may be influenced by a cognitive bias among NLP researchers to map instances of inferior performance to "on par" and all other instances to "better." A more precise description of the results is warranted.
5. The authors provide limited information about the linguistic features used in their model. Specifically:
   a. What is the source of these features?
   b. Is it possible that these features, rather than the monotonicity constraints, are responsible for the model's improved performance in certain cases?

**Minor Points:**

7. The capitalization of "Where" in line 231 is incorrect.
8. The notation for sequences in lines 237 and elsewhere should be revised to follow standard mathematical conventions.
9. The use of Cyrillic font in Figure 1 is unnecessary and may be distracting.

**Additional References:**
The work of Schnober et al. (2016) and Cohn et al. (2016) may be relevant to this research, as they also address monotonic sequence-to-sequence tasks. The Stack LSTM, which predicts a sequence of actions to modify or annotate an input, may also be related to the proposed architecture. The use of greedy alignment, as opposed to summing over all alignments, may be a limitation of the proposed approach.
