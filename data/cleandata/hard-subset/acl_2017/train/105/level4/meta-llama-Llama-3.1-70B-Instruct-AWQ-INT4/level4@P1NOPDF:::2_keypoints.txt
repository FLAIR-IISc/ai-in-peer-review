- Strength: Proposes a novel encoder-decoder model that explicitly enforces monotonic (hard) attention.
- Weakness: Model may effectively be a standard BiRNN with decoupled alignments rather than a fundamentally new architecture.
- Explanation clarity: The paper's description of the method is unclear in several places.
- Alignment ambiguity: It’s unclear whether alignments are 1-to-many, many-to-1, many-to-many, or a mix.
- Simpler baseline missing: A two-stage 1-to-many alignment plus sequence tagger baseline (e.g., Schnober et al. 2016) was not implemented or compared.
- Need comparison details: Authors should clarify how their approach differs from the simpler two-stage method and why that baseline was omitted.
- Limited evaluation: Experiments are confined to morphology tasks and don’t test other monotonic seq2seq tasks where the model might excel.
- Results wording: Claiming “on par or better” is misleading; results reporting should be more precise.
- Feature provenance: Linguistic features (e.g., POS tags) are not well-described—where they came from and whether they drive improvements is unclear.
- Minor editorial/math issues: Equation formatting, sentence casing, punctuation in math, Cyrillic font in Figure 1, and a wording error at line 437 need correction.
- After rebuttal—alignment mix-up: Author response suggests mixed alignment types (1-0, 0-1, 1-1, many-to-many), but this raises further confusion.
- After rebuttal—baseline concern: Authors still didn’t use 1-x (x=0,1,2) alignments plus neural tagger, which would be a more suitable baseline.
- Missing related work: Additional relevant papers (Cohn et al. 2016, Rastogi et al. 2016) and connections to Stack LSTM should be discussed.
- Inference concern: Potential drawbacks of using greedy alignment (vs. summing over alignments like Rastogi et al. 2016) should be addressed.
