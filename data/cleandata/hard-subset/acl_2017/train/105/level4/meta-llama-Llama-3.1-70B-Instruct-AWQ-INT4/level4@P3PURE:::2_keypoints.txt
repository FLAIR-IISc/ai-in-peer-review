- Strength: Proposes a novel encoder-decoder that explicitly incorporates monotonicity for sequence-to-sequence tasks.  
- Weakness: Novelty is disputable; model may be equivalent to a standard BiRNN with decoupled alignments.  
- Weakness: Evaluation is limited to morphology tasks and omits other monotonic sequence-to-sequence tasks.  
- Contribution: Introduces "hard monotonic attention" and evaluates on three morphology datasets; paper is well-written and monotonicity motivation is justified.  
- Ambiguity: The nature of alignments (1-to-many, many-to-1, or many-to-many) is unclear and needs clarification.  
- Baseline concern: A simpler two-stage approach (monotonic 1-to-many alignment then standard tagger) is not compared; authors should explain differences and omission.  
- Evaluation gap: Should test on additional monotonic seq2seq tasks to demonstrate broader superiority.  
- Wording issue: Claiming results are "on par or better" is potentially misleading; report exact outcomes instead.  
- Feature transparency: Little information on linguistic features (e.g., POS); need source and analysis of whether features, not monotonicity, drive gains.  
- Minor fix: Equation (3) notation should be clearer (e.g., use "NN").  
- Minor fix: Line 231 capitalization should be lowercase "where."  
- Minor fix: Use consistent math notation for x1…xn (e.g., x1,\ldots,xn).  
- Minor fix: Remove unnecessary Cyrillic font in Figure 1 to avoid reader issues.  
- Minor fix: Line 437 wording should be corrected to "these."  
- After author response: Reviewer believes alignments may still be mixed up; authors appear to construct many-to-many from 1-0, 0-1, and 1-1 primitives.  
- Reiterated question: Why not use 1-x alignments (as in Schnober et al.) and train a BiLSTM tagger—how would results differ from that simple baseline?  
- AC note: Consider citing Cohn et al. (2016); architecture relates to Stack LSTM-style action prediction methods.  
- AC question: Do authors lose anything by using greedy alignment versus methods that sum over alignments (e.g., Rastogi et al., 2016)?
