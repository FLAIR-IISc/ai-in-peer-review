- Strengths: The authors propose a novel encoder-decoder model that explicitly incorporates monotonicity.

- Weaknesses: The model may essentially be a standard BiRNN with decoupled alignments. Furthermore, its evaluation is limited to morphology tasks, neglecting other monotonic sequence-to-sequence tasks.

- General Discussion:

The authors introduce a new encoder-decoder neural network architecture featuring "hard monotonic attention." This architecture is evaluated on three morphology datasets. While the paper is well-written and presents a novel idea, it also raises several questions and concerns.

The inclusion of monotonicity in sequence-to-sequence tasks is a reasonable approach, given that many such tasks are inherently monotonic. By explicitly enforcing monotonic output character generation, the authors aim to improve the performance of encoder-decoder models in these tasks. However, the explanation of their approach is unclear in some aspects.

Several questions arise from the paper:

1. The authors' alignment method is unclear. The running example in Figure 1 suggests 1-to-many alignments, whereas the description in lines 311-312 implies many-to-1 alignments. Could the authors clarify whether they use 1-to-many, many-to-1, or many-to-many alignments?

2. A simpler approach to monotonic sequence-to-sequence tasks involves a two-stage process. First, input and output characters are aligned monotonically using a 1-to-many constraint. Then, a standard sequence tagger is trained to predict these alignments. For instance, the example "flog" -> "fliege" can be aligned as "f-l-o-g / f-l-ie-ge" and then predicted using a tagger. This approach may have been suggested in previous work, such as [Schnober et al., 2016, Section 4.2].

Two questions arise from this comparison:

2a. How does the authors' approach differ from this simpler idea?

2b. Why was this simpler approach not included as a baseline in the evaluation?

Additional issues with the paper include:

3. The evaluation is limited to morphology tasks, neglecting other interesting monotonic sequence-to-sequence tasks. Evaluating the system on these tasks could have demonstrated its superiority, given its explicit modeling of monotonicity.

4. The authors claim to perform "on par or better" than other models. However, this wording may be misleading, as it maps instances of worse performance to "on par" and all other instances to "better." A more accurate description of the results would be appreciated.

5. The authors provide little information about their linguistic features. From Figure 1, it appears that these features include part-of-speech tags, among others.

5a. Where did the authors obtain these features?

5b. Is it possible that these features, rather than the monotonicity constraints, are responsible for the improved performance in some cases?

Minor points:

6. Equation (3) should be rewritten as $\text{NN}$ or similar.

7. Line 231 should start with a lowercase "where."

8. The math notation in lines 237 and elsewhere should be revised to use commas and dots consistently, as recommended by the math community.

9. The use of Cyrillic font in Figure 1 is unnecessary and may cause issues for readers who do not have the required fonts.

10. Line 437 should read "these" instead of the current wording.

The authors' response clarifies some aspects of their approach, but also raises further questions. The alignments seem to be mixed up, and it appears that the authors are using a combination of 1-0, 0-1, 1-1, and many-to-many alignments.

A more relevant question is why the authors did not use 1-x (x in 0,1,2) alignments, as in Schnober et al. (2016), and train a neural tagger on these alignments. This approach would have provided a more suitable baseline for comparison.

Additional relevant work includes Cohn et al. (2016) and Rastogi et al. (2016), which also explore hard monotonic attention in sequence-to-sequence tasks. The authors may want to consider the relationship between their architecture and the Stack LSTM, which similarly predicts a sequence of actions that modify or annotate an input.

Finally, the authors may want to discuss the potential drawbacks of using a greedy alignment approach, as opposed to summing over all alignments, as in Rastogi et al. (2016).
