Strengths:
The concept of hard monotonic attention introduced in this work is a notable departure from existing approaches, showcasing a unique perspective.

Weaknesses:
The experimental results on morphological inflection generation yield mixed outcomes. The proposed model demonstrates efficacy when training data is limited (e.g., CELEX) and when alignment is predominantly monotonic and less context-dependent (e.g., Russian, German, and Spanish).

General Discussion:
The authors propose a novel neural model for morphological inflection generation, leveraging "hard attention" and character alignments derived via Bayesian transliteration methods. This approach diverges significantly from the state-of-the-art neural models, which rely on "soft attention" to jointly address character alignment and conversion within a probabilistic framework.

The idea is innovative and well-founded, and the paper is well-organized and clearly written. The experimental design is comprehensive, but the proposed method's performance is not consistently superior across all conditions. It appears to be better suited for tasks involving primarily monotonic alignment and less context-sensitive phenomena. To strengthen the paper, the authors could elaborate on the practical advantages of their proposed method, including its implementation simplicity and computational efficiency.
