### Review

**Summary**

This paper introduces a novel neural model for morphological inflection generation that utilizes a "hard monotonic attention" mechanism. This approach relies on character alignments that are pre-computed using an external, unsupervised Bayesian model. This design marks a significant departure from previous state-of-the-art methods, which typically employ "soft attention" to learn both alignment and transduction jointly within a single end-to-end model. The authors conduct a comprehensive set of experiments, showing that their model is particularly effective in low-resource settings and for languages with predominantly monotonic alignments.

**Strengths**

1.  **Novelty of the Approach:** The central idea of decoupling the alignment and transduction steps is novel and conceptually interesting. By using pre-computed, deterministic alignments, the model introduces a strong inductive bias. This challenges the prevailing trend of fully end-to-end systems and provides a valuable alternative perspective on solving structured prediction problems in NLP.

2.  **Clarity and Presentation:** The paper is well-written and clearly articulates the proposed method, its motivation, and its relationship to prior work. The experimental setup is thorough and the analysis is sound.

**Weaknesses**

1.  **Mixed Empirical Performance:** The primary weakness is that the experimental results are somewhat mixed. The proposed model does not consistently outperform the soft-attention baseline across all tested conditions, meaning it cannot be claimed as a new state-of-the-art for the general task of morphological inflection.

2.  **Conditional Effectiveness:** The model's advantages appear to be highly conditional on specific characteristics of the data. As the authors' analysis indicates, it performs best in two main scenarios:
    *   (a) In low-resource settings where training data is limited (e.g., the CELEX dataset).
    *   (b) For languages where the transformation from lemma to inflected form involves mostly monotonic character alignments and is less sensitive to long-range context (e.g., Russian, German, and Spanish).
    This suggests the model's applicability may be limited to languages with certain typological properties, and it may struggle with languages featuring more complex, non-monotonic processes (e.g., Semitic languages).

**Detailed Comments**

The authors present a sound and interesting alternative to the standard soft-attention-based sequence-to-sequence models for morphological inflection. The core trade-off explored here is between the flexibility of soft attention, which can learn complex alignments from scratch, and the strong inductive bias imposed by a pre-computed hard monotonic alignment.

The experiments effectively illustrate this trade-off. The proposed model's strength in low-data regimes and on languages with simple, monotonic transformations demonstrates the benefit of its strong structural prior. By heavily constraining the search space for alignments, the model can learn a more accurate transduction function from less data, provided the alignment assumption holds. However, this same constraint becomes a limitation when tackling more complex phenomena where the greater flexibility of the soft attention model allows it to capture dependencies that the proposed model, by design, cannot.

While the paper does an excellent job of characterizing the conditions under which the model succeeds, its overall impact could be strengthened by a more explicit discussion of its practical merits beyond accuracy. For instance, does decoupling alignment and transduction lead to tangible benefits in terms of computational cost, training speed, or inference time? Is the model simpler to implement or debug than a joint soft-attention system? A discussion of these practical advantages would provide a more compelling argument for adopting this method. Highlighting such benefits would help position the work not just as an alternative that works well in specific niches, but as a *preferable* alternative under certain well-defined circumstances, even when it does not achieve state-of-the-art accuracy. This would make the paper's contribution more convincing and impactful.
