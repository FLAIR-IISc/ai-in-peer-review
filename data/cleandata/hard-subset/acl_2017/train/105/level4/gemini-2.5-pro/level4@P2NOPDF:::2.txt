### Review

**Summary**

The authors propose a novel encoder-decoder architecture with a "hard monotonic attention" mechanism. The model is designed for sequence-to-sequence tasks where a monotonic alignment between input and output is expected. To enforce this, the model decouples the problem into two sub-problems: alignment and transduction. It is evaluated on three morphological inflection and reinflection datasets, where it achieves competitive results.

**Strengths**

*   The primary strength of this paper is its novel encoder-decoder architecture that explicitly models and enforces monotonicity. The motivation is strong, as standard soft-attention models are overly general for tasks that are inherently monotonic, and designing a model to exploit this constraint is a promising direction.
*   The paper is generally well-written, and the core idea is clearly presented.

**Weaknesses and Major Comments**

1.  **Lack of a Simple and Strong Baseline:** A significant concern is the lack of comparison to a simpler, yet powerful, baseline. Many monotonic sequence-to-sequence tasks can be framed as a two-stage process:
    a) First, align the input and output sequences monotonically using a standard tool (e.g., the one proposed by Jiampojamarn and Kondrak), producing 1-to-many alignments.
    b) Second, train a standard sequence tagger (such as a BiLSTM) to predict the aligned output segments for each input character.
    This approach is conceptually simpler, inherently monotonic, and has been explored in prior work (e.g., Schnober et al., 2016). It is unclear how the proposed, more complex model compares to this straightforward tagging-based approach. Its absence makes it difficult to assess the true contribution of the proposed architecture versus simply constraining the task space.

2.  **Ambiguity in the Alignment Process:** The description of the model's alignments is unclear and appears contradictory. Figure 1 suggests a 1-to-many alignment (one input character maps to zero or more output characters). However, the text (lines 311-312) seems to describe many-to-1 alignments. The authors should clarify whether the model supports 1-to-many, many-to-1, or many-to-many alignments, as this is fundamental to the model's operation.

3.  **Limited Experimental Scope:** The evaluation is confined to morphological tasks. While these are suitable testbeds, the model's central claim is its effective handling of monotonicity. Its superiority and generalizability would be more convincingly demonstrated by evaluating on a wider range of monotonic sequence-to-sequence tasks, such as transliteration or grapheme-to-phoneme conversion.

4.  **Relationship to Prior Work:** The paper could be strengthened by discussing its relationship to other relevant work on hard attention and structured prediction.
    *   How does this model's greedy alignment approach compare to that of Rastogi et al. (2016), which also employs hard monotonic attention but marginalizes over all possible alignments?
    *   The architecture's action-based prediction seems related to models like the Stack LSTM or the work of Cohn et al. (2016). A discussion of the connections and differences would provide valuable context.

5.  **Unclear Impact of Linguistic Features:** The paper provides little detail about the linguistic features used (e.g., POS tags inferred from Figure 1). It is important to clarify their source and, more crucially, to analyze their impact. It is possible that these features, rather than the novel architecture, are responsible for some of the reported performance gains.

**Questions for the Authors**

1.  Could you please clarify the exact nature of the alignments your model uses (1-to-many, many-to-1, or many-to-many)?
2.  How does your proposed model differ conceptually from the simpler two-stage baseline described above (monotone aligner + sequence tagger)? Why was such a baseline not included in your experiments?
3.  Could you elaborate on the source and impact of the linguistic features? Have you conducted an ablation study to isolate the effect of the monotonicity constraints from the effect of these features?

**Minor Comments and Typos**

*   The claim of performing "on par or better" (l. 791) could be phrased more precisely. It is better to state the results directly (e.g., "the model achieves competitive results, outperforming the baseline on X and performing comparably on Y").
*   Equation (3): Please format `NN` as a non-italicized operator, e.g., `\text{NN}`.
*   l. 231: "Where" should be lowercase.
*   l. 237 and elsewhere: For sequences like `x1...xn`, mathematical typesetting conventions recommend using commas with `\ldots` ($x_1, \ldots, x_n$) or centered dots with no commas ($x_1 \cdots x_n$).
*   Figure 1: The use of Cyrillic script makes the example inaccessible to readers unfamiliar with the alphabet. An example using the Latin alphabet would be more broadly understandable.
*   l. 437: "this" should be "these".
