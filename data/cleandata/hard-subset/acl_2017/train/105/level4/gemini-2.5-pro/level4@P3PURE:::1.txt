### Strengths
The paper introduces a novel hard monotonic attention mechanism, which represents a significant departure from existing approaches in this area.

### Weaknesses
The experimental results on morphological inflection generation are somewhat mixed. The proposed model is effective when the amount of training data is small (e.g., on the CELEX dataset) and on languages where the alignment is largely monotonic and less context-sensitive (such as Russian, German, and Spanish).

### General Discussion
The authors propose a novel neural model for morphological inflection generation that uses "hard attention," based on character alignments obtained separately via a Bayesian transliteration method. This approach is a substantial departure from the previous state-of-the-art neural model for this task, which employs "soft attention" to solve for alignment and conversion jointly within a single probabilistic framework.

The core idea is both novel and technically sound, and the paper is presented clearly with a comprehensive set of experiments. The primary concern, however, is that the proposed method does not achieve state-of-the-art performance across all conditions. As the results indicate, it is particularly well-suited for tasks characterized by mostly monotonic alignments and limited context-sensitive phenomena.

The paper would be more convincing if it described the practical merits of the proposed method. For instance, a discussion of its ease of implementation or a comparison of its computational cost relative to soft attention models would provide valuable context for its performance and help readers understand the potential trade-offs.
