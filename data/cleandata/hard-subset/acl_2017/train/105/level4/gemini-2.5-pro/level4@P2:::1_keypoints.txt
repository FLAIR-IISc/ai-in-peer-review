- Introduces a novel hard monotonic attention model that decouples alignment and transduction, predicting explicit 'step'/'write' actions.  
- Uses pre-computed character-level alignments (external aligner) to generate oracle action sequences for training.  
- Frames morphological inflection as controlled read/write operations, leveraging near-monotonic input-output alignment.  
- Shows strong empirical performance in low-resource settings, notably outperforming a WFST baseline on CELEX.  
- Demonstrates the model is less prone to overfitting and more robust than soft-attention in small-data regimes.  
- Provides comparative analysis (SVD visualizations) showing hard attention separates character and position info better than soft attention.  
- Well-written paper with comprehensive experiments across three datasets, clarifying where the model excels and fails.  
- Performance is conditional: soft-attention beats the model on languages with non-monotonic/templatic morphology (SIGMORPHON).  
- Main limitation is the strong monotonicity assumption, reducing applicability to languages with complex non-monotonic phenomena.  
- Dependence on an external aligner introduces a potential bottleneck and untested sensitivity to alignment quality.  
- Lacks ablation on alignment quality (e.g., simpler or noisy aligners) to assess robustness.  
- Paper omits discussion and quantitative comparison of practical trade-offs (training/inference time, implementation complexity).  
- Question: How sensitive is performance to pre-computed alignment quality or using simpler aligners?  
- Question: How often does maximizing the single-best derivation produce a different output than marginalizing over derivations, and when is this problematic?  
- Question: How does total training time (alignment + network) compare to end-to-end soft-attention baselines?
