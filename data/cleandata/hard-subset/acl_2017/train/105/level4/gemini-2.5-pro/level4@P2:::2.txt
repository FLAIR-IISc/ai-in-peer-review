### Summary and Contributions

This paper presents a neural encoder-decoder model for morphological inflection generation that incorporates a "hard monotonic attention" mechanism. The central idea is to model the task as a sequence of actions—either writing an output character or advancing a pointer (`step`) across the encoded input sequence. This approach explicitly enforces the predominantly monotonic alignment between a word and its inflected form. The model is trained in a decoupled manner: character-level alignments are first generated for the training data using an external tool, and these alignments are then used to create gold-standard action sequences for training the neural network via cross-entropy loss.

In my view, the main contributions of this work are:

1.  **Strong Empirical Results:** The paper presents a comprehensive evaluation on three standard morphological inflection datasets (CELEX, Wiktionary, SIGMORPHON 2016). The proposed model achieves state-of-the-art or highly competitive results across these datasets, notably outperforming previous methods in the low-resource setting of the CELEX dataset.
2.  **A Well-Motivated Architecture for Monotonic Tasks:** The paper proposes a neural architecture that explicitly constrains the generation process to be monotonic. This is a principled design choice for morphological inflection and proves effective, especially in low-data regimes where more flexible models like soft-attention might overfit.
3.  **Insightful Analysis:** The authors provide a valuable analysis comparing the internal representations learned by their hard attention model and a standard soft attention model. This sheds light on how each model encodes positional and character-level information, offering an explanation for the hard attention model's strong performance with limited data.

### Strengths

1.  **Excellent Performance in Low-Resource Setting:** The model's strongest result is on the CELEX dataset, which contains only 500 training examples per inflection type. Here, it significantly outperforms prior neural models and, notably, is the first neural approach to surpass the accuracy of the highly specialized latent-variable WFST model of Dreyer et al. (2008). This demonstrates the effectiveness of incorporating a strong inductive bias (monotonicity) when training data is scarce, as it helps prevent the model from overfitting (as illustrated in Figure 2).

2.  **Principled and Intuitive Model Design:** The core idea of modeling inflection generation as a series of `WRITE` and `STEP` actions is intuitive and well-suited to the task. Most morphological processes (especially suffixation and stem changes) can be naturally captured by a monotonic transduction. By decoupling the alignment and transduction steps, the model can leverage high-quality pre-computed alignments from the start of training, rather than requiring the network to learn both simultaneously, which can be challenging.

3.  **Thorough Evaluation and Analysis:** The authors validate their model on three different datasets with varying sizes and linguistic properties. This provides robust evidence for the model's effectiveness. Furthermore, the analysis in Section 5, which visualizes the encoded representations, is a significant strength. It provides compelling evidence that the hard attention model relies on its explicit `step` mechanism for positional awareness, while the soft attention model is forced to encode positional information more heavily into its character representations, potentially making it more complex and data-hungry.

### Weaknesses

1.  **Novelty and Comparison to Simpler Baselines:** My main concern is that the proposed method, when viewed as a two-stage process (align, then transduce), may not be substantially different from a simpler, well-established paradigm. One could first align the input and output sequences with a 1-to-many monotonic aligner, and then train a standard sequence tagger (e.g., a BiLSTM) to predict the corresponding output character(s) for each input character. Such an approach would also be strictly monotonic. The paper does not adequately distinguish its method from this simpler baseline, nor does it include such a model in its comparison. Without this, it is difficult to assess the specific contribution of the proposed encoder-decoder control mechanism over a more straightforward sequence tagging formulation.

2.  **Limited Scope of Evaluation:** The paper introduces the model as being suitable for "nearly-monotonic sequence to sequence learning," but the experiments are confined exclusively to morphological inflection. While this is a perfect use case, the broader claim of the model's utility is not substantiated. Evaluating the model on other monotonic sequence-to-sequence tasks, such as grapheme-to-phoneme conversion, transliteration, or lemmatization, would have made the paper's contribution much stronger and more generalizable.

3.  **Insufficient Discussion of Related Architectures:** The action-based generation process (i.e., predicting a sequence of operations like `WRITE` or `STEP`) is conceptually related to a class of transition-based models. For instance, Stack LSTMs similarly predict actions to modify a data structure. The paper would benefit from positioning itself with respect to these architectures to better delineate its novelty. Furthermore, the use of hard monotonic attention invites comparison with Rastogi et al. (2016), who also enforce monotonicity but sum over all possible alignments during training, whereas this work relies on a single, pre-determined alignment. A discussion of the trade-offs of this design choice is missing.

### Questions to Authors

1.  Could you please clarify how your proposed model differs conceptually and empirically from a simpler two-stage baseline? Specifically, a baseline that first learns 1-to-many monotonic alignments and then trains a standard sequence tagger (e.g., a BiLSTM) to predict the aligned output characters for each input character. Why would your proposed architecture be preferable to this approach?

2.  Your training process relies on a single "gold" action sequence derived from pre-computed alignments. In contrast, models like Rastogi et al. (2016) marginalize over all possible alignments. Have you analyzed the impact of relying on a single, potentially imperfect, alignment path? Does this simplification risk propagating errors from the aligner, especially for ambiguous cases?

3.  The decoder takes embeddings for morpho-syntactic features (POS, gender, etc.) as input. Could you elaborate on where these features were sourced from for each dataset? More importantly, could you provide an analysis of their contribution to the model's performance? It would be helpful to understand how much of the performance gain over baselines is attributable to the novel architecture versus the effective use of these explicit linguistic features.
