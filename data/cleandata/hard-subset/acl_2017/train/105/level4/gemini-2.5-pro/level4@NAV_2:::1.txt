**Summary and Contributions**

This paper introduces a neural encoder-decoder model for morphological inflection generation that utilizes a novel "hard monotonic attention" mechanism. This mechanism transduces an input character sequence into an output sequence by learning to predict a series of discrete actions: either writing an output symbol or advancing a pointer over the encoded input (`step`). This design explicitly models the near-monotonic alignment often found in this task. The model is trained on gold action sequences derived from an external character-level alignment tool, simplifying the learning process to a standard cross-entropy loss. The authors evaluate the model on three benchmark datasets, demonstrating its effectiveness, particularly in low-resource scenarios.

The primary contributions of this work are:
1.  The proposal of a novel hard monotonic attention model for sequence transduction. This architecture, based on explicit `write` and `step` actions, is a significant and well-motivated departure from standard soft attention, tailored specifically for tasks with strong local and monotonic alignment patterns.
2.  Strong empirical results that establish a new state-of-the-art on several benchmarks. The model's superior performance on the small CELEX dataset is particularly compelling, as it is the first neural approach to outperform a specialized latent-variable WFST model, highlighting its data efficiency.
3.  An insightful comparative analysis of the learned representations and alignments of hard versus soft attention models. The visualizations in Section 5 effectively illustrate how the two models encode positional and character information, providing a clear rationale for the hard attention model's robustness, especially with limited training data.

**Strengths**

1.  **Novel and Well-Motivated Architecture:** The hard attention mechanism is an elegant and principled approach that directly incorporates the strong prior of near-monotonicity in morphological processes. By framing transduction as a sequence of discrete pointer movements and write operations, the model provides a more constrained and interpretable alternative to the fully-differentiable but less-constrained soft attention mechanism.
2.  **Excellent Performance in Low-Resource Settings:** The paper's most significant empirical result is its performance on the CELEX dataset. As shown in Table 1 and Figure 2, the model significantly outperforms previous neural methods and, crucially, the highly specialized WFST model of Dreyer et al. (2008). This demonstrates a clear advantage in data efficiency and a reduced tendency to overfit, which is a critical problem for neural models in low-data regimes.
3.  **Comprehensive and Thorough Evaluation:** The authors validate their model on three diverse datasets (CELEX, Wiktionary, SIGMORPHON2016), which vary in size and linguistic phenomena. The comparisons are made against a strong and relevant set of both neural and non-neural baselines. This rigorous evaluation provides convincing evidence for the model's effectiveness across different conditions.
4.  **Insightful Analysis:** The analysis in Section 5 strengthens the paper considerably. By visualizing the SVD projections of the character embeddings, the authors provide a compelling explanation for the model's success. The finding that the soft attention model encodes position more strongly in its representations, while the hard attention model relies on the explicit `step` action, offers a clear hypothesis for why the proposed model generalizes better from less data.

**Weaknesses**

1.  **Performance is Not Uniformly State-of-the-Art:** While the model is highly effective, its superiority is conditional. The results on the SIGMORPHON 2016 dataset (Table 3) show that a standard soft attention model performs better on languages with non-concatenative or long-distance morphological phenomena (e.g., templatic morphology in Arabic, vowel harmony in Finnish and Turkish). This suggests that the model's core assumption—near-monotonicity—is also its main limitation, making it less suitable for a subset of morphologically complex languages.
2.  **Reliance on an External Aligner:** The model's training pipeline is decoupled, requiring a pre-alignment step using an external tool to generate the oracle action sequences. This introduces a dependency on the quality of the aligner and raises questions about error propagation. It is unclear how sensitive the model is to alignment errors or how it would perform if a different, perhaps lower-quality, aligner were used. This contrasts with end-to-end approaches that can learn task-specific alignments jointly with the transduction task.
3.  **Lack of Discussion on Practical Considerations:** The paper focuses exclusively on model accuracy and does not discuss practical trade-offs like computational efficiency. A comparison of training time (including the pre-alignment step), inference speed, and model size against the soft attention baseline would be valuable for practitioners seeking to assess the overall utility of the proposed method.

**Questions to Authors**

1.  Could you comment on the practical trade-offs of your model compared to the soft attention baseline? Specifically, how do they compare in terms of training time (factoring in the pre-alignment stage), inference speed, and memory usage?
2.  How sensitive is the model's performance to the quality of the pre-computed alignments? Have you investigated how performance changes when using a simpler or noisier alignment method (e.g., one based on Levenshtein distance) to generate the oracle sequences for training? This would help clarify the importance of the sophisticated CRP-based aligner to the model's success.
