### **Summary and Main Contributions**

This paper presents a neural encoder-decoder model with a "hard monotonic attention" mechanism for the task of morphological inflection generation. The central idea is to reframe the sequence-to-sequence problem as the prediction of a linear sequence of actions—either writing an output character or stepping forward through the encoded input sequence. This is achieved by first using an external tool to create character-level alignments between the source and target words in the training data, and then deterministically converting these alignments into gold action sequences for training. The model is evaluated on three standard morphological inflection datasets and shows strong performance, particularly in low-data settings.

From my perspective, the main contributions of this work are:

1.  **An effective and simple method for incorporating a strong monotonic inductive bias into a neural model for morphological inflection.** The model's design directly enforces the assumption that the output is generated monotonically with respect to the input, which is a natural fit for this task. The success of this approach, especially in the low-resource setting, validates the utility of this explicit constraint.
2.  **State-of-the-art empirical results on multiple standard benchmarks.** The paper provides a thorough evaluation, demonstrating that the proposed model achieves state-of-the-art performance on the low-resource CELEX dataset and the large Wiktionary dataset, while remaining highly competitive on the typologically diverse SIGMORPHON 2016 dataset.
3.  **An insightful analysis comparing the hard attention model with a standard soft attention baseline.** The visualizations in Section 5 shed light on the differences in the learned representations, particularly how each model encodes positional information, providing a better understanding of their respective mechanisms.

### **Strengths**

1.  **Well-motivated use of inductive bias.** The model's core assumption of monotonicity is a strong and appropriate inductive bias for morphological inflection. This explicit constraint is shown to be highly effective, leading to significant performance gains in the low-resource CELEX setting. The learning curves in Figure 2 provide compelling evidence that this hard constraint helps the model generalize better and avoid the overfitting that affects the more flexible soft-attention model on small datasets.
2.  **Model simplicity and training efficiency.** By decoupling the alignment and transduction steps, the authors simplify the training process considerably. The model does not require complex joint training or marginalization over all possible alignments (unlike models such as the RNN Transducer or Rastogi et al., 2016). Instead, it learns from a single, pre-determined action sequence using a standard cross-entropy loss, making the approach conceptually straightforward and efficient to train.
3.  **Thorough and robust empirical evaluation.** The paper presents strong results across three different datasets, covering low-resource, high-resource, and typologically diverse settings. The model is shown to be the first neural approach to outperform the specialized latent-variable WFST model of Dreyer et al. (2008) on the challenging CELEX dataset. This robust performance across varied conditions demonstrates the effectiveness and reliability of the proposed method.
4.  **Clarity of presentation.** The paper is well-written, clearly motivated, and easy to follow. The analysis in Section 5 is particularly valuable, offering useful insights into the model's behavior and providing a clear comparison with the soft attention mechanism.

### **Weaknesses**

1.  **Limited novelty and comparison to simpler, equivalent baselines.** The idea of decoupling monotonic alignment and transduction is well-established. The proposed architecture, with its `step` and `write` actions, can be viewed as a re-framing of a standard sequence tagging task. A very strong and conceptually simpler baseline would be to: 1) use a monotonic aligner to generate 1-to-many character alignments (e.g., 'f'->'f', 'o'->'ie'), and 2) train a standard sequence tagger (e.g., a BiLSTM-CRF) to predict the output string for each input character. Such an approach also enforces monotonicity but avoids the indirection of a transition-based system. The absence of such a baseline makes it difficult to ascertain whether the proposed action-based formulation offers any advantage over a more direct tagging approach.
2.  **Reliance on a single, pre-computed alignment.** The model's training is entirely dependent on a single "gold" action sequence derived from an external aligner. This approach ignores any alignment ambiguity and is susceptible to errors from the alignment tool, which may propagate to the final model. More principled approaches, such as that of Rastogi et al. (2016), handle alignment uncertainty by marginalizing over all possible alignments within a weighted FST framework. The paper does not analyze the model's sensitivity to the choice of aligner or the impact of alignment errors on performance.
3.  **Overstated claims of generality.** The model is introduced as a method for "nearly-monotonic sequence to sequence learning," but its evaluation is confined to morphological inflection. While this is an excellent test case, the claims of generality would be much stronger if the model's effectiveness were demonstrated on other monotonic sequence-to-sequence tasks, such as grapheme-to-phoneme conversion, transliteration, or text normalization. Without this, the contribution appears to be a specialized solution for a single task domain.

### **Questions to Authors**

1.  Your model can be framed as a transition-based system that learns to predict `step` or `write` actions. An alternative, and arguably simpler, method for enforcing monotonicity is to treat the problem as sequence tagging: first, create 1-to-many alignments between input and output characters, and then train a standard sequence tagger (e.g., BiLSTM) to predict the output character(s) for each input character. How does your proposed architecture conceptually differ from this approach, and could you comment on why such a strong and relevant baseline was not included in your experiments?
2.  There seems to be an ambiguity in your description of the alignment process. Section 2.3 states the model must "read all the input elements aligned to an output element...before writing it," which implies a many-to-one mapping from input to output. However, your analysis in Figure 3 shows examples like `o` -> `ie`, which is a one-to-many mapping. Could you please clarify the exact nature of the alignments produced by your aligner and detail the deterministic procedure for converting these (potentially many-to-many) alignments into your linear sequence of `step` and `write` actions?
3.  Could you clarify if all baselines you compare against (e.g., Dreyer et al., 2008; Durrett and DeNero, 2013; Kann and Schütze, 2016a) used the exact same set of morpho-syntactic features as your model? This is crucial for verifying that the observed performance gains are attributable to the model architecture rather than differences in the input feature representation.

### **Additional Comments**

*   The claim in the conclusion of performing "on par or better" is a slight overstatement. While the model is very strong, it does not outperform the soft attention baseline on several of the non-suffixing languages in the SIGMORPHON dataset. A more nuanced summary of the results would be more accurate.
*   Minor formatting/presentation suggestions:
    *   In Equation (3), `NN` should be rendered in roman font (e.g., `\text{NN}`) to distinguish it from variables.
    *   The use of a Cyrillic example in Figure 1 may be difficult for some readers to follow. An example using the Latin alphabet (such as the German example from the introduction) would be more accessible.
