### **Summary and Contributions**

This paper introduces a neural sequence-to-sequence model for morphological inflection generation, centered around a novel "hard monotonic attention" mechanism. In contrast to standard soft attention models that compute a weighted average over all input states, this model's decoder makes a discrete choice at each timestep: either `WRITE` an output character or `STEP` its attention pointer to the next input character. This architecture explicitly models the nearly monotonic alignment often found in morphological transformations. The model is trained on gold action sequences derived from pre-computed character-level alignments, simplifying the learning process to a standard cross-entropy loss. The authors evaluate their model on three datasets, demonstrating strong performance, particularly in low-resource settings.

In my view, the main contributions of this work are:

1.  **A novel hard monotonic attention mechanism.** This is the primary contribution. The architecture provides an elegant method for incorporating a strong inductive bias (monotonicity) directly into a neural sequence-to-sequence model. Decoupling the alignment and transduction steps simplifies the training objective compared to models that must jointly learn both.
2.  **State-of-the-art performance in a low-resource setting.** The paper presents compelling evidence on the CELEX dataset that the proposed model is more data-efficient than soft attention and other neural models. It is notably the first neural model to outperform the strong, task-specific latent-variable WFST model of Dreyer et al. (2008) on this benchmark.
3.  **An insightful comparative analysis of model representations.** The analysis in Section 5, which visualizes the learned encoder representations, effectively illustrates the differences between the hard and soft attention models. It provides a plausible explanation for the hard attention model's data efficiency by showing how it relies less on encoding positional information within its hidden states, as this is handled more explicitly by the `STEP` action.

### **Strengths**

1.  **Novelty and Soundness of the Proposed Model.** The core idea of formalizing morphological transduction as a sequence of `WRITE` and `STEP` actions is novel and well-suited for the task. It imposes a sensible constraint on the problem, which is particularly beneficial when training data is scarce. Unlike soft attention, which must learn the monotonic property from data, this model builds it into its structure, which appears to act as an effective regularizer. The simplification of the training process—by using an external aligner to create a gold sequence of actions—is a pragmatic and effective choice.

2.  **Strong Empirical Results in Low-Resource Scenarios.** The model's performance on the CELEX dataset is a major strength. As shown in Table 1, it achieves an average accuracy of 89.44%, outperforming all prior neural and non-neural baselines. The learning curves in Figure 2 provide clear evidence for the claim that the soft attention model is more prone to overfitting on this small dataset, whereas the hard attention model generalizes better. This result is significant as it demonstrates a clear advantage for this architecture in a challenging and realistic setting.

3.  **Thorough Experiments and Analysis.** The paper is supported by a comprehensive set of experiments across three distinct datasets (CELEX, Wiktionary, SIGMORPHON 2016), covering low-resource, high-resource, and typologically diverse settings. The analysis in Section 5 goes beyond simply reporting accuracy scores. The visualizations of learned alignments (Figure 3) and encoder representations (Figure 4) provide valuable insights into the inner workings of both the proposed model and the soft attention baseline, strengthening the paper's conclusions.

### **Weaknesses**

1.  **Performance is Not Universally State-of-the-Art.** While the model is highly effective for certain conditions, its state-of-the-art claim is not absolute. On the SIGMORPHON 2016 dataset (Table 3), the proposed model excels on languages with predominantly suffixing morphology (Russian, German, Spanish). However, it is slightly outperformed by the soft attention baseline on languages with more complex, non-local phenomena like vowel harmony or templatic morphology (e.g., Finnish, Turkish, Hungarian, Arabic). This suggests that the model's strong monotonic bias, while a strength in many cases, can become a limitation when such assumptions are violated.

2.  **Dependence on an External Alignment Model.** The model's success is contingent on the quality of the alignments produced by an external tool (Sudoh et al., 2013). This introduces a dependency and a potential point of failure. The paper does not analyze the model's sensitivity to the quality of these alignments. For instance, would the model perform as well if a simpler aligner were used, or in a language where the aligner performs poorly? This makes the method a two-stage pipeline, where the performance of the first stage is critical but unexamined.

3.  **Lack of Discussion on Practical Merits.** The authors suggest that their model is simpler, but this claim is not substantiated with a practical comparison. The paper would be more convincing if it included a discussion of computational trade-offs. For example, how does the hard attention model compare to the soft attention baseline in terms of training time, inference speed, or number of parameters? Such an analysis would help readers better understand the practical advantages of the proposed method beyond accuracy.

### **Questions to Authors**

1.  How sensitive is the model to the quality of the pre-computed alignments? Have you experimented with a simpler aligner (e.g., based on Levenshtein distance) or analyzed the impact of alignment errors on the final inflection generation performance?
2.  Could you provide details on the practical trade-offs of your model compared to the soft attention baseline, such as differences in training time, inference speed, or the number of model parameters? This would help clarify the benefits of your approach more concretely.
3.  Given that the model's strength is its monotonic assumption, how might it be extended to better handle the non-local phenomena (like vowel harmony) where the soft attention model had an edge?
