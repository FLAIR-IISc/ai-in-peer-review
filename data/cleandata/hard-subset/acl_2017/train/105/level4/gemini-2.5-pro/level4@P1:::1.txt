### **Summary of the Paper**
This paper introduces a novel neural sequence-to-sequence model for morphological inflection generation. The core contribution is a "hard monotonic attention" mechanism that explicitly models the nearly monotonic alignment between a word and its inflected form. Unlike standard soft-attention models that jointly learn to align and transduce, this approach decouples the two steps. It first computes character-level alignments using an external model and then trains an encoder-decoder network to predict a sequence of `write` and `step` (advance attention pointer) actions derived from these pre-computed alignments. The authors evaluate their model on three benchmark datasets, demonstrating state-of-the-art performance, particularly in low-resource settings and for languages with predominantly suffixing morphology. The paper also includes an analysis of the representations learned by both hard and soft attention models.

### **Strengths**
1.  **Novelty of the Hard Monotonic Attention Mechanism:** The proposed model is a significant and interesting departure from the dominant soft-attention paradigm for sequence-to-sequence tasks. By formalizing the transduction process as a series of explicit `write` and `step` actions guided by a pre-computed alignment, the model incorporates a strong, linguistically motivated inductive bias for monotonicity. This design choice simplifies the learning problem, allowing for straightforward training with a standard cross-entropy loss, and avoids the need for the network to learn the alignment from scratch, which can be difficult with limited data.

2.  **Strong Performance in Low-Data Regimes:** A key strength of this work is its demonstrated effectiveness in low-resource scenarios. On the small CELEX dataset, the proposed model is the first neural approach to outperform the strong, task-specific latent-variable WFST model of Dreyer et al. (2008). The learning curves in Figure 2 provide compelling evidence that the hard attention mechanism is less prone to overfitting than its soft-attention counterpart, making it a valuable contribution for applying neural methods to morphological tasks where training data is often scarce.

3.  **Comprehensive Evaluation and Insightful Analysis:** The authors conduct a thorough empirical evaluation across three distinct datasets (CELEX, Wiktionary, SIGMORPHON 2016), which vary in size and linguistic diversity. This provides a robust assessment of the model's capabilities. Furthermore, the analysis in Section 5, comparing the learned alignments and the SVD projections of character embeddings from both hard and soft attention models, is particularly insightful. It helps to explain *why* the hard attention model performs well and sheds light on the different strategies the two models use to encode positional information.

### **Weaknesses**
1.  **Performance is Not Uniformly State-of-the-Art:** The primary weakness is that the model's strong performance is not consistent across all conditions, leading to somewhat mixed results. While the model excels on the low-resource CELEX dataset and on languages with primarily monotonic phenomena (e.g., Russian, German, and Spanish in the SIGMORPHON dataset), it is outperformed by a soft-attention baseline on languages exhibiting more complex, non-local dependencies, such as vowel harmony or templatic morphology. This suggests that the model's main strength—its strong inductive bias for monotonicity—is also its main limitation, restricting its applicability to a subset of morphological typologies.

2.  **Lack of Discussion on Practical and Computational Aspects:** The paper would be more convincing if it discussed the practical merits of the proposed method beyond accuracy. For example, there is no comparison of computational costs (e.g., training time, inference speed, model parameters) against the soft-attention baseline. The reliance on a separate, pre-computed alignment introduces an additional step to the pipeline; the complexity and computational overhead of this alignment step are not discussed. Quantifying these practical trade-offs would provide a more complete picture of the method's value.

### **Questions to Authors**
Could you please comment on the practical advantages of the hard attention model compared to a standard soft-attention baseline? Specifically, how do the models compare in terms of:
a) Training and inference speed?
b) The number of parameters and memory footprint?
c) Is the initial alignment generation step a significant computational overhead in the overall workflow?
