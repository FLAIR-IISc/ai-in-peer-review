- Strengths:
The concept of hard monotonic attention is a novel contribution and represents a significant departure from existing approaches.
- Weaknesses:
The empirical results for morphological inflection generation show inconsistent performance. The proposed model demonstrates its strengths primarily in low-resource settings (e.g., with small datasets like CELEX) or on languages where alignments are predominantly monotonic and less dependent on context (e.g., Russian, German, and Spanish).
- General Discussion:
The authors introduce a new neural architecture for morphological inflection generation. The model's core innovation is its use of "hard attention," which relies on character alignments derived independently using a Bayesian transliteration model. This approach is fundamentally different from the previous state-of-the-art, which typically employs a "soft attention" mechanism to solve for alignment and conversion jointly within the same probabilistic model.

The central idea is innovative and technically sound, the paper is clearly articulated, and the experiments are thorough. The main reservation is that the proposed method does not consistently outperform the state of the art under all conditions. Its suitability seems to be highest for tasks with highly monotonic alignments and fewer context-sensitive transformations. The paper's argument would be more compelling with an analysis of the method's practical benefits, such as potential advantages in computational cost or ease of implementation.
