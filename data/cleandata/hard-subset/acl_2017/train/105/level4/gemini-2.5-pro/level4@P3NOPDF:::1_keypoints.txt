- Proposes a neural morphological inflection model using hard monotonic attention with precomputed Bayesian character alignments, unlike end-to-end soft-attention systems.  
- Shows strong performance in low-resource settings and for languages with mostly monotonic character alignments.  
- Novel idea: decoupling alignment and transduction introduces a useful strong inductive bias.  
- Paper is well-written, clearly presented, and provides thorough experiments and analysis.  
- Empirical results are mixed; the model does not consistently beat soft-attention baselines across all conditions.  
- Model advantages are highly conditional: (a) low-data scenarios (e.g., CELEX) and (b) languages with largely monotonic transformations (e.g., Russian, German, Spanish).  
- Likely to struggle on languages with complex, non-monotonic morphology (e.g., Semitic languages).  
- Core trade-off: constrained hard alignments reduce data needs but limit ability to capture complex dependencies that soft attention can model.  
- Experiments effectively illustrate this trade-off between bias and flexibility.  
- Paper would be stronger with explicit discussion of practical merits (computational cost, training/inference speed, implementation/debugging simplicity).  
- Need to position the method as a preferable alternative under clearly defined practical circumstances, not only in accuracy-limited niches.
