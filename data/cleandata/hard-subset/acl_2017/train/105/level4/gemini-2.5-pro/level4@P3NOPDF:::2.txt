### **Summary**

The authors propose a novel encoder-decoder neural network architecture incorporating "hard monotonic attention," specifically designed for sequence-to-sequence tasks where a monotonic alignment between input and output is expected. The core idea is to decouple alignment and transduction by first establishing a monotonic alignment between input and output sequences and then training the model to generate outputs that respect this alignment. The model is evaluated on three morphological reinflection datasets.

### **Strengths**

The primary strength of this paper is its novel approach to explicitly modeling monotonicity within an encoder-decoder framework. For many sequence-to-sequence tasks beyond machine translation, such as the morphological tasks tested here, this assumption is linguistically well-motivated. The authors correctly observe that standard attention-based models are unnecessarily general and complex for these problems. Their attempt to build this strong inductive bias directly into the architecture is a valuable contribution, and the paper is generally well-written and clear.

### **Weaknesses and Questions for the Authors**

Despite the novelty, there are several significant concerns regarding the model's clarification, its comparison to simpler baselines, and the scope of the evaluation.

**1. Ambiguity of the Alignment Process:**
The description of the alignment process is unclear. The running example in Figure 1 suggests a 1-to-many alignment, where one input character can map to zero, one, or multiple output characters (e.g., "g" -> "ge"). However, the text in lines 311-312 appears to describe a many-to-1 mapping ("several input characters aligned to 1 output character"). Could you please clarify the precise nature of the alignments your model uses? Does it support 1-to-many, many-to-1, or many-to-many mappings, and how are these formally defined and implemented?

**2. Lack of a Simple, Strong Monotonic Baseline:**
A central weakness is the lack of comparison to a conceptually simpler, yet powerful, monotonic baseline. A straightforward approach would be to:
a) First, align the input and output character sequences monotonically using a standard tool (e.g., the one by Jiampojamarn and Kondrak, or the method in Schnober et al., 2016) to create 1-to-many alignments. For your example `flog` -> `fliege`, this would yield an alignment like `f-l-o-g` / `f-l-ie-ge`.
b) Second, train a standard sequence tagger (e.g., a BiLSTM) to predict the output segment corresponding to each input character (e.g., predict `f` from `f`, `l` from `l`, `ie` from `o`, and `ge` from `g`).

A sequence tagger is inherently monotonic. This two-stage approach is simple, robust, and enforces monotonicity by its very design. My questions are:
*   How does your proposed architecture, which starts from a more general model and adds hard constraints, fundamentally differ from or improve upon this simpler align-then-tag paradigm?
*   Why was such a baseline not included in your experiments? Without it, it is difficult to assess whether the complexity of your proposed architecture is justified.

**3. Limited Scope of Evaluation:**
The evaluation is confined to morphological reinflection. While this is a suitable testbed, the claim of a general model for monotonic seq2seq tasks would be substantially strengthened by evaluating on other relevant tasks where monotonicity holds, such as grapheme-to-phoneme conversion, transliteration, or lemmatization. Demonstrating superiority on a wider range of tasks would make a much more compelling case for the model's utility.

**4. Confounding Effect of Linguistic Features:**
The model incorporates linguistic features such as POS tags (inferred from Figure 1), but their source and impact are not discussed.
*   Could you specify where these features were obtained?
*   More importantly, is it possible that the performance gains observed in some cases are primarily due to these powerful features rather than the novel monotonic architecture? An ablation study that removes these features would be crucial for disentangling the contributions of the model's components.

**5. Relation to Prior Work on Hard Attention:**
The paper could benefit from a more thorough discussion of its relationship to other relevant work.
*   **Rastogi et al. (2016)** also propose a model with hard monotonic attention but sum over all possible alignments. In contrast, your model appears to use a greedy alignment. What are the trade-offs of this design choice?
*   The work of **Cohn et al. (2016)** on incorporating alignment structure into neural models also seems highly relevant.
*   The architecture bears a functional resemblance to models that predict a sequence of actions, such as the **Stack LSTM**. A discussion of these connections would better situate the work within the existing literature.

**6. Phrasing of Results:**
The claim of performing "on par or better" (l. 791) could be phrased more objectively. It is common practice in NLP to transparently acknowledge when a model underperforms on certain subsets and discuss potential reasons, rather than categorizing such results as "on par".
