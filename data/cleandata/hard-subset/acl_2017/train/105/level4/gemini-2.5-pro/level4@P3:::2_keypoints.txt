- Presents a neural encoder-decoder with hard monotonic attention that predicts linear actions (write/step) from precomputed character alignments.
- Contribution: introduces a simple, effective monotonic inductive bias well-suited to morphological inflection, especially beneficial in low-data settings.
- Contribution: achieves state-of-the-art on CELEX and Wiktionary and is competitive on the typologically diverse SIGMORPHON 2016 dataset.
- Contribution: provides analysis and visualizations comparing hard attention to soft attention, highlighting differences in learned positional representations.
- Strength: well-motivated inductive bias that improves generalization and reduces overfitting in low-resource regimes, supported by learning curves.
- Strength: model simplicity and training efficiency by decoupling alignment and transduction and training on a single action sequence with cross-entropy loss.
- Strength: thorough empirical evaluation across low-/high-resource and typologically diverse datasets, notably outperforming a prior WFST model on CELEX.
- Strength: clear writing and useful Section 5 analysis that aids understanding of model behavior versus soft attention.
- Weakness: limited novelty; approach resembles established decoupling and lacks comparison to a simpler 1-to-many sequence-tagging baseline (e.g., BiLSTM-CRF).
- Weakness: relies on a single precomputed alignment, ignoring alignment ambiguity and potential aligner errors without sensitivity analysis.
- Weakness: claims of generality are overstated since evaluation is confined to morphological inflection and not other monotonic seq2seq tasks.
- Question: why was a direct sequence-tagging 1-to-many baseline not included, and how does the action-based model conceptually differ?
- Question: please clarify the aligner outputs and the deterministic procedure converting (possibly one-to-many/many-to-many) alignments into step/write actions.
- Question: did all compared baselines use the exact same morpho-syntactic feature set to ensure fair comparisons?
- Additional comment: the “on par or better” claim is slightly overstated—soft attention outperforms this model on several non-suffixing SIGMORPHON languages.
- Additional comment: minor presentation fixes suggested—render NN in roman in Eq.(3) and replace the Cyrillic example in Fig.1 with a Latin-alphabet example for accessibility.
