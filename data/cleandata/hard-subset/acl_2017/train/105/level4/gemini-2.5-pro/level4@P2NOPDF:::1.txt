**Summary**

This paper proposes a novel neural model for morphological inflection generation that utilizes a "hard attention" mechanism. The key innovation is to decouple the alignment and transduction steps, using a pre-computed alignment from a separate Bayesian transliteration model. This approach is a significant departure from prior state-of-the-art models that use "soft attention" to learn both alignment and transduction jointly. The core idea is sound, the paper is clearly written, and the experiments are comprehensive. However, the model's performance is not uniformly superior to existing methods, showing its main strengths in low-resource settings and on languages with predominantly monotonic alignments.

**Strengths**

The primary strength of this work is its novelty. The idea of using a pre-computed hard monotonic attention for this task is a distinct and interesting alternative to the widely-used soft attention paradigm. It effectively re-frames the problem by separating the "where to edit" (alignment) and "how to edit" (transduction) components.

**Weaknesses**

The experimental results for morphological inflection generation are mixed. The proposed model's effectiveness appears to be conditional:
*   It performs well when the amount of training data is limited (as shown with the CELEX dataset).
*   It is effective on languages where the character alignments are largely monotonic and less dependent on broad context (e.g., Russian, German, and Spanish).
However, it does not consistently achieve state-of-the-art performance across all tested conditions, suggesting its applicability may be limited to specific linguistic typologies or data scenarios.

**Detailed Comments**

The paper is well-written and the proposed method is explained clearly. The experimental setup is thorough, evaluating the model across multiple languages and data conditions, which provides a solid basis for comparison.

My main concern is that the practical motivation for the proposed method could be stronger. While the model is competitive in certain scenarios, it does not universally outperform the soft-attention baseline. The paper would be more compelling if it discussed other potential advantages of decoupling the alignment and transduction processes. For instance, a discussion of practical merits such as ease of implementation, training stability, faster convergence, or reduced computational cost at inference time would provide a more complete picture of the method's contribution. Quantifying the difference in training or inference speed compared to the baseline soft-attention model would be a particularly valuable addition.

This analysis would help readers understand the trade-offs involved and identify the specific situations where this model is the most suitable choice, even if it does not achieve the highest accuracy in every case.
