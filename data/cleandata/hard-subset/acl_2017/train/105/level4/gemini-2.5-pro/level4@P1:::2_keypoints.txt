- Novel encoder-decoder with hard monotonic attention that decouples alignment and transduction using explicit step/write actions.  
- Strong empirical results, achieving state-of-the-art on several morphological inflection benchmarks, notably the low-resource CELEX dataset.  
- Analysis comparing internal representations of hard-attention vs soft-attention models, showing differences in encoding character identity and position.  
- Main strength: linguistically motivated monotonicity bias reduces overfitting in low-resource settings and is empirically effective.  
- Weakness: novelty is debatable since the method resembles applying a sequence tagger to pre-aligned data and lacks comparison to such a baseline.  
- Weakness: evaluation confined to morphological inflection, limiting evidence for generality to other nearly-monotonic tasks (e.g., transliteration, g2p).  
- Question: clarify the precise alignment type used (1-to-many, many-to-1, or many-to-many) from the alignment tool.  
- Question: how does the proposed model conceptually differ from a simpler sequence-tagging approach trained on pre-computed alignments?  
- Question: why was a direct neural monotonic baseline (e.g., BiLSTM tagger on aligned data) not included?  
- Question: why restrict evaluation to morphology and not other monotonic transduction tasks to demonstrate generality?  
- Question: revise the conclusion claim “on par or better” since Table 3 shows soft-attention outperforms hard-attention on 7/10 languages.  
- Question: specify sources of morphological features used by the decoder for each dataset and how to disentangle feature effects from hard-attention effects.
