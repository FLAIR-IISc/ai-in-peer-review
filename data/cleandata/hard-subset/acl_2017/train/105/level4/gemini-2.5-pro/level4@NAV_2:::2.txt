**Summary of the Paper and Contributions**

This paper proposes a neural sequence-to-sequence architecture for morphological inflection generation that incorporates a hard monotonic attention mechanism. The core idea is to leverage the near-monotonic alignment between a word and its inflected form. The model works by first learning character-level alignments independently and then training an encoder-decoder network to predict a sequence of explicit actions—either `write` an output character or `step` the attention pointer forward over the input sequence. This approach decouples the alignment and transduction processes. The model's effectiveness is demonstrated through experiments on three morphological inflection datasets, where it achieves state-of-the-art results, especially in low-resource settings.

The main contributions of this work are:
1.  A novel encoder-decoder architecture with a hard attention mechanism that explicitly models and enforces the monotonic nature of transductions common in morphological tasks. The model's control mechanism, which predicts `step` or `write` actions, is a simple yet effective way to handle this constraint.
2.  Strong empirical results on three standard morphological inflection benchmarks (CELEX, Wiktionary, SIGMORPHON2016). Notably, the model is the first neural approach reported to outperform specialized latent-variable models on the low-resource CELEX dataset.
3.  An insightful analysis comparing the internal representations and alignments learned by the proposed hard attention model versus a standard soft attention model, clarifying how each architecture encodes positional and character information.

**Strengths**

1.  **Well-motivated and Intuitive Model:** The primary strength of this paper is its well-motivated design. While standard sequence-to-sequence models with soft attention are powerful, they are arguably too general for tasks like morphological inflection that are predominantly monotonic. The authors' choice to explicitly build this monotonic assumption into the architecture is both logical and elegant. The mechanism of learning to predict `step` and `write` actions based on pre-computed alignments is a clear and effective way to enforce this structural prior.
2.  **Excellent Empirical Performance, Especially in a Low-Resource Setting:** The proposed model achieves state-of-the-art or competitive results across all three evaluated datasets. The performance on the small CELEX dataset is particularly compelling. It demonstrates that by constraining the model's hypothesis space with a hard monotonic structure, the model is less prone to overfitting and can outperform more complex neural models as well as highly specialized non-neural models in data-scarce scenarios. This is a significant result for applying neural methods to morphologically rich, low-resource languages.
3.  **High-Quality Analysis and Presentation:** The paper is clearly written and easy to follow. The analysis in Section 5 provides valuable insights that go beyond simply reporting accuracy scores. By visualizing the learned alignments and projecting the encoder representations, the authors effectively demonstrate that their model learns linguistically plausible alignments and contrasts how it encodes positional information compared to a soft-attention baseline. This analysis strengthens the paper's claims and our understanding of how these models function.

**Weaknesses**

1.  **Missing Comparison to a Simpler Monotonic Baseline:** The paper's central claim rests on the superiority of its proposed architecture for monotonic transduction. However, it lacks a comparison to a simpler but strong baseline that also enforces monotonicity. For example, one could use a standard monotonic aligner to create 1-to-many alignments and then train a standard sequence labeling model (e.g., a BiLSTM tagger) on this aligned data. Such an approach also decouples alignment and transduction and is inherently monotonic. Without this comparison, it is difficult to fully assess whether the complexity of the proposed decoder and its control mechanism offers a significant advantage over simpler, established methods for monotonic sequence-to-sequence tasks.
2.  **Limited Scope of Evaluation:** The paper presents the model as a general solution for "nearly-monotonic sequence to sequence learning," but its evaluation is restricted to a single task: morphological inflection. To substantiate the claim of general applicability, it would have been beneficial to evaluate the model on other monotonic or near-monotonic tasks such as transliteration, grapheme-to-phoneme conversion, or text normalization. Demonstrating strong performance on a wider range of tasks would have made the paper's contribution much broader and more impactful.
3.  **Ambiguity Regarding the Impact of Linguistic Features:** The model uses morpho-syntactic features (e.g., POS, gender) as input to the decoder, but their source and specific contribution to the model's performance are not detailed. It remains unclear whether the performance gains over some baselines are primarily due to the novel architecture or the inclusion of these rich features. An ablation study removing these features would be necessary to isolate the effect of the hard attention mechanism and provide a more controlled comparison.

**Questions to Authors**

1.  Could you please clarify the precise nature of the alignments used to generate the gold action sequences? Section 2.3 states the model must "read all the input elements aligned to an output element ... before writing it," which implies a many-to-one mapping from input to output. However, the example "flog→fliege" in Figure 3 suggests a one-to-many alignment (o→ie). Can your alignment process produce many-to-many alignments, and if so, how are these deterministically converted into the linear sequence of `step` and `write` actions required for training?
2.  As mentioned in the weaknesses, a simpler monotonic baseline would involve training a sequence tagger (e.g., a BiLSTM) on pre-aligned data. How do you see your model differing conceptually and empirically from such an approach? Could you elaborate on why your proposed model might be advantageous compared to this simpler alternative?
3.  Could you provide more details about the morpho-syntactic features used in your experiments? Specifically, where were these features obtained for each of the three datasets?
4.  Figure 1 provides the main visual explanation of your model, but the use of a Cyrillic example makes it inaccessible to readers unfamiliar with the script. Would it be possible to replace this with an example in a more widely known script, such as the German example from the introduction ("hart" -> "härtestem"), to improve the figure's clarity and impact?
