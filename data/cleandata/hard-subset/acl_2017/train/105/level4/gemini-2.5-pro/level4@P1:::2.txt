### **Summary and Contributions**

This paper proposes a novel encoder-decoder architecture with a "hard monotonic attention" mechanism for morphological inflection generation. The key idea is to explicitly model the largely monotonic nature of this task by decoupling alignment and transduction. The model learns to generate a target sequence by following a pre-computed alignment path with explicit `step` and `write` actions. The authors evaluate this model on three morphological inflection datasets, achieving strong results.

As I see them, the main contributions of this work are:
1.  **A novel neural architecture that enforces hard monotonic attention.** This is well-suited for tasks like morphological inflection and is shown to be particularly effective in low-resource settings where more general models tend to overfit.
2.  **Strong empirical results on several benchmarks.** The model establishes a new state of the art on several datasets, most notably outperforming previous methods on the small CELEX dataset where other neural models have struggled.
3.  **An insightful analysis of learned representations.** The paper includes a valuable comparison of the internal representations learned by the proposed hard attention model versus a standard soft attention model, shedding light on how these models encode character identity and position.

### **Strengths**

The primary strength of this paper is its core idea: incorporating a strong, linguistically motivated inductive bias (monotonicity) into a neural sequence-to-sequence model. General encoder-decoder models with soft attention are overly flexible for this task, which can lead to overfitting, particularly on small datasets. By enforcing a hard monotonic alignment, the model's search space is constrained in a principled and effective way. This approach is well-justified, and the empirical results—especially the strong performance on the low-resource CELEX dataset (Table 1 and Figure 2)—provide compelling evidence for its effectiveness.

### **Weaknesses**

1.  **The novelty of the approach is debatable when compared to simpler alternatives.** The proposed method can be viewed as a re-framing of an established technique: applying a standard sequence tagger (e.g., a BiLSTM) to pre-aligned data. By decoupling alignment from transduction, the novelty of the "hard attention" mechanism is arguably diminished, as the model essentially learns to execute a pre-determined sequence of actions. The paper lacks a comparison to a strong sequence tagging baseline, which makes it difficult to assess the true contribution of the proposed architecture over simpler methods.
2.  **The evaluation is confined to a single task.** While the model is motivated by the general property of "nearly-monotonic" alignment, its effectiveness is not demonstrated on other tasks that share this property, such as transliteration or grapheme-to-phoneme conversion. This narrow evaluation limits the perceived generality and impact of the proposed method.

### **Questions to Authors**

1.  The description of the alignment process is ambiguous. Figure 1 and the 'flog' -> 'fliege' example suggest a 1-to-many alignment (one input character can align to zero, one, or multiple output characters). However, the text (lines 311-312: "requiring to read all the input elements aligned to an output element") seems to imply a many-to-1 alignment. Could you please clarify the precise nature of the alignments derived from the alignment tool and used by your model (1-to-many, many-to-1, or many-to-many)?

2.  A strong and straightforward baseline for monotonic sequence-to-sequence tasks is to frame the problem as sequence tagging. One could first align the input and output characters (e.g., using 1-to-many alignments) and then train a standard tagger, such as a BiLSTM, to predict the aligned output segments for each input character. For your example 'flog' -> 'fliege', the task would be to predict the sequence of tags 'f', 'l', 'ie', 'ge' for the input sequence 'f', 'l', 'o', 'g'.
    a) How does your proposed model conceptually differ from this simpler sequence tagging approach?
    b) Given its simplicity and direct relevance, why was a baseline of this type not included in your experiments?

3.  The paper's impact would be significantly greater if the model were evaluated on other monotonic sequence-to-sequence tasks (e.g., transliteration, grapheme-to-phoneme conversion). Since the core contribution is presented as a general mechanism for monotonic transduction, demonstrating its effectiveness beyond morphology is crucial for validating this claim. Was there a specific reason for limiting the evaluation to morphological inflection?

4.  The claim in the conclusion to perform "on par or better" (line 791) is an overstatement of the results. In Table 3, for instance, the proposed hard attention model is outperformed by the soft-attention baseline on 7 out of the 10 languages. Please revise this claim to more accurately reflect the empirical findings.

5.  The paper provides little detail about the morphological features used as input to the decoder (e.g., POS, gender, number).
    a) Could you please specify the source of these features for each dataset used in your experiments?
    b) How can you disentangle the effect of the hard attention mechanism from the effect of these features? Is it possible that the performance gains on certain datasets are due to differences in feature representations compared to the baseline models?

I remain curious how your model would compare against such a baseline. A sequence tagger is inherently monotonic, and when combined with pre-computed monotonic alignments, it provides a very direct and conceptually simple solution to this task. In contrast, your approach begins with a more powerful and general sequence-to-sequence framework that is then explicitly constrained to be monotonic, which seems like a less direct way to incorporate the same prior knowledge. A comparison would be very informative.
