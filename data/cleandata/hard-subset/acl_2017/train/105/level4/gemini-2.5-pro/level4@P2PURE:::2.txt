**Strengths:**
*   The paper proposes a novel encoder-decoder model that explicitly incorporates a hard monotonicity constraint.

**Weaknesses:**
*   The novelty of the model may be limited, as it could be viewed as a standard BiRNN where the alignment and transduction steps have been decoupled.
*   The experimental evaluation is limited to morphological tasks, despite the model's applicability to a broader range of monotone sequence-to-sequence problems.

**General Discussion:**

The authors propose a novel encoder-decoder neural network architecture featuring "hard monotonic attention" and evaluate it on three morphology datasets. The paper is well-written, generally clear, and introduces the valuable idea of explicitly modeling monotonicity for tasks where it is a natural constraint.

The motivation is clear: many sequence-to-sequence tasks, unlike machine translation, are inherently monotone, making standard attention-based encoder-decoder models potentially suboptimal. This paper's approach is to enforce monotonicity by decoupling alignment and transduction. The model first aligns the input and output sequences monotonically and then trains a transducer to generate the output in accordance with these alignments.

However, the description of this process lacks clarity on a key point, leading to the following questions:

1)  **Alignment Granularity:** The description of the alignment process is unclear. The running example in Figure 1 suggests a one-to-many alignment (one input character to zero, one, or more output characters). However, lines 311-312 seem to describe the opposite: multiple input characters aligning to a single output character. Could the authors clarify whether the model uses one-to-many, many-to-one, or many-to-many alignments?

2)  **Comparison to a Simpler Baseline:** A significant concern is the lack of comparison to a simpler, intuitive baseline for monotone sequence-to-sequence tasks. Such a baseline could be constructed in two stages:
    *   First, align the input and output strings monotonically using a standard aligner (e.g., the toolkit from Jiampojamarn and Kondrak), enforcing a one-to-many mapping. For example, "flog" -> "fliege" could be aligned as "f-f, l-l, o-ie, g-ge".
    *   Second, train a standard sequence tagger (e.g., a BiLSTM) to predict the aligned output segments for each input character.
    This two-stage approach, also discussed in Schnober et al. (2016, Section 4.2), seems conceptually simpler while still guaranteeing monotonicity.
    *   2a) How does the proposed model fundamentally differ from this two-stage approach?
    *   2b) Why was this strong and straightforward baseline not included in the experiments?

**Further Issues:**

3)  **Limited Scope of Evaluation:** The evaluation is limited to morphology. Given that the model's main contribution is its explicit handling of monotonicity, its strength could have been more convincingly demonstrated by evaluating it on other monotonic sequence-to-sequence tasks (as explored in Schnober et al., 2016).

4)  **Interpretation of Results:** The claim of performing "on par or better" (l. 791) seems to misrepresent the results. In cases where the proposed model underperforms the baseline, the results are described as "on par." This phrasing should be revised to provide a more objective summary of the experimental outcomes.

5)  **Linguistic Features:** The paper provides little detail about the linguistic features used. Figure 1 suggests they include POS tags.
    *   5a) What is the source of these features?
    *   5b) Could these features, rather than the monotonicity constraint, be responsible for the performance gains in some cases?

**Minor Points:**

6)  Equation (3): The term `NN` should be reformatted as `\text{NN}` or similar for clarity.
7)  l. 231: "Where" should be lowercased.
8)  l. 237 and elsewhere: For sequences like `x1...xn`, mathematical convention suggests using commas with low dots (`x1, ..., xn`) or centered dots without commas (`x1 ... xn`). The dots should be level with the surrounding symbols.
9)  Figure 1: The use of a Cyrillic font makes the example inaccessible to readers who do not have the font installed. An alternative representation or transliteration would be more inclusive.
10) l. 437: The word "this" should be "these."
