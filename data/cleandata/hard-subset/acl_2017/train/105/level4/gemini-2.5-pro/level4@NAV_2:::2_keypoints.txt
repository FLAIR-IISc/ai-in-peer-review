- (Summary) Proposes a seq2seq model with hard monotonic attention that predicts explicit `step`/`write` actions from precomputed character alignments; shows state-of-the-art results on three morphological inflection datasets, especially in low-resource settings.

- (Contribution) Novel encoder-decoder with hard attention that enforces monotonic transduction via explicit `step`/`write` control.

- (Contribution) Strong empirical performance on CELEX, Wiktionary, and SIGMORPHON2016, notably outperforming prior models on low-resource CELEX.

- (Contribution) Analysis comparing internal representations and alignments learned by the hard-attention model versus a soft-attention baseline.

- (Strength) Well-motivated, intuitive design that leverages the monotonic structure of morphological inflection and decouples alignment and transduction.

- (Strength) Excellent empirical results, particularly in low-resource scenarios, showing reduced overfitting relative to more flexible models.

- (Strength) Clear presentation and insightful analyses (alignment visualizations and representation projections) that support the paper's claims.

- (Weakness) No comparison to simpler monotonic baselines (e.g., BiLSTM tagger on pre-aligned data) to justify added decoder complexity.

- (Weakness) Evaluation scope is narrow (only morphological inflection), leaving the claimed generality to other near-monotonic tasks unverified.

- (Weakness) Ambiguity about morpho-syntactic features: their source and contribution are not ablated or clearly reported.

- (Q) Please clarify the alignment formalism: can it produce many-to-many alignments and how are these deterministically converted into `step`/`write` sequences?

- (Q) How does the proposed model differ conceptually and empirically from a simpler monotonic baseline (e.g., sequence tagger trained on aligned data)?

- (Q) Which morpho-syntactic features were used and where were they obtained for each dataset?

- (Q) Can Figure 1's Cyrillic example be replaced with a more accessible script (e.g., the German example) to improve clarity?
