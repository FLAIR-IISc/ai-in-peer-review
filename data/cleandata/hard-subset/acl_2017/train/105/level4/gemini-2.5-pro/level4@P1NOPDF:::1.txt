- Strengths:
The central contribution of this paper is the introduction of a hard monotonic attention mechanism, which is a novel and significant departure from previous approaches.

- Weaknesses:
The experimental results for morphological inflection generation are somewhat mixed. While the proposed model is effective on smaller training datasets (e.g., CELEX) and for languages where alignments are mostly monotonic and less context-sensitive (e.g., Russian, German, and Spanish), its performance is less competitive under other conditions.

- General Discussion:
The authors propose a novel neural model for morphological inflection generation that uses "hard attention." A key feature of this model is that the character alignments are obtained separately using a Bayesian transliteration method. This stands in contrast to the previous state-of-the-art neural models for this task, which typically use "soft attention" and solve for character alignment and conversion jointly within a single probabilistic framework.

The core idea is both novel and methodologically sound. The paper is clearly written, and the experiments are comprehensive. My primary concern is that the proposed method does not achieve state-of-the-art performance across all conditions. As the results indicate, it seems best suited for tasks characterized by mostly monotonic alignments and less context-sensitive phenomena. The paper would be more convincing if the authors also discussed the practical merits of their proposed method, such as its ease of implementation or its computational cost compared to soft-attention models. This would strengthen the argument for its adoption in the specific scenarios where it excels.
