**Strengths**

The paper proposes a novel encoder-decoder architecture that explicitly models and enforces monotonicity, a key constraint in many sequence-to-sequence tasks.

**Weaknesses**

*   The model's novelty is not clearly distinguished from a standard BiRNN with a decoupled alignment step.
*   The evaluation is limited to morphological tasks, despite the model's potential applicability to a broader range of monotonic sequence-to-sequence problems.

**General Discussion**

The authors propose a novel encoder-decoder neural network architecture featuring "hard monotonic attention," which they evaluate on three morphology datasets.

On one hand, the paper is well-written, generally clear, and presents the valuable contribution of explicitly enforcing monotonicity for this type of task. The motivation for incorporating monotonicity is sound: many sequence-to-sequence tasks outside of machine translation are inherently monotonic, making standard attention-based encoder-decoder models unnecessarily complex. The fact that general models perform well on these tasks is a testament to the power of neural methods, but explicitly modeling this constraint is a logical next step.

The authors' core idea is to enforce monotonic output generation by decoupling alignment and transduction. They first align the input and output sequences monotonically and then train a model to generate outputs that conform to these pre-determined alignments. However, the description of this process is unclear on several points:

1.  **Alignment Type:** Could the authors clarify the nature of the alignments? The running example in Figure 1 suggests 1-to-many alignments (one input character mapping to zero, one, or several output characters). However, this appears to conflict with the description on lines 311-312, which implies many-to-1 alignments. Does the model use 1-to-many, many-to-1, or many-to-many alignments?

2.  **Comparison to a Simpler Baseline:** A simpler approach to monotonic sequence-to-sequence modeling exists. One could first use a standard monotonic aligner (e.g., the Jiampojamarn and Kondrak toolkit) to establish 1-to-many alignments. For instance, `flog` -> `fliege` could be aligned as `f-f, l-l, o-ie, g-ge`. Following this alignment, a standard sequence tagger (such as an LSTM) could be trained to predict the output sequence (`f, l, ie, ge`) from the input sequence (`f, l, o, g`). This approach is discussed in prior work (e.g., Schnober et al., 2016, Section 4.2). This raises two questions:
    a) How does the proposed model differ conceptually from this simpler baseline?
    b) Why was this type of model not included as a baseline in the experiments?

3.  **Scope of Evaluation:** It is a significant missed opportunity that the model was only evaluated on morphology. Given that the core contribution is a general mechanism for monotonic transduction, demonstrating its effectiveness on other monotonic tasks (as explored in Schnober et al., 2016) would have substantially strengthened the paper's claims.

4.  **Reporting of Results:** The claim of performing "on par or better" (l.791) could be phrased more precisely. The current wording appears to categorize results that are slightly worse as "on par" while highlighting any positive result as "better." While the experimental results themselves are acceptable, the summary of these results should be more balanced and objective.

5.  **Linguistic Features:** The paper provides little detail about the linguistic features used. Figure 1 suggests they include POS tags.
    a) What is the source of these features?
    b) Is it possible that the performance gains in some cases are attributable to these features rather than the proposed monotonicity constraints?

**Minor Points**

6.  Equation (3): Please format `NN` as `\text{NN}` for clarity.
7.  l.231: "Where" should be lowercase.
8.  l.237 and elsewhere: The convention for sequences like `x1...xn` is typically `x_1, \ldots, x_n` (with commas) or `x_1 \cdots x_n` (with centered dots). Please ensure consistency.
9.  Figure 1: Using a Cyrillic example may hinder comprehension for readers unfamiliar with the script, making it difficult to follow the logic of the example. An example using the Latin alphabet would be more accessible.
