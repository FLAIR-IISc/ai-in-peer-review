### Summary and Contributions

This paper presents a novel neural sequence-to-sequence model for morphological inflection generation. The core contribution is a "hard" monotonic attention mechanism that is specifically designed for tasks where the input-output alignment is nearly sequential. In contrast to standard soft attention models that learn to align and transduce jointly, this model decouples the two steps. It first computes character-level alignments using an external tool and then trains an encoder-decoder network to predict a sequence of explicit actions ('write' or 'step') that reconstruct the target inflection from the source word.

From my perspective, the main contributions are, in order of strength:

1.  **A Novel Hard Monotonic Attention Model:** The proposed model introduces an intuitive and effective architecture that encodes a strong, appropriate inductive bias for morphological inflection. By treating transduction as a sequence of controlled read/write operations, it simplifies the learning problem and is particularly well-suited for the nearly monotonic alignments common in this task.

2.  **Strong Empirical Performance in Low-Resource Settings:** The paper demonstrates that this constrained model architecture provides a significant advantage when training data is scarce. The results on the CELEX dataset are particularly notable, as this is the first neural model to outperform the specialized latent-variable WFST model of Dreyer et al. (2008), a strong non-neural baseline.

3.  **Insightful Comparative Analysis:** The analysis in Section 5, which compares the learned representations and alignment behaviors of the hard and soft attention models, is a valuable contribution. The SVD visualizations effectively illustrate how the hard attention model separates character and position information, while the soft attention model must conflate them, offering a clear hypothesis for the proposed model's robustness on smaller datasets.

### Strengths

1.  **Novel and Well-Motivated Architecture:** The primary strength is the novelty of the hard attention mechanism. The decision to model inflection generation as a series of 'step' and 'write' actions based on a pre-computed alignment is an elegant way to incorporate domain knowledge (i.e., the near-monotonic nature of the task) into the model architecture. This contrasts sharply with soft attention models, which must learn this structural property from scratch and, as the experiments show, can struggle to do so without large amounts of data.

2.  **Superior Performance on Small Datasets:** The model's effectiveness in low-resource scenarios is a significant practical advantage. The experiments on the CELEX dataset (Table 1, Figure 2) provide compelling evidence that the hard attention model is less prone to overfitting than its soft attention counterpart. By constraining the model's focus, it learns a more generalizable mapping, achieving a new state-of-the-art on a challenging benchmark where previous neural models have underperformed.

3.  **Thorough Evaluation and Clear Presentation:** The paper is exceptionally well-written, clearly explaining the model and its motivation. The experimental evaluation is comprehensive, covering three standard datasets with varying sizes and linguistic properties. This allows for a nuanced understanding of where the model excels (CELEX, Wiktionary) and where its assumptions are less suitable (some SIGMORPHON languages). The analysis section further strengthens the paper by providing valuable insights into the model's internal workings.

### Weaknesses

1.  **Conditional State-of-the-Art Performance:** While the model is very effective under certain conditions, its superiority is not universal. The results on the SIGMORPHON 2016 dataset show that a standard soft attention model performs better on languages with more complex, non-monotonic morphological phenomena (e.g., templatic or circumfixing morphology). This suggests that the model's primary strength—its strong monotonic assumption—is also its main limitation. The paper would be strengthened by a more detailed discussion of these failure cases and the model's applicability boundaries.

2.  **Dependence on an External Aligner:** The model's success is contingent on the quality of alignments provided by an external, independently trained system. This introduces a potential dependency and performance bottleneck that is not explored in the paper. The choice of the alignment tool (Sudoh et al., 2013) seems well-motivated, but it is unclear how sensitive the model is to the quality of these alignments. An ablation study showing performance with a simpler aligner or with artificially noisy alignments would make the results more robust.

3.  **Lack of Discussion on Practical Trade-offs:** The paper focuses entirely on accuracy, but practical considerations like computational cost (training time, inference speed) and ease of implementation are important. The authors claim the model is "much simpler," but this is not substantiated. The two-stage process (align, then train) could potentially be faster than a joint soft-attention model, or it could be slower. A quantitative comparison of these practical aspects would provide a more complete picture of the method's advantages and disadvantages compared to the soft attention baseline.

### Questions to Authors

1.  Could you comment on the sensitivity of your model to the quality of the pre-computed alignments? For example, how does performance change if a simpler heuristic (e.g., Levenshtein alignment) is used to generate the oracle action sequences, or if the alignment quality degrades?

2.  The model's objective function maximizes the likelihood of the best action sequence (derivation), which is an approximation of maximizing the likelihood of the target string. Have you analyzed how often the single best derivation produces a different output string than what would be obtained by marginalizing over all possible derivations? Are there specific types of morphological phenomena where this approximation is problematic?

3.  Could you provide some details on the practical trade-offs of your model? Specifically, how does the training time of the two-stage process (alignment + network training) compare to that of the end-to-end soft attention baseline?
