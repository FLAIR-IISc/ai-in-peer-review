### Summary and General Discussion

The authors propose a novel encoder-decoder neural network architecture that incorporates a "hard monotonic attention" mechanism. The model is evaluated on three morphological datasets.

This is a challenging paper to evaluate. On one hand, it is well-written, generally clear, and presents the novel idea of explicitly enforcing monotonicity in morphological tasks. The motivation for this is well-founded: unlike machine translation, many sequence-to-sequence tasks are monotonic. The fact that general, non-monotonic models perform well on these tasks is a testament to the power of neural approaches, but it also suggests that explicitly modeling this constraint could yield further improvements.

The authors' central idea is to enforce monotonic output generation by decoupling the alignment and transduction steps. Their model first aligns the input and output sequences monotonically and then trains a transducer to generate outputs consistent with these alignments. However, the description of this core mechanism lacks clarity on some key points.

### Strengths

*   The primary strength of this work is its novel encoder-decoder architecture that explicitly models and enforces monotonicity, a key constraint in many sequence-to-sequence tasks.

### Weaknesses

*   The model's novelty is debatable; it could be interpreted as a standard BiRNN where the alignment and transduction steps have been decoupled, rather than a fundamentally new architecture.
*   The empirical evaluation is limited to morphological tasks, which is a missed opportunity to demonstrate the model's general applicability to other monotonic sequence-to-sequence problems.

### Detailed Questions and Comments

1.  **Clarity on Alignment Type:** The paper is unclear about the nature of the alignments. The running example in Figure 1 suggests 1-to-many alignments (one input character to zero, one, or several output characters). However, lines 311-312 seem to describe many-to-1 alignments (several input characters to one output character). Could the authors clarify whether the model supports 1-to-many, many-to-1, or many-to-many alignments?

2.  **Comparison to a Simpler Baseline:** A straightforward and powerful baseline for monotonic sequence-to-sequence tasks appears to be missing. This baseline would involve a two-stage process:
    a) First, align the input and output characters monotonically using a standard tool (e.g., the toolkit from Jiampojamarn and Kondrak) with a 1-to-many constraint. For the example `flog -> fliege` (l. 613), this would yield an alignment like `f-l-o-g` to `f-l-ie-ge`.
    b) Second, train a standard sequence tagger (e.g., an LSTM or BiLSTM) to predict the aligned output segments (`f`, `l`, `ie`, `ge`) for each corresponding input character (`f`, `l`, `o`, `g`). This approach is inherently monotonic and has been explored in prior work (e.g., Schnober et al., 2016, Section 4.2).

    My questions are:
    *   How does the proposed model fundamentally differ from this simpler sequence tagging formulation?
    *   Why was this strong and relevant baseline not included in the experimental comparison?

3.  **Limited Task Diversity:** It is unfortunate that the evaluation is confined to morphology. The model's explicit handling of monotonicity is its main selling point, and its superiority could have been more convincingly demonstrated by evaluating on a wider range of monotonic tasks (as also discussed in Schnober et al., 2016).

4.  **Interpretation of Results:** The claim of performing "on par or better" (l. 791) seems to reflect a common cognitive bias where performance slightly below a baseline is described as "on par". The phrasing should be more precise and objective, although the overall experimental results are acceptable.

5.  **Impact of Linguistic Features:** The paper provides little detail about the linguistic features used (e.g., POS tags inferred from Figure 1).
    *   What is the source of these features?
    *   Is it possible that these features, rather than the monotonicity constraints, are the primary reason for the model's performance gains in certain cases? An ablation study would be helpful here.

### Minor Points

6.  Equation (3): Please format `NN` as a non-italicized operator, such as $\text{NN}$.
7.  l. 231: "Where" should be lowercase.
8.  l. 237 (and elsewhere): For sequences like `x1...xn`, mathematical typesetting conventions recommend using commas with centered dots ($x_1, \ldots, x_n$) or no commas with baseline dots ($x_1 \cdots x_n$).
9.  Figure 1: The use of a Cyrillic font makes the example difficult for many readers to follow or reference. An example using the Latin alphabet would be more accessible.
10. l. 437: "this" should be "these".

It remains unclear how the proposed, more complex model compares to this straightforward and powerful baseline, and I wonder how much the results would have differed.
