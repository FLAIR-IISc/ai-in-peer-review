- Proposes a novel encoder-decoder with hard monotonic attention that decouples alignment and transduction and is evaluated on morphological inflection/reinflection, yielding competitive results.  
- Strength: explicitly models and enforces monotonicity, a well-motivated alternative to soft-attention for inherently monotonic tasks.  
- Strength: paper is generally well-written and the core idea is clearly presented.  
- Weakness: No comparison to a simple strong baseline (monotone aligner + sequence tagger), making it hard to assess the architecture's true contribution.  
- Weakness: Ambiguity about alignment type—figure suggests 1-to-many but text implies many-to-1; clarification needed on supported alignment types.  
- Weakness: Limited experimental scope—only morphological tasks; broader monotonic tasks (e.g., transliteration, G2P) would better demonstrate generality.  
- Weakness: Inadequate discussion of relation to prior hard-attention/structured-prediction work (e.g., Rastogi et al. 2016, Stack LSTM, Cohn et al. 2016).  
- Weakness: Insufficient detail and analysis of linguistic features (source and impact); possible that features drive gains rather than architecture.  
- Question: Please clarify whether the model uses 1-to-many, many-to-1, or many-to-many alignments.  
- Question: Explain conceptual differences from the two-stage monotone-aligner + tagger baseline and why that baseline was not evaluated.  
- Question: Specify the source of linguistic features and provide ablation results to isolate their effect from monotonicity constraints.  
- Minor: Rephrase "on par or better" to state results precisely.  
- Minor: Format Equation (3)ʼs NN as a non-italic operator (e.g., \text{NN}).  
- Minor: Fix typos/capitalization (l.231 "Where"→"where"; l.437 "this"→"these") and improve sequence/math typesetting (use commas with \ldots or centered dots).  
- Minor: Replace Cyrillic example in Figure 1 with a Latin-alphabet example for wider accessibility.
