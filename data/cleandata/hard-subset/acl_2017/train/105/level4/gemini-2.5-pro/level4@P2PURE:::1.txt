**Strengths:**
The core idea of employing hard monotonic attention is novel and represents a significant departure from previous approaches in this area.

**Weaknesses:**
The experimental results for morphological inflection generation are mixed. The proposed model's effectiveness appears to be conditional: it performs well on smaller training datasets (e.g., CELEX) and for languages where alignments are predominantly monotonic and less context-sensitive (e.g., Russian, German, and Spanish).

**General Discussion:**
The authors propose a novel neural model for morphological inflection generation that utilizes "hard attention," based on character alignments obtained separately via a Bayesian transliteration method. This approach is substantially different from the previous state-of-the-art, which uses "soft attention" to solve for character alignment and conversion jointly within a single probabilistic model.

The idea is novel and sound, the paper is clearly written, and the experiments are comprehensive. My primary concern is that the proposed method does not achieve state-of-the-art performance across all conditions. Its suitability is most evident for tasks characterized by mostly monotonic alignments and less context-sensitive phenomena. The paper's contribution would be strengthened by a discussion of the proposed method's practical merits, such as its relative ease of implementation or computational cost compared to soft attention models.
