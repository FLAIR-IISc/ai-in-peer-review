- Strengths: A new encoder-decoder model has been proposed that explicitly addresses monotonicity.
- Weaknesses: The model is probably a simple BiRNN with aligned de-coupled sequences.
- Evaluation was only done on morphology, not on any other monotonic Seq2Seq function.
- General Discussion:
The authors propose a novel encoder-decoder neural network architecture with "hard monotonic attention." They evaluate it on three morphology datasets.
This paper is a hard paper. On one hand, it is well-written, mostly very clear, and also presents a new idea, namely including monotonicity in the morphology function.
The reason for including such monotonicity is very clear: unlike machine translation, many seq2seq tasks are monotonic, and therefore a general encoder-decoder model should not be used in the first place. The fact that they still perform quite well should be considered a strong argument for general neural techniques. The idea of this paper is now clearly to implement a monotonic output character generation. They do this by separating alignment and transduction and first aligning the input-output sequences monotonically, and then training to generate outputs corresponding to the monotonic alignment.
However, the authors are not clear at this point. I have some questions:
1) How do your alignments look? On one hand, the alignments seem to be 1-to-many type (as in the running example, in Figure 1), that is, 1 input character can be aligned with zero, 1, or many output characters. However, this seems to contradict the description given in line 311-312 where the authors talk about several input characters aligned with 1 output character. That is, do you use 1-to-many, many-to-1, or many-to-many alignments?
2) In fact, there is a very simple approach for monotonic Seq2Seq. In the first step, align the input and output characters monotonically with a 1-to-many constraint (any monotonic aligner can be used, such as Giampozamarn and Kondruk's toolkit). Then train a standard sequence tagger (!) to correctly predict these 1-to-many alignments. For example, flog->fliege (your example at l.613): first align as "f-l-o-g / f-l-ie-ge". Now use any tagger (you can use an LSTM if you want) to predict "f-l-ie-ge" (a sequence of 4 length) "f-l-o-g" (a sequence of 4 length). Such an approach may have been suggested in several papers, a reference can be [*, section 4.2] below.
I have two questions:
2a) How does your approach differ from this rather simple idea?
2b) Why didn't you include it as a baseline?
Further issues:
3) It is indeed sad that you only tested on morphology, because there are many other interesting monotonic seq2seq tasks, and you could have shown the superiority of your system by evaluating on these, given that you explicitly model monotonicity (cf. also [*]).
4) You do "equal or better" (l.791). It seems there is a general cognitive bias among NLP researchers to map those examples as "equal" and all the rest as "better" where they perform poorly. I think this wording should be fixed, but otherwise I am fine with the experimental results.
5) You say very little about your linguistic features: from Figure 1, I think they include POS etc.
5a) Where did you get these features from?
5b) Is it possible that these are responsible for your better performance in some cases, rather than monotonicity constraints?
Mundane Matters:
6) Equation (3): Please rewrite $NN$ as $\text{NN}$ or similarly
7) l.231 "where" should be short
8) l.237 and many more: $x1\ldots xn$. As far as I know, the mathematics community recommends writing $x1,\ldots,xn$ but $x1\cdots xn$. That is, the dots should be at the same level as the surrounding symbols.
9) Figure 1: Is it really necessary to use Cyrillic fonts? I can't even address your example here, because I don't have your fonts.
10) l.437: Should be "ये"
