- **Strength:** A new encoder-decoder model has been proposed that explicitly addresses monotony.
- **Weakness:** The model is likely a simple BiRNN, where the alignments are de-coupled.
- Only evaluated on morphology, not on any other monotonous sequence-to-sequence (Seq2Seq) task.
- General Discussion:
The authors propose a new encoder-decoder neural network architecture with "hard monotonic attention." They evaluated it on three morphology datasets.
This paper is quite challenging. On one hand, it is well-written, mostly very clear, and presents a new concept, such as incorporating monotony into the morphology task.
The reason for including such monotony is quite clear: unlike machine translation, many sequence-to-sequence tasks are monotonous, and therefore general encoder-decoder models should not be used in the first place. They still perform reasonably well, which should generally be considered a strong argument for neural techniques. The concept of this paper now clearly applies to a monotonous output character generation. They de-couple the alignment and transduction and first align the input-output sequences monotonically and then train the output to conform to the monotonous alignment.
However, the authors are unclear on this point. I have a few questions:
1) What do your alignments look like? On one hand, the alignments seem to be of a 1-to-many type (as in the running example, Figure 1), that is, 1 input character can be aligned with zero, 1, or multiple output characters. However, this seems to contradict the description given in lines 311-312, where the authors talk about a few input characters aligned with 1 output character. That is, do you use a 1-to-many, many-to-1, or many-to-many alignment?
2) Actually, there is a fairly simple method for monotonous sequence-to-sequence. In the first step, align the input and output characters monotonically with a 1-to-many constraint (any monotonous alignment can be used, such as Geomपोजarn and Kondrak's toolkit). Then train a standard sequence tagger to correctly predict these 1-to-many alignments. For example, flog->fliege (your example in l.613): first align like "f-l-o-g / f-l-ie-ge". Now use any tagger (you can use an LSTM if you want) to predict "f-l-ie-ge" (sequence of length 4) from "f-l-o-g" (sequence of length 4). Such a method may be proposed in multiple papers, a reference can be [*, section 4.2] below.
Here are my two questions:
2a) How does your method differ from this rather general concept?
2b) Why didn't you include it as a baseline?
Further problems:
3) It's really sad that you only tested in morphology, because there are many more interesting monotonous sequence-to-sequence tasks, and if you explicitly modeled monotony, you could have shown the superiority of your system by evaluating on these (cf. also [*]).
4) You perform "on per or better" (l.791). There is a common cognitive bias among NLP researchers where they map bad-performing examples as "on per" and all the rest as "better." I think this wording should be corrected, but otherwise I am fine with the experimental results.
5) You have said very little about your linguistic features: from Figure 1, I assume they include POS, etc.
5c) Where did you get these features from?
5d) Is it possible that these are responsible for some of your good performance, rather than the limitation of monotony?
Minor issues:
6) Equation (3): Please rewrite $NN$ as $\text{NN}$ or similarly
7) l.231 "Where" should be lowercase
8) l.237 and many more: $x1\ldots xn$. As far as I know, the mathematics community recommends writing $x1,\ldots,xn$ but $x1\cdots xn$. That is, the dots should be above the level of the adjacent symbols.
9) Figure 1: Is it really necessary to use Cyrillic fonts? I can't even solve your example here, because I don't have your font.
10) l.437: Should be "these"
