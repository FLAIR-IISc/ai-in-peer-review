- Strength: Introduces a novel encoder-decoder architecture with explicit hard monotonic attention.  
- Weakness: Proposed model may be essentially a standard BiRNN with decoupled alignments.  
- Weakness: Evaluation is limited to morphology and doesn’t test other monotonic Seq2Seq tasks.  
- Core idea: Decouple alignment and transduction by first creating monotonic alignments, then training the model to generate outputs consistent with those alignments.  
- Unclear: The exact nature of alignments (1-to-many, many-to-1, or many-to-many) needs clarification.  
- Question: How does this method differ from a simpler two-step approach (monotone 1-to-many aligner + neural sequence tagger)?  
- Baseline omission: Why was the simpler 1-x alignment + BiLSTM tagger baseline not included?  
- Concern: Restricting experiments to morphology misses other monotonic tasks where the method might show advantages.  
- Wording critique: The claim “on par or better” is overly optimistic and should be rephrased.  
- Feature confound: Linguistic features (e.g., POS) are insufficiently detailed—features might drive gains rather than monotonicity constraints.  
- After author response: Authors clarify they align 1-0, 0-1, 1-1 and compose many-to-many alignments from these.  
- Reviewer follow-up: Still requests comparison to Schnober et al.’s 1-x alignments + neural tagger to measure difference.  
- AC notes: Cite Cohn et al. (2016) as relevant and ask whether the architecture relates to Stack LSTM action-prediction methods.  
- Alignment method question: Does using a greedy alignment lose performance compared to methods that sum over all alignments (e.g., Rastogi et al., 2016)?  
- Minor editorial points: Fix formatting of “NN” in Eq.(3), lowercase “where” at line 231, correct ellipsis formatting, avoid Cyrillic font in Fig.1, and replace “these” with “those.”
