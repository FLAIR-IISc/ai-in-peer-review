## Review of "Morphological Inflection Generation with Hard Monotonic Attention"

This paper presents a novel encoder-decoder neural network architecture with "hard monotonic attention" for morphological inflection generation. The authors evaluate their model on three morphology datasets, establishing new state-of-the-art results. This is a well-written and largely clear paper that addresses an important aspect of sequence-to-sequence modeling – explicitly incorporating monotonicity when appropriate.

**Contributions:**

I identify two primary contributions:

1. **A novel hard attention mechanism for morphological inflection:** The authors propose a model that explicitly models nearly-monotonic alignment, a common characteristic of morphological inflection. This is achieved by decoupling alignment and transduction and using a hard attention mechanism guided by pre-computed alignments.
2. **Strong empirical results on morphological inflection tasks:** The model achieves state-of-the-art performance on three standard datasets, demonstrating its effectiveness in a challenging domain. Notably, the model surpasses previous approaches on the small CELEX dataset, indicating improved data efficiency.

**Strengths:**

1. **Addressing Monotonicity:** The core idea of explicitly incorporating monotonicity into the model is strong and well-motivated. Morphological inflection is inherently a monotone process, and leveraging this property is a logical and potentially beneficial approach.
2. **State-of-the-Art Performance:** The reported results are competitive and demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed model, particularly on the CELEX dataset where data sparsity is a major challenge.
3. **Thorough Evaluation:** The authors evaluate their model on three different datasets, providing a comprehensive assessment of its performance and generalization ability. The inclusion of baselines and ablation studies strengthens the empirical validation.
4. **Analysis of Learned Representations:** The analysis comparing the learned representations of the hard and soft attention models provides valuable insights into how these models capture morphological information. This adds to the scientific contribution beyond simply achieving good performance.
5. **Clear Presentation:** The paper is generally well-written and the proposed model is clearly explained, with a helpful diagram (Figure 1) illustrating the process.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Scope of Evaluation:** While the results on morphology are strong, the model is only evaluated on this specific task. Evaluating its performance on other naturally monotonic sequence-to-sequence tasks (e.g., speech recognition, transliteration) would strengthen the claim of a broadly applicable contribution.
2. **Comparison to a Simpler Baseline:** The proposed approach, while novel, shares similarities with a simpler baseline involving pre-aligning sequences monotonically and then training a sequence tagger to predict the output sequence. The authors do not provide a clear justification for why this baseline was not included in their experiments, and a comparison would help to better assess the added value of their more complex model.
3. **Alignment Details and Greedy Approach:** The description of the alignments used for training (1-to-many, many-to-1, etc.) could be clarified. Furthermore, the reliance on a greedy alignment strategy raises questions about potential suboptimality. Considering a probabilistic alignment approach, or summing over multiple alignments as done in Rastogi et al. (2016), could be beneficial.
4. **Feature Attribution:** The contribution of the linguistic features used as input to the model is not fully explored. It is possible that these features significantly contribute to the performance gains, rather than solely the hard monotonicity constraint.

**Questions to Authors:**

1. Could you provide a more detailed explanation of the alignment process? Specifically, what types of alignments (1-to-1, 1-to-many, many-to-1, many-to-many) are generated, and how are ambiguities resolved?
2. Why was the sequence tagging baseline (pre-align with a monotonic aligner, then train a sequence tagger) not included in the experiments? How do you anticipate your results would differ if this baseline were included?
3. Have you considered exploring a probabilistic alignment approach, or summing over multiple alignments, instead of using a greedy alignment strategy?
4. Could you provide more details on the source of the linguistic features used as input to the model? Have you performed ablation studies to assess their individual contributions to the overall performance?
