The authors propose a novel encoder-decoder neural network architecture with “hard monotonic attention” and evaluate it on three morphology datasets. This paper presents a well-written and generally clear novel idea: explicitly incorporating monotonicity into sequence-to-sequence models for morphology tasks. The motivation is sound – many seq2seq tasks are inherently monotone, and standard encoder-decoder models do not inherently leverage this property. The reasonable performance of general models suggests the power of neural techniques, and explicitly enforcing monotonicity is a logical step. The core idea, decoupling alignment and transduction to first establish monotone alignments and then generate outputs accordingly, is interesting. However, several points require further clarification.

First, the nature of the alignments is unclear. The running example (Fig. 1) suggests a 1-to-many alignment scheme (one input character mapping to zero, one, or multiple output characters). This appears to contrast with the description in lines 311-312, which mentions multiple input characters aligning to one output character. Clarification is needed: does the model employ 1-to-many, many-to-1, or many-to-many alignments?

Second, a relatively straightforward approach to monotone Seq2Seq seems to have been overlooked. A first stage could involve monotone alignment (e.g., using the toolkit of Jiampojamarn and Kondrak) with a 1-to-many constraint. Subsequently, a standard sequence tagger (potentially an LSTM) could be trained to predict the output sequence directly from the aligned input sequence. Schnober et al. (2016) (referenced below) suggest a similar approach. Specifically: 2a) How does the proposed approach differ from this simpler baseline? 2b) Why was this baseline not included in the experimental evaluation?

Further concerns: 3) The evaluation is limited to morphology. Given the explicit modeling of monotonicity, it would strengthen the paper to demonstrate performance on other relevant monotonic seq2seq tasks, as suggested in Schnober et al. (2016). 4) The phrasing "on par or better" (l.791) feels biased. A more objective description of the results would be beneficial. 5) The linguistic features used are not adequately described. 5a) What is the source of these features? 5b) Could these features, rather than the monotonicity constraints, be contributing to the observed performance gains?

Minor issues: 6) Equation (3): please re-write $NN$ as $\text{NN}$ or similar. 7) l.231: “Where” should be lowercase. 8) l.237 and elsewhere: replace $x1\ldots xn$ with $x_1, \ldots, x_n$ or $x_1 \cdots x_n$. 9) Figure 1: The use of Cyrillic font hinders accessibility and prevents proper review of the example. 10) l.437: should be "these".

**After Author Response:**

The author’s clarifications regarding the alignments (1-0, 0-1, 1-1, leading to many-to-many) are appreciated, though the presentation was initially confusing. However, my core concern remains. While comparisons to Nicolai, Cherry, and Kondrak (2015) are made, the absence of a baseline employing 1-x (x in 0,1,2) alignments followed by a neural tagger (e.g., BiLSTM), as suggested in Schnober et al. (2016), is a significant omission. This simpler baseline leverages the benefits of a monotone model (the tagger) built upon monotone alignments. The proposed approach, in contrast, starts with a more general model and imposes hard monotonicity constraints, raising the question of how much performance is gained by the complexity of the approach versus the simpler alternative.

**Additional Notes (from AC):**

Cohn et al. (2016) (http://www.aclweb.org/anthology/N16-1102) is also relevant. Furthermore, the architecture's relationship to methods like Stack LSTMs, which also predict a sequence of actions to modify or annotate an input, should be discussed. Finally, it’s worth considering whether the greedy alignment approach might incur a loss compared to methods like Rastogi et al. (2016) which sum over all possible alignments.
