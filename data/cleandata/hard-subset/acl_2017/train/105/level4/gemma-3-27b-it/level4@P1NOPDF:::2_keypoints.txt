- Strength: Proposes a novel encoder-decoder architecture that explicitly models monotonicity via hard monotonic attention.
- Weakness: May effectively be a BiRNN with alignments decoupled, questioning novelty.
- Weakness: Evaluation is limited to morphology tasks; no experiments on other monotone sequence-to-sequence problems.
- Paper is generally well-written and introduces the idea of explicitly enforcing monotonic output generation.
- Rationale: Many seq2seq tasks are inherently monotone, so enforcing monotonicity is a sensible modeling choice.
- Method: Decouples alignment and transduction—first compute monotonic alignments, then train to generate outputs consistent with them.
- Clarity issue: Unclear which alignment scheme is used—1-to-many, many-to-1, or many-to-many.
- Baseline concern: A simpler pipeline (monotonic 1-to-many aligner + sequence tagger/BiLSTM) exists and was not used as a baseline.
- Question: How does the proposed approach differ from the simple align-then-tag baseline, and why was it omitted?
- Evaluation gap: Demonstrating performance on other monotone tasks would strengthen claims of superiority.
- Wording: The phrase “on par or better” is biased and should be revised.
- Feature confound: Sparse detail on linguistic features (e.g., POS)—ask source and whether features, not monotonicity, drive gains.
- Minor corrections: notation in Eq.(3), lowercase “where” at l.231, use x_1,...,x_n notation, fix Cyrillic font in Fig.1, typo at l.437.
- After-author-response note: Author clarified initial alignments are 1-0, 0-1, 1-1 then composed into many-to-many.
- Remaining question: Why not use 1-x alignments (as in Schnober et al.) and train a neural tagger baseline to compare?
- Related work / architecture questions: Cite Cohn et al. (2016); ask whether architecture relates to Stack LSTM and whether greedy alignment loses compared to summing over alignments (Rastogi et al. 2016).
