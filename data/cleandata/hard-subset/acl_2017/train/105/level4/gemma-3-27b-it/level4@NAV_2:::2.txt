The authors propose a novel encoder-decoder neural network architecture with "hard monotonic attention". They evaluate it on three morphology datasets. 
This paper is a tough one. On the one hand it is well-written, mostly very clear and also presents a novel idea, namely including monotonicity in morphology tasks. 
The reason for including such monotonicity is pretty obvious: Unlike machine translation, many seq2seq tasks are monotone, and therefore general encoder-decoder models should not be used in the first place. That they still perform reasonably well should be considered a strong argument for neural techniques, in general. The idea of this paper is now to explicity enforce a monotonic output character generation. They do this by decoupling alignment and transduction and first aligning input-output sequences monotonically and then training to generate outputs in agreement with the monotone alignments.
However, the authors are unclear on this point. I have a few questions:
1) How do your alignments look like? On the one hand, the alignments seem to be of the kind 1-to-many (as in the running example, Fig.1), that is, 1 input character can be aligned with zero, 1, or several output characters. However, this seems to contrast with the description given in lines 311-312 where the authors speak of several input characters aligned to 1 output character. That is, do you use 1-to-many, many-to-1 or many-to-many alignments?
2) Actually, there is a quite simple approach to monotone Seq2Seq. In a first stage, align input and output characters monotonically with a 1-to-many constraint (one can use any monotone aligner, such as the toolkit of Jiampojamarn and Kondrak). Then one trains a standard sequence tagger(!) to predict exactly these 1-to-many alignments. For example, flog->fliege (your example on l.613): First align as in "f-l-o-g / f-l-ie-ge". Now use any tagger (could use an LSTM, if you like) to predict "f-l-ie-ge" (sequence of length 4) from "f-l-o-g" (sequence of length 4). Such an approach may have been suggested in multiple papers, one reference could be [Schnober et al., 2016]. 
My two questions here are: 
2a) How does your approach differ from this rather simple idea?
2b) Why did you not include it as a baseline?
Further issues:
3) It's really a pitty that you only tested on morphology, because there are many other interesting monotonic seq2seq tasks, and you could have shown your system's superiority by evaluating on these, given that you explicitly model monotonicity (cf. also [Schnober et al., 2016]).
4) You perform "on par or better" (l.791). There seems to be a general cognitive bias among NLP researchers to map instances where they perform worse to "on par" and all the rest to "better". I think this wording should be corrected, but otherwise I'm fine with the experimental results.
5) You say little about your linguistic features: From Fig. 1, I infer that they include POS, etc. 
5a) Where did you take these features from?
5b) Is it possible that these are responsible for your better performance in some cases, rather than the monotonicity constraints?
Minor points:
6) Equation (3): please re-write $NN$ as $\text{NN}$ or similar
7) l.231 "Where" should be lower case
8) l.237 and many more: $x1\ldots xn$. As far as I know, the math community recommends to write $x1,\ldots,xn$ but $x1\cdots xn$. That is, dots should be on the same level as surrounding symbols.
9) Figure 1: is it really necessary to use cyrillic font? I can't even address your example here, because I don't have your fonts.
10) l.437: should be "these"

**Contributions:** The primary contribution of this work is a novel neural architecture for morphological inflection generation that explicitly models the nearly-monotonic alignment between input and output character sequences via a hard attention mechanism. This allows for improved performance, especially in low-resource scenarios, compared to existing methods. A secondary contribution is the thorough experimental evaluation on three established datasets, demonstrating state-of-the-art results and a detailed analysis comparing the proposed model with soft attention mechanisms. Finally, the analysis of learned representations provides insights into how these models encode morphological features.

**Strengths:**
1. **Strong Performance in Low-Resource Settings:** The model demonstrably outperforms existing approaches, including latent variable WFSTs, on the CELEX dataset, which is a particularly challenging low-resource scenario. This is a significant contribution as it addresses a key limitation of neural sequence-to-sequence models. The quantitative results (Table 1) clearly support this claim.
2. **Novel Hard Attention Mechanism:** The introduction of a hard attention mechanism, combined with the explicit modeling of monotonic alignment, is a valuable contribution. This offers a different perspective on sequence transduction compared to standard soft attention approaches and provides a more focused and interpretable learning process.
3. **Comprehensive Experimental Evaluation:** The authors conducted thorough experiments on three diverse datasets (CELEX, Wiktionary, and SIGMORPHON2016). This breadth of evaluation provides strong evidence for the generalizability of the proposed model and allows for a fair comparison with established baselines.
4. **Insightful Analysis of Learned Representations:** The analysis of learned representations (Section 5) offers valuable insights into the model’s internal workings. Understanding how the model encodes morphological features is crucial for further development and improvement. The visualization of representations (Figure 4) is particularly helpful.

**Weaknesses:**
1. **Limited Scope of Evaluation:** While the experiments are comprehensive in terms of datasets, the evaluation is limited to morphological inflection generation. Exploring the model’s performance on other nearly-monotonic sequence transduction tasks would strengthen the claims about the general applicability of the approach.
2. **Lack of Comparison to a Strong Baseline:** The authors do not compare their model to a simple, yet potentially strong, baseline: a character-level alignment followed by a sequence tagger trained to predict the output sequence given the aligned input. This baseline, as suggested in the review and referencing Schnober et al. (2016), would have provided a more direct comparison of the benefits of the proposed architecture.
3. **Ambiguity in Alignment Types:** The description of the alignment process is somewhat ambiguous. Clarifying whether the model uses 1-to-many, many-to-1, or many-to-many alignments is crucial for understanding the model’s behavior. 
4. **Potential Influence of Linguistic Features:** The role of linguistic features (POS tags, etc.) is not fully explored. It’s possible that these features contribute significantly to the observed performance gains, and disentangling their effect from the hard attention mechanism would be beneficial.

**Questions to Authors:**
1. Can you provide more details on the specific alignment types used in your model (1-to-many, many-to-1, many-to-many)?
2. Why was the baseline of character-level alignment followed by a sequence tagger not included in your experiments? Could you comment on how you expect your model to compare to such a baseline?
3. Could you provide a more detailed analysis of the contribution of the linguistic features to the overall performance of the model?
