- Proposes a novel neural model for morphological inflection using a hard attention mechanism guided by separately learned character alignments, unlike prior soft-attention approaches.  
- Main contributions: a hard-attention inductive bias for near-monotonic inflection, state-of-the-art results (notably on low-resource CELEX), and a detailed comparison of hard vs. soft attention representations and alignments.  
- Novelty: explicitly models alignment separately, offering robustness and interpretability by exploiting task structure.  
- Strong low-resource performance: outperforms previous methods on CELEX, showing effectiveness with limited data.  
- Comprehensive evaluation across CELEX, Wiktionary, and SIGMORPHON2016 strengthens result validity.  
- Insightful analysis: hard-attention encodes less positional information and relies on explicit step actions, illuminating behavioral differences with soft attention.  
- Mixed results: performs well on suffixing morphology and stem changes but lags on languages with complex phenomena (e.g., vowel harmony).  
- Dependency on pre-trained alignments: model performance is tied to alignment quality, introducing an external dependency.  
- Limited practical discussion: lacks detailed comparison of implementation complexity and computational cost versus soft attention.  
- Incremental gains: improvements over strong baselines are small on some datasets (e.g., Wiktionary), raising questions about practical impact with ample data.  
- Question: How sensitive is performance to the quality of pre-trained character alignments (ablation with different aligners/noise)?  
- Question: Can the authors provide a detailed comparison of computational cost and implementation complexity vs. standard soft attention?  
- Question: How does the reduced positional encoding in hard attention affect handling of long-range dependencies?  
- Question: What are the limitations on languages with complex morphological phenomena (vowel/consonant harmony) and possible model modifications to address them?  
- Additional notes: paper is well-written, related work is comprehensive, and source code is provided on GitHub.
