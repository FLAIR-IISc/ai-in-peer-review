This paper presents a novel neural model for morphological inflection generation employing a hard attention mechanism, inspired by the nearly-monotonic alignment commonly found in this task. The model leverages separately learned character alignments to guide transduction via a hard attention control mechanism. This approach distinguishes it from prior work utilizing soft attention, which jointly learns alignment and transduction.

The main contributions of this work, as I see them, are: (1) the introduction of a hard attention mechanism specifically designed to exploit the inherent near-monotonicity of morphological inflection, offering a different inductive bias than prevalent soft attention approaches; (2) the demonstration of state-of-the-art performance on several morphological inflection datasets, particularly excelling in low-resource settings (CELEX) where it surpasses previous latent variable models; and (3) a detailed analysis comparing the learned representations and alignments of the hard and soft attention models, providing insights into their respective strengths and weaknesses.

**Strengths:**

1. **Novelty of Approach:** The proposed hard attention mechanism offers a conceptually distinct approach to morphological inflection generation. By explicitly modeling the alignment process separately, the model avoids the complexity of learning alignment jointly with transduction, potentially leading to more robust and interpretable results. This is particularly appealing given the inherent structural properties of the task.
2. **Strong Performance in Low-Resource Scenarios:** The model’s ability to outperform previous state-of-the-art methods, including latent variable models, on the CELEX dataset – a notoriously challenging low-resource dataset – is a significant strength. This demonstrates the efficacy of the hard attention mechanism in scenarios where data is limited.
3. **Comprehensive Experimental Evaluation:** The authors conduct a thorough evaluation across three distinct datasets (CELEX, Wiktionary, and SIGMORPHON2016), assessing performance in various settings and comparing against a diverse set of baselines. This broad evaluation strengthens the validity of their findings.
4. **Insightful Analysis of Learned Representations:** The comparison of learned representations between the hard and soft attention models provides valuable insights into the behavior of each approach. The observation that the hard attention model encodes less positional information, relying instead on the explicit step actions, is a particularly noteworthy finding.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Mixed Performance Across Datasets:** While the model achieves strong results on CELEX, its performance on other datasets, particularly SIGMORPHON2016, is more nuanced. It performs well on languages with suffixing morphology and stem changes but lags behind the soft attention baseline on languages with more complex morphological phenomena (e.g., vowel harmony). This suggests a limitation in the model’s ability to handle long-range dependencies.
2. **Dependency on Pre-trained Alignments:** The reliance on separately learned character-level alignments introduces an external dependency. While the authors justify this choice, the performance is inherently tied to the quality of these pre-trained alignments. Further investigation into the robustness of the model to variations in alignment quality would be beneficial.
3. **Limited Discussion of Practical Considerations:** The paper lacks a detailed discussion of the practical implications of using a hard attention mechanism, such as implementation complexity or computational cost compared to soft attention. A more thorough analysis of these aspects would enhance the paper’s overall impact.
4. **Incremental Improvement:** While the results are positive, the gains over strong baselines on some datasets (e.g., Wiktionary) are relatively small. This raises questions about the magnitude of the improvement offered by the hard attention mechanism in scenarios with sufficient training data.

**Questions to Authors:**

1. How sensitive is the model’s performance to the quality of the pre-trained character-level alignments? Could you provide an ablation study evaluating performance with different alignment models or varying levels of alignment noise?
2. Could you provide a more detailed comparison of the computational cost and implementation complexity of the hard attention model compared to a standard soft attention model?
3. The analysis of learned representations suggests that the hard attention model encodes less positional information. How does this impact the model’s ability to handle morphological processes that require long-range dependencies?
4. Could you elaborate on the limitations of the hard attention mechanism in handling languages with complex morphological phenomena such as vowel or consonant harmony? Are there potential modifications to the model that could address these limitations?

**Additional Comments:**

- The paper is well-written and clearly presents the proposed model and experimental results.
- The related work section is comprehensive and provides a good overview of the relevant literature.
