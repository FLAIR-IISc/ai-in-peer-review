This paper introduces a novel neural model for morphological inflection generation based on “hard monotonic attention,” leveraging character alignments determined through a Bayesian method originally developed for transliteration. This approach represents a significant departure from prior work which typically employs “soft attention” and jointly models character alignment and conversion within a probabilistic framework. The core idea is both innovative and well-motivated.

The paper is clearly written and presents a comprehensive experimental evaluation. The strengths of the proposed model lie in its originality and its effectiveness in specific scenarios. Results demonstrate particular benefit when training data is limited (e.g., the CELEX dataset) and when the inflectional patterns exhibit strong monotonicity and limited contextual sensitivity (as observed in Russian, German, and Spanish). 

However, the experimental results are somewhat mixed overall. While performing well in the aforementioned conditions, the method does not consistently outperform existing approaches across all tested datasets. To strengthen the paper, the authors should more thoroughly discuss the practical advantages of their method. Specifically, detailing the ease of implementation compared to joint alignment models, or outlining any computational benefits (e.g., training speed, memory usage), would be highly valuable. A more nuanced discussion of the scenarios where hard monotonic attention *specifically* excels, and why, would further enhance the contribution. 

In summary, this is a solid paper presenting a novel approach to morphological inflection. While not universally superior, the identified conditions under which it thrives are insightful, and clarifying its practical benefits would significantly increase the paper’s impact.
