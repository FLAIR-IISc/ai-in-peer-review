- Paper proposes a novel encoder–decoder with hard monotonic attention, evaluated on three morphology datasets, arguing monotonicity is appropriate for many seq2seq tasks.  
- Strength: motivation is sound and explicit enforcement of monotonicity is a valuable contribution given neural models’ flexibility.  
- Methodology: approach decouples alignment and transduction by first establishing monotonic alignments, then generating outputs consistent with them.  
- Ambiguity: initial description of alignments suggests both 1-to-many and many-to-1, creating confusion about the actual alignment type used.  
- After author response, alignment process clarified (start with 1-0, 0-1, 1-1 then many-to-many), but core concerns remain.  
- Missing baseline: reviewer requests comparison to a straightforward 1-to-many monotonic alignment (e.g., Jiampojamarn & Kondrak / Schnober et al. 2016) followed by a neural sequence tagger (BiLSTM), which could be a strong, simpler baseline.  
- Question of novelty: need explanation why the proposed pipeline (general model then hard constraints) is preferable to directly training a monotone tagger baseline.  
- Evaluation scope limited to morphology; broader monotonic seq2seq tasks should be included to support generalizability claims.  
- Results phrasing concerns: “on par or better” masks potential poorer performance and should be phrased more accurately.  
- Missing details on linguistic features: source and influence of POS tags (inferred from Fig.1) are not described; gains might be due to these features rather than monotonicity constraints.  
- Minor issues: formatting/typos (Eq.3 notation, lowercase “where” at line 231, use commas not ellipses at line 237, Cyrillic font in Fig.1, typo at line 437).  
- AC-related concerns: cite/relate to Cohn et al. (2016) and Stack LSTMs design choices; justify architectural decisions in that context.  
- Alignment method concern: potential drawbacks of using greedy alignment versus sum-over-all-alignments (Rastogi et al. 2016) should be addressed.
