The authors propose a novel encoder-decoder neural network architecture with "hard monotonic attention". They evaluate it on three morphology datasets.
This paper is a tough one. On the one hand it is well-written, mostly very clear and also presents a novel idea, namely including monotonicity in morphology tasks. 
The reason for including such monotonicity is pretty obvious: Unlike machine translation, many seq2seq tasks are monotone, and therefore general encoder-decoder models should not be used in the first place. That they still perform reasonably well should be considered a strong argument for neural techniques, in general. The idea of this paper is now to explicitly enforce a monotonic output character generation. They do this by decoupling alignment and transduction and first aligning input-output sequences monotonically and then training to generate outputs in agreement with the monotone alignments.
However, the authors are unclear on this point. I have a few questions:
1) How do your alignments look like? On the one hand, the alignments seem to be of the kind 1-to-many (as in the running example, Fig.1), that is, 1 input character can be aligned with zero, 1, or several output characters. However, this seems to contrast with the description given in lines 311-312 where the authors speak of several input characters aligned to 1 output character. That is, do you use 1-to-many, many-to-1 or many-to-many alignments?
2) Actually, there is a quite simple approach to monotone Seq2Seq. In a first stage, align input and output characters monotonically with a 1-to-many constraint (one can use any monotone aligner, such as the toolkit of Jiampojamarn and Kondrak). Then one trains a standard sequence tagger(!) to predict exactly these 1-to-many alignments. For example, flog->fliege (your example on l.613): First align as in "f-l-o-g / f-l-ie-ge". Now use any tagger (could use an LSTM, if you like) to predict "f-l-ie-ge" (sequence of length 4) from "f-l-o-g" (sequence of length 4). Such an approach may have been suggested in multiple papers, one reference could be [Schnober et al., 2016, Section 4.2]. 
My two questions here are: 
2a) How does your approach differ from this rather simple idea?
2b) Why did you not include it as a baseline?
Further issues:
3) It's really a pity that you only tested on morphology, because there are many other interesting monotonic seq2seq tasks, and you could have shown your system's superiority by evaluating on these, given that you explicitly model monotonicity (cf. also [Schnober et al., 2016]).
4) You perform "on par or better" (l.791). There seems to be a general cognitive bias among NLP researchers to map instances where they perform worse to "on par" and all the rest to "better". I think this wording should be corrected, but otherwise I'm fine with the experimental results.
5) You say little about your linguistic features: From Fig. 1, I infer that they include POS, etc. 
5a) Where did you take these features from?
5b) Is it possible that these are responsible for your better performance in some cases, rather than the monotonicity constraints?
Minor points:
6) Equation (3): please re-write $NN$ as $\text{NN}$ or similar
7) l.231 "Where" should be lower case
8) l.237 and many more: $x_1\ldots x_n$. As far as I know, the math community recommends to write $x_1,\ldots,x_n$ but $x_1\cdots x_n$. That is, dots should be on the same level as surrounding symbols.
9) Figure 1: is it really necessary to use cyrillic font? I can't even address your example here, because I don't have your fonts.
10) l.437: should be "these"

The authors propose a novel encoder-decoder neural network architecture with "hard monotonic attention". They evaluate it on three morphology datasets.
This paper presents a well-written and clear exploration of a novel idea: explicitly incorporating monotonicity into morphological tasks. The motivation is sound – many sequence-to-sequence tasks exhibit monotonicity, and standard encoder-decoder models do not inherently leverage this property. The proposed approach of decoupling alignment and transduction and training with monotone alignments is a logical extension. 
However, the paper raises some questions. First, regarding the alignment scheme: The alignments appear to be of the 1-to-many variety (as in the example in Fig. 1), where a single input character can align to zero, one, or multiple output characters. This seems to contrast with the description in lines 311-312, which suggests multiple input characters aligning to a single output character. Clarification is needed: does the model utilize 1-to-many, many-to-1, or many-to-many alignments?
Second, a seemingly straightforward baseline approach is not addressed. One could align input and output characters monotonically with a 1-to-many constraint (using a tool like Jiampojamarn and Kondrak's aligner) and then train a standard sequence tagger (e.g., a BiLSTM) to predict the output sequence from the aligned input.  For example, aligning “flog” to “fliege” as “f-l-o-g / f-l-ie-ge” and training a tagger to predict "f-l-ie-ge". This approach, suggested in Schnober et al. (2016, Section 4.2), seems relevant.
Specifically: 
2a) How does the proposed approach differ from this simpler tagging-based approach?
2b) Why was this tagging baseline not included in the experimental evaluation?
Further concerns:
3) The evaluation is limited to morphology. Considering the explicit modeling of monotonicity, it would be valuable to demonstrate the approach's effectiveness on other monotonic sequence-to-sequence tasks, as discussed in Schnober et al. (2016).
4) The phrasing “on par or better” (l.791) appears overly optimistic in some instances. A more nuanced description of the results would be beneficial.
5) The paper provides limited detail on the linguistic features used. From Fig. 1, it’s inferred that features like POS tags are included.
5a) Where were these features obtained?
5b) Is it possible that these features contribute significantly to the observed performance gains, rather than the monotonicity constraints themselves?
Minor points:
6) Equation (3): Please rewrite $NN$ as $\text{NN}$ or similar.
7) l.231: "Where" should be lowercase.
8) l.237 and elsewhere: Use $x_1,\ldots,x_n$ instead of $x_1\ldots x_n$ for mathematical notation.
9) Figure 1: The use of Cyrillic font hinders accessibility for readers unfamiliar with the script.
10) l.437: Should be “these”.
