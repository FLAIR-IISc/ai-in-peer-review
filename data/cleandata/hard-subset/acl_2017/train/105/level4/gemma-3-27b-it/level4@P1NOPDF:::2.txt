Strengths: A novel encoder-decoder model is proposed that explicitly incorporates monotonicity.

Weaknesses: The model may be essentially a BiRNN with alignments de-coupled. It is only evaluated on morphology tasks, with no assessment on other monotone sequence-to-sequence tasks.

General Discussion:
The authors present a new encoder-decoder neural network architecture featuring “hard monotonic attention.” They evaluate it on three morphology datasets. This paper presents a challenging evaluation. On one hand, it is well-written, largely clear, and introduces a novel idea: explicitly modeling monotonicity in morphology tasks. 

The rationale for incorporating monotonicity is evident: unlike machine translation, many sequence-to-sequence tasks are inherently monotone, suggesting that general encoder-decoder models may not be the most appropriate starting point. The fact that such models perform reasonably well already serves as a strong endorsement of neural techniques in general. This paper aims to explicitly enforce monotonic output character generation by decoupling alignment and transduction, first aligning input-output sequences monotonically and then training the model to generate outputs consistent with these alignments.

However, the authors’ explanation lacks clarity in certain areas. I have several questions:

1) What is the nature of your alignments? The running example (Fig.1) suggests a 1-to-many alignment scheme, where one input character can align with zero, one, or multiple output characters. This seems to contrast with the description in lines 311-312, where the authors discuss multiple input characters aligning to one output character. Do you employ 1-to-many, many-to-1, or many-to-many alignments?

2) A relatively straightforward approach to monotone Seq2Seq involves first monotonically aligning input and output characters with a 1-to-many constraint (using any monotone aligner, such as the toolkit by Jiampojamarn and Kondrak). Subsequently, a standard sequence tagger (e.g., an LSTM) could be trained to predict these 1-to-many alignments. For example, for "flog->fliege" (your example on l.613), first align as "f-l-o-g / f-l-ie-ge." Then, use any tagger to predict “f-l-ie-ge” from “f-l-o-g.” This approach has been suggested in previous work, such as [Schnober et al., Section 4.2]. 

My questions regarding this are:

2a) How does your approach differ from this simpler idea?
2b) Why was this approach not included as a baseline?

Further issues:

3) It is regrettable that testing was limited to morphology, as numerous other monotonic sequence-to-sequence tasks exist. Demonstrating the superiority of your system on these tasks, given the explicit modeling of monotonicity, would strengthen your claims (cf. also [*]).

4) The phrasing "on par or better" (l.791) seems subject to a cognitive bias common among NLP researchers—characterizing weaker performance as “on par” and stronger performance as “better.” This wording should be revised, although I am otherwise satisfied with the experimental results.

5) The paper provides limited detail regarding linguistic features. Figure 1 suggests the inclusion of POS tags, among others.
5a) What is the source of these features?
5b) Is it possible that these features, rather than the monotonicity constraints, contribute to the observed performance gains?

Minor points:

6) Equation (3): Please rewrite $NN$ as $\text{NN}$ or similar.
7) l.231: “Where” should be lowercase.
8) l.237 and elsewhere: Use $x_1, \ldots, x_n$ instead of $x1\ldots xn$. The mathematical community generally recommends this notation.
9) Figure 1: Is the use of a Cyrillic font truly necessary? I am unable to properly interpret your example due to font compatibility issues.
10) l.437: Should be "these."
