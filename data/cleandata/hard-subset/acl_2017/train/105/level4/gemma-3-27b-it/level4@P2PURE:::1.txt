The proposed hard monotonic attention mechanism presents a novel and significant departure from existing approaches.

The experimental results on morphological inflection generation are somewhat mixed. The model demonstrates effectiveness when training data is limited (e.g., CELEX) and when alignment is predominantly monotonic with minimal contextual dependencies (e.g., Russian, German, and Spanish). 

This paper introduces a novel neural model for morphological inflection generation, leveraging “hard attention” and character alignments derived independently via a Bayesian method for transliteration. This contrasts with prior state-of-the-art neural models employing “soft attention,” which jointly model character alignment and conversion within a probabilistic framework. The core idea is both novel and well-justified. The paper is clearly written, and the experiments are comprehensive. However, the method does not consistently achieve state-of-the-art performance across all conditions; it appears particularly well-suited for tasks with largely monotonic alignment and limited context sensitivity. The paper would benefit from a discussion of the practical advantages of this approach, such as ease of implementation or computational efficiency.
