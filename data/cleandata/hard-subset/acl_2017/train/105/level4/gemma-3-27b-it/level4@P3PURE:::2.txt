This paper proposes a novel encoder-decoder neural network architecture with "hard monotonic attention" and evaluates it on three morphology datasets. This is a tough paper. On the one hand, it is well-written, mostly very clear, and presents a novel idea – explicitly including monotonicity in morphology tasks. The reason for including such monotonicity is obvious: unlike machine translation, many seq2seq tasks are monotone, and therefore general encoder-decoder models may not be the most appropriate choice. The fact that they still perform reasonably well speaks to the power of neural techniques in general. The idea here is to explicitly enforce a monotonic output character generation by decoupling alignment and transduction, first aligning input-output sequences monotonically, and then training to generate outputs in agreement with those alignments. However, the authors are unclear on several points.

First, how do the alignments look? The alignments seem to be of the kind 1-to-many (as in the running example, Fig.1), where one input character can be aligned with zero, one, or several output characters. However, this seems to contrast with the description in lines 311-312, where the authors discuss several input characters aligned to one output character. Do the authors use 1-to-many, many-to-1, or many-to-many alignments?

Second, there is a simple approach to monotone Seq2Seq. One could first align input and output characters monotonically with a 1-to-many constraint (using any monotone aligner, such as the toolkit of Jiampojamarn and Kondrak). Then, one trains a standard sequence tagger to predict exactly these 1-to-many alignments. For example, for “flog->fliege” (the example on l.613), one first aligns as “f-l-o-g / f-l-ie-ge”. Then any tagger (even an LSTM) can predict “f-l-ie-ge” (sequence of length 4) from “f-l-o-g” (sequence of length 4). This approach has been suggested in multiple papers, such as [Schnober et al., 2016, Section 4.2]. My questions are: 2a) How does this approach differ from this rather simple idea? 2b) Why was this not included as a baseline?

Further issues:

3) It is a pity that the testing was limited to morphology, as many other interesting monotonic seq2seq tasks exist. Showing superiority on these tasks, given the explicit modeling of monotonicity, would have strengthened the paper (cf. also [*]).

4) The claim of performing “on par or better” (l.791) seems optimistic. There appears to be a cognitive bias among NLP researchers to characterize worse performance as “on par” and better performance as “better”. This wording should be corrected. Otherwise, the experimental results are acceptable.

5) Little is said about the linguistic features used. From Fig. 1, I infer that features like POS tags are included. 5a) Where were these features obtained? 5b) Is it possible that these features, rather than the monotonicity constraints, are responsible for the improved performance in some cases?

Minor points:

6) Equation (3): please re-write $NN$ as $\text{NN}$ or similar.

7) l.231 "Where" should be lower case.

8) l.237 and elsewhere: $x_1\ldots x_n$. The math community generally recommends $x_1,\ldots,x_n$ or $x_1\cdots x_n$. Dots should be on the same level as surrounding symbols.

9) Figure 1: Is it really necessary to use a Cyrillic font? I cannot address the example due to the lack of appropriate fonts.

10) l.437: should be "these".
