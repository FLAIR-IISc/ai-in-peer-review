## Review

**Strengths:** The paper introduces a novel encoder-decoder model that explicitly incorporates monotonicity constraints.

**Weaknesses:** The proposed model may be functionally similar to a standard BiRNN with decoupled alignments. Evaluation is limited to morphological tasks, lacking broader assessment on other monotone sequence-to-sequence problems.

**General Discussion:**

The authors present a new encoder-decoder neural network architecture leveraging “hard monotonic attention,” evaluated on three morphology datasets. This paper presents a compelling, well-written, and largely clear contribution, specifically addressing the inclusion of monotonicity in morphology tasks – a natural consideration given the inherent monotonicity of many sequence-to-sequence problems, in contrast to tasks like machine translation. The reasonable performance of general encoder-decoder models on monotone tasks already underscores the power of neural techniques, and this work aims to explicitly enforce monotonic output generation by decoupling alignment and transduction, first establishing monotone alignments and then training output generation to adhere to them.

However, certain aspects require clarification. First, the nature of the alignments is unclear. While the running example (Fig. 1) suggests 1-to-many alignments – one input character aligning with zero, one, or multiple output characters – this appears to contradict the description in lines 311-312, which mentions multiple input characters aligning to a single output character.  Clarification is needed: does the model utilize 1-to-many, many-to-1, or many-to-many alignments?

Second, a straightforward approach to monotone Seq2Seq involves initially aligning input and output characters monotonically with a 1-to-many constraint (using a monotone aligner like that of Jiampojamarn and Kondrak), followed by training a standard sequence tagger to predict these alignments. For instance, aligning “flog” to “fliege” as “f-l-o-g / f-l-ie-ge” and then training a tagger (e.g., an LSTM) to predict “f-l-ie-ge” from “f-l-o-g.” This approach, possibly described in prior work ([*, Section 4.2]), prompts the following questions: 

2a) How does the proposed approach fundamentally differ from this simpler methodology?
2b) Why wasn’t this baseline included in the evaluation?

Further considerations:

3) The exclusive focus on morphology limits the demonstration of the model's benefits. Evaluation on other monotonic sequence-to-sequence tasks would strengthen the claim of superiority given the explicit modeling of monotonicity (cf. [*]).
4) The phrasing “on par or better” (l.791) seems prone to cognitive bias, potentially representing weaker performance as “on par.” Revision of this wording is recommended, though the experimental results are otherwise acceptable.
5) The linguistic features utilized are not fully detailed. Fig. 1 suggests the inclusion of POS tags, etc.
    5a) What is the source of these features?
    5b) Is it possible that these features, rather than the monotonicity constraints, contribute to the observed performance gains?

**Minor points:**

6) Equation (3): Please revise $NN$ to $\text{NN}$ or similar.
7) l.231: “Where” should be lowercase.
8) l.237 and elsewhere: Use $x_1, \ldots, x_n$ instead of $x1\ldots xn$ or $x1\cdots xn$.
9) Figure 1: The use of Cyrillic font hinders accessibility and prevents proper examination of the example.
10) l.437: Should be “these.”

**AFTER AUTHOR RESPONSE**

The response clarifies the alignment process, suggesting an initial alignment of 1-0, 0-1, and 1-1 relationships, followed by many-to-many alignments. However, the core question remains: why wasn’t a baseline utilizing 1-x (x ∈ {0, 1, 2}) alignments, as proposed by Schnober et al., followed by training a neural tagger (e.g., a BiLSTM) included in the evaluation?  This approach offers a simple and robust monotone model, and a comparison would be valuable. The current method begins with a more general model and then imposes hard monotonicity constraints, while the proposed baseline constructs a monotone model from the outset. 

**NOTES FROM AC**

Also relevant are Cohn et al. (2016), http://www.aclweb.org/anthology/N16-1102. Additionally, the architecture's relationship to methods like the Stack LSTM, which predict a sequence of actions modifying an input, should be considered. Finally, the potential drawbacks of employing a greedy alignment, in contrast to Rastogi et al. (2016)'s approach of summing over all alignments, warrant discussion.
