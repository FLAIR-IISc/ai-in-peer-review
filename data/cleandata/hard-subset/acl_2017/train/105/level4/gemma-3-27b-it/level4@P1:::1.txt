This paper presents a novel neural model for morphological inflection generation employing a “hard attention” mechanism, coupled with character alignments obtained separately using a Bayesian method for transliteration. This approach is substantially different from previous state-of-the-art neural models that utilize “soft attention,” jointly learning character alignment and conversion within a probabilistic framework.

The core idea is novel and well-motivated. The paper is clearly written and the experimental evaluation is comprehensive. However, the performance of the proposed method appears somewhat conditional. It demonstrates effectiveness when training data is limited (e.g., the CELEX dataset) and for languages exhibiting largely monotonic alignments with less context-sensitive phenomena (e.g., Russian, German, and Spanish).

**Contributions:** The primary contribution of this work is the introduction of a hard attention mechanism for morphological inflection generation, effectively leveraging the nearly-monotonic alignment commonly found in this task. A secondary contribution is the thorough evaluation of this model on three established datasets, establishing competitive performance and highlighting its strengths in low-resource settings and for specific language types. Finally, the analysis comparing the hard and soft attention models provides valuable insight into the learned representations and behaviors of these approaches.

**Strengths:**

1. **Novelty of the Approach:** The hard attention mechanism represents a distinct departure from existing sequence-to-sequence models for morphological inflection. By explicitly modeling and enforcing alignment, the model appears to be more robust in low-data scenarios and better suited for languages with clear monotonic relationships between input and output forms.
2. **Strong Performance in Low-Resource Settings:** The model demonstrably outperforms existing approaches on the CELEX dataset, a particularly challenging low-resource benchmark. This is a significant result, indicating the method’s ability to learn effectively from limited data.
3. **Comprehensive Evaluation:** The experiments conducted across three diverse datasets (CELEX, Wiktionary, and SIGMORPHON2016) provide a thorough assessment of the model’s capabilities and limitations. This broad evaluation strengthens the validity of the findings.
4. **Insightful Analysis:** The comparison between the hard and soft attention models is particularly valuable. The analysis of learned representations and alignments offers a deeper understanding of how these different approaches process morphological information.
5. **Clear Presentation:** The paper is well-written and logically structured, making it easy to follow the authors' reasoning and understand the technical details of the model.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Conditional Performance:** While the model performs well in specific scenarios (low-resource data, monotonic alignments), its performance is not uniformly superior across all datasets and languages. The results on SIGMORPHON2016, particularly for languages with more complex morphological phenomena, suggest that the method may struggle with long-range dependencies and non-monotonic transformations.
2. **Reliance on Pre-trained Alignments:** The model relies on externally derived character-level alignments for training. While this simplifies the training process, it introduces a dependency on the accuracy and quality of these alignments. The impact of alignment errors on the model’s performance is not fully explored.
3. **Lack of Practical Considerations:** The paper does not discuss the practical implications of the proposed method, such as implementation complexity or computational cost, relative to other approaches. Providing this information would enhance the paper’s impact and make it more appealing to practitioners.
4. **Limited Discussion of Error Analysis:** A more detailed error analysis, identifying specific types of inflection errors made by the model, would provide further insights into its limitations and potential areas for improvement.

**Questions to Authors:**

1. How sensitive is the model's performance to the quality of the pre-trained character-level alignments? Could the use of noisy or inaccurate alignments significantly degrade performance?
2. Could you elaborate on the computational cost and implementation complexity of the hard attention mechanism compared to soft attention?
3. For the languages on SIGMORPHON2016 where the model underperforms, can you provide a more detailed analysis of the types of morphological phenomena that pose the greatest challenges?
4. Are there any plans to explore end-to-end training of the alignment and transduction components, potentially mitigating the reliance on pre-trained alignments?

**Additional Comments:**

- In the introduction, consider briefly mentioning the potential benefits of a hard attention mechanism (e.g., improved interpretability, faster training).
- The phrase "nearly-monotonic alignment" appears frequently. While accurate, consider varying the wording for stylistic reasons.
- The reference to the link in the experiments section should be removed for anonymity.
