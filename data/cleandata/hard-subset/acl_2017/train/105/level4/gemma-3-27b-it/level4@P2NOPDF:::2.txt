This paper introduces a novel encoder-decoder architecture with “hard monotonic attention” for sequence-to-sequence tasks, evaluated on three morphology datasets. The motivation – that many seq2seq tasks are inherently monotone and shouldn’t rely on general, non-monotonic models – is sound and well-articulated. Neural models’ reasonable performance despite this suggests their inherent flexibility, making the explicit enforcement of monotonicity a valuable contribution. The proposed approach decouples alignment and transduction, first establishing monotonic alignments and then generating outputs consistent with those alignments.

However, several points require clarification. First, the nature of the alignments is unclear. The description suggests both 1-to-many and many-to-1 alignments, creating a potential contradiction. Specifically, what type of alignment (1-to-many, many-to-1, or many-to-many) is ultimately used?

Second, a seemingly straightforward baseline approach has not been considered. Aligning input and output characters monotonically with a 1-to-many constraint (using a tool like Jiampojamarn & Kondrak’s) followed by training a sequence tagger to predict the output sequence from the aligned input could provide a strong baseline. How does the proposed approach differ from this method, and why was it not included in the experiments? [Schnober et al., COLING 2016] suggests a similar approach.

Further concerns include the limited scope of evaluation. While morphology is a valid test case, the absence of evaluation on other monotonic seq2seq tasks limits the generalizability claims, given the explicit modeling of monotonicity. The experimental results are presented as “on par or better,” but this phrasing appears to exhibit a common cognitive bias in NLP research – framing worse performance as “on par.” This should be revised for accuracy. 

The paper lacks detail regarding linguistic features. The use of POS tags (inferred from Fig. 1) is mentioned, but details regarding their source and potential influence on the results are missing. Specifically, could the observed performance gains be attributable to these features rather than the monotonicity constraints?

Minor issues include: Equation (3) should be formatted with $\text{NN}$ or similar notation; line 231, “Where” should be lowercase; line 237, use commas instead of ellipses for listing elements ($x_1, \ldots, x_n$); Figure 1’s use of Cyrillic font hinders accessibility; and line 437 should read “these.”

**After Author Response:** The clarification regarding the alignment process (initial 1-0, 0-1, 1-1, followed by many-to-many) is helpful. However, the core question remains: why not explore the simpler baseline of directly aligning with 1-x (x in 0,1,2) constraints as suggested by Schnober et al. (2016) and subsequently training a neural tagger (e.g., BiLSTM)? This would offer a clear comparison, particularly given that a tagger inherently enforces monotonicity. The proposed approach starts with a more general model and *then* imposes hard monotonicity constraints, while the baseline would directly build a monotone model.

Finally, the potential drawbacks of using a greedy alignment, compared to the sum-over-all-alignments approach in Rastogi et al. (2016), should be considered.
