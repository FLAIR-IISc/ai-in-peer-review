Strengths: The paper introduces a novel encoder-decoder model explicitly designed to incorporate monotonicity constraints.

Weaknesses: The proposed model may be conceptually similar to a standard BiRNN with decoupled alignments. Evaluation is limited to morphology tasks, neglecting other suitable monotone sequence-to-sequence scenarios.

General Discussion:
The authors present a new encoder-decoder neural network architecture featuring "hard monotonic attention," evaluated on three morphology datasets. This work addresses an important point: many sequence-to-sequence tasks exhibit inherent monotonicity, suggesting that general encoder-decoder models are not always the optimal choice. The observed performance of standard models in such cases already demonstrates the potential of neural techniques, and this paper aims to explicitly enforce monotonic output generation through decoupling alignment and transduction, followed by training with monotone alignments. However, clarity regarding the alignment process is lacking. Several questions arise:

1) What is the nature of the alignments? The running example (Fig.1) suggests 1-to-many alignments (one input character mapping to zero, one, or multiple output characters). This appears to conflict with the description in lines 311-312, which indicates multiple input characters aligning to a single output character. Are the alignments 1-to-many, many-to-1, or many-to-many?

2) A relatively straightforward approach to monotone Seq2Seq involves first aligning input and output characters monotonically with a 1-to-many constraint (using any suitable monotone aligner, like the toolkit of Jiampojamarn and Kondrak). Subsequently, a standard sequence tagger could be trained to predict these 1-to-many alignments. For example, for flog->fliege (l.613), alignment could be “f-l-o-g / f-l-ie-ge”, followed by training a tagger (potentially an LSTM) to predict “f-l-ie-ge” from “f-l-o-g”. This approach has been suggested in prior work, such as [*, Section 4.2]. 
My questions are: 
2a) How does the proposed approach differ from this simpler alternative?
2b) Why was this baseline not included in the evaluation?

Further issues:
3) The exclusive focus on morphology is a limitation, as numerous other monotone seq2seq tasks exist. Demonstrating superiority on these tasks would strengthen the argument for explicitly modeling monotonicity (cf. [*]).
4) The phrasing “on par or better” (l.791) appears biased. A more accurate representation of the experimental results is recommended.
5) The description of linguistic features is sparse. Fig. 1 suggests the inclusion of POS tags, etc.
5a) What is the source of these features?
5b) Could these features, rather than the monotonicity constraints, be responsible for performance improvements in some cases?
Minor points:
6) Equation (3): please rewrite $NN$ as $\text{NN}$ or similar.
7) l.231 "Where" should be lowercase.
8) l.237 and elsewhere: $x_1\ldots x_n$. The mathematical convention recommends $x_1,\ldots,x_n$ or $x_1\cdots x_n$.
9) Figure 1: The use of a Cyrillic font hinders accessibility and prevents detailed examination of the example.
10) l.437: should be "these".

AFTER AUTHOR RESPONSE
The author's clarification regarding the alignment process suggests a sequence of 1-0, 0-1, and 1-1 alignments, followed by many-to-many alignments. My primary concern remains: why was a baseline utilizing 1-x (x in 0,1,2) alignments as in Schnober et al. followed by a neural tagger (e.g., BiLSTM) not implemented? Such a baseline is conceptually simple and leverages the inherent monotonicity, potentially offering a strong comparison point. (A tagger naturally enforces monotonicity given the pre-aligned data, while this work introduces monotonicity constraints to a more general model…)
