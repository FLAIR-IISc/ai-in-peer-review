## Paraphrased Review: Morphological Inflection Generation with Hard Monotonic Attention

This paper presents a new encoder-decoder model for morphological inflection generation, incorporating a “hard monotonic attention” mechanism. The model is evaluated on three morphological datasets. 

**Summary of Contributions (as I see them):**

1.  **A novel architecture for monotonic sequence transduction:** The primary contribution is the design of an encoder-decoder model with a hard attention mechanism explicitly designed to enforce a monotonic alignment between input and output sequences. This is a valuable approach, given the inherent monotonicity often present in morphological inflection.
2.  **Strong empirical performance on morphology tasks:** The model achieves state-of-the-art or competitive results on three standard morphological inflection datasets, demonstrating its effectiveness for this task.
3.  **Analysis of learned representations:** The authors provide an analysis comparing their hard attention model with a soft attention model, offering insights into the features learned by both approaches and how they relate to morphological inflection.

**Strengths:**

1.  **Addresses a relevant problem with a logical approach:** The authors correctly identify that many sequence-to-sequence tasks, including morphological inflection, exhibit strong monotonicity. The proposed model directly addresses this characteristic, which is a well-motivated design choice.
2.  **Competitive empirical results:** The model consistently achieves strong performance on the evaluated datasets, outperforming several baselines, particularly in low-resource settings. The improvement over the Dreyer et al. (2008) latent variable model on the CELEX dataset is particularly noteworthy.
3.  **Clear and generally well-written presentation:** The paper is generally well-structured and clearly explains the proposed model and experimental setup. The inclusion of Figure 1 is helpful for understanding the model's operation.
4.  **Insightful analysis:** The comparative analysis of the hard and soft attention models, particularly the discussion of learned representations, adds value beyond simply demonstrating performance gains.

**Weaknesses:**

1.  **Limited novelty of the core idea:** While the specific implementation is novel, the core idea of leveraging monotonicity in sequence transduction is not entirely new. The paper could better position itself within the broader context of related work, such as the neural transducer models and approaches using pre-aligned sequences.
2.  **Lack of evaluation on diverse monotonic tasks:** The evaluation is limited to morphological inflection. The paper would be strengthened by demonstrating the model's effectiveness on other tasks exhibiting monotonic relationships, thus highlighting the generality of the approach.
3.  **Ambiguity regarding alignment types:** The description of the alignment process is somewhat unclear. The paper oscillates between describing 1-to-many and many-to-1 alignments, and it is unclear which type is ultimately used.
4.  **Missing baseline:** A simple, yet strong baseline – aligning input and output characters monotonically (e.g., using a toolkit like Jiampojamarn and Kondrak) and then training a sequence tagger to predict the output – is not included. This baseline would provide a more direct comparison of the benefits of the proposed hard attention mechanism. 
5. **Overly optimistic performance claims**: The language used to describe the performance results (e.g., "on par or better") is potentially biased. A more nuanced and objective reporting of results would improve the paper's credibility.

**Questions to Authors:**

1.  **Alignment Details:** Could you clarify the types of alignments used in your approach (1-to-many, many-to-1, or many-to-many)? Please provide examples to illustrate the alignment process.
2.  **Comparison to Tagging Baseline:** How does your approach differ from first aligning characters monotonically and then training a sequence tagger (e.g., a BiLSTM) to predict the output sequence? Why was this baseline not included in your experiments?
3.  **Feature Source:** Where did the linguistic features (POS tags, etc.) used in your model come from? Could these features be the primary driver of performance gains in some cases, rather than the monotonicity constraints?
4. **Alignment Greediness:** Is there a downside to the greedy alignment used versus approaches like Rastogi et al. (2016) which sum over all possible alignments?
