- Proposal: novel encoder–decoder with hard monotonic attention, evaluated on three morphology datasets.  
- Paper is well-written, clear, and introduces monotonicity explicitly for morphology tasks.  
- Motivation: many seq2seq tasks are monotone, so enforcing monotonicity is appropriate and informative.  
- Method: decouple alignment and transduction—first compute monotone alignments, then train generation to follow them.  
- Ambiguity: unclear whether alignments are 1-to-many, many-to-1, or many-to-many (contradictory descriptions).  
- Suggested simple baseline: 1-to-many monotone alignment followed by a sequence tagger (e.g., LSTM) to predict aligned outputs—reference Schnober et al. (2016).  
- Question: how does the proposed approach differ from that simple align-then-tag baseline, and why wasn’t it included as a baseline?  
- Limited scope: evaluation only on morphological inflection; should test other nearly-monotonic seq2seq tasks to demonstrate broader applicability.  
- Wording issue: phrase “on par or better” is biased and should be corrected for clarity.  
- Feature usage unclear: which linguistic features (e.g., POS) were used and where they came from.  
- Concern: possible that linguistic features, not monotonicity constraints, explain some performance gains—needs disentangling.  
- Strength: strong performance in low-resource setting (CELEX), outperforming latent-variable WFSTs.  
- Strength: novel hard-attention mechanism that models monotonic alignments and offers interpretability.  
- Strength: comprehensive experiments across CELEX, Wiktionary, and SIGMORPHON2016 showing competitive/state-of-the-art results.  
- Strength: analysis of learned representations provides insight into morphological encoding (useful visualizations).  
- Weakness: lack of comparison to the straightforward alignment+tagger baseline weakens claims about architectural benefit.  
- Weakness: ambiguous alignment description hampers understanding of model behavior.  
- Minor/editorial issues: Eq.(3) formatting (NN), capitalization "Where" (l.231), dot notation x1...xn formatting (l.237), Cyrillic font in Fig.1, typo at l.437 ("these").
