- Novel encoder-decoder with hard monotonic attention to enforce monotonic input-output alignment for morphological inflection.  
- Strong empirical performance, achieving competitive or state-of-the-art results on three morphological datasets, especially in low-resource settings.  
- Analysis comparing hard vs. soft attention that provides insight into learned representations and model behavior.  
- Well-motivated approach addressing monotonicity in sequence transduction with a clear, well-written presentation and helpful figures.  
- Notable empirical gains over prior models (e.g., Dreyer et al. 2008) on datasets like CELEX.  
- Limited novelty: monotonicity in sequence transduction is not a new idea and related work (e.g., neural transducers, pre-aligned methods) should be better situated.  
- Evaluation narrow: experiments are confined to morphological inflection and lack tests on other monotonic tasks to demonstrate generality.  
- Alignment description is ambiguous about whether alignments are 1-to-many, many-to-1, or many-to-many.  
- Missing a simple, strong baseline that aligns characters monotonically and trains a sequence tagger for direct comparison.  
- Some performance language is overly optimistic; results should be reported more objectively.  
- Question: Clarify the exact alignment types used and provide illustrative examples.  
- Question: Explain why a monotonic alignment + sequence tagging baseline was not included and how the proposed approach differs.  
- Question: Specify the source of linguistic features (e.g., POS tags) and whether they might drive performance gains.  
- Question: Discuss potential downsides of greedy alignment versus methods that sum over alignments (e.g., Rastogi et al. 2016).  
- Minor editorial/formatting suggestions: fix Equation (3) NN formatting, lowercase “Where” (line 231), use proper sequence notation (line 237), avoid Cyrillic font in Figure 1, and change “these” to “those” (line 437).
