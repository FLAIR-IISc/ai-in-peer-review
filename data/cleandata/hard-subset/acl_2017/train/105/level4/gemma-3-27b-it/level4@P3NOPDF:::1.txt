## Review for ACL 2017

This paper presents a novel neural model for morphological inflection generation, leveraging “hard attention” and character alignments obtained via a Bayesian method originally developed for transliteration. This approach represents a substantial departure from previous state-of-the-art models which typically employ “soft attention” and jointly model character alignment and conversion within a probabilistic framework. The core idea is both novel and theoretically sound. 

The key strength of this work lies in its innovative approach. The introduction of hard monotonic attention to morphological inflection is a significant contribution and offers a distinctly different perspective on the problem. The clear articulation of this method and its differentiation from existing approaches are commendable. The paper is well-written and easy to follow, which facilitates understanding of the proposed architecture and methodology. The experimental setup appears comprehensive, covering multiple languages and datasets.

However, the experimental results present a somewhat mixed picture. While the proposed model demonstrates effectiveness, particularly when training data is limited (as evidenced by the results on the CELEX dataset), its performance is more nuanced under other conditions. Specifically, the model performs best when the alignment between source and target forms is largely monotonic and less sensitive to broader contextual factors – a characteristic observed in languages like Russian, German, and Spanish.  Performance appears less compelling in scenarios demanding more complex, context-aware alignment.

This observation leads to my primary concern: the proposed method does not consistently achieve state-of-the-art results across all evaluated conditions. The paper would be significantly strengthened by a more thorough discussion of the limitations imposed by the assumption of monotonic alignment.  Acknowledging these limitations and clearly defining the types of morphological phenomena where this model excels would enhance the contribution. 

Furthermore, the paper would benefit from a discussion of the practical advantages offered by this approach beyond its performance characteristics.  Specifically, outlining the ease of implementation compared to existing methods, or quantifying the computational cost (training and inference time) would provide a more holistic assessment of its utility. A comparison of model complexity and training efficiency would be particularly valuable.

In summary, this is a well-presented and conceptually strong paper that introduces a novel approach to morphological inflection generation. While the experimental results are promising in specific scenarios, the limitations related to alignment complexity should be addressed. Including a discussion of practical considerations like implementation simplicity and computational efficiency would further solidify the contribution.  I recommend acceptance with minor revisions to address these points.
