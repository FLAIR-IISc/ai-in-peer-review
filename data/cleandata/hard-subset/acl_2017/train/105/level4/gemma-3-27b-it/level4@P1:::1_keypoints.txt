- Novel hard-attention neural model for morphological inflection using separately obtained Bayesian character alignments.
- Approach substantially differs from soft-attention models that jointly learn alignment and conversion.
- Core idea is novel, well-motivated, and the paper is clearly written with comprehensive experiments.
- Method is effective in low-resource settings (e.g., CELEX) and for languages with largely monotonic alignments (Russian, German, Spanish).
- Primary contribution: introducing hard attention that leverages nearly-monotonic alignments common in morphological inflection.
- Secondary contribution: thorough evaluation on three datasets showing competitive performance, especially in low-resource and monotonic cases.
- Analysis comparing hard and soft attention gives valuable insights into learned representations and behaviors.
- Strength: explicit alignment modeling improves robustness in low-data and monotonic scenarios.
- Strength: strong performance on the challenging CELEX low-resource benchmark.
- Strength: comprehensive evaluation across CELEX, Wiktionary, and SIGMORPHON2016 datasets.
- Strength: insightful analysis of learned representations and alignments.
- Strength: clear, well-structured presentation easing understanding of technical details.
- Weakness: performance is conditional and not uniformly superior, struggling on languages/datasets with complex or non-monotonic phenomena (SIGMORPHON2016).
- Weakness: reliance on externally derived character-level alignments; impact of alignment errors not fully explored.
- Weakness: lacks discussion of practical implications such as implementation complexity and computational cost.
- Weakness: limited detailed error analysis identifying specific inflection error types.
- Question: how sensitive is performance to the quality/noise of pre-trained character-level alignments?
- Question: what are the computational cost and implementation complexity of hard attention versus soft attention?
- Question: for underperforming SIGMORPHON2016 languages, which morphological phenomena cause the greatest challenges?
- Question: are there plans to explore end-to-end training of alignment and transduction to reduce reliance on pre-trained alignments?
- Additional comment: consider briefly mentioning potential benefits of hard attention (e.g., interpretability, faster training) in the introduction.
- Additional comment: vary wording of "nearly-monotonic alignment" for stylistic variety.
- Additional comment: remove experiment-link reference to preserve anonymity.
