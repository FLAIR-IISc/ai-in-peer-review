This paper introduces a novel neural model for morphological inflection generation, employing a “hard attention” mechanism and leveraging pre-computed character alignments. This approach distinguishes itself from prior work utilizing “soft attention,” which jointly learns alignment and transduction within a probabilistic model.

**Contributions:** The primary contribution is the introduction of a hard attention model specifically designed to exploit the near-monotonic alignment characteristics inherent in morphological inflection tasks. A secondary contribution lies in the comprehensive evaluation of this model across three established datasets, demonstrating state-of-the-art performance, particularly in low-resource scenarios and with languages exhibiting simpler morphological processes. Finally, the analysis comparing hard and soft attention mechanisms provides insights into the features each model learns and their respective strengths.

**Strengths:**

1. **Novelty of Approach:** The hard attention mechanism, coupled with independently learned alignments, represents a significant departure from existing sequence-to-sequence models for morphological inflection. This offers a fresh perspective on addressing the challenges of this task, and the justification for this approach—leveraging the expected monotonic alignment—is well-reasoned.
2. **Strong Performance in Low-Resource Settings:** The model demonstrably outperforms existing methods on the CELEX dataset, a crucial result given the limitations of available training data for many morphologically rich languages. This suggests the proposed method is more data-efficient than its competitors.
3. **Insightful Analysis:** The comparison between hard and soft attention models, and the subsequent analysis of learned representations, adds valuable understanding to the field. The observations regarding positional encoding and the differing behaviors of the two models are particularly insightful.
4. **Comprehensive Evaluation:** Evaluating the model on three diverse datasets (CELEX, Wiktionary, and SIGMORPHON2016) provides a robust assessment of its generalizability and identifies specific conditions where it excels.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Mixed Performance Across Datasets:** While strong on CELEX and for certain languages within SIGMORPHON2016, the model’s performance is not uniformly superior across all datasets and linguistic phenomena. The reliance on monotonic alignment appears to be a limiting factor in more complex morphological systems.
2. **Dependency on External Alignment:** The pre-computation of character alignments, while simplifying training, introduces an external dependency. The performance is thus potentially sensitive to the quality of this initial alignment step. A more thorough analysis of the impact of alignment accuracy on overall performance would be beneficial.
3. **Limited Discussion of Practical Considerations:** The paper lacks a detailed discussion of the practical implications of the proposed method, such as implementation complexity or computational cost relative to other approaches. Including such an analysis would strengthen the practical relevance of the work.
4. **Presentation of Results:** The extensive tables of results can be difficult to quickly interpret. Condensing or highlighting the most important comparisons could improve readability.



**Questions to Authors:**

1. How sensitive is the model's performance to the quality of the pre-computed character alignments? Could you provide an ablation study demonstrating the impact of different alignment qualities on the final results?
2. Could you elaborate on the computational cost and implementation complexity of the hard attention mechanism compared to soft attention, especially in terms of training and inference time?
3. Given the observed limitations with languages exhibiting complex morphological phenomena, are there potential extensions to the model that could address these challenges, such as incorporating long-range dependencies or more sophisticated attention mechanisms?
