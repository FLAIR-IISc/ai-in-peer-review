- Introduces a novel hard attention mechanism that enforces near-monotonic character alignments for morphological inflection.
- Leverages pre-computed character alignments to guide training and simplify learning.
- Shows strong performance, achieving state-of-the-art on several benchmarks and datasets.
- Demonstrates particular advantages in low-resource settings (notably CELEX).
- Provides a comparative analysis between hard and soft attention with insights into learned representations.
- Innovative and well-justified approach that fits the monotonic nature of many inflection tasks.
- Extensive experiments on three datasets underpin the empirical claims.
- Clear, well-written presentation with useful visualizations.
- Detailed analysis beyond accuracy numbers, explaining model behaviors.
- Practical benefit for morphologically rich languages with limited data.
- Performance is mixed across datasets and degrades when monotonic alignment assumptions fail.
- Dependence on pre-computed alignments creates an external dependency and potential error source.
- Paper lacks quantitative discussion of computational cost and scalability versus soft attention.
- Treatment of linguistic typology and task assumptions is relatively superficial.
- Question: How sensitive is performance to alignment quality and different alignment methods?
- Question: Can you provide detailed training/inference time comparisons and scalability analysis?
- Question: Can the model be modified to handle limited non-monotonicity in complex languages?
- Question: Why choose LSTMs; were GRUs or transformer architectures considered or tested?
- References are extensive and well-formatted.
- Removal of the GitHub link for anonymity is appropriate.
- Overall: a solid, valuable contribution, especially for low-resource and largely monotonic languages.
