## Review: Morphological Inflection Generation with Hard Monotonic Attention

This paper presents a novel neural model for morphological inflection generation employing a hard attention mechanism, motivated by the typically monotonic alignment between input and output characters in this task. The authors leverage pre-computed character alignments to guide training, simplifying the learning process. The model is evaluated on three established datasets and demonstrates state-of-the-art performance in several settings, alongside an analysis comparing it to a soft attention-based model.

**Contributions:**

1. **Novel Hard Attention Mechanism:** The introduction of a hard attention mechanism, specifically designed to exploit the near-monotonic alignment inherent in morphological inflection, is the primary contribution. This differs significantly from existing sequence-to-sequence approaches relying on soft attention, offering a more focused and controlled transduction process.
2. **Strong Performance on Low-Resource Data:** The model demonstrates a significant advantage over previous methods, particularly neural approaches, when training data is limited (as exemplified by the CELEX dataset). This addresses a crucial challenge in morphological inflection generation for morphologically rich languages.
3. **Analysis of Attention Mechanisms:** The comparative analysis between the hard and soft attention models provides valuable insights into the learned representations and behaviors of each approach, shedding light on the features they extract for this specific task.

**Strengths:**

1. **Novelty and Soundness of Approach:** The core idea of leveraging a hard attention mechanism to enforce monotonic alignment is innovative and well-justified. The approach elegantly addresses a core characteristic of morphological inflection.
2. **Empirically Supported Claims:** The extensive experiments on three diverse datasets convincingly demonstrate the model’s effectiveness. Achieving state-of-the-art results on multiple benchmarks strengthens the credibility of the proposed method.
3. **Clear and Comprehensive Presentation:** The paper is exceptionally well-written, clearly explaining the model architecture, training procedure, and experimental setup. The inclusion of visualizations (e.g., Figure 1) further enhances understanding.
4. **Detailed Analysis:** The analysis comparing the hard and soft attention models goes beyond simply reporting performance numbers. The insights into the learned representations provide a deeper understanding of how the models function and why the hard attention approach may be advantageous in certain scenarios.
5. **Addressing a Practical Challenge:** The model’s strong performance in low-resource settings is particularly valuable, given the data scarcity often encountered when working with morphologically rich languages.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Mixed Results across Datasets:** While strong on CELEX and certain language subsets of SIGMORPHON, the model’s performance isn’t universally superior. The dependence on monotonic alignment becomes a limitation for languages with more complex, non-monotonic morphological processes. This limitation should be explicitly acknowledged.
2. **Reliance on Pre-Computed Alignments:** The need for pre-computed character alignments, while simplifying training, introduces an external dependency and potential error source. The performance is contingent on the quality of these alignments. A discussion of the robustness of the system to errors in the pre-computed alignments would be beneficial.
3. **Limited Discussion of Computational Cost:** The paper lacks a detailed discussion of the computational complexity of the hard attention mechanism compared to soft attention. While the authors allude to potential advantages, quantifying the difference in training and inference time would strengthen the arguments for practical adoption.
4. **Superficial discussion of linguistic typology**: The authors relate the performance on SIGMORPHON to linguistic typology. This would benefit from a more in-depth discussion regarding the assumptions made about the task and language-specific features.



**Questions to Authors:**

1. How sensitive is the model's performance to the quality of the pre-computed character alignments? Did you experiment with different alignment methods or levels of alignment accuracy?
2. Can you provide a more detailed analysis of the computational cost (training and inference time) of the hard attention mechanism compared to soft attention, particularly as sequence lengths increase?
3. For languages with more complex morphological processes where the monotonic alignment assumption is less valid, are there potential modifications to the model architecture (e.g., incorporating limited non-monotonicity) that could improve performance?
4. Could you elaborate on the choice of LSTM units and explore whether other recurrent architectures (e.g., GRUs) or transformers might be suitable for this task?

**Additional Comments:**

- The reference list is extensive and well-formatted.
- The github link has been removed for anonymity, which is appropriate.
- The paper provides a solid contribution to the field of morphological inflection generation. While not a universally superior solution, the hard attention mechanism offers a valuable alternative, particularly in low-resource scenarios and for languages with relatively monotonic morphological processes.
