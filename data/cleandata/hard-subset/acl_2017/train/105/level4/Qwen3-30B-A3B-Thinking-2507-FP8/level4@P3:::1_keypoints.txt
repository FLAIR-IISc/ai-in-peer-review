- Introduces a novel hard monotonic attention model that explicitly models near-monotonic character alignments for morphological inflection.  
- Proposes decoupling alignment learning from transduction, contrasting with soft attention approaches.  
- Achieves state-of-the-art accuracy on the small CELEX dataset, outperforming prior neural and non-neural baselines.  
- Demonstrates robustness with limited training data and better generalization on CELEX than previous neural models.  
- Validated across CELEX, Wiktionary, and SIGMORPHON2016 datasets, with strong results on suffixing+stem-change languages (e.g., German, Russian).  
- Alignment predictions correspond to linguistically plausible transformations and handle one-to-many mappings better than soft attention.  
- Representation analysis shows how hard vs. soft attention models encode character position and type, explaining hard attention's data efficiency.  
- Model performance degrades on languages with non-monotonic or context-sensitive morphology (e.g., Arabic, Navajo), limiting generality.  
- Paper lacks empirical details on computational efficiency, training stability, memory usage, and hyperparameter sensitivity to support the “simpler” claim.  
- Uses Sudoh et al.’s (2013) pre-alignment method without justifying its superiority or analyzing error propagation from alignment quality.  
- Asks how the model handles non-monotonic alignments and whether hard attention can be extended for long-range dependencies.  
- Requests quantitative comparisons of training time, memory usage, and convergence speed versus soft attention baselines.  
- Recommends tempering the “state-of-the-art” claim due to dependency on monotonic alignment and clarifying practical trade-offs between hard and soft attention.
