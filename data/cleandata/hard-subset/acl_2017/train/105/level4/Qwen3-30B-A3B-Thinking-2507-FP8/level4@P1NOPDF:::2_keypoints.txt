- Strength: Proposes a novel encoder–decoder with hard monotonic attention to enforce monotonicity in sequence generation, a promising idea for monotonic tasks like morphology.  
- Strength: Writing is generally clear and the core idea of explicitly enforcing monotonicity is well-motivated.  
- Weakness: Model seems conceptually similar to a BiRNN with decoupled alignment and transduction, raising novelty concerns.  
- Weakness: Evaluation is narrowly limited to morphology, omitting other monotonic tasks (text normalization, transliteration, spelling correction) that would better demonstrate generality.  
- Key concern — Alignment ambiguity: The paper inconsistently describes alignments (Figure 1 implies 1-to-many, text implies many-to-1, authors mention 1-0/0-1/1-1), so the alignment type must be clarified.  
- Key concern — Baseline comparison: The method resembles established two-stage monotone approaches (align then tag); the authors should explicitly contrast with and include a “Monotone Tagger” baseline (e.g., Schnober et al., 2016) to isolate the architectural contribution.  
- Key concern — Evaluation breadth: The authors should test additional monotonic sequence tasks (transliteration, text normalization, etc.) to validate applicability beyond morphology.  
- Key concern — Result phrasing: Phrasing like “on par or better” conflates marginal wins and losses; report and phrase results precisely (e.g., “on par” or “worse” where appropriate).  
- Key concern — Linguistic features: It’s unclear where POS/feature inputs came from and whether these features, not monotonicity, explain performance gains.  
- Minor corrections: Fix notation (Equation 3: use \text{NN}), punctuation/capitalization errors (Line 231 “where”), sequence formatting (x_1,…,x_n), avoid Cyrillic in Figure 1, and correct typos (Line 437 “these”).  
- Additional context: Architecture resembles Stack LSTMs (Cohn et al., 2016); authors should clarify differences.  
- Additional context: Using greedy alignments (vs. sum-over-alignments) may sacrifice optimality—discuss trade-offs.  
- Overall: The monotonicity idea is valuable but its impact is undermined by narrow evaluation and missing critical baselines, leaving novelty and practical value unclear.
