- Summary: Proposes a neural model that explicitly models nearly-monotonic character alignments for morphological inflection and achieves strong results on CELEX, Wiktionary, and SIGMORPHON 2016, especially in low-resource settings.
- Strength: Clearly motivated contribution by modeling monotonic alignment structure, improving performance on small datasets compared to standard seq2seq.
- Strength: Empirical results are convincing, with new state-of-the-art on CELEX and improvements over prior neural and non-neural baselines.
- Strength: Analysis of learned representations illuminates differences between hard and soft attention and how they encode linguistic features.
- Weakness: Model may be a straightforward adaptation of seq2seq with decoupled alignment/transduction and not sufficiently distinguished from simpler alternatives.
- Weakness: Evaluation is limited to morphological inflection; generalization to other monotonic seq2seq tasks (e.g., transliteration) is not demonstrated.
- Weakness: Unclear whether gains stem from the monotonic attention mechanism or from input morphological features; evidence disentangling these factors is insufficient.
- Question: Clarify how "nearly-monotonic" alignments are implemented and how 1-to-many and many-to-1 character alignments are handled.
- Question: Explain why a baseline that first computes monotonic alignments then trains a sequence tagger was not included and how the proposed model differs from that simpler approach.
- Question: Provide details on the morphological features used as input—manual vs. automatic—and whether they could explain the performance gains.
- Minor: Revise "on par or better" to "on par or better than previous approaches" for clarity.
- Minor: Spell out acronyms like "13SIA" at first use.
- Minor: Replace problematic Cyrillic font in Figure 1 with standard Latin characters in examples.
- Minor: Use proper math notation, e.g., "NN" should be formatted as a math operator (e.g., NN or NN()) in Equation (3).
- Minor: Write "x1...xn" as "x₁...xₙ" or "x1,...,xn" for proper mathematical formatting.
