

Summary: This paper introduces a neural model for morphological inflection generation that explicitly incorporates monotonic character alignments between input and output sequences. The authors claim their "hard monotonic attention" mechanism provides state-of-the-art results on three morphological inflection datasets, particularly excelling with small training sets. The main contributions as I see them are: 1) A novel neural architecture that decouples alignment learning from transduction, enabling effective monotonic sequence-to-sequence learning; 2) Strong empirical results demonstrating superiority over previous neural and non-neural approaches, especially on small datasets; 3) Insightful analysis of the representations learned by both hard and soft attention models.

Strengths: 
1. The paper makes a compelling case for the importance of modeling monotonicity in morphological inflection tasks. The authors correctly identify that many morphological transformations follow nearly-monotonic character alignments, making standard sequence-to-sequence models suboptimal. Their approach of explicitly modeling this monotonicity through a hard attention mechanism is well-motivated and addresses a genuine limitation in previous work.

2. The most significant contribution is the model's superior performance on small training sets (CELEX dataset), where it significantly outperforms both previous neural models and the latent-variable WFST model. This is particularly noteworthy as small datasets have been a persistent challenge for neural approaches, and the authors successfully demonstrate that their architecture avoids overfitting where others fail. The learning curves in Figure 2 clearly show the hard attention model's superior generalization on the small CELEX dataset.

3. The analysis of learned representations provides valuable insights into how neural models process morphological information. The SVD projections in Figure 4 reveal that the hard attention model encodes character position information less explicitly than the soft attention model, which explains its better performance with limited data. This analysis goes beyond typical model comparisons to offer meaningful theoretical insights about what neural models learn.

Weaknesses:
1. The paper fails to adequately distinguish their approach from simpler alternatives. As noted in the draft review, a straightforward approach would be to first obtain monotonic alignments (1-to-many) using an existing aligner, then train a standard sequence tagger on these alignments. The authors do not include this as a baseline, making it difficult to assess whether their contributions are truly novel or merely a specific implementation of a more general approach.

2. The evaluation is limited exclusively to morphological inflection tasks. The authors claim their model is designed for "nearly-monotonic sequence-to-sequence learning," yet they do not evaluate on other monotonic tasks (e.g., transliteration, abstractive summarization, or machine translation). This omission weakens their claim about the broader applicability of their method.

3. The paper's description of alignment types is confusing. The authors state "several input characters aligned to 1 output character" (lines 311-312), but the examples (like flog→fliege) show 1-to-many alignments (one input character aligning to multiple output characters). This inconsistency undermines the technical clarity of the paper.

Questions to Authors:
1. Could you clarify the alignment type your model uses? Your examples (like flog→fliege) demonstrate 1-to-many alignments, but your text (lines 311-312) suggests many-to-1 alignments. Please specify which alignment type is used and how it relates to the monotonicity assumption.

2. Why did you not include the simpler baseline of first obtaining monotonic alignments (using an existing aligner) and then training a standard sequence tagger on these alignments? How would this baseline compare to your approach in terms of performance and complexity?

3. Given your model's design for monotonic sequence-to-sequence tasks, why not evaluate on additional monotonic tasks beyond morphological inflection (e.g., transliteration or abstractive summarization) to better demonstrate the broader applicability of your approach?

4. In Table 3, your model performs slightly worse than the soft attention baseline on some languages (e.g., Hungarian, Arabic). Could you provide linguistic analysis explaining why the monotonic assumption might be less suitable for these languages compared to suffixing languages like German or Spanish?
