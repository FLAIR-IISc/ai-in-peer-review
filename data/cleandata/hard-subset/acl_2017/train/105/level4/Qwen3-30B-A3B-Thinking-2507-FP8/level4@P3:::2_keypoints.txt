- Proposes a neural model for morphological inflection that explicitly models nearly-monotonic character alignments via a hard attention mechanism.  
- Decouples alignment learning from transduction using independently learned alignments in a simple training procedure.  
- Reports state-of-the-art results on three inflection datasets, especially strong in low-resource settings, and analyzes learned representations against soft-attention models.  

- Significant performance gains on low-resource CELEX (500 examples), outperforming neural and non-neural baselines and avoiding overfitting per dev-set curves.  
- Provides insightful analysis: SVD visualizations show hard attention encodes positional information less strongly than soft attention, giving concrete representational insights.  
- Paper is clearly written and well-structured, with strong motivation and thorough related-work comparison.  

- Evaluation is limited to morphological inflection (monotonic); no experiments on other monotonic seq2seq tasks to show generality.  
- Lacks comparison to a simpler baseline (e.g., BiLSTM sequence tagger trained on monotonic alignments) to isolate the benefit of the architecture versus monotonic constraints.  
- Insufficient analysis of the contribution of linguistic features (POS, gender, etc.); unclear how much gains come from features versus the hard attention mechanism.  

- Please clarify which dataset results are truly “better” versus merely “on par.”  
- Why not include a sequence-tagger baseline trained on monotonic alignments, and how would its performance compare?  
- Provide ablation results showing performance without linguistic features to quantify their impact.  
- If soft attention also learns monotonic alignments, why does hard attention still outperform on CELEX—could positional encoding differences explain this?  

- Clarify the alignment type used (1-to-many, many-to-1, or many-to-many) as descriptions appear inconsistent with examples.  
- Standardize mathematical notation (use subscripts like x_{1:n}, x_1...x_n) for clarity.  
- Replace the Cyrillic font in Figure 1 with standard Latin characters to improve readability.
