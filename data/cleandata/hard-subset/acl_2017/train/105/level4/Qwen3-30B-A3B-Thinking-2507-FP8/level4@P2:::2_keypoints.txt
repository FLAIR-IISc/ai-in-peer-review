- Novel neural architecture that decouples alignment learning from transduction using a hard monotonic attention mechanism, claiming SOTA on three morphological inflection datasets.  
- Claims especially strong performance on small training sets, with hard attention avoiding overfitting.  
- Analysis of learned representations (e.g., SVD projections) showing hard vs. soft attention encode positional information differently.

- Strength: Well-motivated modeling of monotonicity for morphological inflection, addressing limitations of standard seq2seq models.  
- Strength: Significant empirical gains on small-data setting (CELEX), with learning curves showing better generalization than prior models.  
- Strength: Representation analysis provides theoretical insight into what hard vs. soft attention models learn.

- Weakness: No baseline that first obtains monotonic alignments (with an existing aligner) then trains a standard sequence tagger, leaving novelty unclear.  
- Weakness: Evaluation restricted to morphological inflection, so broader applicability to other monotonic tasks is untested.  
- Weakness: Confusing/contradictory description of alignment types (text claims many-to-1 but examples show 1-to-many), reducing technical clarity.

- Q: Please clarify which alignment type the model uses (1-to-many vs. many-to-1) and how this fits the monotonicity assumption.  
- Q: Why was the align-then-tag baseline omitted, and how would it compare in performance and complexity?  
- Q: Why not evaluate on additional monotonic tasks (e.g., transliteration) to support broader claims?  
- Q: Can you explain linguistically why the model underperforms soft attention on some languages (e.g., Hungarian, Arabic) compared to suffixing languages?
