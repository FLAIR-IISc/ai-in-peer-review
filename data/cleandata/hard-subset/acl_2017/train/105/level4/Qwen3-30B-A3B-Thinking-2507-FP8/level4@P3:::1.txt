

**Summary and Contributions**  
This paper introduces a novel neural model for morphological inflection generation that employs a "hard monotonic attention" mechanism, explicitly modeling nearly-monotonic character alignments between input and output sequences. The authors' key contributions, as evaluated by this reviewer, are:  
1. A hard attention mechanism that directly models monotonic alignments (common in morphological inflection) without jointly learning alignments during training, contrasting with soft attention approaches.  
2. State-of-the-art performance on the small CELEX dataset (surpassing prior neural models and latent-variable FSTs), demonstrating robustness with limited training data.  
3. An insightful analysis of learned representations, revealing how both hard and soft attention models encode linguistic features like character position and type.  

**Strengths**  
1. **Novel alignment modeling approach**: The paper's core innovation—decoupling alignment learning from transduction via a hard attention mechanism—addresses a critical limitation of soft attention models (Bahdanau et al., 2014) in low-resource settings. Unlike prior neural work (e.g., Kann and Schütze, 2016a), the model avoids overfitting on small datasets (CELEX) by leveraging pre-learned monotonic alignments. This is evidenced by the model achieving 89.44% accuracy on CELEX, outperforming the best neural baselines (87.65%) and the latent-variable FST (88.30%). Crucially, this is the first neural model to surpass the non-neural state-of-the-art on this dataset.  

2. **Strong empirical validation across datasets**: The model's performance is rigorously evaluated across three diverse datasets (CELEX, Wiktionary, SIGMORPHON2016), demonstrating both robustness (on large Wiktionary datasets) and task-specific efficacy (e.g., superior results on suffixing+stem-change languages like German and Russian). The analysis in Figure 3 and Table 3 shows the model's alignment predictions align linguistically with morphological transformations (e.g., handling one-to-many mappings in "flog→fliege"), unlike soft attention models.  

3. **Insightful representation analysis**: The paper’s comparison of learned representations (Figure 4) provides valuable theoretical grounding for the model’s success. It reveals that the hard attention model encodes position information less explicitly than soft attention (due to its step-based control mechanism), explaining its efficiency with limited data. This analysis bridges the gap between neural model behavior and linguistic phenomena, a significant contribution beyond mere performance gains.  

**Weaknesses**  
1. **Limited generality across linguistic phenomena**: The model’s advantages are restricted to languages with monotonic alignment (e.g., German, Russian) but degrade on languages with context-sensitive morphology (e.g., vowel harmony in Arabic). While the paper acknowledges this (Table 3), it does not sufficiently address *why* this limitation exists or propose remedies. For instance, the model underperforms on Arabic (88.32% vs. soft attention’s 88.86%), yet the paper treats this as a minor footnote rather than a fundamental constraint.  

2. **Lack of practical implementation insights**: The paper claims the model is "simpler" than soft attention approaches but omits critical details about computational efficiency, training stability, or implementation complexity. For example, it does not compare training time, memory usage, or hyperparameter sensitivity against baselines. Without this, the claim of "ease of implementation" remains unsubstantiated.  

3. **Insufficient justification for alignment method**: The paper uses Sudoh et al.’s (2013) character alignment model but does not justify why it outperforms end-to-end alignment learning (e.g., Yu et al., 2016). The results show the model’s success depends on high-quality pre-aligned data, yet the paper does not explore how alignment errors might propagate or whether this dependency limits scalability.  

**Questions to Authors**  
1. How does the model handle cases where the alignment is non-monotonic (e.g., vowel harmony)? Could the hard attention mechanism be extended to incorporate long-range dependencies?  
2. The paper claims the model is "simpler" than soft attention approaches. Please provide quantitative comparisons of training time, memory usage, and convergence speed against the soft attention baselines.  
3. On the SIGMORPHON dataset, the model underperforms on languages like Arabic and Navajo. Could this be due to the alignment method’s inability to capture non-monotonic patterns, and how might the model be adapted for such cases?  

**Additional Comments**  
The paper is well-written and methodologically sound, but the claim of "state-of-the-art" performance should be tempered to reflect the model’s dependency on monotonic alignment. The analysis of learned representations is particularly strong and merits emphasis. The authors should clarify the practical trade-offs of their approach (e.g., when to use hard vs. soft attention) in the discussion.
