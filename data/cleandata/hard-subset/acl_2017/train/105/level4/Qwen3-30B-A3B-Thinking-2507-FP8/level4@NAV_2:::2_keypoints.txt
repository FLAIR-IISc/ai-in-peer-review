- Introduces a hard-attention neural model that exploits nearly-monotonic input–output alignments for morphological inflection.
- Claims three main contributions: hard-attention architecture for monotonic alignments; state-of-the-art results on three inflection datasets, especially with small training sets; analysis comparing hard vs soft attention representations.
- Strength: Outperforms previous models on CELEX, surpassing a latent-variable WFST with limited training data.
- Strength: Provides representation analysis showing hard attention encodes positional information less explicitly, explaining better small-data performance.
- Strength: Achieves strong results across CELEX, Wiktionary, and SIGMORPHON without major architecture changes for different languages.
- Weakness: Architecture appears to be a standard BiRNN with decoupled alignments rather than a fundamentally novel model, undermining novelty claims.
- Weakness: Evaluation is limited to morphological inflection and doesn’t test other monotonic seq2seq tasks to show broader applicability.
- Weakness: Alignment mechanism is not clearly explained (1-to-many vs many-to-1 vs many-to-many), causing confusion for examples like "flog→fliege".
- Question: Please clarify the alignment directionality—paper text and Figure 1 seem inconsistent about 1-to-many vs several-to-one alignments.
- Question: Why not include a simple baseline using a monotone aligner to produce 1-to-many alignments followed by a standard tagger (e.g., BiLSTM)?
- Question: Why limit experiments to morphological inflection instead of also evaluating on other monotonic tasks (e.g., transliteration, summarization) to demonstrate generality?
