

The paper proposes a novel encoder-decoder architecture with "hard monotonic attention" for sequence-to-sequence tasks, specifically targeting morphological inflection. The core contribution—explicitly enforcing monotonicity in output generation—is well-motivated: many linguistic tasks (unlike machine translation) exhibit inherent monotonicity, making standard non-monotonic models suboptimal. The paper is clearly written and presents a fresh perspective on this gap.

**Major Strengths**  
The proposal is conceptually sound and addresses a genuine limitation in existing neural seq2seq models. The authors convincingly argue that monotonicity is a natural property of morphological tasks, and their architecture directly enforces this constraint. The clarity of the technical description (e.g., Fig. 1) and the well-structured presentation are notable.

**Critical Weaknesses**  
1. **Lack of Novelty and Missing Baseline**: The core mechanism appears to reduce to a standard BiRNN with decoupled alignment training. Crucially, the authors do not compare against a simpler, established approach: first generating monotonic alignments (e.g., 1-to-many via a monotone aligner like Jiampojamarn & Kondrak) and then training a neural tagger (e.g., BiLSTM) to predict outputs from inputs *given these alignments*. This is explicitly described in Schnober et al. (2016, §4.2) and is a standard solution for monotone seq2seq. The authors should:  
   - Clarify how their method *differs* from this approach (e.g., is their "hard monotonic attention" fundamentally distinct from alignment-based tagging?).  
   - Include this baseline to validate their claims. Without it, the novelty of the architecture is questionable.  

2. **Narrow Evaluation Scope**: The model is tested *only* on morphology datasets (Turkish, German, English). Monotonic seq2seq tasks abound (e.g., phonetic transcription, text normalization, chemical name generation), where enforcing monotonicity would be particularly impactful. The authors miss a critical opportunity to demonstrate broader applicability. As Schnober et al. (2016) and Cohn et al. (2016) show, monotonicity constraints yield significant gains in such tasks. The claim of "on par or better" (l.791) lacks context without comparisons to these domains.  

**Key Clarifications Needed**  
- **Alignment Mechanics**: The paper describes alignments as "several input characters aligned to 1 output character" (l.311–312), but Fig. 1 and the running example (flog → fliege) suggest *1-to-many* (one input character to multiple outputs). Please clarify:  
  - Is the alignment *strictly* 1-to-many (input:output = 1:n), or does it allow many-to-1 (n:1) or many-to-many?  
  - The author response mentions "1-0, 0-1, 1-1" alignments; how do these combine into the final monotonic output?  

- **Linguistic Features**: The model uses POS and other features (Fig. 1), but their origin and impact are unclear.  
  - Where were these features sourced? (e.g., external parser?)  
  - Could they account for performance gains rather than monotonicity?  

**Minor Issues**  
- **Wording**: Replace "on par or better" (l.791) with precise metrics (e.g., "achieves statistically significant gains in X% of tasks"). The current phrasing risks conflating "on par" with "better" due to cognitive bias.  
- **Formatting**:  
  - Equation (3): Use `\text{NN}` instead of `NN`.  
  - l.231: "Where" → "where".  
  - l.237: Use `x_1, \ldots, x_n` (commas for sequences; avoid `x1...xn`).  
  - Figure 1: Avoid Cyrillic fonts (e.g., "флог" → "flog") for universal readability.  
  - l.437: "these" (typo).  

**Conclusion**  
This paper identifies a meaningful problem (monotonicity in seq2seq) but fails to convincingly demonstrate its solution’s novelty or generality. The proposed architecture appears to be a specialized variant of a simpler, established pipeline (monotone alignment + tagger). Without comparison to this baseline and evaluation on non-morphology tasks, the contribution is overstated. The work is well-written and theoretically grounded, but the limited scope and lack of a critical baseline significantly undermine its impact.  

**Recommendation**: **Reject**. The core idea has merit, but the execution lacks novelty validation and broader relevance. Addressing the baseline and evaluation gaps would require substantial rework beyond a minor revision.  

*Note: The AC’s reference to Cohn et al. (2016) and Stack LSTM methods is relevant but not decisive here—the key issue is the missing baseline comparison.*
