- Novel hard-attention mechanism that models nearly-monotonic character alignments without joint alignment-transduction, improving small-data performance.  
- Achieves state-of-the-art on CELEX with 500 training examples per inflection, outperforming previous neural approaches and Dreyer et al.'s WFST.  
- Analysis shows hard attention encodes less positional information in inputs than soft attention, which may explain its robustness with limited data.  
- Major contribution: substantially better small-data performance (CELEX 89.44% vs prior neural 87.65% and WFST 88.3%).  
- Insightful alignment and representation analysis: hard model emphasizes character identity over position, explaining improved generalization.  
- Simpler, more efficient training by precomputing alignments and using cross-entropy, avoiding alignment marginalization and training instability.  
- Overstates generality: underperforms on languages with vowel harmony/non-monotonic phenomena and lacks discussion of why or how to adapt.  
- Weak case for necessity of hard attention: soft attention also learns monotonic alignments, so it's unclear why hard attention is fundamentally better.  
- Missing practical implementation and computational-cost details (training time, memory, inference speed) for real-world use.  
- Question: Why does this model surpass the latent-variable CELEX result with original data size while Rastogi et al. (2016) needed twice the data?  
- Question: Is soft attention's stronger positional encoding a cause of overfitting, and could positional embeddings let soft models match hard-model performance?  
- Question: For non-monotonic languages (e.g., Turkish), would adding a mechanism for non-monotonicity (e.g., soft attention) improve versatility or undermine simplicity?
