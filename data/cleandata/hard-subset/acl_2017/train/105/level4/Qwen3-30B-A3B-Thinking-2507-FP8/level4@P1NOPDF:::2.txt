

Strengths: The paper introduces a novel encoder-decoder architecture incorporating "hard monotonic attention," addressing an important gap in sequence-to-sequence modeling for monotonic tasks like morphology. The writing is generally clear and well-structured, and the core idea—explicitly enforcing monotonicity in output generation—is promising.

Weaknesses: The model appears conceptually similar to a standard BiRNN with decoupled alignment and transduction, raising questions about novelty. Crucially, the evaluation is limited exclusively to morphology tasks, omitting other monotonic sequence-to-sequence domains (e.g., text normalization, spelling correction) where the approach’s value could be more convincingly demonstrated.

Key Questions and Concerns:  
1. **Alignment Mechanism**: The paper’s description of alignments is inconsistent. Figure 1 suggests 1-to-many alignments (e.g., one input character aligns with multiple output characters), but lines 311–312 imply many-to-1 mappings (multiple inputs aligning to one output). Clarify: are alignments 1-to-many, many-to-1, or many-to-many? The authors’ subsequent discussion of "1-0, 0-1, 1-1" alignments (per author response) further complicates this.  

2. **Baseline Comparison**: The proposed approach resembles a two-stage process already established in the literature (e.g., Schnober et al., 2016):  
   - *Stage 1*: Monotonically align input and output sequences (e.g., using Jiampojamarn & Kondrak’s toolkit).  
   - *Stage 2*: Train a standard sequence tagger (e.g., BiLSTM) to predict output sequences from input sequences *given these monotone alignments*.  
   This method inherently preserves monotonicity without modifying the core encoder-decoder. The authors should:  
   - (2a) Explicitly contrast their method with this established two-stage approach.  
   - (2b) Include it as a baseline (e.g., "Monotone Tagger") to isolate the contribution of their architecture versus the alignment strategy. Their current baseline (Nicolai et al., 2015) does not address monotonicity directly.  

3. **Evaluation Scope**: Limiting evaluation to morphology is a significant limitation. Monotonic sequence tasks (e.g., transliteration, text normalization) are abundant. The authors could have demonstrated broader applicability by testing on these domains—especially since monotonicity is their core innovation.  

4. **Result Phrasing**: The claim "on par or better" (line 791) risks conflating marginal results with meaningful improvements. If the model underperforms in some cases, it should be stated as "on par" or "worse," not "on par or better."  

5. **Linguistic Features**: Figure 1 implies POS and other features are used, but the paper lacks details:  
   - (5a) Where were these features sourced?  
   - (5b) Could they partially explain performance gains, rather than monotonicity constraints?  

Minor Corrections:  
- Equation (3): Use $\text{NN}$ instead of $NN$.  
- Line 231: "Where" → "where."  
- Line 237: Format sequences as $x_1, \ldots, x_n$ (not $x1\ldots xn$).  
- Figure 1: Avoid Cyrillic fonts for readability (e.g., use Latin script for examples).  
- Line 437: "these" (corrected from "this").  

Additional Context:  
- The architecture bears resemblance to Stack LSTMs (Cohn et al., 2016), which also predict sequences of actions modifying input. Clarify how this work differs.  
- The use of greedy alignment (vs. Rastogi et al.’s sum-over-alignments) may sacrifice optimality. Discuss potential trade-offs.  

The paper’s core idea—explicit monotonicity modeling—is valuable, but its impact is undermined by the narrow evaluation and insufficient baseline comparison. Addressing these points would strengthen the contribution significantly. The author response clarified alignment mechanics, but the baseline question (2a–2b) remains unaddressed. The two-stage monotone tagger approach (Schnober et al., 2016) is a critical baseline that should be tested to validate the necessity of the proposed architecture. Without this, the novelty and practical value of the method are unclear.
