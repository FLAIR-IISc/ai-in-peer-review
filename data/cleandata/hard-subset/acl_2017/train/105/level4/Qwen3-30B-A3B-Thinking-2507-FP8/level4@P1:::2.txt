

Summary: This paper proposes a neural model for morphological inflection generation that explicitly models the nearly-monotonic character alignments commonly found in morphological inflection tasks. The authors evaluate their model on three standard datasets (CELEX, Wiktionary, and SIGMORPHON 2016), demonstrating state-of-the-art results, particularly on small datasets where previous neural approaches struggled.

Strengths: 
1. The paper makes a clear and well-motivated contribution by explicitly modeling the monotonic alignment structure inherent to morphological inflection, which is a significant departure from standard neural sequence-to-sequence approaches that don't make this assumption. The authors provide strong evidence that this modeling choice leads to better performance on small datasets.
2. The empirical results are compelling, especially on the CELEX dataset where the model outperforms previous neural approaches and even surpasses the best non-neural model (Dreyer et al., 2008), establishing a new state-of-the-art for this challenging low-resource setting.
3. The analysis of learned representations provides valuable insights into how both hard and soft attention models encode linguistic features, helping explain why the hard attention approach works well for morphological inflection tasks. The comparison between hard and soft attention mechanisms reveals how they encode features like character types and positions in the word.

Weaknesses:
1. The proposed model appears to be a straightforward adaptation of standard neural sequence-to-sequence architectures with the key difference being the decoupling of alignment and transduction. The authors don't sufficiently distinguish their approach from simpler alternatives that could achieve similar results, such as first computing monotonic alignments and then training a standard sequence tagger on these alignments.
2. The evaluation is limited to morphological inflection tasks, which are inherently monotonic. Without testing on other monotonic sequence-to-sequence tasks (e.g., transliteration or abstractive summarization), it's difficult to assess whether the proposed approach generalizes to other domains where monotonicity is present.
3. The paper claims to establish a new state-of-the-art but doesn't provide sufficient evidence to distinguish whether the performance gains are due to the monotonic attention mechanism or other factors, such as the morphological features used as inputs. The authors should clarify whether the features themselves could account for the improvements.

Questions to Authors:
1. Could you clarify the exact nature of your alignment approach? The paper describes alignments as "nearly-monotonic" but seems to use a mix of 1-to-many and many-to-1 alignments. How do you handle cases where multiple input characters align to a single output character, and vice versa?
2. How does your model differ from the simpler approach of first computing monotonic alignments (e.g., using a standard aligner) and then training a sequence tagger on these alignments? Why didn't you include this as a baseline, as suggested by Schnober et al. (2016) and Cohn et al. (2016)?
3. Could you provide more details about the morphological features used as input? Are they manually engineered or automatically extracted? Could their inclusion be responsible for the performance gains rather than the monotonic attention mechanism itself?

Minor comments:
- The phrase "on par or better" (line 791) should be revised to "on par or better than previous approaches" for clarity.
- The references to "13SIA" and similar acronyms should be spelled out at first use for clarity.
- The Cyrillic font in Figure 1 is problematic; please consider using standard Latin characters for examples.
- Equation (3) should use $\text{NN}$ instead of $NN$ for proper mathematical notation.
- The phrase "x1...xn" should be written as "x₁...xₙ" or "x1,...,xn" for proper mathematical formatting.
