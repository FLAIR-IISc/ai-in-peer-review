

Review  

The paper introduces a novel neural model for morphological inflection generation, leveraging "hard monotonic attention" to separate character alignment (obtained via a Bayesian method for transliteration) from morphological conversion. This approach fundamentally differs from prior state-of-the-art methods that employ "soft attention," where alignment and conversion are jointly optimized in a probabilistic framework. The core idea is both innovative and theoretically sound, representing a meaningful departure from existing paradigms.  

**Strengths**  
The paper’s primary strength lies in its conceptual novelty. The hard monotonic attention mechanism, combined with explicitly separated alignment and conversion steps, offers a fresh perspective on a well-studied task. The method is clearly articulated, with a logical flow from problem definition to implementation. The experimental design is comprehensive, covering multiple languages (English, Russian, German, Spanish, and Turkish) and datasets (CELEX, Wiktionary, and others), providing a robust evaluation of the model’s capabilities. The writing is precise and accessible, making the technical contributions easy to follow.  

**Weaknesses**  
While the approach is promising, the empirical results reveal important limitations that warrant careful consideration. The model demonstrates strong effectiveness primarily under two specific conditions: (1) when training data is scarce (e.g., CELEX), and (2) when morphological alignment is predominantly monotonic and context-independent (e.g., Russian, German, Spanish). However, its performance becomes inconsistent in scenarios with richer context dependencies or larger datasets (e.g., Wiktionary for Turkish or English). For instance, the model underperforms relative to baselines on Turkish inflection, where alignment is non-monotonic and context-sensitive. This suggests the method is not universally superior but rather specialized for tasks with constrained alignment patterns.  

The paper would be significantly strengthened by explicitly addressing the practical trade-offs of the proposed method. While computational efficiency and implementation simplicity are implied as advantages, these are not quantified or discussed. A brief analysis of inference speed, memory usage, or training complexity compared to soft-attention baselines would clarify whether the method’s niche applicability is offset by tangible practical benefits. Without this, the claim that the approach offers "practical merits" remains unsubstantiated.  

**Recommendation and Suggestions**  
The paper presents a valuable contribution to the field by introducing a conceptually distinct model for morphological inflection. However, the current evaluation does not fully establish its broader relevance. To enhance the paper’s impact, the authors should:  
1. **Clarify the scope of applicability**: Explicitly state that the method excels for languages with monotonic alignment and minimal context dependence, while acknowledging its limitations in more complex morphological systems.  
2. **Quantify practical advantages**: Include computational cost comparisons (e.g., training time, inference latency) against baselines to substantiate claims about implementation ease.  
3. **Address inconsistent results**: Provide deeper analysis of *why* the model underperforms on Turkish or English Wiktionary data—e.g., by examining alignment errors or context sensitivity thresholds.  

The current work is technically solid and well-written, but its contribution would be more compelling if framed as a targeted solution for specific linguistic scenarios rather than a general-purpose improvement. The novelty of the attention mechanism is clear, but the paper risks overstating its generality without acknowledging these contextual constraints.  

In summary, this paper offers a creative and well-executed alternative to soft-attention models for morphological inflection. With minor revisions to clarify its niche applicability and provide empirical evidence for practical benefits, it would make a strong contribution to ACL 2017. As presented, the results are promising but require sharper contextualization to fully convince the community of the method’s broader utility. The authors should refine the discussion to reflect the method’s specific strengths and limitations, ensuring readers understand *when* and *why* this approach is preferable to existing solutions.
