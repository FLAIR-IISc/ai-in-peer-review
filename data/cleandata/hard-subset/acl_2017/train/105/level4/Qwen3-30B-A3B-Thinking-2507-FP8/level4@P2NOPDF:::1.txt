

Strengths:  
The paper introduces a novel hard monotonic attention mechanism, representing a significant departure from prior neural approaches to morphological inflection generation. This distinct method—separating character alignment (via a Bayesian transliteration model) from inflection conversion—offers a fresh perspective compared to existing state-of-the-art models that rely on joint soft attention. The core idea is theoretically sound and well-motivated, addressing a key limitation in prior work where alignment and conversion were conflated in probabilistic modeling.

Weaknesses:  
The experimental evaluation presents mixed results. While the model excels under specific conditions—such as limited training data (e.g., CELEX) or languages with predominantly monotonic alignments and minimal context dependency (e.g., Russian, German, Spanish)—its performance is less consistent in other scenarios. For instance, it does not consistently outperform baselines on datasets or languages where alignments are non-monotonic or context-sensitive (e.g., Turkish or Finnish). This conditional effectiveness weakens the claim of broad superiority, as the method’s advantages appear constrained to a subset of morphological phenomena rather than generalizable across all tasks.

General Discussion:  
The paper is well-structured, clearly written, and presents a comprehensive empirical evaluation. The proposed framework is a meaningful contribution to the field, particularly for tasks where monotonic alignment dominates. However, the authors should more explicitly contextualize the method’s practical advantages beyond accuracy. For example, how does the hard attention approach compare in terms of computational efficiency, training stability, or implementation simplicity relative to soft attention models? Including such details would strengthen the argument for adoption in real-world applications. Additionally, while the paper demonstrates the model’s efficacy in constrained settings, a more nuanced discussion of its limitations—particularly regarding context-dependent inflection patterns—would enhance its credibility. The current presentation risks overemphasizing generality, given the evidence that performance varies significantly across languages and data regimes. To fully convince reviewers of the method’s broader relevance, the authors should either broaden their experimental scope or clarify the precise conditions under which their approach is optimal. Overall, the work is promising but would benefit from sharper articulation of its scope and tangible practical benefits.
