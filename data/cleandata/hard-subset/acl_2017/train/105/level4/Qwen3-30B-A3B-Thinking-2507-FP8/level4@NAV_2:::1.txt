

Summary: The paper introduces a neural model for morphological inflection generation that employs a hard monotonic attention mechanism, explicitly modeling nearly-monotonic character alignments between input and output sequences. The primary contributions are: (1) a novel hard attention model designed specifically for nearly-monotonic sequence-to-sequence tasks like morphological inflection; (2) state-of-the-art results across three benchmark datasets, particularly demonstrating superior performance on small training sets where prior neural models underperform; (3) an insightful comparative analysis of learned representations between hard and soft attention mechanisms, revealing how each encodes linguistic features.

Strengths: 
1. The hard attention mechanism provides a fundamentally different approach to sequence transduction compared to soft attention models, eliminating the need to jointly learn alignment and transduction. This leads to demonstrably better performance on small datasets (CELEX), where the model surpasses the latent-variable WFST baseline—unprecedented for neural approaches on this dataset—and avoids overfitting observed in soft attention models (Figure 2). 
2. The comprehensive experimental evaluation across three diverse datasets (CELEX, Wiktionary, SIGMORPHON) robustly demonstrates the model's versatility. It achieves the highest average accuracy on Wiktionary and outperforms soft attention baselines on suffixing+stem-change languages (Russian, German, Spanish), where monotonic alignment is linguistically appropriate, providing strong evidence for the approach's validity.
3. The representation analysis (Figure 4) offers valuable theoretical insights by revealing how hard attention encodes positional information less explicitly than soft attention (due to its step-based control mechanism), which explains its data efficiency. This analysis significantly advances understanding of what neural models learn for inflection generation.

Weaknesses:
1. The model's performance advantage is limited to languages with predominantly monotonic alignment and minimal context sensitivity (e.g., Russian, German, Spanish), as evidenced by its underperformance on languages with vowel/consonant harmony (e.g., Arabic, Hungarian) where soft attention excels. The paper does not sufficiently address this scope limitation or provide strategies for handling such cases.
2. The reliance on externally computed character alignments (via Sudoh et al., 2013) adds a dependency not present in joint learning approaches (e.g., Yu et al., 2016), potentially complicating practical deployment. The authors do not compare computational costs or implementation complexity against competing methods, which is a significant omission for a practical contribution.
3. While the model sets a new state-of-the-art on CELEX and Wiktionary, it does not achieve the best results on the SIGMORPHON 2016 shared task (Table 3), where the soft attention model slightly outperforms it. The paper fails to adequately explain why the soft attention model performs better on certain languages, weakening the claim of general superiority.

Questions to Authors:
How does the computational cost (training/inference time) of your hard attention model compare to soft attention baselines (e.g., MED, Luong et al., 2015), particularly given the additional step of precomputing character alignments?
