

# Review for "Morphological Inflection Generation with Hard Monotonic Attention"

## Summary and Contributions
This paper proposes a neural model for morphological inflection generation that explicitly incorporates nearly-monotonic character alignments between source and target words. The authors present a hard attention mechanism that decouples alignment learning from transduction, using a simple training procedure based on independently learned alignments. The key contributions are: (1) a neural model that directly models nearly-monotonic alignments through a hard attention mechanism, (2) state-of-the-art results on three morphological inflection datasets, particularly with small training sets, and (3) a detailed analysis comparing the representations learned by their model and soft attention models.

## Strengths
1. **Significant performance gains on low-resource datasets**: The model achieves state-of-the-art results on the CELEX dataset (only 500 training examples per inflection type), outperforming both neural baselines (Kann and Schütze, 2016; Rastogi et al., 2016) and the non-neural latent variable WFST model (Dreyer et al., 2008). This is particularly important as previous neural models struggled with small datasets, often underperforming compared to traditional methods. The authors demonstrate that their model's hard monotonic attention mechanism prevents overfitting on small datasets, as evidenced by the learning curves in Figure 2 showing superior dev-set performance despite similar train-set accuracy.

2. **Insightful analysis of model representations**: The paper provides a valuable comparison of the representations learned by hard and soft attention models. The SVD visualizations (Figures 4a-d) reveal that the hard attention model encodes positional information less strongly than the soft attention model, which aligns with their hypothesis that the hard attention model's explicit step mechanism handles position information differently. This analysis offers concrete insights into how neural sequence-to-sequence models process morphological information, which is a significant contribution beyond just the model itself.

3. **Clear and well-structured presentation**: The paper is exceptionally well-written, with clear explanations of the model architecture, training procedure, and experimental setup. The authors effectively motivate their approach by highlighting the nearly-monotonic nature of morphological inflection alignments, and their comparison with related work is thorough and appropriate.

## Weaknesses
1. **Limited evaluation scope**: The paper only evaluates the model on morphological inflection tasks, which are inherently monotonic. Without testing on other monotonic sequence-to-sequence tasks (such as abstractive summarization, transliteration, or machine translation), it's difficult to assess whether the hard monotonic attention approach has broader applicability. The authors could have demonstrated the generalizability of their method by testing on additional monotonic tasks, which would strengthen the contribution.

2. **Lack of comparison to a simpler baseline**: The authors don't compare their approach to a straightforward baseline that would directly use the monotonic alignments to train a sequence tagger (e.g., a BiLSTM sequence tagger predicting output characters given input characters with monotonic constraints). This simpler approach would have served as a strong baseline for their method, as suggested in the draft review. Without this comparison, it's unclear how much of their performance gain stems from the specific neural architecture versus the monotonic alignment constraint.

3. **Insufficient analysis of linguistic features**: The paper doesn't adequately address whether the linguistic features (POS, gender, etc.) used as inputs might be responsible for their performance gains in some cases, rather than the monotonicity constraints themselves. For instance, it's possible that the features provide sufficient information to solve the task, making the monotonic attention mechanism less critical. The authors should clarify whether their model performs well without these features.

## Questions to Authors
1. The paper states that the model achieves "on par or better" results than previous approaches (line 791), but this phrasing risks conflating "better" and "on par" results. Could you clarify which results are truly "better" versus "on par" across the datasets?

2. The draft review correctly points out that the paper doesn't compare to a sequence tagger trained on monotonic alignments. Given that your alignment approach is fundamentally about creating a monotonic sequence of actions, why not include this as a baseline? How would results compare to such a simpler model?

3. How much of your performance gain on the CELEX dataset can be attributed to the linguistic features versus the hard monotonic attention mechanism? Could you provide ablation results showing the model's performance without these features?

4. The analysis in Section 5.1 suggests that the soft attention model also learns monotonic alignments. Why does the hard attention model still outperform the soft attention model on the CELEX dataset, given this similarity in alignments? Could this be related to the different ways the models encode positional information?

## Additional Comments
- The paper would benefit from a more precise description of the alignment type used (1-to-many vs. many-to-1 vs. many-to-many). The draft review correctly points out that the description in lines 311-312 seems to contradict the examples shown in the paper.
- The notation in equations (e.g., "x1:n" should be "x_1:n" or "x_{1:n}") and in the text (e.g., "x1...xn" should be "x_1...x_n") needs standardization to conform to mathematical conventions.
- The use of Cyrillic font in Figure 1 is problematic as it makes it difficult to reference the example. Consider using standard Latin characters instead.
