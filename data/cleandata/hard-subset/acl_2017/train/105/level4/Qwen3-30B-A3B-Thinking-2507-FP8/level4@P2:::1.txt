

Summary: This paper introduces a neural model for morphological inflection generation that employs a hard attention mechanism inspired by the nearly-monotonic alignment between input and output character sequences. The authors evaluate their model on three established morphological inflection datasets (CELEX, Wiktionary, and SIGMORPHON 2016), demonstrating state-of-the-art results, particularly with limited training data. They also provide an insightful analysis of the representations learned by both their hard attention model and soft attention models.

Main contributions:
1. A novel hard attention model that explicitly handles nearly-monotonic alignments in morphological inflection, which is a significant departure from previous approaches that either used soft attention or jointly learned alignment and transduction.
2. Demonstrated effectiveness across multiple datasets, establishing a new state of the art on three benchmarks, with particularly impressive results on the small CELEX dataset where previous neural models underperformed.
3. An analysis of the representations learned by both hard and soft attention models, revealing how they encode linguistic features like symbol type and positional information.

Strengths:
1. The paper effectively addresses a critical limitation of previous neural sequence-to-sequence models for morphological inflection: their tendency to overfit with limited training data. The authors' hard attention mechanism, which leverages the inherent monotonic alignment in the task, significantly outperforms prior neural models on the CELEX dataset (500 training examples per inflection type), surpassing the latent variable WFST model by Dreyer et al. (2008) for the first time with a neural approach. This represents a substantial contribution to the field, particularly for morphologically rich languages with data sparsity challenges.

2. The model demonstrates robust performance across different data scales, achieving the highest average accuracy on the Wiktionary dataset (with up to 360k training examples) compared to previous neural and non-neural baselines. This versatility across low-resource and high-resource settings strengthens the case for the proposed approach.

3. The analysis of learned representations provides valuable insights into how both hard and soft attention models encode linguistic features. The authors show that the hard attention model encodes positional information less explicitly in input representations (since it uses step actions for this purpose), which may explain its better performance with limited data. This analysis is particularly insightful and contributes to our understanding of how neural models process morphological information.

Weaknesses:
1. The paper doesn't sufficiently address the model's limitations for languages with complex morphological phenomena requiring long-range dependencies (e.g., vowel harmony, consonant harmony). While the SIGMORPHON results show the model performs slightly worse than soft attention models on these languages, the authors don't thoroughly explain why this occurs or provide concrete suggestions for addressing these limitations, which would strengthen the paper's claims about the model's applicability.

2. The paper lacks a discussion of practical advantages beyond accuracy, such as computational efficiency or ease of implementation. While the training procedure is simpler (using cross-entropy loss instead of joint alignment learning), the authors don't quantify how this affects training time, memory requirements, or implementation complexity. These practical considerations are important for adoption in real-world applications.

3. The comparison with soft attention models is somewhat limited. The authors note their soft attention model outperformed MED due to longer training (100 epochs vs. 20 epochs), but don't address whether the hard attention model could benefit from similar training adjustments. This creates ambiguity about whether the hard attention model's advantages are inherent to its architecture or could be replicated with better training of soft attention models.

Questions to Authors:
1. Could you provide more details about the computational cost and training time of your model compared to soft attention models? Specifically, how does the simpler training procedure (using cross-entropy loss) affect overall training time and resource requirements?

2. The paper shows the hard attention model performs slightly worse on languages with vowel/consonant harmony (e.g., Hungarian, Arabic). Could you elaborate on the linguistic characteristics that make these languages challenging for the hard attention mechanism, and whether there are potential modifications to the model that could address these limitations?

3. The analysis in Figure 4 suggests the soft attention model encodes positional information more strongly in input representations. How does this affect the model's ability to generalize to unseen word forms, and does this have implications for the model's performance with limited training data?
