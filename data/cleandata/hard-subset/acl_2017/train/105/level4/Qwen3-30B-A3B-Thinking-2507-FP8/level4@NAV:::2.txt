

Strengths: A novel encoder-decoder model is proposed that explicitly incorporates monotonicity constraints.  
Weaknesses: The model may simply be an ordinary BiRNN with alignments decoupled from transduction. Evaluation is limited to morphology tasks, with no testing on other monotone sequence-to-sequence applications.  

General Discussion:  
The authors introduce a novel encoder-decoder architecture featuring "hard monotonic attention," evaluated on three morphology datasets. While well-written and clear, presenting a novel idea for enforcing monotonicity in morphology tasks, the paper requires clarification. Monotonicity is justified as a fundamental property of many sequence-to-sequence tasks (unlike machine translation), making general encoder-decoder models suboptimal. The core contribution—explicitly enforcing monotonic output character generation via decoupled alignment and transduction—lacks precision. Key questions:  
1) Alignment type: The paper describes both 1-to-many (e.g., Fig. 1: "f-l-o-g" → "f-l-ie-ge") and many-to-1 (lines 311–312: multiple inputs to one output). Clarify whether alignments are 1-to-many, many-to-1, or many-to-many.  
2) Baseline comparison: A simpler monotone approach exists: (a) monotone align input/output sequences (e.g., using Jiampojamarn & Kondrak’s toolkit), then (b) train a standard sequence tagger (e.g., BiLSTM) to predict the aligned output sequence. This avoids general encoder-decoder models and maintains monotonicity inherently.  
   - 2a) How does the proposed method differ from this baseline?  
   - 2b) Why was this baseline excluded?  
3) Narrow scope: Evaluation on only morphology tasks limits impact. Monotonic sequence-to-sequence applications (e.g., spelling correction, phoneme-to-grapheme) could better demonstrate the model’s value.  
4) "On par or better" (line 791): This phrasing conflates worse performance with "on par." Recommend precise wording.  
5) Linguistic features:  
   - 5a) Source of features (e.g., POS in Fig. 1)?  
   - 5b) Could features—not monotonicity—explain performance gains?  

Minor points:  
6) Equation (3): Replace $NN$ with $\text{NN}$.  
7) Line 231: "Where" → lowercase.  
8) Lines 237+ ($x1\ldots xn$): Use $x_1,\ldots,x_n$ (comma and centered dots).

AFTER AUTHOR RESPONSE  
Clarifications address alignment mechanics, but the core baseline question remains: Why not compare to Schnober et al. (2016)’s 1-to-many alignment + neural tagger approach? A tagger-based baseline would better isolate monotonicity’s contribution versus a constrained general model.
