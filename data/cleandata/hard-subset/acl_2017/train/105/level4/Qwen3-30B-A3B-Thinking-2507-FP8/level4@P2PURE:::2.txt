

Strengths: The paper proposes a novel encoder-decoder model that explicitly incorporates monotonicity constraints.  
Weaknesses: The model may merely be an ordinary BiRNN with alignments decoupled, and the evaluation is limited to morphology tasks without testing on other monotonic sequence-to-sequence applications.  

General Discussion:  
The authors introduce a novel encoder-decoder architecture featuring "hard monotonic attention," evaluated on three morphology datasets. While well-written and clear, the paper presents a compelling rationale for monotonicity in sequence-to-sequence tasks: unlike machine translation, many such tasks (e.g., morphology) are inherently monotonic, making standard encoder-decoder models suboptimal. The authors’ approach enforces explicit monotonic output generation by decoupling alignment and transduction—first establishing monotonic input-output alignments, then training the model to generate outputs consistent with these alignments. However, the methodology requires clarification:  
1) Alignment types are ambiguous. The running example (Fig. 1) suggests 1-to-many alignments (e.g., one input character aligns with zero, one, or multiple output characters), yet lines 311–312 describe "several input characters aligned to one output character," implying many-to-one. Are alignments 1-to-many, many-to-one, or many-to-many?  
2) A simpler monotonic sequence-to-sequence approach exists:  
   a) First align input and output sequences monotonically (1-to-many) using a standard monotone aligner (e.g., Jiampojamarn and Kondrak’s toolkit).  
   b) Train a standard sequence tagger (e.g., BiLSTM) to predict the aligned output sequence from the input (e.g., align "flog" → "f-l-ie-ge" and train a tagger to predict "f-l-ie-ge" from "f-l-o-g"). This method, referenced in Schnober et al. (2016, Section 4.2), would inherently preserve monotonicity.  
   2a) How does the proposed method differ from this approach?  
   2b) Why was this baseline not included?  
3) The evaluation is limited to morphology. Monotonic sequence-to-sequence tasks (e.g., phonetic transcription, spelling correction) offer richer opportunities to demonstrate the model’s advantages, which could have strengthened the contribution.  
4) The claim of "on par or better" (line 791) reflects a common cognitive bias where underperformance is labeled "on par" and all other results as "better." This phrasing should be revised for precision.  
5) Linguistic features (e.g., POS in Fig. 1) are under-specified:  
   5a) Where were these features sourced?  
   5b) Could they explain performance gains rather than monotonicity constraints?  

Minor Points:  
6) Equation (3): Replace $NN$ with $\text{NN}$.  
7) Line 231: "Where" should be lowercase.  
8) Lines 237 and others: Use $x_1, \ldots, x_n$ (not $x1\ldots xn$) per mathematical conventions.  
9) Figure 1: Cyrillic font renders examples unreadable; use standard Latin characters.  
10) Line 437: "these" should be "these."  

[*] Schnober et al. (2016), *Still not there? Comparing Traditional Sequence-to-Sequence Models to Encoder-Decoder Neural Networks on Monotone String Translation Tasks*, COLING 2016.
