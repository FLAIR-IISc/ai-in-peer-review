1. Proposes a hard-attention neural model that exploits nearly-monotonic alignments for morphological inflection.  
2. Evaluated on CELEX, Wiktionary, and SIGMORPHON 2016, achieving state-of-the-art results, especially with limited data.  
3. Provides analysis comparing representations learned by hard and soft attention, showing encoding of symbol type and positional info.  
4. Novelty: explicit hard-attention handling of nearly-monotonic alignments versus prior soft-attention or joint alignment-transduction methods.  
5. Empirical contribution: new SOTA on three benchmarks and strong performance on small CELEX where prior neural models underperformed.  
6. Analytical contribution: insights into how hard vs soft attention encode linguistic features and position.  
7. Strength: reduces overfitting in low-data regimes, outperforming prior neural and WFST models on CELEX (500 examples).  
8. Strength: robust across data scales, achieving highest average accuracy on the large Wiktionary dataset.  
9. Strength: representation analysis suggests hard attention encodes position via step actions, aiding low-data generalization.  
10. Weakness: insufficient analysis of limitations on languages with long-range dependencies (e.g., vowel/consonant harmony).  
11. Weakness: lacks quantitative discussion of practical advantages like training time, memory, and implementation complexity.  
12. Weakness: comparison to soft attention is limited and may conflate architectural benefits with training-regime differences.  
13. Question: request for comparative computational cost and training-time details versus soft-attention models.  
14. Question: ask for explanation of why harmony languages are challenging and potential model modifications to address this.  
15. Question: ask how stronger positional encoding in soft attention affects generalization to unseen forms and low-data performance.
