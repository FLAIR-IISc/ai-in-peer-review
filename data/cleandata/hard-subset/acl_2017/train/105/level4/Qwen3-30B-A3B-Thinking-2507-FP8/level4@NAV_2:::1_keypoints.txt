1. Proposes a hard monotonic attention neural model that explicitly models nearly-monotonic character alignments for morphological inflection.  
2. Main contributions: a novel hard-attention architecture, state-of-the-art results on three benchmarks (notably on small data), and a comparative analysis of hard vs. soft attention representations.  
3. Hard attention removes the need to jointly learn alignment and transduction, improving performance on small datasets (CELEX) and reducing overfitting seen in soft attention.  
4. Comprehensive experiments on CELEX, Wiktionary, and SIGMORPHON demonstrate versatility and strong accuracy, especially on Wiktionary and suffixing/stem-change languages (Russian, German, Spanish).  
5. Representation analysis shows hard attention encodes positional information less explicitly than soft attention, explaining its data efficiency and providing theoretical insight.  
6. Performance is limited to languages with predominantly monotonic alignment and minimal context sensitivity, underperforming on harmony languages (Arabic, Hungarian).  
7. Relies on externally precomputed character alignments (Sudoh et al., 2013), introducing a dependency not present in joint-learning approaches.  
8. Paper omits comparison of computational cost and implementation complexity versus competing methods, weakening practical contribution.  
9. Does not achieve best results on SIGMORPHON 2016 (soft attention slightly outperforms it) and fails to explain these language-specific failures.  
10. Question: What are the training/inference computational costs (including alignment precomputation) compared to soft attention baselines?
