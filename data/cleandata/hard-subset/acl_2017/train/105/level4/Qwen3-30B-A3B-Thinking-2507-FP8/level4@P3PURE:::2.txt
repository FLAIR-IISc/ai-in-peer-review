

Strengths: The paper proposes a novel encoder-decoder architecture incorporating "hard monotonic attention," addressing an important gap in sequence-to-sequence modeling for monotonic tasks. The writing is clear and the core idea—explicitly enforcing monotonicity in morphological generation—is well-motivated.

Weaknesses: The proposed model appears to reduce to a standard BiRNN with alignments decoupled from transduction, lacking significant architectural novelty. Crucially, the evaluation is restricted to morphology tasks, omitting other monotonic sequence-to-sequence applications (e.g., phonological transcription, code generation) where the model’s explicit monotonicity constraint could demonstrate broader utility.

General Discussion: The rationale for monotonicity is sound: many sequence-to-sequence tasks (unlike machine translation) inherently require monotonic output generation, making general-purpose encoders-decoders suboptimal. The paper’s approach—decoupling alignment and transduction by first establishing monotonic alignments and then training output generation to conform to them—is conceptually straightforward. However, key ambiguities and omissions undermine its contribution:

1.  **Alignment Mechanism:** The description (lines 311–312) suggests many-to-one alignments (multiple input characters → one output character), but Figure 1 depicts 1-to-many alignments (one input character → zero, one, or multiple output characters). Clarify whether alignments are 1-to-many, many-to-one, or many-to-many.

2.  **Baseline Comparison:** A simpler, existing approach achieves monotonicity by (a) using a monotone aligner (e.g., Jiampojamarn & Kondrak’s toolkit) to establish 1-to-many alignments, then (b) training a standard sequence tagger (e.g., BiLSTM) to predict the output sequence from the input sequence under these fixed alignments. This method inherently enforces monotonicity without architectural modifications. The paper should:
    -   (2a) Explicitly differentiate its method from this baseline.
    -   (2b) Include this baseline for comparison (as suggested in Schnober et al. (2016), Section 4.2).

3.  **Evaluation Scope:** Limiting evaluation to morphology is a significant limitation. Monotonic sequence-to-sequence tasks abound (e.g., phoneme-to-grapheme conversion, text normalization), and the model’s superiority should be demonstrated on these to validate the core premise.

4.  **Result Presentation:** The phrase "on par or better" (line 791) risks conflating "on par" with "better" due to common cognitive biases in result reporting. Use precise phrasing (e.g., "comparable or superior").

5.  **Linguistic Features:** Figure 1 implies the use of POS and similar features, but their source and potential confounding effect on results are unaddressed:
    -   (5a) Where are these features sourced from?
    -   (5b) Could they explain performance gains more than monotonicity constraints?

Author Response Note: The clarification on alignment (1-0, 0-1, 1-1, then deriving many-to-many) appears inconsistent with the description. The key question remains: Why not compare against the simpler baseline of monotone alignment + sequence tagger (Schnober et al., 2016), which is inherently monotonic and would likely yield comparable results? This baseline would better isolate the contribution of the proposed architecture versus the monotonicity constraint.
