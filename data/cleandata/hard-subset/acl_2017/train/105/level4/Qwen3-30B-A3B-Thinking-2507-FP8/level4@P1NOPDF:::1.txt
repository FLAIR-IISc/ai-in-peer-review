

Strengths:  
The paper introduces a novel approach using *hard monotonic attention* for morphological inflection generation, which represents a substantial departure from prior work. This conceptual shift is well-motivated and clearly articulated.

Weaknesses:  
The experimental results present mixed outcomes. While the model demonstrates clear advantages with limited training data (e.g., CELEX), its efficacy diminishes when alignment is non-monotonic or context-sensitive. Specifically, the method excels for languages like Russian, German, and Spanish—where alignments are predominantly monotonic and context demands are low—but shows less consistent gains in more complex cases. This limitation should be explicitly acknowledged, as it restricts the method’s general applicability.

General Discussion:  
The authors propose a novel neural model for morphological inflection generation that employs *hard attention*, with character alignments precomputed via a Bayesian alignment method (not transliteration, as stated in the draft). This contrasts sharply with prior state-of-the-art approaches that use *soft attention*, where alignment and conversion are jointly optimized in a probabilistic framework. The core idea is both novel and theoretically sound. The paper is well-structured and the experiments are thorough. However, the method does not consistently achieve state-of-the-art results across all conditions. It is particularly suited for tasks with predominantly monotonic alignments and minimal context sensitivity, but its limitations in more challenging scenarios warrant stronger discussion. To bolster the contribution, the authors should clarify the practical advantages of their approach—such as implementation simplicity, training efficiency, or computational cost savings—compared to soft-attention alternatives. Without this, the paper’s broader impact remains underemphasized.
