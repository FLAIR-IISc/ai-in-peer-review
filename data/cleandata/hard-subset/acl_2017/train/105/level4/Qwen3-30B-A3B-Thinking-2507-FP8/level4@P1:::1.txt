

**Summary and Contributions**  
The authors propose a neural model for morphological inflection generation that employs a hard attention mechanism to directly model nearly-monotonic character alignments between source and target words. The key contributions, as I see them, are:  
1. A novel hard attention mechanism that explicitly models monotonic character alignments without joint learning of alignment and transduction, significantly improving performance on small datasets where neural models typically struggle.  
2. State-of-the-art results on the CELEX dataset (with only 500 training examples per inflection type), where the model outperforms both previous neural approaches and the latent-variable WFST model by Dreyer et al. (2008).  
3. A detailed analysis of how both hard and soft attention models learn representations, revealing that the hard attention model encodes positional information less explicitly in the input representations, which may contribute to its robustness with limited data.  

**Strengths**  
1. The model's performance on small datasets is a major contribution. On CELEX, it achieves 89.44% accuracy—significantly outperforming the previous neural state-of-the-art (87.65%) and surpassing the latent-variable WFST model (88.3%) for the first time with a neural approach. This addresses a critical limitation of neural models in low-resource settings, where they typically underperform compared to traditional FST-based methods.  

2. The analysis of learned alignments and representations is insightful and well-executed. The authors demonstrate that both hard and soft attention models learn monotonic alignments, but the hard attention model's representations are more sensitive to character identity and less to position (as shown in Figures 4c–4d). This explains why the hard model generalizes better with limited data, as positional information is explicitly handled via step actions rather than encoded in input representations.  

3. The training procedure is notably simpler and more efficient than joint alignment-transduction models. By precomputing alignments (using Sudoh et al.'s Bayesian method) and training with cross-entropy loss, the authors avoid the complexity of marginalizing over alignments during training, as in Bahdanau et al. (2014) or Yu et al. (2016). This makes implementation straightforward and reduces the risk of training instability.  

**Weaknesses**  
1. The paper overstates the generality of the approach. While the model excels on languages with monotonic suffixing (e.g., Russian, German, Spanish), it underperforms on languages with complex phenomena like vowel harmony (e.g., Turkish, Hungarian) where soft attention models dominate (Table 3). The authors acknowledge this but fail to discuss *why* monotonic alignment is insufficient for these cases or how to adapt the model. This limits the method's perceived applicability.  

2. The argument for hard attention's necessity is weak. The analysis (Figure 3) shows that soft attention models also learn monotonic alignments, and the authors admit that "the soft attention model performs similarly to the hard attention model on the train-set" (Figure 2). If both models learn similar alignments, why is hard attention beneficial? The paper doesn't convincingly demonstrate that the hard mechanism is fundamentally better than a soft model with a monotonic bias.  

3. The paper lacks practical discussion of implementation and computational costs. While the authors note the model is "simpler," they don't quantify training time, memory usage, or inference speed compared to soft attention baselines. This omission is significant for practitioners who must weigh theoretical advantages against real-world deployment constraints.  

**Questions to Authors**  
1. The paper claims the hard attention model is the "first neural model to surpass the latent variable model on CELEX." However, Rastogi et al. (2016) achieved similar results with 2x more data. Can the authors clarify why their model succeeds with the *original* CELEX data size, while Rastogi's did not?  

2. The analysis (Figure 4) shows soft attention encodes positional information more strongly. Is this a *cause* of its overfitting on small datasets (Figure 2), or merely a *correlation*? Could a soft model with explicitly encoded positional features (e.g., via positional embeddings) match the hard model's performance on CELEX?  

3. For languages with non-monotonic alignments (e.g., Turkish), would adding a mechanism to handle non-monotonicity (e.g., via a soft attention module) make the model more versatile, or would it undermine the core simplicity of the approach?
