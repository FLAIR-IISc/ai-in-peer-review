

Summary: The paper presents a neural model for morphological inflection generation that employs a hard attention mechanism designed to exploit the nearly-monotonic alignment between input and output character sequences. The main contributions as I see them are: (1) A hard attention model that explicitly accounts for monotonic alignments common in morphological inflection, (2) State-of-the-art results on three morphological inflection datasets, particularly with small training sets, and (3) An analysis comparing the representations learned by hard and soft attention models, revealing how they encode features like symbol type and sequence position.

Strengths: 
1. The model demonstrates significant improvements over previous approaches on the CELEX dataset, which is particularly noteworthy as it's the first neural model to surpass the performance of the latent variable WFST model of Dreyer et al. (2008) with such limited training data. This addresses a key limitation of neural sequence-to-sequence models for morphological inflection.
2. The paper provides valuable analysis of the representations learned by both hard and soft attention models, showing that the hard attention model encodes positional information less explicitly than the soft attention model, which explains its better performance with small datasets.
3. The model achieves strong results across three diverse datasets (CELEX, Wiktionary, SIGMORPHON) without requiring significant architectural modifications for different languages or morphological systems.

Weaknesses:
1. The proposed model appears to be a standard bi-directional RNN with alignments decoupled from the transduction process, rather than a fundamentally novel architecture. This makes the novelty claim questionable as the approach seems to be a straightforward application of existing ideas.
2. The evaluation is limited exclusively to morphological inflection tasks, without testing on other monotonic sequence-to-sequence tasks (e.g., abstractive summarization, transliteration) that would better demonstrate the broader applicability of the approach.
3. The paper does not clearly explain the alignment mechanism (1-to-many vs many-to-1 vs many-to-many), creating confusion about how the model handles transformations like "flog→fliege" where one input character maps to multiple output characters.

Questions to Authors:
1. The paper describes alignments where "several input characters are aligned to 1 output character" (l. 311-312), but the examples and Figure 1 suggest a 1-to-many relationship. Please clarify the alignment mechanism and address this discrepancy.
2. Why was the simple baseline of using a monotone aligner (e.g., Jiampojamarn and Kondrak) to create 1-to-many alignments followed by training a standard sequence tagger (e.g., BiLSTM) not included? How would results differ from your approach?
3. Why was the evaluation limited to morphological inflection tasks only? Testing on other monotonic sequence-to-sequence tasks would better demonstrate the general utility of your approach.
