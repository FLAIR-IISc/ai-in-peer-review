- Introduces a novel hard monotonic attention mechanism that departs from prior neural approaches to morphological inflection.  
- Separates character alignment (via a Bayesian transliteration model) from inflection conversion, offering a fresh, well-motivated theoretical approach.  
- Addresses a key limitation of prior work by avoiding conflation of alignment and conversion in probabilistic modeling.  
- Empirical results are mixed: strong performance in low-resource settings (e.g., CELEX) and languages with predominantly monotonic alignments (e.g., Russian, German, Spanish).  
- Performance is inconsistent for languages and datasets with non-monotonic or context-sensitive alignments (e.g., Turkish, Finnish), weakening claims of broad superiority.  
- Paper is well-structured, clearly written, and provides a comprehensive empirical evaluation.  
- Authors should explicitly compare practical advantages (computational efficiency, training stability, implementation simplicity) against soft attention models.  
- Needs a more nuanced discussion of limitations, especially for context-dependent inflection patterns, to avoid overstating generality.  
- To strengthen claims of broad relevance, the authors should either broaden experimental scope or clarify the precise conditions under which the approach is optimal.  
- Overall, promising contribution that would benefit from sharper articulation of scope and tangible practical benefits.
