

Strengths: The paper proposes a novel encoder-decoder architecture incorporating "hard monotonic attention," addressing monotonicity—a key characteristic in tasks like morphology where input-output sequence alignment must be strictly ordered. The writing is clear and the core idea is well-motivated.

Weaknesses:  
1. **Model novelty and baseline comparison**: The core architecture appears to be a standard BiRNN with decoupled alignment and generation. Crucially, the paper fails to compare against a simple yet effective baseline: first generating monotonic 1-to-many alignments (e.g., using Jiampojamarn & Kondrak’s toolkit), then training a sequence tagger (e.g., BiLSTM) on these alignments to predict output sequences. This approach inherently enforces monotonicity and would serve as a stronger baseline than the authors’ method, which starts with a general model and imposes constraints. The authors’ description of alignments is inconsistent—Fig. 1 shows 1-to-many (e.g., "g"→"ge"), but lines 311–312 imply many-to-1 (e.g., multiple inputs to one output). Clarification is needed on the alignment type(s) used.  
2. **Limited evaluation scope**: The model is evaluated *only* on morphology datasets. Monotonicity is relevant to numerous other tasks (e.g., phonetic transcription, code generation, or phoneme-to-sound conversion), where the authors could have demonstrated clear advantages. The narrow scope weakens the paper’s contribution.  
3. **Ambiguous performance claims**: The phrase "on par or better" (line 791) risks misrepresenting results. Where performance is worse, it is labeled "on par"; only improvements are called "better." This phrasing should be revised for precision.  

General Discussion:  
The paper’s premise—that monotonicity is inherent to many sequence-to-sequence tasks, making standard encoders-decoders suboptimal—is compelling. However, the proposed solution lacks sufficient novelty or rigor. The alignment mechanism’s inconsistency undermines the technical contribution, and the absence of a strong baseline (e.g., the sequence-tagging approach described above) makes it difficult to assess whether the monotonicity constraint drives the results. The linguistic features (e.g., POS in Fig. 1) are mentioned but not justified: their source and potential confounding effect on performance (rather than monotonicity) require clarification.  

Minor Issues:

Author Response Follow-up:  
The authors clarified that alignments are derived from 1-to-0, 0-to-1, 1-to-1, and many-to-many combinations. However, the core question remains: *Why not compare against the simpler baseline of using monotonic alignments as input to a sequence tagger?* This approach would inherently preserve monotonicity without modifying a standard encoder-decoder. The authors’ method (starting from a general model and adding constraints) may not offer a clear advantage over this baseline.  

AC Notes Integration:  
The paper’s architecture also relates to methods like Stack LSTM (Cohn et al., 2016), which uses a sequence of actions to transform input. Additionally, the use of greedy alignment (vs. Rastogi et al.’s sum-over-alignments approach) may limit flexibility—this trade-off should be discussed.  

Recommendation:  
**Reject**. The paper’s technical contribution is overstated. The model lacks novelty (appears equivalent to a standard BiRNN with alignment decoupling), the baseline comparison is inadequate, and the evaluation is restricted to morphology without broader relevance. The alignment inconsistency and unaddressed linguistic feature confounds further weaken the claims. While the problem (monotonicity) is valid, the solution fails to demonstrate sufficient innovation or empirical superiority over simpler alternatives. To strengthen the paper, the authors would need to: (1) clarify alignment mechanics; (2) compare against the sequence-tagging baseline; (3) evaluate on additional monotonic tasks; and (4) address the linguistic feature confound. Without these, the contribution is marginal.
