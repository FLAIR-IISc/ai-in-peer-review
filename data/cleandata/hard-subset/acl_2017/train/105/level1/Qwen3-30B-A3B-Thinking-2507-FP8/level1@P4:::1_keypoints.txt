- Proposes a hard-attention neural model that explicitly models nearly-monotonic alignments for character-level morphological inflection.

- Demonstrates state-of-the-art results on CELEX, Wiktionary, and SIGMORPHON 2016, with notable gains on small training sets.

- Main contributions: a hard-attention model for nearly-monotonic seq2seq, SOTA performance across datasets (including CELEX), and analysis of learned representations.

- Significant improvement on small datasets: first neural model to beat a latent-variable WFST on CELEX (89.44% vs. 88.3%, p<0.05).

- Thorough experimental evaluation: comparisons across three datasets of varying sizes with strong neural and non-neural baselines, achieving highest average accuracy on Wiktionary.

- Insightful representation analysis: SVD study shows hard attention encodes position less explicitly than soft attention, possibly explaining better small-data performance.

- Limited novelty: hard attention resembles RNN Transducer / Neural Transducer, and the paper insufficiently differentiates or theoretically justifies the independent-alignment choice.

- Lack of theoretical justification: paper attributes better small-data generalization to monotonicity but provides no detailed theoretical explanation for reduced overfitting.

- Limited analysis of limitations: acknowledges poorer performance on languages with phenomena like vowel harmony but doesn't analyze causes or possible adaptations.

- How does the model compare empirically (accuracy, training time, complexity) to RNN Transducer (Graves 2012) and Neural Transducer (Jaitly et al. 2015)?

- Can the authors provide a theoretical analysis explaining why hard attention with monotonic alignment reduces overfitting on small datasets versus soft attention?

- What specific modifications would enable the model to handle non-monotonic morphological phenomena (e.g., vowel harmony), and how would those changes affect performance?
