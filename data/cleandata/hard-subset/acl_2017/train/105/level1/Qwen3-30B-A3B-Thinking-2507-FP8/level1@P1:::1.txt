

# Peer Review: Morphological Inflection Generation with Hard Monotonic Attention

## Summary and Main Contributions

This paper proposes a neural model for morphological inflection generation that employs a hard attention mechanism with a step control mechanism designed to enforce monotonic alignment between input and output character sequences. The authors evaluate their model on three datasets: CELEX (small), Wiktionary (larger), and SIGMORPHON 2016 (multi-language). The paper claims three main contributions: (1) a hard attention model for nearly-monotonic sequence-to-sequence learning, (2) state-of-the-art results on three morphological inflection datasets, and (3) an analysis comparing hard and soft attention models.

As a reviewer, I see the paper's strongest contributions as:
1. The hard attention mechanism with step control that effectively enforces the monotonic alignment property common in morphological inflection, leading to significantly better performance on small datasets compared to previous neural approaches.
2. The empirical demonstration that this model achieves state-of-the-art results on three datasets, being the first neural model to surpass the latent variable FST model on the CELEX dataset (89.44% vs. 88.3%).
3. The insightful analysis comparing hard and soft attention models, revealing how they encode linguistic features differently despite similar alignment patterns.

## Strengths

1. **Practical impact on small-data scenarios**: The paper's most compelling contribution is addressing a critical NLP problem - effective morphological inflection generation with limited training data. The CELEX results (Table 1) are particularly significant, showing the hard attention model substantially outperforms all previous neural approaches (89.44% vs. 87.65% for NWFST) and surpasses the latent variable FST model (88.3%) by a clear margin. This is important because many morphologically rich languages have limited training data, making this a valuable contribution to real-world NLP applications.

2. **Strong theoretical motivation**: The paper correctly identifies that morphological inflection typically exhibits nearly-monotonic character alignments, which the hard attention mechanism directly models. The model architecture is well-justified by the linguistic properties of the task, making it more principled than approaches that simply apply standard sequence-to-sequence models without considering the task-specific alignment properties.

3. **Insightful representation analysis**: The SVD-based analysis of learned representations (Figure 4) provides valuable insights into what neural models learn for morphological inflection. The paper demonstrates that the hard attention model encodes positional information less strongly than the soft attention model, which explains its better performance on small datasets. This analysis goes beyond mere accuracy reporting and contributes to our understanding of neural sequence models for linguistic tasks.

## Weaknesses

1. **Limited novelty justification**: The paper's key innovation (hard attention with step control) is not sufficiently distinguished from prior work on monotonic alignment models. Graves (2012) proposed the RNN Transducer with monotonic alignment, and Yu et al. (2016) improved it. The paper mentions these but doesn't clearly articulate how their approach differs (using independently learned alignments rather than joint learning) as a significant novelty. Without this clarification, the contribution appears incremental rather than groundbreaking.

2. **Inadequate comparison to latent variable FST**: The paper claims to be the first neural model to surpass Dreyer et al.'s (2008) latent variable FST model on CELEX, but the comparison lacks important context. The latent variable FST requires expert linguistic knowledge to set up, while the neural model is purely data-driven. The paper should address this trade-off between expert knowledge and data requirements to make the comparison meaningful and valuable for practitioners.

3. **Superficial analysis of SIGMORPHON results**: The paper attributes the hard attention model's poorer performance on some SIGMORPHON languages (e.g., Arabic, Navajo) to linguistic typology (vowel/consonant harmony), but provides no detailed linguistic analysis to support this claim. The paper should provide specific examples from these languages to demonstrate why the monotonic alignment assumption fails, and what aspects of the model would need to change to handle such cases.

## Questions to Authors

1. The paper states that your model uses "independently learned character-level alignments" but doesn't clarify how these alignments are obtained. Could you elaborate on the Sudoh et al. (2013) alignment model used for training? Specifically, how does it handle cases where multiple alignments are equally plausible?

2. On the SIGMORPHON dataset, your model performs worse than soft attention on languages with vowel harmony (e.g., Arabic). Could you provide examples where the hard attention model fails, and explain why the monotonic alignment assumption breaks down in these cases? How might the model be modified to handle such languages?

3. The paper mentions that "the hard attention model tends to have much more isolated character clusters" (Figure 4). Could you elaborate on what this means linguistically? Does this indicate that the model is learning more distinct morphological features, or is it simply overfitting to the specific training examples?

Overall, this is a strong paper with significant practical contributions to morphological inflection generation, particularly for small-data scenarios. With clearer articulation of the novelty and more detailed analysis of the SIGMORPHON results, it would be an excellent contribution to ACL.
