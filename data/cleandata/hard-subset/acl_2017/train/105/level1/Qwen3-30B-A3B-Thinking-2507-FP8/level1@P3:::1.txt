

# Peer Review

## Summary and Contributions
This paper proposes a neural model for morphological inflection generation that uses a "hard monotonic attention" mechanism, inspired by the nearly-monotonic alignment between input and output character sequences. The authors claim three contributions: a new hard attention model for nearly-monotonic sequence-to-sequence learning, state-of-the-art results on three morphological inflection datasets, and an analysis comparing their model with soft attention models.

As a reviewer, I see the primary contribution as the model's performance on small training sets (CELEX), which addresses a real limitation in morphological inflection research. The secondary contribution is the insightful analysis of learned representations, revealing how both hard and soft attention models encode linguistic features. The hard attention mechanism itself is not entirely novel, though its application to morphological inflection is interesting.

## Strengths

1. **Significant performance improvement on small datasets**: The paper demonstrates a clear and meaningful advantage on the CELEX dataset (the smallest training set), where the authors' model outperforms both neural and non-neural baselines. This is particularly valuable since morphological inflection often suffers from data sparsity, and this is the first neural model to surpass the latent variable WFST model on this dataset. The learning curves in Figure 2 provide strong evidence that the hard attention mechanism prevents overfitting on small datasets.

2. **Insightful representation analysis**: The analysis of learned representations (Figures 4a-d) is a strong point, revealing how the hard attention model encodes position information differently than the soft attention model. The observation that the hard attention model encodes positional information less strongly (since it uses step actions to track position) is a valuable insight into the model's behavior and explains its robustness on small datasets.

3. **Robustness across multiple datasets**: The model achieves state-of-the-art results on the Wiktionary dataset and performs competitively on SIGMORPHON 2016, demonstrating that the approach is not limited to small datasets. The analysis of language typology (Table 3) shows the model's performance varies predictably based on morphological properties, which is an important finding.

## Weaknesses

1. **Limited novelty of the hard attention mechanism**: The paper fails to sufficiently distinguish its approach from prior work. The RNN Transducer (Graves, 2012) and Neural Transducer (Jaitly et al., 2015) both use similar hard alignment mechanisms, and the paper doesn't adequately explain how their model differs or improves upon these. The claim that "this modeling suits the natural monotonic alignment" is not novel, as Graves (2012) already made this argument for speech recognition.

2. **Insufficient ablation studies**: The paper doesn't provide ablation studies to isolate why the hard attention mechanism works better than soft attention. For instance, they don't test a soft attention model with the same training procedure (pre-computed alignments) or compare a hard attention model without the bi-directional encoder. The claim that "the hard attention mechanism prevents overfitting" needs stronger evidence than just comparing to a different model architecture.

3. **Inadequate comparison with relevant baselines**: The paper compares to MED (Kann and Schütze, 2016a) as the soft attention baseline, but MED was specifically designed for morphological inflection. The authors don't compare to other soft attention models that might have been optimized for this task, nor do they address why their implementation of soft attention (which they claim is similar to MED) performs better.

4. **Lack of theoretical justification**: The paper doesn't provide a theoretical argument for why monotonic alignment should be enforced in morphological inflection. While the paper mentions that "nearly-monotonic alignment is commonly found," it doesn't provide evidence that this is true for all morphological systems, which would strengthen the case for their approach.

## Questions to Authors

1. The paper claims their model is the first neural model to surpass the latent variable WFST model on CELEX. Can you clarify why your model achieves this while other neural models (like Rastogi et al., 2016) did not? Is it specifically the hard attention mechanism, or are other architectural choices responsible?

2. In the analysis section, you claim your model "does not encode position information as strongly" because it uses step actions. However, the bi-directional encoder still encodes positional information. Can you clarify how the step actions specifically reduce the need for position encoding in the input representations?

3. The paper mentions that "the hard attention model is much simpler" than soft attention models. Can you quantify this difference in terms of parameters, training time, or inference speed? This would be valuable for practitioners considering implementation.

4. The SIGMORPHON results show your model performs worse than soft attention on some languages (e.g., Turkish). Given that Turkish has vowel harmony requiring long-range dependencies, how does this contradict your claim that "the soft attention model's tendency to overfit on the small dataset" is the reason for your model's advantage on CELEX?
