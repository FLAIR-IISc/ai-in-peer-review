

# Review for ACL 2017

## Summary and Contributions
This paper proposes a neural model for morphological inflection generation using a hard attention mechanism that explicitly models nearly-monotonic alignments between input and output character sequences. The authors demonstrate state-of-the-art results on three standard morphological inflection datasets (CELEX, Wiktionary, and SIGMORPHON 2016), particularly showing significant advantages with small training sets where previous neural models struggled. The primary contributions are: (1) a hard attention model designed for nearly-monotonic sequence-to-sequence learning in morphological inflection; (2) state-of-the-art performance across multiple datasets, including surpassing previous neural and non-neural approaches on the small CELEX dataset; and (3) an insightful analysis of the representations learned by both hard and soft attention models.

## Strengths
1. **Significant performance improvement on small datasets**: The paper demonstrates that the hard attention model achieves the first neural model to surpass the latent variable WFST model (Dreyer et al., 2008) on the CELEX dataset, which has only 500 training examples per inflection type. The model achieves 89.44% accuracy compared to the previous best of 88.3%, a statistically significant improvement (p<0.05). This addresses a well-known limitation of neural sequence-to-sequence models for morphological inflection, which typically require large training sets to perform well.

2. **Thorough experimental evaluation**: The paper provides comprehensive results across three different datasets with varying data sizes (CELEX: very small, Wiktionary: large, SIGMORPHON 2016: medium). The model consistently outperforms previous neural and non-neural baselines, achieving the highest average accuracy (96.55%) on the Wiktionary dataset and showing competitive performance on SIGMORPHON 2016. The comparison includes multiple strong baselines from both neural and non-neural approaches, providing a fair assessment of the model's capabilities.

3. **Insightful representation analysis**: The authors conduct a valuable analysis of the representations learned by both hard and soft attention models using SVD dimensionality reduction. The analysis reveals that the hard attention model encodes position information less explicitly than the soft attention model, which may explain its better performance on small datasets. This provides theoretical insights into how different attention mechanisms encode linguistic features for morphological inflection, going beyond the typical accuracy-focused evaluations.

## Weaknesses
1. **Limited novelty of the hard attention mechanism**: While the authors claim their model is novel, the hard attention mechanism bears strong similarities to the RNN Transducer (Graves, 2012) and Neural Transducer (Jaitly et al., 2015). The paper doesn't sufficiently differentiate their approach from these earlier works beyond empirical results. The key distinction (learning alignments independently rather than jointly) is not theoretically justified, and the paper doesn't provide a clear explanation of why this approach is better than joint learning beyond the experimental results.

2. **Lack of theoretical justification for better performance on small datasets**: The paper attributes the hard attention model's better performance on small datasets to the monotonic alignment assumption, but doesn't provide a detailed theoretical analysis of why this assumption reduces overfitting. The learning curves in Figure 2 show the hard attention model generalizes better, but the paper doesn't explain why this is the case from a theoretical perspective, which limits the broader understanding of the model's advantages.

3. **Limited analysis of model limitations**: The paper acknowledges that the model performs worse on languages with complex morphological phenomena like vowel harmony, but doesn't provide a thorough analysis of why this is the case or how the model could be adapted for such languages. This limits the generalizability of the approach and leaves open questions about the scope of the model's applicability.

## Questions to Authors
1. How does your model's performance compare to the RNN Transducer (Graves, 2012) and Neural Transducer (Jaitly et al., 2015) on the same datasets, particularly regarding training time and model complexity?

2. Can you provide a more detailed theoretical analysis of how the hard attention mechanism with monotonic alignment assumption specifically reduces overfitting on small datasets compared to soft attention models?

3. What specific modifications would be needed to extend your model to handle languages with non-monotonic morphological phenomena like vowel harmony, and how would these modifications affect the model's performance?
