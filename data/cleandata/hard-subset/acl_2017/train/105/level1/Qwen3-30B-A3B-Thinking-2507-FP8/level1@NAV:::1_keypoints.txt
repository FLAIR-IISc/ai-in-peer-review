- Neural model for morphological inflection using hard attention for near-monotonic character alignments; first neural approach to beat Dreyer et al. (2008) on small CELEX data.  
- Empirical validation on CELEX, Wiktionary, and SIGMORPHON shows consistent improvements, especially with small training sets.  
- Analysis compares learned representations for hard vs soft attention, revealing different positional encoding strategies.  
- Addresses neural models' data hunger by achieving strong performance on very small datasets (500 examples), useful for low-resource morphologically rich languages.  
- Comprehensive experiments across three datasets; hard attention outperforms soft attention on suffixing+stem-change languages (Russian, German, Spanish).  
- Representation analysis provides insight into how morphological features are encoded and why hard attention helps with limited data.  
- Novelty claim is weak: proposed hard attention resembles RNN Transducer (Graves, 2012) and Neural Transducer (Jaitly et al., 2015) with limited differentiation.  
- Theoretical justification for assuming monotonic alignments is underdeveloped and primarily empirical.  
- Acknowledged limitations on vowel/consonant harmony languages lack sufficient analysis or proposed solutions.  
- How exactly does this model differ from Graves (2012) RNN Transducer and Jaitly et al. (2015) Neural Transducer?  
- Why does the model underperform on languages with vowel harmony, and what architectural modifications might address this?  
- How does reduced positional encoding in hard attention specifically affect performance on different morphological phenomena?
