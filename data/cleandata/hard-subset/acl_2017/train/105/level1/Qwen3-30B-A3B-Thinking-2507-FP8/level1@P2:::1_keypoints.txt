- Proposes a hard attention model exploiting nearly-monotonic character alignments for morphological inflection generation.  
- Claims three contributions: hard monotonic attention, state-of-the-art results on three datasets, and comparison/analysis with soft attention.  
- Main empirical win is surpassing previous models on CELEX, outperforming both neural and the non-neural WFST (LAT) with only 500 examples per type.  
- Model design is well-motivated by nearly-monotonic morphology and uses step operations to leverage alignment structure.  
- Demonstrates strong empirical results on small datasets, notably meaningful improvements on CELEX versus prior neural and WFST models.  
- Analysis of learned representations shows hard attention encodes positional information less strongly and generalizes better with limited data.  
- Core approach has limited novelty given similarity to Graves (2012) and Yu et al. (2016); distinction of using pre-learned alignments isn’t emphasized enough.  
- Paper lacks ablation studies to isolate contributions of hard attention, bi-directional encoder, and step operations.  
- Insufficient analysis of failure cases, especially for languages with complex morphology like vowel harmony.  
- Question: How does this model compare to the RNN Transducer (Graves, 2012) in performance and training complexity?  
- Question: How does the model compare to non-neural WFSTs in training time and practical implementation?  
- Question: Please provide details on the character alignment method used, its robustness to alignment errors, and the effect of such errors on performance.
