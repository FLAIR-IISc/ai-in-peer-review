- Proposes a neural morphological inflection model using a hard monotonic attention mechanism and claims three contributions: new hard-attention model, state-of-the-art results on three datasets, and analysis vs. soft attention.

- Reviewer views the primary contribution as strong performance on small training sets (CELEX) and a secondary contribution as analysis of learned representations; hard attention itself is not novel.

- Strength: Significant performance improvement on the small CELEX dataset, outperforming neural and non-neural baselines and showing reduced overfitting.

- Strength: Insightful analysis showing hard vs. soft attention encode positional information differently, explaining robustness on small data.

- Strength: Robust results across datasets (Wiktionary SOTA, competitive on SIGMORPHON 2016) and predictable variation by language typology.

- Weakness: Limited novelty—similar hard alignment mechanisms exist (e.g., Graves 2012, Jaitly et al. 2015) and paper does not clearly distinguish itself.

- Weakness: Insufficient ablation studies to isolate why hard attention helps (e.g., no soft-attention with precomputed alignments or hard-attention without bidirectional encoder).

- Weakness: Inadequate comparison to relevant baselines—soft-attention comparisons (e.g., MED) may be insufficient or improperly implemented.

- Weakness: Lacks theoretical justification or evidence that nearly-monotonic alignment holds across morphological systems.

- Question: Why does this model surpass the latent-variable WFST on CELEX when other neural models did not—hard attention specifically or other architectural/training choices?

- Question: How exactly do step actions reduce the need for positional encoding given the bidirectional encoder still encodes position?

- Question: Can the authors quantify the claimed simplicity of the hard-attention model in parameters, training time, or inference speed?

- Question: How do worse results on languages with long-range dependencies (e.g., Turkish) reconcile with the claim that soft attention overfits on small datasets as the reason for CELEX gains?
