

Summary and Contributions: The paper introduces a neural model for morphological inflection generation using a hard attention mechanism designed for nearly-monotonic alignments between input and output character sequences. The primary contribution is the model's ability to achieve state-of-the-art performance on small datasets (CELEX), being the first neural approach to surpass the latent-variable FST model by Dreyer et al. (2008) with limited training data. The second contribution is the empirical validation across three datasets (CELEX, Wiktionary, SIGMORPHON) demonstrating consistent improvements over previous neural and non-neural approaches, particularly with small training sets. The third contribution is the insightful analysis comparing learned representations between hard and soft attention models, revealing how positional information is encoded differently.

Strengths: 1) The paper addresses a significant limitation in morphological inflection research: neural models typically require large datasets to outperform traditional methods. Their model's success on the small CELEX dataset (500 examples) is a major contribution that could benefit morphologically rich languages with limited resources. 2) The comprehensive experimental evaluation across three diverse datasets provides strong empirical evidence for the model's effectiveness, with the hard attention model outperforming soft attention baselines on suffixing+stem-change languages (Russian, German, Spanish). 3) The analysis of learned representations provides valuable insights into how neural models encode morphological features, particularly the reduced positional encoding in hard attention models compared to soft attention, explaining their better performance with limited data.

Weaknesses: 1) The novelty claim is weak, as the hard attention mechanism resembles existing approaches like the RNN Transducer (Graves, 2012) and Neural Transducer (Jaitly et al., 2015), with insufficient differentiation from prior work. 2) The theoretical justification for the monotonic alignment assumption is underdeveloped; the paper relies primarily on empirical results rather than explaining why monotonic alignment is generally valid for morphological inflection. 3) The paper acknowledges limitations on languages with vowel/consonant harmony but doesn't provide sufficient analysis or potential solutions for these cases, which are significant for morphological processing in many languages.

Questions to Authors: 1) How does your model's novelty specifically differ from Graves' (2012) RNN Transducer and Jaitly et al.'s (2015) Neural Transducer? 2) Could you elaborate on why your model underperforms on languages with vowel harmony, and what architectural modifications might address this limitation? 3) The paper claims your model doesn't encode positional information as strongly as soft attention models - how does this trade-off specifically affect performance on different morphological phenomena?
