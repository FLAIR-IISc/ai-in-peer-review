- Proposes a neural hard-attention model with step control to enforce nearly-monotonic character alignments for morphological inflection, evaluated on CELEX, Wiktionary, and SIGMORPHON 2016.  
- Claims three contributions: hard attention for near-monotonic seq2seq, state-of-the-art results on three datasets, and analysis comparing hard vs. soft attention.  

- Strength: Strong practical impact on small-data scenarios—CELEX results outperform previous neural methods and the latent-variable FST.  
- Strength: Well-motivated theoretically by the near-monotonic alignment property of morphological inflection.  
- Strength: Insightful SVD-based representation analysis showing hard vs. soft attention encode positional info differently.  

- Weakness: Novelty not clearly distinguished from prior monotonic alignment work (e.g., Graves 2012, Yu et al. 2016); approach may seem incremental.  
- Weakness: Inadequate contextualization of the comparison to the latent-variable FST—missing discussion of expert-knowledge vs. data-driven trade-offs.  
- Weakness: Superficial analysis of poorer SIGMORPHON performance on some languages (e.g., Arabic, Navajo); lacks concrete linguistic examples and explanations.  

- Question: How are the “independently learned character-level alignments” obtained—details on the Sudoh et al. (2013) aligner and handling of ambiguous alignments?  
- Question: Can you provide concrete failure examples on SIGMORPHON languages with vowel/consonant harmony and explain why monotonic alignment breaks down and how to fix it?  
- Question: What does “more isolated character clusters” mean linguistically—are these distinct morphological features or signs of overfitting?  

- Overall: Strong paper with valuable small-data contributions; needs clearer novelty claims and deeper linguistic analysis of failure cases to strengthen the contribution.
