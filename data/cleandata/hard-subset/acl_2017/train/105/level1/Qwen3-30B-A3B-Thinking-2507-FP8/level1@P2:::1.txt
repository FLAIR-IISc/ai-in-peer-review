

# Review for ACL 2017

## Summary and Contributions

This paper proposes a neural model for morphological inflection generation that employs a hard attention mechanism designed to exploit the nearly-monotonic character alignments commonly found in morphological inflection tasks. The authors claim three main contributions: (1) a hard attention model for nearly-monotonic sequence-to-sequence learning, (2) state-of-the-art results on three morphological inflection datasets, and (3) an analysis comparing their model with soft attention models.

The most significant contribution is the model design that explicitly leverages the monotonic alignment property of morphological inflection. The second most important contribution is the empirical demonstration of the model's effectiveness, particularly on the CELEX dataset where it surpasses previous neural models and the non-neural latent-variable WFST model. The third contribution is the insightful analysis of the learned representations, revealing key differences between hard and soft attention mechanisms.

## Strengths

1. **Strong empirical results with meaningful comparison on small datasets:** The paper demonstrates a clear advantage of the proposed model on the CELEX dataset, where it achieves the first neural model to surpass the latent-variable WFST model (Dreyer et al., 2008) with only 500 training examples per inflection type. This is particularly significant because previous neural models struggled with small datasets, as evidenced by their inferior performance compared to the WFST model. The results on the CELEX dataset (Table 1) show a 1.14% absolute improvement over the previous best neural model (NWFST) and 0.94% over the best non-neural model (LAT), which is highly meaningful for low-resource scenarios.

2. **Clear motivation and well-justified model design:** The authors provide a strong rationale for their approach, correctly identifying that morphological inflection often exhibits nearly monotonic character alignments (e.g., suffixing morphology). The model's design directly addresses this observation through its hard attention mechanism and step operations, which are well-motivated by the task's characteristics. The comparison to the soft attention model (Figure 2) clearly demonstrates how the hard attention mechanism prevents overfitting on small datasets.

3. **Insightful analysis of learned representations:** The analysis of character representations in context (Figures 4a-d) provides valuable insights into how the models encode information. The key finding that the hard attention model encodes positional information less strongly than the soft attention model (since it uses step operations to handle position) is particularly compelling. This explanation for why the hard attention model generalizes better with limited data is well-supported by the evidence and significantly enhances the paper's contribution beyond just presenting a new model.

## Weaknesses

1. **Limited novelty of the core approach:** While the model design is well-motivated, the "hard monotonic attention" approach is conceptually very similar to Graves (2012) and Yu et al. (2016), which also use monotonic alignments. The paper acknowledges these works but doesn't clearly differentiate their contribution. The key distinction—using independently learned alignments rather than jointly training alignment and transduction—is mentioned but not sufficiently emphasized as a novel contribution. This makes it difficult to assess the paper's novelty relative to existing work.

2. **Lack of ablation studies:** The paper fails to provide ablation studies to isolate the contribution of specific model components. For example, it doesn't show how much the hard attention mechanism contributes versus the bi-directional encoder, or how important the step operation is. Without these experiments, it's hard to determine which aspects of the model are truly responsible for the performance improvements.

3. **Insufficient analysis of failure cases:** While the paper notes the model's weaker performance on languages with complex morphology (like vowel harmony), it doesn't thoroughly analyze why the model fails in these cases or provide specific examples. A deeper analysis of failure cases would strengthen the paper by providing more concrete insights into the model's limitations and potential improvements.

## Questions to Authors

1. How does your model compare to the RNN Transducer (Graves, 2012) in terms of both performance and training complexity? The paper mentions Graves (2012) but doesn't clearly differentiate your approach.

2. The paper claims to be the first neural model to surpass the latent-variable WFST model on CELEX, but the Dreyer et al. (2008) model is not neural. How do you compare to non-neural WFST models in terms of training time and practical implementation?

3. Could you provide more details about the character alignment process used to generate the training data for the hard attention model? How robust is this alignment method to errors in the alignment, and how might alignment errors affect model performance?
