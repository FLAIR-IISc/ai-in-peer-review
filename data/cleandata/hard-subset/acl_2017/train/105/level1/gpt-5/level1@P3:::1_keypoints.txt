- Proposes a neural encoder–decoder with hard, monotonic attention where the decoder emits WRITE(symbol) or STEP actions over a biLSTM-encoded input.  
- Uses externally computed character alignments to derive deterministic oracle action sequences for supervised cross-entropy training.  
- Evaluated on CELEX (low-resource), Wiktionary (high-resource), and SIGMORPHON 2016 (10 languages) with competitive or state-of-the-art results, especially in low-data regimes.  
- Includes analyses comparing learned representations and alignments against a soft-attention baseline.  

Main contributions:
- Introduces a simple, effective hard-attention encoder–decoder supervised by external alignments that yields strong low-data performance.  
- Provides extensive empirical evaluation and qualitative analyses (position/character clustering) showing when hard monotonicity helps or hurts.  
- Presents a practical training recipe (oracle action supervision) that avoids latent alignment marginalization and reduces overfitting on small datasets.  

Strengths:
- Achieves strong performance on low-resource CELEX, outperforming neural and non-neural baselines and reducing overfitting relative to soft attention.  
- Methodologically clear and simple: biLSTM encoder + RNN decoder with step/write control trained with standard cross-entropy.  
- Thorough comparisons across multiple benchmarks with per-language/type breakdowns showing where bias helps (e.g., suffixing + stem change).  
- Insightful qualitative analysis demonstrating soft attention often learns monotonic alignments and that hard attention encodes character identity more crisply.  
- Practical relevance for edit-based transduction tasks (inflection, transliteration), especially when labeled data are scarce.  

Weaknesses:
- Strong reliance on an external aligner with no ablation across different aligners or experiments with noisy/gold alignments, limiting robustness claims.  
- Objective mismatch: optimizes best-derivation not best-sequence, with no analysis of multiple derivations, marginalization benefits, or decoding/search behavior.  
- Potentially unfair comparisons: baseline training/early-stopping differences and missing comparisons to location-aware or other edit-action models.  
- Lacks key ablations isolating contributions of feedback embedding, encoder bidirectionality, and hard vs soft control under identical settings.  
- Limited discussion/proposed remedies for languages with non-concatenative phenomena where monotonicity fails (e.g., Turkish, Hungarian, Navajo).  

Questions for authors:
- How sensitive are results to choice/quality of aligner, and were alternative or noisy aligners tried?  
- What decoding strategy was used (greedy vs beam), which beam sizes were tested, and were there search errors due to best-derivation training?  
- What fraction of training pairs involve many-to-one/one-to-many mappings, multiple WRITEs per position, or steps without consumption for insertions?  
- Can you provide ablations for (i) removing previous-output feedback, (ii) unidirectional vs bidirectional encoder, (iii) joint/latent alignment, and (iv) hard vs soft attention under identical hyperparameters?  
- Are gains statistically significant and what is variance across CELEX folds and SIGMORPHON/Wiktionary runs?  
- How do training/inference speeds and data-efficiency compare to soft attention and NWFST, and are additional learning curves available?  

Overall:
- Well-motivated and clearly executed paper with strong empirical results in low-resource settings and a leaning toward acceptance.  
- Addressing alignment dependence, decoding/search considerations, and adding targeted ablations would further strengthen the contribution and clarify applicability.
