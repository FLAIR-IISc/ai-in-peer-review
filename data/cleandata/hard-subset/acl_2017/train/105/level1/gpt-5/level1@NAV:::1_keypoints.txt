- Presents a neural encoder–decoder for morphological inflection using hard, monotonic attention with WRITE and STEP actions, trained via externally learned oracle action sequences.
- Main contribution 1: Simple, effective hard-attention transducer that leverages near-monotonic character alignments, showing strong empirical gains, especially in low-resource settings.
- Main contribution 2: Training recipe that decouples alignment learning from transduction, enabling straightforward cross-entropy training without latent-alignment marginalization.
- Main contribution 3: Analysis comparing representational properties of hard vs. soft attention, including alignment visualizations and SVD projections.
- Strength: Strong empirical performance, surpassing soft-attention baselines on CELEX and achieving best averages on Wiktionary; competitive on SIGMORPHON for suffixing/stem-change languages.
- Strength: Well-targeted inductive bias (STEP/WRITE) matches near-monotonic inflection processes, improving generalization and reducing overfitting relative to soft attention.
- Strength: Simplicity and training stability by using gold action sequences derived from external alignments, avoiding complex inference during training.
- Strength: Interpretability—visualizations and analyses provide clear evidence of differing encodings for position vs. character identity under hard vs. soft attention.
- Weakness: Limited novelty compared to prior monotonic transduction work (e.g., RNN-T, Neural Transducer, Yu et al.); approach is largely incremental.
- Weakness: Dependence on precomputed alignments with no ablations on alignment quality or comparisons to jointly learned alignments; potential sensitivity to noisy oracles.
- Weakness: Mixed performance on non-monotonic phenomena (e.g., vowel/consonant harmony), where the model sometimes lags behind soft-attention systems.
- Weakness: Reporting concerns—MED trained fewer epochs; statistical significance and decoding details (beam vs. greedy) are unclear, complicating comparisons.
- Question: How sensitive is performance to the choice/quality of the aligner, and are results available with alternative or jointly learned alignments?
- Question: What decoding strategy is used (beam size, tie-breaking), and does beam width materially affect accuracy?
- Question: Can the control set be extended (e.g., BACKSTEP/JUMP) to better handle non-monotonic phenomena, and what trade-offs with overfitting would that entail?
