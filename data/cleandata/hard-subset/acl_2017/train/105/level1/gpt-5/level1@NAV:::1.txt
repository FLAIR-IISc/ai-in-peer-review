Summary and Contributions
This paper presents a neural encoder–decoder for morphological inflection with a hard, monotonic attention controlled by two actions: WRITE(symbol) and STEP. Alignments are learned externally and deterministically converted into oracle action sequences, enabling straightforward cross-entropy training. The model achieves state-of-the-art results on CELEX, Wiktionary, and is competitive on SIGMORPHON, and the authors analyze learned alignments and representations against soft attention.

As I see them, the main contributions are:
1) An effective, simple hard-attention transducer that leverages nearly monotonic character alignments, with strong empirical gains, especially in low-resource settings.
2) A training recipe that decouples alignment learning and transduction, avoiding complex latent alignment marginalization while remaining robust in practice.
3) An insightful analysis comparing representational properties of hard vs. soft attention for inflection generation.

Strengths
- Strong empirical performance, particularly in low-resource conditions: On CELEX, the model surpasses both soft-attention baselines and the specialized latent-variable WFST (Dreyer et al.), a notable milestone. Gains also appear on Wiktionary (best average across settings) and on suffixing+stem-change languages in SIGMORPHON.
- Well-targeted inductive bias: The STEP/WRITE control matches the near-monotonic nature of many inflection processes, improving generalization and mitigating overfitting relative to soft attention. The learning curves on CELEX substantiate this claim.
- Simplicity and training stability: By using gold action sequences derived from external alignments, training reduces to standard cross-entropy without forward–backward or sequence-level losses, yet still outperforms models that marginalize alignments.
- Interpretability and analysis: The alignment visualizations and SVD projections provide clear evidence on how position and character identity are encoded differently under hard vs. soft attention, yielding useful scientific insights.

Weaknesses
- Limited novelty relative to prior monotonic transduction (e.g., RNN-T, Neural Transducer, Yu et al.). The main distinction is reliance on external alignments and a simple control; conceptually the approach is incremental.
- Dependence on precomputed alignments and best-derivation training: No ablations on alignment quality or comparisons to jointly learned alignments; potential mismatch if oracle alignments are noisy.
- Mixed performance on non-monotonic phenomena (e.g., vowel/consonant harmony): The model trails soft attention on several SIGMORPHON languages, highlighting limits of strict monotonic control.
- Fairness/reporting details: MED was trained fewer epochs; statistical significance and decoding details (beam vs. greedy) are unclear, complicating strict comparison.

Questions to Authors
- How sensitive is performance to the choice/quality of the aligner? Any results with alternative aligners or jointly learned alignments?
- What decoding strategy is used (beam size, tie-breaking)? Does beam width materially affect accuracy?
- Can the control set be extended (e.g., BACKSTEP/JUMP) to better handle non-monotonic phenomena, and what is the trade-off with overfitting?
