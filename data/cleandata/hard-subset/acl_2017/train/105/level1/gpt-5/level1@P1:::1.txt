Summary
The paper proposes a neural sequence transduction model for morphological inflection that uses a hard, monotonic attention mechanism with explicit step and write actions. Alignments are obtained externally; the model is trained via cross-entropy on oracle action sequences derived from these alignments. The approach outperforms prior work on several benchmarks, particularly in low-resource settings (CELEX), and includes qualitative analysis comparing hard vs. soft attention.

Main contributions (as I see them)
1) A simple, effective hard monotonic attention decoder (step/write over a biLSTM-encoded input) trained from external character alignments, tailored to nearly-monotonic inflection.
2) Strong empirical results: state-of-the-art average performance on Wiktionary, substantial gains in low-resource CELEX (surpassing Dreyer et al.’s latent WFST), and competitive results on SIGMORPHON across diverse typologies.
3) Qualitative analysis of learned alignments and encoder representations for hard vs. soft attention, offering insight into what features are captured.

Strengths
- Strong low-resource performance and clear advantage of the monotonic bias: On CELEX, the model achieves the best average accuracy (89.44) and is, to my knowledge, the first neural model to surpass the specialized latent-variable WFST (88.3). The learning curves demonstrate better dev performance than soft attention due to reduced overfitting, validating the modeling bias for small data.
- Competitive or superior results on large datasets: On the Wiktionary benchmarks, the model attains the best average (96.55), winning in several categories (DE-V, FI-NA, NL-V), outperforming neural baselines without attention and joint alignment-transduction methods (e.g., Yu et al.). This supports the claim that “hard attention + external alignment” is robust even with abundant data.
- Simplicity and training stability: Decoupling alignment from transduction reduces training complexity (no marginalization over alignments, no forward-backward) and enables straightforward cross-entropy optimization. This is a pragmatic contribution compared to RNN-Transducer/Neural-Transducer styles that require more complex training or latent alignment marginalization.
- Insightful analysis: The alignment visualizations show that both hard and soft attention learn near-monotonic alignments, but hard attention executes intuitive operations (e.g., deletions via steps, one-to-many via consecutive writes). The SVD plots suggest systematic character and positional encoding differences, supporting the hypothesis that hard attention externalizes positional control via steps.
- Broad multilingual evaluation and interpretation: The paper contextualizes results by linguistic typology (suffixing + stem change vs. harmony/templatic), helping readers understand when the monotonic bias helps or hurts.

Weaknesses
- Novelty is incremental relative to prior monotonic transduction work: The core idea (monotonic alignment with step/emit actions) is closely related to RNN-T (Graves, 2012), Neural Transducer (Jaitly et al., 2015), and online segment-to-segment models (Yu et al., 2016). The main difference is reliance on external alignments and a simpler training objective. The contribution is thus more empirical/pragmatic than conceptual; this should be acknowledged more explicitly.
- Dependence on external alignments and objective mismatch: The model optimizes best-derivation rather than best-sequence and uses oracle action sequences derived from an external aligner (Sudoh et al.). The impact of alignment noise and the approximation (not marginalizing over derivations) is not quantified. An ablation or sensitivity analysis (e.g., different aligners, noisy alignments) is missing.
- Comparability and methodological details: On SIGMORPHON, the authors’ soft attention baseline is trained for up to 100 epochs with dev selection, whereas MED used 20 epochs, complicating fairness claims. Statistical significance is not reported for differences that are sometimes small. Decoding details (greedy vs. beam, stopping criterion) are not clearly specified; the search strategy could affect results and comparison with baselines that use beam search or ensembling.
- Limited ablations: Key design choices are not dissected (e.g., effect of including previous output embedding si−1, the set of morpho-syntactic feature embeddings f, biRNN size, removing step bias by allowing limited rewinds). Without ablations, it’s hard to attribute gains to the hard attention mechanism vs. other factors.
- Efficiency claims unsubstantiated: While the model is simpler than soft attention in principle (single pointer, local decision), no training/inference time or memory comparisons are provided. Given the practical motivation, efficiency measurements would strengthen the case.

Questions to Authors
- Decoding: Is inference greedy or beam search? What beam width and tie-breaking are used? How is termination handled (EOS vs. pointer-at-end constraints)? Any length normalization?
- Alignment sensitivity: How does performance vary with different aligners or with injected noise? Can the model recover from imperfect alignments, and does joint fine-tuning of alignments help?
- Objective mismatch: Did you try marginalizing over derivations for a given output (e.g., via dynamic programming for monotone alignments) or training with reinforcement/expected risk? Any empirical impact?
- Ablations: (a) Remove previous-output embedding si−1; (b) remove or randomize morpho-syntactic features f; (c) vary encoder directionality (uni vs. bi); (d) compare to soft attention plus monotonic/location-aware constraints. Which factors drive gains on CELEX?
- Efficiency: Can you report training and decoding speed vs. soft attention on CELEX and Wiktionary? Does hard attention reduce inference latency?
- Generality: For non-monotonic languages (Arabic, Navajo), have you considered allowing limited backward moves or small local reordering windows? Would that close the gap to soft attention?

Overall assessment
A well-executed, pragmatic adaptation of hard monotonic attention to morphological inflection with compelling empirical benefits, especially in low-resource settings. While the architectural novelty is modest relative to prior monotonic transduction literature, the paper contributes solid experimental evidence, clear motivation, and useful analysis. Addressing comparability, alignment sensitivity, and ablations would further strengthen the submission. I lean toward acceptance.
