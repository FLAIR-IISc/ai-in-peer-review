Summary
The paper proposes a character-level encoder–decoder with hard monotonic attention for morphological inflection generation. The decoder predicts a sequence over Σy ∪ {step}, where step advances a single-pointer over the biLSTM-encoded input; write actions emit output characters. Training uses external character alignments to derive gold action sequences, enabling simple cross-entropy training. The model is evaluated on CELEX (low-resource), Wiktionary (high-resource), and SIGMORPHON 2016 (10 languages), and compared against neural soft-attention and WFST baselines. The authors also analyze learned alignments and encoder representations.

Main contributions (as I see them)
1) A simple hard monotonic attention transducer trained with externally derived alignments that is notably data-efficient and matches the task’s inductive bias.
2) Strong empirical results, especially in low-resource settings (CELEX), where it surpasses a tailored latent-variable WFST; competitive to SOTA on large datasets.
3) Insightful qualitative analysis comparing hard vs. soft attention encoders (alignment behavior and positional/character-type encoding).

Strengths
- Strong performance in low-resource regime with clear justification: The hard, monotonic control aligns with suffixing and stem-change morphology and regularizes learning. On CELEX, it outperforms LAT (Dreyer et al., 2008) and recent neural baselines; learning curves show reduced overfitting vs. soft attention. This addresses a persistent weakness of neural seq2seq in small data.
- Clean training via external alignments: Decoupling alignment from transduction simplifies optimization, avoids marginalization over alignments, and yields stable training with cross-entropy, while still leveraging context via a biLSTM encoder and next-action conditioning via the decoder.
- Broad, careful evaluation: Three established benchmarks, comparisons to multiple strong baselines (neural and non-neural), and analyses by morphological typology (suffixing, harmony, templatic). Results are consistent with the inductive bias story: best on suffixing+stem-change languages; slightly behind soft attention on languages needing longer-range non-monotonic dependencies.
- Modeling clarity and interpretability: The step/write transition system ties actions to alignment decisions, making the model’s operation and errors easier to inspect than soft attention’s distributed context. The alignment visualizations and SVD plots provide plausible explanations for model behavior.
- Practicality and generality for monotone tasks: The approach is likely applicable to other nearly-monotonic character transductions (transliteration, G2P), with minimal changes.

Weaknesses
- Dependence on external alignments and best-derivation approximation: The model’s objective optimizes a single oracle action sequence per pair, not the probability of the output string marginalized over derivations. This could bias learning to the chosen aligner and potentially mis-handle equivalent alignments. No ablation on alignment quality or alternative aligners is presented, and there is no comparison to joint/marginalized alignment training (e.g., RNN-T/CTC-style).
- Limited advantage beyond monotonic settings: On languages with strong non-local constraints (vowel/consonant harmony, templatic morphology), the hard model underperforms soft attention. The paper argues this via typology but does not explore augmentations (e.g., limited look-back/look-ahead, explicit feature conditioning) to mitigate the gap.
- Fairness and rigor of comparisons: For SIGMORPHON, the soft-attention reimplementation is trained longer (100 epochs) than MED (20), potentially inflating the margin over MED. Statistical significance is not reported for CELEX (across 5 folds), Wiktionary, or SIGMORPHON. Beam size/decoding details (greedy vs. beam) and their effects are not specified, which can materially affect exact-match accuracy.
- Incomplete ablations: The contribution of each component (feature embeddings, previous-output embedding, biLSTM encoder vs. uni, step action vs. learned transition probability) is not quantified. A hard-vs-soft comparison without holding all else equal (e.g., same feature sets and training budgets) leaves some confounds unresolved.
- Evaluation metrics and error analysis: Only exact-match accuracy is reported. Edit distance or per-feature accuracy would better illuminate error types, especially in near-miss cases. Error breakdowns by operation (insertions, deletions, stem changes) and by paradigm size would strengthen the claims about inductive bias.

Questions to Authors
- How sensitive is performance to the external aligner? Can you report results with a different aligner or with noisy alignments, and any correlation between alignment F1 and downstream accuracy?
- What decoding strategy is used (greedy vs. beam)? If beam, what size? How do results vary with beam size and does beam reduce the best-derivation approximation error?
- Do you enforce legality constraints (e.g., preventing step beyond the last input) during decoding? How are insertions at the beginning/end handled?
- Can you report statistical significance across CELEX folds and across languages on SIGMORPHON/Wiktionary?
- Ablations: remove feature embeddings; remove previous-output feedback; replace biLSTM with uniLSTM; replace step with a learned transition probability (à la RNN-T) while keeping external alignments. Which parts drive the gains?
- Could a hybrid model (hard attention plus occasional soft reweighting or location-aware soft attention) close the gap on harmony/templatic languages? Any preliminary results?
- Beyond exact match, what are the average edit distances and error distributions by operation type? Do near-misses cluster around specific morphological features?

Overall evaluation
A well-motivated, simple, and effective model that advances the state of the art in low-resource morphological inflection and remains competitive in higher-resource settings. The work is solid and insightful, though it would benefit from stronger ablations, significance testing, and a deeper study of alignment dependence. I lean toward acceptance.
