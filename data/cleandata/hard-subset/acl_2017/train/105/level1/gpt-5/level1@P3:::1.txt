Summary
The paper proposes a neural encoder–decoder with hard, monotonic attention for morphological inflection generation. The decoder emits either a WRITE(symbol) or STEP action over a biLSTM-encoded input, thereby modeling an almost-monotonic alignment that is typical for inflection. Alignments are not learned jointly; instead, the authors first obtain character-level alignments with an external aligner and deterministically derive oracle action sequences for supervised training with cross-entropy. The model is evaluated on CELEX (low-resource), Wiktionary (high-resource), and SIGMORPHON 2016 (10 languages), where it achieves state-of-the-art or competitive results, particularly strong in low-resource settings. The paper also includes analyses comparing learned representations and alignments with a soft-attention baseline.

Main contributions (as I see them)
- A simple, effective hard-attention encoder–decoder that uses externally learned character alignments to supervise a monotonic, step/write control policy, yielding strong gains in low-data regimes.
- Extensive empirical evaluation across diverse datasets with competitive or SOTA results, and qualitative analyses that illuminate what the model learns (position and character clustering), and when hard monotonicity helps or hurts.
- A practical training recipe (oracle action supervision) that avoids latent alignment marginalization, with favorable optimization behavior and reduced overfitting on small datasets.

Strengths
- Strong performance in low-resource settings: On CELEX, the model outperforms both neural and non-neural baselines, including the purpose-built latent-variable WFST. The learning curves substantiate the claim that hard monotonicity reduces overfitting vs soft attention.
- Methodological clarity and simplicity: The architecture is conceptually clean (biLSTM encoder + RNN decoder with a single-step control) and trains with standard cross-entropy on oracle actions, avoiding the complexity and instability of marginalizing alignments (e.g., RNN-T/CTC-style training) or RL.
- Good coverage of related datasets and comparisons: The paper evaluates against strong baselines (soft attention, NWFST, latent WFST) across three established benchmarks, with per-language/type breakdowns that show where the modeling bias helps (suffixing + stem change) and where it is less ideal.
- Insightful qualitative analysis: The alignment visualizations and SVD projections convincingly demonstrate that (i) soft attention often learns monotonic alignments anyway, (ii) hard attention encodes character identity more crisply while soft attention encodes position more strongly—plausible given each mechanism’s needs.
- Practical relevance: The monotonic step/write abstraction is a natural fit for many edit-based transduction tasks (inflection, transliteration), making the approach likely broadly useful, especially when labeled data are scarce.

Weaknesses
- Reliance on an external aligner: The approach’s success depends on the quality of precomputed alignments. There is no ablation comparing different aligners, noisy alignments, or training with gold vs. learned alignments. This limits understanding of robustness and portability. Moreover, using a CRP-based aligner may entail heuristic choices that could bias the oracle.
- Objective mismatch and decoding details: The model optimizes best-derivation rather than best-sequence (acknowledged). There is no analysis of the impact (e.g., how often multiple derivations exist, or whether marginalizing over derivations would help), nor are decoding details (beam size, search errors, runtime) clearly discussed. It is unclear if greedy decoding suffices and whether beam helps or hurts.
- Fairness and completeness of comparisons: On SIGMORPHON, the paper attributes soft-attention gains partly to longer training and model selection differences versus MED. This raises fairness concerns: either tune baselines to similar early stopping regimes or report both. Comparisons to location-aware soft attention (as used in speech) or other edit-action models are missing; these could be competitive in monotonic settings.
- Limited ablation studies: The paper lacks ablations isolating the contribution of key components: effect of the feedback connection (previous output embedding), feature embeddings, bidirectionality of the encoder, and the hard vs. soft control while holding other factors constant. Such ablations would strengthen causal claims about what drives the gains, especially on CELEX.
- Generalization beyond almost-monotonic languages: Results show weaker performance on languages with non-concatenative or harmony phenomena (e.g., Turkish, Hungarian, Navajo), but the paper stops short of proposing remedies (e.g., chunkwise/monotonic-with-chunks attention, limited reordering, or hybrid models). The discussion could be deeper on when monotonicity breaks and how to adapt.

Questions to Authors
- Alignment sensitivity: How sensitive is performance to the choice/quality of the aligner? Did you try alternative aligners (e.g., Levenshtein-based, probabilistic EM, or joint training with fine-tuning of alignments)? Any experiments with noisy/mismatched alignments?
- Decoding: What decoding strategy is used (greedy vs beam)? What beam sizes were tried, and how do accuracy and speed trade off? Any evidence of search errors due to best-derivation training?
- Oracle coverage: What fraction of training pairs exhibit many-to-one/one-to-many mappings, and how often does the derived oracle require multiple WRITEs per input position? Do you ever need to step without consuming aligned inputs to handle insertions at boundaries?
- Ablations: Can you report ablations on (i) removing the previous-output feedback embedding, (ii) unidirectional vs bidirectional encoder, (iii) replacing external alignment with joint/latent alignment, and (iv) swapping hard vs soft attention under identical hyperparameters?
- Statistical significance and variability: For CELEX, beyond averaged accuracy, can you report variance across folds? On Wiktionary/SIGMORPHON, are observed gains statistically significant?
- Runtime and data efficiency: How do training/inference speeds compare with soft attention and NWFST? Any learning-curve plots vs number of training examples beyond CELEX?

Overall, this is a well-motivated, clearly executed paper with strong empirical results, especially in low-resource settings where the monotonic prior is valuable. Addressing the alignment dependence, decoding/search considerations, and adding ablations would further solidify the contribution and clarify the scope of applicability. I lean toward acceptance.
