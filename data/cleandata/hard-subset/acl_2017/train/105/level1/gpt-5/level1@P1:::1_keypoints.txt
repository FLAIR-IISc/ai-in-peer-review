- Summary: Proposes a neural sequence transduction model with hard monotonic attention using explicit step/write actions trained from external character alignments; shows strong empirical and qualitative results.

- Contribution 1: Introduces a simple, effective hard monotonic decoder (step/write over biLSTM input) trained from external alignments for near-monotonic inflection.

- Contribution 2: Presents strong empirical results—state-of-the-art average on Wiktionary, large gains on low-resource CELEX, and competitive SIGMORPHON performance across typologies.

- Contribution 3: Provides qualitative analysis comparing hard vs. soft attention alignments and encoder representations.

- Strength: Demonstrates clear low-resource advantage and reduced overfitting due to monotonic bias, surpassing prior latent WFST on CELEX.

- Strength: Competitive or superior results on large datasets (Wiktionary), showing robustness of hard attention plus external alignment.

- Strength: Simpler and more stable training by decoupling alignment from transduction, avoiding marginalization/forward-backward.

- Strength: Insightful alignment visualizations showing intuitive operations (deletions via steps, one-to-many via writes) and representational differences.

- Strength: Broad multilingual evaluation framed by linguistic typology, clarifying where monotonic bias helps or hurts.

- Weakness: Incremental novelty—closely related to RNN-T, Neural Transducer, and prior monotonic models; main novelty is pragmatic (external alignments + simpler training).

- Weakness: Dependence on external alignments and objective mismatch (optimizing oracle derivations not marginal likelihood); no sensitivity analysis to alignment noise.

- Weakness: Comparability issues and missing methodological details (training epochs differences, lack of statistical significance, unclear decoding/beam settings).

- Weakness: Limited ablations—key design choices (prev output embedding, feature embeddings, encoder directionality, etc.) are not dissected to attribute gains.

- Weakness: Efficiency claims unsubstantiated—no training/inference time or memory comparisons to soft attention baselines.

- Question: Clarify decoding details—greedy vs. beam search, beam width, tie-breaking, termination and length normalization.

- Question: How sensitive are results to different aligners or noisy alignments; can the model recover or benefit from joint fine-tuning of alignments?

- Question: Did you try marginalizing over derivations or alternative training (reinforcement/expected risk), and what was the impact?

- Question: Provide ablations: remove prev-output embedding, randomize morpho features, vary encoder directionality, compare to monotonic/location-aware soft attention—what drives CELEX gains?

- Question: Report efficiency—training and decoding speed comparisons vs. soft attention on CELEX and Wiktionary; effect on inference latency?

- Question: For non-monotonic languages (Arabic, Navajo), have you considered allowing limited backward moves or local reorderings to close gaps with soft attention?

- Overall assessment: Pragmatic, well-executed adaptation of hard monotonic attention with compelling empirical benefits (especially low-resource); acceptance recommended if comparisons, alignment sensitivity, and ablations are addressed.
