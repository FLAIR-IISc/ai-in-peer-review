Summary and Contributions
This paper proposes a neural encoder-decoder model for morphological inflection generation with a hard, nearly-monotonic attention mechanism. The decoder executes a sequence of WRITE and STEP actions guided by a single attention pointer over a biLSTM-encoded input. Training uses externally induced character alignments to derive gold action sequences, enabling simple cross-entropy training without marginalization over alignments. The model is evaluated on CELEX, Wiktionary, and SIGMORPHON 2016 datasets and compared against neural and non-neural baselines. The authors also analyze the learned alignments and representations versus a soft-attention model.

As I see them, the main contributions (most to least significant) are:
1) A hard monotonic attention encoder-decoder for character-level inflection generation trained from externally induced alignments, achieving strong results especially in low-resource settings.
2) Extensive empirical evaluation across three benchmarks, showing state-of-the-art or competitive performance, notably surpassing a specialized latent-variable WFST on CELEX.
3) Comparative analysis of learned alignments and embeddings between hard and soft attention models, providing insights into what features are encoded.

Strengths
- Strong performance in low-resource settings: On CELEX, the proposed model outperforms prior neural models and the tailored latent-variable WFST (Dreyer et al., 2008). The learning curves indicate better generalization than soft attention in small data regimes, consistent with the inductive bias of monotonic attention. This addresses a known weakness of neural models on CELEX.
- Competitive or best results on larger datasets: On the Wiktionary datasets, the model achieves the best average accuracy across languages/inflection types compared to strong neural and non-neural baselines, including attention-based and non-attention models. This demonstrates robustness beyond low-resource settings.
- Clear, principled modeling of monotonicity: The step/write control mechanism closely matches common monotonic alignments in suffixing languages. Conditioning the decoder on the attended input, morpho-syntactic feature embeddings, and previous output token is straightforward and well-justified.
- Simple and stable training: By decoupling alignment induction from transduction and training with cross-entropy on oracle action sequences, the approach avoids complex forward-backward marginalization while still learning effective alignments. This simplicity likely helps with stability and sample efficiency.
- Insightful analysis: The qualitative alignment visualizations show that both soft and hard attention learn mostly monotonic alignments; the hard model also naturally captures deletions and one-to-many mappings with sequences of STEP/WRITE. The 2-D projections of encoder representations suggest character identity and positional information are encoded differently by hard vs soft attention, supporting the modeling intuition.

Weaknesses
- Dependence on external alignments without sensitivity analysis: The model’s training relies on independently learned character alignments (Sudoh et al., 2013). There is no ablation quantifying how alignment quality affects performance, nor a comparison to joint alignment-transduction training under identical conditions. This limits conclusions about robustness and the true source of gains.
- Best-derivation approximation: Decoding optimizes over action sequences rather than marginalizing over derivations that yield the same output string. While noted, the paper does not provide empirical evidence on the impact of this approximation (e.g., via beam width, marginalization variants, or alignment ambiguity scenarios).
- Limited clarity on decoding/search: The paper states argmax over action sequences but does not specify whether greedy or beam search is used, termination conditions, or constraints to prevent stepping past the end of input. These details matter for reproducibility and performance.
- Mixed results and comparability on SIGMORPHON: The hard attention model performs better on suffixing+stem-change languages but underperforms soft attention on others involving harmony or templatic morphology. While the typological explanation is reasonable, the comparison to MED is confounded by different training regimes (epochs/data usage), and no statistical significance is reported for differences.
- Missing ablations: The contribution of key components (previous-output embedding, feature embeddings, biLSTM vs uni-directional encoders) is not quantified. Similarly, no runtime or parameter count comparisons are provided to substantiate claims of simplicity/efficiency.

Questions to Authors
- How sensitive is the model to the choice and quality of external alignments? Can you report results with alternative aligners or deliberately degraded alignments, and/or compare to joint alignment-transduction training under matched settings?
- What decoding strategy is used (greedy vs beam)? What beam sizes were tried and what is their impact on accuracy and speed? How do you handle termination and prevent invalid action sequences (e.g., stepping beyond the last input)?
- Can you report statistical significance tests for the main comparisons on Wiktionary and SIGMORPHON?
- Do you have ablations on: (a) using previous output token embedding vs none, (b) feature embeddings vs none, (c) biLSTM vs uniLSTM encoders, and (d) training without external alignments (e.g., learned alignments after pretraining)?
- What is the training and inference speed relative to soft attention baselines, and how do parameter counts compare?
- On languages where non-monotonic phenomena are prominent, can limited non-monotonicity be incorporated (e.g., small backward moves, epsilon steps) without losing the model’s simplicity? Any preliminary experiments?

Overall, the paper is clearly written, the method is technically sound within stated approximations, and the empirical evaluation is broad. The main limitations are the reliance on external alignments without sensitivity/ablation studies and incomplete decoding details.
