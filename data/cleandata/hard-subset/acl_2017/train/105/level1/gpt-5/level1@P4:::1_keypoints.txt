- Proposes a hard, nearly-monotonic attention encoder–decoder for character-level inflection generation trained from externally induced alignments.  
- Evaluated on CELEX, Wiktionary, and SIGMORPHON 2016, showing state-of-the-art or competitive results, especially in low-resource settings.  
- Provides comparative analysis of learned alignments and encoder representations between hard and soft attention models.  

- Strong low-resource performance: outperforms prior neural models and a tailored latent-variable WFST on CELEX.  
- Competitive or best average accuracy on Wiktionary across languages/inflection types, showing robustness beyond low-resource cases.  
- Modeling is principled: STEP/WRITE control matches common monotonic alignments and conditions decoder on attended input, features, and previous output.  
- Simple and stable training by decoupling alignment induction and using cross-entropy on oracle action sequences, avoiding marginalization.  
- Insightful analyses: alignment visualizations and representation projections support the modeling choices and show how deletions/one-to-many mappings are handled.  

- Relies on external character alignments without sensitivity analysis or comparison to joint alignment–transduction training.  
- Decoding optimizes over action sequences (best-derivation approximation) without empirical assessment of the approximation’s impact.  
- Decoding/search details (greedy vs beam, beam sizes, termination rules, constraints to prevent invalid steps) are unspecified, hurting reproducibility.  
- Mixed results on SIGMORPHON: underperforms soft attention for some morphologies, and comparisons to MED are confounded by different training regimes and lack significance testing.  
- Missing ablations on key components (previous-output embedding, feature embeddings, biLSTM vs uniLSTM) and no runtime or parameter-count comparisons.  

- How sensitive is performance to aligner choice/quality, alternative/degraded aligners, or joint alignment–transduction training?  
- What decoding strategy was used (greedy vs beam), what beam sizes were tried, and how are termination/invalid actions handled?  
- Are reported differences on Wiktionary and SIGMORPHON statistically significant?  
- Can the authors provide ablations for previous-output embeddings, feature embeddings, encoder type, and training without external alignments?  
- What are training and inference speeds and parameter counts relative to soft-attention baselines?  
- Can limited non-monotonicity (small backward moves or epsilon steps) be incorporated for typologically non-monotonic languages, and are there preliminary results?  

- Overall: clearly written and technically sound with broad empirical evaluation; main limitations are reliance on external alignments and missing decoding/ablation details.
