**Summary of the Paper**

This paper presents a neural model for morphological inflection generation that employs a hard attention mechanism, inspired by the nearly-monotonic alignment commonly found between the characters in a word and its inflection. The model consists of an encoder-decoder neural network with a dedicated control mechanism that determines whether to write an output symbol or advance the attention pointer to the next input state. The authors evaluate the model on three previously studied morphological inflection generation datasets and show that it provides state-of-the-art results in various setups compared to previous neural and non-neural approaches.

**Main Contributions**

1. **Hard Attention Model**: The authors propose a hard attention model for nearly-monotonic sequence-to-sequence learning, which is suitable for the morphological inflection generation task.
2. **State-of-the-Art Results**: The model achieves state-of-the-art results on three previously studied morphological inflection generation datasets, outperforming previous neural and non-neural approaches.
3. **Analysis of Learned Representations**: The authors analyze and compare the learned representations of the hard attention model and the soft attention model, shedding light on the features that these models extract for the inflection generation task.

**Strengths**

1. **Improved Performance**: The hard attention model achieves improved performance on morphological inflection generation tasks, especially on low-resource settings.
2. **Simplification of Model Training**: The use of independently learned alignments simplifies the model training process, enabling the use of correct alignments for the attention mechanism from the beginning of the network training phase.
3. **Insightful Analysis**: The authors provide an insightful analysis of the learned representations of the hard attention model and the soft attention model, which helps to understand the strengths and weaknesses of each approach.

**Weaknesses**

1. **Limited Generalizability**: The hard attention model may not generalize well to other sequence-to-sequence tasks that do not exhibit nearly-monotonic alignments.
2. **Dependence on Alignment Quality**: The performance of the hard attention model depends on the quality of the independently learned alignments, which may not always be accurate.
3. **Lack of Theoretical Analysis**: The paper lacks a theoretical analysis of the hard attention model, which would provide a deeper understanding of its strengths and limitations.

**Questions to Authors**

1. How do the authors plan to extend the hard attention model to other sequence-to-sequence tasks that do not exhibit nearly-monotonic alignments?
2. Can the authors provide more details on the character level alignment process used in the paper, and how it affects the performance of the hard attention model?
3. How do the authors plan to address the issue of dependence on alignment quality, and what measures can be taken to improve the accuracy of the independently learned alignments?
