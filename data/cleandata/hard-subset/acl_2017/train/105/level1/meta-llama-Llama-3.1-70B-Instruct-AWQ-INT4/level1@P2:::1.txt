**Summary of the Paper**

This paper proposes a neural model for morphological inflection generation that employs a hard attention mechanism, inspired by the nearly-monotonic alignment commonly found between the characters in a word and its inflection. The model is evaluated on three previously studied morphological inflection generation datasets and achieves state-of-the-art results in various setups compared to previous neural and non-neural approaches. The authors also perform an analysis of the continuous representations learned by both the hard and soft attention models for the task, shedding light on the features such models extract.

**Main Contributions**

1. The authors propose a hard attention model for nearly-monotonic sequence-to-sequence learning, which is well-suited for morphological inflection generation.
2. They evaluate the model on three previously studied morphological inflection generation datasets and achieve state-of-the-art results in various setups.
3. They perform an analysis of the continuous representations learned by both the hard and soft attention models for the task, providing insights into the features extracted by these models.

**Strengths**

1. **Improved performance on small datasets**: The authors demonstrate that their hard attention model outperforms previous neural and non-neural approaches on the CELEX dataset, which is a small dataset. This is a significant contribution, as many morphologically rich languages have limited training data.
2. **Robustness to varying dataset sizes**: The authors show that their model performs well on both small (CELEX) and large (Wiktionary) datasets, demonstrating its robustness to varying dataset sizes.
3. **Analysis of learned representations**: The authors provide a detailed analysis of the continuous representations learned by both the hard and soft attention models, shedding light on the features extracted by these models.

**Weaknesses**

1. **Limited linguistic analysis**: While the authors provide some linguistic analysis of the learned representations, they could have provided a more in-depth analysis of the linguistic implications of their findings.
2. **Comparison to other models**: The authors compare their model to only a few other models, and it would be useful to compare it to a wider range of models to better understand its strengths and weaknesses.
3. **Overfitting**: The authors mention that the soft attention model overfits on the small CELEX dataset, but they do not provide a detailed analysis of overfitting in their own model.

**Questions to Authors**

1. Can you provide more details on the linguistic implications of the learned representations, and how they relate to the morphological structure of the languages being modeled?
2. How does the hard attention model handle cases where the alignment is not monotonic, such as in languages with complex morphological systems?
3. Can you provide more details on the training procedure, including the hyperparameters used and the optimization algorithm employed?

**Additional Comments**

The authors provide a clear and well-written paper that makes a significant contribution to the field of morphological inflection generation. The use of hard attention is a novel approach that provides improved performance on small datasets, and the analysis of learned representations provides valuable insights into the features extracted by these models. Overall, this is a strong paper that is well-suited for publication in a top-tier NLP conference.
