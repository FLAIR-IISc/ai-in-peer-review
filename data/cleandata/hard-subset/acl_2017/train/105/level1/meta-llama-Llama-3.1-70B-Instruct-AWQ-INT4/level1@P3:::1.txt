**Summary of the Paper**

This paper proposes a neural model for morphological inflection generation using a hard attention mechanism. The model is designed to handle the task of generating a target word given a source word and morpho-syntactic attributes. The authors claim that their model provides state-of-the-art results on three previously studied datasets and performs significantly better with small training sets.

**Main Contributions**

1. A hard attention model for nearly-monotonic sequence to sequence learning, which is particularly suitable for morphological inflection generation.
2. An analysis of the continuous representations learned by both the hard and soft attention models, shedding light on the features extracted by these models for the inflection generation task.
3. State-of-the-art results on three previously studied morphological inflection generation datasets.

**Strengths**

1. **Effective handling of small training sets**: The proposed model outperforms previous neural and non-neural approaches on the CELEX dataset, which has a very small training set. This suggests that the model is robust to overfitting and can learn effectively from limited data.
2. **Improved performance on suffixing+stem-change languages**: The model performs better than soft-attention baselines on languages that employ suffixing morphology with internal stem changes, such as Russian, German, and Spanish.
3. **Simplification of model training**: The use of independently learned alignments to guide the network's attention simplifies the model training process and allows for the use of a simple cross-entropy loss function.
4. **Insightful analysis of learned representations**: The authors provide a detailed analysis of the continuous representations learned by both the hard and soft attention models, which sheds light on the features extracted by these models for the inflection generation task.

**Weaknesses**

1. **Limited generalizability to other languages**: While the model performs well on suffixing+stem-change languages, its performance is slightly lower on languages that employ more context-sensitive morphological phenomena, such as vowel harmony and consonant harmony.
2. **Dependence on pre-computed alignments**: The model relies on pre-computed alignments to guide the network's attention, which may not always be available or accurate.
3. **Lack of comparison to other neural models**: The authors do not compare their model to other neural models that use different attention mechanisms or architectures.
4. **Limited analysis of error types**: The authors do not provide a detailed analysis of the types of errors made by the model, which could provide insights into its limitations and areas for improvement.

**Questions to Authors**

1. How do the authors plan to extend their model to handle languages with more complex morphological phenomena, such as vowel harmony and consonant harmony?
2. Can the authors provide more details on the character-level alignment process used to guide the network's attention?
3. How do the authors plan to address the limitation of relying on pre-computed alignments, which may not always be available or accurate?
4. Can the authors provide more insights into the types of errors made by the model and how they plan to address these errors in future work?
