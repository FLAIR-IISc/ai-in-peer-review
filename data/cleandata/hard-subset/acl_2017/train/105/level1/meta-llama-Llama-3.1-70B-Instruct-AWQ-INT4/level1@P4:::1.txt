**Summary of the Paper**

This paper presents a neural model for morphological inflection generation, which employs a hard attention mechanism to model the nearly-monotonic alignment between the input and output character sequences. The model is evaluated on three previously studied morphological inflection generation datasets and shows state-of-the-art results in various setups. The authors also perform an analysis of the continuous representations learned by both the hard and soft attention models for the task.

**Main Contributions**

1. The authors propose a hard attention model for morphological inflection generation, which models the nearly-monotonic alignment between the input and output character sequences.
2. The model is evaluated on three previously studied morphological inflection generation datasets, showing state-of-the-art results in various setups.
3. The authors perform an analysis of the continuous representations learned by both the hard and soft attention models for the task, shedding light on the features such models extract for the inflection generation task.

**Strengths**

1. The proposed hard attention model is able to effectively capture the nearly-monotonic alignment between the input and output character sequences, leading to state-of-the-art results on various datasets.
2. The model is able to perform well even with small training sets, making it a robust solution for morphological inflection generation tasks.
3. The analysis of the continuous representations learned by both the hard and soft attention models provides valuable insights into the features such models extract for the inflection generation task.

**Weaknesses**

1. The authors do not provide a detailed comparison of their model with other state-of-the-art models, such as the neural-weighted FST model of Rastogi et al. (2016).
2. The model relies on a character-level alignment process, which may not be effective for all languages or morphological phenomena.
3. The authors do not provide a detailed analysis of the computational complexity of their model, which may be an important consideration for practical applications.

**Questions to Authors**

1. Can you provide a more detailed comparison of your model with other state-of-the-art models, such as the neural-weighted FST model of Rastogi et al. (2016)?
2. How does your model handle languages or morphological phenomena that do not exhibit nearly-monotonic alignment between the input and output character sequences?
3. Can you provide a detailed analysis of the computational complexity of your model, and how it compares to other state-of-the-art models?

**Additional Comments**

The paper is well-written and easy to follow, with clear explanations of the proposed model and its components. The evaluation results are impressive, and the analysis of the continuous representations learned by both the hard and soft attention models provides valuable insights into the features such models extract for the inflection generation task. However, the authors could benefit from providing a more detailed comparison of their model with other state-of-the-art models, as well as a more detailed analysis of the computational complexity of their model.
