**Summary**

This paper proposes a neural model for morphological inflection generation that employs a hard attention mechanism. The model is designed to handle the nearly-monotonic alignment commonly found between the characters in a word and its inflection. The authors evaluate the model on three previously studied morphological inflection generation datasets and show that it provides state-of-the-art results in various setups compared to previous neural and non-neural approaches.

**Main Contributions**

1. The authors propose a hard attention model for morphological inflection generation that leverages the nearly-monotonic alignment between input and output character sequences.
2. The model is evaluated on three previously studied datasets, including the CELEX, Wiktionary, and SIGMORPHON2016 datasets, and achieves state-of-the-art results.
3. The authors provide an analysis of the continuous representations learned by both the hard and soft attention models for the task, shedding light on the features such models extract for morphological inflection generation.

**Strengths**

1. The paper presents a novel approach to morphological inflection generation using hard attention, which is well-suited for tasks with nearly-monotonic alignment.
2. The authors provide a thorough evaluation of their model on multiple datasets, demonstrating its effectiveness and robustness.
3. The analysis of the learned representations provides valuable insights into the strengths and weaknesses of both hard and soft attention models for this task.

**Weaknesses**

1. The paper relies heavily on the assumption of nearly-monotonic alignment, which may not hold for all languages or morphological phenomena.
2. The authors do not provide a detailed comparison of their model with other state-of-the-art approaches, such as the Neural Transducer model.
3. The analysis of the learned representations is limited to a small subset of the data and may not be representative of the entire dataset.

**Questions to Authors**

1. How does the model perform on languages with non-monotonic alignment, such as those with vowel harmony or consonant harmony?
2. Can the authors provide a more detailed comparison of their model with other state-of-the-art approaches, including the Neural Transducer model?
3. How do the learned representations change when the model is trained on larger datasets or with different hyperparameters?
