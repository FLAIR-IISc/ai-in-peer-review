This paper presents a neural model for morphological inflection generation utilizing a hard attention mechanism. The authors argue that the nearly-monotonic alignment between input and output characters in morphological inflection lends itself well to a hard attention approach, and demonstrate state-of-the-art results on three datasets (CELEX, Wiktionary, SIGMORPHON2016). The key contributions are: 1) A novel hard attention model tailored for morphological inflection, exploiting the near-monotonicity of the task; 2) Strong empirical results, achieving state-of-the-art performance on multiple datasets, particularly demonstrating improvement on the challenging CELEX dataset; and 3) An analysis comparing the hard and soft attention models, offering insights into the learned representations.

**Strengths:**

1. **Addressing a Key Limitation of Neural Models:** The authors directly address the issue of data sparsity in morphological inflection, a well-known challenge for neural sequence-to-sequence models. The hard attention mechanism demonstrably improves performance on the small CELEX dataset, surpassing previous models specifically designed for this scenario, which is a significant achievement. This highlights the benefit of incorporating inductive biases (monotonicity) into the model.
2. **Strong Empirical Validation:** The paper provides a comprehensive experimental evaluation on three distinct datasets, covering varying levels of morphological complexity and data size. Achieving state-of-the-art results on these benchmarks strengthens the claim that the proposed model is effective and generalizable. The detailed comparisons with existing neural and non-neural baselines are also valuable.
3. **Insightful Analysis of Learned Representations:**  The analysis comparing hard and soft attention models, including the visualization of learned representations, provides a deeper understanding of how the models function. The observation that hard attention encodes less explicit positional information, relying instead on the attention mechanism itself, is a noteworthy finding. This helps explain the model’s strengths and weaknesses.
4. **Clear and Well-Structured Presentation:** The paper is generally well-written and clearly structured. The motivation is well-explained, the model architecture is described in detail, and the experimental setup is clearly laid out.  Figure 1 effectively illustrates the hard attention mechanism.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Novelty of the Hard Attention Mechanism:** While applied to a new domain, the core hard attention mechanism itself is not novel. It builds directly upon existing work (e.g., the work it cites).  The primary novelty lies in the *application* of hard attention specifically to morphological inflection and the insightful observation of its suitability given the task’s characteristics.  The authors acknowledge this, but it still weakens the overall contribution.
2. **Dependence on Pre-Computed Alignments:** The training procedure relies on independently learned character-level alignments. While the authors justify this approach, it introduces an external dependency and potentially limits the end-to-end learnability of the model. The performance is therefore tied to the quality of the alignment model used and may not generalize well to languages where accurate character-level alignment is difficult to obtain. The paper would benefit from a discussion of how the model might perform if trained with weaker or noisier alignments.
3. **Analysis Scope Could Be Broadened:** While the representation analysis is a good start, it could be expanded. For example, it would be interesting to investigate how the model handles non-monotonic dependencies or long-range interactions, given the bi-directional encoder. A more detailed error analysis, identifying specific types of inflection errors, would also be valuable.
4. **SIGMORPHON results discussion:** The authors state that their soft attention model outperforms MED on SIGMORPHON, attributing it to longer training. However, they don't discuss the potential impact of hyperparameter tuning or model size differences. A more thorough comparison of these factors is needed.

**Questions to Authors:**

1. How sensitive is the performance of the model to the quality of the pre-computed character-level alignments?  Have you experimented with using noisier alignments, or with learning the alignments jointly with the transduction process (even if it's less effective)?
2. The paper mentions that the hard attention model may be particularly suitable for suffixing languages. Could you provide more quantitative evidence supporting this claim? Perhaps a breakdown of performance across different language families in the SIGMORPHON dataset?
3.  Could you elaborate on the computational cost of the hard attention mechanism compared to the soft attention mechanism?  Is there a significant difference in training or inference time?
4. The supplementary material is referenced but not provided, can you provide it?

**Additional Comments:**

* It would be helpful to include a table summarizing the hyperparameters used for each experiment.
* The paper could benefit from a more comprehensive discussion of related work on hard attention mechanisms in other sequence transduction tasks.
* The github link to the source code is removed for anonymity. This is appreciated, but makes reproducibility difficult for reviewers.
