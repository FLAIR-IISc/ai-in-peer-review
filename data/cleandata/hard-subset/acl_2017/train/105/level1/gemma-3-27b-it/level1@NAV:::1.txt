## Review of "Morphological Inflection Generation with Hard Monotonic Attention"

This paper presents a novel neural model for morphological inflection generation leveraging a hard attention mechanism designed to exploit the near-monotonic alignment commonly found in this task. The model is evaluated on three standard datasets (CELEX, Wiktionary, and SIGMORPHON2016), achieving state-of-the-art results and demonstrating particular strength with limited training data. The authors also provide an analysis comparing their model to a soft-attention baseline, offering insights into learned representations.

The primary contribution of this work is the successful application of a hard attention mechanism to morphological inflection. While attention mechanisms have been used previously (Kann & Schütze, 2016), the hard attention approach, coupled with a dedicated control mechanism for step/write actions, effectively enforces a monotonic alignment, proving particularly beneficial in low-resource scenarios like the CELEX dataset. A second, significant contribution lies in the strong empirical performance across all three datasets, consistently outperforming previous state-of-the-art approaches. Finally, the analysis of learned representations, comparing the hard and soft attention models, provides a valuable qualitative understanding of the differing encoding strategies employed by each.

**Strengths:**

1. **Strong Empirical Results:** The model consistently achieves state-of-the-art or competitive results on all three benchmark datasets. The significant improvement on the CELEX dataset, overcoming limitations of previous neural models in low-data regimes, is particularly compelling.
2. **Novel Hard Attention Approach:** The proposed hard attention mechanism is a well-motivated and effective adaptation to the characteristics of morphological inflection. The explicit control mechanism promotes a more interpretable alignment and appears to alleviate overfitting.
3. **Comprehensive Evaluation:** The paper provides a thorough evaluation, including comparisons to both neural and non-neural baselines, and considers datasets of varying sizes and linguistic characteristics.
4. **Insightful Analysis:** The analysis of learned representations offers valuable insights into how the model encodes morphological information and highlights the differing strategies of hard and soft attention.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Reliance on Pre-trained Alignments:** The model’s reliance on pre-computed character-level alignments, while simplifying training, introduces an external dependency. The performance is therefore partly reliant on the accuracy of the alignment tool and could be improved with an end-to-end trainable approach.
2. **Limited Discussion of Error Analysis:** While the paper reports strong overall performance, a more detailed error analysis would be beneficial to identify specific failure cases and potential areas for improvement. Are there specific morphological processes that the model struggles with?
3. **SIGMORPHON Results Context:** While competitive on SIGMORPHON, the authors acknowledge their implementation of the soft-attention baseline is stronger than the published MED system. A more direct comparison to the original, published results would provide a clearer picture of the hard attention model’s advantages.

**Questions to Authors:**

1. How sensitive is the model’s performance to the quality of the pre-computed character-level alignments? Have you experimented with different alignment tools or methods?
2. Could you provide a more detailed error analysis, focusing on specific types of morphological errors the model makes? 
3. What is the computational cost of the hard attention mechanism compared to soft attention, and how does it scale with sequence length?
