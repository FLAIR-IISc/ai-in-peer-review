- Proposes a hard attention mechanism that models near-monotonic character alignments for morphological inflection.  
- Achieves state-of-the-art or competitive results on CELEX, Wiktionary, and SIGMORPHON2016, notably strong on low-resource CELEX.  
- Provides comparative analysis showing differences in learned representations between hard and soft attention, noting reduced need for positional encoding in hard attention.  
- Tackles data hunger of seq2seq models, improving low-resource performance via the hard attention design.  
- Leverages near-monotonicity as a well-motivated inductive bias that outperforms unconstrained attention.  
- Evaluates comprehensively across diverse datasets, demonstrating generalizability and robustness.  
- Offers insightful analysis explaining performance differences and the role of positional encoding.  
- Paper is clearly written with helpful figures, especially Figure 1 illustrating hard attention.  
- Relies on pre-computed character alignments (Sudoh et al. 2013), introducing an external dependency and limiting end-to-end alignment learning.  
- Lacks detailed analysis of alignment errors and their impact on transduction performance.  
- Does not compare against some recent stronger neural baselines (e.g., subword/BPE or WordPiece models).  
- Omits thorough hyperparameter sensitivity analysis, reducing reproducibility insight.  
- Provides limited qualitative error analysis to reveal systematic failure modes.  
- Asks about robustness to noisy or low-quality pre-alignments and sensitivity to alignment quality.  
- Requests explanation of challenges in jointly learning alignments with transduction and rationale for separate alignment.  
- Seeks details on hyperparameter tuning and which settings critically affect performance.  
- Asks for example failure cases and which morphological processes the model struggles with.  
- Inquires whether subword units (BPE/WordPiece) were tried and what the outcomes were.  
- Notes removal of a GitHub link for anonymization.  
- Points out inconsistent citation style (e.g., stray “?” after years).  
- Suggests adding discussion of computational cost of hard attention versus soft attention.
