This paper presents a novel neural model for morphological inflection generation, leveraging a hard attention mechanism to model the near-monotonic alignment inherent in this task. The core idea – enforcing a more structured attention pattern – is well-motivated and the experimental results are compelling, demonstrating state-of-the-art performance on several datasets, particularly excelling in low-resource scenarios.

**Contributions:**

1. **Hard Attention Model for Morphological Inflection:** The primary contribution is the introduction of a hard attention mechanism specifically tailored for morphological inflection. This approach, which explicitly models a nearly-monotonic alignment, offers a more interpretable and potentially more efficient alternative to soft attention.
2. **Strong Empirical Results:** The paper demonstrates substantial improvements over existing state-of-the-art methods, especially on the challenging CELEX dataset, effectively addressing the limitations of previous neural models in low-data regimes. This is a significant practical contribution.
3. **Analysis of Learned Representations:** The included analysis comparing the learned representations of the hard and soft attention models provides valuable insight into the behavior of these models and highlights how they encode morphological information differently.

**Strengths:**

1. **Novelty and Motivation:** The proposed hard attention mechanism is a novel adaptation to the inflection generation task. The motivation, stemming from the observed near-monotonic alignment, is clear and well-justified. It logically addresses a known weakness of standard sequence-to-sequence models – their tendency to overfit with limited data.
2. **Strong Performance on CELEX:** The consistently superior performance on the CELEX dataset, where previous neural models struggled, is a particularly strong argument for acceptance. This demonstrates the model’s ability to generalize effectively with scarce training data. Surpassing the latent variable WFST model is a notable achievement.
3. **Comprehensive Evaluation:** The evaluation across three datasets (CELEX, Wiktionary, and SIGMORPHON2016) strengthens the findings and demonstrates the model's generalizability. The inclusion of results on a diverse set of languages (SIGMORPHON2016) is also commendable.
4. **Clear Explanation & Well-Structured Presentation:** The paper is well-written and clearly explains the model architecture and training procedure. The inclusion of Figure 1 is particularly helpful in visualizing the hard attention mechanism.
5. **Insightful Analysis:** The analysis of learned representations, comparing hard and soft attention models, provides useful insights into how these models process morphological information. The discussion regarding positional encoding is particularly interesting.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Dependency on Pre-Aligned Data:** The reliance on pre-computed character-level alignments for training introduces an external dependency. While the authors acknowledge this, it raises questions about the model’s ability to learn alignments from scratch and its robustness to errors in the pre-alignment process. The performance is only as good as the initial alignment.
2. **Limited Explanation of Alignment Model:** The description of the character-level alignment model used (Sudoh et al., 2013) is brief. Providing more detail about this model and its potential limitations would strengthen the paper.
3. **SIGMORPHON2016 Performance Nuances:** While good overall, the model doesn't universally outperform on SIGMORPHON2016, excelling on suffixing languages but lagging on others. A deeper exploration of this discrepancy and the linguistic characteristics influencing performance would be beneficial. A more direct comparison to the original MED system’s training procedure would also strengthen this claim.
4. **Potential for Simplification:**  While the model achieves good results, the architecture seems somewhat complex, with numerous components (bi-directional RNN, hard attention, recurrent decoder with feedback connection). Exploring potential simplifications without significant performance degradation could improve the model’s practicality.

**Questions to Authors:**

1. How sensitive is the model’s performance to the quality of the pre-computed character-level alignments? Have you conducted experiments with noisy or imperfect alignments to assess its robustness?
2. Can you elaborate on the choice of the Sudoh et al. (2013) alignment model? Were other alignment models considered, and if so, why was this one selected?
3. What specific linguistic properties of the languages in SIGMORPHON2016 contribute to the observed differences in performance between your model and the soft attention baseline?
4. Have you explored any simpler architectures for the hard attention model, such as removing the feedback connection in the decoder? What impact did these simplifications have on performance?

**Additional Comments:**

*   The reference to the GitHub repository is helpful for reproducibility, but it was removed for anonymization.
*   The discussion of related work is thorough and provides a good context for the presented research.



Overall, this is a strong paper that presents a novel and effective approach to morphological inflection generation. The experimental results are compelling, and the analysis provides valuable insights. While the dependency on pre-aligned data and the nuances in SIGMORPHON2016 performance are potential weaknesses, they do not detract significantly from the overall contribution. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions addressing the questions raised above.
