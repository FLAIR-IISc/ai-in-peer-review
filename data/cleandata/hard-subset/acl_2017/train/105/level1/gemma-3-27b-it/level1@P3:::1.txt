This paper presents a novel neural model for morphological inflection generation leveraging a hard attention mechanism designed to capture the nearly-monotonic alignment between input and output characters. The authors demonstrate state-of-the-art performance on three established datasets, and provide an insightful analysis comparing their model to a soft-attention baseline.

**Main Contributions:**

1. **Hard Attention Mechanism for Morphological Inflection:** The core contribution lies in the proposed hard attention mechanism, explicitly modeling the almost-monotonic alignment inherent in morphological inflection. This is a well-motivated design choice and effectively leverages the structural properties of the task.
2. **Strong Empirical Results:** The model achieves state-of-the-art or competitive results on the CELEX, Wiktionary, and SIGMORPHON2016 datasets. Critically, the model outperforms previous approaches, including dedicated finite-state models, on the low-resource CELEX dataset – a significant achievement.
3. **Analysis of Learned Representations:** The comparative analysis between the hard and soft attention models offers valuable insights into the features extracted by each approach. The observation about positional encoding being less critical in the hard attention model due to the explicit step action is particularly interesting.

**Strengths:**

1. **Addresses a Key Limitation of Sequence-to-Sequence Models:** The authors directly address the data hunger of standard sequence-to-sequence models, particularly relevant for morphologically rich languages where datasets are often limited. The hard attention mechanism demonstrably improves performance in low-resource scenarios.
2. **Well-Motivated Design Choice:** The exploitation of near-monotonicity in morphological inflection is a logical and effective approach. The hard attention mechanism is well-suited for enforcing this constraint, providing a clear advantage over unconstrained attention mechanisms.
3. **Comprehensive Evaluation:** The thorough evaluation across three diverse datasets—ranging in size and linguistic characteristics—provides strong evidence for the model's generalizability and robustness. The inclusion of SIGMORPHON2016 showcases performance across a variety of languages.
4. **Insightful Analysis:** The analysis of learned representations is not just descriptive but offers explanations for the observed performance differences between the hard and soft attention models. The discussion around positional encoding is particularly compelling.
5. **Clear and Well-Written:** The paper is generally well-written and clearly explains the model architecture, training procedure, and experimental setup. Figure 1 is particularly helpful in illustrating the hard attention mechanism.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Dependence on Pre-Aligned Data:** The reliance on pre-computed character-level alignments for training (using the Sudoh et al. 2013 aligner) is a notable limitation. While the authors justify this design choice, it introduces an external dependency and potentially limits the model's ability to learn alignments independently. A more robust evaluation would include experiments with imperfect or noisy alignments. The authors state this simplifies training, but the complexity is merely *shifted* to the alignment phase.
2. **Limited Discussion of Alignment Error:** The paper briefly mentions the quality of the pre-aligned data but lacks a detailed analysis of alignment errors and their potential impact on model performance. Exploring the types of errors made by the alignment model and their propagation to the transduction task would be valuable.
3. **Comparison to Stronger Baselines:** While the comparison to state-of-the-art methods is solid, recent advances in neural morphology (e.g., models incorporating subword units or more sophisticated attention mechanisms) could provide more challenging baselines. Specifically, investigating the performance against models utilizing Byte Pair Encoding (BPE) or WordPiece might be beneficial.
4. **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** The paper does not thoroughly discuss the sensitivity of the model to hyperparameter choices. A more detailed analysis of hyperparameter tuning and its impact on performance would enhance the reproducibility and generalizability of the results. The experiments seem to have used default parameters for the LSTM layers.
5. **Qualitative Error Analysis:** A more detailed qualitative error analysis (e.g., examining examples where the model fails and identifying patterns) would provide deeper insights into the model's strengths and weaknesses.

**Questions to Authors:**

1. How sensitive is the model performance to the quality of the pre-aligned training data? Did you experiment with adding noise or errors to the alignments to assess robustness?
2. What were the primary challenges encountered when attempting to learn the alignments jointly with transduction, and why was the independent alignment approach ultimately chosen?
3. Could you elaborate on the hyperparameter tuning process? Were any specific hyperparameters found to be particularly critical for performance?
4. Can you provide examples of failure cases, and what types of morphological processes does the model struggle with the most?
5. Did you explore using subword units (e.g., BPE or WordPiece) within the model, and if so, what were the results?

**Additional Comments:**

* The reference to a GitHub link is removed, as requested for anonymization.
* The citation style is inconsistent (e.g., some citations include “?” after the year).
* It would be helpful to briefly discuss the computational cost of the hard attention mechanism compared to soft attention.
