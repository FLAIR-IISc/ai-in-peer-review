This paper presents a neural model for morphological inflection generation using a hard attention mechanism. The core idea is to exploit the nearly-monotonic alignment commonly found between input and output characters in morphological inflection. The model is evaluated on three datasets (CELEX, Wiktionary, and SIGMORPHON2016) and achieves state-of-the-art results on several setups, particularly demonstrating strong performance on the small CELEX dataset. The authors also provide an analysis comparing their hard attention model to a soft attention model, shedding light on the learned representations.

The primary contributions of this work, as I see them, are:

1. **A novel hard attention mechanism specifically tailored for morphological inflection.** The proposed model directly models the nearly-monotonic alignment, which is a key characteristic of this task, and leverages this structure for improved performance. This is a reasonable and potentially impactful approach.
2. **Strong empirical results, particularly on low-resource data.** The model demonstrates state-of-the-art or competitive performance across three datasets, and importantly, outperforms previous models on the small CELEX dataset, where data sparsity is a significant challenge. This suggests the method is effective even with limited training data.
3. **Insightful analysis of learned representations.** The comparison of learned representations between the hard and soft attention models provides valuable understanding of how each model approaches the task and what features they encode. This adds to the broader understanding of neural sequence transduction.

**Strengths:**

1. **Addressing a specific task characteristic:** The hard attention mechanism is well-motivated by the nearly-monotonic alignment inherent in morphological inflection. This focused design is a strong point. The justification in the introduction is clear.
2. **Performance on CELEX:** The consistent outperformance on the CELEX dataset is a significant result. This dataset is known to be challenging, and surpassing previous models, including specialized finite-state approaches, is a strong indicator of the model's effectiveness.
3. **Comprehensive evaluation:** The evaluation on three datasets with varying characteristics (size, language diversity) provides a thorough assessment of the model's generalization ability.
4. **Clear and detailed explanation of the model:** The paper provides a well-structured and comprehensive explanation of the model architecture, training procedure, and experimental setup. The diagrams (particularly Figure 1) are helpful for understanding the hard attention mechanism.
5. **Analysis of attention patterns and learned representations:** The analysis comparing the hard and soft attention models provides valuable insights into the internal workings of each model and how they capture morphological information.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited novelty in the core architecture:** While the application of hard attention to morphological inflection is novel, the underlying encoder-decoder framework with attention is not. The core building blocks are established components in neural machine translation and sequence-to-sequence learning. The paper could benefit from more clearly articulating how the hard attention mechanism fundamentally differs from and improves upon existing attention mechanisms beyond simply enforcing monotonicity.
2. **Dependence on pre-trained alignments:** The training procedure relies on independently learned character-level alignments. While the authors justify this choice, it introduces an external dependency and potentially limits the end-to-end learning capability of the model. The impact of alignment quality on overall performance isn’t fully discussed.
3. **Analysis depth:** While the analysis of learned representations is a good start, it could be expanded. For example, exploring the activations of the decoder at different stages of the transduction process, or visualizing the attention weights for specific examples, could provide more granular insights.
4. **Hyperparameter sensitivity:** The paper does not provide a thorough analysis of the sensitivity of the model to different hyperparameters. This makes it difficult to assess the robustness of the results and to determine the optimal configuration for different datasets.
5. **SIGMORPHON results:** The soft attention model outperforms the hard attention model on the SIGMORPHON dataset, suggesting that the monotonic alignment assumption may not hold universally across all languages and morphological systems. A deeper discussion of the linguistic characteristics of the languages where the hard attention model underperforms would be valuable.

**Questions to Authors:**

1. How sensitive is the model's performance to the quality of the pre-trained character-level alignments? Have you explored different alignment methods or techniques for improving alignment accuracy?
2. Could the performance gap on SIGMORPHON be explained by differences in the degree of non-monotonicity in the morphological processes of the different languages? Can you provide a quantitative measure of monotonicity for each language?
3. What is the computational cost of the hard attention mechanism compared to the soft attention mechanism? Does the hard attention mechanism offer any advantages in terms of training or inference speed?
4. How were the hyperparameters chosen? Was a systematic hyperparameter search performed, and if so, what were the key findings?
