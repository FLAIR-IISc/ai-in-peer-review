- Summary: Proposes a neural encoder–decoder with hard monotonic attention that emits WRITE or STEP actions trained on pre-computed character alignments; shows strong results (especially low-resource) and compares learned representations to soft-attention.

- Contribution 1: Introduces a hard monotonic attention model for morphological inflection that decouples alignment from transduction and outperforms prior baselines on low-resource CELEX.

- Contribution 2: Provides comprehensive empirical evaluation across CELEX, Wiktionary, and SIGMORPHON 2016, establishing competitive or state-of-the-art results.

- Contribution 3: Offers comparative analysis and visualizations contrasting hard-attention and soft-attention learned representations and alignments.

- Strength 1: Strong empirical performance in low-resource settings, notably surpassing a strong WFST baseline on CELEX and showing less overfitting than soft-attention.

- Strength 2: Well-motivated, simple architecture (WRITE/STEP) that leverages precomputed alignments to avoid difficult joint alignment/transduction optimization.

- Strength 3: Clear exposition and useful qualitative analyses (alignment visualizations, SVD of encoder states) that shed light on attention effects.

- Weakness 1: Relies on an external aligner (Sudoh et al. 2013), risking error propagation and reducing end-to-endness; sensitivity to aligner quality is not explored.

- Weakness 2: Assumes near-monotonicity, limiting applicability to languages/phenomena with non-monotonic processes (e.g., templatic morphology, infixation); SIGMORPHON results reflect this.

- Weakness 3: Core idea is conceptually incremental relative to prior hard/monotonic models (e.g., RNN Transducer); clearer positioning and comparisons would help.

- Question 1: How sensitive is final performance to aligner quality (e.g., using simpler Levenshtein-based alignments)?

- Question 2: Why is the soft-attention decoder better for long-range phenomena (like vowel harmony) given a bi-LSTM encoder already has full input context—i.e., is the decoder’s weighted access to the whole input the key advantage?

- Question 3: Please clarify the deterministic rules for converting alignments to WRITE/STEP oracle sequences, especially handling deletions (input→null) and insertions (null→output).
