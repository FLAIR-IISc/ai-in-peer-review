**Summary of the paper**
This paper presents a neural model for morphological inflection using a novel hard monotonic attention. Instead of a soft alignment, the decoder uses an explicit `step` action to advance a pointer over the input. The model is trained on action sequences derived from pre-computed alignments, simplifying the learning objective to cross-entropy loss.

**Main Contributions**
1.  The primary contribution is the hard monotonic attention model, which incorporates a strong inductive bias for sequential transduction tasks. This design is particularly effective in low-resource settings, mitigating the overfitting seen in standard soft-attention models.
2.  The paper provides a strong empirical evaluation establishing new state-of-the-art results across three diverse benchmarks (CELEX, Wiktionary, SIGMORPHON).
3.  An insightful analysis compares the learned representations of hard and soft attention, clarifying how the proposed model explicitly handles positional information.

**Strengths**
1.  The model demonstrates excellent empirical performance. The result on the low-resource CELEX dataset is particularly compelling, as it is the first neural model to outperform a specialized latent-variable FST baseline, validating the model’s inductive bias.
2.  The model's design is conceptually simple and elegant. Decoupling alignment from transduction simplifies the training procedure, avoiding complex joint-learning objectives while effectively capturing the problem's structure.
3.  The analysis in Section 5 is a major strength. The visualizations provide a convincing explanation for the model’s effectiveness, showing how the `step` action allows the encoder to focus more on character identity than position.

**Weaknesses**
1.  The model's assumption of near-monotonicity limits its applicability for languages with non-concatenative morphology (e.g., templatic systems), a weakness suggested by its performance on some SIGMORPHON languages.
2.  The system depends on an external character aligner, and the paper does not explore the model's sensitivity to this component. This makes the approach less end-to-end and introduces a potential point of failure.
3.  The authors' own soft-attention baseline achieves superior results on several morphologically complex languages, which tempers the SOTA claims and suggests the model's advantages are most pronounced for a specific class of morphological phenomena.
