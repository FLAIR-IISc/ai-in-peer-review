**Summary of the Paper**
This paper proposes a neural encoder-decoder model for morphological inflection generation that utilizes a hard, monotonic attention mechanism. The model is motivated by the observation that character alignments in this task are nearly monotonic. Instead of learning a soft, weighted attention over all input characters at each step, the proposed model's decoder learns to perform one of two actions: `WRITE` an output character or `STEP` to advance its attention to the next character in the encoded input sequence. A key aspect of the methodology is that the model is not trained to jointly learn alignments and transductions. Instead, character-level alignments are generated for the training data using an external tool, and these alignments are used to create a "gold" sequence of `WRITE` and `STEP` actions, which the model is then trained to predict using a standard cross-entropy loss. The model is evaluated on three benchmark datasets (CELEX, Wiktionary, SIGMORPHON 2016), demonstrating strong performance, particularly in low-resource settings. The paper also includes an analysis of the learned alignments and representations in comparison to a standard soft-attention model.

**Contributions**
1.  The primary contribution is the development and successful application of a hard monotonic attention model for morphological inflection. The model's design, which decouples alignment from transduction by training on pre-computed alignments, proves particularly effective in low-resource scenarios. The result on the CELEX dataset, where this is the first neural model to surpass the strong latent-variable WFST baseline, is a significant empirical contribution.
2.  A secondary contribution is a comprehensive empirical evaluation across three standard datasets with varying sizes and linguistic properties. This evaluation establishes the proposed model as a new state-of-the-art or highly competitive method for the task.
3.  The paper provides a useful comparative analysis between the proposed hard attention model and a standard soft-attention model. The visualization of learned representations offers valuable insights into how different attention mechanisms may influence what an encoder learns, particularly regarding positional information.

**Strengths**
1.  **Strong Empirical Performance in a Low-Resource Setting:** The model achieves state-of-the-art results on the CELEX dataset, a key benchmark for low-resource inflection generation. As detailed in Table 1, it outperforms not only recent neural approaches (MED, NWFST) but also the specialized latent-variable WFST model of Dreyer et al. (2008), which previous neural models had struggled to beat. The learning curves in Figure 2 provide compelling evidence that the hard attention model is less prone to overfitting on small data compared to a soft-attention baseline, which is a significant advantage.
2.  **Well-Motivated and Simple Architecture:** The model's design is well-justified by the nature of the task. Morphological inflection often involves prefixing, suffixing, and stem changes, which can be modeled effectively as a monotonic transduction. The decision to model this explicitly with `WRITE` and `STEP` actions is intuitive. Furthermore, by using pre-computed alignments to create an oracle for training, the model avoids the potentially difficult optimization problem of jointly learning to align and transduce, and instead uses a simple and stable cross-entropy loss. This design choice appears key to its success on small datasets.
3.  **Clarity and Analysis:** The paper is well-written, and the model is described clearly, aided by the diagram in Figure 1. The analysis in Section 5 adds significant value by moving beyond accuracy scores to investigate *why* the model behaves as it does. The comparison of learned alignments (Figure 3) and the SVD projection of encoder states (Figure 4) provide insightful, albeit qualitative, evidence that the hard attention model learns more focused character representations while the soft-attention model appears to bake positional information into its representations to aid the attention calculation. This analysis contributes to a better understanding of attention mechanisms in sequence-to-sequence models.

**Weaknesses**
1.  **Dependence on an External Aligner:** The proposed method is a two-stage pipeline that relies on an external tool to generate character alignments. This has two potential drawbacks. First, the model's performance is contingent on the quality of this external aligner; errors made by the aligner may propagate or limit the upper bound of the model's performance. The paper does not investigate the sensitivity of the model to the quality of the pre-computed alignments. Second, this requirement makes the system less end-to-end than competing models that jointly learn alignments, adding a dependency and a preprocessing step.
2.  **Limited Applicability to Non-Monotonic Phenomena:** The model's core assumption is that of a nearly-monotonic alignment. While this holds for many languages and morphological processes, it is a strong constraint that may limit its effectiveness on languages with significant non-monotonic processes (e.g., templatic morphology, infixation). The results on the SIGMORPHON dataset (Table 3) appear to support this limitation; while the model excels on suffixing languages, it is outperformed by a soft-attention baseline on a more typologically diverse set of languages. This weakness constrains the generality of the proposed approach.
3.  **Novelty of the Core Mechanism:** The idea of a hard, monotonic attention is not entirely new and builds on prior work such as the RNN Transducer (Graves, 2012) and attention models for speech recognition (Chorowski et al., 2015). The model can be viewed as an instance of a transition-based transducer trained with an oracle derived from an external tool. While the specific implementation and its successful application to this task are novel, the conceptual contribution over related work could be positioned more clearly. For example, a more detailed comparison to the RNN Transducer, which also assumes monotonicity but is trained differently (marginalizing over alignments), would be beneficial.

**Questions to Authors**
1.  Your model's performance is dependent on the character alignments produced by the Sudoh et al. (2013) system. How sensitive is the final inflection accuracy to the quality of these alignments? For instance, what is the performance impact of using a simpler alignment method, such as one based on Levenshtein distance, to generate the oracle action sequences?
2.  In the SIGMORPHON experiments, you suggest that the soft-attention model is better at handling phenomena like vowel harmony due to its ability to model long-range dependencies. However, your bi-directional LSTM encoder also has access to the entire input context at each position. Could you elaborate on why the soft-attention *decoder* is better suited for these phenomena? Is the key advantage its ability to access a weighted summary of the entire input at each decoding step, as opposed to the hard-attention model's focus on a single input position?
3.  The procedure for converting a character alignment into a deterministic sequence of `STEP` and `WRITE` actions seems crucial. Could you clarify the deterministic rules used? Specifically, how are deletions (input character aligned to null) and insertions (output character aligned to null) handled when generating the oracle sequence?
