### Summary and Contributions

This paper introduces a neural encoder-decoder model for morphological inflection generation that utilizes a "hard monotonic attention" mechanism. The core idea is to reframe the transduction task as predicting a sequence of actions: either `WRITE` an output character or `STEP` to advance a pointer to the next input character. This explicitly models the nearly monotonic character alignment typical of morphological processes. A key aspect of the methodology is the decoupling of alignment and transduction; an external character aligner is first used to create a gold sequence of `WRITE`/`STEP` actions for training, which allows the model to be trained simply with a cross-entropy loss. The authors evaluate their model on three standard datasets (CELEX, Wiktionary, SIGMORPHON 2016), demonstrating strong performance.

In my view, the main contributions of this work are:

1.  **An effective and simple model for monotonic sequence transduction.** The formulation of the task as learning a sequence of `WRITE` and `STEP` operations is elegant, interpretable, and provides a very strong inductive bias that is well-suited for morphological inflection. By pre-computing alignments, the model simplifies the learning problem and avoids the complexities of jointly learning to align and transduce.
2.  **New state-of-the-art results on key benchmarks, especially in a low-resource setting.** The paper's most significant result is outperforming the strong, task-specific latent-variable FST model of Dreyer et al. (2008) on the small CELEX dataset. This directly addresses a known weakness of standard neural sequence-to-sequence models, which often overfit on small training sets.
3.  **An insightful comparative analysis of hard vs. soft attention.** The analysis in Section 5, which visualizes both the alignments and the learned encoder representations, provides valuable insights into the differing behaviors of the two attention mechanisms. It persuasively argues that the explicit `STEP` action allows the hard attention model's encoder to learn more position-invariant character representations, contributing to its robustness.

### Strengths

1.  **Excellent Empirical Performance and Generalization:** The model achieves state-of-the-art or highly competitive results across three datasets with varying sizes and linguistic properties. The performance on the low-resource CELEX dataset is particularly compelling. Figure 2 clearly shows that the hard monotonic constraint acts as a powerful regularizer, mitigating the overfitting that plagues the soft-attention model on the same data. This is a significant practical advantage.
2.  **Model Simplicity and Interpretability:** The proposed architecture is conceptually simple and elegant. It replaces the computationally intensive weighted-sum context vector of soft attention with a straightforward pointer mechanism. This not only simplifies the model but also makes its decision-making process highly interpretable—one can directly inspect the sequence of `STEP` and `WRITE` actions to understand how an output was generated. The training procedure, based on a simple cross-entropy loss against a pre-computed oracle, is also much simpler than methods that require marginalizing over latent alignments (e.g., RNN-T).
3.  **Thorough Evaluation and Analysis:** The authors have conducted a comprehensive evaluation against a well-chosen set of strong neural and non-neural baselines. The inclusion of the analysis section significantly strengthens the paper. The qualitative comparison of alignment matrices and the SVD visualization of learned representations move the paper beyond simply reporting scores and offer a plausible explanation for *why* the proposed model works so well, particularly its ability to disentangle character identity from positional information.

### Weaknesses

1.  **Critical Dependency on an External Aligner:** The model's success hinges on the quality of the alignments produced by an external tool. This decoupling is presented as a strength for simplifying training, but it is also a potential point of failure. The model cannot recover from alignment errors made by this upstream component, as it is trained to mimic the derived oracle. The paper lacks a discussion on the model's sensitivity to alignment quality. This dependency may limit the model's applicability to languages or tasks where high-quality character aligners are not readily available or are difficult to train.
2.  **Inherent Limitation to Monotonic Phenomena:** The model's design enforces a strict left-to-right, monotonic traversal of the input. While the bi-directional encoder captures local context, the attention mechanism itself cannot handle non-monotonic processes like long-distance metathesis, infixation, or full-word reduplication. The results on the SIGMORPHON dataset already hint at this weakness, where the model's advantage diminishes for languages with non-concatenative morphology (e.g., Arabic) or long-distance harmony. The paper acknowledges this but could be more explicit about the architectural limitations. The claim that the bi-directional encoder allows it to capture "non-monotone relations" is somewhat misleading, as the core generative process remains strictly monotonic.
3.  **Positioning with Respect to Broader "Hard Attention" Literature:** While the application to morphology is novel, the paper could better contextualize its contribution within the wider literature on hard and monotonic attention mechanisms. For instance, the RNN Transducer (Graves, 2012) also models monotonic alignment, and various hard attention mechanisms have been proposed in computer vision and speech. The key novelty here appears to be the training strategy: using a pre-computed oracle to train a hard monotonic system with a simple supervised loss. This is a clever and effective simplification, but its relationship to and distinction from prior work on monotonic attention could be clarified to better frame the conceptual contribution.

### Questions to Authors

1.  Could you comment on the model's robustness to the quality of the external aligner? Have you analyzed the effect of alignment errors on final performance, or experimented with a simpler alignment method (e.g., based on Levenshtein distance) to generate the oracle? Understanding this sensitivity seems crucial for assessing the model's practical deployment.
2.  The model's performance relative to soft attention declines on languages with non-concatenative phenomena. Do you see a straightforward way to extend your framework to handle such cases? For example, could one introduce additional actions (e.g., `JUMP_BACK`, `COPY(i,j)`) to handle infixation or reduplication, and how might one train such a model?
3.  Footnote 1 notes that the model optimizes for the most likely derivation (action sequence) rather than marginalizing to find the most likely output string. Have you investigated the impact of this approximation? Specifically, how often does beam search find a high-probability derivation that leads to a suboptimal or incorrect final string, and is this a significant source of error compared to the soft-attention baseline?
