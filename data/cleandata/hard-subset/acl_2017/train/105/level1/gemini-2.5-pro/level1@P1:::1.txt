### Summary and Contributions

This paper introduces a neural sequence-to-sequence model for morphological inflection generation. The core novelty is a "hard monotonic attention" mechanism. Unlike standard soft attention which computes a weighted average over all input states, this model's decoder is restricted to two actions at each step: either WRITE an output character or STEP the attention pointer to the next input character in the sequence. This design directly models the strong tendency for monotonic alignments between a lemma and its inflected form. A key aspect of the methodology is that the model is not trained to learn alignments jointly. Instead, character-level alignments are pre-computed on the training data using an external tool, and these alignments are used to create a single "gold" sequence of WRITE/STEP actions for supervised training. The model is evaluated on three datasets (CELEX, Wiktionary, SIGMORPHON) and achieves state-of-the-art results, especially in low-data settings. The paper concludes with an analysis comparing the learned representations of the hard attention model to a standard soft attention model.

As I see them, the main contributions are:

1.  **A simple and highly effective hard monotonic attention model for sequence transduction.** The model's primary contribution is its architecture, which introduces a strong and appropriate inductive bias for tasks like morphological inflection. Its exceptional performance in the low-resource CELEX setting, where it is the first neural model to outperform the strong, task-specific latent-variable FST model of Dreyer et al. (2008), is a significant achievement and demonstrates the value of this targeted architectural constraint.

2.  **A comprehensive empirical validation that establishes a new state-of-the-art.** The authors conduct a thorough evaluation across three distinct and widely used datasets. This demonstrates the model's robustness in different data regimes (low-resource, high-resource, and typologically diverse). The nuanced results on the SIGMORPHON dataset, which show where the model excels (suffixing languages) and where it is slightly weaker (languages with non-local phenomena), add significant credibility to the empirical claims.

3.  **An insightful comparative analysis of model representations.** The analysis in Section 5, particularly the SVD visualizations, provides a compelling explanation for the model's data efficiency. The authors convincingly argue that the hard attention model offloads the burden of learning positional information from the character embeddings to the explicit `STEP` action, whereas the soft attention model must encode position into the representations themselves. This provides valuable insight into the functioning of different attention mechanisms.

### Strengths

1.  **Excellent performance in a low-resource setting.** The standout result is the model's performance on the CELEX dataset. Neural sequence-to-sequence models are notoriously data-hungry, and often underperform specialized models on small datasets. Surpassing the highly-tuned latent-variable FST model of Dreyer et al. (2008) is a major accomplishment. The learning curve in Figure 2 provides clear evidence that the hard attention model is less prone to overfitting than a standard soft attention model, validating the authors' initial motivation. This makes the model very attractive for morphological tasks in languages where training data is scarce.

2.  **The model is elegant, well-motivated, and simple.** The core idea of restricting the decoder to `WRITE` and `STEP` actions is a clean and intuitive way to enforce the monotonicity assumption common in morphology. By decoupling the alignment and transduction steps (using a pre-trained aligner to generate the oracle), the model avoids the complexities of jointly learning both and simplifies the training objective to a standard cross-entropy loss. This simplicity is a significant strength.

3.  **The evaluation is thorough and the analysis is insightful.** The paper does not just present a new model and SOTA numbers. It tests the model under varied conditions and provides a thoughtful analysis of its performance. The breakdown of results on the SIGMORPHON dataset by morphological typology is particularly strong, as it honestly presents the model's limitations and ties them back to the architectural assumptions. Furthermore, the analysis of the learned representations (Section 5) goes beyond a simple error analysis and offers a plausible and interesting explanation for *why* the model is more data-efficient.

### Weaknesses

1.  **Heavy reliance on an external, pre-computed alignment.** The entire training paradigm depends on the quality of the alignments generated by the Sudoh et al. (2013) model. This raises several concerns:
    *   **Sensitivity:** The paper does not investigate the model's sensitivity to the quality of these alignments. How would performance change if a different, perhaps lower-quality, aligner were used?
    *   **Error Propagation:** If the aligner makes systematic errors on certain morphological processes, the transduction model will be trained on flawed data, with no mechanism to recover.
    *   **Applicability:** The approach assumes a reliable character aligner is available or can be trained for the language in question. This may not always be the case and could limit the model's applicability.

2.  **Inherent architectural limitations for non-monotonic phenomena.** The model is explicitly designed for *nearly*-monotonic transformations. While the bidirectional encoder can capture local context to handle stem changes, the monotonic forward-only attention pointer makes it fundamentally unsuited for more complex, non-local phenomena. For example, processes like metathesis (swapping characters), reduplication (copying a non-contiguous part of the stem), or templatic morphology (as in Arabic, which the paper notes is a weaker case) cannot be modeled directly by the `WRITE`/`STEP` sequence. While the model performs surprisingly well on average, its architectural limits should be stated more clearly.

3.  **The "best derivation" approximation is not discussed.** Footnote 1 correctly points out that the model optimizes for the most likely action sequence (derivation), not the most likely output string (which would require marginalizing over derivations). For some input-output pairs, multiple valid action sequences might exist. By deterministically creating a single gold sequence from the alignment, the training process might penalize the model for producing a valid alternative derivation. While this is a common simplification, its potential impact is not discussed.

### Questions to Authors

1.  Regarding the dependence on the aligner: How robust is the model to the quality of the pre-computed alignments? Have you experimented with other alignment algorithms or analyzed the effect of alignment errors on the final inflection accuracy? For example, what percentage of training alignments contain errors, and how does the model cope?

2.  The `flog`->`fliege` example shows the model handling a one-to-two character transformation (`o` -> `ie`). Could you clarify how the gold action sequence is generated for this case? Is it `... STEP(o), WRITE(i), WRITE(e), ...`? And how would the model handle a more complex infixation, where new characters are inserted in the middle of a copied stem segment?

3.  Given the model's success on suffixing languages, have you considered a variant that allows for a `STEP_BACKWARD` action, or a separate reverse model, to better handle prefixing or circumfixing languages?
