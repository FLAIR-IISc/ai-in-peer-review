- Introduces a hard attention neural model tailored to nearly-monotonic input-output alignments for morphological inflection.
- Achieves state-of-the-art or competitive results across CELEX, Wiktionary, and SIGMORPHON 2016, especially in low-resource settings.
- Provides a detailed analysis comparing representations and alignments learned by hard attention versus soft attention.
- Explicitly models monotonic alignments, addressing soft attention’s limitations for morphological inflection.
- Decouples alignment learning from sequence transduction, simplifying training and avoiding joint alignment/decoding complexity.
- Extensive evaluation across datasets with varying resources and linguistic phenomena, showing consistent strong performance for suffixing/stem-change languages.
- Outperforms neural and non-neural baselines on low-resource datasets (notably CELEX).
- Hard attention captures positional and character-level information differently and more effectively in monotonic settings.
- Less effective for languages/tasks with non-monotonic dependencies (e.g., vowel/consonant harmony in Turkish, Hungarian).
- Relies on pre-learned alignments, creating dependency on external alignment tools and limiting applicability where alignments are hard to obtain.
- Lacks comparison to very recent sequence-transduction advances (e.g., transformer-based models).
- Open questions: performance on inherently non-monotonic tasks (transliteration, MT) and whether the model can integrate alignment learning without much added complexity.
- Overall assessment: significant contribution for morphological inflection with strong low-resource performance and insightful analysis, but limited generalizability due to monotonicity and alignment dependencies.
