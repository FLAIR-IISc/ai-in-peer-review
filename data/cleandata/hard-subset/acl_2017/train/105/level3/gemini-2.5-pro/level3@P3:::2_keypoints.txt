- Proposes a sequence-to-sequence model with explicit hard monotonic attention for morphological inflection, using external alignments converted to WRITE/STEP action sequences and a BiLSTM encoder-decoder to predict them.  
- Evaluates on CELEX, Wiktionary, and SIGMORPHON 2016, reporting state-of-the-art results, especially in low-resource settings.  

- Contribution: introduces a novel encoder-decoder that enforces strict monotonicity via a STEP/WRITE action space.  
- Contribution: demonstrates strong empirical performance, notably outperforming prior neural and FST baselines on low-data CELEX.  

- Strength: well-motivated inductive bias—monotonicity is appropriate for morphology and reduces overfitting compared to soft-attention.  
- Strength: decoupling alignment and transduction simplifies learning and yields effective low-resource performance.  
- Strength: clear presentation and useful analysis comparing learned representations of hard- vs soft-attention models.  

- Weakness: may be functionally equivalent to a simpler “align-then-tag” baseline (BiLSTM tagger) that is not included for comparison.  
- Weakness: under-specified oracle creation—paper lacks details on aligner output types (1-to-many, many-to-1, etc.) and deterministic conversion to STEP/WRITE, harming reproducibility.  
- Weakness: relies on greedy external alignments instead of marginalizing over alignments, which may be suboptimal.  
- Weakness: evaluation confined to morphological inflection, limiting claims of generality to other nearly-monotonic tasks (transliteration, G2P, normalization).  

- Question: please clarify the exact alignment types produced and give examples (e.g., many-to-one → how converted to STEP/WRITE).  
- Question: why was a simpler 1-to-many align-then-tag BiLSTM baseline not included, and how is the proposed architecture fundamentally superior?  
- Question: detail the linguistic features used as decoder input (source, representation) and provide ablation showing their contribution vs. the hard-attention mechanism.  

- Additional: avoid vague phrasing like “on par or better”—report exact differences.  
- Additional: discuss connections to transition-based models (e.g., Stack LSTMs) and prior monotonic attention work (e.g., Cohn et al., 2016).  
- Minor fixes: clarify/format NN in Eqn(3), fix capitalization and ellipsis spacing, remove Cyrillic ‘g’ in Fig.1, and correct typo in references (l.437).
