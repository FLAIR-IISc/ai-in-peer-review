- Proposes a neural encoder-decoder with a novel hard monotonic attention that emits discrete `write` and `step` actions to model near-monotonic character alignments.
- Trains on gold action sequences derived from an external character aligner rather than end-to-end alignment learning.
- Reports new state-of-the-art results on CELEX and top average accuracy on Wiktionary, with strong performance across benchmarks.
- Architecture is novel and well-motivated, providing a strong inductive bias suited to morphological inflection.
- Excels in low-data settings, outperforming prior neural and specialized non-neural models on CELEX.
- Provides thorough evaluation across low-, high-resource, and typologically diverse datasets, demonstrating robustness.
- Paper is clearly written with insightful analyses and visualizations (e.g., SVD) that contrast hard vs soft attention behaviors.
- Core monotonicity assumption limits applicability to non-monotonic phenomena (templatic morphology, circumfixing), where soft attention can outperform.
- Reliance on an external aligner makes the method not fully end-to-end and vulnerable to aligner errors.
- Omits discussion of practical trade-offs like training/inference speed and implementation complexity versus soft attention.
- Question: How does computational efficiency compare to soft attention in training and inference?
- Question: How sensitive is performance to the choice or quality of the pre-computed alignments?
- Question: Could the action space be extended (e.g., `step-back` or `jump`) to handle non-monotonic phenomena?
