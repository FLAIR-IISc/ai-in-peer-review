- Core idea: Introduces a hard monotonic attention seq2seq model that uses discrete WRITE/STEP actions instead of soft weighted attention, motivated by near-monotonic character alignments in morphological inflection.
- Training approach: Uses an external character-level aligner to produce gold WRITE/STEP action sequences and trains the model with cross-entropy on these actions.
- Evaluation: Tested on CELEX, Wiktionary, and SIGMORPHON datasets and compared learned representations and alignments against a soft-attention baseline.

- Contribution 1: Proposes a simple, effective hard monotonic attention architecture that encodes a strong inductive bias for near-monotonic string transduction.
- Contribution 2: Achieves state-of-the-art performance in low-resource settings (notably CELEX), outperforming neural and specialized FST baselines.
- Contribution 3: Provides a thorough empirical evaluation and analysis, including visualization of alignments and representation comparisons.

- Strength 1: Model is well-motivated and elegantly operationalizes monotonic alignment via discrete STEP/WRITE actions, constraining the search space.
- Strength 2: Demonstrates clear generalization advantages on small datasets where soft attention overfits.
- Strength 3: Experiments are comprehensive and the paper is clearly presented, enabling nuanced insights across data regimes.

- Weakness 1: Strict monotonicity limits performance on languages with non-local or templatic morphology (e.g., vowel harmony, Arabic), where soft attention can outperform it.
- Weakness 2: Reliance on an external aligner introduces a dependency and potential failure mode; sensitivity to aligner quality is not analyzed.
- Weakness 3: Practical aspects like training/inference time and implementation complexity (two-stage pipeline) are not discussed or compared to soft-attention baselines.

- Question 1: How sensitive is performance to the choice and quality of the external aligner; have simpler aligners (e.g., Levenshtein-based) been tried?
- Question 2: How far can the bidirectional encoder compensate for non-monotonic phenomena, and could the STEP action be relaxed (e.g., allow backward steps) without losing benefits?
- Question 3: What are the computational costs (training time, inference speed) compared to soft-attention models, and how does the two-stage pipeline affect practicality?
