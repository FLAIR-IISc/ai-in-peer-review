**Summary of the paper**

This paper introduces a neural encoder-decoder model for morphological inflection generation that incorporates a hard, monotonic attention mechanism. The core motivation is that morphological inflection is a nearly-monotonic sequence transduction task, and explicitly modeling this constraint should be more effective than relying on a general-purpose soft attention mechanism to learn it. The proposed model first learns character-level alignments between source and target words independently. It then trains an encoder-decoder network to predict a sequence of actions—either writing a character or stepping forward the attention pointer (`WRITE` or `STEP`). This decouples the alignment and transduction processes. The model is evaluated on three datasets (CELEX, Wiktionary, SIGMORPHON) and demonstrates state-of-the-art or competitive performance, notably outperforming previous neural models in a low-resource setting. The authors also provide an analysis of the learned representations, comparing them to those from a soft attention model.

As I see them, the main contributions are:
1.  A novel sequence-to-sequence architecture with an explicit hard monotonic attention mechanism. This introduces a strong and appropriate inductive bias for tasks like morphological inflection, which makes the model more data-efficient.
2.  Strong empirical results, particularly on the low-resource CELEX dataset, where this is the first neural model reported to surpass the specialized latent-variable FST model of Dreyer et al. (2008).
3.  An insightful analysis comparing the internal representations of the hard and soft attention models, shedding light on how they encode positional and character-level information.

**Strengths**

1.  **Novel and Well-Motivated Model Architecture:** The primary strength of this work is the model itself. Instead of using a generic soft attention mechanism that must learn monotonicity from data, the authors enforce it directly via the `STEP` action. This is a well-founded design choice for morphological inflection and other string transduction tasks where alignments are largely monotonic. This strong inductive bias appears to be the key reason for the model's excellent performance in the low-resource CELEX setting, where the soft attention model overfits. This is a clean and elegant way to incorporate domain knowledge into a neural architecture.

2.  **Strong and Comprehensive Empirical Evaluation:** The paper presents a convincing set of experiments across three different datasets, covering low-resource (CELEX), high-resource (Wiktionary), and typologically diverse (SIGMORPHON) scenarios. The model achieves state-of-the-art results, especially on CELEX, which is a significant achievement. The comparison against strong neural and non-neural baselines is thorough and validates the effectiveness of the proposed approach.

3.  **Clarity and Presentation:** The paper is very well-written and easy to follow. The model is described clearly, and Figure 1 provides a helpful visualization of the process. The motivation for hard monotonic attention is well-argued, and the analysis in Section 5 provides valuable insights into the model's behavior and its differences from soft attention.

**Weaknesses**

1.  **Missing Comparison to a Simpler, Obvious Baseline:** The proposed model decouples alignment and transduction, which is a key design choice. However, a much simpler way to implement this idea is to first generate 1-to-many character alignments and then treat the problem as a sequence tagging task, using a standard BiLSTM to predict the output character(s) for each input character. Such an approach is conceptually simpler than the proposed transition system of `WRITE` and `STEP` actions. The absence of this strong and straightforward baseline makes it difficult to assess how much of the performance gain comes from the novel hard-attention action space versus simply from decoupling the alignment and transduction phases. It is unclear what the added complexity of the proposed model buys over this simpler alternative.

2.  **Limited Scope of Evaluation:** While the motivation for the model is for "nearly-monotonic sequence to sequence learning," the experiments are confined entirely to morphological inflection. To demonstrate the broader applicability and impact of the proposed architecture, it would have been beneficial to evaluate it on other monotonic string-to-string tasks, such as transliteration or grapheme-to-phoneme conversion. This would strengthen the claim that the hard attention mechanism is a generally useful tool.

3.  **Unclear Contribution of Linguistic Features:** The model takes as input morpho-syntactic features (e.g., POS, gender, number). However, the paper does not provide details on these features or an ablation study to measure their impact. It is possible that the quality and representation of these features, rather than the hard attention mechanism alone, are responsible for a significant portion of the performance gains over baselines that may not use them or use them differently. This is a potential confounding factor in the results.

4.  **Reliance on a Single "Greedy" Alignment:** The model is trained on a single, pre-determined alignment for each training example. This greedy approach may be suboptimal, as there can be multiple plausible alignments. It also makes the model's performance dependent on the quality of the external aligner. In contrast, other models like Rastogi et al. (2016) learn to marginalize over all possible alignments. The paper does not discuss the potential performance loss from this design choice or analyze the impact of alignment errors.

**Questions to Authors**

1.  Could you please elaborate on why a simpler BiLSTM sequence tagging baseline (operating on pre-aligned data) was not included in the comparison? What specific advantages does your `STEP`/`WRITE` action space offer over such an approach, and how does your model's representational power differ?

2.  Section 2.3 states that the action sequence is deterministically inferred from a character-level alignment. Could you please clarify the nature of these alignments? Does the aligner you use (Sudoh et al., 2013) produce 1-to-0, 0-to-1, 1-to-1, or more complex many-to-many alignments? This detail is crucial for understanding how the oracle is constructed.

3.  In your results, you often use phrases like "on par or better." Could you please clarify with statistical significance tests which of your results represent a significant improvement over the baselines?

4.  Could you provide more detail on the linguistic features used in your experiments? Specifically, what are they, how are they encoded, and have you performed any analysis to isolate their contribution to the model's performance?
