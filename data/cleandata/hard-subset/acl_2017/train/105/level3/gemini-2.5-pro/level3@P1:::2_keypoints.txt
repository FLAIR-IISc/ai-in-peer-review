- Paper proposes a neural encoder-decoder for morphological inflection with a hard, monotonic attention implemented via learned character alignments and a WRITE/STEP action sequence.  
- Core idea: decouple alignment and transduction by precomputing character-level alignments and training the model to emit WRITE or STEP actions.  
- Evaluated on CELEX, Wiktionary, and SIGMORPHON, showing state-of-the-art or competitive results and strong low-resource performance.  

Contributions:
- Novel seq2seq architecture enforcing monotonicity through hard attention, giving a strong inductive bias for string transduction.  
- Empirical result: first neural model to surpass the Dreyer et al. (2008) FST on low-resource CELEX.  
- Analysis comparing internal representations of hard vs. soft attention models, highlighting positional and character encoding differences.  

Strengths:
- Well-motivated model that embeds domain knowledge (monotonicity) cleanly and reduces overfitting in low-data regimes.  
- Comprehensive experiments across low/high-resource and typologically diverse datasets with strong baselines.  
- Clear writing and presentation, with helpful figures and insightful analysis.  

Weaknesses:
- Missing comparison to a simpler baseline: pre-align then BiLSTM sequence tagging (1-to-many) to isolate value of the WRITE/STEP transition system.  
- Evaluation limited to morphological inflection; broader monotonic tasks (e.g., transliteration, G2P) not tested.  
- Unclear role and encoding of morpho-syntactic features; no ablation to measure their contribution.  
- Reliance on a single deterministic, precomputed alignment per example; potential suboptimality and sensitivity to aligner errors, with no analysis of this choice.  

Questions for authors:
- Why was a simpler BiLSTM tagging baseline on pre-aligned data not included, and what specific advantages does WRITE/STEP provide?  
- What alignment types does the external aligner produce (1-to-0, 0-to-1, 1-to-1, many-to-many), and how are oracles constructed?  
- Which results are statistically significant versus baselines?  
- What are the morpho-syntactic features used, how are they encoded, and have you ablated their impact?
