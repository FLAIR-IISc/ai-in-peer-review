### Summary of the Paper

This paper proposes a neural sequence-to-sequence model for morphological inflection generation that incorporates a hard monotonic attention mechanism. The core idea is that since the alignment between a word and its inflection is nearly monotonic, this prior knowledge can be explicitly built into the model architecture. The authors decouple the tasks of alignment and transduction. First, an external character alignment model is used to generate gold alignments for the training data. These alignments are then converted into a sequence of `write` and `step` actions. A BiLSTM encoder-decoder model is then trained to predict this action sequence, effectively learning to jointly align and transduce in a constrained, monotonic fashion. The model is evaluated on three standard morphological inflection datasets (CELEX, Wiktionary, SIGMORPHON 2016) where it achieves state-of-the-art results, showing particular strength in low-data settings.

### Contributions

1.  **A Novel Hard Monotonic Attention Model:** The primary contribution is a novel and elegant encoder-decoder architecture that explicitly models monotonic alignments. By framing the transduction problem as a sequence of `write` (output a character) and `step` (advance the input pointer) actions, the model effectively enforces a hard attention constraint. This is a well-motivated departure from the more general but less constrained soft attention mechanism for tasks where monotonicity is a strong prior.

2.  **State-of-the-Art Empirical Results:** The paper presents strong empirical results, establishing a new state-of-the-art on three widely used morphological inflection benchmarks. The model's ability to outperform previous methods, including specialized WFSTs and soft-attention models, on the very small CELEX dataset is a particularly significant finding, demonstrating its robustness in low-resource scenarios.

3.  **Insightful Comparative Analysis:** The paper includes a valuable analysis comparing the learned character representations and alignments of the proposed hard attention model against a standard soft attention model. This analysis provides insight into how each model encodes positional and character information, suggesting that the explicit `step` action in the hard attention model allows its encoder to focus more on character identity than position, which may contribute to its improved data efficiency.

### Strengths

1.  **Novel and Well-Motivated Architecture:** The proposed model is novel and its design is well-justified by the nature of the morphological inflection task. Decoupling alignment and transduction and training the model on a sequence of explicit actions is a principled way to incorporate the strong prior of monotonicity. This provides a compelling alternative to soft-attention models which must learn this constraint from data alone.

2.  **Strong Performance, Especially in Low-Data Regime:** The model achieves excellent results across all datasets. Its performance on the low-resource CELEX dataset is a standout result, where it is the first neural model reported to outperform the highly specialized latent-variable WFST model of Dreyer et al. (2008). This supports the authors' hypothesis that enforcing monotonicity helps prevent the overfitting to which more flexible models are prone when data is scarce.

3.  **Clear Presentation and Analysis:** The paper is well-written, and the model is explained clearly with helpful diagrams. The analysis in Section 5 that visualizes and interprets the learned representations is insightful and adds significant value, moving beyond just reporting accuracy scores to provide a deeper understanding of the model's behavior.

### Weaknesses

1.  **Missing Comparison to Simpler Sequence Tagging Baselines:** The most significant weakness is the lack of comparison to a simpler and highly relevant baseline. The proposed method of pre-aligning input and output characters and then generating an output could be framed as a sequence tagging problem. A standard BiLSTM tagger that reads the input word and, for each character, predicts the corresponding output character(s) (e.g., predicting 'ie' for 'o', or an empty string for a deletion) would be a natural and powerful baseline. Without this comparison, it is difficult to assess how much of the performance gain comes from the proposed action-based decoder versus the pre-computed alignments themselves.

2.  **Limited Scope of Evaluation:** While the model is motivated as a general approach for "nearly-monotonic sequence to sequence learning," its evaluation is restricted entirely to morphological inflection. To substantiate its broader utility, the model should have been tested on other monotonic string transduction tasks such as transliteration, grapheme-to-phoneme conversion, or text normalization. Without such experiments, the paper's claims of general applicability are unsupported.

3.  **Ambiguity in the Alignment Method:** The paper is not sufficiently clear about the exact nature of the alignments produced by the external tool and how they are converted into the oracle action sequence. It is unclear what types of alignments are handled (e.g., 1-to-many, many-to-1) and how complex cases are resolved. Furthermore, by relying on a single, greedily-chosen alignment for each training instance, the model may be sensitive to alignment errors and may underperform compared to models that can marginalize over multiple possible alignments (e.g., Rastogi et al., 2016), a potential limitation that is not discussed.

4.  **Incomplete Discussion of Related Work:** The model can be viewed as a transition-based system, with a state (current decoder hidden state, current attention pointer) and a set of actions (`write`, `step`). This connects it to a line of work on action-based models like Stack LSTMs. A discussion of these connections is missing from the related work section.

### Questions to Authors

1.  Your model's framework of pre-aligning and then transducing seems closely related to a standard sequence tagging setup. Could you elaborate on the conceptual differences between your action-based decoder and a simpler BiLSTM sequence tagger baseline that predicts aligned output characters for each input character? What are the hypothesized advantages of your model, and why was such a baseline omitted from the experiments?

2.  Could you please provide more detail on the process of converting the character-level alignments into the oracle sequence of `write` and `step` actions? Specifically, how do you handle 1-to-many, many-to-1, and many-to-many alignments? An example would be very helpful.

3.  The use of a single pre-computed alignment for training means your model cannot account for alignment ambiguity. Have you analyzed the impact of errors from the alignment tool on your model's final performance? Do you see this "greedy" approach as a potential limitation compared to models that marginalize over alignments?

4.  Given that the model is presented as being suitable for any nearly-monotonic sequence-to-sequence task, why was the evaluation limited to morphology? Do you have evidence or strong reasons to believe it would also achieve state-of-the-art performance on other tasks like transliteration?

5.  How are the morpho-syntactic features (POS, gender, etc.) represented and integrated into the model? An ablation study analyzing their contribution to the final performance would be useful to disentangle the effects of the features from the model architecture itself.
