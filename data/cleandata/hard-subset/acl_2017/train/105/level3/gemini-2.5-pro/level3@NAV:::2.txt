**Summary of the paper:**
This paper presents a novel encoder-decoder architecture for morphological inflection generation that incorporates an explicit, hard monotonic attention mechanism. The core contribution is a method that decouples alignment from transduction. It first computes character-level alignments using an external tool and then trains a neural model to predict a sequence of actions—either `WRITE` an output character or `STEP` to the next input position—to generate the target string based on the pre-determined alignment path. The authors evaluate this model on three morphological inflection datasets and report state-of-the-art results, particularly in low-resource settings.

**Main Contributions:**
1.  The primary contribution is a novel neural architecture for sequence-to-sequence tasks that enforces a strong monotonicity assumption via a hard attention mechanism, operationalized as a decoder that learns to predict `STEP` and `WRITE` actions.
2.  The paper provides a strong empirical case for this architecture in morphological inflection, demonstrating its effectiveness and achieving state-of-the-art results on several standard benchmarks.

**Strengths:**
1.  The model is well-motivated and conceptually elegant. For tasks like morphology where alignments are predominantly monotonic, building this assumption directly into the model architecture is a more principled approach than relying on a general-purpose soft attention mechanism to learn it implicitly. This design appears to be particularly effective in low-data regimes.
2.  The paper is well-written, and the core idea of decomposing the transduction process into a sequence of explicit actions is presented with clarity. This makes the model's mechanics easy to understand.
3.  The empirical results are strong. The model achieves state-of-the-art performance on multiple datasets, outperforming prior neural and non-neural approaches. Its significant advantage on the small CELEX dataset highlights the practical value of its inductive bias.

**Weaknesses:**
1.  The most significant weakness is the omission of a crucial, simpler baseline. The proposed method is conceptually very similar to a standard "align-then-tag" pipeline. A baseline using a 1-to-many alignment followed by a standard BiLSTM sequence tagger would be necessary to isolate the contribution of the paper's more complex action-based decoder. Without this comparison, it is unclear if the proposed model is meaningfully superior to a much simpler, established approach.
2.  The model’s generalizability is not sufficiently demonstrated. The authors frame their work as a solution for "nearly-monotonic sequence to sequence learning," but the evaluation is confined to morphological inflection. To substantiate claims of broader utility, the method should be evaluated on other monotonic string transduction tasks like transliteration or grapheme-to-phoneme conversion.
3.  Key methodological details are ambiguous, which affects reproducibility and interpretation. The paper does not clearly specify the type of alignments used to generate the oracle action sequence (e.g., 1-to-many, many-to-many). Furthermore, the contribution of linguistic features (e.g., POS tags) is not analyzed, making it difficult to discern whether performance gains are due to the novel architecture or the features themselves. Reporting is also imprecise at times (e.g., "on par or better").
