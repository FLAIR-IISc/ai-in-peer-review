### **Summary and Contributions**

This paper introduces a neural encoder-decoder model for morphological inflection generation that utilizes a novel "hard monotonic attention" mechanism. The core idea is to reframe the sequence transduction problem as learning a series of discrete `write` and `step` actions. At each step, the decoder can either emit a character to the output string (`write`) or advance its attention pointer to the next character in the encoded input sequence (`step`). This approach explicitly models the nearly monotonic character alignment often found in morphological processes. To train the model, gold action sequences are derived from character alignments generated by an independent, pre-trained alignment tool. The authors conduct a comprehensive evaluation across three standard datasets (CELEX, Wiktionary, SIGMORPHON 2016), demonstrating strong performance and providing an insightful analysis comparing the learned representations of their model against a traditional soft attention baseline.

In my view, the main contributions of this work are:

1.  **A novel and effective hard attention model for sequence transduction.** The primary contribution is the model architecture itself, which enforces a strong, yet appropriate, inductive bias for tasks with nearly monotonic alignments. This method proves particularly effective in low-resource settings, addressing a known weakness of standard sequence-to-sequence models.
2.  **New state-of-the-art results on several morphological inflection benchmarks.** The paper presents strong empirical evidence for the model's effectiveness, outperforming previous methods on the low-resource CELEX dataset and achieving the highest average accuracy on the large Wiktionary dataset.
3.  **An insightful comparative analysis of hard and soft attention mechanisms.** The analysis of learned alignments and character representations sheds light on the inner workings of both models, revealing that soft attention implicitly learns to be monotonic and encodes positional information, whereas the proposed hard attention model represents position more explicitly through its `step` action.

### **Strengths**

1.  **Novel and Well-Motivated Architecture:** The proposed hard monotonic attention is a creative and well-justified departure from standard soft attention. By explicitly modeling the transduction process as a series of `write` and `step` operations, the model incorporates a strong inductive bias that is highly suitable for morphological inflection. This design choice is a conceptually clear and significant contribution.
2.  **Superior Performance in Low-Data Settings:** A key strength of this work is the model's outstanding performance on the small CELEX dataset. Neural models are notoriously data-hungry, but this work shows that a well-designed architecture can overcome this limitation. The model not only surpasses the soft-attention baseline but is also the first neural model to achieve better results than the specialized, non-neural latent-variable FST model on this benchmark, highlighting its practical value.
3.  **Thorough and Convincing Empirical Evaluation:** The authors validate their model on three distinct and widely used datasets that cover low-resource, high-resource, and typologically diverse scenarios. The consistently strong results across these benchmarks demonstrate the model's robustness and general applicability for a large class of languages.
4.  **Clarity of Presentation and Analysis:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and easy to understand. The model is described with clarity, and the experiments are well-designed. The analysis section is a particular highlight, providing valuable visualizations and SVD plots that build intuition and explain the behavioral differences between hard and soft attention, elevating the paper beyond a simple presentation of a new model.

### **Weaknesses**

1.  **Limited Applicability to Non-Monotonic Morphology:** The model's core assumption of monotonicity is also its main limitation. As shown in the SIGMORPHON results, the model is outperformed by soft attention on languages with more complex, non-monotonic phenomena such as templatic morphology (Arabic) or circumfixing. The strict forward-only movement of the attention pointer constrains its ability to capture these processes, limiting its universality. The paper would be stronger if it discussed these typological limitations more explicitly.
2.  **Dependence on an External Aligner:** The model's training process is not fully end-to-end; it relies on an external tool to generate character alignments from which the gold action sequences are derived. This creates a pipeline where the final model's performance is dependent on the quality of this preliminary alignment step, and any errors from the aligner may propagate. This contrasts with the elegance of soft attention models that learn to align and transduce jointly.
3.  **Lack of Discussion on Practical Considerations:** The paper omits a discussion of practical trade-offs, such as computational efficiency (training and inference speed) and ease of implementation compared to a standard soft attention model. While the training objective is simpler (cross-entropy on a fixed sequence), it is unclear if this translates to a significant speedup, especially given the overhead of the pre-alignment step. This information would be valuable for researchers and practitioners considering this method.

### **Questions to Authors**

1.  Could you comment on the computational efficiency of your model during training and inference compared to the soft attention baseline? Does the simpler cross-entropy objective and the focused representation at each step lead to significant speedups?
2.  The system's performance is tied to the quality of the pre-computed alignments. Have you investigated its sensitivity to the alignment method? For example, how does performance change if a simpler heuristic (e.g., derived from Levenshtein distance) is used instead of the more complex CRP-based model?
3.  Given the model's limitations with non-monotonic phenomena, do you envision extensions to the hard attention framework that could handle such cases? For instance, could the action space be expanded to include pointer movements like `step-back` or `jump` to a specific position to better model templatic morphology or reduplication?
