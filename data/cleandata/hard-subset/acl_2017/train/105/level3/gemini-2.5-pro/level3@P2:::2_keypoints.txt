- Proposes a neural seq2seq model with hard monotonic attention that enforces monotonic alignments via `write` and `step` actions.
- Decouples alignment and transduction by using an external character aligner to produce oracle action sequences for training.
- Trains a BiLSTM encoder-decoder to predict the action sequences and jointly align+transduce under monotonic constraints.
- Evaluated on CELEX, Wiktionary, and SIGMORPHON 2016, achieving state-of-the-art results and strong low-data performance.

Contributions:
- Introduces a novel hard monotonic attention encoder-decoder that models transduction as `write`/`step` actions.
- Demonstrates state-of-the-art empirical performance, notably outperforming prior models on low-resource CELEX.
- Provides comparative analysis of learned representations and alignments versus soft-attention models.

Strengths:
- Well-motivated, principled architecture that leverages monotonicity priors.
- Robust empirical results, especially in low-data regimes.
- Clear presentation and insightful representation/alignment analysis.

Weaknesses:
- Missing comparison to a simpler BiLSTM sequence-tagging baseline that predicts aligned output characters.
- Evaluation limited to morphological inflection; broader monotonic tasks (transliteration, G2P, text normalization) not tested.
- Unclear description of the external alignment method and conversion to actions; reliance on a single greedy alignment may be fragile.
- Related-work discussion omits connections to transition-/action-based models (e.g., Stack LSTMs).

Questions to authors:
- How does your action-based decoder conceptually differ from a BiLSTM sequence-tagger baseline, and why was that baseline omitted?
- How are character alignments converted into `write`/`step` oracles, and how are 1-to-many, many-to-1, and many-to-many cases handled (example requested)?
- Have you evaluated sensitivity to alignment errors or considered marginalizing over multiple alignments instead of using a single greedy alignment?
- Why was evaluation confined to morphology, and do you expect similar gains on other monotonic transduction tasks?
- How are morphosyntactic features represented and integrated, and can you provide an ablation showing their contribution?
