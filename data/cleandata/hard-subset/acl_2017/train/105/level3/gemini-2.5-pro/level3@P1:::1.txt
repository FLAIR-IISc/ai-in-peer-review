### **Summary and Contributions**

This paper presents a novel neural sequence-to-sequence model for morphological inflection generation. The core of the proposed method is a "hard monotonic attention" mechanism. Unlike standard soft attention models that compute a weighted average over all input encoder states, this model makes a discrete choice at each decoding step: either `WRITE` an output character or `STEP` to the next input character. This approach is motivated by the observation that character alignments in morphological inflection are often nearly monotonic. To train the model, the authors first compute character-level alignments on the training data using an external method and then derive a "gold" sequence of `WRITE` and `STEP` actions, which the model is trained to predict using a standard cross-entropy loss. The paper evaluates this model on three datasets (CELEX, Wiktionary, SIGMORPHON) and provides an analysis of the learned representations in comparison to a soft attention model.

The main contributions of this work are:

1.  **A Novel Hard Monotonic Attention Model:** The primary contribution is the model architecture itself. It introduces a simple yet effective mechanism that explicitly models the monotonic nature of many string transduction tasks. Decoupling the alignment from the transduction model simplifies the training objective and provides a strong inductive bias that proves beneficial, especially in low-data scenarios.
2.  **State-of-the-Art Performance in Low-Resource Settings:** The paper demonstrates empirically that the proposed model achieves state-of-the-art results on the low-resource CELEX dataset, outperforming not only other neural models but also highly specialized latent-variable FST models. This is a significant practical contribution, as many morphological tasks operate with limited annotated data.
3.  **A Thorough Empirical Evaluation and Analysis:** The authors conduct a comprehensive set of experiments across datasets of varying sizes and linguistic diversity. The included analysis of learned alignments and character representations provides valuable insights into the differences between hard and soft attention mechanisms for this task.

### **Strengths**

1.  **Well-Motivated and Elegant Model:** The central idea of using a hard, monotonic attention mechanism is well-motivated by the nature of morphological inflection. The implementation via discrete `STEP` and `WRITE` actions is an elegant way to operationalize this intuition. This design choice provides a strong inductive bias that helps the model learn more effectively from limited data by constraining the search space.
2.  **Excellent Performance on Small Datasets:** The most compelling argument for this work is its superior performance in the low-resource setting. As shown in Table 1 and Figure 2, the hard attention model significantly outperforms the soft attention baseline on the CELEX dataset, where overfitting is a major concern. The learning curves clearly show that while the soft attention model overfits, the hard attention model generalizes better. This demonstrates a clear advantage of the proposed method in a common and challenging scenario.
3.  **Comprehensive Experiments and Clear Presentation:** The paper is very well-written and easy to follow. The experiments are thorough, covering three standard benchmarks that represent low-resource, high-resource, and linguistically diverse conditions. This allows for a nuanced understanding of the model's strengths and weaknesses. The analysis in Section 5, especially the visualization of alignments and learned representations, is insightful and strengthens the paper's claims about how and why the model works.

### **Weaknesses**

1.  **Performance is Limited by the Monotonicity Assumption:** The model's primary strength is also its fundamental limitation. While effective for languages with suffixing and simple stem changes (e.g., German, Spanish), its performance degrades relative to soft attention on languages with more complex, non-local morphological phenomena. The results on the SIGMORPHON dataset (Table 3) illustrate this clearly: the hard attention model is slightly outperformed by the soft attention baseline on languages with vowel/consonant harmony (e.g., Turkish, Hungarian) or templatic morphology (Arabic). While the bidirectional encoder can capture some context, the strictly forward-moving attention pointer is a restrictive assumption that does not hold universally.
2.  **Dependence on an External Aligner:** The model's performance relies on the quality of an externally pre-computed alignment. The paper uses a sophisticated Bayesian model (Sudoh et al., 2013) for this, but it introduces a dependency and a potential point of failure. The paper does not analyze the model's sensitivity to the quality of this alignment. If the aligner performs poorly on a given language pair or phenomenon, errors could propagate and degrade the final transduction performance. In contrast, models that jointly learn to align and transduce are self-contained and can potentially learn alignments better suited to the end task.
3.  **Lack of Discussion on Practical Considerations:** The paper provides a thorough academic evaluation but omits a discussion of practical aspects like computational efficiency and ease of implementation. For instance, how does the training and inference time of the hard attention model compare to the soft attention baseline? One might assume that inference is faster since it does not require computing a weighted sum over all source states at each step, but this is not discussed. Similarly, the two-stage process (aligning, then training) adds an extra step to the pipeline, and its complexity is not compared to the end-to-end soft attention approach.

### **Questions to Authors**

1.  Regarding the dependence on the external aligner: How sensitive is the model to the choice and quality of the alignment algorithm? Have you experimented with simpler alignment methods (e.g., based on Levenshtein distance) to see how performance is affected? This would help clarify whether the sophisticated alignment model is a necessary component for success.
2.  The paper suggests that the bidirectional encoder helps capture non-monotonic dependencies. Could you elaborate on the limits of this? Given that the model still struggles with phenomena like vowel harmony, it seems this is not entirely sufficient. Do you see a straightforward way to relax the strict forward-only `STEP` action to handle a limited degree of non-monotonicity (e.g., allowing the attention pointer to move back one or two steps) without losing the model's core simplicity and regularization benefits?
3.  Could you provide some analysis of the computational costs (e.g., training time, inference speed) of your model compared to the soft-attention baseline? This would be valuable information for practitioners considering adopting your method.
