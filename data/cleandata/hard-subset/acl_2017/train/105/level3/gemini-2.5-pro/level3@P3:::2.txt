### Summary of the Paper

This paper proposes a novel sequence-to-sequence model for morphological inflection generation. The central idea is to leverage the nearly monotonic alignment between a word and its inflected form. Instead of using a general soft attention mechanism, the authors introduce an explicit "hard monotonic attention" model. This model decouples alignment and transduction. First, an external character-level alignment model is used to find alignments between the source and target words in the training data. These alignments are then converted into an oracle sequence of `WRITE(symbol)` and `STEP` (advance pointer) actions. An encoder-decoder network, with a BiLSTM encoder, is then trained to predict this action sequence. The authors evaluate their model on three morphological inflection datasets (CELEX, Wiktionary, SIGMORPHON 2016) and report state-of-the-art results, particularly in low-resource settings. They also provide an analysis of the learned representations, comparing their hard-attention model to a soft-attention baseline.

### Contributions

1.  **A Novel Encoder-Decoder with Explicit Hard Monotonic Attention:** The primary contribution is a new architecture for sequence transduction that enforces a strict, hard monotonicity constraint. By reframing the task as predicting a sequence of `WRITE` and `STEP` actions derived from pre-computed alignments, the model incorporates a strong and appropriate inductive bias for tasks like morphology.
2.  **State-of-the-Art Results on Morphological Inflection:** The paper demonstrates strong empirical results, achieving state-of-the-art or competitive performance across three benchmark datasets. The model shows a particular advantage in the low-resource setting of the CELEX dataset, where it is the first neural model to outperform a strong latent-variable FST baseline.

### Strengths

1.  **Well-Motivated and Novel Model Architecture:** The motivation for enforcing monotonicity in morphological inflection is very clear and compelling. Standard soft-attention models have the capacity but not the obligation to learn this, which can lead to overfitting, especially with limited data. The proposed hard-attention mechanism is a novel and direct way to inject this prior knowledge into a neural architecture. Decoupling the alignment and transduction stages simplifies the learning problem for the neural network, which appears to be highly effective.
2.  **Strong Empirical Performance, Especially in Low-Data Regime:** The model's performance on the CELEX dataset is a significant result. It demonstrates that by incorporating a strong structural prior (monotonicity), the model can learn more effectively from very small datasets (500 examples) than more general and flexible models like soft-attention sequence-to-sequence models, which tend to overfit. This highlights the practical value of the proposed approach for morphologically rich, low-resource languages.
3.  **Clear Presentation and Analysis:** The paper is well-written, and the core idea of the model is presented clearly, aided by helpful diagrams. The analysis in Section 5, which visualizes and compares the learned representations of the hard- and soft-attention models, provides valuable insight into *why* the proposed model works well. It suggests the hard-attention model does not need to encode positional information as strongly in its character representations, as this is handled explicitly by the `STEP` action.

### Weaknesses

1.  **Potentially Equivalent to a Simpler, Uncompared Baseline:** The most significant weakness is the potential that this complex model is functionally equivalent to a much simpler, established approach that is not included as a baseline. By pre-computing alignments, the problem can be framed as a sequence tagging task: for each input character, predict the output character(s). A simple baseline would be to use an off-the-shelf aligner to get 1-to-many alignments and then train a standard BiLSTM sequence tagger to predict the output string for each input character. This "align-then-tag" approach is conceptually simpler and it is unclear what benefit the proposed `STEP`/`WRITE` action space and recurrent decoder provide over it. Without this comparison, it is difficult to assess whether the novelty of the architecture actually contributes to the performance gains.
2.  **Lack of Clarity on Alignment and Oracle Creation:** The process of converting character alignments into the oracle action sequence is under-specified. The paper states it uses the aligner of Sudoh et al. (2013) but does not describe what kind of alignments (1-to-1, 1-to-many, many-to-1, many-to-many) it produces or how these are deterministically converted to a `STEP`/`WRITE` sequence. For instance, how is a many-to-one alignment handled? This is a critical detail for reproducibility and for understanding the model's true behavior. Furthermore, relying on greedy alignments from an external model may be suboptimal compared to methods that marginalize over all possible alignments during training.
3.  **Evaluation is Limited to a Single Task:** The model is framed as a general approach for "nearly-monotonic sequence to sequence learning," but it is only evaluated on morphological inflection. To substantiate the claim of general applicability and demonstrate a broader advantage, the model should have been tested on other monotonic string-to-string tasks, such as transliteration, grapheme-to-phoneme conversion, or historical text normalization. This narrow evaluation scope limits the paper's impact.

### Questions to Authors

1.  Could you please clarify the exact nature of the alignments produced by the external aligner? Are they restricted to 1-to-many, or do they include many-to-1 and many-to-many alignments? Please provide a clear example of how a complex alignment (e.g., many-to-one) is deterministically converted into the gold sequence of `STEP` and `WRITE` actions for training.
2.  The core contribution seems to be a specific way of operationalizing a pre-aligned transduction task. Can you explain why a simpler baseline, such as using 1-to-many alignments to train a standard BiLSTM sequence tagger (e.g., Schnober et al., 2016), was not included? How is your proposed architecture fundamentally different from or superior to this simpler align-then-tag formulation?
3.  The paper mentions using linguistic features (e.g., POS, gender) as input to the decoder. Could you provide more detail on these features, their source, and their representation? Have you conducted an ablation study to analyze how much of the performance gain is attributable to these features versus the hard monotonic attention mechanism itself?

### Additional Comments

-   The phrasing "on par or better" is imprecise. Please report the results and differences directly.
-   Related work: The connection to transition-based models like Stack LSTMs could be discussed, as your model can be viewed as a simple transition system with `ADVANCE` and `WRITE` actions. Relevant work on monotonic attention like Cohn et al. (2016) should also be cited.
-   Minor formatting/typos:
    -   In Eqn (3), `NN` should be defined or formatted consistently (e.g., as a function `f_NN`).
    -   The word "where" introducing a variable definition should not be capitalized.
    -   The ellipsis in `x1,...,xn` should have spaces around it (`x_1, \dots, x_n`).
    -   The unnecessary use of a Cyrillic font for the character 'g' in Figure 1 is distracting.
    -   There appears to be a typo at l.437 in the references.
