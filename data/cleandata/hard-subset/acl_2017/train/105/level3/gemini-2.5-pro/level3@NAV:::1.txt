**Summary and Contributions**

This paper proposes a novel hard monotonic attention model for morphological inflection generation. The core idea is to decouple the alignment and transduction steps, which contrasts with standard soft-attention models that learn both jointly. The model is trained on a sequence of `step` and `write` actions derived from pre-computed character alignments obtained via an external Bayesian transliteration method.

The main contributions of this work are:
1.  The proposal of a new hard monotonic attention architecture that explicitly models the strong monotonic assumption inherent in many morphological inflection tasks. This represents a substantial and conceptually different approach from prior soft-attention mechanisms.
2.  An empirical demonstration that this constrained model can significantly outperform more complex, standard neural models in low-resource settings (e.g., the CELEX dataset), where soft-attention models are prone to overfitting.

**Strengths**

1.  **Effectiveness in Low-Data Regimes:** The most significant strength is the model's performance on small training sets. By enforcing a hard monotonic constraint derived from pre-computed alignments, the model avoids the overfitting issues that plague standard soft-attention models on datasets like CELEX. This makes the approach highly valuable for morphologically rich languages where large parallel corpora for inflection are scarce.

2.  **Novelty and Simplicity:** The proposed hard attention mechanism is a novel contribution to sequence-to-sequence modeling for this task. Decoupling alignment from transduction simplifies the learning problem for the neural network, allowing it to focus solely on the mapping task given a fixed alignment, which proves to be a very effective strategy.

3.  **Clarity and Rigorous Evaluation:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and easy to follow. The authors present a comprehensive set of experiments across three distinct datasets (CELEX, Wiktionary, SIGMORPHON), comparing their model against a strong suite of both neural and non-neural baselines. This thorough evaluation provides convincing evidence for the model's effectiveness under specific conditions.

**Weaknesses**

1.  **Limited Applicability:** The model's primary strength—its reliance on a strong monotonic assumption—is also its main weakness. As the authors’ own results on the SIGMORPHON dataset show, the method is not state-of-the-art for languages with more complex, non-monotonic phenomena (e.g., circumfixing, vowel harmony). This limits the general applicability of the proposed method as a universal solution.

2.  **Lack of Practical Analysis:** The paper fails to discuss important practical considerations. There is no analysis of the computational costs (training time, inference speed, memory usage) compared to soft-attention models. Furthermore, the ease of implementation is not addressed, particularly concerning the reliance on an external alignment tool, which adds a preprocessing step to the pipeline.

3.  **Dependence on External Aligner:** The model's performance is contingent on the quality of the alignments produced by the external Bayesian method. The paper lacks an analysis of the model's sensitivity to alignment errors. It is unclear how the system would perform with a different aligner or how errors from the alignment stage propagate and affect the final transduction quality.
