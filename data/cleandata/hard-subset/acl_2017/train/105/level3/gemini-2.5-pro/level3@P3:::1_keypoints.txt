- Proposes a hard monotonic attention encoder-decoder for morphological inflection using explicit WRITE(symbol) and STEP actions derived from pre-computed character alignments.  
- Uses an external Bayesian aligner to produce oracle action sequences instead of learning alignments jointly.  
- Evaluated on CELEX, Wiktionary, and SIGMORPHON datasets, showing strong performance, especially in low-resource settings.  
- Contribution: a simple, effective hard-attention mechanism tailored to near-monotonic sequence transduction.  
- Contribution: demonstrates superior data efficiency to soft-attention models, notably beating a specialized FST on low-resource CELEX.  
- Strength: well-motivated architecture that provides direct supervision for attention and simplifies learning.  
- Strength: excellent empirical results in low-resource scenarios and reduced overfitting versus soft-attention.  
- Strength: comprehensive experimental evaluation and representational analysis (e.g., SVD visualizations).  
- Strength: clear, well-written presentation of model and experiments.  
- Weakness: limited applicability to non-monotonic or long-range morphological phenomena (underperforms on some SIGMORPHON languages).  
- Weakness: reliance on an external aligner with no analysis of sensitivity to alignment errors or simpler alignment methods.  
- Weakness: lacks quantitative analysis of computational efficiency (training/inference time and memory) compared to soft attention.  
- Question: how robust is the model to alignment noise or to oracle actions from simpler aligners (e.g., Levenshtein-based)?  
- Question: can the authors provide quantitative comparisons of computational cost (training time per epoch, inference speed) between hard and soft attention?
