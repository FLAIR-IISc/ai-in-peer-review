Summary
This paper proposes a neural sequence-to-sequence model for morphological inflection that uses hard monotonic attention. The decoder alternates between two actions: STEP (advance the input pointer) and WRITE (emit an output symbol), leveraging the near-monotonic character alignments common in inflection. Crucially, the model does not learn alignments jointly; instead, it derives oracle action sequences from character-level alignments obtained beforehand using a Bayesian CRP-based transliteration aligner (Sudoh et al., 2013). Trained with simple cross-entropy on these oracle sequences, the model delivers strong results across three benchmarks. It is especially effective in low-resource settings (e.g., CELEX) and on languages whose inflection is largely monotonic with stem changes (Russian, German, Spanish). The paper also includes an analysis comparing learned representations and alignments of hard vs. soft attention models.

Main contributions (as I see them; ordered by importance)
1) A hard monotonic attention decoder for character-level inflection that is trained from externally induced character alignments and an oracle action sequence, offering a substantially different approach from prevalent soft-attention models that jointly learn alignment and transduction.
2) Comprehensive empirical evaluation showing strong performance overall and clear advantages in low-resource settings and in languages with mostly monotonic alignments, surpassing prior neural and non-neural baselines on several datasets.
3) Comparative analysis of learned alignments and encoder representations that provides insight into why hard attention helps in this task and how it differs from soft attention.

Strengths
- Clear methodological novelty relative to prior inflection work: The hard, pointer-based decoder trained from externally induced alignments is a clean departure from soft attention approaches that jointly marginalize over alignments. The formulation as STEP/WRITE actions and direct supervised training on oracle action sequences is elegant and well-motivated by the task’s near-monotonicity.
- Strong low-resource performance: On CELEX, the model achieves higher average exact-match accuracy (e.g., 89.44% vs. 88.30% for the latent-variable WFST), being the first neural model to surpass the specialized latent-variable FST on this dataset. Learning curves further support better generalization and reduced overfitting compared to soft attention in small data regimes.
- Competitive to state-of-the-art at scale and in suitable typologies: On Wiktionary, the model attains the best average across settings and leads on several language/part-of-speech subsets (e.g., German verbs, Finnish nouns/adjectives, Dutch verbs). On SIGMORPHON, it outperforms soft attention in suffixing+stem-change languages (RU/DE/ES), consistent with the monotonic design.
- Simple, stable training: Decoupling alignment induction enables straightforward cross-entropy training without forward–backward or marginalization over alignments, which can reduce optimization instability. The approach leverages a mature Bayesian transliteration aligner to provide high-quality supervision for the control policy.
- Clear writing and thorough experimentation: The paper is well-structured, carefully situates the work against prior methods, and reports comprehensive comparisons across multiple datasets, with useful qualitative and quantitative analyses (e.g., alignment visualizations; SVD of encoder states).

Weaknesses
- Dependence on external aligner: The approach relies on a Bayesian character alignment model to produce oracle action sequences. This pipeline may limit portability and robustness if alignments are noisy or if the aligner’s assumptions misfit certain languages. The paper lacks ablations on alignment quality and sensitivity (e.g., comparing different aligners or injecting noise).
- Limited advantage outside near-monotonic settings: The method is not universally best; it underperforms soft attention on languages with strong long-range or non-local dependencies (e.g., vowel/consonant harmony, templatic morphology such as Arabic, and agglutinative systems). This restricts the method’s applicability and suggests users must match the model to typology.
- Objective mismatch and lack of marginalization: Training maximizes the probability of a single oracle derivation rather than marginalizing over multiple derivations for the same output sequence. While effective empirically, the paper does not analyze when this approximation may hurt, nor whether beam or n-best oracle derivations could improve robustness.
- Missing discussion of practical merits: The paper does not quantify computational cost, memory footprint, or decoding speed relative to soft attention. It also does not discuss implementation complexity when adding an external alignment stage, nor the tradeoff between alignment induction time and training simplicity.
- Limited ablations: Beyond the hard vs. soft comparison, there is little analysis of model components (e.g., effect of decoder feedback, pointer initialization, feature embedding design), or of decoding strategies (greedy vs. beam), which would help isolate what drives the gains.

Questions to Authors
- Alignment sensitivity: How sensitive is performance to the quality of the induced alignments? Could you report results with alternative aligners, ablate aligner training data, or add synthetic noise to alignments to quantify robustness?
- Computational tradeoffs: What are training/inference time and memory comparisons vs. soft attention, including the overhead of alignment induction? How does decoding speed (tokens/sec) compare?
- Decoding details: Do you use greedy decoding or beam search for action sequences? If beam, what beam sizes and effects on accuracy and speed did you observe?
- Derivation vs. sequence objective: Have you tried marginalizing over a small set of plausible derivations (e.g., n-best oracle action sequences), or scheduled sampling that relaxes adherence to the oracle? Does this help in non-monotonic settings?
- Extensibility: Could the control space be augmented (e.g., JUMP(k), COPY spans) to better handle non-monotonic phenomena while preserving much of the model’s simplicity? Any preliminary results?
- Alignment-free training: Can you warm-start with external alignments and then fine-tune the attention policy jointly (semi-supervised alignment), potentially improving coverage in languages like Turkish or Arabic?
