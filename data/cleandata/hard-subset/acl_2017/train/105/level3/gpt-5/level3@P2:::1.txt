Summary and Contributions
This paper introduces a character-level encoder–decoder model for morphological inflection that uses a hard, nearly-monotonic attention mechanism. The decoder produces a sequence of write actions (characters) and a single control action (step) that advances an attention pointer over a bi-LSTM encoding of the input. Alignments are obtained externally at the character level and deterministically converted into oracle action sequences, enabling straightforward cross-entropy training. The model is evaluated on CELEX, Wiktionary, and SIGMORPHON 2016 and is analyzed against a soft-attention baseline in terms of alignments and learned representations.

As I see them, the main contributions (strongest first):
1) A simple and effective hard monotonic attention architecture for character transduction, trained from externally induced alignments via an action-sequence oracle. This cleanly exploits the near-monotonicity of many inflection processes and decouples alignment learning from transduction.
2) Strong empirical results, notably robust gains in low-resource settings (CELEX) and competitive or SOTA average accuracy on large benchmarks (Wiktionary; several SIGMORPHON languages).
3) Analysis comparing hard vs. soft attention: alignment behavior (hard attention learns intuitive monotonic alignments) and representational structure (e.g., positional information), providing insight into why the hard attention generalizes better with little data.

Strengths
- Task-appropriate modeling with clear inductive bias: The write/step control fits the near-monotonic nature of many inflectional transformations (suffixation with occasional stem changes). By forcing a single forward pointer and conditioning on the full encoder context, the model gains both interpretability and a strong bias against overfitting spurious non-monotone mappings. The learning-curve evidence on CELEX (higher dev accuracy despite similar train accuracy vs. soft attention) supports this.
- Strong performance where it matters most (low resource): On CELEX, the model surpasses both neural and non-neural baselines, including the specialized latent-variable WFST (Dreyer et al., 2008), which prior neural models could not beat at this scale. This is an important practical regime for morphologically rich languages with limited task-specific supervision.
- Broad and careful empirical evaluation: The paper compares against competitive neural and non-neural systems on three established datasets, using standard splits and multiple language families. Results are reported per dataset and averaged, with qualitative analyses (e.g., deletion, one-to-many mappings) that demonstrate the model’s behaviors.
- Insightful analysis of learned representations and alignments: The visualization shows both models learn position-aware encodings, but hard attention encodes position less strongly (relying on step actions), potentially aiding generalization. Alignment visualizations highlight interpretable, linguistically plausible monotone alignments under hard attention.
- Clear writing and method transparency: The architecture, training pipeline, and oracle construction are clearly explained, making the approach easy to understand and replicate. The decision to train on externally induced alignments is well motivated and technically simple.

Weaknesses
- Limited applicability beyond monotone regimes: On languages with highly context-sensitive/non-local morphology (e.g., vowel/consonant harmony, templatic morphology), the hard attention underperforms soft attention in SIGMORPHON. The paper acknowledges this, but it limits the method’s universality. A discussion of possible extensions (e.g., limited backward moves, learned skips, or hybrid hard/soft mechanisms) would strengthen the contribution.
- Dependence on external alignments without sensitivity analysis: Training relies on a specific character aligner (Sudoh et al., 2013). There is no ablation on alignment quality, alternative aligners, or robustness to alignment noise. This is important, as poor alignments could degrade the oracle and bias the model.
- Objective mismatch and search approximation not quantified: The model optimizes best-derivation rather than best-output sequence (no marginalization over derivations). While the paper states this works well in practice, there is no empirical comparison (e.g., beam search vs. greedy, effect sizes) or analysis of how often multiple derivations exist and whether this causes errors.
- Incomplete computational/practical analysis: There is no reporting of training/inference time, memory usage, or decoding complexity, nor a discussion of implementation effort relative to soft attention or WFST baselines. Given the claimed simplicity, concrete runtime/complexity numbers would help practitioners.
- Comparability caveats in baselines: For SIGMORPHON, the paper attributes some differences to training schedules (e.g., 100 vs. 20 epochs). This undermines the strictness of the comparison. Statistical significance testing is not reported, and it is unclear whether results are means over multiple runs.

Questions to Authors
- How sensitive is performance to the choice and quality of the external aligner? Have you tried alternative alignment methods or injected noise to test robustness?
- What decoding strategy is used at test time (greedy vs. beam)? If beam search is used, what beam sizes and impacts on accuracy/speed did you observe?
- Can the control mechanism be extended to permit limited non-monotone jumps (e.g., backward step or learned skip) without sacrificing the low-resource gains?
- Do you have runtime and memory comparisons vs. soft attention and WFST baselines for training and inference?
- How often do multiple derivations map to the same output in your datasets, and does marginalizing (or approximating it) change outcomes?

Additional Comments
- Please clarify the morphological feature representation (embedding sizes, composition) and regularization used (dropout, weight decay).
- Report variance across runs and, where feasible, statistical significance on key comparisons.
- Provide more detail on the oracle construction from alignments (e.g., tie-breaking for many-to-many links) and release scripts for reproducibility.
- An error analysis by phenomenon (e.g., harmony, templatic patterns) could guide future extensions.
- If possible, include a small ablation on the feedback connection (previous symbol embedding) to quantify its contribution.
