Summary and claimed contributions
This paper proposes a neural encoder–decoder model for morphological inflection generation that enforces hard, nearly monotonic attention. The decoder predicts a sequence of WRITE(symbol) and STEP actions; attention is a single pointer advanced by STEP. Training is supervised by externally induced character alignments, from which gold action sequences are derived; learning then reduces to cross-entropy over actions. The model is evaluated on CELEX, Wiktionary, and SIGMORPHON 2016, and is compared to soft-attention and WFST-based systems. The paper also analyzes learned alignments and encoder representations, contrasting hard vs. soft attention.

Contributions as I see them (in order of strength):
1) A simple and effective action-based, hard-monotonic attention formulation for character-level transduction trained from external alignments, which yields strong performance in low-resource settings.
2) A thorough empirical evaluation on standard morphology benchmarks showing competitive or state-of-the-art results, especially surpassing prior neural and non-neural systems on CELEX.
3) An insightful analysis comparing learned alignments/representations under hard vs. soft attention for this task.

Strengths
- Strong low-resource performance with a clear inductive bias: On CELEX, the model surpasses Dreyer et al.’s latent WFST and recent neural systems. The learning curves indicate better generalization vs. soft attention, supporting the claim that explicit monotonicity helps avoid overfitting when data are scarce.
- Competitive results at scale: On Wiktionary and SIGMORPHON, the system is on par with or better than strong neural/non-neural baselines across several languages, particularly for suffixing + stem-change systems where monotonicity is appropriate.
- Simple training objective and stability: Decoupling alignment induction from transduction reduces training to supervised sequence prediction. This avoids the complexity of marginalizing over alignments (e.g., CTC/RNN-T, NWFST), yet still yields strong results.
- Useful qualitative analysis: The alignment visualizations and SVD projections shed light on what the models encode (character identity and positional cues), and illustrate how the hard attention mechanism operationalizes edits (e.g., deletions via repeated STEP).
- Clear writing and presentation: The paper is generally well structured and easy to follow, with sensible architectural choices and baselines for morphology.

Weaknesses
- Limited scope beyond morphology: The method’s motivation is broadly applicable to monotone transduction (e.g., transliteration, G2P, some ASR settings), yet evaluation is restricted to inflection. Demonstrating gains on at least one additional monotone task would strengthen generality claims.
- Missing align-then-tag baseline: A simple 1-to-many alignment followed by a BiLSTM sequence tagger/labeler (predicting output symbols and NULL/STEP) is a strong, natural baseline. Without it, it is hard to assess how much the proposed controller adds beyond a standard BiRNN with external alignments.
- Alignment formalism and supervision are under-specified: It is unclear which alignment types are permitted (1–1, 1–0, 0–1, 1–n, n–1, n–m) and how they are deterministically mapped to actions. The paper notes the best-derivation approximation (Eq. 2 vs. Eq. 1) but does not quantify its impact or compare to sum-over-alignments training.
- Ablations on morpho-syntactic features are missing: The source and quality of features (POS, gender, etc.) are not fully described, nor is their contribution disentangled from the hard monotonicity bias. It remains plausible that features drive a nontrivial portion of the gains.
- Reporting and statistical rigor: Phrases like “on par or better” are imprecise; tables mix averages with per-language/per-tagset results without significance testing. More precise reporting and significance tests would help substantiate the claims.

Questions to authors (for response)
- Alignment details: Which alignment types does the Sudoh et al. aligner produce and which do you allow in training (1–0/0–1/1–1/1–n/n–1/n–m)? How exactly are many-to-many alignments linearized into STEP/WRITE actions? Are there cases of non-monotone links and how are they handled?
- Baseline omission: How does your method differ functionally from a simple align-then-label baseline (BiLSTM sequence tagger over aligned input, predicting output characters and NULL/STEP)? Can you add such a baseline and report results?
- Sum-over-alignments vs. fixed alignments: Do stronger aligners or training that marginalizes alignments (e.g., CTC/RNN-T, Rastogi et al.) improve performance? Please provide a comparison or ablation quantifying the impact of using fixed vs. marginalized alignments.
- Feature ablations: What is the performance with no features, or with features replaced by random embeddings? How sensitive is the model to feature accuracy? Please report an ablation isolating the contribution of the hard attention vs. features.
- Decoding and complexity: Do you use greedy or beam decoding over actions? What is the effect of beam size on accuracy and runtime? Any cases of attention pointer running off the input?
- Relation to prior architectures: In what way is your controller different from Stack-LSTM-style transition systems or pointer networks? Is your model effectively equivalent to a BiRNN encoder plus a locally normalized transition system?
- Generality: Can you include at least one additional monotone task (e.g., transliteration, G2P) to demonstrate broader applicability?

Additional comments
- Prior work to discuss: Please relate to Cohn et al. (2016) on alignment in NMT and to transition-based neural architectures (e.g., Stack LSTMs for parsing/segmentation) that also predict action sequences.
- Clarify Eq. (3): Replace “NN(…)” with an explicit probabilistic notation or clarify that it denotes the network’s conditional distribution; fix capitalization in “where”; use consistent ellipsis x1, …, xn; and correct the noted typo around l.437. The Cyrillic font in Fig. 1 is unnecessary and hinders readability.
- Best-derivation vs. best-sequence: Consider quantifying how often multiple derivations produce the same surface output and whether the best-derivation approximation hurts accuracy.
- Significance and precision: Replace “on par or better” with exact statements, and add significance tests for table averages; report standard deviations across folds where applicable.
- Reproducibility: Provide more details about the feature sources, aligner configurations, and hyperparameters; if possible, release alignment supervision alongside code.

Overall, this is a solid paper with a simple, well-motivated inductive bias that yields clear gains in low-resource morphology. Addressing the missing baseline, alignment formalism, and broader evaluation would considerably strengthen the submission.
