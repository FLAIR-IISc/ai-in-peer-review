Summary and Claimed Contributions
The paper introduces a neural encoder–decoder model with a hard, monotonic attention mechanism for morphological inflection generation. The decoder operates over a sequence of two action types—WRITE(character) and STEP—to exploit the near-monotonic alignment typically observed between source and inflected forms. Training is supervised with oracle action sequences derived from independently learned character-level alignments, enabling simple cross-entropy training rather than latent-alignment marginalization. The model is evaluated on CELEX, Wiktionary, and SIGMORPHON 2016 datasets, with analysis comparing learned alignments and representations against a soft-attention baseline.

Contributions (as I see them, strongest first):
1) A simple and effective hard monotonic attention architecture that explicitly models pointer movement and write actions, trained on external alignments, and approximating sequence prediction via best-derivation.
2) Strong empirical results, especially in low-resource settings (CELEX), while remaining competitive or superior on larger benchmarks (Wiktionary; parts of SIGMORPHON 2016).
3) An insightful comparative analysis of the learned alignments and character-in-context representations for hard vs. soft attention.

Strengths
1) Well-motivated modeling choice that exploits task structure. The hard attention with explicit STEP/WRITE closely matches the near-monotonic nature of most inflectional processes (suffixing and stem changes). This inductive bias is supported by the alignment visualizations, where even soft attention tends to produce monotonic alignments, validating the modeling premise.

2) Clear advantage in low-resource regimes. On CELEX, the model surpasses both neural and non-neural baselines, including the Dreyer et al. latent-variable WFST tailored for this task, which prior neural approaches failed to beat with 500 training instances per inflection type. The learning curves further show reduced overfitting relative to soft attention. This is a meaningful result for scenarios where labeled paradigms are scarce.

3) Competitive performance at scale and across languages. On the larger Wiktionary datasets, the model achieves the best average accuracy across multiple language/part-of-speech subsets, outperforming strong neural and non-neural baselines, including approaches without attention and those that jointly learn alignments. On SIGMORPHON 2016, it is strongest for suffixing-with-stem-change languages (e.g., Russian, German, Spanish), aligning with its design assumptions.

4) Simpler training and decoding pipeline. By deriving oracle action sequences from external alignments, training reduces to standard cross-entropy, avoiding forward–backward or reinforcement objectives often needed with latent alignments. This simplicity is a practical plus and may contribute to stability and data-efficiency.

5) Useful analysis of learned representations. The comparative SVD visualizations suggest that both models encode character identity and position, with soft attention appearing to encode positional information more strongly. This sheds light on how the hard attention model can rely on control actions for positional cues, potentially helping generalization with limited data.

Weaknesses
1) Dependence on external alignments and alignment quality. The approach requires a separate alignment step (CRP-based aligner). The paper does not quantify sensitivity to alignment noise, compare alternative aligners, or ablate the effect of oracle quality. This external dependency may limit portability and robustness in domains with noisier or less monotonic mappings.

2) Not universally state-of-the-art across typologies. On SIGMORPHON 2016, performance lags behind soft attention for languages exhibiting strong non-local dependencies (e.g., vowel/consonant harmony). While this aligns with the model’s bias, it underlines that the method is not uniformly superior and may require hybridization or relaxation of monotonicity for such phenomena.

3) Objective mismatch and lack of analysis of best-derivation approximation. The model optimizes the highest-probability derivation rather than marginalizing over derivations that yield the same output. The paper acknowledges this but provides no empirical assessment of when this approximation hurts, nor comparisons to marginalization-based training (e.g., RNN-T style or Yu et al. with learned transition probabilities).

4) Missing practical considerations. There is little discussion of computational cost, training/decoding speed, memory footprint, or beam-search settings vs. greedy decoding. Given the claim of simplicity, reporting runtime and complexity would help assess practicality and reproducibility.

5) Uneven comparison settings and limited ablations. Some baselines (e.g., MED) are trained for fewer epochs; the paper notes its own soft attention implementation trained longer performs better. A more controlled comparison would strengthen claims. Ablations isolating the contributions of (a) external alignments, (b) action set design, and (c) feature embeddings would clarify what drives gains.

Questions to Authors
- How sensitive is performance to the quality of the external aligner? Have you tried simpler aligners (e.g., edit-distance based) or varied alignment noise to assess robustness?
- What decoding strategy is used (greedy vs. beam)? How does beam size affect accuracy and speed?
- Can the model handle limited non-monotonicity (e.g., local swaps) without collapsing accuracy, and could you extend the action set to support jumps or COPY explicitly?
- Do you observe gains from marginalizing over multiple oracle derivations induced by alternative plausible alignments, rather than training on a single oracle?
- What are the training and inference runtimes compared to soft attention on the same hardware? Any notable differences in memory usage?
- How do morpho-syntactic feature embeddings contribute? An ablation (no features vs. features) would be informative, especially in low-resource settings.

Additional Comments
- The paper is clearly written, with helpful figures; more detailed error analysis per language type (beyond a few examples) would further support the typology-based conclusions.
- Reporting variance across runs would help gauge stability, particularly on CELEX.
