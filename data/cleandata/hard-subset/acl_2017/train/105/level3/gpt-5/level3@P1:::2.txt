Summary
The paper proposes a neural encoder–decoder for morphological inflection that replaces soft attention with a hard, monotonic control mechanism. The decoder predicts a sequence of actions from an alphabet augmented with a special step symbol; step advances a single-pointer attention over the biLSTM-encoded input, while write emits output characters. Alignments are not learned jointly: the model is trained from externally induced character alignments that are deterministically converted into gold action sequences. Experiments on CELEX, Wiktionary, and SIGMORPHON show strong performance, especially in low-resource settings. An analysis compares representations learned by hard vs. soft attention and argues that hard attention leverages monotonicity while using a simpler training objective.

Contributions (as I see them)
- A hard monotonic attention architecture for nearly monotone string transduction, trained from external alignments via a simple cross-entropy objective, avoiding marginalization over alignments.
- Empirical evidence that enforcing monotonicity yields clear gains in low-resource morphology and remains competitive at scale; plus a cross-model analysis of learned alignments/representations.
- A practical training recipe that decouples alignment induction from transduction, enabling stable optimization and interpretable control sequences.

Strengths
- Clear performance gains in low-resource settings. On CELEX, the model surpasses strong WFST baselines and recent neural systems, which is notable given the known data-hunger of encoder–decoder models. The observed gap between dev and train for soft attention vs. hard attention (Figure 2) supports the claim that monotonic constraints regularize learning when data are scarce.
- Method matches the structure of the task. Inflection typically involves local edits and suffixing; an explicit step/write control aligns well with the phenomenon, and the pointer mechanism is easy to interpret. The approach captures common one-to-many and deletions via consecutive writes or multiple steps, respectively.
- Simpler and more stable training. By training on oracle action sequences derived from externally learned alignments, the model avoids expensive marginalization or REINFORCE-style training and converges with standard cross-entropy. This contrasts with methods that must sum over alignments (e.g., neural WFSTs) or jointly learn soft alignments.
- Competitive large-scale results and nuanced analysis. The system attains the best averages on Wiktionary and remains close to a strong soft-attention baseline on SIGMORPHON, with a linguistically plausible pattern: hard attention excels for suffixing + stem-change languages, while soft attention slightly leads in languages with long-distance dependencies (vowel/consonant harmony). The encoder analysis (SVD plots) provides insight into positional vs. character-type encoding in hard vs. soft attention.

Weaknesses
- Limited novelty relative to align-then-transduce baselines. Given the reliance on external monotone alignments and an action vocabulary of step/write, the approach is close in spirit to a BiRNN that tags aligned positions with edits (i.e., an align-then-tag pipeline). The paper should more carefully articulate what is fundamentally gained by the recurrent action decoder and pointer, beyond a BiLSTM sequence tagger over 1-to-many alignments.
- Evaluation confined to morphology. While the motivation is broadly monotonic seq2seq, the paper does not test on other classic monotone transduction tasks (e.g., transliteration, grapheme-to-phoneme, OCR post-correction). This limits the generality of the empirical claim that hard monotonic attention is broadly preferable.
- Alignment specification and training signal are under-specified. It remains unclear what alignment classes the external aligner produces and how they are mapped to actions: does the oracle allow 1–0, 0–1, 1–1 that are then collapsed into many-to-many? Are there constraints to ensure true monotonicity? Without clarity, it is hard to assess what portion of the gains stem from the aligner vs. the decoder.
- Missing baselines and ablations. A simple align-then-BiLSTM tagger trained on 1-to-many alignments would be a strong, inexpensive baseline. In addition, an ablation that uses greedy alignment vs. marginalizing over alignments (as in neural WFSTs) would quantify the cost/benefit of the best-derivation approximation. Finally, a comparison to transition-based, action-sequence models (e.g., stack-LSTM-style decoding) would clarify the architectural contribution.
- Feature confound and reporting precision. The source and impact of morpho-syntactic features (POS, gender, etc.) are not fully detailed. It is possible that feature usage rather than monotonic control accounts for some gains. Also, statements like “on par or better” should be replaced by precise, per-language/per-dataset deltas with statistical testing where feasible.

Questions to Authors
- Alignment details: What alignment types are permitted by the external aligner (1–0, 0–1, 1–1, many–1, 1–many, many–many)? How exactly are these converted into step/write sequences? Please include examples for each case.
- Oracle construction: If multiple alignments yield the same output, how is the oracle action sequence chosen? Have you evaluated sensitivity to the alignment tool or to alternative aligners?
- Best-derivation vs. best-sequence: Do you have empirical evidence that approximating argmax over derivations is sufficient? Any cases where different derivations lead to the same y but different model behavior?
- Baselines: How does your model compare to a strong align-then-BiLSTM tagger that predicts edit operations (including COPY/DELETE/INSERT) under 1-to-many alignments? What does your model add beyond that?
- Generalization: Have you tried the model on transliteration or G2P to back up the broader monotonicity claim? If not, what obstacles do you foresee?
- Features: From where are morpho-syntactic features obtained, and how are they represented? Can you provide an ablation on feature usage vs. no features?

Additional comments
- Related work: Please discuss connections to transition-based action decoders (e.g., stack-LSTM parsing/action prediction) and to recent attention mechanisms that incorporate monotonicity or locality constraints.
- Minor edits: Fix NN in Eq. (3) and the lowercase “where”; ensure consistent ellipsis notation (x1, …, xn); correct the typo around line 437; avoid unnecessary Cyrillic font in Figure 1. Replace vague phrases like “on par or better” with exact numbers. Include confidence intervals or significance tests if possible.
