- Proposes an encoder–decoder with explicit hard monotonic attention using external alignments; decoder emits WRITE(symbol) and STEP actions consistent with monotone alignments.
- Decouples alignment induction and transduction by training a neural transition system on precomputed alignments with cross-entropy, avoiding forward–backward marginalization.
- Reports strong results on three morphology benchmarks, especially in low-resource settings, and provides representation analysis comparing hard vs. soft attention.

- Strength: Explicit hard monotonic attention is a clean, principled way to enforce monotonicity, enabling stable training with a simple WRITE/STEP alphabet.
- Strength: Strong empirical performance in low-resource settings with convincing gains over soft-attention and WFST baselines.
- Strength: Paper is clear and well presented, with informative analysis of learned representations and alignment behavior.
- Strength: Simpler and more efficient to train/implement than models that sum over alignments (e.g., RNN-T, NWFST) while remaining competitive.
- Strength: Careful multi-dataset comparisons and useful typological discussion for SIGMORPHON.

- Weakness: Omits a baseline that aligns source→output substrings then trains a BiLSTM tagger (1-to-many align-then-tag), which is a direct lean alternative given the decoupling.
- Weakness: Method may be functionally similar to a BiRNN tagger that predicts per-source output sequences, making the unique benefit of the controller unclear without the missing baseline.
- Weakness: Claims for enforcing monotonicity are only demonstrated on morphology; additional monotone tasks (transliteration, G2P, OCR) are needed for broader claims.
- Weakness: Alignment formalism and training objective are unclear—types of allowed alignments, merging heuristic, and use of best-derivation vs. marginalization are not well specified or quantified.
- Weakness: Reporting is imprecise (phrases like “on par or better”); morpho-syntactic feature sources, coverage, and ablation are under-described, risking confounded improvements.

- Q: Precisely which alignment types (1-1, 1-0, 0-1, many-to-1) are allowed and how are they merged into action sequences; provide deterministic mapping examples for insertions, deletions, and multi-char spans.
- Q: What exact merging heuristic creates many-to-many blocks and can it create non-local WRITE bursts that hurt monotonicity?
- Q: Why not include a 1-to-many alignment + BiLSTM sequence tagger baseline and a non-neural CRF/semi-CRF tagger as lightweight comparisons?
- Q: Do you use greedy decoding or beam search (beam size, impact), and do you apply exposure-bias mitigation like scheduled sampling?
- Q: Quantify the accuracy cost of training/inference on a single gold alignment vs. marginalizing over derivations and compare to NWFST/Rastogi et al. (2016).
- Q: Specify source, encoding, and coverage of morpho-syntactic features and provide ablations (no features; only features; hard vs. soft attention with identical features).
- Q: Can you evaluate on at least one additional monotone task (e.g., transliteration or G2P) to demonstrate generality?
- Q: Discuss connections to Cohn et al. (2016) on structural alignment biases and action-based/Stack-LSTM transition architectures (e.g., Dyer et al.), clarifying similarities/differences in supervision and search.

- Reporting: Replace vague phrases with exact numbers, significance testing, and appropriate micro/macro averages.
- Minor fixes: Clarify Eq. (3) softmax/argmax notation, capitalization and ellipsis spacing, fix typos (line 437), and remove the Cyrillic font in Fig. 1.
- Analysis suggestion: Complement SVD plots with quantitative probing (e.g., linear probes for position/character class).
- Reiteration: Even after clarifying mixed alignments, include the 1-x alignment + neural tagger baseline to quantify the added value of the explicit hard-attention controller.
