Summary and contributions
- The paper proposes an encoder–decoder model with an explicit hard monotonic attention mechanism for character-level morphological inflection. Alignments are obtained externally; the decoder predicts a sequence of WRITE(symbol) and STEP actions consistent with monotone alignments.
- The approach decouples alignment and transduction: first induce character alignments, then train a neural transition system to generate outputs consistent with those alignments via cross-entropy, avoiding forward–backward marginalization.
- The paper reports strong results on three morphology benchmarks, particularly in low-resource settings, and provides an insightful representation analysis comparing hard vs. soft attention.

Strengths
- Explicit hard monotonic attention with external alignments: The model is a clean, principled way to enforce monotonicity where appropriate. It leverages a simple decoding alphabet (WRITE/STEP), avoids learning alignments from scratch, and enables stable training with cross-entropy. This is a clear contribution for tasks with known monotone structure.
- Strong empirical performance in low-resource settings: The CELEX gains over soft attention and WFST baselines are compelling and directly support the motivation that monotonicity is beneficial and should be enforced. The learning-curve analysis strengthens this point.
- Clarity and presentation: The paper is well written and easy to follow. The analysis of learned representations and alignment behavior is informative and goes beyond a black-box empirical report.
- Simplicity and efficiency: Compared to models that sum over alignments (e.g., RNN-T, Neural Transducer, NWFST), this model is simpler to train and implement while remaining competitive or better on morphology.
- Careful comparisons on multiple morphology datasets: Results are competitive or state-of-the-art on several settings; the typological discussion for SIGMORPHON is useful.

Weaknesses
- Missing strong align-then-tag baseline: A simple 1-to-many alignment followed by a BiLSTM sequence tagger (e.g., Schnober et al.-style) is not included. Since your method also decouples alignment and transduction, it is necessary to compare against this lean alternative to assess what the hard-attention control buys beyond tagging with precomputed alignments.
- Possible equivalence to a BiRNN tagger: Given external monotone alignments, the model may be functionally close to a BiRNN that tags each source position with a (possibly empty) output sequence, with a trivial STEP policy. Without the align-then-tag baseline, it is hard to isolate the value of the explicit control mechanism vs. a simpler architecture.
- Evaluation limited to morphology: The paper argues broadly for enforcing monotonicity in seq2seq, but only tests on morphology. Transliteration, G2P, OCR post-correction, or monotone translational variants (low-reordering MT) would substantiate broader claims.
- Alignment type and training objective unclear: It is not clear whether alignments are strictly 1-to-many (and 1-0/0-1), allow many-to-1, or are merged into many-to-many blocks before deriving actions. Moreover, the model optimizes best-derivation rather than marginalizing over derivations (Eq. 2 vs. Eq. 1) and uses greedy/teacher-forced supervision. The cost of this approximation vs. summing over alignments (Rastogi et al., 2016; RNN-T variants) is not quantified.
- Reporting and feature confounds: Phrases like “on par or better” are imprecise; provide exact differences and significance testing. The linguistic features (POS, etc.) are under-described: sources, coverage, and ablations are needed to ensure improvements stem from monotonicity/control rather than features.

Questions to authors
- Alignment formalism:
  - Precisely which alignment types are permitted by Sudoh et al. (2013) here: 1-1, 1-0, 0-1 only, or also many-to-1? Do you subsequently merge alignments into many-to-many blocks? Please give examples and the deterministic mapping from alignment to action sequences, including for insertions, deletions, and substitutions spanning >1 char.
  - After your response: you mention mixed 1-0/0-1/1-1 alignments later merged into many-to-many. Please specify the exact merging heuristic and whether it ever creates non-local WRITE bursts that degrade monotonicity.
- Baselines:
  - Why not include a 1-to-many alignment + BiLSTM sequence tagger baseline? Concretely: align source chars to output substrings; train a BiLSTM to predict the aligned substring (including empty) per source position; reconstruct output by concatenation. How does your controller differ substantively from that pipeline, and why might it be superior?
  - Please also consider a non-neural CRF/semi-CRF tagger atop the alignments as a lightweight non-neural baseline.
- Objective/decoding:
  - Do you use greedy decoding or beam search? If the latter, beam size and impact? Any exposure-bias mitigation (e.g., scheduled sampling)?
  - Quantify whether training/inference with a single gold alignment (best-derivation) loses accuracy vs. marginalization over alignments/derivations. Can you compare to NWFST (Rastogi et al., 2016) with matched data to isolate this factor?
- Features:
  - Specify the source and encoding of morpho-syntactic features. Provide ablations: no features; only features; hard vs. soft attention with identical features. Do features account for the CELEX gains?
- Scope:
  - Can you report on at least one additional monotone task (e.g., transliteration or G2P) to demonstrate generality?
- Related work:
  - Please discuss Cohn et al. (2016) on structural alignment biases in attention and connections to action-based/Stack-LSTM transition architectures (e.g., Dyer et al.) that also predict ADVANCE/WRITE-like actions. What is similar/different in supervision and search?
