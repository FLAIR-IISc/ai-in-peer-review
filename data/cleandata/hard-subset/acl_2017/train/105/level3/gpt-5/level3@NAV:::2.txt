Summary
This paper proposes a neural encoder–decoder for morphological inflection with explicit hard monotonic attention. Alignments are learned externally; decoding alternates between ADVANCE (step) and WRITE actions. The authors evaluate on CELEX, Wiktionary, and SIGMORPHON, claim competitive or SOTA results, and analyze learned representations versus soft attention.

Contributions (as I see them)
- A simple, explicit hard-monotonic attention architecture that decouples alignment from transduction and trains with cross-entropy from oracle action sequences.
- Empirical evidence that enforcing monotonicity improves robustness in low-resource inflection, with competitive performance at scale.
- A qualitative analysis contrasting learned alignments/representations under hard vs. soft attention.

Strengths
- Strong motivation: monotonicity is appropriate for many string transductions; enforcing it reduces overfitting in small-data settings. The CELEX gains substantiate this point.
- Clear, well-written exposition and an intuitive control mechanism (step/write) that yields stable training without latent alignment learning.
- Solid empirical showing on three established morphology benchmarks, including surpassing latent-variable WFST on CELEX and competitive results on larger datasets.
- Useful analysis comparing hard and soft encoders, highlighting positional encoding differences and showing that soft attention also discovers near-monotone alignments.

Weaknesses
- Missing, critical baseline: a simple align-then-tag system (e.g., 1-to-many alignment + BiLSTM sequence tagger in the style of Schnober et al.) could approximate the proposed method. Without it, the incremental contribution of the control mechanism is unclear.
- Unclear alignment type and combination: after response, alignments seem to include 1–0, 0–1, 1–1, later merged into many-to-many. The exact oracle derivation and its impact on modeling capacity need to be specified.
- Scope: evaluation is limited to morphology; no tests on other monotone seq2seq tasks (e.g., transliteration, grapheme-to-phoneme) to demonstrate broader utility.
- Reporting/analysis gaps: “on par or better” is imprecise; provide exact deltas and statistical tests. Linguistic features (e.g., POS) are insufficiently described; sources and ablations are needed to rule out feature-driven gains rather than monotonicity.
- Learning objective: the best-derivation approximation with greedy alignments may underperform sum-over-alignments approaches (e.g., Rastogi et al., 2016); this trade-off is not quantified. Related work such as Cohn et al. (2016) and connections to Stack-LSTM action models deserve discussion.

Questions to Authors
- Precisely what alignment classes are allowed in training/oracle (1–x, x–1, x–y)? How are many-to-many spans linearized into actions?
- How does your method differ in capacity and inference from a 1–x alignment + BiLSTM tagger over actions? Why was this baseline omitted?
- Do greedy oracle alignments hurt compared with marginalizing over alignments? Can you report a comparison or upper bound?
- Please detail feature sources and provide ablations isolating the effect of monotonicity.
- Minor: fix notation/formatting (Eq. 3 “NN”, lowercase “where”, x1,...,xn spacing), l.437 typo, and remove unnecessary Cyrillic font in Fig. 1.
