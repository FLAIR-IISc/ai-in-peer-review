- Introduces a character-level encoder–decoder with hard, nearly-monotonic attention that emits write actions and a step control to advance a pointer over a bi-LSTM encoder.  
- Uses externally induced character alignments converted to oracle action sequences for straightforward cross-entropy training.  
- Evaluated on CELEX, Wiktionary, and SIGMORPHON 2016, and compared against a soft-attention baseline with analyses of alignments and representations.

Main contributions:
- Simple, effective hard monotonic attention architecture for character transduction that leverages near-monotonicity and decouples alignment from transduction.  
- Strong empirical results: robust low-resource gains (CELEX) and competitive/SOTA average accuracy on larger benchmarks.  
- Analysis showing hard attention learns intuitive monotonic alignments and positional/representational differences that help low-data generalization.

Strengths:
- Task-appropriate inductive bias (write/step) yielding interpretability and reduced overfitting to non-monotone mappings.  
- Outperforms prior neural and non-neural baselines in low-resource settings, important for morphologically rich languages.  
- Broad, careful empirical evaluation across datasets, splits, and languages with qualitative analyses of behaviors.  
- Insightful representational and alignment analyses highlighting why hard attention generalizes with little data.  
- Clear writing and transparent description of architecture, training, and oracle construction.

Weaknesses:
- Limited applicability for highly non-monotonic morphologies (harmony, templatic), where soft attention can outperform.  
- Dependence on a single external aligner without sensitivity/robustness analysis to alignment quality or noise.  
- Objective mismatch: optimizes best-derivation not marginalized outputs, with no empirical quantification of search/derivation effects.  
- Missing computational/practical analysis: no training/inference time, memory, or decoding complexity reported.  
- Comparability issues with baselines: differing training schedules, no significance testing, unclear run variance.

Questions for authors:
- How sensitive are results to choice/quality of the external aligner or to injected alignment noise?  
- What decoding strategy is used at test time (greedy vs beam), and what beam sizes/accuracy-speed tradeoffs were observed?  
- Can the control mechanism be extended for limited non-monotone jumps (backward steps or learned skips) while retaining low-resource gains?  
- Do you have runtime and memory comparisons versus soft attention and WFST baselines?  
- How often do multiple derivations map to the same output, and would marginalizing change outcomes?

Additional comments / requested clarifications:
- Clarify morphological feature representation (embedding sizes/composition) and regularization (dropout, weight decay).  
- Report variance across runs and statistical significance for key comparisons.  
- Provide more detail on oracle construction from alignments (tie-breaking for many-to-many) and release scripts.  
- Add error analysis by phenomenon (e.g., harmony, templatic patterns) to guide extensions.  
- Include ablation on the feedback connection (previous symbol embedding) to quantify its contribution.
