Summary and contributions:
- The paper introduces a hard monotonic attention model for morphological inflection that reduces decoding to a sequence of WRITE/STEP actions, trained on externally induced character alignments (via a Bayesian/CRP transliteration aligner). The central empirical finding is that this decoupled, monotonic formulation generalizes better in low-resource regimes and is competitive at scale.
- A simple transition-system formulation with cross-entropy training on oracle action sequences derived from alignments, offering a clear alternative to soft attention that jointly learns alignment and transduction.
- Comparative analyses of alignments and encoder representations for hard vs. soft attention, offering insight into what is being learned.

Strengths:
- Strong low-resource performance: On CELEX, the model surpasses prior neural and non-neural baselines, including the latent-variable WFST, addressing a key weakness of seq2seq with soft attention. The write/step control that enforces near-monotonicity plausibly reduces overfitting and yields better dev accuracy curves.
- Broad and careful evaluation: Results on CELEX, Wiktionary, and SIGMORPHON span diverse languages and data scales, with consistent gains in suffixing/mostly monotonic settings (e.g., German, Russian, Spanish) and competitive averages on larger datasets.
- Conceptual simplicity and training stability: External alignments allow straightforward cross-entropy training without latent alignment marginalization, likely simplifying optimization and improving data efficiency.
- Clear writing and useful qualitative analysis: Visualizations of alignments and SVD plots of encoder states help interpret model behavior and support the monotonicity assumption.

Weaknesses:
- Dependency on external alignments: Performance hinges on the quality/monotonicity of the induced alignments. The paper lacks ablations on alignment source, noise sensitivity, or oracle vs. learned alignment quality, making robustness unclear.
- Limited applicability beyond monotonic regimes: The method is not state-of-the-art in languages with non-monotonic or highly context-dependent phenomena (e.g., vowel/consonant harmony), which constrains generality.
- Objective mismatch: Training searches for best derivations rather than sequences; there is no empirical study of how this approximation affects errors or whether marginalizing over derivations would help.
- Missing practical analysis: No discussion or measurement of computational cost, wall-clock time, or implementation complexity relative to soft attention or WFST baselines; comparability concerns include differing training schedules (e.g., MED epochs) and absence of statistical significance testing.

Questions to authors (optional):
- How sensitive is performance to alignment noise and to the degree of non-monotonicity? Please provide ablations with different aligners and synthetic noise.
- What are the training/inference speed and memory footprints vs. soft attention and WFSTs?
- Does beam search over action sequences reduce the derivation-vs-sequence gap?
- Can the model adaptively relax monotonicity (e.g., limited backward steps) to better handle harmony and templatic morphology?
