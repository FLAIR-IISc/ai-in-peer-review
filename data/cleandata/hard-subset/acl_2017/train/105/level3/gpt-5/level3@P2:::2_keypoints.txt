- Proposes a neural encoder–decoder for morphological inflection using hard, nearly-monotonic attention with decoder actions WRITE(symbol) and STEP.  
- Training uses externally induced character alignments to derive gold action sequences and cross-entropy over actions.  
- Evaluated on CELEX, Wiktionary, and SIGMORPHON 2016, compared to soft-attention and WFST systems.  
- Contribution: simple, effective action-based hard-monotonic attention that performs well in low-resource settings.  
- Contribution: thorough empirical evaluation showing competitive or state-of-the-art results, notably on CELEX.  
- Contribution: analysis comparing learned alignments and encoder representations under hard vs. soft attention.  
- Strength: strong low-resource generalization, outperforming prior WFST and neural systems on CELEX.  
- Strength: competitive at scale on Wiktionary and SIGMORPHON, especially for suffixing + stem-change languages.  
- Strength: simple, stable training by decoupling alignment induction from transduction, avoiding alignment marginalization complexity.  
- Strength: useful qualitative analyses (alignment visualizations and SVD projections) illuminating learned encodings.  
- Strength: clear writing, sensible architecture choices, and appropriate baselines for morphology.  
- Weakness: evaluation restricted to morphology; broader monotone tasks (transliteration, G2P, ASR) not tested.  
- Weakness: missing a natural align-then-tag (BiLSTM labeler) baseline to isolate controller gains.  
- Weakness: alignment formalism and mapping to actions under-specified (which alignment types are allowed, deterministic mapping).  
- Weakness: no ablations isolating the contribution of morpho-syntactic features vs. hard monotonicity bias.  
- Weakness: imprecise reporting and lack of statistical significance testing across results.  
- Question: which alignment types does the Sudoh et al. aligner produce and which are allowed in training?  
- Question: how are many-to-many alignments linearized into STEP/WRITE actions and how are non-monotone links handled?  
- Question: how does the method functionally differ from a BiLSTM align-then-label baseline; can results for that baseline be added?  
- Question: would marginalizing alignments (CTC/RNN-T, Rastogi et al.) or stronger aligners improve performance—any ablation?  
- Question: what is performance without features or with random feature embeddings; how sensitive is the model to feature accuracy?  
- Question: is decoding greedy or beam search; what is beam size effect on accuracy and runtime; any pointer overruns?  
- Question: relation to prior transition/stack-LSTM or pointer-network architectures—are they effectively equivalent?  
- Additional comment: relate to Cohn et al. (2016) and transition-based neural architectures in related work.  
- Additional comment: clarify notation in Eq. (3), fix typos, and remove Cyrillic font in Fig. 1 for readability.  
- Additional comment: quantify best-derivation vs. sum-over-alignments impact and how often multiple derivations yield same output.  
- Additional comment: improve precision in claims (“on par or better”), add significance tests, standard deviations, and more reproducibility details (aligner configs, features, hyperparams).  
- Overall: solid paper with a well-motivated inductive bias and clear low-resource gains; addressing baseline, alignment formalism, and broader evaluation would strengthen it.
