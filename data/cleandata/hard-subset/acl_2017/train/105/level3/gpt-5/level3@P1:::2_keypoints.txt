- Proposes a neural encoder–decoder for morphological inflection using hard, monotonic attention with step/write actions and externally induced alignments converted to oracle action sequences.
- Trains from deterministic alignments using cross-entropy, avoiding marginalization over alignments or REINFORCE-style training.
- Shows strong performance on CELEX, Wiktionary, and SIGMORPHON, especially in low-resource settings.
- Argues hard attention leverages monotonicity to regularize learning and produce interpretable pointer-based control sequences.
- Contribution: a hard monotonic attention architecture for nearly monotone string transduction trained from external alignments.
- Contribution: empirical evidence that monotonic constraints improve low-resource morphology and remain competitive at scale.
- Contribution: a practical training recipe decoupling alignment induction from transduction for stable optimization.
- Strength: Clear low-resource gains, outperforming strong WFST and neural baselines on CELEX.
- Strength: Architecture matches morphological task structure (local edits, suffixing) and provides interpretable pointer control.
- Strength: Simpler, more stable training by using oracle action sequences instead of marginalizing alignments.
- Strength: Competitive large-scale results and analysis showing linguistic patterns where hard vs. soft attention excel.
- Weakness: Limited novelty—approach is similar to align-then-transduce/tag pipelines and needs clearer justification over a BiLSTM tagger.
- Weakness: Evaluation confined to morphology; lacks tests on other monotone tasks (transliteration, G2P, OCR) to support generality.
- Weakness: Alignment specification and mapping to actions are under-specified, obscuring how much gains come from the aligner vs. decoder.
- Weakness: Missing baselines/ablations—no align-then-BiLSTM tagger baseline, no greedy vs. marginalized alignment comparison, no comparison to transition-based action models.
- Weakness: Unclear reporting on morpho-syntactic feature usage and potential confounds; some phrasing lacks precise per-language/dataset deltas and significance testing.
- Question: What alignment types does the external aligner permit (1–0, 0–1, 1–1, many–1, 1–many, many–many) and how are they converted to step/write sequences?
- Question: How is the oracle action sequence chosen when multiple alignments yield the same output, and how sensitive are results to the aligner?
- Question: Is approximating argmax over derivations sufficient—are there cases where different derivations yield different model behavior?
- Question: How does the model compare to a strong align-then-BiLSTM tagger predicting COPY/DELETE/INSERT under 1-to-many alignments?
- Question: Have experiments been run on transliteration or G2P to support broader monotonicity claims, or what obstacles exist?
- Question: Where do morpho-syntactic features come from, how are they represented, and is there an ablation without features?
- Additional comment: Discuss connections to transition-based action decoders and monotonic/locality-constrained attention work.
- Additional comment: Fix minor typos/notation (Eq.3, ellipses, line 437), avoid Cyrillic font in Fig.1, and replace vague phrases with exact numbers and significance tests.
