- Proposes a neural encoder–decoder with explicit hard monotonic attention using externally learned alignments and alternating ADVANCE/WRITE actions.
- Decoding alternates between step (ADVANCE) and WRITE actions; training uses cross-entropy on oracle action sequences.
- Evaluated on CELEX, Wiktionary, and SIGMORPHON, claiming competitive or SOTA performance and analysis versus soft attention.

- Contribution: simple hard-monotonic attention architecture that decouples alignment from transduction and trains on oracle action sequences.
- Contribution: empirical evidence that enforcing monotonicity improves robustness in low-resource morphological inflection, with competitive large-scale performance.
- Contribution: qualitative analysis contrasting learned alignments and representations under hard versus soft attention.

- Strength: strong motivation—monotonicity suits many string transductions and can reduce overfitting in small-data settings (CELEX gains support this).
- Strength: clear, well-written exposition and intuitive step/write control yielding stable training without latent alignment learning.
- Strength: solid empirical results across three morphology benchmarks, surpassing latent-variable WFST on CELEX and competitive on larger sets.
- Strength: useful analysis showing positional encoding differences and that soft attention often discovers near-monotone alignments.

- Weakness: missing critical baseline—an align-then-tag system (e.g., 1-to-many alignment + BiLSTM tagger) could approximate the method and was not compared.
- Weakness: unclear alignment types and combination—details on allowed alignment classes (1–0, 0–1, 1–1, many-to-many) and oracle derivation are insufficient.
- Weakness: limited scope—evaluation restricted to morphology, no tests on other monotone seq2seq tasks (e.g., transliteration, G2P) to show broader utility.
- Weakness: reporting and analysis gaps—phrases like “on par or better” lack precise deltas/statistical tests; linguistic feature sources and ablations are missing to rule out feature-driven gains.
- Weakness: learning objective trade-off not quantified—the greedy best-derivation approximation may underperform sum-over-alignments approaches and related work (e.g., Cohn et al., Rastogi et al.) is not fully discussed.

- Q: Precisely which alignment classes are allowed in training/oracle and how are many-to-many spans linearized into actions?
- Q: How does your method differ in capacity and inference from a 1–x alignment + BiLSTM tagger, and why was that baseline omitted?
- Q: Do greedy oracle alignments hurt compared with marginalizing over alignments—please report comparisons or upper bounds?
- Q: Provide sources for linguistic features and ablation experiments isolating the effect of monotonicity versus feature-driven gains.
- Minor request: fix notation/formatting issues (Eq.3 “NN”, lowercase “where”, spacing in x1,...,xn), correct l.437 typo, and remove Cyrillic font in Fig. 1.
