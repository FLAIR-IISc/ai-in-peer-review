- Introduces a hard monotonic attention model for morphological inflection guided by Bayesian-derived character alignments.
- Evaluated on CELEX, Wiktionary, and SIGMORPHON 2016, showing SOTA on small datasets and competitive results on larger ones.
- Provides detailed analysis comparing learned representations and alignments of hard vs. soft attention models.
- Novelty: explicit monotonic attention is a significant departure from soft attention and well-suited to inflection tasks.
- Strong performance in low-resource settings, outperforming neural and non-neural baselines on small datasets.
- Thorough experimental evaluation across low-, high-resource, and morphologically diverse datasets.
- Insightful findings about reduced reliance on positional encoding and alignment differences between models.
- Clear writing and presentation of model, training, and experiments.
- Mixed results on larger datasets and non-monotonic languages (e.g., Turkish, Arabic), limiting general applicability.
- Paper does not quantify practical benefits like implementation simplicity or computational efficiency.
- Reliance on precomputed alignments adds a preprocessing step and hinders end-to-end learning applicability.
- Claims of state-of-the-art are limited to specific conditions (monotonic, low-resource) rather than universal SOTA.
- Requests quantitative comparison of computational efficiency versus soft attention models.
- Asks how precomputed alignments affect scalability to tasks like MT or summarization.
- Suggests exploring augmentations to handle non-monotonic alignments more effectively.
- Recommendation: accept with minor revisions to clarify practical advantages and address non-monotonic limitations.
