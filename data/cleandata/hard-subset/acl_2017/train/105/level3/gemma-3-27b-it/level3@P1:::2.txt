This paper proposes a novel neural model for morphological inflection generation utilizing a hard monotonic attention mechanism. The core idea is to model the nearly-monotonic alignment commonly found between input and output characters in morphologically rich languages. The model consists of an encoder-decoder network with a control mechanism that either writes a symbol or advances the attention pointer at each step, guided by pre-learned character-level alignments. The authors demonstrate state-of-the-art results on three datasets (CELEX, Wiktionary, and SIGMORPHON2016) and analyze the learned representations, providing insights into the features captured by the model.

The primary contribution of this work is the introduction of a hard attention mechanism specifically tailored for morphological inflection, combined with a decoupled alignment-transduction training strategy.  While soft attention mechanisms have been successfully applied to this task (Kann & Schütze, 2016b), this work argues for and demonstrates the benefits of explicitly modeling the inherent monotonicity through hard attention and pre-computed alignments. A secondary, significant contribution is the strong empirical performance, surpassing previous state-of-the-art models, particularly on the challenging CELEX dataset where data sparsity is a major issue. Finally, the analysis of learned representations, comparing hard and soft attention models, offers valuable insights into how these models represent morphological information.

**Strengths:**

1. **Novelty of the Hard Attention Approach:** The application of a hard attention mechanism, directly enforcing a monotonic alignment, is a conceptually sound and potentially impactful contribution. Morphological inflection inherently exhibits this property, making the explicit modeling a natural and appealing approach.  The decoupling of alignment from the transduction process offers a simplified training procedure compared to jointly learned alignment models.
2. **Strong Empirical Results:**  The model achieves state-of-the-art results on three datasets, demonstrating its effectiveness across different morphological paradigms and data sizes. The significant performance improvement on the CELEX dataset, where the model surpasses even specialized latent variable WFSTs, is particularly noteworthy, indicating the model's ability to handle data sparsity effectively.
3. **Clear and Well-Organized Presentation:** The paper is generally well-written and clearly explains the model architecture and training procedure. The visual depiction of the hard attention mechanism in Figure 1 is particularly helpful in understanding the process.  The thorough experimental setup and comparative analysis contribute to the credibility of the results.
4. **Insightful Representation Analysis:** The comparison of learned representations between the hard and soft attention models provides valuable insights into how each mechanism captures morphological information. This analysis helps understand the strengths and limitations of both approaches.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Potential Equivalence to a Simpler Model:** A significant concern is whether the proposed hard attention model truly offers a substantial advantage over a simpler, more established approach: a character-level alignment followed by a bidirectional LSTM sequence tagger.  The paper doesn’t adequately address this, and the absence of a comparison to such a baseline weakens the claims of novelty. The hard attention mechanism, combined with pre-computed alignments, could be functionally equivalent to forcing a specific alignment pattern within a more general sequence tagging framework.
2. **Limited Scope of Evaluation:** The evaluation is restricted to morphological inflection generation. While the authors acknowledge the applicability to other monotonic sequence-to-sequence tasks, the lack of experimentation on such tasks limits the generalizability of the findings. Demonstrating performance on tasks like abstractive summarization or transliteration would strengthen the argument for the broader impact of the proposed approach.
3. **Imprecise Reporting of Results:** Phrases like "on par or better" lack the specificity required for a rigorous scientific evaluation. Precise statistical comparisons and reporting of confidence intervals are needed to accurately assess the significance of the performance improvements.
4. **Incomplete Description of Linguistic Features:**  The role of morphological features (POS tags, gender, etc.) is mentioned, but not sufficiently detailed. The source of these features, their impact on performance, and a more thorough analysis of their influence are needed. Do the performance gains stem from modeling monotonicity, or simply from effective feature representation?



**Questions to Authors:**

1.  **Comparison to Baseline:** How does the proposed method differ from a simple character-level alignment followed by a bidirectional LSTM sequence tagger? Why was this baseline omitted from the experimental evaluation?
2.  **Alignment Type:** Can you clarify the types of alignments used (1-to-1, 1-to-many, many-to-1, many-to-many)?  The description seems to imply a mix, and the evolution of the alignment process throughout the paper (initial hard alignments becoming more flexible) is unclear.
3.  **Greedy Alignment Impact:** Does using greedy alignments during training introduce a performance bottleneck compared to methods that marginalize over possible alignments (like Rastogi et al., 2016)?
