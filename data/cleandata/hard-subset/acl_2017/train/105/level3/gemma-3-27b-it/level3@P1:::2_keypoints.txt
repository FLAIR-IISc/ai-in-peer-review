- Proposal: a neural encoder–decoder with hard monotonic attention using pre-computed character alignments for morphological inflection, achieving SOTA on CELEX, Wiktionary, and SIGMORPHON2016 and analyzing learned representations.

- Strength: Novelty — explicitly enforcing monotonic alignment with a hard attention/control mechanism is conceptually sound and simplifies training via decoupled alignment and transduction.

- Strength: Empirical performance — strong, state-of-the-art results across datasets, notably large gains on sparse-data CELEX outperforming latent-variable WFSTs.

- Strength: Presentation — clear writing, helpful figures (especially Figure 1), and thorough experimental setup and comparisons.

- Strength: Analysis — insightful comparison of learned representations between hard and soft attention models illuminating how morphological information is captured.

- Weakness: Baseline omission — no comparison to a simpler character-alignment plus biLSTM sequence tagger, leaving open whether the method provides substantive advantages.

- Weakness: Limited evaluation scope — experiments confined to morphological inflection; lack of tests on other monotonic seq2seq tasks (e.g., transliteration, summarization) limits generalizability claims.

- Weakness: Reporting imprecision — phrases like “on par or better” lack statistical rigor; precise significance testing and confidence intervals are missing.

- Weakness: Incomplete linguistic detail — unclear sourcing and impact of morphological features (POS, gender, etc.); unclear whether gains stem from monotonic modeling or feature representations.

- Question: Baseline comparison — why was a character-alignment + biLSTM tagger baseline not included, and how does the proposed method differ from it?

- Question: Alignment types — please clarify which alignment types (1-1, 1-many, many-1, many-many) are used and how alignments evolve or are permitted to change.

- Question: Greedy alignments — does using greedy pre-computed alignments limit performance compared to approaches that marginalize over alignments?

- Additional comments: fix minor formatting issues (NN in Eq.3, lowercase “where”, ellipsis spacing), correct typo on line 437, remove unnecessary Cyrillic font in Figure 1.

- Additional comments: cite and discuss related work such as Cohn et al. (2016) and connections to Stack LSTM/action-based architectures.

- Recommendation: Authors should address the missing simpler-baseline comparison, expand evaluation scope, and provide clearer justification/statistical reporting to strengthen claims during rebuttal.
