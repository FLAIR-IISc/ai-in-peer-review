## Review for ACL 2017 Paper: Morphological Inflection Generation with Hard Monotonic Attention

This paper proposes a novel encoder-decoder model with hard monotonic attention for morphological inflection generation. The core idea is to decouple alignment and transduction by first establishing a monotone alignment and then generating outputs consistent with it. The authors demonstrate state-of-the-art results on three morphological inflection generation datasets.

**Main Contributions (as I see them):**

1. **Exploration of Hard Monotonic Attention:** The paper presents a well-defined and implemented approach to enforce monotonicity in a sequence-to-sequence model, specifically tailored for morphological inflection. While the concept of monotonicity isn't entirely new, applying *hard* attention to achieve this in the context of morphological generation is a valuable contribution.
2. **Strong Empirical Performance:** The model achieves competitive or state-of-the-art results across three datasets, demonstrating its effectiveness for this task. The improvement on the small CELEX dataset is particularly noteworthy, suggesting robustness to data scarcity.
3. **Analysis of Learned Representations:** The analysis comparing learned representations between hard and soft attention models provides some insight into how each approach encodes morphological information, enriching our understanding of these models.

**Strengths:**

* **Novelty of Approach:** The proposed hard monotonic attention mechanism is a clear and well-motivated contribution. It directly addresses the inherent monotonicity of morphological inflection, contrasting with the more general approach of soft attention.
* **Clear Presentation:** The paper is generally well-written and the novel idea is presented clearly, with a helpful diagram illustrating the model's operation. The mathematical notation is mostly clear, although some minor corrections are needed (see "Minor Issues").
* **Strong Experimental Results:** Achieving state-of-the-art or competitive performance on three datasets is a strong indicator of the model’s effectiveness. The ability to outperform existing methods with limited data (CELEX) is particularly impressive.
* **Insightful Analysis:** The comparison of learned representations between the hard and soft attention models provides a valuable qualitative understanding of the model’s behavior. This section highlights the differences in how each mechanism encodes positional and character-specific information.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Potential Equivalence to Simpler Model:** A significant concern is that the proposed model may be functionally equivalent to a standard BiRNN with alignments decoupled. The authors train based on pre-computed alignments, and the hard attention mechanism essentially enforces those alignments. It is unclear if the added complexity of the model provides a substantial benefit beyond simply using a BiLSTM sequence tagger with pre-aligned input.
* **Limited Scope of Evaluation:** The evaluation is restricted to morphological inflection. The authors motivate their work by arguing that monotonicity is present in other sequence-to-sequence tasks. However, they do not demonstrate the model’s effectiveness on any tasks *beyond* morphology. This limits the generalizability claim.
* **Incomplete Alignment Characterization:** The paper initially presents the alignment as being learned independently. However, the author response clarifies that the alignments are actually mixed (1-0, 0-1, 1-1) and later combined into many-to-many. This evolution in the description of the alignment process is confusing and requires further clarification. The nature of these alignments significantly impacts the interpretation of the model's behavior.
* **Reporting Imprecision:** The phrase "on par or better" is too vague. Precise statistical comparisons, including p-values and confidence intervals, are needed to support performance claims. Reporting exact numbers and statistically significant differences is crucial for a rigorous evaluation.
* **Feature Confounding:** The role of linguistic features is not adequately addressed. The authors mention using morphosyntactic features as input, but provide limited information about their source or impact. A more detailed analysis of the features – including ablation studies – is necessary to determine whether the improvements are driven by the monotonicity of the attention mechanism or by the features themselves.

**Questions to Authors:**

1. **Alignment Type and Baseline:** What specific types of alignments (1-to-1, 1-to-many, many-to-1, many-to-many) are generated by the character-level alignment process? Why was a simple 1-to-many alignment coupled with a BiLSTM sequence tagger (e.g., as in Schnober et al.) not included as a baseline? How does the proposed method demonstrably differ from this simpler approach, and what justifies the added complexity?
2. **Greedy Alignment Impact:** Does the use of greedy alignments introduce a performance bottleneck? How do the results compare to methods that sum over possible alignments (e.g., as in Rastogi et al., 2016)?
3. **Generalization to Other Tasks:**  Have you explored applying this hard monotonic attention mechanism to other sequence-to-sequence tasks where monotonicity is expected (e.g., speech recognition, machine translation of morphologically rich languages)? If so, what were the results? If not, what are the plans for such exploration?
4. **Alignment Detail:**  Can you provide a detailed breakdown of the proportions of 1-0, 0-1, and 1-1 alignments that are generated, and how they are combined into many-to-many alignments? This would help clarify the nature of the pre-computed alignment process.

**Additional Comments:**

* Minor formatting issues: Correct the notation in equation (3) to use proper boldface for parameters, ensure proper spacing for ellipsis (x<sub>1</sub>, ..., x<sub>n</sub>), fix the typo on line 437, and remove the unnecessary Cyrillic font in Figure 1.
* Consider discussing connections to Stack LSTM–style action-based architectures (Cohn et al., 2016) in the Related Work section, as both approaches involve a series of discrete actions to generate the output sequence.
