- Presents an encoder–decoder model with hard monotonic attention for morphological inflection, decoupling alignment and transduction.  
- Motivation: monotonicity in morphology makes explicit alignment control beneficial.  
- Presentation is generally clear and experiments cover three datasets.  
- Main contribution: explicitly imposing structured monotonic alignment and comparing learned representations of hard vs soft attention.  
- Strengths: novel architecture, clear writing, state-of-the-art results on several datasets, and a useful ablation comparing hard and soft attention.  
- Major weakness: possible equivalence to a simpler BiRNN with decoupled alignments is not addressed.  
- Missing baseline: no direct comparison to a strong 1-to-many alignment + BiLSTM sequence tagger.  
- Limited scope: evaluation only on morphology; generalizability to other monotone seq2seq tasks (e.g., ASR, transliteration) is not shown.  
- Reporting issue: phrases like “on par or better” lack precise statistical comparison.  
- Feature attribution unclear: unclear whether gains come from enforcing monotonicity or from added linguistic features (POS, etc.).  
- Question 1: How does this method fundamentally differ from a 1-to-many alignment + BiLSTM tagger and why was that baseline omitted?  
- Question 2: Why use greedy alignments in training; were alignment-summing methods (e.g., Rastogi et al. 2016) considered?  
- Question 3: Clarify the evolution and final combination of alignment types (1-to-many, many-to-1, 1-to-1 → many-to-many).  
- Minor requests: fix formatting/notation issues (NN in eqn (3), lowercase “where”, ellipsis spacing, l.437 typo, Cyrillic font in Fig.1) and provide deeper analysis of linguistic features.
