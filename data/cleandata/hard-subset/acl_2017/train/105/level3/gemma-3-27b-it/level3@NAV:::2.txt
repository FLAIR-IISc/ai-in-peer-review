## Review of “Morphological Inflection Generation with Hard Monotonic Attention”

This paper presents a novel encoder-decoder model for morphological inflection generation, leveraging a hard monotonic attention mechanism to enforce alignment between input and output characters. The core idea of decoupling alignment and transduction is well-motivated, given the inherent monotonicity often found in morphological processes and the potential benefits of explicit alignment control. The presentation is generally clear and the experimental setup, encompassing three datasets, is commendable.

The primary contribution of this work, as I see it, lies in the explicit attempt to impose a structured, monotonic alignment onto the sequence-to-sequence learning process for morphology. While attention mechanisms are common, directly modeling and enforcing alignment, particularly in a hard-attention framework, is a worthwhile exploration. Furthermore, the detailed analysis comparing the learned representations of the hard and soft attention models provides valuable insights into the behavior of both approaches.

Strengths of the submission include the novelty of the proposed model architecture, the well-written and generally clear presentation of the concept, and the establishment of state-of-the-art results on several morphological inflection datasets. The ablation study comparing the hard and soft attention mechanisms is also a strong point, offering a better understanding of the model’s workings.

However, the paper suffers from several weaknesses. The most significant concern is the potential equivalence of the proposed model to a simpler BiRNN with decoupled alignments. The authors do not adequately address this, and a direct comparison against a strong baseline like a 1-to-many alignment + BiLSTM sequence tagger (e.g., Schnober et al.) is missing. The evaluation is currently limited to morphology; demonstrating the generalizability of this hard monotonic attention approach on other monotone sequence-to-sequence tasks (e.g., speech recognition, transliteration) would strengthen the claims considerably. Additionally, the reporting of “on par or better” lacks precision and should be replaced with specific statistical comparisons. Finally, the role of the linguistic features (POS tags etc.) is not thoroughly analyzed; it's unclear whether the improvements are due to the monotonicity or simply the features themselves. 

**Questions to Authors:**

1.  How does the proposed method fundamentally differ from a simpler 1-to-many alignment followed by a BiLSTM sequence tagger? Why was this baseline omitted from the experimental evaluation?
2.  What is the justification for using greedy alignments during training? Have you investigated whether using methods that sum over alignments (e.g., Rastogi et al., 2016) would improve performance?
3.  Can you clarify the evolution of the alignment types used (1-to-many, many-to-1, 1-to-1) and how they were ultimately combined into a many-to-many alignment?

**Additional Comments:**

Please address the minor formatting and notation issues mentioned in the original reviewer guidelines (NN in eqn (3), lowercase “where”, proper ellipsis spacing, typo at l.437, Cyrillic font in Fig.1). A more detailed analysis of the linguistic features employed would also be beneficial.
