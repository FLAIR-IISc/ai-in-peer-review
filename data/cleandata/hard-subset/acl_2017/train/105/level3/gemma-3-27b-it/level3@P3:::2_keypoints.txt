- Proposes an encoder-decoder model with hard monotonic attention that decouples alignment and transduction for morphological inflection.
- Applies hard attention to enforce monotonicity in sequence-to-sequence models for morphological generation.
- Demonstrates state-of-the-art or competitive results on three morphological inflection datasets, notably strong on small CELEX data.
- Provides analysis comparing learned representations of hard vs. soft attention models to understand how morphological information is encoded.
- Novelty: hard monotonic attention is a well-motivated contribution targeting inherent monotonicity in inflection.
- Clear presentation with helpful diagrams and mostly clear mathematical notation.
- Strong empirical results indicating effectiveness and robustness to limited data.
- Insightful qualitative analysis showing differences in positional and character-specific encoding between attention types.
- Concern that the model may be functionally equivalent to a simpler BiRNN with pre-computed alignments, questioning added complexity.
- Evaluation scope is limited to morphology, so generalizability to other monotonic seq2seq tasks is untested.
- Alignment description is confusing—initial claim of independent learning contradicts later mixed and many-to-many alignment details.
- Performance reporting lacks precise statistical comparisons (p-values/confidence intervals) and exact significance statements.
- Role and source of morphosyntactic features are under-specified; no ablation to show whether gains come from features or monotonic attention.
- Question: What specific alignment types are produced and why not include a 1-to-many BiLSTM tagger baseline to compare complexity vs. benefit?
- Question: Do greedy alignments bottleneck performance, and how do results compare to methods that sum over alignments?
- Question: Has hard monotonic attention been applied to other monotonic seq2seq tasks (e.g., ASR, morphologically rich MT), and if so, what were results/plans?
- Question: Request for breakdown of proportions of 1-0, 0-1, and 1-1 alignments and how they form many-to-many alignments.
- Minor fixes requested: correct boldface in eq.(3), spacing/ellipsis formatting, fix typo on line 437, remove Cyrillic font in Figure 1.
- Suggests discussing connections to Stack LSTM–style action-based architectures in Related Work.
