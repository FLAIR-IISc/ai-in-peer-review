- Proposes a hard monotonic attention model using pre-computed character alignments for morphological inflection generation.
- Evaluated on CELEX, Wiktionary, and SIGMORPHON2016, achieving state-of-the-art results in several settings, especially low-resource.
- Main contribution: explicit near-monotonic hard attention guided by alignments, plus comparative analysis of learned representations versus soft attention.

Strengths:
- Novelty: explicit hard attention tailored to near-monotonic character alignments.
- Strong low-resource performance, notably on the CELEX dataset.
- Comprehensive evaluation across three diverse datasets, including multilingual SIGMORPHON2016.
- Insightful analysis showing differences in how hard vs. soft attention encode positional/morphological information.
- Clear, well-written presentation with helpful visualizations (e.g., Figure 1).

Weaknesses:
- Reliance on external pre-computed alignments introduces a potential point of failure and breaks end-to-end learning.
- Performance drops for languages with complex morphological phenomena or non-monotonic alignments.
- Lacks discussion of practical considerations like computational cost and implementation complexity of pre-computing alignments.
- Limited error analysis; few concrete examples of failure cases or diagnostic breakdowns.
- Comparisons could include more recent or stronger baselines tailored to specific datasets.

Questions for authors:
- How sensitive are results to the quality of pre-computed alignments and to different alignment tools?
- What is the computational cost of pre-computing alignments for large or morphologically rich datasets?
- What modifications would enable the model to better handle non-monotonic alignments or long-range dependencies?
- Can you provide a detailed error analysis with examples of common misinflections and their causes?
- How does the model’s parameter count compare to the soft-attention baseline?
