- Proposes a novel neural model for morphological inflection using a hard monotonic attention mechanism with a control action to emit a symbol or advance the pointer.  
- Decouples alignment learning from transduction by using pre-learned character-level alignments to guide training.  
- Achieves state-of-the-art results on CELEX, Wiktionary, and SIGMORPHON2016, with particularly strong low-resource performance.  
- Provides analysis comparing learned representations of the hard-attention model to a soft-attention baseline.  
- Main contribution: tailored hard attention for morphological inflection that explicitly models monotonic alignment and uses decoupled training.  
- Secondary contribution: comprehensive experimental evaluation across three datasets demonstrating robustness and identifying strengths/weaknesses.  
- Strength: well-motivated, innovative approach that matches task properties and shows clear gains on monotonic alignment languages.  
- Strength: extensive, rigorous experiments including strong baselines, ablation study, and insightful representation analysis.  
- Strength: paper is clearly written, well-organized, and includes helpful visualizations (Figure 1).  
- Weakness: sensitivity to non-monotonic or context-dependent transformations; performance degrades on languages needing complex, non-monotonic edits.  
- Weakness: insufficient discussion of implementation complexity and computational cost compared to soft attention, including alignment pre-processing overhead.  
- Weakness: limited depth in the comparison with soft-attention—needs more detailed analysis of learned alignments and error patterns.  
- Weakness: insufficient investigation of the impact of the chosen pre-learned alignment model (Sudoh et al., 2013) on final performance.  
- Question: how could the model be modified to handle significant non-monotonicity or complex contextual dependencies?  
- Question: what are the training and inference time costs compared to soft-attention baselines?  
- Question: how sensitive is performance to the quality of pre-learned alignments; any experiments with different aligners or thresholds?  
- Question: can the authors provide detailed examples comparing hard vs soft attention alignments and where hard attention yields better linguistic plausibility?  
- Additional comment: include error analysis to identify systematic error types.  
- Additional comment: compare with recent morphology-aware NMT approaches that use explicit morphological modeling.  
- Additional comment: ensure the GitHub link is functional in the final version.  
- Overall: strong, promising paper; addressing the weaknesses and author questions would substantially strengthen the submission.
