- Core contribution: Introduces a hard monotonic attention model for morphological inflection that uses precomputed character alignments to exploit near-monotonicity in morphological processes.  
- Novelty and soundness: Hard monotonic attention is a distinct, well-justified alternative to soft-attention approaches.  
- Use of external alignments: Character alignments are learned a priori via a Bayesian transliteration method, simplifying model learning.  
- Strong low-resource performance: Outperforms prior state-of-the-art on the small CELEX dataset, showing effectiveness with limited data.  
- Comprehensive evaluation: Experiments across CELEX, Wiktionary, and SIGMORPHON2016 compare against relevant baselines and provide a thorough assessment.  
- Clear presentation: Paper is well-written with a clearly explained architecture and experimental setup.  
- Insightful analysis: Comparison of learned representations for hard vs. soft attention offers valuable insights into their behaviors.  
- Limited applicability: Performance degrades on languages or cases with non-monotonic or context-dependent alignments, limiting universal applicability.  
- Dependency on external alignment: Reliance on separately learned alignments adds an extra step and a potential source of error tied to alignment quality.  
- Missing practical details: Lacks discussion of implementation complexity, computational cost, and memory requirements relative to soft-attention models.  
- Limited ablations: More ablation studies (e.g., effect of different alignment methods) are needed to strengthen analysis.  
- Sensitivity question: How sensitive is model performance to the quality of the independently learned alignments?  
- Computational cost question: What are the rough training and inference time comparisons to comparable soft-attention models?  
- Adaptation question: Can the hard attention mechanism be adapted to handle non-monotonic alignments, perhaps by adding some flexibility?
