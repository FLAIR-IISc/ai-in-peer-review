## Review: Morphological Inflection Generation with Hard Monotonic Attention

This paper presents a novel approach to morphological inflection generation using a hard monotonic attention mechanism. The core idea – leveraging the nearly-monotonic alignment commonly found in morphological processes – is a strong contribution. Unlike prevalent soft-attention models that jointly learn alignment and conversion, this method utilizes character alignments obtained *a priori* via a Bayesian transliteration method, simplifying the learning process. The paper is exceptionally well-written and presents a comprehensive experimental evaluation on three datasets (CELEX, Wiktionary, SIGMORPHON2016).

The primary contribution lies in the demonstration that a hard attention mechanism, guided by independently learned alignments, can be highly effective, particularly in low-resource scenarios like the CELEX dataset where it surpasses previous state-of-the-art models. A secondary contribution is the insightful analysis comparing the hard and soft attention models, highlighting differences in how they represent information and their suitability for different morphological phenomena. Finally, the extensive empirical evaluation across diverse datasets provides a valuable benchmark for future work.

**Strengths:**

1. **Novelty and Soundness:** The hard monotonic attention approach is a distinct departure from existing methods. The rationale behind exploiting the inherent monotonicity in morphological inflection is well-justified and the method itself appears logically sound.
2. **Strong Performance in Low-Resource Settings:** The model’s ability to outperform existing methods on the small CELEX dataset is a significant achievement, demonstrating its effectiveness with limited data. This is a practical advantage for morphologically rich languages where labeled data is often scarce.
3. **Comprehensive Evaluation:** The experiments cover a wide range of datasets and compare against relevant baselines, providing a thorough assessment of the method’s capabilities.
4. **Clear Presentation:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and easy to follow. The architecture is clearly explained, and the experimental setup is well-defined. 
5. **Insightful Analysis:** The comparison of learned representations between the hard and soft attention models provides valuable insights into their behavior and strengths.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Applicability:** While strong in monotonic cases, the method’s performance is not state-of-the-art when alignments become non-monotonic or more context-dependent, as seen on some SIGMORPHON languages. This limits its universal applicability.
2. **Dependency on External Alignment:** The reliance on a separately learned character alignment introduces an additional step and potential source of error. The performance is thus tied to the quality of the initial alignment.
3. **Missing Practical Considerations:** The paper lacks discussion regarding the practical implications of the method.  Information about implementation complexity, computational cost (compared to soft attention), and memory requirements would be beneficial. 
4. **Limited Ablation Studies:** While the comparison between hard and soft attention is valuable, further ablation studies focusing on specific components of the hard attention model (e.g., the effect of different alignment methods) would strengthen the analysis.


**Questions to Authors:**

1.  How sensitive is the performance of the model to the quality of the independently learned character alignments? Could improvements in alignment quality translate to further performance gains?
2.  Could you provide a rough estimate of the computational cost (training time, inference time) of your model compared to a comparable soft-attention model on the same datasets?
3.  Are there any strategies to adapt the hard attention mechanism to handle non-monotonic alignments, perhaps by incorporating a small degree of flexibility into the attention mechanism?
