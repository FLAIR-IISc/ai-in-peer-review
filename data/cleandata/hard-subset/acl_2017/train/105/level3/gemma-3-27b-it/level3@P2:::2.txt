## Review for ACL 2017 – Morphological Inflection Generation with Hard Monotonic Attention

This paper presents a novel encoder-decoder model for morphological inflection generation that incorporates a hard monotonic attention mechanism. The core idea is to explicitly model the near-monotonic alignment commonly found in morphologically rich languages, decoupling alignment from transduction by first learning alignments and then training a network to generate outputs consistent with those alignments. The authors evaluate their model on three datasets (CELEX, Wiktionary, and SIGMORPHON2016) and report state-of-the-art or competitive results.

**Contributions:**

The primary contribution of this work is the proposal of a novel architecture for sequence transduction tasks where monotonicity is a strong prior. Specifically, the hard attention mechanism, combined with a decoupled alignment process, provides a principled way to leverage this prior information. A second, supporting contribution is the strong empirical performance on morphological inflection generation, establishing new state-of-the-art results on several datasets, particularly on the low-resource CELEX dataset. Finally, the analysis of learned representations offers some initial insight into how these models encode morphological information, though further exploration is needed.

**Strengths:**

1. **Novelty of Approach:** The hard attention mechanism, designed to enforce monotonicity, represents a potentially valuable approach for sequence transduction tasks beyond morphological inflection. The decoupling of alignment and transduction is a compelling design choice. The idea of training with independently learned alignments, rather than jointly learning them, is a practical simplification that enables straightforward training with cross-entropy loss.
2. **Strong Empirical Results:** The model achieves state-of-the-art performance on multiple datasets, demonstrating its effectiveness. The particular success on the CELEX dataset, where it surpasses previous models including specifically tailored latent variable WFSTs, is significant and suggests robustness in low-data scenarios.
3. **Clear Presentation:** The paper is generally well-written and clearly presents the proposed model and experimental setup. The explanation of the hard attention mechanism and its relationship to the monotonic alignment is understandable. The figures aid in visualizing the model’s operation.
4. **Thorough Evaluation:** The evaluation includes a range of datasets of varying sizes and complexities, providing a comprehensive assessment of the model’s performance. The comparison to several strong baselines (both neural and non-neural) lends credibility to the results.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Potential Redundancy/Simplicity:** The proposed model may be more similar to existing approaches than the authors suggest. The core idea of learning alignments and then generating sequences conditioned on those alignments bears a strong resemblance to a standard sequence tagging approach, often used in morphology (e.g., using a BiLSTM with a 1-to-many alignment and a softmax layer to predict tags). It is unclear if the proposed hard attention mechanism adds substantial benefit beyond this simpler approach.
2. **Limited Generalizability Assessment:** The evaluation is limited to the task of morphological inflection generation. To fully establish the value of the proposed hard attention mechanism, it would be beneficial to evaluate the model on other sequence-to-sequence tasks where monotonicity is a reasonable assumption, such as abstractive summarization or transliteration. This would demonstrate the broader applicability of the proposed approach.
3. **Incomplete Baseline Comparison:** The paper omits a comparison to a simple yet relevant baseline: a BiLSTM sequence tagger trained with a fixed 1-to-many alignment derived from the same character-level alignment process used for training the proposed model. This baseline would provide a clearer understanding of the contribution of the hard attention mechanism and the overall complexity of the proposed architecture. The lack of this baseline makes it difficult to assess the true novelty of the approach.
4. **Imprecise Reporting & Feature Confounding:** The use of phrases like “on par or better” lacks precision. Results should be reported with specific numbers and statistical significance testing. Furthermore, the role of linguistic features (e.g., POS tags) is not thoroughly analyzed. It’s unclear to what extent the improvements are driven by the features themselves rather than the monotonic alignment.



**Questions to Authors:**

1. **Alignment Type & Comparison to Tagging:** The paper mentions learning alignments, but it's unclear if these alignments are strictly 1-to-1, 1-to-many, or a combination. How do the learned alignments distribute across these types?  Crucially, how does the proposed model perform relative to a simple BiLSTM sequence tagger trained with a 1-to-many alignment derived from the same character alignment process? Why was this baseline omitted from the evaluation?
2. **Greedy Alignment Impact:** The authors use independently learned, deterministic alignments. Does using greedy alignments lead to performance degradation compared to methods that consider multiple possible alignments (e.g., Rastogi et al., 2016)?
3. **Generalization Beyond Morphology:** Have the authors considered evaluating the model on other tasks where monotonicity is a reasonable assumption (e.g., abstractive summarization, transliteration)? If so, what were the results? If not, why not?

**Additional Comments:**

- Please correct minor formatting issues: “NN” in equation (3), “where” should be lowercase, and ensure correct ellipsis spacing (e.g., x<sub>1</sub>,...,x<sub>n</sub>). There is a typo on line 437. The Cyrillic font in Figure 1 is unnecessary.
- The discussion of related work could be expanded to include more recent and relevant architectures, such as Stack LSTMs and approaches utilizing action-based transduction. (e.g., Cohn et al., 2016)
- Clarify the evolution of the alignment type throughout the process. The initial description suggests primarily 1-to-1 alignments, but later mentions combining them into potentially many-to-many alignments.
