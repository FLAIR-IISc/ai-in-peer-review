- Proposes a novel encoder-decoder with hard monotonic attention that decouples alignment from transduction for morphological inflection.
- Achieves strong empirical performance and new state-of-the-art results on several datasets, especially low-resource CELEX.
- Provides initial analysis of learned representations but calls for deeper exploration.

- Novel hard attention mechanism enforces monotonicity and practical decoupling of alignment and transduction.
- Strong empirical results across multiple datasets and competitive baselines, notably on CELEX.
- Clear writing and helpful figures that explain the model and hard attention mechanism.
- Thorough evaluation across datasets of varying sizes with comparisons to several strong baselines.

- Potential redundancy with simpler sequence-tagging approaches; unclear added value of hard attention over a BiLSTM tagger with fixed alignments.
- Limited assessment of generalizability beyond morphological inflection; no evaluations on other monotonic tasks like transliteration or summarization.
- Missing a direct baseline: BiLSTM sequence tagger using the same 1-to-many alignments, making novelty claims harder to assess.
- Reporting is imprecise (phrases like “on par or better”); lacks exact numbers, significance testing, and analysis of the impact of linguistic features.

- What types of alignments are learned (1-to-1, 1-to-many, many-to-many) and how are they distributed?
- How does the model compare to a BiLSTM sequence tagger trained with the same deterministic 1-to-many alignments, and why was that baseline omitted?
- Do deterministic (greedy) alignments degrade performance compared to methods that consider multiple alignments, and have you tested the model on other monotonic sequence tasks?

- Fix minor formatting and typographical issues (NN in Eq.3, capitalization, ellipsis spacing, typo on line 437, remove Cyrillic font in Fig.1).
- Expand related work to include recent architectures like Stack LSTMs and action-based transduction (e.g., Cohn et al., 2016).
- Clarify how alignment types evolve in the paper—initially described as 1-to-1 but later combined into many-to-many—and report exact alignment handling.
