- Introduces a novel hard monotonic attention mechanism that leverages near-monotonic input-output character alignments for morphological inflection.  
- Uses pre-computed character alignments from a Bayesian transliteration method to decouple alignment from neural training.  
- Presents clear writing, model description, and comprehensive experiments across CELEX, Wiktionary, and SIGMORPHON2016.

- Novelty: Hard monotonic attention is a conceptually new alternative to soft attention for morphological inflection.  
- Low-resource strength: Outperforms neural and WFST baselines on small-data scenarios (notably CELEX).  
- Experimental rigor: Thorough evaluation with diverse datasets, multiple baselines, and appropriate metrics.  
- Representation analysis: Insightful comparisons showing hard attention reduces need for positional encoding.  
- Decoupled alignment: Independently learned alignments simplify training and leverage accurate alignment information.

- Limited generalizability: Weaker performance on non-monotonic or highly context-dependent alignment phenomena.  
- Not universally SOTA: Does not outperform the best models in all conditions; lacks per-condition comparative detail.  
- Practical considerations missing: No discussion of implementation complexity, runtime, or memory trade-offs.  
- Reliance on external aligner: Performance depends on quality of pre-computed alignments and sensitivity to alignment errors is not analyzed.

- Which linguistic features or morphological processes cause degradation in non-monotonic languages?  
- What is the computational overhead of the Bayesian transliteration alignment method?  
- How sensitive is model performance to noisy or incorrect pre-computed alignments, and can this be mitigated?  
- What are the training time and memory requirements versus a soft-attention baseline?

- Recommend deeper comparison of learned representations between hard and soft attention.  
- Suggest an error analysis of misinflected outputs to illuminate failure modes.
