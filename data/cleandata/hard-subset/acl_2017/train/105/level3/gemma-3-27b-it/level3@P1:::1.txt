This paper proposes a novel neural model for morphological inflection generation employing a hard monotonic attention mechanism. The core idea is to explicitly model the nearly-monotonic alignment typically found between input and output characters in morphologically rich languages. This is achieved via a dedicated control mechanism within an encoder-decoder framework, allowing the model to either predict an output symbol or advance the attention pointer. A key aspect of the approach is decoupling the alignment learning from the transduction process, leveraging pre-learned character-level alignments to guide training. The authors demonstrate state-of-the-art results on three established datasets (CELEX, Wiktionary, SIGMORPHON2016), and provide an analysis comparing the learned representations of their hard attention model with a soft attention baseline.

The primary contribution of this work is the introduction of a hard attention mechanism specifically tailored for morphological inflection generation. While attention mechanisms have been previously applied to this task (e.g., Kann & Schütze, 2016), the explicit modeling of monotonic alignment, combined with a decoupled training approach, represents a significant departure from prior methods. The effectiveness of this approach, particularly on datasets with limited training data like CELEX, constitutes a substantial contribution. A second contribution lies in the comprehensive experimental evaluation across three diverse datasets, demonstrating the model’s robustness and identifying its strengths and weaknesses. The thorough analysis, comparing the learned representations of hard and soft attention models, provides valuable insights into how these models capture morphological information. 

This paper presents a compelling approach to morphological inflection generation. Several strengths warrant consideration. First, the proposed hard attention mechanism is a well-motivated and innovative solution, directly addressing the inherent properties of the task. The authors convincingly argue for the benefit of explicitly modeling monotonic alignment, and the results on CELEX provide strong evidence of its effectiveness in low-resource scenarios. Second, the experimental evaluation is extensive and rigorous. The comparison against strong baselines, including both neural and non-neural models, demonstrates the competitive performance of the proposed approach. The ablation study and analysis of learned representations are particularly insightful, offering a deeper understanding of the model's behavior. Third, the paper is clearly written and well-organized, making it easy to follow the technical details and appreciate the contributions. The inclusion of a visual depiction of the model (Figure 1) is helpful. Finally, the decoupling of alignment learning from transduction is a pragmatic choice, enabling simpler training and potentially leveraging existing alignment resources.

However, there are several weaknesses that should be addressed. The primary limitation is the sensitivity to alignment monotonicity and context-dependent phenomena. While the model excels when alignments are largely monotonic (e.g., Russian, German, Spanish), its performance is less impressive on languages requiring more complex, non-monotonic transformations. This limitation isn't fully acknowledged or explored, and the authors don't discuss potential avenues for addressing it (e.g., incorporating mechanisms to handle non-monotonicity). Second, the paper lacks a thorough discussion of practical considerations, such as implementation complexity and computational cost. Compared to existing soft attention models, how much more complex is the implementation of the hard attention mechanism? What are the computational overheads associated with the separate alignment learning phase? This information would be valuable for potential users of the model. Third, the comparison with the soft attention baseline, while informative, feels somewhat limited. A more detailed analysis of the differences in learned alignments and prediction errors between the two models could provide further insights into the advantages and disadvantages of each approach. Finally, while the authors mention the use of a character-level alignment model from Sudoh et al. (2013), the influence of this pre-processing step on the overall performance is not adequately investigated.

To further strengthen this submission, I recommend the following:

**Questions to Authors:**

1. Could the authors elaborate on the limitations of their approach when dealing with languages exhibiting significant non-monotonicity or complex contextual dependencies? Are there potential modifications to the model architecture or training procedure that could address these limitations?
2. What is the computational cost (training and inference time) of the proposed model compared to the soft attention baseline?
3. How sensitive is the model’s performance to the quality of the pre-learned character-level alignments? Did the authors experiment with different alignment models or alignment thresholds?
4.  Can the authors provide a more detailed analysis of the differences in learned alignments between the hard and soft attention models, perhaps with examples illustrating cases where the hard attention mechanism leads to more accurate or linguistically plausible alignments?

**Additional Comments:**

*   It would be useful to include a discussion of error analysis. What types of errors does the model make, and are there any systematic patterns?
*   The paper could benefit from a more thorough comparison with recent advances in morphology-aware neural machine translation, which often incorporate similar principles of explicit morphological modeling.
*   The github link should be functional in the final version.



Overall, this is a strong paper with a novel and promising approach to morphological inflection generation. Addressing the weaknesses and responding to the questions raised above would significantly enhance the contribution and increase the likelihood of acceptance.
