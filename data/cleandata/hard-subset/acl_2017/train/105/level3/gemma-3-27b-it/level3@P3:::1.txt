This paper introduces a novel approach to morphological inflection generation using a hard monotonic attention mechanism. The core idea – leveraging the frequently observed near-monotonic alignment between input and output characters – is a strong departure from prevalent soft-attention sequence-to-sequence models that jointly learn alignment and transduction. The method utilizes pre-computed character alignments from a Bayesian transliteration method to guide the learning process, effectively decoupling alignment from the neural network training. The paper is well-written, clearly explaining the model architecture and experimental setup, and presents comprehensive results across three datasets.

**Main Contributions:**

1. **Novel Hard Monotonic Attention Mechanism:** The paper’s primary contribution is the introduction of a hard monotonic attention mechanism specifically tailored for morphological inflection. This is a significant departure from standard soft attention and offers a conceptually different approach to modeling the alignment process.
2. **Effective Performance on Small Datasets:** The method demonstrates a notable strength in scenarios with limited training data (e.g., CELEX), outperforming existing neural and WFST-based models. This is a valuable contribution, as morphological data for many languages is inherently scarce.
3. **Comprehensive Experimental Evaluation:** The thorough evaluation across three diverse datasets (CELEX, Wiktionary, SIGMORPHON2016), and comparison to multiple baselines, provides convincing evidence of the method’s strengths and limitations.

**Strengths:**

1. **Novelty and Clarity:** The hard monotonic attention approach is genuinely novel within the context of neural morphological inflection. The paper articulates the motivation clearly—the inherent monotonicity in morphological processes—and the proposed method directly addresses this. The explanation of the model and its components is remarkably clear and easy to follow, aided by the illustrative Figure 1.
2. **Strong Performance in Low-Resource Settings:** The significant improvement on the CELEX dataset, a notoriously challenging low-resource scenario, is a compelling argument for the method's effectiveness.  This is particularly important, as many morphologically rich languages lack large parallel corpora required for training data-hungry neural models. The paper convincingly demonstrates its ability to overcome this limitation.
3. **Thorough Experimental Design:** The comprehensive experimental setup, including multiple datasets, diverse baselines (neural and non-neural), and appropriate metrics, strengthens the validity of the findings. The inclusion of SIGMORPHON2016 allows evaluation across multiple languages with varying morphological complexities, providing valuable insights into the method’s generalizability.
4. **Insightful Analysis:** The analysis of learned representations (Figures 4a-4d) offers valuable insights into how the hard and soft attention models encode information. The observation about positional encoding being less critical in the hard attention model, due to the explicit control mechanism, is a particularly noteworthy finding.
5. **Decoupled Alignment:** The decision to independently learn character-level alignments is a clever design choice. It simplifies the training process and allows the model to benefit from accurate alignment information from the outset, avoiding the challenges of jointly learning alignment and transduction.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Generalizability to Non-Monotonic Phenomena:** While the method excels in scenarios with mostly monotonic alignments (Russian, German, Spanish), its performance is less competitive when alignments are non-monotonic or highly context-dependent. This limitation is acknowledged by the authors, but a more in-depth discussion of *why* the method struggles in these cases would be beneficial.  What specific linguistic features or morphological processes cause difficulties?
2. **Not State-of-the-Art Universally:** The paper acknowledges that the method isn’t state-of-the-art across all conditions. While this is realistic, a more detailed comparison to the best-performing models on each dataset, highlighting the specific scenarios where the proposed method falls short, would strengthen the assessment.
3. **Missing Practical Considerations:** The paper lacks a discussion of practical aspects such as implementation complexity and computational cost. While the method's performance is promising, its real-world applicability hinges on its efficiency and ease of implementation.  A brief analysis of training time, memory requirements, and potential optimizations would be valuable.
4. **Reliance on External Alignment Tool:** The method’s dependence on an external character-level alignment tool introduces a potential bottleneck. The quality of the pre-computed alignments directly impacts the performance of the model. A discussion of the sensitivity of the method to alignment errors would be helpful.

**Questions to Authors:**

1. Could you elaborate on the specific linguistic features or morphological processes that lead to performance degradation in languages with non-monotonic alignments?
2. What is the computational overhead associated with the Bayesian transliteration method used for alignment? Does this significantly impact the overall training time?
3. How sensitive is the performance of your model to errors in the pre-computed character-level alignments? Have you investigated any methods to mitigate the impact of noisy alignments?
4. Can you provide a rough estimate of the training time and memory requirements for your model compared to the soft-attention baseline?



**Additional Comments:**

* The paper would benefit from a more detailed discussion of the differences in the learned representations between the hard and soft attention models.
* A brief analysis of error cases (e.g., misinflected words) would provide further insights into the method’s strengths and weaknesses.
* The removal of the GitHub link is noted, as per anonymization requirements.
