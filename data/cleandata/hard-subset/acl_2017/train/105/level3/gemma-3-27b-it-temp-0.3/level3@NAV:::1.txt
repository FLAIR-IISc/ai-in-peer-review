## Review of "Morphological Inflection Generation with Hard Monotonic Attention"

This paper presents a novel approach to morphological inflection generation using a hard monotonic attention mechanism. The core idea – leveraging the inherent near-monotonicity of morphological processes – is a strong point, and the authors demonstrate competitive results on standard datasets. The method utilizes pre-computed character alignments to guide the attention mechanism, decoupling alignment and transduction, which is a key distinction from prior soft-attention models. 

The primary contribution of this work, in my view, is the exploration of a hard attention mechanism specifically tailored for morphological inflection. While hard attention isn’t entirely novel in NLP, its application to this task, combined with the use of external alignment information, offers a compelling alternative to end-to-end learning, particularly in low-resource scenarios. A second contribution is the comprehensive experimental evaluation across multiple datasets (CELEX, Wiktionary, SIGMORPHON2016), demonstrating the method’s effectiveness and limitations. Finally, the analysis comparing hard and soft attention, shedding light on the representations learned by each, provides valuable insights into the task.

**Strengths:**

*   **Novelty:** The hard monotonic attention approach is a genuinely new direction for morphological inflection, offering a distinct alternative to existing methods.
*   **Strong Performance in Low-Resource Settings:** The model demonstrably outperforms previous approaches on the small CELEX dataset, highlighting its advantage when data is limited. This is a significant practical benefit.
*   **Comprehensive Evaluation:** The extensive experiments across diverse datasets and comparison to strong baselines provide a robust assessment of the method.
*   **Clear Writing:** The paper is well-written and clearly explains the proposed method and experimental setup.
*   **Insightful Analysis:** The comparison of learned representations between hard and soft attention models is a valuable contribution, offering a deeper understanding of the underlying mechanisms.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Limited Applicability:** The method’s performance degrades when alignments become non-monotonic or context-sensitive, as evidenced by the results on the SIGMORPHON dataset. This limits its generalizability.
*   **Dependency on External Alignment:** The reliance on pre-computed alignments introduces an additional step and potential source of error. The quality of the alignment directly impacts performance.
*   **Missing Practical Considerations:** The paper lacks discussion of implementation complexity and computational cost compared to soft attention models. This information is crucial for assessing real-world usability.
*   **Lack of Ablation Study:** While the authors compare to other models, an ablation study to evaluate the contribution of different components of their model (e.g., the bi-directional encoder, the hard attention mechanism) would strengthen the analysis.

**Questions to Authors:**

*   Could you provide a more detailed analysis of the computational cost (training and inference time) compared to a standard soft attention model?
*   How sensitive is the performance to the quality of the pre-computed alignments? Have you experimented with different alignment methods or explored ways to mitigate alignment errors?
*   What are the potential strategies for extending this approach to handle more complex morphological phenomena that exhibit non-monotonic alignments?
