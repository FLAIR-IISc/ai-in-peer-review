## Review of "Morphological Inflection Generation with Hard Monotonic Attention"

This paper presents a novel encoder-decoder model for morphological inflection generation, leveraging a hard monotonic attention mechanism. The core idea – explicitly modeling the near-monotonic alignment common in morphological processes – is a strong contribution, aligning with the motivation that enforcing monotonicity can be beneficial for sequence-to-sequence tasks, particularly those with inherent structural constraints. The presentation is generally well-written and clearly articulates the approach of decoupling alignment and transduction. The experimental results demonstrate state-of-the-art performance on several morphological inflection datasets.

The primary strength of this work lies in the proposed hard attention mechanism and its application to a challenging task. The model’s ability to surpass previous methods, especially with limited training data (CELEX dataset), is a significant achievement. The analysis comparing the learned representations of the hard and soft attention models provides valuable insights into the model’s behavior and the features it extracts. Furthermore, the detailed description of the network architecture and training procedure allows for reproducibility.

However, a critical weakness is the potential equivalence of the proposed model to a simpler BiRNN with decoupled alignments. The paper doesn’t sufficiently address this concern. A direct comparison against a strong baseline – a 1-to-many alignment followed by a BiLSTM sequence tagger (akin to Schnober et al.) – is missing. This comparison is crucial to demonstrate the added value of the more complex hard attention mechanism. 

Another weakness is the limited scope of evaluation. While the model performs well on morphological inflection, its generalization ability to other monotone sequence-to-sequence tasks remains untested. Evaluating on tasks like speech recognition or abstractive summarization would strengthen the claim of broader applicability. The reporting of results as “on par or better” lacks precision and should be replaced with specific statistical comparisons. Finally, the role of linguistic features is unclear; a more thorough analysis of their contribution is needed to determine if improvements are driven by monotonicity or feature engineering.

**Questions to Authors:**

1.  Could you elaborate on why a 1-to-many alignment + BiLSTM sequence tagger baseline was not included in the evaluation? How does your method demonstrably differ from this approach in terms of performance and complexity?
2.  Given the mixed alignment types observed (1-0, 0-1, 1-1), how does the model handle cases where the alignment isn't strictly monotonic?
3.  Have you investigated the impact of using a greedy alignment strategy versus methods that sum over alignments (e.g., Rastogi et al., 2016)? Could the greedy approach be limiting performance?

**Additional Comments:**

*   Please address the minor formatting and notation issues (NN in eqn (3), lowercase “where”, ellipsis spacing, typo at l.437, Cyrillic font in Fig.1).
*   Discuss the connections to Stack LSTM-style action-based architectures (Cohn et al., 2016) as suggested by the AC note.
*   Clarify the implementation details of the character-level alignment process used for training.
