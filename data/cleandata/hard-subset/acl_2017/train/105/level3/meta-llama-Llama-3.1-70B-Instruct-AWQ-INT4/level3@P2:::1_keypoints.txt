- Proposes a neural model for morphological inflection using a novel hard monotonic attention mechanism to capture near-monotonic character alignments.  
- Uses a separate character alignment process derived from a Bayesian transliteration method to guide attention rather than learning alignment jointly.  
- Evaluates on CELEX, Wiktionary, and SIGMORPHON 2016 datasets with comprehensive experiments.  
- Reports state-of-the-art results in several setups compared to prior neural and non-neural methods.  
- Particularly effective on small training datasets (e.g., CELEX), outperforming previous approaches.  
- Demonstrates robustness across different datasets (Wiktionary, SIGMORPHON 2016).  
- Paper is clearly written and experiments are well presented.  
- Limitation: model is restricted to monotonic alignments and may fail when alignments are non-monotonic or highly context-dependent.  
- Not universally best—performance degrades in settings with non-monotonic or context-dependent phenomena.  
- Lacks analysis of practical aspects such as ease of implementation and computational cost.  
- Raises open questions about performance on non-monotonic alignments and whether the model can be extended to handle them.  
- Requests comparison of implementation difficulty and computational efficiency with other approaches.  
- Suggests authors should extend the model for non-monotonic cases and provide a more comprehensive analysis of practical merits.
