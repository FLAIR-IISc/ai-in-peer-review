**Summary of the Paper**

This paper proposes a novel encoder-decoder model with explicit hard monotonic attention for morphological inflection generation. The model uses a bi-directional LSTM encoder and a decoder with a control mechanism that determines whether to write an output symbol or advance the attention pointer. The model is trained using a simple cross-entropy loss function and independently learned character-level alignments. The authors evaluate their model on three morphological inflection generation datasets and show that it outperforms previous neural and non-neural state-of-the-art approaches.

**Main Contributions**

1. A novel encoder-decoder model with explicit hard monotonic attention for morphological inflection generation.
2. A simple training procedure that uses independently learned character-level alignments to guide the network's attention.
3. State-of-the-art results on three morphological inflection generation datasets.

**Strengths**

1. The proposed model is well-written and presents a novel idea for morphology.
2. The model's performance is on par with or better than previous neural and non-neural state-of-the-art approaches.
3. The model's ability to handle small training sets is a significant advantage.
4. The authors provide a thorough analysis of the learned alignments and representations.
5. The model's simplicity and ease of training are notable advantages.

**Weaknesses**

1. The model may be equivalent to an ordinary BiRNN with alignments decoupled.
2. The evaluation is limited to morphology, and no other monotone sequence-to-sequence tasks are tested.
3. The authors do not provide a clear explanation of the alignment type used (1-to-many, many-to-1, or many-to-many).
4. A simple 1-to-many alignment + BiLSTM sequence tagger baseline is not included or compared.
5. The authors do not provide a clear explanation of how the proposed method differs from the simple align-then-tag approach.

**Questions to Authors**

1. Can you provide a clear explanation of the alignment type used in the model?
2. How does the proposed method differ from the simple align-then-tag approach?
3. Can you provide a comparison with a simple 1-to-many alignment + BiLSTM sequence tagger baseline?
4. Can you evaluate the model on other monotone sequence-to-sequence tasks?
5. Can you provide a more detailed analysis of the learned representations and their relationship to the input data?

**Additional Comments**

1. The formatting and notation in the paper could be improved.
2. The authors should consider using a more precise wording in their results, such as "significantly better" instead of "on par or better".
3. The authors should provide a clear explanation of the linguistic features used in the model and their relationship to the input data.
4. The authors should consider using a more robust evaluation metric, such as BLEU or METEOR, instead of exact match accuracy.
