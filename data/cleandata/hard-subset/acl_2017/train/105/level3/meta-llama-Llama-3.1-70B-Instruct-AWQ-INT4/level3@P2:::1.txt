**Summary**

The paper presents a neural model for morphological inflection generation that employs a hard monotonic attention mechanism. The model is designed to capture the nearly-monotonic alignment between the characters in a word and its inflection, which is commonly found in morphological inflection generation tasks. The authors evaluate their model on three previously studied morphological inflection generation datasets and show that it provides state-of-the-art results in various setups compared to previous neural and non-neural approaches.

**Main Contributions**

1. **Hard Monotonic Attention Mechanism**: The paper proposes a novel hard monotonic attention mechanism, which is substantially different from prior methods. This mechanism allows the model to capture the monotonic alignment between the input and output character sequences.
2. **Separate Character Alignment Process**: The authors use a separate character alignment process, obtained via a Bayesian transliteration method, to guide the attention mechanism. This approach differs from soft-attention models that jointly learn alignment and conversion.
3. **Comprehensive Experiments**: The paper presents comprehensive experiments on three morphological inflection generation datasets, including the CELEX, Wiktionary, and SIGMORPHON 2016 datasets.

**Strengths**

1. **Effective with Small Training Datasets**: The model is effective with small training datasets, such as the CELEX dataset, where it outperforms previous neural and non-neural approaches.
2. **Robustness to Different Datasets**: The model performs well on different datasets, including the Wiktionary and SIGMORPHON 2016 datasets, demonstrating its robustness.
3. **Clear Writing and Presentation**: The paper is clearly written and presents comprehensive experiments, making it easy to follow and understand.

**Weaknesses**

1. **Limited to Monotonic Alignments**: The model is limited to monotonic alignments and may not perform well when alignments are non-monotonic or highly context-dependent.
2. **Not Universally Best**: The model is not state-of-the-art under all conditions, especially when alignments are non-monotonic or highly context-dependent.
3. **Lack of Analysis on Practical Merits**: The paper lacks discussion of practical merits like ease of implementation and computational cost.

**Questions to Authors**

1. How does the model perform when the alignments are non-monotonic or highly context-dependent?
2. Can the model be extended to handle non-monotonic alignments or highly context-dependent phenomena?
3. How does the model compare to other approaches in terms of ease of implementation and computational cost?

**Additional Comments**

The paper presents a novel approach to morphological inflection generation using hard monotonic attention. While the model is effective with small training datasets and performs well on different datasets, it has limitations when dealing with non-monotonic alignments or highly context-dependent phenomena. The authors should consider extending the model to handle these cases and provide a more comprehensive analysis of its practical merits.
