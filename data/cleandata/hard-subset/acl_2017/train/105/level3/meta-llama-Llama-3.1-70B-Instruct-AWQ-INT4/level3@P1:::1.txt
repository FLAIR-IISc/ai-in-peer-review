**Summary**

This paper presents a novel approach to morphological inflection generation using a hard monotonic attention mechanism. The model is designed to handle the task of generating a target word given a source word and its morpho-syntactic attributes. The authors propose a hard attention mechanism that directly models the nearly-monotonic alignment between the input and output character sequences. The model is evaluated on three morphological inflection generation datasets and shows state-of-the-art results in various setups.

**Contributions**

1. **Novel approach**: The paper proposes a hard monotonic attention mechanism, a new approach substantially different from prior methods.
2. **Effective with small training datasets**: The model is effective with small training datasets, such as CELEX, where other neural models tend to overfit.
3. **Robustness**: The model is robust and performs well with large amounts of training examples.

**Strengths**

1. **Clear writing**: The paper is clearly written and presents comprehensive experiments.
2. **Comprehensive evaluation**: The authors evaluate their model on three morphological inflection generation datasets, providing a thorough comparison with prior work.
3. **Effective with small training datasets**: The model's ability to perform well with small training datasets is a significant strength, especially for low-resource languages.

**Weaknesses**

1. **Limited to monotonic alignments**: The model is not universally best and may not perform well when alignments are non-monotonic or highly context-dependent.
2. **Lack of analysis on practical merits**: The paper lacks a discussion of practical merits like ease of implementation and computational cost.

**Questions to Authors**

1. How does the model handle non-monotonic alignments, and are there any plans to extend the model to handle such cases?
2. Can you provide more details on the computational cost and ease of implementation of the model compared to other approaches?
3. How does the model perform on languages with more complex morphological phenomena, such as vowel harmony and consonant harmony?
