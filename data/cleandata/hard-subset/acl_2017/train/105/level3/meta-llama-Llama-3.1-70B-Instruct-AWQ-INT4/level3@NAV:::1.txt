**Summary of the Paper**

This paper proposes a novel approach to morphological inflection generation using a hard monotonic attention mechanism. The model is designed to handle nearly-monotonic alignments between input and output character sequences, which is commonly found in morphological inflection generation tasks. The authors evaluate their model on three previously studied morphological inflection generation datasets and show that it provides state-of-the-art results in various setups compared to previous neural and non-neural approaches.

**Main Contributions**

1. The authors propose a hard monotonic attention mechanism, a new approach substantially different from prior methods.
2. They evaluate their model on three previously studied morphological inflection generation datasets and show that it provides state-of-the-art results in various setups.
3. They perform an analysis and comparison of their model and the soft-attention model, shedding light on the features such models extract for the inflection generation task.

**Strengths**

1. **Novelty and effectiveness**: The proposed hard monotonic attention mechanism is a novel approach that provides state-of-the-art results in various setups, demonstrating its effectiveness in handling nearly-monotonic alignments.
2. **Robustness to small training datasets**: The model performs well even with small training datasets, such as the CELEX dataset, which is a significant advantage over previous neural models.
3. **Comprehensive experiments**: The authors perform extensive experiments on three previously studied morphological inflection generation datasets, providing a thorough evaluation of their model.

**Weaknesses**

1. **Limited generalizability**: The model may not perform well on languages with non-monotonic or highly context-dependent alignments, as shown in the SIGMORPHON 2016 dataset.
2. **Lack of analysis on practical merits**: The authors do not provide a discussion on the practical merits of their model, such as ease of implementation and computational cost.
3. **Comparison to soft-attention model**: While the authors compare their model to the soft-attention model, they do not provide a detailed analysis of the differences between the two models and how they perform on different types of alignments.

**Questions to Authors**

1. How do you plan to extend your model to handle non-monotonic or highly context-dependent alignments?
2. Can you provide a detailed analysis of the differences between your hard monotonic attention mechanism and the soft-attention model?
3. How do you plan to evaluate the practical merits of your model, such as ease of implementation and computational cost?
