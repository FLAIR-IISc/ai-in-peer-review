**Summary of the Paper**

This paper proposes a novel neural model for morphological inflection generation, employing a hard attention mechanism inspired by the nearly-monotonic alignment commonly found between characters in a word and its inflection. The model consists of an encoder-decoder neural network with a dedicated control mechanism, allowing it to attend to a single input state and either write a symbol to the output sequence or advance the attention pointer to the next input state. The authors evaluate the model on three previously studied morphological inflection generation datasets and show that it provides state-of-the-art results in various setups compared to previous neural and non-neural approaches.

**Main Contributions**

1. **Novel Hard Attention Model**: The authors propose a novel hard attention model for morphological inflection generation, which employs a dedicated control mechanism to attend to a single input state and either write a symbol to the output sequence or advance the attention pointer to the next input state.
2. **State-of-the-Art Results**: The authors evaluate the model on three previously studied morphological inflection generation datasets and show that it provides state-of-the-art results in various setups compared to previous neural and non-neural approaches.
3. **Analysis of Learned Representations**: The authors perform an analysis of the continuous representations learned by both the hard and soft attention models for the task, shedding light on the features such models extract for the morphological inflection generation task.

**Strengths**

1. **Novel Approach**: The authors propose a novel approach to morphological inflection generation, employing a hard attention mechanism that is well-suited for the task.
2. **Well-Written Presentation**: The paper is well-written and presents the novel idea clearly.
3. **State-of-the-Art Results**: The authors demonstrate state-of-the-art results on three previously studied morphological inflection generation datasets.

**Weaknesses**

1. **Limited Evaluation**: The evaluation is limited to morphology, and no other monotone sequence-to-sequence tasks are tested.
2. **Unclear Alignment Type**: It is unclear what alignment type is used—1-to-many, many-to-1, or many-to-many.
3. **Lack of Baseline Comparison**: A simple 1-to-many alignment + BiLSTM sequence tagger baseline is not included or compared.

**Questions to Authors**

1. Can you clarify what alignment type is used—1-to-many, many-to-1, or many-to-many?
2. How does the proposed method differ from a simple align-then-tag approach, and why was it omitted?
3. Can you evaluate the model on other monotone string-translation tasks to demonstrate broader advantage?

**Additional Comments**

1. The formatting and notation could be improved, including fixing the notation in equation (3) and proper ellipsis spacing.
2. There is a typo at line 437.
3. The unnecessary Cyrillic font in Figure 1 could be removed.
4. The authors should consider using 1-x alignments and a neural tagger baseline to assess how much the complex model adds.
5. The authors should discuss relevant prior work, including Cohn et al. (2016), and connections to Stack LSTM-style action-based architectures.
