

# Review for ACL 2017

## Summary and Main Contributions

The paper proposes a neural model for morphological inflection generation that employs a hard attention mechanism, designed specifically for the nearly-monotonic character alignments commonly found in morphological inflection tasks. The authors present three main contributions: (1) a novel hard attention model that explicitly handles monotonic alignment rather than learning it jointly with transduction; (2) state-of-the-art results on three previously studied morphological inflection datasets, particularly demonstrating significant advantages on small training sets; and (3) an insightful analysis comparing the representations learned by hard and soft attention models, revealing how they encode morphological features.

## Strengths

1. The model's performance on small training datasets is particularly impressive and represents a significant practical contribution. On the CELEX dataset (only 500 examples per inflection type), the hard attention model significantly outperforms all previous neural and non-neural approaches, including the best latent variable model (Dreyer et al., 2008), which was previously considered the state of the art for such limited data. This is a meaningful contribution to the field, as many morphological resources are scarce, especially for low-resource languages.

2. The paper provides a clear and insightful analysis of the representations learned by both hard and soft attention models. The SVD visualization (Figures 4a-d) effectively demonstrates how the hard attention model encodes position information less explicitly than the soft attention model, which likely contributes to its better performance on small datasets. This analysis provides valuable theoretical understanding of how neural models process morphological information.

3. The experimental evaluation is comprehensive and well-structured, covering multiple datasets (CELEX, Wiktionary, SIGMORPHON) and comparing against both neural and non-neural baselines. The authors correctly identify the key limitation of their approach (performance degradation on languages with complex morphological phenomena) and provide a reasonable linguistic explanation for this pattern.

## Weaknesses

1. The paper's claim of "state-of-the-art" results requires qualification. While the model achieves the best performance on CELEX and on certain languages in the SIGMORPHON dataset (Russian, German, Spanish), it is actually worse than the soft attention baseline on other languages (e.g., Turkish, Arabic, Navajo). The paper should more clearly state that the method is not universally superior but rather excels when morphological phenomena align with the monotonic assumption.

2. The paper lacks discussion of practical implementation considerations. It would be valuable to know whether the hard attention model is simpler to implement than soft attention models, whether it requires more or less training time, and how the additional alignment step (using Sudoh et al.'s method) affects the overall pipeline. This information is crucial for practitioners considering adopting the approach.

3. The paper's explanation for why the model performs better on some languages than others is somewhat superficial. While the authors note that languages with "suffixing+stem-change" morphology (Russian, German, Spanish) benefit from the monotonic assumption, they don't provide sufficient linguistic analysis to convince readers that the model's success is due to this specific property rather than other factors. More detailed linguistic examples would strengthen this argument.

## Questions to Authors

1. The paper states that the hard attention model is "the first neural model to surpass the performance of the weighted FST model with latent variables" on CELEX. How much better is the hard attention model compared to Dreyer et al.'s (2008) model? What is the statistical significance of this improvement?

2. The alignment process (Sudoh et al., 2013) appears to be a key component of the approach. Could you provide more details about how this alignment method was adapted for morphological inflection, and what the computational cost of this alignment step is compared to the neural model training?

3. The paper shows that the hard attention model performs worse on languages with vowel harmony (e.g., Turkish, Arabic) compared to soft attention. Could you provide specific examples from these languages where the monotonic alignment assumption fails, and explain why the soft attention model handles these cases better?

4. Given that the hard attention model's advantage is most pronounced on small datasets, how does the performance gap between hard and soft attention models change as the amount of training data increases? Is there a point where the soft attention model becomes consistently better as data grows?

## Additional Comments

The paper is well-written and clearly structured. The technical details are presented with sufficient clarity, and the experimental results are thoroughly analyzed. The model's simplicity (using standard cross-entropy loss without joint training of alignment and transduction) is a practical advantage that should be emphasized more. The authors should also clarify that their approach is complementary to soft attention models rather than universally superior, as this would make the contribution more precise and valuable to the community.
