

This paper introduces a novel neural model for morphological inflection generation that employs a hard monotonic attention mechanism, contrasting with the soft attention approaches that have dominated recent work. The authors propose a model that explicitly models nearly-monotonic character alignments common in morphological inflection tasks, using a control mechanism that alternates between writing output symbols and advancing an attention pointer through the input sequence. The model is evaluated on three standard morphological inflection datasets: CELEX (small dataset), Wiktionary (larger dataset), and SIGMORPHON 2016 (multilingual).

The primary contribution is the hard monotonic attention mechanism, which provides a substantially different approach from prior neural sequence-to-sequence models. The authors demonstrate that this mechanism enables their model to outperform previous neural and non-neural approaches, particularly on the small CELEX dataset where previous neural models failed to surpass the latent-variable WFST model of Dreyer et al. (2008). A secondary contribution is the comprehensive empirical evaluation across multiple datasets, showing the model's state-of-the-art performance on German verbs, Finnish nouns/adjectives, and Dutch verbs on the Wiktionary dataset. The third contribution is the insightful analysis comparing the learned representations of hard and soft attention models, revealing how the hard attention model encodes positional information less explicitly than soft attention models.

The paper's strengths are compelling. First, the model's effectiveness with small training datasets is particularly noteworthy, as this addresses a significant limitation of neural approaches to morphological inflection. On the CELEX dataset (only 500 training examples per inflection type), the hard attention model achieves 89.44% exact match accuracy, significantly outperforming previous neural models (MED: 87.62%, NWFST: 87.65%) and even surpassing the non-neural latent-variable WFST model (LAT: 88.3%). Second, the learning curves (Figure 2) clearly demonstrate how the hard attention model avoids overfitting on small datasets, as it performs substantially better on the development set than the soft attention model despite similar training set performance. Third, the analysis of learned representations (Figure 4) provides valuable insights into how different attention mechanisms encode linguistic information, particularly regarding positional sensitivity, which helps explain the model's behavior.

The paper's weaknesses warrant consideration. First, the model's performance advantage is limited to languages with mostly monotonic alignments (Russian, German, Spanish) and diminishes on languages with more complex morphological phenomena (e.g., vowel harmony in Turkish, consonant harmony in Arabic). On the SIGMORPHON dataset, the model performs worse than the soft attention baseline on 5 of 10 languages, suggesting the approach is not universally superior. Second, the paper lacks discussion of practical merits such as computational cost and implementation complexity, which would be valuable for practitioners. Third, the paper claims their model is "the first neural model to surpass the performance of the weighted FST model" on CELEX, but doesn't adequately address how much of the improvement stems from the hard attention mechanism versus other aspects of the model architecture. Fourth, the paper doesn't clearly establish how the hard attention approach compares to other monotonic alignment models like the RNN Transducer (Graves, 2012) or Neural Transducer (Jaitly et al., 2015).

The authors' claim that the model outperforms previous approaches across multiple datasets is well-supported by their experiments, but the paper would benefit from a more nuanced discussion of the model's limitations. The analysis of linguistic phenomena (Table 3) is insightful but could be expanded to better explain why the model works well for some languages but not others. The paper would also benefit from a clearer explanation of how the hard attention mechanism specifically contributes to performance, rather than attributing success to the overall model architecture.

The writing is clear and well-organized, with comprehensive experiments that thoroughly evaluate the model across multiple settings. The paper successfully demonstrates that for morphological inflection tasks with nearly-monotonic character alignments, the hard attention mechanism provides a simpler and more effective approach than soft attention models, particularly when training data is limited.

Questions to authors:
1. How does the training time of your model compare to the soft attention baseline, particularly on the larger Wiktionary dataset?
2. The paper states that "the hard attention model is the first neural model to surpass the performance of the weighted FST model" on CELEX. Could you clarify whether this refers to the exact match accuracy on the original dataset splits used by Dreyer et al. (2008), or if you used a different data split?
3. Given the model's performance limitations on non-monotonic languages, would you recommend this approach only for languages with suffixing morphology, or are there ways to adapt it for more complex morphological phenomena?
