

# Review: Morphological Inflection Generation with Hard Monotonic Attention

## Summary
This paper proposes a neural sequence-to-sequence model for morphological inflection generation that employs a hard monotonic attention mechanism. The model decouples alignment and transduction by first learning character-level alignments (using Sudoh et al.'s method), then training an encoder-decoder network to generate output sequences consistent with these alignments. The authors evaluate their model on three morphological inflection datasets (CELEX, Wiktionary, SIGMORPHON 2016), reporting state-of-the-art results on most tasks, particularly with small training sets.

## Main Contributions
1. A novel hard monotonic attention mechanism for sequence-to-sequence learning that explicitly models nearly-monotonic alignments common in morphological inflection.
2. Demonstration that this approach outperforms previous neural and non-neural models, especially with limited training data.
3. An analysis comparing the learned representations of hard and soft attention models for morphological inflection.

## Strengths
1. **Novelty and Motivation**: The paper presents a well-motivated approach to sequence-to-sequence learning with monotonic alignment, which is a common characteristic of morphological inflection tasks. The authors effectively argue that enforcing monotonicity (rather than allowing arbitrary alignments) is appropriate for this domain and can improve performance, particularly with limited training data. The approach is conceptually clean and addresses a real limitation in standard encoder-decoder models for this specific task.

2. **Strong Experimental Results**: The model achieves state-of-the-art results on the CELEX dataset (the smallest dataset), significantly outperforming previous neural models and the latent variable FST model of Dreyer et al. (2008). This is an important contribution, as it demonstrates the model's ability to handle data-scarce scenarios where other neural approaches fail. The model also performs well on larger datasets (Wiktionary and SIGMORPHON), showing robustness across different data sizes.

3. **Clear Presentation**: The paper is well-written with a logical structure and clear explanations of the model's architecture and training procedure. The analysis of learned alignments and representations provides valuable insights into how the model operates, particularly regarding the position encoding in the input representations.

## Weaknesses
1. **Lack of Proper Baseline Comparison**: The most significant weakness is the omission of a simple, direct baseline: a 1-to-many alignment followed by a BiLSTM sequence tagger (as in Schnober et al. or Durrett & DeNero, 2013). The authors' model appears to be functionally equivalent to this simpler approach, with the added complexity of a dedicated attention mechanism. The paper fails to demonstrate how much of the performance gain comes from the "hard attention" component versus the alignment-decoupled approach itself. This omission fundamentally undermines the claimed novelty of the model.

2. **Insufficient Evaluation Scope**: The evaluation is limited to morphological inflection tasks, despite the authors' claim that "monotonicity is appropriate for many seq2seq tasks." The paper should have evaluated the approach on other monotone sequence-to-sequence tasks (e.g., transliteration, abstractive summarization) to demonstrate broader applicability. The lack of such evaluation limits the perceived contribution of the work.

3. **Ambiguous Alignment Type and Implementation**: The paper fails to clearly specify what type of alignment the model uses (1-to-many, many-to-1, or many-to-many). The authors' own analysis shows alignments that are mixed (1-0, 0-1, 1-1) and later combined into many-to-many, which contradicts the claim of "hard monotonic attention." This lack of clarity about the fundamental mechanism of the model weakens the contribution.

4. **Imprecise Reporting**: The claim that the model is "on par or better" than previous approaches is imprecise. The paper should report exact statistical differences (e.g., "significantly better with p<0.05") rather than vague comparative language. The SIGMORPHON results show the model is "slightly less accurate" than soft attention for some languages, yet the authors don't discuss this nuance.

## Questions to Authors
1. The paper claims "hard monotonic attention" but the analysis in Figure 3 shows alignments that are not purely monotonic (e.g., 1-to-2 mappings). Can you clarify how your model handles non-monotonic elements while still claiming to enforce monotonicity? 

2. The proposed model appears functionally equivalent to a 1-to-many alignment followed by a BiLSTM sequence tagger. Why was this simple baseline not included in the comparison? How much of your performance gain is due to the "hard attention" mechanism versus the alignment-decoupled approach?

3. The paper uses greedy alignment but doesn't address whether summing over alignments (as in Rastogi et al., 2016) would yield better results. Does your greedy alignment approach lose performance compared to methods that marginalize over alignments?

4. The authors cite Cohn et al. (2016) and Stack LSTM architectures as related work but fail to discuss how their approach relates to these prior works. How does your model differ from these approaches, and why should they be discussed in the related work section?

5. The paper uses linguistic features (e.g., POS) without explaining their source or analyzing whether they drive the improvements rather than the monotonic alignment mechanism. Please clarify the feature sources and provide an analysis of whether the features or the alignment mechanism are responsible for the performance gains.
