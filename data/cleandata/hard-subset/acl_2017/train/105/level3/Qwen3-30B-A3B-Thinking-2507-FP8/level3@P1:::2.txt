

Summary: This paper proposes a neural model for morphological inflection generation that uses a hard attention mechanism to enforce monotonic alignment between input and output character sequences. The authors claim their model achieves state-of-the-art results on three morphological inflection datasets, particularly excelling with small training sets where previous neural models struggled. They also provide an analysis of the representations learned by both hard and soft attention models.

Main contributions (as I see them):
1. A hard attention model that enforces monotonic alignment for morphological inflection generation, which outperforms previous neural models especially with limited training data.
2. A thorough analysis of the representations learned by both hard and soft attention models, shedding light on how these models encode morphological features.
3. Demonstration that explicitly modeling monotonic alignment (a natural property of morphological inflection) leads to better performance, particularly with small datasets.

Strengths:
1. The paper makes a strong case for the value of enforcing monotonic alignment in morphological inflection generation, which is a natural property of the task. The results on the CELEX dataset (with only 500 examples per inflection type) are particularly convincing, showing the hard attention model outperforms previous neural models and even surpasses the latent variable WFST model of Dreyer et al. (2008) for the first time. This is a significant contribution to the field, as small dataset performance has been a persistent challenge for neural models.

2. The analysis of representations learned by the hard and soft attention models is insightful. The authors show that while both models learn similar monotonic alignments, the hard attention model encodes position information less strongly in the input representations, which may explain its better performance with limited data. This provides valuable theoretical understanding of how attention mechanisms function in this task.

3. The paper is well-written and clearly presents a novel idea for morphological inflection. The architecture is described with sufficient detail, and the experiments are thorough, including comparisons to both neural and non-neural baselines across three datasets.

Weaknesses:
1. The paper does not compare to a simple baseline of 1-to-many alignment followed by a BiLSTM sequence tagger (e.g., Schnober et al., 2016), which would be a natural baseline for this approach. The authors claim their model is novel, but it's unclear how much of the performance gain comes from the alignment technique versus the neural model itself. This is a critical omission that weakens the contribution claim.

2. The evaluation is limited to morphological inflection, and the authors do not test their model on other monotone sequence-to-sequence tasks (e.g., transliteration, abstractive summarization) to demonstrate broader applicability. Without this, it's unclear whether the monotonic alignment approach is generally beneficial or specific to morphological inflection.

3. The paper doesn't sufficiently clarify the alignment type used. The authors mention "nearly-monotonic alignment" but don't specify whether they're using 1-to-many, many-to-1, or many-to-many alignments, and later in the paper, the alignment appears to be mixed (1-0, 0-1, 1-1) and combined into many-to-many. This lack of clarity makes it difficult to understand the model's assumptions and limitations.

4. The role of linguistic features (POS, gender, etc.) isn't well analyzed. The authors don't provide evidence that the improvements come from monotonic alignment rather than the feature information itself. A careful analysis of feature importance would strengthen the contribution.

Questions to Authors:
1. How does your model differ from a simple approach of first producing 1-to-many alignments and then applying a BiLSTM sequence tagger (e.g., Schnober et al., 2016), and why wasn't this baseline included in your evaluation?

2. The paper mentions that your alignment is "nearly-monotonic" but doesn't specify the exact alignment type used (1-to-many, many-to-1, many-to-many). Can you clarify this and explain why the specific alignment type you use is appropriate for the morphological inflection task?

3. You state that your model "outperforms previous neural and non-neural approaches" but don't provide precise statistics. Please clarify the exact performance improvements over baselines (e.g., "the model is 1.8% better than the previous state of the art on the CELEX dataset") rather than using vague terms like "on par or better."

4. The paper doesn't analyze whether the linguistic features (POS, gender, etc.) are driving the performance improvements or whether it's the monotonic alignment. Can you provide an analysis of feature importance or ablation studies on the features?
