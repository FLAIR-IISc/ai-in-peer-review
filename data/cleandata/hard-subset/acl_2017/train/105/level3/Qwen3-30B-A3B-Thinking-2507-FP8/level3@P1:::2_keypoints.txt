- Proposes a hard-attention neural model enforcing monotonic alignment for morphological inflection, claiming state-of-the-art results and strong performance on small training sets.  
- Main contribution: hard attention that enforces monotonic alignment and outperforms prior neural models, especially with limited data.  
- Main contribution: analysis comparing representations learned by hard vs. soft attention, illuminating how morphological features are encoded.  
- Main contribution: shows that explicitly modeling monotonic alignment improves performance, notably on small datasets.  
- Strength: Convincing empirical gains from enforcing monotonic alignment, with strong results on CELEX (500 examples per inflection type).  
- Strength: Insightful representation analysis showing hard attention encodes position information less strongly, which may help with small-data performance.  
- Strength: Well-written paper with clear architecture description and thorough experiments against neural and non-neural baselines across three datasets.  
- Weakness: Missing comparison to a simple 1-to-many alignment + BiLSTM sequence-tagger baseline (e.g., Schnober et al., 2016), weakening claims of novelty.  
- Weakness: Evaluation restricted to morphological inflection; no tests on other monotone seq2seq tasks to demonstrate broader applicability.  
- Weakness: Insufficient clarity on the exact alignment type used (1-to-many, many-to-1, many-to-many vs. mixed 1-0/0-1/1-1), making assumptions and limits unclear.  
- Weakness: Role of linguistic features (POS, gender, etc.) not analyzed; unclear whether gains stem from alignment or feature information.  
- Question: How does your model differ from producing 1-to-many alignments then applying a BiLSTM tagger, and why was that baseline omitted?  
- Question: Please clarify the exact alignment type used (1-to-many, many-to-1, many-to-many) and why it suits morphological inflection.  
- Question: Provide precise performance improvements over baselines (e.g., exact percentage gains on CELEX) instead of vague claims.  
- Question: Can you provide feature-importance analyses or ablation studies to determine whether linguistic features or monotonic alignment drive the improvements?
