

# Review of "Morphological Inflection Generation with Hard Monotonic Attention"

## Summary
The paper introduces a neural model for morphological inflection generation that employs a hard attention mechanism based on nearly-monotonic character alignments between input and output sequences. Unlike prior soft-attention models that jointly learn alignment and transduction, this approach uses independently learned character alignments (via a Bayesian transliteration method) to guide the training of an encoder-decoder network with a dedicated control mechanism. The model excels particularly on small training datasets, outperforming previous neural and non-neural approaches on the CELEX dataset, while maintaining strong performance on larger datasets like Wiktionary and SIGMORPHON.

## Main Contributions
1. A novel hard monotonic attention mechanism that explicitly models the nearly-monotonic character alignments common in morphological inflection tasks, differing substantially from prior soft-attention approaches.
2. Comprehensive empirical validation showing state-of-the-art performance on morphological inflection generation, particularly with small training datasets where previous neural models struggled.
3. A detailed analysis of the representations learned by both hard and soft attention models, revealing how they encode linguistic features like character type and sequence position.

## Strengths
1. **Novel and effective approach to a well-known limitation**: The paper successfully addresses the critical issue of neural models overfitting on small datasets (like CELEX) by leveraging the inherent monotonicity of morphological alignments. The hard attention mechanism is a clean, theoretically sound solution that avoids the need for complex joint learning of alignment and transduction. This represents a significant conceptual contribution that could influence other sequence-to-sequence tasks with similar alignment properties.

2. **Strong empirical validation with appropriate comparisons**: The authors conduct comprehensive experiments across three well-established datasets (CELEX, Wiktionary, SIGMORPHON), providing a thorough evaluation of their approach under varying data conditions. Their demonstration that the model is the first neural approach to surpass the latent variable WFST model on CELEX is particularly compelling and addresses a significant gap in the literature.

3. **Insightful analysis of learned representations**: The paper provides valuable insights into what the models actually learn by visualizing character embeddings in 2D space. The analysis showing how the hard attention model encodes position information differently than soft attention models (through explicit step actions rather than through input representations) offers meaningful theoretical understanding of the model's behavior.

## Weaknesses
1. **Limited scope of applicability**: The paper acknowledges that the method performs poorly when alignments are non-monotonic or highly context-dependent (e.g., languages with vowel harmony). However, this limitation is not sufficiently emphasized as a major constraint to the approach's generalizability. The paper presents the method as broadly applicable, while the empirical results (Table 3) clearly show it underperforms on languages like Turkish, Arabic, and Navajo where morphological phenomena are less monotonic.

2. **Lack of practical implementation analysis**: The paper makes no mention of the computational cost or implementation complexity of the proposed approach compared to soft-attention models. Given that the authors claim the model is "much simpler" than soft attention models, it's surprising they don't provide any discussion of training time, inference speed, or practical implementation considerations that would be crucial for adoption in real-world systems.

3. **Incomplete comparison with related work**: While the paper compares with several neural and non-neural baselines, it doesn't thoroughly address how their approach relates to other methods that also use external alignments (like Jaitly et al. 2015). The paper claims their approach is simpler due to using a cross-entropy loss, but doesn't provide a detailed comparison of the training procedures or how their alignment method compares to other alignment techniques.

## Questions to Authors
1. The paper states the model is "much simpler" than soft attention models, but doesn't provide evidence of training or inference speed. Could you provide timing comparisons between your model and the soft attention baselines on the same hardware?

2. The paper demonstrates strong performance on suffixing languages (Russian, German, Spanish) but weaker performance on other morphological types. Could you elaborate on the specific linguistic features that make a language suitable for your approach, and whether there are ways to extend the method to handle non-monotonic alignments?

3. The authors use a Bayesian transliteration method for alignment, but don't discuss how robust this alignment method is to errors. How would the model perform with imperfect alignments, and is there a way to make the approach more robust to alignment errors?

The paper presents a valuable contribution to morphological inflection generation by addressing a key limitation of neural sequence-to-sequence models on small datasets. While the method has clear limitations in terms of scope, the novelty of the approach and strong empirical results on appropriate data make it a significant contribution to the field. With the suggested clarifications, this paper would make a strong addition to ACL 2017.
