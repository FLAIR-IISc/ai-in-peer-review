- Introduces a novel neural model for morphological inflection using a hard monotonic attention mechanism instead of soft attention.
- Model explicitly alternates between writing output symbols and advancing an attention pointer to model nearly-monotonic character alignments.
- Evaluated on CELEX (small), Wiktionary (larger), and SIGMORPHON 2016 (multilingual) datasets.
- Primary contribution: hard monotonic attention that outperforms prior neural and non-neural approaches, notably on small-data CELEX.
- Secondary contribution: comprehensive empirical evaluation showing state-of-the-art results on German verbs, Finnish nouns/adjectives, and Dutch verbs (Wiktionary).
- Tertiary contribution: analysis comparing learned representations of hard vs. soft attention, showing different positional encoding behavior.
- Strength: strong performance with very small training datasets (CELEX: 89.44% exact match vs. prior neural and WFST baselines).
- Strength: learning curves show hard attention avoids overfitting on small datasets and generalizes better than soft attention.
- Strength: representation analysis gives linguistic insight into positional sensitivity differences between mechanisms.
- Weakness: performance gains are limited to mostly-monotonic languages and degrade on languages with complex phenomena (e.g., Turkish, Arabic).
- Weakness: on SIGMORPHON, the model loses to soft attention on 5 of 10 languages, so not universally superior.
- Weakness: lacks discussion of computational cost and implementation complexity for practitioners.
- Weakness: unclear how much improvement is due to hard attention versus other architectural choices.
- Weakness: insufficient comparison to other monotonic alignment models (e.g., RNN Transducer, Neural Transducer).
- Recommendation: paper needs a more nuanced discussion of limitations and expanded analysis of language-specific behavior.
- Questions for authors: compare training time to soft-attention baseline on large datasets; clarify CELEX data-split used for the claim of beating weighted FST; advise applicability to suffixing/monotonic languages or adaptations for complex morphology.
