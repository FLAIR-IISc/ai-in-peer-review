

# Review for ACL 2017

## Summary and Contributions
This paper proposes a neural model for morphological inflection generation that employs a hard attention mechanism based on nearly-monotonic character alignments. The authors claim three main contributions: (1) a hard attention model for nearly-monotonic sequence-to-sequence learning, (2) state-of-the-art results on three morphological inflection datasets, and (3) an analysis of representations learned by hard and soft attention models. In my assessment, the most significant contribution is the empirical demonstration that a hard attention mechanism with explicitly modeled monotonic alignments outperforms previous neural models on small datasets, particularly on the CELEX dataset where it surpasses the latent variable WFST model of Dreyer et al. (2008) for the first time. The second most significant contribution is the insightful analysis of learned representations, which reveals how the models encode linguistic features. The third contribution is the model architecture itself, though its novelty is somewhat limited by the alignment approach.

## Strengths
1. **Strong empirical results on small datasets**: The model achieves state-of-the-art results on the CELEX dataset, which is particularly impressive given its small size (only 500 examples per inflection type). The hard attention model significantly outperforms previous neural models (MED and NWFST) and even surpasses the linguistically-informed latent variable WFST model of Dreyer et al. (2008), which previously set the benchmark for this dataset. The learning curves in Figure 2 clearly demonstrate that the hard attention model avoids overfitting on small datasets, while the soft attention model does not.

2. **Clear and well-structured presentation**: The paper is exceptionally well-written and organized. The model is described with sufficient technical detail, the experimental setup is clear, and the results are presented with appropriate metrics. The analysis of learned representations (Figure 4) is particularly insightful and adds significant value to the paper.

3. **Meaningful analysis of learned representations**: The paper provides valuable insights into what the models encode. The SVD dimensionality reduction (Figure 4) reveals that the hard attention model encodes position information less strongly than the soft attention model, which the authors correctly attribute to the model's explicit step actions for position tracking. This analysis helps explain why the hard attention model performs better with limited data.

## Weaknesses
1. **Lack of comparison with simpler baseline**: The authors claim their model is novel, but it appears to be equivalent to a simple 1-to-many alignment followed by a BiLSTM sequence tagger (e.g., as in Schnober et al. or similar approaches). The paper fails to include this simple baseline for comparison, making it difficult to assess how much the complex model actually contributes. The authors should address this by adding such a comparison.

2. **Narrow evaluation scope**: The evaluation is limited to morphological inflection generation, with no testing on other monotone sequence-to-sequence tasks (e.g., transliteration, abstractive summarization). Without demonstrating broader applicability, the claim that "nearly-monotonic alignment commonly found between the characters in a word and the characters in its inflection" is appropriate for "many down-stream NLP tasks" remains unsupported.

3. **Ambiguous alignment type**: The paper doesn't clarify whether the alignment is 1-to-many, many-to-1, or many-to-many. The alignment process (Section 2.3) seems to combine these types, which is confusing and makes it difficult to understand the model's fundamental assumptions. The authors should explicitly state the alignment type and justify it.

4. **Insufficient analysis of linguistic features**: The paper reports results using morpho-syntactic features, but doesn't analyze whether the improvements come from the monotonic alignment or from the linguistic features themselves. The authors should provide feature ablation studies to isolate the contribution of the alignment mechanism.

5. **Imprecise reporting**: The paper uses vague terms like "on par or better" rather than providing precise metrics. For example, Table 1 states "Hard 85.8 95.1 89.5 87.2 89.44" but doesn't specify what these numbers represent (accuracy? F1?). The paper should report results with clear metrics and statistical significance.

## Questions to Authors
1. The paper uses "character level alignment" (Section 2.3) but doesn't clarify whether this is 1-to-many, many-to-1, or many-to-many. Please specify the alignment type and explain how it differs from simpler approaches.

2. Why wasn't the simple baseline of 1-to-many alignment followed by a BiLSTM sequence tagger (e.g., as in Schnober et al.) included in the comparison? How does your model differ from this baseline approach, and why is it necessary?

3. The paper claims "nearly-monotonic alignment" is common in morphological inflection, but doesn't demonstrate this on other monotone sequence-to-sequence tasks. Would you consider evaluating on additional tasks (e.g., transliteration) to support the broader applicability claim?

4. The paper uses linguistic features (e.g., POS) but doesn't analyze whether the improvements come from the alignment mechanism or from these features. Please provide feature ablation studies to isolate the contribution of the alignment mechanism.

5. The paper mentions "greedy alignments" but doesn't compare to methods that sum over alignments (e.g., Rastogi et al., 2016). How does the greedy alignment affect performance compared to methods that marginalize over alignments?
