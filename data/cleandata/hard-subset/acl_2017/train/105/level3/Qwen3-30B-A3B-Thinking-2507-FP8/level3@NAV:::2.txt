

This paper proposes a novel hard monotonic attention mechanism for morphological inflection generation, decoupling alignment and transduction by first learning monotonic alignments and then training the encoder-decoder to generate outputs consistent with these alignments. The authors claim three main contributions: (1) a hard attention model for nearly-monotonic sequence-to-sequence learning, (2) state-of-the-art results on morphological inflection datasets, and (3) an analysis comparing their model with soft attention approaches.

Strengths: The paper presents a clear and well-structured proposal of a novel hard monotonic attention mechanism that directly addresses the monotonic alignment pattern common in morphological inflection. The model's architecture is logically designed to leverage this natural alignment structure, and the experimental results demonstrate competitive performance across multiple datasets, particularly excelling on small training sets where neural models typically struggle. The analysis comparing hard and soft attention models provides valuable insights into how these models encode linguistic features.

Weaknesses: The most significant concern is the lack of a proper baseline comparison. The authors fail to include a simple 1-to-many alignment + BiLSTM sequence tagger baseline (e.g., Schnober et al.), making it impossible to assess whether the complex model adds meaningful value beyond this straightforward approach. The model's claimed novelty is questionable, as it may be equivalent to a standard BiRNN with decoupled alignment. The evaluation is limited to morphology, with no testing on other monotone sequence-to-sequence tasks to demonstrate broader applicability. The paper also fails to clarify the alignment type used (1-to-many, many-to-1, or many-to-many) and uses imprecise language like "on par or better" without proper statistical reporting.

Key questions: How does your model fundamentally differ from a simple align-then-tag approach with 1-to-many alignments and a neural tagger? Why was this baseline omitted? How do you address the greedy alignment concern compared to methods that sum over alignments (Rastogi et al., 2016)? Please clarify the mixed alignment types (1-0, 0-1, 1-1) mentioned in the author response and how they're combined into many-to-many.
