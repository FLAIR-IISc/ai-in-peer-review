- Proposes a neural hard-attention model tailored to nearly-monotonic character alignments for morphological inflection generation.  
- Contributions: (1) explicit hard-attention handling of monotonic alignment, (2) SOTA results on three datasets with strong gains on small training sets, (3) analysis comparing representations learned by hard vs. soft attention.

- Strength: Strong performance on small datasets (CELEX), outperforming prior neural and non-neural methods, important for low-resource languages.  
- Strength: Clear analysis (SVD visualizations) showing hard attention encodes position information less explicitly than soft attention, offering theoretical insight.  
- Strength: Comprehensive experiments across CELEX, Wiktionary, and SIGMORPHON, with reasonable identification of limitations on complex morphologies.

- Weakness: “State-of-the-art” claim is overstated; model wins on some languages/datasets but underperforms soft attention on others (e.g., Turkish, Arabic, Navajo).  
- Weakness: Lacks practical implementation details (ease of implementation, training time, effect of alignment step) important for practitioners.  
- Weakness: Explanations for language-specific performance are superficial; needs deeper linguistic examples to support the suffixing+stem-change hypothesis.

- Question: How much better is the hard-attention model than Dreyer et al. (2008) on CELEX, and is the improvement statistically significant?  
- Question: How was the Sudoh et al. (2013) alignment method adapted for inflection, and what is the computational cost relative to model training?  
- Question: Can you provide concrete examples from vowel-harmony languages (e.g., Turkish, Arabic) where monotonic alignment fails and soft attention succeeds?  
- Question: How does the performance gap between hard and soft attention change with increasing training data — is there a data threshold where soft attention becomes consistently better?

- Additional comment: Paper is well-written, technically clear, and the model’s simplicity (standard cross-entropy without joint alignment training) is a practical advantage.  
- Additional comment: Authors should clarify that their approach is complementary to, not universally superior to, soft attention models.
