- Summary: Proposes a seq2seq model with hard monotonic attention that decouples alignment and transduction, evaluated on CELEX, Wiktionary, and SIGMORPHON 2016 with strong results, especially on small datasets.

- Contribution 1: Introduces a hard monotonic attention mechanism tailored to nearly-monotonic alignments in morphological inflection.

- Contribution 2: Shows the approach outperforms prior neural and non-neural models, notably in low-resource settings.

- Contribution 3: Provides analysis comparing learned representations of hard vs. soft attention models.

- Strength 1: Well-motivated and conceptually clean—enforcing monotonicity fits the task and helps with limited data.

- Strength 2: Strong empirical results, achieving SOTA on the smallest dataset (CELEX) and robust performance on larger sets.

- Strength 3: Clear presentation with useful analysis of alignments and position encoding.

- Weakness 1: Omits a simple 1-to-many alignment + BiLSTM baseline, leaving unclear how much gain is due to hard attention versus decoupling.

- Weakness 2: Evaluation is restricted to morphological inflection despite claims of broader applicability to monotonic seq2seq tasks.

- Weakness 3: Alignment type and implementation are ambiguous (mix of 1-0, 0-1, 1-1 merged into many-to-many), conflicting with the "hard monotonic" claim.

- Weakness 4: Reporting is imprecise about significance and does not discuss cases where soft attention outperforms the model.

- Question 1: How does the model handle non-monotonic elements (e.g., 1-to-2 mappings) while claiming to enforce monotonicity?

- Question 2: Why was the 1-to-many + BiLSTM baseline not included, and how much improvement is attributable to hard attention vs. alignment decoupling?

- Question 3: Does greedy alignment lose performance compared to marginalizing over alignments (e.g., Rastogi et al., 2016)?

- Question 4: How does this approach relate to and differ from Cohn et al. (2016) and Stack LSTM architectures, and why aren't they discussed in detail?

- Question 5: What are the sources of the linguistic features (e.g., POS), and could feature use, not the alignment mechanism, be driving the improvements?
