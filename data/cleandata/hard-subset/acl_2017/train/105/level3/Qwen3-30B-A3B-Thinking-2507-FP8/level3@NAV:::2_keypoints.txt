- Proposal: hard monotonic attention that decouples alignment and transduction by first learning monotonic alignments, then training an encoder–decoder to follow them.
- Claimed contributions: (1) a hard attention model for nearly-monotonic seq2seq, (2) state-of-the-art results on morphological inflection datasets, (3) an analysis comparing hard vs soft attention.
- Strength: clear, well-structured proposal explicitly addressing monotonic alignment common in morphological inflection.
- Strength: model architecture is logical and leverages natural alignment structure.
- Strength: competitive experimental results across multiple datasets, particularly strong on small training sets.
- Strength: comparative analysis provides useful insights into how hard and soft attention encode linguistic features.
- Weakness: missing a crucial baseline—1-to-many alignment + BiLSTM sequence tagger (e.g., Schnober et al.)—so added value of the complex model is unclear.
- Weakness: novelty is questionable; model may be equivalent to a standard BiRNN with decoupled alignment.
- Weakness: evaluation is limited to morphology—no experiments on other monotonic seq2seq tasks to demonstrate generality.
- Weakness: alignment type is not clearly specified (1-to-many, many-to-1, many-to-many), and reporting uses imprecise terms like “on par or better” without statistical tests.
- Key question: how does the model fundamentally differ from a simple align-then-tag 1-to-many approach, and why was that baseline omitted?
- Key question: how do you address greedy alignment concerns versus methods that marginalize over alignments (e.g., Rastogi et al., 2016)?
- Key question: please clarify the mixed alignment types (1-0, 0-1, 1-1) and how they are combined into many-to-many alignments.
