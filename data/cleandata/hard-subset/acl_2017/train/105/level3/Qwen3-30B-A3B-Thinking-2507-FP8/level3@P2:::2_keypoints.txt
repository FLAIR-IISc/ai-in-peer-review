- Proposes a hard attention neural model for nearly-monotonic character-level sequence-to-sequence learning.  
- Claims SOTA results on three morphological inflection datasets, notably outperforming prior models on small-data CELEX.  
- Analyzes representations learned by hard vs. soft attention, showing interpretable encoding differences.

- Strong empirical performance on small datasets, with hard attention avoiding overfitting and beating prior neural and WFST baselines.  
- Paper is clearly written and well-structured, with detailed model description and experimental setup.  
- Meaningful analysis (e.g., SVD plots) showing hard attention encodes position less strongly, explaining better small-data behavior.

- Lacks comparison to a simple 1-to-many alignment + BiLSTM sequence tagger baseline to isolate model contribution.  
- Evaluation scope is narrow (only morphological inflection), so broader applicability to other monotonic tasks is unproven.  
- Alignment type (1-to-many / many-to-1 / many-to-many) is ambiguous and not explicitly clarified.  
- Insufficient analysis isolating the effect of morpho-syntactic features vs. the alignment mechanism (no feature ablations).  
- Reporting is imprecise in places (vague phrases, unclear metric labels, no significance testing).

- Clarify whether character-level alignments are 1-to-many, many-to-1, or many-to-many and how they differ from simpler approaches.  
- Explain why a simple 1-to-many + BiLSTM baseline was not included and how the proposed model differs.  
- Consider evaluating on other nearly-monotonic tasks (e.g., transliteration) to support broader applicability claims.  
- Provide feature ablation studies to determine whether gains come from alignment or added linguistic features.  
- Discuss the impact of using greedy alignments versus marginalizing over alignments (compare to methods like Rastogi et al., 2016).
