

This paper proposes a hard monotonic attention mechanism for morphological inflection generation, which represents a significant departure from standard soft-attention approaches. The primary contribution is the novel hard attention mechanism that leverages pre-learned character alignments (via a Bayesian transliteration method) to guide the encoder-decoder process, avoiding the need to jointly learn alignment and conversion.

Strengths: The paper presents a compelling empirical case for the approach's effectiveness on small datasets like CELEX, where it outperforms previous neural models and surpasses the latent-variable WFST model of Dreyer et al. (2008), which is a notable achievement. The clear writing and comprehensive experimental evaluation across three datasets (CELEX, Wiktionary, and SIGMORPHON) demonstrate robustness for monotonic alignment scenarios. The analysis comparing hard and soft attention models provides valuable insights into how these approaches encode linguistic features.

Weaknesses: The method's reliance on monotonic alignments is a significant limitation, as it performs poorly on languages with non-monotonic alignments or high context-dependency (e.g., Hungarian, Arabic), which the authors acknowledge but don't adequately address. The paper lacks discussion of practical implementation considerations, including computational cost and ease of implementation compared to standard approaches. Additionally, while the authors claim state-of-the-art results, they don't sufficiently clarify the specific conditions under which their method is superior, making it difficult to assess its general applicability.

The paper's main contribution is the hard monotonic attention mechanism, which shows promise for specific morphological tasks but requires careful consideration of alignment constraints. The omission of practical implementation analysis is particularly notable for a method that relies on external alignment procedures. The authors should address these limitations in the revision to better position their contribution within the broader landscape of morphological inflection approaches.
