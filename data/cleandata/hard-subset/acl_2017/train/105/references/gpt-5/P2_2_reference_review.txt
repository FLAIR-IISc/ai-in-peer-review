Summary and main contributions
- The paper introduces a hard monotonic attention encoder–decoder for character-level morphological inflection. Decoding is framed as a sequence of WRITE(symbol) and STEP actions over a bidirectional encoding of the input; alignments are precomputed with a character aligner and used to derive oracle action sequences for simple cross-entropy training.
- It provides extensive empirical evaluation across three benchmarks (CELEX, Wiktionary, SIGMORPHON 2016), showing strong gains in low-resource settings (CELEX) and competitive or better performance on larger datasets (Wiktionary). It also contrasts hard vs. soft attention by analyzing alignments and encoder representations.
- It offers clear, interpretable modeling of monotonic alignments typical for suffixing morphology, with a simple training scheme (no forward–backward) and evidence that this bias improves generalization when data are scarce.

Strengths
- Sound, simple modeling with clear inductive bias: The hard attention control mechanism (STEP/WRITE) matches the near-monotonic nature of many inflectional processes. Using precomputed character alignments to derive gold action sequences allows straightforward training with cross-entropy, avoiding the complexity of marginalization over alignments. This design choice is well-justified and aligns with the task structure.
- Strong low-resource performance: On CELEX, the model surpasses both neural and tailored WFST baselines (Table 1; average 89.44 vs. LAT 88.3 and MED 87.62). The learning-curve analysis (Figure 2) convincingly shows lower overfitting than soft attention, directly supporting the claimed benefit of the monotonic inductive bias.
- Competitive large-data results with broad comparisons: On Wiktionary, the model achieves the best reported average across languages/settings (96.55), slightly outperforming strong neural and non-neural baselines. This demonstrates that the hard attention bias does not harm performance when ample data is available.
- Careful, informative analysis: The alignment inspection demonstrates that soft attention tends to learn monotonic alignments anyway, supporting the modeling choice. The SVD plots of encoder states show what information is encoded (character identity vs. position), with a plausible interpretation of why hard attention may require less positional encoding, potentially aiding generalization in low-resource conditions.
- Clarity and reproducibility: The model is described clearly; the action-space formulation and training procedure are easy to reproduce. Use of standard datasets, established splits (CELEX), and widely cited baselines helps comparability. The paper states that code is available (anonymized for review), and uses a public alignment tool.

Weaknesses
- Over-claiming state-of-the-art on SIGMORPHON: The hard attention model is not the top performer overall on SIGMORPHON (Table 3 shows the authors’ soft attention baseline averaging 96.34 vs. hard 95.61). The paper correctly notes hard attention excels for suffixing+stem-change languages (RU/DE/ES) but is weaker on languages with harmony or templatic morphology (FI/TU/HU/NA/AR/MA). The abstract/claims should be calibrated: the contribution is state-of-the-art or best average on CELEX and Wiktionary subsets, and targeted superiority on a typologically motivated subset of SIGMORPHON, rather than blanket SOTA on all three datasets.
- Limited ablations and sensitivity analyses: The approach hinges on external alignments. The paper does not quantify sensitivity to alignment errors or compare alternative aligners, nor does it report performance when training without oracle alignments (e.g., with joint alignment learning), beyond citing Yu et al. (2016). An ablation showing performance vs. alignment quality or a controlled comparison with/without external alignments would strengthen the causal story.
- Decoding details and search: It is unclear whether decoding uses greedy or beam search over action sequences; search errors can be non-negligible in action-based transduction. Reporting the decoding strategy and any effect of beam size would help assess soundness and reproducibility.
- Statistical significance and variance: While CELEX averages over five folds, the Wiktionary and SIGMORPHON gains are small in several conditions. No statistical tests or confidence intervals are reported. Given close numbers, especially in Wiktionary, significance reporting would support the claims.
- Scope and generality: The method’s inductive bias is well-matched to monotonic transformations; performance drops on languages with non-local dependencies (vowel/consonant harmony, templatic morphology). The paper acknowledges this but could discuss extensions (e.g., limited look-back/look-ahead, flexible jump actions) to address non-monotonicity without sacrificing simplicity.

Questions to authors
- What decoding strategy do you use at test time (greedy vs. beam)? If beam, what width, and how sensitive are results to it?
- How sensitive is performance to the choice/quality of the external aligner? Did you try alternative aligners or noisy alignments to quantify robustness?
- Can you provide an ablation where the model is trained without external alignments (e.g., learning alignments jointly) to isolate the benefit of the oracle supervision?
- On SIGMORPHON, did you try augmenting the action space (e.g., limited backward moves) or architectural tweaks to better capture harmony/templatic phenomena, and if so, what was the effect?
- For Wiktionary, are the training/dev/test splits and preprocessing identical to prior work? If not, please clarify differences and any hyperparameter tuning protocol to ensure comparability.

Overall evaluation
The paper meets ACL reviewing criteria well: the method is technically sound, clearly presented, and empirically validated across multiple benchmarks with strong low-resource gains and competitive large-data results. Novelty is moderate—building on RNNT/Neural Transducer/segmental transduction ideas—but the specific instantiation (hard monotonic attention with oracle alignments) and the thorough evaluation and analysis in morphological inflection are valuable. With calibrated claims, decoding details, and modest ablations on alignment dependence, this work merits acceptance.
