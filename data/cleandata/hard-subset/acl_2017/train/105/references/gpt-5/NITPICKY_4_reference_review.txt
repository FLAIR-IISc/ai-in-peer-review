Summary and contributions
- A pointer-based encoder-decoder with hard, monotonic attention for character-level morphological inflection. The decoder alternates between WRITE(symbol) and STEP actions over a biLSTM-encoded input; training uses externally induced, hard character alignments to derive gold action sequences and a simple cross-entropy objective (best-derivation approximation).
- Empirical evaluation on CELEX (low-resource), Wiktionary (large), and SIGMORPHON 2016 (cross-linguistic) with competitive or better accuracy than prior soft-attention and WFST-based baselines; the gains are most pronounced in the low-resource CELEX setting.
- Qualitative analysis comparing learned alignments and encoder representations vs. a soft-attention baseline, suggesting the hard model leverages positional information via STEP rather than encoding it strongly in the representations.

Strengths
- Effective inductive bias for (near-)monotonic transductions. The STEP/WRITE control aligns well with suffixing + stem-change morphology, yielding clear accuracy gains on CELEX and several Wiktionary subsets. This is a simple, targeted design that matches the structure of the task.
- Simplicity and stable training. Supervised alignments remove the need for marginalization over alignments and complex losses; the entire model is trained with standard cross-entropy. This likely explains better generalization in the very low-data regime (CELEX) relative to soft attention.
- Broad, relevant evaluation. The paper covers three widely used datasets with diverse morphological phenomena, and compares against strong neural and non-neural baselines. The CELEX improvements over LAT and NWFST are practically meaningful given the small training size.
- Useful qualitative analysis. The alignment visualizations and 2D projections, while limited, support the claim that the model learns monotone alignments and uses the control mechanism to encode position, validating the design choices.

Weaknesses
- Limited novelty vs. prior monotonic/align-and-transduce models. The approach is closely related to RNN-Transducer/CTC-style models and the Neural Transducer, as well as monotonic attention in ASR. The main difference is using external hard alignments and a STEP/WRITE control; this is a straightforward instantiation rather than a substantial conceptual advance. The paper does not clearly disentangle what is new versus adapted, nor provide ablations to isolate the contribution of supervised alignments.
- Missing key experimental details and ablations affect reproducibility and fairness. Decoding is underspecified (greedy vs. beam, termination criterion, constraints when STEP reaches the end, handling of many-to-many edits). Hyperparameters (hidden sizes, embedding dims, optimizer settings, dropout, parameter counts), training regimen (epochs, early stopping), and preprocessing are deferred to supplementary; these are essential for reproducibility. No ablations on: quality of external alignments; best-derivation vs. marginalization; effect of feeding previous output; effect of features f; beam size. No computational analysis (training/inference speed) versus soft attention.
- Overstated claims and lack of statistical testing. The abstract and conclusion suggest SOTA across three datasets, but on SIGMORPHON the hard model’s average is below the authors’ soft baseline; on Wiktionary, average gains over best prior work are very small. No significance testing (e.g., McNemar/bootstrap) is provided, and some comparisons are against systems with different training choices (e.g., MED trained for 20 epochs vs. authors’ soft trained for 100), raising fairness concerns.
- Reliance on external alignments and objective mismatch not analyzed. The model optimizes the best-derivation approximation and depends on an external aligner; the impact of alignment errors is not quantified. There is no robustness study with noisy alignments, nor a comparison with learned alignments of similar monotonic form, nor with marginalization (e.g., forward-backward) in a comparable architecture.
- Writing/formatting and references need substantial cleanup. Numerous typos and grammatical errors; malformed footnotes in the main text; several reference entries are incomplete or corrupted (question marks, \Q...\E tokens, duplicated/truncated entries); figures/tables are inconsistently referenced; the code link is “removed for anonymity” without an anonymous link. These issues impede clarity.

Questions to authors
- Decoding: Do you use greedy or beam search? What is the termination condition (EOS token vs. pointer at end with no further WRITE)? How do you prevent invalid sequences (e.g., excess STEP beyond input length)? How are many-to-many alignments realized in action space?
- Features: How exactly are morpho-syntactic attributes encoded (per-attribute embeddings concatenated vs. a single tag embedding)? What are the embedding sizes and how are OOV features handled?
- Hyperparameters and training: Please specify H, E, optimizer (Adadelta?), learning rate schedule, dropout, batch size, gradient clipping, parameter counts, training epochs, early stopping/selection criteria, random seeds, and beam width (if any). Provide per-dataset training time and inference speed.
- Baselines and splits: For Wiktionary and SIGMORPHON, did you use identical splits and preprocessing as the compared systems? For MED/SIGMORPHON, did you reimplement under identical settings except attention type? Why is MED trained for 20 epochs while your soft baseline is trained for 100? Please justify fairness.
- Significance and variance: Are the reported improvements statistically significant? Please report standard deviations over multiple runs or per-fold results with significance tests (e.g., McNemar).
- Alignment dependence: How sensitive is performance to the external alignments? Can you report results with a different aligner and with noisy alignments? Any analysis of alignment error types that hurt the model?
- Objective: Did you attempt marginalization over derivations (e.g., forward-backward) with the same action space? If not, can you provide evidence that the best-derivation approximation is sufficient (e.g., correlation between number of derivations per string and errors)?
- Non-monotonic phenomena: Can you mitigate the hard model’s weakness on templatic/harmony languages (e.g., limited lookahead/backward steps, learned skip actions)? Any hybrid approach results?
