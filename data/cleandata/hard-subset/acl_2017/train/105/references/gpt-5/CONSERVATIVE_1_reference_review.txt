Summary and claimed contributions
This paper tackles morphological inflection generation with an encoder–decoder model that uses a hard, nearly monotonic attention mechanism. The decoder emits either a WRITE(symbol) or a STEP action; training relies on externally induced character alignments to derive oracle action sequences, optimized with cross-entropy. The authors evaluate on CELEX (low-resource), Wiktionary (large), and SIGMORPHON 2016 (ten languages), report strong accuracy, and include qualitative analyses of alignments and encoder representations.

As I see it, the main contributions are:
1) A simple, externally aligned hard-attention transducer for near-monotonic string transduction, with a pointer/STEP control that exploits monotonicity.
2) A broad empirical study on three established benchmarks, showing strong performance, especially in low-resource settings, with code availability.
3) Diagnostic analyses comparing hard vs. soft attention (alignment behavior and positional/character representations).

Strengths
- Strong inductive bias for the task: The hard monotonic control matches the prevalent alignment structure in suffixing/stem-change languages, reducing overfitting in small data. The CELEX gains over both soft attention and neural WFST are consistent with this and noteworthy (surpassing Dreyer et al. 2008 on average is a meaningful milestone).
- Simpler, stable training: Relying on oracle actions derived from external alignments avoids forward–backward marginalization and often tricky latent alignment learning. The method is straightforward to train with cross-entropy and conditions on full output history, an advantage over classic FSTs.
- Broad evaluation: The paper covers three well-known datasets, includes older non-neural baselines and recent neural ones, and reports per-language trends (e.g., better on suffixing + stem-change languages). This scope provides a reasonably convincing empirical picture.
- Interpretability/analysis: The qualitative alignment comparisons and encoder-representation projections are informative. They support the monotonicity assumption and shed light on how positional and segmental information is represented, strengthening the methodological narrative.
- Practicality: The hard attention mechanism should be more efficient than soft attention at inference (single attended vector, no weighted sums), though the paper does not quantify this. Code release increases reproducibility.

Weaknesses
- Limited novelty relative to established transduction frameworks: The step/write pointer control, trained from external alignments, is close in spirit to prior monotonic transducers (e.g., RNN Transducer, Neural Transducer) and to classic align-and-transduce FST approaches. The main novelty lies in a specific instantiation and its application to inflection, rather than a fundamentally new model.
- Objective mismatch and decoding details: The model optimizes best-derivation rather than best-sequence, with no marginalization over derivations that yield the same output. The paper does not clarify decoding (greedy vs. beam search), nor analyze the impact of multiple derivations for a single sequence. This is a nontrivial modeling gap that could affect correctness and robustness.
- Dependence on external alignments, with no ablations: The approach hinges on the quality of the character aligner; yet there is no study of sensitivity to alignment errors, alternative aligners, or learned-vs-fixed alignment. This weakens claims of robustness and raises questions about portability to settings where good aligners are not available.
- Fairness and statistical rigor of comparisons: Some comparisons appear uneven. For SIGMORPHON, the authors’ soft-attention baseline is trained longer and tuned on dev, while MED (the official system) is reported with a fixed 20-epoch regime; this confounds conclusions. For CELEX and Wiktionary, there are no statistical significance tests or variance estimates, and margins are sometimes small. For Wiktionary, it is not entirely clear that splits and preprocessing match all prior work. Stronger care is needed to substantiate “state of the art” claims.
- Coverage limitations for non-monotonic phenomena: Results drop for languages with templatic morphology or harmony processes, and the model provides no mechanism to relax strict monotonicity (e.g., limited reordering or chunkwise attention). This restricts generality; a hybrid or adaptively relaxed attention would strengthen the claim of broad applicability.
- Missing engineering details and efficiency analysis: The paper lacks a clear accounting of hyperparameters, model size, regularization, and runtime/memory comparisons to soft attention. Given the claimed simplicity and potential efficiency, quantitative evidence is expected.

Questions to authors
- Decoding: Do you use greedy or beam search at test time? If beam, what width, and how sensitive are results to it? Have you tried marginalizing over derivations that yield the same string (e.g., via constrained lattice or DP), and does it help?
- Alignment sensitivity: How do results change with different aligners, perturbed/noisy alignments, or supervised gold alignments where available? Can you report an ablation with learned alignment (joint or fine-tuned) vs. fixed?
- Fair comparisons: For SIGMORPHON and Wiktionary, can you ensure identical splits/preprocessing and provide statistical significance (e.g., bootstrap or McNemar) and variance across runs? For CELEX, please report variance across folds per system and significance vs. LAT and NWFST.
- Non-monotonic phenomena: Can the model be extended to allow limited non-monotonic moves (e.g., a small backward step or local chunk attention), and does that help for Georgian/Arabic/Navajo?
- Efficiency: Please report training/inference speed, memory, and parameter counts vs. soft attention. Is the hard attention faster at decode time as expected?

Overall, the paper is a solid, largely incremental contribution that appropriately exploits a well-motivated inductive bias and provides broad empirical evidence. Strengthening the rigor of comparisons, analyzing alignment dependence, and clarifying decoding would significantly improve the work.
