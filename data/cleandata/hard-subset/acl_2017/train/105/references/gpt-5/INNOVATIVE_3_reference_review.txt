Summary and main contributions
This paper proposes a hard monotonic attention model for morphological inflection generation. The model is an encoder–decoder with a discrete control mechanism that alternates between WRITE(char) and STEP actions, supervised by externally induced character alignments. It trains with simple cross-entropy over action sequences (best-derivation approximation), rather than learning alignments jointly.

As I see it, the primary contributions are:
1) A simple, explicit hard monotonic attention formulation (STEP/WRITE) supervised by external alignments, yielding strong data efficiency and competitive or SOTA results across three benchmarks.
2) A careful empirical study showing this approach surpasses strong neural and WFST baselines on low-resource CELEX, remains robust on large Wiktionary, and behaves predictably across morphological typologies on SIGMORPHON.
3) Analysis that connects learned alignments/representations to the task (e.g., monotonicity, positional encoding differences vs. soft attention), offering insight into why the model generalizes better with limited data.

Strengths
- Conceptual clarity and novelty in the task context: The explicit STEP/WRITE control with hard, externally supervised attention is a crisp instantiation of monotonic sequence transduction tailored to morphology. While related to RNN-Transducer/Neural Transducer, the formulation here (external alignment, explicit pointer movement, simple CE loss) is both simpler and better aligned with the near-monotonic nature of inflection. This is a bold, clean design that prioritizes inductive bias and data efficiency.

- Strong low-resource performance with a clear rationale: On CELEX, the model outperforms both neural and carefully engineered WFST baselines, notably surpassing the latent-variable WFST—a longstanding strong system for small data. The learning curves and analysis convincingly argue reduced overfitting due to the monotonicity bias and supervised hard attention.

- Competitive/highest results with large data: On Wiktionary, the model achieves the best average accuracy and leads on multiple language/part-of-speech subsets, outperforming both non-attentional neural models and a joint alignment–transduction neural model.

- Insightful analysis of attention and representations: The comparisons to soft attention (monotonic alignments in practice; difference in positional encoding reliance) are illuminating. The visualization and qualitative alignment examples directly support the model’s design choices.

- Practical training and reproducibility: Using external, off-the-shelf alignments and cross-entropy training keeps the approach simple and accessible; code availability further supports reproducibility.

Weaknesses
- Objective mismatch and lack of marginalization: The best-derivation approximation ignores the multiple derivations that yield the same output. While effective empirically, there is no ablation showing whether marginalizing (or approximating it) would help, nor analysis of when the approximation fails (e.g., many-to-one mappings, long insertions/deletions).

- Reliance on external alignments without sensitivity analysis: The approach depends on an external hard aligner and assumes monotonicity. There is no study of how alignment quality/noise affects performance, how robust the method is to misalignments, or whether simple alternatives (e.g., training with auto-aligned or jointly learned monotone alignments) would close the gap.

- Fairness of comparisons and training budgets: The paper notes different training regimes (e.g., MED trained for 20 epochs vs. 100 here) and ensembling elsewhere, which complicates conclusions about superiority. A stricter apples-to-apples comparison (same epochs, model capacity, early stopping criteria, and ensembling policy) would strengthen claims.

- Limited coverage of non-monotonic phenomena: The model underperforms on languages with vowel/consonant harmony and templatic morphology. The analysis is reasonable, but it would be valuable to show extensions (e.g., limited look-back/look-ahead, controlled non-monotone jumps) or hybridization with soft/location-aware attention to handle such cases.

- Missing ablations: Key components deserve ablation: previous-output feedback, feature embeddings f, bidirectionality in the encoder, beam vs. greedy decoding, and the size of the action vocabulary (e.g., DELETE vs. multiple STEP). These would clarify which pieces drive the gains and guide future applications.

Questions to authors
- Decoding details: Do you use greedy decoding or beam search for action sequences? If beam, what beam size and how sensitive are results to it? How do you prevent infinite STEP loops or pathological sequences?

- Alignment sensitivity: How sensitive is performance to the choice of aligner and its hyperparameters? Can you report results with a simpler alignment (e.g., edit-distance/Needleman–Wunsch) and with injected noise?

- Objective approximation: Did you experiment with marginalizing over derivations for a fixed output (e.g., dynamic programming over monotone alignments) or with minimum risk training on output strings?

- Hybrid/extended attention: Have you tried adding a limited non-monotone MOVE(k) or a small windowed soft attention atop the hard pointer for languages with harmony/templatic morphology?

- Fairness controls: Can you provide results where all neural baselines are trained under matched epoch counts/early stopping and with/without ensembling to isolate the effect of the attention mechanism?

Overall evaluation
This paper introduces a clear, elegant hard-attention formulation that is well-matched to morphological inflection and demonstrates compelling data-efficiency benefits along with strong overall accuracy. Despite some missing ablations and reliance on external alignments, the conceptual contribution and empirical gains—especially in the low-resource setting—are significant. I lean toward acceptance.
