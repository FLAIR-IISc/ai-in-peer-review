Summary and main contributions
This paper proposes a character-level encoder–decoder with a hard, monotonic attention mechanism for morphological inflection generation. The decoder predicts a sequence of actions from Σy ∪ {step}, moving a single attention pointer forward (step) or writing an output symbol. Training uses externally induced character alignments to derive gold action sequences, enabling simple cross-entropy training without marginalizing over alignments. The model is evaluated on CELEX, Wiktionary, and SIGMORPHON 2016; it reports strong gains in low-resource settings and competitive results otherwise. The paper also provides qualitative analyses of learned alignments and encoder representations.

As I see them, the main contributions are:
1) A simple hard monotonic pointer network for inflection generation that leverages external alignments and performs very well in low-resource conditions.
2) An empirical study across three datasets with varying resource sizes and morphological typologies, showing advantages and limitations of the hard alignment bias.
3) Qualitative analyses comparing hard vs soft attention alignments and encoder representations.

Strengths
- Strong low-resource performance and compelling inductive bias: On CELEX, the model surpasses both neural baselines and the task-specific latent-variable WFST (LAT), which has historically been hard to beat. Learning curves support the claim that the hard monotonic bias reduces overfitting relative to soft attention on very small data.
- Simple training/inference and interpretability: Decoupling alignment learning from transduction makes training straightforward (cross-entropy on actions) and yields interpretable action sequences that transparently handle deletion (multiple step) and 1-to-many insertions (multiple write). The transition-system view is clear and useful.
- Broad empirical comparisons: Results are reported on three commonly used benchmarks (CELEX, Wiktionary, SIGMORPHON) and compared against competitive neural and non-neural baselines, including a soft attention reimplementation.
- Task bias matches common morphology: The hard monotonic pointer aligns well with suffixing and local stem-change phenomena, yielding consistent gains on those languages in SIGMORPHON.
- Analysis section offers intuition: The alignment visualizations and the representation-space projections, while qualitative, help interpret how the models behave and why the hard model may generalize better with fewer examples.

Weaknesses
- Limited novelty relative to prior hard/monotonic transduction models: The approach is very close in spirit to RNN-Transducer (Graves, 2012), Neural Transducer (Jaitly et al., 2015), and online segment-to-segment transduction (Yu et al., 2016). The key differentiator is training on external alignments and using a step action instead of blanks. Methodological novelty is incremental.
- Decoding/search details are underspecified: The paper seems to use greedy argmax over actions and does not specify beam search, termination criteria, constraints (e.g., disallowing step past the last encoder state), or safeguards against pathological derivations. The best-derivation approximation vs best-sequence is acknowledged but not analyzed; no quantification of search errors or impact of beam width is provided.
- Heavy dependence on an external aligner without ablation: The choice of the Sudoh et al. (2013) CRP aligner is not justified; no experiments with alternative aligners or a joint/learned alignment variant; no sensitivity analysis to alignment noise; and no quantitative measure of monotonicity in the training data. The model’s weaker performance on templatic/harmony languages underscores this limitation but is not explored empirically.
- Experimental fairness and reporting issues: On SIGMORPHON, the soft baseline is trained for 100 epochs while MED is reported at 20 epochs, which weakens the comparative claim. For Wiktionary, exact splits and preprocessing are not fully specified. No statistical significance testing or variance across runs/folds (except CELEX averaging) is reported. Key hyperparameters (hidden sizes, embeddings, feature encoding, optimizer settings, dropout/regularization, beam size) are deferred to supplementary; the main text lacks essential reproducibility details. There is an unclear mention of ensembling in the references, but no description in the methods.
- Analysis is largely anecdotal: The SVD plots provide intuition but no quantitative probes (e.g., linear classifiers predicting character identity/position from encodings). Alignment comparisons are based on a few instances; there is no corpus-level measure (e.g., Kendall’s tau between hard and soft alignments, monotonicity rate).
- Numerous presentation and formatting issues: Several typos and grammatical errors (e.g., “promote the attention pointer the next element”), missing or malformed references, stray footnote markers, and text noting that code links were removed for anonymity appear in the main body. Table/Figure cross-references are sometimes unclear. These detract from polish.

Questions to authors
- Decoding: Do you decode greedily or with a beam? What is the termination condition? How do you prevent stepping beyond x_n? Are there constraints tying the number of step actions to input length? Did you explore beam sizes and their effect on accuracy?
- Alignment dependency: How sensitive is performance to the alignment quality? Can you compare different aligners, or report results with a learned/joint alignment variant (even if weaker)? What proportion of training pairs violate strict monotonicity?
- Reproducibility: Please provide full hyperparameters per dataset (H, E, feature embedding sizes; optimizer, learning rates; dropout/regularization; training epochs; early stopping; beam size). For Wiktionary, what are the exact splits and preprocessing?
- Fair baselines: For SIGMORPHON, can you retrain MED (and your soft baseline) with the same training budget and model selection protocol (epochs, dev selection) to make comparisons fair? Provide variance or statistical tests across multiple runs/folds.
- Features: How are morpho-syntactic features represented across datasets, especially Wiktionary? Are features always available, and do they materially affect performance (ablation without features)?

Overall recommendation: Weak reject. The model is sensible and effective, especially in low-resource settings, but methodological novelty is limited, several experimental comparisons are potentially unfair or under-specified, there is no analysis of search/derivation issues, and the paper has many presentation flaws. With stronger ablations, clearer decoding details, fairer baselines, and cleaned-up writing, this could be a solid contribution.
