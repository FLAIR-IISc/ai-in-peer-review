Summary and contributions
This paper proposes a neural sequence transduction model for morphological inflection that enforces a hard, monotonic attention over the input, implemented as a single forward “pointer” with two actions: STEP (advance pointer) and WRITE(c) (emit a character). Alignments are obtained externally at the character level; these are deterministically converted to oracle action sequences used for cross-entropy training. The model uses a biLSTM encoder over characters, a recurrent decoder conditioned on the last output, and embeddings of morphological features.

Main contributions (as I see them):
1) A simple, effective hard-monotonic attention transducer trained from external alignments that matches or exceeds prior neural and non-neural SOTA across three benchmarks, with particularly strong gains in low-resource settings (CELEX).
2) A careful empirical study across datasets and morphological typologies, including error/behavioral analysis (alignment visualizations, encoder representation projections) that illuminates what the models learn.
3) A compelling demonstration that a strong inductive bias (monotonicity) plus supervised alignments can reduce overfitting and improve data efficiency versus soft attention.

Strengths
- Well-motivated inductive bias and simple training: The model directly encodes a widely valid monotonic assumption for inflection (especially suffixing with stem changes). Decoupling alignment (external, supervised) from transduction enables straightforward cross-entropy training, avoids expensive marginalization, and yields good generalization in low-resource settings. The transition-system view (STEP/WRITE) is clear and connects to edit-based transduction.

- Strong empirical results and coverage: The model is the first neural approach (to my knowledge) to surpass Dreyer et al.’s latent WFST on CELEX; it is competitive or SOTA on large Wiktionary subsets and performs best on suffixing+stem-change languages in SIGMORPHON. Evaluation spans diverse datasets, sizes, and morphological systems, supporting the claims.

- Interpretability and analysis: Attention trajectories align with linguistically plausible monotone alignments; the examples nicely illustrate deletion (multiple STEP), insertion (WRITE without STEP), and substitution. The SVD analysis of encoder states provides insight into how character identity and position are encoded, and the comparison to soft attention is informative.

- Practicality and reproducibility: The approach is simple to implement, avoids complex global objectives (e.g., RNN-T/forward-backward), and the paper promises code and uses a standard aligner. The method should be attractive for practitioners in low-resource settings.

Weaknesses
- Reliance on external alignments and best-derivation training: The pipeline assumes access to good character alignments and trains against a single oracle action sequence. There is no ablation quantifying sensitivity to alignment quality, nor a comparison of external vs learned alignments under the same model. The best-derivation approximation ignores multiple valid derivations and can bias training; alternatives (marginalization via forward-backward, dynamic oracles) are not explored.

- Limited support for non-monotonic morphology: The strictly forward-only pointer cannot handle true non-monotone mappings (e.g., productive prefixation, infixation, stem templatic reordering). The performance drop on languages with harmony/templatic processes is acknowledged. It would be valuable to propose or test minimal extensions (e.g., bounded backward moves, jump or copy actions) and quantify trade-offs.

- Comparative fairness and tuning: Some baselines (e.g., MED) are trained with different epochs/selection criteria; this makes the comparisons less clean. The paper notes training for 100 epochs and dev selection while MED trained for 20 epochs; similar budgets should be enforced or results normalized. Statistical significance is not reported.

- Decoding/search details and termination: It is unclear whether decoding uses greedy search or a beam; whether a beam helps; and how termination is handled (EOS emission vs end-of-input conditions). Without these details, it is hard to assess inference robustness (e.g., loops, over-generation, or early termination).

- Analysis mostly qualitative: The alignment and representation analyses are insightful but anecdotal. Quantitative metrics (e.g., attention monotonicity measures, attention entropy, edit-type confusion matrices, per-tag accuracies) would strengthen the claims about what the model learns and when it excels.

Questions to authors
- How sensitive is performance to alignment quality? Have you tried different aligners or injected noise into alignments to measure robustness? Can the model be fine-tuned without alignments after initial supervised training?

- What decoding strategy is used (greedy vs beam)? Does beam search improve exact-match accuracy, especially on difficult languages? What is the termination criterion and how do you avoid pathological action sequences at test time?

- Can the model WRITE before any STEP to handle prefixation naturally? If not, how does it cope with languages where an output prefix depends on later input characters?

- Did you try training the same hard-attention architecture with latent alignments (e.g., forward-backward over STEP/WRITE lattices or RNN-Transducer-like marginalization) to reduce reliance on a single oracle derivation?

- For SIGMORPHON, could you provide significance tests and the official macro/micro metrics used in the shared task? Also, per-feature or per-paradigm breakdowns to substantiate the typology-based explanation?

Additional comments
- Minor: references contain formatting issues and placeholders; please fix for camera-ready. Clarify hyperparameters (embedding sizes, hidden dimensions, dropout), whether ensembling or model selection was used, and how morphological feature embeddings are constructed. An ablation study (no feature embeddings; no previous-output embedding; no biLSTM) would be very informative.

Overall assessment
A solid, well-executed paper with a clear idea, strong empirical gains in low-resource settings, and good analysis. Despite the reliance on external alignments and some comparison/analysis gaps, I recommend acceptance.
