Summary and contributions
This paper proposes a neural sequence-to-sequence model for morphological inflection generation that uses hard, monotonic attention with an explicit control mechanism. The decoder alternates between WRITE(symbol) and STEP actions over a biLSTM-encoded input; a single attention pointer moves left-to-right and cannot move backward. Training does not learn alignments jointly: character-level alignments are obtained externally (Sudoh et al., 2013), from which oracle action sequences are deterministically derived and optimized with standard cross-entropy. The approach is evaluated on CELEX (low-resource), Wiktionary (high-resource), and SIGMORPHON 2016 (10 languages).

As I see it, the main contributions are:
- A simple and effective hard-monotonic attention architecture that reduces alignment learning to supervised action prediction, enabling stable training and strong performance in low-resource settings.
- Broad empirical evaluation showing: (i) new SOTA on CELEX, including surpassing Dreyer et al.’s latent-variable WFST; (ii) competitive or better average accuracy on Wiktionary; (iii) typology-aware results on SIGMORPHON, with strengths in suffixing+stem-change languages.
- Insightful analysis of learned alignments and encoder representations, comparing hard vs soft attention and revealing how position and character type are encoded.

Strengths
- Strong performance in low-resource settings. On CELEX, the model outperforms both neural baselines (soft attention, NWFST) and the classic latent-variable WFST (LAT), achieving the first reported neural result that beats LAT on average. The overfitting story is compelling: similar train accuracy but improved dev accuracy vs soft attention, consistent with the inductive bias of monotonic control.
- Conceptual clarity and simplicity. The model’s transition-system view (ADVANCE/WRITE) and the use of external alignments eliminate the need for marginalization over alignments (as in RNN-T/segmental models) and make training a straightforward cross-entropy problem. This is attractive for practitioners and easier to debug than joint alignment learning with latent variables.
- Empirical breadth and typology-aware interpretation. Results span three datasets, with a nuanced discussion linking performance differences to morphological system types (suffixing with stem changes vs harmony/templatic systems). This contextualization strengthens the claims about when hard monotonic attention is most appropriate.
- Meaningful qualitative analysis. The paper examines predicted alignments and encoder representations. The observation that both soft and hard encoders encode position and character identity, but with different strengths, is useful and supports the model’s design rationale (explicit positional control reduces the need to bake position into encodings).
- Efficiency implications. Although not quantified, the model’s decoding is linear-time with a single attention pointer and no soft attention computation, which is practically appealing, especially for deployment on character-level tasks.

Weaknesses
- Reliance on external alignments and lack of ablation. The central design choice—training on oracle actions induced from an external aligner—carries risks: bias from alignment errors, sensitivity to alignment quality, and domain dependence. The paper does not report ablations such as different aligners, noisy alignments, or a variant that learns alignments jointly (e.g., via latent marginalization or reinforcement learning). Without these, it is difficult to attribute gains to the control mechanism vs the availability of good supervision.
- Objective mismatch and decoding details under-specified. The paper optimizes the best-derivation approximation (no marginalization over derivations that yield the same surface form), but does not quantify its effect. Moreover, the decoding algorithm (greedy vs beam, beam width, tie-breaking) is not clearly specified; search errors could be non-negligible in cases with fertility > 1 or deletions. A small beam or marginalization could change results; this omission reduces reproducibility and interpretability.
- Limited handling of non-monotonic phenomena. The model enforces left-to-right monotonicity. While sufficient for suffixing and many stem alternations, it is theoretically constrained for phenomena requiring non-local dependencies (vowel/consonant harmony) or more flexible reordering. The SIGMORPHON results reflect this, with underperformance on harmony and templatic languages. The discussion acknowledges this but does not attempt to mitigate it (e.g., a few bounded backward moves, chunkwise monotonicity, or learned fertility models).
- Fairness and rigor of comparisons. For SIGMORPHON, the paper’s soft-attention baseline outperforms MED and the authors attribute this to training for more epochs. This suggests insufficient control of training budgets across systems and makes some SOTA claims less compelling. Across all datasets, statistical significance tests are not reported.
- Missing implementation details hamper reproducibility. Important hyperparameters and training choices are deferred to supplementary material and code (anonymized), but the main paper lacks details on optimizer (likely Adadelta), regularization, feature representation size, and whether models are trained per inflection type or jointly. The impact of morpho-syntactic feature embeddings is not ablated.

Questions to authors
- Decoding/search: Do you decode greedily or with beam search? If beam, what sizes were used per dataset? Did you observe search errors where different derivations map to the same surface form?
- Alignment sensitivity: How sensitive are results to the external alignment quality? Did you try alternative aligners or inject noise to assess robustness? Can your model recover from misalignments?
- Ablations: What is the impact of removing the external alignment (e.g., training with a latent or REINFORCE-based hard attention), or of training with softened/learned alignments? What is the contribution of the morpho-syntactic feature embeddings vs the control mechanism?
- Non-monotonic phenomena: Did you experiment with bounded backward moves or chunkwise monotonic attention to address harmony/templatic languages? If not, do you see systematic error types that such extensions would fix?
- Reproducibility and fairness: For SIGMORPHON and Wiktionary, can you report training budget parity (epochs, early stopping, hyperparameter searches) across baselines and significance tests for the reported improvements?

Overall assessment
This is a solid and timely contribution. The model is simple, well-motivated for morphological inflection, and empirically strong—especially in low-resource conditions—while offering useful analyses. The main concerns are the reliance on external alignments without ablations, under-specified decoding, and limited treatment of non-monotonic systems. With clarifications and minimal additional experiments (alignment sensitivity, decoding details, and perhaps a small beam/ablation), this would make a strong ACL paper. I lean toward acceptance.
