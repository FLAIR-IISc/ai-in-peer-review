Summary and main contributions
- A hard, nearly-monotonic attention encoder–decoder for character-level morphological inflection that operates with a minimal control vocabulary (WRITE(c) and STEP), trained on externally induced character alignments and optimized with cross-entropy over action sequences.
- Broad empirical evaluation on CELEX (low-resource), Wiktionary (high-resource), and SIGMORPHON 2016, with strong accuracy, especially in low-resource settings, and analyses comparing learned alignments/representations with a soft-attention model.
- Qualitative analysis suggesting soft attention tends to encode positional information more strongly, while the proposed hard-attention model uses the STEP mechanism to externalize position.

Strengths
- Strong empirical performance in low-resource: On CELEX, the model surpasses both neural and carefully engineered WFST baselines, addressing a well-known weakness of seq2seq in small-data regimes. The design (explicit STEP, pre-aligned supervision) plausibly regularizes alignment learning and reduces overfitting.
- Simple, interpretable control mechanism: Modeling with explicit STEP/WRITE aligns closely with edit/transduction operations found in morphology, giving clear oracle derivations from alignments and easy-to-debug behavior (e.g., handling deletions via consecutive STEPs, one-to-many via multiple WRITEs).
- Broad, multi-dataset evaluation: Results on CELEX, expanded Wiktionary, and SIGMORPHON 2016, with per-language breakdown and typological discussion, demonstrate robustness and provide useful nuance (e.g., suffixing+stem-change languages benefit more from hard monotonicity).
- Useful analyses: Side-by-side alignment visualizations and 2D projections of encoder states offer insight into what each model encodes (character identity vs. position), and suggest why hard attention may generalize better with few examples.

Weaknesses
- Limited novelty relative to prior transduction literature: The proposed action space (STEP/WRITE) and reliance on external alignments closely resemble Neural Transducer (Jaitly et al., 2015) and are in the family of RNN-T/CTC-style monotonic alignment models. The paper cites some related work but underplays the overlap and does not clearly articulate theoretical or algorithmic advances beyond task-specific adaptation and empirical validation.
- Reliance on external alignments and best-derivation approximation without sensitivity analysis: The model optimizes the best derivation induced from a single alignment and does not marginalize over derivations. There is no ablation on alignment quality, no comparison across alternative aligners/hyperparameters, and no measurement of how alignment noise affects performance. This is central to the claimed advantage yet under-evaluated.
- Incomplete and sometimes unclear methodological details:
  - Decoding is underspecified: Is inference greedy or beam search? What is the termination condition (EOS action vs. consuming input)? How are sequences constrained to be valid (e.g., preventing over-stepping)? How is length controlled?
  - Action vocabulary omits an EOS; Σs = Σy ∪ {step} suggests no explicit termination symbol. This ambiguity risks mismatch between training and inference.
  - Feature embeddings: The composition of f (feature inventory, embedding sizes, treatment across datasets) is not described; for CELEX, are features actually used?
  - Hyperparameters/regularization: core choices (H, E, dropout, clipping, optimizer config, early stopping) are deferred to supplementary material; absent here and critical for reproducibility and fairness.
  - Preprocessing/splits: For Wiktionary, differences among prior works’ data cleaning/splits are nontrivial; the paper does not specify its exact preprocessing or confirm split comparability.
- Comparisons and fairness:
  - For SIGMORPHON, the paper attributes differences with MED partly to training epoch choices and training set composition, but then compares to its own stronger soft-attention implementation trained differently. A controlled re-run of MED (or a single consistent soft-attention baseline under identical regimes) is needed for a fair comparison.
  - No statistical significance testing or confidence intervals; several improvements are small (often <0.5%), making conclusions fragile.
  - No ablations: e.g., training the proposed decoder without external alignments (learned hard attention) or with soft attention but oracle alignments, to isolate where the gain comes from.
- Coverage of non-monotonic phenomena is limited: While the paper notes underperformance on languages with harmony/templatic processes, there is no attempt to extend the action space (e.g., learned jumps, multi-source conditioning) or hybridize with soft attention to address these settings, nor an error analysis to categorize failure modes.
- Presentation and formatting issues that hinder readability and reproducibility:
  - Numerous citation formatting errors, stray footnote markers, broken references, and inconsistent bib entries (e.g., “Ahlberg et al.,?”, “\Q2016\E”).
  - Figures/tables are referenced but not always clearly legible in-text; some captions and footnotes are interleaved awkwardly.
  - Code “available on github” but link removed; without detailed hyperparameters/procedures in the main paper, reproducibility is weak under double-blind constraints.

Questions to authors
- Decoding: Please specify the exact inference procedure (greedy vs. beam width, termination, EOS handling, constraints) and its effect on accuracy and speed. Provide runtime and memory comparisons to soft attention.
- Alignment sensitivity: How does performance vary with different aligners or with noise injected into alignments? Have you tried marginalizing over a small n-best alignment set?
- Feature embeddings: What feature sets are used per dataset, how are they embedded/concatenated, and what are their dimensions? Are CELEX experiments feature-free?
- Validity constraints: How do you prevent invalid sequences (e.g., too many STEPs) at inference? Do you mask STEP at the end of input or enforce consumption constraints?
- Fairness: For Wiktionary and SIGMORPHON, can you provide exact preprocessing and splits, and re-run baselines under identical training schedules? Report statistical tests.
- Ablations: What is the contribution of external alignments vs. the hard attention mechanism per se? Can your decoder trained without external alignments recover similar performance?

Recommendation
While the approach is sensible and empirically strong in low-resource settings, the methodological novelty is limited and the paper suffers from numerous methodological and presentation gaps that prevent confident assessment and reproducibility. I recommend rejection in the current form; with clearer positioning relative to prior transducer models, strengthened experimental rigor (fair baselines, significance, ablations, decoding details), and improved presentation, this work could be a solid contribution. Confidence: high.
