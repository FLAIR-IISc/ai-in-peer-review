Summary
The paper proposes a neural encoder-decoder model for morphological inflection generation that uses a hard, monotonic attention mechanism. The decoder operates over an action space consisting of character outputs plus a step action that advances an attention pointer over a biLSTM-encoded input. Training leverages externally induced character alignments to derive oracle action sequences; the model is trained with standard cross-entropy on these sequences, using a best-derivation approximation rather than marginalizing over derivations. Experiments on CELEX (low-resource), Wiktionary (large), and SIGMORPHON 2016 (medium, typologically diverse) show strong performance, especially in low-resource settings. Analyses compare learned alignments and encoder representations against a soft-attention baseline.

Contributions (as assessed)
- A simple hard monotonic attention model for nearly-monotonic character transduction trained on externally induced alignments, avoiding joint alignment-learning and complex marginalization. This includes a clear step/write control mechanism and a practical training recipe.
- Empirical evidence that this approach is robust in low-resource regimes, outperforming prior neural and non-neural methods on CELEX, and competitive or superior on several Wiktionary tasks. Language-typology-aware results on SIGMORPHON highlight when monotonic hard attention helps.
- Analysis comparing hard vs. soft attention in terms of alignments and encoder representations, providing insights into how positional and character-type information is encoded.

Strengths
- Strong low-resource performance and practical significance: On CELEX, the model surpasses both neural baselines and the specialized latent-variable WFST (LAT), which prior neural models failed to beat. This supports the claim that leveraging monotonicity and external alignments curbs overfitting and reduces data requirements.
- Simple, efficient training objective: By converting alignment supervision into action sequences and using cross-entropy, the method avoids forward–backward over alignments as in RNN-T/segmental models. This improves ease of implementation and potentially training stability, with competitive results against alignment-marginalizing methods (e.g., Yu et al., 2016).
- Clear architectural design suited to the task: The step action naturally covers insertions (multiple writes before step) and deletions (step without write), and the pointer over a biRNN-encoded input captures local and some non-local dependencies while preserving monotonicity. The feature embedding integration is straightforward.
- Empirical breadth and careful comparisons: Experiments cover three commonly used benchmarks with appropriate baselines (soft attention MED, encoder-decoder without attention, WFST variants). Results are broken down by language and typology on SIGMORPHON, with a principled interpretation of when monotonicity helps (suffixing + stem changes).
- Insightful qualitative analysis: Alignment visualizations show both models learn near-monotonic alignments; SVD projections suggest hard attention relies less on encoding positional information in representations (consistent with explicit pointer control), lending a plausible explanation for better generalization with less data.

Weaknesses
- Novelty is incremental relative to prior monotonic/segmental transduction work: The step/write control with a pointer is closely related to RNN Transducer, Neural Transducer, and online segment-to-segment models. The primary novelty lies in using external alignments and a simpler training objective; algorithmic contributions beyond this are limited.
- Objective mismatch and decoding details under-specified: The paper acknowledges optimizing best derivations rather than sequences (no marginalization), but does not quantify its impact or provide details about inference (e.g., beam size, stopping criterion, handling of EOS). This leaves questions about search errors and robustness at test time.
- Reliance on external alignments without ablations: While results against Yu et al. (2016) suggest benefits, the paper lacks ablations showing sensitivity to alignment quality or comparing different aligners. It is unclear how performance degrades with noisy alignments, or how much of the gain is attributable to the external supervision.
- Mixed results on SIGMORPHON and scope of SOTA claims: The hard attention model is not the strongest average performer on SIGMORPHON (soft attention slightly better overall), excelling mainly in suffixing+stem-change languages. Claims of establishing a new state of the art “on three datasets” are stronger than the evidence for SIGMORPHON overall.
- Limited analysis of computational aspects: The paper does not report training/inference speed, memory, or latency comparisons against soft attention or WFST baselines. Given the simplicity of the control mechanism, such measurements would strengthen the practical case.

Questions to Authors
- Decoding: What is the exact inference procedure (greedy vs. beam, beam width)? How is termination handled (EOS symbol, pointer reaching end of input with no more writes, or both)? Any constraints to prevent degenerate loops?
- Alignment supervision: How sensitive is the model to alignment quality? Have you tried alternate aligners or ablations with perturbed/noisy alignments to quantify robustness?
- Derivation vs. sequence objective: Did you evaluate beam/marginalization approximations or ensembling to mitigate derivation search errors? Any empirical evidence that different derivations mapping to the same output cause errors?
- Non-monotonic phenomena: For languages with more non-local dependencies (e.g., harmony, templatic morphology), did you try augmentations (e.g., limited backward steps, skip actions, or a small soft-attention window) to reduce the gap to soft attention?
- Efficiency: Can you report training/inference speed compared to soft attention and WFST-based systems on the same hardware?

Overall, the paper presents a clear, well-motivated hard attention model with strong low-resource performance and solid empirical validation. The main limitations concern incremental novelty relative to prior monotonic transduction work, under-specified inference, and lack of ablations on alignment supervision. The work remains a useful contribution for morphological inflection and related monotonic transduction tasks.
