Summary and contributions
- The paper proposes a neural encoder–decoder with a hard, monotonic attention mechanism for morphological inflection. Decoding is formulated as a sequence of actions over an input-pointer: step (advance pointer) or write (emit a character). Training uses externally induced character-level alignments to derive oracle action sequences, enabling simple cross-entropy training.
- Extensive evaluation on CELEX (low-resource), Wiktionary (larger-scale), and SIGMORPHON 2016 (10 languages) shows strong performance, notably clear gains in low-resource settings and competitive or state-of-the-art results in several high-resource settings.
- Analysis compares the learned alignments and encoder representations of the hard vs. soft attention models, arguing that hard attention better exploits monotonicity, reduces overfitting, and encodes position information differently.

As I see it, main contributions (in order):
1) A simple, effective hard monotonic attention transducer trained from external alignments that achieves strong low-resource performance and competitive high-resource results.
2) A thorough empirical study across multiple benchmarks with comparisons to strong neural and non-neural baselines.
3) Qualitative analysis of alignments and encoder representations illuminating why the model generalizes better in low-resource, monotonic settings.

Strengths
- Strong empirical gains in low-resource. On CELEX, the proposed model outperforms prior neural approaches and surpasses Dreyer et al.’s latent-variable WFST tailored to morphology (Table 1 and learning curves), addressing a known weakness of soft-attention seq2seq under data scarcity. The learning-curve evidence supports the claim of reduced overfitting.
- Well-matched inductive bias. The hard, monotonic pointer fits the predominantly monotonic nature of inflection, yet still allows insertions/deletions and many-to-one/one-to-many via step/write sequences. The transition-system framing is elegant and practical.
- Competitive and often state-of-the-art on larger datasets. On Wiktionary, the method achieves the best average and leads or ties on several language/pos subsets (Table 2), comparing favorably to encoder–decoder without attention and other align-and-transduce systems.
- Clear analysis and insight. The alignment visualizations show both models learn near-monotone alignments, while the SVD plots suggest different encoding of character identity and position: soft attention encodes position more strongly, whereas the hard model externalizes positional control via step. This provides a plausible explanation for generalization differences.
- Simplicity and training stability. By using external alignments and cross-entropy on oracle actions, the model avoids complex marginalization (as in RNN-T/Neural Transducer/S2SNT variants) and is easy to train, yet remains competitive.

Weaknesses
- Dependence on external alignments without ablations. The approach hinges on alignment quality, but there is no sensitivity analysis: e.g., how performance varies with different aligners, noisy alignments, or when jointly learning alignments. A comparison to learned-alignments variants (e.g., Yu et al., RNN-T-like) under matched conditions would strengthen the case for decoupling.
- Baseline parity and significance. Some baseline training choices may not be strictly comparable (e.g., MED trained for fewer epochs; the paper attributes performance gaps partly to training regime). Statistical significance tests across languages/conditions are not reported. A carefully tuned soft-attention baseline under identical optimization/search settings would clarify relative merit.
- Objective/search approximation and decoding details. The model optimizes “best derivation” rather than marginalizing over derivations leading to the same string; the practical impact is asserted but not quantified. Inference details (beam size vs. greedy, EOS handling, termination when pointer reaches end, runtime) are underspecified; search errors vs. model errors are not analyzed.
- Limited handling of non-monotonic phenomena. As acknowledged by results on languages with harmony/templatic morphology (Table 3), the hard monotonic constraint can be limiting. The paper would benefit from trying modest relaxations (e.g., small backward jumps, skip/jump actions, or hybrid soft+hard attention) and reporting their effect.
- Evaluation comparability and scope. For Wiktionary, results are compared to previously reported numbers across different codebases; it is unclear whether preprocessing/tokenization or tag normalization differences affect comparability. Error analysis is largely qualitative; more quantitative breakdowns (e.g., per-feature type, stem-change vs. affix-only cases) would add depth.

Questions to authors
- How sensitive is the model to the choice and quality of external alignments? Did you try multiple aligners or injecting noise into alignments? Can you report performance when using alignments learned on reduced training sets?
- What decoding strategy do you use (greedy vs. beam)? How do you terminate generation (EOS action, pointer at end with no more writes)? Any length-control heuristics? Please report average decoding time and beam if used.
- Are the differences vs. strong soft-attention baselines statistically significant on CELEX, Wiktionary averages, and individual SIGMORPHON languages?
- Can you provide ablations: removing previous-output embedding si−1, removing feature embeddings f, or replacing the biLSTM encoder with a unidirectional one, to quantify which components matter most?
- Did you try simple relaxations of monotonicity (e.g., a limited jump-left action, or a coverage-regularized soft attention) on languages where hard attention underperforms? If so, how did they fare?
- For completeness, can you report the proportion of examples whose induced oracle action sequences violate strict monotonicity assumptions (e.g., cases requiring non-local dependencies), and the error categories where the model fails?
