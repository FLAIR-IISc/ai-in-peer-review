Summary and contributions
This paper proposes a sequence-to-sequence model with hard monotonic attention for morphological inflection generation. The decoder emits either a character or a step action that advances a single attention pointer over a biLSTM-encoded input. Training relies on external character-level alignments to derive oracle action sequences, enabling simple cross-entropy training. The model targets nearly monotonic alignments common in suffixing morphology and conditions on morpho-syntactic features.

As I see them, the main contributions (strongest first) are:
1) A simple, effective hard-attention encoder-decoder tailored to nearly monotonic transductions, trained from independently learned character alignments and using a step/write action space. This reduces overfitting in low-resource settings and yields focused representations.
2) Strong empirical results: new or tied SOTA on CELEX (notably surpassing Dreyer et al.’s latent WFST in low-resource), competitive or superior averages on Wiktionary, and favorable performance on suffixing+stem-change languages in SIGMORPHON 2016.
3) Analysis comparing hard vs soft attention, including qualitative alignment inspection and projection of encoder representations, offering insight into how each model encodes character identity and positional information.

Strengths
- Clear modeling of monotonicity with a minimal, interpretable control mechanism. The step/write action abstraction is well-matched to the task and leads to simpler training (no forward-backward or latent alignment learning), yet still conditions on full input context via a biLSTM. This design choice is convincingly motivated by the morphology of many target languages.

- Strong low-resource performance and robustness. On CELEX, the model not only outperforms recent neural baselines (MED, NWFST) but also surpasses Latent WFST (LAT), historically strong in this regime. The learning curve comparison supports the claim that hard attention regularizes against overfitting compared to soft attention.

- Competitive large-scale results across datasets. On Wiktionary, the approach achieves the top average and is best on several language/inflection subsets, outperforming both non-neural align-and-transduce systems and neural baselines without attention or with joint alignment-transduction. This breadth supports generality beyond the low-resource case.

- Insightful qualitative analysis. The alignment visualizations illustrate that soft attention also learns monotone behavior, justifying the monotonicity assumption, while the hard model performs deletions and one-to-many mappings with intuitive action patterns. The representation analysis suggests complementary encoding strategies: soft attention encodes position strongly, whereas hard attention externalizes position via step actions, possibly aiding data efficiency.

- Practical training procedure. Using an external aligner to derive oracle actions simplifies optimization (standard cross-entropy, teacher forcing), likely improving stability and reproducibility, with code availability mentioned.

Weaknesses
- Reliance on a single external aligner and best-derivation training. The model assumes gold-like alignments from Sudoh et al. (2013) and optimizes the best derivation rather than marginalizing over alignments/derivations. This raises concerns about sensitivity to alignment errors and training-test mismatch. No ablation is provided on alignment quality, alternate aligners, or robustness to noisy alignments. The paper acknowledges the best-derivation approximation but does not quantify its impact.

- Limited breadth of ablations and fairness controls. The comparison to MED/soft attention on SIGMORPHON highlights training protocol differences (number of epochs, dev usage), which could confound conclusions. More systematic ablations (e.g., same training schedule/early stopping, beam vs greedy decoding, feature ablations, encoder size, without external alignment, or with learned alignments) would strengthen causal claims.

- Not universally superior; monotonicity limits. On typologically challenging languages (vowel/consonant harmony, templatic), hard attention underperforms soft attention, suggesting the approach is not broadly state of the art. The paper frames this as typology-driven, but does not explore extensions (e.g., limited jumps, learned skip actions, locality-aware soft attention) that might mitigate the limitation while preserving the low-resource benefits.

- Evaluation scope and statistical rigor. Results are reported as exact-match accuracy; complementary metrics (character error rate/edit distance) and statistical significance tests, especially on CELEX across folds and on Wiktionary subsets, are missing. Error typology analysis beyond a few qualitative examples would clarify where the method helps or fails.

- Decoding and efficiency details are sparse. It is unclear whether decoding uses greedy or beam search, and whether search errors affect sequence vs derivation optimality. Runtime/training efficiency versus baselines (especially forward-backward models) and memory footprint are not reported.

Questions to authors
- Alignment sensitivity: How sensitive is performance to the external aligner choice and its hyperparameters? Have you tried different aligners or adding noise to the oracle actions? Can the model be trained with multiple alignments per pair (e.g., n-best) to reduce oracle brittleness?

- Derivation vs sequence: Do different derivations yield the same output during decoding? If so, did you consider marginalizing or aggregating derivations at decoding time (e.g., cube-pruning over actions) to better approximate Eq. (1)? What decoding strategy do you use (greedy/beam), and what beam sizes affect accuracy?

- Non-monotonic phenomena: Could you extend the action set to allow limited backward moves or jumps, and would that close the gap on languages with harmony or templatic morphology? Any preliminary results?

- Fairness controls: On SIGMORPHON, can you report results with identical training protocols for soft and hard models (epochs, early stopping, dev usage) to isolate the effect of attention type?

- Ablations: What is the impact of removing morpho-syntactic feature embeddings, or of replacing the biLSTM encoder with a unidirectional encoder? How critical is the explicit feedback of the previous output embedding?

- Efficiency: Can you report training/inference speeds and model sizes compared to soft attention and NWFST baselines?

Overall, this is a well-motivated and practically effective approach with clear benefits in low-resource settings and competitive performance more broadly. Addressing alignment sensitivity, adding stronger ablations and significance testing, and exploring limited non-monotonic extensions would substantially strengthen the paper.
