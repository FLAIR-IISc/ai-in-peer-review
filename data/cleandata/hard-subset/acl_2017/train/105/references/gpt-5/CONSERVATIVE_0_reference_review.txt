Summary and claimed contributions
This paper proposes a supervised hard monotonic attention model for morphological inflection generation. The decoder produces a sequence of WRITE(symbol) and STEP actions over a biLSTM-encoded input, guided during training by externally computed character alignments. The approach exploits near-monotonicity common in inflection, trains with simple cross-entropy on action sequences, and conditions on full input and output history. The authors evaluate on CELEX, Wiktionary, and SIGMORPHON16 and include qualitative analyses comparing learned alignments and representations to soft attention.

As I see it, the main contributions are:
- A task-aligned, supervised hard attention encoder–decoder with STEP/WRITE actions trained from external character alignments; this simplifies training relative to joint alignment-learning and matches the structural bias of inflection.
- An empirical study showing strong gains in low-resource settings (CELEX), competitive performance on larger datasets, and a typological breakdown showing when hard monotonicity helps vs. hurts.
- Qualitative analysis of attention/alignment and encoder representations contrasting hard vs. soft attention.

Strengths
- Well-motivated inductive bias: The monotonic STEP/WRITE control mechanism is a natural fit for suffixing and stem-change inflection and avoids the capacity/overfitting issues of generic soft attention on tiny datasets. The CELEX results (Table 1) are compelling, surpassing strong FST baselines (LAT) and prior neural models, addressing a known weakness of seq2seq models in low data.
- Simple, stable training: Decoupling alignment and transduction enables straightforward cross-entropy training without latent alignment marginalization or REINFORCE. This is likely a practical advantage compared to RNN-Transducer/OSNMT-style methods and is supported by the empirical robustness and learning curves (Figure 2).
- Broad evaluation with relevant baselines: The paper evaluates on three standard benchmarks, includes both neural and non-neural baselines, and reports per-language results. The Wiktionary averages are competitive (Table 2), and the typological split in SIGMORPHON16 (Table 3) is informative.
- Insightful qualitative analysis: The alignment visualizations and SVD projections give useful intuition about when and why hard vs. soft attention behaves differently and how position/type information is encoded. This adds value beyond raw accuracy tables.
- Clarity and implementability: The model is clearly described, the objective is explicit (best-derivation), and the use of feature embeddings is standard. Code availability (albeit anonymized) is noted.

Weaknesses
- Limited methodological novelty: Hard monotonic attention with explicit STEP/WRITE closely resembles established frameworks (RNN-Transducer with blank, neural transducer, and online segment-to-segment models). The primary difference is supervising with external alignments and not marginalizing alignments. This is an engineering choice rather than a conceptual advance; the paper would benefit from a sharper positioning and more rigorous comparison to these established methods beyond a brief Related Work mention.
- Reliance on external alignment and best-derivation approximation: Training depends on a specific aligner (Sudoh et al., CRP-based) and uses a best-derivation objective, not marginalizing over alternative derivations that yield the same surface form (acknowledged in §2.2). There is no sensitivity analysis to alignment quality or choice of aligner, and no ablation comparing to joint training or to noisy alignments. This weakens the generality of the approach and makes performance contingent on an external tool.
- Mixed results and over-claims: On SIGMORPHON16, the hard attention model underperforms both the authors’ own soft attention baseline and MED on average (Table 3), with the advantage limited to suffixing+stem-change languages. Claims of “new state of the art” should be more precisely scoped; as written, the abstract/conclusion overgeneralize. Similarly, the gains on Wiktionary are modest and not universal (Table 2).
- Fairness and reporting details: Some comparisons are hard to assess. The paper notes MED was trained for only 20 epochs while the authors’ soft model trained for 100; it is unclear whether the reported MED numbers are the strongest possible under similar training budgets. The text alludes to “an ensembling technique” without specifying where/when it was used. Decoding details (greedy vs. beam, length penalties) are not stated. No statistical significance tests or variance across folds/languages are reported.
- Missing ablations and diagnostics: The paper lacks ablations for key modeling choices (biLSTM vs. uniLSTM encoder, effect of removing explicit previous-output embedding, impact of feature embeddings, beam width, training without alignment supervision, or with imperfect alignments). Error analysis is qualitative; a quantitative breakdown by edit type (copy, substitution, insertion/deletion, nonlocal changes) would strengthen the case for monotonicity.

Questions to authors
- Decoding: Do you use greedy decoding for action sequences, or beam search? If beam, what width, and how sensitive are results? Any length-normalization or constraints?
- Ensembling: Did you ensemble models for any reported numbers? If so, which experiments and how many seeds? Please clarify.
- Alignment sensitivity: How sensitive is performance to the aligner and its hyperparameters? Have you tried alternative character aligners or introduced noise to the gold action sequences?
- Objective choice: Did you attempt marginalization over derivations (e.g., forward-backward over action lattices) or minimum risk training? If so, how did they compare? If not, why is best-derivation sufficient?
- Fairness of baselines: For MED and other neural baselines, did you re-train with comparable training schedules and model capacities, or do you report published numbers trained under different regimes? Can you provide a like-for-like reimplementation comparison?
- Error types and typology: Can you provide a quantitative error taxonomy (by edit type) across languages to substantiate the typological explanation for SIGMORPHON16 differences?

Overall, this is a solid, task-aligned engineering contribution with convincing low-resource gains and careful qualitative analysis, but with limited novelty relative to established hard/monotonic transduction frameworks and some missing experimental controls. I would be inclined toward acceptance if the authors can tighten claims, clarify evaluation/decoding details, and add sensitivity/ablation results; otherwise, it falls short of a clear methodological advance.
