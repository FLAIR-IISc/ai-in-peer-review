- Proposes a hard, monotonic attention encoder–decoder that predicts WRITE(c)/STEP actions and trains with externally induced character alignments via cross-entropy on a best-derivation approximation.  
- Shows strong empirical gains in very low-resource morphological inflection (CELEX) over soft-attention seq2seq and WFST baselines, competitive on large Wiktionary sets, and mixed but analyzed results on SIGMORPHON 2016.  
- Analyzes learned alignments and encoder representations to show differences between hard and soft attention, including positional encoding and when monotonic control helps.

- Architectural bias toward monotonic alignments yields consistent dev-set improvements and mitigates overfitting in low-resource settings.  
- Uses a CRP-based external aligner to derive action sequences, enabling simple, stable cross-entropy training instead of joint latent-alignment marginalization.  
- Broad, strong evaluation across CELEX, multiple Wiktionary settings, and 10 SIGMORPHON languages with comparisons to strong baselines.  
- Qualitative and representational analyses validate monotonic assumptions and show how hard vs. soft attention distribute character/positional information.  
- Clear writing, well-specified action system, code availability, and reproducibility aided by use of a public aligner.

- Methodological novelty is incremental relative to prior hard/monotonic transduction and resembles RNN-T/Neural Transducer-style models.  
- Paper overstates SOTA claims: strong on CELEX and some Wiktionary settings but underperforms soft attention on average for SIGMORPHON; applicability varies by morphophonological processes.  
- Heavy reliance on external alignments without ablation or sensitivity analysis to alignment quality or alternative aligners.  
- Training on a single best derivation (best-derivation approximation) is not analyzed for bias or frequency/impact of alternative derivations.  
- Missing reported efficiency metrics (wall-clock speed, memory) and some implementation details are relegated to the supplement, affecting reproducibility and fairness of comparisons.

- How sensitive are results to alignment quality; can alternative aligners or noisy alignments be reported?  
- What decoding strategy is used (greedy vs. beam), what beam size, and how does this affect accuracy and speed or search errors?  
- Please report training/inference speed and memory comparisons versus soft attention on CELEX and a Wiktionary setting.  
- Could limited marginalization over derivations (e.g., runs of WRITEs) be tractable and did it help if attempted?  
- For languages where hard attention lags, have small relaxations (look-back/jump actions, hybrid soft attention) been tried?

- Overall: solid, well-executed paper with valuable empirical and analytical contributions for morphology; recommend acceptance with toned-down SOTA claims and added clarity on efficiency and alignment sensitivity.
