Summary and perceived contributions
This paper proposes a sequence-to-sequence model for morphological inflection that uses a hard, monotonic attention mechanism with a simple control policy: at each step, the model either WRITEs an output symbol or STEPs the attention pointer forward over a biLSTM-encoded input. Training relies on externally computed character alignments to deterministically derive gold action sequences, enabling standard cross-entropy training rather than joint alignment/transduction learning. The method is evaluated on CELEX (small data), Wiktionary (large data), and SIGMORPHON 2016 (cross-linguistic), achieving strong or state-of-the-art results, particularly in low-resource settings. The paper also analyzes learned alignments and encoder representations, contrasting hard and soft attention.

Main contributions (as I see them), in order of significance:
1) A simple, effective hard monotonic attention model trained from externally derived alignments, that is data-efficient and competitive with (or better than) strong neural and non-neural baselines across diverse datasets.
2) Strong empirical evidence that explicitly modeling (near-)monotonic edit processes substantially improves generalization in low-resource settings, surpassing a tailored WFST latent-variable model on CELEX.
3) Analysis clarifying how hard and soft attention learn alignments and positional/character information, offering insights into why hard attention reduces overfitting in this task.

Strengths
1) Data efficiency and robustness: The model achieves clear gains in low-resource (CELEX) regimes, where neural seq2seq often overfits. Learning curves and average accuracies support the claim that enforcing a near-monotonic prior via hard attention improves generalization. This is practically important for many low-resource languages.
2) Simplicity and training stability: Decoupling alignment estimation from transduction allows straightforward cross-entropy training and removes the need for forward-backward marginalization. The action space (WRITE/STEP) is intuitive, interpretable, and aligns well with edit operations (insertions, deletions, one-to-many mappings).
3) Broad, competitive evaluation: Results span small, medium, and large datasets, with consistent improvements over several strong baselines (including soft attention) on multiple language families and morphological systems. The method is not only competitive at scale but shines especially with small data.
4) Insightful analysis and diagnostics: The qualitative alignment visualizations and representation probes (SVD plots) convincingly show that both models learn monotonic alignments, but the hard-attention encoder relies less on positional encoding—consistent with STEP actions providing explicit position control. This supports the modeling choice and sheds light on what the networks learn.
5) Clear connection to linguistic priors: The paper relates performance differences by typology (e.g., suffixing with stem changes vs. templatic or harmony-heavy systems), appropriately qualifying the method’s strengths and limitations.

Weaknesses
1) Dependence on external alignments and best-derivation approximation: The model’s training quality relies on an external aligner (Sudoh et al., 2013). There is no sensitivity analysis to alignment errors, nor an ablation comparing to learned alignments or different aligners. Additionally, the objective optimizes the best derivation rather than marginalizing over derivations leading to the same output. While effective empirically, the implications and potential failure modes of this approximation are not examined.
2) Limited handling of non-monotonic phenomena: The pointer advances only forward. For languages with substantial non-concatenative morphology (e.g., templatic, circumfixing with reordering, harmony with long-range constraints), performance lags behind a strong soft-attention baseline. The paper acknowledges this but does not explore extensions (e.g., limited backtracking, chunkwise monotonic attention, or enriched edit operations) that might close the gap.
3) Fairness and completeness of baseline comparisons: Some comparisons are confounded by different training protocols (e.g., soft-attention variants trained for different numbers of epochs; MED trained for 20 epochs). There is no statistical significance testing, and not all relevant monotonic-alignment neural baselines (e.g., RNN-Transducer, CTC-like approaches, monotonic chunkwise attention) are included as controlled reimplementations.
4) Missing experimental details and ablations: Decoding strategy (greedy vs beam), termination conditions, constraints to prevent pointer overrun, and hyperparameter summaries are not in the main paper (left to supplement). Key ablations are missing: effect of alignment quality; impact of the feature embeddings; importance of feeding back the last action/character; training without external alignment (joint learning); or marginalization vs best-derivation. These would strengthen causal claims.
5) Limited quantitative error analysis: Beyond a few illustrative examples, the paper could provide more systematic error typology across languages (e.g., insertions vs deletions vs substitutions; stem-change vs affix errors), to better characterize where hard attention helps or hurts.

Questions to authors
- Alignment dependence: How sensitive is performance to the choice/quality of the external aligner? Have you tried alternative aligners or perturbations to assess robustness?
- Decoding: Do you use greedy decoding or a beam? If beam, what size? Does beam search improve over greedy for CELEX or SIGMORPHON?
- Derivation vs sequence objective: Did you attempt marginalization over derivations (e.g., via dynamic programming) on small datasets to gauge the gap relative to best-derivation training?
- Non-monotonicity: Can your controller support limited back-steps or chunkwise monotonicity (e.g., MoChA-like) without sacrificing data efficiency? Any preliminary experiments?
- Features: How are morpho-syntactic features represented and combined (e.g., per-tag embeddings concatenated)? Did you test ablations on feature encoding?
- Runtime/params: How does training time and model size compare to soft attention? Are the gains on CELEX attributable in part to fewer parameters or stronger inductive bias?

Overall recommendation
This is a strong and impactful paper. It offers a simple, well-motivated hard-attention approach that is both empirically effective—especially in low-resource settings—and conceptually clarifying for nearly monotonic transductions. While the reliance on external alignments and some missing ablations temper the theoretical completeness, the practical gains and analyses are compelling. I recommend acceptance, and encourage the authors to: (a) add a sensitivity/ablation study on alignment quality and decoding; (b) clarify decoding and training details; and (c) discuss or explore extensions for moderately non-monotonic phenomena.
