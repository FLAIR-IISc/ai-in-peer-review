Summary and contributions
This paper proposes a neural encoder–decoder model for morphological inflection generation that uses a hard, monotonic attention mechanism driven by “step” and “write” actions. Rather than jointly learning alignments, the model is trained on oracle action sequences derived from independently computed character-level alignments. The approach aims to exploit the near-monotonicity of inflectional transformations, particularly in suffixing languages, to improve generalization in low-data regimes and to simplify training compared to soft attention.

As I see it, the main contributions are:
1) A hard, monotonic pointer-based attention model trained from external alignments, with a simple cross-entropy objective over action sequences (best-derivation approximation). This is a clear and practical alternative to soft attention and to marginalization-based models like RNN-T or segmental models.
2) Strong empirical results across three benchmarks. The model surpasses prior neural and non-neural systems on CELEX (low-resource), achieves the best average results on the Wiktionary suite, and is competitive on SIGMORPHON 2016, especially on suffixing+stem-change languages.
3) Analysis of learned alignments and encoder representations, offering insight into why the model generalizes better with scarce data and how it trades off positional encoding between encoder and control mechanism.

Strengths
- Well-motivated modeling choice for the task: The hard monotonic attention matches the typical alignment structure in inflection generation (especially suffixation and local stem changes). The model operationalizes this via a minimal control set (step/write), which is interpretable and aligns with morphophonological processes (e.g., one-to-many substitutions, deletions via consecutive step actions). This yields a compact inductive bias that seems crucial in low-resource settings.

- Strong low-resource performance and robustness: On CELEX, the approach substantially outperforms soft-attention encoder–decoders and even the tailored latent-variable WFST model. The learning curve analysis convincingly shows reduced overfitting versus soft attention, supporting the claim that explicit monotonic control provides regularization benefits.

- Competitive high-resource results: On the Wiktionary datasets, the method is consistently strong, often best. This indicates that the inductive bias does not hurt when more data is available and that the approach is robust across languages and inflection classes.

- Clear comparison to related neural transduction paradigms: The paper situates the model relative to RNN Transducer, online segment-to-segment, and neural WFST approaches. The decision to train on external alignments simplifies optimization (simple cross-entropy, no forward-backward) while yielding better accuracy than models that marginalize alignments.

- Informative qualitative analysis: The alignment visualizations and SVD plots of encoder representations are insightful. They illustrate that both soft and hard attention discover near-monotone alignments, but the hard-attention encoder encodes character identity more cleanly while relying less on positional encoding, likely because position is handled explicitly via the control mechanism.

Weaknesses
- Dependence on external alignments and pipeline brittleness: The approach relies on a CRP-based aligner to produce oracle action sequences. Alignment errors or biases can propagate to the model, and there is no mechanism to correct them during training. The paper does not quantify sensitivity to the choice or quality of the aligner, nor compare to alternative aligners. An ablation (e.g., noisy or alternative alignments, or partial marginalization over multiple plausible alignments) would strengthen the claim that external alignment is beneficial.

- Objective mismatch and decoding details: The model optimizes a best-derivation approximation rather than marginalizing over derivations that yield the same output. While effective empirically, the impact of this approximation is not analyzed. Similarly, decoding strategy (greedy vs. beam) and constraints (e.g., handling of end-of-input pointer, guaranteeing termination) are not discussed in the main text. These details affect accuracy and reproducibility.

- Limited analysis on languages with non-local or non-monotonic phenomena: On SIGMORPHON languages with vowel/consonant harmony, templatic morphology, or circumfixation, performance lags a strong soft-attention baseline. While the typological explanation is plausible, the paper stops short of diagnosing failure modes or proposing mitigations (e.g., limited backtracking, localized non-monotonic jumps, or hybrid hard/soft mechanisms).

- Fairness and comparability issues in baselines: The paper notes differences in training regimes (e.g., MED trained for 20 epochs vs. 100 here) and that some baselines used proprietary classifiers. Although the authors provide their own soft-attention implementation, it would help to standardize training/early stopping across models where possible, report variance over seeds, and provide statistical significance tests.

- Missing ablations and capacity controls: The model concatenates all morpho-syntactic features into embeddings and combines them with the attended input and previous output embedding. There is no ablation of feature usage, embedding sizes, encoder/decoder depth, or the value of the feedback connection. Such ablations would clarify which components are essential.

Questions to authors
- How sensitive is performance to the external alignment quality? Have you tried alternative aligners or ensembling/marginalizing over multiple alignments during training? Can you report a study that injects noise into alignments to quantify robustness?

- What decoding strategy do you use at test time (greedy vs. beam)? Are there constraints to prevent pathological sequences (e.g., infinite writes without steps, pointer overruns)? How is termination handled?

- Can you provide ablations on the contribution of morpho-syntactic feature embeddings, previous-output feedback, and encoder/decoder sizes? Do these change the observed low-resource advantages?

- On SIGMORPHON languages with harmony/templatic morphology, can the model be augmented with limited non-monotonic moves (e.g., skip/backstep or local re-attend) without sacrificing low-resource generalization?

- Training comparability: For MED and your soft-attention baseline, if you match your own training protocol (epochs, early stopping), do the relative rankings on CELEX and SIGMORPHON change?

Overall assessment
This is a solid, well-executed paper with a clear idea, strong empirical results (notably in low-resource), and useful analysis. The reliance on external alignments is both a strength (simplified, stable training) and a potential weakness (pipeline sensitivity); addressing this with robustness experiments would further strengthen the contribution. I lean toward acceptance.
