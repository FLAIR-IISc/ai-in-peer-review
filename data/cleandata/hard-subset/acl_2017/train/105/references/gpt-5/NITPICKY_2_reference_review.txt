Summary and contributions
- The paper proposes a hard, monotonic attention encoder–decoder for morphological inflection that decouples alignment learning from transduction. The decoder predicts WRITE(c) or STEP actions over a bidirectional character encoder, using gold action sequences derived from externally learned character alignments.
- The model is evaluated on CELEX, Wiktionary, and SIGMORPHON-2016. It is especially strong in low-resource settings (CELEX) and competitive on Wiktionary; on SIGMORPHON, it performs best on suffixing+stem-change languages but trails a soft-attention baseline overall.
- The paper includes qualitative analyses of alignments and encoder representations, contrasting hard vs. soft attention.

Strengths
- Clear modeling of near-monotonic transductions: The step/write control with a single forward pointer is well matched to many inflection patterns and makes the model simple and efficient. The use of a biLSTM encoder preserves global context while the pointer enforces monotonicity.
- Strong low-resource performance: On CELEX, the model outperforms prior neural and non-neural baselines and appears to generalize better than soft attention, with a convincing learning-curve diagnosis of overfitting in soft attention. This is a practically relevant setting.
- Competitive results on larger datasets: On Wiktionary, the model achieves the best reported average among compared systems and improves on several language categories.
- Transparent training objective: Training on oracle action sequences (derived from external alignments) reduces learning complexity to cross-entropy, avoiding the instability of jointly learning alignments and transductions in low-data regimes.
- Informative qualitative analysis: The comparison of alignments and representation geometry offers insights (e.g., positional information being encoded more strongly under soft attention) that are useful to the community.

Weaknesses
- Limited novelty relative to existing monotonic transducers: The proposed framework closely resembles RNN Transducer/Neural Transducer–style models and edit-based transition systems with external or marginal alignments. The main difference is training on deterministic oracle action sequences from an external aligner. The methodological delta appears modest for ACL; novelty is primarily in the specific application and training recipe rather than in the core model.
- Overstated SOTA claims and mixed results: The abstract and contributions suggest “new state of the art on three datasets,” but on SIGMORPHON the hard model underperforms a soft-attention baseline on average (Table 3). Improvements on Wiktionary are small (e.g., +0.21 avg), and no significance testing is reported. Claims should be tempered or supported with statistical tests.
- Fairness and reproducibility concerns: Comparisons are not uniformly controlled. For SIGMORPHON, the authors trained their soft-attention baseline for 100 epochs while MED used 20, then attribute MED’s gap to training regimen, which undermines fairness. Hyperparameters, aligner settings, and decoding details are relegated to supplementary material (not provided here), and code is “available on GitHub” but redacted—leaving reproducibility uncertain for review.
- Objective/search mismatch and decoding details: The paper acknowledges optimizing best-derivation (Eq. 2) rather than best-sequence (Eq. 1) but does not quantify the impact. Decoding details are underspecified: Is decoding greedy or beam? How is termination handled? Are constraints enforced to prevent over- or under-stepping? Without this, it is hard to assess robustness and complexity.
- Heavy reliance on external alignment without ablation: The model’s advantage is attributed in part to using “correct alignments from the beginning,” yet there is no quantitative ablation comparing (i) external vs. learned alignments, (ii) quality/sensitivity of the aligner, or (iii) training with noisy alignments. This makes it difficult to attribute gains to the hard attention per se versus the oracle alignments.
- Presentation and formatting issues: Numerous reference formatting errors/placeholders (“?, Q2016”), typos (“promote the attention pointer the next element”), and inconsistent notation (E used for both input/output embedding dims) detract from clarity. Given the paper’s reliance on figures, exact decoding and model details should be self-contained.

Questions to authors
- Decoding: Do you use greedy or beam decoding over action sequences? Beam width? Termination criterion? Are there constraints to prevent stepping past the last encoder state or writing after EOS? What is the maximum number of consecutive WRITEs allowed per input position?
- Alignment sensitivity: How sensitive is performance to alignment errors? Please report results with alternative aligners or with injected noise into the oracle alignments. What proportion of training pairs are strictly monotone under your aligner?
- Ablations: (a) Hard model with jointly learned monotonic attention (no external oracle); (b) Same architecture but without feeding previous output embedding; (c) Feeding features f to encoder as well as decoder; (d) Removing biLSTM context. Which components are necessary?
- Statistical significance: Are the gains on CELEX and Wiktionary statistically significant per language and on average? Please include tests.
- Fair comparisons: For SIGMORPHON and Wiktionary, can you re-run baselines under the same training schedule/early stopping and report parameter counts and training budgets to ensure parity?

Recommendation
While the approach is sensible, effective in low-resource settings, and clearly analyzed, the novelty appears limited, core claims are partially overstated, fair-comparison and statistical rigor are lacking, and there are many presentation issues. I lean toward rejection in the current form; with tightened claims, stronger ablations, fairer baselines, and cleaner presentation, this could be a solid paper.
