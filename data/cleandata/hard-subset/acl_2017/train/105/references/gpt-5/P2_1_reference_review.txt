Summary and contributions (as I see them)
- A hard, monotonic attention encoder–decoder for character-level transduction that predicts a sequence of WRITE(c)/STEP actions. Training uses externally induced character alignments to derive gold action sequences, enabling simple cross-entropy training (best-derivation approximation rather than marginalizing over derivations).
- Strong empirical results on morphological inflection: clear gains in very low-resource (CELEX) over both soft-attention seq2seq and specialized WFST baselines; competitive to state of the art on large Wiktionary sets; mixed but well-analyzed performance on SIGMORPHON 2016 across typologically diverse languages.
- Analysis comparing learned alignments and encoder representations between hard and soft attention, offering insight into what is encoded (e.g., positional information) and when monotonic control helps.

Strengths
- Clear methodological insight with practical impact in low-resource settings. The control mechanism that enforces nearly monotonic alignment is well matched to inflection, yielding consistent dev-set improvements and mitigating overfitting on CELEX. The comparison of train/dev curves is convincing evidence that the architectural bias, not just capacity, drives generalization.
- Simple, stable training via external alignments and action supervision. Using a CRP-based aligner to derive action sequences avoids joint latent alignment learning, enabling straightforward cross-entropy training. This is a solid engineering contribution relative to RNN-T/Neural Transducer–style marginalization, and the empirical comparison to Yu et al. (2016) underscores the benefit.
- Strong results and breadth of evaluation. The paper evaluates on CELEX, multiple Wiktionary settings (7 language/pos combinations), and 10 SIGMORPHON languages, compares against strong neural and non-neural baselines, and reports consistent improvements where monotonicity holds. The claim of being the first neural system to surpass Dreyer et al.’s LAT on CELEX is meaningful for the community.
- Informative analysis. The qualitative alignment inspection shows that soft attention also learns near-monotone alignments, which validates the modeling assumption. The SVD visualization of encoder states for character identity vs. position provides interpretable evidence for how the two models distribute representational load (hard attention relies less on positional encoding), which links back to the generalization behavior.
- Clarity and reproducibility. The architecture is easy to follow; the action system is well specified; code availability is noted; use of public aligner implementation helps reproducibility.

Weaknesses
- Novelty is incremental relative to prior hard/monotonic transduction work. The main architectural idea—explicit monotonic control with STEP/WRITE and training on external alignments—resembles classic RNN-T/Neural Transducer/online segment-to-segment models. The paper’s novelty lies more in instantiation for morphology and in demonstrating the practical tradeoff (pipeline alignments + simple training) than in a fundamentally new modeling principle.
- Over-claiming SOTA. While the hard-attention model is best on CELEX and has the best average on Wiktionary, on SIGMORPHON the hard model underperforms the authors’ soft-attention baseline on average. The abstract and conclusion read as if the proposed model sets SOTA uniformly; the results suggest a more nuanced story: hard attention is best for suffixing + stem-change languages; soft attention is best for languages with long-range harmony/templatic processes.
- Reliance on external alignments without ablation. The model’s success hinges on alignment quality, yet the paper does not quantify sensitivity to alignment errors or compare alternative alignment methods. An ablation that: (a) trains with noisy alignments, (b) uses a different aligner, or (c) jointly refines alignments (e.g., via EM or scheduled sampling of STEP/WRITE) would strengthen the causal claim that external alignments are the key.
- Best-derivation approximation is not analyzed. Multiple action sequences can produce the same output; training on a single gold derivation while decoding with search may bias learning. There is no analysis of how often alternative derivations arise, whether this harms exact match, or whether simple marginalization over consecutive WRITEs (for one-to-many) would help.
- Missing efficiency measurements and some implementation details. A selling point of hard attention is potential efficiency and lower memory vs. soft attention, but wall-clock training/decoding speed and memory are not reported. Some details that matter to reproducibility and fairness are in supplement only (e.g., beam size, dropout/regularization, embedding/LSTM sizes, feature encoding), and the comparison to MED on SIGMORPHON acknowledges different training regimes.

Questions to authors
- How sensitive is performance to the alignment quality? Can you report results with an alternative aligner and/or with injected noise in alignments?
- What is the decoding strategy (greedy vs. beam)? If beam, what size, and how does it affect accuracy and speed? Any evidence of search/derivation errors?
- Can you quantify training/inference speed and memory vs. soft attention on CELEX and one Wiktionary setting?
- Could limited marginalization over derivations be tractable (e.g., summing over runs of WRITEs aligned to an input position)? If attempted, did it help?
- For languages where hard attention lags (vowel/consonant harmony, templatic), have you tried small architectural relaxations (e.g., limited look-back/jump actions, learnable copy/subsequence edits) or hybridizing with soft attention?

Overall assessment
This is a solid, well-executed paper with a clear idea matched to the task, strong low-resource gains, competitive large-scale performance, and thoughtful analysis. The methodological novelty is moderate, but the empirical and analytical contributions are valuable for the morphology community. I recommend acceptance, with toned-down SOTA claims and, if possible in the final version, added clarity on efficiency and alignment sensitivity.
