Summary and Claimed Contributions
This paper proposes a character-level sequence-to-sequence model for morphological inflection that uses a hard, monotonic attention mechanism. The decoder operates over a sequence of actions consisting of WRITE(symbol) and STEP, moving a single attention pointer forward through a biLSTM-encoded input while emitting output symbols. Training uses externally induced character alignments to derive gold action sequences; the network is trained with cross-entropy rather than marginalizing over alignments. The model is evaluated on CELEX, Wiktionary, and SIGMORPHON 2016 datasets, and includes qualitative analyses comparing hard vs. soft attention alignments and encoder representations.

Contributions (as assessed)
1) A simple, hard monotonic attention architecture (step/write pointer control) trained from externally induced alignments, enabling effective low-resource learning and competitive performance across languages. The core novelty is modest relative to prior hard/monotonic transduction work, but the instantiation and training pipeline are well-suited for morphology.
2) Extensive empirical evaluation showing strong low-resource gains (CELEX) over both neural and WFST baselines and competitive or better results on larger datasets; plus a typology-informed analysis of where monotonic attention helps or hurts.
3) Qualitative analyses of learned alignments and encoder representations that shed light on positional information and character clustering in hard vs. soft attention.

Strengths
- Strong performance in low-resource settings: On CELEX, the model achieves higher average accuracy (89.44) than LAT (88.30), MED (87.62), and NWFST (87.65), addressing a well-known weakness of soft attention and end-to-end neural models on small datasets. The learning curves (Fig. 2) further support reduced overfitting relative to soft attention under limited data.
- Simple, stable training objective: Decoupling alignment induction from sequence modeling allows training with straightforward cross-entropy on oracle action sequences. This avoids the complexity and instability of marginalizing over alignments (e.g., RNN-T/CTC-like training), while leveraging inductive bias appropriate for near-monotonic morphology.
- Comprehensive empirical coverage and strong baselines: Experiments span three benchmarks (CELEX, Wiktionary, SIGMORPHON 2016), with comparisons to competitive neural and non-neural systems. Results are competitive or superior on average in Wiktionary (overall 96.55 vs. best prior 96.34), and the typological breakdown on SIGMORPHON illuminates where monotonicity assumptions align with linguistic phenomena.
- Clear articulation and analysis: The model is described concisely with an intuitive control mechanism. The qualitative alignment visualizations demonstrate that both hard and soft attention tend toward monotonic alignments. The SVD analyses of encoder states provide insight into character-type and positional encoding differences between hard and soft attention.
- Practicality and reproducibility: The approach uses widely available components (biLSTM encoder, simple decoder, external aligner) and standard loss; code availability is noted, which enhances reproducibility.

Weaknesses
- Limited novelty relative to existing monotonic/hard attention frameworks: The step/write control and monotonic pointer closely resemble prior neural transduction methods (e.g., RNN Transducer, Neural Transducer, segment-to-segment models), and the main differences are the reliance on external alignments and application to morphological inflection. The architectural contribution is incremental.
- Dependence on external alignments without sensitivity analysis: The approach presumes a reliable character aligner and uses its output deterministically. The paper does not quantify sensitivity to alignment errors, compare different aligners, or include ablations with noisy alignments. This limits understanding of robustness and may constrain applicability to domains where high-quality aligners are unavailable.
- Best-derivation approximation: Training and decoding optimize over a single action sequence rather than marginalizing derivations that produce the same output. While effective in practice, the objective mismatch (Eq. 1 vs. Eq. 2) is not empirically probed (e.g., via beam search over derivations or alternative training). Potential effects on search errors and calibration are not discussed.
- Mixed results on broader typologies and modest gains on larger datasets: On SIGMORPHON, the hard model underperforms an in-house soft attention model on aggregate (95.61 vs. 96.34), and is weaker on languages with harmony/templatic processes where longer-range dependencies are crucial. On Wiktionary, average gains over prior SOTA are small. Statistical significance testing is not reported.
- Fairness/experimental parity concerns: The paper attributes differences with MED partly to different training regimes (e.g., number of epochs). It is unclear whether hyperparameter tuning and training schedules are matched across baselines, which complicates attribution of improvements solely to the proposed model.

Questions to Authors
- Alignment dependence: How sensitive is performance to the choice/quality of the external aligner? Can you report results with an alternative aligner or with injected noise to quantify robustness? Is there any benefit to joint fine-tuning of alignments and decoder after initial training?
- Decoding and search: Do you use greedy decoding or beam search over action sequences? If greedy, does beam search improve accuracy (especially under the best-derivation approximation)?
- Objective mismatch: Did you try training variants that partially marginalize over derivations (e.g., n-best oracle action sequences) or scheduled sampling between oracle and model alignments? Any impact on stability and accuracy?
- Ablations: What is the contribution of feature embeddings f versus the hard attention mechanism itself? Can you provide ablations removing f or replacing the biLSTM encoder with a uniLSTM to isolate components?
- Computational efficiency: How do training and inference time compare to soft attention baselines for similar model sizes? Does the hard attention incur fewer FLOPs or memory?
- Error analysis: For languages where the model underperforms (e.g., with vowel/consonant harmony or templatic morphology), what patterns of errors arise? Are there straightforward extensions (e.g., limited non-monotonic jumps, look-back in pointer) that improve those cases without sacrificing low-resource robustness?
