Summary and main contributions
The paper proposes a hard monotonic attention encoder–decoder for morphological inflection generation. The decoder operates over a sequence of actions consisting of WRITE(symbol) and STEP, advancing a single attention pointer over a biLSTM-encoded input. Training uses externally induced character alignments to derive gold action sequences; the model is then trained with standard cross-entropy, avoiding latent alignment learning and forward–backward marginalization.

As I see it, the main contributions are:
1) A simple, effective hard-attention transduction model tailored to nearly monotonic character alignments, trained from external alignments and optimized with cross entropy (not alignment marginalization).
2) Strong empirical results, especially in low-resource settings (CELEX), and competitive or SOTA average accuracy on Wiktionary; thorough multilingual evaluation including SIGMORPHON 2016.
3) Analysis comparing hard and soft attention (alignment patterns and encoder representations), offering insights into what these models learn for inflection.

Evaluation against ACL criteria
- Originality/novelty: Moderate. Hard/monotonic attention and action-based transduction have precedents (RNN-T, Neural/Online Transducer, segmental models). The novelty lies in the specific design (explicit STEP control, external character alignments used as oracle) and the application/validation for morphological inflection with a clean training objective.
- Technical soundness: Generally solid. The model and training objective are well-motivated for monotonic transduction. The best-derivation approximation (vs marginalizing over derivations mapping to the same output) is acknowledged; empirical evidence suggests it suffices. Some missing ablations limit the strength of causal claims.
- Empirical evaluation: Strong. Includes small (CELEX), large (Wiktionary), and typologically diverse (SIGMORPHON16) settings, with competitive baselines (neural and non-neural). Clear gains in low-resource; robust average improvements in large data; nuanced outcomes by typology with plausible explanations.
- Clarity: Clear, with a straightforward description of the architecture and training. Figures illustrating alignment behavior and representation analyses are helpful. Some implementation details are deferred to the supplement.
- Reproducibility: Promised code; training relies on an available alignment tool. More explicit hyperparameters, decoding details, and ablations would improve reproducibility.
- Significance: High for the inflection task and, more broadly, for nearly-monotonic sequence transduction, especially in low-resource conditions.

Strengths
1) Strong low-resource performance and simplicity: The model surpasses prior neural and non-neural baselines on CELEX, notably outperforming Dreyer et al.’s latent WFST and recent neural WFSTs. Using oracle alignments with hard control appears to reduce overfitting and improve generalization compared to soft attention.
2) Competitive across data scales and languages: On Wiktionary, the model attains the best average accuracy across language/part-of-speech conditions and wins on multiple subsets (DE-V, FI-NA, NL-V), indicating robustness beyond low-resource niches.
3) Well-argued inductive bias: The explicit STEP mechanism matches the near-monotonic nature of suffixing morphology. The analysis shows soft attention also learns monotonic alignments in practice, validating the modeling assumption, and reveals complementary positional encoding behaviors in soft vs. hard models.
4) Training efficiency and simplicity: Avoiding forward–backward and alignment marginalization simplifies training to standard cross-entropy, potentially reducing implementation complexity and improving stability. This is a practical contribution.
5) Informative qualitative analysis: Alignment visualizations and SVD-based representation projections provide insight into how and why the model works, beyond raw accuracies.

Weaknesses
1) Novelty relative to prior monotonic/hard attention work: The general idea (monotonic alignment, action-based decoding, external alignments) overlaps with RNN-Transducer, Neural/Online Transducer, and segmental models. The contribution is more in the effective instantiation and empirical validation than in fundamentally new modeling ideas.
2) Limited ablations on alignment supervision: Results hinge on the quality of external alignments (CRP-based aligner). It would be important to show sensitivity to alignment errors, compare different aligners, or include a variant that learns alignments jointly to quantify the trade-offs.
3) Best-derivation vs best-sequence approximation: While pragmatic, the choice not to marginalize over derivations can bias training/inference. It would help to quantify how often multiple derivations arise and whether beam search over actions (vs greedy) changes exact-match accuracy.
4) Fairness and completeness of comparisons: On SIGMORPHON, the paper contrasts hard vs. soft attention but does not include more recent or stronger systems beyond MED and a Luong-style attention. On Wiktionary, differences in training regimes (e.g., number of epochs) are acknowledged; more controlled re-implementations/standardization would strengthen claims.
5) Missing details on decoding and efficiency: The paper does not clearly state whether inference is greedy or beam, beam width, speed comparisons with soft attention, or memory/runtime profiling—all relevant given the claimed simplicity.

Questions to authors
- Decoding: Do you use greedy decoding or beam search over action sequences? If beam, what width, and how sensitive are results to it?
- Derivation multiplicity: How frequently do multiple action sequences map to the same target form given your action oracle? Did you try loss that marginalizes over equivalent derivations (e.g., via dynamic programming) or n-best oracle training?
- Alignment sensitivity: How sensitive are results to the choice/quality of the aligner? Have you tried alternative aligners or noisy alignments to test robustness? Any ablation where you train without external alignments (joint learning) for comparison?
- Resource usage and speed: Can you report training/inference time and memory vs. soft attention baselines on the main datasets?
- Generality beyond suffixing: For languages with stronger non-monotonic phenomena (templatic, heavy prefixation/infixation), did you try minor architectural extensions (e.g., limited backward STEP or multi-pointer) and do they help?

Overall assessment
This submission meets ACL reviewing criteria well. While the modeling novelty is incremental, the work is technically sound, clearly presented, and empirically strong. The simple hard-attention design, guided by external alignments, yields tangible gains—especially in low-resource settings—making it a useful contribution for morphological inflection and related nearly-monotonic tasks. I lean toward acceptance, contingent on addressing alignment sensitivity, decoding details, and clarifying comparisons.
