- Proposes a neural encoder–decoder with hard, monotonic attention for morphological inflection using WRITE/STEP control over a biLSTM encoder and oracle action sequences from external character alignments.
- Trains the transducer with straightforward cross-entropy on oracle WRITE/STEP sequences instead of marginalizing alignments.
- Evaluated on CELEX (low-resource), Wiktionary (large), and SIGMORPHON 2016 with comparisons to strong neural and WFST baselines.

- Contribution: simple, effective supervised hard monotonic attention transducer exploiting near-monotonicity of inflection.
- Contribution: strong empirical results, especially in low-resource settings (CELEX SOTA-like gains) and competitive/SOTA averages on Wiktionary; typological analysis on SIGMORPHON.
- Contribution: analysis comparing hard vs. soft attention alignments and encoder representations, arguing hard control improves generalization.

- Strength: achieves strong gains in low-resource regimes and better dev-set generalization than soft attention while matching training accuracy.
- Strength: elegant, task-aligned modeling that simplifies training and avoids alignment marginalization complexity.
- Strength: broad, careful evaluation across datasets and typologies with several language wins and plausible typological explanations.
- Strength: useful qualitative and quantitative analyses showing more linguistically reasonable alignments and different encoder representation properties.
- Strength: practical and reproducible approach with simple implementation and code/dataset details promised.

- Weakness: reliance on external Sudoh et al. aligner without sensitivity analysis or ablation on alignment quality or joint alignment training.
- Weakness: objective mismatch—optimizes best-derivation not best-sequence—and lacks empirical assessment of not marginalizing over derivations; decoding details (beam, termination) and runtime not reported.
- Weakness: incremental novelty relative to prior monotonic transduction architectures; main novelty is supervised hard-attention application to inflection.
- Weakness: worse performance on non-monotonic phenomena (harmony/templatic languages) and no proposed hybrid/non-monotonic extensions.
- Weakness: baseline comparability issues and missing ablations (feature embeddings, prev-output feedback, encoder directionality, beam width, unsupervised attention), and no significance testing.

- Question: How sensitive is performance to the external aligner—results with alternative/noisy aligners or joint training?
- Question: What decoding strategy is used (greedy vs. beam), does beam help, and what is the computational cost vs. soft attention?
- Question: How are insertions/deletions (epsilons) handled in alignment→action mapping—are multiple STEP actions the only deletion mechanism and is expressivity limited?
- Question: Did you try supervising a soft-attention model with these alignments to isolate alignment supervision vs. hard control effects?
- Question: Can you report training/inference speed and parameter counts compared to soft attention to support practicality claims?
- Question: Would restricted non-monotonic moves (e.g., single backward step) help harmony/templatic languages without hurting monotonic ones, and any preliminary results?
