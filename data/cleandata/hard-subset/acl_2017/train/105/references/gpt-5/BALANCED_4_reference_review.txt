Summary
The paper proposes a character-level encoder–decoder model with a hard, monotonic attention mechanism for morphological inflection generation. Instead of learning soft attention, the decoder alternates between two actions: WRITE (output a character) and STEP (advance a pointer over the bidirectionally encoded input). Training uses externally induced character alignments to derive an oracle sequence of actions, enabling simple cross-entropy training rather than marginalizing alignments. The model is evaluated on CELEX (low-resource), Wiktionary (high-resource), and SIGMORPHON 2016 (10 languages), achieving strong results overall and particularly excelling in low-resource settings and in suffixing languages with stem changes. The paper also analyzes learned alignments and encoder representations, comparing hard to soft attention.

Main contributions (as I see them)
1) A simple, effective hard monotonic attention model for nearly-monotonic string transduction, trained from external alignments with a cross-entropy objective (rather than alignment marginalization).
2) Strong empirical results across three benchmarks, including notable gains in low-resource settings (CELEX) and competitive or SOTA results on several Wiktionary inflection types and some SIGMORPHON languages.
3) Analysis comparing hard vs. soft attention, showing both learn largely monotonic alignments and different positional encoding behaviors.

Strengths
- Clear motivation and modeling choice aligned with task structure. Morphological inflection is predominantly monotonic; the proposed STEP/WRITE mechanism exploits this prior. The paper demonstrates that enforcing monotonicity reduces overfitting and improves generalization in small-data regimes (CELEX), which is a common pain point for seq2seq models.
- Strong low-resource performance and competitive large-scale results. The model surpasses prior neural and non-neural baselines on CELEX and achieves top average performance on the Wiktionary benchmarks reported. This is practically valuable for morphologically rich languages with scarce labeled data.
- Simple training objective with good inductive bias. By deriving action sequences from external alignments, the model avoids complex forward–backward marginalization or reinforcement learning and remains easy to train with cross-entropy. The approach is appealing for practitioners and reproducible (code noted, albeit anonymized).
- Informative analysis. The qualitative alignment visualizations confirm that soft attention also tends to be monotonic, validating the modeling assumption. The representation analysis (SVD plots) provides insight into how position and character identity are encoded differently in hard vs. soft attention, supporting the claim that explicit control via STEP reduces the need to encode position strongly.
- Thorough comparisons across methods and datasets. The paper situates the work among relevant baselines (MED/soft-attention, NWFST, latent WFST, Yu et al.’s SSNT), and reports results on diverse morphological typologies (SIGMORPHON), which strengthens external validity.

Weaknesses
- Reliance on external alignments and best-derivation training. Using an independent aligner introduces a pipeline dependency and potential error propagation; moreover, optimizing the single best action sequence (rather than marginalizing over derivations yielding the same output) creates an objective mismatch with test-time search. The paper acknowledges this but does not quantify sensitivity to alignment quality or compare to alignment-marginalizing training on the same data.
- Limited handling of non-monotonic or long-range phenomena. Performance drops on languages with vowel/consonant harmony, templatic morphology, or more complex morphotactics (e.g., Turkish, Arabic, Hungarian, Navajo in SIGMORPHON). While expected, the paper could strengthen the story by exploring hybrid approaches (e.g., monotonic hard attention with limited soft lookback, or explicit global features), or by analyzing error types in these languages.
- Fairness and completeness of comparisons. For SIGMORPHON, the paper attributes outperforming MED partly to training for more epochs and model selection on dev data; this weakens the baseline comparison. Statistical significance testing is not reported. For Wiktionary, details on preprocessing, hyperparameter tuning parity, and exact splits relative to prior work are not fully documented in the main text.
- Missing ablations and design justifications. Key components are not ablated: effect of (a) external vs. learned alignments, (b) different aligners, (c) removing morpho-syntactic feature embeddings f, (d) using beam search vs greedy decoding, and (e) teacher forcing vs scheduled sampling. These would clarify where the gains come from and the model’s robustness.
- Decoding/search and efficiency details are sparse. The paper does not clearly specify decoding strategy (beam size, termination criteria), computational complexity vs soft attention, or latency, which are relevant for practical deployment.

Questions to Authors
- How sensitive is performance to alignment quality? Can you report results with alternative aligners or with injected alignment noise, and/or an ablation where you jointly learn alignments (e.g., via marginalization or a small latent-variable component)?
- What decoding strategy is used (greedy vs beam, beam size), and how does it affect accuracy/latency? Any search errors observed (e.g., pathological STEP sequences)?
- How are morpho-syntactic features f encoded (one-hot vs learned embeddings)? What is their contribution (ablation)? Does conditioning on f change learned alignments?
- Do you use teacher forcing during training? Did you try scheduled sampling or other strategies to mitigate exposure bias?
- For languages with harmony or templatic morphology, did you experiment with limited non-monotonic operations (e.g., a small backward move, or a “peek” attention window) or hybrid hard/soft attention? Any preliminary results?
- Could you provide significance tests for main comparisons (CELEX, Wiktionary averages, SIGMORPHON per-language)?

Suggestions for improvement
- Add ablations on aligner choice, feature conditioning, decoding (beam), and training regime (teacher forcing vs scheduled sampling). Report significance tests.
- Explore alignment-marginalizing objectives (e.g., forward–backward over action sequences) or differentiable monotonic attention variants to reduce dependence on external alignments while preserving monotonic bias.
- Investigate hybrid models for non-monotonic phenomena (e.g., hard monotonic pointer with local soft attention window or global constraint features), and include error analyses per typology.
- Document implementation and tuning details for fair comparison more thoroughly, and release code and scripts to facilitate reproducibility.
