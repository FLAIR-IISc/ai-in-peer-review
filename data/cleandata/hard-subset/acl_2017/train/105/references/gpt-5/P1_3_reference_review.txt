Summary and claimed contributions
- The paper proposes a hard monotonic attention encoder–decoder for morphological inflection generation. The decoder predicts a sequence over an action alphabet Σy ∪ {step}, where step advances a single-pointer over the biLSTM-encoded input and write emits an output character. Training uses externally computed character-level alignments to deterministically construct an oracle action sequence; learning is standard cross-entropy over actions.
- Extensive experiments on CELEX (small), Wiktionary (large), and SIGMORPHON 2016 (10 languages) show strong results: clear gains in low-resource CELEX (first neural model to beat Dreyer et al.’s latent WFST), best average on Wiktionary, and competitive on SIGMORPHON, particularly for suffixing + stem-change languages.
- Analysis compares learned alignments and encoder representations against a soft-attention baseline: both models learn nearly monotonic alignments; the hard model encodes character identity more distinctly and relies less on positional encoding, consistent with the explicit step mechanism.

Main contributions (as I see them)
1) A simple, interpretable hard monotonic attention transduction model trained from external alignments that delivers state-of-the-art or competitive performance, especially under low-resource conditions.
2) Empirical evidence across datasets and typologies that hard monotonic attention is a strong inductive bias for inflection generation, with clear advantages on suffixing/stem-change languages.
3) Insightful qualitative and quantitative analysis of alignments and representations learned by hard vs soft attention models.

Strengths
- Strong low-resource performance and practical significance: On CELEX, the model outperforms both prior neural systems and the tailored latent WFST (Table 1), addressing a known limitation of neural seq2seq approaches that typically need more data. The learning curves show reduced overfitting versus soft attention.
- Simplicity and interpretability: The step/write control mechanism, pointer over a biLSTM-encoded input, and cross-entropy training using oracle actions make the model easy to implement, train, and analyze. The transition-system view (ADVANCE, WRITE(CH)) is clear and practically appealing.
- Solid results at scale: On Wiktionary, the model attains the best average accuracy over seven settings (Table 2), outperforming both non-neural and recent neural baselines (including attentionless seq2seq and an alignment-marginalizing neural transducer).
- Insightful analysis: The alignment visualizations and SVD projections of encoder states reveal that both models learn monotonicity and position encoding, while the hard model’s explicit control reduces the need to encode position, which plausibly helps data efficiency.
- Linguistic framing: The SIGMORPHON results are discussed through morphological typology, pinpointing where the monotonic bias helps (suffixing + stem change) and where it is less suitable (languages with harmony or templatic morphology). This clarity aids applicability decisions.

Weaknesses
- Limited methodological novelty relative to prior monotonic/online transduction: The approach is close in spirit to RNN-Transducer/Neural Transducer/online segment-to-segment models and to Yu et al. (2016). The main differences are reliance on external alignments and decoding with a single pointer and step actions. While effective, the novelty is incremental.
- Dependence on external aligner without ablation: Training relies on Sudoh et al.’s alignments to derive oracle action sequences. There is no study of alignment quality sensitivity, alternative aligners, or a comparison to jointly learned alignments on the same architecture. It remains unclear how robust the approach is when alignments are noisy or in truly low-resource settings where aligners are weaker.
- Objective and decoding details: The paper optimizes best-derivation (not best-sequence) likelihood and acknowledges the mismatch but provides no empirical comparison (e.g., marginalization approximations or different decoding strategies). Decoding strategy (greedy vs beam over actions) and its impact on accuracy and errors are not reported.
- Fairness and completeness of baselines: On SIGMORPHON, the paper notes their soft attention baseline was trained longer (100 epochs) than MED (20), which complicates fairness. On Wiktionary/CELEX, hyperparameter tuning parity across systems is unclear. A stronger comparison to recent joint-alignment neural approaches (e.g., with comparable training budgets) would strengthen claims.
- Limited generality across typologies: The method underperforms soft attention on languages with non-local phenomena (vowel/consonant harmony, templatic patterns). This suggests the proposed model is not broadly superior but rather complementary; an explicit hybrid or adaptive strategy is not explored.

Questions to authors
- Decoding: Do you use greedy or beam search over action sequences? If beam, what width, and how sensitive are results to it? Have you tried sequence-level decoding (e.g., marginalizing short alternative derivations) to mitigate the best-derivation approximation?
- Alignment sensitivity: How sensitive is training to the choice and quality of the external aligner? Can you report results with a different aligner, with injected alignment noise, or with a joint-alignment training variant of your model?
- Error analysis: For SIGMORPHON languages where hard attention lags soft attention, what error types dominate (e.g., harmonies, templatic stem changes)? Are there specific edit patterns (prefixing/infixing) where hard attention fails?
- Feature embeddings: How are morpho-syntactic features represented and shared across tasks/languages? Any ablation on their contribution?
- Efficiency: Can you provide training/inference speed and memory comparisons against soft attention and neural transducer baselines, and comment on scalability?

Additional comments
- The transition-system formulation is compelling; exploring a hybrid that allows limited non-monotonic jumps or a learned “peek” could broaden typological coverage.
- A low-resource study on SIGMORPHON (subsampling training data) would more directly test the claimed advantage beyond CELEX.
