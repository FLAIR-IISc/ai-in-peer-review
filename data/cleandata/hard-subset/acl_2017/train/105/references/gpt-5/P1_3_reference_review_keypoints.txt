- Proposes a hard monotonic attention encoder–decoder that emits step/write actions and is trained from externally computed character alignments via cross-entropy on oracle action sequences.
- Demonstrates strong empirical performance: state-of-the-art on low-resource CELEX, best average on Wiktionary, and competitive results on SIGMORPHON, especially for suffixing + stem-change languages.
- Analysis shows both hard and soft attention learn near-monotonic alignments; the hard model encodes character identity more distinctly and relies less on positional encoding.
- Contribution: simple, interpretable hard monotonic transduction model that is effective and easy to implement and analyze.
- Contribution: empirical evidence that hard monotonic attention is a strong inductive bias for inflection generation, beneficial for certain morphologies.
- Contribution: qualitative and quantitative comparison of alignments and encoder representations between hard and soft attention.
- Strength: strong low-resource performance and reduced overfitting compared to soft attention.
- Strength: model simplicity and interpretability via a clear step/write transition system over a biLSTM encoder.
- Strength: solid large-scale results on Wiktionary, outperforming recent baselines.
- Strength: insightful analyses (alignment visualizations, SVD of encoder states) supporting mechanistic claims.
- Strength: careful linguistic framing linking performance to morphological typology.
- Weakness: methodological novelty is incremental relative to prior monotonic/online transduction work (RNN-T, Neural Transducer, Yu et al.).
- Weakness: relies on an external aligner (Sudoh et al.) with no ablation or sensitivity analysis to alignment quality.
- Weakness: training objective and decoding mismatch (best-derivation likelihood) are acknowledged but not empirically explored; decoding strategy details (greedy vs beam) are missing.
- Weakness: fairness/completeness of baselines and tuning budgets is unclear, complicating comparisons.
- Weakness: limited generality—underperforms on languages with non-local phenomena (harmony, templatic morphology).
- Question: Do you use greedy or beam search over actions, what beam width, and how sensitive are results to decoding strategy or marginalization?
- Question: How sensitive is training to alignment choice/quality; have you tried other aligners, injected alignment noise, or joint-alignment training?
- Question: What error types dominate where hard attention lags soft attention (e.g., harmony, templatic patterns, prefixing/infixing)?
- Question: How are morpho-syntactic feature embeddings represented/shared and what is their ablation effect?
- Question: Provide training/inference speed and memory comparisons versus soft attention and neural transducer baselines.
- Additional suggestion: Explore a hybrid model allowing limited non-monotonic jumps or a learned “peek” to broaden typological coverage.
- Additional suggestion: Run low-resource subsampling experiments on SIGMORPHON to better validate claimed low-resource advantages.
