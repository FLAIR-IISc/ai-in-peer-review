Summary and contributions
This paper proposes a simple, hard monotonic attention model for morphological inflection generation that decodes with two actions—WRITE(symbol) and STEP—to traverse a biLSTM-encoded input while generating output. Unlike common soft-attention seq2seq models, it supervises attention using external character alignments and trains with straightforward cross-entropy on oracle action sequences. The model achieves new SOTA on several benchmarks, with especially strong gains in low-resource settings, and includes an analysis comparing learned alignments/representations to those of soft attention.

As I see it, the main contributions are:
1) A clean, hard-monotonic, pointer-like transducer trained from externally derived alignments, yielding a simple, data-efficient alternative to soft attention in nearly monotonic settings.
2) Strong empirical results, notably the first neural model to surpass the latent-variable WFST (Dreyer et al., 2008) on CELEX, and competitive SOTA averages on larger datasets.
3) Analyses that connect behavior of hard vs soft attention—showing soft attention learns near-monotonic alignments and encodes positional information more strongly; the hard model offloads position to explicit STEP actions.

Strengths
- Conceptual simplicity with a bold, targeted inductive bias: The STEP/WRITE control with hard monotonic attention is an elegant fit to morphological inflection’s near-monotone structure. Training via external alignments avoids complex marginalization and yields a clean, reproducible objective. This is a meaningful modeling contribution for a class of sequence tasks (morphology, transliteration, etc.).
- Clear low-resource benefits and a notable milestone: The model substantially outperforms soft attention and neural WFST baselines on CELEX and, importantly, surpasses the specialized latent-variable WFST. The learning-curve analysis supports the claim that the inductive bias mitigates overfitting in small data regimes.
- Competitive or better performance at scale: On Wiktionary, the model matches or exceeds the strongest published systems across several languages, demonstrating robustness beyond the small-data niche and outperforming attention-less seq2seq and the alignment-jointly-learned segment-to-segment model.
- Empirical insight into representations and alignments: The qualitative alignment comparisons and SVD projections are informative, showing the soft model’s implicit monotonicity and stronger encoding of position, versus the hard model’s reliance on explicit control. This is a useful, broadly relevant insight for the community.
- Practicality and reproducibility: The training pipeline (external alignments + cross-entropy over oracle actions) is straightforward and efficient; code availability is promised. The transition-system view (ADVANCE/WRITE) connects well to known paradigms.

Weaknesses
- Novelty relative to prior monotonic transducers: While novel in morphological inflection and in its exact design/training recipe, the core idea overlaps with RNN Transducer/CTC-style monotonic alignment and Neural Transducer/online segment-to-segment approaches. The paper cites these but could better delineate what is fundamentally new versus a targeted adaptation with external alignment supervision. The contribution may be viewed as a strong application + engineering simplification rather than a fundamentally new learning principle.
- Dependence on external alignments and best-derivation approximation: The model optimizes best-derivation rather than marginalizing derivations; the impact of alignment noise/choice is not quantified. An ablation with different aligners (or noisy alignments) and/or a comparison to joint learning under the same architecture would strengthen the causal claim that externally supervised hard attention is the driver of gains.
- Mixed results on non-suffixing/complex morphologies: On SIGMORPHON, the hard model underperforms on languages with vowel/consonant harmony or templatic morphology. This underscores a limitation of strict monotonic control, but the paper does not explore hybridizations (e.g., limited non-monotone jumps, fertility modeling) or analyze specific error types to guide future extensions.
- Fairness and rigor of comparisons: The paper notes training the soft baseline for 100 epochs and selecting by dev, whereas MED used a fixed 20 epochs; more systematic reimplementations/controls or significance testing would improve confidence in the ranking, especially where differences are small.
- Missing ablations and decoding details: There is little on decoding strategy (greedy vs beam), speed/latency, and parameter counts, or on ablations for feature embeddings, pointer initialization, or the explicit previous-output feedback. These would help isolate which components matter.

Questions to authors
- Can you more precisely articulate novelty vs RNN-T/CTC/Neural Transducer and Yu et al. (2016)? What is enabled by external alignments and the specific STEP/WRITE design that is not achievable (or is harder) in those frameworks?
- How sensitive is performance to the alignment quality and to the choice of aligner? Have you tried simpler aligners (e.g., 1-1 + fertility heuristics) or injected noise to assess robustness?
- Do you use greedy decoding or beam search? If beam search, what beam sizes and are there measurable trade-offs vs soft attention in speed/accuracy?
- Could a hybrid variant (mostly monotonic with occasional learned jumps) help on harmony/templatic languages? Any preliminary experiments?
- Are the CELEX and Wiktionary gains statistically significant? If not computed, can you report per-fold variance and tests for key comparisons?

Overall recommendation
I lean toward acceptance. The paper brings a focused, well-motivated inductive bias with a simple learning recipe that delivers strong practical gains, especially in low-resource scenarios, and offers nice qualitative insights. While the conceptual novelty overlaps with prior monotonic transducers, the adaptation to morphology, the simplicity of training with external alignments, and the empirical impact make this a valuable contribution. Addressing alignment sensitivity, decoding details, and baseline fairness would further strengthen the case.
