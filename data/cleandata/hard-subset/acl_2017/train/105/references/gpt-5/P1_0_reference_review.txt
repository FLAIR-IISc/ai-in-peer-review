Summary
This paper proposes a hard monotonic attention encoder–decoder model for morphological inflection generation. The decoder operates with two actions—WRITE(symbol) and STEP—that jointly control alignment and transduction over a biLSTM-encoded input, with the attention pointer advancing monotonically. Unlike most attention-based approaches, training uses externally computed character-level alignments to derive oracle action sequences and optimizes a simple cross-entropy loss. Experiments on CELEX (low-resource), Wiktionary (large), and SIGMORPHON 2016 show strong results, with notable gains in low-resource settings. The paper also analyzes learned alignments and encoder representations, arguing the model’s inductive bias matches near-monotone morphology and improves generalization.

Main contributions (as I see them)
1) A simple, hard-monotonic attention model trained from external alignments with a step/write control mechanism, enabling stable training with cross-entropy and strong inductive bias for nearly monotone morphology.
2) Extensive empirical evaluation showing state-of-the-art or competitive results across three benchmarks, with clear advantages in low-resource settings.
3) Qualitative analyses of alignments and encoder representations that provide insight into why hard attention works well on suffixing/stem-change languages.

Strengths
1) Strong low-resource performance and compelling inductive bias. On CELEX, the model surpasses both neural (MED, NWFST) and tailored latent-variable WFST (LAT) baselines, achieving the highest average accuracy (Table 1). The learning curve analysis shows reduced overfitting relative to soft attention, consistent with the monotonicity prior that matches typical inflectional mappings.

2) Simplicity and training stability. Reducing training to supervised action prediction using externally learned alignments avoids forward–backward or marginalization complexity (as in RNN-T/segmental objectives), yet performs on par or better. The step/write abstraction is easy to implement and interpret, and the model is less sensitive to data scarcity.

3) Competitive and often state-of-the-art on larger data. On Wiktionary the model achieves the best average accuracy across languages/inflection types (Table 2), outperforming both earlier neural seq2seq (without attention) and alignment-coupled neural models, indicating the approach scales beyond low-resource regimes.

4) Insightful analysis and interpretability. The attention pointer’s behavior and the representation analysis (SVD plots) meaningfully relate learned properties to morphology: both soft and hard models learn monotone alignments; the hard model encodes position less strongly, relying instead on explicit STEP actions. Examples illustrate one-to-many mappings and deletions handled naturally by the control mechanism.

5) Clear link between linguistic typology and performance. On SIGMORPHON, the model excels in suffixing+stem-change languages (RU/DE/ES) and trails soft attention in languages with harmony or templatic patterns (FI/TU/HU/NA/AR), aligning empirical outcomes with the model’s bias. This clarity helps practitioners choose appropriately.

Weaknesses
1) Dependence on external alignments without ablation. The pipeline relies on an external aligner (Sudoh et al., 2013) to derive oracle actions. The paper does not quantify sensitivity to alignment errors, nor compare to variants trained with noisy or alternative alignments, or to joint/latent alignment learning under the same architecture. This makes it hard to disentangle where gains come from: the hard attention bias, the external aligner, or both.

2) Limited handling of non-monotonic phenomena. While acknowledged, the method underperforms on languages with vowel/consonant harmony and templatic morphology (Table 3). The model cannot revise attention or look back once it steps forward, and the paper does not explore mitigations (e.g., limited backward moves, chunked/online monotonic attention, or hybrid soft-hard mechanisms).

3) Objective mismatch (best-derivation vs best-sequence) is untested. The training/decoding optimize the best-derivation approximation rather than marginalizing over derivations mapping to the same output. While the authors claim it works well in practice, there is no quantitative study of its effect versus a forward-sum (CTC-like) objective, which could be feasible for monotone step/write lattices.

4) Experimental fairness and statistical rigor. Some comparisons are hard to interpret: the soft-attention baseline (our implementation) is trained for 100 epochs while MED uses 20; no significance tests are reported; gains on Wiktionary are small in several cases. It would be more convincing to report matched training setups across models, statistical tests, and, if used, whether ensembling is applied in any condition.

5) Missing ablations and error analysis depth. The paper lacks ablation studies probing contributions of: feature embeddings, decoder feedback of previous symbol, beam size, or encoder depth. A more systematic error analysis beyond a few examples (e.g., breakdown by edit types, stem changes vs affixation-only) would strengthen causal claims about where the model helps or fails.

Questions to Authors
- How sensitive is performance to the external aligner’s quality? Have you tried weaker/stronger aligners or adding noise to oracle actions to quantify robustness?
- What decoding strategy is used (greedy vs beam)? If beam, what size, and how does it affect CELEX vs SIGMORPHON performance?
- Can you report significance tests on the main comparisons (especially CELEX and Wiktionary averages)?
- Did you attempt a forward-sum (marginalization over derivations) objective within your step/write lattice? If not, what are the computational bottlenecks?
- For SIGMORPHON languages where hard attention underperforms, would a small allowance for non-monotonicity (e.g., a limited “backstep”) help?

Overall assessment
A solid, well-motivated contribution with an elegant, effective model and strong empirical results in settings that matter (notably low-resource). The method is not radically novel relative to prior monotonic/segmental transduction ideas, but its instantiation for morphological inflection with external alignments, simplicity, and analyses make it a valuable addition. I recommend acceptance after addressing fairness/statistical concerns and clarifying robustness to alignment quality.
