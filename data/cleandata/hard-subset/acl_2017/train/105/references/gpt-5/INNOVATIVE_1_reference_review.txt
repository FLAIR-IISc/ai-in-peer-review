Summary and main contributions
This paper proposes a neural encoder–decoder for morphological inflection that operates with hard, nearly-monotonic attention via a small transition system (WRITE(c) and STEP). Unlike soft attention models, the decoder explicitly advances a pointer over a biLSTM-encoded input and writes output symbols, guided by action sequences derived from external character-level alignments. Training is simple cross-entropy over these oracle actions.

As I see it, the primary contributions are:
1) A simple, explicit hard monotonic attention mechanism (pointer + STEP/WRITE) trained from external alignments, yielding strong data efficiency and competitive or SOTA results.
2) Empirical evidence that this approach outperforms prior neural and non-neural systems in low-resource settings (notably CELEX), and is robust on larger datasets (Wiktionary, SIGMORPHON).
3) Analysis comparing hard vs. soft attention alignments and encoder representations, offering insight into what these models learn for inflection.

Strengths
- Conceptual novelty and clarity: The pointer-based STEP/WRITE control with externally supervised hard attention is a crisp formulation of nearly-monotonic transduction. It bridges traditional align-and-transduce WFSTs and neural seq2seq with an elegant, minimal action set and a clean training signal. This is a meaningful conceptual step beyond generic soft-attention seq2seq and avoids learning latent alignments from scratch.

- Data efficiency and low-resource gains: On CELEX, the model is the first neural approach to surpass the strong latent WFST (Dreyer et al., 2008) and clearly beats soft attention and neural WFST baselines. The learning-curve analysis supports the claim that hard monotonic control regularizes the model and mitigates overfitting when data are scarce.

- Competitive breadth across datasets: Results on Wiktionary are state-of-the-art on average across multiple languages and inflection types, outperforming both neural and feature-engineered align-and-transduce systems. On SIGMORPHON16, the model is best on suffixing + stem-change languages and close to strong soft-attention baselines elsewhere. This suggests the approach is robust and practically useful.

- Training simplicity and inference efficiency: Reducing the problem to predicting an oracle action sequence via cross-entropy is attractive. It avoids forward–backward marginalization over alignments (as in RNN-T/Neural Transducer) and should yield faster and more stable training. Decoding is straightforward given the pointer mechanism.

- Insightful analysis: The qualitative alignment comparisons show the model indeed learns monotonic behavior and can implement deletions and one-to-many mappings naturally. The representation analysis (character identity and position) is a nice touch that helps interpret why the hard attention encoder may generalize better in low data.

Weaknesses
- Relation to prior monotonic transduction is underplayed: The connection to RNN Transducer, segmental/Neural Transducer, and CTC-style monotonicity is acknowledged but could be sharper. The main novelty is using external alignments with a minimal action set and a joint encoder–decoder rather than marginalizing alignments; however, this can be seen as a pragmatic twist on known paradigms rather than a fundamentally new model family. A more thorough comparison (e.g., ablation with learned vs external alignments, or with marginalization) would strengthen the novelty claim.

- Dependence on external alignments: The model relies on a separate character aligner and assumes those alignments are high quality. There is no sensitivity analysis to alignment noise or choice of aligner. It is not clear how robust the method is when gold-quality alignments are unavailable or less reliable for certain languages/phenomena.

- Best-derivation vs best-sequence approximation: The model decodes the most likely action sequence rather than marginalizing over derivations for the same output. While the approximation works empirically, the paper neither quantifies its impact nor provides alternatives (e.g., constrained beam/marginalization) that could close the gap when multiple derivations exist.

- Coverage of non-monotonic/long-range phenomena: On languages with vowel/consonant harmony or templatic morphology, performance lags soft attention. This highlights a core limitation of the hard monotonic assumption. The paper would benefit from discussing mechanisms to relax monotonicity (e.g., limited skips/jumps, small local reordering windows) or hybrid models.

- Experimental fairness/ablations: Some comparisons hinge on different training budgets (e.g., MED trained for 20 epochs vs 100 here). Important ablations are missing: effect of beam size, feature set, decoder history embedding, and the contribution of the biLSTM encoder vs. the control mechanism. A small ablation table could isolate what truly drives gains.

Questions to authors
- How sensitive is performance to the choice and quality of the external aligner? Did you try alternatives or inject noise to quantify robustness?

- What decoding strategy is used at test time (greedy vs beam)? Does a small beam improve results or reduce best-derivation errors?

- Can your framework marginalize over derivations for a fixed output (e.g., via dynamic programming under monotonic constraints) or is that computationally prohibitive?

- Did you attempt joint training of alignments and actions (e.g., using a latent alignment objective or REINFORCE/Gumbel tricks) and, if so, how did it compare?

- For languages where monotonicity breaks (e.g., templatic), could adding a limited “jump” action or a small attention window mitigate the drop? Any preliminary results?

Overall assessment
This is a strong, clean, and novel approach within the morphological inflection space, with particularly compelling low-resource gains and solid analyses. Despite some reliance on external alignments and limited ablations, the conceptual simplicity and empirical strength make it a worthy acceptance.
