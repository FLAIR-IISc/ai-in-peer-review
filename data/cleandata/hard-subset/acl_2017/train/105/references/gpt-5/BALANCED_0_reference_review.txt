Summary and contributions
This paper proposes a character-level encoder–decoder model with hard, monotonic attention for morphological inflection generation. The decoder emits either a WRITE(character) or a STEP action; a single attention pointer moves left-to-right over a biLSTM-encoded input. Training uses externally derived character alignments to convert each example into an action sequence, enabling simple cross-entropy training on gold actions rather than learning alignments jointly. The model is evaluated on CELEX (very low-resource), Wiktionary (larger), and SIGMORPHON 2016 (typologically diverse), and includes an analysis comparing learned alignments and representations to those of soft attention.

As I see them, the main contributions (strongest first) are:
1) A simple, effective hard monotonic attention formulation tailored to nearly monotonic character transduction, trained from externally induced alignments with straightforward cross-entropy, that is competitive or better than soft attention and non-neural baselines, particularly in low-resource settings.
2) An extensive empirical evaluation across three benchmarks, showing strong average performance and clear advantages in the low-resource regime (CELEX) and on suffixing/stem-change languages.
3) An informative qualitative analysis of learned alignments and encoder representations that sheds light on how hard and soft attention behave on this task.

Strengths
- Clear modeling of monotonicity yields strong gains in low-resource settings. On CELEX, the hard-attention model outperforms prior neural models and the strong latent-variable WFST, addressing a known weakness of soft attention when data is scarce. The design (explicit STEP/WRITE control, best-derivation decoding) appropriately biases the model toward the inductive bias of morphological transduction.

- Simple, efficient training and inference. By supervising with oracle action sequences derived from character alignments, training reduces to standard cross-entropy without forward–backward or reinforcement-style estimation. At inference, the pointer advances monotonically with local decisions, offering linear-time decoding and lower memory footprint compared to soft attention.

- Broad and careful empirical coverage and competitive results. The paper compares against relevant neural and non-neural baselines on CELEX, multiple Wiktionary settings (verbs and nouns across languages), and the SIGMORPHON-2016 shared task. The model attains the best average on Wiktionary and is strongest on languages whose morphology aligns with the monotonic assumption (e.g., DE, ES, RU). This typological perspective is appropriate and convincing.

- Useful qualitative analysis. The alignment visualizations illustrate that soft attention tends to be monotonic on this task, supporting the modeling assumption. The representation analysis suggests different positional encoding behaviors across models, providing insight into why hard attention may generalize better with few examples.

- Methodological transparency and reproducibility. The architecture and training pipeline are described clearly, and the use of a public character aligner is specified. The action-based view connects well to transition systems, which many readers will find intuitive.

Weaknesses
- Dependence on external alignments and lack of robustness analysis. The model’s success hinges on the quality of the hard alignments used to create gold action sequences. There is no ablation on alignment quality, nor a comparison to alternative aligners or to training without gold actions (e.g., marginalizing over alignments or learning them jointly). This limits understanding of how brittle the approach is to alignment errors, especially in noisy or low-resource settings.

- Best-derivation decoding and objective mismatch. The model optimizes and decodes the most likely action sequence rather than marginalizing over derivations for a given output string. While the paper notes this approximation works in practice, there is no empirical study of its impact (e.g., comparing greedy vs. beam search, or measuring how often multiple derivations map to one output and whether the approximation hurts).

- Fairness and rigor of comparisons. Some comparisons seem to benefit from different training protocols (e.g., training the soft-attention baseline for 100 epochs vs. 20 epochs in published MED results). There are no statistical significance tests, and the extent of hyperparameter tuning per system is unclear. Stronger claims would require matched training budgets and reporting of variance across runs.

- Limited coverage of non-monotonic phenomena. The approach underperforms soft attention on languages with vowel/consonant harmony or templatic morphology. While expected given the design, there is no exploration of simple extensions (e.g., limited backward moves, skip operations, or chunkwise monotonic attention) that might close the gap without sacrificing the benefits.

- Missing ablations and error analysis. The paper would benefit from quantifying contributions of key components: effect of feature embeddings f, the biLSTM encoder vs. simpler encoders, the feedback connection si−1, and the step/positioning mechanism. The qualitative analysis is helpful but limited; a systematic error typology (e.g., stem internal changes, suffix insertions, deletions) would strengthen conclusions.

Questions to authors
- How sensitive is performance to the choice and accuracy of the external aligner? Can you report results with a weaker aligner, or with noisy alignments (e.g., perturbations), and quantify the degradation?

- What decoding procedure is used at test time (greedy vs. beam)? If beam search is used, what beam size, and how does it affect accuracy and speed? Any evidence that derivation-level decoding differs from sequence-level optima?

- How are morpho-syntactic features f represented and integrated across datasets (shared embeddings across inflection types/languages, dimensionality, regularization)? Any ablation on their contribution?

- Do you train a single model per language across all inflection types, or separate models per paradigm? On CELEX you follow per-inflection splits; what is the setting on Wiktionary and SIGMORPHON? Does multi-tasking across types help?

- Have you tried simple relaxations to strict monotonicity (e.g., allowing a bounded number of backward STEP actions or chunkwise/monotonic chunk attention)? If so, does that help on non-suffixing languages without hurting CELEX?

- Can you provide significance tests for the main claims (e.g., CELEX average accuracy and Wiktionary averages)? Also, please report variance across multiple runs.

Suggestions for improvement
- Add ablations on alignment quality and on key architectural choices (use of si−1 feedback, encoder type, features f) and clarify decoding. Include statistical tests and matched training budgets for comparisons.

- Explore modest non-monotonic extensions (e.g., chunkwise monotonic attention or limited backtracking) to address harmony and templatic phenomena.

- Investigate marginalization over derivations (e.g., via dynamic programming over monotonic alignments or small beams) to reduce objective mismatch while retaining tractability.

- Provide a more granular error analysis across languages and phenomena to guide future modeling choices.

Overall, this is a solid, well-motivated paper with clear contributions and strong empirical results, particularly in low-resource settings. Addressing the robustness and fairness concerns would substantially strengthen the work.
