- Proposes a hard monotonic attention model for character-level inflection using a bidirectional encoder and a decoder with step/write actions trained on external character alignments.
- Trained via cross-entropy on action sequences rather than marginalizing over alignments.
- Achieves SOTA on CELEX (low-resource) and best average on Wiktionary, while being competitive on SIGMORPHON 2016.
- Performs particularly well on suffixing and stem-change languages; lags on languages with non-local morphophonology.
- Qualitative analysis contrasts hard vs. soft attention alignments and encoder representations, arguing hard attention encodes less positional information.
- Simple, interpretable design closely matches near-monotonic morphological alignments and is easy to implement.
- Strong low-resource inductive bias reduces overfitting and outperforms prior neural and non-neural systems on CELEX.
- Competitive/highest results on larger benchmarks, outperforming several neural baselines and an align-and-transduce model.
- Insightful visualizations showing both attention types tend to be monotonic and revealing differences in type vs. position encoding.
- Well-situated relative to related work (soft attention, RNN-T/CTC, neural WFSTs, align-and-transduce), clarifying trade-offs.
- Novelty is incremental relative to prior monotonic transduction/transducer models; main difference is external alignments and single-step pointer.
- Method depends on externally induced alignments and uses best-derivation approximation, without quantifying sensitivity to alignment errors.
- Lacks key ablations and stability analyses (e.g., external vs. learned alignments, morpho-feature embeddings, decoder feedback, beam vs. greedy).
- Comparisons may be unfair or underpowered: differing training epochs, dataset splits, preprocessing, and lack of statistical significance testing.
- Limited coverage of non-monotonic phenomena; no explored extensions (e.g., limited jumps, multi-pointer) to handle templatic or harmony-heavy languages.
- Asks for experiments on sensitivity to alignment quality, including multiple aligners and noisy alignments.
- Requests clarification of decoding strategy (greedy vs. beam) and whether beam search mitigates best-derivation limits.
- Requests ablations isolating contributions of external alignments, morpho-syntactic embeddings, and decoder feedback.
- Wonders whether augmenting the action space (step-k, jump-back, copy) helps on languages where hard attention underperforms.
- Requests assurance of comparable training/regulation across baselines and significance testing when re-running soft-attention baselines.
- Suggests reporting speed/memory benefits of hard attention to strengthen practical claims.
- Recommends a quantitative alignment analysis (monotonicity score, fertility statistics) to complement qualitative figures.
- Asks to clarify end-of-sequence handling and constraints preventing invalid action sequences (e.g., stepping past input).
