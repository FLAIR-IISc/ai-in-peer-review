Summary and contributions
This paper proposes a hard, nearly monotonic attention model for character-level morphological inflection generation. The decoder emits either a WRITE(symbol) or STEP action, with STEP advancing a single-pointer over a biLSTM-encoded input; action supervision is derived from external character alignments. The model is trained with standard cross-entropy on oracle action sequences. Extensive experiments on CELEX, Wiktionary, and SIGMORPHON2016 show competitive or state-of-the-art accuracy, particularly strong in low-resource settings. The paper also provides qualitative analyses comparing learned alignments/representations to soft attention models.

As I see them, the main contributions are:
1) A simple, alignment-supervised hard monotonic attention model that injects an appropriate inductive bias for inflection and attains strong accuracy, especially in low-resource settings.
2) A broad empirical evaluation across three benchmarks with competitive baselines, including WFST-based methods and soft-attention seq2seq.
3) Qualitative analyses of attention/representation behavior contrasting hard vs. soft attention encoders.

Strengths
1) Strong empirical coverage and consistent gains in low-resource: The CELEX results are compelling; surpassing the tailored latent-variable WFST (Dreyer et al., 2008) on small data is notable. This supports the claim that the monotonic inductive bias and supervised alignment help generalization when data are scarce.

2) Methodologically simple and stable training: By relying on external alignments and training with cross-entropy over explicit actions, the approach avoids the instability and complexity of marginalization or REINFORCE used in prior monotonic transducer models (e.g., RNN-T/Neural Transducer/online transduction). This is attractive for practitioners.

3) Appropriate inductive bias for the task: The step/write mechanism operationalizes near-monotonicity in a clean way, aligning well with suffixing and stem-change phenomena. The qualitative alignment examples and the CELEX learning curves support the claim that the model resists overfitting relative to soft attention.

4) Competitive large-scale results: On Wiktionary and several SIGMORPHON languages, the method is at or near SOTA. The advantage over non-attention encoder–decoder (Faruqui et al., 2016) and over the jointly-trained alignment model (Yu et al., 2016) is informative.

5) Useful analysis: The visualization of encoder representations and alignments provides insight into what the models learn (e.g., positional encoding differences between hard and soft attention).

Weaknesses
1) Limited novelty relative to prior monotonic seq2seq/transduction frameworks: The step/write hard attention mechanism strongly resembles established monotonic transducers (e.g., Graves 2012; Jaitly et al., 2015; Yu et al., 2016) and align-and-transduce WFSTs. The main distinction is training on externally-derived, hard alignments with a standard cross-entropy oracle. While practical, the conceptual advance over prior monotonic models is modest.

2) Objective mismatch and lack of analysis: The paper explicitly optimizes best-derivation rather than marginalizing over derivations (Eq. 1 vs. Eq. 2), but provides no quantitative analysis of its impact. There is no ablation on alternative training (e.g., marginalization/forward–backward, or relaxed alignments), nor on the decoder feedback connection. This leaves uncertainty about where gains come from.

3) Dependence on external aligner and missing sensitivity study: The approach hinges on the quality of character alignments (Sudoh et al., 2013), yet there is no robustness analysis (e.g., different aligners, noisy alignments, or reduced-data alignments). In genuinely low-resource scenarios, alignment quality can degrade; it is unclear whether the reported CELEX advantage persists under alignment noise.

4) Comparability and significance: Some baseline setups are not strictly controlled (e.g., authors attribute soft-attention performance differences to different training epochs/selection). Statistical significance is not reported for per-language or average gains. Given the small absolute differences in several tables, this is important for a rigorous claim of SOTA.

5) Scope limitations for non-monotonic phenomena: Results drop on languages with vowel/consonant harmony or templatic morphology (e.g., Turkish, Arabic, Navajo). The paper acknowledges this but does not propose mitigation (e.g., hybrid hard/soft attention or additional actions). This limits generality beyond suffixing/near-monotonic settings.

Questions to authors
1) Decoding details: Do you use greedy decoding or a beam? What beam size and tie-breaking? Is there any constraint to avoid degenerate sequences (e.g., unbounded WRITE without STEP), and how do you ensure termination?

2) Alignment sensitivity: How sensitive are results to the alignment source? Can you report performance with (a) a different aligner, (b) noisy alignments, and (c) gold alignments where available? On CELEX, can you vary training data for the aligner to simulate low-resource?

3) Significance and variance: Please report confidence intervals or significance tests for the main gains (CELEX average, Wiktionary average, SIGMORPHON per-language), and provide standard deviations across folds/runs.

4) Fairness of baselines: For SIGMORPHON and Wiktionary, can you retrain the soft-attention baseline under exactly the same training budget and model selection protocol you use for the hard model, and report side-by-side? For Wiktionary, confirm identical splits and preprocessing to prior work.

5) Efficiency: Can you provide training/inference speed and memory comparisons to a soft-attention baseline? One motivation for hard attention is efficiency; quantitative evidence would strengthen the case.

6) Ablations: What is the effect of removing the explicit previous-symbol feedback, varying the feature embedding design, or weakening the biLSTM encoder? Do gains persist if you allow a limited form of soft attention around the current pointer (local window)?

Overall, the paper presents a solid, practically useful method with strong empirical results, but would benefit from clearer positioning relative to prior monotonic transduction work, stronger experimental controls, and analysis of alignment dependence.
