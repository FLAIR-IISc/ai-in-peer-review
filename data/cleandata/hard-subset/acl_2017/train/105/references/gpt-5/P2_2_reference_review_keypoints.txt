- Introduces a hard monotonic attention encoder–decoder for character-level morphological inflection using WRITE/STEP actions and precomputed alignments to derive oracle action sequences for cross-entropy training.
- Provides extensive empirical evaluation on CELEX, Wiktionary, and SIGMORPHON 2016, showing strong low-resource gains and competitive or better performance on larger datasets.
- Offers interpretable, monotonic alignment modeling suited to suffixing morphology with a simple training scheme that avoids forward–backward marginalization.

- Strength: Simple, well-justified inductive bias—STEP/WRITE matches near-monotonic inflection and enables straightforward training with oracle alignments.
- Strength: Strong low-resource performance—outperforms neural and WFST baselines on CELEX and shows reduced overfitting versus soft attention.
- Strength: Competitive large-data results—best reported average on Wiktionary, indicating the hard-attention bias does not hurt with ample data.
- Strength: Careful analyses—alignment inspection and SVD of encoder states explain differences between hard and soft attention and why hard attention may generalize better.
- Strength: Clear presentation and reproducibility—model, datasets, and code (anonymized) are clearly described and based on public tools.

- Weakness: Overclaims state-of-the-art on SIGMORPHON—hard attention is not top overall and underperforms on harmony/templatic languages; claims should be calibrated.
- Weakness: Limited ablations—no quantification of sensitivity to alignment errors, alternative aligners, or training without oracle alignments.
- Weakness: Unclear decoding/search details—paper omits whether greedy or beam search was used and the effects of beam size on action-sequence search errors.
- Weakness: Missing statistical significance—no confidence intervals or significance tests reported despite small performance differences in some conditions.
- Weakness: Scope/generalization limits—method struggles with non-monotonic phenomena (harmony, templatic morphology) and lacks discussion of extensions to handle them.

- Question: What decoding strategy is used at test time (greedy vs. beam), what beam width if any, and how sensitive are results to it?
- Question: How sensitive is performance to the choice and quality of the external aligner; were alternative or noisy aligners tested?
- Question: Can you provide an ablation training without external alignments (joint alignment learning) to isolate the benefit of oracle supervision?
- Question: Did you try augmenting the action space (e.g., limited backward moves) or architectural tweaks to better capture harmony/templatic phenomena, and with what effect?
- Question: For Wiktionary, are splits and preprocessing identical to prior work, and what hyperparameter tuning protocol was used to ensure comparability?

- Overall: Technically sound, clearly presented, and empirically validated with valuable low-resource gains and competitive large-data performance; moderate novelty but worthwhile contribution that merits acceptance pending calibrated claims and modest additional ablations/details.
