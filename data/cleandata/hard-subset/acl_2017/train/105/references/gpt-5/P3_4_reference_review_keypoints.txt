- Proposed model: encoder–decoder with hard monotonic attention using "step" and "write" actions, trained on oracle action sequences from external character alignments.
- Contribution: A practical hard, monotonic pointer-based attention trained with cross-entropy over action sequences as an alternative to soft attention and marginalization-based models.
- Contribution: Strong empirical performance — outperforms prior systems on CELEX, best average on the Wiktionary suite, competitive on SIGMORPHON 2016, especially for suffixing+stem-change languages.
- Contribution: Analysis of learned alignments and encoder representations explaining improved generalization in low-data settings and positional encoding trade-offs.
- Strength: Modeling choice matches typical monotonic alignment structure in inflection (especially suffixation) and provides an interpretable, compact control set (step/write).
- Strength: Demonstrates strong low-resource performance and robustness, with reduced overfitting relative to soft-attention models.
- Strength: Remains competitive in high-resource settings, indicating the inductive bias does not hurt with more data.
- Strength: Clear situating relative to RNN-T, segmental, and WFST approaches and simpler optimization by using external alignments (no forward–backward).
- Strength: Informative qualitative analyses (alignment visualizations, SVD of encoder reps) showing cleaner character identity encoding and reliance on control for position.
- Weakness: Reliance on external CRP-based aligner creates pipeline brittleness; no sensitivity analysis to aligner choice, errors, or alternatives.
- Weakness: Trains on a best-derivation approximation instead of marginalizing derivations; impact of this objective mismatch is unanalyzed.
- Weakness: Decoding details are under-specified (greedy vs. beam, constraints to prevent pathological sequences, termination handling), affecting reproducibility.
- Weakness: Limited analysis and diagnostics for languages with non-local or non-monotonic phenomena (harmony, templatic morphology, circumfixation) where it lags soft attention.
- Weakness: Fairness/comparability issues in baselines (different training regimes, proprietary classifiers); lacks variance reporting and statistical tests.
- Weakness: Missing ablations and capacity controls (feature embeddings, encoder/decoder sizes, feedback connections) to identify essential components.
- Question: How sensitive is performance to external alignment quality; have alternative aligners or noisy/aligned ensembles been tested?
- Question: What exact decoding strategy is used at test time and what constraints ensure termination and prevent pointer overruns?
- Question: Can you provide ablations on morpho-syntactic feature embeddings, previous-output feedback, and encoder/decoder sizes and their effects?
- Question: Can the model be augmented with limited non-monotonic moves (skip/backstep or local re-attend) to handle harmony/templatic languages without losing low-resource advantages?
- Question: If MED and the soft-attention baseline are trained under the same protocol (epochs, early stopping), do the relative rankings on CELEX and SIGMORPHON change?
- Overall assessment: Solid, well-executed paper with a clear idea, strong empirical results (notably low-resource) and useful analysis; acceptance is recommended pending robustness/ablations on alignment dependence.
