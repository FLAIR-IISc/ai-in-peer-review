Summary
The paper proposes a neural encoder–decoder model for morphological inflection generation with a hard, nearly monotonic attention mechanism. The decoder operates over a simple action space consisting of WRITE(symbol) and STEP, where STEP advances a pointer over a biLSTM-encoded input. Training uses externally induced character-level alignments to derive oracle action sequences, enabling straightforward cross-entropy training without marginalization over alignments. Experiments on CELEX (low-resource), Wiktionary (large), and SIGMORPHON 2016 (multi-typology) show strong accuracy, especially in low-resource settings. The paper also analyzes learned alignments and encoder representations, comparing hard and soft attention behaviors.

Contributions (as assessed)
1) A simple hard monotonic attention model for character-level transduction trained with oracle action sequences derived from external alignments, enabling effective learning with small datasets.
2) Empirical evidence that this approach attains or surpasses competitive baselines across three benchmarks, notably outperforming a tailored latent-variable WFST on CELEX.
3) Analysis of alignments and representations indicating that both soft and hard attention learn monotonic alignments, with differences in positional encoding and interpretability.

Strengths
- Strong low-resource performance with clear evidence. On CELEX, the model attains the highest average exact-match accuracy (89.44) versus MED (87.62), NWFST (87.65), and LAT (88.3), and is the first neural model reported to surpass Dreyer et al.’s latent-variable WFST on this dataset. Learning curves indicate better generalization (higher dev accuracy) than soft attention, consistent with the monotonicity bias reducing overfitting.

- Simple, stable training via oracle actions. By decoupling alignment induction from transduction and training with cross-entropy over WRITE/STEP actions, the method avoids the complexity of marginalizing alignments (as in RNN-T/segmental models), yet achieves competitive results. This simplicity is a practical strength, especially for low-resource settings.

- Competitive or superior results at scale. On Wiktionary, the model achieves the best reported average (96.55), with per-language wins (e.g., DE-N, FI-NA, NL-V) over strong neural and non-neural baselines (Durrett & DeNero 2013; Nicolai et al. 2015; Faruqui et al. 2016; Yu et al. 2016). This supports robustness beyond niche low-resource scenarios.

- Interpretability and alignment faithfulness. The action sequence formalism produces explicit alignments; qualitative examples show linguistically plausible monotonic alignments (e.g., deletion via multiple STEP, one-to-many via consecutive WRITE). The representation analysis (SVD plots) offers insight into how positional and character identity information are encoded differently under hard vs. soft attention.

- Comprehensive evaluation across typologies. Results on SIGMORPHON 2016 cover 10 languages with varied morphology. The model excels for suffixing+stem-change languages (RU/DE/ES) where monotonic assumptions hold well, aligning empirical outcomes with typological expectations.

Weaknesses
- Limited methodological novelty relative to prior monotonic transduction models. The WRITE/STEP control with an encoder–decoder is closely related to RNN Transducer (Graves, 2012), Neural Transducer (Jaitly et al., 2015), and online segment-to-segment transduction (Yu et al., 2016). The main differences are the use of external alignments and a simpler training objective. Novelty lies primarily in application and training strategy rather than core modeling.

- Dependence on external alignments without sensitivity analysis. Training quality hinges on the CRP-based aligner, but the paper provides no ablation on alignment errors, no comparison to alternative aligners, and no joint-training variant. It remains unclear how robust performance is to noisy or suboptimal alignments.

- Objective mismatch and decoding details. The model optimizes best-derivation rather than best-output (no marginalization over derivations). While effective empirically, the paper does not quantify the impact of this approximation or report decoding/search details (e.g., greedy vs. beam), which could affect both accuracy and reproducibility.

- Weaker performance on non-suffixing/long-distance phenomena. On SIGMORPHON, hard attention underperforms soft attention on languages with vowel/consonant harmony or templatic morphology (e.g., TU, HU, NA, AR). The proposed model has no mechanism to handle non-monotonic or long-range dependencies beyond what the encoder captures; additional inductive bias or constrained non-monotonicity might be needed.

- Comparison fairness and significance. Some reported advantages are small (e.g., Wiktionary average 96.55 vs. 96.34), and statistical significance is not reported. The SIGMORPHON comparison notes differences in training protocol (epochs, dev selection) versus MED, potentially confounding claims. An explicit matched setup or significance tests would strengthen conclusions.

Questions to Authors (optional)
- Decoding/search: Do you use greedy decoding or beam search at test time? If beam, what width and what is its impact on accuracy and speed?

- Alignment sensitivity: How sensitive is performance to the choice and quality of the external aligner? Can you report results with an alternative aligner and/or with noisy alignments?

- Derivation vs. sequence objective: Did you try marginalizing over derivations (e.g., by dynamic programming) or approximations thereof? If not, can you quantify how often multiple derivations map to the same output and whether best-derivation decoding causes errors?

- Non-monotonic phenomena: Can the control mechanism support limited backward moves or skips to better handle prefixation/infixation or templatic changes? Any preliminary results?

- Significance: Are the improvements on CELEX and Wiktionary statistically significant over strong baselines?

Additional Comments
- Clarity: Some notation is overloaded (e.g., x for both input symbols and encoded vectors). A brief decoding algorithm box and explicit mention of search strategy would aid reproducibility.

- Reporting: Training time, model size, and inference speed compared to soft attention baselines would be useful, given the claimed simplicity.

- Code: The paper mentions code availability; ensure artifacts and scripts reproduce reported splits and results after de-anonymization.
