Summary and contributions
- Proposes a simple hard monotonic attention encoder–decoder for character-level morphological inflection. The decoder issues WRITE(symbol) and STEP actions over a bi-LSTM encoding, with attention implemented as a single pointer advanced by STEP. Training uses externally induced character alignments to derive gold action sequences and a standard cross-entropy objective (best-derivation approximation).
- Provides empirical evaluation on CELEX (very small), Wiktionary (large), and SIGMORPHON 2016 (medium, typologically diverse). The model is particularly strong in low-resource settings and competitive overall, outperforming soft attention in suffixing/stem-change languages.
- Includes qualitative analysis of alignments and encoder representations, suggesting both soft and hard models learn largely monotonic alignments and position-sensitive encodings, with the hard model relying less on positional encoding due to explicit STEP actions.

Strengths
- Methodological simplicity and clarity: The transition-system view with WRITE/STEP and supervision from external alignments makes training straightforward and avoids complex marginalization. This is a clean engineering instantiation of monotonic transduction aligned with classical WFST intuition.
- Strong performance in low-resource regimes: On CELEX, the approach surpasses both neural and non-neural baselines, including a tailored latent-variable WFST. This addresses a known weakness of soft-attention seq2seq and previous neural WFST approaches in small-data settings.
- Broad, multi-dataset evaluation with typological analysis: Evaluating on CELEX, Wiktionary (7 language/inflection sets), and SIGMORPHON (10 languages) provides a reasonably comprehensive picture. The analysis connecting gains to monotonic/suffixing morphology is well-motivated and consistent with results.
- Interpretability/analysis: Side-by-side alignment visualizations and SVD plots of encoder states are informative and help demystify how the models exploit monotonicity and positional cues. This adds value beyond raw numbers.
- Practicality: Using an off-the-shelf aligner and a cross-entropy objective is computationally attractive compared to transducer models that marginalize over alignments. The explicit pointer mechanism is also efficient at inference.

Weaknesses
- Novelty relative to existing monotonic transducers is limited: The core idea (monotonic alignment, pointer-based decoding, train from external alignments) is close to prior art (RNN Transducer, Neural Transducer, online segment-to-segment transduction). The main difference is reliance on externally induced alignments and an explicit STEP action. The paper should more carefully delineate novelty, and include stronger comparisons to alignment-supervised or monotonic-attention variants trained with forward–backward.
- Reliance on external alignments without robustness analysis: The model’s supervision critically depends on the quality of the CRP-based aligner, yet there is no ablation on alignment noise or comparison across aligners. It is unclear how sensitive performance is to alignment errors, especially for phenomena like one-to-many or deletions. The best-derivation approximation can amplify alignment bias.
- Incomplete fairness and rigor of baseline comparisons: For SIGMORPHON, the paper notes different training regimes (100 epochs and dev model selection vs. MED’s 20-epoch fixed training), confounding claims. Capacity/regularization parity with soft attention is not thoroughly controlled (e.g., do smaller soft models still overfit on CELEX?). No statistical significance tests are reported despite small absolute differences on several conditions.
- Limited generality: Performance drops on languages exhibiting non-monotonic or harmony/templatic phenomena (e.g., Turkish, Maltese, Arabic). The monotonic constraint is a modeling assumption; the paper justifies the drop but does not propose mitigations (e.g., limited reordering, occasional backward steps, hybrid soft/hard attention) or provide error analyses on these cases.
- Missing details on decoding and search: The method appears to decode greedily; beam size, determinism, or constraints are not documented. Given the best-derivation objective, search errors could be material. Without a beam or marginalization, comparisons to methods doing exact/approximate marginalization are less conclusive.

Questions to authors
- Decoding/search: Do you use greedy decoding or a beam? What beam sizes affect accuracy/latency? Any length normalization or constraints on STEP/WRITE counts?
- Alignment robustness: How sensitive are results to the choice/quality of the aligner? Can you report ablations comparing different aligners, and training with noisy/no-alignment supervision?
- Capacity/regularization control: On CELEX, if you reduce soft-attention capacity or add stronger regularization (dropout/weight decay/early stopping), does its dev performance still trail your method? Please provide a controlled ablation.
- Significance: Are the improvements on Wiktionary and SIGMORPHON significant (paired tests over types or folds)? Please report tests for key comparisons.
- Non-monotonic phenomena: Can the controller support limited non-monotonicity (e.g., a BACKSTEP or COPY-JUMP), and if so, does that help on harmony/templatic languages without hurting CELEX?
- Derivation vs. sequence objective: Did you try marginalizing over derivations with dynamic programming given the external alignment lattice, or training with policy gradient to reduce derivation bias?
- Reproducibility: Please summarize hyperparameters, training schedules, and model sizes in the main text, and clarify whether the released code exactly reproduces the reported numbers under anonymization constraints.

Overall, this is a solid, well-executed engineering contribution with convincing low-resource gains and a coherent story about monotonicity. However, claims of methodological novelty should be tempered, and the paper would benefit from stronger robustness/ablations, fairer training parity, and significance testing.
