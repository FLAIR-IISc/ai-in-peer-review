- Proposes a hard, nearly monotonic attention model for character-level morphological inflection with WRITE and STEP actions over a biLSTM-encoded input pointer.  
- Trains from externally computed character alignments to deterministically map training pairs to action sequences, using cross-entropy and avoiding marginalization.  
- Shows strong empirical performance, especially in low-resource settings (CELEX) and on suffixing languages with stem changes; competitive on Wiktionary and SIGMORPHON.  
- Contribution: simple, effective hard monotonic attention trained from external alignments, offering a competitive best-derivation approximation and straightforward training.  
- Contribution: empirical evidence that hard monotonic attention improves low-resource inflection accuracy and is competitive on larger benchmarks.  
- Contribution: analyses comparing hard vs. soft attention give insight into why hard attention generalizes better with small data.  
- Strength: strong low-resource empirical performance (CELEX best average accuracy 89.44), with learning-curve evidence that hard attention overfits less than soft attention.  
- Strength: simple and reproducible training via external alignments and cross-entropy, outperforming some alignment-marginalizing models.  
- Strength: competitive large-dataset results (Wiktionary average 96.55) and typologically grounded analysis showing when monotonicity helps or hurts.  
- Strength: clear analyses and visualizations showing near-monotonic soft attention and differing encoder representations, explaining data efficiency.  
- Weakness: methodological novelty is incremental relative to prior monotonic transduction models (RNN Transducer, Neural Transducer, Yu et al.).  
- Weakness: reliance on an external CRP-based aligner without robustness analysis or comparison to alternative aligners.  
- Weakness: objective mismatch (best-derivation vs. best-sequence) is noted but not quantified or analyzed for its impact.  
- Weakness: decoding and search details are underspecified (greedy vs. beam, constraints to prevent pointer errors not discussed).  
- Weakness: baseline fairness and completeness could be improved (consistent tuning, ablations to isolate gains).  
- Question: What decoding strategy is used at test time (greedy vs. beam), and do beams or constraints affect pointer/sequence errors?  
- Question: How sensitive is performance to the choice/quality of the external aligner; were alternatives or noisy alignments tested?  
- Question: How often do multiple derivations exist for the same output, and does best-derivation training disagree with best-sequence decoding; can beam search mitigate this?  
- Question: Are Wiktionary data splits identical to baselines, and how was comparability ensured?  
- Question: Can you report training/inference speed and memory relative to soft attention and alignment-marginalizing models to support the simplicity claim?  
- Question: How are morpho-syntactic features represented and shared—single model per language/inflection type or fully shared across paradigms?  
- Overall: Technically sound, clearly written, and empirically convincing for low-resource inflection; moderate novelty but practically impactful pending clarifications on decoding, alignment robustness, and baseline tuning.
