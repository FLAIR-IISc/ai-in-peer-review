- Main contribution: A hard monotonic attention encoder–decoder using WRITE(symbol) and STEP actions, trained from external character alignments with standard cross-entropy instead of alignment marginalization.
- Main contribution: Simple, effective model tailored to near-monotonic character alignments with strong low-resource and competitive multilingual results.
- Main contribution: Analyses comparing hard vs. soft attention (alignment patterns and encoder representations) to explain model behavior.

- Originality/novelty: Moderate—concepts build on prior monotonic/hard-attention and transducer work; novelty in specific design and oracle-alignment training.
- Technical soundness: Generally solid and well-motivated for monotonic transduction; uses best-derivation approximation rather than marginalization (acknowledged).
- Empirical evaluation: Strong and comprehensive across small, large, and typologically diverse datasets with competitive baselines.
- Clarity: Clear architecture and training description; helpful figures, though some implementation details are in the supplement.
- Reproducibility: Promised code and available aligner, but more hyperparameters, decoding details, and ablations are needed.
- Significance: High for morphological inflection and near-monotonic transduction, especially in low-resource settings.

- Strength: Strong low-resource performance and simplicity that outperforms prior neural and non-neural baselines on CELEX.
- Strength: Competitive across data scales and languages, achieving best average accuracy on Wiktionary and wins on multiple subsets.
- Strength: Well-argued inductive bias (STEP mechanism) aligned with near-monotonic morphology and supported by analyses.
- Strength: Training efficiency and simplicity by avoiding forward–backward marginalization; uses standard cross-entropy.
- Strength: Informative qualitative analyses (alignment visualizations and representation projections).

- Weakness: Incremental novelty relative to prior monotonic/hard-attention and transducer models.
- Weakness: Limited ablations on alignment supervision and sensitivity to external aligner quality.
- Weakness: Uses best-derivation approximation without quantifying impact of multiple derivations or alternatives.
- Weakness: Comparisons could be more complete/fair—additional recent strong baselines and standardized training regimes needed.
- Weakness: Missing decoding and efficiency details (greedy vs beam, beam width, speed/memory comparisons).

- Question: Is decoding greedy or beam search; if beam, what width and how sensitive are results?
- Question: How often do multiple action sequences map to the same target and did you try marginalizing over or n-best oracle training?
- Question: How sensitive are results to the choice/quality of the aligner; any experiments with alternative or noisy alignments or joint alignment learning?
- Question: Can you report training/inference time and memory versus soft-attention baselines?
- Question: How does the model generalize beyond suffixing (e.g., templatic, heavy prefixation/infixation); did you try extensions like backward STEP or multi-pointer?

- Overall assessment: Technically sound, clearly presented, and empirically strong with useful low-resource gains; lean toward acceptance pending clarification on alignment sensitivity, decoding, and comparative details.
