- Proposes a neural encoder–decoder with hard, monotonic attention that decodes as step (advance pointer) or write (emit char) actions, trained from externally induced alignments via cross-entropy on oracle actions.
- Evaluated extensively on CELEX, Wiktionary, and SIGMORPHON 2016, showing strong low-resource gains and competitive or state-of-the-art results on larger datasets.
- Provides analysis comparing learned alignments and encoder representations, arguing hard attention better exploits monotonicity, reduces overfitting, and externalizes positional control.
- Main contribution: a simple, effective hard monotonic attention transducer that excels in low-resource morphology tasks.
- Main contribution: a thorough empirical study across multiple benchmarks against strong neural and non-neural baselines.
- Main contribution: qualitative analyses (alignments, SVD of encoder states) explaining better generalization in monotonic settings.
- Strength: clear empirical improvements in low-resource settings (CELEX), outperforming prior neural and some tailored WFST approaches with supportive learning-curve evidence.
- Strength: well-matched inductive bias—the hard monotonic pointer naturally fits predominantly monotonic inflection while allowing insertions/deletions via step/write.
- Strength: competitive or state-of-the-art performance on larger datasets (Wiktionary), often leading or tying on subsets and averaging best results.
- Strength: insightful analyses showing both models learn near-monotone alignments and that hard attention externalizes position information, offering a plausible generalization explanation.
- Strength: simple, stable training by relying on external alignments and cross-entropy, avoiding complex marginalization or transducer training.
- Weakness: heavy dependence on external alignments with no sensitivity analysis across aligners, noisy alignments, or jointly learned alignments.
- Weakness: potential baseline parity issues and lack of statistical significance testing; some baselines may be unevenly tuned or trained.
- Weakness: optimization/search approximation—training/decoding optimizes best derivation not marginal likelihood, with decoding/beam/EOS/runtime details and search vs. model error analysis underspecified.
- Weakness: limited handling of non-monotonic morphology (e.g., harmony/templatic languages) and no experiments with modest relaxations of monotonicity.
- Weakness: comparability concerns for Wiktionary results (preprocessing/tokenization/tag normalization) and limited quantitative error breakdowns by morphological phenomenon.
- Question: how sensitive is performance to choice and quality of external alignments, including experiments with multiple aligners or noisy/reduced alignments?
- Question: what decoding strategy is used (greedy vs. beam), termination and EOS handling, any length heuristics, and average decoding time?
- Question: are differences vs. strong soft-attention baselines statistically significant across datasets and languages?
- Question: can the authors provide ablations for components (previous-output embedding, feature embeddings, biLSTM vs. unidirectional encoder) to show component importance?
- Question: did the authors try relaxations of monotonicity (limited backward jumps, hybrid attention) on languages where hard attention underperforms, and what were the results?
- Question: what proportion of examples require non-monotonic oracle action sequences, and what are the main error categories where the model fails?
