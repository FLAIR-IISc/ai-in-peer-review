- Proposes a hard monotonic attention encoder–decoder for morphological inflection using two actions (WRITE, STEP) and externally computed character alignments for supervised action prediction.  
- Training optimizes a simple cross-entropy loss on oracle action sequences derived from alignments, avoiding complex marginalization.  
- Shows strong empirical results on CELEX (low-resource), Wiktionary (large), and SIGMORPHON 2016, with notable low-resource gains.  
- Main contribution: simple hard-monotonic attention with step/write control that provides a strong inductive bias for near-monotone morphology.  
- Main contribution: extensive evaluation demonstrating competitive or state-of-the-art performance across multiple benchmarks, especially in low-resource settings.  
- Main contribution: qualitative analyses of learned alignments and encoder representations explaining why hard attention suits suffixing and stem-change languages.  
- Strength: strong low-resource performance and reduced overfitting relative to soft attention, matching morphological monotonicity.  
- Strength: simplicity and stable training by reducing the problem to supervised action prediction using external alignments.  
- Strength: competitive scaling to larger datasets, often outperforming prior neural and alignment-coupled models on Wiktionary.  
- Strength: interpretability through analysis of attention pointer behavior and encoder representations (e.g., SVD plots).  
- Strength: clear empirical link between linguistic typology and model suitability (excels on suffixing+stem-change, weaker on harmony/templatic languages).  
- Weakness: reliance on an external aligner without ablation or sensitivity analysis, leaving unclear how much gains depend on alignment quality.  
- Weakness: limited capability for non-monotonic phenomena (cannot look back), with no explored mitigations like backsteps or hybrid mechanisms.  
- Weakness: training/decoding optimize best-derivation rather than marginalizing over derivations; no quantitative comparison to a forward-sum (CTC-like) objective.  
- Weakness: experimental fairness and statistical rigor concerns (unequal training epochs, lack of significance tests, small gains in some cases).  
- Weakness: missing ablations (e.g., embeddings, decoder feedback, beam size, encoder depth) and limited systematic error analysis by edit type.  
- Question: how sensitive is performance to external aligner quality and to noise in oracle actions?  
- Question: what decoding strategy and beam size were used, and how does decoding affect results across datasets?  
- Question: can the authors report statistical significance on main comparisons (CELEX, Wiktionary)?  
- Question: did the authors try a forward-sum marginalization objective over the step/write lattice, and what are the computational constraints?  
- Question: would allowing limited non-monotonicity (e.g., backsteps) improve performance on harmony/templatic languages?  
- Overall: solid, well-motivated contribution with elegant method and strong empirical results; recommend acceptance after addressing fairness/statistics and alignment-robustness concerns.
