Summary and main contributions
- The paper proposes a hard monotonic attention model for character-level morphological inflection generation. The model uses a bidirectional encoder and a decoder that alternates between two actions: step (advance a single-pointer over the encoded input) and write (emit an output character). Training is supervised with externally induced character alignments; the model learns to predict action sequences via cross-entropy.
- Empirically, the model achieves strong results across three benchmarks: (a) it sets a new state of the art on CELEX (low-resource) and on average on the Wiktionary datasets, and (b) is competitive on SIGMORPHON 2016, outperforming soft attention for suffixing+stem-change languages and trailing slightly on languages with more complex non-local morphophonology.
- The paper includes qualitative analysis contrasting learned alignments and encoder representations in hard vs. soft attention, arguing that the hard model leverages monotonicity explicitly and encodes positional information less heavily in representations.

Strengths
- Clear, simple modeling tailored to morphology. The step/write control mechanism tightly matches near-monotonic alignments typical of many inflectional systems (especially suffixing and stem changes). The formulation is easy to implement and reason about, and the training objective is straightforward cross-entropy on action sequences rather than marginalizing over alignments.
- Strong low-resource performance. On CELEX, the model outperforms prior neural and non-neural systems, including the strong latent-variable WFST (Dreyer et al., 2008), which several recent neural systems failed to surpass on this dataset. The learning-curve plot supports the claim that the hard attention inductive bias combats overfitting when data is scarce.
- Competitive/highest results on larger data. On the Wiktionary benchmarks, the model achieves the best average accuracy and wins on several language/part-of-speech settings, outperforming both neural baselines without attention and a recent align-and-transduce model that learns alignments jointly (Yu et al., 2016).
- Insightful qualitative analysis. The comparison of alignments shows that soft attention also tends to be monotonic in this task, validating the modeling choice; the visualization of encoder representations is informative, suggesting differences in how models encode type vs. position information. This helps explain where the bias of each attention mechanism pays off.
- Good positioning relative to related work. The paper situates the approach with respect to soft attention seq2seq, RNN Transducer/CTC-style models, neural WFSTs, and align-and-transduce systems, making clear the design trade-offs (external alignments, best-derivation decoding).

Weaknesses
- Novelty versus prior monotonic transduction models is incremental. The hard-attention pointer with step/write and training on external alignments is close in spirit to prior RNN-T/segmental/neural transducer approaches. The main difference is using externally derived hard alignments and a single-step pointer rather than marginalization. While the simplicity is a plus, the conceptual novelty is limited.
- Reliance on external alignments and best-derivation approximation. The method assumes availability and quality of character alignments and optimizes for a single oracle derivation rather than marginalizing over multiple alignments leading to the same output. The paper does not quantify sensitivity to alignment errors or compare different aligners. This is important because alignment quality likely varies across languages and paradigms.
- Limited ablation/stability analysis. There is no ablation for key components: effect of using external alignments vs. learned alignments; effect of removing morpho-syntactic feature embeddings; decoder feedback; beam size vs. greedy decoding; or position of step constraints. Such ablations would clarify whether gains stem from the control mechanism, the inductive bias, or other design choices.
- Fairness/rigor of comparisons could be improved. On SIGMORPHON, the authors attribute better soft attention results partly to training more epochs; on Wiktionary, different systems may use different splits and preprocessing. Statistical significance is not reported except by qualitative statements. A unified training regime and significance tests would strengthen the claims.
- Coverage of non-monotonic phenomena. While the model handles local insertions/deletions/substitutions well, it underperforms soft attention on languages with vowel/consonant harmony and templatic morphology. The paper acknowledges this but does not explore extensions (e.g., limited jumps, multi-pointer, or learned skip lengths) that could preserve efficiency while increasing coverage.

Questions to authors
- How sensitive is performance to the alignment quality? Can you report results with at least two aligners and with noisy alignments to quantify robustness?
- What decoding strategy is used at test time (greedy vs. beam)? Does beam search with small width improve over greedy, and does it mitigate the best-derivation approximation?
- Can you provide ablations isolating the contribution of: (a) external alignments (vs. joint/learned alignment or a trivial monotonic alignment), (b) the morpho-syntactic feature embeddings, and (c) the decoder feedback connection?
- On SIGMORPHON languages where hard attention lags, did you try augmenting the action space (e.g., step-k, jump-back, or copy) or adding limited non-monotonic moves? Any preliminary evidence that small relaxations help?
- Training details and fairness: Were all neural models trained with comparable regularization, early stopping, and epoch budgets? Can you run your soft attention baseline under the same training protocol as the hard model on all datasets and report significance?

Additional comments
- Reporting speed/memory benefits of the hard attention decoder would strengthen the practical case.
- A quantitative alignment analysis (e.g., monotonicity score, fertility statistics) would complement the qualitative figures.
- Clarify end-of-sequence handling and constraints to prevent invalid action sequences (e.g., stepping past the input).
