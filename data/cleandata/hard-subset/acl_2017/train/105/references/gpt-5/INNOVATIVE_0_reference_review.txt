Summary and contributions
- The paper proposes a neural encoder–decoder with hard, monotonic attention for morphological inflection generation. The decoder emits either WRITE(character) or STEP actions over a bidirectional character encoder, effectively modeling a pointer-based, nearly monotonic alignment.
- It trains the model using external character-level alignments to derive oracle action sequences, enabling simple cross-entropy training (best-derivation approximation) rather than marginalizing over alignments.
- Extensive experiments on CELEX, Wiktionary, and SIGMORPHON-2016 show strong performance, including notable gains in low-resource settings, and an analysis comparing hard vs soft attention in terms of alignments and learned representations.

As I see them, the main contributions (in order):
1) A simple and effective hard monotonic attention formulation for morphological inflection that leverages external alignments to achieve data-efficient training and strong accuracy, especially in low-resource regimes.
2) Empirical results that (i) surpass strong WFST baselines on CELEX and (ii) match or exceed SOTA on larger datasets for several languages, demonstrating robustness and practicality.
3) An insightful qualitative analysis contrasting hard vs soft attention alignments and encoder representations, shedding light on what these models learn for morphology.

Strengths
- Strong, novel-in-context modeling choice tailored to the task: The hard pointer with STEP/WRITE is a clean, task-aligned abstraction for nearly monotonic transductions. While hard/monotonic attention variants exist in other domains, the specific formulation and its use with external alignments for inflection is both simple and well-motivated. It decisively exploits the inductive bias of monotonicity that many morphological systems exhibit.
- Substantial low-resource gains: On CELEX, the model surpasses Dreyer et al.’s latent-variable WFST and recent neural baselines. This is practically significant: neural seq2seq models often struggle in low-data settings; showing a neural approach that is competitive and even superior here is noteworthy.
- Competitive to SOTA at scale: On Wiktionary and SIGMORPHON, the approach is generally on par or better than strong neural and non-neural baselines on several language sets (e.g., German verbs, Finnish nouns/adjectives, Dutch verbs; suffixing+stem-change languages in SIGMORPHON). This breadth of evaluation increases confidence in the approach’s general utility.
- Training simplicity and efficiency: By using independently learned alignments, training reduces to standard cross-entropy without forward–backward or REINFORCE, yet still delivers accuracy comparable to alignment-marginalizing models. This lowers engineering complexity and is attractive for practitioners.
- Insightful analysis: The alignment visualizations demonstrate that soft attention also tends to learn monotonic alignments, validating the modeling bias. The representation analysis (SVD) provides plausible hypotheses about how positional information is encoded differently under hard vs soft attention.

Weaknesses
- Limited novelty relative to prior hard/monotonic transduction work: The STEP/WRITE transition system and reliance on external alignments are closely related to classic string transduction/WFST setups and to neural monotonic transducers (e.g., Graves 2012; Jaitly et al. 2015; Yu et al. 2016). The main novelty is the particular instantiation for morphology with external alignments and the empirical finding that it works very well. Readers may view the method as an incremental recombination rather than a fundamentally new modeling paradigm.
- Dependence on external alignments with limited analysis of sensitivity: The approach’s success hinges on the quality of the character aligner, but the paper provides no ablations w.r.t. different aligners, alignment noise, or gold vs. learned alignments. It would be useful to quantify how robust performance is to alignment errors, especially in low-resource settings.
- Best-derivation approximation and decoding details: The paper acknowledges not marginalizing over derivations but does not analyze its impact. It is also unclear whether decoding uses greedy or beam search and whether alternative derivations to the same output (e.g., variable STEP/WRITE sequences) materially affect performance or search errors.
- Applicability beyond nearly monotonic languages: The SIGMORPHON results suggest that for templatic or harmony-heavy systems, soft attention slightly outperforms. The paper states this but does not propose extensions (e.g., limited non-monotone jumps, swap operations, or hybrid attention). This limits the method’s generality across typologies.
- Evaluation/reporting details: Some comparisons (e.g., vs. MED) hinge on different training regimens (epochs, dev usage). The paper should ensure parity in training protocols, clarify any ensembling, and give full hyperparameter details to strengthen fairness and reproducibility.

Questions to authors
- Alignment sensitivity: How sensitive are results to the choice/quality of the aligner? Can you report performance with an alternative aligner or with perturbed alignments?
- Decoding/search: Do you decode greedily or with beam search? If beam, what size? Any evidence of search errors due to the best-derivation training objective?
- Ablations: Can you quantify the contribution of (i) external alignments vs. joint alignment learning, (ii) feature embeddings, and (iii) biLSTM encoder depth/size?
- Generalization to non-monotone phenomena: Have you tried allowing limited non-monotone operations (e.g., a bounded jump or SWAP)? Could a small set of such transitions close the gap on harmony/templatic languages?
- Efficiency: How do training/inference speed and memory compare to soft attention baselines in practice?

Overall evaluation
This is a well-executed, task-aligned, and refreshingly simple approach that delivers clear wins in low-resource morphology and remains competitive at scale. Although conceptually related to prior monotonic transduction work, the instantiation for morphological inflection with external alignments and the demonstrated empirical advantages constitute a meaningful contribution. I recommend acceptance.
