- Proposes a neural encoder–decoder with hard, nearly-monotonic attention that emits WRITE(symbol) and STEP actions, trained from external character alignments with cross-entropy.  
- Strong empirical performance in low-resource settings (CELEX) and competitive on larger benchmarks (Wiktionary, SIGMORPHON 2016), especially for suffixing + stem-change languages.  
- Qualitative analyses contrast hard vs. soft attention, showing learned alignments and encoder representations that explain positional encoding differences.

- Strength: Well-motivated inductive bias—monotonic hard attention yields a compact action space that explicitly handles insertions/deletions/substitutions and reduces overfitting.  
- Strength: Outperforms neural and WFST baselines in low-data scenarios, with learning curves showing reduced overfitting vs. soft attention.  
- Strength: Broad, careful empirical evaluation across multiple benchmarks and a useful typological analysis of when the method helps.  
- Strength: Training is simple and efficient by using external alignments, avoiding complex marginalization over alignments.  
- Strength: Insightful qualitative visualizations (alignments, SVD of encoder states) that plausibly explain model behavior.

- Weakness: Limited methodological novelty relative to prior monotonic/hard-attention and transducer models; main novelty is training on external alignments and application to inflection.  
- Weakness: Heavy reliance on CRP-based external aligner with no robustness or ablation study across different aligners or noisy alignments.  
- Weakness: Training uses a single oracle derivation (no marginalization) and decoding/details (greedy vs. beam, exposure bias) are underspecified and unanalyzed.  
- Weakness: Comparative claims are overstated without statistical significance testing; soft-attention baselines sometimes outperform hard model and baseline training regimes may differ.  
- Weakness: Quantitative analysis of learned representations and alignments is limited; claims are mostly qualitative/anecdotal.

- Question: Do you decode greedily or with beam search, what beam sizes/gains were observed, and is the model sensitive to search errors?  
- Question: How are morpho-syntactic features represented and injected, and were their contributions or joint-vs-separate training ablated?  
- Question: How sensitive is performance to the choice of external aligner (CRP vs. Levenshtein, M2M, WFST) and to injected alignment noise?  
- Question: Did you consider marginalizing over derivations, training with multiple oracle sequences, or techniques (scheduled sampling/MRT) to reduce exposure bias?  
- Question: For non-concatenative languages (Arabic, Navajo), can you provide error analyses and would hybrid monotonic+local soft attention help?  
- Question: Please report statistical significance for improvements and guidance on choosing hard vs. soft models given mixed results.

- Additional comment: Recommend controlled subsampling experiments (e.g., 100/500/1k) to illustrate low-resource behavior and bias–variance tradeoffs.  
- Additional comment: Recommend ablations (no features, uni- vs. bi-LSTM, no prev-symbol feedback) to identify key components.  
- Additional comment: Ensure code, data splits, and hyperparameters are fully documented for reproducibility (link was removed in anonymized version).
