Summary and contributions
- The paper proposes a neural encoder–decoder model with hard, monotonic attention for morphological inflection generation. The decoder predicts a sequence of WRITE(symbol) and STEP actions that jointly transduce and monotonically traverse the input. Training uses externally induced character alignments to derive gold action sequences and a straightforward cross-entropy objective.
- Empirically, the model is especially strong in low-resource settings (CELEX), surpassing a tailored latent-variable WFST baseline, and competitive or better on larger datasets (Wiktionary, SIGMORPHON 2016), with a clear typological pattern: advantages on suffixing + stem-change languages.
- The paper includes qualitative analyses of learned alignments and encoder representations, contrasting hard vs. soft attention.

Strengths
- Well-motivated inductive bias with a simple, effective control mechanism. The nearly-monotonic hard attention matches the structure of inflection, enabling a compact action space that explicitly handles insertions, deletions, and substitutions. The model avoids overfitting in low-resource scenarios by constraining alignments and separating alignment learning from transduction.
- Strong performance in low-resource settings. On CELEX, the model outperforms both neural (MED, NWFST) and the strong latent-variable WFST (Dreyer et al., 2008), addressing a known weakness of neural seq2seq approaches. The learning curves substantiate reduced overfitting compared to soft attention.
- Broad and careful empirical evaluation. Results span three established benchmarks with multiple languages and inflection types. The typological analysis (suffixing + stem-change vs. agglutinative/harmony/templatic) helps interpret when monotonic hard attention is advantageous.
- Training simplicity and efficiency. Using external alignments enables straightforward supervised action prediction with cross-entropy, sidestepping the complexity and instability of forward–backward marginalization over alignments (as in RNN-T or segmental models). This is appealing for reproducibility and practicality.
- Insightful qualitative analysis. Visualizations of learned alignments and SVD projections of encoder states offer plausible explanations of why hard attention encodes position less in the encoder (position is explicit in the control), while soft attention encodes it more strongly. The examples illustrate deletion and one-to-many mappings.

Weaknesses
- Limited methodological novelty relative to prior hard/monotonic transduction work. The step/write control and monotonic pointer resemble RNN Transducer/Neural Transducer/segmental models. The main difference is training on external alignments and application to morphological inflection. The paper would benefit from a sharper positioning and more direct empirical comparisons to monotonic models that marginalize alignments, isolating the effect of external alignments.
- Reliance on external alignments without robustness analysis. The approach’s success hinges on the quality of the CRP-based character alignments. There is no ablation on different aligners (e.g., M2M, Levenshtein, WFST-based) or noise sensitivity. If aligner errors are systematic (e.g., for templatic or non-concatenative morphology), the model may inherit them; the SIGMORPHON results hint at this. A study of alignment quality vs. downstream accuracy is needed.
- Best-derivation approximation and decoding details are underspecified. Training optimizes the single oracle derivation rather than marginalizing over equivalent derivations; at test time it appears to use greedy decoding over actions. The paper does not quantify the impact of beam search or alternative derivations on accuracy, nor does it address exposure bias (no scheduled sampling, etc.). These choices could matter, especially when alignments are ambiguous.
- Fairness and statistical significance of comparisons. On SIGMORPHON, the hard model is not the best overall; the soft attention baseline implemented by the authors performs best on average. Claims of “new state of the art on three datasets” therefore read as overstated without consistent wins or significance testing. On Wiktionary, average gains are small; no significance tests are reported. Training regimes differ (e.g., MED reportedly trained for only 20 epochs), which may disadvantage baselines. Clarification and significance testing would strengthen claims.
- Limited quantitative analysis of representations and alignments. The qualitative plots are informative but anecdotal. Quantitative measures (e.g., alignment monotonicity scores, correlation between encoder state and position, probing classifiers for position/type) would substantiate the claims about what the models learn.

Questions to authors
- Decoding: Do you decode greedily over actions or use beam search? If you tried beam search, what beam sizes and gains did you observe on CELEX/Wiktionary/SIGMORPHON? Any sensitivity to search errors (e.g., step overrun)?
- Features: How exactly are morpho-syntactic attributes represented (attribute–value embeddings, tagset embeddings, or both)? Are features injected at every decoder step, and did you ablate their contribution? Do you train one joint model per language with features, or separate models per paradigm/type on all datasets?
- Alignment sensitivity: How sensitive is performance to the external aligner? Have you compared the CRP aligner to simpler (Levenshtein) or M2M aligners, or to supervised WFST alignments? Any results on injecting noise into alignments to assess robustness?
- Best-derivation approximation: Did you consider marginalizing over derivations consistent with a fixed alignment (e.g., insertions after the same input position) or training with multiple oracle action sequences? Could scheduled sampling or minimum risk training reduce exposure bias?
- Non-concatenative phenomena: For Arabic and Navajo, can you provide error analyses showing failure modes (e.g., place of infixation, long-distance harmony)? Would a hybrid model (monotonic hard attention with limited local soft attention) help?
- Comparisons and significance: Please report statistical significance for improvements on Wiktionary and CELEX. For SIGMORPHON, given your soft attention variant outperforms your hard model on average, how should practitioners choose between them?

Additional comments
- A controlled subsampling study on Wiktionary (e.g., 100, 500, 1k examples) would better illustrate low-resource gains and the bias–variance tradeoff relative to soft attention.
- Ablations (no features, uni- vs. bi-LSTM encoder, no feedback of previous symbol) would clarify which components are most important.
- Please ensure code, splits, and hyperparameters are fully documented for reproducibility; the link is currently removed in the anonymized version.
