Summary
This paper proposes a neural encoder–decoder model for morphological inflection generation that uses a hard, nearly-monotonic attention mechanism. The decoder operates over a sequence of actions consisting of WRITE(symbol) and STEP, advancing a single-pointer over a bidirectional-encoder representation. Unlike standard soft-attention seq2seq models, the alignment is not learned jointly; instead, external character-level alignments are used to derive oracle action sequences, enabling simple cross-entropy training. The model is evaluated on CELEX, Wiktionary, and SIGMORPHON 2016, showing strong results—especially in low-resource settings—and includes analyses comparing learned alignments and representations to those of soft attention.

Main contributions (as I see them)
1) A simple and effective hard monotonic pointer-based seq2seq formulation for morphological inflection, trained from external alignments, that achieves strong accuracy and markedly improves data efficiency in low-resource settings.
2) A thorough empirical evaluation across three benchmark suites, with new SOTA on CELEX and competitive or better performance on multiple Wiktionary categories and several SIGMORPHON languages.
3) Insightful analysis comparing hard vs. soft attention: alignment behavior, representation geometry, and overfitting characteristics.

Strengths
- Strong empirical performance and data efficiency: The model is the first neural approach (to my knowledge) to surpass the specialized latent-variable WFST (Dreyer et al., 2008) on CELEX, a notoriously small dataset. The learning curves on CELEX support the claim that the hard monotonic bias reduces overfitting relative to soft attention while maintaining comparable training accuracy.
- Well-motivated inductive bias for the task: Morphological inflection is often nearly monotonic (especially suffixing with stem changes). The STEP/WRITE transition system closely matches this structure and enables conditioning on full input (via bidi-encoder) and output history (via decoder), combining advantages of FST-like structure with neural modeling.
- Simplicity and training stability: Decoupling alignment from transduction allows straightforward cross-entropy training without forward–backward marginalization. This contrasts with RNN-T/segment-to-segment approaches that optimize over alignments and can be harder to train or tune.
- Broad and careful evaluation: Results are reported on three widely used datasets, with comparisons to strong neural and non-neural baselines (MED, NWFST, LAT, semi-CRF, etc.). The model is robust on large-scale Wiktionary sets and particularly strong on suffixing+stem-change languages in SIGMORPHON.
- Useful analysis and interpretability: The alignment visualizations show that soft attention also tends to be monotonic, supporting the modeling choice; the SVD plots suggest differences in how position information is encoded (soft attention encodes position more strongly, hard attention leverages the control mechanism), shedding light on why the hard model generalizes better with limited data.

Weaknesses
- Reliance on an external character aligner without sensitivity analysis: The approach depends on the quality of precomputed alignments (Sudoh et al., 2013). There is no ablation showing robustness to alignment errors, nor a comparison among different aligners. This leaves open whether gains stem from the modeling choice versus alignment quality.
- Best-derivation rather than best-sequence decoding: The paper acknowledges the mismatch between maximizing over derivations and sequences but does not quantify its impact. Details on decoding (greedy vs beam size, tie-breaking, time complexity) are missing. It is unclear whether modest beam search improves accuracy or mitigates derivation ambiguities.
- Comparability and training-budget concerns: On SIGMORPHON, the paper suggests their soft-attention implementation outperforms MED due to longer training (100 vs 20 epochs). This makes it hard to disentangle modeling from tuning effects, and similar concerns could apply to other baselines. Significance tests are not reported despite statements of “significant” improvements.
- Limited coverage of non-monotonic phenomena: While the hard model competes well on suffixing+stem-change languages, performance drops on languages with vowel/consonant harmony or templatic morphology (e.g., Turkish, Arabic). The paper frames this as typological suitability but does not explore architectural variants (e.g., limited backward steps, local jumps, or hybrid hard/soft mechanisms) that might broaden coverage.
- Incomplete reproducibility details: While code is said to be available (link removed), many implementation choices are in the supplement, and some key hyperparameters are not in the main text (embedding sizes, dropout/regularization, beam width, early stopping). Given the simplicity claim, a concise but complete recipe would be valuable.

Questions to Authors (for rebuttal)
- Alignment sensitivity: How does accuracy vary with different aligners or with injected alignment noise? Can you provide an ablation where you (a) switch aligners and (b) retrain with perturbed alignments to quantify robustness?
- Decoding details: Do you use greedy or beam decoding? What beam widths were tried, and how much do they help? What is the runtime complexity vs soft-attention baselines?
- Best-derivation approximation: Did you attempt any marginalization approximations (e.g., shallow lattices, n-best oracle actions) or minimum-risk training to reduce the derivation/sequence mismatch?
- Regularization and hyperparameters: What regularization (dropout, weight decay) was used? Can you share the exact encoder/decoder sizes, feature embedding sizes, and optimization schedule for the main results?
- Low-resource beyond German: Do the CELEX conclusions hold for other languages in truly low-resource regimes (e.g., SIGMORPHON subsets with 100–500 examples)? Any evidence on broader typological coverage under low data?
- Hybrid or adaptive strategies: Did you try allowing a small number of backward steps or a fallback soft-attention layer for languages with long-range dependencies? Could a typology- or data-driven switch improve the weaker languages?

Additional comments and suggestions
- Consider reporting statistical significance (paired tests) for main tables, especially CELEX and Wiktionary averages.
- An ablation comparing: (i) hard attention with external alignments, (ii) hard attention trained to learn alignments jointly (e.g., via REINFORCE/straight-through), and (iii) soft attention with stronger monotonic constraints would clarify where the gains come from.
- Provide a brief error analysis by phenomenon (e.g., one-to-many grapheme mappings, harmony, templatic patterns) to guide future modeling extensions.
- Reporting training time and memory would strengthen the claim of simplicity/efficiency.

Overall, this is a solid, well-motivated paper with clear empirical benefits and a nice analysis component. The main reservations concern reliance on external alignments and incomplete controls on training budgets and decoding details. Addressing these would make the contribution stronger.
