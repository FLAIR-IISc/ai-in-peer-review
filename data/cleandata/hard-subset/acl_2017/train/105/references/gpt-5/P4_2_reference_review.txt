Summary
The paper addresses morphological inflection generation as a character-level sequence transduction problem and proposes an encoder–decoder model with hard, monotonic attention. The decoder operates over a sequence of actions (WRITE of a character in the target alphabet or STEP to advance an attention pointer over the bi-directionally encoded input). Training uses externally induced, character-level alignments to derive an oracle sequence of actions, enabling straightforward maximum-likelihood training via cross-entropy without marginalizing over alignments. The approach aims to exploit the near-monotonic nature of many inflectional processes, improve data efficiency, and reduce overfitting compared to soft-attention seq2seq models. The model is evaluated on CELEX (low-resource), Wiktionary (larger), and SIGMORPHON 2016 (typologically diverse). It reports state-of-the-art or competitive accuracy overall and particularly strong results in low-resource settings. The paper also analyzes learned alignments and encoder representations, comparing hard and soft attention.

Main contributions (as assessed)
1) A hard, monotonic attention seq2seq formulation for character-level inflection that decouples alignment learning from transduction and trains with simple cross-entropy on oracle action sequences derived from external alignments.
2) Empirical evidence that this model achieves state-of-the-art or competitive performance across multiple benchmarks, with notable gains in low-resource conditions (CELEX), and competitive performance on Wiktionary and SIGMORPHON 2016 where monotonicity holds better.
3) Comparative analysis illustrating how hard and soft attention differ in alignment behavior and positional encoding, offering insight into why hard attention may generalize better under data scarcity.

Strengths
- Strong performance in low-resource settings: On CELEX (≈500 training examples per type), the hard attention model surpasses prior neural and non-neural baselines, including a specialized latent-variable WFST model. The learning-curve evidence shows reduced dev-set overfitting relative to soft attention despite similar training accuracy, supporting the claim that explicit monotonic control improves generalization with little data.

- Simple, stable training objective: By converting the task to predicting a sequence of actions given externally obtained alignments, the approach obviates the need for joint alignment/transduction training or forward–backward marginalization. This simplifies optimization, uses cross-entropy with teacher forcing, and likely contributes to data efficiency and training stability.

- Thorough empirical coverage and competitive results: The paper evaluates across three established benchmarks with multiple languages and typological profiles. On Wiktionary, it achieves the best average accuracy across language/part-of-speech subsets considered; on SIGMORPHON 2016 it is best or comparable for suffixing languages with stem alternations. This indicates robustness beyond the smallest data regimes.

- Clarity of model specification: The action-based decoder, control mechanism, and encoder are described clearly; the distinction between best-derivation training and sequence-level marginalization is explicit. The use of feature embeddings for morphological attributes is well integrated.

- Insightful qualitative analysis: The alignment visualizations (monotonic behavior, one-to-many handling) and SVD projections of encoder states (character identity and position information) provide plausible explanations for observed behaviors and highlight differences between hard and soft attention.

Weaknesses
- Limited novelty relative to established monotonic transduction frameworks: Prior work (e.g., RNN Transducer; online segment-to-segment neural transduction; neural transducer with external alignments) also exploits monotonic alignments and action-like decoding. The main novelty here is the specific application to morphological inflection with a STEP/WRITE pointer mechanism and training on external hard alignments. While valuable, the conceptual advance over existing monotonic models is incremental.

- Dependency on external alignments and best-derivation approximation: Training relies on a separate character aligner (CRP-based), and decoding optimizes a single derivation rather than marginalizing over alignments that yield the same output. The impact of alignment errors on training and inference is not quantified. No ablations compare different aligners, alignment quality, or marginalization vs. best-derivation, limiting evidence for the chosen design.

- Mixed performance on typologically challenging languages: On SIGMORPHON 2016, the hard model underperforms a strong soft-attention baseline on languages with non-local phenomena (vowel/consonant harmony, templatic morphology). While the paper acknowledges this with a typological explanation, it suggests the approach is less suitable when monotonicity or locality is weaker. Hybrid strategies or augmented control for long-range dependencies are not explored.

- Fairness and completeness of comparisons: The paper’s soft-attention baseline is reported to be stronger than MED partly due to longer training (100 epochs with dev selection vs. 20 fixed epochs). Conversely, some comparisons (e.g., to WFST baselines) may have configuration differences outside the authors’ control. Statistical significance testing is not reported for gains on Wiktionary/SIGMORPHON, and ablations (e.g., without feature embeddings, without last-symbol feedback, or with beam search) are missing, making it hard to isolate which components drive improvements.

- Some missing methodological details: Decoding strategy (greedy vs. beam), effect of beam width, and computational efficiency are not specified in the main text. Hyperparameter settings are deferred to supplementary material, and there is no learning-curve analysis for larger datasets to support robustness claims beyond CELEX.

Questions to Authors (optional)
- What decoding strategy is used at test time (greedy vs. beam)? If beam search is used, what width, and how sensitive are results to it?
- How sensitive is performance to the external alignment quality? Have you compared different aligners or measured alignment error rates vs. end-task accuracy?
- Did you evaluate marginalizing over alternative derivations at decoding time (e.g., via constrained search) to mitigate the best-derivation approximation?
- Can you provide ablations quantifying the contribution of: (a) morphological feature embeddings, (b) the last-symbol feedback into the decoder, and (c) bidirectional vs. unidirectional encoders?
- Are the improvements on Wiktionary and SIGMORPHON statistically significant? If so, under which test and significance level?

Overall assessment
The work is clearly presented, technically sound, and empirically convincing for nearly monotonic inflectional transduction, with demonstrated gains in low-resource settings and competitive performance otherwise. The main limitation is modest conceptual novelty relative to prior monotonic transduction models and reliance on external alignments without ablation. Nonetheless, the empirical results and analysis constitute a meaningful contribution.
