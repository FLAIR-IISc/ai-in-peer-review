Summary and main contributions
This paper proposes a hard monotonic attention model for morphological inflection generation. The model uses a biLSTM encoder over character sequences and a decoder that alternates between two actions: WRITE(symbol) and STEP (advance an attention pointer over the encoded input). Training is supervised by externally induced character alignments that are converted into gold action sequences. The authors optimize a cross-entropy loss over action sequences (best-derivation approximation), rather than marginalizing derivations.

As I see it, the primary contributions are:
- Methodological: a simple, explicit, pointer-controlled hard attention transducer trained from external alignments, which exploits near-monotonicity typical of inflection while conditioning on full input and output histories.
- Empirical: strong results across three benchmarks (CELEX, Wiktionary, SIGMORPHON 2016), with especially notable gains in low-resource settings (CELEX), surpassing a strong latent-variable WFST baseline and recent neural baselines.
- Analysis: qualitative analyses of learned alignments and encoder representations, showing that both hard and soft attention models learn largely monotonic alignments and encode character identity and positional information, with differences in how position is represented.

Strengths
- Strong low-resource performance and compelling motivation. The model leverages task structure (near-monotonic alignments) and external alignments to guide learning, yielding substantial gains on CELEX where neural soft-attention models typically overfit. The train/dev curves support the claim that the inductive bias helps generalization.
- Simple, well-engineered architecture with clear training signal. Decoupling alignment induction from sequence modeling enables straightforward cross-entropy training without forward–backward or latent-variable marginalization. The step/write control is interpretable and provides a natural avenue for handling insertions and deletions (multiple WRITEs after a STEP; multiple STEPs before a WRITE).
- Broad empirical coverage with competitive or state-of-the-art accuracy. The model is best on several Wiktionary settings and competitive overall; on SIGMORPHON, it is strongest on suffixing/stem-change languages, aligning with its monotonic bias. These results substantiate the claim that hard attention is robust beyond the tiny CELEX regime.
- Insightful qualitative analysis of alignments and embeddings. The alignment visualizations demonstrate that soft attention often learns monotonicity anyway, validating the modeling choice. The SVD plots suggest both models encode identity and position, but hard attention relies less on positional encoding in the encoder, plausibly aiding data efficiency.
- Clear relationship to prior work. The paper situates itself relative to soft attention, WFSTs (latent variables and neural weighting), and monotonic transducers (RNN-T, neural transducer), highlighting training and inference differences.

Weaknesses
- Best-derivation approximation and reliance on external alignments. The model maximizes over derivations (Eq. 2) rather than marginalizing to solve Eq. 1. This can bias learning when multiple action sequences yield the same output or when alignments are noisy. There is no ablation quantifying sensitivity to alignment quality or showing performance when jointly learning alignments vs. using external ones. Since the approach’s advantage heavily hinges on alignment supervision, robustness checks are important.
- Limited handling of non-monotonic and long-distance phenomena. Results degrade on languages with harmony, templatic morphology, and circumfixing. The model’s strictly forward pointer disallows revisiting earlier input and may be too restrictive when surface forms require non-local dependencies. The paper frames this as expected, but does not explore simple extensions (e.g., limited backward moves, jump/skips, or a COPY action) or hybridizing with soft/location-aware attention for such typologies.
- Comparability and experimental rigor. Some baselines (e.g., MED) are trained under different regimes (20 vs. 100 epochs). The paper’s own soft-attention implementation outperforms MED, suggesting training protocol matters; however, the hard vs. soft comparison should be controlled with identical hyperparameters, early stopping, regularization, and ensembling settings. For Wiktionary, preprocessing/split consistency across compared systems is not fully detailed, and older systems may use different splits. This limits the conclusiveness of average accuracy comparisons.
- Evaluation metrics and error analysis are limited. Only exact match is reported. Edit distance or per-feature accuracy would reveal whether errors are superficial or systematic (e.g., suffix choice vs. stem alternation). A typology-conditioned error analysis would strengthen claims about when monotonic control helps or hurts, beyond averaged numbers.
- Decoding/search details and efficiency not reported. It appears decoding is greedy over actions; no beam width, runtime, or memory comparisons versus soft attention are given. Given the focused context of hard attention, speed and efficiency could be a strong selling point, but is not quantified.

Questions to authors
- How sensitive is the model to alignment quality? Please provide an ablation with different aligners or with injected alignment noise, and compare to a variant trained without external alignments (e.g., learned alignments via marginalization or reinforcement learning).
- What decoding strategy is used (greedy vs. beam)? Does beam search improve accuracy, particularly for languages with more ambiguity? What is the typical action sequence length relative to output length?
- Can you report edit distance and/or per-feature accuracies, and a small typology-aware error analysis, to substantiate claims about phenomena where the model excels or struggles?
- Are the hard and soft attention models trained with identical model sizes, dropout, early stopping, and ensembling? If not, can you add a controlled comparison?
- What is the runtime/memory footprint at training and inference compared to soft attention? Do you observe consistent speedups due to focused attention and linear-time pointer movement?
- For languages with non-monotonic processes, did you try simple extensions (e.g., a limited backward move or a COPY action) and if so, with what effect?

Additional comments
- The qualitative representation analysis is interesting; a more quantitative probing (e.g., linear classifiers predicting character identity/position/features from encoder states) would make the claims stronger.
- A learning-curve study on Wiktionary (subsampled data) across typologies would generalize the CELEX low-resource findings beyond German.
- Clarity: briefly formalize how many-to-one and one-to-many alignments are converted into step/write actions; the examples help but a precise mapping rule would aid reproducibility.
- Releasing code and alignment scripts is appreciated; ensuring identical data splits with prior work (or clearly marking differences) will help future comparisons.

Overall, this is a well-motivated and practically useful contribution: a simple hard-attention transducer trained from external alignments, with strong low-resource benefits and competitive performance more broadly. Addressing robustness to alignment quality, tightening experimental controls, and expanding analysis would further strengthen the paper.
