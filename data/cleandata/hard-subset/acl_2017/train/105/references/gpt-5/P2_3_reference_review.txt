Summary and main contributions
This paper proposes a hard, nearly monotonic attention model for character-level morphological inflection generation. The decoder issues two actions: WRITE(symbol) and STEP, advancing a single attention pointer over a biLSTM-encoded input. Training uses externally computed character alignments to deterministically map each training pair to an action sequence; the model is then trained with standard cross-entropy, avoiding marginalization over alignments. Experiments on CELEX (low-resource), Wiktionary (large), and SIGMORPHON 2016 show strong performance, particularly in low-resource settings and on suffixing languages with stem changes. The paper also includes qualitative analyses of learned alignments and encoder representations compared to soft attention.

As I see them, the key contributions are:
1) A simple, effective hard monotonic attention model trained from external alignments, with a competitive best-derivation approximation and straightforward training.
2) Empirical evidence of superior accuracy in low-resource inflection (CELEX) and competitive or better performance on larger benchmarks (Wiktionary; and on some SIGMORPHON languages), demonstrating the benefits of monotonic hard attention for this task.
3) Analysis comparing hard vs. soft attention (alignment behavior and learned representations), offering insight into why the hard model generalizes better in small data.

Strengths
1) Strong empirical performance in low-resource settings with clear justification. On CELEX, the model attains the best average accuracy (89.44) over four tasks, surpassing MED (87.62), NWFST (87.65), and the strong latent WFST (LAT, 88.3). The learning-curve evidence shows soft attention overfits while hard attention generalizes better, aligning with the modeling assumption of monotonicity. This directly addresses a known weakness of seq2seq models on small datasets.

2) Simple, sound training via external alignments and cross-entropy. By deriving gold action sequences from independently learned alignments, the authors avoid complex marginalization and still achieve competitive accuracy, including outperforming the alignment-marginalizing neural transducer of Yu et al. (2016) on Wiktionary. This simplicity improves reproducibility and efficiency while leveraging a plausible monotonic inductive bias.

3) Competitive results on larger datasets and typologically grounded analysis. On Wiktionary, the model achieves the best reported average (96.55), with wins on DE-V, FI-NA, NL-V. On SIGMORPHON, although the hard model’s average (95.61) trails the authors’ soft baseline (96.34), it outperforms soft attention on suffixing+stem-change languages (RU, DE, ES). The typological analysis is cogent: monotonic alignment suits suffixing/stem-change phenomena, while vowel/consonant harmony and templatic morphology benefit from softer, more global attention.

4) Clear analysis of learned alignments and representations. The visualizations show that soft attention also learns near-monotonic alignments, supporting the modeling assumption. The SVD analysis suggests the soft encoder encodes position more strongly, whereas the hard model can rely on explicit STEP control; this offers a plausible explanation for improved data efficiency.

Weaknesses
1) Novelty is incremental relative to prior monotonic transduction models. The write/step control and monotonic pointer are closely related to RNN Transducer (Graves, 2012), Neural Transducer (Jaitly et al., 2015), and Yu et al. (2016). The main novelty lies in training with external alignments and applying the design to morphological inflection with an explicit derivation target. This is a meaningful but modest methodological contribution; the paper would benefit from a sharper delineation of what is fundamentally new versus adapted.

2) Reliance on external alignments without robustness analysis. The model’s training depends on the quality of the CRP-based aligner (Sudoh et al., 2013), yet there is no study of alignment errors’ impact, nor comparison to alternative aligners (e.g., M2M). Given morphology’s many-to-many and deletion/insertion patterns, demonstrating robustness or alignment sensitivity would strengthen the claim that decoupling alignment and transduction is advantageous.

3) Objective mismatch (best-derivation vs. best-sequence) is acknowledged but not quantified. The model maximizes the probability of a single derivation, not the marginal over derivations for a string. While the approximation works empirically, the absence of an analysis of how often multiple derivations exist and whether this causes decoding errors is a limitation. A small ablation or oracle analysis would help.

4) Decoding details and search are underspecified. The paper seems to use greedy decoding (“when the most probable action is step…”), but does not discuss beam search or constraints (e.g., preventing attention overrun). Given the structured nature of action sequences, a short analysis of search errors and whether beam size affects accuracy would be useful, especially for cases with multiple insertions/deletions.

5) Fairness and completeness of baselines could be clearer. On SIGMORPHON, the authors’ soft attention baseline outperforms MED, and they attribute part of the gap to training longer. While this is honest, it also underlines that training protocol matters; it would be fair to ensure comparable tuning across all neural baselines. Also, more ablations (e.g., training the same hard model without external alignments; or with marginalization) would isolate where the gains come from.

Questions to authors
- What decoding strategy do you use at test time (greedy vs. beam)? If greedy, does a small beam help? Any constraints to prevent pointer errors or ill-formed action sequences?
- How sensitive is performance to the choice/quality of the external aligner? Did you try alternatives (e.g., M2M-G2P) or noisy alignments to test robustness?
- Do multiple derivations frequently exist for the same output? If so, how often does best-derivation training disagree with best-sequence decoding, and does beam search mitigate this?
- For Wiktionary, are the splits identical to those used by all baselines? If not, how did you ensure comparability?
- Can you report training/inference speed relative to soft attention and to alignment-marginalizing models (e.g., Yu et al.)? The simplicity claim would be strengthened by time/memory metrics.
- How are morpho-syntactic features represented and shared across paradigms? Is there a single model per language, per inflection type, or fully shared?

Overall assessment
Technically sound, clearly written, and empirically convincing on low-resource inflection, with competitive results elsewhere. The methodological novelty is moderate but the design is elegant and practically impactful. With clarifications on decoding, alignment robustness, and fair baseline tuning, this would be a strong contribution. I lean positive.
