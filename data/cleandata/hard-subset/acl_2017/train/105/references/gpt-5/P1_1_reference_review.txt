Summary and claimed contributions
This paper proposes a neural encoder–decoder with hard, monotonic attention for morphological inflection generation. The model operates with a discrete control mechanism over a biLSTM encoder: at each decoder step, it either writes a symbol or advances a pointer (step) to the next input position. Training uses externally computed character alignments to derive oracle sequences of WRITE/STEP actions, enabling straightforward cross-entropy training rather than learning alignments jointly. The method is evaluated on CELEX (low-resource), Wiktionary (large), and SIGMORPHON 2016 (typologically diverse) and compared to strong neural and WFST baselines. The paper also includes an analysis of learned alignments and encoder representations vs. soft attention.

Main contributions (as I see them)
1) A simple, effective hard monotonic attention transducer for character-level inflection, trained from external alignments with a deterministic oracle and standard cross-entropy, that leverages the near-monotonic nature of the task.
2) Strong empirical results, especially in low-resource settings (first neural model to surpass Dreyer et al.’s latent WFST on CELEX), and competitive/SOTA averages on Wiktionary; clear typological analysis on SIGMORPHON.
3) Insightful qualitative/quantitative analysis comparing hard vs. soft attention alignments and encoder representations, illuminating why hard monotonic control improves generalization.

Strengths
1) Strong gains in low-resource regime with clear generalization benefits. On CELEX, the model achieves 89.44 average accuracy vs. LAT 88.3, MED 87.62, NWFST 87.65. Learning curves show similar training accuracy to soft attention but substantial dev-set gains, supporting the claim that hard monotonic control regularizes the model effectively when data are scarce.

2) Elegant, task-aligned modeling that simplifies training. Reducing transduction to WRITE/STEP over a monotonic alignment is well-motivated for suffixing/stem-change languages. Supervising hard attention with external alignments yields a simple cross-entropy objective and avoids the instability/overfitting of soft attention on small data; no need for forward–backward or marginalizing over alignments.

3) Broad, careful evaluation across datasets and typologies. The model attains the best average accuracy on Wiktionary (96.55) over strong neural and non-neural baselines, and wins on DE-V, FI-NA, NL-V. On SIGMORPHON, it outperforms soft attention on suffixing + stem-change languages (RU, DE, ES) and the paper offers a plausible typological explanation for when monotonic hard attention helps.

4) Useful analysis of alignments and representations. Visualizations show both models learn near-monotonic alignments, but hard attention adheres more closely to linguistically reasonable alignments (e.g., one-to-many WRITE after a STEP). SVD plots suggest soft attention encodes position more strongly, whereas hard attention can rely on its control mechanism, potentially improving sample efficiency.

5) Practicality and reproducibility. The approach is simple to implement, requires no exotic training tricks, and the authors mention code availability and describe datasets/splits drawn from prior work.

Weaknesses
1) Dependence on external alignments without sensitivity analysis. The model’s supervision relies on the Sudoh et al. aligner; no ablation quantifies how alignment quality affects performance, nor comparisons to joint alignment training under the same architecture. This may partly explain gains over models that learn alignments end-to-end (e.g., Yu et al.), and limits applicability where high-quality aligners are unavailable.

2) Objective mismatch and decoding details. The paper optimizes the best-derivation rather than best-sequence objective and acknowledges not marginalizing over derivations, but provides no empirical assessment of this approximation. Decoding appears greedy (choosing the “most probable action”), with no discussion of beam search, termination criteria, or search errors. Runtime/latency and memory advantages vs. soft attention are also not reported.

3) Incremental novelty relative to prior monotonic transduction. Architectures with monotonic alignment and step-like control (RNN-T, Neural Transducer, online segment-to-segment) exist. The main novelty here is applying a supervised hard attention variant with explicit WRITE/STEP and external alignments to inflection generation. While the empirical contribution is solid, the modeling contribution is evolutionary.

4) Limited performance on non-monotonic phenomena without proposed remedies. Results on languages with harmony/templatic morphology (e.g., FI/TU/HU/NA/AR) lag a strong soft-attention baseline (average 95.61 vs. 96.34). The paper stops short of proposing hybrid or lightly non-monotonic extensions (e.g., limited jumps/back-steps or location-aware attention) that might broaden applicability.

5) Baseline comparability and missing ablations. The authors note differences in training schedules (e.g., longer training/selection on dev for their soft attention vs. MED’s fixed 20 epochs), and there are no significance tests. Important ablations are missing: effect of feature embeddings, previous-output feedback, encoder directionality, beam width, or training without alignment supervision (supervised vs. unsupervised attention).

Questions to authors
- How sensitive is performance to the external aligner? Can you report results with alternative aligners, noisy alignments, or a joint training variant to quantify robustness?
- What decoding strategy do you use (greedy vs. beam)? Does a small beam help, and what is the computational cost compared to soft attention?
- How do you handle insertions/deletions in the alignment-to-action mapping (e.g., epsilons)? Are multiple STEP actions the only mechanism for deletions, and does this ever limit expressivity?
- Did you try supervising a soft-attention model with these alignments (e.g., guided attention) to isolate the effect of alignment supervision vs. hard attention control?
- Can you report training/inference speed and parameter counts vs. soft attention? This would strengthen the practicality claim.
- Could a restricted set of non-monotonic moves (e.g., single backward step) improve performance on harmony/templatic languages without hurting monotonic ones? Any preliminary results?
