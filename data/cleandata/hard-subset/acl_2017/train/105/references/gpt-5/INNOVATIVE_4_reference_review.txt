Summary and contributions
- The paper proposes a neural encoder–decoder for morphological inflection with a hard, monotonic attention controller: at each step the decoder either writes a character or issues a step action to advance a single attention pointer along the bi-LSTM–encoded input. The model is trained from externally computed character alignments, converting them to oracle action sequences and using standard cross-entropy.
- It demonstrates strong empirical results, especially in low-resource settings (CELEX), and competitive or state-of-the-art averages on Wiktionary and mixed but explainable outcomes on SIGMORPHON 2016. The analysis compares learned alignments and encoder representations with soft attention.
- Conceptually, this is a clean re-framing of morphological inflection as a nearly-monotone transition system, trading the flexibility of soft attention/latent alignment for simplicity, inductive bias, and robustness.

As I see it, the primary contributions are:
1) A simple, effective hard monotonic attention mechanism for character-level transduction that leverages external alignments, with a “step/write” action space and straightforward cross-entropy training. This is a meaningful conceptual simplification with a strong inductive bias for inflection.
2) Strong low-resource performance and overall-competitive results across diverse benchmarks, including surpassing a specialized latent-variable WFST on CELEX.
3) Insightful analysis of alignments and positional information in encoder representations, clarifying how hard vs. soft attention behave on this task.

Strengths
- Novel and bold modeling choice with clear inductive bias: The hard monotonic controller with explicit step/write actions is a crisp idea for nearly-monotone character transduction. Decoupling alignment learning (via an external aligner) from neural training yields a conceptually simpler and more stable path than joint alignment-learning or forward–backward marginalization. This is an elegant re-casting that feels broadly applicable to other near-monotone tasks.
- Strong low-resource gains and competitive SOTA: On CELEX, the method outperforms prior neural models and surpasses a tailored latent-variable WFST—an important result given the community’s concerns about neural models’ data hunger. On Wiktionary, the approach maintains high accuracy and best average over prior strong systems, showing robustness beyond the “sweet spot” of small data.
- Transparent decoding and interpretable behavior: The explicit action sequence and pointer movement make the model’s operation easy to inspect. The alignment visualizations convincingly show that the learned hard alignments are appropriate and that many soft-attention alignments are, in practice, monotone—supporting the central premise.
- Useful representational analysis: The SVD plots demonstrate that both models encode character identity and position, but the hard-attention encoder seems to offload position to control actions rather than pumping it into the encoding. This sheds light on why the hard model may generalize better in low-resource regimes.

Weaknesses
- Reliance on external alignments, no sensitivity analysis: The approach assumes and depends on a high-quality monotone character alignment. There is no ablation w.r.t. different aligners, alignment noise, or monotonicity violations. It is unclear how robust the method is when alignments are imperfect (e.g., templatic or reduplicative phenomena), or whether joint refinement could help.
- Best-derivation approximation without justification: The model optimizes/provides the most probable action sequence rather than marginalizing over derivations that yield the same output. While effective in practice, there is no comparison against marginalization (e.g., forward–backward) under the same architecture or even a beam-marginal variant; the potential search/credit assignment bias remains unquantified.
- Incomplete decoding details and fair-comparison concerns: The paper does not clearly specify decoding (greedy vs. beam), stopping criterion, or any constraints to prevent degenerate sequences (e.g., excessive writes without steps). On SIGMORPHON, the soft-attention baseline is trained for 100 epochs and selected by dev performance, whereas MED was reported with 20 fixed epochs; it is unclear whether all baselines were tuned equivalently. Statistical significance tests are not reported despite close wins/losses.
- Limited handling of non-monotonic phenomena: The approach inherently cannot move the pointer backward; it depends on monotone alignment. Results drop on languages with harmony or templatic morphology. The paper discusses this qualitatively but offers no architectural extension (e.g., limited jumps/back-steps, segmental actions, or hybrid soft/hard attention) nor experiments quantifying the failure modes.

Questions to authors
- Decoding: Is inference greedy or beam search? What beam size, if any, and what stopping criterion do you use (EOS, end-of-input plus “no more output”, or both)? How do you avoid degenerate loops (e.g., writing indefinitely without stepping)?
- Alignment sensitivity: How does performance vary with alignment quality? Have you tried alternative character aligners (e.g., M2M aligner) or injected noise to test robustness? Can you quantify the fraction of training pairs that violate monotonicity and its impact?
- Derivation marginalization: Did you experiment with marginalizing over action sequences (e.g., via forward–backward) or with a small beam to approximate marginalization? If so, how did it compare; if not, what prevents it (efficiency, stability)?
- Model scope: For Wiktionary and SIGMORPHON, do you train one model per language (and POS), or a single model with tag embeddings across all inflection types? How large is the feature inventory and embedding size, and how critical is sharing across tags?
- Extension to non-monotone types: Do you foresee a principled way to add limited “back-step” or jump actions (or a hybrid soft/hard attention) without losing the simplicity and low-resource benefits?

Overall, I view this as a strong paper: it introduces a clear and impactful modeling idea that improves low-resource character transduction with elegant analysis. Despite some evaluation and robustness gaps, the conceptual novelty and empirical gains warrant acceptance.
