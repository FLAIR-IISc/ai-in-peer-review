Summary and contributions
- This paper proposes a neural encoder–decoder model with hard, monotonic attention for morphological inflection generation. Decoding is framed as a sequence of actions over a single-pointer attention: either WRITE a character or STEP to advance the pointer over a biLSTM-encoded input. The model is trained on oracle action sequences derived from external character-level alignments; training is via cross-entropy without marginalization over alignments.
- Empirically, the model attains strong or state-of-the-art exact-match accuracy on CELEX and Wiktionary benchmarks and is competitive on SIGMORPHON 2016, excelling on suffixing + stem-change languages. The paper also provides qualitative analyses of learned alignments and encoder representations versus soft attention.
- Primary contributions as I see them: (1) A simple, effective hard monotonic attention decoder trained from external alignments, competitive in low-resource settings; (2) empirical evidence that using monotonic control reduces overfitting and helps in small data; (3) qualitative analysis contrasting representational effects of hard vs soft attention.

Strengths
- Clear, task-appropriate inductive bias: The hard monotonic control matches the near-monotonic character alignments in many inflection tasks. The design is simple (WRITE/STEP), expressive enough to model substitutions, deletions, and insertions, and uses a biLSTM encoder to retain global context.
- Strong low-resource performance: On CELEX (only 500 training examples per task), the model outperforms prior neural and non-neural baselines, including a strong latent-variable WFST tailored for this task. The learning-curve comparison suggests reduced overfitting relative to soft attention.
- Competitive large-scale results: On Wiktionary, the model ties or improves over prior best results on several languages and achieves the highest reported average among compared systems. This shows the approach scales beyond low-resource regimes.
- Insightful qualitative analysis: The alignment plots confirm that soft attention also learns monotonic alignments, supporting the modeling assumption. The SVD visualization suggests different representational emphases (positional encoding stronger for soft attention), which is an interesting finding for the community.

Weaknesses
- Missing or unclear decoding/termination details: Σs is defined as Σy ∪ {step}; no explicit STOP/EOS action is described. How does decoding terminate? What prevents infinite loops or premature stopping? Is there a max action budget, and is beam search used? Greedy vs beam and the handling of end-of-input are not specified. This is a critical methodological omission affecting reproducibility and validity.
- Fairness and comparability concerns: On SIGMORPHON, the paper attributes differences partly to training regime differences (e.g., 100 epochs and dev-selection vs MED’s fixed 20 epochs). On Wiktionary, it is unclear if splits, preprocessing, and evaluation strictly match prior work across all languages/types. No significance testing is provided; several reported improvements are small. These issues undermine strong SOTA claims.
- Reliance on external alignments without ablation: The approach depends on a CRP-based character aligner. There is no ablation comparing different aligners, noisy alignments, or learned alignments vs oracle-derived action sequences on the same model. The claim that external alignments are beneficial relative to joint alignment/transduction is plausible but not rigorously tested in a controlled setting.
- Limited treatment of non-monotonic phenomena: While the model is claimed to handle “nearly” monotonic alignment, the performance degrades on languages with templatic morphology, vowel/consonant harmony, or more complex morphophonology (e.g., Arabic). The paper stops at an empirical observation; no extension (e.g., limited look-back/skip operations, local reordering, or fertility modeling) is proposed or evaluated. The analysis is qualitative and does not quantify failure cases by phenomenon.
- Objective mismatch and search approximation not examined: The paper optimizes the best-derivation (not marginal over derivations that yield the same output) and appears to decode greedily. There is no analysis of search/derivation errors, nor of whether marginalization or modest beam sizes improve accuracy. This undermines claims about the adequacy of the approximation.
- Reproducibility and reporting gaps: Critical hyperparameters, regularization, optimizer details, and architecture dimensions are relegated to supplementary material (not provided here). No training time, decoding speed, or memory footprint comparisons are given. The model’s sensitivity to hyperparameters or data size is not explored beyond one learning-curve plot.
- Numerous formatting and citation issues: Repeated/broken citations (e.g., “? \Q2016\E”), inconsistent author name formatting (e.g., “García.Martínez”), footnote markers dangling (“2”, “3”), missing code link (anonymized without alternative), inconsistent notation (e.g., x reused for embeddings and encoded vectors; index i/j mismatch in Eq. 5), and figures/tables with awkward captions. These hinder readability and suggest insufficient proofreading.

Questions to authors
- Decoding specifics: How do you terminate decoding without an explicit EOS action in Σs? Is there an EOS token, a maximum number of actions, or a learned stop criterion? Do you use greedy or beam search? What beam size and with what effect on accuracy?
- Oracle/action sequence derivation: How are unaligned insertions handled when deriving s1:q from alignments? Can the model WRITE without STEP (i.e., fertility > 1 at the same input position)? How are many-to-one alignments handled? Please specify the exact oracle algorithm.
- Data and splits: For Wiktionary and SIGMORPHON, do you use the exact train/dev/test splits and preprocessing of the baselines? If not, please provide harmonized experiments or re-evaluate baselines under your setup.
- Statistical significance: Are the improvements on CELEX/Wiktionary statistically significant (e.g., paired bootstrap)? Please report.
- Ablations: Can you compare (a) external CRP aligner vs another aligner vs joint training on your architecture, and (b) with/without alignment noise injection? Also, please report effects of beam search and of marginalizing derivations (if attempted).
- Non-monotonic phenomena: Have you tried simple extensions (e.g., limited backward STEP, skip, or copy spans) to improve performance on templatic or harmony-heavy languages? Any error analysis partitioned by phenomenon?
- Reproducibility: Please provide core hyperparameters and training schedules (hidden sizes, embedding sizes for chars/features, dropout/regularization, optimizer settings, early stopping), and decoding limits (max actions) in the main paper or an accessible appendix.
