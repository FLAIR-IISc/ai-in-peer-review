Summary
The paper proposes a hard monotonic attention sequence-to-sequence model for morphological inflection generation. The decoder operates over a derived sequence of actions (WRITE(symbol) or STEP), where training uses externally computed character-level alignments to produce oracle action sequences. The model is evaluated on CELEX, Wiktionary, and SIGMORPHON 2016, showing strong performance in low-resource settings and competitive results elsewhere. The paper also includes qualitative analyses comparing learned alignments and encoder representations to a soft-attention baseline.

Main contributions (as I see them)
- A hard-attention, align-and-transduce formulation that leverages external character alignments to train a step/write controller for nearly-monotonic transduction, applied to morphological inflection.
- An empirical study across three established datasets with comparisons to both neural and finite-state baselines, showing particular gains in low-resource conditions (CELEX).
- Qualitative analysis of learned alignments and encoder representations contrasting hard vs soft attention.

Strengths
- Strong low-resource performance: On CELEX, the method surpasses the latent-variable WFST (Dreyer et al., 2008) and recent neural baselines, addressing a known weakness of encoder-decoder models on very small datasets. The inductive bias toward monotonicity and explicit control appears effective and practically relevant.
- Sensible modeling choice for morphology: The step/write mechanism matches the quasi-monotone nature of many inflectional processes, yielding a compact transition system that is easier to train (simple cross-entropy) than models that marginalize over alignments.
- Breadth of evaluation and baselines: The paper reports results on three commonly used benchmarks with comparisons against both neural (attention/no attention) and WFST-based approaches. The use of standard splits for CELEX and coverage of multiple languages and morphological types is appreciated.
- Interpretability and qualitative analysis: The alignment visualizations and representation probing (SVD projections) provide insights into how the model exploits positional and character identity information, and how step decisions implement insertions/deletions. While qualitative, these analyses help demystify the mechanism.

Weaknesses
- Limited methodological novelty relative to established hard-alignment transducers: The step/write control with monotonic attention trained on external alignments is close in spirit to RNN Transducer/Neural Transducer/online segment-to-segment transduction. The main differences are in using externally derived alignments and cross-entropy training on oracle actions. The contribution feels incremental without a stronger theoretical or algorithmic advance (e.g., principled handling of derivation/sequence marginalization or improved search).
- Objective mismatch and reliance on external alignments: Training maximizes the best derivation rather than the sequence likelihood, and the approach depends on an external aligner (CRP-based). This introduces potential error propagation and limits end-to-end learning. There is no ablation quantifying sensitivity to alignment quality, nor a comparison against learned-alignment variants under identical capacity.
- Gains are small and not consistent across settings; no significance testing: On Wiktionary, average improvements over strong baselines are marginal (e.g., 96.55 vs 96.34). On SIGMORPHON, the hard-attention model is worse on average than the soft attention baseline reported here. Claims of state of the art should be qualified and supported with statistical tests.
- Fairness and reporting: Some comparisons hinge on training regime differences (e.g., MED trained for 20 epochs vs authors’ soft baseline with 100 epochs). Hyperparameters, decoding details (beam vs greedy), early stopping criteria, and runtime/memory are not clearly reported in the main text. Without standardized training budgets and significance testing, it is difficult to attribute gains to the modeling choice rather than tuning.
- Limited handling of non-monotonic phenomena: The model underperforms on languages with more complex, non-local morphophonology (e.g., vowel/consonant harmony, templatic morphology). There is no attempt to relax monotonicity minimally (e.g., limited backward moves, jump/skips) or to quantify failure modes, which reduces generality.

Questions to authors
- Decoding: Is inference greedy or beam search? If beam, what size, and how sensitive are results to beam width? What is the computational profile (training/inference speed) vs soft attention?
- Alignment sensitivity: How robust is the model to alignment noise? Have you tried alternative aligners or perturbed alignments? Can you report performance when training on different alignment sources?
- Objective mismatch: Did you try marginalizing (even approximately) over derivations that yield the same output, or using a beam for training (e.g., sequence-level losses) to reduce exposure bias?
- Ablations: What is the contribution of each component (feature embeddings f, previous-output embedding in the decoder, biLSTM vs uniLSTM encoder)? Can you isolate the gain due specifically to the hard attention vs other architectural choices?
- Statistical tests and reproducibility: Please provide paired significance tests on key datasets and ensure fair, matched training budgets across baselines. Also report full hyperparameter settings and decoding configuration.
