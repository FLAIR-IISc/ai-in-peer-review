Summary
This paper proposes a hard monotonic attention model for morphological inflection generation. The model is an encoder–decoder RNN with a single attention pointer over a bi-LSTM-encoded input; at each time step the decoder chooses either to WRITE a character or STEP the pointer forward. Training is simplified by using externally computed character-level alignments to derive an oracle sequence of WRITE/STEP actions, allowing standard cross-entropy training rather than marginalizing over alignments. The model is evaluated on CELEX (low-resource), Wiktionary (large), and SIGMORPHON 2016 (10 languages), and compared against strong neural and non-neural baselines. The approach achieves new SOTA or competitive accuracy overall, with pronounced gains in the low-resource setting. The authors also analyze learned alignments and encoder representations, showing the model captures monotonicity and positional information in a different manner than soft attention.

Contributions (as I see them)
- A simple, effective hard monotonic attention architecture for nearly monotonic character-level transduction, trained from externally induced alignments via an explicit action sequence.
- Strong empirical evidence that this inductive bias improves generalization in low-resource settings (CELEX) while remaining competitive or better on larger datasets (Wiktionary; partially on SIGMORPHON).
- Insightful analysis comparing hard vs soft attention in terms of alignments and encoder state geometry, shedding light on what these models learn for inflection.

Strengths
- Clear inductive bias matched to the task and compelling empirical gains in low-resource settings. On CELEX, the proposed model achieves 89.44% avg accuracy, surpassing both neural (MED 87.62, NWFST 87.65) and the strong latent-variable FST (LAT 88.3). The learning curves show soft attention overfits while hard attention generalizes better—strong evidence the monotonic control and explicit pointer reduce model capacity in beneficial ways.
- Competitive state-of-the-art performance at scale. On Wiktionary, the model attains the best average (96.55) across seven settings and is best on several sub-tasks (e.g., Finnish N/A, Dutch verbs). It outperforms a non-attention encoder–decoder and a joint alignment + transduction model (Yu et al., 2016), supporting the claim that using high-quality alignments to guide attention is advantageous.
- Sound, simple training objective and efficient decoding. By converting alignments into gold action sequences, training reduces to standard XE without dynamic programming over alignments. Decoding is constrained by monotonic pointer movement, reducing search space; the paper shows this “best derivation” approximation works well in practice.
- Analysis improves understanding. The alignment inspection demonstrates both models learn (near-)monotone behavior; the hard model produces linguistically plausible one-to-many and deletion operations via WRITE/STEP sequences. The SVD plots show character clustering and positional gradients, with the soft model encoding position more strongly, consistent with its need to disambiguate soft attention; this supports the architectural rationale.
- Relevance and impact. Morphological inflection is widely used in MT and other morphology-rich NLP pipelines. The method is general to monotonic string transduction (transliteration, certain speech tasks), and the explicit control mechanism is easy to implement and reason about.

Weaknesses
- Dependence on external alignments and lack of sensitivity analysis. The approach relies on Sudoh et al.’s CRP-based aligner; alignment errors could propagate to the oracle action sequences. There is no study of how alignment quality affects performance, nor comparison across different aligners or noisy alignments. This is important because the principal design choice is to decouple alignment learning from transduction.
- Best-derivation approximation without ablation. The model optimizes over derivations (Eq. 2) rather than sequences (Eq. 1) and does not marginalize over derivations leading to the same output. While results are good, there is no analysis of failure cases attributable to derivation mismatch or an ablation comparing to a marginalization/forward–backward variant (even on a subset).
- Limited comparison to alternative constrained attention baselines. The empirical comparison covers standard soft attention (global/Luong; MED) and RNN/WFST variants, but omits monotonic or location-aware attention variants that could narrow the gap (e.g., location-aware attention or strictly monotonic soft attention). A pointer-network or transition-based baseline trained on the same alignments would isolate what the step/write control contributes.
- Mixed results on typologically diverse languages with limited error analysis. On SIGMORPHON, the hard model underperforms a strong soft attention baseline on several languages with long-range dependencies (avg 95.61 vs 96.34). The typological explanation is plausible but not substantiated by targeted evaluation (e.g., breakdown by phenomenon, edit-distance metrics, or qualitative error patterns). This weakens the generality claim beyond suffixing with stem changes.
- Clarity and reproducibility details scattered. Some key implementation details are deferred to the supplement (hyperparameters, decoding strategy, termination conditions, feature embeddings). The anonymized code link is removed; without precise settings (beam vs greedy, epochs, regularization), exact reproduction may be difficult.

Questions to Authors
- How sensitive is performance to the alignment source? Have you tried alternative aligners or injected noise into alignments to quantify robustness? Could you fine-tune alignments during training (e.g., EM-like refinement)?
- What decoding strategy is used (greedy vs beam)? If beam, what width and impact on accuracy/latency? Any evidence of exposure bias and have you tried scheduled sampling?
- Ablations: (a) remove or randomize morpho-syntactic feature embeddings f; (b) train the same architecture without feeding the previous output embedding; (c) replace hard attention with a location-aware soft attention. How do these affect CELEX vs SIGMORPHON?
- Can the model write multiple unaligned characters before stepping (pure insertions), and how do you constrain termination (e.g., EOS, max steps)? How are prefixing/circumfix cases handled when pointer starts at x1?
- Did you perform statistical significance testing on reported gains (especially CELEX and Wiktionary averages)? If so, please report.

Overall assessment
This is a solid, well-motivated paper with a simple, elegant model that yields clear gains in low-resource inflection and competitive performance at scale. The analysis is thoughtful. The main limitations are reliance on external alignments without robustness study and limited comparisons to alternative constrained attention mechanisms. With minor additions/ablations, this would be a strong accept; as is, I lean positive given the clarity, effectiveness, and relevance.
