Summary and Contributions
This paper presents a character-level encoder–decoder model for morphological inflection generation that uses a hard, nearly monotonic attention mechanism controlled by two actions: WRITE(symbol) and STEP. Alignments are computed externally at the character level; these alignments are deterministically converted into oracle action sequences, and the model is trained with cross-entropy to predict the action sequence. The approach is evaluated on CELEX, Wiktionary-derived datasets, and SIGMORPHON 2016, and supplemented by an analysis comparing hard and soft attention in terms of alignments and learned representations.

Contributions (as assessed):
1) A simple, effective hard monotonic attention formulation for nearly monotonic string transduction trained from external character alignments, enabling straightforward supervised training without forward–backward marginalization.
2) Strong empirical results, especially in low-resource settings (CELEX), where the model surpasses prior neural and non-neural methods, and competitive performance on larger datasets, with typology-aware performance analysis.
3) Qualitative analysis of alignments and encoder representations, showing that both hard and soft attention learn largely monotonic alignments and character/position-sensitive encodings, and offering insight into why the hard control mechanism regularizes better in small data.

Strengths
1) Clear performance gains in low-resource regime. On CELEX (500 training examples per type), the model outperforms both neural (MED, NWFST) and the tailored latent-variable WFST (LAT), achieving the highest average accuracy. The training curves show less overfitting compared to soft attention, supporting the claim that hard monotonic control provides beneficial inductive bias under data scarcity.

2) Methodological simplicity with strong motivation. The architecture is straightforward: a biLSTM encoder provides contextualized character representations; a decoder RNN consumes (last action, features, current attended state) and predicts either STEP or WRITE. Using precomputed character alignments to derive oracle actions reduces training complexity to standard cross-entropy, avoiding the need for alignment marginalization and complex objectives. This also facilitates interpretability and stable training.

3) Breadth and quality of evaluation. Results are reported across three established benchmarks with diverse language typologies. On Wiktionary datasets, the model attains the best average across inflection types and improves several language–part-of-speech combinations. On SIGMORPHON 2016, the analysis links performance differences to morphological typology: the model excels on suffixing plus stem-change languages (RU/DE/ES) where monotonic alignment is a good fit, while being competitive but slightly behind soft attention on languages requiring longer-range dependencies (e.g., harmony, templatic systems).

4) Insightful qualitative analysis. The alignment visualizations show that soft attention also largely learns monotonic alignments, validating the core assumption. The SVD projections of encoder states indicate both models encode character identity and positional information; the hard-attention encoder appears to cluster characters more tightly and encode position less strongly, consistent with the decoder having an explicit position/control signal via STEP. This helps explain the generalization advantage with fewer examples.

5) Practicality. The model’s decoding is conceptually simple, and training requires only off-the-shelf character aligners; no specialized dynamic programming is needed. This lowers the barrier to application and reproduction.

Weaknesses
1) Limited novelty relative to existing monotonic/segmental transduction frameworks. The core idea—hard monotonic attention with externally provided alignments and supervised action prediction—has precedents (e.g., Neural Transducer; RNN-Transducer variants; segmental models). The main new aspect here is the concrete instantiation for morphological inflection with a minimal STEP/WRITE control and the empirical demonstration. This is valuable but incremental.

2) Dependence on external alignment quality and lack of ablation. The approach assumes accurate character alignments and derives oracle actions deterministically. The paper does not analyze sensitivity to alignment errors, compare different aligners, or provide an ablation (e.g., training without external alignments, or with noisy alignments). Since errors or biases in alignments directly supervise the decoder, robustness is an open question.

3) Best-derivation approximation without empirical justification. The model optimizes for the most likely action sequence, not the most likely output string, and does not marginalize over multiple derivations mapping to the same output. The potential impact of this approximation is not quantified (e.g., by comparing to beam search with derivation aggregation or by reporting derivation diversity).

4) Fairness and rigor of comparisons. On SIGMORPHON, the paper’s soft-attention reimplementation outperforms MED, possibly due to different training regimes (e.g., number of epochs, model selection). This suggests that baseline tuning effort differs across systems. No statistical significance tests are reported, and it is unclear whether all systems use identical data splits and ensembling practices. These factors complicate the strength of claims about state of the art.

5) Incomplete procedural details. The decoding strategy (greedy vs beam size, tie-breaking, handling of null alignments), the precise derivation of action sequences for insertions/deletions and many-to-one/one-to-many mappings, and key hyperparameters are deferred to supplementary material. Given that performance hinges on these choices, more detail in the main text would improve clarity and reproducibility.

Questions to Authors (for rebuttal)
- Decoding: Do you decode greedily or with a beam? If a beam is used, what size and does it affect accuracy on CELEX/Wiktionary/SIGMORPHON? Did you observe cases where derivation-level search vs sequence-level optimality diverge?

- Alignment supervision: How are NULL→char insertions and char→NULL deletions represented in the action oracle (e.g., WRITE without prior STEP, multiple STEP for deletions)? How are many-to-one and one-to-many correspondences handled deterministically?

- Robustness: How sensitive are results to the choice/quality of the external aligner? Did you try alternative alignment methods or inject noise to assess robustness? Any ablations on training without external alignments (e.g., learned alignments or relaxed marginalization)?

- Comparisons: For SIGMORPHON, can you confirm identical training data usage and selection protocols across baselines (e.g., dev usage, ensembling)? Can you provide significance testing for key comparisons (CELEX averages, Wiktionary averages, per-language SIGMORPHON)?

- Generality: Can the control mechanism handle common non-monotonic phenomena (e.g., internal templatic reordering) beyond small local changes? Did you attempt variants with limited backward moves or learned skip actions?

- Efficiency: What are training and inference speeds relative to soft attention and to forward–backward models (e.g., Yu et al.)? Any memory/runtime comparisons?

Overall, the paper is clear, technically sound, and empirically strong, with an incremental but well-executed methodological contribution and solid analysis.
