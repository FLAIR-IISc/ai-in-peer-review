## Review for "Morphological Inflection Generation with Hard Monotonic Attention"

**Summary:**

This paper presents a novel neural model for morphological inflection generation utilizing a “hard monotonic attention” mechanism. The core idea is to constrain the attention between input and output sequences to be largely monotonic, mirroring the typical structure of morphological changes (e.g., suffixing). The model learns to either "write" an output symbol or "step" to the next input position, guided by pre-computed character-level alignments. The authors demonstrate strong performance on three datasets (CELEX, Wiktionary, SIGMORPHON2016), achieving state-of-the-art results, particularly in low-resource settings.  They also provide analysis comparing their approach to soft attention models, highlighting differences in learned representations.

**Main Contributions (as I see them):**

1. **Effective Hard Attention for Morphological Inflection:** The introduction of a hard monotonic attention mechanism specifically tailored for morphological inflection is a valuable contribution. It addresses the limitations of standard sequence-to-sequence models in low-resource scenarios by imposing a strong inductive bias consistent with the task’s structure.
2. **Strong Empirical Results:** The paper demonstrates compelling empirical results, surpassing previous state-of-the-art models on multiple datasets, notably achieving better performance than WFST models on the CELEX dataset with limited data – a longstanding challenge.
3. **Analysis of Learned Representations:** The comparative analysis between hard and soft attention models provides useful insights into how these models represent morphological information.  The observation that the hard attention model encodes less positional information is intriguing.

**Strengths:**

1. **Addressing a Key Limitation:**  The paper directly tackles a significant problem in neural morphological inflection: the need for large datasets. The hard attention mechanism demonstrably improves performance in low-resource settings, making the approach more practical for morphologically rich languages where labeled data is scarce. The performance on CELEX is particularly noteworthy.
2. **Sound Motivation and Design:** The rationale behind the hard attention mechanism is well-motivated. Morphological inflection *is* often monotonic, and explicitly modeling this property is a sensible design choice. The separation of alignment learning and transduction, while not novel in general, is effective in this context.
3. **Comprehensive Evaluation:**  The paper includes a thorough evaluation on three diverse datasets, covering different languages and morphological complexities. This robust evaluation strengthens the claims about the model's generalizability.
4. **Clear Presentation & Analysis:**  The paper is well-written and clearly explains the model architecture and training procedure. The analysis of learned representations, while preliminary, offers valuable insights.
5. **Reproducibility:** The authors state that the code is available on GitHub (although the link is removed, it's a good sign), supporting reproducibility of the results.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Novelty of Individual Components:** While the *combination* of hard attention and pre-computed alignments is effective, the individual components are not entirely novel. Hard attention mechanisms have been explored in other sequence transduction tasks, and using pre-computed alignments is a common strategy. The main innovation lies in its application to this specific task.
2. **Dependency on Alignment Quality:** The performance of the model is inherently tied to the quality of the pre-computed character-level alignments. While the authors use a standard alignment model, errors in alignment could propagate and impact the overall performance.  The paper doesn't adequately address the potential impact of alignment errors.
3. **SIGMORPHON Results Less Compelling:**  The performance on SIGMORPHON2016, while good, isn't as dominant as on the other datasets. The authors attribute this to language typology, which is reasonable, but it would be valuable to delve deeper into the reasons why the model struggles more with certain language types. Specifically, a more detailed error analysis would be beneficial.
4. **Lack of Ablation Studies:** The paper would benefit from more ablation studies. For example, what is the impact of *removing* the pre-computed alignment, forcing the model to learn alignment implicitly?  This would help to better understand the contribution of each component.



**Questions to Authors:**

1.  How sensitive is the model performance to the quality of the pre-computed character-level alignments? Can you provide some analysis demonstrating the impact of alignment errors on the final accuracy?
2.  Could you elaborate on the specific linguistic characteristics of the languages where the model performs less well on SIGMORPHON2016 (e.g., languages with non-local dependencies)?  Are there potential modifications to the model that could address these challenges?
3.  Would the performance be impacted if we didn’t have the pre-computed alignments? Can you perform an ablation study and report the results.
4.  The paper mentions a forward-backward approach in related work.  Why did you choose to decouple alignment and transduction, instead of exploring a joint learning approach? What were the trade-offs considered?

**Additional Comments:**

* The paper uses a lot of citations. It might be valuable to shorten the list of citations.
* The discussion of related work could be more focused, grouping papers by the specific techniques they employ rather than listing them chronologically.




**Overall Assessment:**

I recommend **accepting** this paper. While the individual components aren't groundbreaking, the combination of hard attention and pre-computed alignments is a novel and effective approach to morphological inflection generation. The strong empirical results, particularly in low-resource settings, and the insightful analysis make this a valuable contribution to the field. The weaknesses identified are relatively minor and could be addressed during the author response phase.
