This paper proposes a hard monotonic attention model for morphological inflection generation, arguing that it addresses the data efficiency issues of standard sequence-to-sequence models. The authors evaluate their model on three datasets (CELEX, Wiktionary, SIGMORPHON2016) and claim state-of-the-art or competitive performance, particularly in low-resource settings. The key contributions, as presented by the authors, are the model itself, the empirical results demonstrating its effectiveness, and an analysis comparing it to a soft attention model.

However, while the paper presents a technically sound approach and achieves reasonably good results, I find several shortcomings that, in aggregate, significantly diminish its overall impact. The core issue is a relentless focus on minutiae and a lack of broader justification for the chosen design.

**Strengths:**

1. **Thorough Experimental Evaluation:** The authors perform a comprehensive set of experiments across three datasets, comparing their model to a respectable set of baselines. This provides a solid empirical foundation for their claims, allowing for a reasonably fair comparison of performance. The inclusion of SIGMORPHON2016, a multi-lingual dataset, is particularly valuable.
2. **Clear Model Description:** The paper provides a detailed and relatively clear description of the proposed hard attention model. The explanation of the control mechanism and the training procedure is reasonably well-articulated, and the diagrams (particularly Figure 1) aid in understanding the model's architecture.
3. **Competitive Performance:** The model achieves competitive results, surpassing previous state-of-the-art on the CELEX dataset, which is encouraging. Performance on Wiktionary and SIGMORPHON2016 is competitive, though not consistently superior across all languages.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Novelty:** The core idea of enforcing monotonicity in sequence transduction is not novel. Previous work, including the RNN Transducer and related models, explicitly addresses alignment constraints. While the "hard" attention mechanism is a specific instantiation, it feels more like an engineering choice rather than a fundamental algorithmic advance. The claim of a novel "hard attention model" is overstated.
2. **Overemphasis on Implementation Details:** The paper dwells excessively on implementation details, such as the LSTM equations relegated to the supplementary material, and various architectural choices, without adequately justifying *why* these choices were made beyond “it worked”. This creates a sense that the work is more about meticulous engineering than about insightful design.
3. **Questionable Significance of CELEX Results:** The primary claim of outperforming on CELEX is undermined by the dataset's small size and limited scope. The performance gains, while statistically significant, might not generalize to more complex morphological systems or languages. Presenting this as a major breakthrough feels disingenuous.
4. **Unclear Motivation for Decoupled Alignment:** The authors explicitly decouple alignment and transduction, arguing that pre-learning alignments is beneficial. However, the justification for this choice is weak. Recent advancements demonstrate the effectiveness of *jointly* learning alignment and transduction. The authors fail to adequately address why their approach is preferable, particularly in light of existing literature. They state they avoid having to marginalize over alignments, however the computational complexity of this is not fully articulated to justify their design choice.
5. **Analysis Lacks Depth:** The analysis comparing the hard and soft attention models is superficial. While the authors observe differences in learned representations, they don’t delve deeply into *why* these differences arise or what implications they have for the model’s behavior. The discussion about positional encoding feels particularly underdeveloped. The visualization in figures 4 doesn't offer substantial insight.
6. **Formatting Inconsistencies and Typos:** Numerous minor formatting inconsistencies and typos exist throughout the paper (e.g., missing periods, inconsistent capitalization, awkward phrasing). While these are minor individually, their cumulative effect detracts from the paper’s professionalism and suggests a lack of careful review.

**Questions to Authors:**

1.  Why did you choose to decouple alignment and transduction, given the success of joint learning approaches in recent literature? Please provide a more detailed justification, addressing the potential drawbacks of your chosen approach.
2.  The paper repeatedly emphasizes the importance of monotonicity. However, morphological inflection often involves non-monotonic transformations (e.g., vowel alternations). How does your model handle such cases, and what limitations does the monotonic constraint impose on its performance?
3.  Can you provide a more in-depth analysis of the learned representations, going beyond the visualization in Figures 4? What specific morphological features are encoded in the representations, and how do these features differ between the hard and soft attention models?
4.  The implementation details regarding training (e.g. optimizer, learning rate schedule) are sparse. What settings were used, and how sensitive is the model’s performance to these hyperparameters?

**Additional Comments:**

*   The paper uses inconsistent notation.
*   The references are not uniformly formatted.
*   Consider a more rigorous error analysis to identify the types of morphological errors the model makes.



**Overall Assessment:**

I recommend **rejection**. While the paper is technically competent and presents some interesting experimental results, the lack of substantial novelty, the questionable significance of certain claims, and the superficial analysis outweigh its strengths. The paper feels overly focused on implementation details and lacks a compelling argument for the chosen design. The numerous minor flaws further detract from its overall quality. A major revision addressing the weaknesses outlined above, along with a more convincing justification for the contributions, would be necessary for reconsideration.
