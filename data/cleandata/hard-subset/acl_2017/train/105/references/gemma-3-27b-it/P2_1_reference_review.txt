## Review for "Morphological Inflection Generation with Hard Monotonic Attention"

**Summary:**

This paper presents a novel neural model for morphological inflection generation, leveraging a hard monotonic attention mechanism. The core idea is to constrain the attention between input and output character sequences to be largely monotonic, reflecting the common patterns in morphological processes. The authors demonstrate strong performance on three datasets (CELEX, Wiktionary, and SIGMORPHON2016), achieving state-of-the-art results, particularly in low-resource settings. They also provide an analysis comparing their model to a soft-attention baseline, offering insights into the learned representations.

**Main Contributions (as I see them):**

1. **Effective Hard Attention for Morphological Inflection:** The most significant contribution is demonstrating the effectiveness of a hard monotonic attention mechanism for this task. This provides a principled way to exploit the inherent sequential nature of morphological changes, leading to improved performance, especially with limited data.
2. **Strong Empirical Results:** The paper presents compelling experimental results, establishing a new state-of-the-art on several datasets. The performance gains on the CELEX dataset, particularly surpassing the latent variable WFST model, are particularly noteworthy.
3. **Analysis and Insights:** The comparison with soft attention and the analysis of learned representations provide valuable insights into the strengths and weaknesses of both approaches, contributing to a better understanding of how these models learn to handle morphological complexities.

**Strengths:**

1. **Novelty & Soundness of Approach:**  The use of hard monotonic attention is a clever adaptation to the specific characteristics of morphological inflection.  The rationale is well-grounded in linguistic principles and addresses a key limitation of standard sequence-to-sequence models – the need for large datasets to learn alignment.  The explicit modeling of alignment (even if pre-trained) offers advantages in interpretability and potentially generalization.
2. **Excellent Experimental Validation:** The thorough experimental evaluation across three diverse datasets is a strong point. The authors don’t just present positive results; they carefully compare against strong baselines (including both neural and traditional methods), demonstrating clear improvements, especially in the low-data regime.  The consistent performance gains across datasets suggest the robustness of the approach.
3. **Clear and Comprehensive Presentation:** The paper is well-written and clearly explains the model architecture, training procedure, and experimental setup. The visual illustration of the hard attention mechanism (Figure 1) is particularly helpful for understanding the model's operation. The ablation studies presented (even implicitly through comparisons) show the value of different components.
4. **Insightful Analysis:**  The analysis of learned alignments and representations provides valuable insights into how the model functions and the differences between hard and soft attention. These findings contribute to a better understanding of the underlying mechanisms driving the performance improvements.
5. **Addressing a Challenging Problem:** Morphological inflection generation is a crucial component of NLP for morphologically rich languages and impacts downstream tasks like machine translation.  The authors tackle a non-trivial problem with a well-motivated and effective solution.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Dependency on Pre-trained Alignments:**  While the authors justify the use of pre-trained alignments, it introduces an external dependency. The performance is tied to the quality of these alignments. The paper would benefit from more discussion on the impact of alignment errors and potential methods to mitigate them (e.g., incorporating alignment confidence scores or learning alignments jointly).
2. **Limited Discussion of Error Analysis:** Although the authors show *that* their model performs well, they offer limited qualitative error analysis.  Providing specific examples of cases where the model fails and discussing the reasons for those failures would further strengthen the work.  Specifically, it would be helpful to understand what kinds of morphological complexities the model still struggles with.
3. **SIGMORPHON Results – Contextualization Needed:** While the SIGMORPHON results are good, the authors state the soft attention model outperforms their approach on *most* languages. The explanation focuses on morphological typology. While reasonable, a deeper dive into which specific morphological features are challenging for the hard attention model in these languages would be valuable.



**Questions to Authors:**

1. **Alignment Sensitivity:**  How sensitive is your model’s performance to the quality of the pre-trained alignments? Have you experimented with different alignment methods or noisy alignments to assess the robustness of your approach?
2. **Error Analysis:** Can you provide specific examples of errors made by your model, along with explanations of why those errors occurred?
3. **Joint Learning:** Have you explored any approaches to jointly learn the alignments and the transduction model, even if it's a preliminary exploration?
4. **Computational Cost:**  Is there a significant computational cost associated with the hard attention mechanism compared to soft attention, particularly during training?

**Additional Comments:**

*  The references are comprehensive.
*  The code availability is a positive aspect.




**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper that presents a novel and effective approach to morphological inflection generation. The hard monotonic attention mechanism is a well-motivated and successful technique, and the experimental results are compelling. While there are some minor weaknesses, the strengths of the paper significantly outweigh them. I recommend **acceptance** with minor revisions addressing the questions and suggestions above.
