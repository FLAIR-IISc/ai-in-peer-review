This paper presents a hard attention model for morphological inflection generation, addressing the challenges of data sparsity and alignment in morphologically rich languages. The core idea is to model a nearly monotonic alignment between input and output character sequences using a dedicated control mechanism within an encoder-decoder framework. The authors evaluate their model on three datasets (CELEX, Wiktionary, and SIGMORPHON2016) and demonstrate competitive or state-of-the-art performance, particularly on small datasets.

**Main Contributions:**

1. **Hard Attention Mechanism for Morphological Inflection:** The introduction of a hard attention mechanism specifically tailored for the near-monotonic alignment characteristic of morphological inflection is the primary contribution. This allows the model to focus on relevant input characters before generating corresponding output characters, offering a more structured approach compared to soft attention.
2. **Strong Performance on Low-Resource Data:** The model demonstrably outperforms previous neural and non-neural approaches, especially on the small CELEX dataset, indicating an improved ability to learn from limited data. This is a significant practical benefit for morphologically rich languages where large, labeled datasets are often unavailable.
3. **Analysis of Learned Representations:** The authors provide an analysis of the representations learned by their model and compare them to those of a soft attention model. This sheds light on how each model encodes morphological information and offers insights into the strengths and weaknesses of each approach.

**Strengths:**

1. **Clear Problem Definition and Motivation:** The paper clearly articulates the problem of morphological inflection generation and its importance for downstream NLP tasks, particularly machine translation. The motivation for addressing the limitations of existing methods, such as the need for large datasets and the handling of monotonic alignments, is well-established.
2. **Solid Experimental Evaluation:** The model is evaluated on three diverse datasets, covering different languages and dataset sizes. The comparison to strong baselines, including both neural and non-neural approaches, provides a comprehensive assessment of its performance. The ablation studies and parameter settings are detailed enough for reproducibility.
3. **Novelty in Control Mechanism:** The hard attention mechanism with the explicit “step” action to control alignment is a novel and well-motivated design choice. This approach allows the model to directly learn the alignment process, rather than relying on independently learned alignments or implicit alignment modeling through soft attention.
4. **Insightful Analysis:** The analysis of learned representations provides valuable insights into the inner workings of the model and how it differs from the soft attention counterpart. This analysis goes beyond simply reporting performance numbers and contributes to a deeper understanding of the task and the models used to solve it.
5. **Reproducibility:** The authors mention the code being available, contributing to the reproducibility of the results.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Discussion of Alignment Error:** While the model focuses on near-monotonic alignments, the paper lacks a detailed analysis of alignment errors. Understanding the types of alignment errors made by the model could provide insights into its limitations and potential areas for improvement.
2. **Dependence on Pre-trained Alignments:** The training procedure relies on independently learned character-level alignments. Although this simplifies training, it introduces an external dependency and could limit the model's ability to learn alignments from scratch. The impact of the quality of these pre-trained alignments is not thoroughly investigated.
3. **Complexity of Implementation:** The architecture involves multiple components (bi-directional RNN encoder, hard attention mechanism, control mechanism) that add to the model's complexity. This could make it more challenging to implement and optimize compared to simpler approaches. While the paper provides a clear explanation, the implementation details might be intricate.
4. **SIGMORPHON Results Nuance:** The discussion of SIGMORPHON results presents a slight inconsistency – stating the soft attention model is now best, despite the tables suggesting the hard attention model is superior or comparable on most languages. This needs clarification.



**Questions to Authors:**

1. How sensitive is the model’s performance to the quality of the pre-trained character-level alignments? Have you experimented with different alignment methods or alignment error rates?
2. Could you elaborate on the types of alignment errors made by the model? Are there specific morphological processes or language features that are particularly challenging for the hard attention mechanism?
3. How does the performance of the model scale with the length of the input and output sequences? Are there any limitations related to long sequences?
4. Can you provide a more detailed analysis of the computational cost and training time compared to the soft attention baseline?

**Additional Comments:**

* The citation style appears inconsistent at times.
* While the paper mentions an implementation on GitHub, a specific link would be beneficial for reviewers.
* The paper is generally well-written and clearly organized. However, some sections could benefit from more concise language and clearer transitions.
