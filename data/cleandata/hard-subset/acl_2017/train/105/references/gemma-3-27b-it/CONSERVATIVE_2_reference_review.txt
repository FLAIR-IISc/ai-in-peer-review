This paper proposes a neural sequence-to-sequence model for morphological inflection generation utilizing a "hard monotonic attention" mechanism. The core idea is to constrain the attention to move forward sequentially through the input, reflecting the common monotonic relationship between source and target word forms in inflectional morphology. The authors demonstrate competitive results on three benchmark datasets (CELEX, Wiktionary, and SIGMORPHON2016), showing improvements, particularly on the small-data CELEX dataset, and offer an analysis of the learned alignments and representations.

The primary contribution of this work is the demonstration that enforcing a hard monotonic alignment constraint within a neural sequence-to-sequence model *can* be beneficial for morphological inflection, especially in low-resource scenarios. While the idea isn't entirely novel – similar constrained attention mechanisms exist – its application and successful evaluation in this specific morphological context represent a worthwhile contribution. A secondary contribution is the thorough empirical evaluation across multiple datasets, providing a strong baseline for future research. The analysis of learned representations, though not deeply novel, adds some insight into how the model functions.

**Strengths:**

1. **Improved Performance on CELEX:** The significant improvement over previous models, including both neural and non-neural approaches, on the notoriously difficult CELEX dataset is a strong point. This highlights the model’s ability to generalize from limited data, a critical requirement for morphologically rich languages.
2. **Sound Methodology:** The chosen experimental setup is well-defined, and the comparison to established baselines (Dreyer et al., Kann & Schütze, Rastogi et al.) is appropriate and rigorous. The ablation study, implicitly presented through comparison with soft attention, is also useful.
3. **Clear Explanation of the Hard Attention Mechanism:** The description of the hard attention control mechanism is clear and well-illustrated (referencing Figure 1). The connection to a transition system with ADVANCE and WRITE actions provides a helpful conceptual framework.
4. **Comprehensive Evaluation:** Testing on three distinct datasets with different characteristics (size, languages) adds confidence to the robustness of the findings.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Novelty of Core Idea:** The concept of enforcing monotonic alignment isn’t groundbreaking. While the *application* to morphological inflection is valuable, the foundational idea of constrained attention is well-established in other sequence transduction tasks. The paper doesn’t sufficiently contextualize its contribution within this broader research landscape.
2. **Dependence on Pre-Aligned Data:** The model relies on externally learned character-level alignments for training. While the authors justify this choice, it introduces a dependency on the quality of the alignment tool and potentially limits the model's ability to learn alignments directly from raw data. A comparison against models learning alignments jointly would be informative. The claim that this simplifies training feels somewhat weak; it shifts the complexity elsewhere.
3. **Analysis is Superficial:** The analysis of learned representations, while present, lacks depth. Simply showing SVD projections of embeddings doesn’t provide substantial insight into *what* features are being encoded. More targeted analyses (e.g., probing for specific morphological features) would be more compelling.
4. **SIGMORPHON Results are Mixed:** While performing well overall, the model is outperformed by the soft-attention baseline on a substantial portion of the SIGMORPHON languages. This suggests that the hard monotonic constraint isn't universally beneficial and that the linguistic characteristics of the language play a significant role. The explanation provided in the paper feels somewhat ad-hoc.

**Questions to Authors:**

1.  How sensitive are the results to the quality of the pre-aligned data used for training? Have you experimented with different alignment tools or methods, and how do they affect performance?
2.  Could you elaborate on the computational cost of the hard attention mechanism compared to soft attention, particularly during training?
3.  The SIGMORPHON results indicate language-specific performance differences. Can you provide a more detailed analysis of *which* language features seem to benefit most (or least) from the hard monotonic constraint?
4.  While you acknowledge the limitation of not learning alignments jointly, have you considered incorporating a mechanism to refine the initial alignment during training?
