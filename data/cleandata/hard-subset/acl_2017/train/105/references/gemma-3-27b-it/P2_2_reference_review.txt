## Review for "Morphological Inflection Generation with Hard Monotonic Attention"

**Summary:**

This paper presents a novel neural model for morphological inflection generation utilizing a "hard monotonic attention" mechanism. The authors argue this approach is particularly well-suited to the task due to the inherent monotonic alignment between input and output character sequences in morphology. They demonstrate strong performance on three standard datasets (CELEX, Wiktionary, SIGMORPHON2016), achieving state-of-the-art results, particularly in low-resource settings. The paper also includes an analysis comparing the learned alignments and representations of their model with those of a soft attention model.

**Main Contributions (as I see them):**

1. **Effective Hard Attention for Morphological Inflection:** The core contribution is demonstrating the effectiveness of a hard monotonic attention mechanism for morphological inflection. While hard attention isn't novel *in general*, its application and success within this specific task, leveraging the task’s inherent structure, is a valuable finding.
2. **Strong Performance in Low-Resource Scenarios:** The model demonstrably outperforms existing methods, especially on the small CELEX dataset, addressing a critical limitation of previous neural approaches. This is a significant practical benefit.
3. **Analysis of Learned Representations:** The comparative analysis of learned representations between hard and soft attention models provides insights into how each model approaches the task and the type of information they encode.

**Strengths:**

1. **Strong Empirical Results:** The paper consistently achieves competitive or state-of-the-art results across three datasets.  The improvement on CELEX, particularly surpassing the tailored latent variable FST, is compelling evidence of the model's effectiveness.  The results table are well-presented and easy to interpret.
2. **Justification for Hard Attention:** The authors provide a strong rationale for using hard attention, aligning it with the inherent monotonic nature of morphological inflection. This is not merely a methodological choice, but a theoretically motivated design decision.  The experimental results support this intuition.
3. **Clear and Thorough Presentation:** The paper is well-written and logically organized. The model architecture is clearly explained, and the experimental setup is detailed.  The inclusion of a visual diagram (Figure 1) is particularly helpful.
4. **Insightful Analysis:** The comparison between hard and soft attention models goes beyond simply reporting performance numbers. The analysis of learned representations provides valuable insights into the strengths and weaknesses of each approach.  Figure 2 highlighting the learning curves provides useful information for understanding model behavior.
5. **Addressing a Key Limitation:** The problem of data scarcity in morphological inflection is well-acknowledged and the model’s success in low-resource settings directly tackles this challenge.



**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Novelty in Core Components:** While the *application* of hard attention to this task is strong, the underlying components (bi-directional RNNs, attention mechanisms) are not novel. The primary novelty lies in the combination and application, and the paper needs to better articulate *why* this combination is particularly impactful compared to simply applying soft attention with regularization or other techniques to enforce monotonicity.  A more comprehensive comparison to such techniques would strengthen the argument.
2. **Dependence on Pre-Computed Alignments:** The reliance on independently learned character-level alignments for training, while simplifying the training process, feels somewhat like a pre-processing step that sidesteps a key learning challenge.  A more end-to-end approach, potentially incorporating a differentiable alignment mechanism, might be more robust and generalizable.  The paper acknowledges this tradeoff but doesn't fully explore its implications.
3. **Analysis Depth Could Be Increased:** While the representation analysis is a good start, it feels somewhat superficial.  Specifically relating the learned features to linguistic properties (e.g., identifying which dimensions capture information about gender, number, tense) would add considerable value. The visualization in Figures 4 could be enriched with labels or annotations.
4. **SIGMORPHON Results Interpretation:** The authors attribute the slight underperformance on some SIGMORPHON languages to “more context sensitive morphological phenomena.” This explanation feels somewhat hand-wavy and lacks a detailed discussion of *how* hard attention struggles with these phenomena.  



**Questions to Authors:**

1.  Have you explored methods to directly learn the alignment within the model, rather than relying on pre-computed alignments? If so, what were the results? If not, can you elaborate on the reasons for this design choice and potential future directions?
2.  The paper mentions the possibility of representing positional information more explicitly in the hard attention model. Could you provide examples of how the network utilizes the “step” action to capture and leverage positional context?
3.  Can you provide a more detailed analysis of the learned representations, relating specific dimensions to linguistic features (e.g., gender, number, tense) of the inflected word?
4.  Regarding the SIGMORPHON results, could you provide specific examples of input-output pairs where the hard attention model fails and discuss why it struggles with those particular instances?




**Additional Comments:**

*   The reference list is extensive.
*   The claim that the model is "much simpler" than soft attention models requires further justification.  The overall architecture is still complex, and the hard attention mechanism introduces its own challenges.
*   The link to the GitHub repository has been removed. This is fine for blind review.




**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper with a valuable contribution to the field of morphological inflection generation. The authors successfully demonstrate the effectiveness of a hard monotonic attention mechanism and achieve state-of-the-art results, particularly in low-resource scenarios. While the novelty of the core components is limited, the combination and application are insightful, and the analysis provides valuable insights. I recommend **acceptance** with minor revisions addressing the weaknesses outlined above.
