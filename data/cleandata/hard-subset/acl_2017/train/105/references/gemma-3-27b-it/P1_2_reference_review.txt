## Peer Review: Morphological Inflection Generation with Hard Monotonic Attention

**Summary:**

This paper proposes a novel neural model for morphological inflection generation utilizing a “hard monotonic attention” mechanism. The core idea is to constrain the attention mechanism to move sequentially through the input sequence, reflecting the typical monotonic relationship between source and target words in morphological inflection. The authors demonstrate state-of-the-art performance on three benchmark datasets (CELEX, Wiktionary, and SIGMORPHON2016), particularly highlighting improvements in low-resource scenarios where the model surpasses previous methods, including weighted FSTs.  The paper includes a detailed analysis comparing the proposed model with a soft attention counterpart, providing insights into the learned representations.

**Main Contributions (as claimed by authors, in order):**

1.  A hard attention model for nearly-monotonic sequence-to-sequence learning tailored for morphological inflection.
2.  State-of-the-art results on three established morphological inflection datasets.
3.  An analysis comparing the hard and soft attention models, offering insights into the features learned for this task.

**Contributions (as I see them):**

1.  **Effective Exploitation of Monotonicity:** The most significant contribution is the successful application of hard monotonic attention to morphological inflection. This architectural choice, coupled with pre-computed alignments for training, demonstrably improves performance, especially when data is scarce. This highlights the value of incorporating linguistic priors into neural models.
2.  **Strong Empirical Results:** Achieving state-of-the-art results on multiple datasets provides convincing evidence of the model's efficacy. The consistent performance across different languages and datasets strengthens the claim of its general applicability.
3.  **Insightful Analysis:** The comparison between hard and soft attention provides valuable insights into the learned representations and the effect of the imposed monotonic constraint. This analysis goes beyond simply presenting results and contributes to a deeper understanding of the model's behavior.

**Strengths:**

1.  **Superior Performance in Low-Resource Settings:** The model demonstrably outperforms previous approaches on the CELEX dataset (a notoriously small dataset). This is a significant accomplishment, as low-resource scenarios are common in morphological inflection tasks, particularly for less-studied languages. This makes the contribution impactful.
2.  **Principled Approach to Alignment:**  Decoupling the alignment learning from the sequence transduction process and using independently learned, "gold" alignments for training is a smart design choice. This simplifies training and avoids the challenges associated with jointly learning alignment and transduction, while still leveraging the benefits of alignment information.
3.  **Clear and Well-Structured Presentation:** The paper is well-written, clearly organized, and easy to follow. The motivation for the approach is well-articulated, and the experimental setup and results are presented in a comprehensive and understandable manner. The inclusion of a visual representation of the model (Figure 1) is particularly helpful.
4.  **Thorough Evaluation:** The experiments cover a good range of datasets and baselines, providing a robust evaluation of the proposed model. The ablation studies (implicitly through the comparison with soft attention) contribute to understanding the impact of the key design choices.
5. **Insightful Analysis of Learned Representations:** The SVD analysis is a compelling way to visualize and interpret the embeddings learned by the model, providing qualitative support for the hypotheses about the model’s behaviour and feature encoding.

**Weaknesses:**

1.  **Limited Novelty of Hard Attention:** While effectively applied, the hard attention mechanism itself is not entirely novel. The core innovation lies in *how* it's applied to the specific task of morphological inflection by exploiting the inherent monotonicity. The paper should better contextualize the novelty within the broader field of attention mechanisms.
2.  **Dependency on Pre-computed Alignments:** The reliance on pre-computed alignments, while simplifying training, might limit the model's adaptability to scenarios where accurate alignments are unavailable. The paper doesn't thoroughly discuss the potential impact of alignment errors on performance, nor explore strategies for learning alignments jointly or semi-supervisedly. While justified in this work, it’s a limitation.
3.  **Limited Error Analysis:** While the authors provide a detailed comparison with the soft attention model, a deeper error analysis identifying specific types of inflection errors (e.g., incorrect suffix attachment, stem vowel changes) would be beneficial. This would provide further insights into the model's strengths and weaknesses and suggest areas for future improvement.
4. **SIGMORPHON results slightly underperform Soft Attention:** While good, the model does not achieve SOTA on the SIGMORPHON dataset. The explanation given regarding linguistic typology is reasonable, but a more detailed exploration of why the hard attention struggles in those particular cases would be valuable.




**Questions to Authors:**

1.  How sensitive is the model’s performance to the quality of the pre-computed alignments? Have you experimented with noisy or imperfect alignments to assess the model's robustness?
2.  Could you elaborate on the computational cost of the hard attention mechanism compared to soft attention, particularly during training and inference?
3.  You mention that the hard attention model "solves a different objective" than standard sequence-to-sequence learning. Could you provide a more detailed explanation of this difference and its implications for the model’s behavior?
4.  Given the success of the model with small datasets, have you considered exploring transfer learning techniques to further improve performance on languages with limited resources?
5. Can you elaborate on the implementation details of the character-level alignment model by Sudoh et al. (2013)? Does the performance of the proposed model depend on the quality of this alignment process?




**Additional Comments:**

*   The supplementary material should include a more detailed description of the LSTM implementation.
*   The link to the GitHub repository has been removed for anonymity, which is fine. Please ensure the code is made available upon acceptance.
*   Consider discussing the potential applications of this model beyond morphological inflection, such as transliteration or machine translation of morphologically rich languages.



**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper that presents a well-motivated and effectively implemented model for morphological inflection generation. The results are compelling, the analysis is insightful, and the writing is clear. While there are minor weaknesses, they do not significantly detract from the overall contribution. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions addressing the questions and suggestions above.
