This paper proposes a hard monotonic attention model for morphological inflection generation. The authors address the issue of data sparsity in morphologically rich languages by leveraging the inherent monotonic alignment between input and output sequences. They present a novel architecture incorporating a hard attention mechanism, guided by pre-learned character-level alignments, and demonstrate strong performance on three benchmark datasets (CELEX, Wiktionary, and SIGMORPHON2016), achieving state-of-the-art results, particularly in low-resource settings.

The primary contribution of this work is the design and evaluation of a hard attention-based sequence-to-sequence model specifically tailored for morphological inflection. The use of hard attention, combined with pre-learned alignments, appears to be a successful strategy for enforcing monotonicity and improving performance, especially when training data is limited. A second contribution lies in the comprehensive experimental evaluation across three diverse datasets, demonstrating the model's robustness and establishing new state-of-the-art results. Finally, the analysis comparing the learned alignments and representations of the hard and soft attention models provides valuable insights into the behavior of these models for this specific task, highlighting the different strategies they employ to capture morphological information.

**Strengths:**

1. **Strong Performance, Especially in Low-Resource Settings:** The model consistently achieves state-of-the-art or competitive results on all three datasets, and crucially, surpasses previous models on the CELEX dataset when data is scarce. This addresses a critical limitation of neural sequence-to-sequence models, which often require large training corpora. The reported improvements are statistically significant and well-supported by the experimental results.
2. **Effective Use of Hard Attention and Pre-learned Alignments:** The combination of hard attention with pre-learned alignments is a compelling design choice. The hard attention mechanism enforces monotonicity and reduces the complexity of the attention mechanism, while the pre-learned alignments provide a strong inductive bias. This combination appears particularly effective for tasks where a clear monotonic relationship between input and output exists.
3. **Comprehensive Evaluation:** The authors thoroughly evaluate their model on three publicly available datasets, representing different languages and morphological complexities. This provides a strong and reliable assessment of the model’s generalization ability. The inclusion of comparisons to a diverse set of baseline models, including both neural and non-neural approaches, strengthens the validity of the findings.
4. **Insightful Analysis:** The analysis comparing the hard and soft attention models provides valuable understanding of the different ways in which these models represent morphological information. The visualization of learned representations is particularly insightful, demonstrating how the models encode positional and character-specific features.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Novelty of the Hard Attention Mechanism:** While the application of hard attention to morphological inflection generation is novel in this context, the hard attention mechanism itself is not a new concept. The primary novelty lies in the specific integration of hard attention with pre-learned alignments and the resulting performance improvements. The paper would benefit from a more nuanced discussion of the existing literature on hard attention and how this work builds upon it.
2. **Dependency on External Alignment Tool:** The model relies on an external character-level alignment tool (Sudoh et al., 2013) to generate the training data for the hard attention mechanism. This introduces a dependency on the accuracy and quality of this tool. A more thorough analysis of the impact of alignment errors on the model’s performance would be beneficial. How robust is the model to imperfect alignments?
3. **SIGMORPHON Results Could Be Clarified:** The performance on SIGMORPHON2016 is somewhat nuanced, with the soft attention baseline outperforming the hard attention model on several languages. This discrepancy is attributed to the differing morphological phenomena present in those languages, but a more detailed explanation of *why* the hard attention model struggles with these languages would be valuable. Is it simply the lack of monotonicity, or are there other factors at play?
4. **Ablation Studies are Limited:** While the paper provides a comprehensive comparison to baseline models, it lacks a detailed ablation study to isolate the contributions of different components of the proposed model. For example, how does the performance change when removing the pre-learned alignments or using a different attention mechanism?

**Questions to Authors:**

1.  How sensitive is the model’s performance to the quality of the pre-learned alignments generated by the Sudoh et al. (2013) tool? Did you experiment with different alignment tools or methods?
2.  Can you provide a more detailed explanation of why the hard attention model performs less well on the languages in SIGMORPHON2016 that exhibit non-monotonic morphological phenomena (e.g., vowel harmony)?
3.  Could you elaborate on the computational cost of the hard attention mechanism compared to the soft attention mechanism, particularly during training and inference?
4.  Would the performance of the hard attention model be affected if the pre-learned alignments were not perfectly accurate? Is there a threshold of alignment accuracy beyond which performance degrades significantly?
