This paper presents a hard attention model for morphological inflection generation, tackling the task with a nearly-monotonic sequence-to-sequence learning approach. The authors demonstrate strong performance on three standard datasets (CELEX, Wiktionary, and SIGMORPHON2016), achieving state-of-the-art results and, importantly, demonstrating improved performance with limited training data, surpassing previous models on the CELEX dataset. The paper is well-written and clearly explains the proposed model and experimental setup.

**Contributions:**

1. **Hard Attention Model for Morphological Inflection:** The core contribution lies in the introduction of a hard attention mechanism specifically designed to exploit the near-monotonic alignment characteristic of morphological inflection. This is a valuable approach, offering a contrast to the more common soft attention mechanisms.
2. **State-of-the-Art Results:** The paper establishes new state-of-the-art results on three benchmark datasets, demonstrating the effectiveness of the proposed model. This empirical validation is a significant contribution to the field.
3. **Analysis and Comparison:** The thorough analysis comparing the hard attention model with soft attention models, including insights into the learned representations, adds to the understanding of the task and the strengths of different approaches. This comparative analysis is well-executed and informative.

**Strengths:**

1. **Strong Empirical Results:** The consistent performance improvements across different datasets are compelling. The model demonstrably addresses the issue of data scarcity, a common challenge in morphological inflection generation, especially for low-resource languages. The results on CELEX are particularly noteworthy, finally surpassing the latent variable WFST model.
2. **Clear and Concise Explanation:** The paper is exceptionally well-written. The model architecture and training procedure are clearly described, making it easy to understand the key innovations. The use of Figure 1 is effective in illustrating the hard attention mechanism.
3. **Well-Justified Design Choices:** The decision to utilize hard attention, guided by pre-trained alignments, is well-motivated and effectively addresses the limitations of purely data-driven sequence-to-sequence models in low-resource scenarios.
4. **Comprehensive Evaluation:** The evaluation across three datasets with different characteristics (size, language families) provides a robust assessment of the model’s generalization ability.
5. **Insightful Analysis:** The analysis of learned alignments and representations provides valuable insights into how the model functions and what features it learns, contributing to a deeper understanding of morphological inflection.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Novelty of Hard Attention:** While the application of hard attention to this specific task is valuable, the hard attention mechanism itself isn’t entirely novel. The primary novelty lies in its application within this specific context and the combination with the pre-trained alignments. This should be acknowledged more explicitly.
2. **Dependency on Pre-trained Alignments:** The reliance on external, pre-trained alignments is a potential limitation. While the authors justify this choice, it introduces an extra step and potential source of error. The robustness of the system to the quality of these pre-trained alignments isn’t fully explored.  A brief ablation study on alignment quality would be valuable.
3. **SIGMORPHON Analysis Depth:** The discussion of the SIGMORPHON results feels somewhat superficial. While the authors note the varying performance across languages based on morphological typology, a more detailed analysis of *why* the model performs differently on different language families would be beneficial.
4. **Comparison to Recent Work:** The related work section could be more comprehensive, particularly regarding more recent advancements in sequence transduction and attention mechanisms beyond those mentioned. Specifically, transformer-based models should be discussed, even if only to state why they weren’t considered for this specific task.

**Questions to Authors:**

1. How sensitive is the model’s performance to the quality of the pre-trained alignments? Could you provide an ablation study where you use noisier or less accurate alignments?
2. Can you elaborate on the computational cost associated with the hard attention mechanism compared to soft attention, particularly during training?
3.  In the SIGMORPHON experiments, what specific linguistic features of the languages with lower performance (e.g., vowel harmony) make them more challenging for the hard attention mechanism? Could you provide examples illustrating these challenges?
4. While you mention the limitations of the soft attention model's tendency to overfit on small datasets, could you explore regularization techniques to mitigate this issue and potentially close the performance gap?

**Additional Comments:**

*  The reference list is extensive and demonstrates a good understanding of the relevant literature.
*  The github link to the source code is essential for reproducibility and should be maintained.
*  Consider adding a brief discussion of potential future directions, such as applying the model to other sequence transduction tasks or exploring different alignment methods.



Overall, this is a strong paper with significant contributions to the field of morphological inflection generation. The proposed model is well-designed, thoroughly evaluated, and clearly explained. While the novelty of the hard attention mechanism itself may be incremental, its application within this context, coupled with the insightful analysis, makes this a valuable addition to the literature. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions addressing the weaknesses outlined above.
