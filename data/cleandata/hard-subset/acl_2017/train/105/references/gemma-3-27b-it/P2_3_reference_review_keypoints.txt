- Proposes a neural model with hard monotonic attention for morphological inflection that learns "write" or "step" actions guided by pre-computed character alignments.  
- Evaluated on CELEX, Wiktionary, and SIGMORPHON2016, achieving state-of-the-art results, especially in low-resource settings.  
- Main contribution: tailored hard monotonic attention imposes a useful inductive bias for morphology, improving low-resource performance.  
- Main contribution: strong empirical results, notably outperforming WFST on CELEX with limited data.  
- Main contribution: analysis comparing hard vs. soft attention reveals different learned representations and reduced positional encoding in hard attention.  
- Strength: directly addresses data scarcity in neural morphological inflection, with clear gains in low-resource scenarios.  
- Strength: well-motivated model design that leverages the typically monotonic nature of morphological transduction.  
- Strength: comprehensive evaluation across diverse datasets and languages, supporting claims of generalizability.  
- Strength: clear writing and explanation of architecture/training, plus preliminary representation analysis.  
- Strength: authors provide code (link removed here), supporting reproducibility.  
- Weakness: individual components (hard attention, pre-computed alignments) are not entirely novel; novelty is primarily in their combination for this task.  
- Weakness: model performance depends on pre-computed alignment quality, and the paper lacks analysis of alignment error impact.  
- Weakness: results on SIGMORPHON2016 are less dominant and would benefit from deeper error analysis by language typology.  
- Weakness: missing ablation studies (e.g., removing pre-computed alignments) to quantify each component's contribution.  
- Question: How sensitive is performance to alignment quality, and can you quantify alignment-error impact on accuracy?  
- Question: Which linguistic properties characterize languages where performance is weaker (e.g., non-local dependencies), and what model changes might help?  
- Question: What is the effect of removing pre-computed alignments—can the model learn alignments end-to-end (ablation requested)?  
- Question: Why decouple alignment and transduction instead of joint learning, and what trade-offs motivated that choice?  
- Comment: shorten and focus the citation list; group related work by technique rather than chronologically.  
- Overall recommendation: accept — combination is novel and effective with strong empirical results; identified weaknesses are addressable.
