## Review for "Morphological Inflection Generation with Hard Monotonic Attention"

**Summary:**

This paper presents a hard monotonic attention model for morphological inflection generation. The authors address the challenges of data sparsity in morphologically rich languages and the limitations of existing sequence-to-sequence models, particularly their tendency to overfit with limited data. Their model enforces a nearly monotonic alignment between input and output character sequences, leveraging independently learned character-level alignments to guide training.  The model is evaluated on three benchmark datasets (CELEX, Wiktionary, and SIGMORPHON2016), demonstrating competitive or state-of-the-art performance, and particularly strong results in low-resource scenarios.  A detailed analysis compares the model to soft attention approaches, exploring the learned representations and alignment patterns.

**Main Contributions (as I see them):**

1. **Effective Low-Resource Performance:** The most significant contribution is the model’s demonstrated ability to outperform existing methods, *especially* on the CELEX dataset, where data is extremely limited. This addresses a critical practical problem in morphological inflection for many languages.
2. **Principled Hard Attention for Morphology:** The application of hard monotonic attention, guided by pre-trained alignments, is a well-motivated and effective strategy for this task. It’s a clever way to incorporate linguistic priors into a neural network architecture.
3. **Detailed Analysis & Comparison:** The analysis of learned alignments and representations offers valuable insights into how these models approach morphological transduction. This helps to understand what the models are learning and why they succeed.

**Strengths:**

1. **Strong Empirical Results:** The model achieves competitive or state-of-the-art results on all three datasets, consistently demonstrating its effectiveness. The particularly strong performance on CELEX is compelling evidence of its advantage in low-resource settings.
2. **Well-Motivated Approach:** The choice of hard monotonic attention is well-justified given the nature of morphological inflection.  The authors effectively argue that the inherent alignment structure lends itself to this approach.  The decoupling of alignment learning from the core model training is a reasonable and effective strategy.
3. **Thorough Evaluation:**  The authors evaluate their model against a comprehensive set of baselines, including both neural and non-neural approaches. The experimentation is well-designed and provides a strong basis for comparison.
4. **Insightful Analysis:** The analysis of learned alignments (Figure 3) and representations (Figure 4) provides valuable insights into the model's behavior. It supports the claim that the model is learning meaningful representations and effectively utilizing the imposed constraints.
5. **Clear and Well-Written:** The paper is generally well-written and easy to follow. The technical details are clearly explained, and the motivation for the design choices is well articulated.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Novelty in Core Architecture:** While the *application* of hard monotonic attention to morphological inflection is valuable, the core encoder-decoder architecture with attention is not novel. The primary novelty lies in the specific control mechanism and the integration with pre-trained alignments. The paper would benefit from a more nuanced discussion of the architectural contributions relative to existing attention-based sequence-to-sequence models.
2. **Dependence on External Alignment:** The reliance on a pre-trained character-level alignment model (Sudoh et al., 2013) introduces a dependency on the performance of that model. While the authors justify this approach, it would be valuable to explore the sensitivity of the model's performance to the quality of the pre-trained alignments.  Is the performance gain completely reliant on perfect alignment?
3. **SIGMORPHON Results Interpretation:** The discussion of the SIGMORPHON results could be more insightful.  The authors acknowledge that the soft attention model performs better on some languages, attributing it to linguistic typology. However, this explanation feels a bit superficial. A more in-depth analysis of *which* linguistic features correlate with better performance for each model would be valuable. The assertion that the soft attention model’s superior performance is simply due to a longer training period is somewhat weak.
4. **Lack of Error Analysis:** A qualitative error analysis, providing examples of where the model fails and why, would further strengthen the evaluation and provide valuable insights for future work.



**Questions to Authors:**

1. How sensitive is the model’s performance to the quality of the pre-trained character-level alignments?  Have you experimented with different alignment models or with noisy alignment data?
2. Can you provide a more detailed analysis of the linguistic features that differentiate the languages where the hard attention model outperforms the soft attention model versus those where the opposite is true?
3. Could you provide some example cases where the hard attention model fails and discuss potential reasons for those failures?
4. While you mention the computational complexity of marginalizing over derivations, how does the computational cost of training and inference compare between your hard attention model and a comparable soft attention model?




**Additional Comments:**

*   The paper could benefit from a discussion of potential applications of this work beyond morphological inflection, such as transliteration or code-switching.
*   The supplementary material should include more detailed implementation details, including hyperparameter settings and training procedures.




Overall, this is a strong paper that presents a valuable contribution to the field of morphological inflection generation. The model achieves excellent results, particularly in low-resource settings, and the analysis provides valuable insights into its behavior. While the core architecture isn’t entirely novel, the application of hard monotonic attention and the integration with pre-trained alignments are well-motivated and effective. I recommend acceptance.
