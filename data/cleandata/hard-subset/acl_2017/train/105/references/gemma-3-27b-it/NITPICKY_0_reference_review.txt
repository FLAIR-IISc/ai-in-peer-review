This paper proposes a hard monotonic attention model for morphological inflection generation. The authors claim three main contributions: (1) a novel hard attention model for nearly-monotonic sequence-to-sequence learning, (2) state-of-the-art results on three morphological inflection generation datasets, and (3) an analysis comparing the proposed model with a soft attention model, providing insights into the learned representations.

While the paper presents a technically sound approach and achieves competitive results, several issues prevent a positive recommendation. The core contribution—the hard attention mechanism—feels incremental rather than groundbreaking. Monotonic attention is a natural fit for morphological inflection, and framing it as a "hard" attention mechanism doesn’t fundamentally alter the established sequence-to-sequence paradigm. The method feels more like a carefully engineered implementation of a reasonable idea rather than a truly novel architectural innovation.

**Strengths:**

1. **Strong Empirical Performance:** The model achieves state-of-the-art or competitive results on all three datasets (CELEX, Wiktionary, SIGMORPHON2016), especially noteworthy is the performance on the CELEX dataset, where it surpasses the previously best latent variable WFST. This demonstrates the practical effectiveness of the approach.
2. **Thorough Evaluation:** The authors perform a comprehensive evaluation, comparing their model against a range of baselines, including both neural and non-neural methods. The inclusion of multiple datasets adds credibility to the findings.
3. **Clear Presentation:** The paper is generally well-written and clearly explains the model architecture and training procedure. The inclusion of Figure 1, visually depicting the hard attention mechanism, is particularly helpful.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Incremental Novelty:** The core idea of hard monotonic attention, while sound, lacks substantial novelty. It's a logical extension of existing monotonic attention mechanisms and doesn't introduce fundamentally new concepts in neural sequence transduction. The "hard" aspect is largely a training procedure rather than a novel architectural element.
2. **Overemphasis on Implementation Details & Minor Formatting Issues:** The paper is burdened by excessively detailed explanations of implementation choices and frequent parenthetical asides. This clutters the presentation and obscures the key insights. Numerous minor formatting inconsistencies (e.g., inconsistent use of boldface, awkward phrasing, typos) detract from the overall professionalism of the manuscript.  The removal of the github link is appreciated for blinding purposes, but the footnote referencing it's removal is unnecessary.
3. **Insufficient Analysis of Error Cases:** While the analysis comparing the hard and soft attention models is a step in the right direction, it lacks depth. The authors should include a more detailed analysis of *where* the model fails, providing specific examples of incorrect inflections and discussing the potential reasons for these errors. This would enhance the understanding of the model’s limitations and potential avenues for improvement. The discussion on linguistic typology is also superficial.
4. **Training Procedure Reliance on External Alignment:** The reliance on a pre-trained character-level alignment model for generating training sequences is a potential weakness. The performance of the hard attention model is therefore partially dependent on the quality of this external alignment. The paper doesn't adequately address the potential impact of alignment errors on the model's performance. The statement "we instead use a simple training procedure" is misleading; generating the gold sequences is a significant pre-processing step.
5. **Ambiguous Justification for Choices:** The decision to project to a 2D SVD space for the representation analysis feels arbitrary. The authors don’t offer a strong justification for this choice, nor do they clearly explain how the visualization provides meaningful insights into the learned representations beyond a qualitative level.

**Questions to Authors:**

1.  Could you elaborate on the sensitivity of your model’s performance to the quality of the pre-trained character-level alignment? Have you experimented with different alignment models or investigated the impact of alignment errors on the training process?
2.  Can you provide a more detailed analysis of the error cases made by your model, including specific examples and a discussion of the underlying reasons for these errors?
3.  Why was the 2D SVD projection chosen for the representation analysis, and how do you interpret the observed patterns in the visualizations?
4.  The paper states a difference in training procedure between models, but doesn’t specify why the soft attention model was trained for a different number of epochs. Is there a reason to believe that the soft attention model requires a different training schedule or that the convergence criterion differs between models?



**Additional Comments:**

*   The citation style is inconsistent.
*   Several sentences are overly verbose and could be streamlined for clarity.
*   Please proofread carefully for typos and grammatical errors.



**Overall Assessment:**

Despite achieving strong empirical results, the paper's limited novelty and several weaknesses in presentation and analysis lead me to recommend **rejection**. The work represents a solid engineering effort but doesn't offer a significant enough contribution to warrant acceptance in a highly competitive venue like ACL. Addressing the weaknesses outlined above, particularly through a more in-depth error analysis and a clearer articulation of the novelty of the approach, could improve the paper’s potential for future consideration.
