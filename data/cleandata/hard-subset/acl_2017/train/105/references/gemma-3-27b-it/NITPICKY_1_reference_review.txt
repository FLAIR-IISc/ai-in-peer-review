This paper proposes a hard monotonic attention model for morphological inflection generation, arguing it addresses limitations of previous sequence-to-sequence models, particularly with small datasets. The authors demonstrate competitive or state-of-the-art performance on three datasets (CELEX, Wiktionary, SIGMORPHON2016), highlighting improvements on low-resource scenarios. The primary contribution is the hard attention mechanism coupled with independently learned alignments, enabling effective transduction with limited data. A secondary contribution lies in the comprehensive experimental evaluation across diverse datasets and the comparative analysis with a soft attention baseline. Finally, the analysis of learned representations provides some insights into how the model encodes morphological information.

**Strengths:**

1. **Strong Performance on Low-Resource Data:** The most compelling aspect is the model's performance on the CELEX dataset. Consistently outperforming prior work, including specialized latent variable WFST models, in a low-data regime is a significant achievement. This addresses a well-known challenge in morphological generation.
2. **Novelty of Hard Attention & Alignment Decoupling:** While attention mechanisms are prevalent, the explicit enforcement of a hard monotonic alignment, coupled with pre-learned alignments, is a distinct contribution. This simplifies training and potentially provides robustness by guiding the attention process.
3. **Comprehensive Evaluation:** The evaluation across three diverse datasets—differing in size, language, and morphological complexity—provides a thorough assessment of the model’s generalization capabilities. The inclusion of SIGMORPHON2016 is particularly valuable as it represents a standard benchmark for the task.
4. **Clear Presentation of the Model:** The model architecture and training process are described with sufficient detail, and Figure 1 is helpful for understanding the hard attention mechanism. The mathematical formulation is also relatively clear.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Insufficient Justification for Design Choices:** The rationale behind several design choices lacks depth. For example, the specific LSTM architecture used (detailed only in supplementary material) isn’t thoroughly motivated. Why this specific LSTM variant? Why these hidden layer sizes? More justification is needed.
2. **Overstated Novelty of Monotonicity:** The paper repeatedly emphasizes the novelty of modeling the monotonic alignment. However, the inherent monotonic nature of morphological inflection is well-established. While the *implementation* of hard attention to enforce this is novel, framing it as a groundbreaking conceptual shift is overstated.
3. **Limited Error Analysis:** While results are presented, a deeper error analysis is missing. What types of errors does the model make? Are there specific morphological processes it struggles with? Understanding error patterns would provide valuable insights and guide future research.
4. **Minor Inconsistencies and Imprecisions:** Throughout the text, several minor inconsistencies and imprecisions appear. For example, the reference to “github” in the results section is incomplete (link removed, but the text still references it). Equation 2 is stated as solving a different objective than Equation 1, but the connection is not thoroughly discussed. This level of detail matters.
5. **Missing Ablation Studies:** The paper does not include ablation studies to assess the contribution of individual components of the model (e.g., the bi-directional encoder, the feedback connection in the decoder). This makes it difficult to determine which aspects of the architecture are most crucial for performance.

**Questions to Authors:**

1. Can you provide a more detailed justification for the specific design choices made in the model architecture, particularly the LSTM variant and the dimensions of the hidden layers?
2. Could you perform an error analysis to identify common error types and explain why the model struggles with those specific cases?
3. What is the computational cost of the independent alignment process compared to jointly learning alignment and transduction? Is this a practical limitation for very large datasets?
4.  Could you clarify the distinction between the objective functions in Equations 1 and 2 and elaborate on the impact of approximating Equation 1 with Equation 2?
5.  Please provide the complete link to the source code on GitHub.



**Additional Comments:**

*   The references are not formatted consistently.
*   The paper is generally well-written, but careful proofreading is needed to eliminate minor grammatical errors and inconsistencies.
*   The description of the character alignment model of Sudoh et al. (2013) is brief and assumes familiarity with the method. A more detailed explanation would be helpful.

**Overall Assessment:**

Despite several minor flaws, the paper presents a worthwhile contribution to the field of morphological inflection generation. The strong performance on low-resource datasets, the novel hard attention mechanism, and the comprehensive evaluation are all positive aspects. However, the overstated novelty, lack of justification for design choices, and missing error analysis prevent me from strongly recommending acceptance. I lean towards **rejection** due to the accumulation of these relatively minor, but significant, issues. Addressing these points in a revision could significantly improve the paper’s quality.
