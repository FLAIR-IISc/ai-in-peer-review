This paper presents a novel hard attention model for morphological inflection generation, addressing the challenges of small training datasets and the need for explicit alignment information. The authors demonstrate strong performance on three benchmark datasets (CELEX, Wiktionary, and SIGMORPHON2016), achieving state-of-the-art results, particularly on CELEX where it surpasses previous latent variable WFST models.

**Contributions:**

1. **Hard Attention Mechanism for Morphological Inflection:** The core contribution is the design and implementation of a hard attention mechanism specifically tailored for the nearly-monotonic alignment characteristic of morphological inflection. This mechanism enforces a more structured attention pattern compared to soft attention, potentially leading to better generalization with limited data.
2. **State-of-the-Art Performance:** The proposed model achieves competitive or state-of-the-art results across three datasets, demonstrating its effectiveness. The significant improvement on the CELEX dataset, a notoriously challenging low-resource setting, is particularly noteworthy.
3. **Analysis and Comparison with Soft Attention:** The authors provide a detailed analysis comparing the hard and soft attention models, offering valuable insights into the representations learned by each approach. The visualizations of learned representations, showing how positional information is encoded differently, are insightful.

**Strengths:**

1. **Strong Empirical Results:** The consistently strong performance across multiple datasets provides compelling evidence for the effectiveness of the proposed approach. The particularly good results on CELEX, outperforming the previously strong latent variable WFST model, are impressive.
2. **Well-Motivated Approach:** The choice of a hard attention mechanism is well-justified by the inherent monotonicity of morphological inflection. This targeted design choice differentiates the work from generic sequence-to-sequence models.
3. **Clear and Comprehensive Presentation:** The paper is well-written and clearly explains the model architecture, training procedure, and experimental setup. The inclusion of a visual illustration of the hard attention mechanism (Figure 1) is helpful.
4. **Detailed Analysis:** The analysis section goes beyond simply reporting results; it delves into the learned representations and provides insights into the differences between the hard and soft attention models. This strengthens the scientific contribution of the work.
5. **Addressing Data Scarcity:** The model's ability to perform well on small datasets, like CELEX, is a significant advantage, addressing a common challenge in morphological inflection generation for many languages.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Reliance on Pre-trained Alignments:** The model relies on independently learned character-level alignments for training, which introduces an external dependency. While the authors argue this simplifies training, it could limit the model's ability to learn alignments directly from data. The performance sensitivity to the quality of these pre-trained alignments isn't thoroughly investigated.
2. **Limited Ablation Study:** While the comparison with soft attention is helpful, a more detailed ablation study would be beneficial. Investigating the impact of different components of the model (e.g., the bi-directional encoder, the recurrent decoder feedback connection) would provide a deeper understanding of their contributions.
3. **SIGMORPHON Results Discrepancy:** The results on SIGMORPHON2016 show the model performing slightly worse than the soft attention baseline overall. The authors attribute this to the linguistic properties of the languages, but further exploration of this discrepancy could be valuable. What specific language characteristics make the soft attention model more suitable?
4. **Presentation of Equations:** Some of the equations (e.g., Equation 2) lack sufficient context or explanation, making them difficult to fully understand without careful examination of the surrounding text.
5. **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** The paper does not discuss the sensitivity of the model's performance to different hyperparameter settings. Providing some insight into this aspect would enhance the reproducibility and robustness of the findings.



**Questions to Authors:**

1. How sensitive is the model’s performance to the quality of the pre-trained character-level alignments? Have you experimented with different alignment methods or considered techniques to learn alignments jointly with the transduction process?
2. Could you elaborate on the specific linguistic characteristics of the languages in SIGMORPHON2016 that lead to the soft attention model’s superior performance?
3. Could you provide more details on the hyperparameter tuning process and discuss the sensitivity of the model’s performance to different hyperparameter settings?
4. How does the computational cost of the hard attention mechanism compare to that of soft attention, particularly for long input sequences?

**Additional Comments:**

* The links to the supplementary materials and code were removed for anonymization; please ensure they are readily available upon publication.
* In the Related Work section, it would be beneficial to briefly discuss recent advancements in Transformer-based models for sequence transduction, as they have become increasingly popular in NLP.
* The paper would benefit from a discussion of potential limitations and future research directions.



Overall, this is a well-executed and insightful paper that makes a valuable contribution to the field of morphological inflection generation. While some minor weaknesses exist, the strengths of the work, particularly the strong empirical results and the thoughtful analysis, outweigh these concerns. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions addressing the questions and comments above.
