This paper proposes a hard monotonic attention model for morphological inflection generation. The core idea is to explicitly model the nearly monotonic alignment between input and output characters, which is common in morphologically rich languages. The authors evaluate their model on three datasets (CELEX, Wiktionary, and SIGMORPHON2016) and demonstrate state-of-the-art or competitive performance, particularly excelling on smaller datasets where overfitting is a concern.

**Contributions:**

1. **A novel hard attention mechanism for morphological inflection:** The proposed hard attention mechanism, coupled with a bi-directional encoder and decoder with a feedback connection, offers a strong and interpretable approach to modeling the inherent monotonic alignment in morphological inflection. This represents a thoughtful design choice tailored to the specific task.
2. **State-of-the-art performance, especially in low-resource settings:** The model achieves state-of-the-art results on the CELEX dataset and competitive results on Wiktionary and SIGMORPHON2016, demonstrating its effectiveness. The particularly strong performance on CELEX, where data is scarce, highlights a significant advantage over previous neural models prone to overfitting.
3. **Insightful analysis of model behavior:** The analysis of learned alignments and representations provides valuable insights into how the model functions and how it compares to soft attention models.  The observation that the hard attention model doesn't need to encode positional information as strongly as the soft attention model is a notable finding.

**Strengths:**

1. **Strong experimental results:** The paper provides comprehensive experimental results on three established datasets.  The consistent performance improvements, particularly in the low-resource CELEX setting, is a strong argument for the effectiveness of the proposed method.  Table 1 is particularly compelling.
2. **Well-motivated design:** The choice of a hard attention mechanism is well-justified by the inherent monotonic nature of morphological inflection. The design choices – bi-directional encoder, feedback connection in the decoder – are explained clearly and contribute to the model’s performance.
3. **Clear and concise writing:** The paper is generally well-written and easy to follow. The technical details are presented clearly, and the explanations are insightful.  Figure 1 is helpful in visualizing the model architecture.
4. **Thorough analysis:**  The analysis section, particularly the discussion of learned alignments and representations (Figures 3 & 4), adds significant value to the paper. It's not just about demonstrating performance; it's about *understanding* why the model works.
5. **Reproducibility:** The authors mention that source code is available, which is a huge plus for reproducibility and further research.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited discussion of hyperparameter tuning:** The paper doesn't provide detailed information on the hyperparameter tuning process.  While the implementation details are mentioned, a more thorough discussion of how hyperparameters were chosen (e.g., learning rate, hidden layer size) would strengthen the results.
2. **Dependence on pre-trained alignments:**  The model relies on pre-trained character-level alignments. While the authors acknowledge this and justify it, it raises a question about the model's ability to learn alignments from scratch.  A comparison with a system that learns alignments jointly would be valuable.  How sensitive is the performance to the quality of the pre-trained alignments?
3. **SIGMORPHON results are nuanced:** While competitive, the model doesn't consistently outperform the soft attention baseline across all languages on the SIGMORPHON dataset. The explanation relating language typology is reasonable, but a more in-depth investigation of *why* the model struggles with certain languages would be beneficial.  What specific morphological phenomena cause issues?
4. **Minor – Equation (2) clarity:** The explanation of equation (2) could be clearer. It states the model solves a different objective than equation (1), but the implications of this are not fully explored.  A more detailed discussion of the approximation introduced by optimizing for the best derivation would be helpful.



**Questions to Authors:**

1. How sensitive is the model's performance to the quality of the pre-trained character-level alignments? Have you experimented with different alignment methods or alignment confidence thresholds?
2. Could you provide more details about the hyperparameter tuning process? What search strategies were used, and which hyperparameters had the biggest impact on performance?
3. For the SIGMORPHON dataset, can you provide a more detailed analysis of the types of morphological processes that the model struggles with? Are there specific languages or morphological features that consistently lead to errors?
4. Could you elaborate on the implications of solving for the "best derivation" instead of directly maximizing the sequence probability (as mentioned around Equation 2)?  What is the expected impact on performance and efficiency?

**Additional Comments:**

* The related work section is comprehensive and well-organized.
* The paper’s focus on a specific task (morphological inflection) allows for a deep dive into the problem and a tailored solution.
* Consider adding an ablation study to assess the contribution of different components of the model (e.g., bi-directional encoder, feedback connection).



Overall, this is a strong paper with a well-motivated approach, strong experimental results, and insightful analysis. While there are some minor weaknesses, they do not detract significantly from the overall quality of the work. I recommend acceptance.
