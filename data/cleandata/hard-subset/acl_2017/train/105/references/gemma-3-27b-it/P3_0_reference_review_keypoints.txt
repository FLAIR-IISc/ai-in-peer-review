- Proposes a neural morphological inflection model using hard monotonic attention to enforce near-monotonic input-output character alignment.  
- Targets data sparsity in morphologically rich languages and decouples alignment from transduction via pre-computed character alignments.  
- Evaluated on CELEX, Wiktionary, and SIGMORPHON2016 with competitive or state-of-the-art results, especially in low-resource settings.  
- Key empirical claim: outperforms the strong non-neural Dreyer et al. (2008) WFST baseline on CELEX, a notable achievement for neural models.  
- Provides an analysis comparing learned alignments and representations against a soft attention baseline, highlighting benefits of hard attention.  
- Strength: strong empirical results across datasets with clear, reproducible reporting and helpful visualizations.  
- Strength: well-motivated choice of hard monotonic attention given morphological inflection’s monotonic nature.  
- Strength: clear, thorough presentation of model architecture and experimental setup.  
- Strength: insightful analysis of learned character representations and differences from soft attention.  
- Strength: addresses a practical NLP problem with real-world implications for morphologically rich languages.  
- Weakness: hard attention itself is not novel; main contribution is its adaptation/application to this task.  
- Weakness: reliance on pre-computed alignments (Sudoh et al., 2013) creates dependency and potential failure mode; sensitivity to alignment quality not well studied.  
- Weakness: SIGMORPHON2016 “best” claim needs qualification—the soft attention baseline sometimes performs slightly better.  
- Weakness: paper omits discussion of computational cost and training complexity compared to soft attention.  
- Question: how sensitive are results to the quality of pre-computed character-level alignments and alternative aligners?  
- Question: provide a detailed comparison of training time and computational resources versus the soft attention baseline.  
- Question: what regularization techniques were tried to prevent soft-attention overfitting on CELEX?  
- Question: how could the model be extended to capture long-range dependencies in stem-changing languages?  
- Additional: include source code link in final version.  
- Additional: add discussion of potential error cases and model limitations.  
- Overall assessment: strong paper with valuable contributions and convincing results; recommend accept with minor revisions addressing alignment dependency, SIGMORPHON nuances, and computational cost.
