- Proposes a hard monotonic attention model for morphological inflection that constrains attention to move sequentially through the input.
- Demonstrates state-of-the-art performance on CELEX, Wiktionary, and SIGMORPHON2016, with strong gains in low-resource settings.
- Authors claim: (1) hard attention tailored for nearly-monotonic seq2seq learning, (2) SOTA results on three datasets, (3) analysis comparing hard vs. soft attention.
- Reviewer-identified contributions: effective exploitation of monotonicity with pre-computed alignments, strong empirical results across datasets, and insightful analysis comparing attention types.
- Strength: superior performance in low-resource scenarios, notably on the small CELEX dataset.
- Strength: principled design that decouples alignment learning via independently learned “gold” alignments for training.
- Strength: clear, well-structured presentation with helpful model visualization.
- Strength: thorough evaluation across multiple datasets and baselines, including ablation-style comparison with soft attention.
- Strength: SVD-based analysis provides qualitative insight into learned representations.
- Weakness: hard attention mechanism itself is not entirely novel; novelty is mainly in its application to inflection.
- Weakness: dependence on pre-computed alignments may limit adaptability; impact of alignment errors is not explored.
- Weakness: limited error analysis—more specific breakdowns of inflection error types are needed.
- Weakness: does not achieve SOTA on SIGMORPHON2016; needs deeper exploration of failure cases by typology.
- Question: How sensitive is performance to the quality of pre-computed alignments and to noisy alignments?
- Question: What is the computational cost of hard attention vs. soft attention during training and inference?
- Question: What does it mean that hard attention “solves a different objective” and what are the implications?
- Question: Have you considered transfer learning to further improve low-resource language performance?
- Question: Please clarify implementation details and dependency on the Sudoh et al. (2013) character-level alignment method.
- Additional: include more LSTM implementation details in the supplementary material.
- Additional: ensure code is released upon acceptance (anonymized link removed is acceptable for review).
- Additional: discuss potential applications beyond morphological inflection, e.g., transliteration or MT for morphologically rich languages.
- Overall assessment: strong, well-motivated paper with compelling results and analysis; recommend acceptance with minor revisions addressing questions and suggestions.
