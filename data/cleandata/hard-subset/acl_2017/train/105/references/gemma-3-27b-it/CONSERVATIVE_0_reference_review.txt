This paper proposes a hard monotonic attention model for morphological inflection generation, motivated by the observation that this task often exhibits a nearly monotonic alignment between input and output sequences. The authors demonstrate competitive performance on three datasets (CELEX, Wiktionary, and SIGMORPHON2016), surpassing previous state-of-the-art methods, particularly on the small CELEX dataset. They also present an analysis comparing their model to a soft attention baseline, offering some insights into the learned representations.

The primary contribution of this work is the demonstration that a relatively simple, hard-attention mechanism, coupled with independently learned alignments, can achieve strong results in morphological inflection generation, *especially* in low-resource scenarios. While attention mechanisms are common, the specific focus on *hard* monotonic attention and its advantages in this domain are noteworthy. A secondary, albeit valuable, contribution is the thorough experimental evaluation on a diverse set of datasets, and the detailed comparison to existing methods, including both neural and traditional (WFST) approaches. Finally, the analysis of learned representations offers some initial understanding of how these models operate.

**Strengths:**

1. **Strong Performance on CELEX:** The most compelling aspect of this work is the model's ability to outperform existing methods, including latent variable WFSTs, on the notoriously difficult CELEX dataset. This is significant because CELEX is small and represents a substantial challenge for data-hungry neural models. Demonstrating superior performance here lends credence to the authors’ hypothesis that incorporating prior knowledge about the monotonic alignment structure is beneficial when data is scarce.
2. **Sound Methodology:** The approach of decoupling alignment from the transduction process via pre-training is reasonable and well-justified. Leveraging existing alignment methods to provide “gold” transduction/control sequences simplifies training and potentially improves generalization. The use of a hard attention mechanism, while not entirely novel, is appropriately motivated for this task, and its implementation appears technically sound.
3. **Comprehensive Evaluation:** The authors perform a thorough evaluation across three different datasets, covering varying language families and morphological complexities. This broad evaluation strengthens the claim that the model is generally applicable and robust. The inclusion of both neural and non-neural baselines provides a solid benchmark for comparison.
4. **Insightful Analysis:** The analysis of learned representations, while preliminary, is a step towards understanding how these models encode morphological information. The observations regarding positional encoding differences between the soft and hard attention models are interesting and potentially informative.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Novelty:** While the results are good, the core idea of using attention for sequence transduction is well-established. The primary novelty lies in the specific application of *hard* monotonic attention, but the fundamental concept is not groundbreaking. The paper would benefit from a more detailed discussion of how this work builds upon existing attention mechanisms and what specific innovations beyond the application itself are being presented.
2. **Dependence on Pre-trained Alignments:** The reliance on independently learned alignments could be a limitation. While the authors argue that this simplifies training, it also introduces a potential source of error. The performance of the model is ultimately dependent on the quality of these pre-trained alignments. A more robust evaluation might involve experimenting with different alignment methods or exploring end-to-end training with soft alignments.
3. **SIGMORPHON Results Less Compelling:** While results on Wiktionary are positive, the performance on SIGMORPHON2016 is less convincing. The authors attribute the lower performance on certain languages to the limitations of the monotonic alignment assumption. However, this raises questions about the general applicability of the method and suggests that the model may be less effective for languages with more complex morphological processes. A more thorough discussion of these limitations is warranted.
4. **Lack of Ablation Studies:** The paper lacks ablation studies to demonstrate the relative contribution of different components of the model (e.g., the bi-directional encoder, the hard attention mechanism, the recurrent feedback connection). Such studies would help to better understand which aspects of the architecture are most crucial for performance.



**Questions to Authors:**

1. How sensitive is the model’s performance to the quality of the pre-trained alignments? Have you experimented with different alignment methods or investigated the impact of alignment errors on the final results?
2. Could you elaborate on the limitations of the monotonic alignment assumption for languages like Arabic, Navajo, Hungarian and Maltese? Are there potential modifications to the model architecture that could address these limitations?
3. Can you provide some examples of errors made by the model, particularly on the SIGMORPHON2016 dataset, and analyze the reasons behind these errors?
4.  Have you experimented with any techniques to regularize the model and prevent overfitting, particularly on the CELEX dataset, beyond the monotonic alignment constraint?
