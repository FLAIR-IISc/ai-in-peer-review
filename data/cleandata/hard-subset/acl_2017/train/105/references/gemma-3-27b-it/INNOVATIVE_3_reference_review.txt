This paper introduces a hard monotonic attention mechanism for morphological inflection generation. The core idea – enforcing a nearly monotonic alignment between input and output characters – is a compelling and conceptually clean approach to a challenging problem, particularly in morphologically rich languages. While the technical execution isn’t groundbreaking in terms of architectural novelty (it’s a standard encoder-decoder with a specific attention constraint), the focus on *controlling* the attention and aligning it with linguistic expectations is valuable.

The primary contribution is the proposed hard attention mechanism, specifically designed for the inherent monotonicity of morphological inflection. This is a relatively bold move, as most recent work has leaned into soft attention’s flexibility. The paper successfully demonstrates that imposing this constraint doesn’t harm performance and, in some low-resource scenarios, *improves* it, even surpassing more complex models.  A secondary contribution is the empirical validation on three datasets, showing competitive and often state-of-the-art results. The ablation-style analysis comparing hard and soft attention, and examining learned representations, is a welcome addition.

**Strengths:**

1. **Novelty of Approach:** The hard attention mechanism for morphological inflection is a genuinely interesting and relatively unexplored direction. The premise that enforcing monotonicity can be beneficial, especially with limited data, is well-supported by the results. This isn’t simply another tweak to an existing architecture; it's a change in fundamental modeling assumptions.
2. **Strong Performance in Low-Resource Setting:** The demonstrated superiority over previous methods, including latent variable WFSTs, on the CELEX dataset is significant. This addresses a crucial pain point in morphological inflection – the need for large training corpora. The model’s ability to learn effectively with limited data is a major strength.
3. **Clear Presentation and Analysis:** The paper is well-written and the methodology is clearly explained.  The analysis of the learned alignments and representations offers valuable insights into how the model functions and what it learns. The visualization in Figure 3 is particularly effective.
4. **Well-Established Connection to Linguistic Theory:** The authors ground their work in the linguistic properties of morphological inflection, which strengthens the motivation and interpretation of the results. The emphasis on nearly-monotonic alignment is sensible and aligns with linguistic intuition.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Scope of Novelty:**  The core architectural components (bi-directional RNN encoder, LSTM decoder) are standard. The main innovation lies in the attention mechanism. While impactful, this isn’t a fundamentally new neural network architecture.
2. **Dependence on Pre-trained Alignments:** The reliance on pre-trained character-level alignments for training the hard attention model is a potential limitation. While the authors argue this simplifies training, it introduces an external dependency and a potential source of error propagation. The quality of the alignment directly impacts the model's performance. While the choice to use an external alignment is motivated, it does feel somewhat like shifting complexity rather than solving it.
3. **SIGMORPHON Results are Less Convincing:** While performance is strong on suffixing languages, the model underperforms on languages with more complex morphology (vowel/consonant harmony, etc.). This suggests the hard attention mechanism may not be universally applicable and its effectiveness is tied to the specific morphological typology of the target language. More discussion of these limitations is warranted.
4. **Insufficient Error Analysis:** While the alignment analysis is useful, a more thorough error analysis would be beneficial. What types of errors does the model make? Are there specific morphological patterns it struggles with? Addressing this would provide a deeper understanding of the model’s strengths and weaknesses.



**Questions to Authors:**

1. How sensitive is the model’s performance to the quality of the pre-trained character-level alignments? Have you experimented with different alignment methods or error rates in the pre-training phase?
2. Can you elaborate on the reasoning behind choosing the specific character alignment model (Sudoh et al., 2013)? Were other models considered and, if so, why was this one selected?
3. The paper mentions the hard attention mechanism's potential for application to other sequence transduction tasks. Could you provide more specific examples and discuss the challenges of adapting the model to tasks with less inherent monotonicity?
4. The SIGMORPHON results indicate limitations for languages with complex morphology. Do you have any hypotheses about why the hard attention mechanism struggles in these cases? Would incorporating a mechanism for handling long-range dependencies improve performance?

**Additional Comments:**

The supplementary material should include more details about the implementation, including hyperparameters, training procedures, and hardware/software environment.



Overall, this is a strong paper that presents a novel and promising approach to morphological inflection generation. The focus on monotonicity and the resulting improvements in low-resource settings are particularly compelling. While the architectural novelty is limited, the conceptual contribution and empirical validation are significant. I recommend acceptance, with minor revisions to address the weaknesses outlined above.
