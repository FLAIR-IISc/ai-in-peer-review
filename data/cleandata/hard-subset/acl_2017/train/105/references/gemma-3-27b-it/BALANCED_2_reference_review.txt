This paper presents a hard attention model for morphological inflection generation, addressing the issue of data sparsity in morphologically rich languages. The core idea is to model a nearly monotonic alignment between input and output character sequences using a dedicated control mechanism within an encoder-decoder framework. The authors demonstrate strong performance on three benchmark datasets (CELEX, Wiktionary, and SIGMORPHON2016), surpassing previous state-of-the-art results, especially in low-resource settings.

**Contributions:**

1. **A novel hard attention model for morphological inflection:** The proposed model explicitly models the near-monotonic alignment inherent in morphological inflection, offering a more controlled and interpretable approach compared to soft attention mechanisms. This is a significant contribution as it directly addresses a key characteristic of the task.
2. **State-of-the-art performance:** The model achieves new state-of-the-art results on three widely used datasets, demonstrating its effectiveness and competitiveness against existing neural and non-neural approaches. This is particularly noteworthy on CELEX, where it outperforms the latent variable WFST model even with limited training data.
3. **Analysis of model behavior:** The authors provide a thorough analysis of their model, comparing it to a soft attention model and shedding light on the features learned by each. This analysis offers valuable insights into the representations learned for morphological inflection and highlights the benefits of the hard attention mechanism.

**Strengths:**

1. **Strong Empirical Results:** The consistent performance improvements across multiple datasets are compelling. The ability to surpass the latent variable WFST on CELEX with limited data is a particularly strong result, demonstrating the model's effectiveness in low-resource scenarios. This is a significant achievement and makes a strong case for the proposed method.
2. **Clear and Concise Presentation:** The paper is well-written and easy to follow. The model architecture is clearly explained, and the experimental setup is described in sufficient detail for reproducibility. The inclusion of Figure 1 is particularly helpful for visualizing the hard attention mechanism.
3. **Insightful Analysis:** The analysis comparing the hard and soft attention models is insightful. Identifying the differences in learned representations and relating them to the model's performance provides a deeper understanding of the benefits of the proposed approach. The discussion of positional encoding is particularly helpful.
4. **Problem Relevance:** Morphological inflection generation is a crucial task for NLP, especially for morphologically rich languages. Improving performance in this area has clear downstream benefits for tasks like machine translation.
5. **Novelty of Approach:**  The incorporation of a hard attention mechanism, with its explicit control over alignment, is a novel contribution within the context of neural morphological inflection generation. This distinguishes the work from previous approaches relying on soft attention or other modeling paradigms.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Discussion of Error Analysis:** While the authors demonstrate strong overall performance, a more detailed error analysis would be beneficial.  Specifically, exploring cases where the model fails and identifying the types of morphological processes that are particularly challenging would provide valuable insights for future work.
2. **Dependence on Pre-trained Alignments:** The reliance on independently learned character-level alignments for training introduces an external dependency. The performance of the model is therefore partially reliant on the quality of these pre-trained alignments.  A discussion of the potential impact of alignment errors on the model's performance would be valuable.
3. **SIGMORPHON Results - Context Sensitivity:** The observation that the model performs less well on languages with complex context-sensitive morphology (e.g., vowel harmony) is important but could be expanded upon. Further investigation into how the hard attention mechanism could be adapted to handle such phenomena would be beneficial.
4. **Ablation Studies:** While comparisons to other models are provided, ablation studies examining the contribution of different components of the proposed model (e.g., the bi-directional encoder, the feedback connection) would strengthen the analysis and demonstrate the importance of each component.
5. **Implementation Details:** While the paper states the source code is available, a more detailed description of key implementation choices (e.g., hyperparameters, optimization settings) would aid reproducibility.

**Questions to Authors:**

1. How sensitive is the model's performance to the quality of the pre-trained character-level alignments? Have you experimented with different alignment tools or methods for generating these alignments?
2. Could you elaborate on the types of errors the model makes, especially in the context of languages with complex morphological processes? Are there specific morphological rules or phenomena that the model struggles with?
3. What modifications could be made to the hard attention mechanism to better handle context-sensitive morphology, such as vowel harmony?
4. Did you perform any ablation studies to assess the contribution of different components of the model, such as the bi-directional encoder or the feedback connection?

**Additional Comments:**

* The paper uses consistent notation and terminology.
* The related work section provides a good overview of existing approaches.
* The references are appropriately cited.



Overall, this is a strong paper that presents a novel and effective approach to morphological inflection generation. The authors have demonstrated compelling results and provided valuable insights into the learned representations. Addressing the weaknesses outlined above would further strengthen the paper and enhance its contribution to the field. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions.
