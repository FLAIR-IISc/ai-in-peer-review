This paper proposes a neural model for morphological inflection generation utilizing a "hard monotonic attention" mechanism. The authors argue this approach addresses limitations of existing sequence-to-sequence models, particularly their data hunger and reliance on learning alignments. They demonstrate competitive or state-of-the-art results on three benchmark datasets (CELEX, Wiktionary, SIGMORPHON2016), and provide an analysis of learned alignments and representations.

The primary contribution of this work is the demonstration that a constrained, hard-attention mechanism can achieve strong performance on morphological inflection, particularly in low-resource settings. The improvement on the CELEX dataset, specifically surpassing the Dreyer et al. (2008) latent variable WFST model, is a notable accomplishment. A secondary contribution lies in the comparative analysis with soft-attention models, offering insights into the differing strategies employed by each approach for this task. Finally, the comprehensive experimental evaluation across diverse datasets strengthens the validity of the findings.

**Strengths:**

1. **Strong Performance in Low-Resource Settings:** The consistently superior performance on the CELEX dataset, which is deliberately small, is a significant advantage. This addresses a genuine pain point in morphological inflection, where labeled data is often scarce. This provides practical value, unlike many models that only shine with abundant data.
2. **Sound Methodology & Clear Presentation:** The model architecture is well-defined and the training procedure is clearly explained. The use of independently learned alignments for generating training signals is a sensible and pragmatic choice. The paper is generally well-written and easy to follow.
3. **Comprehensive Evaluation:**  The authors evaluate their model across three distinct datasets, demonstrating robustness and generalizability. Including SIGMORPHON2016 provides a broader perspective, as it encompasses multiple languages and morphological types.
4. **Meaningful Analysis:** The analysis of learned alignments and representations is valuable. Demonstrating that the model learns plausible alignments (even if similar to soft attention) and provides some interpretability is a positive aspect. This addresses a common critique of neural models—their "black box" nature.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Novelty in Core Architecture:** While the hard attention *mechanism* is a key aspect, the underlying encoder-decoder framework is fairly standard. The novelty lies in the constraint imposed by the hard attention, but the core building blocks aren't groundbreaking. This makes the contribution incremental rather than transformative.
2. **Overstated Claims of Advantage:** The claim of “significantly better” performance with “very small training sets” needs qualification. While CELEX results are strong, the improvement isn't dramatic across *all* scenarios. Performance gains on Wiktionary and SIGMORPHON2016 are more nuanced. The authors should temper the strong language used to describe the advantages.
3. **Insufficient Comparison to Strong Baselines:** While baselines are included, the implementation details of some are not fully specified. For example, the specific hyperparameters and training procedures for the soft attention baselines should be described in more detail to ensure a fair comparison. Furthermore, the paper could benefit from comparing to more recent and sophisticated WFST-based approaches.
4. **Alignment Analysis Lacks Rigor:** The alignment analysis, while insightful, is largely qualitative. A quantitative evaluation of alignment accuracy (e.g., comparing predicted alignments to gold-standard alignments) would strengthen this section.

**Questions to Authors:**

1. Could you provide more details regarding the implementation of the soft attention baselines (specifically, hyperparameters, training duration, and regularization techniques)?
2. Have you considered exploring variations in the hard attention mechanism, such as incorporating a "skip" action or allowing for non-monotonicity in specific cases where morphological processes require it?
3. The paper states the independently learned alignments are used as “gold transduction+control sequences”. What is the impact on performance if these alignments contain errors? Have you investigated the sensitivity of your model to alignment noise?
4. While you note the hard-attention model is more efficient for small datasets, what are the computational costs associated with training and inference compared to soft attention for *large* datasets?
