This paper proposes a hard monotonic attention model for morphological inflection generation, aiming to address the data sparsity issues often encountered in morphologically rich languages. The authors evaluate their model on three datasets (CELEX, Wiktionary, and SIGMORPHON2016) and report competitive results, particularly on smaller datasets. The primary claimed contributions are: 1) a novel hard attention model for nearly-monotonic sequence-to-sequence learning, 2) state-of-the-art performance on three morphological inflection datasets, and 3) an analysis comparing their model to soft attention models.

However, despite presenting a reasonably well-executed approach and achieving solid empirical results, I find several significant shortcomings that lead me to recommend rejection. My assessment is that the paper prioritizes meticulousness and incremental improvements over substantial novelty and broader impact.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Lack of Substantial Novelty:** The core idea of enforcing a monotonic alignment is not entirely new. Previous work has explored similar constraints and techniques. While the “hard attention” mechanism is a reasonable choice, it doesn’t represent a paradigm shift. The primary technical contribution feels like a careful engineering of existing components rather than a breakthrough innovation. The paper doesn't convincingly demonstrate *why* hard attention is fundamentally better suited for this task beyond empirical observations.

2. **Overly Detailed and Pedantic Presentation:** The paper is excessively detailed, bordering on verbose. Numerous statements are self-evident or reiterate established concepts without adding value. The inclusion of the LSTM equations in the supplementary material feels unnecessary and distracts from the core contributions. This level of detail obfuscates the key ideas and makes the paper difficult to quickly grasp.  For example, the lengthy justification for choosing cross-entropy loss feels contrived.

3. **Incremental Improvement & Limited Generalizability:** While the authors achieve state-of-the-art results on some datasets, the improvements are often marginal. The success on CELEX, a very small dataset, doesn't necessarily translate to broader applicability. The performance on SIGMORPHON2016 is not consistently superior to soft attention models, and the explanation offered is somewhat post-hoc and relies on linguistic typology rather than inherent model properties. The reliance on pre-computed alignments diminishes the end-to-end learning aspect and introduces an external dependency.

4. **Questionable Analysis:** The analysis comparing the hard and soft attention models, while attempting to provide insight, feels superficial. The observation that the hard attention model encodes less positional information is presented without a clear explanation of its implications.  The claim that this is *because* of the step actions is speculative and lacks supporting evidence. The SVD visualizations, while visually appealing, don’t provide strong conclusive evidence.

5. **Formatting and Style Issues:** Throughout the paper, there are several minor formatting inconsistencies (e.g., inconsistencies in citation styles, equation numbering). The writing style is often awkward and convoluted. While not critical, these issues contribute to a sense of lack of polish.



**Strengths:**

1. **Thorough Experimental Evaluation:** The paper includes a comprehensive set of experiments across multiple datasets, demonstrating a clear effort to validate the proposed approach.

2. **Clear Implementation Details:** The authors provide sufficient details about the model architecture and training procedure, which allows for reproducibility.

3. **Addressing Data Sparsity:** The reported performance on the CELEX dataset, a known challenge for neural models, is a positive result and demonstrates the potential of the approach in low-resource settings.



**Questions to Authors:**

1.  The performance gains on the CELEX dataset are highlighted. However, how much of this improvement is attributable to the hard attention mechanism versus other factors (e.g., the bi-directional encoder, the feedback connection)? Provide an ablation study specifically isolating the impact of hard attention.

2.  The use of pre-computed alignments is a significant design choice. What is the sensitivity of the model’s performance to the quality of these alignments? Would the model perform similarly with alignments generated by a different method?

3.  The paper suggests that the hard attention mechanism’s performance on languages with complex morphology is limited. What modifications could be made to the model to address this limitation and better handle non-monotonic dependencies?

**Additional Comments:**

*   Please revise the paper to be more concise and focused on the core contributions.
*   Address the formatting and style issues to improve readability.
*   Provide a more rigorous analysis of the model’s behavior and limitations.
*   The GitHub link should be restored in the final version, if accepted.



In conclusion, while the paper demonstrates technical competence and presents a reasonably effective model, the lack of substantial novelty, the overly detailed presentation, and the incremental nature of the improvements outweigh its strengths. Therefore, I recommend rejection.
