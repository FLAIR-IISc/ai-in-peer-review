This paper proposes a hard monotonic attention model for morphological inflection generation, addressing the issues of data sparsity and overfitting in neural sequence-to-sequence models. The key idea is to explicitly model the nearly monotonic alignment between input and output character sequences, using a control mechanism to decide between writing a symbol and advancing the attention pointer. The model is evaluated on three datasets (CELEX, Wiktionary, and SIGMORPHON2016) and achieves state-of-the-art or competitive results, particularly on the low-resource CELEX dataset.

The primary contribution of this work is the introduction of a hard attention mechanism specifically tailored for morphological inflection generation. This mechanism, coupled with a bi-directional encoder and a recurrent decoder, effectively exploits the inherent monotonic alignment characteristic of this task. The design directly addresses the limitations of standard soft attention models in low-resource scenarios and offers a more interpretable alignment process. A second contribution is the thorough empirical evaluation of the model across three diverse datasets, demonstrating its effectiveness and robustness. The ablation study and comparison with soft attention models provide valuable insights into the model's behavior and the features it learns. Finally, the detailed analysis of the learned representations, examining character clustering and positional encoding, offers a deeper understanding of how the model captures morphological information.

**Strengths:**

1. **Novelty of the Hard Attention Mechanism:** The proposed hard attention mechanism is a well-motivated approach to address the challenges of morphological inflection. Unlike soft attention, it enforces a more structured alignment, which is particularly beneficial when training data is limited. This is a significant technical contribution.
2. **Strong Performance on CELEX:** The model demonstrably outperforms existing state-of-the-art methods, including neural and non-neural approaches, on the CELEX dataset. This is a crucial result as CELEX is a notoriously difficult dataset due to its small size, highlighting the effectiveness of the hard attention mechanism in low-resource settings.
3. **Comprehensive Evaluation:** The evaluation across three datasets—each with different characteristics—provides a strong indication of the model's generalizability. The inclusion of Wiktionary and SIGMORPHON2016 broadens the scope of the study and reinforces the validity of the findings.
4. **Insightful Analysis:** The analysis of the learned representations (character clustering and positional encoding) adds depth to the paper.  It provides valuable insights into the model's internal workings and helps explain its performance.  The comparison with soft attention models further clarifies the benefits of the proposed approach.
5. **Clear Presentation:** The paper is well-written and logically organized. The network architecture is clearly explained, and the experimental setup is described in sufficient detail to allow for reproducibility.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Discussion of Hard Attention Implementation Details:** While the concept of hard attention is clearly articulated, the practical implementation details (e.g., how the "step" action is handled during training, how the gold transductions are derived) could benefit from more elaboration. A deeper discussion of the training procedure would strengthen the paper.
2. **Dependence on External Alignment for Training:** The reliance on pre-learned character-level alignments for training is a potential limitation.  While this simplifies training and ensures correct alignments, it introduces a dependency on the quality of the external alignment tool. The paper doesn't fully address the potential impact of errors in this alignment.
3. **Incomplete Comparison on SIGMORPHON2016:** While the model performs well on some languages in the SIGMORPHON2016 dataset, the analysis of why it performs less well on languages with more complex morphology (e.g., vowel harmony) is superficial. A more in-depth analysis of the model's behavior on these challenging languages would be valuable.
4. **Lack of Error Analysis:** The paper lacks a detailed error analysis. Providing examples of common errors made by the model and discussing the reasons behind them would provide further insights into its strengths and weaknesses.
5. **Presentation of Results:** The tables containing results are well presented, but could be improved by including p-values for statistical significance to better demonstrate the improvement of the proposed method.

**Questions to Authors:**

1.  Could you provide more details on the character-level alignment process used to generate the training sequences? Specifically, what measures were taken to ensure the quality of the alignments, and how were potential errors handled?
2.  How sensitive is the model's performance to the quality of the pre-learned character-level alignments? Did you experiment with different alignment tools or techniques?
3.  For the SIGMORPHON2016 dataset, could you provide a more detailed analysis of the model’s performance on languages with complex morphological features (e.g., vowel harmony)? What types of errors does the model make in these cases?
4.  Could you elaborate on the training process of the hard attention model, specifically how the "step" action is incorporated and what loss function is used for optimizing the control mechanism?
