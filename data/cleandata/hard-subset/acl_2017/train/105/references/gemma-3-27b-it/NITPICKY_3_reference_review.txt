This paper presents a hard monotonic attention model for morphological inflection generation, demonstrating competitive performance on standard datasets and particularly strong results in low-resource scenarios. The authors claim three main contributions: a novel hard attention model for nearly-monotonic sequence-to-sequence learning, state-of-the-art results on three morphological inflection datasets, and an analysis comparing their model to a soft attention baseline, shedding light on the learned representations.

The most significant contribution of this work is the demonstration of strong performance, particularly on the CELEX dataset, where the model surpasses prior latent variable WFST models – a long-standing baseline for this task. This is a noteworthy achievement given the difficulties previous neural models have faced with limited data. The idea of explicitly modeling a monotonic alignment, coupled with a hard attention mechanism, appears to be a beneficial design choice in this context. 

A secondary contribution is the comprehensive experimental evaluation across multiple datasets (CELEX, Wiktionary, SIGMORPHON2016). This demonstrates the robustness of the proposed approach and allows for meaningful comparisons with existing methods. The ablation-style analysis (comparing hard vs. soft attention) provides some insight into the learned representations, although this could be expanded upon (see weaknesses).

Finally, the decoupling of alignment and transduction via independently learned alignments is a reasonable approach that simplifies the training process. While not entirely novel, it’s a sensible choice within the context of this task and its known characteristics.

However, despite these strengths, several weaknesses prevent a positive recommendation. 

First, the presentation is excessively detailed and, at times, verbose. The paper includes a large number of equations and architectural details that do not significantly contribute to understanding the core ideas. A more concise and focused presentation would greatly improve readability. For instance, the detailed LSTM equations in the supplementary material seem unnecessary for understanding the model’s overall functionality.

Second, the novelty of the hard attention mechanism itself is questionable. While the application to morphological inflection is specific, hard attention is a well-established technique in sequence-to-sequence modeling. The authors don't adequately motivate *why* hard attention is particularly well-suited for this specific task beyond stating its natural alignment with the data. The claim of a "novel" model feels overstated.

Third, the analysis of the learned representations is superficial. While the SVD visualization provides some intuition, it doesn't offer a particularly insightful or rigorous analysis. The authors state the soft attention model encodes positional information more strongly, but this observation lacks a deeper explanation or connection to the performance differences observed. A more detailed quantitative analysis of the learned representations, perhaps using probing tasks, would be more compelling.

Fourth, the paper suffers from numerous minor formatting inconsistencies and stylistic issues. For example, some references are formatted incorrectly, and there’s inconsistent use of boldface and italics. Such details, while seemingly minor, detract from the overall impression of rigor. The lack of a link to the source code, despite mentioning it, is also problematic for reproducibility.

Fifth, the performance gains on SIGMORPHON2016 are less convincing. The authors attribute the slight underperformance on certain languages to the morphology of those languages, but this explanation feels ad-hoc. A more thorough error analysis would be needed to support this claim.



Questions to Authors:

1.  Can you provide a more detailed justification for the choice of hard attention over soft attention, specifically highlighting the advantages of hard attention for morphological inflection generation beyond the monotonic alignment property?
2.  Could you provide the link to the source code of your models?
3.  Please elaborate on the potential limitations of your approach and discuss how it might perform on languages with more complex morphological processes that deviate from the nearly-monotonic alignment assumption?
4.  Can you provide a more rigorous analysis of the learned representations, perhaps by quantifying the amount of positional information encoded by each model using probing tasks?
