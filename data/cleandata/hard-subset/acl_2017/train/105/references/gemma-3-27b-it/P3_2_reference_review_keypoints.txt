- Paper introduces a neural model for morphological inflection using a hard monotonic attention mechanism that decides between writing output or advancing the attention pointer.
- Model targets near-monotonic alignment to reduce data hunger and overfitting in low-resource, morphologically rich languages.
- Shows strong low-resource performance, outperforming a latent-variable WFST on CELEX and achieving competitive/state-of-the-art results on multiple datasets.
- Explicit hard attention provides a principled, interpretable way to enforce near-monotonic alignments compared to soft attention learning the alignment implicitly.
- Detailed comparative analysis contrasts hard vs. soft attention and inspects learned representations (e.g., SVD visualization).
- Strong empirical results across CELEX, Wiktionary, and SIGMORPHON2016, with particularly notable gains on CELEX.
- Well-motivated model design and clear explanation of the control mechanism within the encoder-decoder framework.
- Thorough evaluation with comparisons to strong baselines and implicit ablation via soft-attention comparisons.
- Clear writing, helpful figures (e.g., Figure 1), and useful implementation details in supplementary material.
- Insightful representation analysis that sheds light on how the model encodes morphological information.
- Limited novelty of individual components; novelty mainly in applying hard attention to this task.
- Dependence on pre-computed character-level alignments (Sudoh et al., 2013) introduces external dependency and possible sensitivity to alignment errors.
- Slightly worse performance than soft attention on many SIGMORPHON languages; offered explanation (typology) is insufficiently detailed.
- Lack of detailed error analysis identifying common mistake types (e.g., suffix errors, stem changes).
- Questions: sensitivity to alignment errors and different alignment methods; request for detailed SIGMORPHON error analysis.
- Question on computational cost: training/inference overhead relative to soft-attention baselines.
- Question on generalization: applicability of hard attention to other near-monotonic sequence transduction tasks.
- Suggest adding a discussion of hard attention limitations and remedies, and more hyperparameter details for reproducibility.
- Overall recommendation: strong paper with minor revisions to address weaknesses; recommend acceptance.
