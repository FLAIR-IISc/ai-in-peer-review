This paper proposes a hard attention model for morphological inflection generation, leveraging the near-monotonic alignment commonly found in this task. The authors evaluate their model on three datasets (CELEX, Wiktionary, and SIGMORPHON2016) and demonstrate competitive or state-of-the-art performance, particularly excelling in low-resource scenarios. The core contribution is a model that explicitly models alignment, rather than learning it jointly with transduction, utilizing pre-trained alignments to guide training.

**Contributions:**

1.  **Hard Attention Model for Morphological Inflection:** The primary contribution is the development of a novel neural architecture utilizing hard monotonic attention specifically tailored for morphological inflection. This approach directly models the alignment between input and output sequences, which is a characteristic of this task, differentiating it from prior soft attention approaches.
2.  **State-of-the-Art Performance on Multiple Datasets:** The authors establish competitive or state-of-the-art results on three commonly used datasets for morphological inflection, demonstrating the effectiveness of their approach across different languages and data sizes. This empirical validation is a substantial contribution.
3.  **Analysis of Learned Representations:** The paper includes an insightful analysis comparing the learned representations of their hard attention model with those of a soft attention model, providing valuable understanding of how each model encodes morphological information.

**Strengths:**

1.  **Strong Empirical Results, Especially in Low-Resource Settings:** The model demonstrably outperforms previous methods, including state-of-the-art neural and non-neural approaches, on the CELEX dataset, which is a particularly challenging low-resource setting. This highlights the model’s ability to effectively learn from limited data. The results on Wiktionary and SIGMORPHON are also compelling.
2.  **Clear and Motivated Design:** The rationale behind using hard monotonic attention is well-articulated. The inherent near-monotonicity of morphological inflection makes this a sensible and potentially powerful modeling choice. The clear explanation of how the model works, with reference to Figure 1, aids understanding.
3.  **Thorough Evaluation and Comparison:** The evaluation is comprehensive, involving three diverse datasets and comparisons with a wide range of baseline models. The inclusion of both neural and non-neural baselines provides a strong context for assessing the model’s performance.
4.  **Insightful Analysis:** The analysis of learned representations (Section 5 and Figures 4a-4d) provides valuable insights into the behavior of both the proposed model and the soft attention baseline. This goes beyond simply reporting results and contributes to a better understanding of the underlying mechanisms.
5.  **Decoupled Alignment and Transduction:** Decoupling the alignment and transduction stages, leveraging pre-computed alignments, is a clever approach that simplifies training and allows for a more focused learning process.

**Weaknesses:**

1.  **Dependence on Pre-computed Alignments:** While decoupling alignment is a strength, the reliance on the character-level alignment model of Sudoh et al. (2013) introduces a potential dependency on the quality of that alignment.  The impact of alignment errors on downstream performance is not fully explored.
2.  **Limited Discussion of Error Analysis:** While the learned representations are analyzed, a more detailed error analysis of specific inflection cases where the model fails would be beneficial. Understanding the types of errors the model makes could lead to further improvements.
3.  **Presentation of Results:** Table 3 (SIGMORPHON) is difficult to interpret quickly. Adding a boldface to highlight the best results in each column would improve readability. Also, the table refers to morphological phenomena which are not clearly described within the table itself.
4.  **Lack of Ablation Studies:** The paper would benefit from ablation studies to evaluate the individual contributions of different components of the model, such as the bi-directional encoder and the feedback connection in the decoder. This would help to pinpoint the most critical aspects of the architecture.



**Questions to Authors:**

1.  How sensitive is the performance of the model to the quality of the pre-computed character-level alignments? Have you experimented with different alignment models or methods for generating these alignments?
2.  Can you provide examples of specific inflection cases where the model fails, and discuss the potential reasons for these failures?
3.  How does the performance of the model vary across different languages with varying degrees of morphological complexity?
4.  What is the computational cost of training and inference with the hard attention model compared to the soft attention model?

**Additional Comments:**

*   The reference list is extensive and includes all relevant works.
*   The link to the source code was removed for anonymity, which is appropriate.
*   The paper is well-written and generally easy to follow, although some sections (e.g., the mathematical formulation of the model) could benefit from further clarification.
*   Consider including a discussion of the limitations of the proposed approach and potential directions for future research.
