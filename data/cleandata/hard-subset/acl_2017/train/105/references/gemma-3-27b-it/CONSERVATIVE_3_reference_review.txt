This paper proposes a neural sequence-to-sequence model for morphological inflection generation utilizing a "hard monotonic attention" mechanism. The core idea is to constrain the attention to move forward in a largely monotonic fashion, reflecting the typical structure of morphological processes (suffixing, etc.). The authors evaluate this approach on three datasets – CELEX, Wiktionary, and SIGMORPHON2016 – demonstrating competitive or state-of-the-art results, particularly on smaller datasets. The key claimed contributions are: 1) the introduction of the hard attention model for nearly-monotonic sequence transduction, 2) establishing new state-of-the-art results on morphological inflection generation, and 3) analysis comparing the hard and soft attention mechanisms.

I view the primary contribution as the empirical demonstration that a hard attention mechanism can be effective for morphological inflection, *particularly* when data is limited. While the idea of leveraging monotonicity isn’t novel, its application within a neural sequence-to-sequence framework for this specific task, and the resulting performance gains on a challenging low-resource setting (CELEX), are noteworthy.  The extensive experimentation and comparison against established baselines are also positive aspects of this work. The analysis comparing hard and soft attention, while not groundbreaking, provides some insight into the model's behavior.

**Strengths:**

1. **Strong Performance on Limited Data:** The model demonstrably outperforms existing neural and non-neural approaches on the CELEX dataset, a critical finding given the difficulties neural models often face with small datasets. This addresses a significant limitation of prior work in this area. The ability to surpass the Dreyer et al. (2008) latent variable WFST model, specifically designed for this task, is a compelling result.
2. **Comprehensive Evaluation:** The authors evaluate their model on three datasets of varying size and complexity. This demonstrates the robustness of the approach beyond a single, potentially cherry-picked dataset. The inclusion of SIGMORPHON2016, a standard benchmark, adds further credibility.
3. **Well-Established Baselines:** The comparison against a strong set of established baselines (latent variable WFSTs, previous neural models) provides a clear benchmark for assessing the contribution of the proposed approach. The authors don’t simply compare to the most recent work, but also include relevant, older, but still highly competitive methods.
4. **Clear and Detailed Methodology:** The paper provides a clear and detailed description of the model architecture, training procedure, and experimental setup. This facilitates reproducibility and allows for a thorough understanding of the approach. The inclusion of implementation details in the supplementary material is commendable.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Novelty of Core Idea:**  The concept of enforcing monotonicity in sequence transduction isn’t entirely new. While the specific implementation with “hard attention” is a specific choice, it’s not a fundamentally groundbreaking architectural innovation.  The paper would benefit from more clearly articulating what *specifically* distinguishes this approach beyond the application of a well-known principle.
2. **Overstated Claims of State-of-the-Art:** While the results are competitive, claiming *new* state-of-the-art on all datasets feels slightly overstated. The gains on Wiktionary and SIGMORPHON are incremental, and sometimes rely on subtle differences in experimental setup or data splits. The authors should moderate this language and acknowledge the closely competitive performance.
3. **Lack of Ablation Studies:** The paper lacks ablation studies to assess the individual contribution of the hard attention mechanism.  How much of the performance gain is due to the hard attention *versus* other aspects of the architecture (e.g., the bi-directional encoder, the recurrent decoder)?  Without such analysis, it’s difficult to determine the true value of the proposed method.
4. **Analysis is Primarily Qualitative:** The analysis section, while interesting, is largely qualitative. While the visualizations of learned representations are informative, they lack quantitative backing. A more rigorous analysis of the alignment distributions or the learned attention weights would strengthen the conclusions.



**Questions to Authors:**

1. Could you provide ablation study results demonstrating the contribution of the hard attention mechanism, perhaps by comparing performance to a standard soft attention model with otherwise identical architecture and training?
2. You mention that the independent alignment process used for training is crucial. How sensitive is the performance of your model to errors in this pre-computed alignment?  Have you experimented with noisy or imperfect alignments?
3. In the SIGMORPHON results, you attribute the slightly lower performance on certain languages to their morphological complexity. Can you provide a more detailed analysis of *which* specific morphological phenomena pose the greatest challenge for the hard attention mechanism?
4. The paper mentions a forward-backward approach for training in related works. Why was this approach not considered for your model?



**Additional Comments:**

The paper is generally well-written and clearly presented. The extensive references are appreciated.  The link to the code in the original submission has been removed for anonymization, which is appropriate. However, providing a link to a publicly available code repository upon publication would greatly enhance the reproducibility of the work.
