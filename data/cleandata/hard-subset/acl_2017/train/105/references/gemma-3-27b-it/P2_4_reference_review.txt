## Review for "Morphological Inflection Generation with Hard Monotonic Attention"

**Summary:**

This paper proposes a novel neural sequence-to-sequence model for morphological inflection generation. The key innovation is the use of a "hard monotonic attention" mechanism, which constrains the attention to move forward sequentially through the input, reflecting the typical structure of morphological processes. The authors demonstrate strong performance on three benchmark datasets (CELEX, Wiktionary, SIGMORPHON2016), achieving state-of-the-art results, particularly in low-resource scenarios. They also provide an analysis comparing their model to soft attention models, shedding light on the learned representations.

**Main Contributions (as I see them):**

1. **Effective Hard Attention Mechanism for Morphological Inflection:** The most significant contribution is demonstrating the effectiveness of a hard monotonic attention mechanism for this task. By explicitly modeling the sequential alignment, the model avoids some of the pitfalls of soft attention, especially when data is limited.
2. **Strong Empirical Results:** The paper presents compelling experimental results, consistently outperforming previous methods, particularly on the challenging CELEX dataset. This demonstrates the practical value of the proposed approach.
3. **Insightful Analysis:** The analysis comparing hard and soft attention provides valuable insights into the representations learned by these models and explains the benefits of the hard attention approach in this context.

**Strengths:**

1. **Addresses a Key Challenge:** Morphological inflection generation is a crucial component of NLP for morphologically rich languages, and handling low-resource scenarios is a significant practical concern. This paper directly addresses these challenges with a novel approach.
2. **Clear and Well-Motivated Design:** The rationale behind using hard monotonic attention is clearly explained and well-motivated by the inherent characteristics of morphological processes.  The design choices are logical and contribute to the model’s performance.
3. **Comprehensive Evaluation:** The authors perform extensive experiments on three diverse datasets, demonstrating the generalizability of their approach.  They compare against strong baselines, including both neural and non-neural methods.
4. **Strong Performance in Low-Resource Setting:** The model's success on the CELEX dataset, where it surpasses previous methods, is a particularly strong point. This highlights its ability to learn effectively from limited data.
5. **Helpful Analysis of Learned Representations:** The SVD visualization and discussion of the learned representations offer valuable insights into how the model is processing morphological information.  This goes beyond simply reporting performance numbers.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Novelty of Core Components:** While the *combination* of hard attention and morphological inflection is novel, the core components (RNN encoder-decoder, attention mechanism) are well-established. The paper’s novelty lies primarily in the application and adaptation of these techniques, rather than fundamentally new algorithmic breakthroughs.  This doesn't diminish the value, but should be acknowledged.
2. **Dependency on Pre-Computed Alignments:** The reliance on independently learned character-level alignments for training is a potential limitation. While the authors argue this simplifies training, it introduces an external dependency and might not generalize well to languages where accurate character-level alignments are difficult to obtain.  It would be beneficial to see results with end-to-end training, even if performance is slightly lower.
3. **Discussion of Soft Attention Limitations Could Be Deeper:** While the authors touch upon the overfitting of soft attention on small datasets, a more in-depth discussion of *why* soft attention struggles in this context would be valuable.  Specifically, exploring whether the attention weights become too diffuse or fail to capture the sequential nature of morphological processes would strengthen the analysis.
4. **Presentation could be more concise:** The paper is quite lengthy. Some sections, particularly the background and related work, could be condensed without sacrificing essential information.



**Questions to Authors:**

1. **Alignment Sensitivity:** How sensitive is the model's performance to the quality of the pre-computed character-level alignments? Did you experiment with different alignment methods or investigate the impact of alignment errors?
2. **End-to-End Training:** Have you experimented with end-to-end training of the model, where the alignment is learned jointly with the transduction task? If so, what were the results compared to the current approach?
3. **Generalizability of Alignment Approach:** Do you anticipate the pre-computed alignment approach being transferable to languages with significantly different morphological structures where high-quality character-level alignments are harder to acquire?
4. **Ablation Study**: Could you perform an ablation study to quantify the contribution of the hard attention mechanism compared to a soft attention mechanism within the same architecture and training procedure?

**Additional Comments:**

* The paper is well-written and generally easy to follow.
* The inclusion of a visual representation of the hard attention mechanism (Figure 1) is very helpful.
* The link to the source code is appreciated (although anonymized as requested).



**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper that makes a valuable contribution to the field of morphological inflection generation. The proposed hard attention mechanism is effective, the experimental results are compelling, and the analysis provides valuable insights. While the novelty of the core components is incremental, the application and adaptation of these techniques, coupled with the insightful analysis, warrant acceptance.  I recommend acceptance with minor revisions to address the weaknesses outlined above.
