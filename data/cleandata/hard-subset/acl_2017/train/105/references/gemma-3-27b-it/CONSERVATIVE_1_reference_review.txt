This paper proposes a hard monotonic attention mechanism for morphological inflection generation, arguing it addresses the data scarcity issues plaguing neural sequence-to-sequence models in this domain. The authors evaluate their approach on three datasets (CELEX, Wiktionary, and SIGMORPHON2016), claiming state-of-the-art performance and improved robustness with limited training data.

The primary contribution of this work is a seemingly effective method for improving performance on low-resource morphological inflection, particularly on the CELEX dataset, where it demonstrably outperforms previous work. The focus on leveraging the inherent monotonicity of the task via hard attention is a reasonable and potentially impactful design choice. A secondary contribution lies in the comprehensive empirical evaluation across multiple datasets, establishing a strong baseline for future research. Finally, the analysis comparing the learned alignments and representations of the hard and soft attention models provides some insight into the mechanisms driving performance differences.

**Strengths:**

1. **Strong Performance on CELEX:** The model's ability to surpass the latent variable WFST model of Dreyer et al. (2008) on the notoriously difficult CELEX dataset is a significant result. This highlights a practical benefit – improved performance in low-resource scenarios. The results are clearly presented in Table 1 and the accompanying discussion is convincing.
2. **Comprehensive Evaluation:** The authors conduct thorough experiments across three datasets, using established evaluation protocols and comparing against strong baselines. This lends credibility to their claims and allows for a reasonable assessment of the model's generalization ability.  The inclusion of SIGMORPHON2016, with its diverse languages, is particularly valuable.
3. **Justification for Hard Attention:** The rationale behind using hard attention – to exploit the monotonic alignment properties of morphological inflection – is well-motivated and intuitively appealing. The paper provides a clear explanation of how the control mechanism operates, and the visual depiction in Figure 1 is helpful.
4. **Insightful Analysis:** The comparison of learned alignments and representations between the hard and soft attention models is a valuable addition. While not definitive, it offers a preliminary understanding of how these different attention mechanisms capture morphological information.  The discussion of positional encoding is particularly noteworthy.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Novelty of Core Components:** While the combination of hard attention and the control mechanism is a reasonable approach, the individual components (RNN encoders/decoders, attention mechanisms) are well-established in the literature. The core innovation feels incremental rather than groundbreaking. The paper doesn’t convincingly demonstrate *why* hard attention is fundamentally superior, beyond its empirical success on CELEX.
2. **Dependence on Pre-trained Alignments:** The reliance on independently learned character-level alignments for training the hard attention mechanism feels like a significant limitation. While the authors argue this simplifies training, it introduces an external dependency and raises questions about the robustness of the approach if the alignment model performs poorly. The quality of the alignment process is glossed over; a more detailed discussion of its potential impact on the overall system is needed.
3. **Overstated Claims of Generalizability:** While the results on Wiktionary are positive, the performance gains over strong baselines aren’t always substantial.  The claim of “significantly better” performance needs qualification. Furthermore, the performance on SIGMORPHON2016 is mixed; the model is *not* superior across all languages. The linguistic typology discussion, while interesting, feels like post-hoc rationalization.
4. **Presentation of Results:** Table 3, while containing a lot of information, is difficult to quickly interpret. The ordering of languages isn't intuitive, and highlighting statistically significant differences would improve clarity.



**Questions to Authors:**

1.  How sensitive is the model's performance to the quality of the pre-trained character-level alignments?  Could you present results with different alignment models or a noisy alignment process?
2.  Can you provide a more detailed ablation study, isolating the contribution of the hard attention mechanism from other components of the model (e.g., the bi-directional encoder, the recurrent decoder)?
3.  You mention that the soft attention model overfits on the CELEX dataset.  Could you explore regularization techniques (e.g., dropout, weight decay) to mitigate this overfitting and potentially improve its performance?
4.  Regarding the observation that the hard attention model doesn't strongly encode positional information, could this be addressed by incorporating positional embeddings into the encoder, similar to those used in transformer models?



**Additional Comments:**

* The reference to a GitHub repository is helpful, but it should be made accessible to reviewers if possible.
* The phrase "nearly-monotonic" is used repeatedly. A more precise definition of what constitutes "nearly-monotonic" would be beneficial.
* Several references are missing complete information (e.g. author lists).
