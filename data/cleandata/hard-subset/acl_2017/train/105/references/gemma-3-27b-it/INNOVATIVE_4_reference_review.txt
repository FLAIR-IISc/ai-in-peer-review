This paper introduces a novel hard attention mechanism for morphological inflection generation, framing the task as a series of discrete “step” and “write” actions. The core contribution lies in this explicit modeling of nearly-monotonic alignment, decoupling alignment from the decoding process and leveraging pre-computed alignments for training. While the methodology isn’t groundbreaking in terms of neural network architecture (it's a fairly standard encoder-decoder with LSTM), the *approach* to alignment and transduction is a compelling shift. The strong empirical results, particularly surpassing previous state-of-the-art models on the challenging CELEX dataset with limited data, further bolster the significance of this work. A secondary, valuable contribution is the detailed analysis comparing the learned representations and alignments of the hard and soft attention models, providing insights into the strengths and weaknesses of each approach.

**Strengths:**

1. **Novelty of Approach:** The hard attention mechanism, treating inflection generation as a sequence of discrete actions, is a genuinely novel perspective. This contrasts with the prevalent soft attention models and offers a more interpretable and controllable approach, specifically well-suited to the inherent monotonic tendencies of morphological processes. This isn’t just another architectural tweak; it’s a different way of *thinking* about the problem.
2. **Strong Performance on Low-Resource Data:** The paper convincingly demonstrates superior performance, especially on the CELEX dataset, a notorious bottleneck for neural models. This ability to outperform even specialized WFST models with limited training data is a significant achievement, suggesting the hard attention mechanism effectively leverages the structural properties of morphological data.
3. **Insightful Analysis:** The comparison with soft attention models isn’t merely a benchmark; it’s a thoughtful exploration of how each model learns and represents morphological information. The finding that hard attention encodes less explicit positional information (relying instead on the discrete “step” actions) is a particularly interesting observation that warrants further investigation.
4. **Clear Presentation & Thorough Experimentation:** The paper is well-written and presents the method clearly, with a helpful visual representation (Figure 1). The experimentation is comprehensive, covering multiple datasets and comparing against a strong set of baselines.
5. **Decoupled Alignment:** The decision to learn alignments separately and then use them for training is a clever simplification. It avoids the complexities of jointly learning alignment and transduction, allowing for a more focused training process and potentially faster convergence.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Generalization to Complex Morphology:** While performing well on suffixing languages, the model’s performance on datasets like SIGMORPHON2016, featuring more complex morphological phenomena (vowel harmony, consonant harmony), is less impressive. This suggests the hard attention mechanism may struggle with non-monotonic or long-range dependencies, limiting its applicability to all languages. The paper acknowledges this, but a deeper discussion of the limitations and potential extensions would be beneficial.
2. **Dependence on Pre-computed Alignments:** Relying on pre-computed alignments introduces an external dependency and raises questions about the robustness of the model to errors in the alignment process. The paper mentions using a specific alignment tool, but a sensitivity analysis exploring the impact of alignment quality on performance would be valuable.
3. **Lack of Ablation Study:** A more thorough ablation study examining the contribution of different components of the model (e.g., the bi-directional encoder, the recurrent decoder, the control mechanism) would provide a clearer understanding of which aspects are most crucial for performance.
4. **Scalability Concerns:** While the model performs well on the datasets presented, the potential scalability to extremely large vocabularies and complex morphological rules isn’t explicitly addressed. The discrete action space might become computationally expensive for highly inflected languages.



**Questions to Authors:**

1.  Could you elaborate on the choice of the character-level alignment model used (Sudoh et al., 2013)? What other alignment methods were considered, and why was this one selected?
2.  How sensitive is the model’s performance to errors in the pre-computed alignments? Have you investigated the impact of using different alignment qualities or introducing noise into the alignments?
3.  What modifications would be necessary to adapt the hard attention mechanism to handle languages with more complex morphological phenomena, such as vowel or consonant harmony?
4.  Could you discuss the computational complexity of the hard attention mechanism compared to soft attention, particularly in relation to vocabulary size and sequence length?

**Additional Comments:**

*   The link to the source code was removed for anonymization, but it should be re-included in the final version.
*   The paper would benefit from a discussion of related work on constrained decoding or structured prediction, which might offer alternative approaches to enforcing the desired monotonicity.
*   The term “nearly-monotonic” could be more precisely defined.



Overall, this is a strong paper with a novel and promising approach to morphological inflection generation. While some limitations exist, the clear presentation, strong experimental results, and insightful analysis make it a valuable contribution to the field. I recommend acceptance.
