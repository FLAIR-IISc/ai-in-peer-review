## Peer Review: Morphological Inflection Generation with Hard Monotonic Attention

**Summary:**

This paper presents a novel neural model for morphological inflection generation. The core idea is to leverage a hard monotonic attention mechanism within an encoder-decoder framework. This approach contrasts with prevalent soft attention models, and aims to address the data scarcity issues often encountered in morphologically rich languages. The authors demonstrate state-of-the-art or competitive results on three standard datasets (CELEX, Wiktionary, and SIGMORPHON2016), with a particular strength in low-resource scenarios. The paper includes a detailed analysis comparing the proposed model to soft attention approaches, offering insights into the learned representations.

**Main Contributions (as I see them):**

1. **Effective Low-Resource Performance:** The most significant contribution is demonstrating strong performance, *especially* on the small CELEX dataset, outperforming previous models, including those specifically designed for morphological analysis. This addresses a crucial limitation of neural sequence-to-sequence models that require large training sets.
2. **Hard Attention for Morphological Inflection:** The introduction of a hard attention mechanism, specifically tailored for the inherent (near) monotonic alignment characteristic of morphological inflection, is a valuable contribution.  This architectural choice is well-motivated and clearly articulated.
3. **Detailed Analysis & Comparison:** The paper offers a thorough analysis of the learned alignments and representations of both hard and soft attention models, providing valuable insights into the behavior of these models in this specific task.

**Strengths:**

1. **Strong Empirical Results:** The consistent performance across multiple datasets, and particularly the significant improvement on CELEX, lends strong support to the effectiveness of the proposed model. The authors clearly position their work relative to existing state-of-the-art methods.
2. **Well-Motivated Approach:** The rationale behind using hard monotonic attention is sound. Morphological inflection *is* largely a monotonic process, and the explicit modeling of alignment constraints seems beneficial, especially when data is limited. The model’s design directly reflects this linguistic insight.
3. **Thorough Analysis:**  The analysis comparing the learned alignments and representations between the hard and soft attention models is a key strength. This goes beyond simply reporting numbers and demonstrates a deeper understanding of *how* the model works and what it learns.  The visualizations (Figures 3 & 4) are helpful.
4. **Clear and Well-Written:** The paper is well-structured, clearly written, and easy to follow. The technical details are explained thoroughly, and the motivation for design choices is readily apparent.
5. **Practicality and Implementation:** The authors provide details about their implementation and make the code available, which promotes reproducibility and encourages further research.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Novelty of Hard Attention:** While effective, the application of hard attention itself isn’t entirely novel. The paper would benefit from a more detailed discussion of how this specific implementation of hard attention differs from other hard attention mechanisms used in sequence-to-sequence models.  What specific adaptations were made for the morphological inflection task?
2. **Alignment Pre-processing:** The reliance on independently learned alignments for training (using the Sudoh et al. (2013) model) is a potential weakness. The performance is tightly coupled to the quality of *those* alignments. A more robust approach might explore end-to-end learning of alignment *within* the neural network, even if it's more challenging. The paper acknowledges this limitation, but further discussion would be beneficial.
3. **SIGMORPHON Results Interpretation:** The discussion of SIGMORPHON results is somewhat underwhelming. While the model performs well, the authors attribute the slightly worse performance on certain languages to the limitations of hard attention with non-monotonic phenomena.  A deeper dive into *which* specific transformations are causing difficulty and why would strengthen this analysis.
4. **Limited Error Analysis:** While the alignment analysis is helpful, a more detailed qualitative error analysis (examining specific examples where the model fails) would provide valuable insights into the model’s shortcomings and potential avenues for improvement.



**Questions to Authors:**

1.  Can you elaborate on the specific differences between your hard attention implementation and other existing hard attention mechanisms used in sequence-to-sequence models? What design choices were specifically motivated by the morphological inflection task?
2.  How sensitive is the model’s performance to the quality of the pre-computed alignments from Sudoh et al. (2013)? Have you experimented with alternative alignment methods or explored ways to mitigate the impact of alignment errors?
3.  For the SIGMORPHON dataset, could you provide specific examples of morphological transformations that the model struggles with in languages that are not suffixing/stem-changing?  What makes those transformations particularly challenging for the hard attention mechanism?
4.  Could you provide some examples of incorrect predictions made by the model, and explain what the model did "wrong" in those cases? This would help to better understand the model’s failure modes.

**Additional Comments:**

*   The reference list is extensive and well-maintained.
*   The supplementary material is crucial for understanding the LSTM implementation details.




Overall, this is a strong paper with significant contributions to the field of morphological inflection generation. The proposed model is well-motivated, empirically validated, and accompanied by a thorough analysis. Addressing the weaknesses outlined above would further strengthen the paper and increase its impact. I recommend acceptance.
