## Review for ACL: Morphological Inflection Generation with Hard Monotonic Attention

**Summary:**

This paper presents a novel neural model for morphological inflection generation utilizing a hard monotonic attention mechanism. The authors address the challenges of data sparsity in morphologically rich languages, a common issue for neural sequence-to-sequence models.  They propose a model that explicitly enforces a nearly monotonic alignment between the input and output character sequences, guided by pre-computed character-level alignments. Extensive experiments on three datasets (CELEX, Wiktionary, and SIGMORPHON2016) demonstrate competitive or state-of-the-art performance, especially in low-resource settings. A key contribution is surpassing the performance of a strong, non-neural baseline (Dreyer et al., 2008) on CELEX, a feat previous neural models struggled to achieve.  The paper also includes a thoughtful analysis comparing the learned alignments and representations of their model with those of a soft attention baseline.

**Main Contributions (as I see them):**

1. **Effective Low-Resource Performance:** The demonstrated ability to outperform strong baselines, particularly the Dreyer et al. (2008) WFST model, on the CELEX dataset, is a significant result. This highlights the model's effectiveness in scenarios where training data is limited – a practical concern for many morphologically rich languages.
2. **Practical Hard Attention for Morphological Inflection:** The adaptation of hard attention mechanisms to this specific task is valuable. The explicit alignment constraint simplifies training and leverages the inherent monotonic nature of morphological inflection, making it a more targeted and potentially efficient approach.
3. **Insightful Analysis:** The comparison with the soft attention model, particularly the analysis of learned alignments and representations, offers valuable insights into how these models approach the task and highlights the benefits of the hard attention approach in this domain.

**Strengths:**

1. **Strong Empirical Results:** The consistent performance across diverse datasets and comparison with relevant state-of-the-art baselines strongly supports the effectiveness of the proposed model.  The CELEX results are particularly compelling. The authors clearly and comprehensively report their results in a way that allows easy comparison.
2. **Well-Motivated Approach:** The authors convincingly argue for the suitability of a hard monotonic attention mechanism for morphological inflection, given the task's characteristics. The rationale for decoupling alignment and transduction is also well explained.
3. **Clear and Thorough Presentation:** The paper is well-written and logically organized. The model architecture is described clearly, and the experimental setup is detailed and reproducible.  The inclusion of a visual representation of the model (Figure 1) is very helpful.
4. **Insightful Analysis of Learned Representations:** The analysis of learned representations provides valuable insights into how the model encodes information about characters and their positions, and sheds light on the differences between the hard and soft attention approaches.
5. **Addressing a Real-World Problem:** Morphological inflection generation is a crucial component of NLP pipelines for morphologically rich languages, particularly in machine translation. The improvement in this area has direct implications for real-world applications.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Novelty of Hard Attention:**  While effectively *applied* to this task, the hard attention mechanism itself is not novel. The contribution primarily lies in *how* it is adapted and integrated into a specific architecture for morphological inflection. The authors acknowledge this, but it's important to recognize that the core innovation isn’t in the attention *type* but its *application*.
2. **Reliance on Pre-Computed Alignments:** While decoupling alignment simplifies training, the dependence on a pre-computed alignment (Sudoh et al., 2013) introduces a potential point of failure and introduces a dependency on the quality of that alignment model. A more end-to-end approach, even with increased complexity, might be more robust. The sensitivity of performance to the alignment model's performance isn’t thoroughly investigated.
3. **SIGMORPHON Results – Nuance Needed:** The claim of being “the best performing model” on SIGMORPHON2016 needs further qualification. While the results are competitive, the soft attention baseline (their own implementation) performs slightly better overall. The authors attribute this to training details but a more detailed comparison and discussion of the reasons behind the difference would be valuable.
4. **Computational Cost:** The paper doesn't discuss the computational cost of the model, particularly compared to the soft attention baseline.  Hard attention can sometimes be more computationally expensive to train.  A brief discussion of this aspect would be beneficial.



**Questions to Authors:**

1.  How sensitive is the performance of your model to the quality of the pre-computed character-level alignments? Did you experiment with different alignment models, and how did their performance affect the overall results?
2.  Could you provide a more detailed comparison of the training time and computational resources required for your model versus the soft attention baseline?
3.  You mention the soft attention model overfitting on the CELEX dataset. Could you elaborate on any regularization techniques you considered or applied to mitigate this issue?
4.  You mention the limitations of soft attention with stem-changing languages and the benefit of the hard attention method on this type of languages. How do you envision expanding the model to include long-range dependencies for these languages?

**Additional Comments:**

*   The link to the source code should be included in the final version of the paper.
*   A brief discussion of potential error cases and limitations of the model would be beneficial.




**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper that presents a valuable contribution to the field of morphological inflection generation. The model is well-motivated, effectively implemented, and thoroughly evaluated. While the core hard attention mechanism isn't entirely novel, its successful application to this task, coupled with the insightful analysis and strong empirical results, justifies acceptance.  Addressing the weaknesses outlined above, particularly regarding the reliance on pre-computed alignments and a more nuanced discussion of the SIGMORPHON results, would further strengthen the paper.

**Recommendation:** Accept (with minor revisions)
