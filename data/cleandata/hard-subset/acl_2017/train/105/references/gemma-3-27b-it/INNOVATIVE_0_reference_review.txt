This paper introduces a hard monotonic attention mechanism for morphological inflection generation. The core idea – enforcing a nearly monotonic alignment between input and output characters – is a strong and intuitively appealing approach, particularly well-suited to the task's inherent structure. The empirical results are compelling, demonstrating state-of-the-art performance on established datasets, and crucially, a significant improvement on low-resource settings where previous neural models struggled. The analysis of learned alignments and representations, while not deeply novel in itself, provides valuable insights into how the model functions and complements the main contribution.

**Main Contributions (as I see them):**

1. **Novel Hard Attention Mechanism:** The design of a hard attention mechanism specifically tailored to the near-monotonic alignment properties of morphological inflection is the primary contribution. This departs from the dominant soft attention paradigm and offers a compelling alternative.
2. **Strong Empirical Performance, Especially in Low-Resource Scenarios:** The model achieves competitive or state-of-the-art results across multiple datasets, and importantly, overcomes a known limitation of neural models by outperforming a WFST model on the small CELEX dataset.
3. **Insightful Analysis:** The included analysis of learned alignments and representations, while not groundbreaking, adds to our understanding of how these models tackle morphological generation.

**Strengths:**

1. **Elegant and Well-Motivated Approach:** The premise of leveraging the near-monotonicity of morphological inflection is highly sensible. The hard attention mechanism directly embodies this principle, leading to a simpler and potentially more efficient model compared to soft attention alternatives.
2. **Impressive Low-Resource Performance:** The ability to surpass the performance of a specifically tailored WFST model on CELEX is a significant achievement. This highlights the potential of the approach for languages or tasks where large training datasets are unavailable.
3. **Clear and Thorough Experiments:** The authors conduct a comprehensive set of experiments on three different datasets, providing a solid evaluation of the proposed model. The comparison with relevant baselines is fair and informative.
4. **Sound Analysis:**  The analysis of alignments and representations provides some understanding of what the model is learning, although deeper exploration could be beneficial (see weaknesses).
5. **Reproducibility:** The authors mention code availability, which is vital for advancing the field.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Novelty in Analysis:** While the analysis is well-executed, it largely confirms intuitions about attention mechanisms and doesn't reveal profoundly new insights into morphological representation learning. Exploring *why* the hard attention mechanism facilitates better performance in low-resource settings could be more impactful.
2. **SIGMORPHON Performance is Not Dominant:** While competitive, the model isn't clearly superior on SIGMORPHON, suggesting the benefits of hard attention may be more pronounced in specific morphological systems. The authors acknowledge the importance of morphological typology, but a more detailed investigation of which types of languages benefit most from this approach would strengthen the paper.
3. **Training Procedure Dependence on External Alignment:** The reliance on pre-trained character-level alignments feels like a potential bottleneck. While the authors justify this choice, exploring end-to-end training (even with challenges) would be valuable. This also raises questions about the sensitivity of the model to the quality of the initial alignment.
4. **Minor: Presentation of Equations:** Some equations, particularly in Section 2.3, could benefit from clearer notation and explanation.  The connection between equations (1), (2) and (3) could be made more explicit.

**Questions to Authors:**

1. How sensitive is the model’s performance to the quality of the pre-trained character-level alignments? Have you experimented with different alignment algorithms or noise levels in the alignments?
2. Could you elaborate on why the hard attention mechanism is particularly beneficial for low-resource scenarios? Is it a regularization effect, or a more fundamental property of the alignment process?
3.  You mention that the soft-attention model overfits on the CELEX dataset. Could you quantify this overfitting (e.g., by analyzing the gap between training and development accuracy)?
4.  Are there any cases where the hard attention mechanism demonstrably *fails* – e.g., when facing non-monotonic morphological processes? If so, how does the model behave in those cases?



**Additional Comments:**

The reference list is extensive, but formatting inconsistencies were observed (e.g., missing journal/conference details for some entries). Consider checking the reference formatting carefully. Also, given the importance of the SIGMORPHON dataset, expanding the discussion of results on different language families would be helpful.



Overall, this is a strong paper presenting a novel and promising approach to morphological inflection generation. The clear presentation, strong empirical results, and insightful analysis make it a valuable contribution to the field. While some areas could be strengthened, the benefits of the proposed method, particularly in low-resource scenarios, are compelling. I lean towards **accepting** this paper.
