This paper introduces a hard monotonic attention mechanism for morphological inflection generation. The core idea – explicitly modeling the near-monotonic alignment common in morphological tasks – is a genuinely novel and promising approach. While the method isn’t a complete overhaul of sequence-to-sequence learning, it represents a thoughtful constraint and simplification that demonstrably improves performance, particularly in low-resource settings. The empirical evaluation is thorough, covering multiple datasets and comparing favorably to strong baselines. The analysis of learned alignments and representations offers valuable insights.

**Main Contributions (as I see them):**

1. **Novel Hard Attention Mechanism:** The introduction of a hard attention mechanism specifically tailored for the near-monotonic alignment characteristics of morphological inflection is the primary strength. This isn’t just applying an existing attention technique; it’s a conceptual shift that leverages domain knowledge to simplify and improve the learning process.
2. **Strong Performance in Low-Resource Scenarios:** The paper convincingly demonstrates superior performance compared to existing methods, *especially* when training data is limited. This is a critical advantage, as morphological data is often scarce for many languages. Outperforming a tailored latent variable FST on CELEX is a significant result.
3. **Insightful Analysis:** The analysis of learned alignments and representations provides a deeper understanding of how the model functions and what morphological features it captures. This helps validate the approach and points towards potential future improvements.

**Strengths:**

1. **Novelty and Conceptual Clarity:** The hard attention mechanism is a genuinely novel contribution. The authors clearly articulate the motivation behind it – the inherent near-monotonicity of morphological transformations – and translate this into a well-defined model architecture. The clarity of explanation is commendable.
2. **Empirical Validation:** The experimental results are strong and consistent across multiple datasets. The authors comprehensively compare their model to a range of established baselines, including both neural and non-neural approaches. The reported gains, particularly on the CELEX dataset, are statistically meaningful.
3. **Low-Resource Performance:** The model's ability to outperform existing methods with limited training data is a major advantage. This is a practical concern in morphological inflection, and the paper directly addresses it.
4. **Informative Analysis:** The analysis section provides valuable insights into how the model learns and represents morphological information. The visualization of alignments and the discussion of positional encoding are particularly helpful.
5. **Well-Written and Structured:** The paper is generally well-written and organized, making it easy to follow the authors’ reasoning and understand their contributions.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Discussion of Error Analysis:** While the authors analyze learned alignments and representations, a more detailed error analysis would be beneficial. Specifically, identifying common types of errors the model makes (e.g., specific morpheme combinations it struggles with) could provide further insights and guide future research.
2. **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** The paper doesn’t provide extensive details about hyperparameter tuning. A discussion of the sensitivity of the model to different hyperparameter settings would strengthen the robustness of the results.
3. **Scalability Concerns (Potentially):** While the model performs well on the datasets used, the potential scalability to extremely complex morphologies or very large vocabularies isn’t fully addressed. The hard attention mechanism might become computationally expensive in those scenarios.
4. **Comparison to State-of-the-Art on SIGMORPHON:** While the paper mentions being competitive, claiming SOTA on SIGMORPHON is potentially misleading as the soft attention model implemented by the authors outperforms theirs. This could be due to the implementation details, but a more cautious phrasing would be better.

**Questions to Authors:**

1. Can you provide a more detailed error analysis, identifying common types of errors the model makes and potential reasons for those errors?
2. How sensitive is the model's performance to different hyperparameter settings? Can you provide some insights into the optimal range for key hyperparameters?
3. Have you considered any techniques to improve the scalability of the hard attention mechanism to very large vocabularies or complex morphological systems?
4. Can you clarify the difference in implementation details with the soft attention model on the SIGMORPHON dataset which leads to the performance difference?



**Additional Comments:**

The paper is generally very strong. The novelty of the approach, the solid empirical results, and the insightful analysis make it a valuable contribution to the field. While some minor weaknesses exist, they do not detract significantly from the overall quality of the work. I recommend acceptance.
