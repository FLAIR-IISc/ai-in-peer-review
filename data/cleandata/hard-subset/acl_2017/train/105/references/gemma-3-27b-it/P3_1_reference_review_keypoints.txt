- Proposes a neural model with hard monotonic attention for morphological inflection, enforcing near-monotonic input–output alignments to simplify learning and help low-resource cases.
- Reports state-of-the-art or competitive results on CELEX, Wiktionary, and SIGMORPHON2016 benchmarks.
- Provides analysis comparing hard vs. soft attention and examining learned representations.

- Addresses data scarcity in morphological inflection and shows robustness in low-resource settings (notably CELEX).
- Architectural choice is well-motivated by the largely sequential nature of morphological transformations.
- Evaluation is comprehensive across multiple datasets and diverse baselines.
- Analysis offers insights into how models encode positional and character relationships.
- Paper is clearly presented, with helpful figures and released code.

- Novelty is limited mainly to the application/combination of established components (bi-RNNs, attention).
- Depends on externally pre-computed alignments (Sudoh et al., 2013), risking error propagation and lacking end-to-end alignment learning.
- Slightly worse performance than soft-attention on some SIGMORPHON languages; requires deeper linguistic failure analysis.
- Ablation studies are limited and should probe specific components (e.g., bi-directional encoder, decoder feedback).

- How sensitive is performance to noise or errors in the pre-computed character alignments?
- Have you attempted end-to-end alignment learning, and what challenges led you to decouple alignment and transduction?
- Which linguistic features or inflection types cause hard-attention to underperform compared to soft-attention?
- How do training and inference computational costs compare to the soft-attention baseline?

- References are comprehensive, supplementary material clarifies LSTM details, and code availability is a strong positive.

- Overall assessment: strong, well-motivated contribution with practical benefits and thorough evaluation; recommend acceptance with minor revisions.
