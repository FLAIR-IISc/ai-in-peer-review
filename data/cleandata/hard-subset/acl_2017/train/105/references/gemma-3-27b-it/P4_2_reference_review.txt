This paper presents a hard attention model for morphological inflection generation, addressing the issue of data sparsity in morphologically rich languages. The authors argue that their model, by directly modeling a nearly monotonic alignment between input and output character sequences, outperforms previous neural and non-neural state-of-the-art approaches, especially with limited training data. The primary contributions are: 1) A novel hard attention model tailored for nearly-monotonic sequence-to-sequence learning, specifically designed for morphological inflection; 2) State-of-the-art performance on three established datasets (CELEX, Wiktionary, and SIGMORPHON2016), surpassing previous models, notably the latent variable WFST on CELEX; and 3) An analysis comparing the hard and soft attention models, providing insights into the features each model learns for this task.

**Strengths:**

1. **Strong Empirical Results:** The paper demonstrates consistently strong performance across multiple datasets, achieving state-of-the-art results on CELEX and competitive results on Wiktionary and SIGMORPHON2016. The consistent improvement over strong baselines is a significant strength, particularly the surpassing of the Dreyer et al. (2008) latent variable model on the CELEX dataset, which is a notable achievement given the historical performance of that model.
2. **Novelty of Hard Attention Approach:**  The proposed hard attention mechanism, directly modeling the monotonic alignment characteristic of morphological inflection, is a valuable contribution.  While attention mechanisms are common, the *hard* attention design and its focus on alignment control offers a distinct approach and appears well-suited to the specific properties of the task. The decoupling of alignment learning and network training is also a beneficial design choice.
3. **Comprehensive Analysis:** The analysis comparing the hard and soft attention models, examining both alignment patterns and learned representations (through SVD), is a significant strength. This analysis adds depth to the work, providing insights not just into *what* the model achieves, but *how* it achieves it, and shedding light on the learned features.  The discussion connecting the model's design to linguistic typology is particularly insightful. 
4. **Well-Structured Presentation:** The paper is generally well-written and clearly structured. The motivation is clearly stated, the model is described in detail, the experiments are well-designed and reported, and the analysis is thoughtful.  The inclusion of visual aids (e.g., Figure 1) enhances understanding.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Discussion of Error Analysis:** While the paper presents strong overall performance, a more detailed error analysis would be beneficial. Identifying specific cases where the model fails (e.g., complex morphological processes, irregular forms) would offer valuable insights for future improvements.  Understanding the types of errors made could pinpoint limitations of the hard attention mechanism.
2. **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** The paper does not explicitly address the sensitivity of the model to hyperparameter tuning. Providing some discussion of this would improve reproducibility and provide guidance to other researchers. It is not clear how much effort was spent on hyperparameter optimization, and whether the reported performance is robust to changes in hyperparameters.
3. **SIGMORPHON Dataset Nuances:** The claim of competitive results on SIGMORPHON warrants closer examination. While performing well overall, the authors admit the model struggles with languages exhibiting more complex morphological phenomena (vowel/consonant harmony). This suggests the monotonic alignment assumption may be a limitation in such cases and should be explicitly discussed. The observation that the soft attention model performs better on those languages should be investigated further.
4. **Lack of Ablation Studies:** To better understand the contribution of individual components of the model (e.g., the bi-directional encoder, the recurrent decoder feedback connection), ablation studies would be valuable.  Removing or modifying these components and observing the impact on performance would strengthen the justification for their inclusion.

**Questions to Authors:**

1. Could you provide more detailed examples of error cases made by the hard attention model, particularly those that differ from the errors made by the soft attention model?
2.  What was the hyperparameter tuning process? Did you perform a systematic search or rely on heuristics? Could you provide details regarding the range of hyperparameters explored?
3.  Regarding the observation that the soft attention model outperforms the hard attention model on languages with complex morphological phenomena, what are your hypotheses for this difference, and what directions could be taken to address this limitation in future work?
4.  Were ablation studies performed to assess the contribution of the bi-directional encoder and the recurrent decoder feedback connection? If so, what were the results?
