This paper presents a novel approach to morphological inflection generation using a hard monotonic attention mechanism within an encoder-decoder framework. The core idea – forcing a near-monotonic alignment between input and output, guided by independently learned alignments – is a strong and potentially impactful one, particularly for morphologically rich languages. The paper demonstrates competitive results on standard datasets, notably surpassing previous methods on the challenging CELEX dataset, even with limited training data.

The primary contribution is the introduction of the hard attention model for morphological inflection, leveraging the inherent near-monotonicity of the task to simplify the learning process and improve performance, particularly in low-resource settings. A secondary contribution lies in the thorough experimental evaluation across multiple datasets, demonstrating the model’s robustness and highlighting its advantages over soft attention approaches. Finally, the analysis of learned representations offers some initial insights into how the model captures morphological features, though this aspect could be expanded.

**Strengths:**

1. **Novelty of Approach:** The hard attention mechanism, explicitly enforcing a near-monotonic alignment, is a genuinely novel approach to morphological inflection. While attention mechanisms are common, the *hard* constraint and decoupling of alignment learning are a refreshing departure from standard sequence-to-sequence models. This offers a different inductive bias well-suited to the problem.
2. **Performance on CELEX:** The model’s ability to outperform existing methods, including sophisticated latent variable FSTs, on the small CELEX dataset is significant. This strongly suggests the model is more data-efficient, a critical advantage for morphologically rich languages where labeled data is scarce.
3. **Clear and Well-Structured Presentation:** The paper is well-written and logically organized. The model architecture is clearly explained, and the experimental setup is comprehensive.  The inclusion of Figure 1 is particularly helpful for visualizing the hard attention mechanism.
4. **Insightful Analysis:** The comparison with the soft attention model and the analysis of learned representations, while not exhaustive, provide valuable insights into the model’s behavior and highlight the differences in how the two approaches encode morphological information. The observation about positional encoding is intriguing.
5. **Practicality & Decoupling:** The decoupling of alignment learning from the main training loop is a practical and appealing design choice. It avoids the complexity of jointly learning alignment and transduction, and allows leveraging existing alignment tools.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Ablation Study:** While the comparison with a soft attention baseline is present, a more detailed ablation study would strengthen the paper. For example, investigating the impact of different alignment sources (e.g., different alignment algorithms) or varying the complexity of the encoder/decoder would be valuable.
2. **SIGMORPHON Results – Mixed Performance:** While competitive, the SIGMORPHON results show that the model isn’t universally superior.  The explanation based on linguistic typology is plausible, but a more in-depth analysis of *why* the model struggles with certain language types is needed.  Are there specific morphological phenomena it struggles with?
3. **Presentation of Learned Alignments:** While Figure 3 illustrates the alignment, it's a single example. A more quantitative evaluation of alignment quality (e.g., comparing to gold alignments on a larger set) would be beneficial.  It is hard to assess the quality of the alignment from one single example.
4. **Discussion of Error Cases:** The paper would benefit from a discussion of common error cases. What types of morphological transformations does the model frequently misgenerate? This could provide insights for future improvements.
5. **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** The paper doesn't discuss the sensitivity of the model to hyperparameter settings. Providing some insight into this aspect would enhance the reproducibility and usability of the work.

**Questions to Authors:**

1.  Could you elaborate on the choice of the character-level alignment model (Sudoh et al., 2013)? What other alignment methods were considered, and why was this one selected?
2.  Regarding the SIGMORPHON results, can you provide a more detailed analysis of the error patterns for the languages where the hard attention model underperforms?
3.  What are the computational costs associated with learning and applying the hard alignments compared to a soft attention approach?
4.  Would it be possible to adapt the model to handle non-monotonic morphological processes, perhaps by incorporating a more flexible attention mechanism or a different decoding strategy?
