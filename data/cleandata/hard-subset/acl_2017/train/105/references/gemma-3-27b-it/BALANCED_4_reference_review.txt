This paper proposes a hard attention model for morphological inflection generation, addressing the challenges of small training datasets and the need for explicit alignment. The core idea of modeling nearly-monotonic alignments with a dedicated control mechanism – choosing between writing a symbol and advancing the attention pointer – is a sensible approach given the characteristics of the task. The paper demonstrates strong performance on three benchmark datasets, surpassing previous state-of-the-art results, particularly on the low-resource CELEX dataset. The analysis of learned alignments and representations provides valuable insights into the model’s behavior.

**Contributions:**

1. **Novel Hard Attention Mechanism:** The introduction of a hard attention mechanism specifically tailored for nearly-monotonic sequence transduction tasks, effectively decoupling alignment and decoding, is the primary contribution. This allows the model to leverage the inherent structure of morphological inflection.
2. **State-of-the-Art Performance:** The model achieves new state-of-the-art results on three established morphological inflection datasets (CELEX, Wiktionary, and SIGMORPHON2016), demonstrating its effectiveness and practical value.  The performance gain on CELEX, surpassing previous latent variable WFST models, is particularly noteworthy.
3. **Insightful Analysis:** The comprehensive analysis of learned alignments and representations—showing similarities and differences with soft attention models—provides valuable insights into the model’s inner workings and the information it encodes. This is useful for understanding what makes the model successful and for future research.

**Strengths:**

1. **Strong Empirical Results:** The consistently strong performance across multiple datasets provides compelling evidence for the efficacy of the proposed approach.  The ablation study comparing soft and hard attention is particularly useful.
2. **Clear Motivation and Problem Definition:** The paper clearly articulates the challenges of morphological inflection generation, particularly the need for handling low-resource scenarios and explicitly modeling the alignment between input and output.
3. **Well-Designed Control Mechanism:** The hard attention control mechanism—explicitly deciding between “step” and “write” actions—is a clever way to enforce the desired monotonic alignment and simplify the learning process.
4. **Thorough Analysis:** The examination of the learned representations (through SVD) and alignment patterns adds significant value, moving beyond purely empirical evaluation. The link between the hard attention control and positional encoding is a particularly insightful observation.
5. **Reproducibility:** The mention of code availability encourages reproducibility and further research.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Novelty of Hard Attention:** While applied effectively, hard attention itself is not a novel concept. The paper's contribution lies more in the *application* of hard attention to this specific task and the design of the control mechanism, rather than a fundamental innovation in attention mechanisms themselves. This should be acknowledged more explicitly.
2. **Training Procedure Dependence on External Alignment:** The reliance on an independently learned character-level alignment to generate the training sequences introduces a dependency on the quality of that alignment model. The paper acknowledges this, but further discussion on the sensitivity of performance to alignment errors would be beneficial. How robust is the model to inaccuracies in the pre-learned alignment?
3. **SIGMORPHON Results – Language-Specific Performance:** The analysis of results on the SIGMORPHON dataset is somewhat superficial. The paper attributes performance differences to linguistic typology, but a more detailed investigation of *which* languages the model struggles with and *why* could be valuable.  Presenting a breakdown of performance per language in a table, even a smaller one, would be informative.
4. **Missing Error Analysis:** While the alignment analysis is helpful, a traditional error analysis – examining specific examples where the model fails – would further illuminate the model’s weaknesses and potential areas for improvement. What types of morphological processes does the model struggle with?
5. **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** A discussion of hyperparameter sensitivity and the rationale behind the chosen settings would strengthen the paper. Were extensive hyperparameter sweeps conducted?

**Questions to Authors:**

1.  How sensitive is the model’s performance to the quality of the independently learned character-level alignment used for training? Did you explore training with noisy or imperfect alignments?
2.  Could you provide a more detailed breakdown of the SIGMORPHON 2016 results, showing performance per language? This would help understand the model’s strengths and weaknesses across different morphological types.
3.  Can you elaborate on the practical implications of decoupling the alignment and decoding stages? Does it simplify training or reduce computational costs compared to joint learning approaches?
4.  What specific types of errors does the model make? Providing a few illustrative examples of incorrect inflections and a discussion of their causes would be insightful.



**Additional Comments:**

*   Figure 2’s labels are quite small and difficult to read.
*   The related work section is comprehensive, but could benefit from a more critical discussion of the limitations of existing approaches.
*   The discussion of "nearly-monotonic" alignment could be more precise. Are there cases where non-monotonicity is present in the data, and how does the model handle them?
*   Consider briefly mentioning potential future directions, such as applying the model to other sequence transduction tasks beyond morphological inflection.
