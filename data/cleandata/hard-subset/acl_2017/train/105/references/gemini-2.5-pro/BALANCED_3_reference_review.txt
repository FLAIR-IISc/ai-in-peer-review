### Summary of the Paper

This paper proposes a sequence-to-sequence model with a hard monotonic attention mechanism for the task of morphological inflection generation. The key idea is to model the transduction process as a sequence of discrete actions: either writing an output character or stepping forward through the input sequence. This explicitly captures the largely monotonic nature of morphological processes like suffixation. Unlike standard attention models that learn alignments jointly with the transduction task, this work decouples the two: it first uses an external tool to generate character alignments for the training data, then converts these alignments into gold-standard action sequences (of "write" and "step" operations). The encoder-decoder model is then trained with a simple cross-entropy loss to predict these action sequences. The authors conduct extensive experiments on three datasets (CELEX, Wiktionary, and SIGMORPHON 2016), demonstrating that their model achieves state-of-the-art results, particularly in low-resource settings where standard soft-attention models tend to overfit. The paper concludes with an analysis comparing the learned representations and alignments of their hard attention model against a soft attention baseline, providing insights into their differing behaviors.

### Main Contributions

1.  **An effective and data-efficient hard monotonic attention model for morphological inflection.** The primary contribution is a simple yet powerful model that excels in low-resource scenarios. By decoupling alignment and transduction and training the model on a deterministic sequence of explicit "write" and "step" actions, the model avoids the complexities of joint training and is less prone to overfitting, successfully addressing a known weakness of neural models on this task. The results on the CELEX dataset, where it is the first neural model to surpass the highly specialized latent-variable FST of Dreyer et al. (2008), are particularly compelling.
2.  **A thorough empirical evaluation and insightful comparative analysis.** The paper provides a strong empirical case for the model's effectiveness across three diverse, standard benchmarks, covering low-resource, high-resource, and typologically varied settings. Crucially, the analysis in Section 5 goes beyond reporting metrics, offering a plausible explanation for the model's success by comparing the learned internal representations and alignment behaviors against a soft-attention model, thereby shedding light on how different attention mechanisms adapt to this task.

### Strengths

1.  **Excellent performance in low-resource settings.** The paper's most significant strength is its demonstration of superior performance on small datasets. Morphological inflection is often a low-resource problem, and previous neural models have struggled to compete with specialized non-neural systems in this regime. The results on CELEX (Table 1) and the learning curves (Figure 2) provide clear evidence that the proposed hard attention model is more data-efficient and less susceptible to overfitting than soft-attention counterparts. This is a valuable contribution to the field.
2.  **Model simplicity and interpretability.** The proposed method is conceptually simple and elegant. Decomposing the task into a sequence of `WRITE(char)` and `STEP` actions makes the transduction process explicit and interpretable. This design choice simplifies the training objective to a standard cross-entropy loss over a fixed target sequence, circumventing more complex and potentially unstable training procedures like those used in RNN-T models that marginalize over all possible alignments. The resulting derivations are transparent and easier to analyze than the distributed attention weights of a soft-attention model.
3.  **Comprehensive experiments and strong analysis.** The authors evaluate their model rigorously on three different benchmarks, comparing it against a range of strong and relevant baselines, including both neural and non-neural SOTA systems. The paper is also commendably transparent about the model's limitations, noting its weaker performance on languages with non-local morphological phenomena (Table 3). The analysis section is a standout feature, using SVD visualizations to investigate how hard and soft attention models encode positional and character information. This analysis provides valuable insights that help explain *why* the proposed model works well and strengthens the overall scientific contribution of the paper.

### Weaknesses

1.  **Dependence on an external alignment model.** The model's success is contingent on the quality of the alignments produced by the external character aligner (Sudoh et al., 2013). This decoupling, while simplifying training, also means that alignment errors are propagated and the model's performance is fundamentally capped by the aligner's quality. The paper does not investigate the sensitivity of the final model to the quality of these pre-computed alignments. An ablation study using a different or simpler aligner (e.g., one based on Levenshtein distance) would have been valuable to understand this dependency and the robustness of the approach.
2.  **Inherent limitation to monotonic phenomena.** The model's core design assumption is near-monotonicity. While effective for suffixing and stem-internal changes, this makes it architecturally ill-suited for non-monotonic transformations like circumfixation, infixation, or templatic morphology. The results on the SIGMORPHON dataset already confirm this, where soft attention performs better on languages with such phenomena. While the authors acknowledge this, the paper could be more explicit about this architectural limitation. The suggestion in the conclusion that the model could be applied to tasks like machine translation seems overly optimistic, as non-local reordering is a central challenge in MT.
3.  **Lack of discussion on the derivation vs. output objective mismatch.** Footnote 1 correctly points out that the model is trained to find the best *derivation* (action sequence), not necessarily the best *output string*, and that this is an approximation. This is a non-trivial distinction, as multiple derivations can lead to the same output, and beam search over actions may not be equivalent to searching over output strings. A more detailed discussion of the practical implications of this approximation would be beneficial. For example, does this lead to specific types of errors during inference? How is inference performed (e.g., beam search on actions), and have the authors analyzed the diversity of outputs within the beam?

### Questions to Authors

1.  Your model's performance depends on pre-computed alignments. How sensitive is the model to the choice of alignment tool? For instance, what is the impact on performance if a simpler method, like using the edit script from Levenshtein distance, is used to generate the oracle action sequences?
2.  Regarding the approximation mentioned in Footnote 1 (optimizing the derivation probability instead of the output string probability), could you elaborate on your inference procedure? Do you use a standard beam search over the action sequence? Have you observed cases where multiple high-probability derivations in the beam produce the same output string, or conversely, where the optimal derivation produces an incorrect string while a lower-probability derivation would have produced the correct one?
3.  The analysis in Section 5 suggests the soft-attention model encodes position more strongly in its encoder representations, while your model relies on the explicit `step` action. This is a very interesting finding. Have you experimented with providing the decoder with the current attention index as an explicit feature at each step? Would this allow the model to better handle more complex, position-dependent transformations without relying solely on the encoder representations?
