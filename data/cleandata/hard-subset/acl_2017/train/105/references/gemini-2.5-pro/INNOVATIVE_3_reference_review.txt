**Summary of the paper and main contributions:**

This paper introduces a novel hard attention model for morphological inflection generation, a task characterized by nearly monotonic alignments between source and target character sequences. The core idea is to reframe the transduction problem as learning a sequence of actions from an expanded vocabulary that includes both `write(char)` and a special `step` action. The `step` action explicitly advances a read-head over the encoded input sequence. A key aspect of the proposed method is its training procedure: rather than jointly learning to align and transduce, the authors first compute character-level alignments on the training data using an external tool and then deterministically derive a "gold" sequence of `step` and `write` actions. The neural model is then trained with a standard cross-entropy loss to predict this action sequence. The authors evaluate their model on three datasets, demonstrating strong performance, particularly in low-resource settings.

In my view, the main contributions of this work are, in order of strength:

1.  **A novel and conceptually simple architecture for monotonic sequence transduction.** The proposed `step`/`write` action space is an elegant way to incorporate a strong inductive bias (monotonicity) directly into the model's structure. This represents a bold and refreshing departure from the now-standard soft attention paradigm.
2.  **A compelling demonstration of the effectiveness of decoupling alignment and transduction for training.** This work challenges the prevailing assumption that end-to-end joint training is always optimal, showing that a simpler, supervised attention training signal derived from a pre-computed alignment can lead to better generalization, especially when data is scarce.
3.  **State-of-the-art empirical results in a challenging low-resource setting.** The paper is the first to show a neural model surpassing the strong, task-specific latent-variable FST of Dreyer et al. (2008) on the CELEX dataset, directly addressing a known weakness of previous neural approaches.

**Strengths:**

1.  **High Novelty and Conceptual Clarity:** The paper’s main idea is its greatest strength. The hard attention mechanism, implemented as a simple state machine with `step` and `write` operations, is both inventive and intuitive. It replaces the complex, weighted-average "glance" of soft attention with a crisp, decisive action at each time step. This is an excellent example of successfully embedding domain knowledge (the monotonic nature of inflection) into a neural architecture without excessive feature engineering. This kind of bold, first-principles thinking is exactly what the field needs more of.

2.  **Superior Low-Resource Performance:** The authors' core motivation—to build a model that is less data-hungry than standard seq2seq models—is convincingly validated by the experiments on the CELEX dataset. The results in Table 1, and particularly the learning curves in Figure 2, provide powerful evidence that the hard attention model generalizes better and is more resistant to overfitting than its soft attention counterpart. Achieving a new state-of-the-art on this difficult benchmark, and specifically outperforming a highly specialized non-neural model, is a significant achievement.

3.  **Pragmatic and Effective Training Strategy:** The decision to derive the gold action sequence from a pre-computed alignment is clever. While it sacrifices "end-to-end" purity, it simplifies the learning problem immensely. By providing a direct, unambiguous supervisory signal for the attention mechanism from the start of training, the model avoids the potentially difficult exploratory phase of learning an alignment from scratch. This simplification likely contributes significantly to its stability and strong performance.

4.  **Insightful Analysis and Honest Evaluation:** The analysis in Section 5 adds significant value. The SVD visualization and the accompanying hypothesis—that the hard attention model outsources positional encoding to the explicit `step` action, allowing character embeddings to focus more on identity—is plausible and insightful. Furthermore, I appreciate the transparency in the SIGMORPHON results. The authors clearly show where their model excels (suffixing languages) and where it falls short (languages with non-concatenative phenomena), which properly delineates the boundaries of their method and provides a clear direction for future work.

**Weaknesses:**

1.  **Dependence on an External Aligner:** The model's primary strength—its simplified training regimen—is also a potential weakness. Its performance is fundamentally tethered to the quality of the external character aligner. The paper does not explore the model's sensitivity to the choice of this aligner or to potential alignment errors. This dependency makes the system less self-contained and might hinder its application to language pairs where a high-quality aligner is not readily available or is difficult to train.

2.  **Limited Scope of Applicability:** The model's core assumption of near-monotonicity, while powerful for the tasks studied, restricts its applicability. The SIGMORPHON results confirm that it is not well-suited for templatic morphology, circumfixing, or other non-sequential phenomena. While this is a fair trade-off, the paper could be more explicit about these limitations. The contribution is significant for monotonic transduction, but it is not a general-purpose sequence-to-sequence solution.

3.  **Insufficient Comparison with Other Hard Attention Methods:** The related work section could be strengthened by a more direct comparison to alternative ways of training hard attention mechanisms, such as those based on reinforcement learning (e.g., REINFORCE). The proposed "supervised attention" approach is one way to handle the non-differentiability of hard attention; learning a stochastic policy is another. A discussion of the trade-offs between these approaches (e.g., training stability, reliance on pre-computed alignments, final performance) would better contextualize the paper's specific contribution within the broader landscape of hard attention models.

**Questions to Authors:**

1.  Given the model's reliance on pre-computed alignments for training, how robust is it to noise or errors from the alignment model? Have you performed any analysis on the effect of imperfect "gold" action sequences on the final transduction accuracy?
2.  The model's action set (`step`, `write`) strictly enforces a monotonic, forward-only progression through the input. How might you extend this framework to handle morphological processes like infixation or reduplication, which require a more flexible attention pattern (e.g., staying in place, moving backward)? Would simple additions like `stay` or `step_back` actions be feasible, or would they undermine the model's core simplicity and stability?
3.  Footnote 1 mentions the use of a "best-derivation approximation." Could you comment on the potential gap between this and marginalizing over all possible derivations for a target string? In practice, how often does the beam search find multiple distinct action sequences (`s'`) that produce the same top-ranked output string (`y'`)?
