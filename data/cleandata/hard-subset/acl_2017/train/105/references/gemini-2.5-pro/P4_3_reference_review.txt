### Summary and Contributions

This paper presents an encoder-decoder model for morphological inflection generation that utilizes a hard, monotonic attention mechanism. The model transduces an input character sequence (a lemma) into an output sequence (an inflected form) by sequentially deciding between two actions: `WRITE` an output character or `STEP` to the next input character. A key aspect of the methodology is that the alignment and transduction steps are decoupled during training. The model is trained on gold action sequences derived from pre-computed character-level alignments produced by an external tool. The authors evaluate this approach on three standard datasets (CELEX, Wiktionary, SIGMORPHON2016), demonstrating strong performance, particularly in low-resource settings. The paper concludes with an analysis of the model's learned alignments and representations in comparison to a standard soft-attention model.

In my assessment, the main contributions of this work are:
1.  **An effective and simple hard monotonic attention model for morphological inflection.** The model's design, which leverages a pre-computed alignment to simplify the training objective to a standard classification task, is both methodologically sound and empirically powerful. Its success in a low-resource setting where other neural models struggle is a significant finding.
2.  **A strong and comprehensive empirical evaluation.** The paper establishes new state-of-the-art results for neural models on the CELEX dataset and demonstrates competitive performance against strong baselines on the larger Wiktionary and typologically diverse SIGMORPHON datasets, validating the model's robustness across different conditions.
3.  **An insightful analysis comparing hard and soft attention mechanisms.** The investigation into the learned representations provides a clear hypothesis for the proposed model's data efficiency, suggesting that the explicit `STEP` action relieves the encoder from having to encode positional information as strongly as a soft-attention model does.

### Strengths

1.  **Excellent performance in low-resource scenarios.** The most significant strength of the paper is the model's empirical performance on the small CELEX dataset. As shown in Table 1 and Figure 2, the hard attention model outperforms not only previous neural models (which tend to overfit) but also the highly specialized latent-variable WFST model of Dreyer et al. (2008). This demonstrates that the model's strong inductive bias (monotonicity) and simplified training procedure make it a highly data-efficient and practical solution for low-resource morphological tasks.

2.  **Methodological simplicity and elegance.** The proposed mechanism is conceptually simple yet powerful. By framing the transduction problem as a sequence of `WRITE` and `STEP` actions, the model directly incorporates a constraint that is well-suited to the predominantly monotonic nature of inflectional morphology. Decoupling alignment from training by using a pre-computed oracle simplifies the learning problem significantly, avoiding the need for complex marginalization techniques (like in RNN-T) and allowing the model to be trained with a standard cross-entropy loss. This design choice appears to be a primary reason for its stability and effectiveness.

3.  **Valuable analysis of model internals.** Section 5 provides an insightful analysis that strengthens the paper's claims. The visualization of the SVD-reduced encoder representations (Figure 4) offers a compelling explanation for the model's behavior. It suggests that by explicitly handling positional progression via `STEP` actions, the hard attention model allows its character embeddings to focus more on character identity, whereas the soft attention model must conflate character and positional information into its embeddings to guide the attention mechanism. This analysis not only explains the model's data efficiency but also contributes to a better understanding of how attention-based models operate on this task.

4.  **Clarity of presentation.** The paper is very well-written, clear, and easy to follow. The model architecture, control mechanism, and training procedure are described precisely. The experimental setup is thorough, with comparisons to a range of relevant and strong baselines across multiple diverse datasets.

### Weaknesses

1.  **Dependence on an external alignment tool.** The model's training process is entirely dependent on an external character aligner to generate the oracle action sequences. This makes the system a two-stage pipeline, where the quality of the first stage (alignment) necessarily limits the performance of the second (transduction). The paper does not investigate the model's sensitivity to the quality of this alignment; it is unclear how performance would degrade if a simpler or less accurate aligner were used. This dependency also means the model cannot recover from or correct errors made during the alignment step.

2.  **Limited applicability to non-monotonic morphology.** The model's core architectural assumption is near-monotonicity. While this is a valid assumption for suffixation and stem-internal changes common in many of the languages tested, the model is inherently unequipped to handle non-monotonic processes like infixation, circumfixion, or large-scale reduplication. The results on the SIGMORPHON dataset (Table 3) seem to confirm this limitation, as the model performs best on suffixing languages but is surpassed by a soft-attention model on languages with more complex phenomena (e.g., Georgian, Navajo). The paper should be more explicit about this significant limitation on its typological applicability.

3.  **Novelty of the core mechanism.** While the application and training procedure are novel for this task, the fundamental concept of a hard, monotonic attention pointer is not entirely new. Related concepts exist in models like the RNN Transducer (Graves, 2012), which also processes an input sequence monotonically. The primary distinction of the presented work is its use of a pre-computed alignment oracle for training, which simplifies the optimization problem compared to methods that marginalize over all possible alignments. The contribution lies more in the effective simplification and application of this idea rather than the invention of a fundamentally new mechanism.

### Questions to Authors

1.  Your model's performance is contingent on the quality of the alignments produced by the Sudoh et al. (2013) system. How sensitive is the model to the choice of the aligner? Have you explored how performance changes when using a different or simpler alignment method (e.g., one based on Levenshtein distance) to generate the training oracle?

2.  The model's design is inherently monotonic. Could you elaborate on how you envision extending this framework to handle non-monotonic morphological processes, such as infixation or circumfixion? Would it be feasible to add new actions (e.g., `JUMP` or `STEP_BACKWARD`), and what new challenges would this introduce for creating a reliable training oracle from character alignments?

3.  The paper notes that the model optimizes for the best derivation sequence (`s'`) rather than the best output sequence (`y'`). This is an effective approximation. Have you observed cases during inference (e.g., by inspecting the beam) where multiple distinct derivation sequences produce the same output string? Is there a risk that this approximation could cause the model to prefer an incorrect output string simply because its single best derivation is more probable than any single derivation of the correct output string?
