### Summary and Contributions

This paper presents a novel encoder-decoder model for morphological inflection generation that utilizes a "hard monotonic attention" mechanism. The core idea is to replace the standard soft attention, which computes a weighted average over all source-side hidden states, with a simpler, explicit control mechanism. At each decoding step, the model chooses between two actions: `WRITE` an output character or `STEP` the attention pointer to the next input character. This design directly incorporates a strong inductive bias that alignments in morphological inflection are typically monotonic. For training, the model relies on pre-computed character alignments to generate a "gold" sequence of `WRITE` and `STEP` actions, simplifying the training process to a standard cross-entropy loss objective.

In my view, the main contributions of this work are:

1.  **A simple and effective hard monotonic attention architecture:** The primary contribution is the model itself. The `WRITE`/`STEP` mechanism is an elegant way to enforce monotonicity, making the model more data-efficient and interpretable compared to standard soft-attention models for this specific task.
2.  **Strong empirical results, especially in a low-resource setting:** The paper demonstrates state-of-the-art performance across three diverse datasets. Crucially, it is the first neural model to outperform the highly specialized latent-variable WFST model on the small CELEX dataset, a significant benchmark that has been challenging for data-hungry neural methods.
3.  **Insightful analysis comparing hard and soft attention models:** The analysis in Section 5, which visually compares the learned alignments and internal representations (via SVD), provides valuable insights. It shows that soft attention also learns monotonic alignments for this task and suggests that the hard attention model offloads the need to encode positional information into its representations by using the explicit `step` action.

### Strengths

1.  **Excellent performance in low-data regimes:** The paper's most significant strength is its demonstration of superior performance on the CELEX dataset. Neural sequence-to-sequence models are often criticized for their heavy reliance on large training sets. By outperforming not only other neural models but also the strong, tailored WFST model of Dreyer et al. (2008), this work presents a compelling solution for morphological tasks where data is scarce. Figure 2 provides clear evidence that the hard attention model is less prone to overfitting than its soft attention counterpart.

2.  **Well-motivated and elegant model design:** The model's design is simple, intuitive, and strongly motivated by the linguistic properties of the task. Morphological inflection is overwhelmingly a monotonic process of prefixing, suffixing, and local stem changes. The `WRITE`/`STEP` mechanism is a direct and efficient encoding of this prior knowledge. This strong inductive bias is likely the key reason for its data efficiency and robust performance. The decoupling of alignment and transduction simplifies the training procedure significantly, avoiding complex marginalization or reinforcement learning techniques often associated with hard attention.

3.  **Thorough and convincing experimental evaluation:** The authors evaluate their model across three distinct settings: low-resource (CELEX), high-resource (Wiktionary), and typologically diverse (SIGMORPHON 2016). This comprehensive evaluation demonstrates the model's robustness and also honestly highlights its limitations. The finding that the model excels at languages with suffixing and stem changes but is slightly weaker on languages with non-local phenomena like vowel harmony (Table 3) further validates the model's core hypothesis and provides a nuanced understanding of its applicability.

4.  **High-quality analysis of model behavior:** The analysis section is a strong point of the paper. Comparing the alignments learned by the hard and soft attention models (Figure 3) confirms that the monotonicity assumption is sound. The SVD visualization of encoder representations (Figure 4) offers a plausible and insightful explanation for the performance difference: the soft attention model must use its representational capacity to encode position, while the hard attention model handles this explicitly via the `step` action, freeing up its representations to focus on character identity. This kind of analysis is too often omitted and adds significant value to the paper.

### Weaknesses

1.  **Dependence on an external alignment model:** The model's training process is entirely dependent on an external character aligner (Sudoh et al., 2013) to produce the oracle action sequence. This raises questions about the model's robustness. The performance is fundamentally capped by the quality of this external aligner. The paper does not analyze how sensitive the model is to alignment errors or what would happen if a less accurate aligner were used. This makes the system less of an "end-to-end" solution and introduces a dependency that is not explored.

2.  **Limited applicability to non-monotonic tasks:** The model's greatest strength—its strict monotonicity—is also its primary limitation. While the authors acknowledge this in their discussion of the SIGMORPHON results, the paper frames the model as a general contribution for "nearly-monotonic sequence to sequence learning." However, it is not clear how it would handle tasks with even a small amount of non-monotonicity (e.g., reduplication involving non-adjacent segments, or significant reordering in translation). The architecture as presented has no mechanism to look backward, making it unsuitable for a broader class of problems without modification.

3.  **Positioning with respect to other hard attention models could be clearer:** While the paper does a good job citing related work in morphological inflection, its discussion of hard attention in the broader sequence modeling literature is brief. Other "hard attention" models (e.g., in speech recognition or image captioning) often use reinforcement learning or variational inference to learn the attention policy without an external oracle. The paper could be strengthened by more explicitly contrasting its oracle-based training approach with these other methods, highlighting the trade-offs (e.g., simplicity and stability vs. end-to-end learning and reliance on a pre-trained aligner).

### Questions to Authors

1.  How does the model's performance vary with the quality of the pre-computed alignments? Have you experimented with a simpler aligner (e.g., based on Levenshtein distance) or injected noise into the gold alignments to test the model's robustness to alignment errors from the oracle?

2.  The model's strict left-to-right monotonicity makes it difficult to handle phenomena like circumfixing or long-distance consonant harmony, as seen in the SIGMORPHON results. Have you considered simple extensions to the control mechanism (e.g., adding a `step-back` action or allowing a small, fixed-size window of inputs to be attended to) to add flexibility without resorting to a full soft attention mechanism?
