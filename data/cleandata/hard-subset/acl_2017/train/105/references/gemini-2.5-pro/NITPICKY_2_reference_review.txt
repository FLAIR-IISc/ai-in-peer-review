### Review

**Summary of the paper**

This paper introduces a hard attention encoder-decoder model for morphological inflection generation. The model is designed for sequence transduction tasks that are predominantly monotonic. It operates by learning a sequence of actions: either writing a character to the output or stepping a pointer forward along the encoded input sequence. A key aspect of the proposed method is that it does not learn alignments jointly with the transduction task. Instead, it relies on character-level alignments generated by an external tool to create a "gold" sequence of write/step actions for training via a standard cross-entropy loss. The authors evaluate their model on three datasets (CELEX, Wiktionary, SIGMORPHON 2016), reporting strong performance, particularly in low-resource settings. The paper concludes with an analysis comparing the learned representations of the hard attention model with those of a standard soft attention model.

**Main Contributions (as perceived by the reviewer)**

1.  **A Simple and Effective Hard Attention Mechanism:** The primary contribution is a straightforward and intuitive hard attention model based on explicit `step` and `write` actions. Decoupling the alignment from the main training loop simplifies the learning objective and effectively injects a strong, domain-appropriate inductive bias (monotonicity) into the model.
2.  **Strong Empirical Results in a Low-Resource Setting:** The model demonstrates impressive data efficiency, achieving state-of-the-art results on the small CELEX dataset and outperforming a highly specialized latent-variable WFST model, a notable achievement for a general-purpose neural architecture.
3.  **Comparative Analysis of Attention Mechanisms:** The analysis in Section 5, while not groundbreaking, provides valuable visualizations that help elucidate the different strategies employed by hard and soft attention models for this task, particularly concerning the encoding of positional information.

**Strengths**

1.  **Methodological Elegance and Suitability for the Task:** The proposed `step`/`write` mechanism is an elegant solution that directly models the underlying monotonic structure of many morphological transformations (e.g., suffixation, stem changes). This is a well-justified modeling choice that leads to a simpler and more constrained decoding process compared to standard soft attention, which must learn this monotonicity from scratch.

2.  **Excellent Performance on Low-Resource Data:** The results on the CELEX dataset are the most compelling part of the paper. Figure 2 clearly illustrates the model's resistance to overfitting compared to the soft attention baseline, validating the authors' hypothesis that the hard monotonic constraint acts as a powerful regularizer. Surpassing the highly tailored LAT model of Dreyer et al. (2008) is a significant result.

3.  **Clarity of Presentation:** The core model is described clearly, and the motivation for its design is well-argued. The analysis in Section 5, particularly the SVD plots, effectively communicates the differences in learned representations and provides a plausible explanation for the model's data efficiency.

**Weaknesses**

1.  **Over-reliance on an External Aligner and Lack of Detail:** The entire training paradigm rests on the quality of an externally produced character alignment. The paper states that the gold action sequence is "deterministically inferred" from this alignment, but the details of this crucial step are omitted. How are one-to-many, many-to-one, or null alignments handled? The model's performance is fundamentally capped by the quality of this external aligner, and it has no mechanism to recover from alignment errors. A thorough discussion of the sensitivity of the model to alignment quality is missing and represents a significant methodological gap.

2.  **Contradictory Claims about Monotonicity:** The paper repeatedly describes the task as "nearly-monotonic" and claims the bi-directional encoder captures "non-monotone relations." However, the decoder is strictly monotonic—the `step` action can only advance the pointer forward. This makes it unclear how the model could handle phenomena like circumfixation (listed for Georgian in Table 3) or other non-monotonic transformations. The claim that the bi-RNN encoder resolves this is insufficient without a more detailed explanation of how that information is utilized by a decoder that cannot look backward in the input.

3.  **Misleading Interpretation of SIGMORPHON Results:** In Section 4, the authors state, "our implementation of the soft attention model... is now the best performing model on this dataset to our knowledge." This is a commendable result for their baseline but significantly weakens the argument for their proposed "Hard" model, which is outperformed on average by their own baseline on this dataset. The claim in the abstract of "establishing a new state of the art on three previously-studied datasets" is therefore not fully supported by their own results, as their novel contribution is not the state-of-the-art model on the SIGMORPHON dataset. This should be phrased more carefully.

4.  **Acknowledged but Unaddressed Objective Mismatch:** Footnote 1 correctly points out that the model optimizes for the best derivation (action sequence) rather than the best output string, noting this is "effective in practice." While this simplification is understandable, it is a theoretical weakness that warrants more than a passing mention in a footnote. A brief discussion of the potential impact of this approximation—for instance, in cases where multiple derivations yield the same output—would strengthen the paper.

5.  **Severe Formatting and Presentation Issues:** The paper suffers from a striking lack of attention to detail in its presentation, which undermines the credibility of the research.
    *   The References section is rife with errors: stray question marks, unprocessed LaTeX commands (e.g., `\Q1993\E`), and inconsistent citation styles are present throughout.
    *   A garbled, incomplete paragraph appears at the very end of the references, appearing to be a copy-paste error from a footnote or note-taking document.
    *   The labels in Figure 2 are poorly formatted and overlap.
    *   Footnote 3 is excessively long and its content would be better placed in the main text or appendix.
    Such a high density of formatting errors is unacceptable for a top-tier conference submission and suggests a rushed and careless preparation process.

**Questions to Authors**

1.  Could you please provide the exact deterministic rules used to convert a character alignment (including one-to-many and many-to-one mappings) into the sequence of `step` and `write` actions for training? How robust is the model to potential errors from the Sudoh et al. (2013) aligner?

2.  Given the strictly monotonic nature of the `step` action, how would your model handle true non-monotonic morphological processes like infixation or circumfixation? Can you elaborate on how the bi-directional encoder's context vectors help overcome the decoder's structural limitation?

3.  Considering your own "Soft" attention baseline achieves a higher average accuracy on the SIGMORPHON 2016 dataset, could you clarify the claim that your "Hard" attention model establishes a new state of the art on this dataset?
