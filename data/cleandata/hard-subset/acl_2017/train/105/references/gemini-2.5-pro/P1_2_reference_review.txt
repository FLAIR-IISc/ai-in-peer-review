### Summary of the Paper
This paper proposes a novel encoder-decoder model with a "hard monotonic attention" mechanism for the task of morphological inflection generation. The core problem the authors address is that standard neural sequence-to-sequence models with soft attention often require large amounts of training data and tend to overfit in low-resource settings, a common scenario for this task.

The proposed model leverages the observation that morphological inflection is a nearly-monotonic string transduction process. Instead of learning a soft attention distribution over the input sequence, their model's decoder learns to predict actions from an augmented vocabulary containing all possible output characters plus a special `step` action. A `write` action appends a character to the output, while a `step` action advances a "read head" or pointer to the next encoded character in the input sequence.

A key aspect of their method is that it decouples the alignment and transduction steps. For training, they first use an external character alignment tool to generate alignments for the training data. These alignments are then deterministically converted into a "gold" sequence of `write` and `step` actions. The neural network is then trained with a standard cross-entropy loss to predict this action sequence.

The authors evaluate their model on three standard datasets (CELEX, Wiktionary, SIGMORPHON2016) and show that it achieves state-of-the-art results, particularly in the low-resource CELEX setting where it is the first neural model to outperform a strong latent-variable FST baseline. The paper concludes with an insightful analysis comparing the learned representations and alignments of their hard attention model against a standard soft attention model.

### Main Contributions
1.  **A simple yet effective hard monotonic attention model for sequence transduction.** The primary contribution is the model architecture and its associated training paradigm. By formulating the task as predicting a sequence of `write` and `step` actions derived from pre-computed alignments, the authors create a model that explicitly enforces a strong, task-appropriate inductive bias (monotonicity). This simplifies the learning problem compared to jointly learning alignment and transduction, leading to better sample efficiency.
2.  **State-of-the-art empirical results on morphological inflection, especially in a low-resource setting.** The paper provides compelling evidence of the model's effectiveness. Its superior performance on the small CELEX dataset is a significant achievement, as it directly addresses a well-known weakness of neural models. The strong results on the larger Wiktionary and diverse SIGMORPHON datasets demonstrate the model's robustness and scalability.
3.  **An insightful comparative analysis of hard vs. soft attention for this task.** The visualization of learned alignments and character representations (Section 5) provides valuable insights into the mechanisms of both models. The analysis suggests that the hard attention model relies on the explicit `step` action for positional information, while the soft attention model is forced to encode this information more strongly into its character representations, potentially explaining its tendency to overfit on smaller datasets.

### Strengths
1.  **Excellent performance in the low-resource setting.** The most compelling strength is the model's performance on the CELEX dataset (Table 1). It surpasses not only other neural models (MED, NWFST) but also the highly specialized latent-variable FST model (LAT) which was specifically designed for such tasks. The learning curve in Figure 2 provides clear visual evidence supporting the claim that the hard attention model is less prone to overfitting than a comparable soft attention model, making it a very practical solution for morphological tasks where data is often scarce.
2.  **The model is conceptually simple and well-motivated.** The core idea of using an explicit `step` action is an elegant way to incorporate the strong prior of monotonicity. Decoupling alignment from transduction simplifies the training process significantly, reducing it to a standard sequence prediction problem with a simple cross-entropy loss, in contrast to more complex training objectives like those required for RNN Transducers. This simplicity is a major advantage.
3.  **Thorough and convincing evaluation.** The authors evaluate their model on three different datasets that vary in size and linguistic diversity (CELEX, Wiktionary, SIGMORPHON). This comprehensive evaluation demonstrates the model's effectiveness across different conditions. Furthermore, the analysis in Section 5 is excellent; it goes beyond simply reporting scores and attempts to explain *why* the proposed model works well, adding to the scientific contribution of the paper.
4.  **Honest and nuanced reporting of results.** In the SIGMORPHON results (Table 3), the authors honestly report that their model is slightly outperformed by a soft attention model on languages with non-concatenative or long-distance phenomena (e.g., vowel harmony). They correctly connect this to the limitations of their model's monotonicity assumption. This transparency and insightful discussion add to the credibility of the work.

### Weaknesses
1.  **Dependence on an external alignment tool.** The model's performance is critically dependent on the quality of the alignments generated by the Sudoh et al. (2013) tool. The entire training process relies on a "gold" action sequence derived from these alignments. This raises questions about the model's robustness: How would it perform with a simpler or noisier aligner? What happens in languages where this specific alignment tool performs poorly? This dependency makes the overall system less "end-to-end" than soft attention models and introduces a potential point of failure that is not analyzed in the paper.
2.  **The model's core assumption limits its generality.** The hard monotonic attention is a strong inductive bias. While this is a strength for suffixing languages, it is a clear limitation for languages with more complex morphological processes like infixation, circumfixation, or templatic morphology (as hinted at by the SIGMORPHON results for Arabic). The paper claims the BiLSTM encoder can capture non-monotone relations, but the decoder mechanism is strictly monotonic. It is unclear how these two components interact to handle even minor non-monotonic alignments. This limits the model's applicability to the "nearly-monotonic" subset of sequence transduction tasks.
3.  **Lack of key ablation studies.** The paper compares the full system to other published methods, but does not include ablations to isolate the impact of its own design choices. For example, how crucial is the BiLSTM encoder compared to a unidirectional one? More importantly, how does the model's performance change with different pre-computed alignments (e.g., from a simple Levenshtein-based aligner vs. the more complex CRP-based one)? Such an analysis would clarify whether the performance gains stem primarily from the model architecture or from the high-quality external aligner.

### Questions to Authors
1.  Could you elaborate on the model's sensitivity to the quality of the pre-computed alignments? Have you experimented with other alignment methods, and if so, how did it affect performance? An analysis of how often the external aligner makes linguistically implausible alignments and how the model handles them would strengthen the paper.
2.  The paper states that the bi-directional encoder helps capture non-monotone relations. However, the decoder's pointer can only move forward. Could you provide a concrete example of a non-monotonic transformation (e.g., simple metathesis) and explain how the model would handle it? Does the context from the BiLSTM allow the decoder to, for instance, write a character that appears later in the source word before it has "stepped" to its position?
