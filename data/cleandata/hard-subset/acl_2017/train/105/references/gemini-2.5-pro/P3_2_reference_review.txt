### Summary and Contributions
This paper proposes a novel encoder-decoder model with a "hard monotonic attention" mechanism for the task of morphological inflection generation. The core idea is to reframe the sequence generation problem as predicting a series of actions: either `WRITE` a character to the output or `STEP` to advance a pointer over the encoded input sequence. This design explicitly models the predominantly monotonic alignment between a word's lemma and its inflected form. A key aspect of the methodology is that the model does not learn the alignment jointly with the transduction. Instead, it is trained on gold action sequences derived from pre-computed character alignments using an external tool. The authors conduct a thorough evaluation on three standard datasets (CELEX, Wiktionary, SIGMORPHON 2016), demonstrating state-of-the-art performance, particularly in low-resource settings.

As I see them, the main contributions are:
1.  **A simple and effective hard attention model for nearly-monotonic sequence transduction.** The `WRITE`/`STEP` action space provides a strong and appropriate inductive bias for morphological inflection, simplifying the learning problem compared to soft attention.
2.  **A practical training methodology that decouples alignment and transduction.** By using pre-computed alignments to create a gold action sequence, the model can be trained with a simple cross-entropy loss, avoiding the complexities of joint training or reinforcement learning and leading to strong data efficiency.
3.  **State-of-the-art results on low-resource morphological inflection.** The paper presents the first neural model to surpass the highly specialized latent-variable FST model of Dreyer et al. (2008) on the challenging, small CELEX dataset, a significant empirical achievement.

### Strengths
1.  **Excellent Empirical Results and Strong Evaluation:** The paper's claims are well-supported by a comprehensive set of experiments. The model achieves state-of-the-art results on average across all three datasets. The most impressive result is on the CELEX dataset, a known pain point for neural models. The learning curve in Figure 2 clearly illustrates the model's superior generalization on small data compared to a soft-attention baseline, providing strong evidence that its inductive bias helps prevent overfitting. Success across small (CELEX), large (Wiktionary), and typologically diverse (SIGMORPHON) datasets demonstrates the robustness of the approach.

2.  **Elegant and Well-Motivated Model Design:** The proposed architecture is simple, intuitive, and perfectly suited to the problem domain. Morphological inflection is largely a process of monotonic string editing (copying, substituting, adding suffixes). The `WRITE`/`STEP` mechanism directly models this process. This simplicity contrasts favorably with the soft attention mechanism, which computes a weighted average over all input states at every step—a less direct and potentially overly powerful mechanism for this task. The decoupling of alignment and transduction is a clever engineering decision that simplifies the training objective and likely contributes significantly to the model's data efficiency.

3.  **Insightful Analysis:** The analysis in Section 5 adds significant value to the paper. The visualization of alignments in Figure 3 confirms that the hard attention model learns linguistically plausible alignments and that even the soft-attention model converges to monotonic alignments, justifying the core assumption of this work. The SVD analysis in Figure 4 is particularly insightful. It provides a compelling hypothesis for the model's success: by making positional information explicit through the `STEP` action, the encoder is freed from having to encode position into its character representations, allowing it to focus on character identity. This is a great piece of analysis that helps explain *why* the model works well.

### Weaknesses
1.  **Heavy Reliance on an External Aligner:** The model's performance is fundamentally tied to the quality of the pre-computed alignments from the Sudoh et al. (2013) tool. The paper does not investigate how sensitive the model is to the quality or choice of this aligner. This dependency is a significant conceptual weakness, as it makes the approach less "end-to-end" and raises questions about its applicability to languages or tasks where high-quality aligners are not available or are difficult to train. For instance, would the model perform as well if trained on alignments from a simpler heuristic like Levenshtein edit distance? This is a crucial missing ablation.

2.  **Limited Applicability to Non-Monotonic Phenomena:** The model's core strength—its strong monotonic inductive bias—is also its main limitation. While the bidirectional encoder can capture some non-local context, the `STEP`-forward-only attention pointer is ill-suited for more complex, non-sequential morphological processes like large-scale infixation, reduplication, or certain types of circumfixation where the generation process is not strictly left-to-right on the input. The slightly lower performance on languages with templatic morphology (Arabic, Maltese) in the SIGMORPHON results hints at this limitation. While the paper is focused on inflection, this constraint limits the broader applicability of the proposed architecture to other transduction tasks without the near-monotonic property.

3.  **Mismatch Between Training Objective and Inference:** As noted in footnote 1, the model is trained to maximize the likelihood of the single best action sequence (derivation), not the marginal likelihood of the correct output string. At inference, beam search also operates over action sequences. This approximation can be problematic if multiple derivations can produce the same correct output string, as the model may assign low probability to all of them individually, even if their sum would be high. While this is a common practice and works well empirically, it is a theoretical weakness worth noting. It would be valuable to understand the extent of this ambiguity (i.e., how many gold action sequences map to the same input/output pair) in the datasets.

### Questions to Authors
1.  Regarding the reliance on the external aligner: Could you quantify the impact of the aligner's quality? Have you experimented with training the model using gold action sequences derived from a simpler alignment method (e.g., based on Levenshtein distance) to see how performance is affected? This would help understand the robustness of your training pipeline.
2.  How does the model handle circumfixes, for example, the German `ge-...(e)t` past participle (e.g., `sagen` -> `gesagt`)? Could you walk through the `WRITE`/`STEP` action sequence that would be required to generate the prefix `ge-`, transduce the stem, and then generate the suffix `-t`? This seems challenging for a strictly monotonic pointer.
3.  The paper highlights improved performance in low-data regimes. What is the minimum number of training examples where your model starts to outperform the soft-attention baseline? Is there a crossover point on the CELEX data if you subsample it further (e.g., down to 100 or 50 examples)?
