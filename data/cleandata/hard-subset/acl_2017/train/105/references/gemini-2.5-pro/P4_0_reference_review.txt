### Summary of the Paper

This paper proposes a novel encoder-decoder model with a hard, monotonic attention mechanism for the task of morphological inflection generation. The core idea is to reframe the sequence transduction problem as learning a sequence of actions: either writing an output character or stepping forward to the next input character. Unlike soft-attention models that learn a weighted alignment jointly with the transduction task, this model uses an external, pre-trained character aligner to generate a gold sequence of "write" and "step" actions for training. This simplifies the training objective to a standard cross-entropy loss. The model is evaluated extensively on three datasets (CELEX, Wiktionary, SIGMORPHON 2016), demonstrating strong performance, particularly in low-data settings where it avoids the overfitting issues common to soft-attention models. The paper also includes an analysis comparing the learned alignments and internal representations of the proposed hard-attention model against a soft-attention baseline.

### Main Contributions

1.  **A simple and effective hard monotonic attention architecture for sequence transduction.** The key contribution is the method of decoupling alignment from transduction. By using an off-the-shelf aligner to create a deterministic sequence of `write` and `step` actions, the model is trained with a simple supervised objective. This approach provides a strong inductive bias for tasks with predominantly monotonic alignments, like morphological inflection.
2.  **State-of-the-art results in low-data morphological inflection.** The paper presents compelling evidence that the proposed model is particularly well-suited for low-resource scenarios. It is the first neural model reported to outperform a strong, task-specific latent-variable WFST baseline on the small CELEX dataset, directly addressing a known weakness of standard neural sequence-to-sequence models.
3.  **An insightful comparative analysis of hard and soft attention mechanisms.** The analysis in Section 5 provides valuable insights into the behavior of both model types on this task. It confirms that soft-attention models also learn monotonic alignments but visualizes how the proposed hard-attention model learns different internal representations, relying less on encoding positional information directly into character embeddings due to the explicit `step` action.

### Strengths

1.  **Strong Empirical Performance, Especially in Low-Data Regime:** The model's performance on the CELEX dataset is a significant strength. As shown in Table 1 and the learning curves in Figure 2, the hard-attention model achieves higher accuracy and is less prone to overfitting compared to soft-attention baselines. This demonstrates the practical value of its strong inductive bias in data-scarce situations, which are common for many languages.
2.  **Methodological Simplicity and Soundness:** The proposed method is elegant in its simplicity. By separating the alignment and transduction steps, it converts a complex joint learning problem into a standard sequence prediction task. This makes the model straightforward to implement and train using a standard cross-entropy loss, avoiding more complex training procedures like reinforcement learning or marginalization over latent alignments. The architecture is technically sound and well-motivated for the target task.
3.  **Thorough and Comprehensive Evaluation:** The authors evaluate their model across three distinct and standard benchmarks, covering low-resource (CELEX), high-resource (Wiktionary), and typologically diverse (SIGMORPHON) settings. This comprehensive evaluation demonstrates the model's robustness and provides a clear picture of its strengths (on suffixing languages) and relative weaknesses (on languages with more complex, non-local phenomena), lending significant credibility to the paper's claims.
4.  **Clarity and Quality of Presentation:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and easy to follow. The model is described clearly in Section 2, aided by a helpful diagram (Figure 1). The motivation is well-established, the experiments are well-designed, and the analysis is insightful and clearly presented with effective visualizations.

### Weaknesses

1.  **Dependence on an External Aligner:** The model's performance is critically dependent on the quality of the character alignments used to generate the gold action sequences for training. The paper does not investigate this dependency. If the external aligner (Sudoh et al., 2013) produces systematic errors or is unavailable for a given language pair, the model's performance could degrade significantly. The paper would be strengthened by an analysis of the model's robustness to alignment noise or an ablation study using a simpler alignment method.
2.  **Limited Applicability to Non-Monotonic Phenomena:** The model's core architectural assumption is that the alignment between input and output is "nearly monotonic." While effective for suffixation and stem changes, this makes it inherently less suitable for phenomena involving significant reordering, such as circumfixation or infixation. The paper acknowledges this with the SIGMORPHON results, but the discussion of how the bi-directional encoder handles "non-monotone relations" is brief. The hard `step` mechanism remains strictly monotonic, limiting the model's ability to handle long-distance reordering beyond what can be captured in the local context of the encoder states.
3.  **Novelty of Hard Attention Could Be Better Contextualized:** While the application and training method are novel for this task, the general concept of hard attention (i.e., making discrete decisions about where to focus) has been explored in other work. The paper mentions some related work but could more clearly position its contribution relative to other hard attention mechanisms, such as those used in reinforcement learning or pointer networks. The primary novelty lies in using pre-computed alignments to simplify training, and this point could be emphasized more strongly as the distinguishing factor.

### Questions to Authors

1.  Your model's training relies entirely on an external character aligner. How does the quality of this aligner impact final model performance? For instance, have you analyzed how your model performs on examples where the aligner produces a linguistically questionable or incorrect alignment?
2.  The model is based on a "nearly-monotonic" assumption. For the SIGMORPHON languages with more complex morphological processes (e.g., templatic morphology in Arabic, circumfixing in Georgian), could you provide a qualitative analysis of the model's primary error types? Does it fail by incorrectly predicting a `step` or by generating the wrong character sequence?
3.  The model optimizes for the most likely sequence of actions, not the most likely output string, which could lead to search errors. Have you analyzed how often the single best action sequence found during beam search produces the correct word, versus cases where the correct word could have been produced by a different, lower-scoring action sequence?
