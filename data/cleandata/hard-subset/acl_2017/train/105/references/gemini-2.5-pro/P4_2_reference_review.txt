### Summary and Contributions

This paper introduces a neural encoder-decoder model for morphological inflection generation that uses a hard, strictly monotonic attention mechanism. The core idea is to reframe the sequence transduction task as learning a sequence of actions: either writing an output character or stepping forward to the next input character. To train the model, the authors first compute character-level alignments on the training data using an external tool, and then convert these alignments into gold sequences of write/step actions. The model is then trained with a standard cross-entropy loss to predict this action sequence. The authors evaluate their model on three standard datasets (CELEX, Wiktionary, SIGMORPHON 2016), demonstrating its effectiveness, particularly in low-resource settings.

The main contributions of this work are:

1.  **A simple and effective hard monotonic attention model for sequence transduction.** The model introduces a strong inductive bias that is well-suited for morphological inflection. By decoupling alignment from training and using an explicit `step` action, the model simplifies the learning problem compared to soft attention and other latent alignment models.
2.  **State-of-the-art empirical results, especially in low-data regimes.** The paper establishes new state-of-the-art performance on the small CELEX dataset, being the first neural model to outperform a strong, specialized latent-variable WFST baseline. It also achieves state-of-the-art average accuracy on the large Wiktionary dataset, demonstrating the method's robustness.
3.  **An insightful comparative analysis of hard and soft attention models.** The paper provides visualizations and analysis that sheds light on the internal mechanisms of both models. It demonstrates that the proposed model's explicit positional control (via `step` actions) may allow the encoder to learn more position-invariant character representations, which could explain its strong performance with limited data.

### Strengths

1.  **Technical Soundness and Simplicity.** The proposed model is well-motivated, clearly described, and technically sound. The decision to enforce a hard monotonic alignment is a strong but appropriate inductive bias for morphological inflection in many languages. The training methodology, which derives an explicit oracle action sequence from pre-computed alignments, is a clever way to simplify the training process. It avoids the complexities of jointly learning alignments (as in soft attention) or marginalizing over all possible alignments (as in RNN-T models), resulting in a straightforward and efficient training procedure using a standard cross-entropy loss.

2.  **Strong and Comprehensive Empirical Evaluation.** The authors provide compelling evidence for their model's effectiveness across three different experimental settings.
    *   On the low-resource CELEX dataset, the model significantly outperforms previous neural approaches and, crucially, the highly specialized latent-variable WFST model. The learning curves in Figure 2 provide clear evidence that the hard attention model is less prone to overfitting than its soft attention counterpart in this setting.
    *   On the large-scale Wiktionary dataset, the model achieves the highest average accuracy, demonstrating that its strong inductive bias does not hinder performance when ample data is available.
    *   On the typologically diverse SIGMORPHON 2016 dataset, the model performs competitively, excelling on languages with primarily suffixing and stem-changing morphology, which directly aligns with the model's monotonic assumption.

3.  **High-Quality Analysis.** The analysis in Section 5 significantly strengthens the paper. The qualitative comparison of alignments in Figure 3 supports the paper's core hypothesis by showing that even soft-attention models learn largely monotonic alignments for this task, justifying the hard-attention constraint. The SVD visualization of encoder representations in Figure 4 provides a plausible explanation for the model's success. It suggests that by handling position explicitly with the `step` action, the hard-attention model allows the encoder to focus more on character identity, while the soft-attention model must entangle character and positional information in its representations. This is a valuable insight into the functioning of attention-based models on this task.

### Weaknesses

1.  **Dependence on an External Aligner.** The model's two-stage process (align, then train) introduces a dependency on the quality of the external alignment tool (Sudoh et al., 2013). The performance is contingent on obtaining accurate alignments to generate the gold action sequences for training. The paper does not analyze the model's sensitivity to alignment errors or explore how different alignment methods might impact final performance. This makes it difficult to assess how much of the model's success is due to the neural architecture versus the quality of the pre-computed alignments.

2.  **Limited Applicability to Non-Monotonic Phenomena.** The model's primary strength—its hard monotonic assumption—is also its main limitation. While very effective for suffixing, prefixing, and infixing, it is architecturally unequipped to handle non-monotonic transductions like circumfixing or long-distance phonological processes (e.g., consonant or vowel harmony) where the generation of an output character may depend on non-adjacent input characters. The results on the SIGMORPHON dataset, where the soft attention model performs better on languages with such phenomena (e.g., Turkish, Hungarian), confirm this limitation. The scope of the method is therefore restricted to "nearly-monotonic" tasks.

3.  **Clarity on Novelty Regarding Hard Attention.** While the application and training method are novel for this task, the general concept of hard attention or pointer-based decoders is not new in the sequence-to-sequence literature. The paper's related work section is thorough but could more precisely situate the proposed `write`/`step` mechanism relative to other forms of hard attention (e.g., reinforcement learning-based approaches or Pointer Networks) to better delineate the specific novelty of their formulation. The primary innovation appears to be the supervised training of this mechanism via pre-computed alignments, which is a strong contribution, but the framing could be slightly sharper.

### Questions to Authors

1.  How robust is the model to the quality of the alignments used to generate the oracle? Have you considered evaluating the impact of using a simpler/noisier alignment method (e.g., one based on simple edit distance) for training? This would help clarify whether the strong performance is primarily due to the model architecture or the high quality of the alignment oracle.
2.  Footnote 1 notes that the model optimizes for the best derivation (action sequence) rather than the best output string. During inference (e.g., with beam search), have you observed cases where different high-scoring action sequences produce the same output string? Would it be feasible to sum the probabilities of derivations leading to the same output string to re-rank the beam, and do you believe this could improve performance?
3.  Given the model's slightly lower performance on languages with non-local dependencies in the SIGMORPHON dataset, have you considered extensions to handle non-monotonicity in a controlled way? For example, could a limited "jump back" action be added to the decoder's action space, and what challenges would this introduce for the training paradigm?
