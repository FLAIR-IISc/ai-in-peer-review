**Summary and Contributions**

This paper presents an encoder-decoder model for morphological inflection generation that utilizes a "hard monotonic attention" mechanism. Instead of learning a soft, weighted alignment over the source sequence, the model's decoder chooses from a discrete set of actions: either writing an output character or stepping forward to the next input character. This explicitly enforces a monotonic alignment between the input and output sequences. The training procedure is decoupled from alignment learning; gold action sequences are derived from alignments produced by an external tool, and the network is then trained via standard cross-entropy loss to predict these sequences.

From my perspective, the contributions of this work are:
1.  A strong empirical demonstration that a simplified, monotonic attention model can achieve state-of-the-art results on morphological inflection generation, particularly in low-data settings. The model's performance on the CELEX dataset, where it surpasses a strong, task-specific WFST baseline, is a notable result.
2.  A system that effectively incorporates a strong, task-appropriate inductive bias (monotonicity) into a neural architecture. While the architectural components are not entirely novel, their combination and application to this task are well-executed.
3.  A useful analysis comparing the learned representations and alignment behavior of the proposed hard attention model against a standard soft attention baseline, providing some insight into why soft attention also learns monotonic alignments for this task.

**Strengths**

1.  **Excellent Empirical Performance:** The primary strength of this paper is its strong and comprehensive evaluation. The proposed model achieves state-of-the-art or highly competitive results across three standard benchmarks (CELEX, Wiktionary, SIGMORPHON), covering both low-resource and high-resource scenarios. The result on the CELEX dataset is particularly compelling, as it is the first neural model reported to outperform the specialized latent-variable FST model of Dreyer et al. (2008) without requiring additional training data. This provides strong evidence for the model's effectiveness.

2.  **Methodological Soundness for the Task:** The model's core assumption of near-monotonicity is a very good fit for morphological inflection, which often involves prefixation, suffixation, and local stem changes. By building this strong inductive bias directly into the model architecture, the authors simplify the learning problem. This likely explains the model's superior performance in the low-data regime (Figure 2), where a more flexible soft-attention model is prone to overfitting by learning spurious, non-monotonic alignments.

3.  **Clear Analysis and Comparison:** The paper includes a valuable analysis section (Section 5) that compares the proposed model to a soft-attention baseline. The visualizations of alignments (Figure 3) and the SVD projections of encoder states (Figure 4) help build intuition. The finding that the soft-attention model must implicitly learn to encode positional information to achieve monotonicity, whereas the hard-attention model represents this more explicitly through the `step` action, is an interesting insight that helps justify the architectural choices.

**Weaknesses**

1.  **Limited Novelty of the Core Mechanism:** The proposed hard attention mechanism is conceptually very similar to established transition-based systems. The action set of `WRITE(c)` and `STEP` is analogous to systems that process an input buffer and build an output structure. The paper acknowledges this in passing ("An alternative view... is that of a transition system"), but the framing as a novel "hard attention" model may overstate its originality. The core idea of enforcing a hard, monotonic alignment has also been explored in related work like the RNN Transducer (Graves, 2012), which the authors cite. The main novelty appears to be in the specific implementation and training regime, rather than a fundamental new mechanism.

2.  **Reliance on an External Alignment Tool:** The model's training process is not end-to-end. It relies on a separate, pre-existing character alignment model to generate the gold standard action sequences. This makes the system a pipeline, and its performance is critically dependent on the quality of this external component. The paper does not analyze this dependency. How would the model perform if a different, perhaps lower-quality, aligner were used? This reliance on a pre-processing step makes the approach feel less self-contained and potentially less robust than a model that learns to align jointly, such as the soft-attention baseline. This is a significant methodological limitation.

3.  **Narrow Applicability and Brittleness:** The model is explicitly designed for "nearly-monotonic" sequence transduction. While effective for inflection, this is a strong limitation. The model's relative weakness on the SIGMORPHON languages with more complex phenomena (e.g., templatic morphology in Arabic, circumfixing) compared to the more flexible soft-attention model highlights its brittleness. The hard-coded monotonicity prevents it from handling the non-local dependencies required for such transformations. This restricts the potential impact of the method to a narrow class of problems and makes it less of a general-purpose advance in sequence-to-sequence modeling.

4.  **Approximation in the Learning Objective:** As noted in Footnote 1, the model is trained to predict the most likely sequence of *actions* (derivation), not the most likely *output string*. This is a convenient approximation that simplifies training, but it is theoretically less sound than marginalizing over all possible derivations that produce the same output string. While this approximation appears effective for this task, it is a weakness compared to more principled probabilistic models.

**Questions to Authors**

1.  Could you elaborate on the model's sensitivity to the quality of the external aligner used during training? For instance, if you were to inject noise into the pre-computed alignments or use a simpler alignment heuristic, how substantially would the final model's accuracy degrade?

2.  The results on the SIGMORPHON dataset suggest that the strict monotonicity of your model is a disadvantage for languages with non-concatenative morphology. Have you considered ways to relax this constraint, for example, by adding actions that allow for non-monotonic movement (e.g., JUMP, REVERSE)? And if so, how would such a model differ from existing hard-attention mechanisms that are not strictly monotonic?
