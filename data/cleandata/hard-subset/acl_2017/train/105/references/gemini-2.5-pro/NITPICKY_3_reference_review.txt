**Summary**

This paper proposes a sequence-to-sequence model for morphological inflection generation that utilizes a "hard monotonic attention" mechanism. Instead of computing a weighted sum over all input states (soft attention), the decoder at each step chooses between writing an output character or advancing a single attention pointer to the next input character (`step` action). This design choice enforces a strict monotonic alignment between the input and output. To facilitate training, the model decouples alignment and transduction; it first uses an external tool to generate character-level alignments for the training data, from which a "gold" sequence of write/step actions is derived. The network is then trained via standard cross-entropy loss to predict this action sequence. The authors evaluate their model on three datasets (CELEX, Wiktionary, SIGMORPHON2016), claiming state-of-the-art results, particularly in low-data settings. The paper concludes with an analysis comparing the internal representations of their hard attention model with a standard soft attention model.

**Contributions**

1.  The primary contribution is a hard attention model architecture tailored for nearly monotonic sequence transduction tasks. The model's main novelty lies in decoupling the alignment and transduction processes, which simplifies the training procedure compared to models that jointly learn to align and transduce.
2.  The paper provides a strong empirical result on the low-resource CELEX dataset, being the first neural model reported to outperform the specialized latent-variable FST model of Dreyer et al. (2008).
3.  The analysis in Section 5 offers valuable insights into the different encoding strategies of hard vs. soft attention models, suggesting that soft attention models implicitly learn to encode positional information to a greater degree, while the proposed model represents this more explicitly through its action space.

**Strengths**

1.  The proposed model is elegant, simple, and well-motivated for morphological inflection, where alignments are predominantly monotonic. By enforcing this inductive bias directly into the architecture, the model seems to gain a significant advantage in low-data regimes, as evidenced by the strong CELEX results and the learning curves in Figure 2, which show less overfitting compared to a soft attention baseline.
2.  The experimental evaluation is comprehensive, covering settings with very small (CELEX), large (Wiktionary), and typologically diverse (SIGMORPHON) training sets. The comparison to a wide range of both neural and non-neural prior work helps to situate the model's performance effectively.
3.  The analysis of learned representations using SVD projections is insightful. It provides a plausible explanation for the model's effectiveness, particularly its data efficiency: by offloading the burden of representing sequential position from the encoder states to the explicit `step` actions in the decoder, the encoder can focus on learning more generalizable character representations. This is a well-executed piece of analysis that strengthens the paper's overall message.

**Weaknesses**

While the core ideas are sound, the paper is marred by a substantial number of minor flaws in its presentation, methodology, and reporting, which collectively undermine its quality.

1.  **Poor Presentation and Lack of Care:** The manuscript suffers from numerous formatting and stylistic errors that suggest a hasty preparation.
    *   The reference section is in a state of disarray, filled with BibTeX compilation errors (e.g., `et al\.,?`, `\Q1993\E`) and, most alarmingly, a malformed entry at the end that appears to be a fragment of text from another section of the paper. This level of sloppiness is unacceptable for a top-tier conference submission.
    *   There are numerous typographical and consistency errors throughout the text, such as "down-stream" vs. "downstream", "sequence to sequence" vs. "sequence-to-sequence", and inconsistent citation ordering ("Kann and Schütze, 2016b,a").
    *   Key experimental details are repeatedly deferred to the supplementary material (e.g., "The equations for the LSTM variant we use are detailed in the supplementary material", "The implementation details for our models are described in the supplementary material"). This makes the paper self-contained and hinders reproducibility. Essential hyperparameters like embedding dimensions, hidden state sizes, optimizer details, and learning rates must be included in the main text.

2.  **Unexamined Methodological Dependencies:** The model's performance is critically dependent on the quality of an external character aligner. This design choice, presented as a strength for its simplicity, introduces a significant and unanalyzed weakness.
    *   The choice of the aligner (Sudoh et al., 2013) is not justified. Is it the state-of-the-art? How does its performance compare to others?
    *   The paper does not investigate the model's sensitivity to alignment errors. A poor alignment oracle will create a flawed "gold" action sequence, placing a hard ceiling on the model's achievable performance. An error analysis or ablation study showing the impact of alignment quality is sorely needed.

3.  **Lack of Rigor in Reporting and Claims:**
    *   The paper frequently uses the word "significantly" (e.g., "performs significantly better with very small training sets", "significantly more accurate than the model of Yu et al. (2016)") without providing any statistical significance tests to back up these claims. Given the small margins in some tables (e.g., Table 2 average accuracy of 96.55 vs. 96.34), this is a critical omission.
    *   The model optimizes a proxy objective: the likelihood of the best action sequence, not the marginal likelihood of the correct output string (as acknowledged in footnote 1). While a common approximation, its potential negative implications (e.g., search errors, inability to produce a correct string via a different derivation) are not discussed.

**Questions to Authors**

1.  The model's success hinges on the external aligner. Could you conduct an experiment to quantify the model's sensitivity to alignment quality? For instance, how does performance degrade if you use a simpler aligner (e.g., based on Levenshtein distance) or inject noise into the alignments provided by the current aligner?
2.  The improved performance of your re-implemented soft attention model over the MED system on SIGMORPHON is attributed to a longer training schedule (100 vs. 20 epochs). To ensure the comparison is focused on architectural differences, what is the performance of your hard attention model when also trained for only 20 epochs? Is it still competitive with or superior to MED?
3.  Given that SVD is a matrix decomposition method, could you clarify the exact technique used for dimensionality reduction in Figure 4? Was it PCA, or projection onto the top two principal components derived from the singular vectors of the data matrix?
