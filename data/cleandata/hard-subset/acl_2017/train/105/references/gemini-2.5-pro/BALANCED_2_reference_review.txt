### Summary and Contributions

This paper proposes a novel encoder-decoder model with a "hard monotonic attention" mechanism for morphological inflection generation. The core idea is to leverage the predominantly monotonic nature of this task by reframing the generation problem. Instead of learning a soft, weighted alignment jointly with the transduction, the model is trained on a sequence of discrete actions: either `WRITE` an output character or `STEP` the attention pointer forward to the next input character. The "gold" action sequences for training are derived from an independently pre-computed character-level alignment.

The main contributions of this work are:
1.  **A simple and effective hard-attention model for nearly monotonic sequence transduction.** By decoupling alignment and transduction, the model simplifies the training objective to a standard cross-entropy loss over a sequence of explicit actions (`WRITE`/`STEP`), avoiding the complexities of soft attention or marginalization over latent alignments.
2.  **State-of-the-art results in low-resource settings.** The paper presents a strong empirical case for the model's effectiveness, particularly on the small CELEX dataset, where it is the first neural model to surpass a highly-tuned latent-variable FST baseline. This demonstrates a reduced tendency to overfit compared to standard soft-attention models.
3.  **Insightful comparative analysis.** The analysis section provides a valuable comparison of the learned alignments and internal representations of the proposed hard-attention model and a soft-attention baseline, shedding light on how each model encodes positional and character-level information to solve the task.

### Strengths

1.  **Model Simplicity and Elegance:** The proposed mechanism is conceptually simple yet powerful. Framing the problem as predicting a sequence of `WRITE` and `STEP` operations is an elegant way to incorporate a strong inductive bias (monotonicity) directly into the model's structure. This approach simplifies the learning problem significantly compared to soft-attention models, which must learn to implicitly focus on a diagonal alignment, or other transducer models that require more complex training procedures like the forward-backward algorithm.

2.  **Excellent Performance in Low-Data Regime:** The results on the CELEX dataset are a standout strength. The fact that this model significantly outperforms previous neural approaches and, more importantly, a strong, specialized non-neural baseline (Dreyer et al., 2008) is a compelling achievement. Figure 2 provides clear evidence that the hard-attention constraint acts as an effective regularizer, preventing the overfitting that the soft-attention model exhibits on this small dataset. This is a crucial advantage, as many morphological tasks in real-world scenarios are data-scarce.

3.  **Thorough Evaluation and Analysis:** The authors validate their model across three distinct and standard datasets, covering low-resource (CELEX), large-scale (Wiktionary), and typologically diverse (SIGMORPHON) scenarios. The analysis in Section 5 is particularly strong. By visualizing the learned alignments and the SVD projections of encoder states, the paper provides convincing evidence for its claims. The finding that the soft-attention model learns to encode positional information in its representations to achieve monotonicity, while the hard-attention model relies on the explicit `STEP` action, is an insightful contribution that helps explain the behavior of both architectures.

4.  **Clarity of Presentation:** The paper is very well-written, and the model is explained with exceptional clarity. Figure 1 provides an intuitive visualization of the model's operation, which greatly aids understanding. The experimental setup and results are presented clearly, making the paper easy to follow and the contributions easy to appreciate.

### Weaknesses

1.  **Dependence on an External Aligner:** The model's performance is critically dependent on the quality of the alignments produced by the external character aligner (Sudoh et al., 2013). This dependency is a potential weakness that is not fully explored. The paper does not discuss how sensitive the model is to the choice of this aligner, nor does it analyze how errors from the alignment stage might propagate and affect the final transduction performance. If the aligner produces systematic errors for certain phenomena, the model may be unable to recover, as it is trained to mimic this potentially flawed oracle.

2.  **Limited Applicability to Non-Monotonic Phenomena:** The model's core assumption of monotonicity restricts its applicability. While the bidirectional encoder provides some access to global context, the strictly forward-moving attention pointer cannot naturally handle non-concatenative morphology such as infixation, circumfixation, or reduplication. This limitation is acknowledged in the discussion of the SIGMORPHON results, where the soft-attention model performs better on languages with more complex phenomena (e.g., vowel harmony, templatic morphology). The paper could be strengthened by a more detailed discussion or qualitative analysis of these failure cases to better delineate the model's application boundaries.

3.  **Potentially Unfavorable Comparison on SIGMORPHON:** While the authors are commended for implementing a strong soft-attention baseline, the fact that this baseline ultimately achieves better average performance on the diverse SIGMORPHON dataset somewhat tempers the paper's main claims. The explanation that the baseline's superiority over the original MED system is due to longer training seems plausible, but it also highlights that, given sufficient data and training, a more flexible soft-attention model can outperform the more constrained hard-attention model on morphologically complex languages. This suggests the hard-attention model's primary strength may be confined to low-data scenarios or languages with simpler suffixing/prefixing morphology.

### Questions to Authors

1.  Could you elaborate on the model's sensitivity to the pre-computed alignments? Have you investigated how performance changes if a simpler aligner (e.g., one based on Levenshtein distance) is used to generate the oracle action sequences? How does the model cope with examples where the external aligner produces a noisy or incorrect alignment?

2.  In footnote 1, you mention that the model optimizes for the best derivation (action sequence) rather than marginalizing over all derivations for an output string. Have you analyzed the potential impact of this approximation? For a given input-output pair, how often is the "gold" action sequence derived from the aligner ambiguous or one of many plausible paths to the correct output?

3.  Could you provide a concrete example of a failure case from a language like Turkish or Hungarian where non-monotonic dependencies are crucial? It would be helpful to see how the hard-attention model fails and contrast its predicted action sequence with what would be required to generate the correct form.
