### Summary and Contributions

This paper introduces a neural encoder-decoder model with a "hard monotonic attention" mechanism for the task of morphological inflection generation. The core idea is to reframe the sequence transduction problem as learning a sequence of actions: either `WRITE` a character to the output or `STEP` to advance a pointer over the encoded input characters. This approach explicitly models the predominantly monotonic nature of morphological changes. A key aspect of the methodology is that instead of learning the alignments jointly with the transduction model, the authors use an external, pre-trained character aligner to generate a "gold" sequence of actions for each training example. The model is then trained with a standard cross-entropy loss to predict this action sequence. The authors evaluate their model on three standard datasets (CELEX, Wiktionary, SIGMORPHON 2016) and show strong performance.

The main contributions of this work, as I see them, are:
1.  **A simple and effective architecture with a strong inductive bias:** The proposed hard attention model provides an elegant way to incorporate the prior knowledge that morphological inflection is a largely monotonic process. By decoupling alignment from training and using a pre-computed oracle, the model simplifies the learning problem and becomes remarkably data-efficient.
2.  **State-of-the-art results in low-resource settings:** The paper demonstrates that this approach is the first neural method to surpass strong, task-specific non-neural baselines (Dreyer et al., 2008's LAT model) on the very small CELEX dataset, directly addressing a known weakness of standard sequence-to-sequence models.
3.  **An insightful comparative analysis:** The analysis in Section 5, which compares the learned representations and alignment behaviors of the hard and soft attention models, offers valuable insights. It convincingly argues that the hard attention model's explicit `STEP` action offloads the need for the encoder to represent positional information, allowing it to learn more robust character-centric representations, which likely contributes to its data efficiency.

### Strengths

1.  **Excellent Empirical Results and State-of-the-Art Performance:** The paper presents a comprehensive evaluation across three diverse datasets. The results are consistently strong, achieving state-of-the-art performance on both the low-resource CELEX dataset and the high-resource Wiktionary dataset. Beating the highly specialized LAT model on CELEX is a particularly notable achievement and a strong argument for the model's effectiveness where data is scarce.
2.  **Methodological Simplicity and Efficiency:** The decision to decouple alignment and transduction is a key strength. While joint training is often seen as superior, this work demonstrates that for tasks with strong structural priors, using an external aligner to create a deterministic training oracle is highly effective. This avoids the complexities of reinforcement learning or marginalizing over all possible alignments (as in RNN-T), resulting in a simple, stable, and fast cross-entropy training objective. The learning curves in Figure 2 provide clear evidence that this approach reduces overfitting compared to a soft-attention baseline in the low-data regime.
3.  **High-Quality Analysis and Interpretation:** The analysis in Section 5 is exemplary. The qualitative inspection of alignments in Figure 3 supports the authors' claims about how the model handles different phenomena (deletion, substitution). More impressively, the SVD analysis in Figure 4 provides a compelling, data-driven explanation for *why* the model is more data-efficient. The hypothesis that the soft-attention model must "spend" representational capacity on encoding position, while the hard-attention model represents it explicitly, is insightful and well-supported by the visualizations. This analysis elevates the paper from a simple presentation of a new model to a study that deepens our understanding of attention mechanisms in this problem space.
4.  **Clear and Concise Writing:** The paper is very well-written and easy to follow. The model is described clearly, the motivation is well-established, and the experiments are presented logically. The authors do an excellent job of positioning their work within the relevant literature, including classical FST-based methods, other neural models, and related work on hard attention and transducers.

### Weaknesses

1.  **Dependency on the External Aligner:** The model's performance is fundamentally tied to the quality of the alignments produced by the Sudoh et al. (2013) tool. This dependency is a potential point of failure. The paper does not investigate the model's sensitivity to alignment quality. For instance, how would performance change with a simpler or noisier aligner? An end-to-end model, while perhaps less data-efficient, has the theoretical advantage of being able to learn to correct for or ignore noise in the data, whereas this model will faithfully try to reproduce any systematic errors made by the aligner. This trade-off between a strong but potentially brittle inductive bias and a more flexible but data-hungry model deserves more discussion.
2.  **Inherent Limitation to Monotonic Phenomena:** The model's core architectural assumption is near-monotonicity. As the SIGMORPHON results show, this makes it a perfect fit for suffixing and stem-changing languages but less effective for languages with significant non-local or non-sequential phenomena, such as the templatic morphology of Arabic or the vowel harmony of Turkish and Finnish. While the authors correctly identify this in their results analysis, it remains a significant limitation on the model's general applicability. The paper would be strengthened by a discussion of potential extensions to handle such cases (e.g., adding `JUMP` or `STEP-BACK` actions), even if just as future work, as this would better define the boundaries of the proposed approach.
3.  **Approximation at Inference Time:** As noted in Footnote 1, the model finds the most probable sequence of *actions*, which is not guaranteed to produce the most probable output *string*. This is a classic search error issue in transition-based systems. While the model is clearly effective in practice, the paper could benefit from a brief discussion or error analysis on the impact of this approximation. For example, does beam search on the action sequence largely mitigate this? Are there observable cases where the optimal action sequence produces an incorrect inflection, while a sub-optimal sequence would have produced the correct one?

### Questions to Authors

1.  Could you elaborate on the model's robustness to the quality of the external aligner? Have you considered what would happen if a simpler heuristic (e.g., Levenshtein-based alignment) were used to generate the oracle actions? This would help clarify how much of the model's success is due to its architecture versus the quality of the provided alignments.
2.  The aligner produces a single best alignment for each training instance, from which you derive a deterministic action sequence. In cases of genuine linguistic ambiguity in alignment, this forces a single interpretation. How does the model handle this? Do you see any evidence of the model struggling with cases where the oracle's choice might be arbitrary or incorrect?
3.  Your analysis of the SIGMORPHON results suggests the model struggles with non-monotonic phenomena like vowel harmony. Does a qualitative error analysis on languages like Turkish or Hungarian confirm this? Specifically, can you show examples where the model fails to propagate a harmonic feature across the word because its attention is strictly local and forward-moving?
