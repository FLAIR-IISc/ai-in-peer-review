**Summary of the paper and contributions**

This paper proposes a sequence-to-sequence model with a "hard monotonic attention" mechanism for morphological inflection generation. The core idea is to replace the standard soft attention, which computes a weighted average over all input states, with a discrete control mechanism. At each decoding step, the model chooses between two actions: writing an output character or advancing a pointer (`step`) to the next input character encoding. This approach enforces a strict, monotonic alignment between the input and output. A key aspect of the proposed method is that it decouples alignment from transduction; a separate, pre-existing character alignment tool is used to generate gold action sequences for training, which simplifies the learning problem to a standard cross-entropy minimization task.

As I see them, the main contributions of this work are:

1.  Strong empirical results on the task of morphological inflection, particularly in a low-resource setting (CELEX dataset). The proposed model is the first neural approach to outperform the highly specialized latent-variable WFST model of Dreyer et al. (2008) on this benchmark.
2.  The application of a simplified hard-attention, transition-based system to this problem. By leveraging a strong inductive bias (monotonicity) and an external aligner, the model demonstrates high data efficiency compared to more general soft-attention models.
3.  An insightful analysis comparing the learned representations and alignment behaviors of the hard and soft attention models, shedding some light on why the simpler monotonic assumption is effective for this task.

**Strengths**

1.  **Excellent Empirical Performance in a Low-Resource Setting:** The most compelling strength of this paper is its performance on the CELEX dataset. It has been a known issue that standard sequence-to-sequence models with soft attention tend to overfit and underperform specialized non-neural models on small datasets like this one. The authors convincingly show that their model overcomes this limitation, achieving a new state-of-the-art and outperforming the strong LAT baseline by Dreyer et al. (2008). The learning curve in Figure 2 clearly illustrates the improved generalization of the hard attention model over the soft attention baseline on the development set, providing strong evidence for the effectiveness of the approach when training data is scarce.

2.  **Methodological Simplicity and a Well-Justified Inductive Bias:** The proposed model is conceptually simple and directly encodes a strong, justifiable assumption about the task: morphological inflection is often a nearly monotonic process of string transduction. By building this assumption directly into the model architecture via the `step` action, the search space is constrained, which likely contributes to its data efficiency. Decoupling the alignment and transduction steps simplifies the training process significantly, avoiding the complexities of marginalizing over alignments or jointly learning an attention distribution from scratch. This is a sound engineering approach that proves effective.

3.  **Thorough Evaluation and Analysis:** The authors evaluate their model across three distinct datasets with varying sizes and linguistic properties (CELEX, Wiktionary, SIGMORPHON). This demonstrates the robustness of their method. The analysis in Section 5 is particularly valuable. The comparison of learned alignments (Figure 3) and the SVD visualization of encoder states (Figure 4) provide concrete insights into the differences between the hard and soft attention mechanisms. The finding that the soft-attention model implicitly learns to encode positional information to achieve monotonicity, while the hard-attention model relies on its explicit state-tracking, is an interesting and well-supported conclusion.

**Weaknesses**

1.  **Significant Dependence on an External Aligner:** The model's primary weakness is its reliance on a pre-computed alignment to generate the oracle for training. The performance of the entire system is critically dependent on the quality of this external alignment tool (Sudoh et al., 2013). This raises several concerns:
    *   The contribution is less about learning to align and transduce, and more about learning to mimic the output of a given aligner. This reduces the autonomy and novelty of the learning framework.
    *   The paper does not analyze the model's sensitivity to the quality of the alignments. What happens if the aligner is noisy or biased? How would the model perform on a language for which a high-quality character aligner is not readily available? This dependency limits the model's applicability as a standalone, end-to-end solution.
    *   It somewhat unfairly simplifies the problem for the neural model compared to the soft-attention baseline, which must learn alignments from scratch. The comparison is not entirely apples-to-apples, as the proposed model gets a strong supervisory signal that the baseline does not.

2.  **Limited Methodological Novelty:** While the application is effective, the core concepts are not entirely new. Transition-based systems with an oracle are well-established in other areas of NLP (e.g., dependency parsing). Hard attention mechanisms have also been explored previously. The primary novelty here is the specific formulation with `write` and `step` actions for this particular sequence-to-sequence task. The contribution feels more like a successful and clever application of existing ideas rather than the introduction of a fundamentally new technique.

3.  **Reduced Flexibility for Non-Monotonic Phenomena:** The model's main strength—its strong monotonic inductive bias—is also a weakness. It is inherently less flexible than a soft-attention model for handling non-monotonic or long-distance dependencies. The authors acknowledge this when discussing the SIGMORPHON results, where their model is slightly outperformed by the soft-attention baseline on languages with phenomena like vowel harmony. While it performs well on average, this suggests the model is a specialized tool for suffixing-heavy languages, rather than a general-purpose morphological inflector. In contrast, a soft-attention model has the theoretical capacity to learn any alignment pattern, even if it requires more data to do so.

**Questions to Authors**

1.  Could you comment on the sensitivity of your model to the quality of the pre-computed alignments? Have you analyzed the impact of alignment errors from the Sudoh et al. (2013) tool on the final model performance? For instance, what percentage of training examples contain potentially incorrect alignments, and how does the model behave on them?

2.  The training procedure relies on a single, deterministically generated "gold" action sequence. This seems brittle, as multiple action sequences could potentially lead to the correct output (e.g., `step`, `write(a)`, `write(b)` vs. `write(a)`, `step`, `write(b)`). Have you considered training methods that could account for this ambiguity or explore the search space more robustly, such as scheduled sampling or reinforcement learning, to make the model less dependent on a perfect oracle?

3.  The paper frames the model as "simpler" than soft attention. Could you quantify this? Specifically, how does the model compare in terms of the number of parameters, training time per epoch, and inference speed against the soft-attention baseline you implemented?
