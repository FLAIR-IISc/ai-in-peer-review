### **Summary and Contributions**

This paper introduces a novel sequence-to-sequence architecture with a "hard monotonic attention" mechanism, designed specifically for tasks with nearly monotonic alignments, such as morphological inflection generation. Instead of computing a weighted average over all encoder states like standard soft attention, the proposed model's decoder has a discrete choice at each step: either `WRITE` an output symbol or `STEP` its attention pointer to the next input position. A key aspect of the methodology is that the model is not trained to jointly learn alignments. Instead, it leverages alignments generated by an external character-level aligner to create a "gold" sequence of `WRITE` and `STEP` actions for training. The model is then trained with a simple cross-entropy loss to predict this action sequence. The authors demonstrate the model's effectiveness with extensive experiments on three standard datasets (CELEX, Wiktionary, SIGMORPHON2016), achieving state-of-the-art results, particularly in low-resource settings.

In my view, the main contributions of this work are:

1.  **A simple and effective model for monotonic sequence transduction:** The proposed hard attention model, with its explicit `WRITE` and `STEP` actions, provides a strong and appropriate inductive bias for tasks like morphological inflection. The decision to decouple alignment from transduction by using pre-computed alignments simplifies the training process significantly compared to models that require marginalization over all possible alignments.
2.  **Strong empirical results establishing a new SOTA, especially in low-data regimes:** The paper demonstrates that this approach not only matches or exceeds prior work on large datasets but also, crucially, overcomes the known tendency of neural models to overfit on small datasets. Being the first neural model to outperform the specialized latent-variable FST model on the challenging CELEX dataset is a significant achievement.
3.  **Insightful analysis comparing hard and soft attention mechanisms:** The analysis in Section 5, which visualizes and contrasts the learned alignments and internal representations, provides valuable insights into *why* the proposed model works. It convincingly argues that the hard attention model offloads the burden of tracking position from the encoder states to the explicit `STEP` action, which helps its generalization performance.

### **Strengths**

1.  **Excellent performance in low-resource settings:** The paper's most compelling argument is its performance on the CELEX dataset. Neural sequence-to-sequence models are notoriously data-hungry, and their failure to outperform older, more structured models on small datasets has been a persistent issue. This work directly tackles that weakness and succeeds, as shown in Table 1 and the learning curves in Figure 2. The clear gap in development set accuracy between the hard and soft attention models provides strong evidence that the hard attention model's constrained nature acts as a very effective regularizer, preventing overfitting.
2.  **Elegance and simplicity of the approach:** The core idea is conceptually simple yet powerful. By framing the problem as learning a sequence of discrete operations, the model avoids the complexities of both soft attention (calculating and backpropagating through a dense attention distribution) and more complex latent-alignment models (like RNN-T, which requires a forward-backward algorithm). Training becomes a straightforward classification problem over an extended vocabulary, which is both efficient and stable.
3.  **Thorough and well-conducted experiments:** The evaluation is comprehensive, covering three distinct datasets that test the model under different conditions: low-resource (CELEX), high-resource (Wiktionary), and typological diversity (SIGMORPHON). The authors are careful to compare against relevant and strong baselines from both neural and non-neural paradigms.
4.  **High-quality analysis and interpretation:** The analysis in Section 5 is a major strength. It goes beyond simply reporting scores and provides a plausible explanation for the model's success. The SVD visualizations of encoder representations (Figure 4) are particularly illuminating, offering a clear hypothesis that soft attention models are forced to encode positional information directly into their representations, whereas the hard attention model does not need to, potentially freeing up representational capacity for other features. The nuanced discussion of the SIGMORPHON results, linking performance to linguistic typology (e.g., suffixing vs. vowel harmony), further demonstrates a deep understanding of the problem domain.

### **Weaknesses**

1.  **Dependence on an external alignment tool:** The model's success is predicated on the availability of a high-quality, pre-computed character alignment. This decouples the problem nicely but also makes the system a pipeline, with performance dependent on a component that is not part of the core model. The paper does not investigate the model's sensitivity to the quality of these alignments. For instance, how would performance degrade if a simpler aligner (e.g., based on Levenshtein distance) were used, or if the aligner made systematic errors? This makes the approach less self-contained than end-to-end joint training models.
2.  **Limited applicability beyond nearly-monotonic tasks:** The model's primary strength—its strong monotonic inductive bias—is also its main limitation. While perfectly suited for inflectional morphology in many languages, it is fundamentally unsuitable for tasks requiring significant reordering, such as translation between syntactically distant languages. The authors suggest future work in abstractive summarization and MT, but the direct applicability is questionable without significant modification. The paper could be strengthened by a more explicit discussion of the boundaries of its applicability.
3.  **Potential ambiguity of the "best derivation":** As noted in Footnote 1, the model is trained to find the best *derivation* (sequence of actions), not necessarily the best output string, as multiple derivations could lead to the same output. While the authors argue this approximation is effective in practice (which the results support), it remains a theoretical gap. It would be interesting to know if the model ever struggles with cases where multiple valid derivations exist for a single correct output, and if this could lead to optimization instability, especially since the "gold" derivation is determined deterministically from a single alignment.

### **Questions to Authors**

1.  Could you comment on the sensitivity of your model to the quality of the external aligner? Have you experimented with different alignment methods to see how it impacts final accuracy? For example, would using a simple Levenshtein alignment for the training oracle significantly degrade performance compared to the CRP-based aligner?
2.  The model's mechanism seems well-suited for prefixing, suffixing, and infixing. How would it handle morphological processes that involve non-local reordering, such as metathesis (e.g., `abc` -> `acb`)? Would this require a different set of control actions beyond a single forward `STEP`?
3.  Regarding the SIGMORPHON results, you hypothesize that soft attention is better for phenomena like vowel harmony due to its ability to model long-range dependencies. Could the hard attention model be augmented to handle this, perhaps by allowing the decoder to access a global representation of the input (e.g., the final encoder hidden state) in addition to the currently attended character embedding?
