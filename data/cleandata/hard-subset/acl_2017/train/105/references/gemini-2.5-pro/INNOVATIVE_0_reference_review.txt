### Summary and Contributions
This paper introduces a novel encoder-decoder model with a "hard monotonic attention" mechanism for the task of morphological inflection generation. Instead of using a soft, weighted attention over all input states, this model maintains a single pointer to the current input character. At each decoding step, the model chooses between two types of actions: `WRITE` an output character or `STEP` the pointer to the next input character. A key aspect of the work is that it decouples the alignment and transduction phases during training. It first uses an external tool to generate character-level alignments for the training data, from which it derives a gold sequence of `WRITE` and `STEP` actions. The neural model is then trained with a simple cross-entropy loss to predict this action sequence. The authors evaluate their model on three datasets of varying sizes and linguistic diversity, showing that it achieves state-of-the-art results and is particularly effective in low-resource settings.

In my view, the main contributions are:
1.  **A novel and elegant hard attention architecture:** The formulation of sequence transduction as a series of explicit `WRITE` and `STEP` actions is a significant conceptual contribution. It provides a strong, problem-appropriate inductive bias for nearly monotonic tasks, moving away from the more generic (and potentially overly flexible) soft attention mechanism.
2.  **A compelling demonstration of the value of decoupled training:** The paper makes a bold choice to abandon the popular end-to-end joint training of alignment and transduction. By using pre-computed alignments to create a supervised signal for the attention mechanism, the model benefits from a simpler training objective and a clearer learning path, which proves highly effective.
3.  **State-of-the-art results in a challenging low-resource setting:** The paper is the first to show a neural model outperforming the specialized, latent-variable WFST model of Dreyer et al. (2008) on the very small CELEX dataset. This is a noteworthy achievement that highlights the practical benefits of the proposed approach.

### Strengths
1.  **High Novelty and Conceptual Clarity:** The core idea of this paper is refreshingly novel. In a field dominated by soft-attention, the authors propose a fundamentally different, more constrained mechanism that is beautifully tailored to the problem structure. The model is conceptually simple and interpretable: the decoder's actions directly correspond to a human-understandable process of reading the input and writing the output. This represents a bold and successful exploration of an alternative direction in sequence-to-sequence modeling.

2.  **Exceptional Low-Resource Performance:** The results on the CELEX dataset are the paper's strongest selling point. It has been a known issue that standard neural sequence-to-sequence models tend to overfit and underperform on this small dataset compared to more structured, traditional models. This paper's model not only closes that gap but surpasses the previous state-of-the-art. The learning curve in Figure 2 provides convincing evidence that the hard attention model's strong inductive bias acts as a powerful regularizer, preventing the overfitting that plagues the soft attention model in this setting. This is a significant result for the applicability of neural methods in data-scarce scenarios.

3.  **Insightful Analysis:** The analysis in Section 5 is excellent and elevates the paper beyond a simple presentation of a new model. The visualization of the learned alignments (Figure 3) and encoder representations (Figure 4) provides crucial insights into the differences between the hard and soft attention models. In particular, the finding that the soft attention model appears to bake positional information more strongly into its character representations, while the hard attention model can offload this to the explicit `STEP` action, is a fascinating and well-supported conclusion. This analysis helps explain *why* the model is so effective and data-efficient.

### Weaknesses
1.  **Dependence on an External Aligner:** The model's performance is contingent on the quality of the alignments generated by the pre-processing step. This introduces a dependency that makes the method not fully end-to-end and raises questions about its robustness. If the character aligner is of poor quality or unavailable for a particular language pair, the model cannot be trained. While the chosen aligner clearly works well, the paper would be stronger if it included an ablation study on the sensitivity of the model to alignment quality (e.g., by using a simpler aligner or injecting noise into the gold action sequences). This is a minor methodological weakness, however, as the results convincingly validate the overall approach.

2.  **Limited Scope for Non-Monotonic Phenomena:** The model's primary strength—its strong monotonic bias—is also its main limitation. As the authors fairly acknowledge, its performance advantage diminishes on languages from the SIGMORPHON dataset that exhibit more complex, non-local phenomena like vowel harmony. The soft attention model's ability to draw context from the entire input sequence gives it an edge here. This is not a flaw in the paper, but a clear trade-off that defines the model's scope. The work would benefit from a brief discussion on potential avenues for extending the framework to handle such cases, even if speculatively.

### Questions to Authors
1.  The training methodology relies on deriving an oracle sequence of actions from pre-computed alignments. Could you comment on the sensitivity of your model to the quality of these alignments? How do you think performance would be affected if a much simpler method, such as one based purely on Levenshtein distance, were used to generate the oracle?

2.  The model is explicitly designed for *nearly* monotonic tasks. Do you see a path forward for extending this hard-attention framework to better handle non-local dependencies like vowel harmony or circumfixation? For instance, could the decoder be given access to a window of input states rather than just one, or could the action space be expanded to include non-sequential `JUMP` operations?
