### **Summary and Contributions**

This paper introduces a novel sequence-to-sequence model with a "hard monotonic attention" mechanism for morphological inflection generation. The core idea is to reframe the transduction task as learning a sequence of actions: either writing an output character or advancing a pointer (`step`) over the input sequence. This explicitly models the predominantly monotonic nature of morphological transformations (e.g., suffixation, stem changes). A key aspect of the proposed method is that it decouples alignment from transduction during training; it uses an external character alignment tool to generate gold-standard action sequences (the oracle) and then trains the neural network to predict these sequences using a standard cross-entropy loss.

In my view, the main contributions of this work are:

1.  **A simple and effective hard attention model for monotonic sequence transduction.** The proposed `step`/`write` mechanism is an elegant way to incorporate a strong inductive bias into the model. By simplifying the problem to predicting actions from an oracle, the training process becomes more direct and stable compared to models that must jointly learn to align and transduce.
2.  **State-of-the-art results on multiple benchmarks, with particularly strong performance in low-resource settings.** The paper's most compelling result is outperforming a highly specialized latent-variable FST model on the small CELEX dataset, a task where previous neural models have struggled. This demonstrates the model's data efficiency, which is a significant contribution.
3.  **An insightful analysis comparing the hard attention model to a standard soft attention baseline.** The SVD visualizations of the encoder representations provide evidence that the hard attention model relies on the explicit `step` action for positional information, allowing its character representations to be more distinct. In contrast, the soft attention model appears to encode positional information more heavily into the character representations themselves.

### **Strengths**

1.  **Excellent performance in low-resource conditions.** The results on the CELEX dataset are the paper's standout achievement. Previous neural models, including soft-attention variants, have been shown to overfit on this small dataset. By demonstrating superior performance over both neural and strong non-neural baselines (Dreyer et al., 2008), this work provides a compelling solution to a known weakness of neural sequence models. The learning curve in Figure 2 clearly illustrates this advantage, showing the hard attention model generalizes better while the soft attention model overfits.
2.  **The model is conceptually simple and well-motivated.** The assumption of near-monotonicity is very appropriate for morphological inflection in many languages. The proposed mechanism directly models this assumption, providing a strong inductive bias that guides the learning process. The decoupling of alignment and transduction simplifies the training objective significantly, avoiding the need for complex marginalization over all possible alignments (as in RNN-T) and instead using a straightforward cross-entropy loss. This simplicity makes the model easy to understand, implement, and train.
3.  **The experimental evaluation is thorough and convincing.** The authors evaluate their model across three distinct and standard datasets: a low-resource setting (CELEX), a high-resource setting (Wiktionary), and a typologically diverse setting (SIGMORPHON 2016). This comprehensive evaluation demonstrates the model's robustness and clarifies its strengths (on suffixing languages) and weaknesses (on languages with more non-local phenomena). The comparison against multiple strong baselines, including both recent neural models and older, established non-neural systems, is well-executed and contextualizes the contribution effectively.

### **Weaknesses**

1.  **The model's primary weakness is its reliance on a pre-trained, external character aligner.** The entire training paradigm is predicated on the existence of a high-quality "oracle" action sequence, which is derived from these external alignments. This raises several concerns: (a) The model's performance is fundamentally capped by or dependent on the quality of the aligner. The paper does not investigate how the model would perform with a sub-optimal aligner or with alignments generated by different tools. (b) This dependency makes the model less "end-to-end" than soft-attention alternatives. For a new language, one would first need to obtain or train a separate alignment model. (c) It offloads the difficult problem of alignment to a separate, non-trivial system.
2.  **The hard monotonic assumption limits the model's applicability to more complex morphological phenomena.** The authors' own results on the SIGMORPHON dataset demonstrate this limitation. The model excels on languages with suffixing and stem changes (Russian, German, Spanish) but is outperformed by a soft attention model on languages with phenomena like vowel harmony, circumfixing, or templatic morphology. While the model is designed for *nearly*-monotonic transformations, its architecture has no inherent mechanism to handle long-distance dependencies or non-sequential mappings, which soft attention can capture by design. This makes it a specialized tool rather than a general-purpose morphological transducer.
3.  **The positioning with respect to prior work on hard monotonic attention could be stronger.** The paper cites work like the RNN Transducer (Graves, 2012), which also uses a monotonic alignment framework. While the proposed model's training scheme (using a pre-computed oracle) is a key difference, the core concept of moving a pointer forward through the input is not entirely new. A more detailed comparison in the related work section, highlighting the specific advantages of the `step`/`write` formulation and oracle-based training over the RNN-T's forward-backward algorithm, would have strengthened the paper's claims of novelty.

### **Questions to Authors**

1.  How sensitive is the model's performance to the quality and methodology of the external character aligner? Have you experimented with different aligners (e.g., a simple Levenshtein-based one) or simulated noisy alignments to test the model's robustness to alignment errors?
2.  Your re-implementation of the soft attention model achieves a new state-of-the-art on the SIGMORPHON 2016 dataset, outperforming both your proposed hard attention model and the original winning system (MED). Could you clarify the key implementation or training differences that led to this strong baseline result? Does this suggest that with sufficient data and careful tuning, the greater flexibility of soft attention makes it a superior general approach for this task across typologically diverse languages?
3.  Could the hard attention framework be extended to handle non-monotonic phenomena, for instance by introducing actions like `step_backward` or `jump_to(i)`? How would you envision generating an oracle for training such a model?
