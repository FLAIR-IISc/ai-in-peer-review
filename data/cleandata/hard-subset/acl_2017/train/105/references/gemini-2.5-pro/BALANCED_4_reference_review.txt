This paper presents a novel encoder-decoder model with a hard monotonic attention mechanism for morphological inflection generation. The core idea is to reframe the sequence transduction task as learning a sequence of actions: either writing a character to the output or stepping forward along the input sequence. Crucially, the model is trained on gold action sequences derived from pre-computed character alignments of the training data. This decouples the alignment and transduction problems, simplifying the training objective while explicitly encoding the strong monotonic assumption common in morphological inflection. The model is evaluated on three standard datasets (CELEX, Wiktionary, SIGMORPHON) and is shown to achieve state-of-the-art or highly competitive results, particularly in low-resource settings.

The main contributions of this work are:
1.  A simple and effective hard monotonic attention model for sequence transduction. The method of decoupling alignment from generation by using an external aligner to create an oracle for training is a key part of this contribution.
2.  Strong empirical results across multiple benchmarks. The model is notably the first neural approach to outperform the highly specialized latent-variable FST model of Dreyer et al. (2008) on the low-resource CELEX dataset, demonstrating its data efficiency.
3.  An insightful analysis comparing the proposed hard attention model with a standard soft attention baseline, providing evidence for how the two models encode positional information and learn alignments.

### Strengths

1.  **Elegant Simplicity and Effectiveness:** The proposed method is conceptually simple and elegant. By transforming the problem into predicting a sequence of `WRITE` and `STEP` actions based on pre-computed alignments, the authors avoid the complexities of jointly learning to align and transduce. This leads to a straightforward training regime using a standard cross-entropy loss, making the model easy to implement and train. Despite this simplicity, the model proves to be highly effective, achieving state-of-the-art results.

2.  **Excellent Performance in Low-Resource Scenarios:** The paper's strongest empirical result is its performance on the CELEX dataset. Previous neural models, including those with soft attention, have struggled to outperform non-neural, knowledge-rich baselines like the LAT model on this small dataset. This paper demonstrates convincingly (Table 1, Figure 2) that by enforcing a hard monotonic constraint, the model is less prone to overfitting and can generalize better from limited data. This is a significant finding for applying neural methods to low-resource morphological tasks.

3.  **Thorough and Comprehensive Evaluation:** The authors validate their model on three distinct datasets that cover low-resource (CELEX), high-resource (Wiktionary), and typologically diverse (SIGMORPHON) scenarios. The comparison to a wide range of strong neural and non-neural baselines is comprehensive and persuasive. The inclusion of an analysis section that visualizes learned alignments and representations adds significant value, offering plausible explanations for the model's performance advantages and shedding light on the inner workings of attention mechanisms in this context.

### Weaknesses

1.  **Critical Dependency on an External Aligner:** The entire training process relies on an oracle created by an external character alignment tool (Sudoh et al., 2013). This introduces a strong dependency, and the paper does not investigate the model's sensitivity to the quality of these alignments. If the aligner produces systematic errors, the model will be trained to replicate them. It is unclear how robust the method is to different alignment algorithms or noisy alignments. This dependency somewhat limits the model's self-contained nature and introduces an unexamined variable that could significantly impact performance.

2.  **Inherent Limitation to Monotonic Phenomena:** The model's architecture is explicitly designed for nearly-monotonic transformations. While the bi-directional encoder provides some awareness of global context, the decoder's fundamental `STEP`-forward action is a hard constraint that makes it poorly suited for languages with significant non-concatenative morphology (e.g., templatic morphology in Semitic languages) or other non-monotonic processes. The results on the SIGMORPHON dataset, where the model is slightly weaker on languages like Arabic and Navajo, support this. The paper acknowledges this but could be more upfront about the model's typological limitations.

3.  **Unclear Positioning with Respect to Transition-Based Models:** The proposed model can be viewed as a transition-based sequence transducer. The paper frames the contribution in terms of "hard attention," but the connection to the extensive literature on transition-based systems (e.g., for parsing, speech recognition) is not fully explored. The description in the last sentence of Section 2.3 ("An alternative view...") is highly accurate but feels like an afterthought. A clearer positioning of the work within this context would help to better delineate its novelty and contributions.

### Questions to Authors

1.  Could you comment on the sensitivity of your model to the quality of the pre-computed alignments? For instance, how would performance be affected if a simpler alignment method (e.g., based on Levenshtein distance) were used to generate the oracle? How does the model behave when faced with noisy or systematically incorrect alignments in the training data?

2.  The model's design is inherently monotonic. While this is a strength for many languages, it is also a limitation. Have you considered extensions to handle non-monotonic phenomena more gracefully? For example, would it be feasible to add actions like `JUMP` or `STEP_BACK`, and what challenges would you foresee in training such a model, especially in deriving a reliable oracle?

3.  Your re-implementation of a soft-attention model achieved state-of-the-art results on the SIGMORPHON dataset, outperforming the winning MED system. This is an important result on its own. Could you please provide more details about your implementation and training setup (e.g., hyperparameters, number of training epochs vs. MED) that led to this substantial improvement?
