### Review

**Summary of the Paper and Main Contributions:**

This paper proposes a neural sequence-to-sequence model for morphological inflection generation. The core of the model is a "hard monotonic attention" mechanism, where the decoder, at each step, makes a discrete choice to either write an output character or advance its attention pointer to the next character in the encoded input sequence (`step`). A key design choice is to decouple the alignment and transduction processes. Instead of learning alignments jointly, the model is trained on gold action sequences (`write`/`step`) derived from pre-computed character-level alignments on the training data. The authors evaluate this approach on three standard datasets (CELEX, Wiktionary, SIGMORPHON 2016), demonstrating strong performance, especially in low-resource settings. The paper also includes an analysis comparing the learned representations of their hard attention model to a standard soft attention model.

As I see them, the main contributions are:

1.  **A simple and highly effective hard attention model for morphological inflection.** The model's main novelty lies in its training procedure, which uses pre-computed alignments to create a supervised signal for a simple `write`/`step` action space. This simplifies the learning problem significantly and proves to be particularly robust in low-data regimes, where it is the first neural model to surpass a strong, specialized WFST baseline on the CELEX dataset.
2.  **An insightful analysis that provides a compelling explanation for the model's data efficiency.** By visualizing the encoder's latent space, the authors show that their hard attention model learns representations that cluster primarily by character identity, whereas a soft attention model must also encode positional information. This suggests the hard attention model's explicit `step` action frees up model capacity, offering a clear hypothesis for its superior performance on small datasets.

**Strengths:**

1.  **Model Simplicity and Effectiveness:** The proposed model is elegant in its simplicity. By reframing the task from jointly learning to align and transduce to simply predicting a sequence of `write` and `step` actions, the authors remove the need for complex attention computations or marginalization over latent alignments during training. This allows for straightforward training with a standard cross-entropy loss. The fact that this simpler approach achieves state-of-the-art results across multiple datasets is a very strong result.
2.  **Exceptional Performance in a Low-Resource Setting:** The results on the CELEX dataset are a standout achievement. Neural models often overfit on small datasets, yet this model significantly outperforms both soft-attention and neural WFST baselines. Surpassing the highly specialized latent-variable FST model of Dreyer et al. (2008) is a significant milestone and provides strong evidence that incorporating a correct inductive bias (monotonicity) is more critical than model complexity when data is scarce. The learning curve in Figure 2 clearly illustrates this advantage.
3.  **High-Quality Analysis of Learned Representations:** The analysis in Section 5 is excellent and substantially strengthens the paper's contribution. The SVD visualizations (Figure 4) offer a clear, intuitive explanation for the performance difference between hard and soft attention models in low-data settings. The hypothesis that the soft-attention model needs to "use up" representational capacity to encode position, while the hard-attention model handles this explicitly via `step` actions, is compelling and well-supported by the visualizations. This moves the paper beyond a pure performance report into a more scientific exploration of *why* the model works.
4.  **Thorough and Robust Evaluation:** The authors validate their model on three different benchmark datasets that vary in size, language coverage, and morphological complexity. The consistent strong performance across the low-resource CELEX, the large-scale Wiktionary, and the typologically diverse SIGMORPHON datasets demonstrates the general applicability and robustness of the method for languages where monotonic alignment is a reasonable assumption.

**Weaknesses:**

1.  **Critical Dependence on an External Aligner:** The model's success is predicated on the availability of a high-quality external character aligner. The entire training process relies on the "gold" action sequences derived from these alignments. The paper does not analyze the model's sensitivity to the quality of this aligner. How would the model perform if a less accurate aligner were used? How do alignment errors propagate and affect the final model accuracy? Framing the decoupling as a strength (simplicity) overlooks that it also introduces a dependency and a potential point of failure that is not evaluated. An ablation study or analysis on this aspect would make the paper's claims much stronger.
2.  **Inherent Limitation to Monotonic Phenomena:** The model's architecture enforces a strictly monotonic traversal of the input sequence. While this is a good prior for many suffixing languages, it is fundamentally unsuited for non-concatenative morphology (e.g., templatic morphology in Arabic) or phenomena like infixation and reduplication. The results on the SIGMORPHON dataset already hint at this limitation, where the soft-attention model performs better on languages with more complex phenomena. The paper should be more explicit about this scope condition. While it is presented as a model for "nearly-monotonic" tasks, the mechanism itself is strictly monotonic and cannot, for example, jump back in the input sequence.
3.  **Unclear Impact of the "Best Derivation" Approximation:** Footnote 1 correctly points out that the model optimizes for the most likely derivation (action sequence) rather than the most likely output string (which would require marginalizing over all derivations producing that string). This is a common and reasonable approximation, but its implications are not discussed. For a given input/output pair, multiple action sequences could produce the correct string. The deterministic procedure for generating the gold sequence from an alignment picks just one. It is unclear if this choice is always optimal from a learnability perspective or if this approximation has any practical negative consequences on performance.

**Questions to Authors:**

1.  Could you comment on the sensitivity of your model's performance to the choice and quality of the external character aligner? Have you considered analyzing the impact of alignment errors (e.g., by injecting noise into the gold alignments) on the final inflection accuracy?
2.  The `step` action enforces strict monotonicity. How does the model handle phenomena that disrupt a simple left-to-right alignment, such as circumfixation (e.g., German *ge-sag-t*)? Does the pre-computed alignment linearize this in a way the model can handle (e.g., by treating the prefix as a series of `write` operations before the first `step`), and does this approach generalize?
3.  Regarding the "best derivation" approximation (Footnote 1): Can you provide more intuition on when this might be a limitation? For instance, are there cases where the single gold action sequence derived from the alignment is ambiguous or potentially difficult for the model to learn, whereas an alternative derivation leading to the same output string might be easier?
