### **Summary and Contributions**

This paper proposes a neural encoder-decoder model with a "hard monotonic attention" mechanism for morphological inflection generation. The core idea is to reframe the sequence transduction task as learning a sequence of actions, which are either writing an output character or stepping the attention pointer forward to the next input character. This approach imposes a strong inductive bias, assuming a nearly monotonic alignment between the source and target words, which is common in morphological processes like suffixation. To train the model, character-level alignments are first generated using an external tool, from which a gold sequence of write/step actions is derived. The model is then trained with a standard cross-entropy loss to predict this action sequence.

In my view, the main contributions are:

1.  **An effective, simplified transduction model for monotonic tasks:** The primary contribution is the hard attention model itself. By restricting the decoder to a simple set of `write` and `step` actions and relying on pre-computed alignments for training, the authors create a model that is conceptually simple yet highly effective for its target domain. This decouples the alignment and transduction problems during training, which proves to be a very successful strategy.
2.  **State-of-the-art results, especially in low-resource settings:** The paper provides a strong empirical validation of the model. It achieves state-of-the-art results on three standard benchmarks (CELEX, Wiktionary, SIGMORPHON). Crucially, it is the first neural model reported to outperform the strong, task-specific latent-variable WFST model on the very small CELEX dataset, demonstrating its data efficiency.
3.  **Insightful comparative analysis of hard vs. soft attention:** The analysis in Section 5, comparing the learned representations and alignments of the hard and soft attention models, is a valuable contribution. The SVD visualizations provide compelling evidence that the hard attention model offloads the need to encode positional information into the encoder representations, instead capturing it explicitly through the `step` actions, which helps explain its robustness on small datasets.

### **Strengths**

1.  **Strong and appropriate inductive bias:** The model's central assumption—that morphological inflection is nearly monotonic—is a well-chosen inductive bias. For many languages, particularly those with concatenative morphology, this holds true. This constraint simplifies the learning problem significantly, allowing the model to focus on the transduction operations themselves. The excellent results on the low-resource CELEX dataset (Table 1) and the learning curves (Figure 2), which show it avoids the overfitting seen in the more flexible soft-attention model, are direct evidence of this strength.
2.  **Excellent and thorough empirical evaluation:** The authors evaluate their model across three distinct and widely used datasets that cover low-resource (CELEX), high-resource (Wiktionary), and typologically diverse (SIGMORPHON) scenarios. The model consistently performs at or above the state of the art, demonstrating its effectiveness and robustness. Beating the highly specialized LAT FST model on CELEX is a particularly impressive result for a neural approach.
3.  **Clarity and Simplicity:** The model architecture and training procedure are explained clearly and are relatively straightforward. Using pre-computed alignments to create a gold action sequence allows for training with a simple cross-entropy loss, avoiding more complex training regimes like the forward-backward algorithm used in some transducer models. This makes the approach easy to understand and replicate.
4.  **High-quality analysis:** The analysis section is a significant strength. Instead of just presenting results, the authors investigate *why* their model performs well. The alignment visualizations in Figure 3 confirm that the model learns sensible, crisp alignments. The representation analysis in Figure 4 offers a plausible and insightful explanation for the data efficiency of the hard attention model compared to its soft counterpart, linking it to the explicit modeling of position via `step` actions.

### **Weaknesses**

1.  **Dependence on an external alignment tool:** The model's performance is fundamentally tied to the quality of the alignments produced by the Sudoh et al. (2013) tool. The entire training supervision is derived from these alignments. This raises questions about the model's robustness: how would it perform with a different, perhaps lower-quality, aligner? Or in a language where such aligners perform poorly? The paper does not investigate this dependency, making the approach less self-contained than end-to-end systems that jointly learn to align and transduce. This dependency could be a significant practical limitation.
2.  **Limited applicability to non-monotonic phenomena:** The model's main strength is also its main limitation. It is explicitly designed for *nearly-monotonic* alignments. The results on the SIGMORPHON dataset (Table 3) reflect this: the model excels on languages with suffixing and stem changes (e.g., German, Spanish) but is outperformed by the soft-attention model on languages with more complex phenomena like templatic morphology (Arabic) or long-distance harmony (Finnish, Hungarian). While the authors acknowledge this, it frames the model as a specialized tool for a subset of morphological tasks rather than a general solution.
3.  **Slight overstatement of novelty:** The core concept of a hard, monotonic attention or a "pointer" that moves sequentially through the input has precedents in the literature, such as the RNN Transducer (Graves, 2012). The key innovation here is less the mechanism itself and more the specific simplification of decoupling the alignment and training processes. The contribution is more about an effective engineering approach and adaptation of existing ideas than a completely novel mechanism. The framing could be more precise about this positioning.

### **Questions to Authors**

1.  Could you elaborate on the sensitivity of your model to the quality of the pre-computed alignments? Have you considered ablations using different alignment models (e.g., a simple Levenshtein-based aligner) to understand how much the performance relies on the specific CRP-based aligner you chose?
2.  Your model's performance on the SIGMORPHON dataset suggests a clear limitation for non-concatenative morphology. Do you have thoughts on how the architecture could be extended to handle such cases? For instance, would adding actions like `step-back` or allowing larger jumps be feasible, and how do you think that would affect the model's data efficiency and simplicity, which are its key advantages?
3.  In footnote 1, you correctly note that optimizing for the best derivation (action sequence) is not the same as optimizing for the best output sequence. During inference, do you use a simple greedy decoding of the action sequence? Have you experimented with beam search on the action sequence, and does it allow the model to recover from local errors or find better output strings by exploring multiple derivations?
