### Summary of the Paper
This paper addresses the task of morphological inflection generation. The authors observe that standard neural sequence-to-sequence models with soft attention tend to overfit on small datasets, performing worse than established non-neural models like latent-variable weighted finite-state transducers (WFSTs). To remedy this, they propose a "hard monotonic attention" model. The core idea is to leverage the predominantly monotonic nature of morphological transformations. Instead of learning a soft alignment distribution, their decoder predicts a sequence of actions from an augmented vocabulary: either `WRITE(symbol)` or `STEP` (to advance the read pointer over the encoded input). A key methodological choice is to decouple the alignment and transduction steps; the model is not trained end-to-end to discover alignments. Instead, character-level alignments are generated beforehand on the training data using an existing statistical aligner. These pre-computed alignments are then used to create an "oracle" sequence of `WRITE`/`STEP` actions, which the encoder-decoder network is trained to predict via a standard cross-entropy loss. The authors evaluate their model on three standard datasets (CELEX, Wiktionary, SIGMORPHON), demonstrating state-of-the-art performance, particularly in the low-resource setting of the CELEX dataset.

### Main Contributions
1.  **Strong Empirical Validation of a Simplified Attention Mechanism:** The primary contribution is the demonstration that a simple, explicit hard monotonic attention mechanism can outperform more complex soft-attention models on morphological inflection, especially in low-data regimes. The model is the first neural approach to surpass the strong latent-variable WFST baseline of Dreyer et al. (2008) on the challenging CELEX dataset, a significant empirical result.
2.  **An Effective Method Decoupling Alignment and Transduction:** The paper presents a method that successfully leverages pre-computed alignments to guide a neural transducer. While not a fully end-to-end approach, this design choice injects a strong and appropriate inductive bias into the model, simplifying the learning problem and leading to better generalization on small datasets.

### Strengths
1.  **Excellent Results on a Low-Resource Benchmark:** The standout result is the model's performance on the CELEX dataset. Previous neural models, including those based on soft attention, have struggled to match, let alone exceed, the performance of the highly specialized latent-variable WFST model from 2008. By outperforming this baseline, the authors provide compelling evidence for their approach's effectiveness in preventing overfitting and learning effectively from sparse data. This is a rigorous and meaningful comparison against an established, non-trivial baseline.
2.  **Methodological Simplicity and Soundness:** The proposed model is conceptually straightforward. By pre-computing alignments, the model avoids the difficult and potentially noisy process of jointly learning to align and transduce. This simplifies the training objective to a standard sequence prediction task with a fixed target, which is more stable and less prone to optimization issues. The strong assumption of monotonicity is well-justified for many of the languages tested and serves as a powerful inductive bias.
3.  **Thorough Evaluation and Analysis:** The authors validate their model across three distinct datasets with varying sizes and linguistic characteristics. The inclusion of Wiktionary (large data) and SIGMORPHON (diverse languages) demonstrates the model's robustness and also clearly delineates its limitations. The analysis in Section 5, comparing the learned representations and alignments of the hard and soft attention models, is insightful and provides a plausible explanation for the performance differences, particularly regarding how positional information is encoded.

### Weaknesses
1.  **Dependence on an External Aligner:** The model's success is critically dependent on the quality of the pre-computed alignments from an external tool (Sudoh et al., 2013). This breaks the end-to-end learning paradigm that is often a key advantage of neural sequence models. The paper does not analyze the model's sensitivity to the quality of this alignment step. If the aligner performs poorly on a given language pair or phenomenon, the model will be trained on a flawed oracle, with no mechanism to recover. This reliance on a separate, fixed component makes the overall system less self-contained and potentially brittle.
2.  **Limited Novelty of the Mechanism:** The concept of hard attention or pointer-based decoding is not new in itself, and the model can be viewed as a transition-based system with `WRITE` and `ADVANCE` actions. The main novelty lies in using an external aligner to create the oracle for training this system. While effective, this feels more like a clever engineering choice than a fundamental modeling advance. The paper frames this as a new model, but its contribution is arguably more in the training pipeline and the specific application to morphology.
3.  **Inherent Limitation to Monotonic Phenomena:** The model's design strictly enforces monotonicity. While the authors are transparent about this, it is a significant limitation. The results on the SIGMORPHON dataset confirm that the model falters relative to soft-attention baselines on languages with non-concatenative morphology (e.g., Arabic) or long-distance phonological dependencies (e.g., vowel harmony in Finnish, Turkish, Hungarian). A standard soft-attention model is more general and can, in principle, learn such dependencies. The proposed method excels at a specific sub-problem but lacks the flexibility of established approaches.

### Questions to Authors
1.  How sensitive is the model's performance to the quality of the alignments provided by the external tool? Have you experimented with other alignment methods or analyzed the impact of alignment errors on final model accuracy? This seems crucial for understanding the robustness of the proposed pipeline.
2.  The model's architecture is strictly monotonic. Could the proposed framework be extended to handle non-monotonic phenomena, for instance by introducing actions like `STEP_BACKWARD` or `JUMP_TO_INDEX`? Or would such an extension make the problem of inferring an oracle sequence from alignments intractable?
3.  The decision to decouple alignment and transduction appears key to the model's success on small datasets. Could you comment further on this trade-off? Is the performance gain worth sacrificing the elegance and potential power of end-to-end joint learning, which might be superior if sufficient data were available?
