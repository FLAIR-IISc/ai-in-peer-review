### Summary of the Paper

This paper addresses the task of morphological inflection generation, where recent neural sequence-to-sequence models have shown strong performance but struggle in low-data settings. The authors propose a novel encoder-decoder model with a "hard monotonic attention" mechanism. Instead of computing a weighted sum over all input states like in soft attention, this model's decoder maintains a single pointer to the encoded input sequence. At each timestep, it makes a hard decision to either `WRITE` an output character or `STEP` the pointer to the next input character. This architecture explicitly models the nearly monotonic alignment inherent in many morphological processes. A key aspect of their approach is that they decouple alignment from transduction; they pre-compute character alignments on the training data using an external tool and use these to generate a "gold" sequence of `WRITE`/`STEP` actions for training the network via a simple cross-entropy loss. Experiments on three datasets show that this model sets a new state-of-the-art on the low-resource CELEX dataset, outperforming specialized non-neural models, and remains highly competitive on larger datasets like Wiktionary and SIGMORPHON 2016.

### Contributions

1.  **A Novel Hard Monotonic Attention Architecture:** The primary contribution is the simple yet highly effective architecture based on explicit `STEP` and `WRITE` actions. This provides a strong, interpretable inductive bias for sequence transduction problems that are known to be largely monotonic, moving away from the more complex and less constrained soft-attention paradigm.
2.  **A Pragmatic Decoupled Training Strategy:** The paper makes a bold and compelling case for decoupling alignment and transduction. By using pre-computed alignments to generate a gold action sequence, the authors simplify the learning problem immensely, enabling straightforward and stable training. This challenges the prevailing assumption that end-to-end joint learning is always superior and shows a path for effectively incorporating prior knowledge into neural models.
3.  **State-of-the-Art Performance in a Low-Resource Setting:** The work provides a significant empirical contribution by being the first neural model to outperform the strong, domain-specific latent-variable WFST model on the challenging, low-data CELEX benchmark. This directly addresses the key motivation of the paper and demonstrates the data efficiency of their proposed method.

### Strengths

1.  **Conceptual Novelty and Elegance:** The proposed model is a refreshing and bold departure from the standard soft-attention framework. The hard attention mechanism with explicit `STEP`/`WRITE` operations is intuitive, simple, and directly captures the underlying structure of the morphological inflection task. This kind of strong, well-motivated inductive bias is precisely what is needed for models to succeed in data-scarce environments.
2.  **Compelling Empirical Results, Especially on Low Data:** The results on the CELEX dataset are the highlight of the paper. The authors clearly demonstrate that their model's structural assumptions prevent the kind of overfitting that plagues soft-attention models in this regime (as evidenced by Figure 2). Surpassing a highly specialized non-neural baseline like the LAT model is a significant achievement and convincingly validates the entire approach. The strong performance on larger datasets further shows that the model is robust and not limited to only small-scale problems.
3.  **Insightful Analysis of Model Behavior:** The analysis in Section 5 is excellent and provides deep insights into the functional differences between the hard and soft attention models. The visualization of encoder representations is particularly illuminating, suggesting that the hard-attention model can learn more "pure" character representations because it offloads positional reasoning to the explicit `STEP` mechanism. This contrasts with the soft-attention model, which appears to confound character and positional information in its representations to facilitate the attention calculation. This analysis strengthens the paper's claims and provides valuable lessons for the community.
4.  **Simplicity and Practicality:** By leveraging an external aligner, the authors avoid the complex and often unstable training procedures (e.g., reinforcement learning or marginalizing over all alignments) required by other hard attention or transduction models. The resulting training regimen is a simple cross-entropy minimization, making the model easy to implement, debug, and train, which is a significant practical advantage.

### Weaknesses

1.  **Dependence on an External Aligner:** The model's success is predicated on the availability of a high-quality external character aligner. The entire training process relies on the "gold" action sequences derived from these alignments. This raises questions about the model's robustness to alignment errors and its applicability to tasks or language pairs where good aligners are not available. While the decoupled approach is shown to be effective here, it sidesteps the challenge of learning task-specific alignments, which is a key promise of end-to-end models.
2.  **Limited Scope of Applicability:** The model is explicitly designed for *nearly-monotonic* tasks. As the authors' own results on the SIGMORPHON dataset show, its performance advantage diminishes and even reverses for languages with significant non-monotonic phenomena (e.g., templatic morphology, circumfixing, or long-distance vowel harmony). While this is an expected and understandable limitation, it means the model is a specialized tool rather than a general-purpose sequence-to-sequence solution. This should be clearly framed as a conscious design trade-off.
3.  **Inference as an Approximation:** As noted in a footnote, the inference procedure finds the most likely sequence of *actions*, which is not guaranteed to produce the most likely *output string*. For a task with a very small output vocabulary like this one, the distinction may be minor. However, it is an approximation, and it would be useful to understand its potential impact. For instance, an analysis of how often different high-probability action sequences yield the same output string could provide confidence that this approximation is not problematic.

### Questions to Authors

1.  Could you comment on the sensitivity of your model to the quality of the external aligner? For instance, have you experimented with a simpler aligner (e.g., one based on edit distance) or analyzed the impact of a certain percentage of incorrect alignments on the final model performance? This would help clarify how critical the aligner's quality is to the model's success.
