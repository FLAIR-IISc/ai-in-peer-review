### Summary and Contributions

This paper introduces a novel hard monotonic attention model for morphological inflection generation. The core idea is to leverage the predominantly monotonic nature of this task by framing it as learning a sequence of `WRITE(symbol)` and `STEP` actions. The `STEP` action advances a read-head monotonically over the encoded source word. A key aspect of the methodology is the decoupling of alignment and transduction: character alignments are pre-computed using an existing statistical aligner, and these alignments are used to create a "gold" sequence of actions for training. The model is trained with a standard cross-entropy loss to predict this action sequence.

As I see them, the main contributions are:

1.  **A simple and highly effective hard attention architecture for nearly-monotonic sequence transduction.** The formulation of the problem as a series of `WRITE` and `STEP` actions, guided by pre-computed alignments, is an elegant and pragmatic approach that simplifies the training objective significantly compared to models that jointly learn alignments or marginalize over them (e.g., RNN-T).

2.  **State-of-the-art empirical results, particularly in low-resource settings.** The model is shown to be highly data-efficient, becoming the first neural model to outperform the specialized latent-variable WFST of Dreyer et al. (2008) on the small CELEX dataset. It also achieves SOTA results on the larger Wiktionary and typologically diverse SIGMORPHON datasets.

3.  **Insightful analysis into the behavior of hard vs. soft attention.** The paper provides a compelling analysis comparing the learned representations and alignments of the two models. The visualization and SVD analysis suggest that the explicit `STEP` mechanism allows the hard attention model to represent position more directly, freeing up the encoder to focus on character identity, which may explain its superior sample efficiency.

### Strengths

1.  **Elegant Model Design and Strong Motivation:** The model's design is well-motivated by the near-monotonic nature of morphological inflection. The `WRITE`/`STEP` action space is a clever and simple way to enforce this structural prior. Decoupling the alignment from the main training loop is a key strength; it provides a strong, unambiguous supervisory signal from the beginning of training, which likely explains the model's excellent performance on small datasets and its ability to avoid the overfitting seen in the soft attention baseline (Figure 2).

2.  **Comprehensive and Rigorous Evaluation:** The authors evaluate their model across three distinct and standard benchmarks, covering low-resource (CELEX), high-resource (Wiktionary), and typologically diverse (SIGMORPHON) scenarios. This thorough evaluation demonstrates the model's robustness and clearly delineates its strengths (e.g., suffixing languages) and relative weaknesses (e.g., languages with long-range dependencies like vowel harmony). The baselines chosen are strong and represent the state-of-the-art at the time, making the reported gains significant.

3.  **High-Quality Analysis:** Section 5 is a standout component of the paper. It moves beyond simply reporting accuracy scores to provide a deeper understanding of *why* the model works. The comparison of alignment matrices (Figure 3) and the SVD analysis of encoder states (Figure 4) are excellent. This analysis provides strong evidence for the authors' claims about how the model learns and differs from soft attention, specifically regarding the encoding of positional information. This kind of analysis is too often missing in NLP papers and adds considerable scientific value.

4.  **Clarity and Reproducibility:** The paper is exceptionally well-written. The model is explained clearly, both textually and with an intuitive figure. The experimental setup is detailed, and the authors state that code is available, which is commendable.

### Weaknesses

1.  **Dependence on an External Aligner:** The model's success is predicated on the quality of the alignments generated by the Sudoh et al. (2013) tool. This external component acts as an oracle during training, and its errors are necessarily propagated to the sequence transduction model, which has no mechanism to recover from them. The paper lacks a discussion of the sensitivity of the final performance to the quality of this alignment step. What is the performance of the aligner itself? How would the model perform if a simpler, potentially less accurate, aligner (e.g., one based on Levenshtein distance) were used to generate the training data? This feels like a critical component that is treated as a black box.

2.  **Limited Applicability to Non-Monotonic Tasks:** The model's central assumption of monotonicity, while a strength for this specific task, is also its main limitation. This makes it unsuitable for general sequence-to-sequence tasks with significant reordering, such as machine translation between distant language pairs. While the authors rightly focus on a task where this assumption holds, the paper would benefit from a more explicit acknowledgment of this limitation. The results on the SIGMORPHON dataset, where performance drops for languages with non-concatenative phenomena, already hint at the boundaries of this approach.

3.  **Best-Derivation Approximation:** As noted in Footnote 1, the model is trained to predict the single best derivation (action sequence), rather than marginalizing over all possible derivations that yield the correct output string. This is a greedy approximation that can be suboptimal. For example, a one-to-many alignment (`o` -> `ie`) could potentially be generated by `(STEP, WRITE i, WRITE e)` or `(WRITE i, STEP, WRITE e)`. If the aligner only provides one of these as gold, the model is penalized for predicting another potentially valid path. While this seems to work well in practice, a brief discussion of this modeling choice and its potential implications would strengthen the paper.

### Questions to Authors

1.  Could you provide some insight into the choice of the Sudoh et al. (2013) character aligner? Have you experimented with or considered other alignment methods for generating the oracle action sequences? An ablation study showing how model performance changes with different quality aligners would be very informative regarding the robustness of your approach.

2.  Your analysis shows the model is less effective than soft attention on languages with non-local dependencies like vowel harmony. Do you have thoughts on how the hard attention framework could be extended to better capture such phenomena? For example, would it be feasible to augment the decoder's input with a global context vector (e.g., the final encoder state) in addition to the locally attended input vector, or perhaps to allow for a more flexible set of `STEP` actions?
