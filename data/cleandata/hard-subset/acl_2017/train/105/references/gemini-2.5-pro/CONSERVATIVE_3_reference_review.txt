### Summary of the Paper
This paper proposes a sequence-to-sequence model for morphological inflection generation that uses a "hard monotonic attention" mechanism. The core idea is to replace the standard soft attention distribution over encoder states with a discrete set of actions: either `WRITE` a character to the output or `STEP` to advance a pointer to the next input character. The model is trained on gold action sequences derived from pre-computed character alignments generated by an external tool. The authors evaluate their model on three datasets (CELEX, Wiktionary, SIGMORPHON 2016), claiming state-of-the-art results and arguing that their approach is particularly effective in low-resource settings where standard soft-attention models tend to overfit. An analysis is also provided to compare the internal representations learned by their model versus a soft-attention model.

### Contributions
1.  **Empirical Demonstration of a Pipeline Approach:** The primary contribution is the demonstration that a pipeline approach—first generating character alignments with an established tool, then training a neural model to replicate the transduction—is highly effective for morphological inflection. It achieves strong results, especially on the low-resource CELEX dataset where it surpasses previous specialized models.
2.  **Comparative Analysis:** The paper provides a useful analysis (Figure 4) comparing the encoder representations of the proposed model and a soft-attention baseline. This analysis offers some insight into how the different attention mechanisms may encourage the encoder to learn different features (e.g., positional vs. character-identity information).

### Strengths
1.  **Strong Performance on Low-Resource Data:** The model's performance on the CELEX dataset is a significant strength. As shown in Table 1 and Figure 2, it outperforms both contemporary neural models (MED, NWFST) and the highly-specialized latent-variable FST (LAT) model, which previous neural methods struggled to beat. This provides compelling evidence that explicitly modeling the monotonic nature of the task is beneficial when training data is scarce.
2.  **Thorough Evaluation Across Diverse Datasets:** The authors have responsibly evaluated their method on three distinct datasets that cover different scales (CELEX is very small, Wiktionary is large) and typological variety (SIGMORPHON). This allows for a comprehensive assessment of the model's capabilities and limitations.
3.  **Simple and Intuitive Model:** The proposed `STEP`/`WRITE` mechanism is conceptually simple and directly models the monotonic string-to-string transduction process common in suffixing languages. The training procedure, using a standard cross-entropy loss on a derived action sequence, is straightforward and avoids the complexities of reinforcement learning or marginalizing over alignments.

### Weaknesses
1.  **Limited Novelty and Misleading Framing:** The framing of the model as a "hard attention" mechanism is questionable. The model does not *learn* a hard attention policy; rather, it is trained via supervised learning to mimic an oracle derived from an external aligner. This architecture is better described as a transition-based transducer. The core components (BiLSTM encoder, LSTM decoder) are standard. The novelty is therefore not in a new attention mechanism, but in the engineering choice to decouple alignment and transduction. While effective, this is a significant step back from the end-to-end learning paradigm that makes soft-attention models appealing.
2.  **Critical Dependence on an External Aligner:** The entire system relies on a pre-processing step using the Sudoh et al. (2013) alignment model. The quality of these pre-computed alignments is a critical, yet unexamined, variable. The paper does not discuss how alignment errors might propagate, nor does it analyze the performance of the aligner itself on these datasets. This makes the method less general and robust than end-to-end systems like soft-attention models, which can learn task-specific alignments. The method is essentially a neural implementation of the classic "align-and-transduce" pipeline.
3.  **The Monotonicity Assumption is a Brittleness:** The model is explicitly designed for "nearly monotonic" tasks. The results on the SIGMORPHON dataset (Table 3) reveal this to be a significant limitation. The proposed model is outperformed by the authors' own strong soft-attention baseline on 7 out of the 10 languages. It only shows a clear advantage on suffixing languages (Russian, German, Spanish). On languages with more complex, non-concatenative morphology (e.g., templatic Arabic, agglutinative Finnish/Turkish), the more flexible soft-attention model is superior. This suggests that the proposed model is a specialized tool, whereas established soft-attention methods are more general and robust across different language typologies. The overall average accuracy on SIGMORPHON is notably lower for the "Hard" model than the "Soft" model (95.61 vs 96.34).

### Questions to Authors
1.  Your model's performance is fundamentally tied to the quality of the alignments from Sudoh et al. (2013). Could you provide an analysis of this dependency? For instance, what is the performance of the external aligner on these datasets, and how do alignment errors affect the final transduction accuracy? Have you experimented with other alignment methods to see how sensitive your model is to this choice?
2.  The results in Table 3 show that your model underperforms a standard soft-attention baseline on languages with non-suffixing morphology. Does this not suggest that the core assumption of your model limits its applicability, making it less general than established methods? Given that the soft-attention model performs well even on the "nearly monotonic" languages, what is the practical argument for using your more specialized model?
3.  Could you clarify the justification for calling this a "hard attention" model? In the literature, hard attention typically involves a model making a stochastic or reinforced choice of a single input to attend to. Your model, however, is trained to deterministically reproduce an action sequence from a fixed, externally-provided alignment. Wouldn't it be more accurate to describe it as a transition-based system that learns a transduction policy from an oracle?
