### Summary of the submission
This paper introduces a novel sequence-to-sequence model for morphological inflection generation, termed the "Hard Monotonic Attention" model. The core idea is to replace the standard soft attention mechanism with a simpler, more constrained mechanism tailored for tasks with nearly monotonic alignments between input and output sequences. The decoder operates via a sequence of actions: either `WRITE` an output character or `STEP` an attention pointer forward along the encoded input sequence. A key aspect of the methodology is decoupling alignment from transduction during training. An external character-level alignment model is used to pre-process the training data, generating a "gold" sequence of actions for each example. The model is then trained with a standard cross-entropy loss to predict this action sequence.

The main contributions as I see them are:
1.  A simple and effective hard attention architecture that explicitly models the monotonic nature of many morphological transductions, providing a strong inductive bias that is particularly beneficial in low-data regimes.
2.  Strong empirical results establishing a new state-of-the-art on several standard benchmarks, most notably being the first neural model to outperform a highly specialized latent-variable WFST model on the small CELEX dataset.
3.  An insightful comparative analysis with soft attention models that sheds light on how different architectures encode information like character position, providing a compelling explanation for the proposed model's data efficiency.

### Strengths
1.  **Excellent performance in a low-resource setting.** The most significant result is the model's performance on the CELEX dataset. It is a well-known issue that standard sequence-to-sequence models with soft attention can overfit on small datasets. This paper provides compelling evidence (Table 1 and Figure 2) that its more constrained architecture mitigates this problem, outperforming both other neural models and the strong, task-specific LAT model of Dreyer et al. (2008). This is a valuable contribution, as many morphological tasks for real-world languages are low-resource.

2.  **Methodological elegance and simplicity.** The proposed mechanism is conceptually simple yet powerful. By framing the problem as predicting a sequence of `WRITE` and `STEP` actions, the authors inject a strong and appropriate inductive bias for this task. The decision to use pre-computed alignments to generate an oracle for training is a pragmatic and effective choice. It simplifies the training objective to a straightforward classification problem, avoiding the need to marginalize over latent alignments and likely contributing to the model's stability and data efficiency.

3.  **Thorough and convincing evaluation.** The authors evaluate their model on three distinct and widely-used datasets: CELEX (low-resource), Wiktionary (high-resource), and SIGMORPHON 2016 (typologically diverse). This comprehensive evaluation demonstrates the model's robustness and clarifies its strengths and weaknesses across different conditions. The choice of baselines is excellent, including state-of-the-art neural and non-neural systems, which situates the work firmly within the existing literature and validates the significance of the results.

4.  **Insightful analysis of learned representations.** The analysis in Section 5 is a major strength. The visualization of alignments in Figure 3 confirms the hypothesis that monotonic alignment is a valid assumption for these tasks, even for soft attention models. More impressively, the SVD analysis in Figure 4 provides a clear and intuitive explanation for the model's data efficiency. The finding that the soft attention model appears to bake positional information into its character encodings, while the hard attention model can rely on the explicit `STEP` mechanism, is a valuable insight into the inner workings of these models.

### Weaknesses
1.  **Dependence on an external alignment tool.** The model's performance is contingent on the quality of the alignments generated by the Sudoh et al. (2013) tool. While this decouples the problem nicely, it also means the model is unable to learn alignments that the external tool cannot produce. The paper does not discuss the potential impact of alignment errors or the model's sensitivity to the choice of aligner. If the aligner performs poorly on a specific language or morphological phenomenon (e.g., metathesis), the model's ceiling is fundamentally limited by this external component.

2.  **Limited applicability to non-monotonic phenomena.** The model is explicitly designed for "nearly-monotonic" transductions. While highly effective for suffixing and stem changes, it is inherently unsuited for non-concatenative morphology, such as templatic (e.g., Arabic) or infixing patterns. The results on the SIGMORPHON dataset (Table 3) reflect this, where the model excels on suffixing languages but is outperformed by the more flexible soft attention model on languages with more complex phenomena. While the authors acknowledge this, it remains a significant limitation to the model's general applicability in computational morphology. The claim of handling "non-monotone relations" via the bi-directional encoder seems overstated, as the decoder's access to the input is strictly monotonic.

3.  **The "best-derivation" approximation.** As noted in Footnote 1, the model is trained to predict a single, deterministically-derived action sequence for each training example. This is a "best-derivation" approach that ignores the possibility of multiple valid alignments (and thus multiple valid action sequences) producing the same output string. This could potentially make the model brittle by over-penalizing valid alternative derivations during training. While marginalizing over all derivations is computationally difficult, the implications of this simplifying assumption are not discussed.

### Questions to Authors
1.  Could you comment on the sensitivity of your model to the quality of the pre-computed alignments? Have you experimented with other alignment tools or analyzed the effect of alignment errors on final model performance? How does the model handle cases where the aligner produces a clearly incorrect alignment?

2.  The model's action set (`WRITE`, `STEP`) is minimal and enforces monotonicity. Have you considered extending this set to handle a wider range of morphological processes? For example, adding actions like `STEP_BACKWARD` or `COPY_FROM_INPUT` could potentially handle some non-monotonic or templatic phenomena, at the cost of increased model complexity.

3.  The analysis of the learned representations is very interesting. Does the decoder state itself learn to count the number of consecutive `STEP` actions to model deletions or insertions of different lengths, or is this behavior purely emergent from the sequence of LSTM states?
