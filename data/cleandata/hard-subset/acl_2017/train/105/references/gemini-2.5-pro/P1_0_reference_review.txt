### Summary of the Paper and Contributions

This paper introduces a sequence-to-sequence model with a "hard monotonic attention" mechanism for morphological inflection generation. Unlike standard soft attention models that compute a weighted average over all input states, this model uses an explicit pointer to the input sequence. At each decoding step, the model decides between two actions: `write` an output character or `step` the pointer to the next input character. The model is trained on gold action sequences derived from pre-computed character alignments, simplifying the training process to a standard cross-entropy loss. The authors evaluate their model on three datasets (CELEX, Wiktionary, SIGMORPHON) and show it achieves state-of-the-art performance, particularly in low-data settings.

From my perspective, the main contributions are:

1.  **An effective and simple model for monotonic sequence transduction:** The primary contribution is the "hard attention" model itself. It introduces a strong and appropriate inductive bias (monotonicity) for morphology, which proves highly effective. The design choice to decouple alignment from transduction by using an external aligner to create a gold training sequence is a key aspect, simplifying the model's learning task and making it more data-efficient.
2.  **Strong empirical results establishing a new state-of-the-art, especially in a low-resource setting:** The paper demonstrates impressive performance across three diverse datasets. Crucially, it is the first neural model shown to outperform the specialized latent-variable WFST model on the small CELEX dataset, directly addressing a known weakness of previous neural approaches.
3.  **Insightful analysis comparing hard and soft attention:** The analysis section provides valuable insights into the behavior of both attention mechanisms for this task. It visually demonstrates that soft attention learns to be monotonic, validating the authors' core assumption. Furthermore, the SVD analysis of learned representations compellingly suggests *why* the hard attention model is more data-efficient: it offloads positional information to the explicit `step` action, allowing the encoder to focus more on character identity.

### Strengths

1.  **Excellent performance in a low-data setting:** The paper's strongest argument is its performance on the CELEX dataset. Previous neural models, including those with soft attention, have struggled to outperform non-neural, specialized systems like the LAT FST model of Dreyer et al. (2008) on this small dataset. This paper's model surpasses it, as shown in Table 1. The learning curve in Figure 2 provides clear evidence that the hard attention model is less prone to overfitting than its soft attention counterpart, validating the authors' initial motivation. This is a significant result for applying neural models to low-resource languages or domains.
2.  **Well-motivated model design with a strong inductive bias:** The model is conceptually simple and elegant. The assumption that morphological inflection is a "nearly-monotonic" process is well-founded for a large number of languages, particularly those with suffixing morphology. By building this assumption directly into the architecture (via the `step` action), the model constrains the search space, leading to better generalization from limited data. The decoupling of alignment and transduction is a pragmatic choice that pays off, as it allows the model to leverage a high-quality alignment signal from the very beginning of training.
3.  **Comprehensive and honest evaluation:** The authors evaluate their model on three different datasets that vary in size and linguistic diversity. This demonstrates the model's robustness. The results on Wiktionary show it is competitive even with large training sets, while the SIGMORPHON results are particularly insightful. The authors honestly report that their model is slightly outperformed by a soft attention model on languages with more complex, non-concatenative morphology (e.g., templatic Arabic, vowel harmony in Finnish/Hungarian). This transparent analysis clearly defines the scope and limitations of the proposed method, which strengthens the paper.
4.  **High-quality analysis of model internals:** The analysis in Section 5 is a major strength. The visualization of alignments in Figure 3 not only supports the monotonic assumption but also highlights a case where the hard attention model produces a more linguistically plausible alignment than the soft attention one. The SVD visualization of encoder states (Figure 4) is particularly compelling, offering a clear hypothesis for the model's data efficiency by showing how it separates positional and character-identity information differently from the soft attention model.

### Weaknesses

1.  **Dependence on an external character aligner:** The model's success is predicated on the availability of a good character-level aligner to generate the gold `step`/`write` sequences for training. This introduces an extra dependency and a potential point of failure. The performance of the model is likely upper-bounded by the quality of this external tool. The paper does not investigate the model's sensitivity to alignment errors or how it would perform with a lower-quality aligner. This two-stage pipeline is less elegant than an end-to-end model that learns alignments jointly, even if it is more effective in this case.
2.  **Limited applicability to non-monotonic morphological processes:** The model's primary strength—its monotonic inductive bias—is also its main weakness. It is inherently unsuited for non-monotonic phenomena such as infixation, circumfixation, or complex reduplication without modification. While the paper acknowledges this implicitly through the SIGMORPHON results, it could be stated more explicitly as a limitation. For instance, it's not clear how the model would generate a circumfix `ge-...-t` in German past participles if the aligner maps the lemma to the stem in between. This limits its generality as a universal morphological generator.
3.  **Framing of novelty regarding hard attention:** While the application and specific instantiation are novel and effective, the core concept of a "hard" attention or a monotonic pointer has appeared in other sequence transduction literature (e.g., RNN Transducers for speech recognition). The paper does a good job of citing related work, but the contribution could be more precisely framed as a very successful adaptation and simplification of this idea for character-level transduction, rather than the invention of hard attention itself. The key innovation here seems to be the combination of the pre-aligned oracle with a simple `step`/`write` action space.

### Questions to Authors

1.  How robust is your model to the quality of the pre-computed alignments? Have you analyzed the impact of alignment errors from the Sudoh et al. (2013) tool on your model's training and performance? Would it be possible to train the model effectively using alignments from a simpler, less accurate source (e.g., simple edit distance)?
2.  The model is explicitly designed for "nearly-monotonic" tasks. Could you elaborate on how it handles phenomena that are partially non-monotonic, like circumfixation? Does the deterministic procedure for converting alignments to `step`/`write` sequences have a consistent way of handling such cases, and if so, how does the model learn from it?
3.  In Section 2.1, you note that you optimize for the best derivation (`s'`) rather than marginalizing over all derivations for a given output sequence (`y'`). Is this "best-derivation" approximation a significant factor? In your error analysis, did you find cases where the model produced a high-probability derivation for an incorrect output, while a lower-probability derivation would have produced the correct one?
