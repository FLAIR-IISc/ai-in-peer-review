### Summary of the Paper
This paper proposes a novel encoder-decoder model with a "hard monotonic attention" mechanism for morphological inflection generation. The core idea is to reframe the sequence transduction problem as learning a sequence of actions: either `WRITE` a character to the output or `STEP` the attention pointer to the next character in the encoded input. This design explicitly models the predominantly monotonic alignment between source and target words in this task. The encoder is a standard Bi-LSTM. The decoder, at each step, predicts an action from an augmented vocabulary (output characters + `STEP`). A key aspect of the training process is that it is guided by an oracle sequence of actions derived from pre-computed character-level alignments, thus decoupling the learning of alignment from the learning of transduction.

The authors evaluate their model on three standard datasets (CELEX, Wiktionary, SIGMORPHON2016) and show that it achieves state-of-the-art results. Notably, it is the first neural model to outperform a strong latent-variable FST baseline on the small CELEX dataset, demonstrating its data efficiency. The paper concludes with an insightful analysis comparing the learned alignments and representations of their hard attention model against a standard soft attention baseline, suggesting that their model's explicit handling of position via the `STEP` action allows its representations to focus more on character identity.

### Contributions
As I see them, the main contributions of this work are:

1.  **A Simple and Effective Model for Monotonic Transduction:** The primary contribution is the hard attention model itself. The `WRITE`/`STEP` action space is an elegant way to enforce monotonicity, which is a strong and appropriate inductive bias for this specific task. The design choice to decouple alignment from transduction by using a pre-trained aligner to generate a training oracle simplifies the learning problem to a standard cross-entropy loss, avoiding more complex training procedures like marginalizing over all possible alignments.

2.  **Strong Empirical Results Demonstrating Data Efficiency:** The paper provides compelling evidence that this simplified model is not only competitive but often superior to more complex soft attention models, especially in low-resource settings. The results on the CELEX dataset are particularly significant, as they address a known weakness of neural models (overfitting on small data) and surpass a strong, task-specific non-neural baseline for the first time.

3.  **An Illuminating Comparative Analysis:** The analysis in Section 5 provides valuable insights into the behavior of both hard and soft attention models for this task. The visualization of learned alignments and the SVD projection of character embeddings effectively argue *why* the hard attention model performs well: by offloading positional information to the explicit `STEP` action, the encoder can learn more distinct, position-invariant character representations, which is beneficial for generalization.

### Strengths

1.  **Excellent Performance in Low-Resource Scenarios:** The paper's strongest argument is the model's performance on the CELEX dataset (Table 1). It significantly outperforms previous neural models (MED, NWFST) and, crucially, the highly-tuned latent-variable FST model (LAT). The learning curve in Figure 2 provides clear visual evidence supporting the claim that the hard attention model is less prone to overfitting than the soft attention model, making it a very practical choice for morphological tasks where training data is often scarce.

2.  **Model Simplicity and Soundness of Design:** The proposed architecture is conceptually simple and well-motivated. The assumption of near-monotonicity in morphological inflection is valid for a large class of languages. By building this assumption directly into the model's action space, the authors introduce a powerful inductive bias. Decoupling the training of the aligner and the transducer is a pragmatic choice that simplifies the model's objective function and appears to be highly effective in practice.

3.  **Thorough and Robust Evaluation:** The authors evaluate their model across three different datasets that vary in size and linguistic diversity (CELEX, Wiktionary, SIGMORPHON). The fact that the model performs at a state-of-the-art level on the large Wiktionary dataset shows that its benefits are not limited to small data settings. Furthermore, the honest reporting of results on the SIGMORPHON dataset, where the model excels on suffixing languages but is slightly weaker on those with non-local dependencies, helps to clearly define the scope and limitations of the approach.

4.  **High-Quality Analysis:** The analysis in Section 5 is a major strength. It moves beyond simply reporting accuracy scores to provide a deeper understanding of the model's internal workings. The comparison of alignments (Figure 3) and encoder representations (Figure 4) offers a plausible and well-illustrated explanation for the model's superior data efficiency and sheds light on what different attention mechanisms learn for this task.

### Weaknesses

1.  **Dependency on an External Aligner:** The model's performance is critically dependent on the quality of the alignments used to generate the oracle during training. The paper uses a single, sophisticated alignment model (Sudoh et al., 2013) but does not investigate the model's sensitivity to the choice or quality of this aligner. It is unclear how the model would perform if the oracle were generated by a simpler or noisier method (e.g., Levenshtein alignment). This dependency makes the model a two-stage pipeline, and the quality of the first stage is an unexamined variable.

2.  **Inherent Limitation to Monotonic Phenomena:** The model's core strength—its monotonic assumption—is also its primary limitation. As the authors acknowledge in their SIGMORPHON results analysis, it is less suited for languages with significant non-local morphological processes like vowel/consonant harmony, circumfixing, or templatic morphology (as seen in the slightly lower performance on Finnish, Hungarian, Turkish, Arabic, etc., compared to their soft attention implementation). While highly effective for its target domain (suffixing/prefixing morphology), it is not a general-purpose solution for all morphological typology.

3.  **Novelty of the Core Mechanism is Tempered by Prior Work:** While the specific encoder-decoder implementation is novel, the concept of a hard, monotonic "attention" or alignment for sequence transduction has been explored before. For example, the RNN Transducer (Graves, 2012) and related models also operate over monotonic alignments and use a "blank" or null symbol emission that functions similarly to the `STEP` action. The authors correctly differentiate their work by noting the dependency between the encoder and decoder and the simpler training objective, but the conceptual leap is less significant than presented. The main novelty lies in the effective application and simplification of this idea within a modern encoder-decoder framework for this specific task.

### Questions to Authors

1.  Could you elaborate on the model's sensitivity to the quality of the pre-computed alignments? How does performance change if a simpler alignment method, such as one based on edit distance, is used to generate the oracle `WRITE`/`STEP` sequences for training?

2.  The paper notes in footnote 1 that the model optimizes for the best derivation (action sequence) rather than the best output string, which is an approximation. Have you analyzed the practical impact of this? For instance, during beam search, how often do different high-scoring action sequences lead to the same output string? Does this suggest any redundancy in the action space that could be addressed?

3.  Given the model's limitations with non-local phenomena like vowel harmony, do you have any thoughts on how the hard attention framework could be extended to handle such cases? Would it be possible to introduce actions like `JUMP` or to condition the `WRITE` action on a broader, non-local context from the input, while still retaining the model's core simplicity and data efficiency?
