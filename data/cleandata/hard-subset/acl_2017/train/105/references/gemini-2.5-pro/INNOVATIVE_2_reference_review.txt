### Summary and Contributions

This paper introduces a novel encoder-decoder architecture with a "hard monotonic attention" mechanism for the task of morphological inflection generation. Instead of using a soft attention mechanism that computes a weighted average over all input states, this model maintains a single pointer to the current input character. The decoder's action space is augmented with a special `step` action, which advances this pointer, and `write(symbol)` actions, which produce output characters. The core idea is that this architecture explicitly models the predominantly monotonic nature of morphological transformations. A key aspect of the method is its training procedure: instead of learning alignments jointly, the authors use an external character-level aligner to pre-compute alignments for the training set. These alignments are then used to deterministically generate a "gold" sequence of `step` and `write` actions, which the neural network is trained to predict via a standard cross-entropy loss. The model is evaluated on three datasets of varying sizes and complexities, achieving state-of-the-art results, most notably in a very low-resource setting.

From my perspective, the primary contributions of this work are:

1.  **A novel and conceptually elegant hard attention mechanism.** The introduction of an explicit `step` action is a bold departure from the dominant soft attention paradigm. It provides a simple, interpretable, and powerful mechanism for modeling nearly monotonic sequence-to-sequence tasks. This represents a significant new direction for attention-based models in structured domains.
2.  **A compelling demonstration of the benefits of decoupling alignment and transduction.** The authors challenge the prevailing "end-to-end-is-best" dogma by showing that injecting strong structural priors via an external aligner can dramatically simplify the learning problem and lead to superior performance, especially when data is scarce. This pragmatic approach re-validates pipeline-style thinking within a modern neural framework.
3.  **Strong empirical validation in a critical low-data regime.** The paper's most impressive result is its performance on the CELEX dataset, where it is the first neural model to surpass a highly specialized latent-variable WFST model. This directly addresses a major weakness of standard data-hungry neural sequence models.

### Strengths

1.  **High Novelty and Boldness of Approach:** The core idea of this paper is refreshingly innovative. Moving from a complex, fully-differentiable soft attention mechanism to a discrete, explicit pointer-based system is a non-obvious and courageous step. The `step/write` formulation is intuitive and provides a much clearer inductive bias for the task than a generic seq2seq model. This is precisely the kind of work that opens up new avenues for research, rather than offering a minor refinement of existing methods.

2.  **Exceptional Performance in Low-Resource Setting:** The results on the CELEX dataset are a standout achievement. It is well-known that standard seq2seq models with soft attention tend to overfit and perform poorly with only a few hundred training examples. The fact that this model not only works but sets a new state of the art in this setting is a powerful argument for its design. It demonstrates that with the right architecture and structural assumptions, neural models can be made highly data-efficient.

3.  **Insightful Analysis and Explanation:** The SVD analysis comparing the learned representations of the hard and soft attention models is excellent. It provides a clear and compelling hypothesis for the model's success: by handling positional information explicitly through the `step` action, the model frees up its encoder's representational capacity to focus on character identity. This analysis elevates the paper beyond a simple presentation of results to a deeper investigation of *why* the proposed model works.

4.  **Simplicity and Efficiency:** The proposed model is conceptually simpler than soft attention. Decoupling the alignment allows for a straightforward training regime using cross-entropy loss, avoiding more complex training procedures like marginalizing over all possible alignments. This simplicity is a strength, making the model easier to understand, implement, and potentially adapt to other tasks.

### Weaknesses

While I am very enthusiastic about this paper, I will note a few limitations for the sake of a balanced review. However, in my view, these do not detract significantly from the value of its contributions.

1.  **Limited Generality by Design:** The model's primary strength—its strong monotonic inductive bias—is also its main limitation. It is explicitly tailored for nearly monotonic tasks. As the authors' own results on the SIGMORPHON dataset suggest, its advantage diminishes for languages with more complex, non-local phenomena like vowel harmony or templatic morphology, where soft attention's ability to draw information from the entire source sequence is more beneficial. While this is an acceptable trade-off for a specialized model, it does constrain the approach's applicability.

2.  **Dependence on External Aligner Quality:** The model's performance is fundamentally tied to the quality of the alignments produced by the external tool. The paper does not investigate how sensitive the model is to alignment errors or how it would perform with a different, perhaps lower-quality, aligner. This dependency makes the system less self-contained and raises questions about its robustness in scenarios where a high-quality aligner is not readily available.

3.  **Potential Training/Inference Mismatch (Exposure Bias):** The model is trained on a "gold" sequence of actions derived from a perfect alignment, a form of teacher forcing. At inference, it must rely on its own predictions to decide the next action. This introduces exposure bias, as the model is never trained on the states it would reach after making a mistake. While the strong results suggest this is not a crippling issue, it is a known problem in sequence generation that is not addressed.

### Questions to Authors

1.  Could you comment on the model's sensitivity to the quality of the external aligner? For example, have you experimented with a simpler heuristic aligner (e.g., based on Levenshtein distance) to see how gracefully performance degrades? This would help in understanding the model's practical requirements.
2.  The paper states that one-to-many mappings (e.g., `o` -> `ie`) are handled by consecutive `write` operations. Could you clarify how the deterministic process for generating the gold action sequence handles this? Does the external aligner produce a one-to-many alignment (`o` -> `i`, `o` -> `e`), and if so, how is the sequence of `step` and `write` actions ordered in such cases?
3.  Given the model's impressive ability to leverage pre-computed alignments, have you considered an iterative, semi-supervised approach? One could imagine using the trained model's own inferred alignments on the training set to refine the initial alignments and then retraining the model, potentially bootstrapping to an even better final performance.
