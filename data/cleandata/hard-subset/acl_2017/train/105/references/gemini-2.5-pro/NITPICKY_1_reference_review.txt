**Summary of the paper and contributions**

This paper introduces a sequence-to-sequence model with a hard, monotonic attention mechanism for morphological inflection generation. The core idea is to re-frame the task as predicting a sequence of actions—either writing an output character or advancing a pointer over the input sequence (`step`). Crucially, the model is not trained to learn alignments jointly. Instead, it relies on an external character alignment tool to generate an oracle sequence of `write`/`step` actions for the training data, which simplifies the training process to a standard cross-entropy minimization problem.

As I see them, the contributions are:
1.  An empirical validation of a decoupled align-and-transduce approach within a neural framework, demonstrating its effectiveness for morphological inflection, especially in low-resource scenarios.
2.  State-of-the-art results on several benchmark datasets. The model is notably the first neural approach to outperform the highly specialized latent-variable WFST model of Dreyer et al. (2008) on the small CELEX dataset.
3.  A comparative analysis against a soft-attention baseline that provides some insight into how the models represent characters and their positions.

**Strengths**

1.  **Excellent performance in the low-resource setting:** The paper's strongest result is on the CELEX dataset. It directly confronts a well-known weakness of neural sequence-to-sequence models (data inefficiency) and presents a compelling solution. Surpassing the LAT model, which incorporates significant task-specific prior knowledge, is a notable achievement and clearly demonstrates the value of the hard monotonic inductive bias.
2.  **Effective model simplification:** The decision to decouple alignment from transduction and pre-compute the oracle action sequence is an elegant simplification. It replaces the complex, and sometimes brittle, learning dynamics of soft attention with a straightforward supervised classification setup. This design choice appears to be the key driver of the model's robustness on small datasets.
3.  **Comprehensive experimental setup:** The evaluation is conducted across three standard datasets that vary in size and linguistic phenomena (CELEX, Wiktionary, SIGMORPHON 2016). This provides a thorough picture of the model's capabilities and limitations. The inclusion of strong neural and non-neural baselines further strengthens the empirical claims.

**Weaknesses**

1.  **Overstated novelty and dependence on an external oracle:** The concept of a hard-attention transducer that monotonically steps through an input is not fundamentally new (cf. RNN Transducer, Graves, 2012). The primary novelty lies in the training methodology, which offloads the difficult task of alignment to an external tool (Sudoh et al., 2013). This makes the entire system a pipeline, with performance critically dependent on the quality of the external aligner. The paper fails to analyze this dependency; it is unclear how the model would perform if the oracle alignments were of lower quality or generated by a different method. This is a significant unexamined variable.
2.  **Lack of technical clarity and precision:** The paper suffers from several points of ambiguity.
    *   **Objective Mismatch:** Footnote 1 correctly identifies that the model optimizes for the most likely derivation (action sequence), not the most likely output string. This is a crucial theoretical distinction with practical implications for decoding. The paper dismisses this as an approximation that is "effective in practice" without any further discussion. It is unclear how beam search is implemented: does it search over action sequences, potentially returning suboptimal strings, or does it correctly aggregate probabilities for identical output strings generated by different action sequences? This lack of detail is concerning.
    *   **Insufficient Model Description:** The "Control Mechanism" is described in a single sentence that implies a greedy selection of the `step` action. The inference procedure is not formally described, leaving the reader to guess the specifics.
    *   **Superficial Analysis:** The explanation for the model's lower performance on languages with non-concatenative morphology (e.g., vowel harmony) is weak. The authors claim soft attention is better at modeling "long range dependencies," but their own bi-directional LSTM encoder should capture this information in the input representations. A more precise explanation would be that the hard attention decoder can only focus on a single input position's representation at a time, while soft attention can create a composite context vector from multiple non-adjacent positions, which is necessary for such phenomena. The current explanation is imprecise.
3.  **Numerous formatting and presentation errors:** The submission is marred by a lack of polish that detracts from its scientific contribution. The references section is particularly problematic, containing numerous formatting errors (e.g., `et al\.,?`, `\Q1993\E`) and a corrupted, incomplete entry at the very end. Footnote 3 is overly verbose and could have been condensed. The labels in Figure 2 are illegibly small. Such carelessness in the preparation of a manuscript for a top-tier conference is unacceptable and raises questions about the level of care taken in the research itself.
4.  **Potentially unfair baseline comparison:** In the SIGMORPHON experiments, the authors' own implementation of a soft-attention model outperforms the prior state-of-the-art (MED). They attribute this to more extensive training (100 vs. 20 epochs). While this may be true, it means they are comparing their proposed model against their own, more carefully tuned baseline. The fact that their hard-attention model is ultimately beaten by this stronger soft-attention baseline on average complicates the paper's narrative and slightly weakens the claims of the hard-attention model's general superiority.

**Questions to Authors**

1.  Could you quantify the model's sensitivity to the quality of the pre-computed alignments? For instance, how does accuracy change if a simpler or noisier aligner is used to generate the oracle for training?
2.  Please clarify the decoding procedure at test time. Specifically, when using beam search, how do you handle the discrepancy between finding the k-best action sequences and finding the k-best output strings, given that multiple action sequences can map to the same string?
3.  Could you provide a more precise explanation for the performance difference between hard and soft attention on languages with non-local morphological phenomena like vowel harmony, beyond the general notion of "long range dependencies"? Does the context vector produced by soft attention explicitly leverage multiple non-adjacent input states in these cases, and if so, can you show evidence of this?
