### Summary of the Paper
This paper proposes a novel encoder-decoder model with a "hard monotonic attention" mechanism for the task of morphological inflection generation. The core idea is to leverage the observation that this task is often a nearly monotonic character-level transduction. Instead of using a standard soft attention mechanism, the model's decoder learns a sequence of actions from an extended vocabulary: either `WRITE` an output character or `STEP` the attention pointer to the next character in the encoded input sequence. A key aspect of the proposed method is that it decouples the alignment and transduction steps during training. The model is trained on "gold" action sequences derived from pre-computed character alignments generated by an external tool. The authors evaluate their model on three standard datasets (CELEX, Wiktionary, and SIGMORPHON 2016), demonstrating that it achieves state-of-the-art results. Notably, it performs exceptionally well in low-resource settings (CELEX), where previous neural models have struggled compared to specialized non-neural approaches. The paper concludes with an insightful analysis comparing the learned alignments and internal representations of their hard attention model against a standard soft attention baseline.

### Contributions
1.  **A Simple and Effective Hard Monotonic Attention Model:** The primary contribution is the model architecture itself, which explicitly models monotonic sequence-to-sequence transduction through a simple `WRITE`/`STEP` action space. The decision to use pre-computed alignments to create an oracle for supervised training simplifies the learning problem significantly, avoiding the complexities of reinforcement learning or marginalization over latent alignments often required for hard attention models.
2.  **State-of-the-Art Results, Especially in a Low-Resource Setting:** The paper provides a strong empirical contribution by demonstrating the model's effectiveness across multiple benchmarks. The most significant result is on the CELEX dataset, where this model is the first neural approach reported to outperform the highly specialized latent-variable FST model of Dreyer et al. (2008). This directly addresses a known weakness of neural models in data-scarce scenarios.
3.  **Insightful Comparative Analysis of Attention Mechanisms:** The analysis section provides a valuable comparison between the proposed hard attention model and a standard soft attention model. Through visualizations, the authors show that soft attention implicitly learns to be monotonic for this task and that it tends to encode character position more strongly in its representations, whereas the hard attention model relies on its explicit `STEP` mechanism for positional awareness. This analysis sheds light on the inner workings of both models in the context of this specific task.

### Strengths
1.  **Excellent Performance in Low-Resource Conditions:** The most compelling strength is the model's performance on the CELEX dataset. As shown in Table 1 and Figure 2, the hard attention model not only achieves a higher average accuracy but also shows much less of a tendency to overfit compared to the soft attention baseline. This data efficiency is a significant advantage for morphological tasks, where large parallel corpora are often unavailable. The explicit structural assumption of monotonicity acts as a powerful regularizer, which is clearly beneficial when training data is limited.
2.  **Model Simplicity and Elegance:** The proposed mechanism is intuitive and elegant. By framing the problem as learning a sequence of `WRITE` and `STEP` operations, the model directly captures the underlying process of monotonic transduction. Decoupling the training from the alignment discovery (by using an external aligner to generate the oracle) is a pragmatic choice that makes the model straightforward to train with a standard cross-entropy loss, bypassing more complex training procedures.
3.  **Thorough and Convincing Evaluation:** The authors evaluate their model on three different datasets that cover a range of conditions: low-resource (CELEX), high-resource (Wiktionary), and typologically diverse (SIGMORPHON). This comprehensive evaluation demonstrates the robustness of the model. Furthermore, the honest reporting of the SIGMORPHON results—where the model excels on suffixing languages but is slightly behind on languages with more complex, non-local phenomena—strengthens the paper by clearly defining the model's application scope and limitations.
4.  **High-Quality Analysis:** The analysis in Section 5 is a major strength. The visualization of alignments in Figure 3 clearly illustrates how the model handles common morphological operations like deletion and substitution. The SVD projection of encoder representations in Figure 4 provides a fascinating insight into what the models learn, convincingly arguing that the explicit positional mechanism of the hard attention model frees its character representations from having to heavily encode positional information, unlike the soft attention model.

### Weaknesses
1.  **Dependence on an External Aligner:** The model's success is predicated on the availability of a high-quality character-level alignment tool to generate the training oracle. This introduces an external dependency and a potential point of failure. The paper does not investigate the model's sensitivity to the quality of these alignments. If the aligner performs poorly on a given language pair or produces noisy alignments, it could significantly degrade the model's training and performance. This limits the model's "end-to-end" nature.
2.  **Inherent Limitation to Monotonic Tasks:** By design, the model is tailored for nearly-monotonic sequence transduction. The authors acknowledge this when analyzing the SIGMORPHON results, noting weaker performance on languages with phenomena like circumfixing or templatic morphology which may break strict monotonicity. While this is a reasonable trade-off for the targeted task, it significantly limits the model's applicability to other sequence-to-sequence problems with more substantial reordering, such as sentence-level machine translation or abstractive summarization.
3.  **Novelty of Hard Attention:** While the application and implementation are well-executed and effective, the concept of hard, pointer-based attention is not entirely new in the literature. The related work section could be strengthened by positioning the work more clearly with respect to other hard attention mechanisms (e.g., those trained with reinforcement learning) and pointer networks. The primary novelty lies in the specific, simplified training regime enabled by pre-computed alignments, rather than the concept of hard attention itself.

### Questions to Authors
1.  Regarding the dependence on the external aligner: How robust is the model to the quality of the alignments? Have you considered using a simpler, heuristic-based aligner (e.g., one based on edit distance) to generate the oracle and observed how performance is affected? This would help clarify whether the model requires near-perfect alignments or if it can learn effectively from a noisier oracle.
2.  The model architecture seems well-suited for monotonic phenomena but struggles with more complex ones. Could the model's action space be extended to handle limited non-monotonicity (e.g., by adding a `STEP-BACK` action or a short-distance jump)? Or do you believe such an extension would compromise the simplicity and data efficiency that are the model's main strengths?
3.  In the analysis of the learned representations (Figure 4), you suggest that the soft attention model encodes position more strongly to "avoid collisions when computing the weighted sum." Could you elaborate on this hypothesis? Is it because distinct positional encodings for the same character (e.g., 'e' at the start vs. 'e' at the end) lead to more separable vectors, making the weighted average more informative?
