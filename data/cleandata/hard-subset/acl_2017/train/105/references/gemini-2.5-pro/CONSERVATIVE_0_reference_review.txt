**Summary of the paper**
This paper proposes a sequence-to-sequence model for morphological inflection generation that uses a "hard monotonic attention" mechanism. The model's decoder operates by choosing from a set of actions: either writing an output character or advancing a pointer (`step`) to the next position in the encoded input sequence. A key aspect of the proposed method is its training procedure. Rather than learning alignments and transduction jointly in an end-to-end fashion, the authors first compute character-level alignments on the training data using an existing statistical method. These alignments are then used to deterministically generate a gold-standard sequence of `write` and `step` actions. The model is then trained with a standard cross-entropy loss to predict this action sequence. The authors claim that this approach, by enforcing a hard monotonic alignment, is particularly effective in low-data settings, avoiding the overfitting issues of standard soft-attention models. They evaluate the model on three datasets (CELEX, Wiktionary, SIGMORPHON) and report state-of-the-art results, notably outperforming a strong latent-variable WFST model on the small CELEX dataset.

**Contributions**
1.  The primary contribution is an effective and methodologically simple neural architecture for morphological inflection. The combination of an explicit monotonic `step`/`write` mechanism with a training procedure that leverages pre-computed alignments proves highly successful, particularly in low-resource settings where standard neural models often fail to surpass specialized non-neural baselines.
2.  A secondary contribution is the analysis that provides evidence for the model's robustness to overfitting compared to soft attention. The visualizations of the learned encoder representations offer insight into how the explicit modeling of position (via the `step` action) may allow the model to learn more generalizable character representations.

**Strengths**
1.  **Strong Empirical Performance:** The model's performance is impressive across all three benchmarks. The most compelling result is on the CELEX dataset, where the model surpasses the latent-variable WFST of Dreyer et al. (2008). This is a significant achievement, as previous neural models, including those based on soft attention, have struggled to beat this highly specialized baseline on such a small dataset. This provides strong evidence that the modeling assumptions are well-suited for this task and data regime.
2.  **Methodological Simplicity:** The proposed training scheme is a key strength. By decoupling the alignment and transduction steps, the authors avoid the complex and often unstable training procedures required by other hard-attention models, such as reinforcement learning or marginalization over latent alignments (e.g., RNN Transducer). Training the model on a pre-determined "gold" action sequence using a simple cross-entropy loss is straightforward to implement and likely more stable and efficient.
3.  **Effective in Low-Resource Scenarios:** The paper provides a convincing demonstration of the model's utility when training data is scarce. The learning curve comparison in Figure 2 clearly shows the soft-attention model overfitting on the CELEX development set while the proposed hard-attention model generalizes much better. This is a valuable property, as many morphological tasks in various languages are data-poor.

**Weaknesses**
1.  **Dependence on an External Aligner:** The model's design introduces a strong dependency on an external, pre-processing alignment tool. This is a significant methodological weakness. The system is not truly end-to-end, and its performance is upper-bounded by the quality of the alignments provided by the Sudoh et al. (2013) model. Any errors or biases from the aligner are directly propagated into the training signal for the neural network, potentially teaching it suboptimal or incorrect transformations. The paper lacks any analysis of the aligner's quality or the sensitivity of the final model to alignment errors, which is a critical omission.
2.  **Limited Novelty and Generality:** The core idea of a transition system with discrete actions (`step`, `write`) is well-established in NLP. The novelty here lies mainly in the training procedure using a pre-computed oracle. While effective for this specific task, it feels more like a task-specific engineering solution than a fundamental contribution to attention mechanisms. The model's central assumption of near-monotonicity severely limits its applicability. The authors' own results on the SIGMORPHON dataset show that soft attention is superior for languages with non-local dependencies. The claim that the model could be applied to tasks like abstractive summarization or machine translation seems highly speculative and unsubstantiated, as these tasks frequently require non-monotonic alignment.
3.  **Insufficient Ablation and Analysis:** The paper would be strengthened by a more thorough ablation study. The authors attribute the performance gains to the hard attention mechanism, but they do not empirically isolate its effect. For example, a crucial comparison would be against a model with the same architecture trained end-to-end (e.g., using REINFORCE to learn the action policy), which would clarify whether the pre-computed alignment oracle is the key ingredient. Without such analysis, it is difficult to fully disentangle the benefits of the architecture from the benefits of the training procedure.

**Questions to Authors**
1.  Your model's performance is contingent on the quality of the external aligner. Could you provide an analysis of this dependency? For instance, what is the accuracy of the aligner on your datasets, and how does your final model's performance change if a different or less accurate alignment tool is used?
2.  The results on the SIGMORPHON dataset indicate a trade-off: your model excels on languages with suffixing and stem changes but is outperformed by soft attention on languages with more complex, non-local phenomena like vowel harmony. Do you see a path to extending your hard-attention framework to better handle such phenomena, or do you view this as an inherent limitation of the monotonic assumption?
3.  The process of converting a character alignment into a single gold sequence of `step` and `write` actions seems to have ambiguities (e.g., for one-to-many alignments, the `step` could come before or after the `write` actions). How is this conversion performed deterministically, and have you analyzed whether different valid action sequences for the same alignment affect the model's learning?
