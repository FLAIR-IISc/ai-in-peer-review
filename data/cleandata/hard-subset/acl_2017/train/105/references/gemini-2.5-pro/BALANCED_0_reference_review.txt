### **Summary and Contributions**

This paper introduces a novel sequence-to-sequence model with a hard monotonic attention mechanism for the task of morphological inflection generation. The core idea is to reframe the transduction task as predicting a sequence of actions: either `WRITE` a character to the output or `STEP` to advance a pointer over the input sequence. This approach enforces a strict monotonic alignment between the input and output. A key aspect of the methodology is the decoupling of alignment and transduction; the model is trained on gold action sequences derived from pre-computed character-level alignments, simplifying the learning process to a standard cross-entropy minimization problem.

The main contributions of this work, as I see them, are:

1.  **A simple yet highly effective hard attention mechanism that excels in low-data regimes.** By incorporating a strong inductive bias for monotonicity and leveraging pre-computed alignments, the model simplifies the learning task. This allows it to significantly outperform more complex models like soft attention and specialized WFSTs on the very small CELEX dataset, a benchmark where previous neural models have struggled with overfitting.
2.  **Comprehensive empirical evaluation establishing a new state-of-the-art.** The paper demonstrates the model's robustness and effectiveness across three diverse benchmarks: the low-resource CELEX dataset, the high-resource Wiktionary dataset, and the typologically varied SIGMORPHON 2016 dataset. The model achieves state-of-the-art or competitive results across the board.
3.  **An insightful comparative analysis of hard vs. soft attention.** The analysis in Section 5 provides clear evidence and intuition for why the proposed model works. The visualization of learned alignments and the SVD projection of encoder representations offer a compelling explanation of the differences in how the two models handle positional information and learn character representations, adding significant value beyond just reporting accuracy scores.

### **Strengths**

1.  **Exceptional performance in the low-resource setting.** The results on the CELEX dataset are the paper's most significant strength. Overtraining is a well-known issue for neural models on this benchmark. The proposed model is the first neural approach to decisively outperform the strong, task-specific latent-variable FST of Dreyer et al. (2008). The learning curve in Figure 2 provides convincing evidence that the hard attention mechanism acts as an effective regularizer, mitigating overfitting where the more flexible soft attention model fails. This result is a meaningful step forward for neural methods in low-resource morphological tasks.
2.  **Elegant and simple modeling approach.** The idea to decouple alignment from transduction and train on a sequence of `WRITE`/`STEP` actions is both elegant and effective. This design choice transforms a potentially difficult joint learning problem into a straightforward sequence prediction task trainable with a simple cross-entropy loss. The model's success demonstrates that with the right inductive bias, a simpler architecture can outperform more complex end-to-end systems.
3.  **Thorough and convincing experimentation.** The authors evaluate their model across datasets that vary in size (CELEX vs. Wiktionary) and linguistic properties (SIGMORPHON). The comparisons are made against a comprehensive set of strong and relevant baselines, including both neural and non-neural prior art. The results are consistently strong, showing the model is not only a low-resource specialist but also robust and competitive in high-resource settings.
4.  **High-quality analysis that provides valuable insights.** The analysis in Section 5 is excellent and elevates the paper significantly. The comparison of alignments learned by the hard and soft attention models (Figure 3) and the SVD visualization of encoder states (Figure 4) are very well executed. This analysis provides a clear and intuitive explanation for the model's success, suggesting that it represents positional information explicitly via `STEP` actions, freeing up the character embeddings to focus on content, whereas the soft attention model must conflate content and position into its representations.

### **Weaknesses**

1.  **Dependence on an external alignment model.** The model's performance is critically dependent on the quality of the alignments generated by the external tool (Sudoh et al., 2013). This introduces a potential point of failure. The paper does not investigate the model's robustness to alignment errors or explore how the choice of aligner impacts final performance. If the aligner performs poorly on a particular language or morphological process (e.g., non-concatenative morphology), it could severely handicap the model's ability to learn, a limitation not present in end-to-end models.
2.  **Limited applicability to non-monotonic phenomena.** The model's core strength—its assumption of monotonicity—is also its main limitation. The authors rightly note its weaker performance on SIGMORPHON languages with complex, non-local dependencies like vowel harmony or templatic morphology. While this is an acceptable trade-off, the paper could benefit from a more in-depth discussion of its failure modes. For instance, how does it handle phenomena like circumfixation or reduplication, which are challenging for a strictly monotonic pointer? A clearer characterization of these limitations would better define the model's scope.
3.  **Potential suboptimality of the "best derivation" objective.** As noted in Footnote 1, the model is trained to predict the single best action sequence (derivation) rather than maximizing the marginal probability of the correct output string. While this approximation works well in practice, it is a potential weakness. It is not clear how often different derivations could produce the same output or whether greedily predicting the highest-probability action sequence might sometimes lead to a suboptimal final string. An analysis of this approximation, perhaps by comparing beam search over actions to a model that marginalizes over derivations, would strengthen the paper's claims.

### **Questions to Authors**

1.  Could you elaborate on the model's sensitivity to the quality of the pre-computed alignments? Have you experimented with different alignment models or analyzed the impact of alignment errors on the final inflection accuracy? For example, how does the model perform if trained on alignments from a simpler model like GIZA++?
2.  Your analysis suggests the bi-directional encoder should capture context for phenomena like vowel harmony, yet the model underperforms on these languages. Is this because the decoder's access to the input is limited to a single state `xa` at each step, preventing it from integrating non-local information effectively? Could this be addressed, for example, by allowing the decoder to see a small window of input states around the current pointer?
3.  Regarding the SVD analysis, you hypothesize that the soft attention model encodes position more strongly because it needs this information for its attention calculation. This is a compelling idea. Could this be further tested by augmenting your hard-attention model with explicit position embeddings? One might expect this to have little effect on performance if the `STEP` actions already provide sufficient positional information, which would further validate your hypothesis.
