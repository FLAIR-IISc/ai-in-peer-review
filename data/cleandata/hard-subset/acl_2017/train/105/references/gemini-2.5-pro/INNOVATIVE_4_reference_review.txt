### Summary and Contributions

This paper introduces a novel sequence-to-sequence architecture for morphological inflection generation, a task where the alignment between input and output sequences is predominantly monotonic. The key innovation is a "hard monotonic attention" mechanism. Instead of computing a weighted average over all input states at each decoding step (as in soft attention), the decoder makes a discrete choice: either `WRITE` an output character or `STEP` to advance its attention pointer to the next input character. To facilitate training, the model does not learn alignments jointly. Instead, it relies on an external character aligner to generate gold sequences of `WRITE` and `STEP` actions from the training data, which simplifies the training objective to a standard cross-entropy loss.

As I see them, the main contributions are:

1.  **A novel and conceptually simple hard attention architecture.** The introduction of a discrete `WRITE`/`STEP` action space is a bold departure from the now-standard soft attention paradigm. It introduces a strong, appropriate inductive bias for tasks with monotonic alignments, resulting in a more constrained and interpretable model.
2.  **A pragmatic and effective training scheme.** The decision to decouple alignment from transduction by using a pre-trained aligner to generate an oracle is a clever simplification. It bypasses the complexities of jointly learning alignments and allows the model to leverage high-quality alignment information from the very beginning of training.
3.  **State-of-the-art results, particularly in a low-resource setting.** The model is the first neural approach to surpass the performance of highly specialized latent-variable WFST models on the small CELEX dataset, demonstrating its data efficiency and robustness against overfitting where other neural models have struggled.

### Strengths

1.  **High Novelty and Boldness of Approach:** The paper's primary strength is its core idea. In a field dominated by incremental improvements to the soft attention mechanism, this work proposes a fundamentally different and simpler alternative. By replacing the continuous, weighted-sum context vector with a discrete pointer and explicit actions, the authors introduce a strong and linguistically motivated inductive bias. This kind of bold conceptual contribution is precisely what drives the field forward, even if it sacrifices some generality.
2.  **Excellent Performance in the Low-Resource Regime:** The results on the CELEX dataset are particularly compelling. It has been a known issue that standard sequence-to-sequence models with soft attention require large datasets and tend to overfit on smaller ones. The authors not only identify this problem but provide an effective solution. Figure 2 clearly illustrates how the hard attention model's structural constraints prevent it from overfitting, leading to superior generalization on the development set. Achieving a new state of the art on this challenging benchmark is a significant empirical result that validates the architectural choices.
3.  **Conceptual Simplicity and Interpretability:** The proposed model is remarkably simple and elegant. The transduction process is easily understood as a machine that reads an input character, decides whether to move on (`STEP`) or produce output (`WRITE`), and repeats. This is far more interpretable than the opaque process of computing and using a weighted average of all input hidden states. This simplicity also extends to the training procedure, which is a straightforward classification task over actions.
4.  **Insightful Analysis:** The analysis in Section 5 adds significant value to the paper. The visualization of encoder representations provides a compelling hypothesis for the model's success: whereas the soft attention model must implicitly learn to encode positional information to achieve monotonicity, the hard attention model handles position explicitly via the `STEP` action. This allows its encoder to focus more on character identity, potentially leading to more robust and data-efficient representations. The comparison of learned alignments also reinforces the paper's core premise that monotonicity is a key feature of the task.

### Weaknesses

While I am very enthusiastic about this paper, there are a few points that temper its perfection, though they do not undermine the core contribution.

1.  **Dependence on an External Aligner:** The model's performance is fundamentally tied to the quality of the external character aligner used to generate the training oracle. The paper does not investigate how sensitive the model is to the choice or quality of this aligner. This design choice, while pragmatic and effective here, moves the system away from being truly end-to-end and introduces a dependency that could be a bottleneck in other domains or languages where good aligners are not available. However, I view this as a reasonable engineering trade-off rather than a fundamental flaw, as the simplification it enables is a key part of the contribution.
2.  **Limited Applicability:** The model's greatest strength—its strong monotonic bias—is also its greatest limitation. It is explicitly designed for "nearly-monotonic" tasks and would likely fail on tasks requiring significant reordering (e.g., translation between SVO and SOV languages). The results on the SIGMORPHON dataset, where the model is less effective on languages with non-local phenomena like vowel harmony, already hint at the boundaries of its applicability. This is not a strong argument for rejection, as the authors are transparent about this scope, but it is an important context for the work.
3.  **Approximation During Inference:** As noted in a footnote, the inference procedure finds the most likely sequence of actions, which is not guaranteed to be the same as finding the most likely output string (as multiple action sequences could yield the same output). While the authors state this approximation is "effective in practice," it represents a minor theoretical gap. Given the outstanding empirical results, this is a minor point that I am willing to overlook, but it is a point of methodological imprecision.

### Questions to Authors

1.  How does the model handle cases where the external alignment is noisy or incorrect? Have you performed any analysis on the sensitivity of your model's performance to the quality of the alignments used to generate the oracle? For example, what would happen if a much simpler heuristic, like one based on Levenshtein alignment, were used instead of the Sudoh et al. (2013) model?
2.  The `STEP` action moves the pointer strictly forward. Have you considered extensions to handle limited non-monotonicity, for example by adding a `STEP-BACK` action or allowing the decoder to attend to a small, sliding window of the input instead of a single position? This could potentially bridge the gap with the soft attention model on languages with more complex morphological phenomena while retaining much of the model's simplicity.
