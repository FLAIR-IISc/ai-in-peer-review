- Proposes a neural seq2seq model with hard monotonic attention that either writes an output symbol or advances the input pointer for morphological inflection.  
- Claims improved performance over previous neural models, especially on small datasets, and first neural model to beat the latent-variable WFST on CELEX.

- Contribution: Shows simple hard monotonic attention can outperform soft attention for morphological inflection, particularly with limited training data.  
- Contribution: Provides analysis comparing learned alignments and representations between hard and soft attention models.  
- Contribution: Validates model across CELEX, Wiktionary, and SIGMORPHON 2016, demonstrating robustness across dataset sizes and morphological types.

- Strength: Strong empirical results on small datasets (CELEX), with clear learning-curve advantages and reduced overfitting.  
- Strength: Insightful analysis of learned alignments and representations explaining why hard attention generalizes better with limited data.  
- Strength: Comprehensive evaluation across multiple datasets and languages, with particular gains on suffixing+stem change languages.

- Weakness: Limited novelty relative to prior hard-attention/RNN-Transducer-style work; distinction (precomputed alignments) not emphasized enough.  
- Weakness: Insufficient analysis of why the model performs better beyond invoking monotonic alignment; impact of alignment quality and error-correction not explored.  
- Weakness: Incomplete comparison to the most recent neural baselines (e.g., SIGMORPHON 2017/2018, Transformer-based models).

- Question: How does the model compare to Transformer-based approaches, and can hard monotonic attention be integrated into Transformers?  
- Question: Clarify the claim of being first neural model to surpass the WFST on CELEX versus Rastogi et al. (2016) — specify data requirements and exact performance differences.  
- Question: Explain whether the more isolated character clusters in hard-attention representations cause or result from better generalization and how this insight could inform future model design.

- Additional comment: Paper is well-written with released code; empirical and representational analyses are strong, but stronger comparisons to recent neural methods and clearer novelty claims would improve the work.
