

# ACL Review: Morphological Inflection Generation with Hard Monotonic Attention

## Summary of the Paper
The paper proposes a hard attention model for morphological inflection generation that directly models the monotonic alignment between input and output character sequences. The model consists of an encoder-decoder network with a dedicated control mechanism that determines whether to write an output symbol or advance the attention pointer to the next input character. The authors evaluate their model on three datasets (CELEX, Wiktionary, SIGMORPHON2016), demonstrating strong performance especially on the small CELEX dataset where their model outperforms previous neural approaches and even surpasses the best non-neural models. They also provide an insightful analysis of the alignments and representations learned by their model.

## Main Contributions (as I see them)
1. A well-motivated model that explicitly handles monotonic alignments through a hard attention mechanism, which is particularly suitable for morphological inflection tasks.
2. Comprehensive empirical results demonstrating strong performance across multiple datasets, with the key finding that the model significantly outperforms previous neural approaches on small datasets (CELEX).
3. A valuable analysis of the alignments and representations learned by the model, providing theoretical insights into how these models encode linguistic information.

## Strengths

1. **Strong empirical results on a challenging low-resource setting**: The CELEX results are particularly impressive, as the authors demonstrate that their model is the first neural approach to surpass the performance of the latent variable WFST model (Dreyer et al., 2008) on this small dataset. This addresses a key limitation in previous neural approaches to morphological inflection generation. The learning curves in Figure 2 clearly show that the hard attention model avoids the overfitting tendencies of the soft attention model on this small dataset.

2. **Insightful analysis of learned representations**: The analysis in Section 5 is one of the strongest aspects of the paper. The SVD visualizations (Figure 4) provide compelling evidence of how the two models encode linguistic information differently, with the hard attention model being less sensitive to positional information in the input representations. This theoretical insight significantly enhances the paper's contribution beyond just empirical results.

3. **Clear motivation and well-justified model design**: The paper effectively argues why a monotonic alignment approach is appropriate for morphological inflection, citing the linguistic properties of many languages with suffixing morphology. The model's architecture is well-explained and logically follows from this motivation.

4. **Comprehensive experimental evaluation**: The paper evaluates the model across three diverse datasets (CELEX, Wiktionary, SIGMORPHON2016) with multiple language families, demonstrating the robustness of the approach. The comparison to both neural and non-neural baselines is thorough and well-executed.

5. **Clear presentation and organization**: The paper is well-written, with clear explanations of the model architecture and experimental setup. The figures (particularly Figure 1 and Figure 4) are particularly helpful for understanding the model and analysis.

## Weaknesses

1. **Overstated novelty claim**: The paper claims to be "the first neural model to surpass the performance of the weighted FST model with latent variables" on CELEX, but the related work section mentions that Rastogi et al. (2016) also surpassed Dreyer et al. (2008) with twice as much data. The paper should clarify whether the authors' model is the first to surpass Dreyer et al. with the *same* amount of training data. Without this clarification, the novelty claim is potentially misleading.

2. **Limited discussion of model limitations**: The paper notes that the model performs worse on languages with context-sensitive morphology (like vowel harmony), but doesn't adequately address how to handle these cases. For a model claiming to be a general solution for morphological inflection, this is a significant limitation that should be discussed more thoroughly, including potential extensions or modifications to handle such cases.

3. **Insufficient detail about alignment process**: The paper mentions using the Sudoh et al. (2013) character alignment model, but doesn't provide sufficient detail about how it was adapted to the morphological inflection task or how the quality of these alignments was validated. Without this information, it's difficult to assess whether the alignment quality might be a bottleneck for the model.

4. **Insufficient distinction from related work**: The paper mentions RNN Transducer (Graves, 2012) and Neural Transducer (Jaitly et al., 2015) in the related work, but doesn't clearly explain how their approach differs in a meaningful way. The key difference appears to be the use of externally learned alignments rather than joint training of alignment and decoding, but this is a minor technical distinction that doesn't represent a major theoretical contribution.

5. **Lack of ablation studies**: The paper doesn't include ablation studies to isolate the contribution of the hard attention mechanism versus other components of the model. It would be valuable to know how much of the performance gain comes from the hard attention mechanism versus the bi-directional encoder or other architectural choices.

## Questions to Authors

1. The paper claims to be the first neural model to surpass Dreyer et al. (2008) on CELEX. However, Rastogi et al. (2016) reported surpassing Dreyer et al. when given twice as much data. Can you clarify whether your model is the first neural model to surpass Dreyer et al. with the *same* amount of training data? If so, please provide the exact comparison.

2. The paper notes that your model performs worse on languages with context-sensitive morphology like vowel harmony. How do you propose to extend your model to handle such cases, or do you believe these cases are fundamentally outside the scope of a monotonic alignment approach?

3. The paper mentions using the Sudoh et al. (2013) character alignment model. Could you provide more details about how you adapted this model for morphological inflection, particularly regarding cases where the alignment is not strictly monotonic (e.g., vowel harmony)?

4. How much of the performance gain comes from the hard attention mechanism versus other components of the model (e.g., the bi-directional encoder)? Would it be possible to include an ablation study showing the contribution of each component?

5. The paper states that "the hard attention model tends to have much more isolated character clusters" (Figure 4). Could you elaborate on why this property is beneficial for the morphological inflection task, and whether this property generalizes to other datasets or tasks?

## Overall Recommendation

The paper presents a well-motivated approach to morphological inflection generation with strong empirical results, particularly on the challenging small CELEX dataset. The analysis of the learned representations is particularly insightful and valuable. However, the novelty claim is overstated, and the paper would benefit from a more thorough discussion of limitations, especially regarding context-sensitive morphology. The paper is strong enough to warrant acceptance, but these weaknesses prevent it from being a clear acceptance at the highest tier.

**Decision: Accept (with minor revisions)**

The paper makes a valuable contribution to the field, especially with the CELEX results and the insightful analysis. The authors should address the weaknesses identified above, particularly clarifying the novelty claim, expanding on the limitations, and providing more detail about the alignment process. With these revisions, the paper would be an excellent contribution to ACL.
