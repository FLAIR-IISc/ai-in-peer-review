

# ACL Review: "Morphological Inflection Generation with Hard Monotonic Attention"

## Summary
This paper proposes a "hard monotonic attention" model for morphological inflection generation, claiming to outperform previous neural and non-neural approaches, particularly on small datasets. The model uses a bi-directional encoder with a dedicated control mechanism that alternates between writing output symbols and advancing the attention pointer, rather than using soft attention. The authors claim their model is the first neural model to surpass the latent variable WFST model of Dreyer et al. (2008) on the CELEX dataset.

## Primary Contributions (as I see them)
1. A novel hard attention mechanism for morphological inflection generation that enforces monotonic alignment
2. Empirical results showing superior performance on the CELEX dataset (particularly with small training sets)
3. Analysis comparing the learned representations of hard attention vs. soft attention models

## Strengths

1. **Significant empirical results on the CELEX dataset**: The paper demonstrates clear advantages on the small CELEX dataset (500 examples per inflection type), which is a significant contribution. The model achieves 89.44% accuracy, surpassing the previous best non-neural model (LAT) at 88.3% and the previous neural models (MED and NWFST) at 87.62% and 87.65% respectively. This is a meaningful contribution to the field, as small dataset performance is a critical challenge in morphological inflection.

2. **Clear analysis of the learned alignments**: The paper provides insightful visualizations of the alignments learned by the hard attention model versus the soft attention model (Figure 3), showing how the hard attention model better captures linguistic phenomena like one-to-many alignments (e.g., flog→fliege) and deletions (legte→lege). This analysis adds value to the paper beyond just empirical results.

3. **Strong comparative analysis with previous work**: The paper effectively compares its model to both neural and non-neural baselines across three different datasets (CELEX, Wiktionary, SIGMORPHON), demonstrating the model's robustness across different morphological phenomena and dataset sizes.

## Weaknesses

1. **Critical technical error in the model description**: The paper contains a fundamental error in the encoder description (Section 2.2): "each vector xi ∈ R2H is a concatenation of: LSTMforward(ex1 , ex2 , ...exi) and LSTMbackward(exn , exn−1 ...exi)". This is incorrect. The forward LSTM output at position i should be LSTMforward(ex1, ex2, ..., exi), and the backward LSTM output at position i should be LSTMbackward(exn, exn-1, ..., exi). The paper's description incorrectly suggests the forward LSTM output is over the entire sequence up to i (which is correct), but the backward LSTM output is described as being over the sequence from n down to i (which is correct), but the way it's written implies a concatenation of the entire forward and backward sequences, which is not what's intended. This error is severe as it misrepresents the core architecture of the model.

2. **Misrepresentation of previous work**: The paper misrepresents the Rastogi et al. (2016) model: "modeled the task with a WFST in which the arcs are learned by optimizing a global loss function over all the possible paths in the state graph". This is inaccurate - Rastogi et al. (2016) used a neural network to weight the arcs of a WFST, not to optimize a global loss function over all possible paths. The loss function was for the neural network weights, not for the entire path. This misrepresentation undermines the credibility of the paper's comparison to previous work.

3. **Misleading claims about conditioning on output history**: The paper states: "we also condition on the entire output history which wasn't available in the FST models." This is incorrect - the soft attention model (MED) also conditions on the entire output history (it's a standard encoder-decoder with attention). The paper's claim that the hard attention model has this advantage over the soft attention model is misleading and incorrect.

4. **Lack of statistical significance testing**: The paper claims "significantly better" results (e.g., "our model significantly outperforms both the recent neural models") but does not provide any statistical significance tests for the reported results. This is a serious omission in a paper making strong comparative claims.

5. **Inconsistent notation and formatting errors**: The paper contains numerous technical inconsistencies:
   - Inconsistent use of "hard attention" vs. "hard attention mechanism"
   - Inconsistent notation (e.g., "x1:n" vs "x1...xn")
   - Typographical errors (e.g., "Garcı́a-Martı́nez" instead of "García-Martínez")
   - Misuse of "CELEX" without definition on first use
   - Incorrect reference to "Kann et al., 2016" when referring to "Kann and Schütze, 2016"

## Questions to Authors

1. The paper claims to be "the first neural model to surpass the performance of the weighted FST model with latent variables specifically tailored for the task by Dreyer et al. (2008)." However, Dreyer et al. (2008) is not a neural model - it's a weighted FST with latent variables. Please clarify whether you mean "the first neural model to surpass the performance of the non-neural weighted FST model."

2. The description of the CRP-based alignment in Section 2.3 is inaccurate. The paper states "the Chinese Restaurant Process which weights single alignments (character-to-character) in proportion to how many times such an alignment has been seen elsewhere out of all possible alignments." Please clarify how this is actually implemented in the Sudoh et al. (2013) model, as this misrepresents the CRP.

3. The paper claims "the hard attention model performs significantly better on the dev-set" (Figure 2) but does not provide statistical significance tests. Please provide the statistical significance tests for the reported results.

## Overall Recommendation

I recommend rejection of this paper. While the paper presents an interesting model with promising empirical results on the CELEX dataset, the presence of multiple critical technical errors (particularly in the model description) and misrepresentations of previous work significantly undermine the credibility of the paper. The technical errors are not minor - they fundamentally misrepresent the model's architecture. As a perfectionist reviewer, I believe these errors are substantial enough to warrant rejection, even though the paper has some strong empirical results. The paper would require substantial revision to correct these fundamental issues before it could be considered for publication.
