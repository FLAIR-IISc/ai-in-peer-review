

# Review of "Morphological Inflection Generation with Hard Monotonic Attention"

## Summary and Main Contributions

This paper proposes a hard attention model for morphological inflection generation that directly models the nearly monotonic alignment between input and output character sequences. The model uses a bi-directional RNN encoder and a decoder with a dedicated control mechanism that either writes an output symbol or advances the attention pointer to the next input state. The authors evaluate their model on three morphological inflection generation datasets (CELEX, Wiktionary, and SIGMORPHON2016), demonstrating significant improvements over previous neural and non-neural approaches, particularly on small datasets where previous neural models struggled.

The main contributions of this work, in order of strength, are:
1. A hard attention model for nearly-monotonic sequence to sequence learning specifically designed for morphological inflection tasks, with a clear and principled approach to modeling alignment.
2. A new state-of-the-art performance on three previously studied morphological inflection generation datasets, with the key innovation being the ability to outperform previous neural models on small datasets (CELEX) while remaining competitive on larger datasets (Wiktionary, SIGMORPHON).
3. An insightful analysis comparing the hard attention model with soft attention models, revealing how each encodes positional information and other linguistic features, providing valuable theoretical insights into the representations learned by these models.

## Strengths

1. **Significant performance improvements on small datasets**: The paper demonstrates that their model is the first neural approach to surpass the latent variable WFST model by Dreyer et al. (2008) on the CELEX dataset (with only 500 training examples per inflection type). This addresses a critical limitation of neural approaches in low-resource settings, where they typically underperform compared to traditional methods. The results in Table 1 show a clear 1.1% improvement over the previous best neural model (NWFST) and a 1.1% improvement over the non-neural LAT model, which is particularly impressive given the extremely small training set.

2. **Insightful analysis of representations**: The paper provides a thorough analysis of the representations learned by both hard and soft attention models using SVD dimensionality reduction. Figure 4 demonstrates that the hard attention model encodes positional information less strongly than the soft attention model, which explains its better performance on small datasets. This analysis goes beyond typical performance comparisons to provide meaningful insights into the inner workings of neural sequence models for morphological inflection, which is valuable for the field.

3. **Clear linguistic motivation and evaluation**: The authors provide a strong linguistic justification for their approach, noting that morphological inflection often involves suffixing morphology with nearly monotonic alignments. Their evaluation across three different datasets with varying linguistic characteristics (CELEX: German, Wiktionary: multiple languages, SIGMORPHON: 10 languages with different morphological phenomena) demonstrates the robustness of their approach and provides meaningful insights into when it works best.

4. **Practical and efficient training procedure**: The paper's training procedure using independently learned alignments simplifies the model training process compared to joint training approaches (like soft attention models). This is a practical contribution that makes the model easier to implement and train, especially for researchers working with limited resources.

5. **Comprehensive comparison with relevant baselines**: The authors provide a thorough comparison with both recent neural approaches (Kann and Schütze, Rastogi et al.) and traditional methods (Dreyer et al.), making it clear where their model stands in the landscape of morphological inflection generation.

## Weaknesses

1. **Limited novelty of the core approach**: While the hard attention mechanism with step/write actions is well-motivated, it bears significant similarity to prior work on neural transducers (Graves, 2012; Yu et al., 2016). The paper acknowledges this connection but doesn't sufficiently clarify how their specific implementation differs from these prior approaches. The key novelty appears to be the training procedure using independently learned alignments, but this isn't framed as the primary contribution, making it difficult to assess the true novelty of the work.

2. **Lack of ablation studies**: The paper doesn't include ablation studies to isolate the contribution of specific components (e.g., the bi-directional encoder, the hard attention mechanism, the control mechanism). Without these, it's difficult to determine which aspects of the model are truly responsible for the performance gains. For example, it's unclear whether the bi-directional encoder or the hard attention mechanism is more important for the improvements on small datasets.

3. **Limited discussion of failure cases**: The paper mentions that the model performs slightly worse than soft attention on some languages (Turkish, Arabic, etc.), but doesn't provide specific examples or analyze why the model fails in these cases. Understanding these failure modes would provide deeper insights into the limitations of the approach and guide future improvements.

4. **Comparison with more recent baselines**: The paper compares to several strong baselines, but doesn't include the most recent approaches to morphological inflection generation (e.g., Tacke et al. 2020, or more recent work on morphological inflection using transformer architectures). This makes it difficult to assess whether the claimed "new state of the art" still holds against the most recent literature.

5. **Insufficient discussion of computational complexity**: The paper doesn't discuss the computational complexity of the model compared to soft attention models. For instance, does the hard attention mechanism lead to faster training or inference? How does the training time compare to soft attention models? This information would be valuable for practitioners considering adopting the approach.

## Questions to Authors

1. The paper mentions that the hard attention model performs worse than soft attention on some languages (e.g., Turkish, Arabic). Could you provide specific examples of these failure cases and discuss why the hard attention model struggles with them? This would help clarify the limitations of the approach.

2. In Table 3, your model performs slightly worse than the soft attention model on Hungarian and Arabic. Could you elaborate on the linguistic features of these languages that make them particularly challenging for your model, and whether you have any hypotheses about how the model could be improved for these cases?

3. The paper states that your model is "the first neural model to surpass the performance of the weighted FST model with latent variables specifically tailored for the task by Dreyer et al. (2008)" on the CELEX dataset. However, Rastogi et al. (2016) also reported results on this dataset. Could you clarify how your results compare to theirs and why your model performs better?

4. The paper mentions that the training procedure is simpler due to using independently learned alignments. Could you provide a more detailed comparison of the training time and computational resources required for your model versus soft attention models? This would help readers understand the practical trade-offs.

## Overall Assessment

This paper presents a well-motivated approach to morphological inflection generation with strong empirical results, particularly on small datasets where neural models typically struggle. The insights into how different attention mechanisms encode positional information are particularly valuable. However, the novelty of the core approach is somewhat limited, as it builds heavily on prior work on neural transducers, and the paper would benefit from more thorough ablation studies and discussion of limitations.

The paper makes a meaningful contribution to the field by demonstrating that neural models can outperform traditional methods on very small datasets, which is an important step toward making neural approaches more practical for morphologically rich languages with limited resources. The insights from the representation analysis also provide valuable theoretical contributions.

**Recommendation**: Accept. While the paper has some weaknesses regarding novelty and depth of analysis, the empirical results are strong, the insights are valuable, and the work makes a clear contribution to the field. With minor revisions addressing the weaknesses noted above, this paper would be a strong addition to ACL.
