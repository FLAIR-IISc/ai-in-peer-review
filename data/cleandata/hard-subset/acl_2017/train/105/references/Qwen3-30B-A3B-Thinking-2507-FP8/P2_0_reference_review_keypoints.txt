- Summary: Proposes a hard monotonic attention neural model for morphological inflection that either writes a symbol or advances an input pointer, trained with precomputed character alignments and excelling on small datasets.

- Contribution 1: Demonstrates a neural model that outperforms a latent-variable FST on the low-resource CELEX dataset.

- Contribution 2: Presents a simple, well-motivated architecture leveraging near-monotonic input-output alignments with a clear control mechanism.

- Contribution 3: Provides analysis comparing hard vs. soft attention, showing different position encoding behaviors and explaining why hard attention suits inflection.

- Strength 1: Strong empirical gains in low-resource settings (CELEX), achieving statistically significant improvement over prior best.

- Strength 2: Clear motivation and model design that explicitly exploits the monotonic nature of morphological transformations.

- Strength 3: Insightful comparative analysis (e.g., SVD visualizations) revealing how representations differ between hard and soft attention.

- Strength 4: Competitive results across multiple datasets (CELEX, Wiktionary, SIGMORPHON 2016), especially for suffixing languages.

- Weakness 1: Lacks comparison with more recent neural architectures (e.g., transformers) developed after 2016.

- Weakness 2: Insufficient detail on the character alignment procedure and sensitivity to alignment quality.

- Weakness 3: Limited theoretical justification for why hard attention outperforms soft attention beyond empirical observations.

- Weakness 4: Reproducibility concern: code availability claimed but repository link not provided in the paper.

- Question 1: How do results compare to more recent neural models (e.g., transformer-based) for morphological inflection?

- Question 2: Provide details on the Sudoh et al. (2013) alignment implementation and model sensitivity to imperfect alignments.

- Question 3: Can you offer a formal theoretical explanation for why weaker position encoding in hard attention benefits inflection tasks?

- Question 4: How do your CELEX results compare to Rastogi et al. (2016) when using the same data splits and training regimes?

- Overall recommendation: Strong, well-motivated paper with valuable empirical and analytical contributions; recommend acceptance despite evaluation and reproducibility gaps.
