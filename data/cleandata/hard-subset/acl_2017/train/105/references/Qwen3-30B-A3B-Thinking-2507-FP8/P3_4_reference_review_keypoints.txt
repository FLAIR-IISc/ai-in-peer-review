- Proposed a hard monotonic attention model that either writes an output character or advances an input pointer, decoupling alignment learning from transduction.  
- Claims superior performance on small datasets (notably CELEX) and provides analysis comparing hard vs soft attention.

Strengths:
- Strong empirical evidence on small, low-resource datasets (CELEX), showing substantially better performance and less overfitting than prior neural models.  
- Insightful analysis of learned representations: model encodes character identity more strongly and relies on step actions for position tracking.  
- Thorough evaluation across multiple datasets (CELEX, Wiktionary, SIGMORPHON2016) with appropriate baselines and robust cross-language performance.

Weaknesses:
- Questionable novelty: approach closely resembles RNN Transducer (Graves, 2012) and Yu et al. (2016); novelty claim inadequately distinguished from prior work.  
- No theoretical or empirical explanation for why enforcing monotonicity improves generalization on small datasets.  
- Inadequate discussion of limitations, especially poorer performance on languages with vowel/consonant harmony and how to address this.  
- Insufficient detail on how the Sudoh et al. (2013) character alignment model was adapted and validated for morphological inflection, hampering reproducibility.

Questions for authors:
- How does your model fundamentally differ from Graves' RNN Transducer (2012) and Yu et al. (2016) in a way that constitutes a novel contribution?  
- Why does the monotonic alignment assumption improve generalization on small datasets—what architectural or training factors cause this?  
- How do you explain outperforming Rastogi et al.'s neural WFST on CELEX with the same data quantity, given their claims about data scaling?

Overall assessment:
- Well-executed empirical work demonstrating that a simpler hard attention model can outperform soft attention in low-resource morphological inflection, but novelty is limited and major revisions are needed on novelty claims and limitations; recommendation: Weak Accept (major revisions).
