

# Review of "Morphological Inflection Generation with Hard Monotonic Attention"

## Summary
The paper proposes a model for morphological inflection generation that uses pre-learned character-level alignments to guide a neural network with a "hard attention" mechanism. The model is evaluated on three datasets: CELEX (very small, 500 examples per inflection type), Wiktionary (larger dataset with up to 360k examples), and SIGMORPHON2016 (multilingual). The authors claim their model outperforms previous neural and non-neural approaches, particularly on small datasets where neural models typically struggle.

## Main Contributions (as I see them)
1. **Use of pre-learned alignments to guide training** - The most significant contribution is the approach of using independently learned character-level alignments (from Sudoh et al., 2013) to guide the training of the encoder-decoder network.
2. **Demonstration of neural models on small datasets** - The paper shows neural models can outperform non-neural models on the very small CELEX dataset, which is a notable achievement.
3. **Analysis of learned representations** - The paper provides detailed analysis of the representations learned by the hard attention model versus the soft attention model.

## Strengths

1. **Strong empirical results on small datasets:** The paper demonstrates that their model significantly outperforms previous neural approaches on the CELEX dataset (89.44% accuracy vs. 88.3% for the previous best model), which is particularly valuable since neural models typically require large datasets to perform well. This addresses an important practical challenge in morphological processing for morphologically rich languages with limited data.

2. **Detailed analysis of model behavior:** The paper provides a thorough analysis of the alignments predicted by the model (Figure 3) and the representations learned by the encoders (Figure 4). This analysis helps explain why the model might be performing well and provides valuable insights into how neural models learn to handle morphological inflection tasks.

3. **Robust performance across datasets:** The model performs well on both small (CELEX) and large (Wiktionary) datasets, suggesting it's not just a trick for small datasets but has general utility for morphological inflection generation. The results on Wiktionary (Table 2) show the model achieves the highest reported average accuracy across all inflection types.

## Weaknesses

1. **Lack of novelty in the core model:** The fundamental model architecture (bi-directional encoder, RNN decoder with attention) is not novel. The core innovation is the use of pre-learned alignments, but this is not a new technique - Sudoh et al. (2013) already provided a method for character-level alignment. The paper doesn't adequately justify why their specific approach to using these alignments is better than simply using them to guide the attention mechanism in a standard soft attention model. This is a significant weakness as the paper's main claim is built on a non-novel foundation.

2. **Misleading "hard attention" claim:** The model still uses a softmax over the possible actions (step or write), so it's not a truly hard attention mechanism. The paper's use of the term "hard attention" is misleading and could confuse readers. The authors should clarify this in their revision, as the model doesn't actually make discrete, hard decisions about attention.

3. **Insufficient comparison to relevant baselines:** The paper compares to previous neural models but doesn't compare to a soft attention model that uses the same pre-learned alignments. This is a critical omission because it's the most direct comparison to their approach. Without this comparison, it's impossible to determine whether the "hard attention" aspect is actually beneficial or whether it's just the pre-learned alignments that are driving the performance gains.

4. **Overstated significance of "first neural model to surpass" claim:** The paper claims their model is "the first neural model to surpass the performance of the weighted FST model with latent variables specifically tailored for the task by Dreyer et al. (2008)" on CELEX. However, Rastogi et al. (2016) already reported surpassing this model with twice the training data, and the paper doesn't explain why their model is better than Rastogi et al.'s when using the same amount of data (500 examples per inflection type). This claim is misleading and diminishes the significance of the paper's contribution.

5. **Limited analysis of alignment quality:** The paper uses Sudoh et al.'s (2013) alignment model without analyzing the quality of these alignments. It's possible that the pre-learned alignments are of high quality, which could be the main reason for the model's success rather than the hard attention mechanism. A more thorough analysis of the alignment quality would strengthen the paper.

## Questions to Authors

1. How does your model compare to a soft attention model that uses the same pre-learned alignments (from Sudoh et al., 2013)? This is the most direct comparison to your approach, and the paper's current comparison to other neural models doesn't fully address this.

2. The paper claims to use a "hard attention" mechanism, but the model still uses a softmax over possible actions. Can you clarify how this differs from a soft attention mechanism, and why it's beneficial for this task?

3. The paper states that your model is the first neural model to surpass the latent variable WFST model (Dreyer et al., 2008) on CELEX. However, Rastogi et al. (2016) reported surpassing this model with twice the training data. How does your model compare to Rastogi et al. when using the same amount of data (500 examples per inflection type)?

4. The paper uses pre-learned alignments from Sudoh et al. (2013), but doesn't provide any analysis of the quality of these alignments. Can you provide any analysis of the alignment quality, and how it might affect the model's performance?

## Overall Recommendation

I am skeptical of this paper's main contribution due to the lack of novelty in the core model and the misleading "hard attention" claim. While the empirical results on small datasets are interesting and the analysis of learned representations is valuable, the paper's claims about the novelty and significance of the approach are overstated. The paper would need to be significantly revised to address the weaknesses identified above before it could be considered for publication.

I would recommend **Reject** for the following reasons:
1. The core model is not novel, and the paper doesn't sufficiently justify why their specific use of pre-learned alignments is better than existing approaches.
2. The "hard attention" claim is misleading and needs clarification.
3. The comparison to relevant baselines is insufficient, particularly missing a direct comparison to a soft attention model using the same pre-learned alignments.
4. The significance of surpassing a non-neural model on a small dataset is overstated.

The paper's results on small datasets are valuable, but the paper's presentation and claims do not adequately support the significance of this contribution.
