

# Peer Review: "Morphological Inflection Generation with Hard Monotonic Attention"

## Summary and Contributions

This paper proposes a novel neural model for morphological inflection generation that directly models nearly monotonic alignments between input and output character sequences. The key innovation is a hard attention mechanism that either writes an output character or advances the attention pointer to the next input character, rather than using soft attention that weights all input positions.

The main contributions of the paper, in order of strength as I see them, are:

1. **Demonstrating significant improvements on small datasets**: The model is the first neural approach to surpass the latent variable WFST model of Dreyer et al. (2008) on the CELEX dataset, which has only 500 training examples per inflection type. This addresses a key limitation of previous neural approaches that struggled with data sparsity.

2. **A simple yet effective model architecture**: The hard attention mechanism combined with bi-directional encoding provides a clean solution that avoids the need to jointly learn alignment and transduction, simplifying training while maintaining strong performance.

3. **Insightful analysis of learned representations**: The paper provides valuable insights into what neural models learn for morphological inflection, showing how the hard attention model encodes position information differently than soft attention models.

## Strengths

1. **Significant improvement on small datasets**: The CELEX results (Table 1) are compelling, showing that the hard attention model achieves 89.44% accuracy compared to the previous best neural model at 87.65% and the latent variable WFST at 88.3%. This is particularly impressive as the authors correctly note it's "the first neural model to surpass the performance of the weighted FST model with latent variables specifically tailored for the task." For morphologically rich languages with limited training data, this is a major practical contribution that addresses a well-known challenge in the field.

2. **Simple and principled approach**: The model's simplicity stands out. By decoupling alignment learning from transduction (using pre-computed character alignments), the authors avoid the complexity of joint alignment-transduction training while maintaining strong performance. The training procedure is straightforward (cross-entropy loss), making the model easy to implement and understand, which is valuable for reproducibility and adoption.

3. **Insightful representation analysis**: The SVD visualization (Figure 4) provides valuable insights into how the two models encode information. The observation that the hard attention model doesn't encode positional information as strongly as the soft attention model because it uses "step actions" to handle position information is a meaningful contribution to understanding neural sequence models for this task. This analysis goes beyond typical performance comparisons to provide deeper understanding of the models.

## Weaknesses

1. **Limited novelty of the approach**: While the application to morphological inflection is novel, the core idea of hard monotonic attention has been explored in other sequence-to-sequence contexts. The RNN Transducer (Graves, 2012) and subsequent works (Yu et al., 2016) have similar mechanisms. The paper acknowledges this in the related work section but doesn't sufficiently differentiate its contribution from these earlier works, particularly in terms of why the specific implementation for morphological inflection is novel.

2. **Lack of ablation studies**: The paper doesn't provide ablation studies to isolate the contribution of the hard attention mechanism versus other aspects of the model (e.g., the bi-directional encoder). It would be valuable to know whether the improvement over soft attention is due to the hard attention mechanism itself, the bi-directional encoding, or the decoupled alignment approach. Without these ablations, it's difficult to fully assess what component is driving the performance gains.

3. **Limited discussion of failure cases**: The paper shows strong overall results but doesn't analyze specific cases where the model fails. For the SIGMORPHON dataset, the model performs slightly worse than the soft attention model on some languages (e.g., Arabic, Maltese). It would be valuable to understand why this happens and whether these cases relate to the monotonicity assumption of the model.

## Questions to Authors

1. The paper mentions that "the model is the first neural model to surpass the performance of the weighted FST model with latent variables specifically tailored for the task" on CELEX. How do you account for the fact that the Rastogi et al. (2016) neural WFST model also reported strong results on CELEX? Why didn't their model achieve this improvement?

2. The paper states that the hard attention model "does not encode [position] as strongly in the representations of the inputs" because it uses step actions for position information. Could you elaborate on how this might affect the model's ability to handle non-monotonic morphological phenomena, such as vowel harmony or consonant harmony, which are mentioned in the analysis of the SIGMORPHON results?

3. The model appears to be particularly effective on suffixing languages (Russian, German, Spanish) but less so on languages with more complex morphological phenomena. How would you extend this model to better handle languages where the monotonic alignment assumption breaks down?

## Overall Recommendation

This is a well-written paper with significant practical contributions for morphological inflection generation, particularly on small datasets. The demonstration that neural models can outperform traditional FST approaches with limited data is valuable for real-world applications in morphologically rich languages. The model's simplicity and clear analysis of learned representations add to its appeal. While the novelty of the core mechanism is somewhat limited by prior work in sequence transduction, the specific application to morphological inflection with compelling results on standard benchmarks makes this a strong contribution to the field.

I recommend acceptance with minor revisions to address the weaknesses noted above, particularly regarding the novelty discussion and inclusion of ablation studies.
