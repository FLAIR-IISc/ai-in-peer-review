

# Review of "Morphological Inflection Generation with Hard Monotonic Attention"

## Summary and Main Contributions
This paper proposes a neural sequence-to-sequence model for morphological inflection generation that uses a "hard monotonic attention" mechanism. The model directly models the monotonic alignment between input and output character sequences (common in morphological inflection) by having the decoder either write an output character or advance an attention pointer to the next input character. The authors claim three main contributions: (1) a hard attention model for nearly-monotonic sequence-to-sequence learning, (2) state-of-the-art results on three morphological inflection datasets, and (3) an analysis comparing their model with soft attention models.

From my perspective, the most significant contributions are:
1. The empirical demonstration that their model significantly outperforms previous neural approaches on small datasets (CELEX), being the first neural model to surpass the latent variable WFST model of Dreyer et al. (2008) on this dataset.
2. The detailed analysis comparing their model with soft attention models, revealing how the two approaches learn different representations and why the hard attention mechanism is particularly suited for morphological inflection tasks.

## Strengths

1. **Strong empirical results on small datasets:** The paper makes a compelling case for the value of their approach by demonstrating significant improvements on the CELEX dataset (with only 500 training examples per inflection type), where previous neural models underperform. Their model outperforms both neural baselines (MED and NWFST) and the non-neural latent variable WFST model (LAT), which is a significant contribution to the field. This addresses a critical limitation of neural approaches for morphological inflection, where data sparsity is a major challenge.

2. **Insightful analysis of model behavior:** The paper's analysis comparing the hard attention model with soft attention models provides valuable insights into why their approach works well for morphological inflection. The alignment visualization (Figure 3) and representation analysis (Figure 4) clearly demonstrate how the hard attention model learns more monotonic alignments and encodes positional information differently than soft attention models. This analysis goes beyond typical empirical results to provide theoretical understanding of the model's behavior.

3. **Thorough evaluation across multiple datasets:** The authors evaluate their model on three distinct datasets (CELEX, Wiktionary, SIGMORPHON2016) with varying amounts of training data and morphological complexity. This demonstrates the robustness of their approach across different settings and provides a comprehensive picture of their model's strengths and limitations.

4. **Clear and well-structured presentation:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and organized. The motivation is clear, the technical description is accessible, and the results are presented in a straightforward manner. The figures (particularly Figure 2 showing learning curves) effectively illustrate key points about the model's behavior.

5. **Practical contribution to morphological inflection:** The paper makes a concrete contribution to a task with significant practical implications for NLP systems dealing with morphologically rich languages, where inflection generation is crucial for downstream tasks like machine translation.

## Weaknesses

1. **Lack of sufficient novelty claim:** While the hard attention mechanism is well-motivated, the core architecture (bi-directional encoder, decoder with attention) is very similar to prior work, particularly the RNN Transducer (Graves, 2012) and Yu et al. (2016). The paper doesn't sufficiently distinguish their approach from these closely related works, which weakens the novelty claim. The authors mention these works in the related work section but fail to include them in their main experimental comparisons, which is a significant omission.

2. **Insufficient comparison to closely related work:** The paper fails to compare with the RNN Transducer (Graves, 2012), which is a direct predecessor to their approach. Given that Graves' work already used a similar hard attention mechanism with monotonic alignment, this comparison is essential for establishing the novelty and contribution of the current work. The authors should have included these models in their main results.

3. **Vague description of alignment method:** The paper states they use "a character level alignment process" but doesn't provide enough detail about how this alignment is computed or why it's better than alternative methods. The reference to Sudoh et al. (2013) is insufficient to understand the alignment process, and the paper doesn't compare their alignment method to alternatives (e.g., using attention weights from a soft attention model).

4. **Limited analysis of failure cases:** While the paper notes that the model performs worse on languages with vowel harmony, it doesn't provide concrete examples of these failure cases or analyze why the hard attention mechanism fails in these scenarios. This limits the understanding of the model's limitations and potential for improvement.

5. **Lack of ablation studies:** The paper doesn't include ablation studies to isolate the contribution of specific components (e.g., the hard attention mechanism vs. the bi-directional encoder vs. the training procedure). Without these studies, it's difficult to determine which aspects of their approach are truly responsible for the performance gains.

## Questions to Authors

1. How does your model fundamentally differ from the RNN Transducer (Graves, 2012) and Yu et al. (2016) in terms of both architecture and performance? Why weren't these models included in your main experimental results?

2. Can you provide more details about the character-level alignment process you used (Sudoh et al., 2013)? How does it compare to using attention weights from a soft attention model for alignment? What are the specific advantages of your alignment method for this task?

3. The paper states that your model is the first neural model to surpass the latent variable WFST model on CELEX. How does this compare to the results reported by Rastogi et al. (2016), which also claimed to surpass this model with sufficient data? Did they use a different data split?

4. The paper mentions that your model performs worse on languages with vowel harmony. Can you provide specific examples of these failure cases and discuss why the hard attention mechanism is less suitable for these languages compared to the soft attention mechanism?

## Overall Recommendation

This is a well-written, well-executed paper that makes a valuable contribution to the morphological inflection generation task, particularly with respect to small dataset performance. The empirical results are strong and the analysis provides important insights into how these models work for this specific task.

However, the lack of sufficient novelty claim due to the similarity with prior work (particularly RNN Transducer) and the omission of key comparative experiments significantly weaken the paper. With appropriate revisions to address these issues (particularly by adding comparisons to Graves, 2012 and Yu et al., 2016, and providing more detail about the alignment method), this paper would be a strong candidate for acceptance.

**Decision:** Rebuttal recommended. The paper has significant strengths but requires addressing the novelty concerns and adding key comparisons before it can be accepted.
