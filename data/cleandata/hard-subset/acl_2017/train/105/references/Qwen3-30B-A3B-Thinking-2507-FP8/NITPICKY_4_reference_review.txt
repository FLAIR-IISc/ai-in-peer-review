

# ACL Review: "Morphological Inflection Generation with Hard Monotonic Attention"

## Summary
The paper proposes a "hard monotonic attention" model for morphological inflection generation. The key idea is to use a control mechanism that alternates between "step" (advance the attention pointer) and "write" (output a character) actions to model the nearly monotonic alignment between input and output character sequences. The model uses a bi-directional RNN encoder and a decoder with a dedicated control mechanism. The authors claim this approach outperforms previous neural and non-neural models on three datasets, particularly excelling on the small CELEX dataset.

## Main Contributions (as I see them)
1. An evaluation of a transition system approach (with a hard attention mechanism) on morphological inflection generation, showing improved results on the CELEX dataset compared to previous neural models.
2. An analysis comparing the hard attention model with soft attention models, providing insights into the representations learned by these models.
3. The practical advantage of the model for small training sets, which is important for morphological inflection tasks with limited data.

## Strengths
1. **Evaluation on Multiple Datasets**: The paper evaluates their model on three different datasets (CELEX, Wiktionary, and SIGMORPHON 2016), showing consistent improvements over previous neural models, especially on the CELEX dataset with its small training set.
2. **Insightful Analysis**: The analysis of the learned alignments (Section 5) provides useful insights into the model's behavior, even if it's somewhat superficial. The comparison of alignment predictions between hard and soft attention models is particularly valuable.
3. **Practical Impact**: The model's ability to work well with small training sets is a practical advantage, as many morphological inflection tasks suffer from data sparsity.

## Weaknesses

### 1. Lack of Novelty in Core Model (Major Issue)
The paper's core model is not novel. It's a standard transition system approach with a hard attention mechanism that has been used in prior work (e.g., Graves, 2012; Yu et al., 2016). The paper doesn't sufficiently differentiate their approach from these prior works. The "hard attention" terminology is misleading as it's not a standard hard attention mechanism (which typically involves discrete selection of a single input position). The paper's mechanism is better described as a transition system with "step" and "write" actions.

### 2. Overstated Claims (Major Issue)
The paper claims to be "the first neural model to surpass the performance of the weighted FST model with latent variables specifically tailored for the task by Dreyer et al. (2008)" on the CELEX dataset. However:
- The paper doesn't provide evidence that previous neural models didn't already surpass the LAT model on this dataset.
- The paper doesn't provide statistical significance tests for the results in Table 1 (89.44 vs 88.30), making it impossible to assess whether this improvement is statistically significant.
- The paper compares to MED and NWFST, but doesn't clarify if these were previously published as surpassing LAT.

### 3. Methodological Issues (Major Issue)
The paper uses a precomputed alignment (from Sudoh et al., 2013) to derive "gold transduction+control sequences" for training. This is not novel, as it's a standard practice in sequence-to-sequence learning. The paper fails to clearly explain why this approach is better than joint training of alignment and decoding (which is what most recent sequence-to-sequence models do). The paper states "we instead show that in our case it is worthwhile to decouple these stages," but provides no justification for why this decoupling is beneficial for the morphological inflection task specifically.

### 4. Inconsistent Terminology and Formatting (Significant Issue)
- The paper uses "hard attention" to describe a mechanism that isn't a standard hard attention mechanism. This is confusing and could lead to misinterpretation by readers.
- The reference list contains formatting inconsistencies (e.g., "Ahlberg et al\.,? 2015" with backslashes).
- Inconsistent notation: The paper uses "x1:n" in some places and "x1...xn" in others.
- In Table 3, the text "suffixing+stem changes circ. suffixing+agg.+v.h. c.h. templatic" appears to be a typo or formatting error.

### 5. Lack of Statistical Significance (Significant Issue)
The paper doesn't provide statistical significance tests for the results, which is a significant omission in a machine learning paper. Without these, it's impossible to assess whether the improvements over previous models are statistically significant or just due to chance.

## Questions to Authors

1. How does your model specifically differ from the RNN Transducer (Graves, 2012) and Yu et al. (2016) in terms of the alignment learning process? What specific advantages does your approach have over these prior works?

2. Can you provide statistical significance tests (e.g., t-tests) for the results in Table 1, showing that the improvements over previous models are statistically significant?

3. The paper claims to be "the first neural model to surpass the performance of the weighted FST model with latent variables specifically tailored for the task by Dreyer et al. (2008)" on the CELEX dataset. Can you confirm that previous neural models didn't already surpass the LAT model on this dataset, and if so, why weren't they reported in the literature?

4. The paper uses "hard attention" to describe your mechanism, but it's not a standard hard attention mechanism. Can you clarify what you mean by "hard attention" in this context, and how it differs from standard hard attention mechanisms?

5. The paper states "we use a simple training procedure which relies on independently learned character-level alignments." Why is this approach better than joint training of alignment and decoding, which is standard practice in most neural sequence-to-sequence models?

## Overall Recommendation
**Reject**

This paper has significant methodological and presentation issues that outweigh its positive contributions. The core model lacks novelty, the claims are overstated without sufficient evidence, and the paper fails to properly differentiate its approach from prior work. The lack of statistical significance testing and inconsistent terminology further undermine the paper's credibility. While the evaluation on multiple datasets shows some promise, the paper would need substantial revision to address these fundamental issues before it could be considered for acceptance. The paper's claims about being "the first neural model to surpass" the LAT model on CELEX are particularly unsupported and misleading.
