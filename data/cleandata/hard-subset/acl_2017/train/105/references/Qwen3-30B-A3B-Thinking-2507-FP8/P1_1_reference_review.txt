

# Peer Review

## Summary
This paper proposes a new neural model for morphological inflection generation that uses a "hard monotonic attention" mechanism. The model directly models the almost monotonic alignment between input and output character sequences (common in morphological inflection tasks) by using a control mechanism that either writes an output symbol or advances the attention pointer to the next input character. The authors argue that this approach addresses a key limitation of previous neural sequence-to-sequence models (which rely on soft attention) that struggle with small training datasets. They evaluate their model on three established morphological inflection datasets (CELEX, Wiktionary, and SIGMORPHON 2016) and claim state-of-the-art results, particularly on small datasets.

## Main Contributions (as I see them)
1. **A novel approach to morphological inflection with strong empirical results on small datasets:** The model demonstrates significant performance improvements over previous neural approaches on the CELEX dataset (only 500 examples per inflection type), surpassing the latent variable WFST model by Dreyer et al. (2008) - a notable achievement given the data sparsity.
2. **Insightful analysis of attention mechanisms:** The paper provides a detailed comparison between hard and soft attention models, using SVD visualizations to show how they encode positional information differently, which explains why the hard attention model generalizes better with limited data.
3. **Robust performance across multiple datasets:** The model achieves state-of-the-art results on three different morphological inflection datasets, showing both strong performance with small training sets and competitive results with larger datasets.

## Strengths

1. **Significant improvement on small datasets:** The CELEX dataset is notoriously small (500 examples per inflection type), and previous neural models struggled to match the performance of traditional WFST-based approaches. The authors convincingly demonstrate that their model outperforms both the previous neural baselines (MED and NWFST) and the state-of-the-art non-neural model (LAT) on this dataset. This is particularly important because morphological inflection is often studied in low-resource settings, and the ability to perform well with minimal data is a major practical advantage.

2. **Insightful analysis of model representations:** The paper's analysis of the learned representations (Figure 4) is exceptionally strong. The SVD visualization shows how the hard attention model encodes positional information less explicitly than the soft attention model, which explains why it generalizes better with limited training data. This analysis provides valuable theoretical insights into how attention-based models work for morphological inflection, going beyond just reporting accuracy numbers.

3. **Clear evaluation across multiple datasets:** The model's performance is evaluated on three distinct morphological inflection datasets, each with different characteristics (CELEX: very small, Wiktionary: large, SIGMORPHON: diverse morphological systems). The results consistently show that the model either matches or surpasses previous state-of-the-art approaches, demonstrating its robustness across different morphological phenomena.

4. **Practical implementation and training:** The training procedure is straightforward (using cross-entropy loss on pre-computed alignments) and avoids the complex joint training of alignment and transduction used in other neural approaches. The authors provide implementation details and code availability, which is valuable for reproducibility.

## Weaknesses

1. **Lack of novelty in the core approach:** The paper's main idea (using a monotonic attention mechanism) is not entirely novel. The RNN Transducer (Graves, 2012) and related work (Yu et al., 2016) already use monotonic alignment. The paper mentions these works in the Related Work section but doesn't sufficiently differentiate their approach from these prior methods. The key difference (using pre-computed alignments rather than joint training) is mentioned, but the paper doesn't clearly articulate why this specific implementation is novel or why it's better than the approaches used in Graves (2012) and Yu et al. (2016).

2. **Limited discussion of failure cases:** The paper notes that the model performs worse on languages with vowel harmony and consonant harmony (like Hungarian, Navajo, Arabic), but doesn't provide specific examples or analyze why these cases are problematic. A deeper analysis of failure cases would strengthen the paper and help identify the limitations of the monotonic alignment assumption.

3. **Lack of ablation studies:** The paper doesn't include ablation studies to determine the contribution of each component of their model (e.g., the bi-directional encoder, the control mechanism, the step operation). Without these studies, it's difficult to determine which aspects of the model are most important for the performance gains observed. For example, how much does the bi-directional encoder contribute compared to the hard attention mechanism?

4. **Training data dependency on alignment model:** The model relies on pre-computed character alignments (from Sudoh et al., 2013), which means it's not truly end-to-end. The paper doesn't provide sufficient evidence that this alignment model is reliable, especially for languages with complex morphological patterns. If the alignment model makes errors, this could negatively impact the model's performance, but the paper doesn't analyze this potential issue.

## Questions to Authors

1. The paper states that the model is the first to surpass the latent variable WFST model by Dreyer et al. (2008) on the CELEX dataset. However, Dreyer et al. (2008) used a model specifically tailored for morphological string transduction. How does your model's performance compare when using the same feature set as Dreyer et al. (2008), rather than the morpho-syntactic features used in the paper?

2. The paper mentions that the model performs worse on languages with vowel harmony (e.g., Hungarian, Arabic). Could you provide specific examples of these failure cases and explain why the monotonic alignment assumption doesn't work well for these languages? How might the model be extended to handle such cases?

3. The paper claims that the hard attention model learns representations that are less sensitive to positional information than the soft attention model. How does this relate to the model's ability to handle non-monotonic alignments (e.g., when there are internal stem changes that require non-monotonic alignments)? The paper mentions that the bi-directional encoder captures some non-monotone relations, but it would be helpful to see specific examples.

## Overall Recommendation
This is a strong paper with significant empirical results, particularly on the small CELEX dataset, and insightful analysis of the models' representations. The contributions to the field of morphological inflection generation are clear and well-supported by the experiments. The main weakness is the lack of novelty in the core approach compared to prior work on monotonic alignment. However, the paper's clear demonstration of the practical benefits of this approach (especially for low-resource settings) and the insightful analysis of the models' behavior make it a valuable contribution to the field.

I recommend acceptance with minor revisions to address the concerns about novelty and to provide more depth on the failure cases and ablation studies. The paper's strengths significantly outweigh its weaknesses, and the work provides meaningful advances for morphological inflection tasks with limited training data.
