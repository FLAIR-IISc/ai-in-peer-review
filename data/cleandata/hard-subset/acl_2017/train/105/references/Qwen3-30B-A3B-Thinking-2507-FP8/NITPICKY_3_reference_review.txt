

# ACL Review: Morphological Inflection Generation with Hard Monotonic Attention

## Summary
This paper proposes a neural model for morphological inflection generation that uses a "hard monotonic attention" mechanism, where the model either writes a character to the output or advances the attention pointer to the next input character. The authors claim their model outperforms previous neural and non-neural approaches on three morphological inflection datasets, especially on the small CELEX dataset. The paper presents three main contributions: a hard attention model for nearly-monotonic sequence-to-sequence learning, evaluation showing state-of-the-art results, and an analysis comparing their model with soft attention models.

## Main Contributions (as I see them)
1. **Empirical evaluation showing strong results on the CELEX dataset** - This is the most significant contribution as it demonstrates the model's ability to perform well with very small training sets, a notable gap in previous neural approaches.
2. **Analysis of the learned alignments and representations** - The paper provides interesting insights into how the models encode linguistic information, particularly regarding character positions.
3. **The hard attention model itself** - While the model has similarities to prior work on sequence transduction, it's a reasonable extension for the specific task of morphological inflection.

## Strengths
1. **Compelling results on the CELEX dataset** - The paper demonstrates that their model is the first neural approach to surpass the latent variable WFST model of Dreyer et al. (2008) on this small dataset, which is a significant achievement given that previous neural models struggled with data sparsity. The results in Table 1 are strong and clearly show the advantage of the hard attention mechanism.

2. **Insightful analysis of learned alignments** - The comparison between the alignments learned by the hard attention model and soft attention model (Figures 3 and 4) is valuable for understanding the differences between these approaches. The observation that the hard attention model doesn't need to encode positional information as strongly as the soft attention model is particularly interesting.

3. **Clear presentation of the model architecture** - The model definition in Section 2 is generally well-structured, with good explanations of the encoder, decoder, and control mechanism.

## Weaknesses

### 1. Missing Figures (Major Issue)
The paper references multiple figures that are not included in the submission (Figures 2, 3, and 4), which are critical to the paper's claims. Figure 2 is described as showing "Learning curves for the soft and hard attention models on the first fold of the CELEX dataset," which is essential for evaluating the overfitting claims. Figure 3 is referenced in the analysis section as showing "alignments predicted by our model" and is crucial for supporting the claim that the alignments are monotonic. Figure 4 is referenced in the representation analysis section. Without these figures, the paper's empirical claims cannot be properly evaluated, making it impossible to verify the main results.

### 2. Inconsistent Notation and Terminology
There are several inconsistencies in notation and terminology that undermine clarity:
- The paper uses "x1:n" and "x1...xn" interchangeably to represent sequences.
- In Table 1, the column headers use "SIA" but the footnote explains "13SIA" (the paper uses "SIA" in the table but "13SIA" in the footnote).
- The paper states "Σs = Σy ∪ {step}" but then treats "step" as a control operation rather than a character in the output vocabulary, creating confusion about whether "step" is part of the output sequence.
- The model definition says "we replace the search for a sequence of letters with a sequence of write and step actions," but the "step" action is not a letter, creating conceptual confusion.

### 3. Lack of Statistical Significance Testing
The paper claims "significant" improvements over baselines (e.g., "our model is significantly more accurate than the model of Yu et al. (2016)"), but provides no statistical significance tests to back up these claims. In NLP, this is a standard requirement for empirical claims, especially when comparing to baselines. The absence of statistical tests makes it difficult to assess whether the improvements are truly meaningful.

### 4. Formatting and Typos in References
The reference section contains numerous formatting inconsistencies:
- References have inconsistent year formatting: "Kann and Schütze.,? \Q2016\E" instead of "Kann and Schütze (2016)"
- The reference for "García-Martínez et al., 2016" has a typo in the name ("Garcı́a-Martı́nez" instead of "García-Martínez")
- References have inconsistent use of "et al." vs "et al." with periods
- Some references have "?", "\Q", and "E" that appear to be LaTeX compilation artifacts

### 5. Missing Implementation Details
The paper states that "The implementation details for our models are described in the supplementary material section of this paper," but the supplementary material is not provided. Key details that should be in the main paper include:
- Number of training epochs
- Hyperparameters (learning rate, batch size, etc.)
- Details about the character alignment process (Sudoh et al., 2013 implementation specifics)
- How they handled the "step" action in the training process

## Questions to Authors
1. Could you please provide the missing figures (Figures 2, 3, and 4) in your revised submission? These are critical for evaluating your claims about learning curves, alignments, and representation analysis.

2. Could you clarify whether the "step" operation is considered part of the output vocabulary or a control operation? If it's a control operation, why is it included in Σs = Σy ∪ {step}?

3. Could you provide statistical significance tests (e.g., t-tests) for the performance differences claimed in the paper, especially for the CELEX results?

4. Could you clarify the inconsistency between the table headers ("SIA") and the footnote explanations ("13SIA") in Table 1?

5. Could you provide the key hyperparameters used for training (number of epochs, learning rate, etc.) in the main paper, rather than referring to supplementary material?

## Recommendation
**Reject**

While the paper presents an interesting model and demonstrates strong empirical results on the CELEX dataset, the presence of multiple serious issues makes it difficult to properly evaluate the claims. The most critical issue is the missing figures (Figures 2, 3, and 4), which are essential for verifying the main results and analysis. Without these figures, the paper cannot be properly reviewed. Additionally, the inconsistencies in notation and terminology, lack of statistical significance testing, and formatting issues in the references significantly undermine the paper's credibility. As a perfectionist reviewer, I cannot recommend acceptance with these issues unresolved.
