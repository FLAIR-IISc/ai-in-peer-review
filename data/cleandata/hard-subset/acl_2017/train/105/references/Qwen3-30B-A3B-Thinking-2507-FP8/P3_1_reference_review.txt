

# Peer Review: "Morphological Inflection Generation with Hard Monotonic Attention"

## Summary

This paper proposes a novel neural sequence-to-sequence model for morphological inflection generation that uses a "hard monotonic attention" mechanism. The key innovation is modeling the nearly monotonic alignment between input and output character sequences (common in morphological inflection) by having the model choose between "writing" an output character or "advancing" an attention pointer over the input sequence. The model uses pre-learned character-level alignments (from Sudoh et al., 2013) to guide training, rather than learning alignments jointly with transduction. The authors evaluate on three datasets: CELEX (very small, 500 examples per inflection type), Wiktionary (larger, up to 360k examples), and SIGMORPHON 2016 (multi-language). They claim their model is the first neural model to surpass the latent variable WFST model on CELEX and achieves strong results on the other datasets.

## Main Contributions (as I see them)

1. **Demonstrating the effectiveness of hard monotonic attention for morphological inflection with extremely small training sets** - The paper makes a compelling case for the value of their approach on the CELEX dataset, which has been a challenging low-resource benchmark where previous neural models consistently underperformed compared to non-neural approaches.

2. **Insightful analysis of learned alignments and representations** - The paper provides a detailed comparison of the alignments learned by their model versus soft attention models, and an analysis of how position information is encoded in the representations, which explains why their model performs better with limited data.

3. **Comprehensive evaluation across multiple datasets** - The paper evaluates on three distinct datasets with varying sizes and linguistic characteristics, providing a thorough assessment of the model's strengths and limitations.

## Strengths

1. **Significant empirical contribution on a low-resource benchmark**: The CELEX dataset is notoriously small (only 500 training examples per inflection type), and previous neural models consistently underperformed compared to non-neural latent variable WFST models. The authors demonstrate that their model outperforms all previous approaches on this dataset, which is a major contribution to the field. This is particularly important for morphologically rich languages where data sparsity is a common issue, as it demonstrates that neural models can be effective even with very limited training data.

2. **Insightful analysis of learned alignments and representations**: The paper provides a valuable analysis of how the model encodes position information compared to soft attention models. The SVD projections (Figures 4c, 4d) clearly show that the hard attention model encodes position information less explicitly in the input representations because it uses step actions to track position, while the soft attention model encodes position information more strongly in the input representations. This analysis explains why the hard attention model generalizes better with small datasets, as it doesn't rely on the model learning positional information through the encoder.

3. **Comprehensive evaluation across multiple datasets**: The paper evaluates on three distinct datasets with varying characteristics (CELEX: very small, Wiktionary: large, SIGMORPHON: multi-language), providing a thorough assessment of the model's strengths and limitations. The language-specific analysis in Table 3 (particularly the distinction between suffixing+stem-change languages and languages with vowel/consonant harmony) is insightful and well-supported by the results.

## Weaknesses

1. **Limited novelty of the core approach**: While the paper presents the hard monotonic attention mechanism as novel, it's closely related to existing work on neural sequence transduction (RNN Transducer, Yu et al., 2016). The key difference is using pre-learned alignments rather than learning alignments jointly, but this is a relatively small modification compared to the existing literature. The paper doesn't sufficiently differentiate their approach from these related works, and the claim of novelty is not well-supported. The paper would be stronger if it more clearly articulated how this specific implementation differs from and improves upon existing sequence transduction models.

2. **Lack of ablation studies**: The paper doesn't provide ablation studies to isolate the contribution of key components. For example, how much does the hard attention mechanism contribute compared to just using a bi-directional encoder with a standard decoder? How much does the pre-learned alignment help compared to learning alignments jointly? Without these ablation studies, it's difficult to determine which aspects of the model are truly responsible for the performance gains. This is a significant methodological weakness.

3. **Limited discussion of limitations and failure cases**: The paper presents strong results but doesn't thoroughly discuss where the model fails or what its limitations are. For example, on the SIGMORPHON dataset, the model performs slightly worse on languages like Georgian, Turkish, and Arabic (which use vowel/consonant harmony). The paper mentions this but doesn't provide concrete examples of failure cases or discuss why the model struggles with these cases. A more comprehensive discussion of limitations would strengthen the paper's contribution.

## Questions to Authors

1. The paper claims that the hard attention model "conditions on the entire input sequence" using the bi-directional encoder. However, the soft attention model also conditions on the entire input sequence. Could you clarify the specific advantage of the hard attention mechanism over soft attention in terms of how the model conditions on the input sequence?

2. The paper notes that the model performs less well on languages with vowel/consonant harmony (like Georgian and Turkish). Could you provide specific examples of inflection errors on these languages and explain why the model struggles with them? What would need to be changed to improve performance on these languages?

3. The paper uses pre-learned alignments from Sudoh et al. (2013), but doesn't discuss how sensitive the model is to the quality of these alignments. If the alignment is imperfect, how does this affect the model's performance? Would it be possible to learn alignments jointly with the transduction process in a way that's less computationally intensive than existing methods (like the forward-backward approach used in RNN Transducer)?

## Overall Recommendation

This paper makes a valuable contribution to the morphological inflection generation task, particularly for low-resource settings. The empirical results on the CELEX dataset are compelling, and the analysis of learned alignments and representations provides important insights into what neural models learn for this task. However, the novelty claim is somewhat weak, and the lack of ablation studies limits the ability to assess the specific contributions of the model components.

**Recommendation: Accept (with minor revisions)**

The paper is well-written, the experiments are thorough, and the results are significant for a challenging low-resource benchmark. The authors should address the weaknesses mentioned above, particularly by strengthening the novelty claim and providing ablation studies to isolate the contribution of key components. The analysis of the learned representations is particularly strong and represents a valuable contribution to the field.

The paper would be significantly strengthened by a more detailed discussion of the model's limitations and failure cases, as well as a clearer comparison to related sequence transduction models. With these revisions, this paper would make a strong contribution to ACL.
