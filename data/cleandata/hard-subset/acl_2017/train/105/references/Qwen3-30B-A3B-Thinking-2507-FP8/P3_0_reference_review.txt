

# Peer Review: "Morphological Inflection Generation with Hard Monotonic Attention"

## Summary and Main Contributions

This paper proposes a novel neural model for morphological inflection generation that explicitly models nearly monotonic character alignments between input and output sequences. The model uses a bi-directional RNN encoder and a decoder with a hard attention mechanism that decides at each step whether to write an output character or advance the attention pointer to the next input character.

The main contributions of this work, as I see them, are:

1. **Demonstration that a simple hard monotonic attention model can outperform previous neural models on small datasets** (CELEX), specifically being the first neural model to surpass the latent variable WFST model by Dreyer et al. (2008) on this dataset with only 500 training examples per inflection type.

2. **Insightful analysis of the representations learned by the model**, particularly how the hard attention model encodes position information differently than soft attention models, which provides valuable understanding of what these models learn for the inflection task.

3. **Robust performance across multiple datasets** (CELEX, Wiktionary, SIGMORPHON2016), showing the model's effectiveness not just on small datasets but also on larger, more diverse datasets.

## Strengths

1. **Significant improvement on small datasets**: The paper's most compelling contribution is the demonstration that their model outperforms previous neural approaches on the CELEX dataset, which has only 500 training examples per inflection type. This is a critical contribution because previous neural models (including the authors' own soft-attention baseline) consistently underperformed the latent variable WFST model on this small dataset. The paper shows that their model achieves 89.44% exact match accuracy on average across all inflection types, compared to the previous best (LAT) at 88.3%, which is a statistically significant improvement. This addresses a long-standing challenge in neural NLP for morphologically rich languages where small datasets are common.

2. **Insightful representation analysis**: The paper provides a thorough analysis of the representations learned by both the hard and soft attention models using SVD dimensionality reduction. This analysis reveals that the hard attention model encodes character identity more distinctly (with more isolated character clusters) and encodes position information less strongly than the soft attention model, which aligns with the authors' claim that position information is more explicitly handled through the step operations in their model. This analysis goes beyond typical model comparison and provides valuable theoretical insights into how these models function.

3. **Clear demonstration of the monotonic alignment assumption**: The paper effectively demonstrates through examples (Figure 3) that the alignments learned by the hard attention model are linguistically meaningful and match the monotonic nature of morphological inflection in the languages studied. The analysis of specific morphological phenomena like one-to-many alignments (e.g., "flog"→"fliege") and deletions (e.g., "legte"→"lege") provides concrete evidence that the model captures relevant linguistic phenomena.

4. **Comprehensive experimental evaluation**: The paper evaluates the model on three well-established morphological inflection datasets (CELEX, Wiktionary, SIGMORPHON2016) with careful comparison to previous state-of-the-art models. The results are presented clearly with appropriate statistical significance considerations, and the analysis of performance across different language types (e.g., suffixing vs. agglutinative languages) provides valuable insights into the model's strengths and limitations.

## Weaknesses

1. **Lack of clear novelty distinction from prior work**: While the paper cites Graves (2012) and Yu et al. (2016) as related work, it does not sufficiently distinguish its contribution from these prior approaches. The core idea of using a monotonic alignment with a control mechanism (step/write actions) is very similar to the RNN Transducer (Graves, 2012) and the model by Yu et al. (2016), which also use step operations. The main difference appears to be that this paper uses independently learned alignments rather than jointly learning alignments and transduction. However, this distinction is not clearly articulated as a novel contribution, and the paper doesn't sufficiently explain why this specific approach (using precomputed alignments) is better than the joint learning approach used in prior work.

2. **Missing ablation studies**: The paper lacks crucial ablation studies to isolate the contribution of each component of the model. For example, it would be valuable to know:
   - How much the bi-directional encoder contributes to the performance compared to a unidirectional encoder
   - How much the hard attention mechanism contributes compared to a soft attention mechanism
   - How the model performs when using a simpler alignment method (rather than the character alignment model of Sudoh et al., 2013)
   Without these ablations, it's difficult to determine which aspects of the model are truly responsible for the performance gains.

3. **Overstated claims about "first to surpass"**: The paper claims to be "the first neural model to surpass the performance of the weighted FST model with latent variables specifically tailored for the task by Dreyer et al. (2008)" on CELEX. However, Rastogi et al. (2016) also claimed to surpass this model, but required twice as much data. The paper doesn't clearly explain why their model is better than Rastogi et al.'s in terms of data efficiency, or how their approach differs fundamentally from Rastogi et al.'s neural WFST model. The paper states that Rastogi et al. "surpassed the latent variable model only when given twice as much data to train on," but it doesn't provide evidence that their model requires significantly less data to achieve similar or better performance.

4. **Limited discussion of failure cases**: The paper provides a good analysis of the model's success cases, but it lacks a thorough discussion of where the model fails. For example, on the SIGMORPHON2016 dataset, the model performs slightly worse than the soft attention model on languages with complex phenomena like vowel harmony and consonant harmony. However, the paper doesn't analyze why this is the case or provide specific examples of these failure cases, which would be valuable for understanding the model's limitations.

## Questions to Authors

1. The paper claims that their model is the first neural model to surpass Dreyer et al.'s (2008) latent variable WFST on CELEX. However, Rastogi et al. (2016) also claimed to surpass this model with enough data. Could you clarify the exact data requirements (number of training examples) for both models to surpass Dreyer et al.'s performance, and explain why your model achieves this with less data?

2. In Table 3, your model performs slightly worse than your soft attention implementation on some languages (e.g., Arabic, Maltese). Could you provide specific examples of failure cases for these languages, and explain why the hard attention model struggles with these particular morphological phenomena?

3. The paper states that "the model can then be trained using straightforward cross-entropy loss" without needing to learn alignment. However, the alignment is still required as a pre-processing step. How would your model scale to settings where obtaining character-level alignments is difficult or impossible (e.g., for low-resource languages)? Could you provide an estimate of the additional computational cost of the alignment step compared to the main model training?

4. The paper mentions that "the recurrent nature of the decoder, together with a dedicated feedback connection that passes the last prediction to the next decoder step explicitly, enables the model to also condition the current output on all the previous outputs at each prediction step." This is a standard feature of sequence-to-sequence models. Could you clarify how this differs from the standard encoder-decoder architecture with attention?

## Overall Recommendation

The paper presents a well-motivated and well-executed study of a neural model for morphological inflection generation. The most significant contribution is the demonstration of superior performance on small datasets, which addresses a critical limitation of previous neural approaches. The analysis of the representations learned by the model is particularly strong and provides valuable theoretical insights.

However, the paper would benefit from a clearer articulation of the novelty compared to prior work, more thorough ablation studies, and a deeper analysis of failure cases. With these improvements, the paper would make a strong contribution to the field.

**Recommendation: Accept** (with minor revisions to address the weaknesses noted above). The paper's core contribution on small datasets is significant, and the representation analysis is insightful. The weaknesses are important but not deal-breakers for acceptance.
