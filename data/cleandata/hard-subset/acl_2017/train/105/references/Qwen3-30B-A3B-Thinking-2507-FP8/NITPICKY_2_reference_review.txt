

# Review of "Morphological Inflection Generation with Hard Monotonic Attention"

## Summary
This paper proposes a neural sequence-to-sequence model for morphological inflection generation that uses a "hard monotonic attention" mechanism. The model is designed to handle the monotonic alignment common in morphological inflection tasks by using a control mechanism that either writes an output symbol or advances the attention pointer. The authors claim their model outperforms previous approaches on the CELEX dataset with very small training sets and achieves state-of-the-art results on three morphological inflection datasets.

## Main Contributions (as I see them)
1. A neural sequence-to-sequence model with a dedicated control mechanism for nearly-monotonic alignment in morphological inflection generation
2. An analysis of how the model learns representations and alignments compared to soft attention models
3. A demonstration of improved performance on small datasets (CELEX) where previous neural models struggled

## Strengths
1. The paper presents a well-motivated approach to morphological inflection generation, correctly identifying the monotonic nature of the alignment in this task. The authors effectively argue that the monotonic structure of inflection generation is a key characteristic that should be exploited by models.

2. The experimental evaluation is comprehensive, covering three different datasets (CELEX, Wiktionary, and SIGMORPHON2016) with varying amounts of training data. The authors provide a thorough comparison to both neural and non-neural baselines.

3. The analysis of learned representations (Figure 4) is insightful and provides valuable intuition about how different attention mechanisms encode linguistic information. The comparison between hard and soft attention in terms of positional encoding is particularly interesting.

## Weaknesses

1. **Inconsistent and Incomplete Methodology Description**: The paper claims to use "hard attention" but the model actually uses a deterministic control mechanism with a step action. The term "hard attention" is misleading and inconsistent with standard usage in the literature (where "hard attention" typically refers to selecting a single input without a probability distribution). The paper should clarify this terminology to avoid confusion. Additionally, the paper lacks sufficient detail about the character alignment process used to derive the gold transduction+control sequences. Without knowing how the alignment handles non-monotonic cases, it's unclear how robust the approach is.

2. **Insufficient Statistical Justification**: The paper claims to establish a new state of the art, but the reported improvements are marginal (e.g., 1.1% average accuracy improvement on CELEX over the previous best model). The paper does not provide statistical significance tests for these improvements, which is essential for claiming a new state of the art. Without p-values or confidence intervals, it's impossible to determine if the reported improvements are meaningful or just due to random fluctuations in the small test sets.

3. **Inadequate Comparison to Recent Work**: The paper claims to be the first neural model to surpass the latent variable WFST model on CELEX with small training sets, but it doesn't compare to all relevant recent neural models. For example, the paper doesn't compare to the work by Kann et al. (2016) which also achieved strong results on CELEX, and it's unclear why this comparison is missing. The paper also fails to explain why their model outperforms the soft attention model on small datasets when the soft attention model has been shown to be effective on larger datasets.

4. **Formatting and Technical Inconsistencies**: The paper contains numerous formatting errors that undermine its professionalism. References have inconsistent formatting (e.g., "Garcı́a-Martı́nez" vs. "García-Martínez"), the paper uses "et al\." with backslashes in some references but not others, and there are typos in the text (e.g., "Minkov et al., 2007; Toutanova et al., 2008; Clifton and Sarkar, 2011; Fraser et al., 2012; Chahuneau et al., 2013" has inconsistent spacing around semicolons). These are minor but important details that should be fixed in a professional publication.

5. **Lack of Model Complexity Analysis**: The paper claims their model is "simpler" than the soft attention model, but it doesn't provide any quantitative comparison of model complexity, training time, or inference speed. Without this information, it's impossible to assess the trade-offs between model simplicity and performance.

## Questions to Authors

1. The paper claims to use "hard attention" but describes a control mechanism with a step action. How does this differ from the "hard attention" used in other works (e.g., the RNN Transducer)? Could you clarify this terminology and explain why it's not misleading?

2. The paper reports marginal improvements over previous work (e.g., 1.1% average accuracy improvement on CELEX). Were these improvements statistically significant? If not, how do you justify claiming a new state of the art?

3. The paper states that their model "is the first neural model to surpass the performance of the weighted FST model with latent variables specifically tailored for the task by Dreyer et al. (2008)." Why was the Kann and Schütze (2016a) model not included in the comparison? The authors' own Table 1 shows that MED (which is Kann and Schütze 2016a) is already outperformed by their model.

## Final Recommendation

I recommend rejection. While the paper presents an interesting approach to morphological inflection generation and provides a good analysis of the model's behavior, it contains multiple significant flaws that prevent it from being accepted for publication. The inconsistent terminology, lack of statistical significance testing, and formatting errors are particularly concerning for a paper claiming to establish a new state of the art. The marginal performance improvements reported are not sufficient to overcome these methodological weaknesses.

The authors have made a valuable contribution to the understanding of attention mechanisms in sequence-to-sequence models, but the paper as written does not adequately support its claims or provide sufficient detail for reproducibility. These issues need to be addressed before the work can be considered for publication.
