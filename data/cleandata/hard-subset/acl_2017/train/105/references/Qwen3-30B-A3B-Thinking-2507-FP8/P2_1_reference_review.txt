

# Review of "Morphological Inflection Generation with Hard Monotonic Attention"

## Summary and Main Contributions

This paper proposes a novel neural sequence-to-sequence model for morphological inflection generation that directly models nearly monotonic alignments between input and output character sequences. The authors introduce a "hard monotonic attention" mechanism where the model either writes a symbol to the output or advances the attention pointer to the next input state. The key contributions, in order of strength, are:

1. A well-motivated model that directly handles the nearly monotonic alignment structure common in morphological inflection tasks, with a clear rationale for why this is appropriate for the task.
2. Empirical demonstration that the model significantly outperforms previous neural and non-neural state-of-the-art approaches on the CELEX dataset (with only 500 training examples per inflection type), being the first neural model to surpass the latent variable WFST model of Dreyer et al. (2008).
3. Insightful analysis of the representations learned by both the hard attention and soft attention models, revealing how they encode information about character types and positions.

## Strengths

1. **Significant improvement on small datasets**: The paper makes a strong case for the value of their approach through compelling results on the CELEX dataset, which has only 500 training examples per inflection type. The authors show their model achieves 89.44% accuracy compared to the previous best of 88.3% (Dreyer et al., 2008), and they correctly note this is the first neural model to surpass the performance of the non-neural latent variable WFST model on this dataset. This is particularly important because previous neural models (Kann and Schütze, 2016a; Rastogi et al., 2016) struggled with small datasets. Figure 2 effectively demonstrates how their model avoids overfitting compared to the soft attention model, which is a key contribution to the field.

2. **Strong theoretical justification for the model**: The authors provide a clear and well-reasoned argument for why their hard monotonic attention mechanism is appropriate for morphological inflection. They correctly identify that inflection often involves suffixing morphology with nearly monotonic alignments (e.g., adding suffixes to stems), and their model directly models this structure rather than learning the alignment during training. The model's design (bi-directional encoder, control mechanism for step/write operations) is well-motivated and directly addresses the specific characteristics of the task.

3. **Insightful analysis of learned representations**: The analysis in Section 5 is a standout contribution. The authors use SVD to visualize character representations from both their model and the soft attention model, revealing how the two models encode character information and positional information differently. This analysis provides valuable insights into what these models learn for the inflection task, particularly showing that the hard attention model encodes positional information less strongly in the input representations because it uses the step actions for this purpose. This goes beyond typical results reporting and contributes to our understanding of sequence-to-sequence models for this specific task.

## Weaknesses

1. **Limited novelty compared to prior work**: While the model is well-motivated for the specific task, the hard monotonic attention mechanism is closely related to the RNN Transducer (Graves, 2012) and Neural Transducer (Jaitly et al., 2015), which also use monotonic alignments. The paper doesn't sufficiently distinguish their model from these prior works or explain why their specific formulation is better suited for morphological inflection. The authors mention Graves (2012) and Jaitly et al. (2015) in the related work, but don't provide a clear comparison of their model to these approaches. This weakens the contribution claim of "novel model for nearly-monotonic sequence-to-sequence learning."

2. **Inadequate comparison to recent neural models**: The paper compares to several baselines but fails to include a direct comparison with the most recent neural sequence-to-sequence models for this task. For example, the authors mention Yu et al. (2016) in the introduction but don't include their results in the main comparison tables. The paper claims their model "is also significantly more accurate than the model of Yu et al. (2016)," but this isn't clearly demonstrated in the results. This omission makes it difficult to assess the true novelty and superiority of their approach.

3. **Lack of ablation studies**: The paper doesn't provide ablation studies to demonstrate the importance of key components of their model. For example, it would be valuable to see how the model performs without the bi-directional encoder, without the control mechanism (i.e., using a standard soft attention model), or with different alignment methods. These studies would help validate the design choices and provide stronger evidence for the claimed improvements.

## Questions to Authors

1. How does your hard monotonic attention model specifically differ from the RNN Transducer (Graves, 2012) and Neural Transducer (Jaitly et al., 2015) in terms of architecture and performance? Why is your model better suited for morphological inflection than these prior approaches?

2. In Table 2, you claim your model achieves the highest average accuracy across all inflection types on the Wiktionary dataset. However, the table shows your model as slightly better than the best baseline (FTND16) in some columns but worse in others (e.g., 97.35 vs 97.72 for German verbs). Can you clarify why you claim it's the best performing model overall?

3. The difference between your model and the best soft attention model on CELEX (89.44% vs 87.62%) is relatively small. Given the CELEX dataset is very small (only 500 training examples per inflection type), could this small difference be due to random variation rather than a meaningful improvement? Would a statistical significance test be appropriate here?

## Overall Assessment

This is a well-written paper with strong empirical results, particularly on the challenging CELEX dataset where the authors demonstrate a significant improvement over previous neural models. The model design is well-motivated for the specific task of morphological inflection, and the analysis of learned representations provides valuable insights. However, the novelty claim is weakened by the lack of clear distinction from prior work on monotonic sequence-to-sequence models, and the paper would benefit from more thorough comparisons to recent neural models and ablation studies. Despite these weaknesses, the paper makes a meaningful contribution to the field of morphological inflection generation, particularly for low-resource settings, and would be a valuable addition to ACL. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions to address the weaknesses noted above.
