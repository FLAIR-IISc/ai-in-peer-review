

# Peer Review: Morphological Inflection Generation with Hard Monotonic Attention

## Summary
This paper proposes a hard attention model for morphological inflection generation that explicitly models monotonic alignments between input and output character sequences. The model uses a bi-directional RNN encoder and a decoder with a dedicated control mechanism that either writes an output character or advances the attention pointer. The authors claim their model outperforms previous neural and non-neural approaches, especially on small datasets like CELEX (where it surpasses the latent variable WFST model of Dreyer et al., 2008), and also performs well on larger datasets like Wiktionary and SIGMORPHON 2016.

## Main Contributions
1. Demonstrating that a simple hard attention model with explicitly modeled monotonic alignments can outperform more complex soft attention models on small morphological inflection datasets, addressing a practical problem in low-resource NLP.
2. Providing a thorough analysis of learned alignments and representations, showing how the hard attention model encodes positional information differently than soft attention models.
3. Establishing a new state-of-the-art on three established morphological inflection datasets while being the first neural model to surpass the latent variable WFST model on the CELEX dataset.

## Strengths

1. **Practical significance for low-resource settings**: The paper's most valuable contribution is addressing the critical problem of morphological inflection generation in data-scarce scenarios. The demonstrated performance on CELEX (only 500 training examples per inflection type) is particularly significant, as it shows a neural model can outperform traditional FST-based approaches without requiring large training sets. This has direct implications for morphologically rich languages where data sparsity is a major challenge.

2. **Clear methodology and insightful analysis**: The paper provides a well-structured description of the model and a particularly strong analysis comparing the hard attention model to soft attention models. The analysis of learned alignments (Figure 3) and representations (Figure 4) provides valuable insights into how these models operate, showing that both models learn monotonic alignments but encode positional information differently.

3. **Comprehensive experimental evaluation**: The paper evaluates on three different datasets (CELEX, Wiktionary, SIGMORPHON 2016) with thorough comparison to previous state-of-the-art models. The results consistently show that their model performs well across different morphological systems and dataset sizes, providing strong empirical support for their claims.

## Weaknesses

1. **Lack of sufficient novelty distinction**: The hard attention mechanism is not entirely novel, as it closely resembles the RNN Transducer (Graves, 2012) and Neural Transducer (Jaitly et al., 2015). While the paper cites these works, it doesn't sufficiently distinguish its approach from them. The key difference appears to be the use of independently learned alignments rather than joint training, but the paper doesn't make a compelling case for why this is a significant improvement for morphological inflection specifically.

2. **Overstated claims about "first neural model"**: The paper claims to be "the first neural model to surpass the performance of the weighted FST model with latent variables" on CELEX. However, the paper doesn't provide a clear comparison to Rastogi et al. (2016), which the authors themselves mention as having "suffered from the same issue on the CELEX dataset" and "surpassed the latent variable model only when given twice as much data." Without demonstrating that their model achieves better results than Rastogi et al. (2016) on the same amount of data, this claim is questionable.

3. **Limited analysis of model behavior**: While the paper provides an interesting analysis of learned alignments and representations, it doesn't sufficiently explain *why* the hard attention model performs better on small datasets. The claim that the soft attention model "overfits" on small datasets is plausible but not well-supported by evidence. A more detailed analysis of model capacity, complexity, or training dynamics would strengthen this argument.

## Questions to Authors

1. How does your model specifically differ from the RNN Transducer (Graves, 2012) in a way that's beneficial for morphological inflection generation? What architectural changes are most critical for the performance gains on this specific task?

2. You claim your model is the first neural model to surpass the Dreyer et al. (2008) model on CELEX, but don't compare to Rastogi et al. (2016) on the CELEX dataset. Can you clarify whether your model outperforms Rastogi et al. (2016) on the same amount of training data, and if not, what's the difference in data requirements?

3. In Figure 4, you note that the hard attention model encodes positional information less strongly than the soft attention model. Could you elaborate on how this affects the model's ability to handle non-monotonic morphological phenomena, and whether this is a limitation of your approach?

## Overall Assessment

This paper makes a valuable contribution to the morphological inflection generation task, particularly in low-resource settings. The practical significance of their approach for morphologically rich languages is clear, and their experimental results are well-supported. While the core model architecture isn't entirely novel, the careful analysis of how the model learns and the demonstration of superior performance on small datasets provides meaningful progress in the field. The paper would be strengthened by a more rigorous comparison to prior work on neural sequence transduction, particularly clarifying the distinction between their approach and the RNN Transducer framework. With these clarifications, this paper would be a strong contribution to ACL.
