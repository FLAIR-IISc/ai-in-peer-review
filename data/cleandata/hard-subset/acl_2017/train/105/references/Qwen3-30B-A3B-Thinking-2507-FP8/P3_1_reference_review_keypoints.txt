- Summary: Proposes a neural seq2seq model with hard monotonic attention that alternates between writing output characters and advancing an input pointer, trained with pre-learned character alignments and evaluated on CELEX, Wiktionary, and SIGMORPHON 2016.

- Contribution 1: Demonstrates hard monotonic attention is effective for extremely low-resource morphological inflection, outperforming prior models on CELEX.

- Contribution 2: Provides detailed analysis comparing learned alignments and representations versus soft attention, explaining better small-data generalization.

- Contribution 3: Offers comprehensive evaluation across small, large, and multilingual datasets, with language-specific analysis.

- Strength 1: Significant empirical improvement on the difficult low-resource CELEX benchmark, showing neural models can work with very limited data.

- Strength 2: Insightful analyses (e.g., SVD projections) showing how position information is represented differently in hard vs. soft attention models.

- Strength 3: Thorough multi-dataset evaluation and informative language-specific results distinguishing morphological phenomena.

- Weakness 1: Limited novelty—approach closely related to existing sequence transduction work and novelty mainly from using pre-learned alignments.

- Weakness 2: Lacks ablation studies to isolate contributions of hard attention, bi-directional encoder, and pre-learned alignments.

- Weakness 3: Insufficient discussion of limitations and concrete failure cases, especially for languages with vowel/consonant harmony where performance lags.

- Question 1: Clarify the specific advantage of hard attention over soft attention in how the model conditions on the entire input sequence.

- Question 2: Provide specific error examples and analysis for languages (e.g., Georgian, Turkish) where the model underperforms and suggest remedies.

- Question 3: Discuss sensitivity to alignment quality and whether alignments could be learned jointly with transduction efficiently.

- Overall recommendation: Accept with minor revisions—strong empirical and analysis contributions, but needs clearer novelty claims, ablation studies, and discussion of limitations.
