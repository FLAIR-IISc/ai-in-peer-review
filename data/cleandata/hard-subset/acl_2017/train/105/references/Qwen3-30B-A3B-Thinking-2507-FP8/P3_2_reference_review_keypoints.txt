- Proposes a neural model for morphological inflection using hard monotonic attention exploiting near-monotonic input-output alignments.  
- Shows state-of-the-art performance on low-resource CELEX (500 examples per inflection), outperforming a latent-variable WFST.  
- Uses pre-computed character-level alignments to decouple alignment and transduction, simplifying training.  
- Provides analysis showing hard attention encodes character identity and position differently from soft attention.  

- Strength: Strong empirical gains in the low-resource setting, addressing a key NLP challenge.  
- Strength: Clear evidence (learning curves) that hard attention avoids overfitting on small datasets compared to soft attention.  
- Strength: Insightful SVD visualization explaining representational differences and why hard attention may generalize better.  

- Weakness: Novelty is unclear given prior monotonic alignment work (e.g., Graves 2012, Yu et al. 2016); differences not sufficiently emphasized.  
- Weakness: Lacks ablation studies to isolate contributions of hard attention versus encoder architecture and training choices.  
- Weakness: Limited comparison to other monotonic alignment models like RNN Transducer or Neural Transducer.  
- Weakness: Explanation for why hard attention reduces overfitting is superficial and needs deeper analysis of model capacity.  

- Question: How does this model differ architecturally and in training from Graves' RNN Transducer, and how does it perform relative to it on CELEX?  
- Question: Can the authors better explain why hard attention yields more isolated character clusters in SVD and how that aids generalization?  
- Question: Why does an unconstrained neural model outperform a task-tailored latent-variable WFST—solely due to monotonic assumptions or other factors?  

- Recommendation: Accept with minor revisions to clarify novelty versus prior monotonic models and include ablation/comparative studies.
