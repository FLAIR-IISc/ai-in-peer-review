- Proposes a hard monotonic attention seq2seq model that either writes an output symbol or advances the input pointer to model near-monotonic alignments.  
- Empirically outperforms prior neural and non-neural state-of-the-art on CELEX (89.44% vs 88.3% Dreyer et al., 2008) with only 500 examples per inflection type.  
- Provides analysis showing how hard and soft attention models encode character and positional information differently using SVD visualizations.  
- Strong empirical gains on small datasets, with the hard-attention model avoiding overfitting compared to soft attention.  
- Well-motivated theoretical justification: model matches the nearly-monotonic nature of morphological inflection (e.g., suffixing).  
- Insightful representation analysis revealing that hard attention uses step actions for positional encoding, reducing positional information in input embeddings.  
- Novelty is limited: mechanism closely related to RNN Transducer (Graves, 2012) and Neural Transducer (Jaitly et al., 2015) without clear distinction.  
- Inadequate comparisons to recent neural baselines (e.g., Yu et al., 2016) and missing those results in main tables.  
- No ablation studies to justify design choices (bi-directional encoder, control mechanism, alignment method).  
- Question: clarify architectural/performance differences vs RNN/Neural Transducers and why this formulation is better for inflection.  
- Question: clarify the claim of highest average accuracy on Wiktionary given mixed per-language results in Table 2.  
- Question: is the CELEX improvement (89.44% vs 87.62%) statistically significant or could it be due to random variation given small training sizes?  
- Overall: well-written paper with meaningful contributions for low-resource morphological inflection and recommended for acceptance with minor revisions to address comparisons and ablations.
