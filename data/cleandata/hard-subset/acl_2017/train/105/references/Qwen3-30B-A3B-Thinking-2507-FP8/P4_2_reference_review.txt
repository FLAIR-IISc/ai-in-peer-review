

# Review of "Morphological Inflection Generation with Hard Monotonic Attention"

## Summary and Contributions

This paper proposes a neural model for morphological inflection generation that uses a "hard monotonic attention" mechanism to explicitly model the nearly monotonic alignment between input and output character sequences. The authors argue that their approach addresses a key limitation of previous neural models, which require large training sets to perform well on morphological inflection tasks.

The main contributions of this work (as assessed by the reviewer) are:

1. A hard monotonic attention mechanism specifically designed for morphological inflection generation, which better captures the nearly monotonic alignment patterns common in this task.
2. Empirical demonstration that this model outperforms previous neural and non-neural approaches on small datasets (CELEX), establishing a new state of the art on this dataset with only 500 training examples per inflection type.
3. Insightful analysis of the representations learned by the model, showing how hard attention and soft attention models encode different information about character positions and context.

## Strengths

1. **Significant empirical contribution on small data settings**: The paper demonstrates a clear and important advantage of their approach on the CELEX dataset, which is very small (500 examples per inflection type). Their model significantly outperforms both previous neural models (MED, NWFST) and the non-neural latent variable WFST model (Dreyer et al., 2008), establishing a new state of the art. This is a substantial contribution because most neural models struggle with such limited training data, and the authors claim to be the first neural model to surpass the latent variable WFST model on this dataset.

2. **Clear and insightful analysis of learned representations**: The paper provides valuable insights into how the model encodes information through the SVD projections of character representations (Figures 4a-d). The analysis shows that the hard attention model encodes positional information less strongly than soft attention models, which the authors attribute to the explicit use of step actions to model position information. This analysis goes beyond simple performance metrics and provides meaningful understanding of the model's behavior.

3. **Well-justified model design**: The authors provide a clear rationale for their hard attention mechanism, emphasizing how it better models the nearly monotonic alignment patterns common in morphological inflection. The model architecture is relatively simple and well-motivated, with the bi-directional encoder and dedicated control mechanism providing a natural way to model the task constraints.

## Weaknesses

1. **Overstated novelty claim**: The paper's novelty claim is overstated. The approach is closely related to prior work on neural sequence transduction, particularly Graves' RNN Transducer (2012) and Yu et al. (2016). The key difference (learning alignments independently rather than jointly) is not sufficiently justified as a novel contribution. The paper fails to clearly differentiate their approach from these prior works in the related work section.

2. **Insufficient explanation for small-data advantage**: The paper attributes the model's success on small datasets to "harnessing the monotonic alignments," but doesn't provide sufficient evidence or detailed analysis to support this claim. The authors don't demonstrate how the attention patterns of their model differ from soft attention models in ways that specifically help with small data settings, which is a critical gap in their argument.

3. **Inadequate comparison with previous neural models on equivalent data**: The authors claim to be the first neural model to surpass the latent variable WFST model on CELEX, but Rastogi et al. (2016) already showed their neural WFST model required twice as much data to surpass the latent variable model. The paper doesn't address whether their model would outperform the latent variable model with the same amount of data as Rastogi et al. used (1000 examples), which is crucial for evaluating their claim.

## Questions to Authors

1. The paper claims to be the first neural model to surpass the latent variable WFST model on CELEX, but Rastogi et al. (2016) showed their neural WFST model required twice as much data to surpass it. Can the authors provide a more detailed comparison of how their model performs on CELEX with the same amount of training data as Rastogi et al. used (1000 examples) to surpass the latent variable model?

2. The paper mentions using Sudoh et al.'s (2013) character alignment model, but doesn't explain why this is appropriate for morphological inflection generation. Could the authors provide more detail on how this alignment model works and why it's suitable for their task, particularly given the specific characteristics of morphological inflection?

3. The paper attributes the success of their model on small datasets to "harnessing the monotonic alignments," but doesn't provide sufficient evidence for how the attention patterns differ from soft attention models in ways that specifically help with small data. Could the authors provide additional analysis or examples to support this claim?

## Additional Comments

The paper is well-written and well-structured, with clear explanations of the model and experiments. The experimental setup is appropriate for the task, and the results are presented clearly. The analysis section is particularly strong and adds significant value beyond just reporting performance metrics. The model architecture is relatively simple and elegant, which is a positive aspect. However, the paper would benefit from a more thorough comparison with prior work on neural sequence transduction and a more detailed explanation of why the hard attention mechanism specifically helps with small data settings.
