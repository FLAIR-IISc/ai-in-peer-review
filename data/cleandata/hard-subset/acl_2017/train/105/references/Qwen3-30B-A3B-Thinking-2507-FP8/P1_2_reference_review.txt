

# Peer Review: "Morphological Inflection Generation with Hard Monotonic Attention"

## Summary
This paper proposes a neural sequence-to-sequence model for morphological inflection generation that uses a hard monotonic attention mechanism. The key insight is that morphological inflection typically exhibits a nearly monotonic alignment between input and output character sequences (e.g., suffixing morphology), allowing the model to directly model this alignment rather than learning it jointly with the transduction process. The model consists of a bi-directional encoder and a decoder with a control mechanism that either writes an output symbol or advances the attention pointer to the next input character. The authors claim this approach outperforms previous neural models, especially on small datasets, and is the first neural model to surpass the performance of the latent variable WFST model (Dreyer et al., 2008) on the CELEX dataset.

## Main Contributions (as I see them)
1. **Demonstration that a simple hard monotonic attention mechanism can outperform soft attention models on morphological inflection, particularly with limited training data.** This is the most significant contribution, as it shows a clear advantage for the task with small datasets (CELEX) where previous neural models struggled.
2. **Insightful analysis of how the model learns alignments and representations.** The paper provides valuable comparison between hard and soft attention models' learned alignments and representations, shedding light on what neural models learn for this specific task.
3. **Robust empirical validation across multiple datasets and morphological types.** The model achieves strong results across CELEX (small), Wiktionary (larger), and SIGMORPHON 2016 (multi-language) datasets, demonstrating its versatility.

## Strengths
1. **Strong empirical results on small datasets:** The CELEX results (Table 1) are particularly impressive, as this is the first neural model to surpass the latent variable WFST model (Dreyer et al., 2008) on this dataset. The learning curves in Figure 2 clearly show the hard attention model's advantage in avoiding overfitting with limited data, which is a critical contribution for morphologically rich languages where data is scarce.

2. **Insightful analysis of learned alignments and representations:** The analysis in Section 5 provides valuable insights into how the models function. The comparison of alignments in Figure 3 and the SVD projections of representations in Figure 4 reveal important differences between hard and soft attention models. The finding that the hard attention model encodes position information less strongly in its input representations (Figure 4c, 4d) but uses step actions to track position is particularly insightful and explains why it generalizes better with limited data.

3. **Comprehensive evaluation across multiple datasets and morphological types:** The paper evaluates on three different datasets (CELEX, Wiktionary, SIGMORPHON 2016) with varying sizes and morphological characteristics. The results show that the model performs well across all settings, with particular strength on suffixing+stem change languages (Russian, German, Spanish) where monotonic alignment is more natural. This demonstrates the model's robustness and versatility.

## Weaknesses
1. **Limited novelty compared to prior work:** The hard attention mechanism is similar to the RNN Transducer (Graves, 2012) and Yu et al. (2016), which the authors cite but don't sufficiently differentiate from. The key difference is using precomputed alignments rather than joint training, but this isn't presented as a novel contribution. The paper would benefit from a more thorough comparison with these prior approaches and a clearer statement of how this work advances the field beyond these existing methods.

2. **Insufficient analysis of why the model performs better:** While the paper shows the model performs better, it doesn't thoroughly investigate *why* it performs better beyond the monotonic alignment assumption. For example, it doesn't analyze how the quality of the precomputed alignments affects performance, or whether the model learns to correct errors in the alignment. The claim that the model "harnesses the monotonic alignments found in the data" is somewhat superficial without deeper analysis of how well the model actually uses these alignments.

3. **Comparison with recent neural approaches is incomplete:** The paper compares with some recent neural approaches (Kann and Schütze, 2016; Rastogi et al., 2016), but doesn't compare with the most recent state-of-the-art approaches for morphological inflection. For example, the SIGMORPHON 2017 and 2018 shared tasks featured more advanced neural models that might provide better baselines. The paper also doesn't compare with Transformer-based approaches, which have become dominant in sequence-to-sequence tasks.

## Questions to Authors
1. How does your model compare to recent Transformer-based approaches for morphological inflection? Have you considered applying your hard attention mechanism to Transformer architectures, which might benefit from the monotonic alignment assumption?

2. The paper states your model is the first neural model to surpass the latent variable WFST model on CELEX. However, Rastogi et al. (2016) also claim to surpass the latent variable model with more data. Could you clarify the exact data requirements and performance differences between your model and Rastogi et al.'s approach?

3. The analysis in Section 5 shows the hard attention model has more isolated character clusters (Figure 4a, 4b). Could you elaborate on how this relates to the model's performance on small datasets? Is this a cause or a consequence of better generalization, and how might this insight inform future model design?

## Additional Comments
The paper is well-written, clearly organized, and the experiments are thorough. The implementation details and code availability are good practices. The analysis of the learned representations (Figure 4) is particularly strong and provides valuable insights that would benefit the field.

The paper makes a valuable contribution to the understanding of neural models for morphological inflection, particularly regarding the relationship between alignment assumptions and model performance with limited data. While the novelty is somewhat limited compared to prior work on hard attention mechanisms, the empirical results and analysis are strong enough to make a meaningful contribution to the field.

The paper would benefit from a more thorough comparison with the most recent neural approaches for this task and a more detailed explanation of how the hard attention mechanism differs from prior work in a way that justifies the claimed novelty.
