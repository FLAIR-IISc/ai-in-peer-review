

# Review of "Morphological Inflection Generation with Hard Monotonic Attention"

## Summary
This paper proposes a neural sequence-to-sequence model for morphological inflection generation that uses a "hard monotonic attention" mechanism instead of standard soft attention. The model directly models the alignment between input and output character sequences, using a control mechanism to either write an output symbol or advance the attention pointer. The authors claim their model outperforms previous neural and non-neural approaches, especially on small datasets like CELEX, where they claim it's the first neural model to surpass the latent variable WFST model by Dreyer et al. (2008).

## Main Contributions (as I see them)
1. Thorough empirical evaluation across three well-established morphological inflection datasets, including a challenging small-data setting (CELEX)
2. Insightful analysis of alignments and representations learned by the model, providing valuable understanding of how neural models work for this task
3. Demonstration that a simple hard attention mechanism can work well on small datasets where other neural models struggle

## Strengths
1. The paper provides a comprehensive empirical evaluation across multiple datasets (CELEX, Wiktionary, SIGMORPHON) with detailed results and comparisons to previous state-of-the-art approaches. The evaluation is thorough and well-structured, making it easy to understand the model's performance across different settings.

2. The analysis of alignments (Figure 3) and representations (Figure 4) is particularly strong and insightful. The authors provide a clear comparison of how the hard attention model and soft attention model learn different representations, shedding light on what information these models encode for the morphological inflection task. This analysis contributes significantly to our understanding of neural sequence-to-sequence models for morphological tasks.

3. The paper acknowledges the limitations of their approach and provides a linguistic explanation for the performance differences across languages (e.g., suffixing vs. non-suffixing languages), which adds credibility to the work and demonstrates a good understanding of the task's linguistic aspects.

## Weaknesses
1. **Lack of true novelty**: The core idea of "hard monotonic attention" is not truly novel. The paper acknowledges the RNN Transducer (Graves, 2012) and Neural Transducer (Jaitly et al., 2015) in related work, but fails to provide a meaningful comparison with these approaches. The authors claim their model "is the first neural model to surpass the performance of the weighted FST model" on CELEX, but the paper itself states that "the neural WFST model by Rastogi et al. (2016) also suffered from the same issue on the CELEX dataset, and surpassed the latent variable model only when given twice as much data to train on." This suggests the authors' model doesn't solve a fundamental problem but merely works better on a very small dataset that most neural models struggle with, which is not a strong enough claim for a major contribution.

2. **Insufficient justification for hard attention**: The paper claims the hard attention mechanism is superior because it "allows the network to jointly align and transduce while using a focused representation at each step." However, their own analysis (Figure 3) shows that the soft attention model also finds monotonic alignments, undermining the premise that hard attention is necessary for monotonic alignment. The paper doesn't provide sufficient evidence that the hard attention mechanism itself is the key factor for improved performance, rather than just the monotonic constraint.

3. **Misleading claims about novelty**: The paper's claim that their model is "the first neural model to surpass the performance of the weighted FST model" on CELEX is misleading. The paper acknowledges that the neural WFST model by Rastogi et al. (2016) also performed poorly on CELEX but surpassed the latent variable model with twice as much data. The authors' model doesn't represent a fundamental advance over previous neural approaches but rather works better on this specific small dataset, which doesn't constitute a major contribution in my view.

4. **Lack of comparison with similar approaches**: The paper fails to compare with the RNN Transducer (Graves, 2012), which is a very similar approach using monotonic alignment. This is a significant omission, as it would have provided a clearer understanding of how the proposed approach differs from existing work and whether it offers meaningful improvements.

5. **Inadequate explanation for better performance on small datasets**: The paper claims their model works better on small datasets, but doesn't provide a clear explanation for why the hard attention mechanism specifically enables this. Their analysis (Figure 4) suggests the hard attention model encodes position information less strongly, but they don't convincingly explain how this weaker encoding leads to better performance on small datasets.

## Questions to Authors
1. The paper claims your model is the first neural model to surpass the latent variable WFST model on CELEX, but the paper itself states that Rastogi et al. (2016) surpassed the latent variable model only when given twice as much data. How does your model differ fundamentally from Rastogi et al.'s approach such that it achieves better performance with the same small dataset?

2. The paper acknowledges that alignments are "nearly monotonic" (not perfectly monotonic). How does your model handle cases where the alignment is not monotonic, and how does this compare to a soft attention model with a monotonic constraint applied at inference time?

3. The analysis in Figure 4 suggests your hard attention model encodes position information less strongly than the soft attention model. How does this weaker encoding of position information specifically contribute to better performance on small datasets, especially when position information is important for morphological inflection?

4. Why didn't you compare with the RNN Transducer (Graves, 2012), which is a very similar approach using monotonic alignment? How does your model differ from this approach, and why would your approach be expected to perform better?

5. The paper states that "the hard attention mechanism allows the network to jointly align and transduce while using a focused representation at each step." What specific evidence do you have that this is the actual reason for your improved performance, rather than just the monotonic alignment constraint?

## Overall Recommendation
I am not convinced that this paper makes a significant contribution to the field. While the empirical evaluation is thorough and the analysis of alignments and representations is insightful, the core idea of "hard monotonic attention" is not sufficiently novel, and the claims about the model's advantages are not adequately supported by evidence. The paper appears to be a minor variation of existing approaches rather than a substantial advance. I would recommend rejection, as the contribution does not meet the high standards of ACL for a novel and impactful methodological contribution.

I would be willing to reconsider if the authors can provide a more convincing demonstration of novelty (e.g., through comparison with RNN Transducer) and provide stronger evidence that the hard attention mechanism itself is the key factor for their improved performance, rather than just the monotonic alignment constraint.
