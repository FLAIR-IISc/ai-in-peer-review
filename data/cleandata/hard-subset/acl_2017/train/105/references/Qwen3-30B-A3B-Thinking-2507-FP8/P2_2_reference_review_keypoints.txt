- Summary: Proposes a neural model with hard monotonic attention for morphological inflection, using a bidirectional encoder and a decoder that either writes or advances an input pointer.  
- Contribution 1: Introduces a hard monotonic attention mechanism that models near-monotonic alignments and reportedly surpasses a latent WFST on CELEX.  
- Contribution 2: Provides thorough empirical evaluation across CELEX, Wiktionary, and SIGMORPHON showing consistent gains, especially in low-resource settings.  
- Contribution 3: Analyzes learned alignments and representations, showing linguistically appropriate alignments and different positional encoding vs. soft attention.  
- Strength 1: Strong empirical evidence that hard attention avoids overfitting on small datasets and beats soft attention on dev performance.  
- Strength 2: Diverse and comprehensive evaluation against neural and non-neural baselines across multiple dataset sizes and languages.  
- Strength 3: Valuable representational analysis (alignment comparisons, SVD of character embeddings) explaining why the model works better on small data.  
- Weakness 1: Limited comparison to the most recent neural morphological inflection models, leaving the state-of-the-art claim unclear.  
- Weakness 2: Insufficient discussion of limitations and broader applicability, e.g., handling vowel/consonant harmony and relation to general seq2seq methods.  
- Weakness 3: No analysis of computational complexity or runtime trade-offs for the hard attention/control mechanism.  
- Question 1: Is the claim of being the first neural model to beat the WFST on CELEX still valid given more recent work (e.g., Zhang et al., 2017)?  
- Question 2: How could the model be extended to better handle vowel/consonant harmony or other non-suffixing morphologies?  
- Question 3: What are the training and inference time costs compared to other neural approaches?  
- Recommendation: Accept with minor revisions contingent on adding comparisons to recent models, discussing limitations/broader impact, and reporting computational costs.
