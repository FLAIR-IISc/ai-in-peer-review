

# Review of "Morphological Inflection Generation with Hard Monotonic Attention"

## Summary of the Paper and Main Contributions

This paper proposes a neural sequence-to-sequence model for morphological inflection generation that explicitly models nearly monotonic alignments between input and output character sequences using a "hard attention" mechanism. The model alternates between "step" operations (advancing the attention pointer) and "write" operations (outputting characters), with the alignments pre-computed using a character-level alignment model. The authors evaluate their model on three datasets: CELEX (small, 500 examples per inflection type), Wiktionary (larger, up to 360k examples), and SIGMORPHON 2016 (10 languages).

As I assess the contributions, the strongest contributions are:

1. Demonstrating that their model outperforms previous neural approaches on the small CELEX dataset (500 training examples per inflection type), establishing the first neural model to surpass the latent variable WFST model (Dreyer et al., 2008) on this dataset. This is a significant practical contribution for low-resource morphological inflection.

2. Providing a detailed analysis of the learned alignments and representations, showing that both hard and soft attention models learn monotonic alignments but that the hard attention model's explicit alignment mechanism allows it to avoid encoding positional information in the input representations, which may explain its better performance with limited data.

3. Presenting a simple model architecture that achieves state-of-the-art results on all three datasets while using straightforward cross-entropy training, avoiding the more complex joint training of alignment and transduction used in previous work.

## Strengths

1. **Strong empirical demonstration of low-resource performance**: The paper provides compelling evidence that their model significantly outperforms previous neural approaches on the CELEX dataset (89.44% vs. 87.65% for NWFST and 87.62% for MED), which is the first neural model to surpass the latent variable WFST model (Dreyer et al., 2008). The learning curves in Figure 2 clearly show the hard attention model's superior generalization ability on the small dataset compared to the soft attention model, which overfits more quickly.

2. **Insightful analysis of learned representations**: The analysis in Section 5 provides valuable insights into what neural models learn for morphological inflection. The SVD projections in Figures 4a-4d demonstrate how both models cluster similar characters together, but the hard attention model encodes positional information less strongly in the input representations, which the authors convincingly argue explains its better performance with limited training data. This analysis goes beyond just reporting results to provide theoretical understanding of the model's behavior.

3. **Simple yet effective model architecture**: The model is relatively straightforward to implement and train, using a conventional cross-entropy loss with independently learned alignments. This simplicity is a practical advantage over previous approaches that require more complex training procedures (e.g., joint training of alignment and transduction). The authors effectively demonstrate that this simple approach achieves state-of-the-art results across multiple datasets.

## Weaknesses

1. **Limited novelty of the core model**: The paper builds on existing sequence-to-sequence models (Bahdanau et al., 2014) and the concept of monotonic alignment is not new (e.g., RNN Transducer, Graves, 2012; Yu et al., 2016). The paper doesn't sufficiently differentiate their specific model from these prior works, particularly in terms of how it's adapted for morphological inflection. The claimed novelty is not clearly established against the existing literature on monotonic alignment in sequence-to-sequence learning.

2. **Lack of ablation studies**: The paper doesn't provide ablation studies to isolate the contribution of each component of their model. For example, it doesn't show how much of the performance gain comes from the hard attention mechanism versus the bi-directional encoder versus the independently learned alignments. This makes it difficult to determine which aspects of their approach are truly novel and important.

3. **Insufficient statistical significance testing**: The paper claims their model "surpasses the performance of the weighted FST model with latent variables" on CELEX (89.44% vs. 88.3%), but doesn't provide statistical significance testing for this difference. Given the small dataset size, it's important to determine whether this 1.14% improvement is statistically meaningful or could be due to random variation.

4. **Limited generalizability claims**: The paper focuses exclusively on morphological inflection, but doesn't provide evidence that this approach would generalize to other sequence-to-sequence tasks with monotonic alignment (e.g., speech recognition, machine translation). The analysis is limited to this specific task, and the paper doesn't discuss how the model might perform on other domains.

## Questions to Authors

1. How does your model specifically differ from the RNN Transducer (Graves, 2012) and the work by Yu et al. (2016) in terms of architecture and training procedure? What makes your approach particularly suitable for morphological inflection?

2. The paper claims your model is the first neural model to surpass the latent variable WFST model on CELEX, but the improvement is only about 1.14%. Is this difference statistically significant, and how does this compare to the differences between other models in the field?

3. Could you provide ablation studies showing how much of the performance gain comes from the hard attention mechanism versus the independently learned alignments versus the bi-directional encoder?

4. The paper focuses on morphological inflection, but how do you expect this approach to generalize to other sequence-to-sequence tasks with monotonic alignment? What evidence do you have that this is a generalizable approach beyond this specific task?
