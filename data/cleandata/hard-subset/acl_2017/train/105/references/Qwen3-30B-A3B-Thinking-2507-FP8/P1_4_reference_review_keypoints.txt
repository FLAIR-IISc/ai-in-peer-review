- Proposes a neural model for morphological inflection using hard monotonic attention that either writes a character or advances the input pointer.  
- Demonstrates significant improvements on small datasets, notably surpassing the latent-variable WFST on CELEX (89.44% vs previous bests).  
- Uses a simple, principled architecture: hard attention with bi-directional encoder and pre-computed alignments, trained with cross-entropy.  
- Provides insightful analysis of learned representations showing different positional encoding behavior compared to soft attention.  
- Strength: major practical gain for morphologically rich languages with limited data.  
- Strength: model simplicity and straightforward training improve reproducibility and adoption.  
- Strength: SVD visualization offers deeper understanding beyond performance metrics.  
- Weakness: core idea of hard monotonic attention is not novel and relates to prior work (e.g., RNN Transducer, Yu et al.).  
- Weakness: lacks ablation studies to isolate the contributions of hard attention vs. encoder or alignment strategy.  
- Weakness: limited analysis of failure cases, including languages (e.g., Arabic, Maltese) where performance lags.  
- Question: how does this result compare to Rastogi et al. (2016) neural WFST that also reported strong CELEX performance?  
- Question: how does reduced positional encoding in hard attention affect handling of non-monotonic phenomena like vowel/consonant harmony?  
- Question: how can the model be extended to better handle languages that violate the monotonic alignment assumption?  
- Overall recommendation: accept with minor revisions, requesting clearer novelty discussion and inclusion of ablation studies.
