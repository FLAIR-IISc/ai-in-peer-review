- Contribution: Proposes a hard monotonic attention model that transduces via explicit "step" and "write" actions to enforce monotonic alignment.  
- Contribution: Demonstrates strong empirical gains, notably surpassing prior models on small datasets like CELEX.  
- Contribution: Analyzes and compares representations learned by hard vs. soft attention, revealing different positional encodings.

- Strength: Significant improvement on small datasets (CELEX), with learning curves showing reduced overfitting versus soft attention.  
- Strength: Elegant, simple architecture that leverages monotonic alignment without joint alignment-transduction training.  
- Strength: Insightful analysis (e.g., SVD visualization) explaining why hard attention performs better with limited data.  
- Strength: Comprehensive evaluation across multiple datasets and strong baselines, showing robust results.

- Weakness: Limited discussion and systematic analysis of when and why the hard monotonic approach fails, especially for non-monotonic phenomena.  
- Weakness: Insufficient comparison with recent neural methods (e.g., Yu et al. 2016) and weak explanation for why independent alignments outperform joint training.  
- Weakness: No analysis of model complexity (parameter counts, training time) to support the claim of simplicity.  
- Weakness: Dependence on precomputed alignments without assessment of sensitivity to alignment errors.

- Question: Please explain in detail how enforcing monotonicity leads to better generalization on small datasets.  
- Question: Provide linguistic analysis for languages where hard attention underperforms (e.g., Turkish) to show whether non-monotonic morphology is the cause.  
- Question: Provide empirical comparisons of model complexity (parameters, training time) against soft attention baselines.

- Overall recommendation: Strong paper with meaningful contributions; recommend acceptance with minor revisions to address the above questions and weaknesses.
