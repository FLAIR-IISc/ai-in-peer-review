

# Review of "Morphological Inflection Generation with Hard Monotonic Attention"

## Summary
This paper proposes a "hard monotonic attention" model for morphological inflection generation, where the goal is to generate target word forms (e.g., "härtestem" for "hardest") given a source word ("hart") and morpho-syntactic attributes. The authors claim their model is the first neural model to surpass the performance of Dreyer et al.'s (2008) latent variable weighted FST model on the small CELEX dataset. The model uses a bi-directional RNN encoder with a dedicated control mechanism that attends to a single input state per step (either writing an output symbol or advancing the attention pointer). The training procedure uses independently learned character-level alignments to guide the training process, rather than jointly learning alignment and decoding.

## Main Contributions (as I see them)
1. Thorough evaluation of a sequence-to-sequence model with hard monotonic attention on morphological inflection generation across multiple datasets with varying data sizes.
2. Demonstration that the model achieves better performance than previous neural approaches on small datasets (CELEX), which is a significant contribution for low-resource NLP tasks.
3. Analysis comparing the learned alignments and representations between the hard attention model and soft attention models, providing insights into how these models encode information for inflection generation.

## Strengths

1. **Strong evaluation on low-resource setting**: The paper provides a convincing demonstration of the model's advantage on the CELEX dataset (500 training examples per inflection type), which is a significant contribution. The results in Table 1 clearly show that the proposed model outperforms previous neural models and even surpasses the traditional latent variable FST model, which is a notable achievement in low-resource scenarios.

2. **Thorough comparison across multiple datasets**: The authors evaluate their model on three different morphological inflection generation datasets (CELEX, Wiktionary, SIGMORPHON2016) with varying data sizes and linguistic properties. This comprehensive evaluation demonstrates the model's robustness and versatility across different morphological systems.

3. **Insightful analysis of learned representations**: The analysis in Section 5 provides valuable insights into how both hard and soft attention models encode information about character position and identity. The SVD visualization (Figure 4) is particularly compelling in showing how the hard attention model encodes position information less explicitly than the soft attention model, which may explain its better performance on small datasets.

## Weaknesses

1. **Lack of novelty in the core approach**: The paper's main contribution is the application of hard monotonic attention to morphological inflection, but this approach is not novel. The paper cites RNN Transducer (Graves, 2012) and Yu et al. (2016) as related work, which already use monotonic alignment approaches. The paper fails to sufficiently differentiate its approach from these prior works, particularly Yu et al. (2016), which also uses a monotonic alignment but trains the alignment jointly with the decoder. The claim that this is a "new" approach is questionable without a clear, thorough comparison.

2. **Insufficient comparison to relevant baselines**: The paper claims to be the first neural model to surpass Dreyer et al.'s (2008) model on CELEX, but it doesn't provide a comprehensive comparison to all relevant neural models on this dataset. For example, the paper cites Rastogi et al. (2016) as having "suffered from the same issue" but doesn't explain why their model is different or better. A more thorough comparison to all previous neural approaches on CELEX would strengthen the claim of novelty.

3. **Lack of ablation studies**: The paper doesn't provide ablation studies to demonstrate the contribution of specific components of the model. For instance, it doesn't show how the bi-directional encoder contributes to performance compared to a unidirectional one, or how the hard attention mechanism compares to a soft attention mechanism with monotonic constraints. Without these ablations, it's difficult to determine which aspects of the model are truly responsible for the improvements.

## Questions to Authors

1. How does your model specifically differ from Yu et al. (2016), which also uses a monotonic alignment approach but trains the alignment jointly with the decoder? Why do you claim your approach is better without a direct comparison showing the performance difference?

2. The paper claims "the first neural model to surpass the performance of the weighted FST model with latent variables specifically tailored for the task by Dreyer et al. (2008)." However, the paper doesn't provide a comparison to other neural models that might have already achieved this on the CELEX dataset. Can you provide a more thorough comparison to all relevant neural models on this dataset?

3. The paper mentions that "the neural WFST model by Rastogi et al. (2016) also suffered from the same issue on the CELEX dataset, and surpassed the latent variable model only when given twice as much data to train on." But the paper doesn't explain why your model works better with small datasets. What specific aspect of your model allows it to generalize better with limited data compared to Rastogi et al.'s approach?

4. The paper states that "the hard attention mechanism allows the network to jointly align and transduce while using a focused representation at each step." However, the paper doesn't provide a clear explanation of how this "focused representation" leads to better performance on small datasets. Can you provide a more detailed analysis of the specific mechanisms that make the hard attention model more data-efficient?

5. The paper doesn't show how the model performs with varying degrees of monotonicity in the data. For languages with less monotonic morphology (e.g., those with vowel harmony), how does the model perform compared to the soft attention baseline? This would be important to understand the limitations of the approach.

## Recommendation

I would recommend rejection of this paper. While the evaluation is thorough and the results on the CELEX dataset are promising, the paper lacks sufficient novelty. The core approach of using hard monotonic attention for sequence-to-sequence tasks is not new, and the paper fails to adequately differentiate its approach from prior work. Additionally, the lack of ablation studies and insufficient comparison to relevant baselines significantly weakens the claims of novelty and contribution. The paper would need substantial revisions to address these issues before it could be considered for publication.
