- Paper proposes a neural seq2seq model with hard monotonic attention tailored to morphological inflection, using a control action to either write or advance the input pointer.  
- Evaluation on CELEX, Wiktionary, and SIGMORPHON2016 shows state-of-the-art results, especially in low-resource CELEX (500 examples per type).

Main contributions:
- Introduces a hard monotonic attention mechanism that explicitly models near-monotonic input–output alignments in morphological inflection.  
- Demonstrates improved performance over prior neural and non-neural models, notably beating a WFST baseline on CELEX with very little data.  
- Provides analyses of learned alignments and representations to illuminate how the model encodes character identity and position.

Strengths:
- Significant advancement in low-data settings, with clear learning-curve evidence of better generalization than soft-attention models.  
- Strong theoretical motivation: morphological inflection typically exhibits near-monotonic alignments, motivating the model design.  
- Insightful representational analysis showing harder attention yields more distinct character identity encoding and reduced positional encoding in the encoder.  
- Robust empirical results across multiple benchmarks, with top average accuracy on Wiktionary and strong SIGMORPHON2016 performance.  
- Simpler training by using precomputed character-level alignments, enabling correct alignments from the start.

Weaknesses:
- Insufficient direct comparison to related monotonic seq2seq models (e.g., RNN Transducer, Neural Transducer).  
- No ablation studies to isolate the contributions of hard attention vs. encoder or control mechanism.  
- Superficial analysis of why the model excels in small-data regimes; inductive bias and overfitting avoidance not deeply examined.  
- Limited discussion of failure cases and types of morphological phenomena the model struggles with.  
- “Nearly monotonic” is not precisely defined or illustrated with non-monotonic alignment examples.

Questions for authors:
- Why does the model do better on suffixing languages but worse on some languages (e.g., Hungarian, Navajo); is this tied to monotonicity or phenomena like vowel harmony?  
- Can you explain why hard attention leads to more isolated character clusters in SVD visualizations and how that aids generalization?  
- How does the model handle non-monotonic alignments (internal stem changes, vowel harmony), and can you show dataset examples where it succeeds?  
- How does the model’s data efficiency compare to Rastogi et al. (2016) neural WFST, which surpassed the latent WFST given more data?  
- How are multiple valid character alignments handled when using Sudoh et al. (2013) alignments, and how sensitive is performance to alignment quality?

Overall recommendation:
- Strong accept with minor revisions: address comparisons to other monotonic models, add ablation studies, deepen analysis of low-data behavior, and include non-monotonic examples and failure-case analysis.
