

# ACL Review: "Morphological Inflection Generation with Hard Monotonic Attention"

## Summary
This paper proposes a "hard monotonic attention" model for morphological inflection generation, where the neural network uses a hard attention mechanism that either writes an output character or advances an attention pointer to the next input character in a nearly monotonic alignment. The authors claim their model outperforms previous approaches, especially on small datasets like CELEX (with only 500 training examples per inflection type), and provide evaluation on three datasets (CELEX, Wiktionary, and SIGMORPHON 2016).

## Main Contributions (as I see them)
1. A practical implementation of hard attention for morphological inflection that demonstrates strong empirical results, particularly on small training sets.
2. A thorough empirical evaluation across multiple datasets that demonstrates the model's strengths and limitations.
3. An insightful analysis of the alignments and representations learned by the model, providing valuable insights into how neural models process morphological inflection data.

## Strengths

1. **Strong empirical results on the CELEX dataset**: The paper demonstrates that their model achieves 89.44% accuracy on the CELEX dataset, significantly outperforming the previous best neural model (NWFST at 87.65%) and surpassing the latent variable WFST model (LAT at 88.3%) that was specifically tailored for the task. This is a compelling contribution because it addresses a critical challenge in morphological inflection: performance with very limited training data. The results in Table 1 are particularly convincing given the tiny dataset size (500 examples per inflection type).

2. **Insightful analysis of learned alignments**: The paper provides a detailed comparison of the alignments learned by the hard attention model versus soft attention models (Figure 3). This analysis shows that the hard attention model learns more linguistically plausible alignments, particularly for tasks like one-to-many mappings (e.g., "flog→fliege" where "o" maps to "ie"). This is valuable because it provides concrete evidence for why the model works well for certain linguistic phenomena.

3. **Comprehensive evaluation across multiple datasets**: The paper evaluates on three different datasets with varying sizes and linguistic properties (CELEX, Wiktionary, and SIGMORPHON 2016), demonstrating the model's robustness and generalizability. The results on the Wiktionary dataset (Table 2) show the model is competitive with the best previous approaches, and the results on SIGMORPHON (Table 3) reveal that the model is particularly effective for languages with suffixing morphology (Russian, German, Spanish), while being slightly less accurate for languages with vowel harmony (which better suits soft attention).

## Weaknesses

1. **Lack of novelty in the core approach**: The paper's central idea (using hard attention for sequence transduction) is not novel. The authors acknowledge related work (RNN Transducer, Neural Transducer, Yu et al., 2016), but fail to clearly distinguish their approach. The key difference—using independently learned alignments rather than learning alignments jointly with transduction—is presented as a practical implementation choice rather than a significant contribution. The paper doesn't convincingly argue why this specific implementation is better than existing approaches, beyond empirical results. This is a major weakness for a skeptical reviewer who values established methods.

2. **Insufficient theoretical justification**: The paper claims that "the hard attention mechanism allows the network to jointly align and transduce while using a focused representation at each step, rather than the weighted sum of representations used in the soft attention model," but provides no theoretical justification for why this should lead to better performance, especially on small datasets. The paper mentions that the model "enforces the monotonic assumption," but doesn't explain why this assumption is valid for morphological inflection or why it leads to better generalization with limited data. The empirical results support the claim, but the lack of theoretical grounding makes the contribution less compelling.

3. **Overstated claims about novelty**: The paper claims to be "the first neural model to surpass the performance of the weighted FST model" on CELEX. However, the paper doesn't properly acknowledge prior work that might have achieved similar results. For example, the paper doesn't mention the work of Dreyer and Eisner (2011) or other FST-based approaches that might have performed well on small datasets. The paper also states that "the neural WFST model by Rastogi et al. (2016) also suffered from the same issue on the CELEX dataset," but doesn't provide a thorough comparison with all relevant prior neural approaches. This overstatement of novelty is concerning for a skeptical reviewer.

## Questions to Authors

1. How does your hard attention mechanism differ from the RNN Transducer (Graves, 2012) and Neural Transducer (Jaitly et al., 2015) in terms of both architecture and training procedure? The paper mentions these works but doesn't clearly differentiate your approach.

2. The paper claims your model is "the first neural model to surpass the performance of the weighted FST model" on CELEX. However, the paper doesn't provide a thorough comparison with all relevant prior neural approaches. Can you provide a more complete comparison with prior work, including the work of Dreyer et al. (2011) and others?

3. The paper states that the hard attention model works well for "suffixing+stem change" languages but not as well for languages with vowel harmony. Can you provide a more detailed linguistic analysis to support this claim, and explain why the hard attention mechanism is particularly suited for suffixing morphology?

4. The paper mentions that the model "enforces the monotonic assumption," but doesn't explain why this assumption is valid for morphological inflection. Can you provide a more detailed justification for why monotonic alignment is a reasonable assumption for morphological inflection tasks?

5. The paper uses independently learned alignments (from Sudoh et al., 2013) to guide the training of the attention mechanism. How sensitive is your model to the quality of these alignments? Would the model perform as well if the alignments were noisy or imperfect?

## Overall Recommendation
I recommend rejection of this paper. While the empirical results are promising and the analysis is insightful, the paper fails to establish the novelty of its core approach. The hard attention mechanism described is not sufficiently novel compared to prior work, and the theoretical justification is lacking. The paper makes overstated claims about novelty without properly acknowledging relevant prior work. As a skeptical reviewer who prefers established methods and requires extensive evidence for new approaches, I find these weaknesses significant enough to recommend rejection.

The paper does present a practical implementation of hard attention that works well on morphological inflection tasks, but this is not sufficiently novel to warrant publication in ACL. The authors would need to substantially revise the paper to clearly distinguish their approach from prior work, provide stronger theoretical justification, and address the overstated novelty claims before I would consider recommending acceptance.
