

# Review of "Morphological Inflection Generation with Hard Monotonic Attention"

## Summary of the Paper
The paper proposes a neural model for morphological inflection generation that uses a hard monotonic attention mechanism to directly model the nearly monotonic alignment between input and output character sequences. The model consists of a bi-directional RNN encoder and a decoder with a dedicated control mechanism that either writes an output symbol or advances the attention pointer to the next input state. The authors evaluate their model on three datasets: CELEX (very small, 500 training examples per inflection type), Wiktionary (larger, up to 360k training examples), and SIGMORPHON2016 (10 languages with ~12,800 training examples per language).

## Main Contributions (as I see them)
1. A novel neural model for morphological inflection generation that uses a hard monotonic attention mechanism, which directly models the nearly monotonic alignment commonly found in morphological inflection tasks, without requiring joint training of alignment and transduction.
2. Comprehensive empirical evaluation demonstrating superior performance on the CELEX dataset (the first neural model to surpass the latent variable WFST model of Dreyer et al., 2008) and strong results on larger datasets, showing the model's effectiveness across different data sizes.
3. Insightful analysis comparing the hard attention and soft attention models, revealing how they encode linguistic features like character types and positional information, and explaining the model's performance advantages with limited data.

## Strengths

1. **Technical Soundness of the Hard Attention Mechanism**: The paper presents a well-motivated approach to modeling the monotonic alignments common in morphological inflection tasks. The hard attention mechanism is clearly described, and the authors provide a strong rationale for why this approach is better suited for the task than soft attention models. The model architecture is technically sound, with the bi-directional encoder capturing non-monotone dependencies and the control mechanism enabling focused attention. The training procedure, which leverages precomputed alignments, is straightforward and avoids the complexity of joint alignment training.

2. **Significant Performance Improvement on Small Datasets**: The most compelling contribution is the model's performance on the CELEX dataset, which is extremely small (only 500 training examples per inflection type). The authors demonstrate that their model significantly outperforms previous neural models (including the soft attention model of Kann and Schütze) and surpasses the latent variable WFST model of Dreyer et al. (2008), which was previously the state-of-the-art on this dataset. This is a notable achievement since neural models typically struggle with such limited data, and the paper provides clear learning curves showing the hard attention model's resistance to overfitting compared to the soft attention model.

3. **Insightful Analysis of Learned Alignments and Representations**: The paper provides valuable analysis that goes beyond just reporting accuracy metrics. The comparison of alignments learned by the hard and soft attention models, particularly regarding the one-to-many alignment in flog→fliege, demonstrates a deeper understanding of the model's behavior. The analysis of character representations in context using SVD projections reveals how the two models encode positional information differently, with the hard attention model explicitly handling position via step operations rather than encoding it in the input representations. This analysis helps explain why the model performs well with limited data.

4. **Strong Results Across Multiple Datasets**: The model achieves state-of-the-art results on the Wiktionary dataset (with the highest reported average accuracy across all inflection types) and performs well on the SIGMORPHON2016 dataset, outperforming soft attention baselines for suffixing+stem-change languages. This demonstrates the model's robustness across different data sizes and morphological phenomena, showing that it's not just a small-data solution but a generally effective approach.

## Weaknesses

1. **Limited Comparison with Recent Work**: The paper compares with previous neural and non-neural models but doesn't adequately address more recent work that might have tackled similar issues. For example, the paper mentions Yu et al. (2016) but doesn't provide a detailed comparison with other recent sequence-to-sequence models that use specialized attention mechanisms. The authors could strengthen their contribution by discussing how their model relates to or improves upon these more recent approaches.

2. **Lack of Detailed Failure Analysis**: While the paper notes that the model performs slightly worse on languages with vowel harmony (like Hungarian and Arabic) compared to the soft attention model, it doesn't provide a detailed analysis of why this happens or concrete examples of these failures. A deeper examination of failure cases would provide more valuable insights into the model's limitations and help clarify when the hard attention approach is most appropriate.

3. **Precomputed Alignments as a Dependency**: The model's training procedure relies on precomputed character-level alignments from Sudoh et al. (2013). While the authors argue this is a benefit (simplifying training), they don't adequately address the limitation that this requires an external alignment model, which might not be available for all languages or tasks. The paper would be stronger if it included a comparison of performance when using learned alignments versus precomputed alignments.

4. **Limited Novelty of the Hard Attention Concept**: The hard attention mechanism described in the paper is conceptually similar to the RNN Transducer (Graves, 2012) and Neural Transducer (Jaitly et al., 2015), which also use discrete attention mechanisms. The paper doesn't sufficiently differentiate its approach from these existing works. While the specific implementation with a bi-directional encoder and the way the attention is controlled may be novel, this distinction isn't clearly articulated, making it difficult to assess the true novelty of the contribution.

## Questions to Authors

1. Could you provide more details about the specific linguistic phenomena in Hungarian and Arabic that cause the model to underperform compared to the soft attention model? What concrete examples can you provide where the hard attention model fails to capture vowel harmony?

2. How would the model's performance change if the alignments were learned jointly with the model instead of using precomputed alignments from Sudoh et al. (2013)? Could you provide a comparison of performance with both approaches?

3. The paper states that the model is "the first neural model to surpass the performance of the weighted FST model with latent variables specifically tailored for the task by Dreyer et al. (2008)" on the CELEX dataset. However, Rastogi et al. (2016) mention that their neural WFST model surpassed the latent variable model with twice as much data. Could you clarify the exact comparison with Rastogi et al.'s results on the CELEX dataset?

## Overall Assessment

This is a well-written paper that makes a solid contribution to the morphological inflection generation task. The hard monotonic attention mechanism is well-motivated and effectively addresses a key challenge in neural models for this task: performance with small training datasets. The empirical results are strong, particularly on the CELEX dataset, and the analysis of learned alignments and representations adds significant value. While the novelty of the hard attention concept is somewhat limited by prior work, the specific implementation and thorough evaluation make this a valuable contribution to the field. The paper is clear, technically sound, and demonstrates meaningful progress in the area.
