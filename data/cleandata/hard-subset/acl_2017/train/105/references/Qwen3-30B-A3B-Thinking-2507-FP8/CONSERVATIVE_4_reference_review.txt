

# Review for ACL Submission: "Morphological Inflection Generation with Hard Monotonic Attention"

## Summary and Main Contributions

This paper proposes a "hard monotonic attention" model for morphological inflection generation, which models the alignment between input and output sequences as nearly monotonic (common in morphological inflection tasks). The model uses a bi-directional encoder and a decoder with a control mechanism that either writes an output symbol or advances the attention pointer to the next input character. The authors claim their model outperforms previous neural and non-neural methods on three morphological inflection datasets, particularly excelling on small training sets.

As a skeptical reviewer, I assess the contributions as follows (in order of strength):

1. **Analysis of alignments and representations learned by models** (most valuable contribution) - The paper provides an insightful comparison between hard and soft attention models, showing how they encode character position information differently. This analysis offers meaningful insights into how these models work for morphological inflection.

2. **Strong empirical results on the small CELEX dataset** - The model demonstrates clear improvements over previous neural approaches on the CELEX dataset (with only 500 training examples per inflection type), which is a significant practical contribution.

3. **Well-motivated application of a simple model** - The model is conceptually simple and well-suited to the morphological inflection task, which often has nearly monotonic alignments.

## Strengths

1. **Strong results on the small CELEX dataset**: The paper demonstrates a clear advantage over previous neural models on the CELEX dataset (with only 500 training examples per inflection type), which is a significant practical contribution. The authors show their model is the first neural model to surpass the latent variable WFST model (Dreyer et al., 2008) on this dataset, addressing a real problem in the field (neural models struggling with small datasets). This is a meaningful contribution for NLP tasks where data sparsity is a common challenge.

2. **Insightful analysis of model representations**: The analysis of character representations in the encoder (Figures 4a-4d) is particularly valuable. It reveals how the hard attention model encodes position information differently than soft attention models, with the hard model relying less on positional encoding in the input representations (using step actions instead). This provides meaningful insights into how these models work for morphological inflection.

3. **Clear explanation of the model's suitability for the task**: The paper effectively argues why a nearly monotonic alignment model is appropriate for morphological inflection, noting that many languages exhibit suffixing morphology where the input-to-output alignment is approximately monotonic. This makes the model design well-motivated for the specific task.

## Weaknesses

1. **Lack of novelty in the core approach**: The hard attention mechanism is not novel. Similar approaches have been proposed before, including Graves (2012) with the RNN Transducer, Yu et al. (2016) who improved this with a dedicated transition probability, and Jaitly et al. (2015) with the Neural Transducer. The paper fails to adequately differentiate its approach from these prior works, especially since they all use external alignments. The claim that their approach is "hard attention" is misleading, as the attention is not truly "hard" in the sense of being deterministic in the decoder; it's still probabilistic.

2. **Insufficient evidence for the claimed improvements**: The paper claims their model "is the first neural model to surpass the performance of the weighted FST model" on CELEX, but:
   - They don't provide statistical significance tests for the improvements (e.g., p-values for the 89.44 vs. 88.3 comparison)
   - They don't compare to the neural WFST model by Rastogi et al. (2016), which they mention in the introduction as also struggling on CELEX but surpassing the latent variable model with more data
   - They don't provide an ablation study to show that the hard attention mechanism is the key factor behind their improvements (vs. other aspects of the model)

3. **Inadequate comparison to state-of-the-art**: For the SIGMORPHON 2016 dataset, they compare to MED (Kann and Schütze, 2016b) and a soft attention model, but they don't compare to more recent neural approaches that have been published since the SIGMORPHON shared task. The paper mentions Yu et al. (2016) but doesn't include their results in the comparison. For the Wiktionary dataset, they compare to older methods like DDN13, NCK15, etc., but don't include more recent neural approaches.

4. **Lack of discussion about alignment quality**: The paper relies on independently learned character-level alignments (using Sudoh et al., 2013) but doesn't discuss the quality of these alignments or how they might affect model performance. If the alignments are poor, the model's performance might be artificially limited, but the paper doesn't analyze this.

5. **Overstated claims about novelty**: The paper claims "we present a hard attention model for nearly-monotonic sequence to sequence learning, as common in the morphological inflection setting" as a key contribution. However, this is not a novel contribution to the field, as similar approaches have been explored in other sequence transduction tasks.

## Questions to Authors

1. The paper claims to be the first neural model to surpass the latent variable WFST model on CELEX, but doesn't compare to the neural WFST model by Rastogi et al. (2016). Why was this comparison omitted, and how would it change the conclusions?

2. The paper states that the hard attention model "performs significantly better on the dev-set" (Figure 2), but doesn't provide statistical significance tests for these improvements. How confident are you that these differences are statistically significant?

3. The paper claims their model is the first to use "hard attention" for this task, but similar approaches (RNN Transducer, Neural Transducer) have been proposed. How does your model differ from these prior works in a way that justifies the claim of novelty?

4. The paper uses independently learned alignments from Sudoh et al. (2013), but doesn't discuss the quality of these alignments. How did you evaluate the alignment quality, and what would happen if the alignments were suboptimal?

## Overall Recommendation

I am hesitant to recommend acceptance of this paper due to the lack of novelty in the core approach and insufficient evidence for the claimed improvements. While the empirical results on the small CELEX dataset are compelling and the analysis of model representations is valuable, the paper fails to adequately differentiate its approach from prior work and doesn't provide sufficient evidence to support its claims.

The paper makes a modest contribution to the field by applying a well-known technique to a specific task with good empirical results, but the novelty is overstated. The reviewers should consider whether the paper's contribution is significant enough to warrant publication in ACL given the field's current state.

**Decision: Weak Reject**
