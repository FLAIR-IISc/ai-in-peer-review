

# ACL Review: "Morphological Inflection Generation with Hard Monotonic Attention"

## Summary
This paper proposes a hard attention model for morphological inflection generation that directly models nearly-monotonic alignments between input and output character sequences, which is common in morphological inflection tasks. The model uses a bi-directional RNN encoder and a decoder with a control mechanism that either writes an output symbol or advances the attention pointer. The authors evaluate their model on three datasets (CELEX, Wiktionary, and SIGMORPHON 2016), reporting state-of-the-art results, particularly on the small CELEX dataset where their model outperforms previous neural and non-neural approaches.

## Main Contributions
1. A novel neural model architecture for morphological inflection generation that uses a hard monotonic attention mechanism, which is particularly well-suited for the monotonic nature of morphological inflection tasks.
2. Strong empirical results demonstrating that this model achieves state-of-the-art performance on multiple morphological inflection datasets, especially with limited training data.
3. An insightful analysis comparing the hard attention model with soft attention models, revealing how both models learn similar representations despite different attention mechanisms.

## Strengths

1. **Significant improvement on small datasets**: The paper demonstrates that their model achieves state-of-the-art results on the CELEX dataset (with only 500 training examples per inflection type), which is a notable contribution since previous neural models struggled with such small datasets. The authors claim this is the first neural model to surpass a specialized non-neural model (the latent variable WFST) on this dataset. Figure 2's learning curves clearly show that the hard attention model avoids the overfitting that plagues the soft attention model on this small dataset, demonstrating the practical value of their approach for low-resource settings.

2. **Insightful model analysis**: The paper provides a detailed analysis of the alignments learned by the model and the representations of input characters. The comparison between the hard and soft attention models in Figure 3 and Figure 4 is particularly valuable, as it reveals how both models learn similar alignments and representations despite different attention mechanisms. This analysis helps to understand what these neural models are actually learning for morphological inflection tasks, which is often missing in sequence-to-sequence work.

3. **Clear problem formulation and justification**: The paper clearly articulates the problem of morphological inflection generation and the limitations of previous approaches. The authors provide a strong justification for their approach by highlighting the monotonic nature of the alignment in morphological inflection tasks, which is a key insight that informs their model design. The paper effectively connects the linguistic properties of the task to the model architecture.

## Weaknesses

1. **Lack of novelty in the core model**: While the paper presents a hard attention mechanism for morphological inflection, the core idea of using a hard attention mechanism for sequence-to-sequence learning is not entirely novel. The paper mentions the RNN Transducer (Graves, 2012) and Neural Transducer (Jaitly et al., 2015) as related work, but doesn't sufficiently differentiate their approach. The key difference appears to be that they learn alignments independently rather than jointly with the transduction. However, this is a relatively small modification to existing work, and the paper doesn't establish how this small change leads to the significant performance gains they report. The authors should better position their work in the context of prior hard attention approaches.

2. **Limited comparison with other hard attention approaches**: The paper compares with soft attention models but doesn't compare with other hard attention-based models for sequence-to-sequence tasks. For instance, the RNN Transducer (Graves, 2012) and Neural Transducer (Jaitly et al., 2015) use hard attention mechanisms but are trained differently. The paper should compare with these approaches to establish the relative merits of their specific approach. Without these comparisons, it's difficult to assess whether the performance gains are due to the hard attention mechanism itself or the specific implementation details in their model.

3. **Unclear contribution to the broader field**: While the empirical results are strong, the paper doesn't clearly articulate how this work advances the broader field of neural sequence-to-sequence learning. The authors claim it's the first neural model to surpass the latent variable WFST on CELEX, but they don't establish why this is significant beyond this specific task. The paper could better connect the specific contribution to broader implications for sequence-to-sequence learning. For example, the authors should discuss how their approach might generalize to other tasks with similar monotonic alignment properties (e.g., transliteration, abstractive summarization).

## Questions to Authors

1. The paper claims that their model is the first neural model to surpass the latent variable WFST model on the CELEX dataset. However, Rastogi et al. (2016) reported that their neural WFST model surpassed the latent variable WFST when given twice as much data. Could the authors clarify how their model achieves better performance with the same amount of data, and whether this represents a fundamental improvement over the Rastogi model?

2. The paper states that they use independently learned alignments to guide the training of their model. Could the authors elaborate on the robustness of this alignment process? How does the performance of their model depend on the quality of the alignments, and what happens if the alignment is imperfect?

3. The paper's analysis shows that the soft attention model also learns monotonic alignments. Why does the hard attention model still perform better with limited data if the soft attention model can learn similar alignments? Is it because the hard attention model enforces the monotonicity constraint during training, or is there another factor?

4. The paper reports that the hard attention model performs better on suffixing+stem-change languages but worse on languages with more context-sensitive phenomena. Could the authors provide more insight into why this is the case, and whether they see this as a limitation of their approach or a reflection of the inherent nature of the languages?

## Overall Recommendation
I recommend **weak acceptance** for this paper. The paper makes a valuable contribution to the morphological inflection generation task, particularly with its strong results on small datasets and insightful analysis. However, the novelty of the core model is somewhat limited, and the paper doesn't clearly articulate how this work advances the broader field of neural sequence-to-sequence learning.

To strengthen the paper, I recommend:

1. Adding a more detailed comparison with other hard attention approaches (like RNN Transducer and Neural Transducer) to better establish the relative merits of their specific approach.
2. Expanding the discussion to clarify the broader significance of their work beyond morphological inflection.
3. Providing more discussion on the relationship between their approach and prior work on hard attention mechanisms.

With these improvements, I believe this paper would be a strong candidate for acceptance at ACL. The empirical results are compelling, and the analysis provides valuable insights into the behavior of neural models for morphological inflection.
