

# Review of "Morphological Inflection Generation with Hard Monotonic Attention"

## Summary of the Paper
The paper proposes a neural sequence-to-sequence model for morphological inflection generation that directly models an almost monotonic alignment between input and output character sequences. The model uses a bi-directional RNN encoder and a decoder with a control mechanism that either writes an output symbol or advances the attention pointer to the next input state. The authors claim their approach addresses the limitation of previous neural models (which typically use soft attention) that perform poorly on small datasets like CELEX, while also being competitive on larger datasets.

## Main Contributions (as assessed)
1. A well-motivated neural model that leverages precomputed monotonic alignments to guide a simple attention mechanism, demonstrating superior performance on small morphological inflection datasets where previous neural models underperformed.
2. Comprehensive empirical evaluation across three benchmark datasets, establishing new state-of-the-art results on the CELEX dataset (which is particularly significant given its small size).
3. A detailed analysis of the alignments and representations learned by the model, providing insights into how neural models handle morphological inflection tasks.

## Strengths

1. **Significant empirical contribution on small datasets**: The paper demonstrates that the proposed model is the first neural approach to surpass the performance of the latent-variable weighted FST model (Dreyer et al., 2008) on the CELEX dataset, which contains only 500 training examples per inflection type. This is a substantial contribution to the field, as it shows neural models can be effective even with very limited training data, addressing a key limitation of previous neural approaches.

2. **Thorough analysis of model behavior**: The paper provides a detailed analysis of the alignments learned by the model compared to soft attention models (Figure 3) and an analysis of the representations learned by the encoders (Figure 4). This analysis goes beyond just reporting results and offers valuable insights into how neural models operate for morphological inflection tasks, particularly regarding how they encode position information and handle different morphological phenomena.

3. **Comprehensive evaluation across multiple datasets**: The authors evaluate their model on three distinct datasets (CELEX, Wiktionary, and SIGMORPHON2016) with different characteristics. This demonstrates the model's robustness across varying data sizes and linguistic phenomena, showing that it performs well not just on small datasets but also on larger ones, making it a versatile approach for morphological inflection generation.

## Weaknesses

1. **Limited novelty of the core approach**: The paper builds on existing work, particularly the RNN Transducer (Graves, 2012) and Yu et al. (2016), with the main distinction being that the authors precompute alignments rather than learning them jointly. This distinction is not sufficiently novel, as previous work (e.g., Jaitly et al., 2015) has also used external alignments. The paper would benefit from a clearer articulation of how this specific approach differs from prior work in a meaningful way.

2. **Misleading terminology regarding "hard" attention**: The paper describes the attention mechanism as "hard" because it attends to a single input state at each step, but the model is trained with cross-entropy loss on precomputed alignments, meaning it's not truly "hard" (discrete) during training. This is a significant mischaracterization that could confuse readers, as "hard attention" typically implies discrete attention during training (requiring reinforcement learning), which this model does not use.

3. **Mixed performance on SIGMORPHON dataset**: The model performs better than soft attention baselines for suffixing+stem-change languages (Russian, German, Spanish) but slightly worse for other languages (which employ phenomena like vowel harmony). While the authors provide a reasonable linguistic explanation, this mixed performance suggests the model is not universally superior and may not generalize as well as claimed. The paper doesn't sufficiently address how this limitation might affect the model's applicability to other morphological tasks.

4. **Insufficient evaluation of alignment quality**: The paper uses Sudoh et al.'s (2013) character alignment model to precompute alignments but doesn't evaluate the quality of these alignments or their impact on model performance. The quality of the precomputed alignments directly affects the model's training, yet the paper doesn't provide any analysis of alignment accuracy or how errors in alignment might affect the model.

5. **Speculative claims about representation differences**: The paper claims that the hard attention model's weaker encoding of position information "may allow it to perform well even with fewer examples," but this is not strongly supported by the evidence provided. The SVD analysis (Figure 4) shows a difference in how position information is encoded, but the paper doesn't provide evidence that this difference directly causes better generalization with limited data.

## Questions to Authors

1. The paper claims the model "is the first neural model to surpass the performance of the weighted FST model with latent variables specifically tailored for the task by Dreyer et al. (2008)" on CELEX. However, Rastogi et al. (2016) previously surpassed this model with twice as much data. Can the authors clarify the exact comparison (data splits, training procedures) that makes their model the first to surpass Dreyer et al.'s performance on the standard CELEX dataset?

2. The paper states that the hard attention model's representations encode position information less strongly than the soft attention model's, which the authors claim contributes to better performance with limited data. Is there any evidence that this difference in representation encoding directly causes better generalization, or is this correlation without causal evidence?

3. The paper uses Sudoh et al.'s (2013) character alignment model to precompute alignments. How does the quality of these alignments affect the model's performance? Are there cases where the alignment is incorrect, and how does the model handle such cases during training and inference?

## Overall Assessment
The paper presents a well-motivated model that addresses a significant limitation of previous neural approaches to morphological inflection generation, particularly their poor performance on small datasets. The empirical results on the CELEX dataset are compelling and represent a meaningful contribution to the field. However, the novelty of the approach is limited, and the paper contains some misleading terminology regarding "hard" attention. The mixed performance on the SIGMORPHON dataset and insufficient evaluation of alignment quality are also notable weaknesses. Despite these limitations, the paper makes a valuable contribution to the field by demonstrating that neural models can be effective with very limited training data for morphological inflection, and the analysis of learned alignments and representations provides useful insights.
