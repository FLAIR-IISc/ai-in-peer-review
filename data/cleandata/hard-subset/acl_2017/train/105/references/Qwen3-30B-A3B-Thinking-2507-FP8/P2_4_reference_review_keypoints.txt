- Summary: Proposes a hard monotonic attention model for morphological inflection using a bi-RNN encoder and a decoder that either writes or steps, trained from precomputed character alignments and evaluated on CELEX, Wiktionary, and SIGMORPHON 2016 with strong results, especially on small data.

- Contribution 1: Introduces a hard monotonic attention mechanism that explicitly models nearly monotonic character alignments for inflection generation.

- Contribution 2: Empirically outperforms prior approaches on three datasets and is the first neural model to beat the latent-variable WFST on CELEX (small-data setting).

- Contribution 3: Provides analyses of learned alignments and representations (including SVD visualizations) that yield linguistic and model-insightful findings.

- Strength 1: Strong empirical performance with clear practical significance, notably robustness to data sparsity and SOTA results.

- Strength 2: Well-motivated approach that aligns with the nearly-monotonic nature of morphological inflection and shows linguistically sensible alignments versus soft attention.

- Strength 3: Insightful representational analysis showing differences in positional encoding between hard and soft attention models.

- Weakness 1: Limited novelty since monotonic alignment ideas exist in RNN Transducer and related work; differentiation from prior methods is underemphasized.

- Weakness 2: Insufficient analysis explaining why the model generalizes better on small datasets and how capacity/constraints relate to data size.

- Weakness 3: Limited discussion of limitations and extensions, e.g., poorer performance on languages with vowel harmony and other non-monotonic phenomena.

- Question 1: How does this approach differ from RNN Transducer and Yu et al. (2016) in a way that explains the observed gains beyond training procedure differences?

- Question 2: How sensitive is the model to errors in the precomputed character alignments, and how would imperfect alignments be handled in practice?

- Question 3: Can the authors propose extensions to handle vowel harmony and other non-monotonic morphological phenomena, or is the approach fundamentally limited?

- Recommendation: Accept with minor revisions; address novelty clarification, deeper analysis of small-data generalization, and discussion of limitations/extensions.
