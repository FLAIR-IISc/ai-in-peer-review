

# Peer Review: "Morphological Inflection Generation with Hard Monotonic Attention"

## Summary and Contributions

This paper proposes a novel neural model for morphological inflection generation that uses a "hard monotonic attention" mechanism. The key idea is to model the sequence transduction as a series of "step" (advance pointer) and "write" (output character) actions, rather than using soft attention that weights all input characters when generating each output character. The authors claim this better captures the monotonic alignment properties common in morphological inflection tasks.

As I see them, the main contributions of this work are:

1. A simple yet effective hard monotonic attention mechanism for morphological inflection generation that enforces the monotonic alignment property of the task.
2. Strong empirical results showing the model outperforms previous neural and non-neural approaches, particularly on small datasets where neural models typically struggle.
3. An insightful analysis comparing the representations learned by hard and soft attention models, revealing how each encodes positional information.

## Strengths

1. **Significant improvement on small datasets**: The paper demonstrates a meaningful contribution by showing their model outperforms previous neural approaches on the CELEX dataset (Table 1), which has only 500 training examples per inflection type. Their model achieves 89.44% accuracy compared to the previous best of 88.3% (LAT model), making it the first neural model to surpass the latent variable WFST model on this small dataset. The learning curves in Figure 2 clearly show that their model avoids the overfitting tendency of soft attention models on small data.

2. **Elegant and simple architecture**: The hard monotonic attention mechanism is conceptually simple and avoids the need for joint alignment and transduction training. The model's control mechanism (step/write actions) directly leverages the monotonic alignment property common in morphological inflection, making it a well-motivated approach. This simplicity is a strength that contrasts with the complexity of joint training approaches.

3. **Insightful analysis**: The analysis in Section 5 provides valuable insights into how both hard and soft attention models encode information. The SVD visualization (Figure 4) clearly demonstrates how the hard attention model encodes positional information differently than the soft attention model, explaining why it performs better with limited data. This analysis goes beyond typical empirical results and provides deeper understanding of the models' behavior.

4. **Comprehensive evaluation**: The authors evaluate on three well-established datasets with varying dataset sizes (CELEX, Wiktionary, SIGMORPHON2016), comparing against multiple strong baselines. Their results are consistent across datasets, showing robustness of the approach.

## Weaknesses

1. **Limited discussion of the method's limitations**: While the authors note that their approach works best for languages with suffixing morphology (Table 3), they don't provide a systematic analysis of when the hard monotonic approach fails and why. For instance, they don't quantify how much performance drops for languages with non-monotonic transformations, which would help readers understand the scope of the method's applicability.

2. **Insufficient comparison with recent neural approaches**: The paper mentions Yu et al. (2016) as a related work but doesn't provide a detailed comparison of their approach with Yu et al.'s joint training method. The authors claim their model outperforms Yu et al.'s model, but they don't clearly explain why using independently learned alignments leads to better performance. This is a key claim that needs stronger justification.

3. **Lack of model complexity analysis**: The paper claims their model is simpler than previous approaches, but doesn't provide a comparison of model complexity (parameters, training time, etc.). This is important for understanding practical trade-offs, especially since the hard attention mechanism adds a control component that might increase complexity compared to standard soft attention models.

4. **Dependency on alignment quality**: The training procedure relies on pre-computed alignments (using Sudoh et al.'s method), but the authors don't discuss how sensitive their model is to alignment errors. If the alignments are imperfect, the model's performance could be significantly affected, which is an important consideration for real-world applications.

## Questions to Authors

1. The paper claims the hard monotonic attention mechanism "enforces the monotonic assumption of the task," but doesn't explicitly explain how this leads to better generalization on small datasets. Could you provide more detailed analysis of how the monotonic constraint helps with generalization?

2. In Table 3, your model performs slightly worse than the soft attention model on some languages (e.g., Turkish). Could you provide a linguistic analysis of why this might be the case, and whether these languages have non-monotonic morphological phenomena that make the hard attention approach less suitable?

3. Could you provide a comparison of model complexity (number of parameters, training time) between your model and the soft attention baseline, to better understand the practical trade-offs of your approach?

## Overall Recommendation

This is a strong paper that makes a meaningful contribution to morphological inflection generation. The hard monotonic attention mechanism is well-motivated, the empirical results are compelling (particularly on small datasets), and the analysis provides valuable insights. The weaknesses identified are important but do not undermine the core contribution. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions to address the questions above.
