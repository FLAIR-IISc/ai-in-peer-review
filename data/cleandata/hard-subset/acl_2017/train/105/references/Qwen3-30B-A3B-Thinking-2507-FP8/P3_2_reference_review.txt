

# Peer Review: "Morphological Inflection Generation with Hard Monotonic Attention"

## Summary and Main Contributions

This paper proposes a novel neural model for morphological inflection generation that uses a "hard monotonic attention" mechanism. The key insight is that morphological inflection typically involves nearly monotonic alignments between input and output character sequences (e.g., suffixes being added to the end of words), and the authors design a model that explicitly leverages this property.

The main contributions of the paper as I see them (in order of strength):

1. **Demonstrating state-of-the-art performance on small datasets**: The authors show that their model significantly outperforms previous neural approaches on the CELEX dataset (only 500 training examples per inflection type), surpassing even a specifically designed latent variable WFST model (Dreyer et al., 2008). This is a significant contribution because it addresses a critical challenge in morphological inflection: performance with very limited training data.

2. **A simpler training procedure that decouples alignment and transduction**: Instead of jointly learning alignment and transduction (as in Bahdanau et al., 2014), they use pre-computed character-level alignments to guide training. This simplifies the training process and enables the use of correct alignments from the beginning of training.

3. **Insightful analysis of learned representations**: The paper provides a detailed analysis of how the hard attention model encodes character identity and position information compared to soft attention models, revealing important differences in how these models represent linguistic information.

## Strengths

1. **Strong empirical results on low-resource setting**: The paper's most compelling contribution is demonstrating that their neural model outperforms the previous state-of-the-art (Dreyer et al., 2008) on the CELEX dataset with only 500 training examples per inflection type. This is a significant achievement because morphological inflection is notoriously difficult for neural models with limited data. The authors correctly note that "to our knowledge, it is the first neural model to surpass the performance of the weighted FST model with latent variables specifically tailored for the task." This addresses a real problem in NLP: neural models' poor performance on morphologically rich languages with limited training data.

2. **Clear explanation of why the model works well with small datasets**: The paper provides compelling evidence (via the learning curves in Figure 2) that the hard attention model avoids overfitting on small datasets, while the soft attention model (which requires more data) shows clear overfitting. The authors correctly attribute this to the hard attention mechanism enforcing the monotonic alignment assumption, which is well-suited to the morphological inflection task. This is a valuable insight for the field, as it demonstrates how architectural choices can be tailored to specific linguistic phenomena.

3. **Insightful representation analysis**: The SVD visualization of learned representations (Figure 4) provides a compelling explanation for why the hard attention model performs better on small datasets. The analysis shows that the hard attention model encodes position information less explicitly than the soft attention model because it handles position through the "step" mechanism rather than relying on positional information in the input representations. This is a significant contribution as it provides concrete evidence about how neural models learn linguistic features.

## Weaknesses

1. **Lack of clear novelty relative to prior work**: The paper's core idea of using monotonic alignment for sequence transduction is not new. Graves (2012) introduced the RNN Transducer, which uses a monotonic alignment and a similar "step" mechanism. Yu et al. (2016) improved upon this with a learned transition probability. The paper acknowledges these works but doesn't sufficiently differentiate their contribution. The key difference is using pre-computed alignments rather than learning them jointly, but this isn't emphasized enough as a novel contribution. The paper should provide a more detailed comparison with these prior works, including performance metrics.

2. **Insufficient ablation studies**: The paper doesn't provide ablation studies to isolate the contribution of each component of their model. For example, how much of the improvement comes from the hard attention mechanism versus the bidirectional encoder versus the specific training procedure? Without these ablations, it's unclear what aspects of their model are truly responsible for the performance gains. This is a significant methodological weakness in a paper claiming to present a new model architecture.

3. **Limited comparison with other monotonic alignment approaches**: The paper compares to soft attention models and traditional FST approaches, but doesn't directly compare to other monotonic alignment models like the RNN Transducer (Graves, 2012) or Neural Transducer (Jaitly et al., 2015). This is surprising given that these models are directly related to the authors' approach. The paper claims their model "simplifies the model training using a simple cross-entropy loss" compared to Yu et al. (2016), but doesn't show how it compares to Graves' original RNN Transducer, which also uses a monotonic alignment.

4. **Superficial explanation of why the model works better on small datasets**: While the paper demonstrates that the hard attention model works better on small datasets, the explanation is somewhat superficial. The authors state that "the hard attention model performs significantly better on the dev-set" but don't provide a thorough analysis of *why* the monotonic assumption helps prevent overfitting. A more detailed analysis of the model's capacity and how the hard attention mechanism limits overfitting would strengthen the paper.

## Questions to Authors

1. The paper mentions that the RNN Transducer (Graves, 2012) uses a similar monotonic alignment approach. How does your model differ from Graves' RNN Transducer in terms of architecture and training procedure? What performance improvements does your model achieve over Graves' approach on the CELEX dataset?

2. The paper states that "the hard attention model tends to have much more isolated character clusters" in the SVD visualization. Can you provide a more detailed explanation of why this is the case, and how this characteristic contributes to the model's ability to generalize to unseen inflection patterns?

3. The authors claim that their model "is the first neural model to surpass the performance of the weighted FST model with latent variables specifically tailored for the task." However, the latent variable WFST model (Dreyer et al., 2008) was designed specifically for morphological inflection. How do you explain the fact that a neural model without linguistic constraints can outperform a model specifically designed for this task? Is it solely due to the monotonic alignment assumption, or are there other factors at play?

## Overall Recommendation

This paper presents a well-motivated approach to morphological inflection generation that addresses a significant challenge in the field: performance with limited training data. The empirical results on the CELEX dataset are compelling and represent a meaningful contribution to the field. The analysis of learned representations is particularly insightful and provides valuable theoretical understanding.

However, the paper's novelty claim is somewhat weakened by the lack of clear differentiation from prior work on monotonic alignment (Graves, 2012; Yu et al., 2016). The paper would be significantly stronger with a more detailed comparison to these works and with ablation studies to isolate the contribution of the hard attention mechanism.

I recommend acceptance with minor revisions to address the weaknesses noted above, particularly by clarifying the novelty relative to prior work on monotonic alignment models and providing more thorough ablation studies. The paper has strong empirical results and valuable insights that would be of interest to the ACL community, but needs to more clearly position itself within the existing literature.
