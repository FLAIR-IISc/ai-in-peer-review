

# Review of "Morphological Inflection Generation with Hard Monotonic Attention"

## Summary
This paper proposes a "hard monotonic attention" model for morphological inflection generation, which handles the alignment between input and output sequences by using a dedicated control mechanism that either writes an output symbol or advances the attention pointer to the next input character. Instead of jointly learning alignment and transduction (as in standard soft attention models), the authors use independently learned character-level alignments to guide training. The model is evaluated on three morphological inflection datasets (CELEX, Wiktionary, SIGMORPHON2016) and claims to be the first neural model to surpass the non-neural latent variable WFST model of Dreyer et al. (2008) on the small CELEX dataset.

## Main Contributions (in order of strength)
1. A novel hard attention mechanism for sequence-to-sequence learning that uses independently learned alignments, which represents a bold departure from the standard soft attention paradigm.
2. A significant demonstration of the model's effectiveness on small training datasets (CELEX), where it outperforms previous neural models and surpasses the non-neural Dreyer et al. (2008) model.
3. An insightful analysis comparing the learned representations and alignments of hard vs. soft attention models, providing valuable insights into how these models encode morphological information.

## Strengths
1. **Novel approach to alignment**: The core contribution of using hard monotonic attention with independently learned alignments is a bold and significant departure from the standard soft attention paradigm. This addresses a fundamental assumption in neural sequence-to-sequence models (that alignment must be learned jointly with transduction) and offers a simpler, more interpretable alternative. The paper successfully demonstrates that this approach not only works but is particularly effective for the morphological inflection task, where monotonic alignments are common.

2. **Significant results on small data**: The CELEX dataset is notoriously small (only 500 training examples per inflection type), and the paper shows that their model is the first neural model to surpass the performance of the non-neural Dreyer et al. (2008) model on this dataset. This is a major contribution to the field, as handling small data is a persistent challenge in morphological processing, especially for morphologically rich languages. The learning curves in Figure 2 compellingly show how the hard attention model avoids overfitting on small datasets where soft attention models struggle.

3. **Insightful analysis of model behavior**: The paper's analysis of the learned alignments (Figure 3) and representations (Figure 4) provides valuable insights into how these models work. The demonstration that the hard attention model's representations are less sensitive to character position (due to explicit step operations) than soft attention models is particularly insightful and helps explain why the hard attention model generalizes better with small datasets. This analysis goes beyond just reporting results and provides theoretical understanding of the model's behavior.

## Weaknesses
1. **Novelty vs. prior work**: While the paper cites Graves (2012) and Yu et al. (2016), it doesn't clearly articulate how their approach differs from the RNN Transducer and related work. The distinction between "learning alignments independently" vs. "jointly training alignment and transduction" is important, but the paper could better emphasize this difference and how it leads to practical advantages. The related work section could be strengthened by more explicitly contrasting the proposed method with existing approaches.

2. **Implementation details**: The paper relies on an external alignment model (Sudoh et al., 2013) to provide the character-level alignments. However, it doesn't provide sufficient detail on how this alignment model is implemented or how sensitive the results are to the quality of the alignments. This makes it harder to evaluate the practicality of the approach and replicate the results.

3. **Limited generalizability**: The paper only applies the approach to morphological inflection, which is a specific task with naturally monotonic alignments. While this is a valid application, the paper doesn't clearly articulate how the approach could generalize to other sequence-to-sequence tasks with monotonic alignments (e.g., abstractive summarization, transliteration). The authors should discuss the broader applicability of their method.

## Questions to Authors
1. The paper states that the hard attention model is the first neural model to surpass the Dreyer et al. (2008) model on CELEX. Could you provide a more detailed comparison with other recent neural models that might have been tested on this dataset but not reported in the paper?

2. How sensitive is the performance of the hard attention model to the quality of the independently learned alignments? What happens if the alignment model makes errors? Could the model tolerate some alignment errors, or does it require near-perfect alignments?

3. The paper shows that the hard attention model performs slightly worse than soft attention on some languages in the SIGMORPHON dataset (e.g., Arabic, Navajo). Could you elaborate on why this might be the case, and whether the model could be adapted to handle such non-monotonic morphological phenomena?

## Overall Recommendation
I strongly recommend acceptance of this paper. The core contribution—using a hard monotonic attention mechanism with independently learned alignments—represents a bold and novel approach to sequence-to-sequence learning that challenges a fundamental assumption in the field. The paper demonstrates significant empirical results on the challenging CELEX dataset, where the model outperforms all previous neural approaches and surpasses a non-neural state-of-the-art model. The insightful analysis of learned representations and alignments provides valuable theoretical insights into how these models work.

While the novelty could be more clearly articulated against prior work, the contribution is significant enough to warrant acceptance. The paper's focus on a key challenge in NLP (handling small training datasets) with a novel approach aligns perfectly with ACL's interest in bold, innovative contributions. The minor methodological details and limited scope of the evaluation do not detract from the paper's core novelty and significance.

This paper offers a fresh perspective on sequence-to-sequence learning that could influence future work in morphological processing and potentially other tasks with monotonic alignments. I believe it makes a substantial contribution to the field.
