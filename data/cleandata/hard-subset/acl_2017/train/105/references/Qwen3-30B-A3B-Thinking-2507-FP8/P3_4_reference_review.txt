

# Peer Review: "Morphological Inflection Generation with Hard Monotonic Attention"

## Summary and Main Contributions

This paper proposes a hard monotonic attention model for morphological inflection generation, where the network either writes an output character or advances an attention pointer in the input sequence. The authors claim this approach better exploits the monotonic alignment common in morphological inflection tasks. Their main contributions are: (1) a hard attention model that decouples alignment learning from transduction; (2) superior performance on small datasets like CELEX compared to previous neural models; and (3) analysis comparing hard and soft attention mechanisms.

## Strengths

1. **Strong empirical evidence on small datasets**: The paper makes a compelling case for their model's advantage on the CELEX dataset (only 500 training examples per inflection type), where their model significantly outperforms previous neural approaches and even surpasses the latent variable WFST model by Dreyer et al. (2008). This is particularly important as most neural approaches struggle with low-resource settings, and the authors provide clear learning curves (Figure 2) showing how the hard attention model avoids overfitting on the small dataset.

2. **Insightful analysis of learned representations**: The authors' analysis of the representations learned by their model versus soft attention models (Figure 4) is valuable. They demonstrate that their model encodes character identity more strongly while less encoding positional information, which they correctly attribute to the step actions explicitly handling position tracking. This provides meaningful insights into how different attention mechanisms encode information for morphological tasks.

3. **Thorough evaluation across multiple datasets**: The evaluation on three distinct datasets (CELEX, Wiktionary, SIGMORPHON2016) with appropriate baselines demonstrates the robustness of their approach. Their model achieves state-of-the-art results on the Wiktionary dataset and performs well across languages in the SIGMORPHON dataset, showing the model's versatility.

## Weaknesses

1. **Questionable novelty**: The core idea of using a monotonic alignment with a dedicated control mechanism is very similar to the RNN Transducer (Graves, 2012) and Yu et al. (2016). The authors claim their model "differs" by learning alignments independently, but this is a minor variation rather than a substantial contribution. The related work section inadequately distinguishes their approach from these earlier works, making the novelty claim weak.

2. **Lack of explanation for why monotonicity helps**: While the authors claim their model outperforms soft attention models on small datasets because it enforces the monotonic assumption, they provide no theoretical or empirical explanation for *why* this assumption aids generalization. The learning curves (Figure 2) show better dev-set performance but don't explain the underlying reason, which is critical for understanding the contribution.

3. **Inadequate discussion of limitations**: The paper acknowledges their model performs worse on languages with vowel/consonant harmony (Table 3) but doesn't explore why. This is a significant limitation as many morphologically rich languages exhibit these phenomena. The authors should discuss how their model could be extended to handle such cases or why their approach is inherently unsuited for these languages.

4. **Insufficient detail on alignment process**: The authors use "the character alignment model of Sudoh et al. (2013)" without explaining how it's adapted for morphological inflection. For a model that critically depends on alignment quality, this lack of detail makes the approach difficult to reproduce and evaluate. The paper should provide more information about how alignments are computed and validated for morphological tasks.

## Questions to Authors

1. How does your model fundamentally differ from Graves' RNN Transducer (2012) and Yu et al.'s (2016) approach in a way that constitutes a novel contribution for morphological inflection tasks?

2. Can you provide a more detailed explanation of why the monotonic alignment assumption improves generalization on small datasets, beyond simply stating the model avoids overfitting? What specific aspects of the model's architecture or training process enable this?

3. The paper mentions your model outperforms Rastogi et al.'s (2016) neural WFST on CELEX, but Rastogi et al. claim their model surpasses Dreyer et al.'s latent variable model with twice as much data. How do you explain your model's better performance with the same amount of data?

## Overall Assessment

This paper presents a well-executed approach to morphological inflection generation that demonstrates clear empirical advantages on small datasets. The analysis of learned representations provides valuable insights into how attention mechanisms operate for this task. However, the novelty claim is undermined by the close relationship to existing work on monotonic alignment, and the paper would benefit from a more thorough discussion of the model's limitations and why the monotonic assumption helps with generalization.

The main contribution is the demonstration that a simpler hard attention model can outperform soft attention approaches on morphological inflection tasks, especially with limited training data. While the novelty is somewhat limited, the empirical results and analysis provide meaningful insights for the field. With revisions addressing the weaknesses noted above, particularly the novelty claim and limitations discussion, this paper would be a strong contribution to ACL.

**Recommendation: Weak Accept** (with major revisions addressing novelty and limitations)
