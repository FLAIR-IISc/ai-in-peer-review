- Proposes weakly supervised cross-lingual NER using annotation projection with a novel data selection scheme and representation projection via cross-lingual embeddings.  
- Primary contribution: frequency-based data selection for annotation projection that improves performance under low alignment accuracy.  
- Secondary contribution: representation projection mapping embeddings to English for direct model transfer without retraining.  
- Tertiary contribution: co-decoding schemes that combine annotation and representation projection.  
- Addresses a practical problem: building NER for low-resource languages without human annotation.  
- Data selection is effective and empirically validated, yielding +13.9 F1 on Japanese.  
- Representation projection is a clean, scalable embedding-mapping approach using English as a universal space.  
- Strong empirical results: co-decoding (especially rank-based) outperforms individual methods across six languages.  
- Combined system approaches supervised performance (e.g., German 69.3 F1 vs. supervised 72.6 F1).  
- Thorough evaluation on both in-house fine-grained and CoNLL datasets shows robustness.  
- Novelty of representation projection is overstated and closely related to prior embedding-mapping work (Mikolov et al., Guo et al.).  
- Data selection analysis is insufficient; assumes random alignment errors and lacks ablations for systematic errors (e.g., proper noun misalignments).  
- Inconsistent model choices (MEMM for annotation projection vs. neural nets for representation projection) hinder fair comparison and contradict "no retraining" justification.  
- Lacks error analysis explaining why co-decoding improves results and whether rank-based precision prioritization is language-dependent.  
- Claims of "state-of-the-art" are under-justified without quantified gaps and explanations for observed improvements.  
- Question: How does data selection handle systematic alignment errors like multi-token entities (e.g., "United States")?  
- Question: Why use different model architectures for the two projection methods and not a unified model for fair comparison?  
- Question: What specific error patterns does the combined German system correct compared to single methods?  
- Question: How sensitive is representation projection to dictionary size and applicability to languages with fewer translation resources?  
- Suggested improvements: better distinguish representation projection from prior work, use consistent architectures across methods, and add error analysis to clarify why co-decoding works.
