- Summary: Two weakly supervised cross-lingual NER methods—annotation projection with a novel frequency-based data selection for comparable corpora, and representation projection via cross-lingual embedding mapping—plus two co-decoding schemes.

- Strength: Data selection markedly improves performance with poor alignments (e.g., +13.9 F1 Japanese, +20.0 Korean) using a well-motivated frequency-based metric.

- Strength: Representation projection is an efficient model-transfer alternative needing no retraining for new languages, implemented via standard cross-lingual embedding mappings.

- Strength: Rank-based co-decoding consistently outperforms individual systems (e.g., +6.2 F1 for German) and yields SOTA results on CoNLL across evaluated languages.

- Weakness: The method depends on a high-quality source (English) NER system (88.9 F1), a limitation not sufficiently acknowledged for low-resource source scenarios.

- Weakness: OOV handling in representation projection is underdeveloped and unanalyzed, risking degraded performance in low-resource targets (e.g., NN1’s lower F1 for Dutch).

- Weakness: Data selection parameter tuning (q and n) lacks analysis of cross-language generalization and may require per-language calibration.

- Question: How would the data selection scheme perform if the source NER had lower accuracy (<85% F1), and what adjustments would be needed?

- Question: Can representation projection handle OOVs better (e.g., subword units or dynamic embeddings), and how would that affect low-resource language performance?
