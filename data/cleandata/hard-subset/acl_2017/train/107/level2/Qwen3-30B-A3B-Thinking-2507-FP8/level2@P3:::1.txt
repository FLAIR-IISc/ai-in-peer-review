

Summary: The paper proposes two weakly supervised approaches for cross-lingual NER without target-language human annotation: (1) annotation projection with a novel heuristic data selection scheme for comparable corpora, and (2) representation projection via cross-lingual word embedding mapping. It further introduces two co-decoding schemes to combine these approaches. The primary contributions are the data selection scheme for noisy annotation projection and the representation projection method, with co-decoding as a secondary contribution.

Strengths:
1. The data selection scheme significantly improves NER performance when alignment accuracy is low (e.g., +13.9 F1 for Japanese, +20.0 for Korean), addressing a critical gap in prior annotation projection work that ignored alignment quality. The frequency-based metric for selecting high-quality projection-labeled data is well-motivated and empirically validated.
2. The representation projection approach offers a novel, efficient alternative to existing model transfer methods (e.g., Täckström et al., 2012), requiring no retraining for new languages. Its implementation using cross-lingual embedding mappings is sound and leverages standard techniques (Mikolov et al., 2013b) in a targeted way.
3. The rank-based co-decoding scheme consistently outperforms individual systems (e.g., +6.2 F1 for German over best single approach) by leveraging complementary strengths (annotation projection’s high precision vs. representation projection’s balanced recall). This is rigorously evaluated across four languages and CoNLL data, with results showing the combined system outperforms prior SOTA methods on CoNLL test data.

Weaknesses:
1. The dependency on a high-quality source-language (English) NER system is not sufficiently acknowledged as a limitation. While the paper states "no human annotation in the target language," the method requires a pre-trained English NER model (with 88.9 F1 on English test data), which may not be feasible for low-resource source languages or non-English sources. The authors should clarify this constraint in the discussion.
2. The representation projection approach’s handling of out-of-vocabulary (OOV) words is underdeveloped. The paper states OOV words are projected to English embeddings "if found in English vocabulary, or to an NER-trained unk vector otherwise," but no analysis of OOV impact is provided. For low-resource target languages, OOV rates could significantly degrade performance, especially in the representation projection system (e.g., NN1’s F1 drops to 57.4 for Dutch vs. 63.4 for confidence co-decoding).
3. The data selection scheme’s parameter tuning (q and n thresholds) lacks deeper analysis. While Table 2 shows optimal thresholds per language, it does not address how these thresholds generalize across languages or whether they require language-specific calibration. This is critical for practical deployment.

Questions to Authors:
1. How would the data selection scheme perform if the source language NER system had lower accuracy (e.g., <85% F1), and what adjustments would be needed?
2. Could the representation projection approach be extended to handle OOV words more effectively (e.g., via subword units or dynamic embedding generation), and how might this impact performance on low-resource languages?
