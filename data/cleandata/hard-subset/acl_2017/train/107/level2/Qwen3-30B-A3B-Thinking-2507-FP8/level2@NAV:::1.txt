

Summary: This paper proposes two approaches for weakly supervised cross-lingual NER with no human annotation in the target language. The first approach uses annotation projection with a novel heuristic data selection scheme to identify high-quality weakly labeled data from noisy comparable corpora. The second approach projects word embeddings from target languages to a source language (English) for direct application of the source-language NER system. The authors also design two co-decoding schemes to combine the outputs of these two approaches. Evaluation on multiple languages shows their combined systems outperform previous state-of-the-art methods.

Main contributions: 
1. A heuristic data selection scheme for annotation projection that significantly improves performance when alignment accuracy is low (e.g., +13.9 F1 for Japanese, +20.0 for Korean).
2. A novel representation projection approach for direct NER model transfer based on cross-lingual word embeddings.
3. Two co-decoding schemes that effectively combine the outputs of the two projection-based approaches, with the rank-based scheme showing particular promise.

Strengths: 
1. The data selection scheme addresses a critical limitation of previous annotation projection methods by effectively filtering noisy data, which is particularly valuable when alignment accuracy is suboptimal.
2. The representation projection approach provides a simple, language-agnostic solution for cross-lingual NER that avoids the need for retraining for each new language.
3. The co-decoding schemes, especially the rank-based approach, successfully combine the complementary strengths of the two projection methods, demonstrating that 2+2>4 in this context.

Weaknesses:
1. The paper doesn't adequately address how the approach would perform with languages having extremely limited comparable corpora or very low alignment accuracy (below 60%).
2. The evaluation is limited to six languages, which doesn't sufficiently demonstrate the approach's scalability to a wider range of languages or low-resource scenarios.
3. The paper lacks a detailed analysis of why the rank-based co-decoding scheme outperforms the confidence-based approach, which would enhance the understanding of the method's underlying mechanics.

The paper presents a well-executed solution to an important problem in cross-lingual NER with clear experimental validation. While the current evaluation scope is somewhat limited, the proposed methods offer practical value for multilingual NER systems with minimal human annotation.
