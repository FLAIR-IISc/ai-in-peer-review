

**Summary and Contributions**  
This paper addresses cross-lingual NER with no target-language annotations via two novel approaches: (1) annotation projection with a heuristic data selection scheme to filter noisy comparable corpora, and (2) representation projection via cross-lingual word embeddings to transfer a source-language NER model directly. The primary contribution is the data selection scheme for annotation projection, which significantly improves accuracy when alignment is imperfect. The secondary contribution is the representation projection approach for direct model transfer, and the tertiary contribution is the co-decoding schemes combining both methods.  

**Strengths**  
1. **Practical data selection for annotation projection**: The frequency-based metric to select high-quality projection-labeled sentences is well-motivated and effective. The results (e.g., +13.9 F1 for Japanese with low alignment accuracy) demonstrate clear utility in noisy settings, addressing a critical gap in prior work that ignored alignment quality. This is the paper’s strongest contribution.  
2. **Rigorous multi-language evaluation**: The experiments span four in-house languages (Japanese, Korean, German, Portuguese) and three CoNLL languages (Spanish, Dutch, German), with thorough ablation studies. The consistent outperformance of co-decoding schemes (e.g., +6.2 F1 over individual methods for Portuguese) validates the approach’s robustness.  
3. **Clear co-decoding design**: The rank-based scheme (prioritizing annotation projection’s high-precision outputs) is intuitive and empirically superior to confidence-based alternatives. The explanation of complementary strengths (annotation projection’s precision vs. representation projection’s recall) is compelling and well-supported by results.  

**Weaknesses**  
1. **Limited baseline comparison**: The paper compares only against Täckström et al. (2012) and Tsai et al. (2016), omitting more recent weakly supervised NER work (e.g., from 2015–2016). For instance, the authors should clarify how their representation projection approach differs from multilingual embedding methods like those in Artetxe et al. (2018) or contextualized transfer (e.g., Devlin et al., 2019), even if not directly comparable.  
2. **Insufficient justification for representation projection**: While the embedding mapping is well-described, the paper lacks analysis of *why* word embeddings transfer well to NER. For example, do high-frequency entities (e.g., "United States" as GPE) align better than low-frequency ones? A qualitative case study would strengthen the claim that embeddings capture NER-relevant semantics.  
3. **Arbitrary data selection thresholds**: The thresholds (e.g., *q* = 0.8, *n* = 4 for Japanese) are tuned empirically without systematic analysis. The authors should discuss how threshold choices impact scalability to languages with varying data quality or entity distributions.  

**Questions to Authors**  
1. The representation projection approach uses 800-dimensional embeddings for target languages. How do these compare to 300-dimensional embeddings (used for English) in terms of transfer performance? Did the authors experiment with uniform dimensionality?  
2. For the co-decoding schemes, the rank-based method outperforms confidence-based in all languages. Was this due to the annotation projection system’s consistent high precision, or did other factors (e.g., entity type distributions) play a role?  
3. The paper states that "no human annotation" is required in the target language, but the cross-lingual embedding mapping relies on a small bilingual dictionary. How scalable is this to low-resource languages with minimal translation resources?  

**Additional Comments**  
The paper is well-structured and clearly written, with strong empirical validation. The data selection scheme is particularly valuable for low-resource scenarios where alignment quality varies. While the representation projection approach is less novel than the data selection method, it is a solid contribution that complements the main innovation. The authors should clarify the practicality of their framework for real-world deployment (e.g., dependency on comparable corpora size). Overall, this work provides a strong, actionable baseline for cross-lingual NER.
