- Proposes two transfer methods for cross-lingual NER with no target annotations: annotation projection with a heuristic data selection, and representation projection via cross-lingual embeddings; also introduces co-decoding to combine them.  
- Primary contribution: a frequency-based data selection scheme that significantly reduces noise from imperfect alignments.  
- Secondary contribution: direct model transfer using cross-lingual word embeddings.  
- Tertiary contribution: co-decoding schemes that combine annotation- and representation-projection outputs.  
- Strength: Practical and effective data selection metric, yielding large F1 gains in noisy alignment scenarios (e.g., +13.9 F1 for Japanese).  
- Strength: Rigorous multilingual evaluation across seven languages with thorough ablation studies demonstrating robustness.  
- Strength: Clear, intuitive co-decoding design (rank-based) that leverages complementary precision/recall strengths and outperforms confidence-based methods.  
- Weakness: Limited baseline comparisons, omitting some recent weakly supervised and multilingual embedding/contextual transfer work.  
- Weakness: Insufficient analysis explaining why embedding mapping transfers NER-relevant semantics (e.g., frequency effects on entity alignment).  
- Weakness: Data selection thresholds are empirically chosen without systematic analysis of their impact across languages and data qualities.  
- Question: How does using 800-d target embeddings vs. 300-d English embeddings affect transfer performance; were uniform dimensionalities tested?  
- Question: Does rank-based co-decoding win solely because annotation projection has consistent high precision, or do other factors (like entity-type distribution) contribute?  
- Question: How scalable is the approach when the bilingual dictionary for embedding mapping is very small or for extremely low-resource languages?  
- Additional: Paper is well-written with strong empirical validation; data selection is especially valuable, but authors should clarify deployment practicality and dependency on comparable-corpus size.
