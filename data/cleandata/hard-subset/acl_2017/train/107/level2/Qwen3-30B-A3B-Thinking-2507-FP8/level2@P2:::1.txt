

**Summary and Main Contributions**  
The paper proposes a weakly supervised cross-lingual NER approach using two projection-based methods: (1) annotation projection with a novel data selection scheme to filter noisy target-language NER labels from comparable corpora, and (2) representation projection via cross-lingual word embeddings to transfer a source-language NER model without retraining. The primary contribution is the data selection scheme for annotation projection, which significantly improves performance when alignment accuracy is low. A secondary contribution is the representation projection approach for direct model transfer. The third contribution is co-decoding schemes combining both methods.

**Strengths**  
1. **Practical problem addressed**: The paper tackles a genuine challenge in multilingual NLP—building NER systems for low-resource languages without human annotation. This is highly relevant for real-world applications where annotated data is scarce.  
2. **Effective data selection for annotation projection**: The frequency-based metric for selecting high-quality projection-labeled sentences is well-motivated and empirically validated. The +13.9 F1 improvement for Japanese (low alignment accuracy) demonstrates its value where prior methods fail.  
3. **Novel representation projection approach**: The word embedding mapping strategy is a clean, scalable solution for direct model transfer, avoiding the need for language-specific retraining. The use of English as a universal space simplifies deployment.  
4. **Strong empirical results**: The co-decoding schemes (particularly rank-based) consistently outperform individual methods across six languages. For German, the combined system reaches 69.3 F1 (vs. 68.1 for best prior work), approaching supervised performance (72.6 F1).  
5. **Thorough evaluation**: Results on both in-house (fine-grained entity types) and CoNLL (standard 4-type) datasets demonstrate robustness across language and task settings.

**Weaknesses**  
1. **Overstated novelty of representation projection**: The core idea (mapping embeddings to a source language) is closely related to Mikolov et al. (2013b), which the paper cites but doesn't adequately distinguish from. The paper doesn't clarify how their NER-specific adaptation differs from prior cross-lingual embedding work for other tasks (e.g., Guo et al., 2015).  
2. **Insufficient analysis of data selection limitations**: The frequency metric assumes alignment errors are random, but alignment errors may be systematic (e.g., consistently misaligning proper nouns). The paper lacks ablation studies showing how the scheme handles such cases.  
3. **Inconsistent model comparisons**: The annotation projection uses MEMMs, while representation projection uses neural networks (NN1/NN2). This makes fair comparison difficult—why not use the same model architecture for both approaches? The paper claims "no retraining" but doesn't justify the architectural mismatch.  
4. **Lack of error analysis**: The paper shows F1 improvements but doesn't diagnose *why* the combined system works better (e.g., are errors complementary?). For example, the rank-based scheme prioritizes AP's high precision—yet it's unclear if this is universally applicable or language-dependent.  
5. **Under-justified claims about "state-of-the-art"**: The paper states their system "outperforms two state-of-the-art approaches" but doesn't quantify the gap (e.g., "by 1.2 F1 points on German") or explain *why* it outperforms (e.g., better handling of alignment noise vs. model transfer).

**Questions to Authors**  
1. How does the data selection scheme handle systematic alignment errors (e.g., consistent misalignment of "United States" as a single entity)?  
2. Why was the model architecture mismatch (MEMM vs. neural network) chosen for the two projection methods? Would a fairer comparison use the same model?  
3. In Table 5, the co-decoding scheme for German outperforms prior work by 1.2 F1. What specific error patterns does the combined system fix that single methods miss?  
4. How sensitive is the representation projection to the size of the training dictionary? The paper uses 5K-6K word pairs, but does this scale to languages with fewer translation resources?  

**Additional Comments**  
The paper would benefit from: (1) a clearer distinction between their representation projection and existing cross-lingual embedding work, (2) a consistent model architecture for both projection methods, and (3) an error analysis to explain *why* co-decoding works. The approach is promising, but the technical novelty needs sharper articulation.
