## Review of “Weakly Supervised Cross-Lingual Named Entity Recognition via Effective Annotation and Representation Projection”

This paper presents two approaches for cross-lingual NER without target language annotation: annotation projection from comparable corpora and representation projection of word embeddings. The authors further propose co-decoding schemes to combine the outputs of these approaches. The work addresses the significant challenge of building NER systems for low-resource languages, a critical problem in multilingual NLP.

The primary contribution lies in the heuristic data selection scheme for annotation projection. The method for identifying higher-quality projected labels based on frequency analysis appears novel and demonstrably improves performance, particularly when alignment accuracy is low. A second contribution is the exploration of representation projection for direct model transfer. While representation projection itself isn’t novel, the application to cross-lingual NER and integration with annotation projection is a valuable addition. Finally, the investigation of co-decoding schemes, specifically the rank-based approach, presents a practical method for leveraging the strengths of both projection techniques.

Strengths include a thorough experimental evaluation on both in-house and CoNLL datasets across multiple languages. The results consistently demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed data selection scheme and the improvement gained by combining the two approaches. The ablation studies, comparing different neural network architectures and co-decoding methods, provide valuable insights into the contributions of each component. The inclusion of comparisons to existing state-of-the-art methods reinforces the significance of the work.

However, the paper suffers from some weaknesses. The reliance on comparable corpora for annotation projection limits the applicability in scenarios where such resources are unavailable. While the data selection scheme mitigates noise, the performance is still heavily dependent on the initial quality of the comparable corpus and alignment accuracy. The representation projection approach, while effective, lacks detailed discussion on the limitations of linear mappings for capturing complex cross-lingual semantic relationships. Furthermore, the paper could benefit from a more detailed error analysis to identify specific failure cases and areas for future improvement. The discussion of related work could also be more comprehensive, particularly regarding recent advances in cross-lingual embeddings.

**Questions to Authors:**

1.  Could you elaborate on the limitations of the linear mapping used for representation projection and discuss potential avenues for exploring non-linear mappings?
2.  How sensitive is the performance of the data selection scheme to the choice of the thresholds 'q' and 'n'? Is there a method for automatically tuning these parameters?
3.  What is the impact of the size of the training dictionary used for cross-lingual embedding mapping? Is there a point of diminishing returns?



Overall, this is a solid contribution to the field of cross-lingual NER. The proposed methods are well-motivated, thoroughly evaluated, and demonstrate significant improvements over existing approaches. While some limitations exist, the paper offers a practical and effective solution for building NER systems in low-resource languages. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions addressing the points raised above.
