This paper presents two approaches for weakly supervised cross-lingual NER: annotation projection with a data selection heuristic, and representation projection (embedding transfer). It further combines these approaches with two co-decoding schemes. The paper is well-motivated, addressing the challenge of building NER systems for low-resource languages.

**Contributions:**

1. **Effective Data Selection for Annotation Projection:** The heuristic data selection scheme for improving the quality of weakly labeled data via comparable corpora is a strong contribution. The idea of using frequency counts to assess label reliability is intuitive and demonstrably effective, particularly when alignment accuracy is low.
2. **Representation Projection via Embedding Transfer:** The use of cross-lingual embedding mappings to transfer knowledge from a source language NER system to a target language is a sensible approach, and the comparison of different neural network architectures (NN1 and NN2) for the source system is valuable.
3. **Co-decoding Schemes:**  The exploration of co-decoding strategies, especially the "rank-based" scheme that prioritizes the annotation projection system, provides a practical method for combining the strengths of both approaches.

**Strengths:**

1. **Comprehensive Evaluation:** The paper includes extensive experiments on both in-house and publicly available datasets (CoNLL), covering multiple target languages. This allows for a robust assessment of the proposed methods.
2. **Clear Methodology:**  The descriptions of the annotation projection, representation projection, and co-decoding schemes are well-defined and easy to follow. The detailed explanation of the data selection heuristic and the rationale behind the co-decoding schemes are particularly helpful.
3. **Strong Baselines & Comparisons:** The comparison against state-of-the-art cross-lingual NER approaches (Täckström et al., 2012; Tsai et al., 2016) demonstrates the effectiveness of the proposed methods. Achieving better performance on the CoNLL test data is a significant result.
4. **Practical Relevance:** The ability to build NER systems without human annotation in the target language is highly valuable for low-resource scenarios, and the methods presented are potentially applicable to a wide range of languages.
5. **Detailed Ablation Studies:** The comparison of NN1 and NN2, and the analysis of different co-decoding schemes offer insights into the contribution of each component to the overall performance.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Analysis of Failure Cases:** While the paper presents quantitative results, it lacks a detailed qualitative analysis of the types of errors made by the different systems. Understanding *why* the systems fail in certain cases would provide valuable insights for future improvements. For example, the paper mentions AP having high precision but low recall. Further investigation into the types of entities missed by AP would be informative.
2. **Dependence on Alignment Quality:** Although the data selection scheme mitigates the impact of poor alignment, the annotation projection approach remains inherently reliant on the quality of the alignment between the comparable corpora. This dependency should be further emphasized in the discussion.  What is the effect of drastically *poor* alignment? Is there a threshold below which annotation projection becomes ineffective?
3. **Dictionary Size Sensitivity:** The representation projection approach relies on a seed dictionary for learning the cross-lingual embedding mapping. The paper mentions a dictionary size of 5K-6K word pairs, but a more in-depth analysis of the impact of dictionary size on performance would be beneficial. Is there a saturation point?
4. **Complexity of Co-decoding:** While the co-decoding schemes show improvements, their implementation and tuning might be more complex than simply using one of the projection-based approaches. A discussion of the computational cost and practical considerations of implementing these schemes would be useful.
5. **Limited Error Analysis regarding Language-Specific Characteristics:** The paper doesn’t sufficiently address how language-specific characteristics (morphology, syntax) might affect the performance of the proposed methods.




**Questions to Authors:**

1.  Could you provide some examples of the types of errors made by the annotation projection and representation projection systems?  Are there specific entity types that are consistently misidentified?
2.  How sensitive is the performance of the annotation projection approach to the quality of the alignment between the comparable corpora?  Is there a lower bound on alignment accuracy below which the approach becomes ineffective?
3.  What is the impact of dictionary size on the performance of the representation projection approach?  Have you explored dictionary sizes significantly smaller or larger than 5K-6K word pairs?
4. Could you detail the computational cost and complexity of implementing the co-decoding schemes compared to using the individual projection-based approaches?

**Additional Comments:**

*   The paper could benefit from a brief discussion of potential limitations and future research directions.
*   Consider adding a table summarizing the key characteristics of the datasets used in the experiments (e.g., size, number of entity types, language).
*   "Co-decoding" is a somewhat vague term.  Consider a more descriptive name that reflects the specific mechanism employed.



Overall, this is a strong paper that makes a valuable contribution to the field of cross-lingual NER. The proposed methods are well-motivated, thoroughly evaluated, and achieve competitive performance. Addressing the weaknesses identified above would further strengthen the paper and enhance its impact. I recommend acceptance.
