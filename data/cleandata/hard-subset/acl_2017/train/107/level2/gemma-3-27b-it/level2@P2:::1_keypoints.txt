- Heuristic data selection for annotation projection improves projected label quality and boosts performance, especially with poor alignments.
- Projecting word embeddings for cross-lingual NER is a practical approach and useful when combined with annotation projection, though not fundamentally novel.
- Co-decoding schemes, particularly a rank-based method prioritizing annotation projection, effectively combine the strengths of both approaches.
- Thorough evaluation on CoNLL and four in-house languages supports the methods' generalizability.
- Clear motivation and problem definition for building NER systems for low-resource languages.
- Well-defined experiments and ablation studies (NN1 vs NN2, with/without co-decoding) isolate component effects.
- Practical insight: annotation projection tends to have high precision/low recall while representation projection has more balanced precision/recall.
- Methods achieve competitive results, outperforming prior cross-lingual NER approaches on CoNLL test data.
- Representation projection component relies on established techniques (embeddings and linear mapping), limiting novelty.
- Annotation projection still depends heavily on alignment quality despite data selection measures.
- Lack of detailed discussion on hyperparameter tuning (e.g., how q and n were chosen and their impact).
- Insufficient explanation of why the rank-based co-decoding prioritizing annotation projection works so well.
- Co-decoding schemes may be specific to this setup; more general approaches could be explored.
- Missing qualitative error analysis to show which entity types or projection errors are most problematic.
- Question: How sensitive are results to the choice of q and n, and were alternative annotation-quality metrics tried?
- Question: How does the size of the training dictionary for learning the mapping Mf affect performance?
- Question: Can you clarify the intuition behind prioritizing annotation projection in rank-based co-decoding?
- Question: Are there plans to release the code and data for reproducibility?
- Paper is generally well-written and easy to follow.
- Suggestion: add a table summarizing key differences with related work and fix minor introductory sentence-structure issues.
