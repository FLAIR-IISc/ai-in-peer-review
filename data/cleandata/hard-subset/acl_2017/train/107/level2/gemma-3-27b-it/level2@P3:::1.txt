This paper addresses the challenging problem of weakly supervised cross-lingual NER, proposing two main approaches: annotation projection with a novel data selection heuristic, and representation projection via cross-lingual word embeddings. The paper further introduces two co-decoding schemes to combine the outputs of these approaches. The experimental results demonstrate improvements over existing cross-lingual NER methods on both in-house and CoNLL datasets.

The primary contribution of this work is the heuristic data selection scheme for annotation projection. While annotation projection is a well-established technique, the explicit consideration of projection quality via a frequency-based metric and the resulting data selection process represent a valuable addition. The observed improvements, particularly in low-resource scenarios (Japanese, Korean), validate the effectiveness of this approach. A second key contribution lies in the combined system leveraging both annotation projection and representation projection. The co-decoding schemes, particularly the rank-based approach, effectively integrate the strengths of each projection method, leading to superior performance. The thorough experimental evaluation across multiple languages and datasets, including a comparison to state-of-the-art methods, strengthens the credibility of the findings.

Strengths:

1. **Practical Relevance:** The problem addressed is highly relevant to real-world applications where labeled data in target languages is scarce. The proposed solutions offer a viable path toward building multilingual NER systems without costly human annotation.
2. **Well-Defined Heuristic:** The data selection scheme for annotation projection is clearly defined and intuitively motivated. The use of frequency counts to estimate annotation quality is a reasonable and effective approach, and the provided experiments demonstrate its benefits.
3. **Comprehensive Evaluation:** The extensive experimental evaluation across diverse languages and datasets is a significant strength. Comparing against existing methods on standard benchmarks (CoNLL) and in-house data provides strong evidence for the effectiveness of the proposed techniques. The inclusion of both development and test set results is appreciated.
4. **Effective Combination:** The co-decoding schemes demonstrate the benefit of integrating different approaches. The observed performance gains indicate that combining annotation projection and representation projection leads to a more robust and accurate NER system.
5. **Clear Presentation:** The paper is generally well-written and clearly explains the proposed methods and experimental setup. The related work section provides a good overview of existing techniques.

Weaknesses:

1. **Limited Analysis of Error Cases:** While the paper presents overall performance results, a more detailed analysis of error cases would be beneficial. Identifying common errors made by each approach and the co-decoding schemes could provide insights for further improvement. What types of entities are consistently misidentified, and why?
2. **Dependence on Alignment Quality:** The annotation projection approach is inherently sensitive to the quality of the alignment between source and target languages. While the data selection scheme mitigates this issue to some extent, the paper could benefit from a more detailed discussion of the impact of alignment quality on performance. Perhaps a correlation analysis between alignment scores and NER accuracy could be presented.
3. **Parameter Sensitivity:** The performance of the data selection scheme depends on the choice of threshold parameters (q and n). The paper mentions tuning these parameters but lacks a detailed explanation of the tuning process. A sensitivity analysis, demonstrating how performance varies with different parameter settings, would be helpful.
4. **Representation Projection Details:** The description of the representation projection approach, particularly the linear mapping step, could be more detailed. What are the limitations of using a linear mapping? Have non-linear mappings been considered? The choice of embedding dimensionality (300 for English, 800 for others) also warrants further justification.
5. **Comparison to Stronger Baselines:** While the paper compares against previous work, more recent and stronger cross-lingual NER baselines could be included for a more comprehensive comparison.



Questions to Authors:

1. Could you provide a more detailed analysis of the types of errors made by each approach and the co-decoding schemes?
2. How does the alignment quality between the source and target languages affect the performance of the annotation projection approach? Can you quantify this relationship?
3. Can you elaborate on the tuning process for the data selection parameters (q and n)? What methods were used to determine the optimal values for each language?
4. Have you considered using non-linear mappings for representation projection, and if so, what were the results?
5. Could you compare the performance of your combined system to more recent and stronger cross-lingual NER baselines?

Additional Comments:

- The paper refers to “2nd-order MEMM model” – clarifying this terminology (e.g., first-order, second-order referring to the Markov assumption) would improve readability.
- A visual illustration of the co-decoding schemes would be helpful.
