This paper presents two approaches for weakly supervised cross-lingual NER – annotation projection with data selection, and representation projection via word embeddings – along with co-decoding schemes to combine their outputs. The authors evaluate these approaches on both in-house and CoNLL datasets, demonstrating improvements over existing cross-lingual NER methods.

**Contributions:**

1. **Effective Data Selection for Annotation Projection:** The heuristic data selection scheme for refining projected data quality is a significant contribution. The paper convincingly demonstrates that selectively using projection data, based on frequency of projected tags, improves performance, especially when alignment accuracy is low. This addresses a critical issue with annotation projection – the noisiness of the projected labels.
2. **Representation Projection with Embeddings:**  The exploration of projecting word embeddings for cross-lingual transfer is a reasonable and potentially valuable approach. While not entirely novel (cross-lingual embeddings have been explored), its application specifically to NER and its integration with annotation projection represents a contribution.
3. **Co-Decoding Schemes:** The design and evaluation of co-decoding schemes, particularly the rank-based scheme prioritizing annotation projection, show practical insight into combining the strengths of different approaches. This illustrates a pragmatic approach to maximizing performance with limited resources.

**Strengths:**

1. **Comprehensive Evaluation:** The paper presents a thorough evaluation on multiple datasets – both in-house and publicly available (CoNLL). This provides a strong foundation for assessing the generalizability of the proposed methods.  The inclusion of results on four in-house languages is commendable.
2. **Clear Motivation and Problem Definition:** The paper clearly articulates the challenges of building NER systems for low-resource languages and positions the work as a solution to this problem.
3. **Well-Defined Experiments:** The experimental setup is well-defined, and the ablation studies (comparing NN1 vs NN2, with and without co-decoding) help to isolate the impact of individual components.
4. **Practical Insights:** The paper provides practical insights into the limitations of each approach (e.g., AP having high precision/low recall, RP having balanced precision/recall), guiding the design of the co-decoding schemes.
5. **Competitive Results:** The methods outperform state-of-the-art cross-lingual NER approaches on the CoNLL test data, establishing the effectiveness of the proposed techniques.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Novelty in Representation Projection:** The representation projection component, while solid, relies heavily on established techniques (word embeddings and linear mapping). The novelty lies more in its *application* to cross-lingual NER rather than a fundamentally new technique.  The paper could benefit from a more nuanced discussion of how this work differs from existing cross-lingual embedding methods.
2. **Dependency on Alignment Quality:** While the data selection scheme mitigates the impact of poor alignment, the entire annotation projection approach remains heavily dependent on the quality of the alignment between the source and target languages. This limitation isn't fully addressed, and further exploration of robust alignment techniques or alternatives to parallel corpora might be beneficial.
3. **Detailed Hyperparameter Tuning:** The paper mentions tuning parameters (e.g., q and n for data selection), but lacks detailed discussion of the process.  How were these parameters chosen? A more thorough analysis of the impact of these parameters on performance would strengthen the results.
4. **Co-Decoding Complexity:** The rationale behind the "rank-based" co-decoding scheme, while effective, isn’t thoroughly explained.  Expanding on *why* prioritizing annotation projection works so well would be valuable. Also, the schemes seem quite specific to this setup; exploring more general co-decoding approaches could be considered.
5. **Error Analysis:** A qualitative error analysis would provide further insights into the types of errors made by each system and inform future improvements.  What kinds of entities are consistently misidentified? What are the common sources of error in projection?



**Questions to Authors:**

1. Regarding the data selection scheme: How sensitive are the results to the choice of the *q* and *n* thresholds?  Did you explore other metrics for evaluating annotation quality beyond frequency?
2. For representation projection: How does the size of the training dictionary used for learning the mapping *M<sub>f</sub>* impact performance?
3. Concerning the co-decoding schemes: Could you provide a more detailed explanation of the intuition behind the rank-based co-decoding approach?  What makes annotation projection particularly well-suited to being prioritized?
4. Are there any plans to release the code and data used in this study? This would greatly facilitate reproducibility and further research in this area.

**Additional Comments:**

*   The paper is generally well-written and easy to follow.
*   Consider adding a table summarizing the key differences between related work in cross-lingual NER.
*   Minor: The sentence structure in the introduction could be improved in a few places for clarity.
