**Summary and Contributions**

This paper addresses the challenge of building Named Entity Recognition (NER) systems for target languages where no annotated data is available. The authors propose and evaluate two distinct weakly supervised, cross-lingual methods. The first, an "annotation projection" approach, projects NER labels from a source language (English) onto a target language using comparable corpora, and critically, introduces a novel heuristic data selection scheme to filter noisy projections. The second, a "representation projection" approach, learns a mapping to project target language word embeddings into the source language's vector space, allowing a single source-language model to be applied directly. The key contribution is showing that these two disparate methods can be effectively combined via co-decoding to achieve state-of-the-art results. The secondary contribution is the simple yet highly effective data selection heuristic for annotation projection, which substantially boosts performance, particularly in low-resource or poor-alignment scenarios.

**Strengths**

1.  **Thorough and Convincing Evaluation:** The paper’s strongest asset is its comprehensive evaluation. The methods are tested on both a fine-grained in-house dataset (with over 50 entity types) and the standard CoNLL public benchmark across multiple languages. This demonstrates the robustness and general applicability of the proposed framework.
2.  **State-of-the-Art Performance:** The combined system is shown to outperform two strong, contemporary cross-lingual NER baselines on the CoNLL test data. This establishes the proposed method as a new state-of-the-art and a valuable baseline for future research in this area.
3.  **Effective Combination of Orthogonal Approaches:** The paper cleverly synthesizes a data-level projection method (annotation projection) with a model-level one (representation projection). The analysis of the distinct precision/recall trade-offs of each system and the design of co-decoding schemes to exploit these differences is insightful and leads to significant performance gains over either individual system.

**Weaknesses**

1.  **Insufficient Analysis of Co-Decoding Schemes:** The choice between the rank-based and confidence-based co-decoding schemes appears to be purely empirical and dataset-dependent. The paper would be strengthened by a deeper analysis into *why* one scheme outperforms the other on different datasets. For instance, is this behavior linked to the number of entity types, the performance of the base models, or other data characteristics?
2.  **Lack of Discussion on Limitations and Dependencies:** The paper does not explicitly discuss the limitations of its methods. Annotation projection is dependent on the availability and quality of comparable corpora and alignment models, while representation projection relies on a seed dictionary. An analysis of the system's sensitivity to the quality and size of these prerequisite resources is a notable omission.
3.  **Unclear Motivation for NN2 Architecture:** The description of the NN2 architecture, which adds a "smoothing layer," lacks sufficient detail and motivation. The rationale for using a weighted average of prototype vectors as input is not clearly explained, which hinders full understanding and reproducibility.

**Questions to Authors**

1.  Could you elaborate on the intuition behind the differing performance of the co-decoding schemes across the in-house and CoNLL datasets?
2.  How sensitive is the representation projection method to the size and quality of the seed dictionary used for learning the embedding mapping? What happens to performance if a much smaller or noisier dictionary is used?
