- Paper presents a framework for weakly supervised cross-lingual NER using annotation projection (AP) and representation projection (RP) plus co-decoding to build target-language NER without annotated data.  
- AP transfers labels via comparable corpora with a heuristic data selection scheme to filter noisy projections.  
- RP maps target word embeddings linearly into the source space to apply a source-language neural NER model without retraining.  
- Two co-decoding schemes combine AP and RP outputs to leverage their complementary strengths.  
- Evaluated on six target languages, fine-grained and coarse-grained NER, and CoNLL benchmarks, achieving state-of-the-art results.  

Main contributions:
- Integrates data-level (AP) and model-level (RP) transfer into a single effective framework.  
- Introduces a simple quality-scoring data selection heuristic that substantially improves AP.  
- Provides a strong, comprehensive empirical evaluation across languages and tasks.  

Strengths:
- Achieves state-of-the-art performance, outperforming strong prior work on CoNLL.  
- Data filtering for AP yields large F1 gains, especially for languages with poor alignments (e.g., Korean, Japanese).  
- Well-motivated combination of AP and RP with analysis of their precision/recall trade-offs.  
- Clear writing and sound experimental setup using both in-house and public datasets.  

Weaknesses:
- RP offers limited technical novelty, mainly applying existing bilingual embedding mapping techniques.  
- Framework relies on heuristics and tuned parameters (e.g., q, n, co-decoding choice, dictionary size) with little guidance for zero-resource settings.  
- Inconsistent NN model behavior (NN1 vs NN2) across datasets without analysis of why smoothing helps only in some cases.  
- Lacks qualitative error analysis to characterize system-specific and persistent error types.  

Questions for authors:
- How would data selection thresholds (q, n) be set in a true zero-resource scenario without target-language annotations?  
- Is the AP system’s higher precision consistent across languages/datasets, and how would co-decoding adapt if that assumption fails?  
- Why do larger bilingual dictionaries (>5–6K pairs) harm RP performance—overfitting or noisy pairs, and can you hypothesize or analyze this?
