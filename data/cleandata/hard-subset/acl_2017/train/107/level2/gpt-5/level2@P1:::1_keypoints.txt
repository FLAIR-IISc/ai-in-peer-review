- Paper proposes cross-lingual NER without target annotation using annotation projection with heuristic data selection.
- Paper proposes representation projection by learning a linear mapping of target embeddings to English and applying an English NER model.
- Paper introduces two co-decoding schemes (exclude-O confidence-based and rank-based) to combine the two approaches.
- Strong empirical evaluation across 4 in-house languages (>50 types) and 3 CoNLL languages (4 types), showing consistent F1 gains and robustness.
- Co-decoding consistently improves over individual systems and matches/outperforms prior cross-lingual baselines on CoNLL test.
- Methods are simple, resource-light, reproducible in engineering terms, and can scale to new languages (representation projection needs no retraining).
- Frequency-based data selection and linear mapping are straightforward and have clear practical appeal.
- Analysis shows data selection helps most when alignment is weak (e.g., Korean, Japanese), validating the selection heuristic.
- Minimal feature engineering: neural NER uses only embeddings and context, improving transferability across languages.
- Inclusion of supervised upper bounds helps contextualize performance and shows weakly supervised methods approach supervised levels for some languages.
- Core techniques have limited novelty and miss stronger variants (e.g., orthogonal/Procrustes mappings, CSLS, hubness mitigation).
- Data selection heuristic is ad hoc and lacks comparison to alternative selection criteria or prior projection selection methods.
- Dependency on phrase tables and alignments is under-specified; unclear how sentence alignments are obtained for comparable (non-parallel) corpora.
- Practicality for very low-resource languages without reliable aligners/phrase tables is uncertain.
- Reproducibility concerns: in-house datasets, alignment construction, corpus sizes/domains, tokenization, and the 50+ type schema are not fully released or specified.
- Limited ablations and analysis: dictionary size, weighted vs. unweighted mapping, embedding dimensionality, smoothing layer effects, and threshold sensitivity are not explored.
- No statistical significance testing reported on CoNLL test and minimal error analysis (boundary vs. type errors, rare vs. frequent entities, language-specific failures).
- Co-decoding is heuristic and uncalibrated; no exploration of principled fusion methods (stacking, joint decoding, calibration).
- Lack of analysis on whether gains come from complementary coverage or O bias and no oracle/per-type breakdown.
- Questions: How are sentence alignments and phrase tables created for comparable corpora and what supervision/data do they require?
- Questions: Were orthogonal (Procrustes) mappings, unit-normalized embeddings, and weighted vs. unweighted mappings tried, and is mapping quality correlated with NER accuracy?
- Questions: How were data selection thresholds (q, n) tuned and how sensitive are results to them; can learning curves vs. retained data size be provided?
- Questions: In rank-based co-decoding, what fraction of entities come from AP vs. NN, and are gains from boundary or type improvements; was confidence calibration tried?
- Questions: How is the NER-trained unk vector constructed, what fraction of tokens use it at test time, and how does that affect morphologically rich languages?
- Suggestion: Compare to more recent cross-lingual embedding methods (joint training, bilingual skip-gram) to assess mapping simplicity vs. limitations.
- Suggestion: Add qualitative case studies showing corrected vs. discarded projections and typical mapping successes/failures.
- Overall: Well-executed empirical paper with practical, scalable techniques and strong results, but moderate methodological novelty and some missing details for full reproducibility and analysis.
