Summary and contributions
This paper studies cross-lingual NER without target-language annotation and proposes two complementary projection-based approaches plus simple co-decoding to combine them:
- Primary: An annotation projection approach with a heuristic data selection method that filters noisy projected sentences from comparable corpora using an entity–tag frequency table and per-sentence quality/coverage thresholds.
- Secondary: A representation projection approach that learns a linear mapping from target-language word embeddings into English and directly applies an English-only neural NER model at test time.
- Tertiary: Two co-decoding schemes (exclude-O confidence-based and rank-based) that exploit complementary precision/recall profiles of the two systems.

Strengths
- Strong empirical scope with consistent gains across languages and settings. The paper evaluates on four in-house languages with >50 types and three CoNLL languages with 4 types, spanning diverse scripts and alignment quality. The co-decoding improves over both individual systems consistently (e.g., +2.9 to +6.2 F1 on in-house; +1.3 to +3.6 on CoNLL dev), and matches or outperforms strong prior cross-lingual baselines (Täckström et al., Tsai et al.) on CoNLL test. This breadth increases confidence that the methods are robust.
- Simple, resource-light methods with clear engineering appeal. Both the frequency-based data selection and linear embedding mapping are straightforward, easily reproduced, and scale to new languages without retraining (for representation projection). The rank-based co-decoding heuristic is also simple yet effective, aligning with observed precision/recall characteristics.
- Insightful analysis of alignment noise and data selection. Table 2 demonstrates that the proposed selection is most beneficial when alignment is weak (e.g., Korean, Japanese), with large F1 gains over using all projected data. This validates the motivation and offers a practical recipe for noisy comparable corpora.
- Minimal feature engineering, good transferability. The neural NER relies only on word embeddings and context, avoiding language-specific features and making the direct transfer approach broadly applicable. The study of two architectures (NN1 vs. NN2) and their precision/recall trade-offs is informative and leveraged by co-decoding.
- Sensible comparisons to supervised upper bounds. Reporting supervised results in both settings grounds the contributions and shows that combined weakly supervised systems approach supervised performance for some languages.

Weaknesses
- Limited novelty in core techniques; missing comparisons to stronger variants. The weighted bilingual mapping is a direct extension of Mikolov et al. (2013b). Orthogonality constraints and more robust similarity measures (e.g., Procrustes/orthogonal mappings, CSLS or hubness mitigation) are not explored, though known by 2016 to materially improve cross-lingual transfer. Similarly, the data selection heuristic is intuitive but ad hoc; no comparison to alternative selection criteria (e.g., agreement, alignment confidence, length/consistency filters) or to prior projection selection methods is given.
- Dependency on alignment and phrase tables is under-specified. The approach assumes access to a phrase table/dictionary with P(x|y) and sentence-level alignments for comparable corpora. It is unclear how these alignments are obtained for “comparable” (not parallel) data, how much parallel supervision is implicitly used, and how sensitive results are to alignment quality and domain. The practicality for truly low-resource scenarios without reliable aligners/phrase tables is therefore uncertain.
- Reproducibility and resource transparency. Key ingredients (in-house datasets, alignment/phrase table construction, monolingual corpora sizes and domains, tokenization/segmentation for non-segmented scripts, and the 50+ type schema) are not publicly available or fully specified. Without releasing code/resources or using public alignment resources, it may be hard for others to replicate.
- Limited analysis and ablations. Several design choices are asserted but not tested: dictionary size (only a heuristic 5–6k; no sensitivity curve), effect of weights in Eq. 7 vs. unweighted mapping, embedding dimensionality choices (300/800), smoothing layer impact beyond summary metrics, and the specific thresholds (q, n) for data selection (chosen per language; how robust?). No statistical significance testing is reported on CoNLL test. Error analysis (e.g., entity boundary vs. type errors, effect on rare vs. frequent entities, language-specific failure modes) is minimal.
- Co-decoding design is heuristic and lacks principled calibration. The exclude-O rule and rank-based prioritization work well empirically, but the paper does not attempt calibration or joint decoding (e.g., stacking, constrained CRF/Viterbi over combined scores). It is unclear whether the improvements stem from complementary coverage or from artifacts of the O bias; more analysis (e.g., oracle combinations, per-entity-type gains) would strengthen the claim.

Questions to authors
- Alignment and dictionary creation: How are sentence alignments for comparable corpora produced? What supervision and data are required to train the alignment model and phrase tables in low-resource languages? Can you quantify how performance degrades with fewer parallel seeds or noisier alignments?
- Mapping details: Did you try orthogonal (Procrustes) mappings and/or unit-normalized embeddings? How does weighted vs. unweighted least squares affect transfer? Any evaluation of mapping quality (e.g., word translation precision@k) and its correlation with NER accuracy?
- Data selection thresholds: How were q and n tuned (dev sets in each target language? cross-lingually?). How sensitive are results to these thresholds? Can you provide learning curves vs. retained data size?
- Co-decoding: What proportion of final entities are contributed by AP vs. NN in the rank-based scheme, and where do the gains come from (boundary vs. type)? Did you try simple probability calibration (e.g., temperature scaling) to make confidence-based fusion more competitive?
- OOV handling: How is the “NER-trained unk vector” constructed? What fraction of tokens/entities rely on unk at test time, and how does that affect performance on morphologically rich languages?

Additional comments
- Comparing to more recent cross-lingual embedding methods (e.g., joint training on sentence-aligned data, bilingual skip-gram variants) would clarify whether the simplicity of your mapping is a strength or a limitation.
- A brief qualitative case study (corrected vs. discarded projection examples; typical cross-lingual mapping successes/failures) would improve interpretability.

Overall, this is a well-executed empirical paper with practical, scalable techniques and strong results. While the methodological novelty is moderate and some details need clarification, the combination of effective data selection, simple representation projection, and co-decoding delivers meaningful advances and a useful baseline for cross-lingual NER without target-language annotation.
