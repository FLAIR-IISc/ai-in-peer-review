Summary and contributions
- The paper tackles zero-annotation cross-lingual NER via two projection-based approaches and their combination. 
- Contribution 1 (primary): A heuristic data selection method for annotation projection on comparable corpora that scores projected sentences by entity-level tag frequency reliability and entity count; this substantially improves projection quality when alignments are noisy.
- Contribution 2: Direct model transfer via representation projection: learn a linear mapping from target to English embedding space using a small weighted bilingual dictionary; run a single English NER (NN) on mapped target embeddings without retraining.
- Contribution 3: Two simple co-decoding schemes (exclude-O confidence-based and rank-based) to merge outputs, yielding gains over either system alone and improving over prior cross-lingual NER baselines (Täckström et al., 2012; Tsai et al., 2016) on CoNLL.

Strengths
- Strong empirical gains across languages and settings. The annotation data selection yields large improvements when alignment is poor (e.g., +14–20 F1 on Japanese/Korean), and co-decoding consistently improves over individual models. On CoNLL test, the combined systems outperform two published cross-lingual NER approaches; on in-house data the best combined systems approach supervised systems trained on sizable target-language data.
- Practical zero-shot setup with low target-language requirements. The approach uses comparable corpora with alignments (for projection) and a small bilingual dictionary derived from phrase tables (for mapping). The representation projection requires no target-language annotation and no retraining for new languages, which is operationally attractive.
- Sensible and transparent engineering. The annotation selection metric is intuitive and easy to implement; the representation mapping is a weighted least-squares linear transform; co-decoding heuristics are simple yet effective. The paper provides precision/recall breakdowns, highlighting complementary behaviors (AP high-precision/low-recall, NN more balanced), motivating the rank-based merger.
- Broad evaluation. Results are reported on multiple languages (different scripts and morphology) and two settings (fine-grained in-house, 50+ types; CoNLL, 4 types), demonstrating robustness of the approach.

Weaknesses
- Reproducibility and missing resource details. The paper does not clearly specify the sources of comparable corpora, alignment training data/models, or how the phrase tables for dictionaries are obtained (MT system, data scale, domain). Without releasing resources or providing precise recipes, reproducing the in-house and even parts of the CoNLL experiments will be difficult.
- Potential tuning on test sets for in-house selection. For Table 2, “best numbers” for thresholds (q, n) are reported, but there is no mention of a held-out development set for those languages; if thresholds are selected on the test set, the reported gains may be optimistic. In contrast, CoNLL uses dev for model selection, which is appropriate.
- Limited methodological novelty. Annotation projection with sentence/entity reliability filtering and cross-lingual embedding-based model transfer have close precedents. The main innovations (frequency-based selection heuristic, simple linear weighted mapping, and heuristic co-decoding) are incremental rather than conceptually new for ACL.
- Insufficient ablations and analysis. Key choices are not explored: (i) mapping constraints (e.g., orthogonal vs unconstrained), (ii) effect of dictionary size/weighting (only a rule-of-thumb 5–6k pairs reported; claim that larger harms performance is not quantified), (iii) embedding dimensionality/epochs, (iv) architectures (why NN2 helps in-house but not CoNLL), (v) sensitivity to alignment accuracy and the selection thresholds beyond one operating point. An error analysis would clarify failure modes (e.g., boundary conflicts, tag confusions, OOV).
- Risks and biases of the selection heuristic. The sentence quality score relies on corpus-level tag frequencies for entity strings, which may (a) bias toward frequent entities and head nouns, (b) entrench systematic errors from the English NER or aligner (the assumed independence between them is questionable), and (c) devalue rare/low-frequency entities—potentially harming recall or long-tail categories. No mitigation or analysis is provided.

Questions to authors
- How were (q, n) tuned for the in-house languages? Was a held-out development set used, or were thresholds chosen on test? Please clarify the selection procedure and, if on test, provide dev/test splits or cross-validation results.
- What are the exact sources and sizes of the comparable corpora and alignment models for each language pair (in-house and CoNLL experiments)? Can you release the phrase tables/dictionaries or specify how to obtain them?
- Did you try orthogonally constrained mapping (e.g., Procrustes) and/or normalization (mean-centering, length normalization)? If so, how did it compare to the weighted unconstrained least squares?
- Why does NN2 help in-house but not CoNLL? Is this due to entity type granularity, domain, or training data size? Any insights from diagnostics?
- How sensitive is performance to dictionary size and weighting? Can you include a curve showing F1 vs dictionary size and an ablation removing weights wi in Eq. (7)?

Additional comments
- Consider adding character/subword features to the English NN to better handle OOVs and cross-script transfer (especially Japanese/Korean), possibly via CNN/LSTM over characters. This may help representation projection substantially without requiring target-language resources.
- For co-decoding, learned calibration or stacking (e.g., a meta-classifier on span/conflict features) might outperform fixed heuristics, while still being unsupervised in the target language (trained on English and transferred).
- Please report per-entity-type results (at least on CoNLL) and confusion matrices to illuminate which categories benefit from each component.
- Minor: clarify entity inventory (the table lists PEOPLE/TITLEWORK/PRODUCT alongside GPE, which differ from CoNLL types); define how conflicts are resolved when tags disagree but spans match; specify OOV handling for representation projection in more detail (unk vector training procedure).
