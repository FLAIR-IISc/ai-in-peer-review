Summary and contributions
The paper studies weakly supervised cross-lingual NER without human annotation in the target language. Two complementary projection-based approaches are presented together with simple but effective co-decoding strategies.

Main contributions, in order of significance:
1) A rank- and confidence-based co-decoding framework that combines annotation projection (high precision/low recall) with representation projection (more balanced), yielding consistent F1 gains across multiple languages and outperforming prior cross-lingual NER systems (Täckström et al., 2012; Tsai et al., 2016) on CoNLL test sets.
2) An annotation projection method with an effective data selection heuristic for comparable (not necessarily parallel) corpora. The sentence-level quality score based on entity-tag frequency tables and an entity-count threshold substantially improves performance when alignments are noisy (e.g., +13.9 F1 for Japanese; +20.0 F1 for Korean).
3) A direct model transfer approach via weighted least-squares linear mapping of target-language word embeddings into the English space, enabling use of a single English NER without retraining. A smoothing NN architecture (NN2) improves precision/recall balance relative to a plain embedding model (NN1).

Strengths
- Clear empirical gains and strong coverage across languages and settings. The combined systems produce the best weakly supervised F1 in four in-house languages (e.g., rank-based AP+NN2: 59.3 JP, 51.8 KO, 69.3 DE, 69.9 PT) and achieve top dev performance on CoNLL Spanish/Dutch with confidence-based AP+NN1. The gains from co-decoding over each constituent system are consistent (e.g., +2.9 to +6.2 F1 for in-house; +1.3 to +3.6 on CoNLL dev).
- Effective and practical data selection for projection. The frequency-based sentence quality and entity-count thresholds are simple, language-agnostic, and demonstrably impactful when alignment quality is low (Japanese 69.4, Korean 62.0 alignment F1): +13.9 and +20.0 F1, respectively (Table 2). This addresses a known pain point for projection approaches.
- Sensible design exploiting complementary error profiles. The observation that annotation projection has higher precision and lower recall while representation projection is more balanced is used to motivate the rank-based co-decoder, which reliably improves recall without sacrificing precision.
- Resource-efficient cross-lingual transfer. Representation projection avoids retraining per target language and requires only small seed dictionaries (~5–6K pairs) from phrase tables plus monolingual embeddings; adding a new language is straightforward.
- Comprehensive evaluation setup. Results are reported on both open CoNLL data (4 entity types) and in-house data with >50 types, with precision/recall/F1 and comparisons to two strong cross-lingual baselines from the literature.

Weaknesses
- Limited methodological novelty in representation projection. The mapping is a weighted linear regression similar to Mikolov et al. (2013b) with a straightforward weighting by phrase-table probabilities; no constraints (e.g., orthogonality/Procrustes) or alternatives are explored. An ablation against an unweighted mapping and an orthogonal mapping would clarify the incremental value of the weighting.
- Insufficient detail for reproducibility. Key pieces are under-specified: the comparable corpora sources and domains; alignment model training and data; hyperparameters for the NN models (e.g., context window, hidden size, learning rates), the NN2 “prototype vectors” mechanism, and the q/n threshold selection procedure. Without these, reimplementation is challenging.
- Comparability and fairness issues in training setups. For in-house experiments, the projection-labeled training sizes differ substantially across languages (e.g., 246K vs. 3.37M tokens), and different model classes are used across approaches (CRF source for AP; NN source for representation projection; MEMM for target AP), complicating attributions. A clearer accounting of data budget and model capacity would strengthen claims.
- Scope of baselines. On CoNLL, comparisons are limited to Täckström et al. (2012) and Tsai et al. (2016). Additional cross-lingual embedding baselines of the time (e.g., Faruqui and Dyer, 2014; bilingual Procrustes-style mappings) and simple dictionary-based direct transfer CRF/CRF+clusters would help isolate where the gains come from.
- Analysis depth. Beyond macro P/R/F1, there is limited error analysis: no breakdown by entity type, length, capitalization, or OOV; no study of type-inventory mismatch between English (source) and the in-house >50-type targets; and no robustness analysis to dictionary size or domain mismatch.

Questions to authors (for rebuttal)
- Comparable corpora and alignments: What are the sources and domains of the comparable corpora? How are sentence pairs constructed and aligned? Could statistics on overlap, sentence length ratios, and alignment error types be provided?
- NN2 details: How many prototype vectors are used? How are they initialized and updated? What is the exact computation pipeline from word embedding to prototype-weighted representation?
- Mapping ablations: How does performance change with (a) unweighted dictionary regression, (b) orthogonal/Procrustes mapping, and (c) different dictionary sizes (e.g., 1K/3K/10K)?
- Threshold tuning: How were q and n chosen per language? Was any held-out target-language supervision used, or were thresholds tuned unsupervised (e.g., using agreement/consistency signals)?
- OOV handling and coverage: What fraction of tokens/entities are OOV for the monolingual embeddings and for the dictionary? How does the unk vector affect entity boundary detection?

Suggestions for improvement
Essential (feasible within rebuttal/camera-ready):
- Provide detailed hyperparameters and training setup for all models (CRF/MEMM/NN1/NN2), alignment models, and embedding training. Clarify corpora provenance and sizes. Add a brief algorithmic description of NN2’s prototype smoothing and release configuration files or pseudo-code.
- Report ablations for the representation mapping (weighted vs. unweighted; with/without orthogonality) and for the dictionary size. Include at least a dev-set table for one language (e.g., Spanish or German).
- Expand analysis: per-entity-type P/R/F1 and OOV breakdown; illustrate typical success and failure cases for each component (AP vs. projection vs. co-decoding). Include significance testing or confidence intervals where applicable.

Optional (nice-to-have):
- Evaluate the co-decoding strategies with a simple calibration/temperature scaling to partially normalize confidence scores across systems.
- Explore a lightweight agreement-based selection for projection-labeled data (e.g., mutual consistency across multiple alignment passes) to complement the frequency-based q/n heuristic.
- Discuss limitations and portability to languages with disparate scripts/morphology and very low-resource settings, and outline pathways for releasing resources (e.g., frequency tables or selected sentence IDs) to aid reproducibility.

Overall assessment
The work presents a practical and well-executed combination of two projection paradigms with clear empirical benefits. While parts of the methodology are incremental, the data selection heuristic and the co-decoding schemes are effective, and the breadth of evaluation is a strong point. With added clarity, ablations, and analysis, the contribution would provide a solid and reproducible baseline for weakly supervised cross-lingual NER.
