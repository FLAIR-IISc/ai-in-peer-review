Summary and claimed contributions
The paper proposes two weakly supervised approaches for cross-lingual NER without target-language human annotations: (1) annotation projection on comparable corpora with a heuristic data selection scheme based on entity-tag frequency consistency; (2) direct model transfer via linear projection of target word embeddings into English space. It further introduces two co-decoding strategies (exclude-O confidence and rank-based) to combine the outputs. Experiments on in-house datasets (50+ types) and CoNLL (4 types) across multiple languages show that co-decoding improves over either approach alone and outperforms prior cross-lingual NER methods (Täckström et al., 2012; Tsai et al., 2016).

Contributions (as I see them)
- A simple, effective co-decoding framework that consistently yields gains over individual projection-based systems and prior cross-lingual baselines.
- A practical data selection criterion for annotation projection that substantially improves performance under noisy alignments (e.g., +13.9 and +20.0 F1 for Japanese/Korean).
- A weighted least-squares cross-lingual embedding mapping enabling zero-shot NER transfer with only monolingual embeddings and small seed dictionaries.

Strengths
- Strong empirical evidence across languages and settings, including both fine-grained in-house and CoNLL data; improvements are consistent and often sizable (e.g., rank-based AP+NN2 achieving top F1 in 3/4 in-house languages).
- Clear analysis of precision/recall profiles of the two systems and principled co-decoding designs tailored to their complementary errors.
- Practicality: requires no target-language annotations; uses accessible resources (comparable corpora, phrase-table-derived dictionaries).
- Useful ablations: shows impact of data selection thresholds and alignment quality on AP, and comparison of two NN architectures (NN1 vs NN2).

Weaknesses
- Dependence on alignment models and phrase tables limits true low-resource applicability; the approach assumes availability of comparable corpora and reasonable alignments.
- The data selection heuristic relies on surface-form frequency of entities, potentially biasing toward frequent entities and headwords; limited analysis of rare/ambiguous entities and morphology-rich languages.
- Limited reproducibility details: exact corpora, dictionary construction, and full hyperparameters are not fully specified; significance testing is not reported.
- The embedding mapping is linear and unconstrained; no comparison to stronger mapping variants (e.g., orthogonal Procrustes, normalization) or modern multilingual contextual baselines, which could change conclusions.

Questions to authors
- How sensitive are results to dictionary size/quality and to the orthogonality constraint in the mapping? Any ablation beyond the 5–6k pairs heuristic?
- Can you report calibration analyses and significance tests for co-decoding gains?
- How does the selection heuristic affect recall for rare entities, and can morphology/character features mitigate biases?

Constructive suggestions
- Evaluate stronger mapping variants and character/subword features; add calibration or learning-to-rank for co-decoding. Provide detailed reproducibility artifacts (code, data selection scripts), and include comparisons to recent multilingual pretrained baselines.
