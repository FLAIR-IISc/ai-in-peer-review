**Summary of the Paper**

This paper presents two approaches for weakly supervised cross-lingual named entity recognition (NER) with no human annotation in the target language. The first approach is based on annotation projection, where a heuristic data selection scheme is developed to select good-quality projection-labeled NER data from comparable corpora. The second approach is based on representation projection, where word representations in vector space (word embeddings) from a target language are projected to a source language, allowing the source-language NER system to be applied to the target language without re-training. The authors also propose two co-decoding schemes that combine the outputs of the two projection-based approaches.

**Main Contributions**

1. **Heuristic data selection scheme for annotation projection**: The authors develop a data selection scheme that selects good-quality projection-labeled NER data from noisy comparable corpora, improving the accuracy of the annotation projection approach for cross-lingual NER.
2. **Representation projection approach for direct NER model transfer**: The authors propose a new approach for direct NER model transfer based on representation projection, which projects word representations in vector space from a target language to a source language.
3. **Co-decoding schemes for combining projection-based approaches**: The authors design two co-decoding schemes that combine the outputs of the two projection-based approaches, achieving higher accuracy than both individual systems.

**Strengths**

1. **Effective use of comparable corpora**: The authors develop a heuristic data selection scheme that effectively selects good-quality projection-labeled NER data from comparable corpora, which can be noisy and limited.
2. **Improved accuracy for cross-lingual NER**: The authors' approaches achieve higher accuracy for cross-lingual NER, outperforming two state-of-the-art cross-lingual NER approaches on the CoNLL test data.
3. **Flexibility and scalability**: The representation projection approach allows for easy addition of new languages without requiring separate embeddings for each language pair.

**Weaknesses**

1. **Limited evaluation**: The authors only evaluate their approaches on a limited number of languages (Japanese, Korean, German, Portuguese, Spanish, and Dutch) and datasets (in-house and CoNLL).
2. **Dependence on alignment accuracy**: The annotation projection approach relies on accurate alignment between the source and target languages, which can be a limitation for languages with limited resources.
3. **Lack of theoretical analysis**: The authors do not provide a theoretical analysis of their approaches, which would help to understand the underlying mechanisms and limitations.

**Questions to Authors**

1. How do the authors plan to extend their approaches to other languages and datasets?
2. Can the authors provide more details on the training dictionary used for cross-lingual embedding mappings?
3. How do the authors plan to address the limitation of dependence on alignment accuracy for the annotation projection approach?

**Additional Comments**

The authors' approaches show promise for weakly supervised cross-lingual NER. However, further evaluation and analysis are needed to fully understand the strengths and limitations of these approaches.
