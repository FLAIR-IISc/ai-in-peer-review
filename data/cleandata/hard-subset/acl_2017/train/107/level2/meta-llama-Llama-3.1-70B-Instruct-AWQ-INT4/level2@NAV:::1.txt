**Summary of the Paper**

This paper presents two weakly supervised approaches for cross-lingual named entity recognition (NER) with no human annotation in the target language. The first approach is based on annotation projection, where a heuristic data selection scheme is developed to select good-quality projection-labeled NER data from noisy comparable corpora. The second approach is based on representation projection, where word embeddings in the target language are projected into the English vector space to create a universal representation of words in different languages. Two co-decoding schemes are also proposed to combine the outputs of the two projection-based approaches. The experimental results show that the combined system achieves higher accuracy than both individual systems and outperforms two state-of-the-art cross-lingual NER approaches.

**Contributions of the Work**

1. Effective annotation projection approach with a heuristic data selection scheme to select good-quality projection-labeled NER data from noisy comparable corpora.
2. Representation projection approach for direct NER model transfer, where word embeddings in the target language are projected into the English vector space.
3. Two co-decoding schemes to combine the outputs of the two projection-based approaches, achieving higher accuracy than both individual systems.

**Strengths**

1. The paper proposes two novel approaches for weakly supervised cross-lingual NER, which can be used to build NER systems for multiple languages with no human annotation in the target languages.
2. The experimental results show that the combined system achieves higher accuracy than both individual systems and outperforms two state-of-the-art cross-lingual NER approaches.
3. The paper provides a detailed analysis of the strengths and weaknesses of the two approaches and the co-decoding schemes, which can be useful for future research.

**Weaknesses**

1. The paper assumes that the source language (English) has a well-trained NER system, which may not be the case for all languages.
2. The paper uses a limited number of target languages (Japanese, Korean, German, Portuguese, Spanish, and Dutch) for evaluation, which may not be representative of all languages.
3. The paper does not provide a detailed analysis of the computational complexity of the two approaches and the co-decoding schemes, which can be an important consideration for large-scale NLP applications.

**Questions to Authors**

1. How can the proposed approaches be extended to handle languages with limited resources or no available NER systems?
2. Can the co-decoding schemes be improved by incorporating additional information, such as language-specific features or domain knowledge?
3. How can the computational complexity of the proposed approaches be reduced to make them more scalable for large-scale NLP applications?
