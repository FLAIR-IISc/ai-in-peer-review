## Review of "Weakly Supervised Cross-Lingual Named Entity Recognition via Effective Annotation and Representation Projection"

This paper addresses the challenging problem of cross-lingual NER without relying on expensive human annotation in the target language. The authors propose a combined approach leveraging both annotation projection with a novel data selection heuristic and representation projection using word embeddings. They further introduce two co-decoding schemes to integrate the outputs of these approaches.

The primary contribution of this work lies in the effective combination of annotation projection and representation projection. While both techniques have been explored individually, the co-decoding schemes, particularly the rank-based approach, demonstrate a significant performance boost, consistently outperforming individual methods across multiple languages and datasets. The heuristic data selection scheme for annotation projection is a valuable addition, addressing the noise inherent in projected data and improving overall accuracy.

The strengths of this paper are its comprehensive experimental evaluation and clear presentation. The authors evaluate their approach on both in-house and publicly available datasets (CoNLL), covering a diverse set of languages. The ablation studies, comparing different co-decoding schemes and neural network architectures, provide valuable insights into the effectiveness of each component. The detailed description of the experimental setup and parameter tuning enhances reproducibility. The comparison with state-of-the-art cross-lingual NER approaches demonstrates the competitive performance of the proposed method.

However, the paper has some weaknesses. The motivation for choosing specific neural network architectures (NN1 and NN2) could be strengthened with a more detailed justification. While the authors acknowledge the limitations of relying on alignment accuracy for annotation projection, a more in-depth analysis of the impact of alignment quality on performance would be beneficial. Additionally, the paper could benefit from a discussion of potential error analysis – understanding *where* the systems fail could point to future improvements. The reliance on a phrase table for creating the cross-lingual embedding mappings is a potential bottleneck, and exploring alternative methods for obtaining cross-lingual word pairs could be considered.

**Questions to Authors:**

1.  Could you provide a more detailed explanation of the design choices behind the NN1 and NN2 architectures? What specific advantages does the smoothing layer in NN2 offer in the context of cross-lingual transfer?
2.  How does the alignment accuracy between English and the target languages correlate with the performance gains achieved by the data selection scheme?
3.  Can you provide examples of common error types made by the system, and discuss potential strategies to address these errors?



Overall, this is a solid contribution to the field of cross-lingual NER. The proposed approach is well-motivated, thoroughly evaluated, and demonstrates promising results. Despite some minor weaknesses, the paper presents a valuable and practical solution for building NER systems in low-resource languages. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions.
