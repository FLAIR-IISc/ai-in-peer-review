This paper explores weakly supervised cross-lingual NER, combining annotation projection with a data selection heuristic and representation projection via embedding mapping, culminating in co-decoding schemes. The primary contribution, as I see it, is a reasonably thorough empirical investigation of combining established techniques – projection-based transfer learning – with relatively simple, yet effective, improvements (data selection, co-decoding). A secondary contribution lies in the comprehensive experimental setup, evaluating across multiple languages and datasets, including both in-house and standard CoNLL data.

**Strengths:**

1. **Extensive Empirical Evaluation:** The paper presents a substantial experimental evaluation across four in-house languages and three CoNLL languages. This breadth of evaluation is commendable and strengthens the claim of generalizability. The comparisons to baseline systems (Täckström et al., Tsai et al.) and supervised baselines are important for contextualizing the results.
2. **Sound Methodology – Building on Established Techniques:** The core approaches – annotation projection and representation projection – are well-established in cross-lingual NLP. Rather than proposing radically new methods, the authors focus on refining these existing techniques. This pragmatic approach is valuable; the paper demonstrates how thoughtful improvements to established methods can yield significant gains. The data selection heuristic for annotation projection, while not groundbreaking, appears to be effective.
3. **Co-Decoding is a Logical Improvement:** The co-decoding schemes, especially the rank-based approach, logically leverage the strengths of the two projection methods. The observation that annotation projection has high precision but low recall, while representation projection shows the opposite, justifies the effort to combine them.
4. **Clear Presentation:** The paper is generally well-written and clearly explains the different components of the proposed approach. The organization is logical, making it easy to follow the progression of ideas.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Lack of Novelty:** While the empirical work is strong, the individual components lack substantial novelty. Annotation projection and representation projection have been explored extensively. The data selection heuristic is also a fairly standard technique. The paper feels more like an engineering effort – a careful combination and evaluation of existing methods – than a theoretical breakthrough.
2. **Limited Analysis of Error Cases:** The paper focuses primarily on aggregate F1 scores. A more in-depth error analysis would be beneficial. Understanding *where* the systems fail (e.g., specific entity types, ambiguous cases) could provide valuable insights for future improvements. For example, what types of alignment errors most severely impact annotation projection?
3. **Reliance on Existing Resources:** The performance relies heavily on the quality of the English NER system, word embeddings, and parallel corpora. The paper doesn't sufficiently address the sensitivity of the results to these resources. A poor-quality English NER system would likely undermine the entire approach. The phrase tables used for embedding alignment are crucial, and their quality is not discussed in detail.
4. **Overstated Claims:** The claim of "outperforming state-of-the-art" should be tempered. While the results on CoNLL are encouraging, the improvements are not dramatic, and the comparison is limited to two specific approaches. A more comprehensive comparison with recent cross-lingual NER methods would be needed to support this claim convincingly. The paper also states improvements “close to supervised learning”, while there is still a sizable gap.



**Questions to Authors:**

1. How sensitive are the results to the quality of the English NER system used for annotation projection? Did you experiment with different English NER models, and how did the performance change?
2. Can you provide a more detailed error analysis, identifying the types of errors made by each system and where the co-decoding scheme provides the greatest improvement?
3. How was the size of the dictionary for representation projection determined? What impact does the dictionary size have on performance?
4. The paper mentions improvements using the rank-based co-decoding scheme. What is the rationale behind prioritizing the annotation projection system over the representation projection system in this scheme?
5. What specific alignment models were used to generate the alignment information for the parallel corpora?



**Additional Comments:**

*   The acronym "NN" is used inconsistently to refer to both neural networks in general and specific network architectures (NN1, NN2).
*   It would be helpful to include a table summarizing the sizes of the training data used for each language and approach.
*   Consider adding a discussion of the computational cost of the proposed methods.
