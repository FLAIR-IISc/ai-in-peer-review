## Peer Review: Weakly Supervised Cross-Lingual Named Entity Recognition via Effective Annotation and Representation Projection

**Summary:**

This paper presents a weakly supervised approach to cross-lingual Named Entity Recognition (NER) leveraging both annotation projection and representation projection. The authors address the challenge of adapting NER systems to new languages without requiring costly human annotation. They propose a heuristic data selection scheme for improving annotation projection quality and a method for projecting word embeddings to enable direct model transfer. Finally, they explore co-decoding schemes to combine the outputs of both approaches.  The work is evaluated on both in-house and CoNLL datasets across multiple languages.

**Main Contributions (as I see them):**

1. **Practical Improvement to Annotation Projection:** The data selection scheme for improving annotation projection quality is a solid contribution. The heuristic of considering both annotation quality *and* entity count provides a useful filter for noisy projection data, demonstrably improving performance, particularly in low-resource scenarios and with imperfect alignment.
2. **Comprehensive Evaluation:** The paper provides a relatively thorough evaluation across multiple languages and datasets (in-house and standard CoNLL). This allows for a more robust assessment of the proposed methods and provides a useful comparison point with existing work.
3. **Combination of Approaches:** Exploring the combination of annotation projection and representation projection through co-decoding is a reasonable approach and adds to the practical value of the work. The demonstrated improvements through co-decoding suggest a synergy between the two methods.



**Strengths:**

1. **Relevance to a Key Problem:** Cross-lingual NER, especially in low-resource settings, is a vital research area.  Reducing the reliance on expensive, human-annotated data is highly desirable.  This work directly tackles this challenge.
2. **Clear Methodology:**  The paper presents a clear and well-defined methodology. Each component (data selection, representation projection, co-decoding) is explained with sufficient detail, allowing for reproducibility. The NER models used (CRFs, MEMMs, Neural Networks) are also clearly described.
3. **Empirical Validation:** The extensive experimental results provide strong evidence of the effectiveness of the proposed methods. The performance gains achieved, particularly with the data selection scheme and co-decoding, are significant, and the comparison to existing approaches (Täckström et al., Tsai et al.) is favorable.
4. **Practical Focus:**  The focus on improving *practical* performance in cross-lingual NER, particularly by addressing the real-world issues of noisy data and alignment errors, is a strong point.  The proposed data selection scheme is a good example of this.
5. **Well-Organized Presentation:** The paper is generally well-written and organized, with a logical flow of ideas. The sections are clearly defined and contribute to the overall understanding of the work.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Novelty of Individual Components:** While the *combination* of techniques is worthwhile, the individual components – annotation projection, representation projection, and co-decoding – are not fundamentally novel. Each has been explored in prior work. The paper's contribution lies more in the effective integration and application of these existing techniques to the specific problem of cross-lingual NER, with the data selection heuristic being the most novel component.  This should be acknowledged more explicitly.
2. **Dependence on Alignment Quality:**  The annotation projection approach remains heavily dependent on the quality of the parallel corpora alignment. While the data selection scheme mitigates this issue, the paper doesn't adequately address scenarios with *very* poor alignment. A more in-depth analysis of the impact of alignment quality, beyond the current observation that improvements are more pronounced with low alignment accuracy, would be beneficial.
3. **Limited Analysis of Embedding Mapping:** The representation projection approach relies on a pre-trained mapping between languages. The paper doesn’t delve into the limitations of this approach, specifically cases where semantic ambiguities or structural differences between languages lead to poor projections. An analysis of the types of words that are poorly projected, and the impact on NER performance, would be valuable.
4. **Parameter Tuning Sensitivity:**  The performance of the data selection scheme is sensitive to the choice of parameters *q* and *n*. The paper mentions tuning these parameters, but doesn't provide a systematic analysis of their impact or guidance on how to choose optimal values for different languages and datasets.
5. **Supervised Baseline Detail:** While the paper provides supervised baseline results, more detail on the training data used for these baselines (e.g., source, size, annotation guidelines) would be helpful for a more thorough comparison.



**Questions to Authors:**

1.  Could you provide a more detailed analysis of the types of errors made by the representation projection approach, specifically relating to words that are poorly projected across languages?
2.  How sensitive are the results of the data selection scheme to the choice of the *q* and *n* parameters? Could you provide guidelines or recommendations for choosing appropriate values for different languages and datasets?
3. The paper mentions using alignment models with varying F1 scores. How much does alignment accuracy correlate with the improvement achieved by the data selection scheme? Can you quantify this relationship?
4. Given the acknowledgement that individual components aren’t entirely novel, what specific design choices or combinations were crucial for achieving the observed performance gains?



**Additional Comments:**

*   The use of tables is excellent for presenting results.
*   Minor grammatical edits would improve readability. For instance, "de-detect" (page 8) should be "detect".
*   Consider discussing the computational cost of the different approaches.



**Overall Assessment:**

This is a solid and well-executed piece of work. While the individual components aren't groundbreaking, the combination of techniques, the practical improvements to annotation projection, and the thorough evaluation make this a valuable contribution to the field of cross-lingual NER. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions to address the weaknesses outlined above.
