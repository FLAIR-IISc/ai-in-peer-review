## Review of "Weakly Supervised Cross-Lingual Named Entity Recognition via Effective Annotation and Representation Projection"

**Overall Assessment:** This paper presents a solid and well-executed investigation into weakly supervised cross-lingual NER. It combines two established approaches – annotation projection and representation projection – and offers practical improvements to both, culminating in a co-decoding scheme that yields competitive results. While not groundbreaking in terms of novel concepts, the paper demonstrates a strong engineering effort and provides valuable insights into practical considerations for cross-lingual NER. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions.

**Summary & Contributions:**

1. **Effective Data Selection for Annotation Projection:** The paper’s primary contribution is a heuristic data selection scheme for improving the quality of projection-labeled data, which demonstrably boosts performance when alignment accuracy is suboptimal. This is a practical and impactful addition to the annotation projection paradigm.
2. **Representation Projection via Embedding Mapping:** The paper successfully applies representation projection, leveraging pre-trained word embeddings and a linear mapping to transfer knowledge from English to other languages. While the concept isn’t new, the implementation and integration with the annotation projection approach are well done.
3. **Co-decoding for Combined Systems:**  The introduction of co-decoding schemes, particularly the rank-based approach, offers a simple yet effective way to combine the strengths of the two projection-based systems, leading to further performance gains.



**Strengths:**

1. **Practical Focus & Thorough Evaluation:** The paper is grounded in practical challenges of cross-lingual NER, specifically addressing the issue of noisy projection-labeled data.  The extensive experimentation across four languages (in-house data) and three languages (CoNLL) demonstrates a strong commitment to validating the proposed methods. The inclusion of both in-house and standard datasets enhances the credibility of the results.
2. **Clear Methodology & Implementation Details:** The paper clearly describes the various components of the system (CRFs, MEMMs, neural networks, embedding mappings) and provides sufficient detail regarding implementation choices, making it relatively easy to reproduce the results.  The discussion of the feature engineering and model selection process is also helpful.
3. **Demonstrated Improvements:** The experimental results consistently show improvements over baseline systems and, in many cases, outperform existing state-of-the-art approaches (Täckström et al., 2012; Tsai et al., 2016) on the CoNLL dataset. The gains, especially for languages with weaker alignment data, are noteworthy.
4. **Well-Structured & Readable:** The paper is well-organized, with a clear flow of ideas. The writing is generally clear and concise, making the technical details accessible.



**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Novelty:**  While the improvements are valuable, the core ideas (annotation projection, representation projection, combining models) are not novel. The paper primarily focuses on *optimizing* existing techniques rather than introducing fundamentally new concepts.  The authors acknowledge this but downplay it somewhat in the introduction.
2. **Dependency on External Resources:** The performance of the representation projection heavily relies on the quality of the pre-trained word embeddings and the available translation dictionaries.  A more detailed analysis of the impact of these resources on the final performance would be beneficial.  The paper doesn't explicitly discuss the potential for bias introduced by these resources.
3. **Supervised Baseline Comparison:**  While the paper compares against other weakly supervised methods, the performance gap between the best combined system and fully supervised systems, particularly with limited supervised data, is significant. A more detailed discussion of why these techniques still fall short of fully supervised performance, and potential directions to close that gap, would be valuable.
4. **Co-Decoding Scheme Justification:** The rationale for prioritizing the annotation projection system in the rank-based co-decoding scheme feels somewhat arbitrary. While the empirical results support this choice, a more theoretical justification or a more thorough ablation study investigating different weighting schemes would strengthen this aspect of the work.




**Questions to Authors:**

1.  Could you provide a more detailed discussion on the potential biases introduced by the pre-trained word embeddings and translation dictionaries used in the representation projection approach?  How might these biases affect the performance of the system, particularly for low-resource languages?
2.  You mention the differing characteristics of the annotation projection and representation projection systems (precision vs. recall).  Is there a clear relationship between alignment accuracy and the relative performance of these two approaches?  Could you elaborate on the conditions under which one approach is likely to be more effective than the other?
3.   Regarding the rank-based co-decoding scheme, could you elaborate on any attempts to dynamically adjust the prioritization of the annotation projection system based on the specific input sentence or language? Would this be computationally feasible?




**Additional Comments:**

*   The paper would benefit from a brief discussion of potential error analysis to identify common failure modes and inform future research directions.
*   Consider adding a table summarizing the characteristics of the datasets used (e.g., size, number of entity types, domain).
