- Overall: solid, well-executed study of weakly supervised cross-lingual NER; recommend acceptance with minor revisions.

- Contribution: combines annotation projection with a data-selection heuristic, representation projection via cross-lingual embeddings, and a co-decoding scheme.

- Main novelty: empirical demonstration that the data-selection heuristic substantially improves annotation projection, especially with poor alignments.

- Strength: strong empirical results across multiple languages and datasets, with convincing ablation studies.

- Strength: practical, reproducible implementation addressing noisy data and limited resources.

- Strength: comprehensive evaluation on diverse languages (in-house + CoNLL), supporting generalizability.

- Strength: clear, well-organized presentation and technical exposition.

- Strength: tackles a relevant problem—cross-lingual NER for low-resource languages—with an effective pragmatic solution.

- Weakness: limited novelty—the core techniques themselves are not new; novelty lies mainly in their combination and the heuristic.

- Weakness: results remain sensitive to alignment quality; need deeper analysis of alignment–performance correlation.

- Weakness: insufficient detail on feature engineering for CRFs/MEMMs and omission of gazetteers/character-level features from neural models.

- Weakness: co-decoding rank-based rationale is under-explained; unclear why prioritizing annotation projection is best.

- Question: provide detailed analysis of how alignment accuracy affects the effectiveness and returns of the data-selection strategy.

- Question: why exclude gazetteers and character-level representations from neural models, and would their inclusion affect representation projection?

- Question: clarify the intuition and evidence for prioritizing annotation projection in the rank-based co-decoding scheme.

- Question: report computational cost of CRF/MEMM training and inference, particularly with many entity types.

- Comment: calling older works (Täckström et al., Tsai et al.) "state-of-the-art" may be an overstatement.

- Comment: add a brief discussion of limitations, including reliance on a high-resource source language and potential biases from representation projection.
