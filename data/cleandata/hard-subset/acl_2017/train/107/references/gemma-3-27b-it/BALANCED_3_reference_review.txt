This paper investigates weakly supervised cross-lingual NER, addressing the challenge of limited labeled data in target languages. The authors propose a data selection scheme for annotation projection, a representation projection approach using word embeddings, and co-decoding schemes to combine the outputs of both approaches. The experimental evaluation across multiple languages demonstrates promising results, outperforming existing methods on the CoNLL dataset.

**Contributions:**

1. **Effective Data Selection for Annotation Projection:** The heuristic data selection scheme demonstrably improves the accuracy of annotation projection, particularly when alignment accuracy is low and the projected data is noisy. This is a significant contribution, as it directly addresses a key limitation of existing annotation projection techniques.
2. **Representation Projection via Embedding Alignment:** The approach of projecting word embeddings from target to source languages for direct NER model transfer is a reasonable and well-executed idea. The successful transfer of an English NER system without retraining is a noteworthy result.
3. **Co-Decoding Schemes for Combining Projections:**  The proposed co-decoding schemes, especially the rank-based approach, effectively combine the strengths of both projection techniques, leading to improved performance compared to individual methods. This demonstrates a practical way to leverage the complementary nature of the two approaches.

**Strengths:**

1. **Clear Problem Definition & Motivation:** The paper clearly articulates the problem of cross-lingual NER with limited resources and provides a strong motivation for the proposed approaches.
2. **Comprehensive Experimental Evaluation:** The evaluation is extensive, encompassing both in-house and publicly available datasets (CoNLL), and covers multiple target languages. This provides strong evidence for the effectiveness of the proposed methods. The inclusion of comparisons to strong baselines (Täckström et al., 2012; Tsai et al., 2016) is crucial.
3. **Well-Defined Heuristic for Data Selection:** The data selection scheme is based on a sound principle (frequency-based reliability) and is shown to be effective in experiments. The careful consideration of both quality and quantity of selected data is commendable.
4. **Solid Baseline Models:** The use of established NER models like CRFs, MEMMs, and neural networks as the foundation for the projection techniques strengthens the validity of the results.
5. **Detailed Implementation Details:** The paper provides sufficient detail regarding the implementation of the models, the training procedures, and the feature sets used. This allows for reproducibility and further research.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Novelty in Core Techniques:** While the *combination* of techniques and the data selection heuristic are valuable, the underlying techniques (annotation projection, representation projection) are not fundamentally new. The paper doesn't adequately highlight how these are substantially improved beyond previous work beyond the data selection.
2. **Lack of Ablation Studies:**  The paper would benefit from ablation studies to better understand the contribution of each component of the co-decoding schemes. For instance, it's unclear why the rank-based scheme consistently outperforms the confidence-based scheme.  Dissecting the benefits of each element would enhance understanding.
3. **Dependence on Alignment Quality:** The annotation projection approach's success is heavily reliant on the quality of the parallel corpora and alignment information.  The paper acknowledges this, but doesn’t sufficiently discuss strategies to mitigate the impact of low-quality alignments *beyond* the data selection heuristic.
4. **English-Centric Approach:** The approach is heavily biased towards English as the source language. While justified by the prevalence of English resources, the paper could briefly discuss the potential for using other source languages or a multi-source approach.
5. **CoNLL Results Marginal:** While the paper claims to outperform previous methods on CoNLL, the improvements are relatively small. A more in-depth analysis of error cases could reveal limitations and areas for further improvement.



**Questions to Authors:**

1. Could you elaborate on the limitations of the alignment models used for annotation projection, and discuss potential strategies for handling noisy alignments beyond the proposed data selection scheme (e.g., more sophisticated weighting of projected labels)?
2. The rank-based co-decoding scheme consistently outperforms the confidence-based scheme. Can you provide a more detailed explanation of why this is the case, and potentially conduct an ablation study to isolate the impact of different components of the rank-based scheme?
3. How sensitive are the results to the choice of embedding size (300 for English, 800 for target languages)? Did you experiment with different embedding sizes, and if so, what were the results?
4.  Could you provide some example cases where the representation projection method performs well/poorly, demonstrating what types of entities or linguistic structures are better handled by this approach?

**Additional Comments:**

* The paper is well-written and generally easy to follow.
* Typographical errors are minimal.
* Consider adding a table summarizing the performance of different components (annotation projection, representation projection) alone and in combination on each dataset for a clearer overview.
* The reference list appears complete.



**Overall Assessment:**

This is a solid paper that presents a practical and effective approach to weakly supervised cross-lingual NER. While the underlying techniques are not entirely novel, the data selection heuristic and the co-decoding schemes represent valuable contributions. The comprehensive experimental evaluation provides strong evidence for the effectiveness of the proposed methods. Despite some minor weaknesses, I recommend **acceptance** of this paper. Addressing the questions in the author response phase could further strengthen the work.
