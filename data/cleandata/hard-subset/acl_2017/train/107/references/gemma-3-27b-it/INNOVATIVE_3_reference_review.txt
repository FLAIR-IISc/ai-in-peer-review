This paper tackles the challenging problem of weakly supervised cross-lingual NER. It proposes two main approaches: a refined annotation projection technique with data selection, and a novel representation projection method leveraging word embeddings. A key contribution is the exploration of *combining* these two approaches via co-decoding schemes.

**Strengths:**

1. **Novel Combination of Approaches:** The most compelling aspect is the attempt to fuse annotation projection (a more established technique) with representation projection (a relatively newer idea for NER). While both individual approaches are reasonable, the exploration of co-decoding to leverage their complementary strengths is a genuinely novel and interesting direction. This is a bold move, and even if the gains aren’t massive, the *idea* of synergistic combination is valuable.
2. **Practical Focus & Data Selection:** The heuristic data selection scheme for annotation projection directly addresses a major pain point – noisy projection data. This isn’t a groundbreaking algorithmic innovation, but it's a practical, well-motivated improvement that demonstrably boosts performance, especially in low-resource scenarios. The focus on improving projection quality, rather than simply increasing quantity, is appreciated.
3. **Empirically Grounded:** The paper presents reasonably comprehensive experiments across multiple languages (both in-house and CoNLL datasets). The inclusion of comparisons to existing cross-lingual NER systems (Täckström et al., Tsai et al.) and, crucially, *supervised* baselines, provides valuable context for evaluating the proposed methods. The ablation studies regarding the co-decoding schemes are also valuable.
4. **Clear Presentation:** The paper is generally well-written and easy to follow. The motivation is clearly articulated, the methods are described with sufficient detail, and the experimental setup is understandable.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Depth of Analysis in Representation Projection:** While the representation projection approach is interesting in concept, the paper lacks a deep exploration of its underlying mechanisms. The discussion of embedding mappings feels somewhat cursory. A more detailed analysis of which languages transfer well and *why*, and potentially exploring different mapping techniques, would strengthen this contribution.  It feels like a somewhat 'plug-and-play' component rather than a thoroughly investigated method.
2. **Co-Decoding Schemes Feel Ad-Hoc:** The co-decoding schemes (exclude-O confidence-based and rank-based) are presented as heuristics without significant theoretical justification. The rationale for prioritizing annotation projection in the rank-based scheme is stated, but could benefit from more formalization or analysis. The results show improvements, but feel somewhat fragile – are these gains consistent across languages, or are they sensitive to parameter tuning?
3. **Modest Gains:** While the results demonstrate improvements over baselines, the overall gains aren’t enormous.  The performance still lags significantly behind fully supervised models.  This isn’t necessarily a fatal flaw (given the weakly supervised setting), but it does raise the question of whether the complexity added by the combination and co-decoding is fully justified by the performance boost. The paper needs to better emphasize the trade-offs and practical benefits despite the modest performance gains.
4. **Feature Engineering Reliance:** The paper mentions that the English NER system used for projection is a CRF model trained with a substantial amount of data. This raises the question of whether the performance is heavily reliant on the quality of this pre-trained English NER system. The representation projection method uses word embeddings, but the underlying NER model still seems to depend on carefully engineered features.



**Questions to Authors:**

1. Can you provide a more detailed analysis of the representation projection approach? Specifically, what factors seem to influence the effectiveness of the embedding mapping for different language pairs?
2. Could you provide a more rigorous justification for the co-decoding schemes, perhaps through a theoretical analysis of the expected error reduction?  Or provide a more thorough exploration of parameter sensitivity of these schemes?
3. How sensitive are the results to the quality of the initial English NER system used for annotation projection? Could the proposed data selection scheme mitigate the effects of a less accurate English NER system?
4.  How computationally expensive are the representation projection and co-decoding stages compared to training a standard NER model in the target language (assuming some limited labeled data is available)?



**Additional Comments:**

The paper could benefit from a more nuanced discussion of the error analysis. What types of errors are still prevalent after applying the proposed methods? Are there specific entity types that are particularly challenging to transfer across languages? The related work section is a bit sparse; adding a few more recent papers on cross-lingual transfer learning would be helpful.



**Overall Assessment:**

Despite some weaknesses, this paper presents a valuable contribution to the field of cross-lingual NER. The combination of annotation and representation projection, coupled with the practical data selection scheme, demonstrates a thoughtful approach to a challenging problem. While the gains aren't dramatic, the novelty of the combination and the empirical validation make this a worthwhile submission. I recommend **acceptance**, leaning towards the higher end of the acceptance range, as the bold approach and clear presentation outweigh the minor methodological shortcomings.
