This paper tackles the challenging problem of cross-lingual NER with zero resource target languages. The core contribution lies in the combination of two relatively straightforward, yet effective, approaches – informed annotation projection and representation projection – alongside a co-decoding scheme. While neither individual technique is groundbreaking, the pragmatic combination and demonstrated performance gains, particularly given the minimal reliance on parallel data and human annotation, are noteworthy. A secondary, and valuable, contribution is the empirical analysis demonstrating the benefits of careful data selection for annotation projection, a commonly overlooked aspect.

**Strengths:**

1. **Pragmatic and Effective Combination:** The paper doesn’t attempt to reinvent NER, but intelligently combines existing techniques. The synergy between annotation projection (enhanced by data selection) and representation projection is compelling. The co-decoding scheme further refines the results, suggesting a strong understanding of the strengths and weaknesses of each component. This approach feels directly applicable to real-world scenarios where large, high-quality parallel corpora are unavailable.
2. **Data Selection Heuristic is Valuable:** The proposed data selection scheme for annotation projection is a significant practical contribution. Recognizing that alignment quality drastically impacts projection performance and addressing it with a frequency-based heuristic is a sensible and empirically validated improvement. This highlights a common pitfall in projection-based methods and offers a concrete solution.
3. **Strong Empirical Results:** The experiments, conducted on both in-house and CoNLL datasets, demonstrate consistent and significant improvements over existing weakly-supervised baselines (Täckström et al. and Tsai et al.). While the gap to fully supervised systems remains, the results showcase a compelling step forward, especially considering the zero-shot nature of the target languages.
4. **Clear Presentation and Thorough Evaluation:** The paper is well-structured and clearly explains the methodologies. The extensive evaluation across multiple languages and datasets builds confidence in the generalizability of the findings.  The ablation studies (comparing NN1 vs. NN2) are also appreciated.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Novelty in Core Components:**  Annotation projection and representation projection are established techniques. The primary innovation isn’t in *what* is done, but *how* it’s combined and refined. While effective, the underlying ideas lack significant conceptual novelty.
2. **Reliance on Existing Resources:** The method relies heavily on pre-trained word embeddings and a source language NER system. The performance is therefore inherently limited by the quality of these components. While this is common in cross-lingual transfer, a more thorough discussion of the impact of different embedding qualities and source model performance would be beneficial.
3. **Co-Decoding Scheme Justification:** The rationale behind the “rank-based” co-decoding scheme (prioritizing the annotation projection system) feels somewhat ad-hoc. A more principled justification, perhaps based on error analysis of each system, would strengthen this component. Further investigation into different weighting schemes for co-decoding could also be fruitful.
4. **Limited Error Analysis:**  While the overall F1 scores are reported, a more detailed error analysis would provide valuable insights. Understanding *where* the system struggles (e.g., specific entity types, languages, or alignment errors) would inform future improvements.



**Questions to Authors:**

1.  Could you provide a more detailed analysis of the impact of the source language NER system’s performance on the target language results? Specifically, how does the F1 score of the English NER system correlate with the performance gains observed in the target languages?
2.  The rank-based co-decoding scheme prioritizes the annotation projection system. Can you elaborate on the specific error patterns that motivated this design choice?  Have you explored alternative weighting schemes beyond simply prioritizing one system?
3.   What are the computational costs associated with training and applying the cross-lingual embedding mappings, particularly for resource-scarce languages?
4.  Could you discuss the limitations of this approach when dealing with languages with significantly different syntactic structures or morphological complexities compared to English?




**Additional Comments:**

*  The paper would benefit from a discussion of potential biases introduced by the reliance on English as the pivot language.
*   Minor: “nn” should be expanded on first usage.
*   It might be useful to include a comparison to recent zero-shot NER models that leverage pre-trained multilingual language models (e.g., XLM-RoBERTa).



**Overall:**

Despite the lack of groundbreaking novelty in individual components, the paper presents a compelling and pragmatic solution to cross-lingual NER with limited resources. The combination of informed annotation projection, representation projection, and a sensible co-decoding scheme yields significant performance improvements. The paper is well-written, thoroughly evaluated, and provides a valuable contribution to the field. I recommend **acceptance**. While the work isn't a paradigm shift, it represents a solid engineering effort with practical relevance, and I am willing to overlook the lack of radical innovation in favor of its demonstrable efficacy.
