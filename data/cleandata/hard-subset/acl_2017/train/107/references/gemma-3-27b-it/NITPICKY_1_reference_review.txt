This paper proposes weakly supervised cross-lingual NER methods combining annotation projection with data selection and representation projection via word embedding mapping, further refined by co-decoding schemes. The primary contribution lies in the heuristic data selection scheme for annotation projection, aiming to mitigate noise from comparable corpora. A secondary contribution is the exploration of representation projection for direct model transfer, leveraging word embeddings. Finally, the co-decoding schemes, while incremental, attempt to integrate the strengths of both approaches.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Lack of Novelty in Core Components:** While the combination of existing techniques is explored, the individual components – annotation projection, representation projection with word embeddings, and co-decoding – are not novel. The core innovation, the data selection scheme, is a relatively simple heuristic based on frequency counts. Its theoretical grounding is weak, and the improvements reported, while statistically significant, are not substantial enough to warrant strong acceptance given the maturity of the underlying techniques. The rationale for choosing specific thresholds (q and n) feels arbitrary and lacks deeper analysis.

2. **Insufficient Ablation Studies:** The paper presents results for multiple configurations (different NN architectures, co-decoding schemes) but lacks sufficient ablation studies to understand the *relative* contribution of each component. For example, how much does the data selection scheme *specifically* improve performance versus simply using a larger, unfiltered dataset? Is the performance gain from NN2 (with the smoothing layer) truly significant, or is it within the margin of error? A more granular analysis is needed to demonstrate the efficacy of each proposed component.

3. **Overstated Claims & Limited Comparative Analysis:** The claim of “outperforming state-of-the-art” (Täckström et al. 2012; Tsai et al. 2016) on CoNLL data is presented without sufficient context. The paper only briefly mentions these approaches and doesn’t provide a direct comparison table showing the precise scores achieved by those methods *using the same evaluation setup*. A proper comparison would require reproducing those results or carefully extracting them from the original papers. The improvement seems modest, and the phrasing is misleadingly strong. Further, the comparison is limited to CoNLL; the in-house dataset results show less dramatic gains.

4. **Presentation and Detail Issues:** The paper suffers from a number of minor issues that detract from its clarity and credibility. The figures (specifically Figure 1) are not referenced in the main text and are essential for understanding NN2 architecture. The notation is inconsistent (e.g., using both 'l' and 'l′' for labels). The description of the alignment models is lacking – details like the features used are absent. The phrase “conflict with entities” in the co-decoding section is vague and requires more precise definition. The explanation of the weight wi in section 4.2 isn’t clear: it's a probability, but how is it calculated and what’s its practical impact?

5. **Limited Error Analysis:** The paper lacks a detailed error analysis. What types of errors are made by each system? Are there specific entity types or linguistic phenomena that are particularly challenging? Understanding the error patterns could guide future improvements and provide valuable insights.



**Strengths:**

1. **Comprehensive Experimental Evaluation:** The authors evaluated their approach on a variety of languages and datasets (both in-house and public), which demonstrates a commendable effort to assess the generalizability of their methods. The results provide a reasonable basis for assessing performance.

2. **Practical Focus:** The paper addresses a relevant and challenging problem – building cross-lingual NER systems with limited resources. The focus on weakly supervised learning and leveraging existing resources (comparable corpora, word embeddings) is practical and valuable.

3. **Well-Defined Problem and Clear Structure:** The paper clearly outlines the problem, proposes solutions, and presents the experimental setup in a structured manner. The organization is logical and easy to follow, despite the aforementioned detail issues.




**Questions to Authors:**

1. Can you provide a direct comparison table showing the performance of Täckström et al. (2012) and Tsai et al. (2016) on the CoNLL test data, using the exact same evaluation setup as your experiments?
2. Please provide a detailed explanation of how the weight *wi* is calculated in section 4.2. What are the practical implications of this weighting scheme?
3. Could you conduct ablation studies to quantify the contribution of the data selection scheme separately from the size of the training data?
4. What is a more precise definition of “conflict with entities” in the context of the co-decoding scheme? Can you provide examples?
5. Can you provide an error analysis showing the most common types of errors made by each system, and how the co-decoding scheme addresses these errors?

**Additional Comments:**

*   Figure 1 should be properly referenced.
*   Notation should be consistent throughout the paper.
*   Provide more details about the alignment models used.
*   Consider adding a discussion of the limitations of the proposed approach.



**Overall Recommendation:**

Reject. While the paper presents a reasonably thorough experimental evaluation, the lack of significant novelty, insufficient ablation studies, overstated claims, and minor presentation issues make it unsuitable for acceptance in its current form. Addressing the weaknesses outlined above, particularly providing a more rigorous comparative analysis and stronger justification for the proposed heuristics, would be necessary for reconsideration.
