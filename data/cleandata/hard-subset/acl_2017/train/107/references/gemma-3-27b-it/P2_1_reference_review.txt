## Review of "Weakly Supervised Cross-Lingual Named Entity Recognition via Effective Annotation and Representation Projection"

**Summary:**

This paper tackles the challenge of cross-lingual NER with no target language annotation. It proposes two main approaches: (1) an improved annotation projection method incorporating a data selection heuristic to mitigate noise from comparable corpora, and (2) a representation projection method utilizing cross-lingual word embeddings to directly transfer an English NER model. Finally, it presents two co-decoding schemes to combine the outputs of both approaches. The paper demonstrates improvements over existing weakly supervised methods and achieves competitive results, approaching supervised performance in some cases.

**Main Contributions (as I see them):**

1. **Effective Data Selection for Annotation Projection:** The heuristic data selection scheme is a practical and valuable contribution.  Addressing the quality of projection labels, especially with noisy comparable corpora, is a significant issue, and this approach demonstrably improves performance.
2. **Combined System via Co-Decoding:**  The exploration of combining the two distinct approaches (annotation and representation projection) via co-decoding is a smart strategy. The reported improvements demonstrate the benefit of integrating different perspectives.
3. **Comprehensive Experimental Evaluation:** The paper evaluates the proposed methods on both in-house and publicly available datasets (CoNLL), across multiple languages, providing a strong empirical basis for the claims.



**Strengths:**

1. **Practical Problem & Clear Motivation:** The problem of cross-lingual NER without labeled data is well-motivated and practically relevant, especially for low-resource languages.  The paper clearly articulates this motivation.
2. **Solid Experimental Setup:** The use of multiple languages and datasets (both in-house and CoNLL) strengthens the validity and generalizability of the results. The comparison to strong baselines (Täckström et al., Tsai et al.) is also important.
3. **Demonstrated Improvements:** The experiments consistently show improvements over existing methods, particularly with the data selection heuristic and the co-decoding scheme. The gains, while not massive, are statistically significant and demonstrate the efficacy of the proposed techniques.  The approaching of supervised performance is encouraging.
4. **Detailed Explanations:**  The paper provides sufficient detail regarding the methodologies, including the CRFs, neural networks, data selection, and co-decoding schemes.  This allows for reproducibility and understanding.
5. **Clear Presentation:** The paper is generally well-written and logically organized.  The introduction provides a good overview of the problem and the proposed solutions.



**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Novelty of Individual Components:** While the combination and application are well-executed, the individual components (annotation projection, representation projection) are not groundbreaking. Both have been explored extensively in previous work. The novelty resides in the specific combination and data selection heuristic.  The paper could benefit from more clearly positioning itself *relative* to prior work on each individual component.
2. **Dependence on Alignment Quality:** The annotation projection method remains heavily reliant on the quality of the word alignment between source and target languages. While the data selection helps, poor alignment will still fundamentally limit performance. This dependency isn’t sufficiently discussed in terms of real-world applicability – what happens when *good* alignment data isn’t available?
3. **Complexity of the Co-Decoding Schemes:** The explanations of the co-decoding schemes (especially the rank-based one) could be clearer.  More intuition or examples would help understand the decision-making process and why it works.
4. **Limited Analysis of Failure Cases:** The paper lacks detailed analysis of *where* the systems still fail. Identifying common error patterns would provide valuable insights for future research.  For example, are certain entity types consistently misidentified? Are there specific linguistic phenomena that pose challenges?




**Questions to Authors:**

1. **Alignment Robustness:**  How sensitive are the results of the annotation projection approach to variations in alignment quality?  Have you explored methods to improve alignment quality *specifically* for this task, perhaps by integrating alignment quality scores into the data selection process?
2. **Data Selection Parameter Tuning:** What strategies were used to determine the optimal values for the `q` and `n` parameters in the data selection scheme?  Was a separate development set used for parameter tuning?
3. **Co-Decoding Intuition:** Could you provide a concrete example illustrating how the rank-based co-decoding scheme operates, and why it outperforms the confidence-based scheme?
4. **Error Analysis:**  Could you provide some insights into the types of errors that the combined system still makes?  Are there specific entity types or linguistic constructions that pose particular challenges?



**Additional Comments:**

*   The paper would benefit from a discussion of computational resources required for training and inference.
*   The choice of 300/800 dimensions for the word embeddings seems somewhat arbitrary.  Was any experimentation done with different dimensions?



**Overall Assessment:**

I believe this paper is a **strong accept**. While the individual components aren’t entirely novel, the combination of a pragmatic data selection heuristic for annotation projection, a representation projection approach, and a well-designed co-decoding scheme delivers a significant improvement in cross-lingual NER performance. The thorough experimental evaluation and clear presentation further strengthen the submission. Addressing the weaknesses outlined above, particularly regarding alignment robustness and a more detailed error analysis, would further enhance the paper.
