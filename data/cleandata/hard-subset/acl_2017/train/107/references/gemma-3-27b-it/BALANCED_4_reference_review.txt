This paper addresses the challenging problem of weakly supervised cross-lingual NER, proposing two main approaches: annotation projection with data selection, and representation projection. The authors also introduce co-decoding schemes to combine the outputs of these two approaches. The work is well-motivated, tackles a relevant problem, and presents a comprehensive set of experiments.

**Main Contributions:**

1. **Effective Data Selection for Annotation Projection:** The heuristic data selection scheme for improving the quality of projection-labeled data is a significant contribution. The authors identify and address the issue of noisy projection data due to alignment inaccuracies, which is a critical factor often overlooked.  The experimental results clearly demonstrate the benefit of this approach, particularly when alignment quality is low.
2. **Representation Projection via Embedding Mapping:**  The approach of projecting word representations into a shared space for direct model transfer is a reasonable and well-explained contribution.  While the idea of cross-lingual embeddings isn't novel, the application to NER, and the specific implementation details (training dictionaries, linear mapping), are valuable.
3. **Co-Decoding Schemes for Combining Projections:** The exploration of different co-decoding strategies (confidence-based and rank-based) to leverage the complementary strengths of the annotation projection and representation projection methods is a strong addition. The empirical demonstration that combining the outputs leads to improved performance is noteworthy.

**Strengths:**

1. **Thorough Experimental Evaluation:** The paper includes a comprehensive evaluation using both in-house and publicly available datasets (CoNLL). The experiments cover multiple target languages, providing strong evidence for the generalizability of the proposed methods.
2. **Clear and Detailed Methodology:**  The paper provides clear explanations of the different components of the proposed approaches, including the data selection scheme, embedding mapping process, and co-decoding strategies. The inclusion of implementation details (e.g., neural network architectures) enhances reproducibility.
3. **Strong Baseline Comparisons:**  The authors compare their methods against state-of-the-art cross-lingual NER approaches (Täckström et al., 2012; Tsai et al., 2016), demonstrating improvements over existing techniques.
4. **Practical Focus:**  The problem addressed—building NER systems for low-resource languages—has significant practical implications. The weakly supervised nature of the approach makes it highly applicable in real-world scenarios where labeled data is scarce.
5. **Well-Written and Organized:** The paper is generally well-written and organized, with a logical flow of ideas and clear presentation of results.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Novelty of Individual Components:** While the combination of techniques is valuable, the individual components (annotation projection, representation projection) are not entirely novel. The novelty lies in the specific implementations and the synergistic combination of these approaches.  The paper could benefit from more clearly articulating *how* their implementation differs from prior work and provides unique advantages.
2. **Reliance on Parallel Corpora (for Annotation Projection):**  The annotation projection approach still relies on comparable corpora with alignment information. While the data selection scheme mitigates the impact of alignment errors, the availability of such corpora is still a limitation for some language pairs.  The authors could briefly discuss alternatives to parallel corpora for annotation projection, such as machine translation.
3. **Dictionary Size Sensitivity:** The performance of the representation projection approach appears sensitive to the size of the training dictionary.  A more detailed analysis of the relationship between dictionary size and performance would be beneficial. The justification for the chosen dictionary size (5K-6K word pairs) could be strengthened.
4. **Limited Analysis of Error Cases:** While the results are presented clearly, a more in-depth analysis of the types of errors made by the different systems would be valuable. Understanding the specific challenges faced by each approach could inform future research directions.
5. **Inconsistent Reporting of System Details:** The descriptions of the NER models used (CRFs, MEMMs, Neural Networks) are quite detailed in Section 2, but the specifics of the training process (e.g., hyperparameters, regularization) are not consistently provided for all experiments.

**Questions to Authors:**

1.  Could you provide a more detailed comparison of your representation projection approach to other cross-lingual embedding techniques, specifically highlighting the advantages of your method?
2.  How does the performance of the data selection scheme vary with different levels of alignment accuracy between the source and target languages?
3.  What is the impact of different alignment tools on the performance of the annotation projection approach?
4.  Have you explored alternative co-decoding strategies beyond confidence-based and rank-based approaches? For example, have you considered using a trainable model to combine the outputs of the two systems?
5.  For the representation projection approach, could you analyze the quality of the projected embeddings for different languages and identify any systematic biases or limitations?



**Additional Comments:**

*   Consider adding a table summarizing the different datasets used, including their size, entity types, and source.
*   The phrase "conflict" in the description of the rank-based co-decoding scheme could be clarified.
*   In the conclusion, briefly mention potential directions for future work, such as exploring unsupervised embedding mapping techniques.
