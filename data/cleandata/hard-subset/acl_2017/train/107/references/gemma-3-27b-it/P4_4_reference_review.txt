This paper addresses the challenging problem of weakly supervised cross-lingual NER, proposing a combination of annotation projection with data selection and representation projection. The authors also introduce co-decoding schemes to combine the outputs of these two approaches.

**Summary of the Paper and Contributions:**

The paper investigates methods to transfer NER capabilities from English to other languages without relying on human-annotated data in the target languages.  It explores two main avenues: (1) projecting annotations from English to other languages using comparable corpora and a data selection heuristic to improve quality, and (2) projecting word representations (embeddings) from target languages into the English space, enabling direct application of an English NER model. Finally, the authors combine the outputs of these two approaches using co-decoding schemes.

The primary contributions are:

1. **A heuristic data selection scheme for annotation projection:**  This method aims to filter noisy projection-labeled data by selecting sentence pairs based on frequency analysis of projected entity tags, improving the accuracy of the target-language NER system when alignment quality is low.  This is arguably the most significant contribution, addressing a practical issue in annotation projection.
2. **Representation projection based on embedding mapping:** This approach transfers knowledge by projecting target language word embeddings into the English embedding space, allowing a pre-trained English NER model to be directly applied.
3. **Co-decoding schemes:** These schemes combine the outputs of the annotation projection and representation projection approaches to further improve performance. The rank-based co-decoding scheme seems to provide modest gains.

**Strengths:**

1. **Addresses a relevant and challenging problem:** Cross-lingual NER with limited resources is a crucial area of research. The paper directly tackles the difficulty of acquiring annotated data in multiple languages.
2. **Thorough experimentation:** The paper presents extensive experiments on both in-house and publicly available datasets (CoNLL), covering multiple target languages (Japanese, Korean, German, Portuguese, Spanish, Dutch). This provides strong evidence for the effectiveness of the proposed methods.
3. **Clear explanation of the data selection heuristic:** The data selection scheme based on frequency analysis is well-explained and intuitively appealing.  The reasoning behind using frequency to estimate annotation quality is sound.
4. **Comparison to state-of-the-art:** The paper explicitly compares the proposed methods with existing cross-lingual NER approaches (Täckström et al., 2012; Tsai et al., 2016), demonstrating performance improvements.
5. **Systematic exploration of co-decoding schemes:**  The evaluation of different co-decoding schemes provides insight into how to best combine the strengths of the two projection-based approaches.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited novelty of representation projection:** The representation projection method relies on established techniques like word embeddings and linear mapping. While functional, the approach doesn’t present significant algorithmic innovation beyond applying existing methods to the NER task. The main innovation is demonstrating its efficacy in this context.
2. **Dependence on alignment quality:** The annotation projection approach is heavily reliant on the quality of the alignment between comparable corpora.  Although the data selection scheme mitigates this issue, the performance is still intrinsically tied to alignment accuracy. The paper acknowledges this, but further investigation into more robust alignment strategies could be beneficial.
3. **Complexity of the combined system:**  The pipeline involves multiple components (English NER, alignment, data selection, embedding mapping, co-decoding), making the system potentially complex to implement and tune.  A more streamlined approach could be desirable.
4. **Detailed error analysis is lacking:**  While the results are quantitatively presented, a deeper qualitative error analysis could provide valuable insights into the types of errors made by the system and suggest directions for future improvement. What types of entities are consistently misclassified?  Why does the annotation projection approach struggle with certain languages?



**Questions to Authors:**

1.  Could you elaborate on the specific characteristics of the comparable corpora used for annotation projection (e.g., domain, size, alignment method)?  How sensitive are the results to the quality and characteristics of these corpora?
2.  The data selection scheme uses thresholds 'q' and 'n'. How were these thresholds determined? Was any automatic parameter tuning employed, or were they set empirically?
3.  You mention that the confidence scores of the two systems are not directly comparable.  Did you explore any methods for normalizing or calibrating these confidence scores before applying the co-decoding schemes?
4.  Could you provide some examples of errors made by the system, particularly cases where the co-decoding scheme fails to improve upon the individual systems?




**Additional Comments:**

* The paper is generally well-written and clearly organized.
* The notation is consistent and easy to follow.
* The related work section provides a good overview of the relevant literature.
*  Consider adding a table summarizing the parameters used in the experiments (e.g. embedding dimensions, CRF/MEMM order, NN architectures).
