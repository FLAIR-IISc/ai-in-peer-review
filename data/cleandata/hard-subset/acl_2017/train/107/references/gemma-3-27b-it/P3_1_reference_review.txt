## Review: Weakly Supervised Cross-Lingual Named Entity Recognition via Effective Annotation and Representation Projection

**Summary:**

This paper tackles the challenging problem of cross-lingual Named Entity Recognition (NER) without relying on expensive human annotations in target languages. The authors propose a combination of two weakly supervised approaches: (1) annotation projection with a novel data selection heuristic to mitigate noise from imperfect alignment, and (2) representation projection using cross-lingual word embeddings. Finally, they introduce co-decoding schemes to combine the outputs of these two systems. The paper presents extensive experimental results on both in-house and CoNLL datasets, demonstrating performance improvements over existing cross-lingual NER methods.

**Main Contributions (as I see them):**

1. **Practical Data Selection for Annotation Projection:** The heuristic data selection scheme for noisy comparable corpora is a significant and practical contribution. Addressing alignment inaccuracies in projection-based methods is crucial for real-world applicability, and this approach offers a simple yet effective solution.
2. **Combined System & Co-Decoding:**  The integration of annotation projection and representation projection via co-decoding, and especially the rank-based scheme, shows a clear improvement in performance, demonstrating the benefit of combining diverse weakly-supervised signals.
3. **Comprehensive Empirical Evaluation:**  The paper provides a thorough evaluation across multiple languages and datasets (in-house and CoNLL), offering a solid benchmark for future research.



**Strengths:**

1. **Relevance & Significance:**  Cross-lingual NER is a vital task, and minimizing the need for labeled data is highly desirable. This work directly addresses this need with a practical and well-motivated approach.
2. **Sound Methodology:** The proposed data selection heuristic is logical and well-explained.  The use of frequency-based scoring to assess annotation quality is a reasonable approach given the inherent noise in projection. The representation projection method builds on established techniques (word embeddings and cross-lingual mappings) and applies them effectively to NER.
3. **Strong Empirical Results:** The experimental results consistently demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed methods, particularly the combined system, outperforming established baselines on multiple datasets. The improvements on the CoNLL data are particularly compelling.
4. **Clear Presentation:** The paper is generally well-written and organized. The introduction provides sufficient background, and the individual sections clearly explain the proposed techniques.
5. **Thorough Comparison:** Comparing against Täckström et al. (2012) and Tsai et al. (2016) provides crucial context and demonstrates the advancement of the proposed approaches.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Novelty in Individual Components:** While the *combination* of methods is strong, the individual components (annotation projection and representation projection) are not fundamentally new. The novelty lies primarily in the data selection scheme and the co-decoding strategy. This doesn't invalidate the work, but should be acknowledged in the paper.
2. **Dependence on Alignment Quality:** The annotation projection approach is still heavily reliant on the quality of the alignment between English and the target language. While the data selection helps, the paper doesn't fully explore scenarios with *very* poor alignment, where the benefits of this approach might diminish significantly.  A more detailed analysis of the impact of alignment accuracy on performance would be valuable.
3. **Feature Engineering in MEMM:** The description of the features used in the MEMM model is relatively sparse. It would be helpful to provide more details about the feature set, as feature engineering can significantly influence performance.  Is the limited feature set a deliberate design choice to facilitate cross-lingual transferability, and if so, this should be explicitly stated and justified.
4. **Hyperparameter Tuning:** The paper mentions tuning parameters, but lacks details regarding the tuning process (e.g., search space, evaluation metric).  More transparency here would enhance reproducibility.
5. **Error Analysis:** A more detailed error analysis would be insightful.  What types of errors are still being made by the combined system? Are there specific entity types or linguistic phenomena that are particularly challenging?

**Questions to Authors:**

1.  Could you provide more details about the features used in the MEMM model? Specifically, what types of features were included (e.g., word shape, POS tags, contextual words)?
2.  How sensitive is the performance of the annotation projection approach to the alignment accuracy?  Could you present results on datasets with varying levels of alignment quality, or explore the impact of using different alignment models?
3.  How were the hyperparameters for the data selection scheme (q and n) and the neural network models tuned?  What search space was used, and what evaluation metric was optimized?
4.  Can you provide a more detailed error analysis, identifying the common types of errors made by the combined system?
5.  The paper states a deliberate choice *not* to use gazetteers or character-level representations.  Could you elaborate on this decision, and potentially explore the impact of incorporating these features in a future work?



**Additional Comments:**

*   Consider mentioning related work on noise-robust learning or domain adaptation techniques, as these might be relevant to the annotation projection approach.
*   The use of "unk" vectors for out-of-vocabulary words could be explained more clearly.
*   Minor: There are a few instances of awkward phrasing throughout the paper (e.g., "conflict with entities detected").




**Overall Assessment:**

This is a solid and well-executed paper that makes a valuable contribution to the field of cross-lingual NER. The proposed approach is practical and effective, and the experimental results are convincing. While the individual components aren't groundbreaking, the combination of techniques, along with the thoughtful data selection scheme and co-decoding strategy, represents a significant advance. I recommend acceptance, with minor revisions to address the weaknesses outlined above.
