This paper addresses the challenging problem of weakly supervised cross-lingual NER. The authors propose a combination of techniques: a data selection scheme for annotation projection, a representation projection approach utilizing word embeddings, and co-decoding schemes to combine the outputs of these two approaches.

**Summary and Contributions:**

The primary contribution of this work is a practical and effective combination of annotation projection and representation projection for cross-lingual NER. Specifically, the data selection heuristic for annotation projection, while conceptually simple, demonstrably improves performance when alignment quality is low. Secondly, the exploration of representation projection as a direct transfer method is valuable. Finally, the two proposed co-decoding schemes provide a sensible way to leverage the strengths of both projection methods, improving overall performance. The extensive experimental evaluation on both in-house and CoNLL datasets further strengthens the work.

**Strengths:**

1. **Strong Empirical Results:** The paper presents a comprehensive evaluation across four in-house datasets and three CoNLL datasets. The results consistently demonstrate improvements over individual approaches and, importantly, outperform existing state-of-the-art cross-lingual NER systems (Täckström et al. 2012, Tsai et al. 2016) on the CoNLL test sets. This is a significant strength, providing compelling evidence of the effectiveness of the proposed methods. The improvements, while not massive, are consistent and statistically relevant.

2. **Practical Approach and Heuristic Justification:** The proposed data selection scheme for annotation projection is a practical solution to the problem of noisy projected data. The heuristic, based on frequency counts of projected entities, is well-motivated and easy to implement. The authors acknowledge the importance of alignment accuracy and provide evidence that the data selection scheme mitigates the impact of low alignment quality.

3. **Comprehensive Evaluation of Co-Decoding:** The exploration of two distinct co-decoding schemes—confidence-based and rank-based—is a valuable addition. The observation that the rank-based scheme often outperforms the confidence-based scheme highlights the subtleties of combining outputs from weakly supervised systems and suggests that simply relying on confidence scores can be misleading.

4. **Clear Presentation & Detailed Experiments:** The paper is generally well-written and clearly explains the proposed methods. The experimental setup is detailed, including the size of datasets, model parameters, and evaluation metrics.  The thoroughness of the experimentation contributes significantly to the credibility of the results.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Novelty of Individual Components:** While the *combination* of techniques is valuable, the individual components (annotation projection, representation projection, co-decoding) are not novel in themselves. The novelty lies primarily in the integration and the specific implementation details (e.g., the data selection heuristic). A stronger statement of the paper's contribution as an *engineered system* rather than introducing fundamentally new techniques would be more accurate.

2. **Dependence on External Resources:** The success of the representation projection approach heavily relies on the quality of pre-trained word embeddings and the availability of a translation dictionary. The paper acknowledges this, but a more detailed analysis of the impact of different embedding qualities and dictionary sizes would be beneficial. The limitations of this dependency should be explicitly discussed.

3. **Lack of Error Analysis:** While the paper presents quantitative results, a more in-depth error analysis would provide valuable insights into the types of errors made by each system and the reasons why the co-decoding schemes are effective. Understanding the specific error patterns could guide future improvements. For example, are there specific entity types that are particularly challenging for the projection methods?

4. **Limited Discussion of Computational Cost:** The paper doesn’t discuss the computational cost of the different approaches. For example, how does the training time of the neural network models compare to the CRF/MEMM models? This information would be helpful for assessing the practical feasibility of the proposed methods.



**Questions to Authors:**

1.  Could you provide a more detailed discussion of the limitations of the representation projection approach, particularly regarding its dependence on the quality of word embeddings and the translation dictionary? Specifically, how does performance degrade as the size of the dictionary decreases?
2.  Could you share some examples of the types of errors made by the annotation projection and representation projection systems? This would help to understand the strengths and weaknesses of each approach and the reasons why the co-decoding schemes are effective.
3.  What is the computational cost (training time, inference time) of the different components of the proposed system, particularly the neural network models?
4.  The paper mentions using a phrase table to create the training dictionary for representation projection.  What is the size of these phrase tables, and how does the size of the phrase table affect performance?

**Additional Comments:**

*   The notation in equation (7) seems inconsistent with the description. It’s described as a weighted least squares problem, but the equation looks like it's minimizing a sum of squared errors. Clarification would be helpful.
*   The paper frequently refers to "state-of-the-art" results from Täckström et al. (2012) and Tsai et al. (2016). A quick overview of *how* these methods work within the introduction would make the paper more self-contained.
