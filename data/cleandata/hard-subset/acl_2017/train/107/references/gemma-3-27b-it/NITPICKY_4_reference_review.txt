This paper proposes weakly supervised cross-lingual NER methods, combining annotation projection with a data selection heuristic and representation projection via embedding mapping. The primary contribution lies in the data selection scheme for annotation projection, aiming to mitigate noise from comparable corpora alignment. A secondary contribution is the exploration of representation projection for direct model transfer, and finally, the co-decoding schemes to combine the outputs of the two approaches.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Lack of Novelty in Core Techniques:** While the combination of existing techniques is presented, neither the annotation projection nor the representation projection approaches are fundamentally novel. Annotation projection has been extensively explored (Zitouni & Florian, 2008; Ehrmann et al., 2011), and representation projection leveraging word embeddings is also well-established (Mikolov et al., 2013a,b). The paper doesn't present any *new* algorithmic breakthroughs in these core components. The data selection heuristic is a practical improvement, but not a groundbreaking methodological advancement.

2. **Insufficient Justification for Design Choices:** Several design choices lack sufficient justification. The choice of 300-dimensional embeddings for English and 800 for other languages is stated without rationale. The thresholds 'q' and 'n' in the data selection scheme are treated as tunable parameters, but the paper doesn't provide a detailed analysis of their impact or guidance on selecting appropriate values beyond experimentation. The motivation for NN2’s smoothing layer is stated as empirically improving performance, but a deeper understanding of *why* it works is missing.

3. **Inconsistent Evaluation and Reporting:** The paper reports results on both in-house and CoNLL datasets. However, the in-house data has over 50 entity types while CoNLL has only 4. This makes direct comparison of F1-scores misleading. Furthermore, the size of the training data used for the final systems isn’t consistently reported (e.g., the number of tokens used for training the target-language NER system after applying the data selection scheme is missing). The tables are dense and difficult to quickly interpret.

4. **Limited Error Analysis:** The paper lacks a thorough error analysis. Understanding *where* the systems fail (e.g., specific entity types, linguistic phenomena) would provide valuable insights for future work. The statement that AP has high precision/low recall and NN2 has a more balanced precision/recall is a starting point, but a more detailed investigation is needed.

5. **Formatting and Presentation Issues:** The paper contains several minor formatting inconsistencies. For example, the citation format varies slightly (e.g. (Täckström et al., 2012) vs. (Tsai and Roth, 2016)). Equation (1) is poorly formatted and difficult to read. Additionally, Figure 1 is referenced in the text but is missing from the submitted manuscript.




**Strengths:**

1. **Practical Data Selection Scheme:** The proposed heuristic for data selection in the annotation projection approach is a practical and valuable contribution. It addresses a key challenge in weakly supervised cross-lingual NER—the noise introduced by imperfect alignment in comparable corpora. The experimental results demonstrate a significant improvement in F1-score, particularly for languages with lower alignment accuracy.

2. **Comprehensive Experimental Setup:** The paper presents a relatively comprehensive experimental setup, evaluating the proposed approaches on multiple languages and datasets (both in-house and CoNLL). This allows for a more robust assessment of the methods’ performance and generalizability.

3. **Co-Decoding Improves Performance:** The exploration of co-decoding schemes to combine the strengths of annotation projection and representation projection is a positive aspect. The experimental results suggest that combining the outputs of the two systems can lead to improved performance compared to using either system alone.

**Questions to Authors:**

1. Could you provide a more detailed justification for the choice of embedding dimensions (300 for English, 800 for other languages)?
2. What is the rationale behind the selection of the minimum frequency threshold for creating the training dictionary for representation projection? How sensitive are the results to this threshold?
3. Please clarify the training data sizes (in tokens) for the target-language NER systems *after* applying the data selection scheme for each language.
4. Can you provide a more detailed error analysis, identifying common error types and potential reasons for the errors?
5. Please include Figure 1 in the revised manuscript.



**Additional Comments:**

* The abstract could be more concise and highlight the most significant contributions.
* The related work section could benefit from a more critical discussion of existing approaches and how the proposed methods differ.
* Please address the minor formatting inconsistencies throughout the paper.




**Overall Assessment:**

While the paper presents a reasonable set of experiments and a practical improvement in data selection for annotation projection, the lack of significant methodological novelty and the presence of several weaknesses warrant a **Reject** recommendation. The paper’s contribution is incremental rather than groundbreaking, and the presentation could be substantially improved. Addressing the weaknesses outlined above could potentially strengthen the paper, but in its current form, it does not meet the standards for acceptance at ACL.
