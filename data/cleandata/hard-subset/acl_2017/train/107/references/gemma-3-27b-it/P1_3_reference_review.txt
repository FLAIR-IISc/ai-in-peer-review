## Peer Review: Weakly Supervised Cross-Lingual Named Entity Recognition via Effective Annotation and Representation Projection

**Summary:**

This paper tackles the challenging problem of cross-lingual NER with zero annotation in the target language. It proposes two main approaches: (1) improved annotation projection via a data selection scheme that prioritizes high-quality projected labels, and (2) direct model transfer based on projecting word representations from the target language into the source language’s embedding space. Finally, it explores co-decoding schemes to combine the outputs of these two approaches. The paper demonstrates improvements over existing cross-lingual NER methods on both in-house and CoNLL datasets.

**Main Contributions (as I see them):**

1. **Effective Data Selection for Annotation Projection:** The proposed heuristic for selecting high-quality projected data, based on entity frequency analysis, is a valuable contribution. This addresses a key weakness of traditional annotation projection – the impact of noisy alignments – and demonstrates measurable performance gains.
2. **Empirical Evaluation of Combined Approaches:** The systematic evaluation of combining annotation projection and representation projection through co-decoding provides a practical insight into leveraging different strengths of these approaches.  The results clearly show the benefit of combining the methods.
3. **Comprehensive Experimental Setup:** The thorough experimental setup, including multiple languages, diverse datasets (in-house and CoNLL), and comparison with established baselines, strengthens the validity of the findings.



**Strengths:**

1. **Addresses a Significant Problem:** Cross-lingual NER with limited resources is a crucial area of research. The paper directly addresses this challenge with practical and well-motivated approaches.
2. **Clear and Well-Organized Presentation:** The paper is well-written and logically structured. The explanations of the methods are clear, and the experimental setup is described in detail, making it easy to understand and reproduce the work.
3. **Strong Empirical Results:**  The consistently positive results across multiple languages and datasets demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed methods. The improvements over existing state-of-the-art systems are significant, particularly for the data selection scheme.
4. **Practical Insights:** The analysis of the strengths and weaknesses of each approach (annotation projection vs. representation projection) and the rationale behind the co-decoding strategies provide valuable practical insights for researchers and practitioners in the field.
5. **Thorough Comparison to Baselines:** The comparison to Täckström et al. (2012) and Tsai et al. (2016) provides important context and demonstrates the advancements made by the proposed methods.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Novelty in Individual Components:** While the combination of techniques and the data selection heuristic are valuable, the individual components (annotation projection, representation projection, CRFs, neural networks) are not novel. The core innovation lies in the integration and optimization of existing techniques.  A stronger claim of novelty would benefit from more clearly articulating *how* the representation projection differs fundamentally from existing methods.
2. **Dependency on Alignment Quality (for Annotation Projection):** While the data selection scheme mitigates the impact of noisy alignments, the annotation projection approach remains fundamentally limited by the quality of the initial alignment. This dependency isn’t thoroughly discussed – what are the limits of the data selection when alignments are *very* poor?
3. **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** The paper mentions tuning parameters (q and n for data selection) but doesn’t provide sufficient detail on the tuning process. A more detailed discussion of the sensitivity of the results to hyperparameter choices would be beneficial. It would be useful to see an ablation study evaluating the contribution of each parameter to performance.
4. **Limited Analysis of Error Cases:**  The paper focuses primarily on overall F1 scores. A more detailed error analysis, identifying common types of errors made by each approach, would provide deeper insights into their strengths and weaknesses. What types of entities are most challenging for each method?




**Questions to Authors:**

1. **Regarding Representation Projection:** Could you elaborate on the specific differences between your representation projection approach and existing methods for cross-lingual embedding alignment (e.g., those mentioned in Section 4.4)? What specific design choices contribute to its effectiveness?
2. **Data Selection Thresholds:** What strategies did you employ for determining the optimal values for the 'q' and 'n' thresholds in the data selection scheme?  Was this done via a development set, or another method?  Were different thresholds used for different languages?
3. **Alignment Quality Threshold:** Is there a quantifiable threshold for alignment quality below which the data selection scheme becomes ineffective?  Have you experimented with incorporating alignment confidence scores directly into the data selection process?
4. **Error Analysis:** Could you provide a qualitative error analysis, highlighting common types of errors made by the different systems? This would help understand the limitations of each approach and identify areas for future improvement.



**Additional Comments:**

*   The figures mentioned in the paper (e.g. Figure 1) are not included in the submitted document.
*   The paper uses a lot of citations. While appropriate, it could be slightly condensed to improve readability.




**Overall Recommendation:**

I recommend **acceptance** of this paper. While the individual components aren’t groundbreaking, the effective integration of existing techniques, the insightful data selection heuristic, and the comprehensive experimental evaluation make a valuable contribution to the field of cross-lingual NER. Addressing the weaknesses outlined above in the author response would further strengthen the paper.
