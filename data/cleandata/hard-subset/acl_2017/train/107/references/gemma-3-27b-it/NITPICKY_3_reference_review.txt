This paper proposes weakly supervised cross-lingual NER techniques, combining annotation projection with a novel data selection heuristic and representation projection via cross-lingual word embeddings, further enhanced by co-decoding schemes. The primary contribution is the data selection scheme for annotation projection, intended to mitigate noise from imperfect alignment in comparable corpora. A secondary contribution is the application of representation projection for direct transfer, circumventing the need for retraining in target languages. Finally, the co-decoding schemes attempt to leverage the strengths of both approaches.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Lack of Substantial Novelty:** While the combination of techniques isn't entirely trivial, the individual components – annotation projection, representation projection, and co-decoding – are all well-established concepts in cross-lingual NLP. The data selection heuristic, while potentially helpful, appears relatively simple and lacks a strong theoretical justification beyond its empirical effectiveness. The paper doesn't convincingly demonstrate a novel algorithmic breakthrough.

2. **Insufficient Rigorousness in Experimental Setup:** The experimental setup feels somewhat ad-hoc. The parameters for data selection (q and n) are chosen without clear justification, and the optimal values vary considerably across languages. The choice of neural network architectures (NN1 vs. NN2) and the justification for the smoothing layer in NN2 are insufficiently explained. The hyperparameter tuning process lacks detail, leaving the reader unsure if the reported results are robust or simply lucky.

3. **Overstated Claims & Inconsistent Results:** The claim of outperforming "state-of-the-art" (Täckström et al. and Tsai et al.) is somewhat misleading. The comparisons on the CoNLL dataset are limited to the test set, while the main body of the results focuses on in-house datasets. Furthermore, the performance gains over existing methods aren’t substantial, and in some cases, the improvements are minimal.  The performance difference between NN1 and NN2 is significant, yet not explained in a way that makes the architecture choice obvious or reproducible. Table 3 shows inconsistent gains across languages, raising concerns about generalizability.

4. **Presentation and Detail:** The paper suffers from a lack of polish. The equations are not always clearly explained (e.g., the explanation of equation 1 feels incomplete), and the figures (specifically Figure 1) are not referenced in the text. Minor formatting issues (e.g., inconsistent spacing, hyphenation) further detract from readability.  The description of the alignment models used for annotation projection lacks detail – crucial for understanding the quality of the projected labels.  The impact of dictionary size on embedding mapping is noted, but a proper ablation study would be more informative.

5. **Limited Error Analysis:** There is no error analysis presented. Understanding the types of errors made by each system (and how the co-decoding schemes address these errors) would be invaluable. Knowing *why* the annotation projection system has high precision but low recall, or *why* NN1 sometimes outperforms NN2, would strengthen the paper considerably.




**Strengths:**

1. **Comprehensive Evaluation:** The paper presents results on multiple datasets (in-house and CoNLL) and across several languages, providing a reasonable breadth of evaluation. This allows for some assessment of the generalizability of the proposed techniques.

2. **Practical Focus:** The work addresses a relevant and challenging problem – cross-lingual NER with limited resources. The focus on weakly supervised approaches is appealing given the cost of creating high-quality annotated data for multiple languages.

3. **Clear Structure & Problem Definition:** The paper is generally well-structured and clearly defines the problem being addressed. The introduction provides sufficient background and motivation.

**Questions to Authors:**

1.  Can you provide a more detailed justification for the chosen parameters (q and n) in the data selection scheme?  What sensitivity analysis was performed to ensure the results are not overly dependent on these specific values?
2.  Can you elaborate on the rationale behind the smoothing layer in NN2 and provide an ablation study demonstrating its impact?
3.  What specific characteristics of the comparable corpora (e.g., domain, topic, size) influence the effectiveness of the annotation projection approach, and how can these characteristics be exploited to improve performance?
4.  Provide a more detailed comparison of your work against recent advances in cross-lingual embeddings (beyond Mikolov et al. 2013b), and justify why your approach is competitive.
5.  Can you provide a qualitative error analysis, detailing the types of errors made by each system and how the co-decoding schemes attempt to correct them?



**Additional Comments:**

*   Please provide the code and data used in your experiments to facilitate reproducibility.
*   Correct the minor formatting issues throughout the paper.
*   Improve the clarity and completeness of the descriptions of the alignment models.
*   Figure 1 is not referenced in the text.



**Overall Assessment:**

While the paper presents a reasonably thorough exploration of weakly supervised cross-lingual NER, the lack of substantial novelty, the insufficiently rigorous experimental setup, and the overstated claims lead me to recommend **rejection**. The paper needs significant revisions to address the weaknesses outlined above before it could be considered for acceptance. The contributions, while valuable, do not currently warrant publication in a highly competitive venue like ACL.
