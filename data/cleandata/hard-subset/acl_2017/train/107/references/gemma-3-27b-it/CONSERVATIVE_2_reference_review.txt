This paper explores weakly supervised cross-lingual NER, combining annotation projection with a data selection heuristic and representation projection with embedding mappings. The primary contribution is a practical demonstration of combining these two established techniques, alongside a relatively straightforward, yet effective, data selection method for improving annotation projection quality. A secondary contribution lies in the co-decoding schemes, though these feel less novel.

**Strengths:**

1. **Solid Empirical Evaluation:** The paper presents a fairly thorough empirical evaluation across multiple languages, using both in-house and publicly available datasets (CoNLL). This breadth of experimentation is a significant strength, demonstrating the generalizability of the proposed approach. The consistent reporting of results and clear comparisons to baselines are commendable.
2. **Practical Combination of Established Techniques:** While neither annotation projection nor representation projection is novel, the paper effectively combines them, addressing a common challenge in cross-lingual transfer – leveraging limited resources. The pragmatic approach of building upon existing, well-understood methods is valuable.
3. **Effective Data Selection Heuristic:** The data selection scheme for annotation projection is a sensible and demonstrably effective improvement. The intuition behind prioritizing frequently projected tags is sound, and the results show consistent gains, particularly when alignment accuracy is lower. This adds a practical improvement to an otherwise standard approach.
4. **Clear Presentation:** The paper is generally well-written and clearly organized. The explanations of the different components are understandable, and the experimental setup is detailed enough for replication.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Lack of Novelty:** The core ideas presented – annotation projection, representation projection, and combining different systems – are all well-established in the literature. The paper doesn’t introduce any fundamentally new algorithmic breakthroughs. The improvements are incremental rather than transformative.
2. **Limited Analysis of Representation Projection:** While representation projection is included, the analysis feels superficial. The paper doesn't delve deeply into *why* certain embedding mappings work better than others, or explore alternative mapping strategies.  Simply stating NN2 is better than NN1 lacks sufficient justification, and the role of embedding dimensionality isn’t explored.
3. **Overstated Claims of Outperformance:** The claim of outperforming “state-of-the-art” approaches (Täckström et al., 2012; Tsai et al., 2016) needs qualification. These papers are somewhat dated, and it’s unclear if they represent the current state-of-the-art. A comparison to more recent, strong baselines would strengthen this claim. The improvement in CoNLL results is modest.
4. **Dependency on Parallel Corpora/Dictionaries:** Both approaches rely heavily on the availability of parallel corpora (for alignment in annotation projection) or translation dictionaries (for representation projection). The paper doesn't adequately address scenarios where these resources are scarce or unavailable. This is a significant limitation for real-world application to low-resource languages.

**Questions to Authors:**

1. Could you provide a more detailed discussion of why the rank-based co-decoding scheme outperforms the confidence-based scheme, particularly regarding the observed characteristics of the annotation projection and representation projection systems?
2. How sensitive are the results to the size of the dictionary used for representation projection? A graph showing performance as a function of dictionary size would be helpful.
3.  Considering the reliance on parallel data or dictionaries, what strategies have you considered or might be viable for addressing the challenges in very low-resource scenarios where these resources are severely limited?
4. Can you provide a more up-to-date comparison with more recent cross-lingual NER methods, especially those leveraging pre-trained multilingual language models (e.g., mBERT, XLM-RoBERTa)?



**Additional Comments:**

*   The description of the NER models (CRFs, MEMMs, Neural Networks) feels somewhat lengthy and standard. A more concise overview would suffice, focusing instead on the specific configurations used in the experiments.
*   The paper would benefit from a discussion of error analysis. What types of errors are most common with each approach, and where do the co-decoding schemes provide the greatest improvements?
