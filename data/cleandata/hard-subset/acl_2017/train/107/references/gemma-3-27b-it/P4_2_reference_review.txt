This paper tackles the challenging problem of weakly supervised cross-lingual NER, aiming to transfer knowledge from a resource-rich English NER system to new languages without relying on human annotation in the target languages. The authors propose two primary approaches: annotation projection with a novel data selection scheme, and representation projection using cross-lingual word embeddings. They further introduce co-decoding schemes to combine the outputs of these two approaches.

**Main Contributions:**

1. **Heuristic Data Selection for Annotation Projection:** The proposed data selection scheme for annotation projection, leveraging frequency-based analysis of projected entities, demonstrably improves the accuracy of the target language NER system, particularly when alignment accuracy is low. This addresses a known weakness of annotation projection approaches.
2. **Representation Projection via Embedding Mapping:** The application of cross-lingual word embedding mappings for direct model transfer presents a viable alternative to annotation projection. This enables application of the English NER system directly to target languages without retraining.
3. **Co-Decoding Schemes:** The co-decoding schemes, particularly the rank-based approach, effectively integrate the strengths of both projection-based systems, leading to further performance gains. This shows a good understanding of the complementary nature of the two approaches.

**Strengths:**

1. **Comprehensive Experimental Evaluation:** The paper presents a thorough experimental evaluation on both in-house and publicly available datasets (CoNLL). The evaluation across multiple languages (Japanese, Korean, German, Portuguese, Spanish, Dutch) strengthens the generalizability of the findings.
2. **Clear Problem Definition and Motivation:** The introduction clearly articulates the problem of low-resource NER and motivates the proposed weakly supervised approaches. The discussion of existing approaches (annotation projection and direct model transfer) provides valuable context.
3. **Practical Improvements to Existing Techniques:** The data selection scheme for annotation projection and the co-decoding schemes are practical enhancements to established techniques, making the contributions immediately useful. The clear explanation of why the rank-based co-decoding performs well based on the properties of the two source systems is insightful.
4. **Detailed Implementation and Model Descriptions:** The paper provides sufficient details regarding the implementation of the NER models (CRFs, MEMMs, Neural Networks) and the cross-lingual embedding mappings, allowing for reproducibility.
5. **Performance Gains:** The reported performance gains over state-of-the-art cross-lingual NER approaches on the CoNLL dataset are significant and demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed methods.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Novelty in Core Techniques:** While the combination and refinement of existing techniques are valuable, the core components (annotation projection, word embeddings, neural networks) are not novel. The primary contribution lies in their effective application and integration.
2. **Dependence on External Resources:** The representation projection approach relies on the quality of the phrase tables used to build the cross-lingual word embedding mappings. The impact of phrase table quality on performance isn’t fully explored or discussed.
3. **Hyperparameter Tuning Details:** The paper mentions tuning parameters (e.g., q and n in the data selection scheme) but lacks detailed information on the tuning process. More insight into how these parameters were chosen and their impact on performance would be beneficial.
4. **Error Analysis:** The paper lacks detailed error analysis. Understanding the types of errors made by the system (e.g., false positives, false negatives, entity boundary errors) would provide valuable insights for future improvements.



**Questions to Authors:**

1. How sensitive are the performance gains from the data selection scheme to the quality of the initial alignment between the English and target language corpora?
2. What is the impact of using different methods for generating cross-lingual word embedding mappings (e.g., using different dictionaries, different alignment algorithms)?
3. Could you provide more details on the parameter tuning process, specifically for the data selection thresholds (q and n)? Did you use a development set for tuning, and what criteria did you use to select the optimal values?
4. Can you share some examples of cases where the co-decoding scheme successfully corrects errors made by the individual projection-based systems?



**Additional Comments:**

*   The notation could be made more consistent throughout the paper.
*   A table summarizing the sizes of the training data for each language would be helpful.
*   Consider providing ablation studies for the co-decoding schemes to isolate the contribution of each component.
