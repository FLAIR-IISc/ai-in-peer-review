This paper explores weakly supervised cross-lingual NER, combining annotation projection with a novel data selection heuristic and representation projection via embedding mapping, culminating in co-decoding schemes. The primary contribution is a practical, if incremental, combination of established techniques – annotation projection and representation learning – to address a well-defined problem. A secondary contribution is the empirical evaluation across multiple languages and datasets, providing a reasonably comprehensive assessment of the proposed methods. Finally, the data selection heuristic for annotation projection is a modest improvement over naive projection.

**Strengths:**

1. **Comprehensive Evaluation:** The paper presents results on a diverse set of languages (Japanese, Korean, German, Portuguese, Spanish, Dutch) and datasets (in-house and CoNLL), demonstrating the generalizability of the proposed approaches. This breadth of experimentation is commendable and allows for a more robust assessment than focusing on a single language pair.
2. **Sound Methodology (Mostly):** The core techniques – CRFs, neural networks, word embeddings, alignment-based projection – are well-established. The paper doesn't introduce fundamentally new algorithms; rather, it intelligently combines existing ones. This reliance on proven methods increases confidence in the results.
3. **Practical Focus:** The paper tackles a practical problem – cross-lingual NER with limited resources. The focus on weakly supervised learning and avoiding reliance on expensive human annotation is valuable. The co-decoding schemes represent a reasonable effort to improve performance by leveraging complementary strengths of different approaches.
4. **Clear Presentation:** The paper is generally well-written and logically organized. The descriptions of the models and methods are clear and concise, making it relatively easy to follow the proposed approach.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Lack of Novelty:** The paper’s contributions are largely incremental. While the combination of techniques is sensible, none of the individual components are novel. The data selection heuristic, while helpful, feels like a fairly straightforward application of frequency-based filtering and doesn’t represent a significant algorithmic breakthrough. The embedding projection is a standard technique.
2. **Insufficient Justification for Design Choices:** Several design choices lack strong justification. For example, the rationale for choosing specific neural network architectures (NN1 and NN2) is weak, relying primarily on empirical observation. Similarly, the choice of thresholds for the data selection scheme is not adequately explained. More rigorous analysis and ablation studies would strengthen the paper.
3. **Limited Error Analysis:** The paper lacks a thorough error analysis. Understanding *where* the systems fail – types of entities misidentified, specific linguistic phenomena causing errors – would provide valuable insights and guide future improvements. Simply reporting F1 scores is insufficient.
4. **Dependence on External Resources:** The performance heavily relies on the quality of the alignment models and phrase tables used for projection. The paper acknowledges this but doesn't sufficiently discuss the potential impact of alignment errors on the results. A more detailed analysis of alignment quality and its correlation with NER performance is needed.
5. **Supervised Baseline Discrepancy:** The reported supervised baseline results vary significantly across languages. While not necessarily a flaw, it makes comparison with the weakly supervised results less straightforward. A more consistent supervised baseline would provide a clearer benchmark.



**Questions to Authors:**

1. Can you provide a more detailed explanation of the rationale behind the choice of NN1 and NN2 architectures? Were ablation studies performed to evaluate the contribution of the smoothing layer in NN2?
2. How sensitive are the results to the thresholds (q and n) used in the data selection scheme? Did you explore alternative methods for determining these thresholds, such as grid search or optimization based on a development set?
3. Can you provide a more detailed error analysis, identifying common error patterns and the types of entities that are most difficult to recognize?
4. What is the estimated alignment accuracy for each language pair used in the annotation projection approach? How does the alignment accuracy correlate with the performance of the NER system?
5. Could you explain the specific details of the phrase table used for embedding alignment? How was the phrase table created and what are its characteristics?



**Additional Comments:**

The reference to "state-of-the-art cross-lingual NER approaches proposed in Täckström et al. (2012) and Tsai et al. (2016)" feels somewhat weak, as those approaches are several years old. A more recent comparison with current SOTA methods would be beneficial. I suggest a more in-depth discussion of the limitations of the proposed approach and potential directions for future research.
