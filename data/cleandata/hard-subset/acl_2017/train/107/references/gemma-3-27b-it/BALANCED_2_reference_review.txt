This paper tackles the important problem of cross-lingual NER with no human annotation in the target language. The authors propose a combination of approaches: a data selection scheme for annotation projection and a representation projection method. They further introduce co-decoding schemes to combine the outputs of these two methods. The paper is well-written and clearly explains the motivation, methods, and experiments.

**Contributions:**

1. **Effective Data Selection for Annotation Projection:** The heuristic data selection scheme demonstrably improves the accuracy of cross-lingual NER when using annotation projection, particularly in low-resource scenarios with noisy comparable corpora. This is a practical and significant contribution, as annotation projection is a common starting point for cross-lingual transfer.
2. **Representation Projection with Word Embeddings:**  The approach of projecting word embeddings to enable direct model transfer is a reasonable and well-executed technique. While representation projection isn't novel in NLP, applying it specifically to cross-lingual NER and showing its effectiveness within a combined system is valuable.
3. **Co-Decoding Schemes for Combining Views:** The proposed co-decoding schemes, particularly the rank-based approach, demonstrate a practical way to leverage the complementary strengths of the different projection methods.  The recognition that annotation projection has high precision and representation projection has better recall, and designing the combination accordingly, is a thoughtful addition.

**Strengths:**

1. **Comprehensive Evaluation:** The authors evaluate their methods on both in-house and publicly available datasets (CoNLL), covering multiple target languages. This provides strong evidence for the generalizability of their approach.
2. **Clear Explanation of Methods:** The paper is very well-written and the proposed methods are explained clearly and concisely, with sufficient detail for replication. The inclusion of the frequency table example in Section 3.1 is particularly helpful.
3. **Practicality of Approach:** The combination of annotation projection with data selection, coupled with representation projection, offers a pragmatic solution for cross-lingual NER in realistic scenarios where high-quality parallel data is scarce.  The reliance on readily available resources (word embeddings, comparable corpora) is a strength.
4. **Strong Baselines & Comparisons:** Comparing against existing state-of-the-art cross-lingual NER approaches (Täckström et al., 2012 and Tsai et al., 2016) and including supervised learning baselines provides a clear benchmark of performance.
5. **Ablation Study through Co-decoding:** Examining different co-decoding schemes offers insight into the complementary strengths of each projection method.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Novelty in Individual Components:** While the combination is effective, the individual components (annotation projection, representation projection) are not groundbreaking innovations.  The paper's strength lies in the integration and the data selection heuristic, rather than a novel algorithmic breakthrough.
2. **Dependency on Alignment Quality:** The annotation projection component remains heavily dependent on the quality of the alignment between the source and target languages. While the data selection scheme mitigates this issue, the method still struggles when alignment is poor.  A more robust alignment approach or alternative strategies for dealing with noisy alignments would be beneficial.
3. **Parameter Tuning & Sensitivity:**  The data selection scheme introduces parameters (q and n) that require tuning.  The paper mentions tuning these parameters, but a more detailed discussion of the sensitivity of the results to these parameters would be helpful.  How robust are the results to different settings of q and n?
4. **Lack of Error Analysis:**  A more in-depth error analysis could provide valuable insights into the types of errors made by each system and the reasons why the co-decoding scheme is effective.  For example, are certain entity types more challenging to transfer than others?
5. **Limited Discussion of Computational Cost:** The paper does not discuss the computational cost of training and applying the different models, particularly the representation projection component which involves learning embedding mappings.




**Questions to Authors:**

1. How sensitive are the results to the choice of threshold parameters (q and n) in the data selection scheme?  Can you provide a more detailed analysis of the effect of these parameters on performance?
2.  Could you elaborate on the alignment methods used and the characteristics of the alignment quality for each language pair? How does alignment quality directly correlate with the performance gains observed with the data selection scheme?
3. What is the computational cost of learning the cross-lingual embedding mappings, particularly for languages with limited resources?
4.  Can you provide examples of errors made by each system (annotation projection, representation projection, and the combined system) to illustrate the benefits of the co-decoding scheme?

**Additional Comments:**

* The formatting of Table 2 is slightly awkward; it may be more readable to include column headers for each configuration (e.g., “(q=0, n=0)”, “(q=0.8, n=4)”).
* The discussion of related work is somewhat limited. A more comprehensive overview of existing cross-lingual NER techniques would be helpful.



**Overall:**

This is a solid and well-executed paper that presents a practical and effective approach to cross-lingual NER. While the individual components are not novel, the combination, data selection heuristic, and co-decoding schemes offer a valuable contribution to the field. The comprehensive evaluation and clear writing make this paper a strong candidate for acceptance. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions to address the weaknesses identified above.
