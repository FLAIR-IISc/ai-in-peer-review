This paper proposes a weakly supervised cross-lingual NER system combining annotation projection with a data selection scheme and representation projection. It further introduces co-decoding schemes to integrate the outputs of these two approaches. The contributions, as I see them, are: 1) A heuristic data selection scheme for annotation projection that demonstrably improves performance when alignment accuracy is low. 2) A representation projection approach utilizing word embeddings to facilitate direct model transfer without retraining in the target language. 3) Exploration of co-decoding schemes to combine the outputs of the two proposed approaches, leading to improved overall performance.

**Strengths:**

1. **Practical Improvement in Annotation Projection:** The data selection scheme for annotation projection is a significant strength. The authors correctly identify that noisy projection data can negatively impact performance, and their heuristic based on frequency analysis provides a practical method for mitigating this issue. The experimental results consistently demonstrate improvements across multiple languages when using the data selection scheme, particularly when alignment accuracy is low. This addresses a key challenge in annotation projection and increases its applicability in real-world scenarios.
2. **Comprehensive Evaluation:** The paper presents a thorough evaluation of the proposed methods.  The experiments cover multiple languages (Japanese, Korean, German, Portuguese, Spanish, Dutch) and utilize both in-house and standard CoNLL datasets. This broad evaluation provides strong evidence for the generalizability of the approach and allows for meaningful comparisons to existing methods.  The inclusion of both development and test set results (for CoNLL) is commendable.
3. **Clear Presentation of Models & Schemes:** The paper clearly describes the underlying models (CRFs, MEMMs, neural networks) and the proposed co-decoding schemes. The explanations are concise yet sufficient for understanding the methodology. The inclusion of architecture diagrams (for neural networks) and illustrative tables (frequency table for data selection) enhance clarity.
4. **Outperformance of Baselines:** The paper successfully demonstrates that the proposed system outperforms state-of-the-art cross-lingual NER approaches (Täckström et al., 2012; Tsai et al., 2016) on the CoNLL test data, providing evidence of the novelty and effectiveness of the approach.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Novelty in Core Techniques:** While the *combination* of techniques is valuable, the individual components (annotation projection, representation projection, neural networks for NER) are not novel in themselves. The core innovation lies in the data selection heuristic and the co-decoding schemes, but a stronger claim of novelty regarding these specific components would be beneficial. The paper would be strengthened by a more detailed comparison of the proposed data selection scheme to existing data selection strategies in related fields.
2. **Hyperparameter Tuning Details:** The paper mentions tuning parameters for the data selection scheme (q and n) but lacks specifics regarding the tuning process.  It is unclear how these parameters were chosen for each language and whether a systematic search was conducted. Providing details about the parameter tuning methodology (e.g., grid search, validation set performance) would increase reproducibility and confidence in the results.
3. **Detailed Error Analysis is Missing:** Although the authors observe general trends in the performance of different components (e.g., annotation projection having high precision, low recall), a more detailed error analysis is lacking. Identifying common error types and analyzing their causes could provide valuable insights for future improvements. For example, are there specific entity types that are consistently misidentified? Are errors related to specific alignment issues?
4. **Dependence on Phrase Table Quality:** The representation projection approach relies heavily on the quality of the phrase table used to create the initial dictionary. The impact of phrase table quality on the performance of representation projection is not fully explored. A sensitivity analysis evaluating performance with varying phrase table sizes or quality metrics would be informative.



**Questions to Authors:**

1.  Could you provide more details on the parameter tuning process for the data selection scheme (q and n)? What metrics were used to evaluate performance during tuning, and how were the optimal values chosen for each language?
2.  What is the impact of phrase table quality (size, alignment confidence) on the performance of the representation projection approach? Have you performed any analysis to assess the sensitivity of the method to phrase table variations?
3.  Can you elaborate on the types of errors made by each component of the system (annotation projection, representation projection)? A more detailed error analysis would help understand the strengths and weaknesses of each approach.
4.  How does the performance of the proposed co-decoding schemes vary with different weighting schemes or confidence thresholds? Have you explored alternative methods for combining the outputs of the two projection-based systems?

**Additional Comments:**

*   The paper is generally well-written and easy to follow.
*   Consider adding a discussion of potential limitations of the approach, such as the dependence on parallel or comparable corpora and the challenges of handling low-resource languages.
*   The citation formatting appears inconsistent in a few places. Please ensure consistency throughout the manuscript.
