This paper addresses the challenging problem of weakly supervised cross-lingual NER, leveraging both annotation projection and representation projection techniques. The authors propose a data selection scheme for annotation projection and a method for projecting word embeddings to facilitate model transfer. Finally, they present two co-decoding schemes to combine the outputs of these approaches.

**Summary and Contributions:**

The primary contribution of this work is a practical and effective combination of annotation projection with informed data selection, and representation projection for cross-lingual NER. While individual components (annotation projection, representation projection, and combining outputs) have been explored previously, the specific data selection heuristic for annotation projection and the empirical demonstration of combining these approaches to outperform existing methods represents the most significant contribution. A secondary contribution is the thorough experimental evaluation on both in-house and publicly available datasets (CoNLL), demonstrating the effectiveness of the proposed methods across multiple languages. A third, less significant, contribution is the investigation of different co-decoding strategies.

**Strengths:**

1. **Strong Empirical Results:** The paper provides comprehensive experimental results on multiple languages and datasets (in-house and CoNLL). The reported improvements over existing cross-lingual NER approaches, particularly on the CoNLL datasets (Table 5), demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed methods. The consistent performance gains across different languages strengthen the generalizability of the approach.
2. **Practical Data Selection Heuristic:** The proposed data selection scheme for annotation projection (Section 3.1) is a practical and intuitive approach to address the noisy nature of projected data. The use of frequency scores to assess annotation quality is a sensible strategy, and the experimental results (Table 2) clearly show the benefits of this scheme, especially when alignment accuracy is low.
3. **Comprehensive Evaluation of Co-Decoding Schemes:**  The exploration of both confidence-based and rank-based co-decoding schemes, and the observation that rank-based schemes are more effective, offer valuable insights into combining different projection-based systems. This demonstrates a careful consideration of how to best leverage the strengths of each individual approach.
4. **Clear Presentation of Models:** The paper provides a relatively clear description of the underlying NER models (CRFs, MEMMs, and neural networks) and the projection methods, allowing for a good understanding of the proposed system.  The inclusion of details regarding implementation choices (e.g., smoothing layer in NN2) is helpful.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Novelty in Core Techniques:**  Both annotation projection and representation projection are established techniques in cross-lingual NLP. The novelty lies in the specific combination and refinement of these techniques, rather than in fundamentally new algorithmic breakthroughs. The paper would benefit from a more nuanced discussion of how this work advances beyond existing methods beyond simply achieving better performance.
2. **Dependency on Alignment Quality:** While the data selection scheme mitigates the impact of noisy alignment, the performance of the annotation projection approach remains heavily dependent on the quality of the initial alignment between source and target languages. This dependency is acknowledged but could be further addressed with a more thorough error analysis of alignment-related failures.
3. **Hyperparameter Tuning Details:** The paper mentions tuning parameters for the data selection scheme (q and n), but lacks detailed information on the specific tuning process used. Providing details about the validation set and search strategy would enhance reproducibility and understanding.
4. **Limited Analysis of Failure Cases:** While results are presented, a more in-depth analysis of the types of errors made by the system would be valuable. Understanding *why* the system fails in certain cases could lead to further improvements. For example, are there specific entity types or languages where the performance is particularly poor?

**Questions to Authors:**

1. Could you elaborate on the specific criteria used for selecting the training dictionary used for cross-lingual embedding mapping? What effect does dictionary size have on performance, and how was the optimal size determined?
2. The paper mentions that neural network model NN2 with the smoothing layer generally performs better. Could you provide more insight into *why* this is the case? What specific properties of the smoothing layer contribute to the improved performance?
3.  What is the computational cost associated with the data selection scheme?  Is the added computational complexity significant, especially for large comparable corpora?
4. You state the CoNLL English training data size as "~200K tokens". Could you please provide the exact token count?



**Additional Comments:**

* The notation in equation (5) could be clarified.  ‘n(y)’ represents the number of entities in sentence ‘y’, but the summation is over entities ‘e’ *in* ‘y’, which is confusing.
* The formatting of the tables is slightly inconsistent (e.g. different decimal precision).
* The introduction could benefit from a more focused statement of the paper’s specific contribution, rather than a general overview of the challenges in cross-lingual NER.
