## Peer Review: Weakly Supervised Cross-Lingual Named Entity Recognition via Effective Annotation and Representation Projection

**Summary:**

This paper presents a weakly supervised approach to cross-lingual NER, aiming to transfer knowledge from English to other languages without requiring target language annotations. The authors explore two main techniques: (1) annotation projection with a novel data selection scheme to mitigate noise from comparable corpora, and (2) representation projection based on cross-lingual word embeddings.  Finally, they propose co-decoding schemes to combine the outputs of both systems.  The work is evaluated on both in-house and CoNLL datasets for multiple languages, demonstrating improvements over existing cross-lingual NER methods.

**Main Contributions (as I see them):**

1. **Effective Data Selection for Annotation Projection:** The heuristic data selection scheme for noisy comparable corpora is a practical and impactful contribution. Addressing alignment inaccuracies is crucial for annotation projection, and the proposed method demonstrably improves performance.
2. **Combination of Two Distinct Transfer Approaches:** Combining annotation projection and representation projection via co-decoding offers a more robust and accurate solution than relying on either technique in isolation. This provides a useful framework for leveraging complementary strengths.
3. **Comprehensive Experimental Evaluation:**  The evaluation across multiple languages and datasets (including both in-house and publicly available data) provides a strong validation of the proposed methods.



**Strengths:**

1. **Practicality & Relevance:** The problem addressed – building NER systems for low-resource languages – is a highly relevant and important challenge in NLP. The weakly supervised approach is well-motivated given the cost of manual annotation.
2. **Sound Methodology:** Both the data selection heuristic and the representation projection approach are based on established principles (statistical modeling, word embeddings) but are adapted and applied effectively to the cross-lingual NER task. The careful consideration of alignment accuracy in annotation projection is particularly commendable.
3. **Strong Empirical Results:** The reported results consistently show improvements over existing methods, particularly on the in-house datasets where the authors demonstrate substantial gains.  The CoNLL results are competitive and show the methods generalize well.
4. **Clear Presentation:** The paper is generally well-written and clearly explains the proposed methods. The inclusion of specific details regarding implementation (e.g., neural network architectures) and experimental setup enhances reproducibility.
5. **Thorough Evaluation of Co-Decoding Strategies**: The exploration of different co-decoding schemes demonstrates a thoughtful effort to maximize the benefits of combining the two projection-based approaches.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Novelty of Individual Components:** While the *combination* is effective, the individual components (annotation projection, representation projection) are not fundamentally novel. Existing literature extensively covers these techniques. The paper's strength lies in the adaptation and integration, rather than groundbreaking new algorithms.
2. **Dependency on External Resources:** The performance of the representation projection relies heavily on the quality of the phrase table used to create the cross-lingual embeddings. The paper doesn’t deeply discuss the impact of phrase table quality or explore alternatives to phrase tables for building cross-lingual mappings.
3. **Lack of Ablation Studies:** While the CoNLL results show the final combined system is strong, it would be helpful to see ablation studies that isolate the contributions of each component (e.g., data selection, specific neural network architecture in representation projection) to better understand their individual impact. 
4. **Limited Error Analysis:** The paper lacks detailed error analysis.  Understanding *where* the systems fail (e.g., specific entity types, ambiguous cases) would provide valuable insights for future improvement.  
5. **Parameter Sensitivity**: The paper briefly mentions tuning parameters for the data selection scheme (q and n). A more in-depth discussion on the sensitivity of the models to these and other hyperparameters (e.g. embedding dimension, alignment thresholds) would be valuable.



**Questions to Authors:**

1. **Phrase Table Impact:** Could you elaborate on the impact of phrase table size and quality on the representation projection results? Have you experimented with alternative methods for building cross-lingual embeddings that don’t rely on phrase tables (e.g., adversarial training)?
2. **Data Selection Parameter Tuning:** What strategies were employed to tune the parameters ‘q’ and ‘n’ in the data selection scheme?  Did you observe significant variations in performance across languages depending on the chosen thresholds?
3. **Error Analysis Details:** Could you provide a brief error analysis, highlighting common error types made by the systems? This would help pinpoint areas for future improvement.
4. **Computational Cost:** What is the computational cost of training and applying the proposed methods, especially the representation projection step, compared to supervised training?
5. **Generalizability to other languages:** Given that performance is significantly impacted by alignment accuracy, how does the proposed approach scale to languages with lower-quality alignment resources?



**Additional Comments:**

*   Consider adding a table summarizing the size of the training data used for each language and method.
*   The paper could benefit from a discussion of potential limitations and future research directions (e.g., incorporating character-level information, exploring more sophisticated co-decoding schemes).




**Overall Assessment:**

This is a solid and well-executed paper that presents a practical and effective approach to cross-lingual NER. While the individual components aren't revolutionary, the intelligent combination and careful experimental validation make it a valuable contribution to the field. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions addressing the weaknesses outlined above.
